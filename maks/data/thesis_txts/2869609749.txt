Abstract
Despite the growing demand for electrical energy, building­integrated photovoltaics (BIPV)
currently represent only a fraction of the photovoltaic (PV) market due to regulatory gaps and
compliance challenges. Although BIPV offers a viable solution for integrating PV with building
components, thereby increasing the energy independence of buildings, the initial costs and
the need to meet both IEC and ISO standards pose significant impediments to the wide
adoption of this technology.
Furthermore, a standardized procedure for assessing surface
appearance and glare potential is needed, as certain surfaces appear more reflective, leading
to visual discomfort in urban areas.
This thesis maps the implementation and characterization of a hemispherical goniometer
using a robotic arm in an existing laboratory setup for measuring the spectral bidirectional
reflectance distribution function (BRDF). The initial setup measures the reflectance properties
of BIPV materials in a single plane. The improved setup, however, extends this capability to
full hemispherical measurements, which can be used for digital rendering, assessing material
isotropy, and determining the glare risk of reflective surfaces.
The calibrated setup demonstrates its measurement capabilities on four non­colored BIPV
modules with different surface textures.
All surfaces are characterized by distinct
appearances. Some have symmetrical BRDF distributions with respect to the incident light,
while others exhibit more anisotropic characteristics. The measurements indicate much lower
BRDF values for BIPV samples with satin­finished, semi­textured, and deeply textured glass,
and an overall lower risk of glare than reference PV glass.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
In recent years, the research of building­integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) has been increasing
due to the ability to combine solar photovolatics (PV) technology with building elements like
façades, windows, and roofs [1]. According to International Energy Agency (IEA) [2], BIPV
play a substantial role in the adoption of renewable energy.
Although BIPV have growth
potential, their market position is limited compared to the broader PV market, with estimates
ranging from 300 MW to 500 MW in Europe and approximately 2 GW worldwide [3].
IEA [3] states, that manufacturers currently need to perform tests according to both IEC
standards for electrical components and ISO standards for building materials.
This dual
requirement can result in higher costs, delays, and market unpredictability. Therefore, the wide
adaptation of BIPV depends on the development of a well­defined and focused standardization
framework, which takes into account quality, reliability, performance, and safety.
When developing BIPV modules, it is essential to consider not only efficiency and modularity
but also the optical properties of the modules. This involves carefully selecting the glass to
enhance architectural design and improve human visual comfort, as highly reflective and
specular surfaces can cause glare in urban areas [4].
The widespread use and social acceptance of BIPV systems require the development of
methods for evaluating their visual integration and energy impacts [5]. Wilson et al. [5] and
Babin et al. [6] agree that the visual appearance of BIPV modules can be assessed using the
bidirectional
reflectance
distribution
function
(BRDF)
with
a
gonioreflectometer
or
a
goniophotometer.
Hence, by assessing the angular­dependent appearance and electrical performance of BIPV
materials, more efficient, visually appealing, cost­effective, and safe BIPV components can be
developed.
1.2
Research Objectives
The goal of this thesis is to extend the measurement capabilities of the reflective properties of
BIPV materials by transitioning from single plane to full hemispherical measurements through
the integration of a robot arm. Currently, there is an existing experimental gonioreflectometer
setup at DTU Electro, which is used for single plane spectral BRDF measurements; however,
it does not allow for hemispherical measurements. By conducting hemispherical rather than
single plane measurements, more in­depth light scattering behaviors can be observed.
This involves defining necessary rotations for hemispherical BRDF, manufacturing essential
parts, integrating the robot arm with the current setup, making a control program and
calibrating the system.
The upgraded system is tested with four BIPV samples aiming to
characterize their reflective properties and glare risk.

The quantities radiant flux Φ, irradiance E, and radiant exitance M are scalar quantities,
representing only the magnitude.
In contrast, radiant intensity lΩand radiance Lλ,Ωalso
depend on direction [7].
Radiant flux Φ refers to the amount of energy that flows through a surface or a specific area in
space per unit time. It pertains to the total power that is received, transmitted, emitted, or
reflected. Irradiance E is the measure of radiant flux per unit area at a specific point on a
surface and radiant exitance M refers to the received power emitted by a surface.
Radiance LΩrepresents the amount of radiant flux that is emitted, reflected, transmitted, or
received by a surface, measured per unit solid angle and per unit projected area. Whereas
the radiant intensity lΩmeasures only the radiant flux per unit solid angle.
2.1.2
Reflection Types
When light interacts with a material, a complex interaction occurs, determined by the physical
properties of the light and the characteristics of the material. Upon hitting a surface, light may
be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted. The degree of light reflection or transmission varies
based on the positions of the observer and light source relative to the surface’s normal ⃗N and
tangent [8].
Reflections of light­surface interactions can be classified into specular reflectance and
transmittance, and diffuse reflectance and transmittance [7].
Both types of reflections are
visualized in Figure 2.1, where θi represents the angle of incidence and θr represents the
angle of reflection. Alternatively, θr can also be referred to as the viewing angle.
Specular reflection occurs when all parallel incident rays are reflected and transmitted as
parallel rays. The angles of reflection are equal to the angles of incidence for all reflected
rays. Similarly, the angles of transmission either match the angles of incidence or adhere to

2.1.5
BRDF classification
BRDF can be classified into two categories which are anisotropic and isotropic [12]. According
to [8], the most real­world BRDFs are likely more isotropic, although many surfaces have
subtle anisotropy.
Isotropic
Isotropic reflectance properties refer to a surface whose reflectance is unvarying with respect
to surface rotation around ⃗N. An example of such a material is smooth plastic [8].
Anisotropic
Anisotropic reflectance properties refer to a surface whose reflectance changes with respect
to surface rotation around ⃗N. Examples of anisotropic materials include brushed metal [14]
and wood [15].
2.1.6
Glare Risk
Glare occurs due to an improper range or distribution of luminance, or from significant
luminance contrasts. This can either lead to discomfort glare, which causes discomfort without

significantly affecting the ability to see objects, or disability glare, which reduces the ability to
see objects clearly without necessarily causing discomfort [16]. In addition, exposure of the
retina to high irradiance can cause short­term or long­term eye injury, including degeneration
or blindness [17].
Glare can be caused by reflection, mainly its specular component, which occurs when the
rays of the sun are reflected into the field of view. In order to prevent glare when developing
photovoltaic modules, various techniques of surface treatment including anti­reflective (AR)
coatings, glass satination, or glass texturing are developed [6].
When comparing the glare risk for different PV installation types, the risk is much greater for
BIPV than for building applied photovoltaics (BAPV). This is due to the angle and tilt at which
the modules are installed [6].
By measuring BRDF at various incidence angles over the sample’s hemisphere, the resulting
total BRDF can be compared to the threshold values introduced in [6], thereby evaluating the
glare risk.

State of the Art in Gonioreflectometry and BIPV
A gonioreflectometer is used to measure the spectral reflectance of surfaces, and it can
assess both specular and diffuse reflectance. Such device consists of a light source, a sample
material placed on a goniometer, and a detector [12]. Each setup can vary based on the
choice of light source, sensor, spectrometer, and the number of degrees of freedom (DOF) of
the rotated sample.
While gonioreflectometry has been used in the BIPV field, it is more commonly applied in
areas such as remote sensing [18] and computer graphics [12].
Goniometers capable of performing the rotations required for hemispherical measurements
using the BRDF function have been developed.
Some feature motorized turntables [19],
whereas others use robotic arms [20], [21].
In the study by Li et al. [19], an instrument and methodology for acquiring hemispherical BRDF
measurements were developed. The setup was demonstrated by measuring an anisotropic
textile sample at various incidence angles across most of its hemisphere. The resulting data
indicated
reflectances,
including
several
reflection
peaks,
off­plane
reflections,
and
backscattering.
Similarly, research conducted by Zhiyuan et al. [20] focused on the development of a
high­accuracy gonioreflectometer for BRDF measurements, specifically for measurements of
a solar diffuser.
The study measured the uncertainty budget of the BRDF measurements,
which is key for the precise radiometric calibration of optical remote sensors on satellites

during on­orbit calibration. Although the applications of the proposed goniometer in this thesis

A study by Wilson et al. [5] discusses the appearance of BIPV modules, which depends on
various factors such as the observer’s position, the optical properties of their components, and

The study classifies the BRDF method as a suitable means of

products would provide a more detailed examination.
Overall, the insights provided by the mentioned studies can be utilized to develop a goniometer
that extends the measuring capabilities of the existing setup. The new gonioreflectometer can
then be used for BIPV research, including the assessment of surface appearance, various
coloration techniques, glare risk, and angular­dependent electrical performance.

3.1
BRDF Measurement Setup Overview
The measurement system is based on an experimental setup which was previously improved
and characterized by Babin [10]. The main difference in the new system is the use of a robotic
arm as a goniometer. The goniometer can rotate around three axes, unlike the former in­plane
goniometer, which could only rotate around one axis. The new goniometer allows the sample
to be rotated out of plane, enabling hemispherical measurements.
The experimental setup consists of the goniometer, a collimated light source, a sensor on a
rotation stage and a spectrometer.
The goniometer, rotation stage and spectrometer are
controlled with a lab desktop running a Python script that allows for fully autonomous
hemispherical BRDF measurements. The measuring system is illustrated in Figure 3.1 and its
parts are further described below.
Figure 3.1: Upgraded experimental setup
3.1.1
Light Source
The light source comes from a laser­driven light source (LDLS) which delivers a broadband
light.
The LDLS is collimated with three off­axis parabolic mirrors (OAP), ensuring a flat
intensity distribution.
The mirrors and LDLS are enclosed in a housing, and the light is
projected through a pinhole to eliminate divergent components. This configuration provides
balance between spatial intensity distribution and overall intensity reduction [10].
The
illuminated spot has ∼38 mm in diameter.
3.1.2
Sensor and Spectrometer
The reflected light from a sample under test is collected by a sensor, which is a reflective
collimator. The sensor is attached to a rotation stage and is situated in the plane of irradiance.
The sensor and spectrometer are connected with an optical fiber with 600 µm core diameter.
Although the spectrometer measures photon counts in the spectral range from 191 to 990 nm,
all calculations are limited to 320 to 800 nm, which covers the spectrum visible to the human
eye, approximately ranging from 380 to 780 nm [24].

3.1.3
Goniometer
To perform hemispherical measurements according to BRDF, which is described by four
angles, i.e., incoming θi, ϕi and outgoing θr, ϕr, four DOF are required by the measurement
setup.
As shown in Figure 3.2, the goniometer rotates a sample about three axes (x, y, z).
The
sensor is rotated about the same x­axis as the sample by a rotation stage, resulting in the
required four DOF.
Due to the mismatch in DOF with the goniometer having 3 DOF and the rotation stage having
1 DOF and considering that the BRDF involves θi, ϕi and θr, ϕr, it is necessary to apply
transformations between the goniometer, rotation stage, and BRDF coordinate systems.
These transformations are described in detail in Section 3.2.3.
The robot arm has a positioning repeatability of ± 0.1 mm [25], indicating its ability to return to
a specific position within this tolerance. The sample holder can fit samples with dimensions
ranging from 6 x 6 cm to 21 x 35 cm. The clamps of the sample holder have V­shaped jaws
that accommodate thicknesses from approximately 0 mm to 6 mm, allowing for the
measurement of BIPV samples of various sizes. To attach the calibration sample, a 3D printed
reduction is used (as seen in Figure 3.8b). The maximum sample payload is 1.5 kg, which is
more than sufficient, since most of the samples have a smaller weight.
The hemispherical measurments are performed up to θi, θr = 80◦, since the clamps protrude
by a few millimeters on each side of the sample. This limits the sensor’s field of view and the
light source’s ability to fully illuminate the sample, resulting in lower reflection signals and
higher measurement uncertainty.

from Universal Robots’ STEP file [26].

3.2.2
Control Software
A control software was developed to control the goniometer as well as the rotation stage and
spectrometer, making the BRDF measurements fully autonomous. The program consists of
three files: main.py, which contains the control sequence, and robot_control.py
and sensor_control.py, which contain functions that are called within the main.py. These
files can be found in Section B. The functionality of the control code is visualised in Figure 3.9.
When running the control script, the parameters for the robot arm and the motorized sensor
stage are set, and the spectrometer is initialized.
For each hemispherical measurement,
scanning path data are read, containing individual angular combinations at which the sample
is measured. The generation of the sampling path is described in Section 3.3.
The control loop iterates through each sampling path data, and the rotation stage and robot
positions are calculated while accounting for boundaries.
Once the rotation stage and robot reach the measured position, the integration time of the
spectrometer is defined based on the scanning path data. In order to reduce signal noise
collected by the spectrometer, the device collects five measurements, which are then
averaged. Additionally, the dark count and non­linearity corrections are enabled, to improve
the quality of the measurements. The data are saved iteratively during the process.

Robot Arm Control
The robot arm comes with an interface teaching pendant. Although this interface can be used
to program the robot, it is preferable to utilize the robot’s remote control capability, as the robot
features remote control via TCP/IP. Therefore, the robot is controlled remotely using URScript
commands [28].
To move the robot’s joints to specific positions, the movej function [29] is used. This function
automatically calculates the most efficient trajectory using inverse kinematics and is defined as:
movej(q, a = 0.5, v = 1.5, t = 0, r = 0)
(3.1)
where q stands for an array of 6 joint positions in radians, a is the acceleration in (rad/s2), and
v is the velocity in (rad/s). The t variable, which specifies move time, and the r variable, which
defines blend radius, are not used and are thus set to zero. Parameters for acceleration and
velocity were selected to move the sample efficiently while minimizing jitter.
The
implementation of the movej function can be found in the code provided in Listing
robot_control.py.
Since βy rotation requires combination of Shoulder, Elbow and Wrist 1 joints, a function that
calculates the necessary angles of these joints must be implemented.
The function inverse_kinematics (see Listing robot_control.py) is based on a script found
in [30] and is visualized in Figure 3.10. The function takes predefined parameters as input,
which are defined as follows:
• X: Horizontal position of the sample intersection point O with respect to the Base joint.
• Y : Distance of the point O from the Base joint.
• ϕ: Sum of βy and the angle between the a3 link and the X­axis, which is equal to 90◦
when βy = 0.
• a1, a2, a3: Lengths of the arms’ links.
• b: Distance from the a3 link to the center of the sample holder’s vice.
It should be noted that the selected distances on the X and Y coordinates influence the range
of βy. Hence, the optimal distances that yield a βy range of −85◦to 50◦are X = 0 mm and
Y = 340 mm.
When the function is called, the reference points X and Y are adjusted to the end of line b,
which intersects with link a3 and is perpendicular to it.
This is done by calculating new
coordinates Xnew and Ynew :
Xnew = X −b · sin(ϕ)
Ynew = Y + b · cos(ϕ)
(3.2)
Next, the intermediate values nx, ny are computed using link a3, and θ2 is calculated with
cosine rule. Then, using sine and cosine values from the new coordinates, θ1 is determined.
Lastly, θ3 is calculated as the difference between ϕ and the sum of θ1 and θ2.

The newly implemented hemispherical goniometer facilitates measurements of BIPV modules
and extends the capability for analysis and characterization of reflectance properties and
surface appearance. The new device complies with the design and functionality prerequisites
as it can perform the rotations required for BRDF measurements.
The control code successfully manages the entire setup and allows for reasonably fast
acquisition of hemispherical measurements. Acquiring eight hemispherical measurements for
each sample took approximately 2 hours.
After converting raw data to BRDF, the measurements revealed inconsistencies in the
reflectances of the white sample.
These were particularly apparent for measurements at
higher angles of incidence (θi = 30◦). This implies misalignment of the measurement setup,
likely caused by the goniometer or the sensor.
In contrast, the measurements of BIPV samples show intriguing findings.
The analyzed
results of hemispherical BRDF demonstrate the application capabilities of the improved setup,
clearly identifying distinct reflectance properties of each measured sample with different
compositions and surface structures.
Since the new setup allows measurements in 4D, it can also identify whether the BRDF of the
sample is isotropic or anisotropic.
This is a significant improvement compared to a single
plane goniometer.
The reference PV glass sample, which has a surface made of glass commonly used in PV
modules, showed reflectances with strong specular peaks capable of causing discomfort glare
at all measured incidence angles.
The two semi­textured and deeply textured samples show somewhat similar BRDF due to
their surfaces. The sample with deep texture appears more anisotropic as the reflected light
appears less symmetric, likely due to the depth of the texture. Conversely, the semi­textured
sample exhibits a more isotropic appearance as the light scatters highly symmetrically when
rotating the sample around the surface normal.
While the BRDF of the sample with
semi­texture reached the discomfort glare threshold at θi = 30◦,
both modules show
significantly lower BRDF than the reference PV glass as their surfaces cause the light to
scatter quite broadly.
The BRDF of the satinated sample was identified as isotropic, and its appearance was the
most diffuse out of the measured samples. Additionally, the overall BRDF values were the
lowest of all measured samples.
As concluded in [6], the glare risk of any of these modules cannot reach reflectance levels that
would permanently damage the retina of the eye. Moreover, the texturized and the satinated
samples reduce the glare levels significantly to discomfort glare compared to the reference PV
glass.
5.2
Limitations
One of the limitations of the study has been the old rotation stage below the sample holder,
which restricted BRDF measurements in certain regions. Although this did not significantly
Implementation and Characterization
of a Hemispherical Goniometer
45
==PAGE:57==
limit the analysis of the BIPV samples, it would be valuable to obtain measurements across
the whole hemisphere using the Spectralon sample to review the reflectance symmetry at
more sampling directions.
Since the goniometer was limited by the rotation stage and due to the time­consuming nature
of an uncertainty measurement,
no angular­dependent uncertainty analysis has been
performed. Hence, the measurement uncertainty of the entire setup cannot be presented.
Conversely, the obtained measurements from the calibration sample revealed discrepancies
in the reflectance distribution.
A limitation that persists is the range of βy rotation, which is limited to 50◦due to the
dimensions of the arm and the sample holder. This restricts measurements of certain regions
at incident and reflected angles greater than 50◦.
5.3
Recommendations for Further Research
To further analyze the accuracy of the goniometer, it is suggested to take measurements with
a finer sampling grid.
This approach would provide a higher reflection resolution over the
hemisphere of the calibration sample.
Additionally, once the old rotation stage is removed and the goniometer and sensor are
realigned, a comprehensive uncertainty analysis of the entire gonioreflectometer setup can be
performed to estimate the uncertainty of each element in the setup.
Such a procedure is
extensively detailed in [20] and [13].
The gathered BRDF data can be further used for appearance modeling, including the ray
tracing program recommended by [22]. Apart from non­colored BIPV samples, the setup can
also be used to measure colored samples, enabling further investigation of different coloration
techniques. In addition to optical properties, the setup allows for angular dependent analysis
of the electrical properties of various BIPV modules.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to extend the measurement capabilities of the reflective properties
of BIPV modules by integrating a robot arm for full hemispherical measurements. This was
successfully achieved by designing and fabricating the necessary components, implementing
control software, and developing a measurement methodology. The measured raw data was
then converted to BRDF values and summarized.
The results demonstrated distinct reflectance properties for each BIPV sample, while
highlighting the impact of glass selection on appearance under varying illumination and
viewing directions. Since the samples can now be rotated around their surface normal, the
measurements revealed which samples had isotropic or anisotropic BRDF properties.
The reference PV glass sample showed strong specular peaks, while the semi­textured, and
deeply textured samples exhibited multiple lower specular peaks and more broad scattering
due to their textures. The satinated sample exhibited the most diffuse reflection as well as
lowest BRDF values.
Additionally, all textured samples showed a significant glare risk
reduction compared to the reference PV glass.
Although the measurements were partially restricted, the results demonstrated the extent of
measurement possibilities using the upgraded setup.
The new device allows for a more
detailed analysis of the angular dependent optical as well as the electrical properties of various
BIPV samples. Since the measurements of the calibration sample indicated discrepancies,
future work should focus on refining the measurement setup and improving its accuracy.
