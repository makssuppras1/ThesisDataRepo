Abstract
The El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is a complex climate phenomenon with significant impacts
on global weather and climate. Understanding these impacts and making informed decisions in various
sectors require accurate forecasting of ENSO events. In this thesis, a deep learning-based model is
developed to predict the nino3.4 index, a key climate indicator, using real-world ocean observation data
from satellites. Synthetic data, commonly used in other models, is avoided, and only actual observations
are utilized.
Two methodologies that have promising result in the literature, Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNN) and convLSTM, are explored to assess their predictive abilities based on satellite data. Addi-
tionally, the effect of including additional variables, such as Sea Surface Salinity and Salinity Anomalies,
along with Sea Surface Temperature and SST Anomalies, is studied to evaluate their potential for im-
proving predictive models. Finally, the efect on temporal proximity to input and forecasting steps is
studied in all combinations from 3 to 24 months.
Promising results are obtained through empirical analysis. The CNN model shows good performance
in predicting scores when using only SST maps, while the convLSTM performs even better when all
available features (SST, SSTA, SSS, SSSA) are incorporated, although 10 years of SST and SSTA data
had to be omitted due to the availability of salinity data only from 1993 onwards. Although the results
for forecasting steps up to 2 years in the future are not as favorable, the CNN model, selected as the
final model, demonstrates potential for accurate long-term predictions with some adjustments to its
architecture after fine-tuning.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, forecasting the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) phenomenon, has garnered sig-
nificant attention due to its substantial impacts on weather patterns, ecosystems, and global climate.
Accurate ENSO forecasting plays a crucial role in understanding and preparing for potential climate
anomalies, such as droughts, floods, and extreme weather events, which can have severe socioeconomic
consequences worldwide.
One prevailing challenge in ENSO forecasting lies in identifying eﬀicient and reliable prediction
models. The literature has showcased successful deep learning models for ENSO forecasting, which
exhibit promising results in capturing complex spatial and temporal dependencies within the data.
However, a notable aspect is that many of these models rely on synthetic data generated from climate
simulations, raising concerns about the validity of their predictions based on real-world observations.
In our research, we aim to shift the focus towards using only ocean observation data derived from
satellite monitoring. This approach is motivated by the belief that the real-world observations inherently
encapsulate the true dynamics of the Earth’s climate system, providing a more accurate representation of
ENSO behavior. By relying solely on observational data, we hope to avoid potential biases introduced
by synthetic data and improve the credibility of our predictions. We also focus particularly in the
prediction of the nino3.4 index, as it has been shown that this index determines whether the ENSO
cycle is at an el nino or la nina phase.
Deep learning methods have shown immense potential in learning complex patterns and relationships
from large datasets. Leveraging these techniques for ENSO forecasting offers a promising avenue to
tackle the intricate nature of the climate system and provide reliable predictions for the nino3.4 index.
The ability of deep learning models to automatically extract relevant features and identify spatial and
temporal dependencies aligns perfectly with the requirements of ENSO forecasting.
While dynamical models that incorporate the physics of the ocean have been widely used for ENSO
predictions, they come with their own set of challenges. The complexity and computational cost of such
models can be prohibitive, making them less practical for real-time forecasting. Also, it is essential to
have a deep knowledge of the domain to be able to model these dynamics accurately. In contrast, deep
learning models can offer eﬀicient and rapid predictions, making them more applicable for operational
use and do not demand such a domain knowledge of the field.
Our motivation stems from the desire to create a deep learning-based ENSO forecasting model that
utilizes only observational data and achieves comparable accuracy to existing models. By doing so, we
aim to show that it is realistic to construct a model that is not reliant on synthetic data but rather
grounded in real-world observations. Our ultimate goal is to contribute to the advancement of ENSO
forecasting, leading to better preparedness and mitigation strategies for the impacts of this climatic
phenomenon on a global scale.
1.2
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to implement a deep learning model for the prediction of the nino3.4
index, a crucial climate indicator, through the analysis of ocean observation data based on satelite
monitoring. The primary objective is to uncover the unique insights and contributions that can be

derived from analyzing observational data based on satelite monitoring, compared to studies and models
incorporating synthetic data and simulations.
The first aspect of this research focuses on model selection, specifically examining the predictive ca-
pabilities of satellite data for the nino3.4 index. The thesis compares two methodologies, Convolutional
Neural Networks (CNN) and convLSTM, which have demonstrated promising results in the literature.
However, most existing models in the literature also incorporate synthetic data. Therefore, this study
aims to identify a model that can achieve good predictions solely based on satellite data, thus we build
two models from scratch and explore their predictive capabilities.
Building upon the exploration of satellite data, the thesis investigates the impact of incorporating ad-
ditional variables alongside the commonly studied Sea Surface Temperature (SST) and SST Anomalies.
Specifically, the variables of Sea Surface Salinity and Salinity Anomalies are considered. By evaluating
the effects of including these additional variables on the forecasting accuracy of the nino3.4 index, this
research aims to assess their potential for improving predictive models. The goal is to understand the
extent to which these variables contribute to enhancing the forecasting capabilities of the model.
Furthermore, the thesis examines the influence of temporal proximity on forecasting performance.
The study investigates different combinations of lookback steps (past data) and forecasting steps (fu-
ture prediction) to determine their impact on the accuracy and reliability of nino3.4 index forecasts.
By analyzing various temporal distances, this research aims to identify the optimal time frames for
data analysis and forecasting within the implemented model.
This investigation contributes to the
understanding of the relationship between temporal proximity and forecasting performance, shedding
light on the dynamics of the nino3.4 index over time.
In summary, this thesis aims to explore the distinct contributions of analyzing ocean observational
data based on satelite monitoring exclusively, assess the impact of incorporating additional variables on
forecasting accuracy, and investigate the influence of temporal proximity on forecasting performance.
By addressing these research objectives, this study contributes to the field’s understanding of the nino3.4
index, refines predictive models, and provides valuable insights for climate analysis and forecasting.

2.1
The El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) phenomenon
The El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is a recurrent climatic phenomenon characterized by fluctu-
ations in the temperature of waters in the central and eastern regions of the tropical Pacific Ocean[69].
Over a span of approximately three to seven years, there are notable variations in the surface wa-
ter temperatures across a significant expanse of the tropical Pacific Ocean, exhibiting deviations of
approximately 1°C to 3°C from the average.
This alternating pattern of warming and cooling, known as the ENSO cycle, exerts a direct influence
on the distribution of rainfall within the tropics and exerts a significant impact on weather conditions
across the United States and other global regions. The acronym ”EN” denotes elevated sea surface
temperatures in the central and eastern regions of the tropical Pacific Ocean, signifying warm extremes.
Conversely, the acronym ”SO” stands for the Southern Oscillation, a phenomenon characterized by
oscillating alterations in sea level surface pressure between Darwin, Australia, and Tahiti, resembling a
seesaw-like pattern[45].
El Niño and La Niña represent the extreme phases of the ENSO cycle, while the intermediate phase
between these two extremes is referred to as ENSO-neutral[69]. More specifically, El Niño refers to a
period of above-average sea surface temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific, which can
last for several months to a few years. During an El Niño event, there is a weakening of the trade winds,
which are the prevailing winds blowing from east to west across the Pacific[68]. This weakening allows
warm water to spread eastward, as seen in Figure 2.1, leading to the warming of the surface waters in
the central and eastern Pacific. As a result, the typical patterns of atmospheric circulation and rainfall
are disrupted, leading to various climate anomalies worldwide[69].
On the other hand, La Niña is characterized by below-average sea surface temperatures in the

Throughout history, our comprehension of this phenomenon has always been intertwined with its
societal implications. As early as the 1500s, Peruvian fishermen made a significant connection between
periodic warm waters and the abundance of anchovy in their catch. Concurrently, Peruvian farmers
noticed that warm seawaters were linked to heightened rainfall patterns. The designation ”El Niño”
was assigned to this oceanic warming occurrence due to its tendency to transpire around Christmas, in
reference to the Christ child. Towards the end of the 19th century, Peruvian geographers delved further
into the climate conditions along the coast[66]. They observed a customary transition from cold to
warmer oceanic conditions towards the conclusion of each year, which they attributed to the influence
of a southward warm current[73]. Furthermore, these geographers witnessed that in certain years, the
initiation of warm conditions exhibited greater intensity than usual and was accompanied by unusual
oceanic and climatic phenomena.
Independent of this work, Sir Gilbert Walker, a British physicist, conducted a study on the rela-
tionship between the Indian monsoon and meteorological conditions in other parts of the world[66].
His findings led to the discovery of the Southern Oscillation (SO), a large-scale fluctuation in sea-level
pressure between the western and eastern Pacific as we defined it in the first paragraph of this section.
The poles of this oscillation were identified near Darwin, Australia, and Tahiti. This discovery marked
the first scientific indication of the interconnections of weather conditions across distant regions of the
tropical Pacific.
It wasn’t until the 1960s that scientists realized the episodic warming of sea surface temperature
(SST) off the Peruvian coast was part of a larger disturbance extending westward along the equator to
the date line[60]. This phenomenon was found to be associated with the Southern Oscillation, and the
term ”El Niño” became associated with unusually strong warming events occurring every 2-7 years in
conjunction with basin-scale tropical Pacific Ocean anomalies.
2.1.1
ENSO Mechanisms: Self-Sustained Oscillators of ENSO
Expanding on this discovery, Jacob Bjerknes, a Norwegian-American meteorologist, proposed a mech-
anism to connect El Niño and the Southern Oscillation. He postulated that El Niño represents an
oceanic manifestation of a large-scale ocean-atmosphere interaction. Bjerknes demonstrated that the
long-term persistence of climate anomalies associated with the Southern Oscillation, including changes
in the tropical Pacific trade winds, was closely tied to slowly evolving SST anomalies in the equatorial

Pacific[112]. This suggested a positive feedback process in which warming (cooling) of SST in the Pacific
causes the trade winds to slacken (strengthen), leading to ocean circulation changes that reinforce the
SST tendency. While the development of El Niño warming conditions and its opposite extreme, La
Niña, could be explained, understanding the transitions between these states remained a challenge that
would require further research.
During the 1970s and 1980s, significant progress was made in understanding the connection between
El Niño and the Southern Oscillation. Observational studies provided detailed insights into wind, SST,
and rainfall anomaly fields during El Niño events[60]. The role of equatorial Kelvin waves in inducing sea
level and SST effects was recognized, and numerical models were developed to simulate El Niño-related
oceanic variability[73].
In 1980, a model proposed by Gill provided a useful framework for tropical atmospheric circulation,
specifically applicable to ENSO. This was followed by research focused on simulating and understanding
the global-scale atmospheric responses associated with ENSO[66].
Meanwhile, the Tropical Ocean-
Global Atmosphere Program (TOGA) was established as a 10-year international research effort to
study and predict ENSO and its global impacts. The program led to the creation of a near-real-time
ENSO observing system and the development of models that simulated ENSO phenomena accurately[3].
2.1.1.1
The Delayed Oscillator
These modeling successes contributed to a refined theoretical interpretation of ENSO, particularly
regarding the mechanisms behind the quasi-regular, multiyear ENSO oscillation.
The positive and
delayed negative feedback processes were identified as key factors driving the observed ENSO oscillation,
leading to the development of the Delayed Oscillator theory.
According to this theory, the positive feedback process refers to the interaction between the ocean
and atmosphere in which warming (cooling) of SST in the Pacific weakens (strengthens) the trade winds.
This weakening of the trade winds, in turn, leads to changes in ocean circulation that reinforce the SST
tendency, creating a positive feedback loop[60].
However, the delayed negative feedback process plays a crucial role in the oscillation. It involves
the propagation of equatorial Kelvin waves and their reflection as westward-propagating Rossby waves.
The Rossby waves shallow the thermocline (the boundary between warm surface waters and deeper cold
waters) as they move eastward, which eventually reverses the initial SST warming around 6 months
after the onset of the warming in the eastern Pacific[60]. This delayed negative feedback acts as a
damping mechanism for the positive feedback, contributing to the oscillatory nature of ENSO
The 1982-1983 El Niño event, which was the largest on record at that time, further propelled scientific
interest and triggered intensified efforts to observe, understand, and predict ENSO. It highlighted the
need for real-time oceanic observations and paved the way for subsequent advancements in ENSO
research[73].
2.1.1.2
The Recharge Oscillator
In 1985, Wyrtki [104][105] first introduced the idea of a warm water buildup in the western Pacific as
a critical precondition for the development of El Niño. It was understood that prior to the onset of
El Niño, the upper ocean heat content or warm water volume in the entire tropical Pacific gradually
accumulated over time. This gradual buildup, often referred to as ”charging,” set the stage for the
subsequent occurrence of El Niño events.
During an El Niño event, warm water is flushed or discharged towards higher latitudes, resulting
in a redistribution of heat content. This discharge of warm water marks a significant transition in the
tropical Pacific, with the region becoming colder (La Niña). Following the discharge, the tropical Pacific
slowly begins the process of rebuilding warm water, often referred to as ”recharge,” in preparation for
the next occurrence of El Niño.
Building upon Wyrtki’s work, Jin [25][26] further emphasized the importance of the recharge and
discharge processes. Using the coupled model developed by Zebiak and Cane in 1987 [22], Jin formulated

and derived the Recharge Oscillator model. This model offered a deeper understanding of the dynamics
at play during ENSO events.
During the warm phase of ENSO, equatorial central Pacific westerly wind anomalies and equatorial
eastern Pacific warm sea surface temperature anomalies lead to a divergence of Sverdrup transport.
This divergence causes the discharge of heat content from the equatorial region. As a result, the entire
equatorial Pacific experiences an anomalous shallow thermocline depth during the transition phase. This
shallow thermocline allows for the pumping of cold waters into the surface layer through climatological
upwelling, giving rise to the cold phase of ENSO [26].
Conversely, during the cold phase of ENSO, the recharge process begins again. The coupled ocean-
atmosphere system oscillates on interannual time scales due to the recharge-discharge process, leading
to the recurrent occurrence of El Niño and La Niña events. This oscillation plays a pivotal role in
shaping global climate patterns and impacts.
2.1.1.3
The Western Pacific Oscillator
By the end of the 80s, researchers began to recognize the need for a more comprehensive understanding
of ENSO that encompassed the interplay between the eastern and western Pacific interannual anomaly
patterns [95]. It became evident from consistent observations that during the warm phase of ENSO,
there existed distinct patterns [79]. The equatorial eastern Pacific exhibited warm sea surface tem-
perature anomalies, while simultaneously, the off-equatorial western Pacific experienced cold SST and
shallow thermocline depth anomalies [98].
Drawing from these valuable observations, Weisberg alongside Wang [103] undertook the task of
formulating a conceptual model known as the Western Pacific Oscillator for ENSO. This groundbreak-
ing model aimed to address the previous oversight of the western Pacific in the Delayed Oscillator
framework. Specifically, it highlighted the crucial role played by off-equatorial SST anomalies and their
accompanying anomalous anticyclones in the western Pacific, which induce notable wind anomalies in
the equatorial western Pacific. These induced wind anomalies, in turn, exert influence on the intricate
evolution of ENSO.
According to the Western Pacific Oscilator theory, convection-related condensation heating in the
equatorial central Pacific [19] leads to the formation of a pair of off-equatorial cyclones with westerly
wind anomalies along the equator. These equatorial westerly wind anomalies play a crucial role in
deepening the thermocline and increasing SST in the equatorial eastern Pacific, providing positive
feedback for the growth of ENSO anomalies. In contrast, the off-equatorial cyclones induce Ekman
pumping, which raises the thermocline in that region. Consequently, a shallow off-equatorial thermocline
anomaly expands across the western Pacific, resulting in a decrease in SST and an increase in sea level
pressure (SLP) in the off-equatorial western Pacific [98].
During the mature phase of El Niño, the off-equatorial anomalous anticyclones trigger equatorial
easterly wind anomalies in the western Pacific. These equatorial easterly wind anomalies induce up-
welling and cooling, propagating eastward as a forced oceanic response. This negative feedback mecha-
nism enables the coupled ocean-atmosphere system to oscillate, contributing to the recurrent nature of
ENSO.
The insights provided by the Western Pacific Oscillator enhance our comprehension of the complex
dynamics underlying ENSO. By considering the interactions between the eastern and western Pacific,
we gain a more comprehensive understanding of the processes driving the oscillations of ENSO on
interannual timescales.
2.1.1.4
The Advective-Reflective Oscillator
In 1997, a conceptual Advective-Reflective Oscillator model for ENSO was proposed by Picaut [77],
offering further insights into the dynamics of this complex phenomenon. The model highlights a positive
feedback mechanism driven by zonal currents, specifically the advection of the western Pacific warm
pool towards the east during El Niño. However, several negative feedbacks counteract this eastward
movement, pushing the warm pool back to its original position in the western Pacific.

The first negative feedback arises from anomalous zonal currents associated with wave reflection
at the western boundary. As the upwelling Rossby waves propagate westward, they eventually reach
the western boundary and reflect as upwelling Kelvin waves. These Kelvin waves, characterized by
westward zonal currents, exert a force that opposes the eastward displacement of the warm pool [95].
Similarly, the second negative feedback stems from anomalous zonal currents associated with wave
reflection at the eastern boundary. The eastward propagating downwelling Kelvin waves eventually
reach the eastern boundary and reflect as downwelling Rossby waves. These Rossby waves, featuring
westward zonal currents, act to push the warm pool back towards the west [95].
Furthermore, a third negative feedback arises from the mean zonal currents converging at the eastern
edge of the warm pool. These converging currents contribute to the restoration of the warm pool’s
original position in the western Pacific [95]. he warm phase of ENSO, equatorial westerly wind anomalies
in the central Pacific generate upwelling Rossby waves and downwelling Kelvin waves. The westward
propagation of upwelling Rossby waves leads to their reflection as upwelling Kelvin waves upon reaching
the western boundary. Similarly, the eastward propagation of downwelling Kelvin waves results in their
reflection as downwelling Rossby waves at the eastern boundary.
The presence of westward zonal
currents in both the upwelling Kelvin and downwelling Rossby waves contributes to the opposing force
against the eastward displacement of the warm pool [95].

aries and the influence of mean zonal currents, compel the coupled ocean-atmosphere system to oscillate,
ultimately contributing to the intricate dynamics of ENSO.
2.1.1.5
The Unified Oscillator
During the 00s, further advancements were made in the understanding of ENSO, with researchers delving
deeper into the complex dynamics of this climate phenomenon. Building upon the existing knowledge
and the various oscillator models proposed, scientists sought to refine and enhance the understanding
of ENSO’s mechanisms and its multi-mechanistic nature.
One notable development during this period was the increased recognition that ENSO encompasses
multiple mechanisms operating simultaneously.
The Unified ENSO Oscillator model formulated by
Wang [97] played a crucial role in this realization. By incorporating the dynamics and thermodynam-
ics of the coupled ocean-atmosphere system, this model provided a comprehensive framework that
accounted for the observed eastern and western Pacific anomaly patterns.
Researchers acknowledged that ENSO is not solely driven by a single mechanism but rather a
complex interplay of different factors. The relative importance of these mechanisms was found to be
time-dependent, underscoring the dynamic nature of ENSO. Picaut [78] conducted observations that
shed light on the existence of diverse ENSO mechanisms, further supporting the notion of ENSO as a
multi-mechanism phenomenon.
With the understanding that ENSO operates through multiple mechanisms, scientists aimed to
unravel the specific dynamics and interactions involved. This period witnessed increased efforts to refine
and expand the existing oscillator models, seeking to capture the intricacies of the ocean-atmosphere
coupling and its influence on ENSO variability.
During the decade of 2010, the understanding of ENSO mechanisms and the development of ENSO
forecasting techniques continued to progress, driven by the growing recognition of its significance and the
need for accurate predictions. Building on the advancements made in the previous decade, researchers
focused on unraveling the specific dynamics and interactions that govern ENSO behavior [95]. The
realization that ENSO operates through multiple mechanisms became more pronounced, emphasizing
the intricate interplay of various factors. This understanding underscored the time-dependent nature
of ENSO, with the relative importance of different mechanisms varying over time. As our comprehen-
sion has deepened, the focus has been shifted towards the crucial task of accurate ENSO forecasting,
recognizing the importance of anticipating its occurrence in advance to mitigate its potential impacts.
Prior to delving into the models and methodologies of ENSO forecasting that have been devised,
it is imperative to examine the significance of ENSO forecasting and comprehend why it is crucial to
anticipate its occurrence in advance.

2.2
The importance of ENSO Forecasting
Climate variability, particularly influenced by the El Niño-Southern Oscillation, has wide-ranging effects
on various sectors of society and economy. Extensive research has been conducted to understand the
impacts of ENSO and its potential applications in water resources, agriculture, and public health [95].
These sectors differ in their complexity, decision-making environment, and focus on climate variability
or extremes.
In the water resources sector, ENSO has a significant influence on the hydrologic cycle, which
subsequently affects water availability. Studies have shown that ENSO affects global and regional pre-
cipitation patterns, with El Niño associated with below-normal precipitation and La Niña associated
with above-normal precipitation [17]. However, there are geographic variations in these patterns. For
example, El Niño leads to high precipitation in southern Brazil but below-normal precipitation in cen-
tral Indonesia, southern Philippines, South America, and southern Africa [17]. La Niña, on the other
hand, causes above-average precipitation in Australia and northwest South America but below-normal
precipitation in central United States and India. These patterns have implications for agriculture, as
droughts and floods can affect crop yields. ENSO also influences the severity of hydrologic extremes,
such as heavy precipitation, floods, and droughts [51]. The spatial extent of tropical droughts and
the occurrence of floods show correlations with ENSO events, with regions like Australia, South and
Central America experiencing strong teleconnections between streamflow and ENSO [11]. The con-
nection between ENSO and streamflow enables seasonal forecasting, supporting water management
decisions related to water allocation, reservoir operations, and flood planning. However, the uptake of
seasonal climate forecasts in water resource management is hindered by barriers such as perceived lack
of accuracy, risk aversion, and institutional constraints [46]. Nevertheless, regions like Brazil, India,
and the Philippines, where ENSO strongly influences precipitation, have the potential to benefit from
ENSO-related climate information in water management [12].
In the agricultural sector, the study of the links between ENSO and agriculture has evolved over time.
While early research focused on understanding the relationship between ENSO and climate patterns,
subsequent work has aimed at utilizing ENSO information to anticipate and manage the impacts of
climate fluctuations on agriculture and food security [33].
Studies have shown that in general, regarding the agricultural sector, ENSO impacts crop growth,
yield, and agricultural practices. While the use of quantitative modeling approaches based on weather
data for prediction and decision support was already established before ENSO research, seasonal cli-
mate prediction catalyzed the development of new methods for incorporating predictive and historic
climate information in agriculture [76].
Unlike the water sector, agriculture has a more decentral-
ized decision-making environment with a larger number of individual actors, such as farmers, making
climate-sensitive decisions. This sector faces challenges in incorporating ENSO information due to re-
mote settings, resource constraints, and infrastructure limitations. Decision-making in agriculture can
be influenced by the full range of climate variability or response to extremes [13]. For example, reser-
voir management and farm management decisions consider the full range of climate variability, while
responses to floods or climate-sensitive disease epidemics focus on extremes. Understanding the ENSO
impacts on agriculture allows for informed decision-making regarding crop choices, irrigation demands,
and risk management strategies. Despite the potential benefits, the adoption of ENSO-related climate
information in agriculture varies across regions and is influenced by factors such as institutional reforms,
risk perception, and accessibility of forecast information.
More specifically, ENSO has been found to have a significant influence on rainfed crop yields and
production in major cropping regions affected by ENSO-related rainfall variations [2]. These regions in-
clude parts of Australia, the United States, India, Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Southeast South America,
Mexico, and Zimbabwe. The influence of ENSO on crop response is particularly pronounced in drought-
prone environments. The effects of ENSO on rainfed summer crop production tend to be stronger in the
southern hemisphere, where ENSO activity peaks during the northern winter. However, more research
is needed to test these generalizations systematically [67].
Irrigation plays a role in buffering the impacts of climate on crops. The relationship between ENSO
and irrigated crops is more complex than that of rainfed crops. For example, in Southeast Asia, the

influence of ENSO on irrigated rice production can be attributed to variations in both yield (due to

in tropical cyclone occurrence, affecting the likelihood of extreme precipitation and wind hazards in the
Caribbean, North America, and Southeast Asia [48]. Understanding the relationships between ENSO
and health outcomes can support preparedness and response efforts, especially in regions where ENSO

related predictability observed in the tropics. Cholera, for example, has been extensively studied in
relation to ENSO, with warmer coastal sea surface temperatures favoring the growth of Vibrio cholerae
bacterium. However, the relationship between ENSO and cholera may not be consistent over time,
indicating a complex interaction.
Public health outcomes related to climate can also be influenced by impacts in other sectors, such as

developed to address aflatoxin contamination. Hydrometeor ological disasters pose significant public
health threats, with the relationship between health-related disasters and ENSO varying in different
regions.
Determining causal relationships between ENSO and health outcomes remains a challenge in ob-
served phenomena. However, studies have identified associations between La Niña conditions preceding
human influenza pandemics. Seasonal climate forecasts tailored for the health sector have been devel-
oped where clear relationships between public health outcomes and SST in the equatorial Pacific (linked
to ENSO events) are established [87].
Advances in climate science, satellite-based environmental monitoring technologies, data manage-
ment, analysis, visualization, and dissemination technologies have opened up new opportunities for
managing climate-related health risks. Integrating climate information into routine health decision-
making requires policy buy-in, engagement with decision-makers at national and local levels, building
cross-sectoral capacity, and ensuring the availability of appropriate methodologies, tools, and data.

2.3
ENSO Forecasting Methods
Enso forecasting methods have evolved over the years, transitioning from primarily physical models
to an increased reliance on statistical approaches.
This transition has been driven by a deepening
understanding of the complex mechanisms underlying El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) and the
need for more accurate and robust predictions. As our knowledge of ENSO processes has advanced, it
has become clear that traditional physical models alone may not capture the full complexity of ENSO
dynamics.
This realization has led to the exploration and development of statistical methods that
leverage historical data and empirical relationships to enhance ENSO forecasting.
The increased understanding of ENSO, gained through extensive research into its physical mecha-
nisms, has provided valuable insights into the processes that drive ENSO variability. The identification
of key atmospheric and oceanic teleconnections, such as the Walker circulation and the interactions
between the tropical Pacific Ocean and the overlying atmosphere, has paved the way for improved
forecasting methodologies. The recognition of important precursors and climate indices, such as sea
surface temperatures, zonal wind anomalies, and oceanic heat content, has allowed for the development
of statistical models that capture the relationships between these predictors and ENSO behavior.
In general, ENSO forecasting methods can be broadly categorized into three primary classifications:
dynamical models, statistical models, and hybrid models [90]. Among these classifications, Deep Learn-
ing models can be considered as a subset of statistical models but with the fast growth of research on
this domain it can be classified as as seperate category. In this thesis, the primary focus is on Deep
Learning models, however it is considered essential to provide a concise overview of the other ENSO
forecasting methods as well.
2.3.1
Dynamical Models
Dynamical models, also referred to as coupled models, utilize intricate physical equations to replicate
the intricate interactions between the atmosphere and the ocean [90]. These models encompass the
essential physical processes that govern ENSO, including mass, momentum, and energy conservation.
By incorporating these processes, these models can simulate the dynamic behavior of the coupled
ocean-atmosphere system and its responses to external influences such as variations in solar radiation
or greenhouse gas concentrations. The significance of dynamical models lies in their ability to predict
ENSO events by replicating the intricate interactions between the ocean and atmosphere.
Within
the realm of dynamical models, two primary subcategories exist: Coupled General Circulation Models
(CGCMs) and Intermediate Coupled Models (ICMs) [90].
2.3.1.1
Coupled General Circulation Models (CGCMs)
CGCMs are comprehensive climate models that simulate the interactions between the atmosphere and
ocean. They have undergone significant advancements over the years, incorporating a wide range of
physical processes and improving their representation of ENSO dynamics. An important milestone
in the development of CGCMs was the pioneering work of Cane and Zebiak [10]. They introduced

the first successful CGCM, which captured the essential characteristics of ENSO behavior. Since then,
more sophisticated models have been developed, such as the Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory
(GFDL) CM2.1 [18] and the Community Earth System Model (CESM) [49].
CGCMs primarily focus on representing the fundamental physical processes and feedback mech-
anisms driving ENSO. These models incorporate equations describing ocean currents, heat transfer,
atmospheric circulation, and other essential factors. By simulating these processes, CGCMs provide a
comprehensive understanding of the climate system and its response to ENSO. Their strengths lie in
capturing the underlying physics and simulating complex interactions within the climate system. This
capability has contributed to significant advancements in our understanding of ENSO dynamics and
the ability to simulate long-term climate projections. However, CGCMs require substantial computa-
tional resources and involve uncertainties associated with parameterization schemes and model biases [5].
These factors can affect the performance and reliability of CGCMs for operational ENSO forecasting.
Noteworthy CGCMs include the GFDL CM2.1, which represents the coupled atmosphere-ocean
system with a high level of detail, allowing for improved ENSO simulation [18]. The CESM, developed
by the National Center for Atmospheric Research, is another influential CGCM that integrates various
components, including the atmosphere, ocean, land surface, and sea ice, to capture ENSO dynamics
and their impacts on global climate [49]. The Community Climate System Model (CCSM4) is also
notable for its contribution to ENSO prediction and climate studies [29].
Generally, CGCMs offer a comprehensive representation of ENSO dynamics and interactions within
the climate system. They have been instrumental in improving our understanding of ENSO and sim-
ulating long-term climate projections. By capturing the intricate physics and feedback mechanisms,
CGCMs provide a holistic perspective on ENSO and its impacts on global climate. However, their
high computational requirements and uncertainties in parameterizations limit their operational usage
for real-time forecasting. The accurate initialization of CGCMs is crucial, and model biases may in-
troduce uncertainties into the predictions. Model biases refer to systematic differences between model
simulations and observed climate data, which can affect the accuracy and reliability of ENSO forecasts
[5].
2.3.1.2
Intermediate Coupled Models (ICMs)
ICMs, in contrast to CGCMs, focus primarily on the atmosphere-ocean interaction while simplifying
other components of the climate system.
These models strike a balance between computational ef-
ficiency and capturing essential ENSO dynamics. An important milestone in ICM development was
the formulation of the Bjerknes stability index by Jin that we refered to in Section 2.1 , providing a
theoretical framework for ICMs. The index relates the rate of change of sea surface temperature to the
depth of the oceanic mixed layer and represents the stability of the coupled system.
ICMs have been successful in predicting ENSO events, especially in the short-to-medium range. One
of the most renowned ICMs is the Zebiak-Cane model [22] - that we referred to in Section 2.1 as well,
which provided a breakthrough in ENSO forecasting. The Zebiak-Cane model introduced a simplified
representation of the coupled atmosphere-ocean system, enabling the simulation of ENSO events and
their evolution over time. Another notable ICM is the Wang-Sui model [103], which improved the
representation of important physical processes and further enhanced ENSO prediction capabilities.
ICMs are computationally more eﬀicient than CGCMs, making them suitable for operational fore-
casting and sensitivity studies. By focusing on the critical atmosphere-ocean coupling, ICMs allow for
faster simulations and real-time prediction of ENSO events. However, the simplified representations
in ICMs may overlook certain processes and complexities associated with ENSO dynamics, potentially
limiting their accuracy compared to CGCMs.

historical ENSO indicators. This method entails organizing, summarizing, and analyzing past ENSO

data. Statistical models employed in ENSO prediction can be categorized as linear or nonlinear. Linear
statistical models are developed using techniques like multiple linear regression, canonical correlation,
and Markov chains. On the other hand, nonlinear statistical models primarily utilize machine learning

2.3.2.1
Traditional Linear Statistical Models
Traditional statistical models have played a significant role in ENSO forecasting, with two prominent
approaches being the Holt Winters (HW) method and the autoregressive integrated moving average
(ARIMA) method. The HW method, classified as a short-term statistical approach, utilizes exponen-
tially weighted moving averages to predict time series data characterized by seasonal patterns and
repeating forms. Through a technique called ”exponential smoothing,” this method effectively reduces
the volatility of time-series data, facilitating a better understanding of its underlying patterns.
In 2014, Mike et al. [88] employed the HW model to forecast the sea surface temperature (SST)
index in the Niño3 region spanning from 1933 to 2012. Impressively, their forecasts achieved a root
mean square error of 0.303 and 1.309 for 1-month and 12-month predictions, respectively. However, it
is worth noting that the HW model has limitations when applied to periodically stationary time series.
To address this, the researchers proposed an enhanced version known as the dynamic seasonal model
(DSM). Experimental results revealed that the DSM outperformed both the deterministic seasonal
model and the original HW model in predicting the monthly Nino3 in sample analysis area [88].
Another widely used traditional statistical method for ENSO forecasting is the autoregressive inte-
grated moving average (ARIMA) method. ARIMA, also referred to as the integrated moving average
autoregressive model, has proven effective in analyzing time series data. In a study conducted in 2011
by Matthieu et al. [40], ARIMA was applied to investigate the temporal correlation between monthly
P. falciparum case numbers and ENSO, measured by the Southern Oscillation Index (SOI), at Cayenne
General Hospital from 1996 to 2009. The findings revealed a positive effect of El Niño with a 3-month
lag on P. falciparum cases (p < 0.001). Additionally, incorporating SOI data into the ARIMA model
resulted in a 4% reduction in the Akaike information criterion (AIC) [54].
Researchers have proposed various other statistical models to enhance ENSO forecasting. Penland
et al. [74] introduced a representation of Indo-Pacific Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies (SSTAs)
as a stable linear process driven by spatially coherent stochastic forcing. Through observations, they
derived the relevant parameters that best fit this stable linear process, assuming stability and linearity.
Experimental results demonstrated that the optimal model achieved a sample correlation of 67% for
predicting two time series 7 months in advance. Tseng et al. [94] developed a multiple linear regression
model that relied on five evolutions of thermocline depth anomalies and zonal surface wind modulation
over a 25-day period. This model successfully post-reported all ENSOs except the 2000-2001 La Niña.
Additionally, Xue et al. [108] utilized the linear Markov model, incorporating sea surface temperature,
sea level height, and wind stress as predictors, achieving a forecast skill score of 0.8 when the forecast
period was set to 6 months. Kondrashov et al. [50] obtained an ENSO stochastic forcing model through
polynomial regression analysis, achieving a correlation coeﬀicient exceeding 0.6 when forecasting for a
6-month period.
Despite the wide usage of traditional statistical methods in ENSO forecasting, it is crucial to ac-
knowledge that the ENSO phenomenon itself is highly complex and exhibits nonlinear trends over time.
Consequently, traditional statistical methods often yield poor fitting results when applied to nonlinear
datasets, making them less suitable for complex pattern recognition and knowledge discovery purposes.

2.3.4
Hybrid Models
Hybrid models represent a fusion of both dynamical and statistical approaches, effectively combining
the strengths of each methodology. The objective is to capture the underlying physical mechanisms
that drive ENSO dynamics while leveraging statistical techniques to enhance prediction accuracy. The
development of hybrid modeling for ENSO forecasting has been marked by significant milestones.
One crucial milestone in hybrid modeling is the introduction of the Canonical Correlation Analysis
(CCA) approach, as proposed by Barnston and Ropelewski [4]. CCA identifies the primary modes of
climate variability and their interconnections, providing a statistical foundation for hybrid modeling.
Another milestone is the Climate Forecast System (CFS) developed by the National Centers for Envi-
ronmental Prediction (NCEP). The CFS combines dynamical simulations from CGCMs with statistical
post-processing techniques to generate ENSO predictions [83].
The CFS has demonstrated skill in
predicting ENSO events on seasonal timescales.
Another prominent model is a Dynamical-Statistical Forecasting Model of the ENSO index based on
the improved self-memorization principle proposed by Hong et al. [42]. This integrates both dynamical
and statistical components, allowing for the incorporation of physical processes and historical statistical
relationships.
This model has shown promise in capturing the complexity of ENSO dynamics and
generating skillful predictions.
As already mentioned, such models aim to combine the strengths of dynamical and statistical ap-
proaches to enhance ENSO predictions.
By incorporating dynamical simulations, they capture the
underlying physical processes and feedback mechanisms that drive ENSO, resulting in a more realistic
representation of the climate system. At the same time, statistical techniques enable the utilization of
historical data and empirical relationships to improve prediction accuracy.
However, they also come with limitations. The reliance on dynamical models can introduce biases
and uncertainties associated with model physics and parameterizations. Moreover, the statistical com-
ponents may be sensitive to the quality and availability of historical data. Additionally, effectively
combining the different components and addressing non-linearities in the ENSO system can present
challenges.
In recent years, such models have witnessed further advancements through the integration of domain
knowledge and Deep Learning techniques. Hybrid models now incorporate not only dynamical and
statistical approaches but also leverage the power of Deep Learning algorithms to extract complex
patterns and relationships from vast amounts of data [24]. The inclusion of domain knowledge, such as
physical processes and climate dynamics, in conjunction with Deep Learning capabilities, enhances the
understanding and prediction of ENSO dynamics. This integration enables hybrid models to unlock
new possibilities for more accurate and reliable ENSO forecasting, contributing to improved climate
projections and aiding in climate risk assessment and mitigation efforts.

Concepts
Deep Learning is a subset of machine learning that focuses on training artificial neural networks to
perform complex tasks. It involves the use of multiple layers of interconnected artificial neurons, known
as deep neural networks, to process and analyze data. One of the key advantages of deep learning is
its ability to automatically learn hierarchical representations of data, enabling it to extract meaningful
features and patterns from raw input. This allows deep learning models to excel in various domains,
such as computer vision, natural language processing, and speech recognition. Deep learning has revolu-
tionized these fields, achieving state-of-the-art performance in tasks such as image classification, object
detection, language translation, and speech synthesis. With its ability to handle large-scale datasets and
learn intricate representations, deep learning has become a powerful tool in solving complex problems
and has opened up new possibilities in artificial intelligence research and applications.
The success of deep learning can be attributed to several factors. Firstly, the availability of massive
amounts of data has been crucial in training deep neural networks. With more data, deep learning mod-
els can learn more robust and generalized representations, leading to better performance. Additionally,

gradient descent, has enabled the training of deep neural networks with many layers. These algorithms,
combined with activation functions, regularization techniques, and optimization strategies, contribute
to the successful training and convergence of deep learning models. Overall, deep learning has proven
to be a game-changer in the field of machine learning, pushing the boundaries of what is possible in
terms of data analysis, pattern recognition, and decision-making.
3.1
Artificial Neural Networks (ANN)
Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) are machine learning algorithms that excel in recognizing patterns
by learning from data and are inspired by the structure and functionality of the human brain.
The human brain is composed of approximately 1010 neurons, which act as computing elements and
communicate through a connection network with approximately 104 connections per element. Artificial
neural networks (ANNs) function as parallel distributed computing networks and share some funda-
mental characteristics with biological neural systems (Figure 3.1). When considering ANNs, there are
multiple input signals, denoted as X = [x1, x2, ..., xn], which are received by the neurons. Each input
is assigned a relative weight, represented as W = [w1, w2, ..., wn], that determines its influence on the
overall impact. These weights are adaptive coeﬀicients within the network and govern the strength of
the input signal. The output signal of a neuron, referred to as NET, is computed by a summation block,
analogous to the biological cell body, which algebraically combines the weighted inputs [53].

6. Calculate the necessary updates for weights w in a way that minimizes this error.
7. Add up the calculated weights’ updates w to the accumulated total updates W.
8. Repeat steps 2-7 for several instances comprising an epoch.
9. Adjust the weights w of the network by the updates W.
10. Repeat steps 2-9 until all instances in the training set are processed. This constitutes one iteration.
11. Repeat the iteration of steps 2-10 until the error for the entire system (error defined above or
the error on the cross-validation set) is acceptably low or the predefined number of iterations is
reached.
3.1.1.4
Training of the Network
The backpropagation algorithm performs gradient descent on the error surface by modifying each weight
in proportion to the gradient of the surface at its location.
It is known that gradient descent can
sometimes cause networks to get stuck in a depression in the error surface should such a depression
exist. These are called ’local minima’, which corresponds to a partial solution for the network in response
to the training data. Ideally, we seek a global minimum (lowest error value possible); nevertheless, the
local minima are surrounded and the network usually does not leave it by the standard backpropagation
algorithm. Special techniques should be used to get out of a local minimum: changing the learning
parameter, the number of hidden units, but notably by the use of the momentum term (α) in the
algorithm. The momentum term is chosen generally between 0 and 1. Taking into account this last
term, the formula for modifications of weights at epoch t + 1 is given by:
∆wkj(t + 1) = ηδkxk + α∆wkj(t)
(3.6)
The learning rate (η) and the momentum term (α) play an important role in the backpropagation
algorithm. If the values of these parameters are wrong, the network can oscillate, or more seriously,
it can get stuck in a local minimum. In generall, there are many optimization techniques for ANNs
training that will be presented in a next subsection.
3.1.1.5
Testing the Network
Testing of data is an essential step to evaluate the performance and effectiveness of the trained network.
In the context of backpropagation, both training and test sets are required. The training set is used
to train the network by adjusting the weights, while the test set is employed to assess the network’s
performance after training completion. During the testing phase, input patterns are provided to the
network, and the output patterns generated by the neural network are compared with the desired output
patterns. The agreement or disagreement between these two sets provides valuable insights into the
network’s performance.
To ensure proper validation of the trained network, it is crucial to have a third independent data
matrix that is completely separate from the training and test sets. If a suﬀicient number of examples are

3.1.2
Activation Functions
Activation (or transfer) functions play a crucial role in ANNs by enabling them to learn intricate patterns
within the data. Acting as a decision-maker, the activation function determines the information to be
transmitted to the next neuron in the network. It takes the output signal from the preceding cell and
converts it into a format suitable for the subsequent cell.
Non-linear activation functions are essential in neural networks for several reasons. Firstly, they
emulate the behavior of neurons in the human brain, ensuring that the neuron outputs remain within
specific bounds. This is vital because as we said in Section 3.1.1.2 the input to the activation function
is a weighted sum of inputs, and unconstrained values can lead to computational challenges in deep
networks with numerous parameters. Certain activation functions, like softmax, yield distinct output
values for different input ranges, offering valuable properties.
==PAGE:33==
3.1 Artificial Neural Networks (ANN)
25
The primary attribute of an activation function is its capability to introduce non-linearity into
the neural network. Linear classifiers or layers have limitations in learning and representing complex
patterns effectively. By incorporating multiple layers and employing non-linear activation functions,
ANNs acquire the ability to capture intricate non-linear relationships within the data.
To achieve this, specific non-linear activation functions are placed between layers. These functions
introduce complexity and empower the network to comprehend and process high-dimensional patterns
eﬀiciently. To summarize, an activation function should have the following features [47]:
• Vanishing Gradient problem: The issue of the Vanishing Gradient problem arises in Neural
Networks trained using gradient descent. In the backward propagation step, which utilizes the
chain rule to compute weight adjustments and minimize loss, this problem becomes apparent.
Let’s consider a two-layer network, where the first layer is denoted as f1(x) and the second layer
as f2(x). The overall network can be represented as o(x) = f2 (f1(x)).
When calculating the weights during the backward pass, we obtain o′(x) = f2(x)⋆f ′
1(x). Here,
f1(x) is a composite function that includes an activation function, Act (W1x1 + b1), after the first
layer. By applying the chain rule once more, we can observe that f ′
1(x) = Act (W1x1 + b1)* x1,
indicating its direct dependence on the activation value.
Now, let’s envision the chain rule propagating through multiple layers during backpropagation.
If the value of the activation function, Act 0 , falls between 0 and 1 , multiple such values will
multiply together when computing the gradient for the initial layers. Consequently, this diminishes
the gradient’s value for the initial layers, impeding proper learning. In other words, the gradients
tend to vanish due to the depth of the network and the activation function progressively shifting
the value towards zero. This phenomenon is commonly known as the vanishing gradient problem.
To mitigate the vanishing gradient problem, it is desirable for the activation function to avoid
shifting the gradient towards zero.
• Zero-Centered: An ideal activation function should exhibit symmetry around zero, ensuring
that the gradients do not favor a specific direction. This property helps maintain the stability
and convergence of the training process.
• Computational Eﬀiciency: Activation functions are applied at each layer of a deep network
and are computed numerous times.
Therefore, it is important for activation functions to be
computationally eﬀicient, allowing for faster training and inference.
• Differentiability: Since neural networks employ gradient-based optimization algorithms like
backpropagation, the activation function used in each layer should be differentiable or at least have
differentiable components. This enables the calculation of gradients and facilitates the learning
process.
One commonly used activation function is the sigmoid function, which maps the input to a smooth
S-shaped curve between 0 and 1. Sigmoid functions are advantageous when dealing with binary classi-
fication problems, as they squash the input to a probability-like output. However, they suffer from the
vanishing gradient problem, which limits their effectiveness in deep neural networks.
Softmax is another popular activation function that is particularly useful in multi-class classification
tasks. It takes a vector of real numbers as input and normalizes it into a probability distribution over
multiple classes. Softmax ensures that the sum of the probabilities is equal to 1, making it suitable
for problems where the output needs to represent class probabilities. However, softmax is not ideal for
regression tasks or cases where multiple classes can be activated simultaneously.
The hyperbolic tangent (tanh) function is similar to the sigmoid function but maps the input to a
range between -1 and 1. Tanh functions are useful for capturing more complex non-linear patterns as
they provide a stronger gradient signal compared to sigmoid functions. However, like sigmoid, tanh can
also suffer from the vanishing gradient problem.
Another very popular choice is the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU). It replaces negative input values
with zero, resulting in a piecewise linear function. ReLU is computationally eﬀicient and helps mitigate
==PAGE:34==
3.1 Artificial Neural Networks (ANN)
26
the vanishing gradient problem. However, ReLU can lead to the ”dying ReLU” problem, where neurons
become non-responsive and result in dead pathways during training.
Leaky ReLU is an extension of ReLU that addresses the dying ReLU problem. It introduces a small
positive slope for negative input values, preventing neurons from completely dying. Leaky ReLU retains
the computational eﬀiciency and non-linearity of ReLU while reducing the likelihood of dead neurons.
The choice of the best activation function depends on the specific task and network architecture.
Sigmoid and softmax functions are suitable for classification problems, while tanh and ReLU (or its
variants) are commonly used in hidden layers of deep neural networks. There is no universally superior
activation function as their performance varies depending on the problem at hand, the dataset, and the
network’s architecture. In Figure 3.5 the most commonly used activation functions are presented, their

3.1.5
General Hyperparameters
In Deep Learning, hyperparameters are parameters that define the architecture and behavior of the
Neural network model. These hyperparameters need to be set before the training process begins and
can significantly impact the model’s performance. The most common hyperparameters are the following.
Some of them have been already discussed some others will be discussed in next sections. Here are briefly
presented [32]:
• Learning Rate: The learning rate determines the step size at which the model’s parameters are
updated during training. It controls how quickly or slowly the model learns from the data. A
higher learning rate can lead to faster convergence but may risk overshooting the optimal solution,
while a lower learning rate can result in slower convergence or getting stuck in suboptimal solutions.
• Batch Size: Batch size refers to the number of training examples processed in each iteration of
the optimization algorithm. It influences the trade-off between computation eﬀiciency and the
quality of the parameter updates. Larger batch sizes can provide better convergence but require
more memory and computational resources.
• Number of Epochs: An epoch represents a complete pass through the entire training dataset.
The number of epochs determines how many times the model will see the training data during
training. Setting the appropriate number of epochs is crucial to prevent underfitting (insuﬀicient
learning) or overfitting (excessive learning) of the model.
• Network Architecture: The architecture of the deep neural network includes the number of
layers, the type of layers (e.g., convolutional, recurrent, fully connected), and the number of units
or nodes in each layer. The network architecture determines the capacity and complexity of the
model and can greatly influence its ability to learn and generalize from the data.
• Activation Functions: Activation functions introduce non-linearity into the neural network and
help in modeling complex relationships in the data. Choosing the appropriate activation functions,
such as ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit), sigmoid, or tanh, can impact the model’s ability to learn
and capture important patterns.
==PAGE:39==
3.2 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)
31
• Regularization Techniques: Regularization techniques such as L1 and L2 regularization, dropout,
and batch normalization are used to prevent overfitting by adding constraints or penalties to the
model’s weights. These techniques help in generalizing the model’s predictions and improving its
performance on unseen data.
• Optimizer: The optimizer determines the algorithm used to update the model’s weights during
training.
Popular optimization algorithms include stochastic gradient descent (SGD), Adam,
RMSprop, and Adagrad. Each optimizer has its own characteristics and hyperparameters that
need to be set, such as momentum, learning rate decay, and adaptive learning rates.
• Dropout Rate: Dropout is a regularization technique that randomly sets a fraction of the
inputs to 0 during training, which helps in preventing overfitting. The dropout rate determines
the probability of setting a neuron’s output to 0 and is typically set between 0.2 and 0.5.
• Initialization of Weights: The initial values assigned to the weights of the neural network can
influence the model’s training dynamics and convergence. Proper weight initialization techniques,
such as Xavier or He initialization, can help in mitigating vanishing or exploding gradients during
training.
• Early Stopping: Early stopping is a technique where training is halted if the model’s perfor-
mance on a validation set starts to degrade. It helps in preventing overfitting and finding an
optimal point where the model generalizes well.
3.2
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)
In the field of Deep Learning, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have emerged as a powerful class
of Artificial Neural Networks primarily designed for analyzing visual imagery. Unlike traditional Neural
Networks, CNNs employ a mathematical operation called convolution instead of general matrix multi-
plication, making them highly effective in processing pixel data and widely used in image recognition
and processing tasks. CNNs find applications in various domains such as image and video recognition,
recommender systems, medical image analysis, natural language processing, and more.
One distinguishing characteristic of CNNs is their ability to leverage shared-weight architecture
through convolution kernels or filters. These filters slide across input features, producing translation-
equivariant responses known as feature maps. While CNNs are often referred to as Shift Invariant or
Space Invariant Artificial Neural Networks, it is important to note that they are not always invariant
to translation due to the downsampling operation applied to the input.
Inspired by biological processes, CNNs mimic the organization of the animal visual cortex. Just
like individual cortical neurons responding to stimuli in a restricted region known as the receptive
field, CNNs capture local dependencies in images through convolutional operations. One significant
advantage of CNNs is their ability to automate the learning of filters, eliminating the need for hand-
engineering feature extraction. This independence from prior knowledge and human intervention makes
CNNs highly advantageous in various image classification tasks.
In summary, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have revolutionized the field of Deep Learning
by effectively analyzing visual data. Their ability to process pixel data, exploit hierarchical patterns,
and automate feature extraction sets them apart from traditional neural networks.
By leveraging
convolution and regularization techniques, CNNs have become a powerful tool in deep learning and
have propelled advancements in numerous applications, including ENSO forecasting.

Architecture
A typical CNN architecture is composed of various layers, including convolutional layers, pooling layers,
and fully connected layers. In CNNs, convolutional layers perform convolutions on the input matrix,
typically using the Frobenius inner product (a binary operation that takes two matrices and returns a

used in these layers is commonly the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) function. This convolution operation
generates feature maps that serve as inputs for subsequent layers in the network such as pooling layers,
fully connected layers, and normalization layers.

3.2.1.2
Pooling layers
Pooling layers in Convolutional Neural Networks play a crucial role in reducing the dimensionality of
data and controlling overfitting. There are two common types of pooling used in CNNs: max pooling
and average pooling.
Max pooling takes the maximum value from each local cluster of neurons in the feature map, while
average pooling computes the average value. These pooling operations are implemented as non-linear
down-sampling techniques. Max pooling, in particular, is widely used and partitions the input image
into sub-regions, outputting the maximum value from each sub-region [32].
The main purpose of pooling layers is to progressively reduce the spatial size of the representation,
thereby reducing the number of parameters, memory footprint, and computational load in the network.
This process is known as down-sampling.
By reducing the spatial size, pooling layers also control
overfitting and help manage computational resources [32].
Typically, pooling layers are inserted between successive convolutional layers in a CNN architecture.
They contribute to local translation invariance, but for global translation invariance, a form of global
pooling needs to be employed. Pooling layers operate independently on each depth or slice of the input,
resizing it spatially. A common form of max pooling uses filters of size 2×2 with a stride of 2, resulting

3.2.1.3
Fully connected layers
Fully connected layers, also known as MLP layers, connect all neurons from one layer to another. The
flattened matrix resulting from previous layers is fed into these fully connected layers for classification
purposes. The weights and biases associated with each neuron in the network are adjusted iteratively
through the learning process [32].
3.2.1.4
Receptive field
In CNNs, the connectivity pattern between neurons varies depending on the layer type. In convolutional
layers, neurons have a restricted area in the previous layer known as their receptive field, from which
they receive input. Typically, this receptive field takes the form of a square, such as a 5 by 5 grid of
neurons. On the other hand, in fully connected layers, the receptive field covers the entire previous
layer, and each neuron receives input from all the neurons in that layer.
As a result, in convolutional layers, each neuron gathers information from a larger input area com-
pared to previous layers. This expanded receptive field is achieved by repeatedly applying convolutions,
which consider not only the value of a pixel but also the values of its neighboring pixels. By doing so,
convolutional layers can capture local spatial patterns effectively.
To manipulate the size of the receptive field, alternative approaches to standard convolutional layers
can be employed. For instance, dilated convolution or atrous convolution expands the receptive field
without increasing the number of parameters [32]. It achieves this by incorporating visible and blind
regions. Additionally, dilated convolutional layers can have filters with multiple dilation ratios, enabling
variable receptive field sizes.
3.2.1.5
Weights
The weights in a CNN are typically organized in filters, also known as kernels. Each filter is responsible
for detecting a specific feature or pattern in the input (e.g., a particular shape). During the training
phase, the network learns to adjust the filter weights to emphasize important features and suppress
irrelevant information. This enables the network to effectively identify and classify objects or patterns
in images, videos, or other types of data.
By leveraging the power of convolutional operations and weight sharing across multiple neurons,
CNNs can eﬀiciently extract hierarchical representations of the input, capturing both local and global
features. The learning process involves fine-tuning these weights through backpropagation, where the
network adjusts the weights based on the error between its predicted output and the desired output.

3.2.2
Hyperparameters
• Depth: The depth refers to the number of neurons in a layer that connect to the same region of
the input volume. In the context of convolutional layers, each neuron in a depth slice shares the
same weights and biases. The depth dimension of the output volume represents different filters
or kernels, each of which learns to activate for a different feature in the input [32].
• Kernel Size: The kernel size refers to the dimensions (width and height) of the filter that is
applied to the input data during the convolution operation. It plays a crucial role in determining
the receptive field of each convolutional layer. A larger kernel size covers a larger area of the
input data, resulting in a broader receptive field and capturing more spatial information. This
can be beneficial for tasks that require understanding global context. Conversely, smaller kernel
sizes focus on local regions, allowing the network to capture finer details and detecting more
fine-grained features within the input data [32].
• Stride: Stride controls the allocation of depth columns around the width and height of the
input volume. It determines the spatial displacement of the convolutional filter as it moves across
the input volume. A stride of 1 means the filter moves one pixel at a time, resulting in heavily
overlapping receptive fields between the columns and larger output volumes. A stride greater than
1 (e.g., 2) reduces the overlap between receptive fields and leads to smaller spatial dimensions of
the output volume. Stride plays a role in downsampling the input volume and can affect the
spatial resolution of the output [32].
• Padding: Padding is an additional technique used in convolutional layers to adjust the spatial
size of the output volume. It involves adding extra border pixels (often zeros) to the input volume
before applying the convolution operation. Padding helps control the output volume’s spatial size,
especially when the goal is to preserve the spatial dimensions of the input volume. By adding
padding, it is possible to ensure that the convolutional filter can cover the entire input volume
and produce an output volume of the same spatial size.
Commonly used padding techniques
include ”same” padding, where the padding size is chosen such that the output volume has the
same spatial dimensions as the input volume, and ”valid” padding, where no padding is added,
resulting in a smaller output volume [32].
• Number of Filters: The number of filters determines the depth or the number of channels in the
output feature map of a convolutional layer. Each filter is responsible for learning and detecting
different features in the input data. By increasing the number of filters, the network can learn
more complex patterns and achieve a higher level of feature representation. This allows the model
to capture a richer variety of information from the input data and enhances its ability to recognize
diverse patterns and structures [32].
• Filter size: The filter size in a CNN specifies the dimensions of the spatial filter used during
convolution. As the filter slides over the input data, it performs element-wise multiplication with
the local receptive field, extracting spatial features. The choice of filter size influences the level
of detail captured by the network. Smaller filter sizes excel at capturing fine-grained details and
intricate patterns, which can be advantageous for tasks that require precise localization.
On
the other hand, larger filter sizes are effective in capturing broader patterns and global context,
enabling the model to recognize higher-level features [32].
• Pooling type and size: As already mentioned pooling is a technique employed in CNNs to
reduce the spatial dimensions of the input feature map while retaining important information.
The pooling type, such as max pooling or average pooling, determines the operation performed
during downsampling. Max pooling selects the maximum value within a pooling window, while
average pooling calculates the average value.
The pooling size defines the dimensions of the
pooling window that slides over the feature map. Pooling helps to summarize and abstract the
spatial information, making the network more robust to small spatial variations and reducing the
==PAGE:44==

overall dimensionality of the data. More analytically we have defined the pooling operation in
Section 3.2.1.2.
• Dilation: Dilation is a parameter that controls the spacing between the values in the filter. A
dilation rate of 1 means adjacent values are used, while a dilation rate greater than 1 introduces
gaps between the values. By increasing the dilation rate, the receptive field of the network can be
expanded without significantly increasing the number of parameters. This enables the model to
capture larger patterns and gather more contextual information from the input data. Dilation is
particularly useful when dealing with larger images or when there is a need to capture long-range
dependencies in the data [32].
3.2.3
Regularization methods
Regularization is an essential aspect of CNNs, aimed at preventing overfitting and improving generaliza-
tion. Unlike fully connected networks, where each neuron is connected to all neurons in the next layer,
CNNs utilize the hierarchical patterns present in the data. They break down the input into smaller,
simpler features represented by filters, which are applied to different regions of the input to extract rele-
vant information. As the network progresses through layers, these features are combined and assembled
into more complex patterns, enabling the network to learn increasingly abstract representations. This
hierarchical approach ensures eﬀicient learning of complex patterns while minimizing overfitting risks.
One widely used method is dropout, introduced in 2012 Hinton et al [41]. Dropout combats overfit-
ting by randomly deactivating individual nodes during training with a probability of 1 −p, effectively
reducing the effective network size. This stochastic dropout forces the network to learn more robust
and generalizable features. Mathematically, during training, the output of a node in a layer can be
represented as follows:
output = dropout( input ) × mask
(3.23)
where the mask is a binary vector that indicates which nodes are active (1) and which are deactivated
(0) according to the dropout probability p. During inference, the dropout layer is deactivated, and the
scaled output is used to maintain the expected value:
output = input × p
(3.24)
DropConnect is a similar regularization technique to dropout, introduced by Wan et al. in 2013
[96]. Rather than dropping nodes, DropConnect randomly drops connections between layers. Each unit
receives input from a random subset of units in the previous layer, promoting model robustness and
preventing overfitting. The output of a node in a DropConnect layer can be represented mathematically
as:
output = dropconnect ( input ) × mask
(3.25)
where the mask indicates which connections are active and which are dropped based on the DropConnect
probability p
While dropout and DropConnect are effective for fully connected layers, convolutional layers have
specific connectivity patterns that may not fully benefit from these techniques. In 2013, Zeiler and
Fergus introduced stochastic pooling as an alternative to deterministic pooling operations in CNNs.
Stochastic pooling replaces conventional pooling operations with a stochastic procedure, where the
activation within each pooling region is randomly chosen according to a multinomial distribution based
on the activities within the region. This approach eliminates the need for hyperparameters and can be
combined with other regularization techniques like dropout and data augmentation to further enhance
generalization.
Explicit regularization techniques can also be employed to prevent overfitting. Early stopping is
a straightforward technique that stops the training process before overfitting occurs, preventing the
model from excessively fitting the training data.

performance if stopped prematurely. Another approach is to limit the number of parameters in the

3.3
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN)
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are a significant category of Deep Learning models, complementing
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Unlike feed-forward neural networks (eg the MLP described
in Section 3.1.1) , RNNs possess an internal memory, allowing them to process sequences of inputs
effectively. This characteristic makes RNNs well-suited for handling sequential data and tasks such as
unsegmented, connected handwriting recognition or speech recognition, where the order of inputs plays
a crucial role.
The fundamental property of an RNN is its recurrent nature, meaning it performs the same com-
putation for each input in a sequence while considering the past computations. Upon generating an
output, the output is copied and fed back into the recurrent network. Consequently, RNNs can make
decisions by incorporating information from the current input as well as the learned knowledge from
previous inputs.
One of the distinctive features of RNNs is their ability to utilize their internal state, or memory, to
process sequences of inputs. Unlike other neural networks where inputs are considered independent of
one another, RNNs excel in capturing the dependencies and relationships among inputs.
RNNs are particularly effective in tasks that require handling variable-length input and output
sequences. Due to their internal memory, RNNs can process arbitrary sequences of inputs, making
them well-suited for tasks like predicting the next word in a sequence. Similar to the human brain,
RNNs assign greater importance to the recent information, enabling them to anticipate and generate
coherent sentences in conversations. For instance, an RNN trained to translate text can learn that the
translation of the word ”dog” may vary depending on whether it is preceded by the word ”hot” or not.
The memory capabilities of RNNs enable them to retain information from previous steps, influencing
decision-making in the current step. By considering both the current input and what it has learned
from previous inputs, an RNN calculates its current state using a combination of the present input
and the previous state. This mechanism creates a feedback loop where the output from the previous
step becomes the input for the current step. As a result, information cycles through this loop, allowing
RNNs to capture dependencies and long-term dependencies in sequential data.
However, they do possess certain limitations that can impact their performance. One significant
limitation is the vanishing gradient problem.
During backpropagation, the gradients in traditional
RNNs diminish exponentially as they propagate through time. This makes it challenging for the network
to capture long-term dependencies in sequences, as the influence of earlier inputs on the current state
becomes negligible. Consequently, traditional RNNs struggle to effectively learn and represent long-term
dependencies.
On the other hand, the exploding gradient problem refers to the opposite situation.
Here, the
gradients grow exponentially during backpropagation, leading to unstable training and diﬀiculties in
optimizing the network parameters. These problems hinder the ability of traditional RNNs to capture
and model complex long-term dependencies accurately.
Another limitation of traditional RNNs is their diﬀiculty in capturing long-term dependencies in
sequences. As the recurrent computations are repeated through time, errors and noise accumulate,
making it challenging for the network to remember information from earlier steps when making predic-
tions. This limitation restricts the modeling capacity of traditional RNNs, particularly in tasks that
require capturing intricate long-term dependencies.
Furthermore, traditional RNNs lack explicit memory cells to store and access information over time.
This absence poses a challenge for the network in retaining and utilizing relevant information from
earlier steps, which hinders its ability to handle tasks that involve retaining information over long
sequences.
In terms of computational eﬀiciency, traditional RNNs process sequential data sequentially, limiting
their parallelization capabilities. Each step in the sequence depends on the preceding step, preventing
eﬀicient parallel processing. This limitation can impact the performance of traditional RNNs, especially
on hardware architectures optimized for parallel computation, such as GPUs.
Additionally, traditional RNNs require fixed-length inputs and outputs, which can be problematic
when dealing with sequences of varying lengths. Padding or truncation of sequences may be necessary,

leading to information loss or ineﬀiciencies in processing.
Moreover, traditional RNNs consider only the current input and the information stored in the
internal state when making predictions. They lack the ability to explicitly capture context from a wider
range of inputs, which may limit their understanding and representation of complex sequential data.
In order to address the limitations of traditional RNNs, advanced variants have been developed,
including Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), and transformer-based
models. These variants aim to overcome the challenges faced by traditional RNNs and improve their
ability to model and understand sequential data. By incorporating memory cells, additional gates, and
self-attention mechanisms, these advanced architectures have demonstrated significant advancements
in capturing long-term dependencies, handling variable-length sequences, and effectively utilizing con-
textual information.
While we will refer briefly to some common variants here, it is worth noting that our focus in this
thesis is on LSTM variants and more specifically to convLSTM, as we also implement the convLSTM
model alongside the CNN, thus we will include theoretical information in a separate subsection only for
the LSTM models.
The LSTM, or Long Short-Term Memory, is a type of RNN architecture specifically designed to
address the vanishing gradient problem encountered when training traditional RNNs on long sequences.
LSTM introduces a memory cell and three gates (input gate, forget gate, and output gate) to regulate
the flow of information within the network. This architecture enables LSTM to selectively retain or
discard information over long sequences, making it highly effective at capturing long-term dependencies.
On the other hand, the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) is another RNN variant that tackles the
vanishing gradient problem while offering a more eﬀicient alternative to LSTM. GRU consolidates the
input and forget gates of LSTM into a single update gate and introduces a reset gate to control the
information flow. This simplified architecture enhances computational eﬀiciency compared to LSTM
while maintaining competitive performance.
Another common architecture is the Bidirectional RNN (BiRNN), which extends the traditional
RNN architecture by incorporating information from both past and future contexts. It consists of two
separate RNNs, with one processing the input sequence in the forward direction and the other in the
reverse direction. By considering information from both directions, BiRNNs can capture dependencies
in both past and future information, making them well-suited for tasks where the complete context is
crucial.
Additionally, the Hierarchical RNN (HRNN) is a hierarchical variant of RNN that introduces mul-
tiple levels of RNNs to capture dependencies at different time scales. It combines a lower-level RNN,
which processes local temporal patterns, with a higher-level RNN that handles patterns at a higher tem-
poral resolution. This hierarchical structure empowers HRNNs to effectively model complex sequential
data by capturing dependencies over various time scales.
Finally, although not strictly RNN architectures, transformer-based models have gained popularity
for processing sequential data. Transformers utilize a self-attention mechanism, allowing them to cap-
ture dependencies between all positions in the input sequence. They have achieved remarkable success
in various natural language processing tasks, such as machine translation and language understanding
(for instance, chatGPT is based on transformers).

3.3.2
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) is an advanced type of recurrent neural network (RNN) that ad-
dresses the limitations of traditional RNNs that we mentioned earlier, particularly the vanishing gradient
problem, and allows for capturing long-term dependencies in sequential data.
LSTM networks are particularly effective in processing and predicting sequential data such as speech,
video, and time series data. They excel in tasks involving long-term dependencies, where traditional
RNNs struggle due to the vanishing gradient problem. As mentioned before, the vanishing gradient
problem occurs when the gradients used to update the network’s parameters become extremely small,
hindering the learning process. In contrast, LSTM units mitigate this problem by allowing gradients
to flow unchanged, ensuring that important information from previous time-steps can be retained and
influence the network’s predictions effectively.
LSTM networks consist of two essential elements: gates and cells. Specifically, the gates within an
LSTM framework include the input gate (it), the forget gate (ft), and the output gate (ot). These gates
are integral in regulating the flow of information throughout the network and overseeing the storage
and retrieval of past input information. We will present the mathematical equations that define the
behavior of these gates [92] and we will explain how they function, considering the sigmoid activation
function, which is commonly employed in such architectures.

new input should be ignored, while a value close to 1 means that the input should be remembered.
The forget gate (ft) controls the extent to which the previous memory cell should be forgotten. It
follows a similar process as the input gate, taking the concatenation of the previous hidden state and
the current input and passing it through a weight matrix (wf) and bias (bf), followed by a sigmoid
function. The forget gate output determines how much of the previous memory cell’s contents should
be retained (close to 1) or forgotten (close to 0).
The output gate (ot) regulates the amount of information that should be output from the memory
cell. It takes the concatenation of the previous hidden state and the current input, applies a weight
matrix (wo) and bias (bo), and passes it through a sigmoid function. The output gate’s output is then
used to scale the memory cell’s contents before sending it to the next hidden state and generating the
output of the LSTM block.

As it is seen, the input gate activation it is calculated by summing the weighted contributions of the
current input, previous hidden state, and previous cell state, followed by the application of the sigmoid
activation function. The resulting activation determines the amount of new information to be stored in
the cell state at the current time step, which will be further used to update the cell state and compute
the hidden state in the ConvLSTM model.
The forget gate activation ft at time step t which determines how much of the previous cell state
Ct−1 should be forgotten or preserved for the current time step, is calculated by:
ft = σ (Wxf ∗Xt + Whf ∗Ht−1 + Wcf ◦Ct−1 + bf)
(3.40)
where:
• Wxf is the weight matrix for the forget gate applied to the current input Xt. It captures the
influence of the current input on the forget gate activation.
• Whf is the weight matrix for the forget gate applied to the previous hidden state Ht−1. It captures
the influence of the previous hidden state on the forget gate activation.
• Wcf is the weight matrix for the forget gate applied element-wise to the previous cell state Ct−1.
It captures the influence of the previous cell state on the forget gate activation.
• bf: The bias term for the forget gate. It provides an additional learnable constant that can shift
the forget gate activation.
Overall, the forget gate activation ft is calculated by summing the weighted contributions of the
current input, previous hidden state, and previous cell state, followed by the application of the sigmoid
activation function. The resulting activation determines how much of the previous cell state information
should be forgotten or preserved in the current cell state calculation.
As we mentioned before, by incorporating the forget gate in the ConvLSTM model, the network
can learn to selectively retain or discard information from previous time steps, allowing it to capture
long-term dependencies in the input sequences.
Regarding the output gate activation ot at time step t, it determines how much of the cell state Ct
should be exposed as the hidden state Ht at the current time step, and is computed as follows:
ot = σ (Wxo ∗Xt + Who ∗Ht−1 + Wco ◦Ct + bo)
(3.41)
where:
• Wxo is the weight matrix for the output gate applied to the current input Xt. It captures the
influence of the current input on the output gate activation.
• Who is the weight matrix for the output gate applied to the previous hidden state Ht−1.
It
captures the influence of the previous hidden state on the output gate activation.
• Wco is the weight matrix for the output gate applied element-wise to the current cell state Ct. It
captures the influence of the current cell state on the output gate activation.
• bo is the bias term for the output gate.

As we can see, the output gate activation ot is calculated by summing the weighted contributions
of the current input, previous hidden state, and current cell state, followed by the application of the
sigmoid activation function (in our specific example in Figure 3.11). The resulting activation determines
how much of the cell state information should be exposed as the hidden state at the current time step.
By adjusting the output gate, the ConvLSTM model can control the flow of information and selec-
tively reveal or hide specific aspects of the cell state. This mechanism allows the model to focus on
relevant information when generating the hidden state, enabling it to capture and emphasize important
features for subsequent tasks or predictions.
Regarding the cell state Ct at time step t in which we referred in the three previous equations, it
represents the memory of the model and is responsible for preserving and updating information over
time. It is calculated by:
Ct = ft ◦Ct−1 + it ◦tanh (Wxc ∗Xt + Whc ∗Ht−1 + bc)
(3.42)
where:
• Ct is the cell state at time step t. It stores and carries information across different time steps.
• ft is the forget gate activation at time step t. It determines how much of the previous cell state
Ct−1 should be forgotten.
• it is the input gate activation at time step t. It determines the amount of new information to be
stored in the cell state.
• tanh is the hyperbolic tangent activation function. It squashes the input to a value between -1
and 1, enabling the model to capture both positive and negative values.
• Wxc is the weight matrix for the input gate applied to the current input Xt. It captures the
influence of the current input on the input gate activation.
• Whc is the weight matrix for the input gate applied to the previous hidden state Ht−1. It captures
the influence of the previous hidden state on the input gate activation.
• bc is the bias term for the cell state.
As we can see, the cell state Ct is updated by combining two terms. The first term, ft ◦Ct −1,
represents the contribution of the previous cell state after applying the forget gate. It allows the model
to selectively forget or preserve specific information from the previous time step, as we already know.
The second term, it◦tanh (Wxc ∗Xt + Whc ∗Ht−1 + bc), computes the new information to be added
to the cell state. It involves the input gate activation it and the hyperbolic tangent function applied to
a combination of the current input, previous hidden state, and a bias term. This term determines the
relevance and influence of the current input and previous hidden state on updating the cell state.
By combining these two terms, the cell state Ct is updated to capture both the relevant information
from the past and the new information at the current time step. The ConvLSTM model can learn
to retain or discard information over time, allowing it to capture long-term dependencies and make
informed predictions.
Finally, the hidden state Ht at time step t represents the output of the model, capturing the relevant
information from the current cell state:
Ht = ot ◦tanh (Ct)
(3.43)
where:
• Ht is the hidden state at time step t. It contains the output information that the model generates
based on the cell state.

• ot is the output gate activation at time step t. It determines how much of the cell state information
should be exposed as the hidden state.
• Ct is the cell state at time step t.
The hidden state Ht is computed by applying two operations. First, we apply the element-wise
multiplication between the output gate activation ot and the hyperbolic tangent function of the current
cell state Ct. This operation allows the model to selectively amplify or attenuate the information from

By combining these operations, we compute the hidden state Ht, which contains the relevant infor-

Description
The nino3.4 index represents the average sea surface temperature anomaly in a specific region. It is
calculated by considering the area bounded by 5°N to 5°S latitude and 170°W to 120°W longitude (see
Figure 4.1). This region is known for its significant variability on El Niño time scales and is situated

Figure 4.1: Tracking the four regions of the SST in the tropical Pacific [99].
Predicting the nino3.4 index is of utmost importance for accurate forecasting of ENSO phenomenon.
This index, along with other ENSO indicators based on sea surface temperatures, provides valuable
insights into the state of the tropical Pacific Ocean and helps monitor and anticipate the occurrence of
El Niño and La Niña events. More specificaly, to identify an El Niño or La Niña event, the 5-month
running-average of the nino3.4 index is assessed. If the index exceeds +0.5°C for El Niño or falls below
-0.5°C for La Niña for at least six consecutive months, an event is recognized. These thresholds serve
as criteria to determine the intensity and duration of ENSO events [24].
Moreover, understanding the nino3.4 index helps in forecasting regional climate anomalies. Different
parts of the world experience diverse effects during El Niño and La Niña events. Some regions may
witness increased rainfall and flooding, while others may face drought conditions, as we have already
mentioned in Section 2.1. By predicting the nino3.4 index, we can provide early warnings and guidance
to governments, communities, and industries, enabling them to make informed decisions regarding water
resource management, agricultural practices, and disaster preparedness.
Accurate forecasting of the nino3.4 index also aids in improving climate models and enhancing our
understanding of the underlying mechanisms driving ENSO (see Section 2.1.1). By studying historical
nino3.4 index values, we can identify patterns and relationships between ENSO and other climate
phenomena, such as the Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) and the Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD). This
knowledge contributes to the refinement of climate models, which in turn assists in making more reliable
long-term climate projections.

4.2
Sea Surface Temperature (SST)
4.2.1
Description
Sea surface temperature is a key measurement in understanding climate patterns and predicting weather
conditions.
Various advanced technologies, including satellite microwave radiometers, infrared (IR)
radiometers, in situ buoys, and ships of opportunity, have enabled us to obtain precise measurements
of SST. These measurements are taken at different depths, depending on the instrument used. For
example, buoys typically have sensors located around 1 meter depth, while satellite instruments like
IR and microwave radiometers measure temperatures at depths of about 20 micrometers and a few
millimeters [85].
The measurement of SST from space provides a unique perspective, offering a depth-related view
depending on the frequency of the satellite instrument employed. This distinction is critical in accurately
assessing thermal variability in the upper ocean [85]. A comprehensive understanding of the sea surface
temperature allows for better weather prediction, ocean forecasts, and various coastal applications,
including fisheries forecasts, pollution monitoring, and tourism initiatives [85]. Furthermore, SST maps
serve as valuable tools for oceanographers, meteorologists, and climate scientists in their quest for
scientific research and data analysis.
Historically, global SST data was primarily obtained through IR satellite retrievals, but their cov-
erage was limited due to cloud interference.
The launch of microwave retrievals, such as the TMI
(TRMM Microwave Imager), in 1997 revolutionized SST monitoring, as microwaves could penetrate
through clouds, providing improved coverage and enabling measurements even in the presence of cloud
cover [30]. This breakthrough was particularly beneficial in tropical cyclone forecasting, where accurate
SST measurements were previously hindered by cloud obstructions. The advent of microwave instru-
ments in 1998 brought about a significant improvement in measuring SST in cyclone-prone regions
[85].
The sensitivity of vertically polarized microwave brightness temperature to SST within the 4 to 11
GHz frequency range has proven instrumental in obtaining precise SST data. Alongside SST, bright-
ness temperature is influenced by factors such as sea-surface roughness, atmospheric temperature, and
moisture profile. Advanced microwave instruments like TMI, AMSR-E, AMSR2, WindSat, and GMI
utilize multiple frequencies and polarizations to discern the distinct signatures of surface roughness and
atmospheric effects, thus allowing for accurate retrieval of SST [30].
Among these instruments, AMSR-E and AMSR2 feature an additional 7 GHz channel, which sig-
nificantly enhances the estimation of sea-surface roughness, ultimately leading to improved accuracy
for SSTs below 12°C [30]. These cutting-edge instruments concurrently retrieve vital parameters like
wind speed, columnar water vapor, cloud liquid water, and rain rate, using all available channels [30].
By leveraging the strengths of each channel, scientists can overcome obstacles such as sun-glitter, rain,
or proximity to land, ensuring nearly complete global coverage of SST measurements on a daily basis,
making these maps indispensable for our research undertaking.

4.3
Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies (SSTA)
4.3.1
Description
Sea surface temperature anomalies (SSTA) refer to deviations from average conditions in the tempera-
ture of the ocean’s top layer. While some anomalies occur without specific patterns or trends, certain
ones hold more significance, such as those associated with the El Niño and La Niña climate cycles in
the Pacific Ocean [86]. As we have already mentioned, these periodic shifts, which happen irregularly
every 3-6 years, result in either warmer or cooler sea surface temperatures along the equator. They
have a notable influence on global weather patterns.
The impact of SST anomalies extends beyond weather patterns. They play a crucial role in altering
the exchange of heat between the ocean and the atmosphere. This modification leads to the generation
of anomalous heating patterns, with their effectiveness in triggering global-scale responses reliant on
their ability to induce Rossby waves [86]. Achieving this requires perturbations in the vorticity field,
brought about by the production of anomalous vertical motion and vortex tube stretching.
Intense and localized SST anomalies can indicate deviations in the path or strength of ocean currents,
such as the Gulf Stream Current off the east coast of the United States. Meanwhile, persistent anomalies
over several years can serve as indicators of regional or global climate change, including global warming.
One example is the warm anomaly consistently observed in the Arctic Ocean, which intensifies during
the Northern Hemisphere summer due to reduced summer sea ice extent [59].
Understanding the generation of Rossby waves from SST anomalies involves considering the balance
between diabatic heating, horizontal temperature advection, and adiabatic cooling due to vertical mo-
tion. The generation of Rossby waves depends on the dominant process between horizontal temperature
advection and adiabatic cooling. In the extratropics, SST anomalies primarily generate low-level heat-
ing, which is counterbalanced by horizontal temperature advection. In contrast, positive SST anomalies
in the tropics lead to enhanced convection and diabatic heating, offset by adiabatic cooling [59].
Tropical SST anomalies have a particularly pronounced impact in the Western Pacific, where the high
average sea surface temperature facilitates a significant increase in evaporation due to temperature’s
exponential effect on saturation vapor pressure. Cumulonimbus convection relies on low-level conver-
gence and upper-tropospheric divergence driven by mass continuity. Low-level convergence moistens
and destabilizes the environment, sustaining convection, while upper-level divergence generates vortic-
ity anomalies. When westerly winds prevail in the region of upper-level divergence, a forced vorticity
anomaly initiates a stationary Rossby wave train. Observations during the Northern Hemisphere winter
support the concept of tropical SST anomalies driving low-frequency variability in the extratropics [59].

Sea Surface Salinity (SSS) and Anomalies (SSSA)
4.4.1
Description
Sea Surface Salinity (SSS) is a critical parameter that measures the concentration of dissolved salts in
the top layer of the ocean. It is expressed in parts per thousand (ppt) or practical salinity units (psu)
and plays a significant role in understanding ocean dynamics, climate patterns, and the water cycle [21].
The salinity of seawater is mainly influenced by evaporation, which increases salinity by leaving behind
dissolved salts, and precipitation and freshwater input from rivers and melting ice, which decrease
salinity by dilution [21].
Salinity Anomalies (SSSA) refer to deviations from the average salinity levels in a particular region
or the global ocean. These anomalies can be positive or negative, indicating higher or lower salinity
levels compared to the long-term average [89]. Various factors contribute to the formation of salinity
anomalies, such as ocean circulation patterns, precipitation and runoff, sea ice formation and melting,
and ocean-atmosphere interactions, including phenomena like El Niño and La Niña which is the case
we are interested in in this occasion.
Studying sea surface salinity and salinity anomalies is essential for the understanding of the ENSO
phenomenon especially in the case of Dynamical Models (see Section 2.3.1). Advanced satellite technolo-
gies, such as the Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity (SMOS) mission and the Aquarius/SAC-D mission,
have significantly improved our ability to monitor and map these variations on a global scale [21]. Ad-
ditionally, in-situ measurements obtained from research vessels and oceanographic buoys play a crucial
role in validating and refining satellite data. However, as we have already mentioned, it was only at
the late 20th century that we managed to acquire satellite data and develop these technologies.
In general, salinity data are used in conjunction with other oceanic parameters like sea surface
temperature and ocean currents to analyze the complex interactions between the atmosphere and the
ocean.
Understanding salinity anomalies is vital for predicting extreme weather events, improving
climate models, and assessing the impact of climate change on ocean ecosystems and coastal regions.
However, it is out of the scope of this thesis to study the physical importance of sea surface salinity
and how the anomalies occur.

6.1
Test of Different Features
At this part, we examine the performance of our model when additional features are included. However,

From these results, we can gain insights and understand some aspects of our model’s behavior.
Firstly, when using a short input sequence (e.g., 3 months), the model can effectively learn patterns and
variations influencing the target variable over a few months, leading to relatively accurate predictions
for the immediate future. However, as the forecast horizon extends further into the future, longer-term
dependencies come into play, and the model might struggle to capture these complex relationships,
resulting in increased prediction errors.
The increasing input data length (e.g., 18 and 24 months) provides the model with a larger historical
context. However, utilizing such long historical periods might also introduce more noise and irrelevant
information, hindering the model’s ability to identify the most relevant features for accurate predictions.
The model might face challenges distinguishing between signal and noise, leading to less precise forecasts.
The occurrence of peaks for certain months can be attributed to the complex spatial interactions
across the Pacific Ocean that influence the nino3.4 index, as discussed in Section 2. The relationship
between the input SST global maps and the nino3.4 index may change over time due to various climate
phenomena. Longer input sequences could introduce more non-stationarity, which our current model
architecture and hyperparameters might not be capable of capturing. Shorter input sequences (e.g., 3
months) might better capture immediate and local spatial dependencies, while longer input sequences
may struggle to effectively represent broader spatial patterns impacting the nino3.4 index’s behavior over
longer forecast horizons. As we designed our model specifically for 3 months ahead predictions without
re-tuning it for different lead times, it might not be optimized to capture longer-term dependencies
effectively.
The scarcity of data also plays a crucial role. The state-of-the-art models achieving remarkable
predictions up to 18 months ahead, as mentioned in Section 2.3.3 (e.g., the model of Ham et al [38]),
implement deep learning models using transfer learning and synthetic data for training, while using the
1982 and onward data only for validation and testing. Although we achieved decent results predicting
3 months ahead with our limited data, we have also showed in Section 4.1.3 that classical time series
analysis methods like ARMA models are equaclly good and more computationally and time-eﬀicient
for shorter forecast horizons. However, our model shows potential for larger forecasting horizons, but
it lacks stability in its predictions (e.g., performing well up to 6 months ahead with 9 months of past
data but failing to capture the 7th month).
Finally, when considering the convLSTM in the discussion, it is intriguing to observe that it achieves
good scores when using all the input features. This success can be attributed to the capabilities of
the convolutional and LSTM operations in ConvLSTMs, which enable the model to learn hierarchical
features. Specifically, the convolutions allow the model to capture local spatial patterns, while the LSTM
memory cells capture temporal dependencies, making it effective for sequential data like ours. Moreover,
the recurrent connections in ConvLSTMs facilitate eﬀicient feature interactions between different input
features at multiple time steps, enabling the model to better exploit the information from the various
features.
The architecture of ConvLSTMs is well-suited for capturing spatio-temporal dependencies, which
aligns with the characteristics of our data. These dependencies play a significant role in influencing the
behavior of the nino3.4 index over time. By effectively capturing these dependencies, the model can
make more accurate predictions. Considering the model’s performance for longer forecasting periods,
it would be interesting to explore its capabilities further. However, conducting extensive investigations
for longer forecasting periods requires significant time and resources, which were limited in our study.

7.1
Summary of Findings
In conclusion, we have successfully implemented two models for predicting the nino3.4 index. Both
models were designed with a 3-month input of past data to forecast 3 months ahead. Throughout
the analysis, we focused on utilizing the CNN model, which demonstrated greater effectiveness in
predicting the nino3.4 index based on global SST maps. On the other hand, the convLSTM model
showed significantly improved performance when additional features were incorporated, such as SSTA,
SSS, and SSSA. Additionally, the CNN model exhibited advantages in terms of eﬀiciency, with respect
to both time and computation.
It is noteworthy that both models were developed from scratch, and although we didn’t surpass the
state-of-the-art 18-month accurate predictions, we achieved strong short-term forecasting for 3 months
ahead. Importantly, our research did not rely on large synthetic datasets; instead, we solely utilized
provided and available ocean observational data based on satellite monitoring.
Furthermore, a particularly interesting finding was the demonstration of the usefulness of salinity
data in the convLSTM model. This observation is significant, as existing literature lacks deep learning
models that incorporate salinity data to predict the nino3.4 index.
Lastly, our investigation revealed the importance of having as much data as possible. We observed a
significant deterioration in the CNN model’s performance when we reduced a decade of SST data. This
finding highlights the criticality of our data split for training, validation, and testing, as our chosen split
yielded the most optimal results. When we reduced the training data in favor of the other two sets, we
experienced inferior outcomes. This result also indicates the potential for achieving even better results
in the future as more data becomes available.
7.2
Limitations
During the implementation of our project, we encountered several limitations that can be divided into
two main categories: Exogenous Limitations and Limitations in our Methodology and Model.
In the first category, one significant limitation was time. The entire process of data collection, pre-
processing, and model implementation turned out to be time-consuming and took longer than expected.
We faced challenges along the way, and the lack of significant domain knowledge further added to the
complexities. While we explored various approaches, some of them were not included in the final report
as they did not lead to significant breakthroughs. Despite these challenges, given the project’s six-month
duration, we made the best possible use of time resources to deliver the most promising outcome.
Another limitation was the scarcity of data. As is commonly known, ocean observational data based
on satellite monitoring is relatively limited. Although we were aware of this from the beginning, we
chose to tackle this challenge and overcome it. Additionally, we can quote Wang et al. [101] from
their upcoming paper (September 2023) as they refer to this exact limitation in their state-of-the-art
literature review, stating that ”ocean observation data is very limited while ocean simulation data is
abundant.” It is essential to note that deep learning models generally require a large amount of data to

ensure accurate predictions, making it challenging to implement a robust prediction model based solely
on ocean observational data.
The limitation of resources also played a significant role. For instance, the most complex model
we tested, the convLSTM, pushed the computational and time resources to the limits of our computer
systems.
Now turning to the limitations in our Methodology and Model, they are interconnected with the
aforementioned ones. First, in our methodology, building and tuning our model with only a 3-month
input and forecasting step without retuning it for different scenarios in terms of input features or
different input-output timesteps proved to be a limitation. As a result, our model faced diﬀiculty in
generalizing well in different scenarios.
However, we attribute this diﬀiculty to the limited data we had at our disposal. Considering that a
similar CNN model with the same simplicity in its architecture can predict quite accurately in different
scenarios up to 18 months ahead (as seen in the model of Ham et al. [38]), we must acknowledge that
this model incorporates almost two centuries of synthetic SST data for training, while using the real
data we employed for the entire project (the SST data from 1982 onwards) only for validation.
Lastly, in the model itself, the 2D CNN architecture we implemented has certain limitations. While
CNN2D models are effective in capturing spatial patterns and relationships in the data, they lack the
ability to explicitly model temporal dependencies. The nino3.4 index is a measure of the sea surface
temperature anomalies in the central and eastern equatorial Pacific over time. Ignoring the temporal
aspect might limit the model’s ability to capture long-term trends and seasonality present in the nino3.4
index. Additionally, while CNN2D models can automatically learn features from raw data, they may
not be the most eﬀicient architecture for capturing complex climate patterns, especially if higher-level
features or long-term dependencies are essential for predicting the Nino3.4 index accurately. Lastly, the
performance of CNN2D models might be sensitive to the spatial resolution of the input data. If the
36x72 grid size does not fully capture the relevant patterns or contains too much spatial noise, it could
impact the model’s predictive capability.
7.3
Future Work
While various other strategies are available, we have chosen to focus on utilizing ocean observational
data without incorporating simulated data or adding domain knowledge by modeling the physical
characteristics of our features, as is done in hybrid models. Our primary goal was to emphasize the
deep learning aspect of the model and its potential with real-world data.
Our research serves as a solid baseline for further investigations. We propose some future steps in
three directions: improvements in the models, enhancements in the methodology, and consideration of
other architectures.
Regarding model improvements, specifically for the CNN model, we propose using it as a baseline
and fine-tuning its architecture with 6 months of input data to predict 24 months ahead. In Table
6.2, we observe that the MSE for this scenario is relatively low, indicating that adjusting the model
to capture the specific temporal dependencies of 6-months input instead of 3, and finding patterns for
24 months ahead prediction instead of 3, could lead to significantly better results. The same holds for
adjusting the model to predict 12 months ahead with 3 months as input, as we can see that the MSE
result is nearly the same.
To reduce the time and computational demands of the convLSTM model and conduct a more thor-
ough analysis, we suggest downsizing the spatial grid of all data maps to the nino3.4 area, using a grid
of (2,10) instead of (36,72). While we conducted some preliminary trials with this setup and obtained
promising results, time constraints prevented us from fully exploring this direction and including it in
our research report.
Regarding improvements in the methodology, we propose three approaches.
Firstly, we suggest
rerunning the entire process, but this time incorporating feedback and making adjustments in the
models and choose the best model at the end of the process. In our initial comparison of 2 models,
we tested everything without performing any hyperparameter re-tuning or changes in the architecture.

Unlike other scientists who have access to almost 2 centuries of (synthetic) data, in our cases was very
diﬀicult to build a model that generalizes well across different scenarios. Thus, recalibrating the models
at each step to achieve optimal results in each case seems to be a promising approach.
Secondly, we recommend exploring a completely different approach. In Section 4.2.3, we discussed
the seasonality in the ENSO cycle and shared some insights from an analysis we conducted on the
SST data. Unfortunately, due to time limitations, we couldn’t include the seasonal component in our
research, and we proceeded with the model implementation from scratch. Now that we have a good
baseline with ready-to-use data, built models and a good summary of the literature, someone can use
our work as a baseline and continue with the data we have collected, but adopt a different approach.
Specifically, splitting the data into seasons and attempting seasonal predictions could reveal special
spatio-temporal dependencies in the data for each season that might be challenging to identify with a
straightforward implementation like ours.
Finally, the third approach regarding the methodology is to handle the testing of different timesteps
of inputs and outputs differently. Adjusting the selected model to each step and employing ensemble
learning techniques could prove beneficial. Ensemble learning, a machine learning technique that com-
bines multiple individual models (learners) to make more accurate and robust predictions, has shown
potential in mitigating the weaknesses of individual models and enhancing overall performance.
Concerning other architectures, we recommend exploring the use of 3D models that can eﬀiciently
capture both temporal and spatial dependencies in the data.
Implementing a CNN3D model or a
CNN-LSTM model and conducting separate analyses for each could provide valuable insights. CNN3D
extends the concept of 2D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN2D) - like the one we implemented -
by incorporating an additional temporal dimension to capture temporal patterns and movements in the
data. This makes CNN3D well-suited for handling 3D volumes directly, making it useful in applications
with video data or medical imaging, among others. Given that our data consists of a series of sequential
pictures, this approach seems highly suitable.
On the other hand, CNN-LSTM is a hybrid deep learning model that combines Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNN) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. It is designed to process sequential
data with spatial information, such as videos or image sequences. The main difference from ConvLSTM
is that CNN-LSTM incorporates a CNN layer before the LSTM layer, allowing it to extract spatial
features from the input data before feeding it to the LSTM. This enables better capturing of spatial
patterns and relationships. Conversely, ConvLSTM directly applies convolutions on the input sequences
using LSTM cells, making it suitable for tasks that require modeling spatial dependencies over time,
such as video prediction and spatio-temporal forecasting.
Both of these models showed promise in our preliminary experiments, indicating their potential for
accurate predictions. Finally, considering more advanced approaches like Graph Neural Networks on
our dataset might prove highly beneficial and open up new avenues for improving predictions.
