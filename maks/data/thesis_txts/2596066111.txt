Dansk Resumé
Dette projekt er baseret på nylige fund, der anvender faststof-materialer som et alterna-
tiv til luft-baseret THz-detektion, som i det seneste årti har været standarden i ultra-
bredbånds THz-detektion. Fordelene ved en faststof-detektor i forhold til luft-baserede
metoder inkluderer reducerede krav til probe-puls energi og påførte spændinger, hvorved
en ultra-bred båndbredde bibeholdes, såvel som en stort dynamisk rækkevidde.
Vejen til en fungerende faststof-baseret THz-detektor består af en teoretisk under-
søgelse, en design-fase udført ved hjælp af elektromagnetiske simuleringer, efterfulgt af
fabrikation og eksperimentel test af detektoren. Dataanalysen er baseret på de indsam-
lede tids-domæne-data, hvorfra spektrene udvindes.
Vi finder ud af, at vores design baseret på sammenlåste, smalle elektrode-fingre giver
os stærk detektions-ydelse, med båndbredde på > 20 THz og i nogle tilfælde mere end
35 THz for påførte spændinger i området 120-350 V og med probe puls-energier mindre
end 1 ￿J. Dynamiske rækkevidde er i samme størrelsesorden som i vores luft-baserede
detektions-system, men pga. fabrikationsfejl forventes dette at blive meget højere for
faststof-detektoren. Vi studerer detektorens ydelse som en funktion af påført spænd-
ing, probe-puls-energi og polariserings-konfiguration af de indkommende THz og probe
stråler. I de resulterende spektre diskuterer vi nogle interessante træk, der skyldes fonon-
resonanser i silica-glas, såvel som hvad vi antager at være en resonans-effekt forårsaget
af elektrode-geometrien.
Afslutningsvis viser vores faststof THz-detektor potentialet til at konkurrere med luft-
baserede metoder med hensyn til absolut ydeevne i båndbredde og dynamisk rækkevidde,
mens den er i stand til delvist eller fuldstændigt at erstatte elektro-optisk sampling som
en praktisk THz-detektionsmetode. De anvendte spændinger og probe-puls-energier, der
er nødvendige, er 2-3 størrelsesordener lavere end nødvendigt for lignende ydelse med
luft-baseret detektion og vil kunne sænkes yderligere med fremtidige design-iterationer.

Abstract
This project is based on recent findings that employ solid state materials as an alternative
to air based THz detection methods, which for the past decade has been the standard in
ultra-broadband THz detection. The advantages of solid-state over air based methods
include reduced requirements in probe pulse energy and applied bias voltages, while
retaining an ultra-broad bandwidth, as well as a large dynamic range.
The path to a working solid-state based THz detector consists of a theoretical study,
a design phase aided by electromagnetic simulations, followed by the fabrication and
experimental testing of the device. The data analysis is based on the collected time-
domain data, from which the spectra are extracted.
We find that our design based on interlocked, narrow electrode fingers gives us strong
detection performance, with bandwidths of > 20 THz and in some cases pushing 35 THz,
for applied bias voltages in the range of 120-350 V and with probe pulse energies less
than 1 µJ. Dynamic ranges are similar to that of our air-based detection system, but
due to fabrication flaws, this is expected to be pushed much higher for the solid-state
detector. We study the detector performance as a function of applied voltage, probe
pulse energy and polarization configuration of the incoming THz and probe beams. In
the resulting spectra, we discuss some interesting features, which are due to phonon
resonances in the silica glass, as well as what is believed to be a resonant effect caused
by the electrode geometry.
In conclusion, the solid-state THz detector shows the potential to compete with air-
based methods in terms of absolute performance in bandwidth and dynamic range, while
being able to partially or completely replace electro-optic sampling as a practical THz
detection method. The applied bias voltages and probe pulse energies needed are 2-3
orders of magnitude lower than needed for similar performance with air based detection
and will be able to be further lowered with future design iterations.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
The Terahertz Gap
The term ”terahertz (THz) gap” is the colloquial name for the region in the electromag-
netic spectrum which is bordered on one side by micro and radio-waves, and on the other
by mid/near-infrared and optical waves, as seen in Figure 1.1. It is loosely defined as
the frequency interval between 0.3 THz and 30 THz, spanning millimeter, submillimeter
and far-infrared wavelengths.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the THz gap, which bridges the spectral region between
electronics and photonics. Image adapted from [1].
At higher frequencies, we find the infrared and optical waves. In these regions, the
field of photonics exploits the properties of solid-state materials and especially semi-
conductors, in order to generate, guide and detect infrared and optical radiation, the
emission and absorption of which are based on the principles of discrete quantum me-
chanical energy transitions.
At lower frequencies, we find radio and micro-waves, whose technological exploitation
is the basis of the field of radio technology and microwave electronics, wherein gener-
ation and detection of radiation are well explained with classical transport theory, i.e.
acceleration of charges.
These higher and lower frequency regions have one thing in common: They are not

easily extendable into the intermediate THz region. This is illustrated in Figure 1.2
from 2014, which shows that coherent THz radiation is several orders of magnitude less

the context of electromagnetic generation, coherence refers to the degree of alignment

In electronic antennae, electrons have a finite carrier mobility, which results in in-
creasing difficulty in the generation of electromagnetic waves as the frequency increases
to tens or hundreds of GHz (hundredths to tenths of THz).
In photonic semiconductor devices, the most popular materials typically have a
bandgap energy of a few eV, with some being as low as tens or hundreds of meV [3]. The
energy of a photon with frequency of 1 THz is 4.1 meV, which explains why it cannot
excite carriers across the bandgap and therefore cannot generate a measurable current
in a semiconductor photodiode. Therefore, during the past half century, photonics has
sought alternate methods in order to more efficiently generate and detect coherent THz
pulses. Some of the most widely established generation methods include photoconduc-
tive (PC) antennae, surface emitting semiconductors, optical rectification and two-color
air plasma. For detection, the most used methods are PC antennae, electro-optic (EO)
sampling and terahertz-field-induced second harmonic (TFISH) generation, the latter of
which is the method used in this project. All of these methods employ optical or infrared

One of the main applications of THz technology is terahertz time-domain spec-

Many molecular compounds have unique spectral ”fingerprints” in the THz region,

some of which are: Security purposes, e.g. identification of narcotics and explosives.
Medical technology, e.g. disease diagnostics and pharmaceutical analysis. In general,
many fields where efficient material composition analysis is crucial would benefit greatly
from THz-TDS.
However, these applications depend on efficient, compact and cost-competitive gener-

1.2
A Brief History of Air Photonics
Before delving into the specifics of this project, we briefly review the history of air
photonics, as it is on the foundation of this sub-field that the work of this thesis is built.
At the onset of the millennium, D. J. Cook and R. M. Hochstrasser showed that
intense THz pulses could be generated by focusing an ultrashort near-infrared laser
pulse and its second harmonic onto air, inducing a THz radiating plasma [6].
Six years later, in 2006, J. Dai, X. Xie and X.-C. Zhang demonstrated the corre-
sponding air-plasma based detection mechanism: Generating second harmonic pulses by
superimposing and focusing a THz pulse and a near-infrared pulse [7], the framework
for which had been laid out in 1999 by D. J. Cook et al. [8].
In 2008, N. Karpowicz et al. improved upon the detection method by introducing
a kV AC bias at the focal point, improving dynamic range and capability for coherent
detection, i.e. recording both electric field amplitude and phase information [9]. This
method is now commonly referred to as air-biased coherent detection (ABCD).
These discoveries laid the foundation for the emerging field of THz wave air photonics,
which has since then been refined and has evolved into the superior method of generating
and detecting ultra-broadband THz pulses, capable of reaching bandwidths of >100 THz
and peak electric field strengths of MV/cm [10, 11].
Three years ago, in 2017, A. Tomasino et al. explored the possibility of incorporat-
ing a solid-state material (fused silica) as the detection medium instead of air, while
still retaining the other characteristics of the ABCD scheme, e.g. the applied AC bias
[12]. This system, dubbed solid-state-biased coherent detection (SSBCD), exploited the
nonlinear and dielectric properties of silica to improve the THz-induced signal at the
second harmonic frequency of the infrared probe. It allowed a reduction of the voltage
of the applied bias by roughly one order of magnitude (down to 50 V), while retaining
large DR and bandwidth. Furthermore, it allowed a reduction in necessary probe pulse
energy by three orders of magnitude (100 nJ). They used a simple gap structure with
fused silica deposited onto two gold electrodes, as seen in Figure 1.4.

1.3
This Project
This brief history review leaves us at the onset of this project, the aim of which is to
explore SSBCD: First and foremost designing, fabricating and testing a working solid-
state detector. In the process we will consider the limitations of SSBCD and assess
the ways in which we can improve such a solid-state detector. Specifically, we seek to
optimize the detector performance in two ways:
1. Choosing a material with appropriate nonlinear attributes in the electrode gap
could lead to an increased conversion efficiency in the nonlinear TFISH process.
We will explore the possibility of using chalcogenide glasses, which have been shown
to possess strong nonlinear attributes [13].
2. By controlling the geometry of the detector’s metal electrodes, we can utilize the
phenomenon of THz field enhancement (FE), wherein the electric field of a THz
pulse impinging on a metallic micro-structure is greatly enhanced by mobilized
carriers. The geometrical design also impacts the distribution of the applied bias
field, and these effects together can be tuned to increase the nonlinear conversion.
The overall aim is to design and fabricate a SSBCD THz detector with a large TFISH
generation conversion efficiency, leading to an improvement in DR. This will enable us
to both reduce the required probe pulse energy from tens of µJ and down to below one

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter, we will review the essential theoretical and technical framework required
to understand THz generation and detection in ABCD/SSBCD THz-TDS setups. Read-
ers well-versed in nonlinear optics and THz wave air photonics may wish to skip parts
of sections 2.1 and 2.2.
2.1
Nonlinear Optics
Both the generation and detection methods used in this project require a basic un-
derstanding of some key concepts of nonlinear optics, namely the nonlinear induced
polarization and the nonlinear susceptibility.
2.1.1
Linear Induced Polarization
When an atom is placed in an external electric field, the electronic cloud is displaced
with respect to the nucleus, resulting in an electric dipole with dipole moment ⃗p =
q⃗d, where ±q is the nucleus/electron charge and ⃗d is the displacement vector pointing
from the negative to the positive charge. The dipole moment can also be written as
⃗p(t) = ε0α ⃗E(t), where α is the polarizability of the atom and ⃗E(t) is the time-dependent
applied electric field.
In the macroscopic picture, the linear induced polarization is given by
⃗P(t) = ε0χ(1) ⃗E(t),
(2.1)
and is the electric dipole moment per unit volume, where χ(1) is the linear suscep-
tibility and ε0 is the vacuum permittivity. The susceptibility therefore represents the
degree to which a material is polarized in response to an applied electric field.

In this linear regime, the atomic potential is approximately harmonic, which means
that a material subjected to a sinusoidal electric field will simply result in a sinusoidal
induced polarization, at the same frequency as the applied field. This is the basis of the
Lorentz oscillator model.
2.1.2
Nonlinear Induced Polarization
The induced polarization presented in the previous section is only valid in the linear
regime, where the applied electric field is sufficiently weak. If instead, a highly intense
electric field is applied, the linear expression is no longer valid and necessitates the
introduction of higher order terms.
The induced polarization can be written as a power series,
⃗P(t) = ⃗P (1)(t)+ ⃗P (2)(t)+ ⃗P (3)(t)+... = ε0
[
χ(1) ⃗E(t) + χ(2) ⃗E2(t) + χ(3) ⃗E3(t) + ...
]
, (2.2)
where χ(2) and χ(3) are the second and third-order susceptibilities.
⃗P (1)(t) is the
linear induced polarization introduced in the previous section and the rest of the terms
make up the nonlinear induced polarization, P NL(t) = ⃗P (2)(t) + ⃗P (3)(t) + ....
The nonlinearities arise because the atomic potential can no longer be considered
harmonic and therefore the linear relation between applied electric field and induced
polarization no longer holds, as per. The structure of a given material decides whether
the second or third-order term is the dominant nonlinear term. The corresponds to the
addition of quadratic or cubic terms in the Lorentz model, see Figure 2.1.
In this project, we mainly work with third order nonlinear effects. In Appendix A,
a brief summary of the principles behind the second order nonlinear effect of second
harmonic generation (SHG) is given, which is used in two-color plasma THz generation.

2.1.4
Phase Matching
As we have already seen, in nonlinear frequency conversion processes, we fulfil conser-
vation of energy, e.g. ω4 = ω1 + ω2 + ω3. The degree to which we satisfy conservation
of momentum, i.e. phase matching, is also critical in achieving efficient nonlinear con-
version. For our collinear case with three input fields and one output field, it is given
by
k4 = k1 + k2 + k3.
(2.4)
In a dispersionless medium, the phase mismatch, ∆k, is automatically zero, because
all the different waves travel at the same speed in the medium, allowing a buildup of
the output field. When dispersion is introduced, the output field travels at a different
speed than the input fields, leading to a nonzero phase mismatch which increases back-
conversion of the power, reducing the overall nonlinear conversion efficiency of the system
[15], see Figure 2.4.

2.2
Terahertz Wave Air Photonics
The field of THz wave air photonics seeks to overcome several obstacles presented by
other methods of generating and detecting pulsed THz radiation. As previously stated,
the other most commonly used generation and detection methods are PC antennae
(generation/detection), optical rectification (generation) and EO sampling (detection),
all of which suffer from a number of drawbacks, summarized in the following list [2]:
• Solid-state based methods do not generate and detect a smooth spectrum across
the entire bandwidth of the THz region. This is due to phonon resonances and
absorption of the materials. Reflections on the boundaries of the materials can
also interfere destructively, worsening the problem.
– Using air as the generation or detection medium alleviates these problems,
since air is a gas, and does therefore not have phonon resonances [17], in
addition to having no boundary reflection issues. The air used must be dry
to circumvent the large attenuation in water vapor.
• PC antennae and optical rectification are subject to saturation effects when using
high excitation intensities, which in turn may lead to laser induced damage of the
THz emitter.
12
==PAGE:22==
2.2 Terahertz Wave Air Photonics
13
– Air is continually replaced and high excitation intensities can therefore be
used without harming THz generation or detection.
• For practical applications, remote sensing is not viable if using PC antennae, op-
tical rectification or EO sampling, since the attenuation of THz radiation is very
large in ambient air, and you therefore have to generate and detect very closely to
a given sample.
– Air based methods can be used remotely, since the place of THz generation
and detection can be freely changed, simply by adjusting the focus of the op-
tical and near-infrared beams, which have much lower attenuation in ambient
air.
• In solid-state methods, the dispersion of the nonlinear material used provides im-
perfect phase matching conditions, causing a reduced TFISH generation. This
necessitates a very short interaction length with a large nonlinear susceptibility,
i.e. thin films of highly nonlinear materials.
– Air and air plasma have low dispersion, and therefore naturally small phase
mismatches, even with several millimeters of air plasma interaction length.

simultaneously focused, a fundamental fs laser pulse and its second harmonic (typically

erties. In this plasma, the pulses mix to generate THz transients, as seen in Figure

2.2.2
THz Detection: Four-Wave Mixing
After generating a THz pulse and propagating it through the region of interest, e.g.
transmission or reflection of a given sample, it must be detected again in order to extract
the desired information.
Phenomenologically, THz detection in air is the inverse of the corresponding genera-
tion process: In generation, a fundamental laser pulse and its second harmonic are mixed
in an air plasma to produce a THz pulse. In detection, a THz pulse and a fundamental
infrared pulse are mixed in an air plasma to produce a signal at the second harmonic
frequency of the probe beam, which is then detected by a photomultiplier tube (PMT)
or avalanche photodiode (APD). This is a FWM process and in this case not merely
an approximation, since the involved laser fields are much smaller, leading to little ion-
ization, i.e. no plasma induced. It consists of two separate processes, in which three
photons combine, their frequencies adding to (near) zero [2]: 2ω = ω + ω + ΩTHz and
2ω = ω + ω −ΩTHz. It is crucial to note that while the THz photons add or subtract
very little energy in the two processes compared to the fundamental excitation photons,
they are required, since third-order nonlinear processes dictate that for one output pho-
ton, three input photons must be combined. For generation of photons at 2ω, the third
photon cannot be another photon of frequency ω, as this would instead lead to third
harmonic generation. Therefore the detection FWM process hinges on the availability
of THz photons.

2.2.2.2
Solid-State-Biased Coherent Detection
SSBCD stands as a proposed departure from air based photonics, in which a solid-state
material is used as the detection medium, instead of air.
If a material with a larger dielectric strength than air (3 · 106 V
m [20]) is used, we
can utilize a larger bias field, Ebias, without detector damage. Detection can then be
achieved with smaller gap structures and lower bias voltages.
Furthermore, we can choose a detection material with a higher χ(3) than air plasma,
thus increasing TFISH intensity, which is proportional to the square of the nonlinear
susceptibility. All other things held equal, an increase of a factor 100 in third order
susceptibility will lead to a factor 10000 increase in TFISH generation. This allows a
significant reduction in required probe pulse energies needed.
Some of the inevitable downsides include the possible introduction of phonons and
reflections, which can poke holes in, and otherwise alter, the detection bandwidth.

Positive charges accumulate on one side and negative charges on the other, causing an
effective enhancement of the electric field inside and immediately around the gap, due
to the capacitive effect of opposite charges. This is illustrated in Figure 2.10.

Chalcogenide glasses have been studied extensively in the past decades and have been
found to have many interesting electrical and optical properties. They are amorphous
semiconductors based on sulphur, selenium or tellurium and exist in many configurations.
In this project, we are interested in them for their nonlinear properties, namely a large
χ(3), which has mainly been studied the context of fiber optics, whereas we will utilize
them as a thin film.
We will compare the properties of arsenic triselenide (As2Se3) and arsenic trisulfide
(As2S3) and use the glass which exhibits the most desirable attributes.
Our comparison is based on optical properties such as reflectance, bandgap and
nonlinear coefficient, as well as damage thresholds in regards to optically induced laser
damage and dielectric breakdown.
2.4.1
Bandgap
Since the chalcogenide glasses are semiconductors, their bandgap will indicate the max-
imum wavelength of a photon which they can linearly absorb. Any absorption of the
probe beam may lead to reduced detector performance, although it is possible that ab-
sorption in the glass may be negligible, due to the low thickness of the glass film. As
mentioned, the standard probe beam wavelength is 800 nm, but this would likely be
changed in order to accommodate the properties of the chosen nonlinear material.
For the S-based glass, we have a bandgap of Eg = 2.35 eV [28], corresponding to a
wavelength of 528 nm.
For the Se-based glass, we have a bandgap of Eg = 1.76 eV [29, 30], corresponding
to a wavelength of 705 nm.
Both of these wavelength values lie below the standard probe beam wavelength, and
we therefore do not expect linear band edge absorption to be a problem with either. How-

3.1
Fabrication Workflow
In order to optimize our detector design process, we must first understand the limitations
imposed by our pre-selected fabrication methods. The process flow is summarized in the
following list:
1. We start with a cleaned and polished 4” fused silica wafer, 500 µm thick.
2. We spin coat 1.5 µm of negative photoresist on top of the wafer.
3. With the use of a maskless aligner, we expose the resist to our design pattern. This
reduces the solubility of the photoresist in the exposed areas.
4. The resist is developed in TMAH, removing the areas of the resist which were
not exposed in the maskless aligner. Now we have a negative image of our gold
electrode pattern on our wafer.
5. Using e-beam evaporation, we deposit a uniform layer of 10 nm titanium (for
adhesive purposes), followed by 190 nm of gold.
6. In a lift-off process, using a combination of chemical remover and ultrasound vi-
brations, we remove the remaining photoresist, leaving the wafer with only our
desired gold pattern. At this point, the only thing missing from the wafer is the
deposition of the nonlinear gap material. We use two different methods; one for
silica and one for As2Se3.

3.2
CST Simulations
CST Studio Suite 2020 is a 3D electromagnetic software tool, with a number of different
solvers at its disposal, allowing simulation and analysis of electromagnetic fields and
components. Like in a CAD program, we can make 3D models, assign materials to
our structures and apply appropriate boundary conditions. We then define the proper
electromagnetic excitation sources, such as a plane wave or a potential bias, and can
then extract a full 3D data set of the relevant vector and scalar fields, such as the electric
or magnetic field. We will employ the electrostatic solver to simulate the field of the
applied bias and the frequency domain solver to simulate the THz FE.
In general, a simulation runs through a certain number of phases, illustrated in Figure
3.1. A rough tetrahedral mesh is generated over the designed simulation volume. The
relevant fields due to the excitation sources are calculated in the mesh points. Areas
between mesh points are interpolated by the available data points. The mesh undergoes
an adaptive refinement, increasing the number of tetrahedrons, which especially focuses
on areas around boundaries, edges and places with high field gradients. The relevant
fields are then re-calculated and compared with the previous iteration in predefined probe
locations. If the fields have converged to within the specified margin, or if the maximum
allowed number of refinement passes has been reached, the simulation terminates and
displays the results.
Figure 3.1: Simulation flowchart.
31
==PAGE:41==
3.2 CST Simulations

3.2.1
Electrostatic Solver: Bias Field
After constructing our 3D geometry, we can assign electric potentials to our two gold
electrodes as the excitation source. The electrostatic solver then calculates the electric
field in the simulation domain. This then gives us the Ebias term from Eq. 2.11.
In general, we apply a voltage of 1 V to one electrode and 0 V to the other. The
resulting fields scale linearly with the actual applied bias, so if we in the laboratory end
up using 350 V, we can simply multiply the bias simulations by 350 to evaluate the
actual values of the field distribution.
Although the actual applied bias is oscillating, the sinusoidal oscillations are so slow
compared to the duration of the THz and probe pulses, that it is essentially static, and
hence we simulate it as such. If a square bias is used, the electric field is in fact static over
the duration of the laser pulses (with a switching polarity between consecutive pulses).
3.2.2
Frequency Domain Solver: THz FE
The frequency domain solver is based on the finite element method and is essentially a
mathematical method with which Maxwell’s equations are solved. It is a monochromatic
method, meaning that for each simulation run, we only evaluate the field distribution
at a single frequency. If we wish to explore the frequency dependence of our simulated
detector, we must therefore perform a series of simulations at incremental frequencies.
The resulting field distribution is the ETHz term from Eq. 2.11.
3.2.3
Model
In general, we have two gold electrodes, separated by a gap in which in the majority of
the nonlinear FWM takes place. The gap extends in the y-direction and the electrodes
are respectively positioned in the positive and negative x-domains, see Figure 3.2.
The origin of our coordinate system is placed in very middle of the xy-plane and
at the bottom of the gold/gap in the z-direction, i.e. the exact midpoint of the gap is
found at coordinates (x, y, z) = (0, 0, h/2).
In general we use a coarse mesh for the substrate, electrodes and other areas of little
interest. In and around the gap structure, we locally define a much finer mesh, such
that the strong field gradients are properly resolved, and so that we do not unnecessarily

3.2.3.1
Symmetry Planes and Boundary Conditions
In order to reduce the computation time, we can sometimes exploit the fact that we
have a symmetry plane in the yz-plane.
In terms of boundary conditions, in the +x, −x and −z directions we use the open
boundary condition, which simply extends the bordering material to infinity. This is
valid, since our simulation volume is only a very small subvolume of the actual detector.
In the +z direction, from where our THz pulse is incident, we use ”open (add space)”,
which acts like the ”open” condition, except it adds a region of vacuum, allowing the
simulation of reflections on the top of the detector. In the +y and −y directions we use
periodic boundary conditions. For the simple gap structure, we could also use the open
boundary condition, but for more complicated gap structures, we want the pattern to
extend beyond the simulation volume.
3.2.4
Simulation Validity
In order to test the validity of our simulations, we attempt to verify some known results.
3.2.4.1
Convergence
In general, we use a simulation setup with five field probes, placed in regions with high
field gradients. The relative change in electric field values at these probe points from one
simulation pass to the next are used to determine whether the simulation has converged
or not. We use a convergence criterion of 3%, meaning that when the relative change
of the field values at the five probes is below 3% for two consecutive mesh refinement
passes, the simulation is considered converged.
In order to ensure a reasonable simulation time, we set the max number of refinement
passes to 12, but make sure to be weary of bad convergence in cases where the simulations
reach 12 passes.
3.2.4.2

The electric field just outside a conductor must be perpendicular to the conductor sur-
face [47]. In other words, since the electric field inside a conductor is zero, the field

Lasers
The 800 nm excitation pulses are delivered by a Spectra Physics Spitfire Ace Ti:Sapphire
laser, with a pulse duration of ≈40 fs. In a standard ABCD system these 800 nm pulses
would be used for both pump and probe pulses, since air is >90% transparent at the
fundamental and second harmonic wavelengths. For As2Se3, this is not the case, and
we therefore need to convert the Spitfire pulses to longer wavelengths, using a Light
Conversion TOPAS HE-PRIME optical parametric amplifier. The TOPAS works on the
principle of optical parametric amplification, in which a strong pump beam and a weak
signal beam are mixed. Each pump photon is split into two photons: One photon is
stimulated by a signal photon and thus has the same wavelength, effectively amplifying
the signal beam. The other photon, possessing the residual energy, builds up to what
is called the idler beam. This is a second order nonlinear effect described by difference-
frequency generation. Thus we have a large range of possible signal and idler wavelengths
at our disposal, given by

4.1.1.7
Boxcar Averager
In order to extract a small and short signal from a noisy background, two important
electronic pieces of equipment must be used, the first of which is the boxcar averager.
With a laser repetition rate of 1 kHz and an APD bandwidth on the order of GHz,
the temporal window of the signal of interest occupies only around a millionth of the
signal outputted by the APD, i.e. a nanosecond of signal in a millisecond of noise.
A boxcar averager exploits the periodicity of the signal, together with a defined
integration window, which is aligned with the arrival of the signal of interest. The signal
is integrated over the given window, averaged over a number of periods and the result
is outputted until the next cycle of integration and averaging begins. This is illustrated
in Figure 4.3.
Figure 4.3: Boxcar working principle. Image from [50].
This has several implications: Any noise outside the integration window is ignored,
any low frequency noise is suppressed by the averaging process and any high frequency
noise is limited by the inverse of the integration window [50]. Second, the signal of
interest now occupies a much larger timeslot than the one nanosecond per millisecond
and is thus easier to handle subsequently.

4.3
Dynamic Range
To evaluate the DR of a given detector, we employ the method presented by Naftaly
and Dudley, who remark that there exist ”disparate and often contradictory methods”
of signal evaluation in the context of time-domain spectroscopy, and that time domain
data should be evaluated with the following method [51]:
1. Measure the time-domain trace and note the value of the peak maximum.
2. Measure the noise signal in the absence of THz, e.g. before the arrival of the main
pulse.
3. The mean signal in the absence of THz should be constant. Calculate its standard
deviation.
4. DR = THz peak/SD of noise.
The two SSBCD articles by Tomasino et al. [12, 19] use the same method of DR
evaluation.

The dip around 13.5 THz can be explained by phonon absorption in the fused silica.
A TO phonon with phonon height of HTO = 8.42 is found at 446 cm−1 = 13.4 THz [43].
In a way, this phonon dip validates that we actually achieve TFISH generation in the
gap area. Tomasino et al. did not run into this phonon absorption, as they did not have
the capability to generate and detect above 10-11 THz.
A LO phonon is also found at 505 cm−1 = 15.1 THz, but with a much smaller phonon
height, HLO = 1.13, which is why we do not see a dip at 15 THz. Instead, we see a peak.
One possible explanation considered is whether it could be a resonant effect, caused by
the THz pulse: The mobilized carriers responsible for the THz FE undergo relaxation
oscillations when the electric field of the THz pulse is no longer present at the electrodes.
From the data we extract the temporal length of the ringing to be ∆t = 0.066 ps.
This corresponds to a frequency of 15.2 THz, which is exactly where the peak is seen
in the spectrum. The equivalent wavelength is 20 µm, which could possibly indicate
that our 10 µm gap structure acts as a sort of resonant cavity, where a standing half
wavelength could resonate after the THz pulse is gone, causing a peak in the spectrum.

situation would be different in the two different polarization configurations, and that
the resonance would be stronger when the THz field is polarized perpendicularly to
the electrode fingers, owing to the larger cross-section as seen by the THz pulse in the
polarization direction. More experimental data and simulations with different design
parameters would be necessary to help us solidify our explanation of this phenomenon.
It would be very interesting to see what would happen if we changed the width of the
gaps from 10 µm to 11.2 µm = 13.4 THz, as we would then superimpose this supposed
resonance onto the spectral hole caused by the aforementioned phonon. The two very
different physical effects might partially cancel each other out, leaving a relatively smooth

With our limited laboratory time, we tried to get a signal with the 1 µm gap structure,
similar to that of [19], but were not successful. There are several possible reasons as to
why our attempts were unfruitful:
• We used a lens with focal length 300 mm to focus our probe beam, compared
to the 100 mm used by Tomasino et al.. Consequently, our focus beam diameter
(≈60 µm) is relative large compared to the 1 µm gap structure, and as such, a
larger portion of the probe energy does not reach inside the gap, compared to
the case with a tighter focus. A shorter focal length of the probe focusing lens
might partially solve this problem, but this tighter focus introduces the possible
problem of laser induced damage on the detector, requiring a reduction in probe
pulse energy.
• Any second harmonic signal generated inside such a small structure could be sub-
ject to diffraction effects, i.e. bending around edges and corners and thus causing
an enlarged beam divergence. Since we use an APD with an active area diameter
of 1 mm [49], it is possible that the generated second harmonic signal spreads
outside the collection area. A possible fix would be to use a PMT instead, which
can have a much larger active area.
• Our detectors were attached to a make-shift mount with double-sided tape, with
the additional weight of the attached crocodile clamps. As was the original plan, a
sturdier construction should be custom-made and tailored to the detector dimen-
sions, including electrode connections for the bias. It was also originally the plan
to use computer controlled translation stages instead of manual ones, such that
an alignment program could be used to automatically find a signal.

small, which greatly limits the possible generation and throughput of TFISH intensity.
This is contrasted by the ease with which we achieved a detection signal with the fin-
ger structures of the ”200/2/10” design. The active region, and therefore the TFISH
generation, are orders of magnitude larger, while having the same laser fluence damage
threshold as the 1 µm gap.
Unfortunately, we failed to deposit silica on our new design and thus could not
compare the performance of the ”200/2/10” design to these newer designs, which may

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion and Outlook
The central result of this project is the demonstration of solid-state based THz detec-
tion, with usable bandwidths spanning the 0-20 THz regime and in some cases pushing
beyond 30 THz, which is unexpectedly high, see Figure 6.1. It therefore surpasses the
performance of our ABCD setup, which is no trivial feat, given as our ABCD system is
one of few in the world capable of such ultra-broad bandwidths in air.
Our detector combines the best of both worlds from EO detection (probe energies
below 1 µJ) and ABCD (ultra-broad bandwidth), without the need for bias voltages in
the kV range. The observed spectrum peaks at relatively high THz frequencies compared
to ABCD, indicating that it would be especially well suited for high frequency THz
detection. The solid-state detector could partially or completely replace EO sampling
in the THz laboratory.

6 Conclusion and Outlook
76
The DR exhibited by our detector is relatively low compared to the results of
Tomasino et al., but this mainly due to the failure of our silica deposition, leaving the
silica cover layer too thin to apply adequate bias voltages. An iterative design process to
further optimize the finger electrode style design, coupled with a more robust selection
of fabrication methods, could pave the way to a SSBCD detector with very large DR
values, while retaining an ultra-broad bandwidth. As the large TFISH interaction area
significantly improves the ease of alignment, it would be easy to integrate the solid-state
detector into both commercial and scientific systems, contrasted to the simple micro-gap
detector.
Due to deposition difficulties and time constraints, we did not get to explore the
capabilities of chalcogenide glasses as the nonlinear gap material, but have prepared
the necessary infrastructure (e.g. ventilation system and longer probe wavelength) for
future research into this topic.
We have studied the detector performance as a function of bias voltage, probe en-
ergy and polarization configuration and we have touched upon some very interesting
phenomena in the observed spectral features of the detector, such as the self-cancelling
effect seen in the parallel polarization configuration, the alleged resonance effect, as well
as the phonon absorption in silica. These features should be studied further with differ-
ent designs to deepen our understanding of the underlying physics, in order to further
improve upon detector design.
A number of possible improvements in the iterative process has been found, both
issues that we had expected from the start, as well as new issues that rose throughout
the course of the project.
• Design
– To optimize the CST simulation process, the use of cluster computing would
provide a valuable decrease in computational time. Especially for simulation
in the high THz frequencies, the simulation time can quickly rise beyond what
is reasonable for a project with a relatively short time frame.
– The MATLAB evaluation script could be generalized to three dimensions,
so it could be used to more accurately evaluate relative performance differ-
ences between detectors. Furthermore, as CST Studio supports MATLAB
integration, it might be possible to integrate the whole script, in order to
76
==PAGE:86==
6 Conclusion and Outlook
77
avoid having to export the relevant CST simulation data from two different
simulations (electrostatic and frequency domain solvers). This could enable
new automation options in terms of geometry optimization, where different
design parameters could more readily be evaluated.
– It would be worth to consider the gold thickness, h, as a variable which also
could be optimized in the design process. It has been shown that the THz
FE depends on the relative sizes of the gold thickness and gap width [52].
• Fabrication
– Overall, the fabrication methods worked well, except for the final step, i.e.
the deposition of nonlinear gap materials. For silica deposition, an adhesive
layer of titanium should be applied, not only between substrate and gold film,
but also between gold film and silica top layer.
– For the chalcogenide glass deposition, a dedicated spin coater should be used,
since the drop casting method does not produce reliable results and might re-
sult in a too thick layer (> 0.5 mm), preventing probe beam transmission. A
purged glovebox deposition environment would also be ideal, in order to avoid
premature exposure to oxygen. The problem is that we cannot use equipment
which we do not wish to contaminate with arsenic. Furthermore, the concen-
tration of As2Se3 in EDA solvent as well as the annealing time/temperature
should be optimized, in order to prevent the risk of deposition failure.
• Experiment
– As described, our setup did not permit the use of less than 120 V of bias
voltage, or the lock-in amplifier would not function properly. This should be
corrected, either by switching to a high voltage amplifier with a lower ampli-
fication factor, or by amplifying the signal sent from the function generator
to the lock-in amplifier.
– The transmission spectrum of As2Se3 should be measured to allow the se-
lection of an optimal probe wavelength. The dielectric strength and LIDT
should also be mapped out in the experimental setup, so that a safe working
range of values can be established.

electrode design, since we have only managed to test a single type of detector. A possible
idea already mentioned would be to increase the gap size between the finger electrodes
from 10 µm to 11.2 µm, in order to align the spectral phonon absorption dip with the
possible resonance.

APPENDIX A
χ(2) - Second Harmonic
Generation
When a material does not possess a center of inversion, χ(2) becomes the dominant
nonlinear term.
This is also the case for an isolated atom, in which an electron is
situated in an anharmonic potential. The situation is analogous to the deformation of
a spring: For small applied forces, the spring compresses and expands equally - this
is the linear regime. But when the applied forces become sufficiently large, the spring
compresses less than it expands, meaning that there is a nonlinear relation between the
applied force and the spring deformation. The anharmonic potential can be seen in
Figure 2.1 (left).
A χ(2)-material subjected to a sufficiently large sinusoidal electric field, will respond
in its induced polarization, not only with the original frequency, but also with new
frequency components. This is illustrated in Figure A.1.
Figure A.1: Example second-order nonlinear response of a non-centrosymmetric mate-
rial. Left: Applied electric field (black) and resulting induced polarization
(orange). Right: Frequency components of the induced polarization. Fun-
damental (red), second harmonic (blue) and DC (green).

eration (SHG), in which two photons of frequency ω combine to create one photon of
frequency 2ω. The induced polarization at the second harmonic frequency (and thus the
electric field of the second harmonic) can be written as

SHG is a special case of sum-frequency generation, in which the frequencies of two
input fields (ω1 and ω2) add up to produce a new output field (ω3 = ω1 + ω2). This is
called a three-wave mixing process, since it involves a total of three fields and also exists
in the form of difference-frequency generation, where ω3 = ω1 −ω2.
