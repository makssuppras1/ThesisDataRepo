Abstract
This thesis explores how alkali metal cations and applied potential affect CO2 adsorption
on a Cu(111) surface under electrochemical conditions. Using density functional theory
(DFT) with both explicit water and the solvated jellium method (SJM), different setups were
tested to study adsorption energies, reaction barriers, and charge distributions. Static DFT
and NEB calculations showed that systems with explicit cations can significantly lower the
barrier for CO2 adsorption compared to setups without cations or with only implicit solvent.
However, the results also show that it is not simply the type of alkali metal ion that controls
the properties of the electric double layer (EDL) or the reaction kinetics. Instead, several
factorsвҖ”such as how close the ion is to the surface, how it interacts with the CO2 molecule,
and its position in the solvent layerвҖ”seem to all influence the outcome. Because these
effects are strongly coupled, it is difficult to identify clear trends based on ion identity
alone. The results suggest that future studies should focus on isolating individual effects
like ion coordination or field strength to better understand what controls the activity at
electrochemical interfaces.

Introduction and Motivation
Chemical, biological, and allied industries produce essential products that sustain and
advance modern society. Many of these industrial processes rely on catalysis, which
accelerates chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy. Catalysis is broadly
categorized into homogeneous catalysis, where the catalyst, reactants, and products exist
in the same phaseвҖ”often a solutionвҖ”and heterogeneous catalysis, where the catalyst
exists in a different phase than the reactants, typically a solid interacting with gaseous or
liquid reactants [1].
Catalysis plays a critical role in manufacturing, with an estimated 85% of industrial prodВӯ
ucts involving catalytic processes at some stage of production. In fact, roughly 90% of all
chemical processes include a catalytic step, underscoring catalysisвҖҷs vast economic and
industrial significance. Its impact spans major industries such as energy, pharmaceutiВӯ
cals, and transportation and more, contributing an estimated 30вҖ“40% of global GDP [2,
3]. Given its wideВӯreaching influence and importance, catalysis remains a cornerstone of
scientific and technological advancement.
Among catalytic technologies, heterogeneous electrocatalysis is a particularly vital subВӯ
field. Here, the catalyst facilitates electrochemical reactions at the electrode surface. OfВӯ
ten, the electrode itself acts as the catalyst or supports a catalytic layer. Electrochemical
reactions involve two coupled halfВӯreactions: an oxidation process at the anode and a
reduction process at the cathode. Electrocatalysis is central to renewable energy appliВӯ
cations such as fuel cells, water splitting, and CO2 reduction, making it a key enabler for
sustainable energy solutions.
One of the most promising technologies in this area is the electrochemical CO2 reducВӯ
tion (CO2RR), which enables the conversion of CO2вҖ”a greenhouse gasвҖ”into valuable
chemicals and fuels using renewable electricity. This approach supports the carbon cyВӯ
clus, depicted in Figure 1.1, by utilizing CO2 captured from industrial emissions or even
directly from the atmosphere [4].

1.1
Principles of CO2 Reduction
Despite its abundance, CO2 is a very stable molecule, making it quite inert, as it represents
carbon in its most oxidized and thermodynamically stable form under ambient conditions
[5]. Therefore, converting CO2 into useful products requires a catalyst and a significant
energy input, typically provided via an electrolyzer. In a standard setup, CO2RR occurs
at the cathode, where it competes with the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), while the
counterВӯreaction, typically the oxygen evolution reaction (OER), takes place at the anode.
A typical laboratory scale CO2 electrolyzer such as the HВӯcell shown in Figure 1.2 consists
of a cathode where CO2 is reduced (producing species like CO or formate), an anode
where OER occurs, an electrolyte for ion conduction and CO2 transport, and a separator
(e.g., ionВӯexchange membrane or porous diaphragm) to isolate the halfВӯreactions. An
external voltage drives electrons from anode to cathode.
The CO2RR involves several steps: (1) CO2 transport from the gas phase into the bulk
electrolyte, (2) diffusion to the catalyst surface, (3) adsorption onto the surface, (4) molecВӯ
ular dissociation and intermediate formation (e.g., *COOH, *CO, *CHO), (5) electron transВӯ
fer to these intermediates, (6) product desorption, and (7) transport of the products away
from the interface [7].
Among elemental metallic catalysts explored for CO2RR, copper (Cu) holds a uniquely adВӯ
vantageous position [8]. While most transition metals either bind reaction intermediates
too strongly (leading to poisoning) or too weakly (preventing reaction), copper binds modВӯ

reaction energy barriers are still elusive. These factors are crucial in determining the
activity and selectivity of a catalyst under operating conditions.

tronic structure, and reaction coordinates under varying electrochemical environments,
this study aims to uncover fundamental insights that could guide the rational design of
more efficient and selective catalysts for CO2 reduction.
1.2
The role of alkali metal ions
Alkali metal cations in the electrolyte play a crucial role in CO2 reduction reactions. Since
the 1990s, research has shown that these cations can increase CO2RR activity by more
than ten times and improve selectivity toward multiВӯcarbon products on copper electrodes
[9]. The effect increases with cation size, following the order Li+ < Na+ < K+ < Cs+.
Recent studies reveal that CO2RR cannot occur on copper, silver, gold, and tin oxide
surfaces without alkali metal cations in the electrolyte. This means these cations are not
just helpful but necessary for the reaction to proceed [9].

The reason for this size effect lies in how different cations interact with CO2 molecules on
the surface. Small cations like Li+ and Na+ bind to only one oxygen atom of CO2, while
larger cations like K+ and Cs+ can bind to both oxygen atoms at once. This double binding
by larger cations helps bend the CO2 molecule more easily, making it more reactive.
Computer simulations show that the energy barrier for CO2 activation decreases as the
cation size increases. The difference between small and large cations is about 0.05 eV,
which makes the reaction five times faster [9].
The necessity of alkali metal cations becomes clear when considering the competing hyВӯ
drogen evolution reaction. Without these cations, hydrogen production dominates beВӯ
cause protons can easily reach the electrode surface. Alkali metal cations help CO2 reВӯ
duction by pushing protons away from the surface through electrostatic repulsion, giving
CO2 molecules a better chance to react.
These findings are important for designing better CO2 reduction systems. Using larger
cations like K+ or Cs+ in the electrolyte can significantly improve both the activity and
selectivity of CO2 reduction catalysts.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Surface reactivity
Surface interfacial interactions are central to a wide range of technologically significant proВӯ
cesses, including corrosion, adhesion, materials synthesis, electrochemistry, and heteroВӯ
geneous catalysis. Understanding how atoms and molecules interact with solid surfacesвҖ”
particularly metal surfacesвҖ”is key to explaining and optimizing catalytic mechanisms at
the atomic scale.
These interactions can be effectively described using molecular orbital theory and band
structure theory, which form the foundation for analyzing chemical bonding at interfaces.
With recent advances in computational chemistry, it is now possible to model adsorption,
bond dissociation, and new bond formation at surfaces with remarkable accuracy. This
progress has enabled the prediction of reactivity trends and provided critical insights into
catalytic behavior. Importantly, these theoretical findings are often validated against exВӯ
perimental data from surface science studies, including adsorption energies, geometries,
vibrational spectra, and activation barriers for elementary reaction steps.
In the context of catalysis, interfacial reactivity typically begins with the adsorption of reВӯ
actants onto the catalyst surface. Frequently, this step involves the dissociation of one
or more reactant molecules, often requiring the breaking of a strong chemical bondвҖ”an
event that lies at the core of catalytic activity. Accordingly, a detailed understanding of the
physics and chemistry governing adsorption and bond activation at surfaces is essential
for advancing catalyst design.
Surface interfacial interactions are generally categorized into two regimes: physisorption,
involving weak van der Waals forces, and chemisorption, which entails the formation of
chemical bonds. These regimes will be discussed separately to highlight their distinct
energetic and electronic characteristics [10]. We limit ourselves to these regimes, since
this thesis does not investigate dissociative behaviour at the liquidВӯsolid interface.

2.3
Density Functional Theory (DFT)
A foundational breakthrough in electronic structure theory was established by Hohenberg

electron density, in accordance with the variational principle. This implies that minimizing
the energy with respect to the electron density yields the exact ground state density, and
therefore, energy.

2.4.1
Nudged Elastic Band
From thermodynamics it can be determined whether a reaction occurs favorably or not,
however that does not paint the whole picture. Between two adjacent stabile configuraВӯ
tions in the energy landscape there must necessarily be a barrier. To transition from one
state to the other, the configuration must reach a saddle point i.e. a Transition State (TS).
The path from one state to the other over the saddle point is the minimum energy path
(MEP).
The Nudged Elastic Band (NEB) [18] method is a computational technique used to deterВӯ
mine the MEP on a potential energy surface (PES), typically corresponding to reactants
and products in a transition process, or in the case of this thesis, the adsorption of an
adsorbate. The MEP provides insight into the activation energy, which makes NEB a
powerful tool for studying catalysis. The core idea of NEB is to represent the reaction
pathway using a discrete set of images (or replicas) of the system. These images interpoВӯ
late between the initial and final states and are connected by virtual springs to maintain
continuity of the path. During optimization, the forces on these images are modified to
ensure they converge onto the MEP, rather than simply forming a linear interpolation.

2.5
Bader charge analysis
Bader charge analysis is a widely used method for partitioning the electron density of a
system into atomic contributions, providing quantitative insight into the charge distribution
in molecules and at surfaces [20]. The method is based on the concept of zeroВӯflux surВӯ
faces in the gradient of the electron density, which naturally define regions (or вҖқbasinsвҖқ)
associated with individual atoms. By integrating the electron density within these basins,
one obtains atomic charges that reflect the net gain or loss of electron density relative to
the neutral atom.
This approach is particularly valuable in DFT studies of catalysis, where understanding
the redistribution of charge during adsorption and reaction is key to elucidating reaction
mechanisms. For example, in CO2R on metallic surfaces, Bader charge analysis can
reveal how electrons are transferred from the surface to the adsorbed CO2, helping to
identify activation steps and correlate them with geometric changes.
In the context of electrochemical systems, Bader charge analysis also allows for the evalВӯ
uation of charge compensation mechanisms, the role of explicit electrolyte ions, and the
response of the interfacial charge distribution to applied potential. By comparing Bader
charges at different stages of the reaction pathway (e.g., initial, transition, and final states)
and under different electrochemical conditions, one can gain valuable insights into how
surface charge and local electric fields modulate adsorption energies and activation barВӯ
riers.
While Bader charges do not capture the entire complexity of chemical bonding (since
they do not explicitly assign shared or delocalized charges), they provide a physically
meaningful, computationally accessible measure of charge transfer. This makes them a
powerful tool for interpreting the results of DFT calculations in studies of surface reactivity
and electrocatalysis.
In this thesis, Bader charge analysis is employed using the Henkelman et al. algorithm [21],
to examine the evolution of charge distribution along the reaction coordinate for CO2 adВӯ
sorption on Cu(111) in the presence of various alkali metal cations and different applied
potentials.

4
Results and discussion
This chapter presents the results of a DFTВӯbased investigation into CO2 adsorption on a
Cu(111) surface in the presence of alkali metal cations (Li+, Na+, K+) under electrochemiВӯ
cal conditions, by the incorporation of a hybrid solvation scheme, using both explicit and
implicit solvent. The main goal is to understand how the applied potential, solvation enviВӯ
ronment, and the identity of the cation, both individually and in combination, influence the
energetics and structural behavior of the system. To establish a baseline for comparison,
reference calculations are also included where either only explicit water or only implicit solВӯ
vation is used, allowing us to disentangle the role of specific solventвҖ“solute interactions
from more general electrostatic effects.
The discussion begins with an overview of ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulaВӯ
tions, which were carried out to capture the natural, unbiased evolution of the system at
finite temperature. These simulations help identify the typical arrangement of species in
the electrolyte and offer a more physically realistic starting point for further analysis. InВӯ
stead of relying on idealized, handВӯbuilt structures, AIMD allows us to observe how the
system вҖқwantsвҖқ to behave, which is particularly important in a complex environment like
the electrochemical interface.
Based on representative snapshots taken from these AIMD trajectories, three key configВӯ
urations were optimize: (1) an initial state (IS), where the CO2 molecule remains solvated
in the electrolyte near the electrode surfaceНҫ (2) a final adsorbed state (FS), where the
molecule is bound to the Cu(111) surface, and (3) a reference state where the CO2 is reВӯ
moved entirely from the simulation cell. Each of these configurations was evaluated both
in the presence and absence of alkali metal cations, giving a total of five distinct systems
per snapshot. The aim is to use these static calculations to gauge important descriptors of
the electrical double layer (EDL) Вӯ a central property to electrochemical reactions [26], to
provide clues as to how alkali metal ions and an applied potential, or an interplay between
the two, affect the EDL. Furthermore the goal is also to understand the energetics of each
system.
The final part of the chapter focuses on the reaction pathway between the solvated and
adsorbed states. Using the NEB method, we compute MEPs to identify the kinetic barriВӯ
ers associated with CO2 adsorption under different electrochemical conditions. To gain
deeper insight into the underlying charge transfer processes, Bader charge analyses
are performed along the NEB pathway, revealing how charge redistributes between the
molecule, metal surface, and surrounding electrolyte as the reaction progresses.
Taken together, the aim of these results is to provide a detailed picture of how solvation,
electrode potential, and cation coordination collectively shape the thermodynamics and
kinetics of CO2 adsorption, a critical activation step in electrochemical CO2R.

Conclusion and outlook
The results of this thesis show that the identity of the alkali metal ion (Li+, Na+, or K+) is
not by itself a good descriptor for predicting how the electric double layer or reaction barВӯ
riers behave. Although systems with explicit cations show stronger coupling between the
applied potential and the reaction path, itвҖҷs not just the ion type that causes this. Instead,
the effect seems to depend on a mix of factorsвҖ”especially how the ion is positioned relВӯ
ative to the CO2 molecule, how strongly it coordinates with it, and the structure of the
surrounding solvent.
These factors all influence each other, making it hard to separate them and identify any
simple trends based only on which alkali metal is used. This means that just looking at
the ion type isnвҖҷt enough to explain or predict catalytic behavior. The advised approach,
based on this thesis, for future work would be to control one variable at a timeвҖ”such as
keeping the ion position fixed or changing only the coordination environmentвҖ”so we can
better understand which effects matter most. This would help in designing more effective
