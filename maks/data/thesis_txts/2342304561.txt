Abstract 
High gravity (HG) brewing refers to the practice of brewing worts with a higher than 
traditional initial concentration of dissolved wort solids (16-20°P, where 1°P refers to 
1°Plato which represents 1 g dissolved solids per 100 ml wort). Brewing at HG offers 
breweries an increase in production capacity and reduction in energy costs with little 
to no extra capital expenditure and an extension in product range as more beer styles 
become popular. Fermenting HG wort into highly alcoholic beer is challenging due to 
the extreme environment it imposes on the brewing yeast. The major stresses 
associated with HG brewing are nutritional deficiencies, hyperosmotic pressure at the 
start of fermentation and ethanol toxicity at later stages. As an effect, HG brewing 
can result in incomplete fermentations or excessive production of flavour compounds 
such as higher alcohols and esters. 
Much research has been done on HG brewing and strategies have been developed to 
counteract the problems associated with it. As a result, many commercial breweries 
now ferment HG worts successfully. Brewing at very high gravities (VHG, >20°P) still 
proves problematic, however, as the problems associated with HG brewing are 
exacerbated at still higher gravities and fewer studies have been done on this subject. 
A number of variables, such as an increased pitching rate and supplementing metal 
ions, have been found to increase ethanol yield in HG brewing. A comprehensive 
study of VHG brewing, comparing these variables under identical fermentation 
conditions has been lacking, however, making it unclear which variable provides the 
largest benefit. In this study, the individual effect of these variables on yields of 
ethanol and flavour compounds in VHG brewing was compared. The effect of the 
combination of variables, found to increase ethanol yield, was then studied. The 
objective of this study was to gain a greater understanding of the limitations 
currently affecting VHG brewing and examine ways to exceed them, with a specific 
challenge to brew beer with 14% (v/v) ethanol from 25°P wort within 2 weeks in order 
to verify the results of the study, and to reduce the time required to fully ferment 
VHG wort. 
In this study, beer with an ethanol concentration of 14.0% (v/v) was brewed in 14 days 
from 25°P wort with supplementation of 250 ppm magnesium. An ethanol 
concentration of 14.0% (v/v) was also reached fermenting a 35°P wort with the 
combination of 100 RPM stirring and supplementations of 10 mM NH4Cl, 5 ppm zinc 
and 250 ppm magnesium, albeit with high concentrations of residual sugars. 
Furthermore, an ethanol concentration of 10.1% (v/v) was reached in 3 days with the 
same combination in 25°P wort, an increase of 2.5 percentage points compared to a 
non-supplemented standard. Only one report on VHG beer brewing was found where 
final ethanol yield exceeded the highest one obtained in this study (16.2 and 14.2% 
(v/v) from 31 and 28°P wort, respectively). Other studies, fermenting worts of 24-28°P 
resulted in beers with an ethanol yield of 10.5-11.9% (v/v). It can be concluded that 
==PAGE:6==
supplementing wort with magnesium has the most significant beneficial effect on 
ethanol yield for the wort and yeast strain used. Furthermore, it can be concluded 
that combining beneficial variables has a synergistic effect, reducing the time 
required to fully ferment VHG wort. A further study, determining the ratio of 
variables required for optimal fermentation performance, should be conducted.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
In traditional brewing, worts of about 12°P (where 1°P refers to 1°Plato which 
represents 1 g dissolved solids per 100 ml wort) are fermented to produce beer 
containing ethanol concentrations of around 5% (v/v). Under these circumstances, 
water volume is virtually unchanged from wort to fermented beer. The practice of 
brewing with high gravity (HG) worts (16-20°P) became popular following the energy 
crisis in the 1970‘s and by the mid 1980‘s many commercial breweries had adopted 
the practice (Anderson, 2005). The appeal of brewing HG worts is two-fold. First, and 
most importantly, fermenting HG wort produces beer of greater alcohol percentages 
which can subsequently be diluted down to suitable alcohol levels with carbonated 
water. This saves both water and energy, the biggest cost factors in brewing 
operations (Piddocke, Olson & Heldt-Hansen, 2009), as less wort volumes are boiled, 
and allows breweries to increase their production capacities without much additional 
capital expenditure. Second, the recent increase in craft breweries around the world 
has resulted in the a variety of beer styles being brewed, including stronger beers 
such as barley wines (Kell 2016). 
Brewing HG worts, however, has not come without its challenges as stress factors 
that have minor effects when brewing at standard gravities are exacerbated at higher 
ones. The biggest adverse effect is on the fermenting yeasts  themselves who have to 
adapt to increasingly hostile environments with increasing gravities. This can 
diminish yeast cell vitality and metabolic capacity, resulting in sluggish or incomplete 
fermentations, where any costs saved are outweighed by extended fermentation times 
or lost product. It is therefore important that HG worts are able to fully ferment 
without a significant increase in fermentation time. These problems have been largely 
addressed and commercial breweries now regularly ferment HG worts as standard 
practice without major complications. As the process of HG brewing becomes more 
optimized, however, brewers keep pushing the limits on wort gravity and many have 
started fermenting worts at very high gravities (VHG, >20°P) where they are faced 
with even greater challenges. 
 
1.2 Thesis objectives 
The objective of this study was to gain a greater understanding of the limitations 
currently affecting VHG brewing and examine ways to exceed them, with a specific 
challenge to brew beer with 14% (v/v) ethanol from 25°P wort within 2 weeks in order 
to test the results of the study, and to reduce the time required to fully ferment VHG 
wort.

1.3 Thesis disposition 
The introduction in section 1 serves to outline the problems brewers face in HG and 
VHG brewing. This is followed by a literature review in section 2 which shows what 
work has already been done in order to tackle these problems. The literature review 
reveals that many variables can positively influence ethanol yield in HG and VHG 
brewing but a comprehensive study, comparing the effects of these variables, is 
lacking. The literature review gave rise to research questions which are covered in 
section 1.4. An experiment was set up in order to answer the research questions and 
thus fulfil the thesis objectives. Section 3 details the materials and methods used in 
the experiment. 
Results of the experiment are presented and discussed in section 4. The section falls 
into 3 parts: 
 
In the first part, six yeast strains were screened to determine their resistance to 
ethanol toxicity. The two most resistant strains were then used to brew VHG wort 
in order to determine which strain yielded more ethanol.  
 
In the second part, the higher ethanol yielding strain was used to ferment VHG 
with changes in a single variable in order to determine which variable had the 
biggest impact on production of ethanol and flavour compounds. 
 
In the third part, beneficial variables were combined in fermentations at rising 
gravities using both strains selected in the first part in order to determine 
whether their combination would result in a synergistic effect on production of 
ethanol and flavour compounds. 
The conclusions of the experiments and recommendations for further work are 
presented in section 5. 
 
1.4 Research questions 
The aim of the literature review is to identify the biggest challenges and stresses 
associated with HG and VHG brewing along with the steps that have thus far been 
taken to mitigate them. A number of different variables were reported to positively 
influence fermentation performance. However, most reports studied in the literature 
review only examined the effect of a single variable at a time under very dissimilar 
fermentation conditions that make systematic and conclusive comparison very 
difficult. As a result, it was impossible to conclusively determine which variable has 
the biggest positive impact on ethanol yield. 
Yeast is no different from other living organisms in that it needs a proper 
environment and adequate nutrition in order to survive. The environmental and 
==PAGE:21==
3 
nutritional requirements of yeast are multifaceted, as demonstrated by the literature 
review. This thesis tests the hypothesis that a higher ethanol yield and faster 
consumption of fermentable sugars could be achieved with a synergistic combination 
of variables shown to be individually beneficial under similar conditions. Therefore, 
this research attempts to answer the following questions: 
 
What is the impact of each variable on ethanol yield when all other variables are 
left unchanged? 
 
Does the combination of variables found to be beneficial in the study result in a 
synergistic effect, increasing ethanol yield and reducing the time needed to fully 
ferment VHG wort?

2 Literature review 
A literature review was done in order to determine the greatest challenges and 
stresses associated with HG and VHG brewing along with the factors thought most 
important for their mitigation. Additionally, a review was done on beer flavour 
compounds in order to gain a better understanding of how VHG fermentations might 
affect their intensity. 
 
2.1 Method 
The literature review was conducted using the DTU Findit search engine. Review 
articles, giving an overview of HG and VHG brewing processes, were first viewed and 
their cited reference studies subsequently considered. Only articles studying the yeast 
species Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Saccharomyces pastorianus were considered 
and all mentions of yeast in the literature review and thesis refer to these two species 
of yeast. 
2.2 HG and VHG brewing: Advantages, 
challenges, stresses and yields 
2.2.1 Advantages and challenges 
Advantages of HG brewing are numerous. Savings in energy costs and increased 
brewery production capacity were already mentioned and while they present the 
biggest benefit, other advantages also follow. Consistency in production is increased 
as factors such as flavour compounds and alcohol percentages are more easily 
controlled with dilution. Haze forming compounds are also more easily precipitated at 
higher concentrations, resulting in beers with better physical stability (Boulton & 
Quain, 2001, Priest & Stewart, 2006). 
The challenges associated with HG brewing are also numerous. Yeasts have a hard 
time thriving under HG conditions as they are subjected to a number of stresses over 
the course of fermentation. They have to adapt to fluctuations in osmotic pressure, 
dissolved oxygen concentrations, temperature, pH, nutrient availability and ethanol 
concentrations. Of these, the biggest detriment associated with HG brewing is 
hyperosmotic stress at the beginning of fermentation and wort nutrient deficicencies 
and ethanol toxicity at later stages (Udeh, Kgatla & Jideani, 2014, Gibson, 2011).

2.2.2 Hyperosmotic stress 
Osmotic stress has been defined as any situation where the imbalance of intra- and 
extracellular osmolarities is large enough to have a damaging effect on physiology. In 
yeast this can occur both at low external osmotic potential (hypo-osmotic stress), 
characterized by an influx of water and cell expansion, and high external osmotic 
potential (hyperosmotic stress), where the cell exudes water and shrinks as a result 
(Csonka & Hanson, 1991). Increased external osmolarity is a common occurence for 
many types of cells, most of which respond to it by accumulating compatible solutes 
that increase intracellular osmolarity, reaching balance with the extracellular 
environment. The compatible solute differs between organisms but glycerol serves 
this purpose in yeast (O‘Rourke, Herskowitz & O‘Shea, 2002). The main source of 
osmotic stress in HG brewing is the high concentration of dissolved fermentable 
sugars at the start of fermentation, resulting in hyperosmotic pressure. Symptoms of 
hyperosmotic pressure in yeast are diminished cell sizes, loss of viability and slow 
growth, leading to poor fermentation performance (Gibson, Lawrence, Leclaire, Powell 
& Smart, 2007). The high osmolarity glycerol (HOG) pathway regulates the 
hyperosmotic stress response in yeast and glycerol production is rapidly increased as 
a result of its activation, with more glycerol being produced as gravities increase 
(Panchal & Stewart, 1980). Glycerol can be secreted from the yeast cell by simple 
diffusion through the cell membrane, a slow process, or by active transport 
mechanisms, which are much faster (Priest & Campbell, 2003). Upon being pitched 
into HG wort, yeast cells shrink rapidly as water streams out of them in an effort to 
equalize osmotic pressure across the cell membrane. Active glycerol transport is 
terminated as a consequence which, coupled with increased glycerol synthesis, 
increases intracellular osmotic pressure as glycerol rapidly accumulates. As sugars 
get taken up and fermented, hyperosmotic pressure is reduced and active transport is 
initiated anew (Dihazi et al., 2004, Scanes et al., 1998). The high osmolarity glycerol 
(HOG) pathway is not activated under exposure to ethanol and yeast reacts to ethanol 
toxicity in a different manner (Priest & Campbell, 2003). 
 
2.2.3 Nutritional deficiencies 
Yeast requires many different variables, especially nutrients, to be present for 
optimal fermentation performance. High wort gravities are usually achieved by the 
addition of syrup adjuncts to normal gravity (~12°P) wort. This decreases the ratio of 
nutrients to sugars and can lead to a number of problems if one or more essential 
nutrients are fully exhausted before all fermentable sugars are consumed. These 
include reduced fermentation rates, a higher chance of stuck fermentations, a relative 
increase in production of volatile flavour compounds such as esters and higher 
alcohols, an extended lag phase duration, high concentrations of residual sugars in 
the finished beer and poor foam stability in the final product (Gibson, 2011, Priest & 
Stewart, 2006). The most important nutrients required by yeast for adequate 
fermentation performance are covered in section 2.2.

2.2.4 Ethanol toxicity 
The detrimental effects of ethanol toxicity are incurred by increased permeability of 
the yeast cell membrane, likely as a result of ethanol insterting itself within its 
hydrophobic interior (Gibson, 2011). Owing to this, nutrient transport accross the cell 
membrane is reduced and thus the yeast cells‘ ability to maintain an adequate 
interior environment. As a results, the effects of ethanol toxicity are unpredictable but 
include growth inhibition, enzyme inactivation, lipid modification and reduced 
viability, respiration and sugar uptake. Yeast cells suffering from ethanol toxicity 
exhibit irregular cell shapes, making cell division and thus possible serial re-pitching 
of residual yeast precarious (Gibson et al., 2007). Traditional views hold that 
resistance to ethanol toxicity relies entirely upon the genetic makeup of individual 
yeast strains, with sake, wine and distilling strains being more tolerant to ethanol 
than beer brewing strains. Studies, however, have shown that ethanol concentrations 
of up to 16% (v/v) can be reached by beer brewing strains under nutritionally 
enchanced circumstances, where nutrients directly implicated in yeast cell structural 
integrity appear most beneficial (Casey, Magnus & Ingledew, 1984). 
 
2.2.5 Ethanol yields 
The wort used in most fermentations performed for this thesis had an intitial gravity 
of 25°P. A search was therefore conducted for studies fermenting wort of similar 
gravities to get an idea of the ethanol yields and fermentation times that could be 
expected to be reached in this study. Factors other than initial wort gravity were not 
considered in this search. 
Pátková, Šmogrovičová, Dömény and Bafrncová (2000) made beer with an ethanol 
concentration of 10.5% (v/v) from 24°P wort within 8 days. O‘Connor-Cox, Paik and 
Ingledew (1991) made beer with an ethanol concentration of 10.9% (v/v) from 24°P 
wort in 6 days. McCaig, McKee, Pfisterer and Hysert (1992) made beer with an 
ethanol concentration of 11.7% (v/v) from 24°P wort in 15 days. O‘Connor-Cox and 
Ingledew (1990) made beer with an ethanol concentration of 11.9 % (v/v) from 28°P 
wort in 8 days. Casey et al. (1984) made beer with an ethanol concentration of 14.2 % 
(v/v) from 28°P wort within 8 days A beer with an ethanol concentration of 16.2% (v/v) 
was also made from 31°P wort in 15 days in the same study, although with a high 
amount of residual sugars.

2.3 Imporant factors relating to HG brewing 
2.3.1 Wort sugars 
Wort sugars consist of sucrose, glucose, fructose, maltose, maltotriose and dextrins, of 
which yeast is able to assimmilate and utilize all but dextrins. Carbohydrate uptake 
by yeast is an ordered process where sucrose, glucose and fructose are assimilated 
first, through simple diffusion, followed by maltose and maltotriose, with active 
transport through maltose and maltotriose permeases. Maltose and maltotriose are 
subsequently broken down into glucose units by the enzyme α-glucosidase (Stewart, 
2009, Priest & Stewart, 2006, Cruz, Batistote & Ernandes, 2003). At a quick glance, it 
might therefore appear that the relative quantities of wort sugars would be of little 
importance. It has been established, though, that fermenting worts containing high 
amounts of glucose and fructose generally produces more volatiles than fermenting 
worts with proportionally higher amounts of maltose (Dekoninck, Verbelen, Delvaux, 
Mulders & Delvaux, 2012, Stewart & Younis, 1998). Moreover, uptake of other wort 
sugars is actively repressed while glucose is available in the wort (Trumbly, 1992). 
 
2.3.2 Agitation 
The only agitation traditionally provided in brewing fermentations has been through 
the fluid turbulence incurred by rising CO2 bubbles released by the yeast. This is 
referred to as natural agitation since no mechanical device is introduced. Natural 
agitation can be increased by providing CO2 nucleation sites in the fermenting wort. 
This is often achieved by leaving precipitated wort solids in the fermenter which has 
been shown to increase fermentation speed (Schisler, Ruocco & Mabee, 1982) and a 
similiar, but reduced, increase can be achieved by adding nucleation site bearing 
artificial materials (Kühbeck, Müller, Back & Kurz, 2007). 
Forced agitation has not been widely utilized in the brewing industry as it is feared 
that rotating agitators will shear the yeast. Recent studies suggest, however, that 
forced agitation does not significantly damage the yeast cells and an increase in 
fermentation speed has been reported by the use of mechanical stirring along with a 
reduction in vicinal diketone concentration (Nienow, McLeod & Hewitt, 2010). A 
naturally agitated fermenter is a multilayered environment where yeast cells sink to 
the bottom with trub or flocculate and rise to the top of the fermenting wort, forming 
a thick layer of cells. As a result, temperature and yeast cell exposure to nutrients 
varies considerably throughout the fermenter. Stirring likely boosts fermentation 
rates by homogenizing the fermenter environment and increasing yeast cell velocity 
in solution, which increases their contact with nutrients and eliminates temperature 
differences in the fermenter (Nienow et al., 2010, Pajunen & Maekinen 1975, Boulton, 
2009). 
Pajunen and Maekinen (1975) reported an increase in fermentation rate and a 
reduction in vicinal diketones with forced agitation. Nienow et al. (2010) did a study 
==PAGE:27==
9 
on shear damage in yeast cells under mechanically agitated fermentation conditions 
and found that cells were not significantly damaged. Furthermore, fermentation time 
was reduced by 1-2 days in ale and lager fermentations while producing more of 
higher alcohols and less of esters. Boulton (2009) reported a fermentation time 
reduction of 10-29% with forced agitation in 5 commercial breweries. Boswell, Hewitt 
and Nienow (2002) reported a decrease in fermentation time, with increased higher 
alcohol production and reduced ester formation. Finally, Boswell, Varley, Boon, 
Hewitt and Nienow (2003) reported increased yeast growth, fermentation rates and 
higher alcohol production, with supressed ester formation. In addition to the effects 
described above, most of the studies described increased consistency in the finished 
product when using forced agitation. 
 
2.3.3 Pitching rate 
Pitching rate refers to the number of yeast cells added to wort at the start of 
fermentation and is usually expressed in cells/ml. No consensus exists on optimal 
pitching rates as they are heavily dependent on the yeast strain used and 
fermentation conditions although rates generally range from 106-108 cells/ml. Fix 
(1999) mentions that fermenting at higher rates may lead to a broad spectrum of off-
flavors, an increase in autolysis, loss of hop flavour and aroma and clarification 
problems. Increased pitching rates are often mentioned as a solution to poor 
fermentation performance in HG worts and a number of studies have been conducted 
demonstrating the positive effect of pitching at higher rates, although results are 
rarely consistent. 
Kucharczyk and Tuszýnski (2015) report an increase in fermentation rate at higher 
pitching rates, without affecting concentrations of esters and higher alcohols, at the 
cost of a larger proportion of wort sugars going toward the production of cell mass. 
Nguyen and Viet Man (2009) report an increase in fermentation rate, sugar uptake 
rate and final ethanol concentration but also increased diacetyl levels at increased 
pitching rates. Erten, Tanguler and Cakiroz (2007) report an increase in fermentation 
rate and cell counts with no effect on final ethanol concentration, a reduction in 
diacetyl, 2,3-pentanedione, 2- and 3-methyl-1-butanol and isoamyl acetate and an 
increase in 2-methyl-1-propanol with increased pitching rates. Suihko, Vilpola and 
Linko (1992) report an increased fermentation rate with unchanged diacetyl levels at 
higher pitching rates. Finally, Verbelen et al. (2008) report an increase in 
fermentation rates at the cost of higher concentrations of fusel alcohol and diacetyl, 
although ester production was reduced with increased pitching rates. The only result 
of increasing pitching rates consistently observed, therefore, was an increase in 
fermentation rate. None of the articles reviewed used the same method and 
differences in final ethanol concentrations and diacetyl, ester and higher alcohol 
formation are likely due to differing fermentation conditions.

2.3.4 Free α-amino nitrogen (FAN) 
Nitrogen compounds are important for a number of metabolic processes in yeast, 
including cell growth and sugar consumption (Yano, Tsuda, Imai, Ogawa & Ohkochi, 
2008). The main source of nitrogen for yeast in wort are individual amino acids, 
ammonium ions and small peptides formed from the proteolysis of barley proteins in 
the malting and mashing process (Lekkas, Stewart, Hill, Taidi & Hodgson, 2005). 
These nitrogen sources, assimilable by yeast, are collectively referred to as Free α-
Amino Nitrogen (FAN). Yeast is unable to assimilate peptides larger than 3 units but 
is able to synthesize all required amino acids de novo from FAN sources (Kevvai, 
Kütt, Nisamedtinov & Paalme, 2016). Wort contains 19 of the 20 essential amino 
acids which are assimilated in an orderly manner through active transport, using a 
large number of transport enzymes, since amino acids don‘t permeate into the cell 
freely (Priest & Stewart, 2006). Wort amino acids are divided into four groups 
depending on their order of uptake. These groups, along with the amino acids they

2.3.5 Oxygen, fatty acids & sterols 
Oxygen is important in the initial stages of fermentation as it is required for the 
synthesis of essential membrane components, unsaturated fatty acids and sterols 
(Moonjai, Verstrepen, Delvaux, Derdelinckx & Verachtert, 2002). Synthesizing ample 
sterols is vital for the yeast cell as it ensures the permeability of the cell membrane 
and thus the yeasts ability to take in wort sugars and other essential wort nutrients. 
Oxygen is assimilated rapidly from wort, usually within the first few hours of 
fermentation, and no ethanol production takes place meanwhile. Although capable of 
both aerobic and anaerobic respiration, yeast much prefers the former, where around 
30 ATP are created per glucose unit as opposed to 2 ATP per glucose unit under 
anaerobic conditions (Priest & Campbell, 2003), where ethanol is formed as a waste 
product. This short period of oxygen uptake at the beginning of fermentation is called 
lag time and is of vital importance for the rest of the fermentation period as rapid 
reproductive growth occurs and yeast cells prepare themselves for nutrient uptake 
(Fix, 1999, Priest & Campbell, 2003). Oxygen and the lipids required for sterol 
synthesis are often in sub-optimal amounts in wort, especially HG wort, due to 
dilution with syrup adjuncts and the fact that oxygen solubility decreases with higher 
concentrations of wort solids (Casey et al., 1983). 
Although reproductive growth slows down in yeast once oxygen is depleted, it does not 
stop until a limiting amount of sterols is reached (Aries & Kirsop, 1977). Thus, in 
order to shorten lag times and circumvent the problem of poor oxygen solubility at 
HG, supplementation with fatty acids and sterols has been studied. Casey et al. 
(1984) report fermenting a 31°P wort to an ethanol concentration of 16.2% (v/v) beer 
through supplementing the wort with 24 ppm ergosterol, 0.24% (v/v) Tween 80 (a mix 
of fatty acids) and yeast extract, although fermentable sugars were not fully 
consumed. Dragone, Silva, Silva and Lima (2003) report optimal ethanol productivity 
for 20°P wort with supplementation of 24 ppm ergosterol, 0.24% (v/v) Tween 80 and 
yeast extract. Moonjai et al., (2002) report a minimal effect on ethanol production 
with the supplementation of linoleic acid. The supplementation of yeast extract in the 
first 2 studies is unfortunate since yeast extract contains a greatly variable mixture of 
nutritional compounds, even when supplied by the same company (Grant & Pramer, 
1962), making results hard to reproduce and clarify. Ergosterol has been shown to 
increase yeast cells tolerance to both ethanol toxicity and hyperosmotic stress 
(Gibson, 2011) making its supplementation an interesting variable to investigate with 
regards to HG brewing.

2.3.6 Metal ions 
Metal ions play a part in most aspects of brewing and yeast performance, including in 
the activation of enzymes involved in metabolism, cell-to-cell interactions such as 
flocculation, maintenance of structural integrity and functionality of intracellular 
organelles, regulation of nutrient uptake mechanisms, in governing gene expressions 
and in stress tolerance, such as osmotic stress and ethanol toxicity (Udeh & Kgatla, 
2013, Walker, 2004). Barley and malt are rich sources of both vitamins and metal ions 
although their amounts can differ widely depending on many factors including growth 
conditions, soil quality, and malting procedure (Gibson, 2011). Similarly, metal ion 
requirements of yeast depend on the yeast strain used as well as fermentation 
conditions (Walker, 2004). At standard gravities, malt should provide metal ions in 
sufficient amounts to ensure adequte fermentation performance although this may 
not be the case for brewing at HG, especially in cases of sugar adjunct additions, 
where relative metal ion concentrations are diluted or their required amounts are 
increased due to the stressful impact of HG brewing on yeasts (Fix, 1999). Although 
yeasts require a number of metal ions in trace amounts for optimal performance, it 
has been shown that zinc, magnesium and calcium are the most important for 
fermentation performance and ethanol production (Gibson, 2011, Walker, 2004). 
 
2.3.7 Zinc 
The importance of zinc (Zn2+) in fermentation has been well established and zinc is 
the most commonly added supplement in the brewing industry (Gibson, 2011). It 
plays diverse roles in fermentation, where it acts as a structural or catalytic cofactor 
for a large number of proteins, which include enzymes associated with alcohol 
synthesis, most notably alcohol dehydrogenase (De Nicola, Hall, Melville & Walker, 
2009, Udeh et al., 2014). Zinc is especially important in HG brewing as it is necessary 
for a wide range of processes associated with responses to ethanol stress. The role of 
zinc in fermentation performance and resistance to ethanol toxicity has not yet been 
fully elucidated but it is utilized in a myriad of processes and it has been estimated 
that around 3% of the yeast proteome function is dependant on zinc (Gibson, 2011, 
Zhao & Bai, 2012). Zinc supplementation has been shown to stimulate the synthesis 
and accumulation of trehalose and ergosterol, both compounds that enforce structural 
rigidity of the cell membrane and thus the yeast cells resistance to ethanol toxicity, 
since it has the biggest impact on the cell membrane itself (Zhao, Xue, Ge, Yuan, 
Wang & Bai, 2009). Membrane rigidity is further strengthened as zinc makes bridges

2.3.8 Magnesium & calcium 
Magnesium (Mg2+) is the most abundant intracellular divalent metal ion in yeast and 
plays many different roles in yeast physiology and metabolic processes (Walker, 
2004). These include a role as a cofactor in more than 300 enzymes, many of which are 
involved in metabolic and bioenergetic pathways such as ATP synthesis and other 
vital processes such as yeast growth are impossible in the absence of magnesium 
(Udeh, 2013 & Walker, 2004). Furthermore, it helps maintain the tertiary structure of 
proteins and thus the structural integrity of cell organelles, along with being able to 
shield charged phospholipids which helps maintain cell membrane structure, 
especially under stressed conditions (Walker, 2004). In ethanol production, 
magnesium acts as a cofactor for several glycolytic enzymes, making it essential for 
the conversion of sugar into ethanol. 
Calcium (Ca2+) has long been thought of as one of the more important metal ions in 
brewing, although it seems to play a minor role in the intracellular processes of yeast 
(Rees & Stewart, 1997). Rather, calcium is actively excluded from yeast cells and its 
function in brewing is mostly extracellular, where it binds to the cell wall and thus 
helps maintain membrane structure and has a direct effect on processes such as 
flocculation (Walker, 2004, Rees & Stewart, 1997). The beneficial effects of calcium 
are counteracted by the fact that it antagonises the uptake of magnesium in yeast and 
therefore suppresses magnesium dependent processes. Excess calcium in wort can 
inhibit yeast growth and fermentative abilities as it binds competitively to enzymes 
that have an essential requirement for magnesium (Walker, 2004). Yeast cells have a 
minimum requirement of 42.5 and 10-20 ppm for magnesium and calcium, 
respectively (Rees & Stewart 1999). 
Walker, Birch, Chandrasena & Maynard (1996) report an increased ethanol yield in 
various liquid media using industrial yeast strains where ratios of magnesium to 
calcium were high and a decreased yield where they were lower. Furthermore, they 
report increased ethanol yield for a number of yeast strains with magnesium 
supplementations, although the effect was strain dependent. Rees & Stewart (1997)

2.4 Flavour compounds 
2.4.1 Glycerol 
Glycerol is a sugar alcohol that gives a smooth sweetness to beer flavour. It is the 3rd 
most abundant compound produced in beer fermentation, after ethanol and carbon 
dioxide (Modig, Granath, Adler & Lidén, 2007). In beer, it has been reported to have a 
threshold limit of detection at 10 mg/ml, a much higher threshold than other flavour 
compounds, with higher concentrations increasing sweetness (Langstaff & Lewis, 
1993). Glycerol synthesis is vital to yeast as it plays an important part in maintaining 
redox balance in yeasts growing under anaerobic conditions. Glycerol is formed by 
reducing the glycolytic intermediate dihydroxyacetone phosphate to glycerol-3-
phosphate using NADH as a reducing agent, followed by dephosphorisation to yield 
glycerol. NADH is formed as a result of biomass production. It needs to be reoxidised 
to maintain redox balance in the cell and glycerol production is the yeast cells primary 
means of doing this (Modig et al., 2007, Nordström, 1968). This close tie to biomass 
production means that glycerol production is likely at its strongest at the start of 
fermentation, especially in HG worts where it is also produced as an osmoprotectant, 
as discussed in section 2.2. 
 
2.4.2 Higher alcohols 
Higher alcohols, also referred to as fusel alcohols, are alcohol compounds produced by 
yeasts which contain more carbon atoms than ethanol. Compounds known as 2-
oxoacids are formed as an intermediary step in the ethanol production of yeasts. 
These either undergo transamination, in order to form amino acids, or 
decarboxylation to form aldehydes that are, in turn, reduced to alcohols. The major 2-
oxoacid formed in yeast metabolism is pyruvate which is turned into acetaldehyde 
and further reduced to ethanol. Although pyruvate is the major one, yeast produces a 
range of other 2-oxoacids as a part of normal metabolism or transamination of 
externally derived amino acids. Higher alcohols are formed by the decarboxylation 
and subsequent reduction of these 2-oxoacids (Priest & Campbell, 2003, He, Dong, 
Yin, Zhao, Chen, Wan, Chen, Hou, Liu & Chen 2014). The higher alcohols measured 
for this project, along with their flavour contributions and organoleptic thresholds, 
can be found in table 2.2. 
 
2.4.3 Esters 
A number of volatile esters are produced by yeasts during fermentation and 
collectively they contribute a pleasant fruity flavour to beer. They are produced by an 
enzyme-catalyzed reaction between a higher alcohol and acetyl-CoA (Yoshioka & 
Hashimoto, 1983), whereupon they are rapidly diffused through the cellular 
membrane into the fermenting wort (Kobayashi, Shimizu & Shioya, 2008). Conditions 
==PAGE:34==
16 
contributing to ester production are not entirely understood although it is exacerbated 
in HG brewing. It seems, though, that a number of factors, ranging from wort 
nutrient composition to fermentor design, either increase or decrease overall ester 
production and the matter is further complicated by some factors increasing 
production of certain esters while decreasing production of others (Verstrepen, 
Derdelinckx, Dufour, Winderickx, Thevelein, Pretorius & Delvaux, 2003, Priest & 
Campbell, 2003). The main limiting factor on ester production appears to be acetyl-
CoA availability. Biological processes in yeast that also require acetyl-CoA, such as 
cell mass production, seem to compete for its availability, meaning that an icrease in 
those processes should be reflected in reduced ester synthesis (Verstrepen et al., 
2003). The volatile esters measured for this project along with their flavour 
contributions and organoleptic thresholds, can be found in table 2.2. 
 
2.4.4 Aldehydes and vicinal diketones 
Aldehydes and ketones are some of the most impactful off-flavour compounds in beer 
and one of the main objectives of beer maturation is their removal. Acetaldehyde is 
made in abundance during fermentation as its generation is the penultimate step in 
ethanol production but most of it is efficiently reduced to ethanol. Despite this, 
enough of it is generally secreted from the cell to have an impact on the final flavour 
of the beer contributing a „grassy“ flavour (Frenkel & Dudai, 2016, Priest & 
Campbell, 2003). 
The vicinal diketones, diacetyl and 2,3-pentanedione, contribute a buttery taste to 
beer. They are made from intermediates of amino acid synthesis from 2-oxoacids, 
namely the synthesis of valine and isoleucine from pyruvate and 2-oxobutyrate, 
respectively. The first intermediates in valine and isoleucine synthesis are α-
acetolactate and α-aceto-α-hydroxybutyrate. These compounds leak from the cell into 
the fermenting wort where they are spontaneously decarboxylated to form the vicinal 
diketones: Diacetyl, from α-acetolactate, and 2,3-pentanedione from α-aceto-α-
hydroxybutyrate. The diketones are subsequently taken up by the yeast and reduced 
to 2,3-butanediol and 2,3-pentanediol, compounds that have no impact on flavour. In 
contrast, acetaldehyde reacts spontaneously with several wort compounds to form 
flavourless complexes so any undesired flavour stemming from acetaldehyde is 
rapidly removed. Since the removal of the vicinal diketones relies on direct contact 
with yeast, fermentation should not be stopped until they are adequately removed 
and this maturation stage can add significantly to overall required fermentation time 
(Fix, 1999, Priest & Campbell, 2003). The flavour contribution and organoleptic 
thresholds of acetaldehyde and the vicinal diketones can be seen in table 2.2.

An overview of the main metbolic routes in yeast, responsible for the generation of the 
compounds mentioned in the literature review and thus relevant to this thesis, can be 
seen in figure 2.1. 
 
Figure 2.1 The main metabolic routes in yeast, used to generate compounds relevant to this thesis.

process, where the formation of one compound can either facilitate or deter the

(Dekoninck et al., 2012), even though the volatile flavour compounds are end

metabolism. The literature review demonstrated that each variable considered had a 
different way of affecting the prodcution of flavour compounds, cell mass and ethanol. 
In this thesis, an attempt will be made to explain this difference in production by 
viewing the fermentative metabolic process as a whole.

3 Materials and methods 
3.1 Materials 
3.1.1 Wort 
An all-malt wort, produced in-house using a 13 litre mashing system with a heat 
exchanger, was used for all fermentations. The mash profile was as follows: 3.7 kg of 
2-row pilsner malt were added to 13 litres of water. An α-amylase rest was performed 
at 66±1°C for about 70 minutes, until the wort reached a density of 22°P. A β-amylase 
rest was then performed at 74±1°C for about 10 minutes, until the wort reached a 
density of 23.5°P. A limited sparge was performed until the run-off wort reached a 
density of 19°P and the wort was subsequently reduced to a final density of 25°P by 
boiling. No exogenous enzymes were used and no hops were added during the boil. 
The wort was put in a freezer at -40°C immediately following the boil. 
For the experiments, the 25°P all-malt wort was diluted down to 12°P. Pure maltose 
was then added to reach gravities of 25, 30 and 35°P, depending on experimental 
conditions (see section 3.2.3). This was done in order to mimic commercial brewery 
conditions, where high wort gravities are most commonly achieved by adding sugar 
adjuncts to the wort. Maltose was chosen as the adjunct over glucose since uptake of 
other wort sugars is repressed in the presence of glucose and high glucose 
concentrations in wort are associated with increased concentrations of higher alcohols 
and esters in VHG brewing (Trumbly, 1992, Dekoninck et al., 2012, Stewart & 
Younis, 1998). Every fermentation performed for this project was done using this 
wort, where maltose was added to the diluted 12°P all malt wort to increase sugar 
concentrations, unless explicitly stated.

3.2 Experimental methods 
This section describes methods used for the experimental part of the thesis. 
 
3.2.1 Propagation and incubation of yeast 
An identical procedure was used every time for the propagation and incubation of the 
yeast, both in the ethanol toxicity screening and in the subsequent fermentation 
experiments. Yeast from the stock culture was propagated on YPD-Agar plates at 
30°C for 72 hours. A single yeast colony was transferred to 20 ml of 12°P wort in a 
sterile 50 ml Falcon tube and incubated at 25°C in a rotary shaker at 150 RPM. After 
48 hours, the pre-culture was transferred into a shake flask with 250 ml 12°P wort 
and incubated at 25°C in a rotary shaker at 150 RPM for 72 hours. 
 
3.2.2 Ethanol toxicity screening 
All six yeast strains were screened for their resistance to ethanol toxicity, both in 
solution and on YPD-Agar plates, at ethanol concentrations of 5 and 10% (v/v). 
Resistance to ethanol toxicity on YPD-Agar plates was tested by propagating 300 
colony forming units of each yeast strain on YPD-Agar plates containing 5 and 10% 
(v/v) ethanol, as described in section 3.1.3. The yeasts were then incubated at 30°C for 
72 hours and the number of colonies grown from the initial 300 colony forming units 
counted.  
Resistance to ethanol toxicity in solution was tested by pitching 107 cells/ml of each 
yeast strain in solutions of 12°P and ethanol at concentrations of 5 and 10% (v/v). The 
yeasts were then incubated at 30°C for 48 hours under mildy shaking conditions and 
cells/ml in each solution subsequently counted. 
 
3.2.3 Fermentation conditions  
All fermentations were performed in 1 litre bottle fermenters (see section 3.1.4) at 
20°C with a working volume of 800 ml. Fermentations performed for the thesis fall 
into 3 categories: Standard fermentations, using only the standard wort (see section 
3.1.1) at different gravities; single variable fermentations, using a 25°P standard wort 
along with a single variable added or changed; and combined variable fermentations, 
using standard wort at different gravities and a combination of all variables found to 
increase fermentation performance in the single variable fermentations. All 
fermentations performed for the project were done in duplicate. Pitch rate was 107 
cells/ml for all fermentations, unless explicitly stated.

Single variable fermentations 
Single variable fermentations were performed at 25°P and with the BE-256 yeast 
strain only, for a duration of 14 days. Each fermentation had a single variable added, 
like supplementation of FAN, or changed, like increasing pitching rate, in order to 
determine their effect on ethanol production. A total of 9 single variable fermentations 
were performed which can be further divided into 3 categories: non-nutritional 
alterations, non-metal ion supplementation and metal ion supplementation. The first 
category comprises fermentations in which nutritional aspects were not altered, a 
fermentation with 100 RPM stirring added and another at a pitch rate 10 times 
higher than usual (108 cells/ml). The second category consists of fermentations in 
which non-metal ion nutrients were supplemented, a fermentation with 10 mM 
NH4Cl added, one with 30 ppm ergosterol added and one using a 25°P all-malt wort 
(no maltose added). The last category contains fermentations in which metal ion 
nutrients were supplemented, 2 fermentations with 5 and 50 ppm Zn2+ additions and 
2 with 250 and 500 ppm Mg2+ additions. A list of all single variable fermentations, 
along with their added or changed variables can be seen in table 3.3. 
 
Combined variable fermentations

fermentations were combined in order to determine if this further increased 
fermentation performance. Combined variable fermentations were performed at 25, 
30 and 35°P for both the BE-256 and WLP 099 yeast strains, for a duration of 7 days. 
A list of all combined variable fermentations, along with the variables added, can be 
seen in table 3.3.

3.2.4 Sample collection 
Samples were collected from about mid-height in the fermenter without prior mixing 
every 24 hours for all fermentations. No mixing was done before taking samples both 
in order to simulate normal brewing conditions and to get an accurate count of cells in 
solution each time. Samples were collected from the fermenter with a 10 ml syringe, 
equipped with a luer lock. A sample size of 5-10 ml was collected each day of ongoing 
fermentations save for 100 ml at the last day. 1 ml of each sample was used for cell 
counting purposes but the rest was filtered through a 0.2 µM cellulose acetate filter, 
immediately upon collection, and subsequently frozen at -80°C.

3.3 Analytical methods 
This section describes methods used for analyses of wort, beer and yeast. 
3.3.1 Cell counts 
Cells were counted every 24 hours for all fermentations, immediately following 
sample collection, in a Fuchs-Rosenthal counting chamber. The vitality of cells was 
determined using the methylene blue staining method (Hjortshöj et al., 1992). Only 
live cells were counted. 
 
3.3.2 HPLC 
Ethanol, glycerol and sugar concentrations of all wort and beer samples were 
determined with high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) using a DIONEX 
UltiMate 3000 (Thermo SCIENTIFIC) autosampler connected to a DIONEX P680 
HPLC Pump and equipped with a Shodex RI-101 refrective index detector. The HPLC 
was equipped with an Agilent Hi-Plex Ca column. For the analytical procedure, 20 µl 
beer samples were injected onto the column. The HPLC was used with the following 
temperature program: 85°C for 30 min at a flow rate of 0.600 ml/min. 
 
3.3.3 GC-MS 
Volatile flavour compounds of beer samples were determined with headspace gas 
chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) using a GC-Combi PAL (CTC 
Analytical, Zwingen, Switzerland) on an Agilent 6890 gas chromatograph (GC) 
(Agilent Technologies, Inc., Wilmington, Germany) equipped with an Agilent 5979 
Mass Selective Detector. The GC-MS was equipped with a DB-WAXETR column (30 
m × 0.25 mm, 0.50 μm film thickness). For the analytical procedure, 4 ml of beer 
sample containing 50 µl internal standard (2-butanol) was transferred into a 10 ml 
headspace vial. The vial was heated to 50 °C for 15 min and hereafter 500 µl from the 
headspace were injected into the GC inlet at 150 °C in split mode using a split ratio of 
25:1. The GC was used with the following temperature program: 35 °C for 5 min and 
then raised at 1 °C/min and finally 25 °C/min to 180 °C for 2 min. Helium served as 
carrier gas at a flow rate of 0.8 ml/min. The mass spectrometer was used in electron 
ionization mode and operated in single ion monitoring 
 
3.3.4 FAN analysis 
FAN levels were measured for every day of the 25°P BE-256 standard and 10 mM 
NH4Cl single variable supplementation fermentations using the ninhydrin method at 
570 nm and using glycine as a standard (Lie, S. 1973).

‚Super High Gravity Ale Yeast‘. The strains‘ clear superiority in resistance to ethanol, 
compared with the other strains, did therefore not come as a big surprise.

Having demonstrated superior ethanol yields, the BE-256 yeast strain was selected 
over the WLP 099 to perform the subsequent single variable fermentations. 
Additionally, 30 and 35°P standard fermentations were performed with the BE-256 
strain for comparison reasons in the combined variable fermentations. 
Ethanol production was nearly identical in the 25 and 35°P standard fermentations 
but more glycerol was initially produced in the 35°P fermentation, likely as a response 
to increased osmotic pressure. Interestingly, ethanol production was diminished in 
the 30°P fermentation compared with the other two, indicating that increasing initial 
gravity does not neccessarily correlate with lowered ethanol production, as might be 
expected. Glycerol production was not increased initially for the 30°P fermentation, 
contrary to what was observed with the 35°P one. This is likely the reason for poorer 
overall fermentative metabolism in the 30°P fermentation, where an inadequate 
initial response to hyperosmotic pressure had a debilitating effect on the yeasts 
physiology and subsequent fermentative ability.

unexpected trend, as seen in figure 4.3 (B), where cell counts peaked around days 2 
and 3 and again around days 7 and 8. The latter peaks are consistent with the 25°P 
fermentation, which reached peak cell counts at day 8. These standard fermentations 
were, however, the only ones where cell counts in solution peaked so late in the 
process and looking at cell counts for the rest of the fermentations shows them 
generally peaking around days 2 or 3. Fermentation is the most vigorous between 
days 0 and 3 and the peaks observed on days 2 and 3 likely stem from the natural 
agitation of rising CO2 bubbles. Although results from the 30°P standard 
fermentation are inconsistent with the 25 and 35°P ones, results for the duplicate 
30°P fermentations were similar every day, indicating that the results achieved were 
not a result of experimental error.

fermentable sugar was utilized for the production of cell mass. 
This overall reduction in fermentative metabolism likely comes as a result of yeast 
cells being overly numerous. The wort utilized for the study was on the brink of being 
deficient in nutrients, most notably metal ions, and increasing initial cell counts 
possibly resulted in proportionally less nutrients being available for each yeast cell. 
This would not have been a problem at the start of fermentation as wort nutrients 
would still have been available to a certain degree and the initial increase in ethanol 
production presumably comes as a result of having more fermenting yeast cells 
overall. Nutrients would have been used up quicker, however, and the sharp decrease 
in ethanol production between days 3 and 4 likely comes as a consequence of their 
depletion, resulting in a sluggish fermentation. An increase in pitching rate would 
therefore likely have to be accompanied by a complementary increase in nutrients. 
A significantly higher production of isobutanol and acetaldehyde was observed at 
increased pitching rates, with ester synthesis being unaffected, save for a reduction in 
isoamyl acetate formation. Ethyl propionate and 3-methyl-1-butanol concentrations 
were slightly over the organoleptic limit, although not after the beer would be diluted 
down to 5% (v/v) ethanol. This was not the case with acetaldehyde concentrations and 
the beer would need further aging before being consumed. 
The only consistent result of increasing pitching rates observed in the literature 
review was an increase in fermentation rates. None of the articles reviewed used the 
same method so differences in final ethanol concentrations and diacetyl, ester and 
higher alcohol formation may be due to differing fermentation conditions. The 
findings of this study most closely resemble Erten et al. (2007) who report an increase 
in fermentation rate, cell mass production and isobutanol, no effect on final ethanol 
concentration and a reduction in diacetyl, 2,3-pentanedione, 2- and 3-methyl-1-
butanol and isoamyl acetate. 
The results of the non-nutritional alteration fermentations suggest that stirring is of 
interest to the VHG brewer if ethanol production needs to be speeded up. The time 
saved could, however, be nullified by a longer required aging time. Increasing pitching 
rate seems to be of less importance, at least if done without nutritionally enhancing 
the wort in a way to accommodate the increased cell counts.

Supplementing ergosterol reduced ethanol production for every day, compared with 
the standard, as seen in figure 4.6. Glycerol production was comparable to that of the 
standard, as seen in figure 4.7 (A), although final glycerol concentrations were lower 
due to glycerol uptake by the yeast between days 8 and 9. Table 4.9 shows that the 
fermentation did not reach a high attenuation and a disproportionate amount of 
fermentable sugars was used for cell mass production. 
In the literature review, no report was found where only the effect of ergosterol 
supplementation in beer fermentations was studied. Rather, both Casey et al. (1983) 
and Dragone et al. (2003) reported greatly increased ethanol production on 
supplementations of 24 ppm ergosterol, 0.24% (v/v) Tween 80 and yeast extract. The 
aim of this study was to find out about the effect of individual variables on 
fermentation performance. Tween 80 is a mixture of fatty acids and yeast extract 
contains a plethora of various nutrients in unpredictable amounts (Grant & Pramer, 
1962) so neither can be considered strictly as a single variable. Supplementing with 
ergosterol was chosen over linoleic acid since ergosterol is an end product of yeast 
sterol synthesis and thus does not require wort oxygen to be present in order to be 
utilized, as opposed to linoleic acid (Priest & Campbell, 2003), possibly resulting in 
==PAGE:61==
43 
poor utilization of linoleic acid. The results of this study suggest that the increased 
effect on ethanol production, reported in the literature, did not come as a result of the 
ergosterol supplementation. 
Supplementing ergosterol likely gave the yeast too ample resources for propagation 
purposes, resulting in low production of ethanol. According to Aries et al. (1977), yeast 
growth doesn‘t stop until a limiting amount of sterols is reached. Given the abundance 
of supplemented ergosterol, it is likely that propagation was going on for a much 
longer time than in other fermentations. This is supported by the fact that sugar 
consumption and ethanol production seemed to be steadily ongoing until day 14, 
whereas it had largely stopped by that point in most other fermentations. This 
hypothesized increase in cell mass production would likely not be adequately 
represented in the cell counts as natural agitation and thus amount of cells in 
solution would be diminished, due to lowered ethanol production. Cell counts in 
solution, seen in figure 4.7 (B), were initially increased compared to the standard, 
however, despite decreased ethanol production, suggesting that total cell counts were 
much higher. This is supported by the increased proportion of sugars utilized for the 
production of cell mass, as seen in table 4.9. 
Production of higher alcohols, especially isobutanol, was increased with ergosterol 
supplementation despite lower ethanol production and concentrations of 3-methyl-1-
butanol exceeded the organleptic threshold, although not after adjusting ethanol 
concentrations to 5% (v/v) ethanol. Isoamyl acetate synthesis was reduced while ethyl 
acetate synthesis was slightly increased. The most significant change in volatile 
flavour concentrations was in acetaldehyde, however, its concentrations more than 
doubled under ergosterol supplementation, indicating that it was not reduced to 
ethanol efficiently. 
The 25°P all malt single variable fermentation was not performed with the idea that 
an all malt wort would be used in the combined fermentations if it turned out to be an 
improvement on the standard in terms of ethanol production. Rather, it was 
performed for comparison reasons. Total ethanol concentrations were increased, 
compared to the standard, between days 1 and 9 with the vast majority of ethanol 
being produced in the first 3 days, as seen in figure 4.6. Ethanol concentrations at day 
3 were the highest in the 25°P all malt fermentation compared with every other single 
variable fermentation, as seen in table 4.8. It also had the highest peak cell count of 
all single variable fermentations, as seen in table 4.9 and figure 4.7 (B), although only 
slightly more sugar was used for cell mass production as compared to the standard, 
and the high cell count in solution likely comes as a result of the vigorous 
fermentation rate and thus superior wort agitation. This is supported by the fact that 
cell counts rapidly drop after day 2, which coincides with the drop in daily ethanol 
production. 
Glycerol production was increased in the first days of the 25°P all malt fermentation, 
as seen in figure 4.7 (A), although final glycerol concentrations were similar to the 
standard, indicating an enhanced response to hyperosmotic pressure without affecting 
final flavour, like in the ammonium ion supplementation fermentation. Although all 
==PAGE:62==
44 
fermentable sugar was rapidly consumed in the all malt fermentation, as seen in 
Appendix D, it only reached an attenuation of 89%. This is likely due to the fact that 
the all malt wort had proportionally more non-fermentable dissolved wort solids than 
the other fermentations. 
Concentrations of isobutanol, isobutyl acetate and acetaldehyde were increased in the 
all malt fermentation compared to the standard, as seen in table 4.10. The increase in 
acetaldehyde is disconcerting as diluting the beer down to 5% (v/v) ethanol would not 
bring its concentrations below the organoleptic threshold, and the beer would require 
some aging before being palatable. 
Overall metabolism seems to have been improved in the all malt fermentation, 
resulting in a complete fermentation in little time. This demonstrates that the 
nutrients found in wort are likely sufficiently abundant for VHG brewing and 
fermenting an all malt wort might prove better than fermenting one with a high 
amount of sugar adjuncts, especially if no other nutrient supplementation is provided. 
That way, time per batch could be reduced resulting in increased production capacity, 
although maturation time would have to be taken into account due to the high 
acetaldehyde levels observed in this study.  
The results of the non-ion nutritional supplementation fermentations indicate that 
supplementing ammonium ions to wort is a tempting prospect for the VHG brewer, 
especially if beers with fruity aromas are to be produced. The results of the FAN 
analysis further indicate that increasing the amount of supplemented ammonium 
might be beneficial as FAN was rapidly consumed. Fermenting an all malt VHG wort 
could also be interesting, although malt prices would have to be taken into account. 
Ergosterol supplementations should be avoided, however, at least in the absence of 
other supplements such as yeast extract or Tween 80.

It is unlikely that toxic concentrations of these metal ions were reached since the 
yeast managed to assimilate and utilize a majority of fermentable sugars, indicating 
that metabolism was not severely lacking, only the conversion of sugars to ethanol. It

toxicity and thus each yeast cells ability to propagate more often and for a longer 
time. When yeast cells propagate, cell division leaves a scar on the mother cell, 
slightly damaging the membrane, and the cell only ceases to be able to propagate 
when the membrane has suffered too much accumulated damage, usually after 20-30 
divisions (Fix, 1999). This number of divisions is likely to be reduced in VHG brewing 
as the biggest damage incurred by ethanol stress is on the membrane itself. As a 
result, a yeast cell subjected to ethanol stress likely experiences this critical point of 
membrane damage sooner and any factor mitigating the stress should prolong it.

Supplementing metal ions at such high concentrations was inspired by Rees and 
Stewart (1997), Rees and Stewart (1998) and Udeh et al. (2014) who reported 
increased ethanol production at high concetrations of these metal ions. Despite the 
poor ethanol production observed in this study, their importance to HG fermentations 
has been firmly established (Gibson, 2011, Walker, 2004, Udeh & Kgatla, 2013), albeit 
not necessarily at such high concentrations. Table 4.14 shows that the metal ions 
were abundantly supplemented as magnesium and zinc were far from being fully 
consumed in their respective supplementation fermentations. Higher amounts of 
metal ions may, however, have been consumed over the course of fermentation and 
then re-released into wort as cells underwent death and lysis. Measuring metal ion 
concentrations for every day of the fermentations would therefore be an interesting 
analysis. Concentrations at end of fermentation were high, however, indicating that 
they were not fully consumed over the course of fermentation. It was therefore 
decided to re-perform the fermentations with reduced metal ion supplementations. 
Zinc concentrations are generally reported to have a beneficial upper limit of 5 ppm 
(Kunze & Satyanarayana, 2009) but no such limit exists for the Mg2+ ion. Thus, 
fermentations were performed with additions of 5 ppm Zn2+ and 250 ppm Mg2+. 
Fermenting at reduced metal ion concentrations showed an improvement on the 
standard in both fermentation rate and final ethanol production, as seen in figure 4.9. 
Fermentation speed was slightly reduced as compared to the standard for the 5 ppm 
Zn2+ addition until day 4, where it started demonstrating greater ethanol production 
capabilities under the increasingly toxic ethanol concentrations. The 250 ppm Mg2+ 
addition fermentation delivered the highest final ethanol concentration of all the 
single variable fermentations, reaching 14.0% (v/v). The curve of ethanol production 
in the 5 ppm zinc supplementation fermentation had a more gradual incline than 
other beneficial single variable fermentations, producing proportionally more ethanol 
in the latter week, compared with the others. The same effect was observed with 50 
ppm zinc supplementations, although less overall ethanol was produced there. This 
suggests that the metabolic capacity of zinc supplemented fermentations is less 
hampered by ethanol stress, indicating that zinc is a better protector against it than 
magnesium.

Higher alcohol production was greatly increased compared to the standard in the 250

The metal ion supplementation fermentations are consistent with the literature in 
that the optimal amount of their supplementations seems to be highly strain 
dependent. The generally beneficial concentrations of zinc in wort are much more well 
established than for magnesium and supplementing the reported upper limit of 5 ppm 
was found to increase ethanol yield in this study. More research should be done on 
magnesium supplementations as their importance has been demonstrated in this 
study. 
The results of the mineral ion supplementation fermentations indicate that 
supplementations of zinc and magnesium are of paramount importance to the VHG 
brewer, although the right amounts of supplementation needs to be determined first.

4.2.3 Combined variable fermentations 
Once the single variable fermentations had been performed, the ones that showed a 
beneficial effect on ethanol production were combined in fermentations of 25, 30 and 
35°P with both the BE-256 and WLP 099 yeast strains. These variables were stirring 
at 100 RPM and supplementations of 10 mM NH4Cl, 250 ppm Mg2+ and 5 ppm Zn2+ 
and they will henceforth be referred to as the combined variables. 
One aim of fermenting with the combined variables was to find out whether the 
combination would result in reducing the time required to fully ferment VHG wort. 
Current brewing industry practices hold that fermentations should be complete 
within about 7 days, as longer fermentation times would result in an overall reduction 
in production capacity which would, in turn, counteract the advantages of brewing at 
VHG (M. Hansen, brewery manager at Bryggeri Skovlyst, personal communication, 
February 5, 2016). The single variable fermentations demonstrated that a majority of 
ethanol was produced within the first week of fermentation so extending fermentation 
time by another week would result in a diminished overall ethanol production on a 
per day basis for the VHG brewer. The combined variable fermentations were thus 
terminated at day 7.

4.3 Overall discussion 
Of the single variable fermentations, the highest ethanol yield was achieved with 
supplementation of 250 ppm magnesium. Glycolysis is highly dependent on 
magnesium being present as it is a cofactor for enzymes used in several steps of the 
process. This suggests that glycolysis may be a rate limiting step in the ethanol 
production process, making its enhancement important. The second highest ethanol 
yield was achieved with supplementation of 5ppm zinc, a cofactor for many yeast 
enzymes, most importantly alcohol dehydrogenase. This suggests that ensuring 
adequate enzyme function in the brewing yeast is very important to the VHG brewer, 
although the ethanol production promoting effect of these metal ions likely also comes 
in part from increased resistance to the effects of ethanol toxicity. 
An overall trend in glycerol production was observed in most fermentations where it 
was generally at its most vigorous in the first two days of fermentation. It then 
usually slowed down considerably, with yeast cells even reassimilating some of it at 
times. This drop in production was generally seen before ethanol production had 
mostly ceased, indicating that the production of these two compounds was not closely 
related. Rather, glycerol production coincided more with the production of cell mass, 
which was also generally at its most intense in the first two days. This trend is 
consistent with the literature as glycerol is reported to be the yeast cells primary 
means of maintaining redox balance which is disturbed by production of cell mass. 
Rapid glycerol production also comes as a response to hyperosmotic pressure at the 
start of fermentation and these two factors likely go hand in hand. 
Every combined BE-256 fermentation produced a similar amount of glycerol. The 
same goes for the combined WLP 099 fermentations, with glycerol concentrations 
being nearly identical each day. This is counter to what is reported in the literature 
where glycerol production is said to increase as gravities, and thus osmotic pressure, 
increase. As discussed before this may either be explained by yeast strains having a 
set preferred glycerol concentration, which is only realized when adequate nutrition is 
provided, or that more glycerol was produced on a per cell basis, as cell counts 
dropped at higher gravities. 
Attenuation was nearly always better than advertised for both yeast strains used (see 
section 3.1.2). This likely because of the fact that the wort fermented for this thesis 
was heavily supplemented with pure maltose, which is fully fermentable by yeast. 
The advertised attenuation values are more directed towards homebrewers, who tend 
to ferment all malt worts at lower gravities. Attenuation also measures 
unfermentable wort solids, the concentration of which will be proportionally higher in 
all malt worts, resulting in higher final gravities even if all fermentable sugars are 
consumed. This is supported by the fact that attenuation was relatively low for the all 
malt fermentation despite fermentable sugars being completely consumed. 
Concentrations of  higher alcohols, especially isobutanol, were increased in all single 
and combined variable fermentations, compared with the standards, except for the 
ammonium ion supplementation single variable fermentation. This may indicate that 
==PAGE:79==
61 
a large proportion of wort amino acids consisted of valine and leucine. 
Transamination of these amino acids leads to the formation of 2-oxo-isovalerate and 
2-oxo-isocaproate which, in turn, can be decarboxylated and reduced to isobutanol and 
3-methyl-1-butanol, respectively (Priest & Campbell, 2003). Upon being supplemented 
with ammonium ions, the yeast would possibly not have needed to derive its nitrogen 
from transamination, allowing it to directly utilize these amino acids and thus 
limiting the availability of their corresponding 2-oxoacids for higher alcohol 
production. Increased concentrations of acetaldehyde were generally observed in 
fermentations where ethanol production was reduced compared to the standard 
possibly suggesting that the increased acetaldehyde concentration was a symptom of 
nutritional deficiencies. This is supported by the fact that the considerable increase in 
acetaldehyde production for the 100 RPM stirring single variable fermentation did not

case in any of the combined variable fermentations, however, and any excessive 
acetaldehyde concentrations in other fermentations could be removed with proper

The 25°P 250 ppm magnesium supplementation single variable fermentation 
demonstrated that sugar consumption can be almost entirely shifted toward ethanol 
production rather than the production of cell mass. Supplementing more nutrients 
caused the yeast cells to produce ethanol faster, at the cost of increased cell mass 
production, resulting in a lower ethanol yield for the same amount of supplied 
fermentable sugar. Cell mass production is the yeast‘s preferred route and ethanol is 
only produced in order to generate ATP to maintain intracellular functions or produce 
more cell mass (Walker, 2004). The amount of supplementation in the combined 
variable fermentations appears to have been sufficient to allow the yeast to propagate 
rather than just maintain intracellular functions There likely exists a ratio of 
supplementation, therefore, where the yeast cells are forced to remain in a state of 
maintenance, resulting in a faster fermentation and reduced amount of sugar utilized 
for cell mass production, than in the combined variable fermentation. This ratio is, 
however, likely to be strain dependent.

5 Conclusions and suggested future 
work 
5.1 Conclusions 
The objective of this study was to gain a further understanding of the limitations 
associated with VHG brewing and ways to mitigate them, with a specific challenge of 
brewing beer containing 14% (v/v) ethanol, and to reduce the time needed to fully 
ferment VHG wort. A literature review gave rise to two research questions: What 
impact each individual variable had on ethanol yield when all other variables are left 
unchanged and would the combination of beneficial variables have a synergistic effect 
on ethanol yield and the time required to fully ferment VHG wort. The results of the 
subsequent experiment demonstrate that additions of stirring, FAN, zinc and 
magnesium increase ethanol yields in VHG brewing for the wort and yeast strain 
used. Supplementing magnesium has the biggest positive effect on ethanol yield of all 
variables. In this study, 14.0% (v/v) ethanol was produced within two weeks with 
supplementations of 250 ppm magnesium. Performing fermentations with 100 RPM 
stirring and supplementations of 10 mM NH4Cl, 5 ppm zinc and 250 ppm magnesium 
reduces the time required to fully ferment VHG wort without excessive flavour 
compound production, although ethanol yield is only slightly increased. Ethanol yield, 
cell mass production and concentrations of flavour compounds in the finished beer are 
yeast strain dependent. The BE-256 yeast strain produces ethanol faster than the 
WLP 099 strain, although the WLP 099 strain may be able to produce higher ethanol 
yields, given ample time. 
 
5.2 Suggested future work 
Optimal supplementation ratios of variables have yet to be determined in order to 
maximize ethanol yields without excessive production of cell mass or flavour 
compounds. A further study, determining these ratios, should be conducted. The study 
should incorporate all variables experimented on in this thesis in a range of 
concentrations and intensities. Fully homogenizing the fermenting wort by agitiation 
before taking samples for cell counts would make it easier to draw relationships 
between sugar consumption and subsequent production of cell mass, glycerol and 
ethanol. Using experimental design software would facilitate data analysis and allow 
for a better determination of the individual effects of each variable and its synergistic 
effects on other variables. This way, general relationships governing ethanol and 
flavour compound production in VHG brewing can be inferred. Optimal 
supplementation ratios are likely to be dependent both on yeast strain used and 
initial wort composition. Despite this, getting a comprehensive view of variables at 
==PAGE:82==
64 
different concentrations and intensities would likely lead to a generally optimal 
supplementation scheme, which would lead to increased ethanol yield in 
fermentations indepenent of yeast strain used. The results of the study should be 
tested on a pilot plant scale in order to determine their applicability to the brewing 
industry.  
Once optimal ratios of supplementation have been determined, a follow-up cost 
benefit analysis should be conducted. One of the main reasons for VHG brewing is 
cost reduction, so deriving optimal supplementation ratios is of little use if  it doesn‘t 
save breweries money. The cost of compounds supplemented could be compared with 
the cost of VHG brewing with yeast extract, an all malt wort or simply using sugar 
adjuncts without any supplementation. The analysis could incorporate results from 
the further study, where the cost of different sources of supplements could be 
determined. For instance, if providing FAN by enzymes proved to impart a slight 
benefit to fermentation perfomance compared with ammonium ion additions, using 
enzymes would be deemed optimal by the study. Enzymes might be much more 
expensive than ammonium ions, however, so overall costs could be reduced by using 
ammonium ion, without impacting fermentation performance severely. 
Ways to alleviate VHG brewing related stress factors should also be studied. Initial 
hyperosmotic pressure could, for instance, be reduced by switching from batch 
fermentations to fed-batch. This way, fermentable sugar concentrations could be kept 
at non-stress inducing levels while the amount of overall supplied sugars remained 
high. The effect of supplementing nutrients like metal ions and FAN at different 
stages of fermentation could also be studied using this fed-batch setup, which would 
be beneficial as not much research has been done on optimal times of 
supplementations. Reducing the effects of ethanol stress might prove trickier. Water 
and ethanol are highly miscible so directly removing ethanol from fermenting wort 
without drastically changing fermentation conditions might prove problematic. One 
way could be to exploit the fact that ethanol boils at lower temperatures than water. 
Distilling the wort at boiling temperatures is of course out of the question as that 
would kill the yeast but vacuum distillation at normal temperatures might work. This 
would, however, require a study of the effect of vacuum on the physiology and 
metabolic capacity of yeast. Another way would be to develop ways of increasing the 
structural integrity of the yeast cell wall and membrane, which can likely be achieved 
by the correct supplementations of metal ions, sterols and fatty acids to the wort. 
Beer brewing remains at the interesting boundary between art and science because of 
the complex relationship between yeast and its environment. The author hopes that 
this study has moved the boundary towards science in a way that may prove useful 
for the world‘s brewers and their customers.
