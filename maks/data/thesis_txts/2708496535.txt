Abstract
In this thesis, different AI methods were used to solve the lashing problem of stowage
planning to give an overview of how well they are suited to solve the problem. The inte­
gration of such a model into an optimization algorithm was explicitly considered by pre­
dicting per container stack and not on a whole stowage plan. This is a novel approach
compared to the existing literature. Tree­based methods are well suited for this prob­
lem and solve it at a comparable performance level as an existing neural network model
of the industrial partner. XGBoost matches not only the accuracy, but is also up to 10x
faster than the neural network. A deeper and wider neural network with adjustments to
the hyper­parameters increases the accuracy significantly, but is slightly slower than the
existing neural network due to the increased model size. Two further approaches were
tested to improve the performance even more. First, specialized models for each cluster
found in the data increase either speed or accuracy, but it is unsure if clustering is appli­
cable to the data that is seen during an actual optimization scheme. The data here was
partially automatically generated and this influences the clusters which are present in the
data. Second, a combination of a XGBoost classifier and the improved neural network
can be used to combine the fast XGBoost model with the more accurate neural network.
The classifier sorts out all data points below a lashing violation threshold, which means
the neural network must do inference on less data points. However, this approach only
increases the overall inference speed for large batch sizes of data. A second classifier
which sorts out data points with very high lashing violations can reduce the data points
for the neural network further. This works better regarding the inference time, but only
the NN is still faster depending on the batch size. The models were also tested on a sec­
ond vessel with a different data set and it was shown, that the introduced approaches are
transferable to another vessel.

Introduction
In 2019, around 80 % of all goods were carried on sea and according to Placek (2021) the
container trade value was around 14 trillion US Dollars. Container ships move from port
to port on their route and at each port some but not all containers are unloaded and new
containers are loaded. Each container is allocated to a specific slot on the ship before
it is loaded. This assignment to a position in a vessel is called stowage planning and it
is a crucial process for two reasons. First, the stability of the ship is influenced heavily
by the allocation of containers because of the weight distribution. Second, because the
economic viability of transporting goods between ports is mainly influenced by fuel costs
and the length of port stays. Since not all containers must be unloaded at each port, it
is important that containers for a later port do not block containers of the current port. If
this happens, it is referred to as ”overstowage” and it extends the port stay of the vessel.
Thus, it is important to optimize the stowage plan. Finding good and fast optimization
algorithms for this problem is an ongoing research area. Fast generation of a stowage
plan is important, because they are typically generated only hours before they arrive at a
port. One of the issues that has not been considered much in the research is lashing as
part of the optimization problem. Lashing is the process of securing the containers on the
vessel with lashing rods (see figure fig. 1.1 for an example).
Figure 1.1: Lashing of containers1
If the force applied to the lashing connection is too high, it can break and the contain­
ers might fall off of the vessel. The lashing force depends on multiple factors such as
weight of the containers, ship structure, ship balance and weather. If the optimization
algorithm already includes the lashing force, it will not generate plans that violate lashing

constraints. A lashing violation occurs, when the lashing force on a container exceeds a
threshold and as a consequence, the lashing connection might break, especially in rough
weather. However, calculating the exact lashing force is time consuming and not trivial.
This might be a reason, why most research does not include the lashing violation. Only
Chaemin Lee, M. Lee, and Shin (2021) predict the lashing violation with a Deep Neural
Network (DNN) after the optimization. Jensen and Ajspur (n.d.) include the lashing forces
in their revenue management optimization model, but they do not calculate or predict the
lashing forces, rather they define a solution space of legal container combinations. Thus,
this thesis will answer the following research question:
Which Artificial Intelligence methods can be used to solve the lashing problem in
stowage planning and how viable are they in the context of an optimization frame­
work both in terms of accuracy and speed?
The goal is to give an overview over different Artificial Intelligence (AI) methods and eval­
uate their accuracy and speed. The industrial partner of the project is Sealytix, a software
start­up which offers a solution for revenue management in liner shipping. Stowage plan­
ning is part of this solution. Currently, they use a small neural network (NN) to determine
the lashing violation during the optimization which is accurate enough to be viable prac­
tically. But even though it is faster than the exact calculation, the NN takes significant
amount of computation time and is a bottleneck in their software. Thus, it is also a goal to
find a method that is at least as accurate as the current Sealytix model, but faster. Sealytix
provides the necessary data to train the AI models. Several contributions to the literature
are made in this thesis:
• Chaemin Lee, M. Lee, and Shin (2021) predict the lashing violation only after the
stowage plan optimization based on the whole stowage plan. Therefore, a novel
approach based on the information of a container stack is presented here to predict
the lashing violation of said stack. This makes it possible to predict the lashing
violation already during the optimization.
• Tree­based methods and a bigger NN are used to predict the lashing violation. They
are evaluated against the existing smaller NN of Sealytix. The new bigger NN is very
accurate, while the tree­based methods are as accurate as the smaller Sealytix NN,
but much faster. Especially XGBoost is interesting due to its high speed and good
accuracy.
• To improve the results of XGBoost even further, dedicated models for each clus­
ter found in the data are trained. This makes XGBoost even more accurate while
maintaining its speed advantage.
• To combine the speed of XGBoost and accuracy of the new NN, a XGBoost classifier
is used to sort out data instances with low or very high lashing violation before the
NN is used. This approach works well when the lashing violation is predicted on
more than 10,000 data points at once. On less data points, it is slower than just
using the NN.
• By using data of two different ships, it is demonstrated that the presented approaches
transfer well and not only work in isolation for one ship
The rest of the thesis is structured as follows: After presenting the general layout of con­
tainer vessels and stacking rules of containers in the next section, the current literature on
stowage optimization and lashing prediction is discussed in the following chapter. Then,
the data provided by Sealytix is explained. After that, a background section for the differ­
ent models utilized in this thesis is provided. Following that, two benchmark models are

1.1
The container ship
In fig. 1.2, the layout of a container ship can be seen. A container ship is divided into
a below­deck and an on­deck section and the sections are separated by a removable
hatch cover. Because the containers can not fall off of the vessel below­deck and the
containers there are secured with cell guides, the lashing problem only applies on­deck.
On­deck, the containers are secured with lashing rods and twist­locks, each with limited
strength. A twist­lock is a mechanical connector and is used to connect each corner of
two stacked containers with each other. Containers are allocated a cell and the cell is
defined by a bay, row and tier number. Each cell has a fore and an aft slot. This means, a
cell holds either two 20­foot containers or one 40­foot container. However, there are also
special containers like 45­foot containers, higher containers which are called high­cube
(HC), refrigerated containers called reefers, containers for dangerous goods (IMO) and
out­of­gauge (OOG) containers, which are open containers for extra large goods. Normal
containers are abbreviated as DC, which stands for dry cargo. The capacity of a con­
tainer ship is measured in twenty­foot equivalent units, also TEU. Nowadays, the largest
container ships have a capacity of almost 24,000 TEUs.

The lashing forces are influenced mainly by wind forces, the stack height and weight and
the metacentric height (GM). The metacentric height is the distance between the meta­
center and the center of gravity of the vessel (see fig. 1.3). The metacenter is the point
where ”the buoyancy force intersects the center line” of the vessel (Pacino, Delgado, et
al. (2012):p. 19). Simplified, the higher the GM, the lower is the probability of a ship cap­
sizing. Simultaneously, the lashing forces are higher, due to the up­righting forces. Large
vessels also have lashing bridges to offer additional points to connect the lashing rods to
the ship. With lashing bridges, containers in higher tiers can also be secured with lashing
rods.

Stacking rules
Stacking containers on a container ship is subject to a couple of rules, that must be fol­
lowed. Since these regulations are important to understand the context of automated
stowage planning optimization, the most common stacking rules are listed here, adapted
from Delgado, Jensen, Janstrup, et al. (2012):p. 2:
• 20­foot reefers must be placed in reefer slots and a 40­foot reefer must be placed
in a cell with at least one reefer slot
• 20­foot containers can not be stored on 40­foot containers
• weight and height limits of stacks must not be exceeded
• length of cells must be considered (some cells can only hold 20­foot or 40­foot con­
tainers)
• containers must have physical support underneath
• only one container can be placed in one slot
• a cell must be either empty or with both slots occupied
Some of these rules may seem trivial, but they must be explicitly considered in an algo­
rithm for it to generate a valid stowage plan.

Literature Review
Many modern approaches to generate stowage plans for container ships decompose the
problem into two sub­problems: the Master­Bay Planning problem and the stowage plan­
ning. Some papers deal with both problems, but many deal only with either of the two
problems. The Master­Bay planning allocates containers or container types on a large­
scale to bays or other large­scale locations on the vessel to ensure stability of the ves­
sel and reduce overstowage. This problem is concerned with ship­wide requirements
and does not consider container­wise or stack­wise implications of container allocation.
Therefore, it is not directly connected to the lashing problem and literature dedicated to
only this sub­problem will not be reviewed here.
Stowage planning assigns each container to their individual slot and it is here that the
implications of assigning a container to a specific slot must be considered. One of these
implications is the resulting lashing force on the container and the respective stack. Thus,
the literature dealing with this sub­problem will be reviewed more in­depth. Additionally,
an overview of the recent literature on actually determining the lashing forces will be given.
Research on the topic of stowage planning has been done for many years. Botter and
Brinati (1992) published a comprehensive mathematical programming model. However,
this model could not be solved for commercial ship sizes. Thus, in the following years
many researchers simplified the problem to get a solvable model and/or applied heuris­
tic approaches to find good solutions. Avriel and Penn (1993) proposed a binary Integer
Programming (IP) model to solve a very basic version of the problem, where all contain­
ers are of equal size and stability requirements are ignored. Today, this is also known
as the basic container stowage planning problem (CSPP). They also concluded, that the
hard­ and software at that time was not able to solve exact models even if they are sim­
plified.
So additionally, they proposed a heuristic approach.
Dubrovsky, Levitin, and
Penn (2002) applied a genetic algorithm (GA) to minimize container movements and Am­
brosino, Sciomachen, and Tanfani (2004) used a linear programming model to assign
containers to their respective slot. In a following work Ambrosino, Sciomachen, and Tan­
fani (2006) decomposed the stowage planning problem into three phases. In the Master
Planning Phase, the containers were assigned to a subset location on the ship, in the
Stowage Planning Phase a, linear programming model assigned them to their slot and in
the last step, the solution was checked for feasibility in terms of stability and optimized
with a local search (LS) algorithm. Delgado, Jensen, and Schule (2009) were the first to
make use of constraint programming (CP) to solve the stowage planning problem under­
deck and increased the speed with which this problem could be solved. Another approach
was presented by Pacino and Jensen (2009) based on the work of Pacino (2008). This
approach utilized a local search heuristic to assign containers to their cells under deck.
However, all these approaches simplified the problem by not including all constraints or
by relaxing constraints. For example, the models only considered 20­ and 40­foot con­
tainers, only some ship stability requirements or only under­deck containers. Also, the
test data size only considered container ships with up to 176 TEUs.
In a series of work Low et al. (2011) also presented a three step approach, but only for the
stowage planning problem. Here, a basic stowage plan was generated in a first step with
a heuristic approach. In the second step, the plan was adjusted for stability and safety
requirements. This included the weight limit of stacks, bending and stress moments, bal­
last tank optimization and line of sight limits. In the third step, the plan was optimized for
Application of AI methods to solve the lashing problem of stowage planning
5
==PAGE:20==
re­handles, cost and berth time (see also Zeng et al. (2010) and F. Liu et al. (2010)). The
examined data sets include reefers, high­cube and hazardous containers and was up to
7000 TEUs big.
Following their previous work, Pacino, Delgado, et al. (2011) published a paper with mod­
els for the Master­Bay Planning problem and the stowage planning problem. The Master­
Bay Planning considers most stability constraints like GM stability, trim and draft limits,
weight and shear forces and is also extended to multiple ports to include effects of con­
tainers at future ports. To solve the stowage plan, the aforementioned CP model is used
in combination with the LS algorithm for the instances, where the CP model does not find
a solution within one second. As before, the stowage planning only considered stack­
ing rules, stack weight and height limits and 20­foot, 40­foot and reefer­containers. The
problem size increased by looking at vessels with up to 10,000 TEUs and slot allocation
of over 2000 containers in up to 70 locations.
Pacino and Jensen (2012) improved on their previous LS algorithm but still only con­
sidered under­deck containers and later extended the model to include HC containers
(Pacino and Jensen (2013)). Delgado, Jensen, Janstrup, et al. (2012) built an IP and a
CP model to solve the problem to optimality, but also only consider under­deck contain­
ers.
A new approach based on a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP)
improved the performance on stowage planning published by Parreño, Pacino, and Alvarez­
Valdes (2016). In particular, the algorithm performed better on larger instances with a lot
of containers to allocate. It was overall more reliable and faster than previous approaches
based on IP or LS. It included containers containing dangerous goods but not HC con­
tainers and the algorithm followed the common stacking rules, as presented in chapter 1.
Roberti and Pacino (2018) presented an improved model to solve the CSPP by employing
a Mixed Integer Programming formulation. It still simplified the problem heavily like Avriel
and Penn (1993). However, they were able to solve the model and they also increased
the examined problem size to 10 different ports and vessels with a capacity of up to 5000
TEUs.
Yifan, Ning, and Weijian (2016) employed a GA to assign 42 containers to a bay. The
algorithm optimizes for overstowage, yard crane shifts and minimizes the total weight gap
between the planned containers and the optimal weight distribution. Zhang and Chung
Yee Lee (2016) also utilize a GA to optimize the stowage plan for two objectives each with
three sub­objectives: stability and number of re­handles (overstowage). The stability in­
cludes the GM, angle of list (the degree the ship is leaning to one side) and the trim value.
Both GA approaches do not include any lashing constraints or optimization. Another GA
was presented by Cohen et al. (2017) to solve both the Master­Bay problem and Stowage
planning problem. It included not only reefers, container stacking rules, trim, draft and line
of sight, but also the wind force. The wind force is not the lashing force, but this constraint
aims at reducing the total force applied on the stack and thus, the lashing force. Azevedo
et al. (2018) proposed an interesting GA approach by combining the quay crane schedul­
ing problem with the stowage planning problem to improve the overall solution for both
problems. However, the stowage planning step was simplified a lot, e.g. by assuming
that all containers have the same size and weight. Some stability constraints were con­
sidered, but not lashing.
Yifan, Ning, Xia, et al. (2017) published the first research approach in stowage planning
that utilizes AI methods, more specifically Reinforcement Learning. They trained an agent
to generate a stowage plan, optimize for a staircase shape sequence during loading and
minimize reshuffles and yard crane shifts. The test instances were very small with only
19 containers and the agent did not consider any lashing.

Parreño­Torres et al. (2021) tried to fill the gap between decomposition approaches that
can handle most real­life constraints and non­decomposition approaches, that solve the
CSPP. They developed an IP model which outperforms previous IP models in the litera­
ture and included 20­foot and 40­foot containers, container weights, stability constraints
and stacking constraints in different combinations. While this approach resembles real­
life applications more closely, it still lacks some complexity with more container formats,
and lashing is also not included.
As presented, the ability of models to solve more and more complete and therefore, com­
plex versions of the problem has increased over the years. Also, the examined problem
size has increased. But none of the presented literature include lashing predictions or
optimization thereof. Jensen and Ajspur (n.d.) include lashing forces in their revenue
management optimization model of a liner cargo flow network. But they do not compute
the lashing forces. They rather define a solution space of legal container combinations
for a given minimum and maximum GM. The container combinations are the length and
weight combinations of the containers and the maximum number of containers per com­
bination was calculated with a professional loading computer. The more containers of
one combination are loaded, the less containers can be loaded of another combination.
The solution space is a polytope with t­dimensions plus the GM dimension, where t is the
combinations of containers. Given this, the trade­off between different container combi­
nations is linear, which allows the integration into a linear programming model.
Chaemin Lee, M. Lee, and Shin (2021) were the first to include lashing violation predic­
tions as part of the stowage planning optimization. A multimodal DNN was trained to
predict the lashing force on each individual container. The network uses the ship’s con­
ditions GM, wind speed and roll angle as wells as the complete stowage plan as inputs
and predicts ten lashing force components for each slot, which are used to calculate the
lashing force. The lashing force is returned as a percentage value: if the lashing force is
higher than 100 %, the lashing force is too high and must be decreased by swapping con­
tainers. The multimodal network utilizes two dense blocks, one convolutional block and
one Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) block and merges their respective results. This re­
sult is run through a last dense block, which outputs the ten lashing components as final
result. The model is not integrated into the stowage optimization because it is used af­
ter the stowage planning tool has allocated the containers on deck. If the given container
configuration results in any lashing force being over 100 %, then the violating container(s)
are swapped with lighter ones and the new lashing forces are calculated.
The model was trained on over 100,000 stowage plans with varying ship conditions and
container weights. The scaled mean squared error (MSE) on the test set is 0.88. Scaled
means that the error is scaled to the lashing force unit. Since the lashing force is given in
percentage, the mean error is 0.88 % on the test set. Given a real­life example with 969
containers, the difference between the predicted lashing force and the true lashing force
is:
• greater than 10 %: 4 times (0.4 %)
• greater than 5 %: 16 times (1.7 %)
• greater than 1 %: 161 times (16.6 %)
The performance of this model will be used as benchmark in this thesis.
While the model performs arguably well, the approach has some drawbacks:

• The model does not predict the lashing force directly, but rather the 10 lashing force
components. Thus, an additional calculation step is needed and depending on the
complexity of this calculation, this takes some time. The calculation from the 10
lashing force components to the lashing force was not described in the publication.
• The whole stowage plan is needed to predict the lashing forces and the more con­
tainers the plan contains, the longer the prediction will take.
Neither the training time nor the inference time are stated in the publication. The infer­
ence time is important, because if the time to predict the lashing force is too long, the
deployment of the model into an optimization framework, in which the lashing violation is
calculated at each iteration, might not be sensible. Especially frameworks based on meta­
heuristics need thousands or more iterations to find a good solution and if each iteration
is slowed down to calculate the lashing force, the whole algorithm might take too long in
the end to be useful. An approach based on a single stack rather than a whole stowage
plan is interesting from a metaheuristics point of view because by calculating the lashing
violation on the fly, the algorithm can use this information to make better decisions and
not risk creating a plan, that must be changed at the end. Also, the algorithm can include
the lashing violation as an objective and minimize it overall. This is preferable for two
reasons: First, it gives room for multi­port optimization and short­notice changes in the
containers that must be loaded. For example, more containers are added or the weight
of the containers changes. Second, the lower the lashing forces are, the lower is the risk
of long­term damages to the lashing rods or the lashing bridge.
Therefore, the approach presented in this thesis will predict the lashing force directly from
a single stack of containers. This should increase the inference speed compared to the
presented model. But because the inference speed of the presented model is not known, it
will not be possible to validate this. The approach is novel and explicitly considers how the
prediction model can be integrated into an optimization framework. By giving an overview
over different AI methods and how well they are suited for this task, this will hopefully
inspire and enable new research of methods that actually use the lashing violation and
examine the impact this has on the performance of such methods.
Literature on lashing and container stacks mechanics
As with the stowage planning problem, the research on the mechanical behaviour of con­
tainer stacks and lashing has gotten increased attention in recent years. Kirkayak and
Suzuki (2008) started working on container stack dynamics with two stacked 20­foot con­
tainers and performed an experimental and numerical study. The same research direction
was followed by De Souza, Kirkayak, and Suzuki (2010). Over the next few years, the
experimental and numerical studies got more comprehensive and included more con­
tainers, stacks, numerical depth and finite­element (FE) analysis by the same authors
(see De Souza (2010), Kirkayak, De Souza, et al. (2011), De Souza, Kirkayak, Suzuki,
et al. (2012), De Souza, Kirkayak, Watanabe, et al. (2013)). None of these studies are
intended nor suited to be used in an optimization algorithm because of their speed, and
not even all include lashing.
Bulian et al. (2015) presented a simulation of vessel behaviour including cargo­related
failures and Moro et al. (2015) couple a non­linear FE analysis to look at the container
stacks in more detail with the simulation. Another non­linear FE model to calculate the
container and lashing forces was presented by Wolf, Eisen, and Wobig (2019) to deal with
the increasingly large container vessels and their lashing forces. Y. Chen et al. (2020)
developed a simplified procedure for the analysis of on­deck container stacks aimed for
on­board use. C. Li, Wang, J. Liu, and Cai (2021) continued the work on the dynamics of
container stacks first with a four­tier container stack and then with a seven­tier container

stack (see C. Li, Cai, and Wang (2021)). They focused on investigating different lashing
arrangements and their corresponding lashing force and dynamic displacement with ex­
perimental and numerical studies.
Yuan et al. (2018), C. Li, Wang, and Cai (2020) and C. Li, Wang, and J. Liu (2020) anal­

Background section for AI models
Before describing the results of each model that was trained, a brief overview over the
tested regression models will be given. As described by Gèron (2019):p. 8, regression is
the task ”to predict a target numeric value, [...], given a set of features [...]”. The utilized
models are all machine learning models. ”Machine learning (ML) is the field of study that
gives computers the ability to learn without being explicitly programmed.”1. As described
by NG (n.d.) in his course, machine learning is a subset of AI and the goal of AI is to
make ”computers act intelligently”. So, ML only includes the learning part of computers,
while AI also includes making decisions and for example acting on what the computer has

4.1
Random forest
A random forest is a collection of multiple decision trees. A decision tree consists of nodes
and depending on the exact algorithm, each node has two or more children. Scikit­learn
uses the Classification and Regression Tree (CART), which produces binary splits. At
each node, one feature and a threshold value for that feature are used to split the tree.
An example is provided in fig. 4.1, where a node asks if weightstack ≤100. Then, the
”true” path follows to the next node and the ”false” path follows to another node and each
could potentially have another ”question”. Nodes are being split recursively until either
(a) a predefined depth limit is reached or (b) a split does not improve the tree’s training
accuracy anymore. The instances that follow the path to a node are the associated in­
stances (”samples”) with that node and during training, the target value of these instances
are known. So, the average target value and the MSE (or any other loss function) of that
node can be calculated. The decision, which feature to use next for a split, is made by
searching for the feature and the threshold, that minimize the loss function the most. If no
split exists that reduces the loss function (or the depth limit is reached), a node is called a
leaf because it has no children. A prediction for a new instance is made by following the
path through the tree and returning the average target value of the associated instances
of the resulting leaf. Because the path can be followed, the results of decision trees and
therefore, all tree­based methods, are interpretable. Additionally, they are quite easy to
use and capable of capturing complex patterns, which makes them accurate predictors
for many problems. They also need little to no data preparation. For example, standard­
ization is not needed, which is important in the context of an optimization algorithm as this
step adds computation time. A data point is standardized by subtracting the mean of the
feature and dividing by the standard deviation. For a linear regression or NNs, this is an
important step. For a more detailed explanation on decision trees, the reader is referred
to Gèron (2019):p. 175­186.

In a random forest, each decision tree is trained slightly differently due to induced ran­
domness. The final prediction is the average of each single decision tree (also called
predictor), which makes the overall prediction better. Randomness can be induced by

• choosing the feature for a split from a random sub­set of all features
• choosing a random sub­set of the training set for each tree
• training each tree on different random sub­sets of the original training set with bag­
ging or pasting
Bagging is the selection with replacement, pasting is selection without replacement. This
means, when using bagging, some instances are included more than once. Because
decision trees are powerful and capable of capturing complex patterns, they can overfit
easily. Overfitting means, they fit the training data extremely well but as soon as they must
predict on a different data set with slightly different patterns, they perform poorly. In other
words, it does not generalize well. This is also called the variance and bias trade­off, as
described in Gèron (2019):p. 134. A high variance means that the model performs well
on the training set because it learned all the specialized patterns to capture the variances
in the training data. However, this usually comes at the cost of overfitting. A high bias
can be described as the model being biased towards a certain outcome, which reduces
the variance of the outcome. Usually, this means worse performance on the training set
but a better generalization capability. If the bias is too high, the model can also underfit,
which means it does not fit the training data well. Therefore, the right balance between
variance and bias must be found during training. For this, hyper­parameters exist which
define how each tree is grown. One of the most important ones is how deep each tree can
grow maximally. The deeper a tree, the higher is its variance. Others are how many data
points are needed in a node before it can split, how many leaves are allowed, how many
samples must be in a leaf and more. For a random forest, the described randomness can
be tuned additionally and also influences the variance­bias trade­off. When the training
and the results are explained in the next chapter, the hyper­parameters will be discussed
a bit more in detail.
Random forests can be improved with boosting, which will be explored next. As described
by Gèron (2019):p. 199, ”the general idea of most boosting methods is to train predictors
sequentially, each trying to correct its predecessor”.
4.2
AdaBoost
The AdaBooster used in Scikit­learn is based on Drucker (1997). Like for a Random For­
est, each tree is trained on a subset of the whole training set. Initially, each instance has
the same probability to be selected for training and the first tree is trained. Then, all in­
stances are used to make predictions and the error of these predictions is used to adjust
the weight with which each instance is selected for the next training set. The higher the
error of an instance, the higher the probability that this instance will be selected to be part
of the next training set. Each tree is trained like this and thus, the training set increas­
ingly consists of difficult instances the model must focus on. By calculating the average
loss of each tree, a prediction confidence can be assigned to each tree. To get the final
prediction, the trees that are more confident are weighted higher and the predictions are
combined using the weighted median.
4.3
XGBoost
XGBoost stands for Extreme Gradient Boosting. Gradient boosters do not improve each
new tree by tweaking the selection weights. They rather ”fit the new tree on the residual
errors made by the previous tree” (from Gèron (2019):p. 203). That means, the first tree
is trained as usual on the training set with the normal target values y. For the second tree,

new targets are calculated with y2 = y−α ˆy1, where ˆy1 is the prediction of the first tree and
α is the learning rate. Then, the third target values are calculated on the residual error of
the second tree and so on. The learning rate is needed to slow down learning because
otherwise these models overfit quickly as described by Brownlee (2016). XGBoost also
utilizes CARTs and offers a Scikit­learn API (Application Programming Interface), which
makes it easy to use. However, additional optimizations are implemented like using an
approximate algorithm for split finding instead of an exact greedy algorithm and optimiza­
tion of memory usage and cache access. These changes aim to increase scalability and
speed compared to other tree­based methods like presented before.
4.4
LightGBM
LightGBM stands for Light Gradient Boosting Machine and has a similar approach as
XGBoost, but differs in the details. As the name says, it aims at being a ”lighter” frame­
work to allow very fast training and inference speed while maintaining high accuracy for
many problems by reducing the number of data instances and the number of features.
The two techniques LightGBM uses for this are called Gradient­based One­Slide Sam­
pling (GOSS) and Exclusive Feature Bundling (EFB). GOSS decreases the sample size
at each iteration by randomly discarding instances with small gradients. This means,
”the training error for this instance is small and it is already well­trained” as described
by Ke et al. (2017):p. 3. GOSS also compensates for the change in the data distribu­
tion which happens after discarding some instances. GOSS is not used by default. EFB
bundles mutually exclusive features and thereby, reduces the feature space. Mutually ex­
clusive features are features that never take non­zero values simultaneously. EFB is used
by default. Furthermore, LightGBM grows trees leaf­wise and not level­wise and ”uses
histogram­based algorithms which put continuous features into discrete bins”2. Leaf­wise
means that one path is fully grown from the seed node down to the leaf before the next
path is grown. In level­wise growth, each depth level of the tree is fully grown before
opening the next level. Like XGBoost, it offers a scikit­learn API.

4.6
Probabilistic regression
A loss function like MAE or MSE tries to minimize the average error of each prediction,
which is sensible if one wants to predict as accurately as possible. With a loss function
like this, the model basically learns the average target value given a certain input. It is
also possible to shift the prediction intentionally and predict quantiles. In statistics, quan­
tiles are used to describe how many percent of data points of a distribution are greater
or smaller than the given quantile. The median is the 50 % quantile. Similarly, when the
learning of a model is shifted to learn quantiles, it learns the quantile boundary. It means,
the model is intentionally trained to predict a value higher or lower than the actual value
and depending on the chosen quantile, this value can be interpreted as probability, that
the real target value is below or above the predicted value. A popular loss function to train
a model like this is called ”pinball­loss” because of its shape (see fig. 4.3). The adapted
equation by Steinwart and Christmann (2011) can be seen in eq. (4.5), where ˆy is the
prediction and y the true value. With τ = 0.2 as in the graph, predictions smaller than
y generate a smaller loss than predictions greater than y. In the graph y is 0. A model
trained with the pinball loss tends to predict values below y as this generates on average
a lower loss and this is how the predictions are shifted. With τ = 0.2, chances are only 20
% that y will actually be below ˆy. If τ = 0.8 is selected, the graph is mirrored, predictions
above y are favored and chances are 80 % that y is below ˆy. An intuitive explanation for
the pinball loss is for example provided by Vandeput (2021).

5.3.1
GPU
A Graphical Processing Unit (GPU) is capable of doing much more parallel computations
than a CPU and therefore, is well suited to accelerate machine learning tasks. For ex­
ample, when training a NN, each instance in a batch can be calculated in parallel. CPUs
can do parallel computations as well, but their parallel computation power is much more
limited. If a GPU is available that supports CUDA, Keras automatically utilizes it. CUDA
is an API by NVIDIA to do calculations on one of their GPUs. To see if training the NN
on a GPU would bring any benefits, the same NN as before was trained on a NVIDIA
Tesla V100 with 16 GB of memory. The accuracy does not change, but in fact the train­
ing time increases to over 10 hours. The relative training time on the GPU to the CPU
is only reduced by increasing the batch size, which reduces the accuracy which in turn
would mean longer training to improve the accuracy again. As investigated by Lind and
Pantigoso (2019), a GPU only increases the speed of training for complex networks, es­
pecially if they are or include CNNs. On a simple model, the CPU can even outperform
the GPU, which is also the case here. A dense NN with four hidden layers may be bigger
than the Sealytix NN, but it is still not a complex model. After all, it has only approximately
159,000 trainable parameters, where for example a ResNet50 network by He et al. (2016)
has over 25,000,000 trainable parameters. ResNet is a famous network for image recog­
nition that won the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge 2015.
In table 5.14 the inference speed of the CPU trained model versus the GPU trained model
can be seen. The inference speed of the GPU is lower on small batch sizes and higher
on bigger batch sizes, which is an interesting behaviour. It seems like the GPU is faster in
starting the calculation, which is why it is faster on lower batch sizes. But once the CPU
is starting and can keep running calculations, it becomes faster than the GPU. A similar
effect as during training occurs, where the CPU outperforms the GPU because the model
is quite small. Contrary to the NN here, ResNet and networks like it do benefit from a GPU

5.8
Summary
So far, it has been shown that tree­based models are well suited to solve the lashing prob­
lem. They can be as accurate as the current Sealytix NN model but with a lower inference
time. Especially XGBoost and LightGBM are promising due to their accuracy and speed,
depending on the batch size. Furthermore, the NN can be improved significantly without
losing much inference speed and probabilistic regression provides an opportunity to make
even more information available to an optimization algorithm.
In the next two chapters, two ideas are pursued to possibly push the results even further.
The first idea is to use a clustering method to cluster the data into groups and then train a
model for each of those groups. The assumption is that each group will be more coherent
than the whole data set, which should make it easier for a model to learn patterns and
predict the lashing violation. This would allow shallower trees which are even faster than
the ones presented so far.
The second new approach is to combine a classification model with an ”exact” regression
model. The idea is to only use the slower regression model on instances where the ex­
act lashing violation is needed and on the other instances a simpler classification model,
which only must be predict whether the lashing violation is above or below a threshold.

Clustering
As could already be seen in fig. 3.2, the data seems to have clearly separable groups.
Clustering algorithms are unsupervised learning techniques as the algorithms find the
clusters by themselves without being told which data point belongs to which cluster. Per­
haps the most common clustering technique is the k­Means algorithm by Macqueen (1967)
which is also included in scikit­learn. It creates k clusters by initializing k centroids ran­
domly in the n­dimensional space of the data set. Iteratively, each data point is assigned
to the centroid it is closest to. Afterwards, the centroids position for each cluster are up­
dated and the process begins again. The algorithm stops (a) after a predefined number of
iterations or (b) when the cluster assignment and centroid positions converge. k is defined
by the user. For a more detailed description, see Gèron (2019):p. 238­249. However,
the algorithm does not perform well on clusters with ”varying size, different densities, or
non­spherical shapes”. As can be seen in fig. 3.2, all of which is the case for the H data
set after applying PCA.
DBSCAN is a clustering technique that finds regions of high density and thus, is able
to find clusters where k­Means has problems. DBSCAN works by counting for each in­
stance how many other instances are within a certain distance. If enough other instances
are within the specified distance, this instance is considered a ”core instance”. Core in­
stances are basically instances within regions of high density. A core instance and its
neighbours always belong to the same cluster. It is important to note, that a neighbour­
hood can consist of many core instances and therefore, a sequence of core instances
form a cluster. Instances that are not a core instance nor are in a neighbourhood of one
are considered anomalies. In Gèron (2019):pp. 255–258, a more detailed explanation
is provided. A problem with the algorithm is that it quickly consumes much RAM if not
properly optimized. This problem occurred on the H set with the scikit­learn adaptation of
DBSCAN as its time complexity is O(m2) with m being the number of instances and it was
not possible to create satisfying clusters. OPTICS is a similar density­based clustering
method like DBSCAN, also implemented in scikit­learn, and better suited for large data
sets. But, the clusters were also not satisfying because they consisted of many micro
clusters or clusters included data points that did not fit visually together. Another algo­
rithm that did not find satisfying clusters was a Gaussian Mixture model. As described
by Gèron (2019):p. 260, it ”assumes that the instances were generated from a mixture
of several Gaussian distributions whose parameters are unknown”. Instances from the
same distribution are of the same cluster. The algorithm typically finds ellipsoid­shaped
clusters. Some of the clusters in the H set seem to be ellipsoid­shaped, but overall it
did not work well because the grouped data points did not belong together visually or too
many or too little clusters were found.
The chosen method to perform the clustering is HDBSCAN by McInnes, Healy, and As­
tels (2017). It is an extension of DBSCAN and OPTICS. It builds a minimum spanning
tree, in which nodes (the data points) are connected witch each other based on the weight
of the edges. Simplified, the weight of an edge is the distance between the two nodes.
The edges are dropped until there is no lower weight edge that connects components,
where a component is a collection of close nodes. The resulting tree is used to build a
cluster hierarchy (see fig. 6.1). DBSCAN stops here and makes a hard vertical cut to de­
fine the final clusters. The distance at which the cut occurs is defined by the user. But in
HDBSCAN, each of the splits in the cluster hierarchy are merged such that no cluster has
less points in it than the minimum cluster size, which is defined by the user. This means
Application of AI methods to solve the lashing problem of stowage planning
41
==PAGE:56==
that a bigger cluster can possibly be split into two smaller clusters if they each are big
enough. The final clusters are selected by choosing the clusters in the hierarchy that are
the most stable. Stable clusters include many data points and stay together as a cluster
over a long distance before they possibly split into two sub­clusters. Also, if a parent clus­
ter is selected to be a final cluster, the descendants can not be selected anymore. If any
point is not in a cluster, it is declared as an anomaly like in DBSCAN. These extensions
enable HDBSCAN to find better and more versatile clusters. The Python package is also
better optimized and needs less RAM.

6.1
Clustering with HDBSCAN
The main parameter to be defined by the user in HDBSCAN is the minimum cluster size.
Another parameter is min_samples, which is a measure of how conservative the clustering
is. By default, it is equal to the minimum cluster size. The higher the value, the more
conservative is the clustering and the more points will be assigned as anomalies. Thus,
areas must be denser to be declared as a cluster. Setting min_samples to a small value
can result in a lot of micro clusters, which could all belong to the same bigger cluster. The
parameter cluster_selection_epsilon can be tuned to merge these micro clusters. The
higher the value, the more micro clusters are merged together. Here, the following values
were selected for the three parameters:
• min_cluster_size = 1500
• min_samples = 200
• cluster_selection_epsilon = 0.5

7
Classification and Regression
combination
Instead of using a regression model to predict the lashing violation as exactly as possible,
a classification model could be used to determine if the lashing violation is above or below
a certain threshold. This is generally an easier problem and it can be expected that the
model would be fast and accurate. As described in chapter 2, in the context of an optimiza­
tion algorithm the exact lashing violation is preferred because then the lashing violation
can also be minimized, which increases the flexibility for short­term changes. However,
for this it does not matter if the lashing violation is significantly below 1. For example, it
does not really matter if the lashing violation is 0.5 or 0.6. As long as it is known that the
value is far away from 1, it is good enough. To take advantage of a faster classification
model but still have the accuracy of a regression model, both models can be combined.
This can be achieved by using the classification model to reduce the number of instances
on which the regression model must make a prediction on. In algorithm 3 the general
procedure is described. The idea is, that with this approach the overall speed of a slow,
but accurate regression model can be increased. To test this, an XGBoost classification
model was used together with the new NN. XGBoost was chosen because it achieves a
high accuracy for the classification task, while LightGBM is overfitting strongly and only
achieves 95 % accuracy on the test set. Though as before, the LightGBM model is faster
on small batch sizes (see. fig. 7.1).
Algorithm 3: Classification and regression combination
1 Load the classification and regression model;
2 Classify(data);
3 data_for_regression = instances where the class == 1, so where the lashing violation
is above the threshold;
4 lashing violation = prediction of the regression model on data_for_regression;
The chosen threshold is 0.7 because then the instances that will be sorted out for the
regression model will have a lashing violation which is low enough that the accurate value
is not needed. The chosen hyper­parameters for the XGBoost classifier are:
• n_estimators = 10
• max_depth = 15
• eta = 0.4
A binary classification problem like this is easier than the regression because the model
only has to make one decision and can be overall less precise on how big the lashing viola­
tion actually is. Therefore, the model can be smaller compared to the XGBoost regression
model from section 5.2.3, and with less trees it is sensible to increase the learning rate.
In fig. 7.1, the inference speed of the classifier can be seen compared to the previously
presented regression models (xgb_0.7). On big batch sizes it is quite fast, but on smaller
batch sizes it is a bit slower actually. Its accuracy on the training set is over 99.9 % and
on the test set 99.7 %. In table 7.1, the average difference in inference speed of the com­
bined approach versus only using the NN can be seen. Negative values indicate that the
combined approach is slower. For each batch size the difference was averaged over 100

Discussion
In the previous chapters, different tree­based methods, a NN model, models for individual
clusters and a combined approach of classification and regression have been presented
to solve the lashing problem in stowage planning. However, one thing that has not been
examined is how feature engineering might be able to improve the performance. It might
be possible to generate new features out of the existing ones that help the models to find
patterns. An example could be to calculate the average height and weight of the neigh­
bouring container stacks for each instance. According to Sealytix, the neighbouring height
and weight do not influence the lashing violation of a stack. So this particular feature would
possibly not help, but it serves as an example of feature engineering. Depending on the
model, different engineered features have different effects on the prediction capability of
the model as explored by Heaton (2016). For example, tree­based methods require or
benefit from different engineered features than a NN. Systematic feature engineering has
shown great potential in various applications like in the winning entry of the KDD Cup
2010 by Yu et al. (2011). Similarly, the accuracy of the tree­based methods could im­
prove with new appropriate features, as their accuracy was lower than the NN. In the
context of an optimization algorithm this could be especially interesting for LightGBM, as
it is the fastest model on lower batch sizes but has a slightly lower accuracy than XGBoost.
Another method to improve the accuracy without any speed loss was already presented:
assigning the instances to a cluster before predicting the lashing violation proved to be a
good method to make the prediction easier for the model. However, the clusters found
are influenced by the way Sealytix generated and curated the data. For example, pure
20­foot container stacks that were generated on purpose. This does not mean that no
pure 20­foot container stacks are generated by an optimization tool. But it is an exam­
ple of how the generation step influences the available data set. It remains unclear how
transferable the clusters are to stacks purely generated by an optimization tool and if any
clusters are present at all. Due to the restrictions of the stacking rules it is not unlikely
that the stacks do indeed form clusters during the optimization. An improved data set is
needed to examine this. Thus, it remains an open question if this approach will be an
improvement in practice.
Additionally, the assignment step of an instance to a cluster takes extra computation time,
which increases the overall inference time of predicting the lashing violation. This was al­
ready discussed in detail at the end of section 6.2 and the KNN method provided by scikit­
learn to assign new instances to clusters does make the inference significantly slower.
With a faster assignment method this approach could become more interesting. One
possible alternative to the scikit­learn KNN implementation could be the faiss library by
Johnson, Douze, and Jégou (2017). It is developed by Facebook AI Research and ac­
cording to Adamczyk (2020), inference is 17 times faster on the classical MNIST data set.
Another possibility could be a graph based approximate nearest neighbour search like
DiskANN by Devvrit et al. (2019).
Hardware acceleration is a common way to increase training and inference speed in ma­
chine learning. Here, only for the NN the influence of using a GPU instead of a CPU was
tested. The use of a GPU did not provide any benefits, as the model seems to be too
small to take advantage of the massive parallel computation power GPUs provide. En­
abling the GPU for XGBoost produced an error which could not be fixed and for LightGBM
Application of AI methods to solve the lashing problem of stowage planning
55
==PAGE:70==
the necessary dependencies could not be installed. Therefore, the influence of a GPU
on the tree­based methods could not be evaluated, but it might have potential for further
speed improvements.
Probabilistic regression is an interesting opportunity for an optimization algorithm in the­
ory, because it provides the algorithm with additional information to make better decisions.
But a downside is, that multiple models are needed for this, which increases the inference
time significantly. Probably three models are needed: one normal regression model for
the actual prediction and two for the upper and lower probabilistic regression. Instead
of using the two complete models, another possibility could be to use the average differ­
ence of the upper and lower bounds to the true target value. This will be faster, but the
probabilistic regression will be less accurate and therefore, could leave out much infor­
mation. As could be seen in the visualizations, the difference to the true lashing violation
was small on average for LightGBM and the NN, but there were still many data points that
were much further away.
Finally, all the models and data handling in this thesis was written in Python and with
Python libraries like scikit­learn and Keras. Python was chosen because of its ease of
use and the readily available, well developed libraries for data science, AI and machine
learning. While these are some benefits of Python, other languages like C++ or Julia are
generally faster than Python. For this reason, some of the utilized libraries like XGBoost1
and LightGBM2 make much use of other languages. Looking at GitHub, 40­50 % of their
code is C++. Nonetheless, using Python as main language does influence the speed and
for example scikit­learn3 and Keras4 are more than 90 % Python. If the improvements in
inference speed from XGBoost or LightGBM models are not enough, then it might make
sense to look into another language. Julia could be especially interesting as they de­
scribe in their documentation, that it aims to be as easy to use as Python, as fast as C
and geared towards ”scientific and numerical computing”. The Julia community is growing
continuously and for example XGBoost offers a Julia API like the Python API. To build a
NN, Flux by Innes (2018) can be used, which is a library for machine learning and written
100 % in Julia.

Conclusion
Different AI methods were tested and introduced to successfully solve the lashing prob­
lem in stowage planning with a novel approach based on container stacks instead of a
whole stowage plan. While doing this, the context of optimization was important and the
models were evaluated in terms of speed, accuracy and possible other benefits for an
optimization algorithm. Simpler models like linear regression or a SVM are not suited to
solve the problem, but tree­based methods give a good accuracy and are generally faster
than the Sealytix NN. Especially XGBoost excels at the problem, as it provides accuracy
on the level of the Sealytix NN but is roughly 10x faster across small and big batch sizes.
LightGBM is an interesting alternative as it is even faster than XGBoost on small batch
sizes, but it is slightly less accurate and slower on big batch sizes. A deeper and wider
NN with improved hyper­parameter selection can push the accuracy much further without
much loss in inference speed compared to the Sealytix NN. Two more approaches were
tested to push the results even further.
First, clustering the data before doing the lashing prediction with a dedicated model for
each cluster is a promising approach. While maintaining a similar inference speed, each
dedicated model is more accurate than the single model. It is also possible to increase
the inference speed by reducing the size of the models, while keeping the accuracy level
of the single model. But, the speed increase is only significant on batch sizes greater than
1000 and it does not include the assignment step to a cluster. Additionally, the question
if any clusters are presented in the data during a real optimization remains open.
Second, to exploit the low inference speed of XGBoost with the high accuracy of the NN,
a combined approach of a XGBoost classification model and the NN regression model
was tested. Indeed, the combined approach is faster on bigger batch sizes, but slower
on small batch sizes.
With the limitations and open questions of these two new approaches, XGBoost or Light­
GBM seem to be the best models to solve the lashing problem, depending on the batch
sizes and the accuracy requirements.
The work done in this thesis provides the basis for interesting topics for future research. In
terms of clustering, the prospect of a faster KNN methods might make the approach more
interesting from the inference speed point of view. Second, the examination of clusters
during an actual optimization algorithm will reveal if the approach is viable practically. For
this, a new data set is needed with container stacks generated only by an optimization al­
gorithm. Another topic for more research is the effect of utilizing a GPU for the tree­based
methods. For the NN it did not provide any benefits because the NN is not large enough
to benefit from the parallel computation power. For the tree­based methods, this could
be different. Not investigated here was feature engineering and how it might support the
models in finding patterns. Especially lighter models like LightGBM might gain accuracy
with additional or different features while maintaining their speed advantage. More re­
search in this area could open up other opportunities. Finally, the presented analysis was
done isolated from an optimization algorithm. For future research it would be interesting,
how an AI model is actually implemented into an optimization algorithm and what effects
this has on the results and the speed. Moreover, what batch sizes are realistic and optimal
and how much would an optimization algorithm benefit from more information given for
example by a probabilistic regression? Does knowing the quantile boundaries improve
the decision making in practice and has this any effect on the quality of the results? Is the
speed effect positive because better decisions can be made to arrive at a good solution
Application of AI methods to solve the lashing problem of stowage planning
57
==PAGE:72==
faster or do the additional models take too much time?
Answering these questions and implementing an AI model into an optimization algorithm
will provide new perspectives and pushes the research to be even more practically ori­
ented. The basis for more research in this area is given with the presented thesis, and
the question how to solve the lashing problem in stowage planning with AI methods has
been answered successfully. Now, it can be explored how solving the lashing problem
effects the optimization part in stowage planning.
