Abstract
The main objective of this thesis is to view the possibilities of weighing an object with gen-
eral collaborative robotic arms. Three methods are investigated and compared. The first
one is to derive a model and use the current/torque relationship in the motors to estimate
the forces at the end-effector. The second one is to use the available measurements of
a robotic arm; some robots include an internal model to estimate the forces at the end-
effector, which can be used to estimate the weight of an object while it is moved from A to
B. The third and final method is to test a force-torque sensor that can be easily integrated
at the end-effector of a robotic manipulator.
Linear calibration was implemented to improve the weighing estimations of each ap-
proach. Four paths are analyzed, where an optimal interval is chosen for weighing to
be performed. Both static and dynamic procedures are explored, and different accelera-
tion is tried out for the dynamic weighing. The three methods showed similar outcomes
for both the static and dynamic tests, but the modeling approach presented slightly better
results, while the force-torque sensor was not far behind.
Keywords — Collaborative Robots, Signal Processing, Force Torque Sensor, Robot Modeling,
Sensor Calibration.

1
Introduction
These days, food processors often use either static or dynamic scales to weigh food
products prior to processing steps in a food production line. The weight information is
essential to optimize raw material use and handling of the product.
The use of collaborative robots is becoming broader every year since they provide a way
for robotic arms to work alongside people safely and reliably. Force control allows them
to work in almost unknown environments by sensing excess force applied to them. Well
implemented force control can therefore allow a robot to perform advanced or flexible
automation tasks. These force control methods are built upon force estimations or force
measurements at the surface, joints, or the end-effector of the robot. The use of those
estimates/measurements can be further extended to estimate the weight of an object
being held or transferred by the robotic manipulator.
1.1
Weight Estimation on Robotic Manipulators
Weight estimation of objects using a Collaborative robot has been performed before,
mainly through force measurements or estimations. Universal robots have an example
on their website [1] where the UR3e, UR5e, and UR10e models are used to estimate the
weight of an object using measurements from the embedded force-torque sensor avail-
able in the e-series models. The weight is found by dividing the force by the gravitational
acceleration. The results of the experiments show only accuracy of 0.196 to 0.31.
Few studies have performed contact force estimations at the end-effector, which is closely
related to weight estimations through the end effector. In [2] the author uses a force es-
timation method based on the assumption of a constant current-torque relationship. The
torques were estimated using a model of the manipulator derived by the author, based
on the measured currents, and by using the position, velocity, and acceleration to esti-
mate the generalized momentum. The difference between the estimated torques and the
expected torques in the joints indicated the presence and magnitude of contact forces.
This method requires a detailed model of the robot, which is not always available from the
manufacturer. The author derived three different models where the difference lay in the
mass distribution in the links of the manipulator and compared them in order to get the
best estimation. The result showed that forces larger than 10 N could be detected.

The authors in [3] proposed a way to estimate forces using modeling. Similar to [2], the
UR5 was modeled in the format of generalized momentum and the dynamic parameters
identified without using external sensors. The current measurements were optimized us-
ing a mode-switching moving average based on a variable time period. A Kalman Filter
was used to estimate the forces and torques using the optimized currents. A Classical
Kalman Filter was compared to Adaptive Kalman Filter, and the Adaptive Kalman Fil-
ter showed better performance as it was able to utilize measured values over a shorter
timeframe upon recognition of an external wrench and could thus, determine the noise
characteristics more precisely.
A new method to estimate external forces without force sensors based on an identified
model together with a built-in torque sensor was developed in [4]. Experiments were car-
ried out on the Kinova Jaco2 robot manipulator to verify the method’s effectiveness. The
PSO algorithm developed in the paper was compared with other intelligence algorithms.
The PSO algorithm gave the best result. The experiment demonstrated that the proposed
external force estimation method could successfully estimate the external force acting on
the end-effector of the robot manipulator with an RMS error of 0.7N.
1.2
Overview of Available Cobots
Today, many Cobot manufacturers offer different types of manipulators, such as Fanuc,
KUKA, Universal Robots, TechMan Robots, AUBO Robotics, ABB, Doosan Robotics,
DOBOT, Epson Robots, and Locus Robotics. The most common type of Cobot is the
6 degree of freedom Cobots. Degrees of freedom are the number of axes around which
the robot can move. To get an acceptable estimate of weight, ideally, a Cobot equipped
with sensors that are of use for the purpose, like force or torque sensors, would be really
useful. Today many Cobots are equipped with force-torque sensors either in the wrist or
joints. Being able to access direct Force/Torque measurements from an end effector or
joints would be beneficial to estimating the weight of an object. Still, those sensors are
not always available as a built-in feature of the Cobot, and they are expensive to add on.
An example of a Cobot is the LBR iiwa 7 R800 - 14 R820 from a company called KUKA
[5]. The LBR iiwa Cobot is equipped with highly sensitive force-torque sensors at each
joint. As opposed to other force-limited robots that read the current in their motor, the LBR
has sensors that detect micro impacts.
Another Cobot is the KR 5 SI from a company called MRK system [6]. KR 5 SI has

1.3
Goal of the Thesis
A UR5 industrial manipulator is used as a case study for weight estimation in this thesis.
This study aims to set up a test stand with a UR5 robotic manipulator and investigate the
feasibility of estimating the weight of an object statically and dynamically, where dynamical
weighing is performed during both constant velocity and accelerating movements. Three
different methods are implemented and compared as follows:
1. The first method is to model the dynamics of the UR5 manipulator and estimate the
weight at the end-effector by utilizing the current, position, velocity, and acceleration
measurements in the UR5.
2. The second method examines the already available force estimates at the end-
effector from the UR5 robot, accessible through the URscript.
3. The third and final method is to add a force-torque sensor to the UR5 manipulator.
Since the new versions of the Universal robots, the URe series, and Cobots from
other manufacturers already have embedded force-torque sensors, this thesis will
propose that as well as a solution.

Modeling for General Robotic Arms
This chapter demonstrates the concept of making a model for general industrial robots,
to estimate force at the end-effector. Section 2.1 includes the introduction and a short
description of the idea of modeling. It presents the basic concepts behind the modeling
theory and explains both rotation and translation. Section 2.2 presents the derivation of
the forward kinematics and how to model a kinematic chain. Section 2.3 introduces ve-
locity kinematics, a method to approximate the inertia tensors. The Jacobian is presented
along with the static force/torque equation. Section 2.4 includes the dynamic model equa-
tions based on the Euler-Lagrange method. Section 2.5 presents coulomb and viscous
friction models.
2.1
Modeling Method of a Robotic Manipulator
The modeling of manipulators can be broken down into step-by-step procedures. First,
the rigid motions and homogeneous transformations are defined to represent the posi-
tions and orientations of objects, along with translations between coordinate frames. To
understand the theory, it is important to have the basic concepts of modeling in mind.
2.1.1
Basic Concepts of Modeling Theory
Before diving into the theory behind modeling a manipulator, a few basic concepts are
necessary to have in mind and are based on [10] and [11]. Coordinate frames are rep-
resented with two parentheses, e.g., frame A is denoted as {A}. The axes of this frame
are denoted as xA, yA, and zA.
In robotics, there is a term called Pose Algebra used to determine the object’s position
and orientation relative to some coordinate system. The information obtained from this
pose algebra can be used to allow a robot to manipulate an object or avoid moving into
the object. A combination of translation and rotation is said to be the pose of an object.
To describe the pose of a rigid body in R3, there are six dimensions needed: Three to
define its position and three to describe the orientation. The pose of a coordinate frame
is represented as AξB which means that {B} is relative to {A}, in other words, AξB is
the vector that points from the origin of frame {A} to the origin of frame {B}. The world
coordinate frame is generally denoted as {o0x0y0z0} as in [11]. If two vectors are pointing

2.2
Kinematics
Kinematics is a term that describes the motion of a robotic arm. Forward kinematics is
about finding the pose of the end-effector (position and orientation) given the values of
the joint axes. The Denavit-Hartenberg convention is a method for attaching reference
frames to the links of a spatial kinematic chain and to create homogeneous transformation
matrices. The forward kinematics can therefore be derived using those matrices. In order
to derive the dynamic equations for the manipulator, the Euler-Lagrange method can be
used. To obtain forces at the end-effector, the geometrical Jacobian has to be obtained to
convert torques in the joints to forces at the end-frame. Additionally, unknown parameters
like friction have to be considered.
2.2.1

arm all the way to the end-effector. A robot arm consists of n joints and n + 1 links. The
joints are commonly numbered as i ∈[1 : n] and the links i ∈[0 : n]. Joints can be of a
different type in different robots, for example, prismatic or revolute. A joint variable qi is

2.3
Velocity Kinematics
While forward kinematics introduces a method for modeling a kinematic chain from the
base frame to the end-effector, velocity kinematics is about deriving the velocity relation-
ships, relating the linear and angular velocities of the end-effector to the joint velocities.
This is done by introducing the Jacobian matrix.

is only a 0.3 % chance that a single measurement will be outside that interval. The signal

5.1.2
Statistic Analysis of the Data
Since the results are built on statistic theory, it is worth describing the theory used to obtain
the results in the upcoming sections. For each of the 4 paths, there are 15 tests made on
3 weights and a no-load. This is in total 240 weighing tests. Let’s go over the estimation
methodology used to analyze the probability errors in the measurements.
5.1.2.1
Standard error
The standard deviation implies the spread of the data set. By following the 68-95-99 rule,
one standard deviation, σ, will guarantee that it is 68% likely the measured data will be
within the interval µ±σ. Two times the standard deviation, 2σ, will guarantee that it is 95%
likely that the measured value will be within the interval µ ± 2σ. Finally, it is 99.7% likely
that the measurements will be within the interval µ ± 3σ. A good overview of standard
deviation is given in 4.2.1. The following procedure is implemented to obtain the standard
error (SE). First, the mean value of each of the fifteen tests is calculated. Secondly, the
population means are then calculated by taking the mean value of the formerly fifteen
mean values. Lastly, the standard deviation of that is calculated. This standard deviation
is simply called the standard error.
5.1.3

called ”No Load.” as mentioned in section 5.1. Making measurements through each path
with no load attached at the end-effector is important for the model and UR5 estimation

are considerably lower than in the previous path, but the confidence intervals are quite
high. The movement of this path is clearly the most diverse in terms of positions, velocity,
and accelerations, so this method was expected to show the worst results. Regarding the
model results, the diverse movement will affect the estimations of ˆτm, and in this case,
estimating the inertia matrix D(q) more accurately could provide better estimations. The
UR5 shows the best overall results in this case. Again, creating a separate biasing method
for this path could provide better estimations. Therefore, based on these results, the UR5
method is preferred for this path, and it could differentiate objects in the weight interval of

6
Discussion
There are several types of robotic arms on the market, as was mentioned in the introduc-
tion. Therefore, the weighing results can vary between manipulators. Some have built-in
force-torque sensors in each joint, while others have only current measurements and/or
estimated force/torque in each joint available. The current measurements can be used
to estimate the forces in each joint. This thesis approached the relationship between the
current and the force as linear, which is the case for many DC motors but not most AC
motors. The UR5 robot used for the experiments has AC motors in each joint. Another
factor affecting the torque estimation is the friction, which was modeled using Coulomb
and Viscous friction models. The parameters for the motors are usually not available to
the public, which makes it more challenging to estimate the joint torques. In this case, a
force-torque sensor in each joint could be a benefit in obtaining the force in the joints. The
methods introduced in this thesis showed that it is possible to scale the current measure-
ments using the equation of a line to fit the joint torque estimation, but not perfectly. This
was described in the model verification section, 4.1.2. Furthermore, the joint torques can
be converted through the Geometrical Jacobian to obtain the forces at the end-effector
frame.
When an object is moved from A to B, the manipulator follows some path routines. The
path that is chosen for the robot can make a difference. It can be a benefit to choose
a specific optimal interval on the selected path between A and B that the robotic arm
follows every time it moves back and forth. The force estimation procedure should be
implemented over that interval. The measurements can be done either statically or dy-
namically; it depends on the user’s choice. In chapter 5, four paths have already been
analyzed and compared. Both static and dynamic measurements were made in linear
and joint space, estimations with different velocities and acceleration were compared,
and bias correction was implemented. The target acceleration affects the inertia matrix
in the equation of motion directly. If the acceleration is zero, this matrix will not affect
the force/torque outcome. Therefore, it can be positive for the results to choose high ac-
celeration to exclude the inertia matrix from the force estimation process if the dynamic
weighing is implemented. There are three methods to choose from; the weighing can be
made through a user-made model, the weighing can be done through the internal sen-
78
Product weight estimation using robotic arms
==PAGE:93==
sors and model implemented inside the robotic arm, or the weighing can be done with a
force-torque sensor which is attached at the end-effector.
The reason for weighing should also be considered. Suppose the weighing is used to
distinguish between characteristics of objects through their weight. That is possible if the
weight range between the objects is greater than the confidence interval CI indicates.
The confidence intervals are chosen to be three times the standard error, and it will be
guaranteed that about 99.7% of the measurements will be in-between that interval. That
preference depends on the user, a 95% or ever 68% confidence interval might suffice for
certain applications, which would increase the usability of the methods described in this
thesis. Additionally, according to this thesis, if an accurate weighing is to be performed,
the results will always have some error, which differs between methods. The confidence
intervals and the standard errors are shown in the tables in section 5.2.
6.1
Future Work
One significant restriction in implementing the UR5 modeling was the unavailability of
parameters that describe the motors of the UR5. Suppose these parameters are available
for the user. In that case, the modeling can be made more precise, i.e., the torque of the
AC motors could be estimated dynamically instead of linearly.
When estimating the equation of motion that describes the UR5 joint torques without fric-
tion and external effects, only the target joint torque values are available. Those target
values do not represent the actual joint torques. A robot with torque measurements in the
joints could erase a lot of trouble from the process. The UR5 from the c-series was used
for this thesis due to its availability. A newer e-series model could be more beneficial to
use due to the fact that it has force-torque sensors embedded at the end-effector. The
force-torque sensor used in this thesis is an older and more inaccurate version. There do
also exist plenty of other types of manipulators that do include force-torque sensors in the
joints.
The estimation of the inertia matrix in the equation of motion was lacking, as seen in the
model verification process. A further investigation and tuning of those parameters should
be performed to correct those errors.
As mentioned in [2], an optimization algorithm could be investigated to estimate the friction
model parameters together with the mapping between the measured currents and the
Product weight estimation using robotic arms
79
==PAGE:94==
modeled torques.
System identification methods could also be investigated further to identify the parameters
of the equation of motion. That could potentially be an easier route to results for the joint
torques rather than modeling the UR5 from scratch, as the modeling is a delicate process
where the smallest errors can throw the estimation off.
The environment that a robotic arm works in can significantly impact weighing results.
Common environmental effects are wind, water, dirt, cold air, and hot air. This thesis did
not cover any experiments on the environmental impact on the robot, which could be a
good idea to do.
The Kalman filter is a robust tool that has proven its capability in the scientific world. As
mentioned in the introduction, the authors in [14] showed that the Adaptive Kalman Filter
gives a better force estimation outcome. The paper used the Kalman Filter to conduct
contact force and torque estimation with the optimized current. The Filter could replace
the calibration process in section 5.1.4 because it’s an optimization algorithm, and it could
be used to do that process automatically.
The weighing experiment was implemented in this thesis, so the end-effector was always
pointed down toward the earth. It could be interesting to investigate the weighing method
when the end-effector is oriented in another direction, such as 90 degrees or 45 degrees
away from the z-axis. This method was explored in small details in [1], using a force-
torque sensor.

Conclusion
The results of the three methods showed that it is possible to estimate the weight of ob-
jects using a collaborative robotic arm. The modeling method gave the best overall results.
It had the lowest error of all the three methods for all the four paths investigated. That
showed that the bias correction worked well for that method. The method was selected
as the best result for three out of four paths. The UR5 method showed a consistent error
between the mean values and the actual weights. That could indicate that the bias cor-
rection method should be improved. This method was chosen as the best result for one
out of four paths. The force-torque sensor method had slightly less errors between the
mean values and the actual weights than the UR5 method. It had the lowest overall confi-
dence intervals, which confirms that it has the best precision. Similar to the UR5 method,
the bias corrections should be improved to try to compensate for the errors. The results
are dependent on the paths that the robot follows. Optimizing the paths while consider-
ing factors like velocity and acceleration should be considered. Minimizing factors like
accelerating movements while measuring the weight of an object will benefit the weight
estimation. A good overview of the final results can be seen in tables 5.12 and 5.13.
