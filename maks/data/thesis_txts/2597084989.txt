Abstract
The perception of waste is changing from something to dispose of, thus causing loss of re-
sources in the waste management system, to focusing on the recyclable resources in the
waste, that can lead to substitution of virgin raw materials and a reduction of environ-
mental impacts and losses of resources.
The waste management system in Denmark is
also changing from focusing on incineration to focusing on recycling, thus reducing the
loss of resources by reducing the incineration processes. The 98 municipalities in Denmark
have their own waste management system with sorting criteria on how to collect house-
hold waste, which subfractions are accepted and how the conditions of the waste should
be. Therefore, there is a potential of 98 different waste management systems and sorting
criteria. The aim of this thesis was to analyze and compare the sorting criteria between
the municipalities to estimate whether there was a standardization in the sorting criteria,
including a comparison with standardized sorting criteria found in literature. The focus
of this thesis was on the fractions from the Danish Government’s 50% recycling target and
the plan on a climate-neutral waste sector. Data on the sorting criteria in the municipalities
were extracted from sorting guides, household waste regulations and information online.
The aim of the thesis was partially met because the sorting criteria were analyzed but only
for 10 municipalities. The 10 municipalities were chosen to represent municipalities with
different populations and placements across Denmark. The sorting criteria of the 10 munic-
ipalities were examined and analyzed. A comparison between the analyzed data showed,
that the municipalities varied in collected fractions, including the number of fractions, how
they were collected, either source separated or commingled, and differences in the accep-
tance of some subfractions and some applied conditions. Additionally, it was discovered,
that conditions varied among the fractions and they could vary within the individual mu-
nicipality because of different sources of information. Compared with standardized sorting
criteria from the literature, some municipalities would have to change their acceptance of
subfractions and the applied conditions. It was concluded that there was no standardiza-
tion in naming or composition of some of the fractions. Moreover, it was concluded, that
ﬁve fractions had to be collected in one or more municipalities, where one municipality did
not have to implement collection of any of the fractions to achieve the fractions decided by
the Danish Government. Furthermore, two municipalities have to phase out the collection
of a MGP fraction by 2025. It was recommended to the Danish EPA to establish a standard-
ization in naming of fractions and subfractions. Moreover, it was recommended that the
standardized pictographs from literature should be evaluated if they have to be established.
Furthermore, it was recommended, that the municipalities produce simple sorting criteria
and that they include the sorting criteria in all sources of information.

Sammenfatning
Opfattelsen af affald skifter fra noget, som man skal skille sig af med, hvilket forårsager tab
af ressourcer i affaldssystemer, til at fokusere på de genanvendelige ressourcer i affaldet,
hvilket kan medføre substitution af jomfruelige råstoffer samt reducering af miljømæssige
effekter og tab af ressourcer. Affaldssystemer i Danmark forandres også fra at fokusere på
forbrænding til at fokusere på genanvendelse og derved reducere tab af ressourcer ved at
reducere forbrændingsprocessen. De 98 kommuner i Danmark har hver deres affaldssys-
tem med sorteringskriterier om hvordan affaldet skal sorteres, hvilke emner er accepteret
og hvilke krav, der er til affaldet. Derved er der potentielt 98 forskellige affaldssystemer
og sorteringskriterier. Formålet med dette speciale var at analysere og sammenligne sorter-
ingskriterierne mellem kommunerne for at estimere om, der er en standardisering i sorter-
ingskriterierne, inklusiv en sammenligning med standardiseret sorteringskriterier fundet i
litteraturen. Fokusset i dette speciale var på de genanvendelige fraktioner fra Regeringens
50% genanvendelsesmål og planen om en klimaneutral affaldssektor. Data angående sor-
teringskriterierne fra kommunerne blev udvundet fra sorteringsguider, husholdningsregu-
lativer og informationer på nettet. Formålet i dette speciale blev delvist opnået, da sorter-
ingskriterierne blev analyseret, men dog kun for 10 kommuner. De 10 kommuner var valgt
til at repræsentere kommuner med forskellige indbyggertal og placeringer i Danmark. Sor-
teringskriterier for de 10 kommuner blev studeret og analyseret. En sammenligning mellem
det analyserede data fra kommunerne viste, at kommunerne varierede i indsamlede frak-
tioner, inklusivt antallet af fraktioner, hvordan de blev indsamlet, enten via kildesortering
eller kildeopdeling, og forskellene mellem accepteringen af visse emner og krav. Derudover
blev det afsløret, at kravene varierede mellem fraktionerne og de kunne variere indenfor
den samme kommune på grund af forskellige informationskilder. Sammenlignet med stan-
dardiseret sorteringskriterier fra litteraturen, skal nogle af kommuner ændre deres accept af
emner og krav. Det blev konkluderet, at der ikke var en standardisering i navngivningen af
nogle af fraktionerne. Derudover blev det konkluderet, at fem fraktioner skal indsamles i en
eller ﬂere kommuner, hvor kun én kommune skal ikke implementere indsamling af nogle af
fraktionerne, for at opnå fraktionerne bestemt af Regeringen. Endvidere to kommuner skal
udfase indsamlingen af en MGP fraktion inden 2025. Det blev anbefalet til Miljøstyrelsen
at etablere en standardisering af navngivningen af fraktioner og emner. Derudover blev det
anbefalet at de standardiserede piktogrammer fra litteraturen skal evalueres, hvis de skal
blive etableret. Endvidere blev det anbefalet, at kommunerne laver simple sorteringskri-
terier og at de inkluderer sorteringskriterierne i alle kilder.

Preface
This report is written as the ﬁnal Master thesis by master student Mette Vesterskov Hansen
as a part of a Master of Science degree in Environmental Engineering from DTU Environ-
ment, Department of Environmental Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark.
The report was carried out under the supervision of Senior Researcher at DTU Environment
Anders Damgaard and Professor at DTU Environment Thomas Fruergaard Astrup. The re-
port corresponds to 32.5 ECTS credits. The report was written in the time period from the
6th of January 2020 to 24th of July 2020.
The thesis revolves around the sorting criteria for household waste in the waste manage-
ment system for the Danish municipalities. The topic of the Master thesis was chosen be-
cause the waste management system in Denmark has gone through changes in the recent
years as Denmark is changing from a country with incineration as the main focus to recy-
cling to prevent losses of resources. There is currently limited information on the speciﬁc
sorting criteria for household waste in the municipalities with an overview on whether
there is a standardization in the sorting criteria on waste fractions, accepted and not ac-
cepted subfractions, conditions applied for the waste fractions and the difference in sources
of information for the conditions.
I would like to thank my supervisors, Anders Damgaard and Thomas Fruergaard Astrup,
for guidance and support throughout my thesis work. A special thanks to Anders Damgaard
for guidance and encouragement through supervisor meeting an especially through the on-
line supervisor meetings in the challenging times of the COVID-19 pandemic.
Last but not least, I would like to thank Michael, Lærke and Ann for the support and feed-
back, they have given me during the writing process of my Master thesis.

Glossary
Collection arrangement The collection of household waste in either a collection system or
a drop off system (Danish: Indsamlingsordning)
Commingled Two or more material fractions collected together in a waste fraction e.g., a
commingled Paper/cardboard fraction (Danish: Kildeopdeling)
Drop off Collection arrangement where citizens bring and drop off waste in public contain-
ers or at recycling centers (Danish: Bringeordning)
Household collection Collection arrangement where waste is collected at or near house-
holds (Danish: Henteordning)
Household waste Waste generated and collected from households
Material fraction Sub-classiﬁcation of waste according to the material for example paper,
glass, metal, plastic, wood and cardboard
Missorting Waste is not sorted correctly, e.g., a milk carton is disposed off in the paper
waste fraction (Danish: Fejlsortering)
Municipal waste Household waste and waste similar to household waste in composition
from other sectors such as food services, retail, health services and education
Organic waste Biodegradable part of household waste consisting of avoidable and unavoid-
able food waste. Used to describe biodegradable waste from municipalities named
either "biowaste" (Danish: Bioaffald), "food waste" (Danish: Madaffald) or "organic
waste" (Danish: Organisk affald)
Packaging Product used for storage, handling, protection, delivery and presentation of any
type of product made out of any kind of material (Danish: Emballage)
Residual waste The remaining of the household waste, when the recyclable waste fractions
and organic waste have been removed (Danish: Restaffald)
Secondary raw material Materials that are recycled and replaces virgin raw materials
Subfraction Material fraction divided into a subfraction e.g., for paper it is envelopes, mag-
azines, newspapers, printing paper etc. (Danish: Emner)
Source separate A material fraction collected by itself as a waste fraction e.g., Paper fraction
(Danish: Kildesortering)
Virgin raw material Material extracted in its raw form from the Earth such as metal ores
Waste fraction Fraction of a source separated material fraction e.g., Paper or a commingled
fraction with two or more material fractions e.g, Paper/cardboard
Waste type For example household waste, bulky waste, garden waste etc.

1
Introduction
"Waste is a left-over, a redundant product or material of no or marginal
value for the owner and which the owner wants to discard"
Thomas H. Christensen
(Christensen, 2011)
Every day, resources are lost in the waste management systems because of factors such as
waste treatment, complexities of products and consumer behavior including lack of knowl-
edge on waste sorting as well as the perception of waste (European Environment Agency,
2019a). The resources are disposed of in the waste management system because of the
traditional economy model, linear economy, where raw materials are extracted and after
use disposed of.
The linear economy depends on the resources that the Earth provide
(Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2013).Currently, the human demand for resources lead to the
Earth’s resources being used 1.75 times faster than they can be renewed in the ecosystems,
thus there is a need of almost two whole Earth planets to fulﬁll the demand (Global Foot-
print Network, 2020). In the waste management system, both renewable and non-renewable
resources are lost because of how waste is perceived. Waste is perceived as something to dis-
pose of by either incineration or landﬁlling. The consequence of the perception is the contin-
ued need for extracting raw virgin materials and loss of resources. However, the perception
of waste is shifting towards focusing on the resources in waste, that can be recycled and
thereby reduce the need for raw virgin materials and the loss of resources, thus a circular
economy model (Regeringen, 2013; European Environment Agency, 2019a).
In Denmark, incineration has been the common practice in the waste management system
for disposal of waste for many years. The incineration practice has resulted in approximately
80% of household waste have been combusted within the last decade (Regeringen, 2013). In
2018, 51% of the generated household waste was incinerated (Madsen et al., 2020). Thus,
recyclable resources in the household waste are lost because of the incineration. To reduce
the loss of recyclable resources, the Danish Government has issued several strategies over
the last seven years such as a Resource strategy, a Circular economy strategy and a Climate
plan for a green waste sector and circular economy (Regeringen, 2013; Regeringen, 2018b;
Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2020a). Some of the initiatives in the strategies are a recycling
target of 50% for household waste for 2022, creation of standardized pictographs and sort-
ing criteria for the waste fractions collected at households and sectors with waste similar
to household waste (Regeringen, 2013; Regeringen, 2018b; Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet,
2020a).
Currently, all of the 98 municipalities in Denmark have their own waste management sys-
tem with own sorting criteria, thus the citizens might have to adjust to the speciﬁc sorting
criteria in the municipalities. By standardizing the sorting criteria, all of the municipalities
will collect the same waste fraction, making it easier for the citizens. Additionally, by creat-
ing standardized sorting criteria, there is a potential of collecting a higher quantity of wastes
leading to a higher recycling rate and thereby a reduced loss of resources. The standardized
1
==PAGE:19==
sorting criteria should be made with inputs from e.g., the waste management companies,
the industry, municipalities and citizens. Some studies have analyzed the sorting guides
for the Danish municipalities to create the standardized sorting criteria. However, in-depth
data on the sorting criteria in regards to the waste fractions, subfractions and conditions,
including the sources of information for the conditions, have not been published before. A
project by Dansk Affaldsforening (2018) focused on creating standardized sorting criteria
for waste fractions collected at household and recycling centers. Åstrøm-Andersen et al.
(2019a) focused on how recyclable wastes were affected by sorting criteria, where sorting
and treatment facilities were interviewed to analyze the potential of sorting criteria. Fur-
thermore, Dansk Affaldsforening (2019) created standardized sorting criteria for 12 waste
fractions collected at households by gathering information from the municipalities’ sorting
guides and through workshops with the waste management companies, municipalities and
the industry. On June 16th 2020 the Danish Government decided, that 10 waste fractions
from households are to be collected in all the municipalities, standardized pictographs and
sorting criteria have to be used for the fractions (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2020a). The
ﬁnal standardized sorting criteria for the 10 waste fractions have not been published yet.

Theoretical background
In this chapter, the theoretical background in relation to resources and circular economy
including targets in the European Union and Denmark will be presented. Moreover, in-
formation on the composition of household waste and household waste fractions from the
recycling target in Denmark will be described. Finally, literature on the standardization of
the sorting criteria in Denmark will be reviewed and presented.
2.1
Resources and economies
Globally, the human population has doubled, the resource extraction has more than tripled
and the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) has quadrupled in the last 50 years (Oberle et al.,
2019). To meet the human demand for resources the global extraction of resources (biomass,
fossil fuels, metals and non-metallic minerals) has increased from 27 billion tonnes in 1970 to
92 billion tonnes in 2017. The demand for resources with resource extraction and processes
over the last 20 years have had negative impacts on environment impacts such as being
responsible for more than 50% of the climate change impacts and over 90% of the water
stress and loss of biodiversity (Oberle et al., 2019).
The continuous resource demand is because of the linear consumption in the linear economy
model, that has been the traditional economic model since the industrialization. In the lin-
ear economy model, also known as the "take-make-waste" model, virgin raw materials are
extracted, then manufactured into products, the products are used and after use the prod-
ucts are considered as waste and disposed of by either landﬁlling or incineration. These two
types of waste disposal result in both renewable and non-renewable resources being lost
because of limited or no recovery of resources (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2013; Beaulieu
et al., 2015; Guldmann, 2016). Non-renewable resources are ﬁnite resources with a limited
supply because they cannot regenerate fast enough to meet the demand and consumption.
If the consumption of non-renewable resources continuous as in the linear economy with
no recovery, there will be an increase in environmental impacts and extraction of resources
resulting in depletion of non-renewable resources over time (Guldmann, 2016; Oberle et al.,
2019).
To ensure the resources from products are no longer lost, the perception of waste has to
change from something to dispose of to a resource that has the ability to be reused or recy-
cled (Regeringen, 2013). The change in perception of waste is related to the transition from
the traditional linear economy model to an alternative economy model: circular economy
(Beaulieu et al., 2015). More waste is generated when the economy is doing well; therefore,
a decoupling of the GDP and the consumption of resources is needed to ensure that the
economic growth can continue with using less resources (Regeringen, 2013; Beaulieu et al.,
2015). To reach a sustainable future the well-being of humans and the economic activities
have to be decoupled from the environmental impacts and the use of resources (Oberle et al.,
3
==PAGE:21==
2019). The concepts of the circular economy are e.g., toxic chemicals are eliminated, renew-
able energy sources are used and waste is eliminated by design (Ellen MacArthur Founda-
tion, 2013). The resources are in a closed material loop for as long as possible resulting in a
limited loss of both renewable and non-renewable resources (Guldmann, 2016). The circular
economy will ideally result in reduction or removal of the need for extraction of virgin raw
materials and a reduction of the energy and emissions that would otherwise occur because
of the extraction and processing of raw virgin materials (Guldmann, 2016). The purpose
of recycling materials is to substitute raw virgin materials, the recycled materials are also
known as secondary raw materials (European Union, 2018a).
To close the material loop in the circular economy, the quality of the secondary raw materials
have to be similar or the same in the new product as in the original product (Eriksen et al.,
2019). However; if the functionality and the quality of the secondary raw material is reduced
compared to the original material, the recycling is considered to be "downcycling" (Ellen
MacArthur Foundation, 2013; Eriksen et al., 2019). For example White paper can be recycled
approximately 7 times as the same quality white paper, if the white paper is recycled into
newspaper, the paper materials and the functionality of the newspaper is reduced to a lower
quality and cannot be recycled as white paper again. The newspaper can only be recycled as
newspaper or downcycled to card stock etc. (Neidel and Kromann, 2019). When materials
are recycled to a lower quality, it is not possible to reach high quality materials by recycling;
therefore, secondary raw materials of low quality cannot substitute high quality materials
(Eriksen et al., 2019). An example of downcycling is the recycling of printing paper to toilet
paper, the paper materials in the toilet paper and the functionality goes from a high quality
to a low quality. When materials are recycled to a low quality, it is not possible to reach high
quality materials by using the low quality materials; therefore, secondary raw materials of
low quality cannot substitute high quality materials (Eriksen et al., 2019).
Figure 1 illustrates the traditional linear economy in the middle of the ﬁgure and the cir-
cular economy consisting of two systems: a biological nutrients system (the biosphere)
on the left and a technical nutrients system (the technosphere) on the right. The smaller
the circle, the more of the product value is preserved and the savings of e.g., energy, re-
sources, greenhouse gas emissions are higher. For the technical system, the materials are
typically non-biodegradable and non-renewable e.g., metals and plastics. Most of the prod-
uct value is preserved, when the product is maintained, then reused/redistributed, refur-
bished/remanufactured and lastly recycled. However, for recycling it is the value of the
materials that is preserved because the value of the product is lost (Ellen MacArthur Foun-
dation, 2013). For the biological system, the materials are biodegradable and renewable. The
most product value is preserved when the products are cascaded for different use and gen-
erating continued value, then when the materials are converted into fuels and chemicals in
a bio-reﬁnery and lastly when the materials are digested anaerobically or composted to pro-
duce nutrients, which can be used in the biosphere (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2013). The
nutrients from the digestate or compost can be used as e.g., fertilizer in agriculture, where
especially organic farmers are in the need of nutrients (Pedersen et al., 2016).

2.2
Waste hierarchy and circular economy in the EU
In Europe, approximately 7-10% of the generated waste in Europe is municipal waste, that
covers household waste (HHW) and waste similar to HHW from other sources e.g., waste
from food services, education, health services, retail (European Union, 2018a). The waste
stream in municipal waste and HHW consists of heterogeneous materials, meaning the com-
position of the waste is mixed and complex, which can inﬂuence the quality of the secondary
raw materials because of a number of issues e.g., impurities (European Union, 2018a; Erik-
sen et al., 2019).
In the Waste Framework Directive from 2008, the European Commission (EC) made targets
for reuse and recycling of waste and adopted a waste hierarchy consisting of ﬁve prioritized
steps to protect the environment by management of waste and prevention of waste genera-
tion (European Union, 2008). The Member States should implement separate collection for
at least paper, glass, plastic and metal by 2015 (European Union, 2008). For waste material
fractions from HHW and waste streams similar to HHW such as paper, glass, metal and
plastic, the preparation for recuse and recycling should increase to a minimum of 50% by
weight by 2020. (European Union, 2008).
5
==PAGE:23==
A prioritized waste hierarchy is illustrated by an upside down pyramid, where the highest
priority is the top of the pyramid (the green step) and the lowest priority is the bottom of the
pyramid (the red step) is illustrated on Figure 2. The highest priority is prevention of waste,
followed by preparing for reuse where the product’s function and value are maintained when
the product is reused. Next is recycling, where the waste materials are processed into raw
materials and can be used in production of new products. Second to last priority in the
pyramid is (energy) recovery where the waste is for example incinerated, and the recovered
energy can substitute other materials such as fossil fuels. Lastly on the prioritized pyramid
is disposal of waste by methods such as landﬁlling, where the materials are lost (European
Union, 2008; Bourguignon, 2016).
Figure 2: Waste hierarchy with the highest priority is the green step prevention at the top and the lowest
priority is the red step at the bottom disposal of the upside down pyramid (Bourguignon, 2016)
A circular economy in the European Union (EU) is expected to e.g., create new jobs, boost
Europe’s economy and competitiveness by ensuring resources while reducing the environ-
mental impacts. The circular economy should beneﬁt both the business, the environment
and ensure that there will be resources for future generations. Some of the measures, the
EU are taking to transition towards a circular economy, includes: reuse and recycling of
products, waste prevention and ecodesign (European Commission, 2015).
The European Commission adopted, in December 2015, a Circular Economy Action Plan that
would support and accelerate the transitioning towards establishing a circular economy in
the EU. The Action plan covered actions towards the whole life cycle of products to close
the material loop and develop markets for the recycled materials: secondary raw materials
(European Environment Agency, 2019b; European Commission, 2020). The Action Plan was
completed in 2019 with a total of 54 actions (European Commission, 2020). In addition to
the Action Plan, the EU amended the following six waste directives in 2018: Batteries and
Accumulators Directive, End-of-life-vehicles Directive, Landﬁll Directive, Packaging and Packaging
Waste Directive, Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive and Waste Framework Direc-
tive (European Environment Agency, 2019b). The amended directives set ambitious targets
6
==PAGE:24==
and clariﬁed or established rules to implement a circular economy in Europe, some of the
targets from the Waste Framework Directive:
• Implementation of either separate collection or separation and recycling at source for
bio-waste by 2023 (European Union, 2018a)
• Municipal waste prepared for reuse and recycling shall reach a minimum of 55% by
2025, 60% by 2030 and 65% by 2035 (by weight) (European Union, 2018a)
• To meet the 2030 target of halving food waste set by the United Nations (UN) in the
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the Member States should aim at reaching the
EU target of reducing food waste of 30% by 2025 and 50% by 2030 (European Union,
2018a)
• Implementation of separate collection of textiles and household hazardous waste by
2025 (European Union, 2018a)
Bio-waste is deﬁned by the European Commission as "biodegradable garden and park waste,
food and kitchen waste from households, ofﬁces, restaurants, wholesale, canteens, caterers
and retail premises and comparable waste from food processing plants" (European Union,
2018a).
In addition to the targets from the Waste Framework Directive, the amended Packaging and
Packaging Waste Directive set new targets for the minimum recycling of speciﬁc packaging
materials. Packaging is deﬁned as a product used for storage, handling, protection, delivery
and presentation, the packaging is any type of product made out of any kind of material.
Packaging consists of either of the three types:
• Primary packaging or sales packaging: Packaging that holds the product to the ﬁnal
user e.g., plastic wrapping around magazines (European Union, 1994)
• Secondary packaging or grouped packaging: Packaging outside the primary packag-
ing or packaging that gather products e.g., gathering primary packaging products. The
products is not affected, when the secondary packaging is removed (European Union,
1994)
• Tertiary packaging or transport packaging: Packaging can be used to prevent damages
because of transportation or physical handling of products (European Union, 1994)
The new targets in the amended Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive were made because
they would lead to an increase in the environmental, social and economic beneﬁts (European
Union, 2018b). The metal packaging was separated into two metal waste factions: ferrous
metals and aluminum. The separation was done to increase the amount of aluminum that
would be recycled, which will result in a reduction of CO2 emissions and increase in energy
savings (European Union, 2018b). The minimum recycling targets for packaging waste for

2.3
Resource strategy in Denmark
Because many resources are lost when the waste is incinerated, the Danish Government is-
sued a resource strategy Denmark without Waste, Recycle more - incinerate less in 2013. The re-
source strategy focuses on shifting the waste treatment from incineration to recycling. With
more waste being recycled, more materials will be circulated back into the economic cycle.
Other focus areas of the resource strategy are to decouple the environmental impacts and
the economic growth to reduce the environmental impacts from waste and ensure there is a
high quality in recycling. The resource strategy consists of several focus areas with targets
or expected results. The resource strategy has set a recycling target for HHW to increase the
recycling of materials in HHW:
• 50% of the generated HHW for seven material fractions (organic waste, cardboard,
paper, glass, metal, plastic and wood) should be recycled in 2022 (Regeringen, 2013)
The target is based on the European Commission’s target in the Waste Framework Directive
from 2008, a minimum of 50% (by weight) of HHW such as paper, glass, plastic and metal
should be recycled by 2020 (European Union, 2008). The Danish Government additionally
8
==PAGE:26==
included cardboard, wood and organic waste to their 2022 target (Regeringen, 2013). The
recycling target is ambitious because the Danish Government want to more than double
the recycling of the seven waste fraction in HHW, in 2011 22% of the HHW fractions were
recycled (Regeringen, 2013).
The municipalities have the main responsibility for the generated waste, HHW especially,
and it is municipality companies who own the majority of the incineration plants in Den-
mark. Therefore, to reach the recycling target set by the Danish Government, the munici-
palities are an important factor. Each municipality must decide itself how to organize the
collection and treatment of waste and which services are provided in its waste management
(Regeringen, 2013). The recycling target is national; therefore, it is expected that all of the
municipalities participate and organize their waste management system to help achieve the
target (Justesen and Nielsen, 2014). The resource strategy was evaluated in 2016 to check
up on the targets and expected results in the different focus areas. One of the initiatives
to increase the recycling of HHW was to establish "kommunepuljen", where projects and
knowledge could be shared between the municipalities. The evaluation stated that 49% of
HHW will be recycled in 2022 based on increasing recycling rates and initiatives in the mu-
nicipalities. Based on the evaluation the 50% target will almost be reached (Rambøll, 2017).
2.4
Municipal waste and composition of household waste in Denmark
In Denmark, the common practice for waste treatment was disposal by landﬁlling until the
1990’s. Several initiatives such as a landﬁll tax and a total ban of biodegradable waste sent
to landﬁlls in 1997 have resulted in a reduction of HHW sent to landﬁlls from 33% in 1985
to 4% in 2008 (Kjær, 2013). Since the 1990’s, incineration has been the common practice
for waste treatment. The beneﬁts of incineration includes reducing the quantity of waste
and generating energy recovery (e.g., electricity and heat) from the waste and consequently
substituting the production of energy from for example fossil fuels (Hulgaard and Vehlow,
2011; Kjær, 2013). Approximately 80% of the generated HHW has been incinerated over the
last centuries in Denmark (Regeringen, 2013).
Denmark is the country in Europe, who produces the highest amount of municipal waste
per citizens, which Denmark has been since 2011. In 2018, the amount of municipal waste
per citizens was 816 kg. Municipal waste consists of both HHW and waste resembling
HHW from other sectors. Majority of the Danish municipal waste originate from house-
holds, where the largest share of the municipal waste consists of residual waste (Madsen et
al., 2020). The method for calculating the recycling rate for the municipal waste is changed
from 2020, to make up how much of the collected municipal waste that is actually recycled.
Previously, the recycling rate was based on how how much municipal waste, that was col-
lected for recycling. The actual recycling rate is based on the municipal waste that is actually
recycled after it has been sorting and impurities etc., have been removed from the recyclable
municipal waste (Madsen et al., 2020). In 2018, 50% of the municipal waste was collected
for recycling, based on the new calculation method and rates of loss, the estimated actual
9

recycling rate was 42% (Madsen et al., 2020).

mark. With a total generation of HHW equivalent to approximately 3,489,000 tonnes, where

a population of approximately 5.7 million people and a generation of 3,489,000 tonnes of

2.5
Collection of source separated and commingled material fractions
The collection arrangement for waste is either a collection system or a drop off system de-
cided by the individual municipality. The collection system can be at households e.g., a
full-service collection or curbside collection either individual or joint. The drop off system
are e.g., public collection points in containers or recycling centers, where the citizens bring
the waste to the public containers or recycling centers away from their household (Nilsson
and Christensen, 2011). The municipalities have to implement collection arrangement for
the following waste fractions of HHW:
• Paper waste in settlements with over 1000 citizens (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet,
2012)
• Cardboard waste including cardboard packaging in settlements with over 1000 citizens
(Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2012)
• Glass packaging waste in settlements with over 2000 households (Miljø- og Føde-
vareministeriet, 2012)
• Recyclable metal packaging waste (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2012)
• Recyclable plastic packaging waste (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2012)
The glass, metal and plastic waste are packaging waste according to the deﬁnition given by
European Union, 1994. The glass, metal and plastic packaging wastes will in the remaining
of the thesis be described as their material fraction, thus glass packaging waste is described
as glass or glass waste, metal packaging waste is described as metal or metal waste and
plastic packaging waste is described as plastic or plastic waste.
The waste is either source separated, where each material fraction is collected individually
in its own bin/container or its own compartment of the bin, or commingled, where two
or more material fractions are collected together in the same bin/container or in the same
compartment of the bin (Neidel and Kromann, 2019). The reason behind choosing to source
separate or commingle material fractions differs between the municipalities, some munici-
palities focuses on the economy, others on the citizens should have it as easy as possible and
others focuses on getting source separated materials (REFA, 2017).
Additionally, material fractions such as metal and plastic can also be collected in the resid-
ual waste and be separated at a facility for recycling afterwards; however, the quality of
metal separated from residual waste is reduced compared to source separated metal be-
cause of missorting, impurities etc. For the plastic, some researchers claim that the quality
is the same whether the plastic is source separated or separated from residual waste, while
other researches claim that the quality of plastic in residual waste is reduced compared to
source separated plastic (Fredenslund et al., 2019). The collection of metal and plastic in
the residual waste will not be assessed in the thesis because residual waste was not of the
focus of the thesis. For household collection the material fractions can be collected in bins or
containers with 1-compartment, 2-compartment or 4-compartments (Mønsted et al., 2019).
12

and containers, for both household collection and drop off, are either above-ground, partly
above-ground or underground (Nilsson, 2011). For collection of HHW some municipalities
handle the collection themselves, other municipalities form an intermunicipal waste collec-
tion company and other municipalities put the collection of waste out to tender (Mønsted
et al., 2019).

The beneﬁts for the environmental impacts are highest when material fractions are source
separated, one of the reasons is because the recycling of metal and plastic are of a greater
extent resulting in positive impacts on e.g., greenhouse gases (Kromann et al., 2019). Source
separation of one material fraction can have an inﬂuence on other waste fractions. For ex-
ample implementation of source separation of plastic results in a greater extent of sorted
organic waste or when the quantity of organic waste is reduced in residual waste, the quan-
tity of other fraction decrease in the residual waste as the fractions are being sorted out from
the residual waste (Petersen et al., 2016; COWI, 2017a).
2.6
The seven HHW fractions for recycling target in Denmark
This section describes the seven recyclable material fractions, organic waste, paper, card-
board, glass, metal, plastic and wood, from the 50% recycling target for HHW in the re-
source strategy "Denmark without Waste" by the Danish Government set in 2013 (Regerin-
gen, 2013).
2.6.1
Organic waste
A way towards achieving the 50% recycling target for HHW is to implement collection of
organic waste for treatment (Justesen and Nielsen, 2014). Organic waste from households
is biodegradable waste consisting of avoidable food waste, which is food that could have
been consumed such as leftovers, and unavoidable food waste, which is food that is not
suitable for consumption such as egg shells (Pedersen et al., 2016). The organic waste can be
13
==PAGE:31==
collected from households for biogas production or composting purposes, the citizens can
also home compost the organic waste. If the organic waste is not collected at households or
home composted, it will be disposed off in the residual waste, which is incinerated (Lerche
et al., 2018).
To ensure the nutrients in the organic waste are not lost because of incineration, the organic
waste can be treated to produce for example biogas or be composted at a composting facil-
ity. These two processes can create fertilizer, that can be used in the agriculture (Pedersen
et al., 2016). Organic waste collected from households (and the service sector) are treated in
eight pre-treatment facilities across Denmark, one of the eight facilities is public owned and
receives only organic waste from households (Werther et al., 2019). In pre-treated organic
waste, the quantity of contaminants is correlated with the quality of sorting and the col-
lection of the organic waste (Pedersen et al., 2016). Impurities such as glass, metal, plastic,
rocks, dirt and gravel can occur in the organic waste because of missorting. These impuri-
ties can be difﬁcult to remove if, for example the plastic bag with the organic waste content
is torn into small pieces (Pedersen et al., 2016). Comparing the energy utilization from the
biogas production and the incineration of organic waste, it can be considered equal. How-
ever, there is a small environmental beneﬁt because the digestate can be used as fertilizer
on agricultural land, hence the phosphorus in the digestate returns to the biological system
which results in a reduction of resource consumption for production of mineral fertilizers
(Pedersen et al., 2016; Kromann et al., 2019).
The number of municipalities who have implemented collection of organic waste have in-
creased through the last couple of years. In 2015, 18 out of 98 municipalities had imple-
mented collection of organic waste, which was increased to 31 municipalities in 2017 and
again increased to 39 municipalities in 2019 (COWI, 2017d; Rothmann and Petersen, 2018;
Werther et al., 2019). On Zealand in 2016, 21 out of 45 municipalities had implemented
collection of organic waste at households with a quantity of approximately 40,000 tonnes.
There is a potential of 120,000 to 200,000 tonnes of organic waste if all of the 45 municipali-
ties on Zealand have implemented collection of organic waste by 2022 (Pedersen et al., 2016).
By including organic waste in the 50% recycling target set in the resource strategy, the Dan-
ish Government expected the collection of organic waste to increase from 50,000 tonnes in
2013 to up to 300,000 tonnes in 2022 (Regeringen, 2013). The Danish EPA expected approx-
imately 200.000 tonnes of organic waste to be collected from households in 2018 whereas
the actual collected quantity of organic waste from households in 2018 was approximately
95,000 tonnes (Kreilgård and Jørgensen, 2015; Madsen et al., 2020).
2.6.1.1
Bags and bins used for collection of organic waste
The quantity, quality and collection efﬁciency of the organic waste depend on distribution
of bags and the type of bag and bins used for collection of the organic waste. When bags are
distributed to the citizens for the collection of organic waste, the collection efﬁciency, quality
and quantity of the organic waste are higher than if the citizens have to buy and use their
14
==PAGE:32==
own bags. Furthermore, when the bags are distributed, there are less impurities and mis-
sorting in the organic waste; therefore, the quality of the organic waste is higher (Petersen
et al., 2016). For the citizens, the functionality of the bag is the primary preference, followed
by the bags’ signaling effect and lastly the design of the bag (Petersen et al., 2016). The func-
tionality of the bag is the primary preference because some citizens experience difﬁculties
with odor, leakage, tearing depending on some types of bag. These difﬁculties can result in
a reduction of the collection efﬁciency and quantity of the organic waste. Furthermore, the
quality of the organic waste is inﬂuenced by the signaling effect of the bags (Petersen et al.,
2016; COWI, 2017c).
The pre-treatment facilities receiving the organic waste from households differ in the type
of bags they want to receive the organic waste in. The facilities generally accept three types
of bags for the collection of organic waste: paper bags, bio bags and plastic bags. However,
one of the facilities accepts only organic waste collected in paper bags (COWI, 2017c). Ex-
amples of the three acceptable types of bags are illustrated on Figure 5, where the ﬁrst three
bags from the left are plastic bags, the fourth and ﬁfth bag from the left are bio bags and the
last bag from the left is a paper bag (COWI, 2017c). Bio bags can be either biodegradable or
non-biodegradable, and consist of 0% to 100% bio-based plastic, where the remaining per-
centages of the bio bags are made out of fossil-based plastic (COWI, 2017d). Some citizens
experience difﬁculties with the bio bags and paper bags such as leakage, tearing and odor,
which can cause a reduction of quantities of collected organic waste, additionally, some
citizens and garbage collectors experience problems with the bags getting stuck in the bin
(Petersen et al., 2016; COWI, 2017d). Some facilities prefer to receive organic waste collected
in plastic bags because the quantity of organic waste is higher after pre-treatment. The or-
ganic waste can intertwine with paper bags and bio bags, that causes the removal of the
organic waste with the bags, resulting in a lower quantity of organic waste going to the
biogas production. The treatment of the organic waste determines which type of bag the
facilities prefer or accept (COWI, 2017c).

plastic bags, the fourth and ﬁfth bag from the left are bio bags and the last bag from the left is a paper

After pre-treatment, the bio bags are frequently found in the biopulp compared to plastic
bags and paper bags, because the bio bags are torn into small pieces, that are difﬁcult to
remove from the biopulp. The second highest quantity of impurities in the biopulp are from

==PAGE:33==
plastic bags and lastly impurities from paper bags (COWI, 2017d). When organic waste is
collected using bio bags or paper bags, there is a low quantity in the biopulp of physical
impurities, such as plastic, glass, composite materials and metals, these impurities end up in
the organic waste because of missorting. The use of plastic bags for organic waste collection
result in the highest quantity of physical impurities in the biopulp (Petersen et al., 2016;
COWI, 2017d). At the agricultural ﬁelds, the paper bags will decompose over time, the bio
bags will not degrade at the biogas facility but will decompose over time at the ﬁelds and
the plastic bags will have a limited decomposition on the ﬁelds. Thus, there are both pros
and cons for all three types of bags (COWI, 2017c). In 2018, it was decided in the Order on
the use of waste for agricultural purposes by Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet (2018), that the limit
values for the biopulp and compost when used in the agriculture should be:
• Biopulp and compost: A weight based limit value of 0.5% weight percent per dry
weight for physical impurities larger than 2 mm (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2018)
• Biopulp: A weight based limit value of 0.15% weight percent per dry weight for plastic
larger than 2 mm (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2018)
• Biopulp: An area-based limit of 1 cm2 per percent of dry weight measured in 1 liter
biopulp for plastic larger than 2 mm (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2018)
Experiences from the Danish municipalities show, that the quantity of collected organic
waste differ depending on the type of bin used for collection. From single-family housing,
the organic waste is generally collected in a 1-compartment bin or a 2-compartment bin.
In the 2-compartment bin, the organic waste is typically in one of the compartments and
residual waste is in the other compartment. The municipalities where the organic waste is
collected in a 1-compartment bin collect on average 1.6 times as much organic waste than the
municipalities where the organic waste is collected in a 2-compartment bin (Rothmann and
Petersen, 2018). Additonally, the garbage collectors experience some problems with the 2-
compartment bins especially when bio bags have been used for the organic waste collection
resulting in more work to remove the bags from the bin (Petersen et al., 2016).
2.6.2
Paper, cardboard, and food and beverage cartons for recycling
In 2018, 167,000 tonnes of paper waste, including paper packaging, and 70,000 tonnes of
cardboard waste, including cardboard packaging, were collected from households (Mad-
sen et al., 2020). For paper, cardboard and card stock, the recycling rate is between 70-95%
(COWI, 2016b). In the future, it is expected that the quantity of paper collected for recycling
will decrease because of factors such as electronic mails, online newspapers etc., whereas it
is expected that the quantity of cardboard for recycling will increase because of online shop-
ping (COWI, 2016b; Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a). There is a potential of 32,000 tonnes of
used beverage cartons (UBC) from the Danish households, UBC has a recycling rate up to
90% in Germany and Sweden, whereas according to Vestforbrænding, the recycling rate is
16
==PAGE:34==
approximately 70% (COWI, 2016b; Knudsen, 2018; Jensen et al., 2019). At the present mo-
ment, Fredericia municipality is the only municipality, who collects food cartons and bev-
erage cartons at households, where in 2016, 14.2 tonnes of UBC was collected at recycling
centers and from households for recycling in Fredericia municipality (Knudsen, 2018). The
food and beverage cartons consist of food cartons such as pizza boxes and beverage cartons
such as milk cartons and juice cartons (COWI, 2016b).
There are two companies in the pulp and paper industry in Denmark, Brdr. Hartmann and
Skjern Paper, these companies receive the paper fraction. However, majority of cardboard,
paper and card stock collected from Danish households are sent to facilities in Sweden and
Germany (COWI, 2016b). At the present, there are no facilities in Denmark, who accept and
process beverage cartons and food cartons, thus these material fractions are sent to Swedish
and German facilities (COWI, 2016b). There are differences in the demand from companies
for example Skjern Paper demands non-contaminated and non-commingled material frac-
tions but commingled material fractions can be sold without any problems in the general
market (COWI, 2016b). Food scraps, coloring, coating and embossing on the materials are
accepted in facilities in Sweden and Germany, as they do not cause problems for the pro-
duction, while the facilities in Denmark want to avoid food scraps, some of the facilities
accept coloring, coating and embossing on the materials (COWI, 2016b). Food scraps can
cause problems in the Danish working environments where the waste is sorted manually.
However, neither foreign facilities, Fredericia municipality nor Dansk Affald, a company
who processes and market recyclable materials, have experienced problems in the working
environment (COWI, 2016b).
Paper and cardboard can be collected as source separated fractions, commingled together
in a waste fraction or commingled with other material fractions. Information differs regard-
ing whether the same quality of the paper and cardboard material fractions can be achieved
when paper and cardboard are commingled. Some experiences from Denmark and England
show that the quality of the paper fraction is reduced when it is commingled with other ma-
terial fractions. Other experiences from Denmark show that the quality is the same for the
paper and cardboard material fractions whether they are source separated or commingled,
because the commingled paper/cardboard fraction is treated at a sorting facility, where the
fraction is sorted into the two material fractions and impurities caused by missorting are re-
moved from the fractions (COWI, 2018; Neidel and Kromann, 2019). Thus the same quantity
of paper and cardboard are lost whether they are source separated or commingled (Neidel
and Kromann, 2019). Other experiences state that whether paper and cardboard should be
commingled or source separated depend on the collection and treatment of the other mate-
rial fractions collected in the municipality (COWI, 2018).
There is a market for all of the paper and cardboard based material fractions whether they
are source separated or commingled; however, the revenue of the fractions differ. Contam-
ination from organic materials cause a low revenue and a smaller market for sales (COWI,
2016b). The highest revenue is for a source separated paper fraction. To reach the highest
revenue for the paper fraction, the fraction may only contain 2-3% of cardboard based prod-
ucts (COWI, 2018). A smaller market for sales would be available if paper and cardboard
17
==PAGE:35==
are commingled together with beverage cartons. Additionally, the commingling creates a
potential for food contamination, that could create issues for the recycling of the cardboard
and paper fraction. However, when beverage cartons are commingled with a commingled
metal/plastic fraction, there would be no issue with food contamination and there would
be no loss in the quantity or quality of the metal and plastic material fractions (Neidel and
Kromann, 2019). The municipalities focus primarily on the practicality and service with re-
gard to the source separated or commingled fraction and not so much on the revenue or the
market of the material fractions (COWI, 2016b).
2.6.3
Glass fraction
Glass, especially beverage glass bottles, have been collected for reuse and recycling for over
100 years in Denmark (COWI, 2017b). A national deposit system for glass bottles was in-
troduced in 1942 in Denmark, because of resource scarcity in consequence of World Was II
(Carlsberg, 2018). Glass collected in the deposit system is not assessed in the thesis because
they are outside the scope. From households, 137,000 tonnes of glass packaging and 5 tonnes
of glass was collected for recycling in 2018 (Madsen et al., 2019). Glass waste is either reused
or recycled, it cannot be incinerated if tried the glass will turn up in the bottom ash. Com-
pared to glass production using virgin raw materials, there is a reduction of environmental
impacts, resource use and energy use when the glass is reused or recycled (Miljøstyrelsen,
2020a).
For many years, the collection arrangement for glass waste from households have been for
the citizens to bring the glass and drop it off in public containers or at the recycling cen-
ters. However, in recent years more municipalities have implemented a collection system at
households, where the glass waste is collected in bins (COWI, 2017b) The implementation
of collection at households have been done to increase the service provided for the citizens,
some places it is difﬁcult to set up public glass containers and to reach the recycling target of
50% set by the Danish Government (COWI, 2017b; Jørgensen et al., 2019). Collection of glass
waste at households has been implemented in 54 municipalities in 2019. Furthermore, 27 out
of the 54 municipalities also have a drop off system, where the citizens can drop off the glass
waste to in public containers. Some municipalities continue to have the implementation of
the drop off system because they want to decrease the number of bins at households (Jør-
gensen et al., 2019). The household collection system lead to approximately 2% of missorting
in the glass waste whereas in the drop off system, there is approximately 0.5% missorting in
the glass waste. Therefore, the quality of the glass waste is higher when it is being dropped
off. Additionally, the household collection system is more expensive than the drop off sys-
tem whereas the household collection provides a better service for the citizens (Jørgensen
et al., 2019). Municipalities, who have implemented both household collection and a drop
off system, collect a higher quantity of glass waste compared to municipalities who have
only one of the two collection systems (Jørgensen et al., 2019).
The glass is either source separated or commingled with one or more material fractions such
18
==PAGE:36==
as metal or metal and plastic when it is collected at households. In the drop off system, the
glass waste is generally source separated (COWI, 2017b). When the glass is collected in a
commingled fraction at households, the handling is rougher compared to source separated
glass because of the extra treatment in collection, transshipment, sorting etc., resulting in
more and smaller shards of glass. The rougher handling causes less glass to be used in re-
cycling of new glass products, the smaller shards of glass are instead used as for example
ﬁller. Because ﬁller has a decreased value compared to glass products, the glass is downcy-
cled and the quality of the recycling is reduced (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019b). The quality
of the glass is reduced with a higher degree of pulverization of the glass (Jensen et al., 2019).
In the drop off system of source separated glass, the quantity of whole bottles are 30-50%
of the total collected glass quantity compared to glass collected at households either source
separated or commingled with other material fraction results in 10-20% whole bottles of the
total collected glass quantity (COWI, 2017b). Therefore, the more rough handling of the
glass fraction collected at households result in a reduced quantity of whole glass bottles for
reuse (COWI, 2017b). The priority of collecting whole bottles for reuse, even if it is more in
agreement with the principle of circular economy, has decreased since the packaging fee was
halved in 2011. Because the packaging fee was halved, the incentive to collect whole bottles
disappeared, because the expenses for sending the glass fraction as shards to treatment are
lower than the expenses for the treatment of whole bottles. Sending the glass fraction as
shards is done in most municipalities; however, some sorting facilities prioritize reuse of
whole bottles by sorting them from the remaining glass waste (COWI, 2017b; Jørgensen et
al., 2019). Whole bottles are primarily sold to wine producing EU countries where the shards
of glass are recycled in Denmark (COWI, 2017b).
The glass fraction should not be commingled with material fractions of soft materials or ab-
sorbent materials such as textiles, paper and cardboard or soft plastic because these fractions
can be contaminated with the glass shards (COWI, 2017b). The recycling facility Reiling A/S
recommend the glass should only be source separated and not commingled with other frac-
tions such as metal or plastic because the materials can intertwine when the fractions are
commingled which can result in repeating sorting, thus increasing the expenses or losses
(Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a). When metal and glass are commingled in a waste fraction,
there is a potential of loss in the quantity of glass. The loss can be caused because of contam-
inants from the metal, where small pieces can cause issues in the glass recycling given that
the quantity of contaminants allowed a glass fraction for recycling is low. Additionally, the
loss can be caused because of the more rough handling resulting in small glass shards; how-
ever, the loss depends on the sorting facility (Neidel and Kromann, 2019; Åstrøm-Andersen
et al., 2019b).
In Denmark, 22 municipalities collect a commingled waste fraction of metal, glass and hard
plastic (MGP) (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019b). The handling of the commingled MGP frac-
tion is rough causing a high quantity of smaller glass shards because of more steps in the
treatment such as transfers and an extra sorting step, coarse sorting, where the materials
are separated in the sorting facilities. The rougher handling causes the quantity of glass
for recycling is reduced in the commingled MGP fraction compared to a source separated
19
==PAGE:37==
glass fraction (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019b). The quantity of contaminants such as chi-
naware, ceramics, plastic bags and missorting of glass waste are approximately 2-5% for
both the source separated glass faction and the commingled MGP fraction (COWI, 2017b).
Small pieces of hard plastic contaminate the quality of the glass for recycling. The sort-
ing facilities are experiencing difﬁculties in separating the small pieces of plastic from the
glass. There is a need for separation to reach the purity requirements from the glass facto-
ries to produce new glass packaging (Neidel and Kromann, 2019). The contaminants in the
glass fraction from the commingled MGP fraction causes the quality of the glass to not be
good enough to be sold as it. Thus to be sold, the sorted glass from the MGP fraction have
to be commingled with other glass waste with a lower quantity of contaminants (Åstrøm-
Andersen et al., 2019a). On the basis of the quality of the glass and plastic collected in the
MGP fraction is reduced compared to source separated, the MGP does not meet the require-
ments from the EU Commission in the Waste Directive to promote high quality recycling.
Therefore, the Danish Government has issued the municipalities to phase out of the MGP
fraction by 2025 (European Union, 2018a; Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2020a).
Contaminants such as chinaware, stoneware, ceramics, plastic bags, metal as well as mis-
sorting of cardboard and paper can occur in the glass fraction (COWI, 2017b). Chinaware,
stoneware and ceramics can be removed from the glass fraction at the sorting facilities, they
are recycled with a recycling rate of almost 100%, whereas the remaining of the contami-
nants and missorting are incinerated (COWI, 2017b; Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019b). Con-
tamination from food scraps are allowed in the glass waste as the treatment facilities can
handle the contamination. The glass waste containing food scraps will be left in a pile for
approximately three months, while microorganisms remove the food scraps from the glass;
therefore, the glass does not need to be cleaned or rinsed (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a).
The treatment facilities also accept lids on the glass packaging, thus the citizens do not have
to remove the lids before disposing of the glass waste (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a).
2.6.4
Metal
Metal waste collected for recycling from households was in 2018, 14,000 tonnes of metal
packaging and 125,000 tonnes of metal (Madsen et al., 2019). In addition to household col-
lection, the metal material fraction can also be brought to drop off in public containers and
recycling centers (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020b). Beverage cans have been a part of the deposit sys-
tem since 2002 because the packaging for beer and soft drinks became free to choice (Carls-
berg, 2018). The metal collected in the deposit system is not assessed in the thesis. Metal
collected at households is either source separated or commingled with one or more mate-
rial fractions such as glass, plastic, cardboard or polystyrene (Neidel and Kromann, 2019).
The sale price for metal is higher when it is source separated than commingled with other
material fractions (Mønsted et al., 2019).
The metal fraction consists of ferrous metals, for example iron or steel, and non-ferrous
metals, for example aluminum. The different types of metal are separated at the sorting fa-
20
==PAGE:38==
cility using treatments such as magnets before the metals are sold for recycling (Neidel and
Kromann, 2019). Recycling of metals can happen over and over again, thus resulting in re-
duction of energy and environmental impacts compared to the extraction, processing etc.
of virgin raw metal materials (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020b). When aluminum and steel are recy-
cled the energy savings are signiﬁcant compared to the production of virgin aluminum and
steel. For recycling of aluminum, approximately 5% of the energy is needed compared to
the production of virgin aluminum (Damgaard and Christensen, 2011). Metal disposed off
in the HHW, that is incinerated will turn up in the bottom ash, where it can be recovered
for recycling. However, the recovered metal is of a reduced quality compared to metal col-
lected for recycling (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020b). Hence the recovered metal is downcycled and
the quality of recycling is reduced. Metal waste from households might contain contami-
nants such as food scraps, the food scraps have to be scraped out of the metal waste for the
sorting facilities to handle the metal for recycling (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a).
The quality and quantity loss are the same for the metal whether it is source separated or
commingled with a glass fraction (Neidel and Kromann, 2019). When metal is commingled
with glass and plastic in the MGP fraction, the quality and quantity of the metal is the same
as the quality and quantity of the metal in a source separated fraction. However, if there is a
large quantity of soft plastic because of missorting in the MGP fraction, there is a potential
for the metal and soft plastic to intertwine, thus resulting in a loss of the quantity of metal
as the metal is removed with the rejected soft plastic (Neidel and Kromann, 2019; Åstrøm-
Andersen et al., 2019b).
2.6.5
Plastic
There is two types of plastics, a synthetic plastic, also known as conventional plastic where
the majority of the plastic is fossil-based, and a bio-based plastic, made out of 0-100% biomass.
There are no requirements for a minimum amount of biomass before a plastic can be called
bio-based plastic in Europe (Hann et al., 2020). Plastics can be either compostable, biodegrad-
able or non-biodegradable. Compostable plastic is primarily used in the products of bags
(Hann et al., 2020). Bio-based plastic is not necessarily compostable even though it can be
made out of biomass (COWI, 2017c). The different composition and biodegradability of
plastic are illustrated on Figure 6. Where the plastics on the top left is non-biodegradable
bio-based plastics, on the top right is biodegradable bio-based plastics, bottom left is non-
biodegradable fossil-based plastics and bottom right is biodegradable fossil-based plastics.
The abbreviations are the names for the different plastic types with the different proper-
ties (Hann et al., 2020). Biodegradable plastic can degrade to water and CO2 under speciﬁc
conditions (Hann et al., 2020).

large volume, more plastic is collected for recycling when the collection is frequent, other-
wise the citizens dispose of the plastic waste in the residual waste (COWI, 2017a; Svantesson
and Fjeldberg, 2017). There is tendency that the highest quantity of plastic is collected when
soft plastic and hard plastic is commingled and when the collection of the plastic waste is
frequent. Additionally, the highest quantity is collected when the municipalities have just
implemented the collection of plastic for recycling compared to municipalities who have
had a plastic collection arrangement for years (Svantesson and Fjeldberg, 2017).
Plastic products can be put in three categories according to their quality: high quality plas-
tic products, used for food packaging because they meet the legal requirements, medium
quality plastic products, used for pharmaceuticals, toys, electrical and electronics, and low
quality plastic products, used for non-food packaging, automotive, building, construction
and other products (Eriksen et al., 2019). When recycled, the secondary raw plastics can only
substitute their own level of quality or to a lower quality. Thus high quality plastics can sub-
stitute all three qualities (high, medium and low quality plastics), the medium plastics can
substitute medium and low quality plastics and the low quality plastics can only substitute
low quality plastic (Eriksen et al., 2019). If the low quality plastics turn up in the recycling
of high quality plastic, the quality of the recycled plastic will be reduced, hence it will be
downcycled. The quality of the secondary raw plastics can additional be reduced because
of impurities. There are four different types of impurities, which can occur in plastic waste,
chemical impurities and three physical impurities: missorting items, lower grade items, and
interfering items (Eriksen et al., 2019). An example of missorting items is the presence of a
lid made out of one plastic type on a product made out of another plastic type. Thus, the
plastic lid should be removed from the product to remove the impurity (Åstrøm-Andersen
et al., 2019b).

Standardization of waste sorting criteria in Denmark
Each of the 98 municipalities in Denmark decide their own sorting criteria for HHW, thus
which material fractions are accepted for collection, e.g., paper, cardboard and glass, if the
material fractions have to be source separated or commingled and which subfractions are
accepted and not accepted in the material fractions e.g., newspapers, magazines, ﬂiers etc.

Conditions to be applied to the fractions are also decided by each municipality such as
whether glass packaging should be rinsed or not, may have lids on or not etc. (Regerin-

HHW, the private waste treatment operators and collectors must adjust to the individual
municipalities and consequently the collection becomes more expensive and the scaling of
the operations are difﬁcult (Regeringen, 2018b).
2.7.1
Circular economy strategy in Denmark
In September 2018, The Danish Government adopted a circular economy strategy, Strategy
for Circular Economy, More value and better environment through design, consumption, and recy-
cling. The reason behind the circular economy strategy is the potential for the environment,
the competitiveness for the Danish companies and to contribute to the UN’s SDGs by transi-
tioning towards a circular economy. Achieving a circular economy in Denmark will result in
e.g., a reduction of CO2 emissions by 3-7%, reduce the use of virgin raw materials through
reused and recycling and increasing the GDP up to 45 billion Danish kroner by 2035. The
GDP will increase because of e.g., lowering costs on production and increasing revenues
(Regeringen, 2018c). The circular economy strategy consists of six focus areas with a total of
15 initiatives, where one of the initiatives is: Initiative 9: Promoting more harmonised collection
of household waste. The initiative focuses on producing a standardization on the waste sorting
criteria and collection of HHW across Denmark (Regeringen, 2018b). The standardization
criteria will be made in a cooperation between, among others, the Danish Government, busi-
ness communities, municipalities and professional organizations (Regeringen, 2018b). By
implementing the initiative and creating a more homogeneous collection of waste, the Dan-
ish Government wants to achieve a waste management system that is better environmen-
tally and generate economic proﬁts by making the waste collection more efﬁcient (Regerin-
gen, 2018b).

2.7.4
Standardized sorting criteria
A project was published focusing on how recyclable household wastes were affected by sort-
ing criteria through interviews of sorting and treatment facilities in February 2019 (Åstrøm-
Andersen et al., 2019a). The recyclable waste are organic waste, paper, cardboard, glass,
metal and plastic (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a). The interviewed sorting and treatment
28
==PAGE:46==
facilities believe that losses at recycling will be reduced if there is a standardization of the
sorting criteria for recyclable HHW. The highest priority for the facilities is, that the materials
in the waste do not cause problems for the environment, health or safety and do not cause
a reduction in the quality of the recycled material or causes damage to the facility. In the
project, interviewed customer centers identiﬁed that some citizens are confused by some of
the pictographs created by Dansk Affaldsforening (2018). The citizens are confused by the
pictographs for glass, organic waste and cardboard. The bottle for the glass fraction con-
fuses citizens because of the interpretation, that other types of glass such as jam jars are not
accepted in the glass fraction. The apple for organic waste confuses some citizens, because
they understand it as animal food waste is not accepted in the organic waste fraction, only
vegetable waste. Furthermore, the cardboard box for cardboard fraction causes confusion
because the cardboard box is understood as a moving box, while the sorting criteria might
write "small cardboard" (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a). There are some subfractions, that
the sorting and treatment facilities do not want. For examples they do not want to receive
egg trays in the cardboard fraction, given that egg trays are not very suitable for recycling
because of e.g., poor quality of ﬁbers, risk of food contamination from egg shells and the
glue used for the labels. Brdr. Hartmann, who produces egg trays in Denmark, stated that
egg trays can be disposed of through composting. Additionally, Reiling, a glass recycling fa-
cility, wants to keep glass waste source separated and states that glass waste can be scraped
clean and lids can be kept on to reduce impurities and water from entering the glass waste.
It was found that sorting criteria for recyclable wastes can either be simple, material based
(e.g., paper, glass, metal) and include a few examples of accepted subfractions, or very de-
tailed with examples of both accepted and not accepted subfractions, including rules on
separation and cleanliness of the wastes. A loss of recyclable waste or missorting can be
caused if citizens have to make a subjective estimate or need to have thorough knowledge
on the materials (Åstrøm-Andersen et al., 2019a). The project recommended to implement
standardized sorting criteria, that are material based and can include a few examples of not
accepted subfractions, such as PVC is not accepted in the plastic fraction.
As an offshoot of the pictographs system, Dansk Affaldsforening, KL and the Danish EPA
published a project in March 2019. The project was on standardizing sorting criteria, which
were based on workshops with the industry, waste management companies and munici-
palities (Dansk Affaldsforening, 2019). The standardized sorting criteria are for 12 material
fractions from households: organic waste, residual waste, paper, cardboard, glass, metal,
soft plastic, hard plastic, hazardous waste, textiles, small electronics and batteries. The stan-
dardized sorting criteria will ensure that the 12 fractions will have the same meaning across
Denmark. Furthermore, a standardized sorting criteria could lead to a higher quality of re-
cycling due to a more homogeneous waste composition (Dansk Affaldsforening, 2019). The
sorting guides for all 98 of the Danish municipalities were assessed as a baseline for the
workshops where differences between subfractions were identiﬁed. There was a total of
seven workshops with different topics. In the workshops some subfractions, that had dif-
fered between the municipalities, were discussed. The agreed standardized sorting criteria
are provided in Appendix D. In the plastic workshop, a Empty and the rest in the hair princi-
ple was agreed to be used as the cleanliness condition for the plastic fraction. In the glass

and metal workshop, a Empty or the rest in the hair principle was agreed to be used as the
cleanliness condition of the glass and metal fraction. The phrasing of the two principles dif-
fer from the two workshop, but the meaning is the same, that the glass, metal and plastic
fraction need to be emptied and or scraped clean but do not need to be washed or rinsed

accepted or not accepted in the fraction. The sources of information for the municipalities’
sorting criteria were placed in References sheet.

3.4
Uncertainties
During the working progress on the thesis, uncertainties were discovered in regards to the
literature and sorting criteria. The uncertainties are given below.
• The selection of the 10 municipalities might not be representative enough for the mu-
nicipalities in the region or municipalities with approximately the same population
• The assumption that there is no distinction between collection at single-family houses
and multi-family houses. The collection at multi-family houses can be in containers
where fractions are source separated, while at single-family houses, the fraction can
be source separated or commingling of two or more fractions. Additionally, at single-
family housing bins with 1-, 2-, or 4-compartments can be used
• Bulky waste was excluded from the thesis; however, some of the municipalities might
have collected one or more fractions as a part of bulky waste
• Information in either the HHW regulation, sorting guide or online might not be up-
dated according to the waste fractions that were collected in the municipality
• Whether the sorting criteria vary between the housing types within the municipality
• There is a potential that not all information on the sorting criteria from the individual
municipality was located
• Sources of information for the sorting criteria were not speciﬁed, for example the in-
formation from various sorting guides could be merged and describe as "information
from sorting guide"
• Names of waste fractions were based on information from sorting guides. However,
the names could have been described differently in the other sources of information in
the municipality
• The names of subfractions were written as overall names or commonly found names
for the speciﬁc subfractions, the speciﬁc names for the subfractions in the individual
municipality was written in footnotes
• The examples of subfractions might have varied depending on the source of infor-
mation. A distinction between the sources could have shown which information the
citizens will ﬁnd depending on the source
• Some subfractions were merged together to reduce the number of presented subfrac-
tions, thus they were speciﬁed in the footnotes
• There is a potential of mistranslated words
• The values used for the numbers, tables and ﬁgures regarding generation of household
waste and generation of municipal waste, composition of household waste, composi-
tion of residual waste and recycling rates

of the waste fractions shown in Table 3 are deﬁned by the individual municipality in the
information from sorting guides. However, for one of the fractions, the composition of the
fraction differed between the municipalities even though they have the same name. The
Plastic/metal waste fraction collected in Brønderslev and Copenhagen municipality consisted
of a commingled fraction of soft plastic, hard plastic and metal, whereas the Plastic/metal
waste fraction collected in Holstebro municipality was a commingled fraction consisting of
hard plastic and metal. Thus, the three municipalities had the same name for their waste
fractions, but the composition of the waste fraction differs. Additionally, there were munic-
ipalities who collected the same material fractions in a commingled fraction, but the name
of the waste fractions differed. Bornholm municipality collected a commingled paper and
cardboard fraction named Paper/cardboard, whereas in Næstved, Odense and Aarhus mu-
nicipality the commingled paper and cardboard fraction was named Paper/small cardboard.
Hence the four municipalities collected the same material fractions but the naming of the
waste fractions differed. Thus, for the 10 municipalities, there was no standardization in re-
gards to naming the fractions. Because there is no standardization, the citizens could cause
missorting in the particular fraction. Therefore, it would be beneﬁcial to establish a stan-
dardization the names of the fractions, to ensure that citizens will know in every municipal-
ity, what is accepted in the fraction based on the name.

fraction, Metal/EPS, and remaining seven waste fraction were source separated fractions.

material fraction was EPS, which was not collected in either of the other nine municipalities.

4.3
Conditions for the waste fractions
All 10 municipalities had stated conditions, that had to be applied before disposal. The
conditions were described for either individual subfractions or the overall waste fraction.
However, the conditions have been gathered to describe the conditions of overall waste
fraction and not for the speciﬁc subfractions. The conditions were described in the sorting
criteria of the waste fractions. Data on the conditions were found either in the municipality’s
sorting guides, HHW regulations or online on websites, either the municipality’s website or
the intermunicipal waste collection company’s website. However, there were no distinction
between the different websites and the sources were referred to as website or online in the
thesis. The HHW regulation described conditions for the individual material fractions; how-

There are both pros and cons for using either bio bags, paper bags and plastic bags for the
collection of organic waste. For example a con for bio bags is, that the bio bags are found
more frequently in the biopulp after pre-treatment than the plastic and paper bags (COWI,
2017d). A con for using bio bags and paper bags is a potential reduction of the quantity of
organic waste going to biogas production, because the organic waste can be removed with
the bio bags in the pre-treatment (COWI, 2017c). However, as the organic waste in Fredericia
municipality is composted, the paper bags will potentially not be removed before treatment
and therefore neither will the organic waste. A con for the plastic bags is the quantity of
impurities of other materials such as plastic, glass etc., occurring in the bio pulp is higher
than when bio bags or paper bags are used for collection of organic waste (COWI, 2017d). A
pro for using plastic bags is that they do not leak or tear as the bio bags and paper bags can
do because of liquids (COWI, 2017d). If the quantity of collected organic waste is reduced
because of the problems with the paper bag, it would be beneﬁcial to use paper bags that
can contain liquids and thus do not leak. For the ﬁve municipalities, who collect organic
waste for biogas production, there is currently no conclusion from the literature on which
type of bag is the best. It depends on what the municipalities value the most, whether it is
the functionality, signaling effect, design, cost etc. However, the impurities from the plastic
and physical impurities in the biopulp must be within the limit values decided by Miljø- og
Fødevareministeriet (2018) in the Order on the use of waste for agricultural purposes.

The principles used to describe the cleanliness for the plastic fraction and the glass and metal
fractions differed in the project on standardizing sorting criteria by Dansk Affaldsforening
(2019). The principle was phrased as Empty and the rest in the hair (Danish: Tøm og resten
i håret) in the plastic fraction and as Empty or the rest in the hair (Danish: Tøm eller resten i
håret) for the glass and metal fraction. Thus, the meaning of the principle differ between
the municipalities. The Empty and the rest in the hair can be interpreted as the plastic should
be emptied and the citizens should deliberately put what is left in the plastic waste in the
hair, while the Empty or the rest in the hair principle can be interpreted as, the plastic should
be emptied or the citizens should put the rest in the hair. The principle for the cleanliness
of the glass, metal and plastic fraction may have derived from the saying in the drinking
culture: Bottoms up or the rest in the hair (Danish: Bund eller resten i håret). The saying means,
that people have to ﬁnish their alcohol beverage or ﬂip it upside down and empty it out
on their hair. Thus, they have to empty the beverage so nothing will fall onto their hair
when they lift it upside down. Empty or the rest in the hair should be the principle to describe
the cleanliness of the glass, metal and plastic fraction because of the interpretation being
the best to describe the cleanliness conditions. Additionally, because the principle have
potentially derived from a saying from a drinking culture, it might not be all of the citizens,

• Minimum 60% by 2030 (European Union, 2018a)
• Minimum 65% by 2035 (European Union, 2018a)
As mentioned in Section 2.4, 50% of the municipal waste was collected for recycling in 2018

cannot be compared with the targets for municipal waste. Thus, the estimated actual recy-

have to increase by 13 percentages points to reach the minimum recycling target of 55% by
2025, increase by 18 percentages points to reach the minimum recycling target of 60% by
2030 and increase by 23 percentages points to reach the minimum recycling target of 65% by
2035.

who produce waste similar to HHW, will have to increase the collection of the municipal
waste. The largest share in collected municipal waste is residual waste, where households
contribute to the largest share of residual waste (Madsen et al., 2020). By implementing
the collection of the recyclable wastes set by the Danish Government in the municipalities,

Conclusions, Recommendations and Further Research
In this chapter, the conclusions from the thesis in regards to the aim and objectives will
be presented. Furthermore, recommendations based on the conclusions and report will be
given. Finally, further research, based on the literature and analysis, will be suggested.
5.1
Conclusions
The initial aim of the thesis was to analyze if there is a standardization in the sorting crite-
ria for the household waste collected in the Danish municipalities. The conclusions of the
objectives to reach the aim are given below.
• Research on resource strategies, targets to increase recycling of resources, recyclable
household waste fractions and standardization of wastes in literature
The European Commission and Danish Government have established strategies with targets
for wastes such as municipal, packaging and household waste to increase the recycling of re-
sources and transition towards a circular economy. In 2013, the Danish Government created
a recycling target of 50% by 2022 for household wastes such as organic waste, cardboard,
paper, glass, metal, plastic and wood. Standardized pictographs for the collection system
at recycling centers and households were published in 2018 and a project on standardized
sorting criteria was published in 2019. Furthermore, in 2020, the Danish Government de-
cided that all municipalities have to use the same pictographs and make the collection of
household wastes streamlined by having all the municipalities to implement collection of 10
fractions at or near households: organic waste, residual waste, paper, cardboard, food and
beverage cartons, glass, metal, plastic, hazardous waste and textiles. Furthermore, standard-
ized sorting criteria have to be applied to the household waste; however, it is not published
yet and will be ﬁnalized by the Danish EPA.
• Collection of data on the sorting criteria in the municipalities using sorting guides,
household waste regulations and information online
The objective is partially met, because the aim this thesis was to analyze the sorting criteria
of all 98 municipalities in Denmark; however, the sorting criteria was only analyzed for 10
municipalities. The reduction of municipalities was decided to achieve a comprehensive
and detailed analysis of the sorting criteria, where the 10 municipalities were chosen to
represent the different regions and populations in Denmark. The fractions of focus was
decided to be seven out of the 10 waste fractions decided by the Danish Government, thus
organic waste, paper, cardboard, glass, metal, plastic and food and beverage cartons. Six out
of these seven fractions were also from the 50% recycling target from 2013 (organic waste,
paper, cardboard, glass, metal and plastic).
82
==PAGE:100==
• Analyzing and comparing the sorting criteria of waste fractions in regards to fractions,
subfractions and conditions to investigate if there is a standardization in the sorting
criteria from the Danish municipalities including comparing the sorting criteria from
the municipalities with standardized sorting criteria from literature
In the analysis, it was discovered that the 10 municipality overall collected 15 waste frac-
tions. Some of the fractions were source separated material fractions and others were com-
mingled fractions consisting of two or more material fractions. Two out of the 10 munici-
palities have to phase out the collection of a MGP fraction by 2025, decided by the Danish
Government. Five fractions had to be collected in one or more municipality to achieve the
seven fractions of focus (organic waste, paper, cardboard, glass, metal, plastic and food and
beverage cartons), decided by the Danish Government. Only one out of the 10 municipali-
ties did not have to implement collection of a fractions decided by the Danish Government.
The municipalities had either examples of accepted subfractions or examples of accepted
and not accepted subfractions for the waste fractions. Some of the municipalities differed
in the subfractions, and for some subfractions, the acceptance differed within the individual
municipality. To achieve the standardized sorting criteria from the literature, some munic-
ipalities had to change their acceptance of the subfractions, while other subfractions deter-
mined in the municipalities were not deﬁned in the standardized sorting criteria from the
literature. All municipalities had conditions to be applied for the fractions. However, some
conditions varied between the municipalities and even within the individual municipality
depending on the source of information. To achieve the conditions in the standardized sort-
ing criteria from literature, the municipalities should either remove conditions or implement
the ones decided in the literature. During the analysis, it was discovered that there was no
standardization in naming the waste fractions, thus some municipalities collected the same
material fractions but had different names for the fractions, while other municipalities had
the same name for fractions that accepted different material fractions. Additionally, it was
established, that there was no standardization in the naming of the subfractions or the phras-
ing of the conditions.
• Evaluate standardized sorting criteria and pictographs from literature
A few issues were discovered in regards to the standardized sorting criteria and pictographs
found in the literature. The principle for the cleanliness condition for the glass, metal and
plastic have to be deﬁned correctly. Moreover, there were identiﬁed subfractions among
the 10 municipalities, that were not deﬁned in the literature. Additionally, there were some
inconsistencies in the deﬁnition of subfractions and fractions. Some of the standardized
pictograph found in the literature confuses the citizens, thus they are not easy for the citizens
to understand.
• Correlate the recycling rates in Denmark to the recycling targets found in literature
With the current rise of recycling rates, it might be possible for Denmark to reach the recy-
cling target set by the Danish Government in 2013. However, some of the regions in Den-
mark are far from the recycling target. Because of new calculation methods for the recycling

For the packaging waste, some of the recycling rates already exceed the future recycling

Danish Government and the European Commission, more municipalities have to imple-

5.2
Recommendations
Based on the ﬁndings in the thesis, the following recommendations will be provided to the
Danish Government, the Danish EPA and the municipalities.
The Danish Government or the Danish EPA should evaluate the standardized pictographs
by Dansk Affaldsforening (2020) if these are to be implemented in the municipalities. They
should be evaluated because of them causes confusion for the citizens, and they have to be
intuitive and easy for the citizens to understand. Thus, the citizens should know more or
less which waste that is accepted in the fraction based on the pictograph.
When the Danish EPA will be ﬁnalizing the standardized sorting criteria, it would be recom-
mended to establish a standardization on how to name the fractions. Currently, the name
and accepted fractions differ in some of the waste fractions. By establishing a standardiza-
tion on how to name the fractions, the citizens will know which material fractions, that are
accepted in the waste fraction based on the name.
It is additionally recommended, that the Danish EPA establish a standardization on how to
name the subfractions. Thus a cohesive sorting criteria will be achieved and the potential of
confusion for the citizens will be reduced.
When presenting the standardized sorting criteria, it is recommended, that the municipal-
ities produce a sorting criteria, that is simple, a few examples of not accepted subfractions
could be included. Additionally, the municipalities could create an extended sorting guide
with more examples of accepted and not accepted subfractions could be presented. How-
ever, the extended sorting guide should not contain additional conditions for the fractions.
Furthermore, it is recommended, that the municipalities implement the established sorting
criteria in all sources of information. It must be easy for the citizens to ﬁnd the information.
Additionally, if looking online it should be able for the citizens to ﬁnd the sorting criteria
without having to download a sorting guide, thus the information should be placed on the
website as well.

5.3
Further research
Through the work on the thesis, ﬁndings in the literature and during the analysis on where
research could be performed were discovered. Thus, the following can be further researched.
Because the Danish Government accepts a commingled fraction of metal and glass at house-
holds, there is a potential of accepted the commingled fraction in public containers. How-
ever, it has to be established, that the quality and quantity of glass waste for recycling is not
reduced, if metal and glass are commingled in public containers compared to glass source
separated in public glass containers.
The Danish Government expects between 2-4 bins for the 10 waste fractions at single-family
housing. An investigation on this expectation including a comparison of the use of a 1-
compartment, 2-compartment and 4-compartment in regards to recycling quantities and
qualities. Additionally, it could also be compared with the practices of collection done in
Fredericia municipality, where bags are used instead of bins for multiple waste fractions.
The recycling potential of the wastes collected in the bulky waste could be investigated with
the purpose of eliminating the collection of bulky waste and instead have the citizens drop
off the waste at the recycling centers.
The potential of EPS at households could be researched to consider an implementation of
EPS collected at or near households because only 28 municipalities have implemented col-
lection at their recycling centers and recycling centers are not easily accessable for all citi-
zens.
Through the thesis, it was discovered that Danes have produced the most municipal waste
per citizens since 2011 in Europe. Therefore, it can be investigated why the Danish citizens
generate the most municipal waste in Europe, how do the rules, decided in the EU, add
the generation of municipal waste e.g., rules for packaging of organic food and what can be
done to reduce the generation of municipal waste in Denmark?
During the literature search, a paper on micro- and nanoplastic particles from plastic tea
bags are released into brewed tea and with a potential of entering the human body was dis-
covered. There is additional a potential for the particles to reach the environment. Because
tea bags (and ﬁlters) are accepted in the organic waste, it could be investigated if there are
micro- and nanoplastic particles present in the organic waste and if so, it the particles turn
up in the digestate and the agricultural ﬁelds.
