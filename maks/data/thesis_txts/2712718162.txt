Abstract
Bacteria form biofilm and thereby become more tolerant to antibiotics. The understanding of
biofilm formation is thus very important for optimising antibiotic treatment.
Quorum sens-
ing is believed to play an essential role in biofilm formation, however more research is needed
to fully understand biofilm formation and the role of quorum sensing. The aim of this study
is to investigate biofilm formation and antibiotic treatment of the multidrug resistant bacteria
Pseudomonas aeruginosa including the relevance of quorum sensing using mathematical mod-
elling.
Analytical theorems were applied to show positivity of the solutions of the biofilm model in
the presence and absence of antibiotics. Numerical simulations in COMSOL Multiphysics were
used to test model parameter sensitivity compared to experimental results in centrifugally driven
lab-on-a-disc systems [21] as well as model robustness and the relevance of quorum sensing.
The mathematical models show qualitative agreement with the experimental observations with
regards to mass flow rates, nutrient concentrations, and initial conditions in the absence of antibi-
otics. There are no agreement regarding mass flow rates in the presence of antibiotics. However,
the model shows robustness regarding antibiotic kill rate, the inlet concentration of antibiotics,
and the depletion rate of antibiotics. Quorum sensing was shown to be of great importance con-
cerning the formation of islands of biofilm.
In conclusion, the mathematical models can describe biofilm formation qualitatively. Espe-
cially the implementation of planktonic cells and quorum sensing are found to be important in
this aspect. Further modifications of the models as well as additional experimental results are
needed to fully elaborate on the quantitative aspects of biofilm formation.

Introduction
Bacteria are everywhere. They are necessary for life to form, but also for a large spectrum of
illnesses. It can be an engineering challenge to exploit bacteria in some systems or avoid them
in other systems. In all cases a good understanding of bacteria and their interactions is very
important. Most bacteria are seen to gather in so called biofilms, which are high densities of bac-
teria in an extracellular matrix. The bacteria change their characteristics when part of a biofilm,
e.g. they lose their flagella, become more tolerant to antibiotics, and are able to survive on less
nutrients. The increased tolerance to antibiotics is of particular interest for medical treatments
of ill-carrying bacteria. Biofilms have been found to be 10-1000 times more tolerant to antibiotics
compared to planktonic bacteria [8]. It is therefore important to understand the biofilm formation
to find effective ways to treat ill-carrying, biofilm forming bacteria.
Biofilm formation is a complex field of study which has experienced increased interest in re-
cent decades. Experimental microbiological work is obviously important and different biological
theories have been developed from this work. A key theory is that quorum sensing plays an im-
portant role in biofilm formation. Quorum sensing is the bacteria’s ability to communicate and
coordinate expression, e.g. forming biofilm. The bacterial communication is mediated through
sensing of signal molecules. The bacteria then respond to the local density of signal molecules by,
e.g. changing their properties and forming biofilm.
Abundance of biological data have given a natural entry for co-disciplinary work between bi-
ology and mathematics. When data is available, mathematical modelling is a very strong tool
for analysing the data and find patterns. Ultimately mathematical modelling has the potential
to predict the near future of a system or its response to parameter variations and perturbations.
These are very strong qualities, since fast and cheap computer simulations might support, comple-
ment, precede or even replace comprehensive and expensive experiments. Further, mathematical
analysis of the models may indicate properties or find unknown patterns and thereby guide the
future research in the field.
Over the last decades, a vast number of different mathematical models have been proposed
and examined. Both deterministic and stochastic models being discrete, continuous, and a mix
hereof have been proposed [22], [28]. All the types of models are good in their own ways and have
contributed to the understanding of biofilm formation. Deterministic and continuous models have
the advantage, that they are easier to handle numerically, which is an advantage since analytic
solutions seldom exist. A natural environment for such models is partial differential equations
(PDEs), since the biofilm evolves both spatially and temporal. Furthermore, analytical proper-
ties are easier derived for deterministic and continuous models. This is a huge advantage since
analytical properties such as existence, uniqueness, positivity, and boundedness of the solutions
of the model may be proved. Without such analytic results, numerical analysis is based upon
uncertain grounds, since the model might have either no solution, infinite solutions, or unphysical
solutions such as negative concentrations of bacteria.
This Master’s thesis aims to use mathematical modelling to enlighten the microbiological field
of biofilm formation. A mathematical model of biofilm formation from [4] is analysed and de-
veloped and numerical simulations in COMSOL Multiphysics (from now on just COMSOL) is
compared with experimental results from [24]. To concretize the aims of this thesis the objectives
are given:
1. Envoke analytical results from the literature to ensure positivity of the model solutions.
2. Perform a sensitivity analysis of important parameters of the model and compare numerical
simulations in COMSOL with experimental results when varying the chosen parameters.
2
==PAGE:5==
3. Include antibiotics in the model, such that positivity and robustness for parameter variations
is still invoked and compare numerical simulations in COMSOL with experimental results
of antibiotic treatment.
4. Investigate the relevance of quorum sensing in mathematical modelling of biofilm formation
and account for how to implement it in the given model.
We will focus on the gram-negative bacteria Pseudomonas aeruginosa (PA). These bacteria
are known to cause several severe infections, e.g. colonizing the lungs of cystic fibrosis patients
[3] and sepsis [20]. Since PA is multidrug resistant, they are inherently difficult to treat. The
PA bacteria are known to form biofilm on surfaces, thus enforcing the tolerance to antibiotics.
To construct smart treatment for PA it is thus necessary to know in-depth details of its biofilm
formation. We hope that the mathematical modelling of PA in this thesis will enhance the un-
derstanding of its biofilm formation and antibiotic treatment.
In Chapter 2 we introduce the experimental setup and results from [24], the background for
RAD models, a specific biofilm model from [4], and COMSOL as a numerical tool. In Chapter 3
we modify the model from [4] to satisfy a theorem of positivity from [14]. In Chapter 4 we perform
a sensitivity analysis of the model by varying important parameters and comparing the numerical
simulations in COMSOL with experimental results when possible. In Chapter 5 antibiotics are
introduced in the model. A sensitivity analysis on some of the antibiotic parameters are performed
and numerical simulations are compared with experimental result. In Chapter 6 quorum sensing
is analysed in a simpler model proposed in [15]. The quorum sensing model is modified to be
implemented in our model for biofilm formation. In Chapter 7 we discuss our findings. In Chapter
8 we give a conclusion. In Chapter 9 we give an outlook for future perspectives.

Background
This chapter aims to introduce the experimental, physical, and mathematical background of
the mathematical modelling of PA done in this thesis.
The experimental background is the
microbiological experiments on lab-on-a-discs performed by Laura Serioli et al. and reported in
[24]. The physical background will be a derivation of the important diffusion and advection present
in the experiments. The mathematical background will be an introduction of the mathematical
model used to model these exact experiments in the bachelor project [4].
2.1
Experimental background
This section introduces the experimental work done by Laura Serioli et al. in [24]. The aim of [24]
is to test whether it is possible to use a simple lab-on-a-disc to perform long-term cell cultivating
under influence of fluid flow. The lab-on-a-disc gives several advantages compared to traditional
pressure-driven set-ups, specially being small in size, cheap, and easy to use. The fluid flow is
driven by the centrifugal forces present when spinning the disc. The flow speed is thus controlled
by the spinning frequency. At mass flow rates at the order of Mt = 0.3 −2 µL/min laminar flow
is observed and the experiment can run for 1-7 days without fluid handling. The lab-on-a-disc
used can be seen in figure 1.
Figure 1: Schematic illustration of the lab-on-a-disc used in the experiments. Image from [4].
The culture medium is introduced in the inlet reservoir and the disc is set to rotate, such
that the culture medium flows into the cell chamber and fills it up. Glucose is used as the nu-
trients in the culture medium in one of three concentrations, Cg = [0.3, 3, 30] mM. When the
cell chamber is filled with culture medium the disc is stopped and the bacteria are introduced
in the cell chamber with a syringe with a thin needle with a diameter of 0.4 mm through the
inoculation. The inoculation is then sealed. The disc is held at rest for one hour to let the bac-
teria adhere in the cell chamber. Then the disc is set to rotate again such that the flow rate is
either Mt = 0.3 µL/min or Mt = 1.0 µL/min. The bacteria are cultured like this for 72 hours.
Using fluorescence, laser, and a microscope it is possible to take images of both living and dead
cells for later data analysis. These images were taken at 1, 6, 24, 48, and 72 hours. It was later
possible to calculate both the living and dead bacterial biomasses from the images. At 72 hours
antibiotic is introduced in the inlet reservoir with a pipette. The disc is rotated for further 24
hours. At 72+24 hours, the antibiotic treatment is supposed to be done, and a last image is taken.

==PAGE:7==
The results using the lab-on-a-disc are in general agreement with results obtained on a tra-
ditional pressure-driven set-up. The biofilm growth depends both on the glucose concentration,
the flow rate, and responds well to the antibiotics. The biofilm grows faster for higher concentra-
tions of glucose, but only marginal faster at Cg = 30 mM compared to Cg = 3 mM. Also, the
biofilm only forms a monolayer at the highest concentration of glucose. This indicates, that at
Cg = 30 mM we have reached a saturation level for the glucose. The biofilm also grows faster
at Mt = 1.0 µL/min compared to Mt = 0.3 µL/min, supposably because of a better access to
glucose, which are transported with the fluid flow. In contrast the antibiotic treatment work
better at Mt = 0.3 µL/min compared to Mt = 1.0 µL/min.
An important characteristic of the biofilm formation is that the planktonic bacteria gathers
in small “islands” where they develop the biofilm. The biofilm formation occurs on surfaces, thus
at the rim of the cell chamber in this case. Most biofilm is formed close to the outlet of the cell
chamber, supposably due to the flow. The formation of biofilm on the rim of the cell chamber in
small islands is one of the main characteristics, which we wish to capture with the mathematical
models. With good models, it is possible to predict outcomes of experiments, and thereby suggest
changes in experimental set-ups and direct the direction of new experiment studies.

Physical background
In this section advection and diffusion are derived from general principles.
2.2.1
Advection
We start by deriving the advective contribution to the governing equations. The advection terms
describe that small particles are transported by the bulk movement of the fluid (the culture
medium) in the system. We thus need to analyze how the fluid behaves to describe the advection.
The fluid is a water-based solution containing small molecules and particles such as the bacteria,
its signaling molecules, glucose, and eventually antibiotics. Since the concentrations of all these

2.3.2
Reactions
For the reactions in the model, we will in general assume very simple reactions, in most cases the
simplest of all, namely linear increase or decrease. We have two reasons to use a linear relation for
most reactions. Firstly, it simplifies the model. This is important, since we want to direct focus
to what we believe are the most important aspects, namely the nonlinear diffusion coefficient of
the biofilm, the introduction of planktonic bacteria, and later the introduction of quorum sensing.
We want to understand the influence of these before introducing further complexity from the re-
actions. Secondly, we do not have much experimental work to aid in developing reliable physical
explanations for the different reactions, which can be implemented in a PDE model like ours.
We will however start with the one exception from linear growth. The growth of bacteria is
well understood and has been modelled in several ways. We will implement the standard Monod
equation, where the bacteria growth depends on the limiting substrate, which is assumed to be

In reality the bacteria can convert between the three different types of bacteria, that is plank-
tonic bacteria can become active bacteria and vice versa, and active bacteria can become dormant
bacteria and vice versa. Planktonic bacteria cannot become dormant directly, rather they have
to become active first, and next dormant. However, the conversion from dormant to active is not
significant at a constant concentration of nutrients, rather it happens when the living conditions
improve, thus we neglect this conversion here. The conversion rate between the types of bacteria
is modelled as being linear with constant rate kij, for bacteria going from type i to type j. This
is naturally not a physical correct modelling, e.g. can a lonely planktonic bacteria in the middle
of the cell chamber suddenly become active - and thereafter dormant. In reality the planktonic
bacteria can only become active, when introduced in a biofilm, which are only present at the rim
of the cell chamber. However, a physical description of this is complex and not the scope of the
models in this thesis.
Lastly, the planktonic bacteria can die with lysis rate kp. When a planktonic bacteria dies it
will be carried out of the cell chamber with the flow, since it is still under influence of advection.
We will thus not treat the dead planktonic cells in this model, but just assume that they disappear.
The biofilm bacteria are very viable, and even though a few of them may die, we neglect this effect
in this model.
2.3.3
Advection
For the advection we will assume that the biofilm is not affected by it, while the smaller molecules
such as the planktonic bacteria, oxygen, and later antibiotics and signal molecules are affected
by the advection. The active and dormant bacteria are assumed to be part of a biofilm. Since
experiments show, that the flow does not carry the biofilm out of the cell chamber, the active and
dormant bacteria are not affected by the fluid flow in the model. The physical reason, is that the
adhesive forces between the cell chamber walls and the biofilm are stronger than the advective
force of the laminar fluid flow. Also, adhesive forces within the biofilm ‘glues’ the biofilm together,
such that it acts like one when under influence only of the weak forces in the fluid flow.
The planktonic bacteria and oxygen are on the other hand carried by the advective forces in
the fluid flow. This is introduced in the model in the classical way as
−∇· (uC) = −(C∇u + u · ∇C).
(2.29)
Since the fluid is assumed to be incompressible the divergence of the flow is zero ∇u = 0, thus
the advection simplifies to

(2.30)
for i = P, O. As mentioned earlier u is obtained by solving the stationary Stokes equation
(2.9) and is thus a known variable when solving eq. (2.20).
The fluid flow is assumed to be independent of the biofilm, planktonic bacteria, and oxygen
in the fluid. This is naturally a simplification, since the biofilm will affect the flow at the edges
slightly. However, since the biofilm is very thin, boundary effects from the biofilm is small, and we
will neglect it. Experimentally the thickness of the biofilm is found to be below 30 µm. Similarly
we will neglect the effect the molecules have on the density and viscosity and treat the culture
medium as pure water at room temperature. A very delicate model would have to include the
biofilm formations effects on the fluid flow, such that the interaction would apply both ways.
However, to capture the effects of biofilm formation qualitatively, we believe a steady flow field
suffice.

2.4
COMSOL
In this subsection we give some details on the set-up used for simulations in COMSOL Multi-
physics. COMSOL is a commercial multiphysics simulation software able to simulate real-world
designs and processes. It is based on the finite element method (FEM) for numerically solving a
set of governing (partial) differential equations. COMSOL has different built-in physics modules
and mathematical modules which can be coupled to give multiphysics models. When setting up
the physics and/or mathematical modules, COMSOL generates the underlying weak formulation
of the coupled system of PDEs, which is then solved numerically using the FEM.
We use two different physics (i) ’creeping flow’ to simulate the fluid flow and (ii) ’transport
of diluted species’ to simulate the evolution of the bacteria and oxygen. Later the mathematical
module ’coefficient form PDE’ is added to model the quorum sensing. The fluid flow from the
creeping flow is applied to the planktonic bacteria and oxygen through an advective term coupling
the two physics. Two different ’transport of diluted species’ are thus necessary, one with advection
for the planktonic bacteria and oxygen and one without advection for the active and dormant
bacteria.
Both stationary and time-dependent studies are available in COMSOL and we use both. Since
the fluid flow has a stationary solution the ’creeping flow’ physics is solved with a stationary study.
The solution is then used as an input for the time-dependent study used for the ’transport of di-
luted species’ and the ’coefficient form PDE’. The evolution of the bacteria, oxygen, antibiotics,
and signaling molecules are inherently time-dependent, thus the need for the time-dependent
study. When modelling antibiotics, we first use one time-dependent study (i) without antibiotics
to grow the bacteria as in the experiments. Then the solution of (i) is used as the initial condition
15
==PAGE:18==
for a second time-dependent study (ii), where antibiotics are introduced. In this way we model
the experimental conditions, where the bacteria are grown without antibiotics for 72 h and then
antibiotics are introduced for further 24 h.
One of the most important steps in numerical studies using the FEM is the meshing of the
given geometry. Meshing is a key component, since it determines the accuracy of the model,
because the governing equations are solved within each mesh-element. The finer the mesh, the
better resolution in the solution - and the more computer time is needed.
It is a tricky and
important task to find an optimum in mesh size, since too coarse meshes give wrong solutions
and too fine meshes give impractical long simulation time. Often, the mesh refinement needed
is not homogeneous throughout the domain. The most important tool to verify the mesh size is
to perform a convergence test [16], [6]. In a convergence test an error estimate is calculated at
different mesh sizes and the squared errors should decrease linearly as function of decreasing mesh
size at a logarithmic scale. For the error estimation the best known solution Uref is compared
to the solutions obtained at the different meshes. COMSOL uses the Euclidean norm for the
error estimation [1]. The absolute error estimate E and the relative error estimate Erel are then
respectively

locally can give negative concentrations, which is intrinsically wrong even though it minimizes
the local error estimate. We therefore choose linear basis functions, which also works well with a
large number of mesh elements since they are computationally cheap.

Simulation study of Model B
In this chapter, we will study Model B using simulations in COMSOL. We will investigate how
the model reacts under different mass flow rates, nutrient inlet concentrations, and initial condi-
tions. Model B is developed in [4], but solutions are only given for one set of parameters. Such
a system of nonlinear PDEs typically depends heavily on the parameters of the model. We will
therefore investigate the behaviour of the model when varying three important parameters. The
first parameter is the flow velocity given by the mass flow rate. Experimental results are given
for two different mass flow rates, Mt = 0.3 µL/min and Mt = 1 µL/min in [24], which is why
we investigate these two flows. The second parameter is the inflow concentration of oxygen Oin,
used as a measure of nutrients. Experimental results are given for three different concentrations
of glucose Cg in [24], namely Cg = [0.3, 3, 30] mM.
To compare, we simulate three different
concentrations of oxygen with a factor of 10. The third and last parameter we investigate is
the initial percentage of active and planktonic bacteria, ηX and ηP . Since there initially are no
dormant bacteria, ηX + ηP = 1, we will give ηX and thereby imply ηP = 1 −ηX. The inclusion
of planktonic bacteria in the model is the most significant addition in [4] compared to models in
the literature. Therefore, we find it important to test how the model reacts to different ratios of
planktonic bacteria to active bacteria as initial condition.
When simulating Model B in COMSOL, we detect singularities for times t > 48 h. These
are unphysical and most probably due to numerical issues. To resolve those problems require in-
depth analysis of the COMSOL algorithms. Since this is not the scope of this thesis, we will leave
this problem for future research and restrict the use the model to t ≤48 h. This is unfortunate
since the experiments run for t = 96h. However, the experimental results are not quantitatively
comparable to the results of the simulation anyways due to the experimental methods of data
collection as we will see. Comparison between experiments and simulations will therefore merely
be qualitative and not quantitative. For qualitative comparison, the non-identical times are not
a problem. We will simulate Model B for half the time of the experiments, thus experimental
measurements at t = 72 h will be compared to simulation results at t = 36 h.

Model B with antibiotics: Model BA
In this chapter, we include antibiotics in Model B and name the model Model BA. How bacteria
and antibiotics interact is strongly dependent on the type of bacteria and antibiotics. We will
restrict ourselves to one type of antibiotics, namely Ciprofloxacin, treating PA which was studied
experimentally in [24]. In the experiments, the biofilm is grown for 72 h before the antibiotics
are introduced for the next 24 h. The antibiotics are introduced in the inlet reservoir, which in
simulations corresponds to a Dirichlet boundary condition on the inlet. In the simulations, the
biofilm is grown for t = 36 h and antibiotics are then introduced for the next 12 h. When intro-
duced, the antibiotics kill planktonic and active bacteria with two different rates. The planktonic
bacteria are carried out by the flow when they dies and in the simulation the active bacteria be-
come dormant when they die, such that we can investigate the ratio of living and dead cells and
compare with the experimental results from [24]. Experimentally dormant cells are considered
alive, since they can become active again at any time. However, since the model does not a priori
includes dead bacteria we will in the model regard the dormant bacteria as dead. This is justified
since the dormant bacteria cannot become active again in the model, thus in this sense they are
dead. Further, this simplifies the modelling, since we do not have to introduce yet another type
of bacteria.
Two important measures when treating bacteria with antibiotics are the minimal inhibitory
concentration (MIC) and the minimum bactericidal concentration (MBC). MIC describes the
lowest concentration of antibiotics at which bacteria growth is inhibited and MBC describes the
lowest concentration of antibiotics at which bacterial erradication occurs. To quantify the MIC
and the MBC, experiments are to be performed. However, mathematical models robust to varia-
tions in its parameters have the potential to simulate the antibiotic treatment and thereby guide
the experimental observations of the MIC and the MBC. We will here test Model BA’s robustness
to three important parameters, the kill rate of the active bacteria kAX, the inlet concentration of
antibiotics Ain, and the kill rate of the antibiotics kA.
PA biofilm are found to be 10-1000 times more tolerant to antibiotics than its planktonic
bacteria [8]. Experimental studies show that the MIC of Ciprofloxacin also depends on the con-
centration of the biofilm and planktonic bacteria [27]. After 3 days of cultivation, the studies
show a MIC of 0.125 µg/ml for planktonic bacteria and 4 µg/ml for biofilm. Based on this, the
ratio in kill rates is set at kAP /kAX = 32, where kAP is the kill rate of planktonic bacteria and
kAX is the killing rate of active bacteria. The concentration of antibiotics in the culture medium
after t = 32 h is 4 µg/ml corresponding to 10 µM, which is also used in [24]. In the simulations,
this is implemented as a boundary condition on the inlet Ain = 10 µM.

Quorum Sensing
In this chapter, we introduce quorum sensing, which is believed to be important for biofilm forma-
tion [29]. Quorum sensing is the ability to adapt cell characteristics based on cell communication
through signal molecules and corresponding receptors. Since the signal molecules induce cell reg-
ulation in its own cell type, they are called autoinducers. In the case of PA, the signal molecules
are N-acylated homoserine lactones (AHLs), which bind to the LasR receptors [2]. The communi-
cation helps forming biofilm, e.g. by giving information of the local density of bacteria. By using
this information, the bacteria can direct its movement towards higher densities of bacteria and
thereby gather and eventually form biofilm when the bacteria density is adequate [21].
One way to model the role of quorum sensing in biofilm formation is in terms of chemotaxis.
Chemotaxis is the cell’s ability to move in response to a concentration gradient of molecules. In
this case, we will assume that the bacteria move towards the positive concentration gradient of
the signal molecules. The reason for this is that the concentration of signal molecules will be
higher in places with higher concentration of bacteria, thus movement in direction of the positive
gradient of signal molecules will let the bacteria gather in islands of high bacteria density.
First, we will analyse quorum sensing as introduced in [15] in a model of only biomass and
signal molecules. The model is given in eq. (6.1) and is named Model X. Thereafter, we will
modify the model, such that the source terms can be justified by simple biological arguments and
the initial and boundary conditions match Model B. This will allow us to directly implement
quorum sensing into eq. (2.20). The modified quorum sensing model is given in eq. (6.10) and is
named Model Y .

7
Discussion
In this chapter, the results of the previous chapters are discussed and related to the objectives.
For convenience the objectives will be given at the start of each section.
7.1
Positivity criterion
The first objective is
• Envoke analytical results from the literature to ensure positivity of the model solutions.
Analytical properties are crucial in mathematical modelling. A model has to satisfy some basic
analytical properties to be of any relevance. Existence and uniqueness of solutions are of course
necessary.
Concentrations are inherently non-negative, thus solutions of the models therefore
have to stay non-negative for all times. This is the positivity criterion, which we have shown that
Model B and Model BA satisfy after some small modifications of the diffusion coefficients. This
is a strong result since we are sure that the model can be interpreted physically. It is therefore of
great importance that future extensions and modifications of the model also satisfy the positivity
criterion.
When extending the model to Model BA by including antibiotics, we ensure that the positiv-
ity criterion is still satisfied. This is ensured since the terms killing the bacteria and antibiotics
are proportional to both the bacterial concentration and antibiotic concentration, such that they
vanish at zero concentration. It should also be ensured, that the positivity criterion is still satis-
fied, when implementing quorum sensing in the model. When including quorum sensing in terms
of chemotaxis of the signaling molecules as we do, it is of great importance to ensure that the
positivity criterion is also satisfied numerically, since the chemotaxis term tends to give negative
concentrations.
The natural analytical extension of positivity is to bound the solutions of the model. The
positivity criterion is essentially a lower bound. Mathematically, a proof of an upper bound is
therefore closely related with the proof of a lower bound such as positivity. An upper bound have
a physical interpretation since the bacteria can only pack itself finitely dense in the biofilm. Thus,
the upper bound would account for that. A bound of the concentration in [0, 1] would be easy to
interpret physically since 0 would correspond to no bacteria and 1 would correspond to the most
densely packed concentration of bacteria. An analytical result of an upper bound would therefore
strengthen Model B.
7.2
Sensitivity analysis
The second objective is
• Perform a sensitivity analysis of important parameters of the model and compare numerical
simulations in COMSOL with experimental results when varying the chosen parameters.
The purpose of Model B is to capture the biofilm growth seen in the experiments of [24]. The
model does well on an important qualitative aspect since it finds the biofilm to grow on the walls
of cell chamber, as observed in the experiments. The implementation of planktonic bacteria was
in [4] shown to be important for the biofilm to grow on the walls.
For the model to be useful, it has to be robust under parameter variations, meaning that it
should, at least qualitatively, show the same tendencies as experimental observations when dif-
ferent parameters are varied. Model B show the same tendency as experiments when varying
the mass flow rate. In both cases, the total biomass increases with increasing flow. This can be
explained through higher nutrient concentrations for higher mass flow rate. This is shown for the
39
==PAGE:42==
simulations in figure 8 where oxygen is a measure of the nutrients. It would be of great interest
to verify this experimentally as well.
The concentration of nutrients is of great importance for the biofilm growth. The concentration
of oxygen is interestingly observed to be time-independent in the simulations. In the simulations,
it is most likely due to the advection carrying the oxygen dominating the local consumption of
oxygen by the bacteria. The question is whether this smooth distribution of oxygen reflects the
actual distribution of nutrients in experiments, or if the local consumption of the nutrients are
visible in experiments. Experimental measures of the spatial and temporal changes in the nutri-
ent concentration is therefore of great interest. Such experimental data would enable us to verify
further developments of the model. If the nutrient concentration are found to be stationary, the
nutrients can be implemented much simpler and numerically cheaper by a stationary solution
coupled to the time-dependent solution, just as for the fluid flow. If the distribution of nutrients
change locally in space and time due to the consumption by the bacteria, this should be imple-
mented in the model.
Model B is shown to be too sensitive to changes in the oxygen inlet concentration. Experi-
mentally, the bacterial growth shows to be nearly linear across varying nutrient concentrations.
Varying the oxygen inlet concentration the simulations show both linear decrease, linear increase,
and exponential increase. This supports the idea that the modelling of the interaction between
nutrients and bacteria might be too simple, most likely due to the stationary oxygen concentra-
tion. Under all circumstances, the model has to be modified to be more robust under changes in
the oxygen concentrations. These modifications should preferably be guided by either biological
knowledge of the nutrient-bacteria interaction or experimental results of the changes of nutrients
in space and time during bacterial growth.
Finally, we test the models robustness for different initial bacterial distributions. This is rele-
vant since we do not truly know the initial distribution of planktonic bacteria and biofilm. Also,
biofilm is seen to form in all kinds of systems and therefore apparently for all kinds of initial condi-
tions. For more initial planktonic bacteria and less active bacteria, we see less growth of bacteria
in the middle of the cell chamber, which is in alignment with the experimental observations. As-
sumably these initial conditions are also closest to the actual initial conditions, assuming that the
bacteria are planktonic when injected into the cell chamber. However, experimental measures of
the planktonic bacteria would help establish the correct initial conditions as well as explore the
importance of planktonic bacteria for biofilm formation.
7.3
Antibiotic treatment
The third objective is
• Include antibiotics in the model, such that positivity and robustness for parameter variations
is still invoked and compare numerical simulations in COMSOL with experimental results
of antibiotic treatment.
The model show robustness of the antibiotic parameters when including antibiotic treatment
of the bacteria in the model. The efficiency of the antibiotic treatment increases with increasing
bacterial kill rate kAX and antibiotic inlet concentration Ain as expected. The antibiotic concen-
tration is smoothly distributed similar to the oxygen, and this concentration in the cell chamber
depends heavily on the depletion rate kA.
However, in one important aspect the model does not capture the experimental results for
antibiotic treatment. Experimentally, the antibiotic treatment is seen to be more efficient for
Mt = 0.3 µL/min compared to Mt = 1.0 µL/min in [24].
The simulations show the oppo-
site, namely that the antibiotic treatment is more efficient for Mt = 1.0 µL/min compared to
Mt = 0.3 µL/min. This suggest that the model neglects some important details of the antibiotic
40
==PAGE:43==
treatment. These details might stem for the interaction between antibiotics and bacteria. The
interactions will however depend on the type of bacteria and antibiotics. To account for details
in the interactions, the models might therefore need to be specific to the type of bacteria and
antibiotics. A compromise between more specific and accurate models vs. more general and less
accurate models have to be made. In all cases experimental measurements of the antibiotic concen-
tration would be interesting in order to guide the modifications of the antibiotic implementation
in the model.
7.4
Relevance of quorum sensing
The fourth objective is
• Investigate the relevance of quorum sensing in mathematical modelling of biofilm formation
and account for how to implement it in the given model.
Quorum sensing is widely regarded as being important in biofilm formation. Here, quorum
sensing is implemented in terms of chemotaxis, meaning that the bacteria diffuse in direction of
a positive gradient of signaling molecules. This is smart, since it is a simple way to implement
quorum sensing. However, it also poses some problems since diffusion in direction of a positive
gradient potentially leads to negative concentrations. This is also observed in the simulations of
Model Y . Analytical, this can be avoided by letting the diffusion coefficient be proportional to
the concentration of bacteria, thus being zero for no bacteria. However, numerically this method
seems to fail since we do see negative concentrations. The positivity of the model is important,
thus this issue should be accounted for. Extensive numerical manipulations might be needed.
Qualitatively, quorum sensing does however show to be important to describe that biofilm
forms in discrete islands of biofilm. Model Y capture this island formation both for stochastic
initial conditions as well as for smooth initial conditions of bacteria meaning that it is an intrinsic
property of the model and not just due to some specific initial conditions.
Unfortunately, quorum sensing in terms of chemotaxis turns out to be numerically difficult to
implement in Model B. Due to numerical problems we have not been able to obtain simulations
of Model B with the implementation of quorum sensing as in Model Y . Taking into account
positivity, possible singularities, and other numerical challenges, it should however be possible.
Note, that we only have discussed the qualitative correspondence between the simulations
and experimental observations.
Modifications of the models are needed, preferably based on
experimental measurements of planktonic bacteria, nutrients, antibiotics, and signaling molecules.
Eventually this should lead to models and parameters capturing the real setting well enough, such
that quantitative comparison between the simulations and experiments are possible.

8
Conclusion
Bacteria become more tolerant to antibiotics when forming biofilm. The understanding of biofilm
formation including the relevance of quorum sensing is therefore of great interest in order to op-
timise antibiotic treatment. Mathematical modelling have the potential to guide this research.
Under assumption of existence and uniqueness, the solutions of Model B and Model BA are
shown to satisfy the positivity criterion. Negative concentrations are seen for the model of quorum
sensing in terms of chemotaxis in Model Y . This might be due to numerical issues rather than
the absence of analytical properties. Regardless, the existence of negative solutions does not align
with a physical reliable implementation of quorum sensing.
Mathematically, the reason behind the increased biomass when increasing mass flow rate is
found to be the increased concentration of oxygen throughout the cell chamber. For a chosen mass
flow rate, the oxygen concentration is however also observed to be stationary in the simulations.
This show that the oxygen can either be implemented much simpler or that it does not capture the
real temporal behaviour of the nutrients. Experimental measures of the nutrients are needed to
account for this. Further, the simulations are shown to be too sensitive to the inlet concentration
of oxygen also indicating the need for experimental measures to guide modifications of the model.
Initial conditions of purely planktonic bacteria are shown to capture the qualitative findings of the
experimental results in [24] best. This supports the importance of planktonic bacteria to model
biofilm formation found in [4].
Introducing antibiotics, the model is robust to changes in antibiotic kill rate, inlet concen-
tration of antibiotics, and antibiotic depletion rate.
However, the efficiency of the antibiotic
treatment in Model BA is found to increase with increasing mass flow rate. This contradicts the
experimental results in [24] where the antibiotic treatment is more efficient for lower mass flow
rate. Modifications to the antibiotic interaction with the bacteria is needed to account for this.
Experimental measurements of the antibiotics will be a great help in guiding these modifications.
The models are fairly successful in capturing important aspects of biofilm formation such as
the biofilm growth of the walls in Model B and the island formation of biofilm in Model Y .
These are however only qualitative similarities to the experimental observations. Quantitative
comparison is still not possible. Experimental measurements to guide parameter estimation and
model modifications are important for quantitative comparison to become possible.

9
Future perspectives
To improve the understanding of biofilm formation, it is essential for mathematical models and
microbiological experiments to guide and complement each other. This will lead to more profound
understanding of biofilm formation than any of the two research fields could obtain separately
and thus ultimately lead to more efficient treatment of bacterial infections.
One aspect in which mathematical modelling can contribute to this expansion of knowledge is
to quantify the current knowledge of biofilm formation. It can enlighten the relevance of specific
parameters, interactions, and properties of the system. Furthermore, mathematical modelling
have the potential to discover properties and patterns of the system not necessarily visible in
experiments. This may, e.g. be attractors in the system or restrictions on the parameter space.
These findings can guide the experimental work in a more productive direction, which in return
can help develop the quantitative reliability of the models. Ultimately, simulations of the models
can substitute for expensive and time consuming experiments.
The natural extension of this thesis would be to resolve two important issues of the models
investigated here. First, quorum sensing should be implemented in Model B, such that the pos-
itivity criterion is satisfied both analytical and numerical and that simulations can be run in a
reasonable time frame. Second, the interaction between bacteria and antibiotics should be taken
into account in the model. The implementation of this interaction should be guided by experi-
mental observations.
