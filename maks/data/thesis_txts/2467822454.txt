Abstract
The focus in this thesis has been on the burning behaviours of electric cars through simulation
with the computational ﬂuid dynamics program FDS. Five electric cars were placed next to each
other in a parking facility, with a spacing of 80 cm between them. The middle car was set to
ignite in the battery pack located beneath the car. The main results studied were the ﬁre spread
to adjacent cars and the heat released to the construction.
Two scenarios were simulated to see the effect of air ﬂow on the ﬁre. The ﬁrst was a simpler
scenario where there was no ﬂow of air except the convection that occurs due to the heat from
the burning cars. The second scenario had an applied air ﬂow of 1.5 m/s going through the
facility, blowing at the sides of the cars.
In the ﬁrst scenario the time for the ﬁre to spread to the ﬁrst adjacent car was measured to 3
minutes and 31 seconds. This is a relatively fast ﬁre spread compared to the time usually used
for ﬁre design. The heat to the construction is at the maximum measured to 1182 °C.
The second scenario used slightly less time for the ﬁre to reach the next car. Here it took 2 min-
utes and 57 seconds. The air ﬂow was directed in the same way as the natural air ﬂow occurred
in the ﬁrst scenario. In the early stages the ﬁre gets more violent compared to scenario 1, but is
faster subdued from the air ﬂow over time. The maximum heat to reach the construction is in
the second scenario measured to around 1300 °C.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Fire in car parks can do a lot of damage. Usually, it does not cause many fatalities, but the cars
themselves and the parking facilities are at high risk if a ﬁre should erupt. There has not been
performed a lot of experimental research on how large scale ﬁres behave in car parks as this
would be too costly. However, some experience has been gained from unfortunate events where
large car parks have burst into ﬂames [3, 14, 36]. Most of the knowledge about the different
aspects of a car park ﬁre is extracted from computer models. The models are often based on
experiments where single cars have been tested. These create a good representative of actual
scenarios, but there is no guarantee that an actual ﬁre would behave in the exact same way.
The research performed and knowledge gained from previous studies have most often focused
on internal combustion engine (ICE) cars, either gasoline or diesel fuelled. However, as there is
an increase in cars running on more clean energy, a need arises to ensure the safety of these as
well. These types are most often driven by either a battery-powered electric engine, hydrogen-
powered electric engine or natural gas combustion engine. In certain countries, like Norway,
the amount of battery electric vehicles (BEVs) is increasing, and little research has been done
regarding the way this type of car behaves during large scale ﬁres.
Some countries have over the last decade experienced a large growth in electric vehicles (EVs),
with China having the largest total amount of EVs at the end of 2018 [7] and Norway the largest
amount per inhabitant [45]. The increase in sales of electric cars in these and other countries
show a positive exponential curve, and many countries aim for a large growth of EVs due to
their positive environmental impact.
The electric cars of today have proven to acquire a high safety rating both related to impact
damages and probability of ﬁre occurrence. However, there have been instances in which they
catch ﬁre and therefore it is just as important to determine their behaviour during ﬁre and how
to act upon it. Although this is not to say that they are unsafe related to conventional combus-

or the chargers.
1.2
Object and aim
As presented in the previous section, there are some instances over recent years of electric ve-
hicles catching ﬁre for various reasons. Therefore, the object of this thesis is to get a better
understanding of how commercial electric vehicles behave during ﬁre. A large scale ﬁre will
be simulated to achieve this purpose. There will be ﬁve cars included in the study, with the ﬁre
starting in the middle car. The cars will be placed in a parking facility, and the main focus is
to see the heat transfer these cars emits to the surrounding structure in the parking garage while
on ﬁre. Since there are several cars included in the ﬁre, another focus will be to see the rate at

which the ﬁre spreads from one car to the adjacent cars. The key questions this thesis is focused
on answering are as follows:
• How much heat is transferred from a large scale ﬁre with several electric cars to the
structure of a parking lot?
• How fast will the ﬁre spread from one electric car to adjacent electric cars?
1.3
Methodology
To achieve the results for this thesis the computational ﬂuid dynamics program Fire Dynamic
Simulator (FDS) version 6.7.0 will be used to create and analyse the model of the ﬁve electric
vehicles within the parking facility. The results received from the program will be analysed
further to draw a conclusion for the ﬁre related to the electric cars. To ensure that the results
from the model is representative, they will be veriﬁed through simpler hand calculations of the
same scenarios.
1.4
State of the art
As mentioned previously, there is a lack of experimental research related to the speciﬁcs of elec-
tric vehicles on ﬁre. This is mainly to the extent of large scale car ﬁres i.e. experiments where
entire commercial cars have been tested for ﬁre. However, several researches have studied the
effect of single or bundles of battery cells during ﬁre. Studies that are relevant for the purpose
of this thesis will be presented in this section. How their results will be applied will be further
discussed in chapter 2.
Two bachelor students in Norway did an experimental ﬁre test to a 2012 model Peugeot iOn
electric vehicle in 2013 [1]. This is a small electric vehicle, released in 2011 with a driving
range of 120-130 km. The battery is a lithium-ion battery with a capacity of 16 kWh, a weight
of 240kg and a nominal voltage of 330 V. The study showed that the electric car burned with
higher temperatures than what a combustion car normally does, measuring the maximum tem-
perature to 1200 °C. The car burned for 90 minutes and no attempts were made to put out the
ﬁre. Since the time of this study, new electric cars have gotten larger batteries with more power
and longer driving distances. The speciﬁcs of these newer cars related to ﬁre is not known to a
degree of entire cars, but if six year old cars also burn more ferociously than fuel driven cars, it
is likely that newer EVs burn even more ferociously due to the increase in power.

2.2.2
Cause of ﬁres
From examples where electric vehicles have caught on ﬁre, it is seen that there are a few dif-
ferent ways this can happen. As described in chapter 1, some of the reasons for ﬁre can be
mechanical damage to the battery, short circuits somewhere inside the battery or engine, or ex-
ternal heating of the battery. In theory overcharging lithium ion batteries could also be a cause
for ﬁre [44], but through this study there have not been found any record of this happening in
EVs as the cars have well designed safety measures to prevent this. Of the other three causes
mentioned in chapter 1, all have been improved upon by the car manufacturers. For example the
mechanical damaging of the battery in Tesla was approached by heightening the car during high
velocity. The short circuit problem are being solved by inserting more safety measures through
the code of the car’s software system. Even though a lot of measures are taken to ensure the
safety, certain cars have caught ﬁre during the last couple of years.
Whatever the cause of the ﬁre is, they all lead to the one reaction that occurs within a lithium ion
battery, thermal runaway, as shown by Wang et al. [56]. When a lithium ion battery reaches a
15
==PAGE:30==
Chapter 2. Theory
certain temperature, a crucial element within the battery cell called the electrolyte starts break-
ing down. This makes the anode able to come into contact with the cathode, and thermal
runaway is set in motion. This reaction creates oxygen within the cell, which makes the ﬁre
able to be sustained even in tight enclosures and aids in heating up the rest of the cell and the
surroundings. When a battery cell is surrounded by a large amount of other cells, as they are in
a car battery, they are all heated up in a chain reaction that spread very rapidly if not prohibited
to do so. For example in Tesla batteries, the cells are distributed in modules which are again
separated from other modules. Within every module, a ﬁre is able to spread fast. However, to
spread from one module to another the heat has to go through an insulating layer which acts as a
safety measure for ﬁre. This will be further addressed in the next section. Even though the cars
have safety measures like this, there are examples where the ﬁre have still spread throughout
the battery in a matter of a few minutes, as was the case for the quite recent car park ﬁre in
Shanghai [48].

will ﬂow back through the power source and the positive lithium ions ﬂows back to the metal
oxide material. Here the lithium ions and the electron will get back together to make the stable
state, ready for another recharge.

When considering multiple of these single cells burning at the same time, the HRR for one
cell will not be simply multiplied by the amount of cells on ﬁre. As found by Chen et al. in
2018, equation 1.3 and equation 1.4 must be used to expand the HRR. Equation 1.3 makes an

various battery cells. In the current study, the simulated car battery will contain 7000 of these
cells, similar to what is used in Tesla cars [13].

FDS is a computational ﬂuid dynamics simulating program. It was created by the National
Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and the Technical Research Centre of Finland
(VTT), and is free to use for everyone. It was ﬁrst released to the public in 2000 and has since
that time had many upgrades to newer versions [33]. This chapter will present how the simula-
tion for this thesis is created through FDS.

in FDS. When creating geometry in FDS, everything is made of cubes, which makes it more
complicated to model curved surfaces than straight ones. The rest of the garage ﬂoor would

The mesh for the FDS model has been reﬁned in order to ensure that the results will be as ac-
curate as possible without making the model use too much time and storage. This is done by
running several models where the only difference is the size of the mesh cells. At a certain size
of the cells the results are very similar to the results from the mesh with lower resolution, and
then it is no longer necessary to make the mesh any ﬁner. The type of results that are compared
are the heat release rate and temperature spread, as these are the main effects from EV ﬁres that
are looked at in this study.

There will be two different scenarios analysed for this thesis, where the difference between

ventilation of the parking facility. By modelling one without ventilation and one with, it will be
clear to see what kind of impact this will have on the ﬁre.
3.5
Limitations of FDS model
FDS is a good program for simulating ﬁres, however, there are certain aspects of actual ﬁres that
the program is unable to account for. In this section these limitations will be presented along
with how they may have affected the simulation for this thesis.
It is not possible to model jet ﬂames or explosions in FDS, or any other type of extremely
rapidly spreading ﬁres. FDS is limited to low speed ﬁres when calculating heat transfer and
smoke transfer. This might affect the results in this thesis some, as certain car battery ﬁres have
been subject to explosions during ﬁre spread. However, this is not the case for most ﬁres and
when it does happen, it is minor explosions in relation to the entire battery ﬁre. FDS is still able
32
==PAGE:47==
3.6 Veriﬁcation of model
to calculate for relatively fast spread of ﬁre throughout the battery.
As have been pointed out previously, the geometry in FDS is created by rectangular cubes. This
can be a problem for certain simulations where the geometry mostly consist of bent shapes. For
this simulation however, most of the geometry ﬁts this criteria well, with exception of the cars
themselves. All the parts of the building in the modelled section of the car park in Oslo is built
with geometry that ﬁts well for FDS. The cars are however limited to having its parts made into
rectangular cubes instead of rounded corners. This will have some effect on the results, but not
signiﬁcantly much.
The car in the model is made up of various electric vehicle studies which are not all about the
same type of batteries and EVs. The results from the model can therefore not be directly linked
to speciﬁc cars, but rather to electric vehicles in general. The materials that are used in the car
are also taken from a relatively old study on car materials and may not apply directly to newer
vehicles.
For the ﬁre calculation, FDS assumes that the reaction between oxygen and fuel occur instan-
taneously. Since the car park in this thesis is closed, this may not be the case for the entire
car. The batteries on the other hand can be justiﬁed in regards to this because when lithium ion
batteries reach a certain heat, oxygen is released and will then react inside of the battery.
The value used for the heat release rate of the battery is taken from studies on battery cells and
is then scaled up to the size of the entire battery using a formula, as shown in chapter 2. This
will have some insecurities as the formula have not been experimentally veriﬁed for such a large
expansion of cells.

Discussion
In the ﬁrst three chapters the literature study, application of literature, model design and assump-
tions have been presented, with the results from these presented in chapter 4. In this chapter the
results presented will be discussed in the sense of what they represent and mean, and whether
they are applicable to be used in further studies similar to this.
The results, as presented in the previous chapter, indicate that the electric vehicles designed in
this thesis behave quite differently than conventional ICE cars have been found to do during
ﬁre. The battery poses a high risk during ﬁre in regards to ﬁre spread and heat release. Whether
the results here are applicable in relation to reality is debatable, as a few assumptions have been
made during the design of the simulation. A major assumption to take into account is the fact
that the battery cells were not taken from studies on a single type of battery, but rather from
several types that are used in EVs. This makes the model a representation of EVs that might
be safer than some and more severe during ﬁre than others. Further studies on speciﬁcs of the
various battery types on the market must be conducted in order to make simulations that are
more precisely related to speciﬁc cars. However, as shown in chapter 1 the results presented
here do necessarily not deviate too much from certain scenarios that have occurred to EVs, as
some of the ﬁres have been quite violent.
Another important assumption to take into account is that the battery ﬁre were scaled up from
the values found for single batteries. This ﬁre will not necessarily have the same speciﬁcs in
reality as it did in this thesis. Even though a formula made for scaling up the ﬁre properties of
batteries were used, the amount used here is very large and errors can occurs because of this.
It is important that studies similar to this are conducted. Electric cars are still relatively new
to the automotive industry, and under constant development. The safety of newer cars entering
the market must be ensured, whether it be EVs, ICE vehicles or any other form of fuel. For the
purpose of further studies on EV ﬁres, the approach used in this thesis can serve as inspiration
of a way to design a car ﬁre and make a simulation.

Chapter 5. Discussion
50
==PAGE:65==
6
Conclusion
This thesis has studied burning electric cars in a parking garage using a computer simulation.
The main points the simulation was intended to answer was the temperature developed by these
cars to their surroundings, and how fast the ﬁre spread from one car to adjacent cars. The results
show that the ﬁre spreads relatively fast from one car to the next due to violent ﬂames erupting
from all the car’s battery packs. In addition and for the same reason, the heat emitted to the
surrounding construction is also high.
Two scenarios were studied. The difference between them being an air ﬂow added to the second
scenario. In scenario 1 the ﬁre lasted longer than in scenario 2, but the surrounding structure
reached a higher temperature in scenario 2. In scenario 1 the ﬁre spread to the next car in 3
minutes and 31 seconds, and the concrete reached a temperature of 1182 °C. In scenario 2 it
took 2 minutes and 57 seconds for the ﬁre to spread to the next car, and the temperature to the
construction was measured to almost 1300 °C.
By comparing the two scenarios it is clear what effect the air ﬂow had on the ﬁre. By blowing
sideways at the cars it helped spreading the ﬁre in the air ﬂow direction. This also made more
cars burn simultaneously, hence creating more heat transfer during that period. However, the
ﬁre lasted around 15 minutes shorter in scenario 2 than in scenario 1. So even if the tempera-
tures on the construction was higher in the second scenario it does not necessarily mean that the
structure took more damage from it.
The study show that electric vehicles most likely have different properties during ﬁre than tra-
ditional internal combustion engine vehicles. With new powerful battery packs they burn fe-
rociously and with high heat release. This is a subject that should be further studied to verify
whether electric vehicles need a different approach for design of parking facilities.

Chapter 6. Conclusion
52
==PAGE:67==
7
Further Work
The electric vehicle market is under constant growth and the research around these cars should
to some extent keep up with this development in regards to its’ safety. This thesis has shown
that under certain assumptions electric cars poses a larger threat than what is expected from
cars today. In this regard there are some aspects that must be further looked into to back up the
results found here, along with looking at other similar approaches.
To be able to make as precise simulations of car ﬁres as possible, experimental studies must be
conducted to ensure that accurate numbers are used for the ﬁre properties of the cars. There is
a lack of studies that are focused on large scale battery ﬁres, both battery modules and entire
battery packs. The theory behind this thesis is that the battery cells are scaled up to a larger
amount, which have some uncertainties along with it. With data from larger scaled experiments
the simulation would be more precise.
Along with large scale experiments, more studies should also look into various types of batter-
ies that are used in electric vehicles. As have been mentioned throughout the thesis, there are
studies on single, and bundles of cells. However, there are still a large number of cell types that
have not been studied in this way. These cells should also be looked into.
Another important factor for simulations is to have mathematical formulas for scaling up the
properties of one cell to more cells. The formulas used in this thesis is from a simpliﬁed method,
and to ensure more precise results a better approach could be used. Certain cars contain a lot of
battery cells and newer cars could possibly have even more. It would be helpful to be able to
simulate these without necessarily needing to test each car experimentally.
The car parks that are used today are often designed for conventional ICE vehicles, with charg-
ing stations for EVs being put in later. Through further studies it could be looked more into
whether these car parks are sufﬁciently designed for EVs or not. If the ﬁres from electric cars
are as dangerous as proposed by this study, there might emerge a need for a new standard in
regards to charging stations in parking facilities. For this purpose, interesting aspects can be to
further look into the requirements regarding the material properties of the construction, and the

ceiling height.
In this thesis the ﬁre was modelled with ﬁve cars. It would be useful to investigate ﬁre involving
more cars. Through the literature study for this thesis, there was not found any incidents of very
large EV ﬁres. However, as the amount of electric cars are growing, it is not impossible that a
large car ﬁre could happen some time in the future.
