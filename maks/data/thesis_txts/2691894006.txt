Abstract
Over the last decade computer vision technology has had a rise in popularity. Convolutional
neural networks (CNNs) are structured to work well with image or video data, but take a long
time to execute, especially for diﬀicult classification tasks. In order to apply the CNNs in real
world applications, they have to be able to run on smaller devices, such as phones or automated
devices. In this thesis we implement three approaches for speeding up deep CNNs based on two
tensor decomposition methods, PARAFAC and Tucker. All approaches are tested on a series of
models of different depth, on the data sets MNIST, CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. Each model is
trained from scratch by using the mentioned approaches. The direct training of the decomposed
models method has previously been rejected due to poor results in deep CNNs. We found that
training networks directly from scratch yielded good results, achieving similar levels of accuracy
as the baseline model, while requiring much less memory, and slightly less computation time.
The Back and Forth training method was used on a single model, and while achieving decent
accuracy, proved unable to converge properly. The Alternating Tensor Compose Decompose
training method was proposed earlier this year, and it offered a method that utilises the full
parameter and the decomposed parameter spaces. We re-implement and reformulate the method,
such that it complies with the 4D PARAFAC, and the Tucker2 decomposition methods. The
ATCD method did not achieve comparable final accuracy.

Training procedure of each method. The space depicted by the large circle is the

Introduction
During the last decade deep learning and artificial neural networks (ANN) have become a large
and revolutionary topic in computer intelligence [1]. Especially convolutional neural networks
(CNN) have played a large role in image recognition tasks. Due to advances in computational
power, this resource has recently become feasible and thus very valuable. Since 2012, when
AlexNet [2] was introduced, many improvements and advancements have been discovered con-
cerning CNN’s. The depth of a network determines the number of parameters and the number
of addition-multiplication operations (AM operations) required to compute the convolution op-
erations. In most circumstances the depth of a network also determines the limit of accuracy
of the model and the increase in training complexity [3]. A diﬀicult computer recognition task
requires a deep and advanced CNN. As a consequence only ”super computers” can train deep
networks and solve these tasks.
Applying the final model weights to images is much less computationally expensive than finding
them, and is referred to as running the model. The time it takes to run the model on images
unseen in the training phase, is referred to as the execution time or inference time. If the num-
ber of parameters and the execution time of a model is decreased radically, running a model
on small devices such as mobile phones or automated computers would be feasible. Since image
recognition is a versatile subject, many applications benefit from being able to execute on small
devices.
One way to reduce memory requirements and improve the execution time of a model, is by
changing the structure of the CNN model. By decomposing the kernel tensors that determine
the convolution of the network, such that the amount of parameters and the number of AM
operations of the model will be reduced.
Several studies has been conducted on different decomposition methods of tensors, their applica-
tions in CNN, and how to achieve maximum accuracy with limited resources [4][5][6]. A common
decomposition scheme that has been examined extensively for developing models that execute
fast, is tensorised inference [7]. Tensorised inference refers to the following list of actions, where
the decomposition takes place after the model has been trained [5][6].
1. Create a model
2. Train the model
3. Decompose the kernel tensor for a single layer, or decompose the kernel tensors for all
layers
4. Fine tune the model
5. Test the model
In tensorised inference, the first and second step can be exchanged by importing a pre-trained
model. The drawback of this method is that the parameters of the final decomposed model are
trained only in the fine tuning step. Since the filters are computed before the decomposition
step, the rank of the decomposition required can be estimated. This means that the reduction
of accuracy of the decomposed model can be predicted. This does not necessarily mean that
the final decomposed model requires the estimated amount of parameters in order to reach the
desired level of accuracy. A smaller model might be able to reach similar level of accuracy and
faster execution times. The training schemes proposed in this thesis do not estimate the rank
of kernels, but train the decomposed kernels directly, thus circumventing the second and third
3D Convolution and Compression in Deep-learning Backbone Architecture
1
==PAGE:18==
step of the described inference training procedure. The fourth step is changed using the different
training schemes.
1.1
Hypothesis
When a CNN model is decomposed, each layer becomes a sequence of layers.
The loss of
the network defines the gradients for each parameter of the network, and is calculated by the
backpropagation algorithm. The parameters in later layers of the network are calculated with
high accuracy. The gradients for the parameters in early layers of the network are calculated
using the loss of the network, as well as all the other parameters of the network. If a model is
deep, the gradients for the parameters of the early layers might become small or inaccurate, and
the model cannot train properly. This is called the vanishing/exploding gradient problem. Since
a decomposed model is deeper than a full model, it is hypothesised that a decomposed model is
harder to train than a full model, a decomposed model is more prone to vanishing/exploding or
inaccurate gradients, and that a deep decomposed model is not able to train at all.
1.2
Aim of thesis
The aim of this thesis is to reconstruct and refine a novel tensorised training scheme [7]. A
tensorised training scheme, in contrast to tensorised inference, train the decomposed elements
directly. Tensorised training methods should train decomposed models that maintain the accu-
racy of the full model, while decreasing the execution time. Tensorised training is of interest
as image recognition and classification tasks are increasing in complexity, demanding deeper
and more advanced networks. As such tasks become more common, deep and advanced CNNs
must be able to execute on devices such as computers, industry related image devices, tablets
or mobile phones.
1.3
Objectives and Contribution
This thesis will be a further study of decomposing kernels to reduce the necessary computer
power of running CNNs. Before contributions can be made, a set of objectives are completed,
creating a foundation for the thesis:
• Explain the fundamentals of traditional neural networks, and CNNs.
• Create models that can achieve decent accuracy on three different data sets, by under-
standing and applying multiple CNN techniques.
• Explain the link between different tensor decompositions and convolution operations.
• Briefly review previous CNN studies and findings.
Afterwards this thesis aims to add the following contributions:
• Analyse and test the inherent properties of directly decomposed models, using two decom-
position techniques on multiple models and data sets by comparing the execution time
and accuracy to that of the corresponding full networks.
• Create a training scheme, BAF, that establishes an intuition of the ATCD training scheme,
as well as describe the schemes abstractly and concretely.
• Re-implement, analyse and evaluate a novel training scheme called ATCD (Alternating
Tensor Composition Decomposition), which was published earlier this year [4].
• Expand the training scheme scope, and create new theory and applications for ATCD,
subsequently testing the effectiveness of the created schemes.

3.1.2
Regularisation and Standardisation Layers
Regularisation and standardisation layers are used to improve the performance of the network.
There are many different techniques that can be used to regularize or standardize. In this section
the regularisation and standardisation layers and techniques that have been used in this thesis
are explained.
Pooling
Pooling is a CNN specific method used for down sampling images. It simplifies the information of
a convolutional layer by reducing the size of the activation map, but retains the most important
features found. Pooling is implemented after convolutional layers. Since the output image has
reduced spatial dimension, the amount of parameters required in subsequent layers is decreased.
Two types of pooling exists. Average-pooling which maps the mean of pixels in a region, to a
single pixel in the output image, and max-pooling which maps the maximum value pixel to a
single pixel in the output image. In this thesis max-pooling will be used. The intuition behind
max-pooling is that the exact location of the maximum pixel value is not important, but that
the value is the most important feature from the receptive field [12].

Dropout
A dropout layer reduces over-fitting by setting some inputs to zero. The elements of the input
are selected independently, and are set to zero depending on a probability drawn from a Bernoulli
distribution [16]. Using a dropout layer in the context of fully connected layers will zero out
neurons. This corresponds to removing different features found by the network.
Dropout in the context of convolutions layers will zero out entire channels. This yields similar
interpretations as before, since each channel in an image represents a feature, and thus removing
a channel in the image removes a feature.
When a model finds many features and some of them are set to zero, training will be harder.
This is called regularisation, and causes the model to generalize better, and not over-fit to the
training set.
Dropout is disabled when evaluating the validation set, since the parameters have already been
trained, and as many features as possible should be used to classify correctly.

The batch normalisation operation standardises the output of each convolutional layer, such
that the next layer receives information that has less variance. If a layer is changed by the
backpropagation and step algorithm, such that the output of that specific layer decreases the
overall loss of the network for the given inputs, but the inputs of that layer then changes, then
the new parameters in the given layer does not necessarily have the intended effect.

3.1.4
Sets
When a NN has been defined for a given problem, actual learning begins. When approaching
a problem in machine learning, the data is usually split into three sets. The training set, the
validation set and the test set. In this thesis we omit the test set, since no hyperparameters are
tuned, and no step size is modified, making the validation set untouched by the training.
In the training phase the training set is given to the network. The samples in the training set
are passed through the layers of neurons, and the network will automatically derive rules – or
train parameters – to recognize structure in the data. From this it is clear that a large training
set will result in better training accuracy [12]. Thus the validation set is typically only a fraction
of the size of the training set.
An epoch describes when all the training data has been used a single time. As the name suggests
this is the most computationally demanding phase in CNN development. The amount of epochs
until the training accuracy does not increase, is not deterministic. That is to say that a model
might train to optimality in a few epochs, or need more epochs to get to a similar level of
accuracy, or get completely stuck and never reach the desired level of accuracy. The amount
of epochs required for optimal training depends on the size of the model and the number of
parameters.
After each epoch the accuracy of the model on the validation set is recorded, to see if the rules
learned by the network generalise well, or if they are too specific for the training data [12].
After the validation step the hyperparameters, if any, are updated. If the rules are too specific,
a means of regularisation might be employed to decrease the training accuracy, but in turn
increase generalisation or validation accuracy. The accuracy of the (BL) full model in this thesis
works as a baseline for the decomposed models, and therefore the hyperparameters do not need
to be changed.

A tensor is a multiway array, represented by an array of elements that transform according to
certain rules under a change of coordinates [15].

3.2.1
Tensor Decomposition
In many CNNs the amount of parameters is large. This amount of parameters is necessary as
CNNs need many parameters in order to find a good local minimum [6]. This means that after a
network has been trained, this redundancy should be removable, without much loss of accuracy.
This can be achieved by tensor decomposition of the kernels. The general method is to split
the dimensions of the kernels into parts, that can be applied in succession. By breaking the
kernels apart into smaller kernels, memory usage is reduced, and if the network is big enough,
the inference speed should also be smaller.
In this thesis the main methods for decomposing is Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) decom-
position, also called the Canonical Decomposition (CD or CPD), and the Tucker2 decomposition,
defined in [5].

4
Previous Work
In this section the literature that precedes this thesis is presented. The sources that treat the
decomposition of filters in a CNN are separated into two categories; tensorised inference and
tensorised training. Tensorised inference refers to the action of decomposing a trained network,
and then training again, while tensorised training refers to the action of training a decomposed
network from scratch. The inference step refers to the execution of the network on validation
data or new data. First the literature of some network types that historically has changed the
way CNNs are constructed, is briefly covered.
4.1
Architectures
The architecture of a network has a great influence on the final performance.
The design
ideas that determine the shape of a network have to be justified. Two network types that have
particularly interesting architectures are highlighted in this section, the VGGnet and the ResNet
architectures.
VGGnet: The VGG model was one of the first standardised models that used the same 3 × 3
convolution with one layer of zero padding multiple times. The receptive field of each pixel
in an activation map in a VGGnet is increased by adding more layers, rather than increasing
the kernel size. Additionally the amount of channels of the model is increased as the spatial
dimension is down-sampled, such that each convolution operation requires approximately the
same amount of addition-multiplication operations, O. This makes error-finding and improving
the model easier, as the number of hyperparameters is decreased [14].
O(D, c, 2c) = D2 · c · 2c · 32,
(4.1)
= (D/2)2 · 2c · 22c · 32,
(4.2)
= O(D/2, 2c, 22c).
(4.3)
In the above equations the inputs of O indicate the spatial dimension of the input image, the
channel depth of the input image, and the count of filters in the kernel. This count is also
the channel depth in the next layer. Between the layers a pooling operation occurs for down-
sampling the image. The calculation is simple, since the kernel has its center in each pixel of
the input image exactly once.
ResNet: Residual Networks was more recently created. The architecture is simple and can be
implemented into other types of networks. A pass from a layer is made around the convolution
operation, see Figure 4.1. This pass has 2 functions: To make the model locate changes and to
make the backpropagation less error prone.
Since the activation map is passed around the convolution layer, the convolution layer can
calculate the best image to add to the previous image, instead of calculating a new image. This
makes training easier, as the gradient landscape is more even [21]. Since the gradients are also
passed around the convolution operation in the backpropagation, the first layers of the network
have more accurate gradients. ResNet models are typically very deep, and thus take longer to
execute.

4.2
Tensorised Inference
As described in the beginning of this chapter, tensorised inference refers to the decomposition
of a fully trained network, that is then fine-tuned. The scheme is also described in the first list
in Chapter 1. The decomposition greatly decreases the number of parameters of a model and
should decrease the inference time.
4.2.1
CPU speedup via CD-decomposition
In [6], Lebedev et al. proposed a simple two-step tensorised inference method based on CD
decomposition and discriminative fine-tuning. Using a non-linear least square algorithm the
optimized 4D weight kernels are decomposed into a sum of a small number of rank 1 tensors
that can replace the full convolutional layer with a sequence of four convolutional layers. The
method has been tested on two CNNs; a 36-class character classification net (CharNet) and the
standard ImageNet (AlexNet). The results for the CharNet showed an 8.5 times CPU speedup,
with only minor accuracy drops (of 1%) and ImageNet deceased the CPU time for the second
layer by a factor 4, at the cost of 1% increase of the overall top-5 classification error [22]. Only
expensive CNN layers were decomposed.
4.2.2
Tucker2 decomposition
The decomposition method described in subsection 3.2.5 is investigated in detail in [5]. The
article uses the tensorised inference training scheme, and the ranks chosen for each kernel after
the initial training of the full model are determined by a method called global analytic variational
Bayesian matrix factorization. This is a key difference from the tensorised training scheme, where
the rank of each kernel is determined before training begins. This adds an additional benefit as
the model that is decomposed can be directly imported from a database of pre-trained models.

==PAGE:52==
After the decomposition step the accuracy of AlexNet on the ImageNet2012 dataset is calculated
to have dropped more than 50 %. This means that the parameters after the decomposition are
much worse in terms of accuracy than the full set of parameters. If the parameters after the
decomposition are considered as initialization parameters for a decomposed network, they are
excellent. The models were not able to train from scratch. Batch normalisation is not used,
which might be the cause of the inability to train.
4.3
Tensorised Training
While tensorised inference seeks to decompose the used filters after the training phase, tensorised
training does this in the training phase. The reason for doing this is reducing the inference time
additionally, such that the time it takes to decompose the (large) filters is removed from this
process.
Tensorised training is a lesser researched field than tensorised inference, which makes the liter-
ature search more limited.
4.3.1
Alternating Tensor Compose-Decompose Method
In [4], Lee et al. propose an alternating tensor compose-decompose method (ATCD) for the
training of low-rank CNNs. A rank-1 CNN is used which has a structure alternatively contain-
ing 3-D rank-1 filter and 1-D filters in the training stage and a 1-D structure in the testing stage.
It is assumed that the decomposed network is diﬀicult to train directly as it gets deeper, which
makes the gradient flow vanish faster as the path gets longer. Besides this it is assumed that the
reduction of parameters causes a different gradient flow than the normal CNN, which makes it
diﬀicult to find an appropriate solution for the parameters. The ATCD method is tested on four
public datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and Cat and Dog dataset) with four networks,
containing convolutional and linear layers, activated by ReLU, and it is regularized by dropout,
batch normalisation and max-pooling. By these tests it is shown that the method improves
validation accuracy with respect to the directly decomposed network and inference time with
respect to the standard CNN. The method has been elaborated in section 3.3 and will be further
elaborated in Chapter 5, as one of the proposed training approaches in this thesis is based on
the article.

7.1
Regular Training
This training method is the one that should give the best test accuracy, since the method trains
the full baseline network (BL). This network does not section each convolution into multiple
convolutions, and it has a larger parameter space to work within. A flowchart of the training
procedure is presented in Figure 7.2a.
Before training can occur, the parameters are initialized. This is an important step, since the
initial parameters determine the shape of the steps of the algorithm. When the training begins,
the entire training data set is used n times, once per epoch. In every epoch the data is sectioned
into batches, that are passed to the model. The prediction of the labels of these batches, and the
true batch labels, determines the loss function, which in turn determines how each parameter
affects the loss, namely ∂Ks,h(i, j)/∂L. These derivatives are then used to take steps. The step
method is not the steepest decent (SD), as depicted in the flowchart, but the Adam algorithm.
When the algorithm has run through all the data, it moves on to the next epoch, unless it is
the last epoch, in which case it exits the training phase.
Training the model in a parameter space with redundancy improves the training speed [5].
The result of this training method amongst the others will be compared at the end of this
chapter.
7.2
Direct Decomposition training
If the training of the full BL model is complete, then it is possible to estimate the 3 or 4 order
tensor that defines the set of filters or kernels for that layer by a rank R PARAFAC or Tucker2

and is not trained to work with those parameters specifically. This training method initializes
a model that has parameters such as a rank R decomposition of a full BL model and trains
those parameters from scratch. As stated previously the benefit of having fewer parameters and
shorter inference time is desirable. However, a consequence of the reduction of parameters is,
that it can result in a poor training and a low accuracy.

7.3
Back and Forth training
The BAF method is experimental, and it was developed in this thesis. The theoretical benefits
of the training method might be the most interesting property of the method, since it is very
unstable and expensive to run.
The method builds on the concepts introduced in Figure 7.1. The gradient flow is more accurate
in the full space, but the model is most valuable in the small space. The Back and Forth (BAF)
algorithm performs steps like the BL network, using gradients that are computed in the same
way. After each step each filter or kernel is decomposed using rank R 3D or 4D PARAFAC.
Then the decomposed elements are used again to produce a rank R tensor, that is dissimilar to
the initial filter or kernel. In the end, the final decompositions are extracted and used to test
the model. Under the training phase the method has many parameters to guide the gradients,
but each updated kernel or filter is suppressed into a smaller parameter space.
A flowchart of the training procedure is presented in Figure 7.2b.
An obvious drawback is the amount of decompositions required. A decomposition is computa-
tionally expensive, and has a degree of randomness associated with it. This can lead to a loss
of the accuracy gained from training, when PARAFAC optimizes the estimate of the order 3
or 4 tensors. The intricacies of the steps are lost when the decomposition-composition step is
applied.
7.4

This is the most advanced of the training methods. Since the model has been described in
detail in section 3.3, a short outline of the model is given here. The idea of the method is to
use the gradient step from the full model and take the actual step in the decomposed model
parameters. This is done in a more clever fashion than in the BAF method, as the parameters
of the decomposed model are stored throughout the training and updated outside the training
framework. The decomposed parameters are then used as in the BAF method to create the full
set of parameters of smaller ranks. This method works for filters (3D) and kernels (4D). The
full model is discarded, and the elements defining each kernel is kept and placed into a direct

Discussion
In this chapter the methods, models, results and approach of the thesis will be discussed. The
scope of the project and findings concerning decomposed convolutional neural networks will also
be considered.
One important finding is that direct decomposed convolutional neural networks train better
than expected.
The initial hypothesis made it clear that direct decomposed networks train
poorly since they are deeper, and that another method must be implemented to make them
reach decent accuracy [6][4][5]. The networks in [6] and [5] do not utilise batch normalisation
([17]) and specifically state, that the decomposed networks cannot train from scratch. If batch
normalisation was added to the studied networks this might not be the case. In [4] some direct
decomposed networks were able to train, while others were not. Initially batch normalisation
was not implemented in the models considered in this thesis. After finding that the ATCD
method did not train even on MNIST data, batch normalisation was utilised. More research
on the difference in decomposed networks with and without batch normalisation is required to
make decisive conclusions.
Since different data sets are used in combination with different models and different methods,
the number of models needed to be trained quickly increased. If only the CIFAR-100 data set
was used, and versions of the same network was studied, more time could have been dedicated
to testing the methods.
Testing the 4D decomposition methods using additional ranks, e.g 16, 32, 64 and 128, or using
a rank dependent on the size of the kernel tensor in each layer would be of interest. Although
the execution time also increases with the rank of the decomposition, testing higher ranks would
still be valuable, even though the execution time of the decomposed networks was higher than
the theoretical limit represented by the decrease in AM operations.
As stated in [5] the 1 × 1 convolutions, used in all decomposed networks structures, are cache
ineﬀicient, leading to increased execution time. This could explain the difference in expected
execution time and actual execution time.
The 4D decomposed network and the 3D decomposed networks have similar execution times,
even though the 4D decomposed networks have two 1 × 1 convolution operations, rather than
the one 1 × 1 convolution operation in the 3D decomposed networks. However the execution
times for all decomposition methods are similar. 1 × 3 and 3 × 1 convolutions are suspected to
be cache ineﬀicient as well, but this has to be examined further to reach decisive conclusions.
Timing execution of convolutional neural networks is not straightforward, and different meth-
ods had to be considered before reaching a timing method that was stable. Creating a neutral
environment, such that timing is consistent, proved a diﬀicult task. The execution times were
recorded using the HPC cluster of DTU for models trained on the CIFAR data set, and execu-
tion times were recorded on personal computers for the models trained on the MNIST data set.
The MNIST data recorded times might be less consistent since they are recorded on personal
computers, but still show significant differences, giving at least a general sense of the execution
time of the decomposed networks. The data set was loaded slightly differently, and it was not
able to execute on the high performance computing clusters.
Since the rank 1 direct 3D decomposition method performed well, testing the method for larger
ranks would only increase the number of hyper parameters and implementation details, and was
not therefore not prioritised.
The ATCD method was not implemented to train ResNet type structured CNN’s, even though
the model type might help train the parameters more steadily. This is left as future work.
Some studies investigate the decomposition in AlexNet [2]. In [6] the initial layers are decom-
3D Convolution and Compression in Deep-learning Backbone Architecture
64
==PAGE:81==
posed, and in [5] all layers are decomposed. It would be of interest to test the proposed methods
on the AlexNet in order to compare results with previous findings. This is also left as future
work.
A dynamic convergence stop criterion for creating box plots faster might help reach more quan-
titative results, since many methods reach their final training and validation accuracy so quickly.
The computer number format of the parameters of the model is a hyper-parameter that could
have been tuned. Since parameters determined by a smaller number of bits does not necessarily
decrease the accuracy, the potential speed up acquired by switching data type could be valuable.
Training time could be decreased by using different batch size and step size combinations. These
are additional hyper-parameters and the optimal combination is left as future work.
Many training schemes use learning rate schedulers, meaning that the learning rate changes
during training. This means that the final steps of the model training are small, and thus that
the model can settle into local minima more easily. It was found that direct decomposed models
might be more sensitive to step size, as each parameter affects the input image as a part of
a chain of convolutions. Models were only trained using steepest descent or the Adam step
method, and only with one learning rate each. These are also hyper-parameters that should be
researched further when studying direct decomposed models.
When placing the decomposed elements found by ATCD into a direct decomposed network,
the validation accuracy is changed. The operations, as seen by the forward passes in the back-
propagation examples, should be identical. It is believed that small differences introduced by
computer accuracy, propagate through the network, since each pixel value in the output image
of each layer in a direct decomposed network is a product of multiple addition-multiplication
operations. It turns out that the difference introduced has a regulatory effect in small convo-
lutional neural networks, since the validation accuracy for the direct decomposed network with
decomposed elements found by the ATCD method has a higher accuracy than the full network
with kernels having the same rank as decomposed elements, as seen in Table 9.4 and Table 9.5.
In deeper networks these differences propagate and introduces unexpected shifts in the input of
each layer, that is then propagated and contributes to a decrease in validation accuracy, as seen
in Table 9.6 and Table 9.7. Fine tuning a direct decomposed network, after placing the decom-
posed elements found by the ATCD method, will mitigate the decrease in validation accuracy.
The ATCD method did not perform as well as previously documented [4]. Since the method was
re-implemented, implementation errors or misinterpretations could be the cause of the difference
in effectiveness.
If a direct decomposed network is not able to train properly, the ATCD method could be used
at the end or the beginning of training to create new training schemes. These schemes should
be tested and evaluated in order to determine their effectiveness. However, the results of this
thesis have not found any evidence that suggests that a direct decomposed network cannot train
when assisted with batch normalisation, and it is shown that due to the decreased number of
parameters the decomposed networks train faster, and that the direct 3D decomposed network
can achieve higher final validation accuracy than the BL model.

Conclusion
This project aimed to implement and test a novel tensorised training scheme. In tensorised
training, the decomposed kernel elements that determine the convolutions in a CNN are trained
directly. The objective was to find a method that could maintain the accuracy of the full model,
while decreasing the execution time. This has been done by testing two decomposition methods,
the PARAFAC decomposition in 3D and 4D and Tucker2 decomposition in 4D, and four training
schemes, standard, direct, BAF and ATCD, on different CNNs. The standard training scheme
is the unaltered method for finding optimal weights of a CNN in the full parameter space, and
it is used to create a baseline, BL, network. The direct training scheme is a tensorised training
scheme, that trains the weights of a CNN where each convolution operation is decomposed into
a sequence of smaller convolution operations. The BAF training scheme is proposed by us, and
it determines the optimal weights of a decomposed network by taking steps in the full parameter
space and decomposing and composing the kernels after every step. The ATCD training scheme
likewise utilises the full parameter space and the decomposed parameter space, but takes steps in
the decomposed elements determined by the composition expression that relates the two spaces
[4]. The ATCD method was developed for the 4D PARAFAC rank R and the Tucker2 rank R
decomposition in this thesis. In [4] the weights of the filters in each kernel were shared. In this
thesis each filter is separated such that no weights are shared, except in subsection 9.4.1 where
the shared weights method is tested.
The direct training scheme for 3DCD decomposed networks was found to achieve a similar level
of accuracy as the baseline network, while decreasing the number of parameters, and in some
cases decreasing the execution time.
The scheme for rank 1 4DCD and Tucker2 performed
poorly, but decreased the number of parameters extensively. Performance of both 4D decom-
position methods increased when rank was set to 8, and the direct Tucker2 decomposed model
achieved only slightly lower accuracy for the MNIST data set than the BL model. The decrease
in execution time was positively related to the depth of the decomposed network. The fact
that deep direct decomposed networks were able to train, and were believed to be limited by
the number of parameters rather then depth, is in opposition to the hypothesis described in
Chapter 1, that states that the depth of the network increases the training diﬀiculty.
The BAF training scheme did not achieve impressive results, but it did showcase that a method
connecting the full and decomposed parameter spaces was possible. The method had no guar-
antee of convergence, but it was able to improve accuracy fast, when the parameter spaces
aligned.
The ATCD training scheme did not perform at the same level as the direct method, despite
utilising the full parameter space. The ATCD training scheme achieved lower accuracy than the
direct training scheme for every model-decomposition method combination. Training Tucker2
decomposed networks with ATCD yielded better final validation accuracy than training 4D
PARAFAC decomposed networks with ATCD. Similarly to the direct training scheme, the rank
8 4D decomposed models yielded higher accuracy than the rank 1 4D decomposed models. The
ATCD method with shared weight was tested and performed consistently worse than the ATCD
with no shared weights.
When weights for a deep decomposed CNN are found using a corresponding full network, such
as in the BAF training scheme and the ATCD training scheme, the final decomposed network
needs to be fine-tuned, because of propagating computational errors. In future work it is recom-
mended to take notice of the learning rate of direct decomposed networks, since a sequence of
small convolutions are prone to vanishing/exploding gradients. Additionally, it is recommended
to utilise batch normalisation between convolutional layers and activation functions, since mod-
3D Convolution and Compression in Deep-learning Backbone Architecture
66
==PAGE:83==
els tested in this thesis achieved much higher accuracy when this operation was implemented.
Before the training methods proposed in this thesis can be implemented on industry networks,
the application of vector kernels has to be optimised. If this is done, and the networks have been
constructed as in this thesis, then 3D direct PARAFAC decomposed networks can be trained
from scratch, and they are expected to have comparable accuracy to the full model. The 3D
dirct PARAFAC decomposed networks also require slightly less computation time and much less
memory, thus making it more feasible to run the networks on smaller devices, such as mobile
phones, or automated systems.
