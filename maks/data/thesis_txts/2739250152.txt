Abstract
This thesis presents the robust design and development of a HyperSpectral Imager (HSI) for
spaceborne remote sensing, meant to detect atmospheric elements such as water vapor and
oxygen for the HYPerspectral Smallsat for Ocean observation (HYPSO) research campaign.
The imager operates in the near-infrared spectrum, expanding the imaging capabilities of its
predecessors in terms of wavelength range. A state-of-the-art detector from SiOnyx was inte-
grated in the design, and the instrument was wavelength calibrated to well above 1 000 nm. A
key aspect of this work is the application of exact constraint design principles to ensure robust-
ness in the mechanical assemblies, particularly related to the extreme loads that are induced
during a launch procedure.
The design process involved extensive use of optical modeling software (ANSYS Zemax Op-
ticStudio) to assess the sensitivity of optical part tolerances. The geometric image aberrations
smile and keystone were selected as the figures of merit for the analysis. The sensitivities were
quantified through simulations and further validated by experimental testing of a functional
prototype. The obtained results facilitated further concept generation to increase robustness of
the mechanical sub-assemblies in the instrument. A set of requirements and design principles
were formulated to form a solid foundation for further research and development.
Collaboration with experts from OpenHSI and the European Space Agency was crucial in
achieving accurate interpretations of the model results. The resulting prototype not only meets
the stringent performance requirements for spaceborne applications but also addresses the
need for mechanical stability and minimal variation in part positioning. The findings of this
work demonstrate that the robust design principles applied here can be effectively translated
to other hyperspectral instruments, contributing to more precise acquisition of hyperspectral
data for the HYPSO mission to aid environmental monitoring.

Introduction
Monitoring our vast oceans and terrestrial landscapes is more important than ever in aiding the
environmental research of the planet. Remote sensing systems, particularly those deployed on
satellites, provide a repetitive and consistent view of the Earth that is invaluable to observing
short-term and long-term changes and the impact of human activities [1]. The remarkable
orbital speed of a satellite allows for quick re-visit times over specified geographical areas,
opening for data collection in almost real-time.
Remote sensing is the activity of measuring some object properties using data acquired at a
distance, either from an aircraft or a satellite [1]. This is typically performed by capturing
images of a scene on the ground having two spatial dimensions, rendering the image as two-
dimensional. In hyperspectral imaging, an additional spectral dimension is added, where each
pixel not only represents spatial coordinates but also contains a spectrum of light intensity
across various wavelengths. Most of us are familiar with the concept of representing color us-
ing Red-Green-Blue (RGB). Instead of being limited to discrete color channels, a hyperspectral
camera can capture a large range of colors from the observed scene, often hundreds of wave-
lengths.
Hyperspectral imaging unlocks detailed information about marine ecosystems. Their dynamic
behavior can be detected and estimated from Chlorophyll-a (Chl-a) concentrations, further
used as an indication of the phytoplankton biomass in the oceans [2]. As with all other or-
ganisms and materials, Chl-a has its own unique absorption feature meaning that it absorbs
certain wavelengths, leaving a characteristic signature in the transmitted light that can be de-
tected.
1.1
The HYPSO Campaign
This task of monitoring the oceanic water bodies is one of the research endeavors at NTNU
Small Satellite Lab (NTNU SmallSat Lab), located in Trondheim, Norway. It is an institution
that mainly develops and builds hyperspectral instruments, including essential electronic com-
ponents, meant for an operational lifetime in space. These are integral parts in the research
campaign called the HYPerspectral Smallsat for Ocean observation (HYPSO). The main objec-
tive of the HYPSO mission is to provide low latency hyperspectral data products to support
marine environmental monitoring [3]. It focuses on detecting, classifying and monitoring al-
gal blooms in the oceans and along coast lines [2]. Given the broad versatility of these data
products, they can be beneficial for a range of other applications as well.
On January 13 in 2022, the HYPSO-1 Cube Satellite (CubeSat) was launched into space with the

1.2
Motivation
The main payload onboard both HYPSO-1 and HYPSO-2 is the HSI Version 6 (V6). This instru-
ment is capable of detecting light in the Visible (VIS) portion of the electromagnetic spectrum,
similar to what human eyes can see, and extends slightly into the Near-Infrared (NIR) region.
Its full spectral range is 400 – 800 nm which is achieved by a transmission grating design.
A third satellite, for now nicknamed HYPSO-3, is in the early design and planning phase. There
is a desire to reach further into the NIR region to access more detailed and precise information
in the collected data. Professor Fred Sigernes at the University Centre of Svalbard (UNIS) has
proposed an optical design for a new instrument, named HSI V7, that is expected to detect
a bandwidth of 600 – 1200 nm. The expanded range made available by the new instrument
is illustrated in Figure 1.2. Here, the total bandwidth is anticipated to be widely broadened
by the addition of the V7 with some overlap into the VIS spectrum. The V7 is planned to be
implemented as a payload in the future satellite HYPSO-3.

A special type of detectors, also called image sensors, are required to succeed in detecting NIR
light. Conventional Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor (CMOS) detectors have lim-
ited performance at wavelengths beyond the VIS due to silicon being natively insensitive to
NIR, and the typical thicknesses of active silicon material [5]. SiOnyx LLC offers a new state-
of-the-art detector and claims it can detect the anticipated range illustrated in Figure 1.2 while
keeping read noise low even in low-light environments. Hence, the image detector Sionyx
XQE-1350 will be integrated in the HSI V7 instrument.
A broader wavelength range, especially into the NIR part of the electromagnetic, can facilitate
Atmospheric Correction (AC) of the captured hyperspectral data. The signal that is received
and measured by an HSI in orbit is referred to as the Top-of-Atmosphere (ToA) radiance. When
observing bodies of water from space, the water-leaving radiance contains vital information
about their composition. Unfortunately, less than 15% of the measured signal is attributed to
this light upwelling from beneath the ocean surface, depending on the spectral range and ocean

light is scattered and absorbed along various paths before reaching the HSI instrument. The
purpose of the AC procedure is to remove the atmospheric scattering and surface effects from

The focus in this thesis is to realize the optical design by Sigernes in terms of a functional

further quantified.
The future HYPSO-3 CubeSat is planned to carry four instruments, doubling the number hosted

optical designs and components, it is anticipated that the sources of variation in their embod-

How can the mechanical integration of a new detector housing in the HSI V7 be solved, and can

Theoretical Background
In this section, the theoretical foundations that are critical to the development and function-
ality of the V7 instrument are presented. This includes both mechanical principles and how
misalignment of parts affect image quality, and optical theory that dictate the behavior of light
as it passes through the system. The combination of these two disciplines form the basis of
optomechanics, the field that integrates mechanical and optical engineering.
The optical design will be presented in the following. As mentioned in the introduction, this
design was developed by Professor F. Sigernes at UNIS. It features an almost linear optical
path, which serves as the basis for the mechanical part configuration. Concurrently with this
project, Sigernes conceptualized a similar but more compact design that bends the optical path
by 90◦using an optical prism. The findings related to the embodiment design and instrument
performance are detailed in the unpublished article ”A Near Infra-Red Hyper Spectral Imager” by
Sigernes et al. These findings will be compared with the results presented in this thesis.
2.1
Optical Design
The optical diagram of the HSI V7 is shown in Figure 2.1. The layout closely resembles that of
the V6, with the main difference being a steeper diffraction angle, denoted as β. The main parts
are the front imaging objective (L0), high-precision slit (S), collimating objective (L1), blazed
transmission grating (G), focusing objective (L2), and finally the Focal-Plane Array (FPA). The
latter refers to the rectangular array of pixels that reside in the focal plane of the focusing
objective. For clearing up, the detector refers to the complete electronics assembly and includes
the FPA. D0 and D1 are the glass lens diameters. Before examining the design in detail, the
term focal plane must be defined. It refers to the plane where light, after passing through a lens,
is focused and converges to a point.

Incoming light passes through the front lens objective, L0, and is focused onto the focal plane
at the slit, S. The divergent light that exits the slit is then collimated by the collimating lens,
L1, meaning that the diverging rays from the slit are refracted to make them parallel. The
diffraction grating, G, then disperses the collimated light into its constituent spectral compo-
nents. Lastly, these are focused onto the FPA by the focusing lens objective, L2. All three lens
objectives have a fixed focal length of f0 = f1 = f2 = 50 mm.
The amount of light that can pass through an objective is governed by the size of the aperture
opening. This size is called clear aperture diameter, and is commonly adjustable in standard
lens objectives made for commercial use. The aperture stop, AS, should define the maximum
size of the light cone and be located at the grating [8]. The f-number, usually denoted f/# or
f/number, is a standard parameter that quantifies the aperture size of a lens relative to the focal
length, providing a measure of light-gathering ability [9]. Its relationship is expressed as

2.1.1
Diffraction of Light
One of the key components in the optical design is the transmissive diffraction grating. That is
a square piece of glass that is mechanically blazed to achieve a triangular groove profile on one
side. The grating in this design has been blazed with 300 lines/mm, so that the groove spacing,
a, is approximately 3.33 µm wide. The triangular profile has an inclination of γ = 30.7◦. Figure
2.2 demonstrates how a ray of light is diffracted through the grating.
The incoming, or incident, light of wavelength λ has some angle α while the diffracted light ex-
its the grating with a new angle β. The light is diffracted simultaneously in multiple directions,
depending on its spectral order, k. The grating equation describes the relationship between
these parameters [10],
k λ = d (sin α + sin β)
(2.2)
In the HSI V7, the center wavelength is set to λc = 900 nm at the first spectral order. The
incoming light travels along the grating normal, meaning that α = 0◦. Solving for β in Equation
2.2 yields a diffraction angle of approximately 15.7◦.

It is a common issue that the continuous spectrum (or bandwidth) from the first spectral order,
which the instrument is designed to examine, is contaminated by light from higher orders of
diffraction. There is often some overlap between the spectra, especially causing second order
effects but can be tackled by using optical filters on the HSI. There are also correction schemes
to identify and correct for the second order effects [2]. This topic will not be further covered in
the thesis.

There is a trade-off between cost and the spectral information that can be obtained by the in-

such as oxygen and water vapor in the atmosphere or not.
2.1.3
C-Mount Optics
The mounting interface in the EO lens objective is a C-mount, often found in machine vision

Wavelength Calibration
Calibration is the process of converting sensor specific units, such as pixel number or sensor
count, to physical units, such as wavelength or radiance [2]. There are a number of calibra-
tion procedures that should be applied to the instrument prior to launch in order to fine-tune
performance parameters. Among these, a clear distinction can be made between mechanical
adjustments of optical components (described in Section 4), and data-related calibrations such
as wavelength calibration.
A fundamental calibration procedure in hyperspectral instruments is determining the pixel to
wavelength relationship. The FPA consists of a series of rows and columns of photon-sensitive
pixels, where the pixel columns are parallel with the FPA height. As the diffraction grating

on the FPA, the relationship between wavelength and pixel column index can be determined.

spatial image. The relationship can further be approximated using a second or third order

2.7
Types of Optical Perturbations
When an optical component within the hyperspectral imaging system is perturbed, the pro-
duced image on the FPA will be affected. The datum axis for these perturbations is known
as the optical axis, a straight line that passes through the geometric center of a spherical lens.
In optical theory, this axis is commonly referred to as the principal axis and aligns with the
Z-axis in a three-dimensional coordinate system [18]. The X- and Y-axes form a plane that is
perpendicular to the optical axis. The image plane, or the physical FPA on the detector, coin-
cides with this XY-plane at the end of the optical train. Further, there are mainly three types of
perturbations that can occur, as shown in Figure 2.11.
Despace
Decenter
Tip/tilt
Figure 2.11: Terms for various perturbations of optical elements in the instrument. The gray area
represents the nominal position of a single element. Adapted from [18].
A round optical element can experience three types of perturbations: axial (despace), lateral
(decenter), and angular (tip/tilt) [18]. In mechanical terms, these misalignments correspond
to translations and rotations along the three axes of a three-dimensional coordinate system.
Despace refers to translation along the optical axis, moving out of the XY-plane, while decenter
involves translation within the XY-plane in two directions. Since a round lens is typically insen-
sitive to rotation about the optical axis, optical theory defines tip and tilt as rotations about the
X and Y axes. For clarity, the width direction of the FPA aligns with the X-axis, and the height
aligns with the Y-axis. Given that neither the diffraction grating nor the FPA in the V7 optical
system are round or insensitive to such rotations, this type of rotation is specifically referred to
as roll.

4.1
Selecting Figures of Merit
In tolerancing hyperspectral imaging systems, there is a need to go beyond the common met-
rics made available by commercial optical design software as those may not reflect directly the
important aspects of instrument performance [21]. The recommended limits of smile and key-
stone, that cause curvature and skewing of spectral lines, have been defined in literature. M. B.
Henriksen reported that the general requirement for HYPSO instruments is a pixel shift of less
than 1 pixel for smile and keystone, after correction [2]. The shifts are naturally larger before
corrections are applied, and it is preferable to achieve as low values of smile and keystone in
the raw data as possible.
These two figures of merit have been chosen as the measurable output response of the Zemax
simulations and experimental testing. The reasoning is that they can be quantified with both
the functional prototype in a test setup, and through simulation with the optical model. In
this dual approach, the model can be validated by the experimental data. Nevertheless, the
formulation of the requirement, stating less than 1 pixel after correction, must be addressed.
The smile and keystone can be ”unwarped” through a Python distortion model using raw data
taken with the instrument [22]. This correction procedure has been applied to the experimental
data in the test setup to align with the requirement formulation, as described in Section 4.3.3.
Other common metrics used in sensitivity analyses and tolerancing of hyperspectral instru-
ments are three types of response functions. These are termed cross-track, along-track and
spectral response functions, and abbreviated as CRF, ARF, and SRF, respectively. Recreating
these metrics experimentally and through simulation was deemed as too intricate considering
the time frame of the project. Details about the response functions can be found in [21] and
[23].

4.2
Ray Tracing in Zemax OpticStudio
Zemax is a powerful optical modeling software used for a range of applications within optical
engineering. Rays are used to model the propagation of light through optical systems such
as an HSI, from a source point and on to the final image surface [24]. This is referred to as
first-order or geometrical optics.
Zemax offers two modes of ray tracing, either sequential or non-sequential. In sequential mode,
rays are limited to propagating from one object to the next through a predefined sequence of
surfaces, hitting each surface only once [25]. Non-sequential ray tracing implies that there is
no predefined sequence of surfaces which rays that are being traced must hit. While imaging
systems are well described by sequential surfaces, a non-sequential mode allows for analysis
of stray light and scattering [26].

4.2.3
Quantifying Smile and Keystone Shift
The groove alignment of the diffraction grating has a dimensional tolerance of ± 0.5◦. Since
the model that had been developed for generating spectral lines seemed to function properly,
perturbations of optical components could now be carried out. The grating in the model was
therefore perturbed to its worst-case tolerance limits. That means that the etched grooves in
the grating was rotated ± 0.5◦about the optical axis.
In total, three sets of macro runs were performed, one for the nominal design and two for the
positive and negative worst-case limits. As described earlier, smile is the change of central
wavelength as a function of field position, while keystone is change in magnification as a func-
tion of wavelength [2]. The obtained data could now be processed using Python to quantify
the spectral and spatial misregistration.
Smile
The smile effect could be computed by finding the difference between the maximum and min-
imum along the spectral dimension for each spectral line. This step was repeated 18 times for
each spectral line within the set. Further, the spectral line with the maximum coordinate shift
due to smile was identified. Lastly, the coordinate shifts of the 18 lines within the set were av-
eraged to find a common average value. This procedure was repeated three times for the three
sets of macro runs.
Keystone
In order to quantify the effect of keystone, a more extensive method had to be applied. If the
spectral lines can be thought of as columns, the rows across the spectral lines were used to find
their deviation in the spatial direction. One row at any given spatial height should ideally be
perfectly horizontal. Directly using the image coordinates would not capture the full keystone
magnitude. Therefore, each row were linearly approximated using the numpy.polyfit()
function to generate 115 rows that spanned along the entire width of the FPA.
Figure 4.5 displays the generated rows, where the row position is represented by the color bar
on the right. In similarity with the smile computation, the difference between the maximum
and minimum spatial height along each row was identified. This was repeated for all 115 rows
to find a maximum shift, and then averaged to find a common average value. Again, this entire
procedure was performed three times for the three sets of macro runs.

4.3
Model Validation using Experimental Data
A test procedure was set up in the optical lab to characterize smile and keystone of the HSI V7
experimentally. The method is based on generating a set of Ground Control Points (GCPs) in
the spatial-spectral frame [1] using a large collimator, rotation stage, spectral lamp and a 3D

max simulations, that can reveal the spatial and spectral shift due to smile and keystone. M. B.
Henriksen has described the method of creating these points in full detail in [2]. A prerequisite
is to first determine the relationship between wavelength and pixel columns of the FPA.
4.3.1

4.3.2
Calibrating Mechanical Parameters
Prior to the experimental setup, including the process of wavelength calibration, a set of me-
chanical adjustments were carried out. The first step was to achieve correct spectral focus so
that the spectral lines in the spatial-spectral frame were as narrow as possible, as described in
Section 2.8. The second step involved rotating the slit and FPA to achieve straigt spectral lines.
The slit tube is designed to allow for such rotation of the slit post-assembly.
Each lens objective has one focus ring that can be twisted to adjust the focus. In theory, the focus
should be set to infinity for all three objectives, but fine-adjustment is needed after assembly
so that the incoming light coincides with the slit focal plane, and the focal plane of the FPA.
By trial and error, it was observed that only the collimating objective and the focus objective
(L1 and L2) affected the width of the spectral lines. Any focus setting other than infinity on
the focus objective (L2) would widen spectral lines, while the focus setting on the collimating
objective could be adjusted to find the ideal spectral focus. Since focus adjustment of the front
objective (L0) did not affect the spectral line width, it was assumed that this setting would
determine the spatial focus. This observation aligned with the spatial focusing procedure that
is documented in the internal ”HYPSO-2 Flight Model (FM) As-Built” report [30]. Further efforts
were not made to find the ideal spatial focus, as it would not impact the gathered data in the
upcoming experimental test setup.
In addition to setting the correct focus, a calibration of the slit and FPA was conducted. This
process involved rotating the high-precision slit within the slit tube and the FPA mounted
on the black SiOnyx development board, both around the optical axis. The objective was to
achieve straight spectral lines that align parallel to the spatial axis. A critical aspect of this cali-
bration is that two parameters, the slit height and the FPA height, should ideally be positioned
relative to a shared datum, which is the vertical grooves in the diffraction grating.
However, when rotating the slit and the FPA, it was impossible to determine which component
was misaligned relative to this common reference. It was observed that when both were ro-
tated together in the same direction and deliberately set to a misaligned position relative to the
datum, the spectral lines retained their spectral positions but became spatially skewed relative
to each other. It is assumed that rotational misalignment between the slit, grating, and FPA can
cause pixel misregistration, and should be addressed. Nevertheless, the two components were
positioned as accurately as possible, and the straightness of the spectral lines was validated by
confirming that the top and bottom of the spectral lines appeared at similar spectral positions.

Concept Generation
This section of concept generation for the HSI V7 is two parted. The identified design flaws
from the HSI V6 design that can introduce variation will be presented first, followed by a series
of concepts that will mitigate this variation. Since the V6 and V7 are nearly identical optically,
the mechanical design features can be inherited and further improved. It is beneficial for fur-
ther reading to define the term variation. In Robust Design Methodology (RDM), sources of
variation for design parameters can be attributed to five categories: production and assembly
tolerances, load deformations, variation due to ambient conditions (such as changes in tem-
perature or humidity), and variation over time (such as wear or creep) [31]. In the following,
identified sub-problems that reduce robustness are outlined along with some concrete require-
ments for the new design.
5.1
Identification of Sub-Problems
5.1.1
Detector Housing
Section 4 provided valuable insights into how performance parameters, such as smile and key-
stone, are impacted by production tolerances. As noted in the closing remarks, even minor
variations in groove alignment can degrade instrument performance, which highlights how
exceptionally sensitive the system is to variation. This sensitivity indicates that the optical sys-
tem cannot tolerate a detector housing that is simply fixed in place using screws or bolts, as
the cumulative effect of stacking tolerances would exceed the allowable range for performance
degradation. Consequently, it is required that the detector and its FPA, the rectangular array of
pixels, can be calibrated in terms of its rotational position either during or post-assembly.
The instrument is exposed to extreme loads during the launch procedure, in terms of shock
and vibration. Due to the series of meticulous calibration steps that are performed prior to
launch, any movement of the optical components during this procedure would compromise
the accuracy of the hyperspectral data. This has already caused errors for HSI V6 onboard the
HYPSO-1 CubeSat. Henriksen et al. has reported a noticeable spatial pixel shift of 7 pixels
during the in-orbit calibration [32]. Upon examination of the data it was found that either the
entrance slit or the full detector housing has moved and caused the misalignment.
The interface between the detector housing in the V6 and the rest of the optical system is only
a threaded connection. A thread has one Degree of Freedom (DOF) and that is rotation. This
underconstrained design flaw in the V6 directly conflicts with the calibration mechanism for
the high-precision slit that was implemented in the HYPSO-2 version of the instrument. Any

If the loads from shock and vibration during launch exceed these frictional forces, the grating

This is made possible due to the slot being deeper than the thickness of the grating, likely a

diffraction grating along the optical axis, preferably with some elastic material.
5.2
Setting New Requirements
To summarize the information presented above, the various requirements are compiled into a
list. The first requirement applies to the detector housing, and although the they seem contra-
dicting, they are complementary. In (1a), the housing and its FPA must be capable of rotation
about the optical axis during calibration. However, once the calibration process is complete,
the detector housing must be securely fixed, ensuring that all its DOFs are constrained. One
requirement that is not illustrated or touched upon above, is that the machined platform must
constrain the front optical train from slid axially along the V-groove.
(1) Detector housing
(1a) It shall be possible to rotate the FPA about the optical axis during or after instru-
ment assembly to compensate for the groove alignment tolerance of the diffraction
grating.
(1b) All degrees of freedom for the detector housing shall be constrained to prevent any
launch-induced rotations.
(2) High-precision slit
(2a) The high-precision slit shall be exactly constrained without application of epoxy ad-
hesives.
(3) Diffraction grating
(3a) The diffraction grating shall be exactly constrained to prevent translation along the
optical axis.
(4) Instrument Platform
(4a) The instrument platform shall constrain any axial movement of the front optical
train along direction of the optical axis.

and washers.
The detector, containing the photon-sensitive FPA, has been mounted in a new housing in the
back of the optical train. The focus objective is mounted to the housing by C-mount thread,
as in the previous design. In this updated design, a mounting surface has been added to the
machined platform that allows for the detector housing to be secured after calibration. This sur-
face features two clearance holes, machined to either a normal or coarse tolerance and enables
a certain range of rotation to compensate for the groove alignment tolerance of the diffraction
grating during assembly.
To align with the typically used fastener dimension for the V6, two long metric M4 machine
screws can be used to secure the housing. During the calibration procedure, which involves
finding the ideal rotation of the housing about the optical axis, the flat surface of the housing
is aligned against the platform mounting surface. Since the lens objective and the housing
is mounted together, no decenter from the optical axis will be introduced as the objective is
resting in the V-groove during calibration. When the calibration procedure is finished a set of
nuts and washers (for example Nord-Lock washers) can be used to axially fasten the housing.

The rotation procedure has been illustrated in Figure 5.6. Metric M4 machine screws have
a normal and coarse clearance hole tolerance of 4.5 and 4.8 mm, respectively. According to
ISO standard 279:1979, three tolerance classes exist for clearance holes for M4 fasteners - fine,
normal and coarse. In the proposed design, these holes are parallel and their centers are spaced
43 mm apart. When rotated during calibration, the two threaded bolts can move up and down
along the path of a thought circle, limited by the clearance holes. This range is indicated by
the purple area, and can be calculated using simple trigonometry. Given a normal clearance
tolerance, the rotational range of the FPA is ±0.67◦while a coarse tolerance yields a range of
±1.07◦, assuming a metric M4 dimension is used for the bolt. An assembled and exploded

5.3.1
Assembling the High-Precision Slit
When transitioning away from the use of epoxy adhesives for mounting the high-precision slit,
two-part assembly can instead be used. A new design is suggested in Figure 5.9, where the
round foil with the slit cutout is placed in a counterbore. This resembles the existing solution,
but instead of applying epoxy the foil is compressed into the counterbore by a protruding lip
on a second disk. The two parts are compressed together by three metric M2 machine screws.
Figure 5.9: Slit assembly without epoxy application.
This solution opts for a slightly more cumbersome assembly process. In the current design,
one disc is placed radially into the the slit tube and clamped down by the top bracket, allowing
for rotation of the slit post-assembly. In order to avoid a tolerance stack of the front and rear
disc, and the slit foil, one disc is inserted radially while the other is inserted axially. Finally, the
two parts are fixed by the three screws. The disc that features the protruding lip has a smaller
diameter so that it can fit through the threaded part of the slit tube. This is illustrated in the
exploded view in Figure 5.10.

The idea becomes even clearer from the section view of the assembled slit tube in Figure 5.11.
Only the disc with the largest diameter is seated in the internal groove. It is crucial that this

is to tolerance the fit as H7/h6, which is a commonly used as a sliding or transition fit which

5.3.2
Grating Cassette with Exact Constraints
The grating cassette assembly remains similar to the V6 design, but has been added with three
Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer (EPDM) rubber gaskets. The rear part of the cassette has
three small notches or slots where the gaskets can be inserted. Assembling the cassette involves
placing the diffraction grating into its slot first, and clamping it into one corner as before. The
rear part with the three gaskets can be laid flat on a table to avoid them from falling out, and
the front cassette part can be aligned on top. Lastly, the machine screws are inserted to secure
the two parts of the grating cassette. It is recommended that the use of dowel pins, as done for
the V7 functional prototype, is continued for the production of the final cassette. This allowed
for precise mating between the front and rear part.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
6
Results and Discussion
In this thesis, a series of mechanical design solutions has been proposed to increase the instru-
ment robustness. The main objective, in practical terms, was to conceptualize a new sensor
housing that could mount the SiOnyx detector XRB-1350 and its FPA. An extensive concept
generation was carried out, capturing and correcting former design flaws, so that the overall
robustness of the instrument was increased as described in Section 5.
6.1
Proof of Concept
One of the final test setups was to capture hyperspectral data cubes with the HSI V7 instrument.
The camera was placed on a rotational stage for telescopes on a rooftop of NTNU SmallSat
Lab, and scanned across Trondheim city. The purpose was to mainly capture the fog, skies and
clouds to determine if the acquired data showed prominent lines of water vapor and oxygen.
Their presence was confirmed by assessing individual spatial-spectral frames. In Figure 6.1, a
two-dimensional spatial composite of the captured data cube is displayed. The image shows
Trondheim city and the fjord and surrounding terrain. Three colors have been attributed to the
NIR image in order to differentiate the spatial features in the scene.
Figure 6.1: Pushbroom NIR image of Trondheim city and the Trondheim fjord.

The new design of a detector housing that is proposed in the conceptualization part of this the-
sis allows for rotational adjustments of the FPA while ensuring mechanical integrity once fixed
to the optical train by the mounting surfaces. However, prototyping is necessary to validate

RQ2:

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
This thesis has successfully demonstrated the design and development of a robust hyperspec-
tral pushbroom instrument for spaceborne remote sensing applications. Through the appli-
cation of exact constraint design principles, the mechanical assemblies were engineered to
withstand the harsh loads of a space launch while maintaining precise alignment and mini-
mal variation in part positioning. The optical performance of the imager, particularly in the
near-infrared spectrum, was validated through extensive simulations in Zemax OpticStudio,
ensuring its capability to detect atmospheric elements such as water vapor and oxygen.
7.1
Future Work
The following points or tasks have been selected for future work:
• Find a working method of capturing 12-bit data with the SiOnyx development kit, as only
8-bit data could be captured during the testing phases.
• Characterize instrument capabilities when using KOWA lens objectives instead of the Ed-
mund Optics #67-717 objectives and see if longer wavelengths can be detected compared
to the range demonstrated in this thesis.
• Investigate if a different approach can be developed for calibrating the rotational position
of the slit and FPA relative to the grating.
• Characterize the HSI V7 instrument more extensively to see if the water vapor and oxygen
bands in the hyperspectral data can be used for atmospheric correction.
