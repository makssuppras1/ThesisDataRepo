Abstract
Tilting-pad journal bearings (TPJB) are commonly used for applications with high load and
rotational speed due to their excellent stability properties.
However, does the TPJB lack
damping at high rotational speeds. To reduce vibrations and increase the operational range,
Ilmar Santos developed the idea of adjustable radial injection of high-pressurized oil into the
clearance between the bearing and rotor to change the dynamic properties of the TPJB. This
master thesis treats the TPJB with a constant radial oil injection and revolves around a single
degree of freedom (SDOF) test rig with a 2-pad TPJB with load on pad (LOP) with a constant
radial oil injection.
The objective was to model and validate a mathematical model of the SDOF test rig with
a constant radial oil injection, where the DOFs and dynamics of the pads of the TPJB are
included. Unfortunately, it has not been possible to validate the mathematical model due to
poor disagreement of the FRFs and the dynamic properties between the mathematical model
and the experimental tests.
Some simplified mathematical models have been made to find
potential errors in the modeling, where different degrees of freedom of the pads had been
removed, but without any breakthroughs.
By examining the dynamics of the 2-pad LOP TPJB, was there from an analytical inves-
tigation found that it is sufficient to model the TPJB without the DOFs of the pads in the
x-direction to capture all the dynamics of a 2-pad LOP TPJB.
From analytical investigations, with the DOFs of the pads included in the mathematical
model, was odd dynamic behavior detected with configurations with a large amount of damping
compared to stiffness. The odd dynamics that occur with configurations whit a large amount
of damping compared to stiffness can the author not explain. Especially since the dynamic
coefficients calculated from the mathematical of the TPJB are contradictory to the FRFs and
dynamics properties calculated.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Rotating machinery has been crucial for technological developments, and the bearing is the
key component that supports the rotor while ensuring free rotation of the rotor to transfer
energy. There is a wide variety of bearings, and the most common in everyday life is the roller
bearing. Nevertheless, for applications with high loads and rotational speed are hydrodynamic
bearings preferred, mainly because the oil film provides damping, thus reducing the vibrations.
The most simple type of hydrodynamic bearing is a journal bearing, where the rotor (journal)
rotates in a slightly bigger bore. The rotor is lifted by the hydrodynamic pressure, which is
generated by the oil forced through the converging gap of the journal and housing of the journal.
However, when increasing the angular velocity the journal bearing will become unstable due to
the cross-couplings of the fluid film forces acting on the journal. The instability issue from the
cross-couplings of the fluid film forces can be fixed with the design of the tilting-pad journal
bearing (TPJB). The TPJB allows the pads of the bearing to tilt, dramatically reducing the
cross-couplings of the fluid forces acting on the journal and thereby the oil caused instability.
The damping of the TPJB is however decreased when the angular velocity is increased, this
magnifies the vibrations and reduces the operational range of the machinery.
To increase the operational range did Santos [7] in 1994 developed the idea of adjustable
radial injection of high-pressurized oil into the clearance between bearing and rotor to change
the dynamic properties of the TPJB and reduce vibrations. This idea of hybrid lubrication
combines the effect of hydrodynamic lubrication from the TPJB and static lubrication from
the injection of high-pressurized oil. Furthermore, applying sensors to detect the displacement
and acceleration of the rotor makes it possible for the TPJB to function with active lubrication.
For the TPJB with active lubrication is the injection of oil controlled by a high response servo
valve, which with a feedback control loop makes it possible to control the journal bearing
actively. This TPJB with radial injection of high-pressurized oil is the focus of the master
thesis.
Since Ilmar Santos first developed the idea of radial oil injection in 1994, has numerous
contributors been involved in the development. This master’s thesis is built on the basics of
work from Santos and co-authors and the research carried out by previous bachelor, master,
and Ph.D. theses.
1.2
Project scope
This master thesis aims to model and validate the TPJB with a constant radial oil injection of
the single degree of freedom (SDOF) test rig placed at DTU, where the DOFs and dynamics
of the pads of the TPJB are included in the mathematical model. The SDOF test rig is a rigid
rotor-lever system that rotates around ball bearings at one end and is supported by a TPJB with
radial oil injection at the other end. The TPJB for the test rig is a 2-pad journal bearing with
load on pad (LOP). The idea of a mathematical model is to be a ”mathematical twin” which
accurately can represent the dynamics test rig. Experimental tests are performed to validate
the mathematical model, where FRFs and the dynamic properties of the mathematical model
and the experimental tests are compared.
The mathematical model of the TPJB is multiple degrees of freedom (DOF) system, where
the DOF and dynamics of the pads are included. The mathematical model of the TPJB is a

TEST RIG
2
Test Rig
In this section is a description of the test rig presented. The test rig was designed by Santos
and Nielsen [5] in 2010 and has been modified several times since. This test rig’s original and
primary purpose was to validate the mathematical model of the tilting pad journal bearing
TPJB with radial oil injection and determine its parameters.
The test rig is a rigid rotor-lever system with a single degree of freedom of rotation around
its pivot. In Fig. 2.1 can a sketch of the test rig be seen, and in Fig. 2.2 can a picture of the test
rig be seen. The rotor-lever system rotates around ball bearings at one end and is supported
by a TPJB with two tilting pads at the other end. At the end of the arm can a static load
Fload be applied to increase the load in the bearing. The static load is applied by a turnbuckle
and measured by a force transducer. If no static load is applied, is the turnbuckle decoupled.
At the of the arm, can there be added multiple discs to increase the inertia of the system. For
excitation of the system is a shaker and a force transducer placed at the end of the arm. The
rotor-lever system is coupled to an electrical motor that drives the rotor through a pulley.
For the injection of high-pressurized oil into the clearance between the bearing and rotor are
an orifice machined on the pad surface. The injection of the high-pressurized oil is controlled
by a high-response servo valve, which is connected to a high-pressure pump unit (0-20 Bar).
For passive lubrication of the journal bearing, low-pressurized oil is added via a low-pressure
pump unit (0-2 Bar). The test rig is equipped with multiple sensors: which are listed below
and can be seen in Fig. 2.1:
• High-sensitive displacement sensor located at the end of the arm.
• Accelerometer located located at the end of the arm.
• Pressure sensors for measuring the oil injection pressure at lower and upper pad.
• Thermocouples for measuring the oil injection temperature at lower and upper pad.
• Force transducers for shaker and static load.
• Encoder for rotor position and speed.

2.1
Tilting-Pad Journal Bearing With Radial Oil Injection
The key component of the test rig is the TPJB with radial injection of high-pressurized oil.
The TPJB is a 2-pad load on pad bearing. The pads of the TPJB are made of brass and lie on
steel pivots, which the pads tilt around. Allowing the pads to tilt reduces the cross-couplings of
the fluid forces acting on the journal. By reducing the cross-couplings of the fluid forces acting
on the journal, oil caused instability decreased. The pads have an offset of 0.5, which implies
that the pads tilt centrally along the length of the pads. The offset of 0.5 allows the rotor to
run clockwise and counterclockwise, but this lowers the load bearing capacity. The preload of
the bearing impacts the bearing dynamics coefficients. For chaining the preload, can different
sizes of spacing plates be used in the house of the bearing. The preload factor PL is defined
from the ratio of the machined radial clearance Cp and the assembly radial clearance Cb:
PL = 1 −Cb
Cp
(2.1)
An increased preload creates a pad film thickness with stronger converging and diverging zones.
This increases the bearing stiffness and prevents unloading of pads. Unloading of the pads can
lead to pad flutter, which can result in bearing failure [10]. In Table 2.1 can the properties for
the TPJB of the test rig be seen.
The TPJB of the test rig can function with passive lubrication, where only low-pressurized
oil is added and where it purely works as hydrodynamic lubrication. Hydrodynamic lubrication
relies on the relative motion between the journal and the housing of the journal. The hydrody-
namic pressure is generated when the non-slip condition forces the oil through the converging
gap of the journal and housing of the journal, the fluid film thickness gradient generates the
hydrodynamic pressure, which levitates the journal. When increasing the rotational speed, the
journal goes toward the center of the housing. The fluid film thickness gradient will become
zero if the journal reaches the center and runs concentrically with the housing. When this
happens, there will be no hydrodynamic forces and the journal will become unstable.
The TPJB can also function with hybrid lubrication, where it combines hydrodynamic lu-
brication and hydrostatic lubrication. The hydrostatic lubrication is generated by the injection
of high-pressurized oil into the clearance between the bearing and journal. The hydrostatic
lubrication has two main advantages. Due to the low angular velocity of the journal under
start-up, is the hydrodynamic lubrication not sufficient enough to generate a fluid film that can
separate the journal and the housing. With hydrostatic lubrication can the journal be levitated
under start-up and avoid contact between the journal and housing. The constant injection
of high-pressurized oil at higher rotational speeds will also increase the damping and reduce
vibrations.
The TPJB can also function with active lubrication, where it also combines hydrody-
namic lubrication with hydrostatic lubrication.
With active lubrication is the injection of
high-pressurized oil varied by a feedback control loop.

3.2
Mathematical Model Of TPJB With Radial Oil Injection
This section presents the mathematical model of the TPJB with radial oil injection.
The
mathematical model of the TPJB is a provided program in Matlab from [2], where there have
been numerous contributors to the development of the mathematical model of the TPJB. The
writing of this section is therefore inspired by [4, 3, 2, 1, 11]. The TPJB-program has been
modified to correspond to the TPJB of the test rig. In short terms, does the TPJB-program
calculate the bearing dynamic matrices for a given configuration.
The procedure for obtaining the dynamic coefficient is an iterative process, which is illus-
trated in a flow chart in Fig. 3.2. First is an initial guess of the journal and pads positions
stated. From the position of the journal and pads is the fluid film thickness calculated, and
then is the modified Reynolds equation (MRE) solved to obtain the pressure distribution on the
pads. The fluid film forces are then found by integration over the pressure distribution on the
pads. The fluid film forces, the pivots’ reaction forces and the external loads are then balanced.
If no balance is obtained and the residuals are too-high, a new guess of the equilibrium position
of the journal and pads is calculated. This procedure is repeated until the residuals from the
force balance are sufficiently low, which gives the equilibrium position of the journal and pads.
Finally, can the linearized dynamic coefficient of the TPJB be calculated from the equilibrium
position of the journal and pads.

3.2.2
Dynamic Modeling
When the equilibrium position of the journal and the pads has been determined, can the fluid
forces of the oil film be modeled as linear springs and dampers. The springs’ and dampers’ lin-
earized dynamic coefficients can be determined with two different perturbation methods. The
first method, called Perturbation Theory, is an analytical method to determine the dynamics
coefficients. For the Perturbation Theory is the bearing dynamic coefficients determined from
the steady state condition of the equilibrium positions by applying a Taylor series and assum-
ing a harmonic excitation of the bearing degrees of freedom. The second method is Allaire’s
13
==PAGE:23==
3

perturbation method. The principle for the perturbation method to determine the dynamic
coefficients is to subject a small perturbation, where the dynamic coefficients can be found from
the restoring force and the associated perturbation. The Perturbation Theory is more convo-
luted compared to Allaire’s perturbation method which is more intuitive. For the understating
of the dynamics of the TPJB is Allaire’s perturbation therefore presented more in deep in this
master thesis.

not have any physical meaning. This implies that this method can not be used to determine

DISCUSSION
13
Discussion
Original was the aim of this master thesis to develop a methodology for the optimal control
design of actively-controlled journal bearings. However, in the process, it became more apparent
that the mathematical model had poor accuracy in predicting the dynamics of the test rig. The
model-based controller for the TPJB with adjustable radial oil injection relies on the accuracy
of a mathematical model. The project was therefore descaled to focus on the TPJB with a
constant radial oil injection, and the analytical work and results produced for a PD model-based
controller are left out.
The dynamic coefficients of the mathematical of TPJB has previously been verified and does
also look correct for this master thesis. The mathematical model of the TPJB with the DOFs
of the pads included has not earlier been used for this test rig. However, it has been used for a
test rig comparable to an industrial overhung compressor with a 4-Pad TPJB with load between
pads, with fine results. Nevertheless, has there for this master thesis been a poor agreement
between the mathematical model and experimental tests.
The setup for the test rig of this master thesis was previously used with a static load and no disc
attached to the test rig. It is possible that better results would have been obtained by applying
a static load. With a static load applied will the stiffness be increased and the damping will be
reduced, which would move it away from the area with a large amount of damping compared
to stiffness, where the odd and contradicting dynamics appear. With the static load applied,
should the stiffness of the static load be considered. The stiffness of the static load may be so
small compared to the stiffness of the TPJB that it can be neglected. Early in the process,
where there performed a numerical analysis with a static load, it was noticed that a high static
load would lead to unloading of the upper pad, leading to pad flutter. The unloading of the
upper pad is easiest detected from the equilibrium position from the TPJB-program, where the
radial displacement of the pad is zero.
The unloading of the upper pad, with a fixed static load applied, would appear at a corre-
sponding/relatively low angular velocity. By increasing the angular velocity, will the equilibrium
position of the journal be moved upwards. Both the increase in angular velocity and the higher
gradient of the fluid thickness for the upper pad will increase the hydrodynamic pressure for
the upper pad, which will prevent the unloading of the upper pad. For most cases of unloading,
will the equilibrium position for the angle of the upper pad also be zero, and here does the
dynamics coefficients from the TPJB-program seem reliable. This is investigated by plotting
the dynamic coefficients as a function of the angular velocity, where the angular velocity is
sweeping from angular velocities where unloading of the upper pad appears to angular veloci-
ties where unloading of the upper pad does not appear. However, for cases of unloading with a
relatively low angular velocity and static load (E.g. RPM=2500, Fstatic = 600, nDisc=2), will
the TPJB-program find an equilibrium position for the angel of the upper pad to a negative
value. The is properly non-physical, and here does the dynamics coefficients from the TPJB-
program seem unreliable. When applying a static load, one must ensure that the equilibrium
position and dynamic coefficients from the TPJB-program are reliable.
By applying a too-high static load, cavitation may be formed in the fluid during the exper-
imental tests, which the TPJB-program does not consider.
Some numerical analysis has also been performed with a preload of 0.5 instead of a preload

CONCLUSION
14
Conclusion
The main goal of this master thesis was to model and validate the mathematical model of the
SDOF test rig with a TPJB with a constant radial oil injection, where the DOFs and dynamics
of the pads of the TPJB are included. The mathematical model of the TPJB was a provided
program in Matlab, where numerous contributors have been involved in the development of the
program. The provided Matlab program was for a 4-Pad TPJB with load between pads. The
Matlab program has been modified to correspond to the 2-pad TPJB with LOP for the SDOF
test rig. The movement of the journal in the x-direction has been constrained to correspond
with the physics of the SDOF test rig.
Unfortunately, it has not been possible to validate the mathematical due to poor disagreement
of the FRFs and the dynamic properties between the mathematical model and the experimental
test. For finding potential errors has some simplified mathematical models been made, where
different degrees of freedom of the pads had been removed. The simplified mathematical model
is listed below.
Full model
DOFs = [y, αp1, αp2, xp1, xp2, yp1, yp2]
Simplified model 1
DOFs = [y, αp1, αp2, yp1, yp2]
Simplified model 2
DOFs = [y, yp1, yp2]
Simplified model 3
DOFs = [y]
An analytical investigation has been performed between the full model and simplified model
1 (where the xp DOFs of the pads are not included). This investigation has shown that it is
sufficient to model the TPJB without the xp DOFs of the pads to capture the dynamics of the
2-pad LOP TPJB.
To validate the simplified mathematical models where FRFs from all simplified models com-
pared with FRFs from experimental tests, with varying angular velocity of the journal (RPM
= [2000,4000]), varying inertia by changing the number of discs attached to the test rig (nDisc
= [2,3,4,5]) and no static load applied. Due to poor disagreement could none of the simplified
models be validated. From the changes in eigenfrequency when varying the number of discs, it
could be detected that the models were too sensitive to changes in the system’s inertia, even
though the inertia and sensitivity to adding more discs for the mathematical model were scaled
down from the basics of an experimental test.
Odd dynamic behavior was detected from the mathematical models with multiple DOFs of the
TPJB (simplified model 1 and 2) with an angular velocity of 2000 RPM. Therefore, to further
investigate the odd dynamics, an analytical analysis was performed for simplified model 1, where
the dynamics were mapped when varying the angular velocity and the system’s inertia. From
this analysis, could the odd and contradicting dynamic behavior be detected with configurations
whit a large amount of damping compared to stiffness. The odd and contradicting dynamic
behavior deviates from the general dynamics of a journal bearing, where an increase in angular
velocity will increase the stiffness and reduce the damping. With configurations with a large
amount of damping compared to stiffness is the odd and contradicting dynamic apparent, and
there it could be seen that an increase in angular velocity will decrease the eigenfrequency, where
the eigenfrequency should be increased by the increase of the stiffness coefficients of TPJB.
