Preface
Submission of the thesis partially fulfills the study program Sustainable Energy — Thermal
Energy at the Danish Technical University.
This report contains studies of solutions to mitigate overheating in a commercial building
with glass facades. The software program IDA ICE simulates the solutions for research to the
thesis. The thesis will first have an introduction where the thesis goal is presented, followed
by a literature study of how the popularity of glass facades is rising and the connected
challenges this aesthetic facade choice faces.
The thesis continues to explain the methodology and approach when creating the base case
and investigating different solutions. The results are presented with discussion and limita-
tions successively. Then, the conclusion is presented, summarizing the best solutions. Lastly,
ending the report, there is an appendix with supplementary findings.

Abstract
The thesis aims to decide on two solutions that mitigate overheating most efficiently regard-
ing energy usage — one passive solution and one systematic solution.
The best passive solution is external shading which is controlled by the sun. The shading
is activated when the solar radiation level exceeds 100 W/m2 on the exterior facade. This
solution decreased the energy for cooling needs by −46.4%. The overall energy usage was
decreased by −10.5%. The overheating compared to the base case was the lowest of all
passive solutions.
The best systematic solution is passive chilled beams. The systematic solution utilized more
energy for cooling, but the passive chilled beams had the lowest percentages of overheating
while ensuring an acceptable indoor climate.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The thesis does introduce the thesis goal, followed by the challenges of glass-facade buildings.
The following literature study builds upon the importance of looking into these challenges
before introducing the simulated building. Chapter 2 explains the simulated building further.
Lastly, criteria from standards and regulations are presented, as well as assumptions made
for the building.
1.1
Thesis goal
This thesis aims to decide the most efficient measure against overheating in a glass-facade
building by simulating different measures against overheating in a glass-facade commercial
office building. A base case will be made and simulated in IDA ICE. There are then passive
measures studied, which are changes done to the building envelope, and systematic measures,
which are changes done to the building system, keeping the envelope untouched. Modified
versions of the base case corresponding to the passive and systematic measures are simulated
and compared to the base case.
The time frame for the simulations is a year, from January—December 2022. The thesis
includes a hypothesis that improved U-values in windows decrease the threat of energy
loss during winter. The data through the warmer months could support or contradict the
hypothesis that the low temperatures during winter no longer increase the heating usage in
buildings.
Each solution has the best scenario chosen. Positive and negative impacts on the cooling
1
==PAGE:15==
energy needs are detected when comparing the simulated solutions, which is the main factor
when deciding the best scenario. The analysis also includes the energy needs for heating
and the total energy usage for context. The PPD for each scenario shows if the scenarios
have an acceptable level. The PPD also aids in the decision of the best scenario. The best
scenario has its overheating presented as a percentage of occupied time to evaluate whether
the overheating is mitigated or not.
The thesis goal is to identify one passive and one systematic solution of the
investigated measures which mitigate the energy usage connected to cooling
in a glass-facade building.
1.2
Delimitations
The thesis does not include full-scale measurement data to compare with the simulated
data. The simulation result compares well with actual data [1], and a hypothetical full-scale
duplicate is not realistic to acquire for this work-load. The thesis will not investigate passive
or systematic measures for a building with an envelope other than glass facades. The results
will not include a profound discussion around the heating need in the building. However,
the heating need may aid in understanding the energy usage context.
Results with low energy use and no overheating are unreasonable if the PPD is above the
limit. However, performing a complete analysis of the indoor environment is not necessary,
as the thesis goal is to mitigate overheating. Only thermal conditions and energy use are
studied, and the PPD is used as a parameter to see if the energy use over a year seems
reasonable.
Building materials and components are not justified in the thesis. Most building materials
and components are standards or defaults in the simulation tool. However, changes done to
the envelope and materials are justified.
1.3
Literature study:
Glass-facade buildings and its
challenges
The popularity of glass facades for aesthetic and psycho-social environment purposes has
been rising in the last years [2, 3, 4], so modern architecture often includes glass to area ratios
that far exceed the minimum criteria in building regulations. The minimum criterion for
2
==PAGE:16==
windows in Denmark is 10% of the floor area [5]. The argument for increasing this percentage
is to increase daylight and rich views, strengthening the psycho-social environment. The users
of the building support these factors. However, without planning, the energy balance of the
building can result in substantial variations [6] compared to a building with a less glazed
envelope area.
Compared to other building envelope materials, windows stand out as significant sources
of thermal losses and gains throughout the year.
While Bryn and Matusiak [7] suggest
a reduction in window area to develop low-energy buildings, the rising popularity of glass
facades seems contradictory when focusing on a greener future.
The energy usage in building stands for almost 40% of all energy used in Denmark [8], and the
energy used in buildings mainly accounts for heating, ventilation, and lighting. By renovating
existing buildings and building with high energy standards for the future, it is possible
to decrease the percentage of energy usage in buildings.
However, the decrease cannot
compromise the indoor climate. It is not a well-working solution if a building is performing
well regarding energy, but it is impossible to occupy due to the thermal environment.
The popularity of glass facades as an aesthetic feature is increasing [3], but overheating can
cause the occupants to experience an unsatisfactory indoor climate. A high share of windows
can increase overheating [5] which negatively affects the indoor climate.
HVAC systems
intend to provide comfortable and healthy indoor environmental conditions, and they often
respond to temperatures measured with various sensors but not directly to radiation caused
by sunlight. The solar transmittance influences the mean radiant temperature by heating
surfaces, which affects the PMV. It was found that occupants closer to glazed facades during
warm and sunny weather conditions would experience discomfort [9] because of a rise in
PMV caused by exposure to direct solar transmission through the window.
Blum Winther states that if the energy usage in a building should decrease, it is necessary
to start with the facade. The facade can affect the energy usage in its building up to 90%
[10]. The passive measures of a building can help save energy for space cooling and heating
if used correctly.
The most efficient buildings are equipped with smart house solutions to regulate the influence
of solar irradiation. The passive measures of the facade are critical, and the orientation of
a building can affect its energy use and losses. When using different measures, it is possible
to utilize the solar heat gain in favor of the occupants when facing east and west during
summer and south during winter [7]. Dengel et al. highlight the importance of solar shading
3
==PAGE:17==
on facades facing the sun during the summer months to reject solar transmittance [11].
Facades in the future will be dynamic [10] to abide by the solar behavior amongst other
parameters.
The weather varies over the year in different seasons but can also vary throughout the day.
Buildings with glass facades are the most vulnerable to variations in weather [12], which
is very fluctuating in the Nordic countries. Glass-facade buildings are renowned for being
unsuitable for cold climates due to high heat loss during winter.
However, the question of whether glass-facade solutions in Scandinavian countries during
winter are a poor energy-saving solution or not is less relevant in 2022. Exchanging windows
with a better U-value and choosing windows wisely when building a new glass facade is
essential to remove the association that glass facade buildings have with heat loss in colder
climates. The winter scenario of too much heat loss is no longer a common challenge due
to improvements in the window U-values. A window with three-layered glazing has less
heat transfer than a single pane window. A decrease in U-values also leads to higher heat
accumulation inside the building during warmer periods, which leads to overheating. Instead
of saving costs regarding heat loss, it is then necessary to spend money and energy on cooling.
Electrical sources often power cooling devices, which is not preferable if society wants to
distribute the power from the grid more wisely in the future. If space heating and cooling
are sourced by district heating and cooling, it is possible to prioritize electricity for transport
to reduce city emissions.
Cooling solutions are not standard in Scandinavian countries, but the demand is growing
with highly insulated building envelopes and high internal and external heat loads. Several
solutions are available, and the most suiting solution for glass-facade buildings, out of the
chosen seven measures, will be resolved in this thesis.
A passive measure against overheating is venting hatches. It is not a new solution because
it is known as a fire escape solution. This solution is also helpful for venting on a facade
with fixed frame windows. Venting solutions with low U-values are also helpful on facades
with less solar exposure, where glazed facades are unwanted due to less solar heat gain and
mainly heat losses. A venting hatch can have the same U-value as the wall, which removes
the issue of heat loss while simultaneously offering the possibility of fresh air and cross-flow
natural ventilation.
A systematic solution utilizing district cooling is floor cooling and chilled beams. The reason
4
==PAGE:18==
for the growing interest in chilled beams is their energy saving potential [13, 14], as well as
their aesthetic. The solution causes minimal space requirements. Compared to air cooling
solution, it is possible to transfer the same amount of energy in pipes eight-teen times less
than air ducts [13].
Research in regards to overheating and cooling in glass facades is needed to keep utilizing
these glazed facades as a design feature. From having small windows as a light source to
having them cover a whole facade is progress, but the facade solution can still be improved
to develop buildings suitable for the future.

SketchUp: Creation of building body
Before being built in SketchUp, sketches of the hypothetical building were visualized on

2.2.2
Windows
The windows have a central role in this analysis, and their properties are described below:
• The U-value is the thermal transmittance through a structure, and in this case, a
window. A lower U-value indicates less thermal transmittance than a higher value.
• The visible transmittance is the percentage of light that can pass through the win-
dow without the window reflecting or absorbing the light. Visible transmittance is
relevant for the amount of daylight in the building and electricity for lighting if the
building has sensors that activate when daylight is insufficient.
14
==PAGE:28==
• The solar heat gain coefficient or solar transmittance is the ratio of sunlight that
is transformed into heat inside the zones. A higher value of this parameter is positive
during winter but could result in a cooling need during summer.
The modeled windows are suitable for a passive house. It is necessary to choose windows with
sufficiently low U-value to be able to assume energy loss of low impact during winter, seeing
as the investigated thesis problem is overheating. The window properties selected are from
the product "3-lags Optima Træ/Alu A" from Bedst og Billigst A/S [20]. A simplification
was done regarding the windows by scaling them bigger than intended by the producer.
The frame was also made smaller, so the window could not open, which it was originally
designed for. The frame in the simulation is at 0.05. Its Uw is 0.78 W/(m2 K) and its Ug
is 0.52 W/(m2 K). The window’s g-factor is 0.53. An energy labeling certificate is included
in Appendix A. The window has energy classification A, a Denmark requirement for new
buildings after 2020 [8].
The building is a glass-facade building, so windows take up almost the whole facade, but
not 100%. The windows have frames that take up 0.05 percent of the glazed facade. The
windows also had to be placed on a wall. The windows cannot overlap, so there were slits of
external wall between the windows to avoid IDA ICE errors of object intersection. Therefore,
the total area of windows is 448.7 m2, which covers 91.2% of the exterior walls. The window
to floor ratio is 59.7%.

2.3
Solution measures preparations
Several simulations were performed to investigate whether the seven chosen solutions implied
mitigation of overheating.
The first four solutions presented were passive changes.
The
following three solutions were changes done to the system of the building.
The passive
changes were the orientation of the building, exterior shading of windows, implementation
of venting hatches, and changes to window properties. The systematic changes were chilled
beams in the ceiling, district heating floor cooling, and CO2- and temperature sensors added
to the ventilation system.
It is not possible to compare all seven solutions against each other. The passive solutions
do not include extra design power, so these measures benefit from this compared to the sys-
tematic solutions if all were to be compared similarly. Therefore the passive and systematic
changes are compared to the base case separately.
The design power for the systematic solutions was chosen to be similar to see which solution
is more efficient against overheating from the same base case power. If a solution shows the
result of less overheating when still meeting the PPD requirement, it is determined to be
more efficient against the overheating caused by solar radiation.
2.3.1
Passive solution: Orientation
The building floor plan was created with the possibility of gaining sunlight from all sides.
The window area to the floor is not bigger or smaller in any direction, which is common in
other buildings to take advantage of sunlight coming from the southwest and less energy loss
from windows facing northeast. The building in this thesis has two sides of different lengths,
which can influence the result. The entrance of the building is initially facing north. The
facade with the entrance door is the starting point, namely the original north facade.
The building was simulated eight times while rotated 45° in a clockwise orientation. By
manually dragging the north arrow, orientation was changed in "site shading and orientation."
Manual changes can cause the degrees not to be consistent and perfectly rising with 45°
between each simulation, but this minor fault is considered insignificant.
2.3.2
Passive solution: Shading
It is known that shading helps reduce overheating, but it is still interesting to compare the
shading solution with other measures in the thesis. The generic drop-arm awning was chosen
22
==PAGE:36==
to shade the windows from the solar radiation. The shading was placed on the window’s
exterior as shown in Figure 2.9.
The solar radiation is measured on the outside of the building. External solar shading, as in
this simulation, is more efficient than internal shading because the sun rays are not entering
the building as they would with internal shading [11]. The external shading was drawn when
the solar radiation level exceeded 100 W/m2 on the exterior facade.
Figure 2.9: Building shown with solar shading from north-west. The entrance door on the
north facade is not shaded because it is not a glazed facade.
There were performed three different simulations concerning shading. The differences are
how the shading was controlled. The shading in the first simulation was controlled by "Sun"
only. As mentioned, the drop-arm awning was drawn when the solar radiation was 100 W/m2
or higher. The second simulation extends the control to "Sun AND Schedule." The schedule
was chosen dependent on the zone. The third and last simulation was a less realistic scenario
where the shading was "Always drawn". It was interesting to see what would happen with
the energy usage distribution between heating and cooling when the shading was drawn at
all times.

2.3.3
Passive solution: Venting hatches
Since the windows on the facade have small frames, they cannot open. Opening a window is
satisfactory for the occupants when the operative temperature is high due to the air change
and breeze happening because of pressure differences. Venting hatches are insulated openings
in the structure, similar to a typical wall. A venting hatch is not similar to a window, which
will decrease the window to floor percentage of the glass facade. However, because of the
thermal environmental benefits, it is a passive measure in this thesis.
The venting hatches were made in IDA ICE by setting the fraction of the window frame
to 0.9999 because a value of one results in an error. PI-sensors control the opening of the
hatch. There was no integrated or external window shading chosen. The U-value was set to

2.3.5
Systematic solution: Passive chilled beams
Glazed facades allow more solar radiation, which results in higher solar heat gain. The heated
air temperature causes the air to rise, which is the buoyancy effect. This phenomenon implies
that the hottest air is below the ceiling. An occupant would be more negatively affected by
a warm head or cold feet than a cold head and warm feet. A temperature difference over
5°C for the head causes dissatisfaction for occupants [16], so the buoyancy effect must be
defied. One solution is to install chilled surfaces that use radiation to cool down the room.
Chilled beams are devices that use a refrigerant or water to provide a uniform temperature
reduction without cold air circulation, and noise [25]. Cold air is usually perceived as some-
thing positive in the summer, but the air velocity should never exceed 0.2 m/s [16] to satisfy
indoor climate restrictions. Therefore, according to manufacturers, air velocity is not an
issue with passive chilled beams, which cools down with radiation and rely on convection.
The "CoolDev" device in IDA ICE operates completely analogous to waterborne radiators
[26].
The minimum space between the beams is 0.5 m as stated in DS/EN 14037 [27]. The beams
in IDA ICE were placed directly on the ceiling, which fulfills the criterion. The standard also
states that the emissivity should not be any lower than 0.8. This minimum value was inserted
in the properties of the chilled beams. The minimum surface temperature was assumed to
be the same as the water inlet temperature, which is 5°C.
This installation was implied in IDA ICE by implementing the room unit "CoolDev" on
all internal ceilings and roofs in zones where occupants were situated, such as the meeting
rooms, canteen, entrance, kitchen, and north and south offices. The device covered the whole
ceiling in the respective zones to place a uniform design power of 40, 50, and 100 W/m2.
The design power of 40 W/m2 was the default in IDA ICE, 50 W/m2 was an arbitrary value,
and 100 W/m2 was the same design power as the ideal coolers. 100 W/m2 is also a step
away from the previous two to see if the jump in design power has a significant effect.

Chapter 4
Discussion
The discussion chapter circles back on findings in the results chapter to further discuss the
similarities and dissimilarities between the different solutions.
The chapter is separated
into sub-chapters dividing the passive and systematic changes, followed by a sub-chapter
discussing similarities and general findings.
The discussion chapter also includes a sub-chapter of limitations, discussing assumptions
and simplifications. Lastly, a sub-chapter regarding future work is included to review what
could be done further in a similar analysis.
The simulation results show that a lower U-value increases the energy needed for cooling,
indicating more overheating. From the base case solution and following solutions, it is clear
that the cooling need is higher than the heating need in this glass facade building. This
confirms the hypothesis stated in the introduction.
4.1
Passive solutions
Compared to the systematic solutions, the passive solutions had the most successful changes
when considering energy usage. When a solution mitigated the energy needs for cooling, the
building had less heat that had to be removed in the first place. Regarding the percentage
of hours above 27°C, only the orientation solution resulted in any decreases compared to the
base case. The passive changes did not induce any possibilities to utilize more power since
all systematic parameters were untouched. This fact implies that the passive measures had
a better chance of lowering the energy use than the systematic solutions.
50
==PAGE:64==
Orientation
The orientation showed a small but favorable change in all orientations compared to the
base case. This implies that the orientation in the base case is not a great starting point,
and choosing another orientation would save energy. The west direction came out as the
best scenario, but the east direction was a close second. The close to similar result can be
explained by the ground floor plan. The entrance is not as occupied as the canteen, so the
internal gains in the canteen will cause the difference in the two best orientations, east and
west.
Orientation is an essential part of a design process, as this passive measure can result in
energy savings if rotated correctly. If the area does not restrict a building, it is helpful to
investigate by simulation which orientation can positively affect the building energy use. It is
allowed to assume that orientation does not affect the cost of a building process compared to
other solutions in the study. The cost is a great incentive to value this passive measure, even
though the changes could be less significant than other, possibly more expensive, solutions.
Shading
The shading solution showed excellent results and the extreme scenario where the sun shading
was "Always drawn" implied that 50% of the cooling need comes from the sun. Internal
gains and volumetric heat capacity are other sources that induce cooling needs. The shading
controlled by "Sun" showed an overall lower energy usage than the "Always drawn" scenario
because of the energy usage for heat. The shading controlled by "Sun" had a more economical
energy balance where the building was allowed solar irradiation to some extent to reduce the
energy needed for heating while shading away excess irradiation causing overheating.
Shading as a passive measure is easy to implement either during construction or after. Shad-
ing is an excellent way of saving energy for cooling during the rehabilitation of a building if
the building does not have a shading system or an older manually operated solution.
Venting hatches
The venting hatches showed a decrease in both heating and cooling need. The decrease in
cooling needs was more self-explanatory than the reduction in energy for heating. The cooling
need was lowered due to zones being able to ventilate naturally with cross-flow ventilation
to exchange heated air with fresh and cooler air. The higher U-values could cause a decrease
in heating in the area of the hatches. It was not a large area in question, but this seems
reasonable due to the low decrease percentage. A higher U-value will reduce heat losses
through the facade and reduce the need for produced heat.
51
==PAGE:65==
Venting hatches are helpful against overheating thanks to natural ventilation and not only as
emergency escape solutions. The result has shown that it is wise to include venting hatches
to decrease energy usage, especially for cooling needs. Venting hatches are more challenging
to implement in a finished building, so a wise move would be to include this solution early
in the planning process of a building.
Window properties
The last passive change with changes in window parameters confirmed the hypothesis that
the low U-values benefitted heat losses but were a drawback for the cooling need. The result
showed that a lower U-value saved energy for heating but increased energy for cooling. The
energy for the cooling increase is not advantageous when greener alternatives usually source
the heating, and electric energy sources usually source cooling. If the electric sources are
chosen for cooling, there should be a break-even where the optimal balance is calculated.
The window properties solution resulted in the best mitigation of overheating. Due to a
realistic aspect, it is not possible to decide this solution is the best in the study, but the
result gives an insight into what should be prioritized when building a glass facade building
in the future. The scenarios of lowering the values to the extent in the scenarios are not
realistic yet, but it shows how important it is.
The values show some meaningful results for the energy need and overheating. When plan-
ning a building with a window facade, it is essential to choose wisely and aim for windows
with low U-value and low solar heat gain coefficient. The latter is even more important to
aim for if the goal is to mitigate overheating.
4.2
Systematic solutions
The systematic changes showed an increase in both cooling and heating, except the ventila-
tion sensors showed a decrease in heating in all scenarios. The high design power can reason
for the increase in energy need compared to the passive solutions.
Passive chilled beams
The passive chilled beams increased the energy usage for heating and cooling, and it was
clear that a higher share of energy was directed towards cooling. An increase in energy usage
was detected in the results when the design power increased. Therefore, the best scenario
in this solution is the scenario with the smallest increase. The hours above 27°C were well
kept below the limits. The PPD in the passive chilled beams solution was the lowest of all
52
==PAGE:66==
solutions. The occupants are not feeling too hot due to the higher energy used for cooling.
The overheating is mitigated as desired.
Chilled beams are a solution that demands an investment, but the energy source, district
cooling, is a better option than electric cooling. When cooling is supplied by a cooling other
than electricity, it is possible to use this high-value source on something else without having
to expand the electricity grid [29]. That is one reason why district cooling is recommended.
Floor cooling
The floor cooling option showed a similar increase in energy usage as the passive chilled
beam option, but not to the same extent. The hours of overheating were less than the base
case, and the PPD is below 10%. The floor cooling uses less energy than the passive chilled
beam solution, but the overheating hours are larger than those for the passive chilled beams.
They are all acceptable, but as the thesis goal is to decide which solution mitigates the
overheating, the floor cooling does not score as high as the passive chilled beams.
A cooling floor is an excellent alternative to electric cooling in buildings for the same reasons
as chilled beams. It is a cheaper alternative to electric cooling for the end-users because the
implementation of floor cooling/heating is an investment that would save money with time.
The results from the cooling floor also resulted in less energy use than the chilled beams,
which is vital to many when deciding on space cooling and heating.
Ventilation sensors
The sensors showed that the cooling energy usage correlates with the amount of air supplied
into the zone.
Since the heat gain is high during summer due to the large area of the
glazed facade and small amount of people, it is not clever to have CO2-sensors alone in a
ventilation system. This scenario leads to the highest energy need for cooling of the three
scenarios compared to the base case. The ideal coolers must utilize more power to mitigate
the heat in the zones that were not removed with the ventilation system in the base case.
The scenario of having temperature sensors also increased the cooling need, but not to the
same extent as the CO2-sensor.
The temperature sensors also decreased the energy needs for heating more than the CO2-
option. The higher decrease could be because the CO2-option supplies the zones with the
same air volume throughout the year, but the temperature-option supplies less air volume
during winter and does not remove as much heated air as the CO2-option.
A combination of these two sensors results in the best scenario. The energy saved for heating
53
==PAGE:67==
will be slightly less than the temperature scenario due to the occupants and CO2 affecting
the air volume during winter. However, the total energy for heating is still less than with the
CO2-sensor alone. The combined energy use for cooling is the lowest of the three scenarios,
meaning that this option is the best regarding overheating mitigation. All scenarios have the
same PPD, so this factor does not affect the decision of the most successful scenario. The
overheating is higher than the base case, which means that the sensor solution is not the
best solution out of the systematic solutions. Ventilation sensors in the form of temperature
and CO2 are not mitigating overheating.
It is interpreted from the results that a higher supply volume results in less cooling need, as
long as the supply air temperature is less than the heating setting temperature. Sensors are
typical supplements in smart ventilation systems to not use more energy than needed. From
the results, it can be seen that several sensors used simultaneously result in a more desired
outcome. More parameters are cared for if several sensors are installed. It is also wise to
learn from this that a sensor controlled by CO2 will only supply after occupants, so a larger
zone with fewer occupants per square meter would result in insufficient supply air volume,
as seen in this simulation and Table 3.15.
4.3
Similarities
The occupants are seemingly more comfortable on the second floor than on the first floor.
Those floors are the office floors, where the occupants are most of the time. Hours with a
temperature over 27°C also show that the ground floor is quite comfortable.
From all solutions, it is visible that the size of the rooms was important. The zones with
the highest percentage of hours over 27°C are the smaller ones. Even when the orientations
were changed, the smaller rooms were still the zones of the highest issue.
The kitchen,
kitchenettes, and meeting rooms are the most volatile zones with the highest percentages of
overheating. Changing the size of the rooms would make a more significant distinction from
the other solutions. The size of rooms was not studied in this thesis but would be interesting
to look into further.
Primary energy
Primary energy factors were inserted for the respective energy sources. The ideal coolers
and systematic changes have electric energy sources with the highest primary energy factor.
The energy use graphs in the results chapter Chapter 3 show the total energy usage, which
could show a different picture if primary energy factors were neglected. The energy usage
54
==PAGE:68==
affected the comparison of the systematic solutions in that the passive chilled beams and
floor cooling were supplied with district cooling. The ventilation sensors relied on electrical
sourcing. A third solution relying on district cooling would be fairer in comparing systematic
solutions.
Combine several solutions
The thesis aims to decide which solutions are the best from the passive and systematic
solutions. It is not normal to only be able to choose one solution when creating a building
in the real world. Therefore it is worth mentioning that an optimal solution would be to
connect the solutions studied in this thesis.
4.4
Limitations
The sub-chapter includes limitations that restrict or defect the thesis and its study.
Worst zones
The worst zones were not given too much attention in the analysis. It defected the study by
not having ideal heaters or coolers, which results in these zones being warm. The decision
was assumed acceptable because of several factors. This simplification was done to limit the
simulation time. Neglecting the worst zones did not affect the PPD or overheating in the
shown zones because no occupants were placed in the respective zones. However, it did affect
the AHU energy usage. The fact that the zones in question were not changed throughout all
simulations in the analysis means that they caused the same amount of energy use in every
simulation. The energy use result was viewed as a percentage change from the base case, so
the worst zones did not affect the solutions differently.
The middle zones should be made as one zone to represent all middle zones which could be
elevators, escape staircases, corridors, and toilets. If new simulations were to be done, these
zones would be included as various zone with the same installations as the other zones. One
collective zone would positively affect the simulation time rather than several small zones
with individually placed installations.
Placement of ideal coolers in systematic solutions
In a real scenario, the systematic solutions would have benefitted from not having ideal
coolers. The reason behind implementing cooling solutions to the system was to research
the effect on cooling. AHU cooling and ideal coolers seemed extensive to implement at the
same time. Ideal coolers were first removed in all three systematic solutions for this reason.
55
==PAGE:69==
The removal showed unreasonable results. The cooling need decreased by 100%, and more
than 60% of the occupants were dissatisfied. There were no devices that cooled the zones.
It would be unreasonable to compare all systematic solutions where two had ideal coolers,
and one did not. Therefore, the decision to re-implement the ideal coolers was taken.
Distinction from model and real world
While creating the base case, shortcuts were made. The knowledge of all building compo-
nents and criteria is unknown; therefore, the building is not eligible to be placed in a real
environment. The main staircase is worth mentioning because the air is supposed to travel
freely from one floor to another, but it is not mirrored in the model. An attempt to merge
zones over different floors was made, but this was not supported in IDA ICE.
Placement of occupants
The building’s occupants were placed centrally in the zones and did not represent the correct
scenario in the real world. The central placement of occupants is also one reason the air
temperature was used as a sensor in the systematic solutions, not operative temperature.
4.5
Future work
Additional simulations
To investigate whether trends would continue or break, further simulations could have been
done with venting hatches, chilled beams, and floor cooling. The venting hatches had a
growing pattern with the number of hatches. Future work could be to see if the increase would
continue if even more hatches were implemented. The chilled beams and floor cooling showed
decreasing and increasing trends, respectively, with the design power. Further investigations
could include even more extreme simulations to see if the trend would continue.
Cost of solutions
An idea of future additional work when solutions are compared is to include the cost of
the different solutions. In this thesis, the goal was to find out which solution mitigated the
overheating best, but when including a financial aspect, is it possible to find the solution
with the best economically realistic view. If a solution showed to mitigate overheating but
had a more considerable investment, it is interesting to find out when the installation would
break even.
An optimal balance
If the cost of heating and cooling and the usage is known, it is possible to find a value where
56
==PAGE:70==
the cost is minimized while ensuring a comfortable indoor environment. The thesis has shown
results that a lower U-value would increase the cooling need in a glass-facade building. A
higher U-value would increase the heat loss through the facade. With this reality, it would
be interesting to investigate whether it is an optimal balance between overheating and heat
loss.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The results from Chapter 3 are summarized in this chapter. The passive and systematic
solutions are separated in Sections 5.1 and 5.2.
5.1
Best passive solution
The general outtakes of the passive solutions are that smaller rooms should face north, where
the solar radiation is less during a year. The orientation should be considered after zone
sizing if the building area is close to an equilateral square, like in this example. Shading
should be present externally when solar irradiation is sufficient. Venting hatches should be
present to ventilate, and preferably on two sides of a building to generate cross-ventilation
during warm periods of the year. The windows covering the facade should have a low solar
heat gain coefficient and a low U-value. If the U-value is too low, it can increase the cooling
need, but a higher U-value uses more energy overall, which is not a wanted compromise.
The best results from the passive solutions regarding the mitigation of energy use for cooling
alone are shading with control "Always drawn" and lowering the solar heat gain coefficient
in the window properties. Their percentage decreases are −50.5% and −65.3%, respectively.
However, these solutions do not achieve great results when considering total energy use. The
energy usage for heating in the same scenarios is increasing by 28.4% and 49.0%, so the total
energy usage is not as impressive as the cooling need decrease alone.
In addition, these scenarios are not realistic. The scenario where shading is "Always drawn"
is not realistic because the purpose of the windows is diminished. The scenario where the
58
==PAGE:72==
SHGC is lowered is also unrealistic in today’s window market.
Therefore, the best solution is to have external shading controlled by "Sun." This scenario
had an energy usage decrease of −10.5%, the highest energy usage decrease of all passive
solution simulations. The cooling need was decreased by −46.4% while 13.6% only increased
the heating need. The most crucial result was overheating, which was mitigated most in this
scenario.
5.2
Best systematic solution
The ventilating sensors resulted in the most significant decrease in total energy use. However,
they had the worst results of overheating and PPD, which are the most critical parameters of
the study. The high PPD and percentages of overheating mean that the energy usage should
have been higher to satisfy the occupants. The energy used was not sufficient to satisfy the
occupants and thermal environment.
The systematic solutions did not mitigate the energy usage for cooling needs but reflected
the higher share of energy usage for cooling than heating in a glass-facade building. The
floor cooling solution had the lowest increase in energy need for cooling and had an efficient
effect on overheating compared to the base case. Still, the passive chilled beams decreased
the overheating more and is the best option of the systematic solutions. The best scenario
of the passive chilled beams scenarios was where the design power was 100 W/m2 which had
an increase of 11.3%.
