Abstract 
The concept of a circular economy has arisen as a potential solution in an era of increasing concerns about 
the environment and an urgent need for sustainable practices. While several companies are adopting 
circularity, the construction industry is still struggling to move away from a linear model. Despite 
increased awareness, practical implementation is behind due to a lack of standardized techniques and 
preliminary research on real-world applications in the construction sector. 
This thesis seeks to fill these gaps by investigating the challenges and opportunities associated with 
adopting a circular economy in construction. It explores the implementation of a platform-based approach 
to scale up upcycling activities in the construction industry, with a focus on the London market. The 
research aims to address the challenges faced in achieving standardization and effective communication 
among stakeholders.  
Through a case study of a specific construction project from the company GXN, data was gathered 
through interviews with key stakeholders to identify the main barriers and requirements for better 
standardization. Additionally, it was possible to map the supply chain as well as map the company’s 
processes against a specific timeline. The findings highlight the significance of data and communication 
as common elements and propose the development of a framework consisting of modules and interfaces 
to enhance documentation and communication within the upcycling process.  
This research contributes to the growing field of circular economy practices and sets the stage for further 
exploration of platform-based solutions in the construction industry. The thesis provides valuable insights 
into the potential benefits of a platform approach and emphasizes the importance of stakeholder 
collaboration in achieving better standardization.

1.2. Background 
As of 2022, the global population is expanding at a rate of 0.84% per year. From 1959 (3 billion) to 1999, 
the world's population increased by 100% to 6 billion (Worldometers, 2022). As the world population 
grows, resources are more limited and more difficult to access. There is an imminent need to make a change 
to be able to continue using the Earth’s resources. Resource consumption, especially in the construction 
industry (CI), must be lessened as it accounts for 40% of the resource consumption (van Stijn & Gruis, 
2020). 
The construction industry accounts for most of the waste generated on the planet. More particularly, in the 
EU it accounts for more than 35% of its total waste generation (European Commission, 2020). Additionally, 
there are almost non-existent markets for high-quality secondary materials in this industry (Danish Ministry 
of Environment and Food, 2019). As a result, downcycling constitutes the majority of Construction and 
Demolition Waste (C&DW) recycling despite high recycling rates (Eurostat, 2020). At the European level, 
policies have been created to considerably increase the sector's sustainability (European Union, 2022). This 
is why selective demolition has become a crucial component of C&DW in upcycling. Selective demolition 
can be beneficial since it allows for the recovery of high-quality material fractions for recycling or reuse. 
Circular economy (CE) is a relatively new concept that has seemed to offer a way out of these resource 
consumption and waste generation problems. Nevertheless, circular economy notions are still not widely 
used in the CI, despite the recognition of an issue and a solution offered (Adams et al., 2017). There needs 
to be a shift towards more sustainable thinking. Scholars and experts are attempting to fill the gap on this 
matter that persists. They are doing so by trying to provide more information and insight on how to 
implement solutions for this problem.  
1.3. Case study 
This thesis will focus on the analysis of the company GXN. A concise introduction of the company will be 
given, as well as a circular economy project it is currently involved in. Further descriptions on its processes

1.3.1. GXN 
GXN is a daughter company of 3XN, a firm of architects whose headquarters are in Denmark. GXN was 
founded in 2007 by 3XN as an independent company with the remit of doing research about the new trends 
and techniques that are impacting architecture. Their focus relays on innovation and circular design, 
intending to incorporate greener solutions. It aims to change the industry as a whole, with a particular focus 
on scalability (Poulsgaard, 2023).  Since GXN is primarily concerned with the London market, this thesis 
will focus on examining this specific area of their business. Therefore, regulations and statements should 
conform to the London government's regulatory trends. 
In the early 2000s, there was a big change in the market as sustainable building certifications started to 
make a big impact in the UK. There are already hundreds of sustainability certifications for the built 
environment and, as the emphasis on certifying buildings grows, the number is expected to expand 
(Guldager Jensen & Birgisdottir, 2018). Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment 
Method (BREEAM) was first introduced in 1990, and other certification programs also emerged, like 
Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) (Guldager Jensen & Birgisdottir, 2018). This led 
to the emergence of a new demand for sustainable solutions and architecture, which provided GXN the 
drive it needed to succeed.

1.3.4. RIBA  
Besides the CircOp Project, GXN has participated in many initiatives with the purpose to continue 
developing their company further into the CE. GXN’s projects are remarked on their structure, which follow 
what is called RIBA, a Plan of Work. In 1963, the RIBA Plan of Work was established as a framework to 
be used by architects on construction projects, serving as a guide in the whole industry. Their purpose is to 
“deliver better buildings and places, stronger communities and a sustainable environment” (RIBA, 2020). 
The Plan of Work consists of eight stages for managing the design and construction process. Figure 3 below 
shows the eight stages and the order they should be followed. 
 
Figure 3. RIBA Stages from OneTrace (2023) 
- 
Stage 0: Strategic Definition. At this stage, the client's needs and the overall project goals are 
defined. 
- 
Stage 1: Preparation and Briefing. In this stage, the project brief is created, the budget and program 
are set, and the design team is selected. 
- 
Stage 2: Concept Design. At this stage, the initial design concept is developed and presented to the 
client for feedback. 
- 
Stage 3: Developed Design. In this stage, the design concept is refined, specific design proposals 
are created, and planning permission and other required approvals are obtained. 
- 
Stage 4: Technical Design. In this stage, specific technical drawings and specifications are created, 
and the work from multiple consultants and experts is coordinated. 
- 
Stage 5: Manufacturing and Construction. During this stage, managing the construction process 
involves picking contractors, handling contracts, and supervising the construction activity. 
- 
Stage 6: Handover and Close Out. This stage includes getting the building ready for use, testing 
and commissioning its systems, and making sure it complies with any relevant regulations and 
specifications. 
- 
Stage 7: In Use. During this phase, the building's performance is monitored, and any necessary 
maintenance or repairs are made. 
RIBA has been used by GXN for numerous projects, specifically is currently guiding the case study that 
this thesis will be focusing on, LIB. It is important to notice the placement of the stage where LIB finds 
itself. GXN is working in Stages 4 and 5, which corresponds to the technical design and the Manufacturing 
and Construction, having just overcome Stage 3, spatial coordination, at the end of 2021.

1.4. Problem statement 
The construction industry is characterized for its complexity and dynamism. The numerous stakeholders 
that are involved in its processes create several challenges. Moreover, previous studies have shown that 
traditional construction methods of project delivery have produced numerous issues related to 
fragmentation (Mohd Nawi et al., 2014). The fragmentation in the construction industry is a reflection of 
the severe specialization that exits whitin it (Jones et al., 2022). This specialization has caused a separation 
in three dimensions in how projects are delivered. There is (1) vertical fragmentation, where diverse 
companies are in charge of different aspects of the project, including design, production, assembly, and 
operation. Next, there is (2) horizontal fragmentation, which is referred when multiple parties give 
complementary products and services (offered by experts) at nearly the same phase in a process. Lastly, (3) 
longitudinally fragmentation is represented at the completion of a project, when teams that were working 
on it are frequently replaced or transferred. Because team members bring their expertise with them to their 
next projects, any knowledge gained throughout the project is lost as a result (Jones et al., 2022).  
One of the main challenges that GXN is trying to overcome is the forementioned standardization in the 
execution of their projects. More specifically, GXN lacks standardization in its processes when it comes to 
the fourth stage of the RIBA plan of work, called technical design, when technical drawings and 
specifications are created. These specifications create many challenges in the development of the projects, 
such as the specific requirements that each material needs. Buildings are seen as material banks, and 
establishing a correct material workflow for the company would enable a quicker course of action when 
decisions are made.  
Thus, the key problem of this study is the evaluation and mapping of potential material flows from a 
demolition case. This would enable quicker and better project decision-making, which would ultimately 
boost the environmental effect of upcycling activities. Its primary output is an evaluation tool and workflow 
designed for the London context but transferable to other relevant countries.  
Hence, the main outcome of this project is the standardization of GXN’s process with contractors through 
the scaling of the circular economy. To be able to do so, three different tasks were carried out: (1) mapping 
of the supply chain; (2) identification of a key upcycled material to integrate into the value chain; (3) 
specifications and generalization of information coming from the contractors.  
 
In this context, the research question of this thesis is “How can GXN scale up its upcycling activities 
through the standardization of its interfaces?” Additionally, a series of sub-questions were formulated to 
assist in addressing this research question, the three of them help on different matters to be able to solve 
this problem.  
To find out how GXN can achieve the mentioned scalability, it is necessary to explore in depth the different 
requirements that stakeholders, and more specifically, contractors need in their processes. This is done by 
asking (1) What are the main requirements to be provided to contractors for better standardization? 
The research also aims to develop a methodology for evaluating and mapping the material flows due to 
their importance in the supply chain. Therefore, a sub-question to explore this matter would be (2) How 
can potential material flows be evaluated and mapped to help the decision-making of GXN processes with 
platforms? Lastly, for the correct implementation of standardization within their processes firstly it must be 
known the barriers that could be encountered. The research intends to answer (3) What are the key barriers 
and challenges faced by stakeholders in implementing upcycling practices in the construction industry?

2.1. Circular Economy 
The Ellen MacArthur Foundation is focusing on circular economy since its creation in 2005. It has defined 
the concept of CE as a framework for systems-level solutions that address issues including pollution, waste, 
biodiversity loss, and climate change (Macarthur Foundation, 2022). On the contrary, society is used to a 
linear concept. The foundation defines linear economy, also known as the take-make-waste economy, as a 
“system where resources are extracted to make products that eventually end up as waste and are thrown 
away” (Macarthur Foundation, 2022). Geissdoerfer et al. (2017) refer to circular economy as a regenerative 
model that lowers emissions and waste. The make-use-dispose linear economy model will inevitably give 
way to a CE model since linear-based models are linked to mass production and resource consumption 
without taking the physical limits of resources into account (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). Thus, the efficient 
use of resources is key to achieving a regenerative system. The different dimensions that these resources 
are held in the supply chain must be known in order to define their boundaries, challenges, and indicators.  
According to the Ellen MacArthur Foundation (2015), a circular strategy could assist the construction 
industry in reducing its environmental footprint and avoiding growing prices, delays, and other effects of 
unstable commodity markets. At the same time, CE is growing in popularity among academics, 
professionals, and policymakers globally, with everyone highlighting the enormous potential of switching 
to circular thinking (Akhimien et al., 2021). Even though it is just starting to be implemented, there exists 
a gap to be filled regarding its development and improvement.

There are different types of barriers according to knowledge, data, and regulations. Many scholars, 
particularly in the context of CI, have expressed concern about the lack of scalability and execution 
solutions in existing circular business models. It has the effect of making the shift to CE in the construction 
industry dependent on critical mass and scalable solutions (Hyldborg et al., 2022).  
There is a particular study carried out by Hossain et al. (2020),  where it is stressed a compilation of ten 
different challenges for implementing a circular economy into the construction industry. The ten challenges 
bring to light the restrictions and gaps in a variety of areas, including (1) design, (2) material choice, (3) 
supply chain, (4) business model, (5) relevant policy, (6) uncertainty and risk, (7) collaboration among 
actors, (8) knowledge among stakeholders, (9) integration of urban metabolism, and (10) methodology for 
CE evaluation. From them, it is concluded that the construction sector has a chance to switch to a more 
sustainable strategy by implementing the circular economy. For this to be successful, there needs to be a 
focus on the creation of new technologies, enhanced management techniques, and increased cooperation 
between all parties. Governments and authorities must also promote the transition to a circular economy. 
Moreover, the challenge that requires the most attention is the lack of collaboration between all stakeholders 
due to its importance of being able to achieve a common goal (Hossain et al., 2020). 
Høibye and Sand (2018) presented three strategies to speed up the transition towards Circular Economy in 
the construction industry in the Scandinavian regions. The first strategy involves defining the standards and 
specifications for the quality of building materials, such as technical performance, recycling rate, and 
traceability. The second strategy focuses on developing guidelines for reused building products and 
building products made from recycled resources. This includes setting minimum requirements for green 
resource efficiency and sharing information on reused building products containing recycled materials. The 
third and final strategy involves establishing waste and demolition plans, which include screening, 
mapping, and sorting valuable building products for further reuse. This strategy also includes setting 
recycling rates and end-of-waste criteria, as well as providing guidelines and training for demolition 
companies. The presented strategies could be applied to other regions and help with the transition toward a 
more sustainable future.  
On a final note, policies focusing on environmental impact have increased considerably. Science-based 
targets (SBT) are set for companies to reduce their greenhouse emissions. They were created in order to 
comply with the Paris Agreement, to reduce the temperature of the Earth, and tackle global warming 
(Science Base Targets, 2023). One of the CE’s goals is to support sustainable long-term development, and 
along with the SBTs, the CE also aids the accomplishment of the United Nation's SDGs. This opportunity 
exists in the various ways that both individuals and companies interact with the CE (Ghufran et al., 2022). 
According to Sparrevik et al. (2021), a recursive structure is required for this application of environmental 
performance assessment for CE in the CI. 
2.1.1. CE in the UK 
In the UK, it is estimated that 158 Megatons, or 37%, of the total annual materials input, is lost (WRAP, 
2016). Regulations and legislation established in the past years are forcing the CI to follow more sustainable 
options. In the UK, all constructions, both existing buildings and new ones, should follow and fulfill the 
Circular Economy Statement, in compliance with London Plan Policy SI 7(A) (Greater London Authority, 
2022).  
According to London Plan Policy SI 7(A), circular economy (CE) is one in which materials are kept in use 
for as long as possible at their best value before being reused or recycled, leaving a minimal amount of

2.2. Upcycling  
Upcycling refers to the process of transforming waste materials or valueless products into new materials or 
goods that are higher quality or more beneficial to the environment (it can also have economic and social 
benefits) than they were in their prior use (Oyenuga et al., 2017).  Cambridge Dictionary defines upcycling 
as “the activity of making new furniture, objects, etc. out of old or used things or waste material.”                  
According to these two definitions, its interpretation can be seen both at an industrial and product level. On 
both the macro and micro levels, upcycling is becoming more widely acknowledged as an optimistic 
approach to cutting down on material and energy use (Cooper & Kettley, 2014). 
At the moment, the lack of information and case studies on this technique creates a risk aversion in the field 
(Cooper & Kettley, 2014). Thus, it is essential to build a strong waste management infrastructure and share 
successful project implementation expertise if upcycling is to be widely used effectively. It is necessary to 
introduce and spread selective demolition techniques, collect, and store construction and demolition waste 
separately on the construction site, recycle and dispose of materials parallelly with construction to cut down 
on transportation costs (Scolaro & De Medici, 2021). 
Gálvez-Martos et al. (2018), argue that the ideal option in the construction industry from the perspective of 
the circular economy is to reuse the entire structure. Additional studies have shown that dismountable 
buildings have a higher potential for recycling since their component pieces are extremely adaptable and 
can be reused, upcycled, or downcycled with ease (Akhimien et al., 2021) However, upcycling is seen 
sometimes as having low applicability. Companies need a system that monitors the movement of materials 
throughout each product's lifecycle while creating plans for how to recycle those materials for use in future 
products. Achieving such a systemic strategy is challenging (McDonough & Braungart, 2013).  Kyungeun 
Sung (2015) states the main findings on barriers and disadvantages that tackle the implementation of such 
systemic strategy. These being: 
• 
Potential trade-offs between the present value and quality of the materials or products and their 
potential for future upcyclability. 
• 
Immature upcycling streams with varying levels of technological capability that are unable to 
manage all kinds of materials. 
• 
Complicated processes make it difficult and inefficient to gather, clean, sort, dry, and homogenize 
materials.  
• 
Variable supplies of materials with controlled quality (in terms of composition and impurities).

2.3. Platform thinking 
The definition of platform is widely discussed since it has many meanings across different industries and 
contexts. Thuesen et al. (2022) address how it can be defined from a technological standpoint as "a set of 
stable components that support variety and evolvability in a system by restricting the links among the other 
components". On the other hand,  Baldwin and Woodard (2008) state an ecosystem approach according to 
Robertson and Ulrich (1998), which focuses on the actors in the platform ecosystem. They define platforms 
as a collection of resources shared by many products, including components, procedures, expertise, people, 
and relationships. The platform idea is key to advancing modern methods of construction or an 
industrialized approach to construction. Platform thinking is seen as a business strategy that shifts focus 
from traditional supply and demand theories to ones that include the market, the product, or a variety of 
viewpoints (Mosca et al., 2020). 
Platform thinking has undergone substantial development over the past ten years and has established itself 
as a key tactic for managing the rising complexity of value production (Thuesen et al., 2022). Platforms 
offer to address numerous obstacles to adopt completely circular building methods and promote the 
industry's efficiency due to their inherent innovation capabilities and productivity benefits (Jones et 
al.2020). 
Platform thinking is increasingly being considered, which makes sense given that it has the potential to 
provide value and boost productivity (Mosca et al., 2020). However, the main constraint is the lack of 
research in this field. The use of platform alternatives in the construction industry and their potential effects 
on supply chain involvement and management do not appear to be well covered in the existing studies. 
Although some case studies can be used as guidelines, there is still a long way to go to fill in this gap. 
The Transforming Construction Network Plus (N+), a research program conducted by UK Research and 
Innovation, is mobilizing a new movement in the building community to address the most urgent issues in 
the CI, among others. In their digest series nº2 they address the framework developed initially by Thomas 
et al. (2014), and extend it, categorizing the different types of platforms: platform organizations, product 
platforms, platform ecosystems, and market intermediaries (Mosca et al., 2020). These variants’ definitions 
of platforms give a general idea of how the construction industry can apply any of them to its benefit and 
development. The UK Government in particular has highlighted the value of product platforms in the 
Construction Playbook (Government, 2022). Moreover, given that “product platform” is the type of 
platform that the CI has given the most importance to (Grabham & Manu, 2022), an additional definition 
must be stated.

3. Methodology  
3.1. Approach 
In the thesis, deductive and inductive reasoning are combined in an abductive fashion. To find gaps in the 
industry and develop a thorough understanding, a literature review on circular economy, upcycling, and 
platform thinking was first carried out. The circular economy within the construction industry was the main 
topic and the common ground for most of them. After that, a theoretical framework was created using 
multiple workshops and the case study of a particular construction project, with the goal of illustrating how 
platforms might make it easier to integrate circular economy principles and improve communication. 
Data was collected through six interviews with stakeholders involved in the case study. These interviews 
provided insights into the challenges and potential improvements. Subsequently, the data was analyzed and 
integrated into the theoretical framework. Conclusions were drawn from the analysis, synthesizing the 
empirical findings with the existing theoretical knowledge. 
The thesis adopts an abductive approach to combine deductive reasoning (derived from the literature 
review) with inductive reasoning (derived from empirical data) in order to generate new perspectives and 
explanations. This method allows knowledge to be developed iteratively, bridging the gap between theories 
and observations. To provide a clearer explanation of this method, it can be defined that with an abductive 
technique one can gather data to investigate a phenomenon, uncover themes, and explain patterns in order 
to develop a new theory or alter an existing theory that is then tested using additional data collection 
(Saunders et al., 2016). 
3.1.1. Case study 
As mentioned earlier, this thesis follows a case study. A case study approach has the potential to provide 
insights from in-depth investigation into the analysis of a phenomenon in its real-world context, leading to 
detailed empirical descriptions and a formulation of a theory (Yin, 2003). Case study research uses both 
quantitative and qualitative research to acquire these insights, and typically adopts a mixed methods 
approach to fully comprehend the dynamics of the case (Saunders et al., 2016). 
The case study of the construction project followed in this thesis was crucial to gather valuable information. 
It provided a practical setting in which to examine potential problems related to adopting upcycling and the 
circular economy in the construction sector. Through interviews, observations, and data collection, valuable 
insights were gained into stakeholder perspectives, project dynamics, and construction processes. 
Following a case study approach has allowed for a complete analysis and evaluation of the company under 
study.  
Overall, the case study was an essential source of contextual and practical information that allowed for a 
thorough analysis and evaluation of the advantages, disadvantages, and solutions to upcycling and circular 
economy methods in the construction industry.

4.1. Circular Economy  
This framework focused on circular economy will be based on understanding the potential advantages of 
upcycling Construction and Demolition Waste (CDW) and researching strategies to identify as well as map 
material flows.  
The term CDW is used to describe waste produced by commercial activities such as building construction, 
maintenance, demolition, and deconstruction (Gálvez-Martos et al., 2018). Material classification is an 
important part of the construction industry as well as for the creation of flows within its processes. 
Therefore, assigning the correct identifier in accordance with current industry requirements (by designating 
them as waste, when appropriate) should be necessary before processing them according to their: physical 
qualities, preservation status, and remaining technical performances (Addis, 2012).  
According to Hyldborg et al., (2022), it is essential to standardize and decide how CDW should be managed 
(transported, stored, processed, and reinstalled) as well as quantify and verify the sustainability performance 
if the reuse of secondary building materials is to become a common practice in the construction industry. 
This statement is important to be taken into consideration to develop tools to help create the desired material 
flow. The 3R’s principle and waste hierarchy have been found to be able to assist in this matter.  
4.1.1. 3Rs and Waste Hierarchy 
The 3R’s principle stands for reduce, recycle, and reuse. Reducing implies the creation of less waste, cutting 
the problem at its source. Recycling is the practice of using waste materials as resources. Lastly, reusing is 
the process of finding a new use for outdated or unwanted goods that you may otherwise discard (Rogue, 
2018). By focusing mostly on the first of the 3 R's, "reduce," then "reuse," and finally "recycle," waste can 
be minimized effectively. Through the use of a potential circular economy, the 3R’s principle could close 
loops in industrial ecosystems. This principle prevents waste from being produced and turns waste into 
resources. In figure 5, it can be seen how the construction and demolition of a building without a circular 
economy mindset would lead to inefficient results. The building is constructed to, later on, be demolished 
after some years, and materials go to waste. The 3R’s principle tries to avoid this sequence by introducing 
new steps in between before demolishing a building, either by reusing or recycling materials.   
 
On another matter, the Waste hierarchy tool helps categorize the practices employed in the use of waste. It 
differentiates from the 3R’s framework considering that it illustrates the order of priority in waste handling. 
This is represented on an inverted pyramid with 5 different stages. At the top, it is found the most preferred

4.2. Platforms 
As it was previously stated, there are different variants of the definition of a platform and, in this context, 
a platform-thinking and product platform approach will be the main focal points. The importance of taking 
into account both definitions of a platform is crucial in this thesis. Platform thinking will be a valuable 
perspective that encourages stakeholder integration, knowledge exchange, and cooperation (Mosca et al., 
2020). In addition, the idea of a product platform will be useful as a framework for determining and 
including crucial components in the use of circular solutions. This section intends to create a thorough 
understanding of platforms and their function in enabling the adoption of circular practices within the 
construction industry by merging these two concepts together.  
The goal is to create a framework with the basic concepts of a platform to later connect it to the notion of 
CE. First, the components of a platform must be established for an understanding of its entirety. The 
definition and identification of its key components will show how the platform functions and how its parts 
relate to each other. In addition, platforms have modules attached and, in between, different interfaces link 
them together. A further explanation of them will also be necessary. Finally, determining which elements 
and processes should be standardized, and where flexibility and customization must be preserved, is found 
as a critical aspect of the platform development. However, it is crucial to remember that, despite its value, 
standardization is not enough in itself to create a successful product platform. Given the scope and time 
limitation of this thesis, a full strategy to develop and implement a product platform will not the be primary 
objective. Among other things, deep research on market segmentation will not be carried out, meaning that 
the exercises of grouping market opportunities will be left as a possible topic for future investigation and 
research. 
The Product Platform Rulebook has been taken as a reference to develop a framework that serves as a guide 
for these definitions in the following subsections. This Rulebook presents an overview of the technical 
requirements, design principles, and operational procedures necessary to guarantee the platform's 
consistency, dependability, and scalability (Construction Innovation Hub and UK Research and Innovation, 
2022). The product platform approach is recognized as the most beneficial for the building sector. 
Nonetheless, in the construction industry, the scope of product platforms is really wide, as well as it is its 
implementation and development. On account of this wideness, a proper strategy must be established for 
its correct performance in an organization.  
4.2.1. Domains 
It is important to understand the context in which product platforms interact within the CI. The Construction 
Innovation Hub and UK Research and Innovation (2022) depict the differentiation of three main areas, also 
called ‘domains’. Each of them has essential functions and how they interact with each other is one of the 
main challenges of product platforms.   
(1) The Client domain represents the demand in the built environment. It consists of a group of 
individuals in charge of designing, running, and managing buildings and other structures. Large 
entities like the government, as well as homeowners that own and manage individual properties, are 
included in this. 
(2) The Project domain represents those who deploy the activities in the CI, meaning that they are the 
ones who carry out the design, delivery, and management. It involves thousands of firms, ranging 
from big multinational contractors and consultants to small, specialized contractors.

(3) The Product domain represents those who enable the development of these activities. They are in 
charge of the extraction, production, and manufacturing of materials and products. Again, it ranges 
from big multinationals to small companies supplying all kinds of materials.  
These domains face diverse challenges, mainly related to the lack of communication between them. The 
project domain serves as the intermediary (and translator) between the client's needs and technical solutions,

asset operations. This alludes to the theory from (Jones et al., 2022) referring to the fragmentation found in 
the CI. It can be difficult to achieve cost savings through economies of scale because of this lack of 
communication between the customer and product domains.

4.2.3. Interfaces 
According to the Cambridge Dictionary, an interface is defined as “the place where two systems come 
together and have an effect on each other”. However, it can also be seen as communication or interaction, 
meaning that it is not always seen as something tangible. Interfaces are required to ensure that all the data 
is processed and shared across modules. In particular, product platforms must have clear interfaces that 
suppliers and designers of supplementary or related items can access. 
The RIBA plan can be used as a tool that helps to smooth the interfaces of a platform. The CI can use the 
RIBA stages by providing a framework for communication and collaboration between the different 
stakeholders participating in a project. For instance, if architects and other designers collaborate closely 
with engineers, contractors, and other stakeholders they can determine the requirements and specifications 
for the product platform throughout the first RIBA stages of concept design and evolved design. This can 
aid in ensuring that the various modules and interfaces are created and built to satisfy the project's and the 
stakeholders' requirements.  
Interfaces are key for GXN to enable an information flow and to be able to find a way to improve 
communication among the participants of the projects. Consequently, based on the case study, interfaces 
will be the main enablers, and the final theoretical framework will be reflected on them.  
4.2.4. Simplicity within frameworks 
The construction industry is a fast-moving sector and simplicity is key within the projects for a faster and 
better implementation of new processes. It has been shown how simplicity within frameworks has numerous 
advantages compared to more complex notions, suggesting numerous organizations to use more 
straightforward methods.  
From a platform point of view, a simpler framework would imply the reduction of steps, which results in 
having fewer decision-making points, ultimately leading to fewer errors. As a result, processes for 
managing projects, collaborating with others, and exchanging data become more efficient. Big platforms 
and complex machines do not seem to work in practice because everything is so fast-moving and bespoke. 
Hence the use of simple tools is preferred (Poulsgaard, 2023). Construction organizations can rapidly grow 
and customize the platform to support their operations and projects thanks to faster implementation and 
scalability. 
The principle of simplification can be achieved by applying three levers: (1) rationalization, (2) 
standardization, and (3) modularization. Rationalization in the CI is defined by unwanted variation 
eliminated through simplification, reduction of complexity, and using the advantages offered by 
manufacturing and prefabrication techniques. Secondly, to improve overall efficiency, standardization can 
be implemented by using the same components across various technical platforms or products.

4.3. Process Mapping 
Process Mapping can help the reader understand the mapping of a specific case of material flow. This tool 
will therefore be used to develop this thesis’ case study work and material flow. It aims to help understand 
the processes to build and implement a platform through the information obtained.  
Process mapping is a great starting point to create IT support tools and can result in considerable efficiency 
and quality improvements in construction engineering. Some of the key advantages of process mapping are 
the increase in understanding of actors' roles and activities, assistance in identifying strategic, process, and 
IT requirements, or serving as the foundation for IT systems (Klotz et al., 2008).  
In the planning, design, and construction of sustainable buildings, mapping the "as-is" situation improves 
understanding of the process by 5-27% and results in improvements to efficiency and quality (Klotz et al., 
2008). There are many different types of process maps and how they should be chosen depends on the use 
they are going to be given. In this case, three maps were selected due to their relevance to this study’s 
processes. They are (1) swimlane maps, (2) Gantt charts, and (3) stakeholder maps. In the following 
subsections they will be displayed along with their main objectives for this thesis development.  
4.3.1. Swimlane Map 
A swimlane map is a process diagram that divides the process into lanes that stand for various departments, 
functions, or people. The process map is referred to as a "swimlane" because it looks like a pool with lanes 
designating the different process groups (Eureka, 2016). They are also known as Deployment Map or Cross-
Functional Chart, representing a more complex flowchart rather than just a basic diagram.  
They are useful when a process has several parties participating, and it is composed of different stages. 
Responsibilities are clearly stated and confusion about who does what is avoided. In order to facilitate this,

organization while the columns depict the processes/activities in a timeline.

4.3.2. Gantt Chart 
A Gantt Chart is a table that shows how a project will progress and all the components that will be used on 
it. It displays all forthcoming tasks and deadlines in a chronological format. They are an effective tool for 
viewing the complete landscape of one or many projects. It enables to see which tasks are interdependent 
and upcoming milestones (Gantt, 2023). Therefore, having a better knowledge of how delays in their lane 
can impact deadlines, delivery dates, and the overall business process can benefit department members.  
Because Gantt charts only display tasks and their dependencies in flowchart format swimlanes are different 
in this regard. Swimlanes can demonstrate responsibility for each work within a lane, demonstrating a better 
way to show processes (Wrike, 2023).

4.3.3. Stakeholder Mapping Analysis 
Stakeholders “refer to persons, groups or organizations that must somehow be taken into account by leaders, 
managers, and front-line staff” (Bryson, 2004). The correct identification of all the stakeholders is key for 
a correct mapping and analysis. Stakeholder mapping helps identify the stakeholders who can influence the 
most an activity either favorably or negatively, or who are most likely to be impacted by it, allowing for 
the development of successful methods of engagement for future actions (Ginige et al., 2018). 
The LIB case study, by undergoing through this stakeholder mapping, will facilitate the visualization of all 
the actors involved and who can influence the project. Additionally, showing the interconnections between 
them can also allow to show possible inefficiencies and missing pieces. 
In order to see how each affected stakeholder should be treated according to their interests and level of 
influence, Mendelow’s Power-Interest Matrix will be explained and used as one of the tools in this research. 
The consequential analysis will be established in in section 7, analysis.  
The Power-Interest Matrix is a simple tool developed by Mendelow in 1991 to display the stakeholder’s 
categorization as per their power and interest (Mendelow, 1991). Figure 11 illustrates how the vertical axis 
indicates the negotiating power of stakeholders, while the horizontal axis shows the level of interest in the 
construction project. 
• 
The first set of stakeholders has little influence over the decision-making process and little interest 
in it. Typically, one would identify small- to medium-sized suppliers or subcontractors in this group 
who do not provide special equipment, materials, services, or expertise. As a result, a large number 
of businesses may offer comparable or identical services, tools, or materials in the same market.  
• 
The second group is distinguished by a high degree of interest and minimal power. Since they do 
not control the local market, the stakeholders in this group could be small or medium-sized 
businesses with limited negotiating power. They might be opportunistic and seek their own benefit.  
• 
The third category is made up by stakeholders with less interest in the project but strong negotiating 
power. This group's stakeholders may consist of medium- to large-sized businesses.  They might 
be experts in a particular area of a building or offer specialized tools or materials. 
• 
Stakeholders with strong levels of interest and negotiating power make up the last group. They 
have a lot of power over the supply chain, which could have an impact on or even threaten the 
project's final outcome. Stakeholders in this category could include medium- to large-sized 
businesses with experience in specific construction tasks.  They may have opportunistic or strategic 
interests (Rompoti et al., 2020).

4.4. Circular Economy and Platform thinking 
In this section, both circular economy and platform thinking will be set into context as one. The goal is to 
develop a tool that merges the two ideas together and can help with the notions previously explained in 
sections 4.1 and 4.2., along with the mapping tools from 4.3. 
Circular Economy and Platform Thinking are two concepts closely related. The connection between these 
two concepts is a literature gap that still needs to be filled out by academics. Although it has been further 
developed and studied with digital platforms or collaboration platforms. Platform thinking can assist with 
some of the major issues regarding circular construction. More specifically, in the CI, a clearly defined 
product platform can facilitate developing capabilities and integrating a fragmented sector (Thuesen et al., 
2022). 
The notion of CE can be seen as a model to minimize and reutilize waste, maximizing the available 
resources. The value of these resources must be kept at its maximum so they can be used for as much time 
as possible. On the other hand, platform thinking is a concept that can allow building materials and 
components to be more easily reused and recycled. This is achieved thanks to enabling better 
communication and exchange of information between stakeholders through its interfaces. Thus, the 
potential link between both concepts can be used to test how circular economy can enable a better flow of 
communication to help the decision-making of GXN processes with platforms. This theory can be related 
to the hypothesis Thuesen et al. (2022) developed in their systematic literature review: “Platforms enable 
detailed documentation of circular practices and solutions”. Having detailed documentation and 
centralization of data would standardize processes and help with decision-making and communication.  
To establish a connection between these two ideas and explore the hypothesis further, a toolbox was created 
in the section below. This toolbox's major goal is to thoroughly evaluate and document circular practices 
especially for the case study.

6. Case study observations analysis 
In this section it will be laid out the data obtained from the interviews and workshops. These observations 
aim to help the reader gain a better understanding throughout the whole process of the case study. Linking 
these observations with previous theories explained can enlighten the results of this research.  
6.1. Challenges 
In the different interviews held with the participants, some challenges were found commonly repeated 
among the interviewees. Communication and time have been noted as the main challenges, along with other 
secondary issues.  
6.1.1. Communication  
Communication is the exchange of thoughts and ideas between experts engaged in related or 
complementary tasks. During a project, ensuring that it goes smoothly might be one of the most important 
challenges. Both, the demolition and the steel contractor working on the LIB project noted the lack of 
communication and relationships among the stakeholders. They would have liked to be more involved in 
the process which, overall, with a better flow of communication could have been solved. The pre-demolition 
auditor likewise noted the low frequency they interacted with GXN; with more regular communication they 
could have got more out of the process. The demolition contractor mentioned that it would have allowed 
them to be more influential and fully invest in the story the architects wanted to portray. To this statement, 
GXN agreed and remarked on the importance of fully carrying out ideas on behalf of having proper contact.  
“Ideas get lost, and designs could be improved if communication was done more continuously.” – Henry 
Glogau 
The clarity in communication can have a big impact on how successful it is. Even if interactions take place 
often, the entire process may be jeopardized if data and dialogues are not transparent and clear. The project 
manager is aware of its role to act as an interface between the stakeholders and agrees that more information 
is essential to avoid uncertainty and unclarity.  
Some interesting points on fragmentation in the industry were revealed in the interview with the executive 
architects. It was stressed that the fragmentation affecting the CI can lead to poor communication due to 
teams that experience discontinuities, having miscommunication, delays, cost overruns, and a lack of 
innovation. The supply chain’s fragmentation which Jones et al. (2022) refer to is recognized in this case 
study, having so many intermediaries is one of the consequences of the beforementioned lack of a proper 
information flow.  
Solving this problem must be a priority since ongoing communication among the stakeholders is vital for 
the correct flow of information. To address this challenge, circularity should be implemented. It would help 
the connection between all the actors, closing the information flow.  
6.1.2. Time  
Time in a construction project can imply the investment of enormous amounts of money if not managed 
properly. In the LIB plan, one of the main challenges that all interviewees recalled was how the timing of 
engagement from GXN in the project wasn’t ideal. Theoretically, everyone should join from the starting 
point, so delays are avoided.

All things considered, the time challenge distinguished in this research can be addressed in different

challenges that are also worth facing. They will be introduced in the next subsection. 
6.1.3. Other challenges 
There were some other challenges mentioned along the way which are relevant to this study. Bureaucracy, 
supply chain ownership, and materials specifications are of special interest due to their possible implication 
to GXN’s room for improvement.  
Firstly, one of the challenges noted by the demolition contractor was how bureaucracy sometimes can get 
in the way during the development of a project such as LIB. Having to go through a specific stakeholder to 
approve all the decisions can sometimes be counterproductive. Bureaucracy can cause delays and affect the 
decision-making in different levels. To alleviate this problem solutions like process streamlining, increased 
transparency, and improved stakeholder communication could solve it.  
Secondly, the main contractor has its own supply chain, and for this project the supply chain was inherited 
based on the subcontractors GXN were talking to beforehand. A supply chain that an organization inherits 
from prior partners or subcontractors might provide a number of difficulties and problems. Due to 
unfamiliarity, there may be issues with coordination and integration as well as communication and 
information flow.  
Lastly, the executive architect debated the difficulties that not knowing the origin of materials can generate. 
If a manufacturer doesn't claim ownership of the product, it may be difficult to determine whether the

material complies with any standards. This challenge can be overcome by not using any of the upcycled

These two last challenges resemble once again the fragmentation within the CI studied from Jones et al. 
(2022). The construction contractor’s challenge particularly fits in the vertical fragmentation, which 
happens when diverse companies oversee different aspects of the project. Each party in the supply chain is 
in charge of a certain task, like design, production, assembly, or operation. When a company inherits a 
supply chain from past partners or subcontractors, it implies that they are taking over the obligations and 
connections those parties created during earlier phases of the project. The vertical separation between 
various entities involved in the construction process is the primary cause of the challenges and issues that 
come with inheriting a supply chain. This explains why the main contractor found itself having to work 
with an already appointed manufacturer they didn’t have in their supply chain and the occurrence of 
subsequent problems.  
On the other hand, the challenge of not knowing the origin of materials due to the frequent turnover of 
companies in the construction industry over the years aligns with the concept of longitudinal fragmentation. 
This fragmentation is characterized by the replacement and transfer of teams when projects are finalized. 
When these teams switch between projects, the new team may not have the necessary information about 
the origin or source of the materials utilized in earlier projects. The inability to transfer information and 
preserve continuity can make it difficult to trace the origin of materials, understand their qualities, or ensure 
compliance with laws or sustainability standards.  
6.2. Improvements 
In addition to the challenges mentioned, there were some improvements suggested by the different parties. 
They are directly related to certain challenges and will be addressed in this section.  
6.2.1. Priorities and aspirations  
The main priority of a project is finding the balance between achieving a good program and at a good price. 
Nevertheless, delivering according to the ESG aspirations makes it hard to accomplish this. There is always 
a slight compromise between the aspirations for upcycling and the commercial impact for the developers.  
To achieve a good program, there must be a good plan from the beginning, which is essential for the success 
of the project. It must be agreed on what are the materials going to be used for, their costs, and achievable 
quantities. Many of the contractors would have wanted to be better informed of the goals and of what 
needed to be achieved. If the stakeholders have their requirements clear, the process itself can prevent time 
spent on queries.   
Moreover, there needs to be a distinct differentiation between a project requirement and an aspirational 
requirement. Something aspirational means that it wants to be achieved but it is not known how to be 
accomplished or used for. On the other hand, a project requirement is a goal that the client states is 
mandatory to obtain. In this case study, from the executive architect’s point of view, the requirement to 
include upcycled material wasn’t clear. There needed to be a better understanding of what the recycled 
materials could be used for, meaning it was seen only as aspirational. This may be related to the notion 
brought up by Hyldborg et al. (2022) on the fundamental step in determining how CDW should be managed. 
It also alleges the need of its standardization, which is the next improvement in this analysis.  
To sum everything up, it was made evident by the discussions with contractors the necessity of clearly 
articulating priorities from the very beginning in the construction industry. Priorities that are not clearly

expressed might waste time and resources. To perform their jobs well, contractors require a clear 
understanding of their obligations and responsibilities. The industry can minimize inefficiencies, avoid 
misunderstandings, and support more efficient project execution by setting clear priorities at the start of the 
project. Therefore, after analyzing the observations from the interviews, the main objective should be to 
have a proper stronger priority list and having the client understand what an aspirational goal is and what 
must be a requirement.  
On a final note, it is necessary to remark that these priorities need to be clear and simple. A simplification 
of documentation can facilitate a better overall understanding of the project’s requirements. This need for 
simplification is closely related to previously introduced improvement, standardization.  
6.2.2. Standardization 
In a construction project, simplification and standardization go hand in hand. Both concepts aim for a 
reduction in complexity and the steps taken during a process. Throughout the interviews, it became evident 
that the simplification of the documentation and specifications was an improvement to aim for.  
The pre-demolition auditor, who in this case is a well-experienced circular economy advisor, pointed out 
how there should be work put into standardization. She recalls the problem in the construction industry 
regarding the untransferable characteristic that exists from project to project when teams do things 
differently. Results are not comparable due to having done projects quite differently every time they are 
developed. Therefore, from the conversation held with the auditor, it can be extracted that the CI should 
adopt standardized practices and processes. By implementing standards that can be applied across different 
projects, it would be easier to ensure that the processes are consistent and comparable, essentially leading 
to more accurate evaluations of project outcomes.  
“There's a lot of work going on by multiple actors effectively trying to do the same thing, but all doing it a 
bit differently. I think if we can have standards for some of these things, it would greatly streamline 
processes” – FF 
Platforms don’t differ far from this concept of standardization. The auditor highlighted that, overall, it will 
be easier to have one big platform rather than having many different small ones. It is crucial to move in the 
same direction using the same templates. As a result, the auditor alleges the standardization needed in 
platforms besides an easy user interface where everything needed is found right away. Taking all into 
consideration, there is an evident link with this standardization, which is the need for the centralization of 
information from GXN. When asked in the interviews, everyone claimed that simplicity would be key for 
future documentation used in projects. The standardization of everything into a single transferable 
document is something wished for by many contractors. It would allow them to improve the challenge they 
had with communication and provide them with a better list of specifications.  GXN is currently working 
on different templates to be able to provide this data to the parties involved during the project. 
Briefly to conclude, the main contractor pointed out that not only the formatting of this single 
documentation is important. It would also be advantageous to have just one person to illustrate the rest 
instead of having 5-6 people under the same product. What the contractor meant by this is that having just 
one person to act as a mediator, as an interface, who later informs the rest of the team would accelerate the 
process.

Upcycling  
The point taken from all the interviewees regarding upcycling in the years to come was very similar. 
Upcycling is taking over the CI and has a huge impact on the view of how to recycle materials. Although 
it is starting to get known in the construction sector, there needs to be more education on it. Upcycling 
could be implemented and brought on board faster if all the people involved had more knowledge of its 
processes, benefits, and risks. The interviewees claimed that GXN opened their eyes to all of the potential 
uses that could be given to the different materials. This shows the education that is needed and, when 
properly implemented, could benefit the CI.  
The project manager gave some insight regarding upcycling in the future, mentioning that even though it is 
mainly only on the radar for big developers, in the coming years it will filter down to more small, medium 
enterprises (SME). Specially in the London market, embodied carbon credentials on projects are highly 
scrutinized, hence the demand for this kind of sustainable solutions is on high development.  
There was a common agreement on how upcycling does not have a big impact on the carbon footprint of a 
construction project but, despite that, the storytelling behind it is remarkable.  Since most of the embodied 
carbon sits in the construction phase, maintenance of the building, and the decommissioning of the new 
building, upcycling takes a back seat. An approach to promote it would be by showing the client how 
upcycling has a carbon value, increasing this way the interest of the developers. From the perspective of 
the contractor, unless there is genuine evidence to demonstrate to the client that their current actions will

7.1.2. Supplier requirements 
Fulfilling and, most importantly knowing, the supplier’s requirements are crucial for a proper development 
of construction projects. Sample sizes, quantities, and quality, among others, are required specifications 
that need to be included as part of the study analysis.   
 
The overall objective of the case study is to develop a more organized and compliant method for handling 
the data and information, and collecting the necessary stakeholder specifications. GXN hopes to standardize 
its templates and use the same language when structuring its models. They are attempting to use the same 
naming schemes and materials for each project by centralizing their information in Excel documents. 
Ultimately, they would like to create two separate Excels. The first one would be for internal use, having a 
“ready to go” excel for each project. It would have a quick filtering process, with the same naming materials 
including associated material carbon numbers, and a low diagram tool with material streams. The second 
Excel would consist of a separate external sheet for communication, so stakeholders can discuss cost, time, 
and risk. Excel is widely available, so making it familiar to all businesses while keeping things simple 
would avoid complications in the future.  
 
To study if the Excels would achieve the desired outcome, the simplification principle introduced by 
Deloitte (2021) ought to be followed. Firstly, rationalization should be applied. This is accomplished by 
eliminating unwanted variation, meaning that all unnecessary data should be avoided. The Excels should 
accommodate only simple categories that enable an easy lecture of the information. The main goal of this 
step is to optimize the flow of information. Secondly, the lever of standardization must be used, improving 
the efficiency. This is under development and continuously being improved by GXN. In the digitalization 
workshop, they displayed how they are trying to utilize the same name materials and procedures and 
considering the same type of stakeholder involvement, if not the same, as similar as it can get. Lastly, 
modularization is achieved through the aforementioned creation of modules, by sharing a structure and 
focusing on a specific function, and having the flexibility to work independently when necessary. 
 
This need to collect specifications alleges the first strategy from Høibye and Sand (2018), first introduced 
in the literature review, developed to speed up the transition towards Circular Economy in the construction 
industry. The first strategy involved defining the standards and specifications for the quality of building 
materials, such as technical performance, recycling rate, and traceability. However, sometimes the 
obtainment of these specifications can end up being a bigger challenge than predicted. In a workshop held 
with different actors from the CI, where GXN participated, it was acknowledged that there are certain 
barriers needed to be overcome to implement circular practices. In particular, certifications and material 
traceability are examples of necessary specifications from suppliers challenging to get. It was discussed 
how it is challenging to guarantee the quality and sustainability of recycled or reused materials due to a lack 
of certifications that are easily accessible and a lack of transparency in material traceability. 
 
The discussion has led to the conclusion that GXN must increase transparency, while also working to raise 
suppliers' awareness, in order to overcome this challenge of obtainment of specifications so thereafter can 
be updated in their centralized data base.

7.2. Upcycling in GXN 
Based on the comments from the case study observations, more in-depth insights can be extracted regarding 
the future of upcycling in the industry. GXN ought to continue pressuring and demonstrating to their clients 
the imminent need to implement circular practices. Upcycling, even though not having a big carbon impact 
on the overall project, in the long term is seen as an investment that will provide a higher value to the 
construction industry. To achieve a correct implementation of an upcycling strategy, the barriers stated by 
Kyungeun Sung (2015) must be overcome. In this case study, it has become apparent that three out of the 
four barriers have been, directly or indirectly, pointed out: 
• 
Potential trade-offs: in order to overcome the trade-offs between the current value and future 
upcyclability, research and development efforts could concentrate on developing new approaches 
that strike a balance between durability and simplicity in disassembling. GXN’s intention to look 
for simplicity in their designs, along with easy separation of materials, could mitigate this 
constraint.  
• 
Immature upcycling streams: the development of upcycling streams can be addressed through 
knowledge sharing and cooperation. Thanks to the upcycling expertise that GXN can provide, as 
well as other accomplished organizations, by sharing documentation and developing standardized 
processes, this challenge can be lessened. This aligns as well with this thesis’ objective to promote 
collaboration between stakeholders.  
• 
Complicated processes make it difficult and inefficient to gather, clean, sort, dry, and homogenize 
materials: Even though some automation of the processes could help on this matter, no specific 
data has been mentioned related to this challenge and, therefore, no indicated solution can be 
extracted for this case study.  
• 
Inconsistent supply and process complexity: In order to create consistent material sources and 
establish clear requirements for acceptable materials, there is a need for interaction with suppliers 
and stakeholders. If cooperation happens in the industry, it could be established quality standards 
and recommendations for upcycling procedures.  
 
By tackling these constraints, the implementation of upcycling can be encouraged, promoting a circular 
economy and sustainable resource management in the construction sector.  
 
Where does the upcycling process stand in this project? In the waste hierarchy, previously explained in 
chapter 3, it can be seen how the upcycling techniques of GXN match with the second section at the top of 
the pyramid, “reuse”. Steel-section elements could be reused and the whole portal frame of buildings can 
be reclaimed for reuse.

7.3.2. Modules and interfaces 
As stated earlier, modules are characterized by common repeatable elements. These commonalities can be 
in the form of four categories. These being (1) kit of parts (physical components) such as the materials for 
buildings, (2) production processes (methods used to produce those parts), (3) knowledge in the form of 
information, requirements, regulations etc, and (4) people and relationships (individuals): contractors, 
authorities. 
In this case, data and communication have been found as the main common repeatable elements, acquiring 
mainly the form of knowledge. GXN wants all the information standardized and centralized, so the 
decision-making is smoother. The repetition is, in this case, that GXN will be able to just let someone fill a 
very standardized template and smoothly merge that into their process, that’s where is also the repetition. 
Data sharing across the various components would be more streamlined and effective through the use of 
interfaces. By utilizing these interfaces, stakeholders involved in the demolition, sorting, and upcycling 
processes can have access to important data, encouraging cooperation and intermodular interaction. 
After taking these modules and interfaces’ characteristics into consideration, it is important to bear in mind, 
as previously explained, the insights provided by Thuesen et al. (2022). They determined that since in the 
CI the documentation lacks the appropriate level of detail, it is impossible to compare and evaluate multiple 
approaches, which makes it difficult to accomplish continuous growth in the performance of circular 
solutions. However, in this case study, it has been noticed how this can be addressed with platforms. They 
can enable the stakeholders contributing to GXN’s projects to get access and participate in a centralized 
repository of data, improving the tracking, analysis, and evaluation of circular practices.  
“Platforms enable detailed documentation of circular practices and solutions.” 
Considering the commonality of data and communication, the challenges highlighted during interviews can 
be effectively addressed throughout the modules and their corresponding interfaces. By implementing these 
solutions, it becomes possible to enhance the level of detailed documentation and facilitate extended 
communication among stakeholders. 
For example, in the demolition module, the challenge of providing clients with a clear plan for material use 
can be solved by incorporating a feature that tracks the position and quantity of steel, ensuring transparency 
and effective communication of the utilization plan. Similarly, the difficulty of strengthening 
communication and relationships in the classification module can be addressed by introducing an interface

7.4. Final discussion 
In this final discussion, it will be argued how the research questions have been answered and provided the 
information initially requested. Throughout this study, it was possible to establish a thorough 
understanding of the advantages and application of a platform strategy in the context of achieving circular 
economy goals within the construction sector by combining the knowledge acquired from literature, 
industry insights, and direct engagements with experts in the field. 
The main research question consisted of investigating how GXN can scale up its upcycling activities 
through the standardization of its interfaces. It has been examined by conducting a thorough analysis of the 
current practices and challenges within GXN's upcycling activities. The research involved studying the 
existing interfaces and processes involved in upcycling, identifying areas of improvement, and proposing 
a framework for standardizing these interfaces. The suggested framework would recommend implementing 
specific interfaces such as tracking the location and quantity of materials, assessing the materials for 
upcycling potential, and tracking the circulation of materials during the upcycling process. GXN can 
increase documentation, transparency, and monitoring of the development of circular solutions by 
standardizing these interfaces.

aspirations for the use of materials (including recycling and upcycling), and the necessary time frame. A 
well-defined time frame for establishing standardized processes was clearly important in assuring efficient 
and effective standardization across contractors. 
Finally, through the set of interviews conducted with experts within the construction industry, the sub-
research question “What are the key barriers and challenges faced by stakeholders in implementing 
upcycling practices in the construction industry?” could be answered. A collection of numerous challenges 
and potential improvements regarding the construction project case study have been summarized. 
Particularly it can be highlighted the lack of communication, relationships, and misconception of priorities 
and aspirations, as the main difficulties encountered. Furthermore, challenges that might arise during the 
implementation of upcycling strategies have also been attempted to be tackled.  
This research attempts to provide insights, recommendations, and guidelines that can be used in real-life 
scenarios by investigating these practical aspects. This complies with the goal of improving practical 
knowledge and facilitating the implementation of circular economy strategies in the construction industry. 
While this study may have theoretical foundations, the primary emphasis tends to be on producing practical 
contributions that can generate tangible changes and improvements in industry practices.

8. Limitations and future research 
8.1. Limitations 
One of the main limitations of this thesis is the time limit specified for the research. There is a constrained 
amount of time to go extensively into the topic and collect exhaustive data, with only a few months available 
for research. Furthermore, as the project under study has not yet been completed, not all essential data is 
available yet. This constraint makes it difficult to conduct a thorough analysis and reach clear findings. 
Additionally, because the project is still in progress, it's possible that certain stakeholders haven't completed 
their crucial tasks, which can impact the comprehensiveness of the insights that were obtained. It is critical 
to identify these limitations and understand how the project's incomplete nature and the resulting data gaps 
may affect the thesis' findings and conclusions. 
Another significant limitation of this thesis is the primary reliance on data obtained from human sources 
rather than concrete datasets or numerical information. The research predominantly focused on gathering 
insights, perspectives, and experiences from stakeholders involved in the project. While valuable qualitative 
data was collected through interviews, workshops, and discussions, the absence of quantitative data or 
established datasets makes it difficult to come to firm conclusions or make statements that are statistically 
substantiated. Furthermore, because the field under investigation is very new, it's conceivable that there 
aren't yet any complete, reliable data to back up the observations. The absence of publicly accessible data 
that could have supported the research and findings is a result of the uncertainties and changing nature of 
this industry. 
The last limitation found is the sample size of workshops and interviews. The study's wide scope and the 
engagement of various companies led to a very limited sample size of participants, despite the fact that 
some companies sent multiple representatives. Due to the small sample size, there is a greater chance that 
personal preferences and biases will affect the results of the data collection. The data gathered from a small 
number of participants might not fully reflect the diversity of issues, viewpoints, and potential solutions 
that exist in the sector. As a result, the thoughts and points of view of a small group of people may have an 
impact on the thesis' results and conclusions. 
8.2. Future research 
Future research may be expanded on the findings of this thesis by investigating the construction industry in 
other countries and focusing on markets outside of London. Although the current case study and research 
are based on the London market, it would be beneficial to explore how similar or distinct challenges and 
practices appear in construction industries of different countries within the European Union (EU), or even 
outside of it. Researchers can learn more about regional differences in regulations, policies, and 
industry practices that may affect the implementation of platforms and the notions of circular economy by 
investigating these other markets. This comparative analysis would enable a deeper knowledge of the 
contextual factors impacting the adoption of circular practices and the efficacy of platforms.  
In this thesis, only two definitions of platforms were used. However, it could be further investigated other 
definitions and conceptualizations in relation to the beforementioned platforms as well as the circular 
economy. Additionally, contrasting and comparing various definitions of platforms might reveal if they are 
applicable and appropriate in the particular context of the construction sector. This study could identify any 
potential overlaps or inconsistencies between various platform conceptualizations and show how they 
contribute to the objectives of the circular economy. It might even highlight cutting-edge strategies and

9. Conclusions 
In conclusion, this thesis has explored the implementation of a platform approach in the context of scaling 
up towards a circular economy in the construction industry. Through the examination of the demolition, 
categorization, and upcycling modules, along with their corresponding interfaces, it has become evident 
that platforms offer significant advantages in terms of detailed documentation, improved communication, 
and enhanced collaboration. By addressing the challenges identified through stakeholder interviews and 
utilizing the commonality of data and communication, these modules provide practical solutions for 
achieving greater transparency, efficiency, and sustainability in construction projects. 
The case study conducted within the London market has provided valuable insights into the potential of 
platform thinking and product platforms to drive circular practices and solutions. The findings indicate that 
by centralizing data, standardizing processes, and facilitating effective communication, platforms enable 
stakeholders to make informed decisions, track materials throughout the value chain, and optimize the 
upcycling process. The integration of the company's existing excel-based system further demonstrates the 
compatibility and adaptability of the platform approach. 
However, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of this study, including the limited sample size of 
interviewees and the focus on steel materials. Future research should expand the scope to include a broader 
range of stakeholders and materials, as well as explore other geographical markets to enhance the 
generalizability of the findings. 
Overall, this thesis contributes to the growing body of knowledge on circular economy practices in the 
construction industry by providing a framework for utilizing platforms to overcome challenges, promote 
sustainability, and drive the transition towards a circular economy. By adopting a platform approach and 
using the proposed modules and interfaces, companies can enhance their capabilities, foster collaboration 
among stakeholders, and achieve long-term sustainability goals.
