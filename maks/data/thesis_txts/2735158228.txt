Abstract
The need for accurate and reliable modelling of transportation has, in recent years, fa-
cilitated the growth of Agent-Based transportation Models (ABMs). While in many ways
superior to traditional models, there is still a scarce amount of literature on evaluating
their performance. In particular, validation of scenario years is largely unexplored, and
the process of calibrating an ABM is inherently complex and computationally intensive.
This study aims to provide additional experience on calibration and validation through
application to two different ABMs. The COMPASS model for Copenhagen is validated
against car, bicycle and public transport data. SimMobility, an open-source simulator, is
calibrated through a novel and advanced Bayesian Optimization approach. Results show
that COMPASS predictions are skewed due to the Covid-19 pandemic, but generally the
model has a solid performance, and the most influential parameters of SimMobility are
calibrated. A new example of successful ABM validation is added to the body of litera-
ture, and an innovative calibration approach is shown. The latter is adaptable to other
ABMs, allowing for future research in this direction.

Introduction
1.1
Context and motivation
Our world is increasingly urbanized, with 56.9% of the global population now living in cities,
and this number is projected to increase in the coming decades (“UNCTAD Handbook of
Statistics 2023”, 2024). Urban life offers a great deal of opportunities, but with it comes a
vast array of problems that need to be managed. One such problem is transportation. The
main cause of traffic congestion is the imbalance between the increasing demand for traffic
volume and the relatively insufficient supply of traffic infrastructure (Qiao et al., 2021).
Additionally, the transport sector accounts for about 25% of total emissions in the EU
(“Transport and mobility”, 2024). If the world is to remain at a safe level of global warming
of 1.5°C, as set out in the 2015 Paris Agreement, CO2 emissions from the transport sector
must fall by more than 3% per year to 2030 (“Transport - Energy System - IEA”, 2024).
In order to manage transportation systems optimally, expansion of infrastructure should
be carefully considered. Investments into infrastructure need to be informed by sound
analyses. These analyses usually rely on predictions of future traffic behavior: it is cru-
cial that predictions are accurate and reliable. Traffic models are used to simulate and
describe transportation systems and predict consequences of alterations to the system.
Traditionally, traffic models are built upon the four-step model and have trips as the central
unit of analysis. They are developed on the basis of historical trends and represent typical
average conditions for a given time period. These models have a very rigid structure and
can only present data in an aggregated form (Carvalho Bustamante, 2022).
In the last decades, the state-of-the art of travel demand models advanced from traditional
four-step models, passing through tour-based models, to Agent-Based Models (ABMs)
(Monteiro et al., 2022). The latter simulate a person’s activities for a typical day, and only
afterwards, their mobility demand is modelled. An agent-based approach to modelling
is more advanced as it describes the phenomenon at hand (the need for transportation)
through the processes that cause it (Franziska Klügl, 2012). These models are valuable
as they can facilitate decision-making processes by offering a nuanced comprehension
of traveller behaviour (Kagho et al., 2020)
Specifically, ABMs simulate the personal daily activity schedule (DAS) of each individual
in the population. Transportation is then modelled as the derived demand of the individual
wishing to take part in various activities. Because ABMs explicitly model the individual de-
cision process and behaviour, the approach is much more realistic than modelling strictly
from origin-destination (OD) flows or travel survey data.
Two different ABMs are used in this study. The COMPASS (Copenhagen Greater Area
Model for Passenger Transport) model is an ABM intended for the simulation of traffic in
the Greater Copenhagen Area (GCA). It is used for the strategic exploration of transport
scenarios, on a long timeframe and through a macroscopic approach. This model has
been calibrated on base year 2017, and simulates scenario years 2021, 2025 and 2035.
Transportation modes simulated in the model are: walk, cycle, car and public transport.
The second model, SimMobility, is an open-source microscopic mobility simulator, which
has been used for Singapore (Marczuk et al., 2015) and Boston (Viegas de Lima et al.,
2018), and is now being applied to Denmark. It is highly adaptable and, as a collab-
orative project, it is still growing. It has been conceived at the Future Urban Mobility

Research Group at the Singapore-MIT Alliance for Research and Technology (SMART).
Being adaptable, modelled modes are easily changed for each context. In the Danish
case, they are: walk, cycle, car and public transport.
To ensure ABM robustness and reliability, their validation and calibration is essential. Val-
idation is an indirect evaluation of the model output at a later phase than model develop-
ment (Parady et al., 2021). Calibration aims at adjusting the model parameters so that
the modelled behaviour captures the scenario behaviour as best as possible (O’Sullivan,
2004). This thesis explores the calibration and validation of the two mentioned ABMs.
The present work is written in partial collaboration with Københavns Kommune (KK). Hav-
ing an up-to-date model for traffic in the GCA is of great interest for the municipality of
Copenhagen. Indeed, future investments in transport infrastructure and policies depend
on the correct predictions of the COMPASS model.
1.2
Problem statement
In academic literature there is a scarce amount of work on analysing the performance of
ABMs. Despite the increasing adoption of ABMs in practice, comprehensive validation
of various components that attempt to model mobility behaviour has yet to occur (Khan
et al., 2021). One important limitation in existing literature is that most validation research
is for the base year only. It is crucial to evaluate future years, as this tells how well the
model is replicating real travel patterns. This allows for accurate traffic analyses, which
can safely guide investments in new infrastructure.
Though more researched than validation, calibration is problematic due to the sheer num-
ber of parameters in the model. Calibration procedures often depend on simplified as-
sumptions, to avoid increasing computational requirements or complexity. This means
that there has to be a prioritization of parameters to calibrate (Schulze et al., 2017). Meth-
ods that can cope with large numbers of parameters, interaction of different levels of sub-
models, and high computational requirements are needed.
1.3
Goals
The performance of COMPASS is evaluated to reveal how well it replicates real-world
data. This thesis also explores the lack of data in the ABM validation field, and brings
additional resources and experiences. Additionally, the evaluation illustrates the extent
to which travel patterns were influenced by Covid-19, and the lingering visibility of these
effects one year after the implementation of the strictest containment measures.
The calibration aims to fine-tune the parameters of SimMobility which have the most influ-
ence in model outputs, in a computationally efficient way. This process opens the way for
studying applications of an enhanced calibration procedure to other large-scale models.
1.4
Thesis outline
This work is structured as follows: first, Chapter 2 gives a comparison of the structures
and functioning of the two models, COMPASS and SimMobility. Existing literature on
the topics of ABM calibration and validation is then explored in Chapter 3. Methods used
throughout this work are explained in Chapter 4. The validation and calibration processes
are explained respectively in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6. As a note, it has not been possi-
ble to run SimMobility applied to the Danish case due to external limitations. Instead, a
version of SimMobility applied to a fake Virtual City has been utilized. Results are then
discussed, along with limitations of the present work, in Chapter 7. Finally, conclusions
and indications for future research are explored in Chapter 8.

2
COMPASS and SimMobility Comparison
This chapter will give a brief description of the characteristics of COMPASS and SimMo-
bility, as well as a more in-depth outline of the ABM part of each model. More detailed
descriptions are given for the parts responsible for generating each individual’s Daily Ac-
tivity Schedule (DAS).
2.1
General model comparison
COMPASS is an activity-based state-of-art traffic model for car, bicycle, public transport
and walking. The model covers the Greater Copenhagen Area (GCA), and it describes an
average weekday traffic. The base year is 2017. COMPASS was developed in the 2010s
to succeed the Øresund Trafik Model (OTM), which is a tour-based model for the same
region (Vuk, 2011). Reasons for developing COMPASS were to gain a better understand-
ing of consequences of introducing road pricing in Copenhagen as well as congestion
problems on the access roads to the city (Vuk and Petersen, 2006).
The calculation assumptions are user-specified and include population, workplaces, prices,
economic growth, tariff assumptions and assumptions for external traffic to and from the
GCA. The demand calculations include a goods and truck traffic model for calculating the
number of goods and truck trips. The traffic demand for transport is calculated using an
activity-based model, integrated with a car ownership model, a model for calculating ticket
prices for public transport, a model for calculating school and workplace location and a
model for calculating parking search time. In addition, the calculation assumptions include
the coded road and path network as well as the public transport network for the individ-
ual calculation. The road and path network is based on an extract from OpenStreetMap
(OSM), while the public transport network is based on an extract from the Rejseplanen
(Kommune, 2022).
SimMoblity is a new simulation platform, developed at the Singapore-MIT Alliance for
Research and Technology (SMART), that integrates various mobility-sensitive behavioral
models within a multi-scale framework that considers land-use, transportation and com-
munication interactions (Y. et al., 2015). It is made up of three separate modules: Long-
Term (LT), Mid-Term (MT) and Short-Term (ST), which focus on different time horizons
(from seconds to years).
SimMobility is fully modular in a way that each level can run independently and only ac-
cess other levels when necessary. SimMobility is also integrated because it concurrently
simulates demand and supply at each level, as well as interactions between different lev-
els. The LT level provides population characteristics and land-use configuration to the MT,
which also transmits trip-chains to the ST level. The ST provides performance measures
to the MT, which provides accessibility measures to the LT. This feedback loop is crucial
to the functioning of SimMobility, and is repeated several times in a simulation run.
SimMobility’s peculiarity is in that it operates within a multi-scale simulation platform that
considers land-use, transportation and communication interactions. It focuses on impacts
on transportation networks, intelligent transportation services and vehicular emissions,
thereby enabling the simulation of a portfolio of technology, policy and investment options
under alternative future scenarios (Adnan et al., 2015). These characteristics make it
stand out from similar ABMs.

2.2
ABM structure
2.2.1
COMPASS
The ABM part of COMPASS is called DaySim. It is divided into different submodels in a
specific hierarchy, which is shown in Figure 2.1. Models work on each individual house-
hold. Initially, long term choices, such as work location and car ownership, are modelled
(Long-Term models section). Choices that influence the overall pattern of activities are
modelled next (Day-Pattern models). Subsequently, DaySim defines the specific infor-
mation for tours (such as time, mode, purpose, destination). This is at the Tours models
level. And finally, the stops that each individual makes on tours, and the trip chunks, are
modelled, along with their details.
Figure 2.1: COMPASS DaySim flow diagram.
The Long-Term submodels are the work and school location models, the pay to park
at workplace model, the auto ownership model and the transit pass ownership model.
Further comments on the car ownership model are given in Section 5.2.1. The Day Activity
Pattern models are executed next. They are the following.
Primary Family Priority Type
Primary Family Priority Type represents a high priority household decision to spend time
together for in-home activities (Vuk et al., 2015). Simply put, this can be seen as qual-
ity time that a family chooses to spend together. This influences subsequent travel be-
haviours, and is thus placed at the top of the hierarchy. In COMPASS, it is modelled
jointly with the household’s choice of whether to conduct one or more joint tours for non-
mandatory purposes. They are both binary choices, which result in a four-alternative
Multinomial Logit (MNL) model.
HH Day Pattern Type
The Household Day Pattern Type model determines each household member’s priority
of activities for the day. For each person, mandatory and non-mandatory travel is de-
termined. The model has 81 alternatives and is formulated as an MNL. The first four
household members are modelled in detail; from the fifth onwards, a simpler 3-alternative
MNL is used. This model creates a pattern of activities for the lower-hierarchy models to
follow.

Person Mandatory Activities
Given the activity pattern, the mandatory activities are modelled. This includes at-home
work, number of mandatory tours and potential presence of intermediate stops for each
individual with mandatory activities. The submodels in this section are the Work at Home,
the Mandatory Tour Generation, and the Mandatory Stop Presence model. The work at
home model is discussed in deeper detail in Section 5.2.2.
Joint Mandatory Half Tours
Once the household’s need for mandatory travel is established, the next set of models
determines joint travel to and/or from mandatory activities. Joint travel is in the form of
half-tours, which can either be paired or unpaired. Joint travel can either be partially or
fully joint.
To model these tours, a Joint Half Tour Generation model determines the occurrence and
type of joint half tour. This is followed by a Fully Joint Half Tour Participation (or Partially
Joint Half Tour Participation, accordingly) model to determine which household members
take part in the tour. This pair of models is repeated until the generation model determines
that no more joint half tours occur.
Joint Non-mandatory Tours
Similarly to mandatory tours, non-mandatory tours are modelled through a pair of models
to determine tour number and purposes, and participation.
Person Day Pattern
The last of the day level models are constrained by all the prior model outcomes. This
pair of models determines the number and purpose of each tour, and the purposes of
eventual intermediate stops. First, the Day Pattern Model determines the presence of
tour and stop purposes. Second, the Person Tour Generation determines the number of
tours for each purpose that the pattern model determined to be present. The number of
intermediate stops for each purpose is left to be determined subsequently, as the tours
determined here are being simulated.
To constrain tour possibilities as tours are being simulated, each person’s simulated tours
are recorded and time windows are updated. Overall, COMPASS DaySim simulates the
details of each household’s tours in the following priority order:
1. Partially joint half tours
2. Fully joint half tours
3. Remaining individual mandatory tours
4. Joint non-mandatory tours
5. Remaining individual non-mandatory tours
2.2.2
SimMobility
The ABM part of SimMobility is called Mid-Term (MT). It is capable of simulating daily
travel at both the household and individual levels. The traffic dynamics are simulated
using a mesoscopic simulator.
The activity schedule simulation is based on sequential application of hierarchical discrete
choice models using a Monte-Carlo simulation approach. It includes the activity sequence
(including tours and sub-tours), preferred modes, departure times by half-hour slots, and
destinations.
The SimMobility MT structure consists of three components: Pre-day, Within-day and
Supply simulators. Pre-day generates the daily activity schedule, modelling and simulat-
ing individual daily activity travel pattern using 22 models. Within-Day simulates departure

times and route choice behavior, incorporating re-scheduling (en-route decisions). Supply
simulator takes care of network attributes and the supply system in relation to both public
and private transport. These last two models interact with each other, allowing users to
modify their decisions based on real-time network conditions (Y. et al., 2015).
PreDay is made up of three levels of models: the day pattern level, which models whether
the individual will travel during the day, and if traveling, for which combination of primary
activities; the tour level, which models the mode, destination and time of each primary
activity and sub-tour, and the intermediate stop level, which models the secondary activi-
ties, as well as the mode, destination and time when each secondary activity takes place
(Monteiro et al., 2022). The SImMobility PreDay flow is shown in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: SimMobility PreDay flow diagram.
Day Pattern Level
A binary logit model predicts if an individual will travel or not. The utility is expressed
as a function of alternative-specific variables and characteristics of the individual. Sub-
sequently, a MNL predicts the number of tours. A tour can be simple (work, education,
shopping or other), or complex (combining two or more of the previous purposes). Lastly,
another MNL predicts the exact number of stops. It simulates the choice of an individual
of making a tour with no stops, one single stop or multiple stops, and their purpose.

Tour Level
The tour models will predict the exact number of tours for each of the tour purposes, as
predicted by day pattern model. There are four submodels in this section for each of the
tour purposes. Work and Education use MNLs, while Shopping and Other use a binary
logit, to predict either 1 or 2 tours.
A work place model will predict whether an agent with an usual work location will go to
the usual work location or not for a particular work tour. This choice is only for those with
a fixed work location. For those without, the choice does not take place. The result of this
model will determine if it is necessary to predict work tour location.
A MNL is used to predict the combined choice of mode and destination for a work-based
tour. The choices available will depend on the number of modes and destinations avail-
able. A MNL time-of-day model for other tours predicts the choice of the 30-minute time
window for pursuing the tour. There are 48, 30-minute time blocks.
Additionally, two mode choice models predict the mode for education or usual work tours,
and for other tours. Time of day models at tour level jointly predict the arrival time and
departure time for the primary activity in a tour. To model time of day choice in the context
of discrete choice model, time is divided into the mentioned blocks.
Intermediate Stop Level
A MNL predicts if an individual will pursue an intermediate stop, and its purpose, or no
intermediate stop at all. An additional MNL predicts the arrival or departure time for each
stop, based on whether the stop is inbound or outbound.
2.3
Final words
When comparing the two models, it is important to consider which purpose they are built
for, as this then is reflected in their capabilities and structures. COMPASS arose from the
need to strategically evaluate transport patterns while introducing major policy or infras-
tructural changes. SimMobility is more future-oriented, as it considers innovative tech-
nologies in its formulation, and higher-scale interactions such as land use and communi-
cations.
Most notably, SimMobility is adaptable and lacks the specificities of COMPASS to the
Danish case, such as specific modelled modes and transportation behaviours (for ex-
ample, connecting multiple modes with public transport and the possibility of taking the
bicycle on board trains). Additionally, the COMPASS modelling flow has households as
the central unit, and SimMobility individuals. The former must therefore resolve conflicts
in schedules and availabilities of family members. The latter allows for multi-passenger
travel and trip-chaining, but the DAS is built around each separate individual.
Finally, both ABMs follow the same structure, which can interact with upper- and lower-
level components of the model: daily pattern scheduling, tours, and stops definition. This
approach defines the general travel pattern first, and the model fills in the details as it
continues simulating.

3
Literature Review
This chapter outlines prior research efforts regarding the validation and calibration of
transportation ABMs. For each topic, an overview of classic and innovative methods is
given, as well as a description of which model components are deemed appropriate to be
addressed, and concrete examples from relevant research.
3.1
Validation
As mentioned previously, literature on performance evaluation of ABMs is more scarce
than for traditional models. However, even for traditional models, research has found that
only 18,1% of papers reported validation performance measures (Parady et al., 2021).
Efforts are more focused on base year validation than scenario year validation.
Basic validation approaches consist of, for example, comparing predicted market shares
against observed shares, or the estimated traffic volumes at a number of predefined
road segments against information from external traffic counts, viewed through 45-degree
plots. Downsides of these approaches is that they do not provide numerical measures to
quantify model validity (Parady et al., 2021).
Alternatively, in literature several goodness-of-fit statistical tests are widely used to ana-
lyze the error, such as R2 value, root mean squared error (RMSE), and mean absolute
percentage error (MAPE), among others (Chen, 2009). Authors of Abraham and Hunt,
1999, conducted goodness-of-fit tests for the verification of the model MEPLAN.
Most of the operational activity-based modeling frameworks involve validating critical com-
ponents (e.g. activity duration, start time, and frequency) for the base year (Roorda et al.,
2008). In the validation of SACSIM, authors validated tour and trip frequency, distribution
and timing against survey data, and network data against traffic counts and transit vol-
umes (Bradley et al., 2010). Again, the verification of MEPLAN was done on the mode
choices and trip lengths of various activities (Abraham and Hunt, 1999). Ghasri et al.,
2017, used a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to check if the trip purpose, time, mode length,
and total number of trips distributions of observed and predicted values are statistically
similar.
3.2
Calibration
In literature, calibration has been defined various times. O’Sullivan, 2004 define cali-
bration as adjusting the model parameters so that the modelled behaviour captures the
scenario behaviour as best as possible. The main goal is to find the parameter value
set that maximizes an objective function, or rather, returns the highest similarity to the
observed behavioural data (Pellegrino, 2022). Essentially, it is an optimization problem
over a set of parameters that influence the output of the model, compared with real-world
data.
Calibration of ABMs presents two main problems: the curse of high dimensionality, and
the presence of hierarchical structures. The former refers to the very high number of
parameters that make up an ABM. The large size of the parameter space, and the lack of
available data to use for calibration (especially behavioural calibration), require that some
parameters be prioritized for calibration, and other excluded. The hierarchical structures,
that is, the interaction of different submodel levels and the emergence of sub-behaviours
8
Performance Evaluation and Calibration of Agent-Based Transportation Models
==PAGE:17==
at different levels, means that the overall behaviour calibration might misrepresent the
correct patterns at a finer spatial scale.
Compared to traditional transport models, the calibration of ABMs requires more compu-
tational power, as the latter make use of a larger set of parameters, and especially due
to their non-linear and stochastic nature. Strategies have to be undertaken to reduce the
complexity of the calculation. Some of the main strategies used in scientific literature are
outlined in the following paragraphs.
Basic approaches, such as hill-climbing (Beven, 2012) and trial-and-error (Sin et al.,
2007), have the advantage of being remarkably simple and intuitive. However, they are
not suited to large-scale adaptations and complex scenarios: the former relies on a gradi-
ent search by performing incremental searches in the parameter set, therefore it is easily
stuck in a local optimum. The latter is a manual method, which becomes quickly imprac-
tical over a large parameter space.
Another family of methods is that of Genetic Algorithms (GA). They emulate the principles
of natural selection on a population of parameters. Iterative evolutions from an initial
arbitrary parameter set lead to a global optimum. Issues with GA algorithms reside in
their overreliance on a correct definition of the objective function (Railsback, 2001), and
convergence problems when applied to large scale stochastic simulation networks.
Alternative methods of calibration rely on Sensitivity Analysis (SA), especially when his-
torical data is unavailable (Franklin et al., 2002). SA requires running an extensive series
of simulations, where a subset of parameters is kept fixed to analyse the impact of the
changed parameters. Authors of Lima Azevedo et al., 2015, have successfully used a
global SA based calibration, where different SA techniques could be chosen depending
on the complexity of each step. In this way, they estimated 101 parameters using a multi-
step approach, allowing for a significant reduction of the number of simulations and for
the use of detailed analysis to more sensitive parameters set.
In the last years, Bayesian Optimisation (BO) has been widely applied in machine learning
scenarios, where many hyperparameters need to be tuned (Agnihotri and Batra, 2020).
It is especially useful as it can be used to optimize any black-box function. BO seeks
the global optimum by a sequential sampling strategy and approximates the probability
function through a surrogate model and surrogate response surface. With a dedicated
acquisition function, there is a balance between exploring uncertain regions and exploiting
them. Among the benefits of BO, is that is does not depend on the initial starting point of
the search, and it is not a local-search method. The procedure used in the present study
builds heavily upon the work of Zerzis, 2023, which in turn is an adaptation of Agriesti
et al., 2023.
As the number of parameters in each level of the ABM structure can be quite significant,
it is also important to note which parts of the model to calibrate. Studies focused on the
adjustment of alternative-specific constants (ASCs), which tend to capture the effects of
variables ignored in the estimation phase (Vuk and Petersen, 2006) (Cambridge System-
atics, 2008). Other researchers have proposed that ABMs could be calibrated to external
OD flow information derived from traffic counts. Their most promising result was at the
model level, where they proposed a heuristic approach to adjust only destination-choice
model parameters (Cools et al., 2010).

4.1.1
Data collection and preprocessing
Data used in the validation process comes from several different sources, depending on
the transportation mode. Observed data for walk and bike is available through Køben-

taken from the MASTRA dataset, from the Danish Road Directorate (Vejdirektoratet). For
public transport data, DTU’s Rejsekort database is used, with anonymized aggregated
counts. Car ownership and work-at-home data are taken from Danmarks Statistiks web-

filtered out.
Count data with multiple observations for each observation point was averaged into a

each other, which corresponded to the same COMPASS road network link (for simplicity,
COMPASS does not include the whole road network). Observations referring to the same
network link were again averaged into a single value, to avoid double-counting.

were expanded into Average Daily Traffic (ADT), to account for a 24-hour period, with a

4.2.4
Bayesian optimization
The calibration of SimMobility parameters is done through an innovative algorithm, based
on Bayesian Optimization (BO). BO is applied in disparate scientific fields, with positive
results, for example in machine learning, population genetics, and pathogen spread.
Overview
Essentially, BO is a black-box problem: the optimization is based solely on inputs and
outputs, as the real objective function is not known, and only its behavior can be observed.
A probabilistic model of the objective function is thus created, which through iterative
sampling of the parameter space becomes more closely aligned with the true objective
function. The aim of BO is to find the global optima of this modelled function.
The acquisition function determines where the iterative sampling of the parameter space
should evaluate the modelled objective function next: it decides, that is, the balance be-
tween exploration and exploitation. After each new observation is added to the know
observations set, the modelled objective function is fitted again.
In formal terms, a simulation model is a generative stochastic process, and its calibration
involves statistically inferring a finite number of parameters θ ∈Rd using observations Yo:

Acquisition function: Expected Improvement
The RF is used to estimate the acquisition utility of parameter values which have been
sampled through the acquisition function.
The acquisition function is a heuristic that
guides the optimization process. It acts as a balance between the exploitation (choice
of parameters in areas where the utility already yields good results) and the exploration
(choice of parameters in areas that yield the most information about the target function).
In the present case, the acquisition function used is the Expected Improvement (EI). This
is a highly attractive method that balances local and global search (Jones et al., 1998). It
provides several benefits regarding exploration-exploitation balance, by resulting in high
values where the prediction µ(θ) is high (exploitation) and also where the uncertainty σ(θ)
is high (exploration). Furthermore, it is efficient, as it requires only one parameter, the
location of the maximum, to be optimized.
The expression describing the EI can be formulated as:

Utility Maximization: L-BFGS-B
To find the set of parameters that maximize the acquisition utility, the Limited Memory
Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno with Box constraints (L-BFGS-B) algorithm is used
(Byrd et al., 1995).
It belongs to the quasi-Newton optimization methods. It is a gradient-based optimization
approach that is designed to approximate the Newton method for large scale problems.
Limited memory means it does not store the full approximation to the second order partial
derivatives but only the vectors necessary to define the approximation, improving memory
usage for high dimensional optimization problems. It is box constrained in that it allows
for upper and lower bound constraints on the model parameters.
4.2.5
Calibration procedure
The calibration procedure ensures that several different parameter space combinations
are explored, before accepting the most favourable one. Initially, PreDay is run with 10
combinations of parameters which are generated through LHS, and so 10 separate inac-
curacy values are extracted.
Target data from COMPASS is used to calculate the inadequacy: trip travel times per-
centages for modes and purposes, total trip share percentages for modes and purposes,
and OD matrices for 10 ToD periods.
Once the first 10 parameter combinations are calculated, they are used to train the RF
regressor. Afterwards, 30 parameter sample spaces are extracted through LHS. For each
of these samples, the inadequacy is found using the previously trained regressor. This
avoids having to run the simulation for 30 samples, which is much more resource inten-
sive.

A utility value is calculated from the predictions of the RF regressor, through the EI func-

parameters, calculated through the RF regressor, are used for a PreDay run, and saved

This procedure is repeated for 5 iterations, with the RF regressor retrained on the up-

is up to date with the available information on parameter distributions and corresponding
inadequacy values.

6
SimMobility Calibration
The procedure proposed here, to be used in the calibration of SimMobility for Denmark,
adopts a top-down approach. Upper-level submodels are calibrated first, then lower-
level, and finally a mix of the most influential parameters of the two is calibrated again.
This facilitates an exploration of the interdependencies between the submodels, which in
turn enables the calibration to focus solely on the parameters that undergo the greatest
change and have the most influence on the model output.
Due to external limitations, it was not possible to utilize SimMobility applied to the Danish
case. In its place, a version of SimMobility applied to a fake Virtual City has been utilized.
The process here outlined can therefore only be taken as an indication of methodology,
and meaningful results must be extracted from running SimMobility for Denmark, once it
will be ready. Acknowledging this, the rationale for the calibration method and general
trends are here shown. The Python code used for this analysis can be found on GitHub1,
as an adaptation of previous work2.
Acknowledging these limitations, it is here pointed out which target data was used. For
travel time shares and mode shares, the data comes directly from COMPASS. For the OD
matrices divided by ToD, though the data from COMPASS is available, it is not comparable
to the data from the Virtual City, because the zoning system is different. Therefore, a copy
of an OD matrix resulting from the Virtual City is made, and random noise is added.
6.1
Upper-level submodels calibration
Initially, upper-level submodels are calibrated. They model the most influential decisions
which constrain successive submodels. In SimMobility, these are the Day Pattern Level
models:
1. Day Pattern Binary: travel or no travel
2. Day Pattern Tours: number of tours
3. Day Pattern Stops: number of stops
In total, the three models comprise 297 parameters, which is a rather large calibration set.
A parameter space with 10 different parameter combinations is generated through LHS.
After the RF regressor has been trained, a calibration round consisting of 5 iterations of
the BO algorithm is undertaken. The RF model is trained again before the third iteration.
The outcome of the calibration is visualized in Figure 6.1, where the 25 parameters which
underwent the biggest variation from their initial value are shown. Four of the parame-
ters are related to car ownership, potentially signifying that particular attention should be
placed on their value.

Lower-level submodel calibration
Successively, the same procedure is applied to lower-level models. In particular, 4 sub-
models are tested here, the Tour Time of Day models. They are one for each tour purpose:
Education, Work, Shopping, and Other. These models simulate the departure times and
arrival times of tours. It has been deemed appropriate to test these models, as it is im-
portant to replicate specific travel patterns throughout the day (for instance, rush-hours
and quiet hours). Time of day information is also used in the objective function, with the
OD matrices divided by time of day and weighed according to the number of trips in each
period, thus ensuring that the model fits the observed data properly.

modelled with different periods (from 2π

Discussion
7.1
Discussion
The COMPASS model has been validated on its scenario year 2021. Literature suggests
that almost half of travellers’ travel patterns from before the pandemic did not recover
afterwards (Yao et al., 2022). Data for 2021 does indeed diverge from model predictions.
The divergence is less pronounced for 2022, as the shock effect of Covid-19 diminished.
General patterns can be taken from the analysis.
Primarily, there is an overall lower estimation precision than for the baseline year. The
main error metric used for comparing car and bike data, the %RMSE, is 5-10% higher
for 2021 than for 2017. Errors in submodel calculations are also 2-10 times higher than
2017. This imprecision can partly be attributed to smaller sample sizes available for 2021
and 2022 data than for 2017, which allow for larger propagation of uncertainties.
Car trips are modelled quite accurately, except for bigger roads such as highways, where
there is a consistent overestimation. Since the COMPASS road network does not include
most of the smaller roads (such as residential roads), it is expected that overestimation
occurs in these roads: traffic that would be divided among parallel roads gets loaded into a
single link, increasing its volume. This was the case also in the 2017 analysis. However,
with 2021 data, it is more clear that the overestimation is a recurrent effect not just for
small roads, but also for bigger roads.
Additionally, COMPASS underestimated cycle traffic both for 2021 and 2022. This is in
accordance to post-Covid trends: bike usage increased, as opposed to car and public
transport ridership (Schaefer et al., 2021). Surprisingly, the lowest volume links are mod-
elled quite well – the opposite would be expected, as explained above. This is clarified by
the fact that most of the observations in this range (100 out of 104) are from the MASTRA
dataset, which is more accurate than the KK dataset. On the other hand, most observa-
tions in the two highest ranges are from the KK dataset (158 out of 226).
Predictions for public transport show quite high divergence from observed data. A large
part of this can be attributed to the data used: only Rejsekort data, which does not include
all users. Despite, this there is a clear tendency of the model to overestimate passengers.
Again, this is in line with post-Covid trends, as citizens use public transport less. Walk
data can only be superficially analyzed. With this concession, the model shows no sign
of inaccuracy on replicating real world patterns.
Trip shares and purposes resemblance to the available data is satisfactory, but because
the data is at the national level, it is hard to assess whether COMPASS is replicating
the travel patterns accurately. Nevertheless, the discrepancies in modelled and observed
data have a plausible explanation, which would suggest an overall positive outcome of
the model predictions. More detailed data would be needed for a sharper analysis.
Regarding car ownership choices, though the results are not properly up to date in the
model, the error range is comparable to that of the base year validation. It is especially
clear that results specifically for the Copenhagen municipality are very satisfactory.
The calibration of SimMobility only happened on a restricted version of the model, applied
to a factitious Virtual City. The focus was on calibrating submodels which have the most
impact in generating agents’ DAS. Upper-level model parameters are explored first, to

calibrate the top parameters which influence the subsequent agent choices. ToD sub-
models parameters from the lower level of the simulation flow are also calibrated, as they
influence distribution of trips through the 24-hour period. Since the objective function used
in calibration also aims to minimize the discrepancies between observed and simulated
ToD OD matrices, ToD models are calibrated against targeted and trusted data.
The results obtained simply serve an illustrative function, as they are not based on real
world data. The process will have to be repeated and expanded once SimMobility can be
applied to the Danish context. However, as general patterns, it emerges that upper-level
models parameters need specific calibration efforts. From the ToD submodels, work tours
showed the highest need for parameter calibration.
In a previous application of the Bayesian Optimization algorithm used here, an improve-
ment of over 17% over initial error values was found. The present improvement is only a
tenth of that, at 1.9%. However, it is stressed that this is only illustrative and has no cor-
roboration in real-world data. Furthermore, the error function used to calculate the error
is modified to include bigger and more descriptive datasets, and the error indicator itself
is changed from RMSE to NRMSE.
7.2
Limitations
This study provides some useful results in terms of validation, and general guidelines in
terms of calibration. Limitation with both parts must be acknowledged, and considered
when designing future studies.
It is to be noted that the data used for validation is of mixed quality and reliability, and cer-
tain approximations were necessary. Limitations include KK count data being taken from
a single working day, and counted manually. Data from TU refers to all of Denmark, not to
the GCA. It is still representative of travel patterns, but behaviors may change slightly be-
tween areas with different socio-economic conditions. Similarly, Danmarks Statistiks data
refers to the GCA, so no data is available specifically for the Copenhagen Municipality.
Data from the Rejsekort dataset refers only to Rejsekort users; it contains no information
about other transport trips.
Regarding the calibration, it is clear that the results were heavily constrained by the im-
possibility of applying SimMobility to Denmark. A general outline of the process is given,
which must however be repeated and analyzed once the model becomes available. There
are intrinsic limitations to the BO algorithm used, which have already been acknowledged
in literature. It is possible that a RF regressor may be overfitting the data, and that the
LHS method might leave unexplored certain areas of the parameter space which could
have a positive influence on the inadequacy.
When choosing which submodels and parameters to calibrate, many others are left out:
this is unavoidable, to keep computational costs to a minimum, but has repercussions on
the model predictions. Exactly which parameters to leave out of the calibration process
must be considered carefully, to ensure that all the most influential effects are involved
in the calibration. This can be achieved through a targeted sensitivity analysis, or more
extensive and precise tests on parameter values.
Lastly, several runs of the simulation and optimization algorithm are needed to account for
stochasticity and produce stable results. This is something to be explored in the future.
For the present analysis, though higher repetitions would indeed secure a more reliable
result, the data is still not referring to real-world cases, so accuracy is not affected.

Conclusion
8.1
Conclusion
This thesis focuses on Agent-Based Models for transportation: their advantages over
traditional transportation models have been explained, and the two models used in this
study have been analyzed and compared. The problems of validation and calibration of
ABMs have been addressed. One of the main contributions of this work is to present a
new procedure for validating a large-scale ABM, and to apply an innovative parameter
calibration approach to a second large-scale ABM.
A discussion on the concrete results of the validation of the COMPASS model has been
given. In particular, model inaccuracies in replicating real-world travel patterns have been
highlighted, and possible motivations for this have been suggested. Both model output
data and internal submodels data has been used for validation. The main patterns to
have emerged are an underestimation of bicycle trips, and an overestimation of car trips
in highways. Both of these patterns are more pronounced when validating against 2021
data than 2022 data.
On the calibration side, while it has not been possible to utilize the innovative Bayesian
Optimization approach to the Danish application of SimMobility, a general overview of the
method has been given. It has been applied instead to Virtual City, and the procedure for
calibration has been described and is ready for replication on SimMobility Denmark, once
it becomes available.
Results show that validation can offer insights into adapting models to changing travel
patterns. In particular, they show how Covid-19 has shifted specific behaviours even
two years after the introduction of the first containment measures. Calibration results
are harder to interpret and require replication on a concrete scenario, but insights into
parameters to investigate and overall error reduction offer promising prospects.
8.2
Further research
Future research should prioritize data reliability, expand the scope of validation, and refine
calibration strategies. COMPASS scenario year 2025 will soon be able to be evaluated:
more accurate public transport and travel survey data will positively affect findings. Cal-
ibration of SimMobility will of course need to be done on the Danish case: several runs
of the BO algorithm and different parameter settings are recommended, to fully explore
the potentials of the algorithm. Use of alternative models instead of RF, which can tend
to overfit the data, is also recommended.
