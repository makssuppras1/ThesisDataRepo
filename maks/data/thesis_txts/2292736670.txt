1. Abstract 
 
The aim of this thesis was to study the phase behavior of reservoirs fluids under HPHT using a PVT 
apparatus. In order to achieve this, literature on the bubble point of different alkane mixtures was 
studied, with methane being one of the compounds. Also, constant mass expansion (CME) 
experiments and visual observation were performed for several compositions of the system methane 
+ n-decane up to 200°C, for the verification of the apparatus and to build the phase envelope. 
Moreover, the mixtures were modelled and compared to experimental results. The main results of 
this study were that the increase of temperature leads to expansion of the system. Additionally, for 
mixtures with more gas, the bubble point was found in higher pressure. Moreover, the results from 
visual observation come in great agreement with CME experiments. What is more, the results from 
modelling showed that different models can describe different compositions well. Lastly, our CME 
results were close to literature data for similar compositions verifying the PVT apparatus, however 
further research is required.

2. Introduction 
 
The conventional sources of oil and gas decline leads the oil and gas industry to search and explore 
resources in deeper formations where high pressures and high temperatures (HPHT) prevail 
(Shadravan & Amani 2012; Halliburton 2011). As high pressure can be defined any pressure above 
690 bar while as high temperature is any temperature exceeding 149°C. However, in the near future 
the HPHT environment would be considered to have a pressure higher than 1034 bar and a 
temperature greater than 149°C (Shadravan & Amani 2012). For the production of reservoirs under 
the above conditions, it is crucial to understand the phase behavior of the reservoir fluids. In order 
to depict the phase behavior, phase diagrams are used that show the experimental or mathematical 
determinations of the conditions under which the different phases exist (Ahmed 2001).  There are 
different types of these diagrams such as pressure-composition (p-x1-y1), pressure-temperature (p-
T), and temperature–composition (T-x1-y1) (Smith et al. 2007). p-T diagram is one of the most 
useful diagrams for depiction of the phase behavior because it can be used to depict the steps of 
fluid production from reservoir to surface conditions and contribute to the decision of the 
production plan (Ayala 2006). Specifically, p-T diagrams help to the categorization of reservoirs 
and hydrocarbon systems as well as the description of the phase behavior of reservoir fluids 
(Ahmed 2001). One example can be seen in Figure 1(a). The area within the envelope is the two-
phase region and is separated from the single-phase region by a thermodynamic boundary (Ayala 
2006). The most important elements of the diagram are the critical point which is defined as the 
point where the properties of the liquid and gas phase are identical. At this point, the temperature 
and pressure are called critical temperature and critical pressure correspondingly. Also, there are the 
bubble point curve and the dew point curve, where the former separates the liquid phase from the 
two-phase region and the latter separates the gas phase from the two-phase region. Other significant 
information from the p-T diagram is the quality lines which are depicted with dashes. These lines 
show the temperature and pressure conditions for equal volumes of liquid and converge at the 
critical point. Lastly, the maximum temperature above which liquid cannot be formed 
independently of the pressure is called cricondentherm and the maximum pressure above which gas 
cannot be formed independently of temperature is called cricondenbar (Ahmed 2001). One 
phenomenon that comes in contrast to common sense is called retrograde condensation and occurs 
in high pressure gas mixtures with temperature between the critical and the cricondentherm 
temperature. In particular, during depletion at constant temperature, a first dew point will occur (D2) 
and further decrease of pressure will lead to more liquid until liquid approaches a maximum at 
certain pressure (B). This increase of the liquid with decreasing pressure contrasts the common 
intuition (Stenby & Yan 2005).

The knowledge of the bubble point allows the prediction, the plan and the handling of oil 
production from reservoirs (Coats & Smart 1986; Hosein & Dawe 2011). It is defined as the 
pressure where the first few molecules leave from the liquid and form a small bubble of gas 
(McCain 1990). According to Schlumberger Oilfield Glossary (2015), gas in solution exists in all 
reservoir oils from the time of discovery. More specifically, this property is important to know 
because at the bubble point or below, the oil production declines since the gas that emerges from oil 
moves faster towards the surface. This is attributed to its lower viscosity and consequently its higher 
mobility (Craft & Hawkins 1959; Smith et al. 2007). In case of gas reservoir, the correspondence 
property is the dew point. The pressure at which the first oil droplet occurs from the gas is defined 
as dew point (Danesh 1998). Both, the bubble point and dew point are called saturation pressures. 
The volumes at these pressures are used as a reference volume and the measured volumes for each 
pressure depletion step are correlated to this volume (Danesh 1998; Hosein 2004). The 
determination of bubble point can be achieved by performing pressure-volume-temperature (PVT) 
experiments. 
There are different PVT experiments which are used depending on the fluid type either oil or gas. 
More specifically, for oil, there is constant mass expansion (CME) or constant composition 
expansion (CCE), differential liberation (DL) and separator test, while for gas there are CME, 
constant volume depletion (CVD), and separator test.  
The CME experiment is a pressure-volume test that is performed in order to find the relation 
between pressure and volume for the reservoir fluid. In the CME for oil, a sample in a single phase 
with known volume is introduced in a PVT cell and then heated to the studied temperature. Then 
the sample is expanded and thus the volume of the fluid is increased causing the decrease of 
pressure (Pedersen et al. 2014). The expansion of the system leads to the formation of gas at 
saturation pressure and to the formation of two phases. Phase is defined as any homogeneous and

physically separable part of a system. Those parts are separated from other by definite bounding 
surfaces (McCain 1990). At each stage of the experiment, the volume and the pressure are recorded 
and a plot of pressure versus volume will depict the bubble point at the break point of the plot. More 
specifically, this phenomenon is due to the change of compressibility of the fluid from single phase 
to two phase region, since the compressibility of gas is greater than the compressibility of liquid 
(Schlumberger 2015). 
Depending on the initial reservoir pressure and temperature related to the phase envelope, reservoir 
fluids can be divided in the following types: Gas condensate, natural gas which is subdivided in dry 
and wet gas, volatile oil and black oil. In case of natural gas, the location of its cricondentherm 
temperature is lower than the reservoir temperature. The difference between dry and wet gas is 
found on the surface conditions. More specifically, if the production of the fluid is within the two-
phase region, the fluid is considered as wet gas; if it is in the single phase, it is dry. For gas 
condensate, the initial reservoir temperature is between the critical and cricondentherm temperature. 
In this case, the phenomenon of retrograde condensation can take place. As far as the volatile and 
black oil are concerned, their initial reservoir temperature is lower than the critical temperature. 
Their difference lies on the fact that volatile oil is lighter than black oil and that the critical 
temperature of volatile is very close to reservoir temperature, therefore it is called near critical fluid 
(Stenby & Yan 2005). Typical phase diagrams are depicted in Figure 1(b).  
 
 
The aim of this project is to study the phase behavior of reservoir related fluids under high pressure 
high temperature conditions by using PVT studies. For this purpose, literature on bubble points of 
alkane binary and ternary mixtures, methane being one of the compounds, was studied for finding 
relevant systems. Additionally, the bubble points of the system methane and n-decane were 
determined at several compositions to verify the HPHT PVT apparatus that is installed in the CERE 
laboratory. Specifically, the visual method and constant mass expansion (CME) were performed for 
the following mole compositions: 
1. 0.4 methane + 0.6 n-decane 
2. 0.6 methane + 0.4 n-decane 
3. 0.85 methane + 0.15 n-decane 
The range of temperature for the two first mixtures was 30°C to 200°C and 21°C to 200°C 
respectively. The last mixture was studied twice, the first time only with visual observation for 
temperature range from 30°C to 150°C and the second time both with visual (20°C to 200°C) and 
CME experiments for certain temperatures (20°C to 125°C). In order to distinguish the two above 
mixtures, they are called first and second time. The results for all mixtures were compared to 
literature data. What is more, a phase envelope using pressure and temperature (p-T diagram) was 
plotted for all systems in great temperature ranges. Lastly, the above measured systems were 
modeled using the PVT software SPECS for verifying the reliability of different EoS for the 
prediction of phase envelope of the studied system.

3. Literature Review 
 
After literature research, it can be concluded that the phase behavior of the system methane-n-
decane was studied in the past by several researchers. This can be attributed to the fact that these 
alkanes constitute main components of hydrocarbon fluids. Specifically, Sage et al. (1940) studied it 
at temperature range 21°C to 121°C and pressures up to 310 bar, while Reamer et al. (1942), 
studied it for the same temperatures and pressures up to 345 bar. Later, Rijkers et al. (1992a) 
performed measurements on the phase behavior of methane-n-decane at temperature range -33°C to 
42°C and pressure up to 400 bar. Regarding densities, Audonnet and Padua (2004) studied the 
system of methane-n-decane from 25°C to 120°C and up to 750 bar in several compositions. Canet 
et al. (2002) measured the densities of methane-n-decane system at temperatures 20°C to 100°C and 
pressures up to 650 bar and extrapolated up to 1400 bar. Literature data for bubble point pressures 
used for system methane-n-decane is depicted on Figure 2. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Some other mixtures that have been studied were the system methane-n-dodecane and methane-n-
hexadecane. The former was studied by Rijkers et al. (1992b) at temperatures between -33°C to 
42°C and pressures up to 250 bar. The latter was studied by Glasser et al. (1985) at temperatures 
between 12°C and 87°C and pressures up to 850 bar, by Lin et al. (1980) for temperatures 190°C to 
430°C and pressures up to 250 bar and by Rijkers et al. (1993) from 12°C to 42°C and pressures up 
to 250 bar. 
Also, some ternary mixtures were studied such as the methane-propane-n-decane system from 
Wiese et al. (1970) at temperatures between 4°C to 238°C and pressures up to 690 bar. Also, the 
system methane-butane-n-decane that was studied by Urlic et al. (2003) at temperature region -3°C 
to 217°C and pressures up to 240 bar.

4. Materials and Methods 
 
In this chapter, all materials and methods as well as the whole equipment used are described in 
order to give all necessary information for performing the experiments in terms of this thesis. 
Specifically, information is given about chemicals, how the equipment functions and a description 
of all the procedures and the experiments. Moreover, a modelling section is included. 
 
4.1  Materials 
 
For the performance of the experiments, the chemicals used were methane obtained from Metan 
scientific, AGA gas AB, with 5.5 purity >99.9995% and n-decane from Sigma Aldrich, Reagent 
plus, with purity 99.6%. Also, for the cleaning procedure, toluene from Sigma Aldrich, Chromasolv 
® for HPLC, and with purity 99.96%, ethanol from VWR Prolabo Chemicals, 96% vol and nitrogen 
gas Instrument Nitrogen 5.0, Nitrogen, komprimeret, UN 1066 AGA A/S were used. 
 
4.2 Experimental Apparatus 
 
The equipment used for the experiments was PVT 240/1500 from Sanchez Technologies. It consists 
of a PVT cell with a large window at the end and a motorized piston inside. The pressure is 
measured using a pressure sensor and temperature is measured by Pt 100 which is inserted in the 
wall of the cell.  A camera is placed in front of the window to record the experiments inside the cell. 
The cell is surrounded by a frame for safety reasons and the whole set up is connected to a 
computer with appropriate software. The set up can be seen on Figure 3. In order to operate the cell, 
Falcon software (Figure 4) is used while the camera is set using Euclide software.

As it can be seen on Figure 4 the cell has two main valves, a top and a bottom one, that can be 
operated both automatically and manually. Each valve leads to four smaller ones which can be used 
as inlets or outlets. One of the small valves on the top is connected to a reference pressure 
transducer. This information is used to control the pressures that the fluids are inserted to the cell 
and also to calibrate the pressure transducer that is in the cell body and measure experiments 
pressure. Falcon software allows the setting of parameters such as pressure, temperature and 
volume which can be also plotted versus time. Other functions are that the piston can be moved to a 
specific volume using constant flow rate or apply constant pressure in the cell. Also, there is an 
option for mechanical stirring by means of four retractable blades attached to the end of the piston 
in order to make the mixture homogeneous. The whole cell can be rotated and placed in 
predetermined positions such as 135°, 90°, 45°, 0° or in any desired position by using a reversal 
function, while the rate of rotation is 360°/min. Using Euclide software, the internal part of the cell 
can be seen (Figure 5) and recorded with pictures. 
 
There are some safety limitations that should be applied during the operation of the equipment that 
are maximum pressure ramp 1 bar/s and maximum temperature ramp 5°C/min. Also the maximum 
volume of the cell is 240 ml. (Sanchez Technologies Instruction Manual).

4.3 Methods 
 
4.3.1 Cleaning Procedure  
 
In order to clean the cell, it was placed at 45°. Then the pressure was lowered until volume reaches 
the maximum capacity (high end of stroke) which is 250 ml. Afterwards, the gas phase (methane) 
was released from top valve, while the liquid phase (n-decane) was released from bottom valve. 
Then nitrogen was flushed into the cell to dry it. Subsequently, the cell was filled with toluene using 
a cylinder and a pump, and after stirring was switched on, toluene was released through the bottom 
valve to remove any particles from o-rings or remaining mixture. The above step was repeated one 
more time using toluene and twice using ethanol. Between the steps, nitrogen was flushed into the 
cell to dry it. Lastly, vacuum conditions were applied into the cell.

4.3.3 Mixture Preparation

4.3.4 Visual Observation 
 
Before performing CME, the bubble point pressure was determined with visual observation using 
the dynamic procedure. More specifically, the pressure in the cell was set about 50 bar more than 
the theoretical value so as to have only one phase. Then, by lowering the pressure with constant 
pressure, a rough estimation of bubble point was made. This was done in order to limit as much as 
possible the range of values of pressure where the bubble point appeared. Then, the pressure was set 
around one bar above the value where the bubble point was noticed. Afterwards, with a constant 
flow rate of 0.5cm3/h the pressure was decreased and with stirring switched on, the appearance of 
the bubble point was recorded. That happened when colors were noticed on the center of the cell, 
indicating that gas emerged. 
At this point, it should be mentioned that for the mixture 0.85 methane + 0.15 n-decane for both 
times, a step procedure was also used because some pictures were not very clear and it could not be 
distinguished whether was bubble point or dew. The pressure in the cell was set 2 bar higher than

4.3.5 CME  
 
For the determination of bubble point pressure using the PVT software, the cell was set at 135° and 
the procedure of CME was selected. Then the desired temperature was chosen and there was 
waiting time of 1 hour upon stirring for its stabilization. Afterwards, and since the bubble point 
pressure was estimated with visual observation previously, the procedure of CME started at around 
50-100 bar above this pressure and was divided in three stages. The first was the monophasic stage 
where the pressure decreased with steps of 10 bar and a flow rate of 10 cm3/h and at each step all 
required data such as pressure, volume, temperature and one picture of the system were saved. At 
the second stage, the detection of the bubble point pressure was performed by decreasing pressure 
in steps of 0.2 bar and flow rate of 5 cm3/h. The criterion in order to detect the bubble point was 
tolerance of 30% in slope change of the p-V curve. The third stage was the diphasic where the 
pressure decreased by setting volume steps and can be seen on Table 4. Between the steps of all 
three stages there was a waiting time of 10 minutes where the change of pressure was recorded and 
in case that it was higher than the tolerance pressure (0.5 bar) another waiting of 10 minutes was 
performed otherwise the procedure continued (Sanchez Technologies Instruction Manual).  
The bubble point pressure is found at the break point of the p-V curve. The CME procedure is

4.4 Modelling 
 
4.4.1 SPECS  
 
The studied mixtures were modelled using the SPECS program for the determination of the phase 
behavior using different Equations of State (EoS) and comparing with the experimental data. This 
program is used for phase equilibrium calculations and has been created by the Center for Phase 
Equilibria and Separation Processes (IVC-SEP) of the Department of Chemical Engineering at the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The calculations are performed using different 
thermodynamic models and algorithms which offer the possibility for modelling very complex 
mixtures such as polar and non-polar mixtures, oil mixtures, polymer-solvent mixtures and aqueous 
electrolyte solutions. Among other calculations that can be performed by using algorithms available 
in SPECS are the bubble and dew point of a mixture and the phase envelope which have been used 
in terms of this thesis (IVC-SEP Department of Chemical Engineering DTU 2009).

in the petroleum and chemical industry were applied. These are the Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) 
and the Peng-Robinson (PR) cubic EoS. The former was a modification proposal from Giorgio 
Soave of the Redlich-Kwong equation in 1972 (Soave 1972) and is used for describing accurately

5.2.3 Comparison of CME with Literature for Mixture 0.6 methane + 0.4 n-
decane 
 
The obtained results from CME experiments were compared to literature data to verify the PVT 
apparatus. Specifically, literature data from Sage et al. (1940) and Reamer et al. (1942) were used, 
however the method applied by them is different than our method and a brief description is given 
below. In the first article, a known weight of methane and n-decane was introduced in a container 
and the volume change was achieved by adding or withdrawing mercury. For the determination of 
the volume of the mixture, the height of mercury was measured, while the pressure at equilibrium 
was measured using calibrated pressure balance. For the temperature, a platinum resistance 
thermometer was used, while in order to achieve equilibrium a mechanical agitator was placed in 
the chamber (Sage et al. 1940). Reamer et al. (1942) wanted to extend the study of Sage et al. on the 
same system but with some differentiations. The main difference was the collection of gas samples 
under isobaric, isothermal conditions in order to determine the composition of the dew point gas. 
Additionally, a magnetic agitator was used instead of a mechanical, while the temperature was 
controlled using electrical control equipment.

6. Conclusions 
 
The processing of the experimental results and the comparison with literature data and SPECS 
modelling led to the following basic conclusions.  
One important observation for all mixtures was that the system expanded in higher temperatures. 
Also, CME results and visual observation were similar in all mixtures, thus visual observation is an 
accurate method for estimating the saturation pressure of reservoir fluids. In the case where 
mixtures get opaque and visual observation cannot be applied, CME procedure can be used 
providing accurate results. Additionally, the CME results form a smooth part of the phase envelope 
for the three mixtures. It is important to note that the deviations from literature data were lower 
when CME results were compared to data with compositions closer to ours. The fact that the 
deviations were low proves that the PVT apparatus is able to reproduce literature data with good 
accuracy and good agreement between CME results and literature data. Another observation related 
to the composition of the mixtures is that the more gas in the mixture the higher bubble point 
pressure is found. Regarding the saturation pressures, for the studied temperatures of 0.4 methane + 
0.6 n-decane and 0.6 methane + 0.4 n-decane were on the left of the critical point thus they were 
bubble points. For 0.85 methane + 0.15 n-decane, during the procedure of visual observation a 
cloud appeared that did not subside for temperatures between 104°C and 150°C thus it was not 
possible to determine whether it was a bubble or dew point. This happened because these 
temperatures were close to the critical point. 
The modelling results using SPECS showed that model SRK with kij 0.0411 predicts better the 
CME results of the mixtures 0.4 methane + 0.6 n-decane and 0.6 methane + 0.4 n-decane while for 
the mixture 0.85 methane + 0.15 n-decane the best model is PC-SAFT and kij 0. Also, for the first 
two mixtures, the results for all models with the use of the optimal kij have lower AAD. For the 
mixture 0.85 methane + 0.15 n-decane this is valid only for model PR, while SRK and PC-SAFT 
have better results for kij 0. From the above observations of modelling, it is concluded that the 
system methane + n-decane cannot be described well by using only one model for the whole 
composition range. Also, the use of an optimal kij does not always entail better results. 
Finally, more research related to the phase behavior of hydrocarbon mixtures in high pressure and 
high temperature conditions is required.
