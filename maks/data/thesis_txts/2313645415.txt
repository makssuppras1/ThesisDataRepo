How Cognitive Biases can Lead to 
Irrational Decisions in Projects, and 
How to Avoid Making Them 
 
Master’s thesis – Summer 2016 
Martin Jacobsen – S062088 – Engineering Management 
 
Abstract 
          Important decisions in project contexts; like whether to start a project or not; should be 
guided by facts, and not partly biased by an uninformative numeric value, perceived by a 
decision maker at an earlier point. The present research was a two part study. The first part 
demonstrated that an uninformative initial profit forecast of a possible new project (the 
anchor), affected the decision maker’s perception of expected profit, thereby confirming the 
existence of an anchoring effect. It further demonstrated that the willingness to start the 
project was unaffected by this anchor. The second part investigated if the consider-the-
opposite (CtO) method, a known de-biasing method, mitigated the anchoring effect. More 
specifically, this method induced decision makers to consider reasons that argued against 
that the initial profit forecast was correct. The results indicate a mitigation, but not as 
expected. Surprisingly, the CtO method had a noteworthy decreasing effect on perceived 
expected profit in both the low and high anchor group. Furthermore, as well in both anchor 
groups, this method markedly reduced the number of decision makers that were willing to 
start the project. It was argued that the CtO method could be seen as a negative test 
strategy that could be used as a de-biasing method for the consequences of that people 
generally are biased to believe and confirm. Thereby, it was theoretically concluded that the 
systematic use of the CtO method, generally should improve judgement. 
Keywords: Cognitive biases; anchoring effect; de-biasing method; consider-the-opposite; decision 
making in projects; positive test strategy; negative test strategy

Introduction 
“Whereas experts are as susceptible to anchoring influences as novices, they feel more 
certain about their judgements. Expertise thus does make a difference. Ironically however, 
this difference is only apparent in the subjective not the objective quality of the judgement.”  
Englich et al. (2006) 
 
The anchoring bias describes the tendency to assimilate a judgement towards an 
initially perceived numeric value (the anchor). The concept of anchoring was introduced by 
Tversky and Kahneman (1974). As an example, one of their tasks was to estimate the 
percentage of African countries in the United Nation. First the participants were shown the 
number 10 or 65, which respectively was the low and high anchor. Then they were to 
indicate whether the answer was lower or higher than that number; followed by giving their 
final judgement. The median answers for the low and high anchor, were respectively 25% 
and 45%. The anchoring effect has since been demonstrated in a variety of domains: 
general knowledge questions (Strack and Mussweiler, 1997; Mussweiler and Strack, 1999; 
Epley and Gilovich, 2001), probability estimates (Plous, 1989; Chapman and Johnson, 1999, 
Experiment 2), performance judgement (Thorsteinson et al., 2008), legal judgement (Englich 
et al., 2005; 2006), purchasing decisions (Wansink et al. 1998; Mussweiler et al. 2000), 
forecasting (Critcher and Gilovich, 2008), negotiations (Galinsky and Mussweiler, 2001), and 
more. Studies have shown that both experts and novices are equally prone to the anchoring 
effect (Joyce & Biddle, 1981; Northcraft & Neale, 1987; Wright & Anderson, 1989; Englich et 
al., 2006). Furthermore, it can in some cases persist over time (e.g. Jani, 2010; Mussweiler, 
2001), thereby having a significant distorting effect on subsequent judgement.  
Project contexts are prone to anchoring effects since numeric values are inevitable. 
Whether they are about cost estimations, risks, completion times, profit forecasts, etc., 
numeric values will always be present, and thereby have the possibility of causing anchoring 
effects that are both powerful and durable. For the anchoring effect to have negative 
consequences in respect to making the right decisions in projects, the anchor needs to be 
different from what would be a realistic value, meaning either too pessimistic or too 
optimistic, or simply; inaccurate. Flyvbjerg (2008) describes optimism bias and strategic 
misrepresentation1 as the two explanations that best accounts for forecasting inaccuracy.

Strategic misrepresentation can e.g. lead to deliberate optimism in own projects, or 
deliberate pessimism about competition’s projects. Several causes for the optimism bias can 
be found in the literature, of which the following should be mentioned. Kahneman and 
Tversky (1979a, 1979b) demonstrate that human judgement is generally optimistic due to 
overconfidence and insufficient regard to distributional information. Shepperd et al. (2002) 
describes the motivation for self-enhancement, and Kunda (1990) motivated reasoning, as 
causes. In an experiment Kutsch et al. (2011) demonstrated that the initial profit forecast, 
which was highly optimistic for 75% of the cases, served as a reference point, and acted as 
an anchor that the teams hold on to. For a significant proportion of the teams (nearly a third), 
this anchoring effect persisted throughout all phases of project execution; which further 
underpins that project contexts are prone to anchoring effects. 
The focus of this study will be on the possible anchoring effect caused by an 
expressed initial profit forecast of a project that has not yet been commenced. The reason 
for this choice is twofold. Firstly that financial considerations are a key aspect of project 
selection; and secondly that an argument could be made for that the decision of whether to 
start a project or not, is the most important decision in the entire project. Starting the wrong 
project can end up costing unnecessary funds, resources, time, opportunity cost, competitive 
advantage, etc. It is evident from research on success and failure rates of projects worldwide 
(CHAOS, 2009), that only around 30% of all projects is delivered on time, on budget, with 
required features and functions; and just above 20% are either cancelled before completion, 
or delivered and never used. Even though there are many reasons contributed to project 
failures, this research highlights the importance of choosing the right projects. It is therefore 
important to make sure that decision makers, make objective go/no-go decision based the 
right information, and that they are not biased by an uninformative initial profit forecast, 
perceived at an earlier point. Said in another way, the go/no-go decision needs to be build 
on an extensive business case analysis made by the company regarding a new project, and 
not partly biased by a profit forecast made a week earlier by an uninformed optimistic 
employee.  
There have only been a few anchoring studies that has a real-life information-rich 
setting, combined with the anchor simply being in the midst of the information, and not 
emphasized by drawing attention to it (e.g. Northcraft and Neale, 1987; and somewhat 
Thorsteinson et al., 2008). None have been made in the domain of projects. It is this gap in 
knowledge the current study is aiming to close, which leads to the following research 
question and subquestion: 
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
● RQ1: What effect does an anchor regarding the expected profit of a project have on 
the perceived expected profit? 
○ RQ1a: What effect does this anchor have on the willingness to start the 
project? 
 
This study will more specifically aim to simulate a real-life information-rich setting, in 
the situation where an initial profit forecast of a possible new project, is perceived by the 
decision maker before an extensive business case analysis has been made. If this anchor 
distorts the perception of expected profit and/or the willingness to start the project; thereby 
validating the existence of an anchoring effect; what can be done to mitigate this distortion? 
A viable answer could be the consider-the-opposite (CtO) method, a known de-biasing 
method that has been successful in mitigating a variety of different judgemental distortions, 
such as: hindsight bias (Arkes et al., 1988), overconfidence (Koriat et al., 1980), biased 
hypothesis-testing, and biased processing of novel information (Lord et al., 1984), as cited 
through Mussweiler et al. (2000). In the case of mitigating the anchoring effect, Chapman 
and Johnson (1999) and Mussweiler et al. (2000) have both found success using the CtO 
method2. This leads to the second research question and subquestion: 
● RQ2: Does the consider-the-opposite method mitigate the anchoring effect? 
○ RQ2a: What effect does the consider-the-opposite method have on both the 
perceived expected profit, and the willingness to start the project? 
 
The structure of the paper is as follows. A thorough literature review of the anchoring effect 
will identify which anchoring mechanisms are likely to be involved in the specific situation 
this study is aiming to simulate; as well as the limitations and boundary conditions of these 
mechanisms. Then, a brief literature review will argue for the high likelihood of the initial 
profit forecast being inaccurate. Thereafter, the literature reviews are used to build a 
theoretical model of the experiment, as well as being used to design it, of which the latter 
part will be covered in the methodology section. Then follows the results, data analysis and 
discussion; continuing with a section of contributions to practice. Finally, a reflection section 
will cover limitations of the study, as well as how these could be eliminated; and end with 
suggestions to further research.

Literature review - The anchoring effect 
Two basic anchoring mechanisms; one described by the selective accessibility 
model, and another by the anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic; are known from literature to 
cause anchoring effects3. This section will take a psychological perspective in reviewing how 
these anchoring mechanisms works, as to determine which one is likely to be involved in the 
specific situation this study is aiming to simulate. It will further go through limitations and 
boundary conditions of this anchoring mechanism, as well as how to mitigate the anchoring 
effect caused by it4.   
The selective accessibility model 
In the standard anchoring paradigm, as described in the example in the introduction, 
participants have to perform two consecutive tasks: a comparative and an absolute 
judgement. Anchoring effects in this paradigm are caused by an anchoring mechanism 
described by the selective accessibility model, which consists of two hypotheses: selectivity 
and accessibility.  
The selectivity hypothesis argues that participants solve the comparative judgement 
by “selectively” generating semantic knowledge that is consistent with the notion that the 
value of the judged quantity (the target) is equal to the anchor (Mussweiler and Strack, 
1999)5. The reason for the quotation marks will be explained later. 
The accessibility hypothesis argues that this generated semantic anchor-consistent 
knowledge, subsequently will be more easily accessible, and thereby more likely to be used 
to answer the absolute judgement; which is what causes the anchoring effect (Mussweiler 
and Strack, 1999). 
The selectivity hypothesis 
Breaking it down, there are two steps involved in the generation of semantic anchor-
consistent knowledge.  
Firstly: the comparative task seems to consist of two tests, namely that the target is 
either below or above the anchor. Psychologically, however, participants seem to test

whether the target is exactly equal to the anchor (Strack and Mussweiler, 1997). See 
Mussweiler and Strack (1999, p. 139, footnote 2) that argues that this way of testing is more 
diagnostic than to test whether the target is either below or above the anchor. 
 
Secondly: now that the comparative question psychologically have been changed to 
a hypothesis that states that the target is equal to the anchor; how is this hypothesis tested? 
As cited through Strack and Mussweiler (1997), Trope and his collaborators (Trope & 
Bassok, 1982, 1983; Bassok & Trope, 1984; Trope et al., 1984; Trope & Liberman, 1996) 
have argued that participants, in the situation of hypothesis testing, rely mostly on 
information that is consistent with the hypothesis, and not possible alternatives. Adopting the 
terminology of Klayman and Ha (1987) this is called a positive test strategy. The general 
tendency towards a positive test strategy has been widely replicated, and data suggests that 
people use this test strategy as a general default heuristic (Klayman & Ha, 1987). Results 
from Chapman and Johnson (1999, Experiment 1) supports this, by documenting that 
participants spent more time considering target features that were similar to the anchor, 
except when explicitly instructed to identify different features.  
As Mussweiler and Strack (1999) describes, in accordance with this positive test 
strategy, participants may generate a mental model of the target being equal to the anchor, 
by “selectively” retrieving semantic anchor-consistent knowledge from memory. 
The accessibility hypothesis 
 
The selective accessibility model assumes that the process mediating the anchoring 
effect is akin to semantic priming. As suggested by semantic priming research (Higgins, 
1989, 1996, 1997; Sedikides & Skowronski, 1991; Wyer & Srull, 1989; as cited through 
Mussweiler and Strack, 1999), the above described retrieval of semantic anchor-consistent 
knowledge, will increase its subsequent accessibility. In order to generate the absolute 
judgement, judges then rely primarily on easily accessible knowledge (Higgins, 1996; Wyer 
& Srull, 1989), which now is anchor-consistent. The absolute judgement will therefore be 
assimilated towards the anchor, thereby causing the anchoring effect. As evidence of this, 
Strack and Mussweiler (1997) found that answering the comparative question in a more 
elaborate fashion, thereby likely generating more anchor-consistent knowledge, shortened 
the answering time on the absolute judgement; which supports the accessibility hypothesis. 
Chapman and Johnson (1999) found larger anchoring effects when judges had more target 
information available, than judges who had little. Furthermore, judges that due to a sad 
mood6 generated more anchor-consistent knowledge in the comparative task, showed larger

anchoring effects than judges in a neutral mood (Bodenhausen et al., 2000). This testifies to 
the notion that in making the absolute judgement, judges rely primarily on the easily 
accessible semantic anchor-consistent knowledge that was generated in the comparative 
task; thereby confirming the accessibility hypothesis. 
Strength and durability 
 
In the standard anchoring paradigm, forewarnings of the anchoring effect and 
financial incentives to avoid it are found to have little or no effect (Chapman & Johnson, 
2002; Tversky & Kahneman, 1974; Wilson et al., 1989), which underpins its robustness.  
This though, leaves one disparity to be accounted for. Semantic priming effects are 
rather fragile, and are affected by factors of which anchoring effects are independent, such 
as: the extremity of the primes (Herr, 1986; Herr, et al., 1983), motivational factors (Martin et 
al., 1990), awareness of the influence (Strack et al., 1993) and correctional instructions 
(Wegener and Petty, 1995), as cited by Mussweiler and Strack (1999). If this anchoring 
effect is mediated by a process akin to semantic priming, what explains the disparity in 
robustness? The answer could be found in one important difference between the two 
paradigms. In the standard priming paradigm the primed information is typically externally 
provided by an experimenter, whereas it is internally generated by the participants 
themselves in the standard anchoring paradigm (Mussweiler and Strack, 1999). Research 
on belief perseverance (Davis, 1997) suggests that it may be this self-generation that is 
responsible for the robustness disparity. 
In addition to the robustness discussed above, anchoring effects caused by the 
anchoring mechanism described by the selective accessibility model is found to have a 
temporal durability of at least a week. Mussweiler (2001) made three experiments testing the 
durability of the anchoring effect, in which the comparative and absolute judgement was 
separated by a one-week delay. Each of the experiments varied in how likely it was that the 
participants encountered information about the target during the week. Even when the target 
was about the mean temperature in Germany7, where the participants were highly likely to 
encounter information regarding the target on a daily basis, reliable anchoring effects 
occurred. The data also surprisingly shows that the magnitude of the anchoring effect 
remained unchanged over this period of time.

The anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic 
In the standard anchoring paradigm, the anchors are externally provided. In the case 
of self-generated anchors, evidence suggests that another anchoring mechanism, described 
by the anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic, is causing the anchoring effect (Epley and 
Gilovich, 2001). A self-generated anchor is likely to be used when the following three 
requirements are present: no comparative judgement precedes the absolute; the judge does 
not know the exact answer; and the judge has a known reference point to adjust from. As an 
example, take the question: what is the freezing point of vodka? If the judge does not know 
the target answer, it is likely that the freezing point of water is known, as well as that vodka 
freezes at a lower temperature. The freezing point of water then serves as a reference point, 
or self-generated anchor, where the judge starts the downward adjustment until a 
satisfactory answer is reached. This adjustment is generally found to be insufficient (Epley 
and Gilovich, 2001, 2006), which is what causes the anchoring effect. 
The difference between the two cases, is found in that externally provided anchors, 
even outrageous ones, have to be taken seriously, if only for a moment; whereas self-
generated anchors are known from the beginning to be wrong. Since there then is no reason 
to consider whether this anchor is equal to the target, there is thus no engine for generating 
semantic anchor-consistent knowledge. This is what led Epley and Gilovich (2001) to 
propose that anchoring effects are caused by insufficient adjustment, rather than semantic 
anchoring, when the anchor is self-generated. 
The reason for the insufficient adjustment was found to be due to the fact that people 
stop their adjustment when it falls within an implicit range of plausible values (Epley and 
Gilovich, 2006). The final answer therefore tends to lie near the anchor-side of this implicit 
range. On average though, the target is likely to lie closer to the middle of the range. 
An interesting difference from semantic anchoring is that anchoring-and-adjustment 
is found to be effortful, which means that anything that increases a judge’s willingness or 
ability to seek a more accurate answer, tends to reduce the magnitude of this anchoring 
effect (Epley and Gilovich, 2006). In their experiments, participants who were motivated and

A psychological perspective 
Drawn from the above findings about reducing the anchoring effect, it seems that 
such mitigating manipulations, like forewarnings and incentives to avoid it, only have an 
effect when adjustment processes are invoked, as they are when the anchors are self-
generated. Why is that? To answer this question, the terminology from Kahneman (2011) 
about the two different ways the human brain works10, System 1 and System 2 are adopted, 
and briefly explained below. 
System 1 operates quickly and automatically, with little or no effort, and with no 
sense of voluntary control. We are born prepared to observe and perceive the world around 
us, and as we do this, System 1 automatically assesses the new information according to 
the mental model of the world already created from previous experiences. If something 
differs, our mental model evolves, and is continuously updated according to the new 
information. There are though several limitations of which the following are relevant in this 
regard: it cannot be turned off, has little understanding of logic and statistics, as well as 
being biased to believe and confirm11. 
System 2 is the conscious, reasoning self. It allocates attention to effortful mental 
activities; makes choices; decides what to think about and what to do; along with having the 
ability (which System 1 does not) to construct thoughts in an orderly series of steps. It is also 
in charge of doubting and logical reasoning. In this regard, the relevant limitations of System 
2 are that it requires effort and attention to work, and has a limited budget of attention to 
allocate. 
As Kahneman (2011) puts it, there is an anchoring mechanism for each system. One 
in System 2 that is a deliberate process of adjustment that requires mental effort; and one 
that occurs by a priming effect, which is an automatic manifestation of System 1. This 
explains the fact that forewarnings and financial incentives only mitigate the anchoring effect 
when it is caused by anchoring-and-adjustment.

 All together, the above part of the literature review suggests that in the situation of 
interest in this study, it is semantic anchoring that is in play. The reason being that here, the 
initial profit forecast is provided by an external source to be perceived by the decision maker. 
Since semantic anchoring occurs in System 1, and we therefore cannot exert any conscious 
control over it, semantic anchor-consistent knowledge is automatically generated as soon as 
the initial profit forecast is perceived. This is the reason for the quotation marks on 
“selective” above. It is not a choice, and cannot be avoided. But how then, can semantic 
anchoring effects be mitigated? 
Mitigating the semantic anchoring effect 
As pointed out in the introduction, the consider-the-opposite (CtO) method has been 
proven successful in mitigating a variety of judgemental distortions. The mechanism of the 
method is neatly demonstrated by Koriat et al. (1980) that asked their participants to answer 
trivia questions and thereafter estimate the probability that each of their answers were 
correct. First of all, overconfidence in their estimates was observed. Secondly, when 
prompting for reasons that could indicate that their answers were correct, their estimates 
were unchanged and therefore still overconfident. When prompting for reasons that could 
indicate that their answers were wrong, it reduced the overconfidence. Two conclusions can 
be drawn from this. First, it indicates that decision makers naturally consider reasons that 
they might be right, which is why prompting them to do so changes nothing12. Second, it 
illustrates that decision makers usually fail to consider opposing reasons, which is why 
prompting them to do so have an effect.  
From a semantic anchoring perspective, the consequence of System 1 being biased 
to confirm, and furthermore operate beyond our voluntary control, is the generation of 
semantic anchor-consistent knowledge as soon as the anchor is perceived. Therefore, the 
aim of the CtO method in this regard is to generate semantic anchor-inconsistent knowledge, 
so that this knowledge likewise is easily accessible in the moment of judgement, thereby 
counterbalancing semantic anchoring. It should be added that this would likely not eliminate 
the semantic anchoring effect. The reason being that it is System 2; our conscious self, 
which is in charge of doubting and logical reasoning; that has to perform the CtO method. 
The workings of System 2 is both effortful with a limited budget of attention, as well as it 
being far less efficient than System 1 (Kahneman, 2011), which argues for a mitigation 
instead of an elimination.

This suggests that the CtO method; and more specifically inducing decision makers 
to consider reasons why the implications of the anchor may be wrong; constitutes a strategy 
for mitigating the semantic anchoring effect (Mussweiler et al., 2000). From a hypothesis-
testing perspective, the CtO method can be seen as a negative test strategy13. It therefore 
makes sense that a negative test strategy can be used to counterbalance an otherwise used 
positive test strategy. Both Chapman and Johnson (1999) and Mussweiler et al. (2000) have 
found success using the CtO method to mitigate the anchoring effect. To quote the latter: 
“Considering the opposite appears to be the first mechanism that successfully reduces the 
anchoring bias”. 
Limitations and boundary conditions of semantic anchoring 
In the following, limitations and boundary condition of semantic anchoring will be 
reviewed, as to guide the later development of the theoretical model, as well as to guide the 
detailed design choices of its operationalization into the experiment. This section will 
furthermore argue for the transferability of the results obtained in this study, to a real-life 
setting. 
Relevance of the generated semantic anchor-consistent knowledge  
There have been several studies where the relevance of the generated semantic 
anchor-consistent knowledge to the absolute judgement has been tested. One study had a 
comparative judgement about the height of the Brandenburg Gate, where the absolute 
judgement pertained its width, and vice versa (Strack and Mussweiler, 1997, Study 1). 
Another changed from a comparative judgement about temperatures in Antarctica, to an 
absolute judgement of temperatures in Hawaii (Strack and Mussweiler, 1997, Study 2). Yet 
another, where the comparative judgement pertained the number of floors in the World 
Trade Centre, and the absolute judgement pertained the number of members in the United 
Nations (Mussweiler and Strack, 2001b, Study 2).  
Taken together, the results of these studies suggest that the magnitude of the 
anchoring effect depend on how applicable the generated semantic anchor-consistent 
knowledge is to the absolute judgement. If both judgements pertained the same judgemental 
target, and the semantic knowledge thereby had a chance to exert influence, a reliable

anchoring effect occurred. If not, the anchoring effect was either weak or not significant. It is 
therefore necessary for this study to make sure that the generated semantic anchor-

Extremity of the anchor (plausible/implausible) 
 
As described in the section of the selective accessibility model, the more semantic 
anchor-consistent knowledge generated, the shorter were the answering time on the 
absolute judgement, and the larger anchoring effects were observed. Following this line of 
thought, an implausible anchor should according to semantic anchoring cause a less 
pronounced effect. The reason being that with an extremely implausible anchor, the decision 
maker would only have to rely on minimal categorical knowledge to dismiss the hypothesis 
that the anchor is equal to the target (Strack and Mussweiler, 1997); thereby not generating 
as much semantic anchor-consistent knowledge. Take for instance the example used by 
Mussweiler and Strack (1999): is the river Elbe longer or shorter than 45.000 km? Here, the 
decision maker will only have to generate the knowledge that no river is that long. In this 
experiment however, the anchoring effect was more pronounced for implausible than for 
plausible anchors. This result is consistent with that previous research also has found 
anchoring effects using extremely implausible anchors (Chapman & Johnson, 1994; Strack 
& Mussweiler, 1997). As Mussweiler and Strack (1999) notes, it is possible that different 
mechanisms are involved when implausible rather than plausible anchors are used. 
Specifically they suggest, continuing their above example, that an adjustment process from 
45.000 km to a plausible boundary value is first used, followed by testing the hypothesis that 
the boundary value is equal to the target, as according to semantic anchoring. This 
explanation though, assumes that the chosen boundary value could be the correct answer. If 
a self-generated anchor, known by the decision maker to be wrong, is chosen as the 
boundary value instead, the mechanism in play would be anchoring-and-adjustment.  
 
Whichever explanation is correct, the point is that when an implausible anchor 
enters the equation, it thus becomes unclear which mechanism is in play. Since the CtO 
method is a de-biasing method for semantic anchoring, it is important that the anchors in this 
study remain plausible. 
Does expertise diminish the anchoring effect? 
 
It has been widely demonstrated that anchoring occurs independently of 
participants’ expertise (Joyce & Biddle, 1981; Northcraft & Neale, 1987; Wright & Anderson, 
1989; as cited through Mussweiler et al., 2000). Northcraft and Neale (1987) describes two 
conclusions regarding this. Experts are as susceptible to anchoring as amateurs; and they 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
are less likely to either admit or understand its influence on their decision. This is seconded 
by Englich et al. (2006), as described by the quote in the beginning of the introduction. In 
their experiment, they demonstrated the anchoring effect with legal professionals that had 
received extensive training in the domain of critical judgement; with experience in similar 
sentencing decisions as used in the experiment; and with motivation for an accurate 
judgement.  
This suggests that an anchoring effect found in the current study, using students; is 
transferrable to expert decision makers. 
Transferability from an experimental to a real-life setting 
 
Besides the just discussed issue of transferability from students to experts; the 
same issue of transferability can be discussed regarding going from an experimental setting 
with limited information, to a real-life information-rich setting. At least three studies have 
covered this. First, in Thorsteinson et al. (2008) the participants had large amounts of 
relevant information about the target they were to judge regarding performance. Second, 
Englich et al. (2006) collaborated with legal experts to create the cases they used in their 
experiments. The legal experts participating in these experiments, judged whether this 
information was realistic on a 1-9 point scale, where 9 is exactly as it would be in a real 
case. The mean score was just above 7, indicating that it was quite close to reality. Third, 
Northcraft and Neale (1987) used the same procedure in creating their information on a real-
estate appraisal, as well as that they used the actual real-estate in the experiment. 
 
Reliable anchoring effects were found in all three studies, which suggests that 
decision makers operating in real-life information-rich settings, are just as susceptible to 
anchoring effects as they would be otherwise. 
Relevance of the anchor, does it matter? 
 
Continuing the example above with the well trained legal decision makers from 
Englich et al. (2006), shows another detail. In Study 3, the anchoring effect occurred even 
though the anchor value was know by the participants to be completely irrelevant, since they 
determined this value themselves by throwing a pair of dice. In Study 2, on the other hand, 
the anchor came as a sentencing demand from the prosecutor, which could be seen as 
relevant information. Despite this difference in anchor relevance, the magnitude of the 
anchoring effect in these two studies, were found to be similar.  
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
This underpins the fact that a completely uninformative anchor, like an optimistic 
employee blurting out a profit forecast for a project, can cause an anchoring effect that can 
influence important decisions. 
Alternative methods for causing anchoring effects 
When deviating from the standard anchoring paradigm, as well as not asking 
participants questions that would cause a self-generated anchor; anchoring effects have still 
been demonstrated in several studies (Northcraft & Neale, 1987; Wilson et al., 1996; 
Critcher and Gilovich, 2008; Nunes and Boatwright, 2004; Reitsma-van Rooijen & Daamen, 
2006; Mussweiler & Englich 2005; Thorsteinson et al., 2008). This section will highlight the 
most relevant in respect to the current study, and discuss the mechanisms likely to have 
caused the observed anchoring effects. 
Purely numeric influences 
 
As mentioned in the section - relevance of the generated semantic anchor-
consistent knowledge, a weak anchoring effect was still observed when semantic processes 
were incapacitated. In other words, this anchoring effect was caused by an anchoring 
mechanism open to purely numeric influences. Wilson et al. (1996, p. 389) introduced the 
term: the basic anchoring effect; which defines an anchoring effect that is caused by a 
completely uninformative anchor, and where decision makers are not asked to consider this 
anchor as a possible target value. As they both predicted and observed, and further 
seconded by Brewer and Chapman (2002), basic anchoring effects occur only under 
conditions where decision makers pay sufficient14 attention to the anchor.  
A variation to circumvent this requirement was done by Critcher and Gilovich (2008). 
Instead of making decision makers pay sufficient attention to the anchor before the 
judgement, the anchor would be within sight in the moment of judgement, which made up for 
the lack of attention to it. Their most interesting result, in respect to the current study, were 
found in Study 3, where they asked participants how much they would be willing to pay for a 
meal in a restaurant shown to them in a picture. The name of the restaurant was either 
Studio 17 or Studio 97. In analysing the results, they divided the participants into two groups. 
One group for the participants who correctly could recall the name of the restaurant (the 
anchor value), and one group that could not. Anchoring was only found in the group that

whereas copying down one page (a total of 7 numbers) did not” (Wilson et al., 1996). 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
19 
could not recall the name. As Critcher and Gilovich (2008) suggest, it is likely that the 
participants with a correct memory of the anchor value were engaged in a more thorough 
thought process; thereby generating and considering more information about the judgement, 
which then almost eliminated the impact of the anchor.  
This leads to a discussion about the mechanism causing the above described basic 
anchoring effects; as well as poses the question: why does a more thorough thought process 
almost eliminate basic anchoring effects? Anchoring effects caused by an anchoring 
mechanism open to purely numeric influences, have been suggested caused by numeric 
priming (Brewer & Chapman, 2002; Jacowitz & Kahneman, 1995; Wilson et al., 1996; Wong 
& Kwong, 2000; as cited through Reitsma-van Rooijen & Daamen, 2006). As Mussweiler 
and Strack (2001b) found, anchoring effects caused by numeric priming are fairly transitive 
and short-lived. The argument is that people are constantly exposed to arbitrary numeric 
values in their daily routines (mobile numbers, dates, times, product prices, etc.). According 
to numeric priming, all these numeric values will, along with the anchor value, be more easily 
accessible in a subsequent judgement, thereby reducing the sole impact of the anchor. This 
reasoning could also answer why a more thorough thought process almost eliminated the 
basic anchoring effect.  
The above suggests that purely numeric influences likely will not be strong enough to 
cause an anchoring effect in a real-life information-rich setting, where thorough thought 
processes and numeric values are plentiful. Especially not in a situation, equivalent to the 
one the current study is aiming to simulate, where there likely will be a temporal delay 
between the perception of the anchor and the subsequent judgement. 
Semantic anchoring outside the standard anchoring paradigm 
 
It has been speculated that semantic anchoring only may contribute to anchoring 
effects obtained in the standard anchoring paradigm (Epley and Gilovich, 2001, 2004). This 
speculation was though refuted by Mussweiler & Englich (2005). In their study, they 
demonstrated that an anchor that was subliminally presented during the evaluation of a 
target, caused an anchoring effect in the subsequent judgement of the target. Specifically (in 
Study 2 and 3) they asked the participants to think about the average price of a new midsize 
car, while subliminally flashing the anchor value (20.000 and 40.000 German Marks, for 
respectively low and high anchor) 10 times. In Study 2, the absolute judgement followed, 
demonstrating an anchoring effect. In Study 3, a lexical decision task followed, measuring 
the latency in recognizing target words. Participants receiving the low anchor were faster at 
recognizing words associated with inexpensive rather than expensive cars, and vice versa 
with participants receiving the high anchor. As discussed in the section - the selective 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
accessibility model, this suggests that semantic anchor-consistent knowledge was generated 
while thinking about the price of a new midsize car; and thereby rendering it more easily 
accessible to the subsequent lexical decision task. These results demonstrate that semantic 
anchoring can cause anchoring effects outside the standard anchoring paradigm, even when 
no conscious attention is drawn to the anchor. 
 
In an anchoring study on employee performance judgement, Thorsteinson et al. 
(2008) examined whether there was a difference in the magnitude of the anchoring effects, if 
caused either within or outside the standard anchoring paradigm. The performance was 
judged on a 1-9 point scale. Within the standard anchoring paradigm, the participants were 
asked whether a specific performance score was lower or higher than 1/9, followed by a 
judgement. Outside the standard anchoring paradigm, the participants were just before their 
judgement, shown an example of how to perform it, either will all scores 1 or all scores 9. No 
difference in magnitude of the anchoring effect was found. To examine the mechanism 
causing the effect outside the standard anchoring paradigm, Study 3 varied the target of the 
example that preceded the judgement. One where the example-ratings still pertained an 
employee (similar condition), and one where the example-ratings pertained a printer 
(dissimilar condition). If semantic anchoring were in play, the magnitude of the anchoring 
effect should be larger in the similar condition. If this was not the case, basic anchoring 
would be the likely cause. Even though a statistically significant difference between the two 
conditions were not found; the low anchor caused lower scores, and the high anchor caused 
higher scores in the similar compared to the dissimilar condition. In their discussion, they 
also acknowledge that perhaps the example-ratings of the printer may still have had 
sufficient semantic relatedness to cause an anchoring effect. Overall, the above as well 
suggests that semantic anchoring works outside the standard anchoring paradigm. 
 The experiment about a real-estate appraisal conducted by Northcraft and Neale 
(1987) should also be mentioned. Here the anchor (the listing price of the real-estate) was 
simply in the midst of the information, and not emphasized by drawing attention to it. Due to 
conversational inferences (Grice, 1975), it is likely that the participants have inferred that the 
provided listing price was relevant for the judgement, and have used it as an initial standard 
of comparison. This comparison process requires the generation of semantic knowledge 
about the target (the real-estate), which suggests that the observed anchoring effect, likely is 
caused by semantic anchoring (Mussweiler and Strack, 1999). Even if the listing price was 
not inferred as relevant, it is still likely that System 1 automatically would have generated 
semantic knowledge consistent with a real-estate worth as much as the listing price, thereby 
causing the anchoring effect.

21 
The three studies above, and especially the last, points to the fact that an anchor in the 
midst of an otherwise information-rich context, just by its presence, can cause semantic 
anchoring effects.

Literature review - The inaccuracy of profit forecasts 
As mentioned in the introduction, for the anchoring effect to have negative 
consequences in respect to making the right decision, the initial profit forecast needs to be 
inaccurate. In other words, a too pessimistic initial profit forecast might exert influence 
towards a no-go decision, when a go decision should have been chosen, and vice versa with 
a too optimistic initial profit forecast. Flyvbjerg (2008) describes optimism bias and strategic 
misrepresentation as the two explanations that best accounts for forecasting inaccuracy. 
Strategic misrepresentation can be traced to political and organizational pressures, 
and is a concept that describes that people deliberately and strategically manipulate values 
of benefits and costs to increase the likelihood that it is their projects, and not the 
competition’s, that gain approval and funding. This concept can therefore explain both that 
the initial profit forecast in own projects, is too optimistic; and it being too pessimistic when 
statements about competition’s projects are made.  
In the literature, several causes for the optimism bias can be found, of which the 
following should be mentioned in regards to this study:  
Firstly, Kahneman and Tversky (1979a, 1979b) demonstrate that human judgement 
is generally optimistic due to overconfidence and insufficient regard to distributional 
information (as cited through Flyvbjerg, 2008). By distributional information, is meant data on 
variation from the expected outcome, e.g. expressed in common statistical measures such 
as standard deviation and variance15. The consequence of this is, as Flyvbjerg (2008) 
phrase it: “Thus people will underestimate the costs, completion times, and risks of planned 
actions, whereas they will overestimate the benefits of the same actions”. This common 
behaviour is called the “planning fallacy” (Lovallo and Kahneman, 2003, p. 58). 
Secondly, the motivation for self-enhancement is another cause for optimism. 
Optimistic predictions are gratifying, and therefore self-enhancing. It simply feels good to 
think that positive events will happen. Evidence of this, are findings that people tend to focus 
on what they want to happen, rather than what might or will happen (Cantril, 1938; Sherman, 
1980; as cited through Shepperd et al., 2002). Moreover, as cited through Shepperd et al. 
(2002), the desirability of an event has a direct relationship with the perceived likelihood that 
the event will occur (Biner et al., 1995). Perloff and Fetzer (1986) found the effect of 
desirability on comparative optimism16 especially true for events that are personally 
                                               
15 The insufficient regard to this type of information is in accordance with that our System 1 has little 
understanding of statistics. 
16 Another term for optimism bias (Shepperd et al., 2002)

23 
important. Taken together, this suggests that if there exists a desirability by an employee to 
be part of a possible new project; whether this is based on career advancement 
opportunities, the enthusiasm of an intrapreneur (e.g. an R&D employee excited about a 
breakthrough), or any number of other reasons; an optimistic initial profit forecast from this 
employee is highly likely.  
Thirdly, for the same reasons as mentioned above, the following continues the idea 
that an employee wants to be part of a possible new project. Starting the project therefore is 
an outcome that has personal importance, i.e. is a directional goal. Motivated reasoning 
suggests that people are more likely to arrive at those conclusions they want to arrive at 
(Kunda, 1990), e.g. that the project would be profitable and therefore started. The 
mechanism at play is similar to the mechanism of the confirmation bias, where people 
search memory for those beliefs and rules that could support their desired outcome. 
Motivated reasoning though, further argues that people also creatively may combine the 
accessed knowledge to construct new beliefs that logically could support the desired 
outcome (Kunda, 1990). In this case, construct beliefs that support an otherwise too 
optimistic initial profit forecast. Kutch et al. (2011) e.g. found evidence of motivated 
reasoning as one of the contribution to the observed optimism in the initial profit forecasts 
made by the teams. 
Finally, people tend to overestimate the probability of conjunctive events17 (Tversky 
and Kahneman, 1974). Even if the probability of each individual event is high, the overall 
probability of conjunctive success can be quite low if the number of events is large. The 
tendency to overestimate the probability of conjunctive events can be explained by the 
mechanism of anchoring-and-adjustment. Since the completion of an undertaking, such as a 
project, typically has a conjunctive character, this overestimation of probability leads to 
unwarranted optimism in the evaluation of the likelihood that a plan will succeed (Tversky 
and Kahneman, 1974); which likely could translate to an optimistic initial profit forecast. 
All and all, this suggests that it is highly likely that the initial profit forecast will be 
inaccurate, and thereby have the possibility of causing an anchoring effect that could affect 
the subsequent go/no-go decision.

The theoretical model 
To recap the focus, Figure 1 shows the real-life project context this study is aiming to 
simulate, as to determine the possible anchoring effect of an uninformative initial profit 
forecast perceived by the decision maker. 
 
Figure 1: The real-life project context this study is aiming to simulate. 
In a real-life project context like this, the decision maker would likely have an idea of whether 
the perceived initial profit forecast was good or bad. In other words, whether it was low or 
high compared to company standards. This is assumed to be the case moving forward. 
Following that premise, the initial profit forecast would likely cause a semantic anchoring 
effect. As covered in the literature review, the perception of the initial profit forecast would 
likely also cause a basic anchoring effect. This effect is though rather transitive and short-
lived, and would due to constant exposure to arbitrary numeric values in our daily routines, 
likely be wiped out in the period from the perception of the initial profit forecast to the go/no-
go decision. Going forward, all information perceived in this period is defined as contextual 
noise. 
As opposed to basic anchoring, semantic anchoring is found to be robust enough to 
cause an anchoring effect that, despite contextual noise, did not diminish in magnitude over 
the course of a week. This suggests that the semantic anchoring effect caused by the initial 
profit forecast, has the potential to survive the temporal delay between its perception and the 
go/no-go decision, and thereby influencing it.  
On a more detailed note on semantic anchoring in this specific context; theory 
proposes that the generated semantic anchor-consistent knowledge would be something like 
the following18. For a low initial profit forecast, it would likely be knowledge of a troublesome 
project, like: lack of funding, breakdown of equipment, internal political issues, employee 
dissatisfaction, missing deadlines, other companies stealing market share, unhappy 
customers, etc. For a high initial profit forecast, the opposite would apply. This generated 
semantic anchor-consistent knowledge should then affect the perception of expected profit. 
Therefore, the semantic anchoring effect should be measurable in the decision maker’s 
perception of expected profit, which leads to the first hypothesis:

a project is therefore seen as risk-seeking behaviour (Keil et al., 2000). Prior research have 
found that decision makers tend to exert risk-seeking behaviour when risk perception is low, 
and risk-averse behaviour when risk perception is high (March and Shapira 1987; Staw et al.

interesting. This was though not chosen. The reason is that inquiries about this, would draw

somewhat similar to the mechanism of the CtO method. Incorporating risk perception into

possibly diminish the contrast between the conditions, thereby making it harder to detect the

● H3b: Perceived expected profit below the success criteria cut-off value, will correlate

Methodology 
This section will go through who participated in the experiment, how they were 
recruited, as well as describe the procedure of how it was conducted. It will furthermore 
present the most important parts of the content of the experiment, and continue with a 
section explaining the thoughts behind the design choices. 
Participants 
In collaboration with the professor of the DTU course: Project Management 42430 
2016, 115 of the course’s students were recruited. Even though participation was voluntary, 
it was suggested by their professor that they participate, since it would fit with their 
curriculum regarding complexity and human behaviour in projects. A chocolate bar was 
offered as compensation. 
Procedure 
The experiment was done in succession of one of the course’s weekly lectures, in the 
same lecture hall so that the students could remain seated. In a short introduction, they were 
told that it was a study on behavioural decision making in the domain of project 
management, specifically about decisions made with limited information. They were 
furthermore told that the normal pattern of decision making were in three steps: 
● Step 1: New idea/opportunity 
● Step 2: Investigate – seek more information 
● Step 3: Make decision 
In the experiment to follow, they would have to go through these three steps. Specifically, 
they were to play the product portfolio manager that would be introduced to a new product 
opportunity, followed by a business case analysis, and then a go/no-go decision. After that 
would follow a couple of post-experimental questions. 
 
The students were then divided into four groups of roughly equal size, and were 
instructed, on their laptops, to enter the course’s online platform and press on the Google 
Form link corresponding to their group number. The groups were divided as follows19: 
● Group 1: normal condition - low anchor 
● Group 2: normal condition - high anchor 
● Group 3: CtO condition - low anchor 
● Group 4: CtO condition - high anchor

The design process 
Overall design choices 
As stated in the introduction, this study deviates from most anchoring studies, in that 
it aims to simulate a real-life information-rich setting, where the anchor simply is in the midst 
of the information; and investigate whether the anchoring effect is strong enough to survive 
the contextual noise in such a setting. The literature review suggests that the only anchoring 
effect strong enough to survive the contextual noise, is the semantic anchoring effect. To 
cause this, two requirements had to be fulfilled.  
Firstly, to enable semantic anchor-consistent knowledge to be generated, or retrieved 
from memory once the anchor is perceived, the specific project for the experiment needed to 
be a type of project of which the participants had prior knowledge. Among different 
alternatives: construction project, acquisition of a company, outsourcing, internal expanding 
(e.g. new department), etc.; a new product opportunity was chosen. The reason is found in 
the following. Not all the participants came from the management study line, which meant 
that they might not know much about the above alternatives. Even though they might not 
either have personal experience with a new product opportunity and product launch, they are 
all consumers. Thereby they would be familiar with the fact that good products, whether they 
are cheap or expensive, gets bought by customers and generate profit, whereas bad 
products do not. They would likely also have some employee experience, which would leave 
them knowledge about that some companies have better and more skilled employees than 
==PAGE:34==
 
33 
others; would be better at both developing and producing products than others, and thereby 
generating more profit than other companies. Due to these consumer and employee 
experiences, the participants’ knowledge about a new product opportunity was judged 
sufficient to cause a semantic anchoring effect. 
 The second requirement was that the framing of this fictitious situation needed to be 
thorough enough to ensure that the participants, once they perceived the anchor, would 
understand whether it was low or high, and hence generate semantic knowledge consistent 
with this understanding. To accomplish this, it required a relatively detailed descriptive text, 
as shown above. This though, increased the duration of the experiment, which meant that it 
was only possible, due to time constraints, to include one fictitious situation. Since each 
participant cannot be exposed to both normal and CtO condition, or both to low and high 
anchor in the same fictitious situation, a within-subject design was not possible. This resulted 
in a between-subject design, with the four groups mentioned above. 
Regarding a sufficient sample size of an anchoring experiment like this, Englich et al. 
(2006) was used as guidance. Here they stated that the sample size in either Study 1 or 2 
individually was too small to examine how expertise influenced decisions; but by combining 
the two studies, thereby getting 41 experts and 40 non-experts, the analysis was possible. 
Hence a sample size of approximately 40 in each group would suffice. There were around 
200 students in the course, so the chosen 4-group design fitted this requirement. Not all 
students attended the lecture the day of the experiment, and a couple of students walked out 
when it was stated that it was voluntary, resulting in the 115 participants. This was not ideal, 
but most of the statistical analyses met their cell count requirements nonetheless. 
Anchoring studies usually compares the effect of the low and high anchor without 
having an unanchored control group. This has several reasons behind it (Englich et al., 
2006, p. 194-195), of which the following two accounted for the choice of no control group in 
the current study. First, as discussed in the literature review, it has been demonstrated that 
any sufficiently accessible numeric value can serve as an anchor (Mussweiler & Englich, 
2005; Wilson et al., 1996; Critcher and Gilovich, 2008). Providing no anchor thus does not 
ensure that no anchor is used. The unanchored control group could therefore merely be a 
self-anchored control group. Second, whether the low or high anchor has a stronger effect, is 
of minor importance compared to the interest in the magnitude of the anchoring effect, which 
can be measured without a unanchored control group.  
==PAGE:35==
 
34 
The pilot tests of the experiment, also contributed to some overall design choices. In 
a previous version of the experiment, the mean of the probability distribution20 was 
2,500,000 instead of the chosen 1,500,000. In the pilot tests, even though the low anchor 
was used, 3 out of 4 resulted in a go decision. One of the reasons given was that even 
though the probability of success was 50%, the probability for an actual negative profit was 
less than 5%, which made him take the risk. For this reason, the mean of the distribution 
was moved to 1,500,000 as shown in Figure 4 above, which increased the percentage of an 
actual negative profit to around 15%. Furthermore, in this previous version of the 
experiment, one of the pilot testers did not notice the anchor. Since this is of crucial 
importance, as well as noticing the success criteria cut-off value, two adjustments were 
made. The general text was simplified as to reduce the cognitive load, and the crucial 
information was boxed and bolded as shown earlier.  
Even though the cognitive load in the introduction was kept at a minimum, an overall 
design choice was to keep the level of contextual noise high, as to simulate reality as closely 
as possible. This was the reason behind the rather information-rich business case analysis. 
Introduction 
 
To cause a semantic anchoring effect, it was important to make sure that the 
participants understood the fictitious situation, as mentioned above. This is the reason that 
the introductory presentation before the experiment, also included an explanation of both the 
situation and the role of product portfolio manager the participants were to play.  
ABC Inc. and your role in it 
 
Besides keeping the description short and concise, there were two main goals here. 
Firstly, to clearly state the success criteria cut-off value, which was done via the boxing and 
bolding. Secondly, to emphasise that the company had experience in product launches, so 
the participants would trust the information in the business case analysis. 
A new product opportunity 
The main goal here was to clearly state the anchor, which was accomplished in the 
same way as described above. It was furthermore important, that if given attention to the 
origin of the anchor, it would clearly stand out as uninformative. Several clues underpinned 
this. First, “By asking around in R&D”. Second, “Based on how much we sold of a similar product 
some years ago”. Third, “how much we think this new product could be sold for”. Even 
participants from different study lines than management, is expected to know that it is not the

uninformative, thus removes any conversational influences (Grice, 1975), e.g. that the

Regarding the extremity of the anchors, the literature review states the importance

keep the plausibility of either anchor at the same level.  
 
A last content-choice in this part is worth mentioning. It was chosen to incorporate 
statements that held the possibility of an ambiguous interpretation, depending on which 
anchor that was given. Examples of this are found in “a finding that has the potential to be 
used in a new product” and “which opens up the possibility of developing this new product”. A 
pessimistic interpretation (e.g. due to a low anchor), would be to focus on that “potential” and 
“possibility” means nothing certain; and that the new product is not yet developed, which 
means an unknown developing time. An optimistic interpretation of the same words (e.g. due 
to a high anchor), would be focus on the possibilities of this new product. The same 
ambiguity concept was intended with “we believe that this product will … open up for a new 
market”. New markets can both be profitable, but also rather risky. 
CtO condition 
The formulation of the sentence that should force the participants to generate 
semantic anchor-inconsistent knowledge was inspired by Mussweiler et al. (2000). In Study 
1, where the anchor was an estimated price of a car, the formulation of the CtO question 
was: “What would you say argues against this price?”. This lead to the formulation: “Think 
about reasons that argue against that the just mentioned initial profit forecast is correct. Of 
those reasons, please write down the two reasons that you believe best argues against it.” 
Forcing the participants to write down two reasons was done as to check whether the CtO 
method had been applied.

Business case analysis 
 
One of the goals of the business case analysis was to make it thorough enough to

Another goal was to continue the ambiguity concept described above, as to make

factor had an uncertainty. Secondly, the first three parts of the business case analysis: 
external, internal and customer analysis; were written so they were respectively slightly

The decision 
 
For semantic anchoring to cause an anchoring effect, the generated semantic 
anchor-consistent knowledge needs to be allowed to exert influence on the subsequent 
judgement, as discussed in the literature review. In other words, since the anchor in this 
study is about profit, the question measuring the anchoring effect also needed to be about 
profit, which therefore was abided.  
 
The initial intention with the perceived expected profit question was to ask 
participants for a specific value, thereby receiving answers on a continuous scale. This 
intention was though abandoned due to the time constraints of the experiment. The reason 
being that these constraints meant that the business case analysis could not be created 
containing actual numbers for each cost and benefit factor; as this would have required too 
much time to read and understand. Since the business case analysis then was created on a 
conceptual basis without values, it meant that the only actual values were the success 
criteria cut-off value and the anchor. Due to this conceptual design of the business case 
analysis, the probability distribution would likely present itself as one of the only real 
informative parts, and therefore primarily be used for the judgement. The fear was that if the 
answers were allowed on a continuous scale, most of the participants, who are 
mathematically skilled engineers, would not be able to resist the urge to choose the mean 
value, even if they were affected by the anchor. Therefore the answering opportunities were

Post-experimental questions 
 
The theoretical reason for including risk propensity as a control variable is found in 
the following. As cited through Keil et al. (2000), Sitkin and Pablo (1992) proposed that there 
theoretically exists a direct effect of risk propensity on risk behaviour. Sitkin and Weingart 
(1995) tested this theoretical claim, but found no direct effect. Keil et al. (2000) furthered this 
research, and tested whether risk propensity would have a significant direct effect on 
willingness to continue a project; but did not find support for this hypothesis either. What was 
found though, was that risk propensity affected risk behaviour through risk perception (Sitkin 
and Weingart, 1995). Specifically, decision makers with high risk propensity tended to have 
low risk perception, leading to a higher willingness to take risks. The results of Keil et al. 
(2000) supported such a relationship. For this reason, the question about risk propensity was 
included, specifically to see if participants with high risk propensity were more willing to 
make a go decision. 
 
The CRT was included for two reasons. First, and most important, it was used as a 
test of whether the participants understood simple written language and had an 
understanding of logic and numbers; which is required in an experiment like this. Second, it 
was meant as gift to the participants, meaning an addition to the curriculum about human 
behaviour. The results of this test would give them an idea of whether they are more 
predispositioned to get caught by cognitive biases than others24, which could be a valuable 
realization.

Method for cleaning the data 
 
Since this experiment investigated the semantic anchoring effect, the most 
important aspect was that the participants understood the fictitious situation, as well as 
perceived both the success criteria cut-off value and the anchor. If this was not the case, 
there would be no engine for semantic anchoring. A qualitative approach, with an interview 
with each participant after the experiment, would have been the only conclusive way to verify 
that these requirements were fulfilled. Due to time constraints, this was not possible. Instead, 
several indicators from the answers could be used to judge the participants’ understanding. 
First, the qualitative answers of both the main reason(s) for the decision, as well as the two 
reasons in the CtO condition. Second, whether the perceived expected profit and the 
decision were consistent with the success criteria25. Third, whether there was a general 
seriousness in the answers. Fourth, the CRT. 
 
The basic premise in cleaning the data was that no answer should be deleted 
unless it clearly violated the requirements. A clear violation was defined as the following: 
● Using a clearly wrong argument for the decision.  
○ E.g. one participant based her go decision on that there were a 90% chance 
of getting the result the company wanted.  
● Not being serious. 
○ E.g. answering “for free chocolate” as the reason for the decision. 
● In the CtO condition, not giving at least one valid reason that would indicate that the 
CtO method had been understood and applied. 
○ E.g. answering “dunno” in both reasons, or “a lot of money” and “a big risk to 
take with an unknown project”, are not valid reasons. 
● Answering all three CRT-questions wrong, combined with that none of the qualitative 
statements indicated an understanding of the fictitious situation.

Discussion 
 
This section will interpret the results, and both discuss what they mean, as well as 
try to explain the observed deviations from what was expected. It will go through the most 
important results, thereby either confirming or refuting the hypotheses. In the end, a general 
discussion will answer the research questions posed in the introduction. 
Hypothesis 1 and 2 
H1: Despite the contextual noise, the low anchor group will have a significantly lower perceived 
expected profit than the high anchor group, thereby validating the existence of an anchoring effect. 
 
H2: The consider-the-opposite method will have a mitigating effect on the anchoring effect. 
H2a: Compared to the normal condition, the consider-the-opposite method will in the low 
anchor group have an increasing effect on perceived expected profit. 
H2b: Compared to the normal condition, the consider-the-opposite method will in the high 
anchor group have a decreasing effect on perceived expected profit. 
 
The statistically significant association between anchor and perceived expected 
profit31, validates the existence of an anchoring effect, thereby confirming H1. The same 
association in the CtO condition showed a nonsignificant result32, indicating that a mitigation 
of the anchoring effect has taken place. An initial interpretation would therefore be to confirm 
H2. This interpretation though, becomes less conclusive when incorporating the results 
regarding H2a and H2b. As evident by comparing Figure 6 and 7 above, the CtO method 
had a decreasing effect on perceived expected profit in both the low and high anchor group. 
This is in accordance with H2b, but not with H2a. A visual representation of these results can 
be seen in Figure 17 below. Here, the percentage of below-answers is depicted in both 
conditions (see e.g. Figure 3 for comparison).

This relationship between condition and perceived expected profit, poses two similar, but yet 
different questions. Why did the CtO method increase the number of below-answers so 
much in both anchor groups?, and why did the CtO method not work as expected? 
 
In respect to the first question, the CtO method was only intended to focus on the 
inaccuracy of the anchor. But if this left the participants in a state where they would question 
the validity of all the information given in the experiment, it would probably make them less 
likely to be optimistic. An argument for this being the case could be found in that people are 
biased to believe (Gilbert, 1991). As Kahneman (2011, p. 80-81) explains it, a statement is 
initially and automatically believed by System 1. Only then can System 233 decide whether or 
not to unbelieve it. Since System 2 is generally lazy and require effort to work, the 
unbelieving does not always happen. This suggests that questioning the validity of the 
information in the experiment (unbelieving it), not necessarily happens, except when 
prompted to do so (e.g. by the CtO method). Furthermore, as touched upon in the section - 
the theoretical model, the focus on inaccuracy would likely also bring the participants 
attention to issues and risks that could be associated with a new product opportunity. This 
argument is supported by the following that looks at the interpretation of the ambiguous 
information incorporated in the experiment. In the normal condition, when not forced to 
deviate from the otherwise default heuristic of using a positive test strategy; motivation for 
self-enhancement34 argues that it simply feels good to think that positive events will happen. 
This suggests that the negative interpretation of the ambiguous information might not get 
much attention in this condition. In the CtO condition, the CtO method can, as discussed in

information, thereby reducing the otherwise normally present optimism. The above 
reasoning could explain the large increase in below-answers, and also why this increase

Before answering the second question, another result will firstly be discussed; 
namely that far-below and far-above answers35 only were found in the normal condition. As 
mentioned in the section - the design process, the anchor is expected to be recognized as 
uninformative, and therefore disregarded. In the normal condition, this might not happen 
since System 1 i biased to believe. In the CtO condition, the more thorough thought process 
might accomplish this. Also, conversational influence (Grice, 1975) in regards to the CtO 
method, could cause participants to infer that one should disregard the anchor. In either 
case, when the anchor is disregarded, the probability distribution would likely be interpreted 
as one of the few remaining informative parts; thereby increasing its importance. Relying 
more on the probability distribution in the CtO condition, could explain why no far-below and 
far-above answers were found. Another argument is found by looking at the interactions 
between System 1 and System 2. Firstly, impulses and intuition has been attributed to 
System 1. Secondly, System 2 has the power to overrule these impulses and intuitions 
(Kahneman, 2011). This interaction is likely what most participants experienced in the CRT. 
Here, the intuitive answers that automatically came to mind (System 1) were wrong. To 
answer correctly, they would have to overrule these impulses and apply a more thorough 
thought process (System 2). As Tversky & Kahneman (1974) describes, even individuals 
with intensive training in statistics, are prone to the same statistical biases as laymen, when 
they think intuitively. This suggests that the extreme answers in the normal condition could 
be due to participants relying more on impulses and intuition, e.g. generated by the anchor. 
The more thorough thought process caused by the CtO method, thus also explains why no 
extreme answers were found in this condition. 
 
Back to the second question of why the CtO method did not work as expected. Two 
explanations are likely candidates for an answer. The first explanation argues that the 
observed anchoring effect was not caused by semantic anchoring. If this is the case, then it 
would make sense that the CtO method did not work as expected, since it here specifically is 
a de-biasing method for semantic anchoring. In the section - the design process, two

requirements were mentioned as necessary for causing a semantic anchoring effect. Prior 
knowledge about a new product opportunity and launch was the first. It was argued that both 
consumer and employee experience would account for this prior knowledge. It is though 
possible that the lack of personal experience with product opportunities, as well as 
knowledge of the process from idea to launch; could keep the amount of generated semantic 
anchor-consistent knowledge at a rather low level. The second requirement was that the 
participants would have to understand the fictitious situation to a point where they 
automatically would understand whether the initial profit forecast was low or high, as to 
generate semantic knowledge consistent with this understanding. The key word here, is 
automatically. The reason being that semantic anchoring occurs in System 1, which 
operates automatically. If this level of understanding is not achieved; e.g. due to the 
participants not remembering what the success criteria cut-off value was, and therefore 
would have to reread it after perceiving the initial profit forecast; there would be no engine for 
semantic anchoring. Since many of the participants probably only read the success criteria 
cut-off value once, it is especially likely that this second requirement was violated.  
 
If this is the case, what then accounts for the observed anchoring effect? Basic 
anchoring could be the answer. As discussed in the literature review, in a real-life 
information-rich setting, equivalent to the one this experiment is aiming to simulate, purely 
numeric influences would likely not be strong enough to survive the contextual noise and 
cause an anchoring effect. But since the business case analysis was created on a 
conceptual basis without values, the only actual numeric values were the success criteria 
cut-off value, and the initial profit forecast. Furthermore, the experiment only lasted half an 
hour, thereby not leaving much time to increase the accessibility of other externally 
perceived numeric values. This suggests that basic anchoring could account for the 
observed anchoring effect. Besides this, as mentioned in the literature review, Critcher and 
Gilovich (2008, Study 3) suggested that a more thorough thought process was what caused 
the mitigation in their otherwise observed basic anchoring effect. This means that the more 
thorough thought process caused by the CtO method, could explain why a mitigation of the 
anchoring effect was observed going from the normal to the CtO condition. 
 
The second explanation for why the CtO method did not work as expected, is that it 
might not have been applied correctly. According to theory, for the CtO method to work as a 
de-biasing method for semantic anchoring, it should generate semantic anchor-inconsistent 
knowledge. Therefore, as Mussweiler et al. (2000) states, considering reasons why the 
implication of the anchor may be wrong, might constitute such a strategy. As described 
earlier, the formulation of the CtO question was inspired by their question: “What would you 
say argues against this price?”, which lead to: “Think about reasons that argues against that 
==PAGE:54==
 
53 
the just mentioned initial profit forecast is correct.” There are though one key difference 
between this experiment and their study. When the car expert, in their study, got asked the 
CtO question, he had the car in front of him. This means that he would look at the car and 
find evidence for it being in better or worse condition than the anchor would imply. In other 
words, it would be the implications of the anchor that were found evidence against, as theory 
suggests. In this experiment, the quite similar CtO question would not have the same effect. 
The reason being that here, the participants did not have the implications of the anchor in 
front of them, as the car expert had with the car. Here, the product has not yet been 
developed, which means that there are no employee statements of how the development 
process went, no sales numbers, no customer statements about owning the product, and so 
forth. As with the car, this kind of evidence would have been something one could have 
looked at to generate reasons arguing against the implications of the initial profit forecast. 
This evidence was though not present, which meant that, “Think about reasons that argues 
against that the just mentioned initial profit forecast is correct.”, simply lead to the 
participants questioning the validity of the forecast. Thus, it can be questioned whether or 
not semantic anchor-inconsistent knowledge has been generated in accordance with how 
theory suggests it.  
If the second explanation is the correct answer to why the CtO method did not work 
as expected, then the observed anchoring effect could still have been caused by semantic 
anchoring and not basic anchoring, as the first explanation concluded. Which explanation is 
correct, and thereby whether the observed anchoring effect is caused by semantic 
anchoring, basic anchoring, or a combination, remains an unanswered question. 
In total, H1 is confirmed, but with no confirmation on which anchoring mechanism is 
in play. H2, H2a and H2b can neither be confirmed nor refuted, since the CtO method either 
might have been applied to counteract basic anchoring, or might have been applied wrong 
entirely.  
Hypothesis 3a and 3b 
H3a: Perceived expected profit above the success criteria cut-off value, will correlate positively with 
willingness to start the project. 
H3b: Perceived expected profit below the success criteria cut-off value, will correlate negatively with 
willingness to start the project. 
 
Table 2 above shows that 51% of the participants that answered below, also 
answered no-go; where 90% of the participants that answered above, also answered go. An 
initial interpretation would be that H3a is confirmed and H3b is refuted. Even though the 
==PAGE:55==
 
54 
go/no-go decision in this experiment was simplified to a judgement regarding profit, where a 
clear success criteria cut-off value was set, the data shows that several other factors was 
considered. These factors were mostly interpretations of the information given in the 
experiment, like: diversifying by opening up for a new market, high level of company 
experience, the new patent, big variation in what the customers were willing to pay, etc. As is 
also evident in Table 4 above, by far the most of these factors argued for a go rather than a 
no-go decision; which is consistent with what could be expected due to the optimism bias, as 
discussed in the literature review. The consequence of this is the following. If answering 
below, there are still a lot of other factors that argues for a go decision, which could explain 
why 49% of the below-answers, answered go anyways. If answering above, the many other 
factors also arguing for a go decision, could likewise explain why only 10% of above-
answers, switched to no-go.  
Even though H3a and H3b technically are respectively confirmed and refuted, the 
above indicates that this conclusion is situational. In other words, in a slightly different 
version of the fictitious situation, containing more factors arguing for a no-go rather than a go 
decision, it might have been the reverse conclusion. Therefore this is not an informative or 
useful result. 
Anchor vs. decision 
 
Whether the anchoring effect observed in the normal condition was caused by 
semantic anchoring, basic anchoring or a combination, it was not strong enough to influence 
the willingness to start the project. This result was quite clear since all p values were greater 
than .8. The discussion just above could likely also explain this. Even though the 
participants’ perception of expected profit were affected by the anchor, the decision of 
whether to start the project or not, is a complex decision containing many factors to consider; 
thereby reducing the sole influence of the otherwise biased perception of expected profit. 
Condition vs. decision 
As opposed to the anchor, the condition had a statistically significant association with 
the decision. Compared to the normal condition, when the CtO method was applied, 26% 
more of the total answers were no-go. A likely explanation for this result is found by 
continuing the discussion from above, about why the CtO method increased the number of 
below-answers so much in both anchor groups. As argued, the CtO method, as applied in 
this experiment, would likely cause the participants to use a more thorough thought process, 
question the validity of the given information, and probably also bring their attention to 
possible risks associated with a new product opportunity. The likely consequence of this is 
==PAGE:56==
 
55 
that the risk perception in the CtO condition would be higher than in the normal condition, 
which would explain the large increase in no-go answers (Keil et al., 2000). 
As would now be evident, the CtO method varies depending on the specific 
circumstances of its use. As a de-biasing method for semantic anchoring, the aim is to 
consider reasons why the implications of the anchor may be wrong. In this experiment, it 
induced participants to consider reasons that argue against the validity of an information. In 
Koriat et al. (1980) it was used as a de-biasing method for overconfidence, by prompting 
participants for reasons that their answers might be wrong. What these instances have in 
common, is that the CtO method was used as a de-biasing method for the consequences of 
that System 1 is biased to believe and confirm. In the remainder of this paper, the CtO 
method denotes a de-biasing method to counteract these two biases36. 
 
The question then becomes: are the results in the CtO condition more accurate 
than in the normal condition?; or more broadly, does the CtO method always improve 
judgement? Naturally, the answer to the last question would be no; the CtO method does not 
inevitably improve judgement (Mussweiler et al., 2000). The reason being that the CtO 
method could distort a previously accurate judgement. That being said, adopting the 
reductionist’s view, the logical premise for an optimal decision, is complete information and 
certainty (Geraldi and Stingl, 2016). Of course this cannot be achieved in real-life, but 
nevertheless it suggests that the more information available, the better the judgement. This 
is also in agreement with the common advice of looking at reason both for and against a 
choice, before making a decision. Since System 1 is biased to believe and confirm, thereby 
automatically using a positive test strategy; it makes sense that incorporating a negative test 
strategy as well, to test more widely; should improve judgement. Since the CtO method can 
be seen as such, the above reasoning suggests that its systematic use, generally improves 
judgement. 
Risk propensity vs. decision 
 
As mentioned in the section - the design process, the reason risk propensity was 
included as a control variable, was to validate the findings reported in Keil et al. (2000), 
which suggested that participants with high risk propensity, would be more willing to make a 
go decision. Figure 15 and 16 above shows that no participants with high risk propensity 
chose no-go in the normal condition, whereas some in the CtO condition did. At least in the

General discussion 
RQ1: What effect does an anchor regarding the expected profit of a project have on the perceived 
expected profit? 
RQ1a: What effect does this anchor have on the willingness to start the project? 
 
RQ2: Does the consider-the-opposite method mitigate the anchoring effect? 
RQ2a: What effect does the consider-the-opposite method have on both the perceived

All and all, the above discussion provides the necessary information to answer the 
research questions, which are recapped above. The confirmation of H1 validates the 
existence of an anchoring effect caused by the perception of an initial profit forecast. This 
answers RQ1. The answer to RQ1a is that the anchor did not have an impact on the 
willingness to start the project. That being said, it has to be stated that even though the 
anchoring effect usually is transferrable from an experimental to a real-life setting, as well as 
being independent of expertise; the anchoring effect would likely be stronger for a decision 
maker in a real-life situation, equivalent to the one in this study. The reason is found in that 
the arguments against a semantic anchoring effect being caused in this experiment (see 
discussion section - hypothesis 1 and 2, above), would not be present in a real-life situation. 
Therefore, the theoretical claim that semantic anchoring effects are strong enough to survive 
the contextual noise in such a situation, thereby influencing the go/no-go decision; still needs 
to be tested empirically.  
 
The CtO method did not work as expected, which suggested that it either might

discussed above, when interpreting the CtO method as a de-biasing method to counteract

Contribution to practice 
 
In their popular book: Why Smart People Make Big Money Mistakes – and How to 
Correct Them, Belsky and Gilovich (1999) warn people that they may be prone to 
confirmation biases and anchoring. Several common recommendations of how to avoid 
these cognitive biases are offered, like: doing more thorough research before making 
important decisions, or by broadening the board of advisors (as cited through Furnham and 
Boo, 2011). The most important advice though, is that they urge people to show a little more 
humility when it comes to one’s own judgement. The tough realization that we are not the 
paragons of reason we assume ourselves to be, is the first step in avoiding significant errors 
of judgement.  
 
The CtO method, as discussed above, is suggested as a de-biasing method for the 
consequences of that System 1 is biased to believe and confirm. Some of these 
consequences are semantic anchoring, overconfidence (Koriat et al., 1980), and that people 
in general pay more attention to what a statement means, rather than its origin and thereby 
its validity (Kahneman, 2011, p. 114). Due to the variations in consequences, there are also 
variations in when to apply the CtO method, and how to do it. In respect to semantic 
anchoring, it should be applied as soon as a numerical value pertaining a subsequent 
decision is perceived. How to apply it; is to consider reasons why the implications of the 
numeric value may be wrong. In the case of overconfidence, it should be applied in the 
moment of deliberation regarding either answering a question or generating an opinion. How 
to apply it; is to ask yourself for arguments that indicate that your answer or opinion is 
incorrect. In respect to that System 1 is biased to believe, the CtO method should be applied 
when being presented with an important piece of information38. How to apply it; is to question 
the origin and thereby validity of the information, e.g. pay attention to its informativeness and 
the sample size behind its generation39.  
By applying the CtO method as just explained, data suggests that it will enable 
decision makers in project contexts to be more resilient towards being biased by both 
inaccurate information and numeric values (forecasts); as well as to mitigate the general 
tendency to be overconfident. Therefore, when the CtO method is understood as a de-
biasing method to counteract that System 1 is biased to believe and confirm, then its 
systematic use generally should improve judgement.

In a broader sense, since System 1 cannot be turned off, errors of intuitive thought 
are difficult to prevent. Firstly, because System 2 may not be aware of the error happening. 
Secondly, if it did, it would require System 2 to step in an overrule System 1, which requires 
effort and attention that may not be available in our limited budget. Due to the slow and 
inefficient workings of System 2, constantly monitoring and evaluating every suggestion from 
System 1 would be an impractical and straining way to live. As Kahneman (2011, p. 28) puts 
it: “the best we can do is a compromise: learn to recognize situations in which mistakes are 
likely and try harder to avoid significant mistakes when the stakes are high”. In other words; 
familiarize yourself with the most common and powerful cognitive biases40, as to have a 
chance of preventing their effect when the stakes are high; e.g. when deciding whether to 
start a project or not.

Reflections 
 
This section will go through the most important limitations of the current study, as 
well as how these could be eliminated in an alternative operationalization of the experiment. 
Finally, areas of further research will be suggested. 
Limitations of the experiment 
 
As mentioned earlier, Englich et al. (2006) suggested around 40 participants in 
each group to make their analysis possible. Jani (2010) argued that the sample size in their 
two groups, 36 and 35, might have been too small to pick up the differences between 
students and experienced project managers. In this experiment, after cleaning the data, the 
sample size in the four groups ended up being 25, 30, 21 and 20, which was not ideal. Even 
though most of the statistical analyses met their respective cell counts, the small sample size 
is a limitation. This is both in respects to restricting the number of suitable statistical tests, as 
well as the possibility of not having sufficient power to pick up differences that might have 
been there. 
 
In retrospect, it could have been interesting to have incorporated answering time as 
a control variable. Firstly, because a low answering time could have indicated that the crucial 
information about both success criteria and anchor was not properly understood. Secondly, 
because a longer answering time could have indicated a more thorough thought process, 
which might have changed the influence of e.g. risk perception or the anchor itself.  
 
Another limitation is found in that the experiment was not calibrated well enough to 
center the go/no-go decision in the normal condition around 50/50. Since this was heavily 
skewed towards a go decision, it is possible that an anchoring effect caused by the low 
anchor could have been trumped, and therefore not detected. As mentioned earlier, this 
calibration was attempted by moving the mean of the probability distribution, so that the 
likelihood of a negative profit increased. Having had more iterations of pilot testing would 
likely have solved this issue. That being said, investigating the effects of the CtO method 
was still possible, since it only required the difference between the conditions. 
 
In respect to measuring the anchoring effect, the biggest limitation of this 
experiment is that the mean value was represented in both intervals on either side of it. As 
can be seen in Appendix 3, those two intervals were respectively: 1,000,000 to 1,500,000; 
and 1,500,000 to 2,000,000. This means that if a participant had a perceived expected profit 
of exactly the mean; either interval could be just as good as the other. The consequence of 
this is that there theoretically are no ways of separating the just below-answers from the just 
above-answers, which means that there are no ways of detecting an anchoring effect. The 
==PAGE:62==
 
61 
conclusion of H1 should therefore, at least theoretically, have been inconclusive. A way to 
have solved this issue would have been to change the interval-ends close to the mean, to 
respectively 1,499,999 and 1,500,001. This would though have excluded the mean value 
completely. Therefore, asking for the perceived expected profit on a continuous scale would 
have been the right solution. But as discussed in the section - the design process, time 
constraints lead to a conceptual design of the business case analysis without values, which 
further lead to not choosing a continuous scale. 
 
In retrospect, aiming to simulate a real-life decision regarding whether or not to 
seize a product opportunity might have been too complex. In itself, the idea about creating 
an experimental environment as close to the real-life situation as possible, made sense. The 
fault is found in mainly two areas. First, the time constraints prohibited a perhaps thorough 
enough introduction to the fictitious situation. As discussed earlier, this might have violated 
the requirements for causing a semantic anchoring effect, which was the aim for this study. 
Second, a go/no-go decision regarding a new product opportunity is a decision that has 
many other factors to consider than just profit. These many factors, made it difficult to isolate 
how the perception of expected profit influenced the decision, which was also evident in that 
26% of the participants disregarded the success criteria. A fictitious situation that still had 
kept the level of contextual noise high, but with fewer factors influencing the final decision, 
would have been preferable. Requirements and suggestions for an alternative 
operationalization will briefly be discussed in the section below. 
Alternative operationalization  
 
In an alternative operationalization, most of the design choices would still be kept 
unchanged, as they are made on a theoretical basis. The focus will be to make sure that the 
limitations discussed above, will not be replicated. Therefore, a larger sample size, 
incorporating answering time as a control variable, and having several iterations of pilot 
testing, would be improvements to make. Having the answers on a continuous scale would 
likewise be important. In regards to the go/no-go decision, which by its nature has a 
dichotomous answer, a transformation is needed to accomplish this. Inspired by Jani (2010), 
this can be done in two steps. First, ask the go/no-go question as before. Second, have a 
subsequent question asking participants to indicate, by selecting a number between 0 and 
100, how confident they are in their decision41. By coding the go/no-go decision as (+1/−1) 
and multiplying this with the selected number, a continuous scale ranging from −100 to +100 
would have been created.

While the above improvements are all important, making sure that the requirements 
for causing a semantic anchoring effect are followed is the most essential of all. As 
discussed earlier, these requirements are firstly that the fictitious situation needs to be about 
a type of project of which the participants have prior knowledge; and secondly to ensure that 
the participants understand this fictitious situation completely. Furthermore, as mentioned 
just above, the fictitious situation should preferably have fewer factors influencing the go/no-
go decision than a new product opportunity has. A suggestion for an improved alternative 
operationalization could be a go/no-go decision about employee training. This fictitious 
situation would be much simpler to understand. It would also mostly exclude the influence of 
external factors (like: competitor movements, opening up for new markets, patents, etc.) and 
simply focus on internal factors, e.g. the performance of a single department. Thereby, the 
situation would be kept simple enough to incorporate numeric values in the business case 
analysis, while still complying with the time constraints of the experiment; which would open 
up for the possibility of allowing the answers on a continuous scale. Furthermore, having the 
fictitious situation centered around a decision about employee training (education), would 
ensure that it would be closer to the reality of the participants (the students).  
Whether or not this alternative operationalization would cause a semantic anchoring 
effect; is hard to say. In a more general sense, within the time constraints of this 
experiment42, it might not be possible to get participants to understand any fictitious situation 
enough to cause a semantic anchoring effect. If this is the case, a more elaborate 
experiment would be needed. 
Further research 
 
As stated in the section - general discussion, the anchoring effect would likely be 
stronger for a decision maker in a real-life situation, equivalent to the one in this study. 
Therefore, the theoretical claim that semantic anchoring effects are strong enough to survive 
the contextual noise in such a situation, thereby influencing the go/no-go decision; still needs 
to be tested empirically. This is suggested as further research. 
 
Anchoring effects caused by semantic anchoring have proven to persevere over a 
duration of at least a week, without diminishing in magnitude (Mussweiler, 2001). Since a 
week in a project context is a rather short period of time, further research could aim at 
determining the temporal boundaries of semantic anchoring effects.

Refraining from expressing numeric values would eliminate anchoring effects 
completely. Therefore, urging employees not to make e.g. uninformed profit forecasts, would 
be a step in the right direction. That being said, numeric values are such a big part of the 
communication in projects that they cannot be avoided. But what if the language around how 
numeric values are expressed, changed. As also suggested by Geraldi and Stingl (2016), 
research could explore whether probabilities or ranges of values would cause the same 
anchoring effects as a single numeric value. An example of this could be to explore whether 
expressing a profit forecast as a confidence interval, would have a different effect than only 
expressing the mean value. 
 
Finally, future research is urged to empirically test the theoretical claim that the 
systematic use of the CtO method; used as a de-biasing method for that System 1 is biased 
to believe and confirm; generally should improve judgement.

Conclusion 
 
There have only been a few anchoring studies that has a real-life information-rich 
setting, combined with the anchor simply being in the midst of the information, and not 
emphasized by drawing attention to it. The current study aimed at simulating exactly this, 
where the anchor was an initial profit forecast of a possible new project, that was perceived 
by a decision maker before any analytical information was available about the project.  
It was found that this anchor affected the decision maker’s perception of expected 
profit, thereby confirming the existence of an anchoring effect. It was further found that this 
anchoring effect was not strong enough to affect the subsequent go/no-go decision, as no 
statistical significant association between anchor and decision were found. 
In addition, it was investigated whether the CtO method mitigated the anchoring 
effect; and more specifically how it affected the perception of expected profit, as well as the 
willingness to start the project. The results indicate a mitigation, but not as expected. 
Surprisingly, the CtO method had a noteworthy decreasing effect on perceived expected 
profit in both the low and high anchor group. Furthermore, as well in both anchor groups, this 
method markedly reduced the number of decision makers that were willing to start the 
project. A discussion of the reasons behind these results concluded that the CtO method 
could be seen as a negative test strategy to be used as a de-biasing method for the 
consequences of that System 1 is biased to believe and confirm.  
Adopting the reductionist’s view: that the logical premise for an optimal decision is 
complete information and certainty; it was concluded that the systematic use of the CtO 
method, generally should improve judgement. In other words, since System 1 is biased to 
believe and confirm, thereby automatically using a positive test strategy; it makes sense that 
incorporating a negative test strategy as well, to test more widely; should improve 
judgement.  
It is suspected that the anchoring effect would be stronger for a decision maker in a real-life 
situation, equivalent to the one in this study. Therefore, the theoretical claim that anchoring 
effects are strong enough to survive the contextual noise in such a situation, thereby 
influencing the go/no-go decision; still needs to be tested empirically. This is suggested as 
further research. Furthermore, since the anchoring effect up till now only has been verified 
lasting a week, which is a rather short period of time seen in a project context; research 
could also explore the temporal boundaries of the anchoring effect. Finally, future research is 
urged to empirically test the theoretical claim that the systematic use of the CtO method; 
==PAGE:66==
 
65 
used as a de-biasing method for that System 1 is biased to believe and confirm; generally 
should improve judgement.

Appendix 5 – System 1 and System 2 
Please take a quick look at the following 3 questions, and provide an initial answer 
to them before reading on. 
 
Source: [10] 
The two ways of thinking 
To observe your mind in automatic mode, take look at Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2: Source: [11] 
Many things simultaneously happened here. Just as easy as you noticed that the man's hair 
is black on the top and grey on the sides, you knew that he was scared. What further came 
to mind was probably thoughts about what he is looking at. If measured, it is also likely that 
==PAGE:85==
 
84 
your heart rate increased. You did not intend to assess his mood, or identify possible 
reasons for why he got this frightened, it just happened to you. It was an instance of fast 
thinking[12]. 
 
Now consider the following math problem, and try to solve it. 
13 x 27 
You recognized immediately that this is a multiplication problem. You might also have had 
the feeling that you would be able to solve it, if given some time. If asked whether the 
answer could be 57 or 3200, you would fast and intuitively know that this was not the case. 
On the other hand, if asked the same with 332, a fast and intuitive acceptance or rejection 
would probably not come to mind. To solve it, you would have to slow down your thinking, try 
to recall the rules of multiplication you learned in school, and apply them. Either if you solved 
it, or gave up (the answer is 351), this was a deliberate mental process that required effort 
and attention. You just experienced a prototype of slow thinking.[12] 
Psychologists have for decades been interested in the two ways of thinking, fast 
and slow. Keith Stanovich and Richard West defined the terms System 1 and System 2 for 
respectively fast and slow thinking,[12] which will be used onwards, and described further in 
the sections below. 
System 1 
System 1 operates quickly and automatically, with little or no effort, and with no 
sense of voluntary control. We are born prepared to observe and perceive the world around 
us, orient attention, recognize objects, and avoid losses.[12] This is an innate skill humans 
share with other animals. As we observe the world around us, System 1 automatically 
assesses the new information according to the mental model of the world already created 
from previous experiences. If something differs, our mental model evolves, and is 
continuously updated according to the new information. Putting your hand into the flame of a 
candle, hurts. That information is stored, and accessed without intention and effort,[12] which 
will probably result in you not doing it again. Other activities become fast and automatic 
through prolonged practice, e.g. how to gear-shift when driving a car, or understanding 
nuances of social interaction. 
 
 
==PAGE:86==
 
85 
Below is a list of some examples of automatic activities attributed to System 1, as well 
as a list of some of its limitations:[12] 
● 
Answer the question 2 + 2 = ? 
● 
Detect that one object is more distant than another. 
● 
Orient to the source of a sudden sound. 
● 
Reading simple words (in a language you understand; and, if learned to read) 
● 
Understand simple sentences. 
● 
E.g. “Try not to think of an apple”. 
● 
Recognize common facial expressions. 
● 
Associate “capital of France” with “Paris” (for most people). 
Limitations: 
● 
It cannot be turned off. 
● 
Has little understanding of logic and statistics. 
● 
Gullible and biased to believe and confirm. 
● 
Generates a (too) coherent model of the world. 
● 
Focuses on existing evidence and ignores absent evidence (WYSIATI). 
● 
Exaggerates emotional consistency (halo effect). 
● 
Computes more than intended (mental shotgun).∗ 
● 
∗Will be explained later. 
System 2 
System 2 is the conscious, reasoning self. It allocates attention to effortful mental 
activities, like the multiplication problem above; makes choices; decides what to think about 
and what to do; along with having the ability (which System 1 does not) to construct thoughts 
in an orderly series of steps.[12] It is also in charge of doubting and logical reasoning. 
Below is a list of some of the activities attributed to System 2:[12] 
● 
Check the validity of a complex argument. 
● 
Brace for the starter gun in a race. 
● 
Look for a man with grey hair. 
● 
Focus attention on the passes of the ball in a basketball game. 
● 
Maintain a faster walking speed than is natural for you. 
● 
Focus on the voice of a particular person in a crowded and noisy room. 
● 
Compare two cars for overall value. 
==PAGE:87==
 
86 
The highly diverse operations of System 2 have one thing in common: they all 
require attention and effort. We all have a limited budget of attention, and if adequate 
attention is not given to a specific activity, you will perform less well, or simply not be able to 
perform the activity at all.[12] This is why the phrase “pay attention” is apt. Said in another 
way, when your budget of attention is used, you are effectively blind to anything else. A great 
demonstration of this, is The Invisible Gorilla.[13] In short, participants were shown a video of 
a basketball court where two teams were passing the ball to their teammates. One team with 
white shirts and the other with black. Before the video started the participants were told to 
count the number of passes made by the white team, ignoring the black players. During the 
video a woman in a gorilla suit appears for 9 seconds before moving on. Of the many 
thousands of people that participated, only about half noticed anything unusual. This 
illustrates two important facts of the mind. We can be blind to the obvious, and we are blind 
to our blindness. 
Besides the limited budget of attention, System 2 has another limitation, which is 
that effortful activities interfere with one another.[12] This is the reason you should not try to 
calculate 13 x 27, while making a right turn in your car crossing a busy bike lane; or why you 
automatically stops your conversation with the passenger in the car, when a dangerous 
situation occurs in the traffic. 
Below is a list of some of the limitations of System 2: 
● 
Requires effort and attention to work. 
● 
Has a limited budget of attention to allocate. 
● 
When your budget of attention is used, you are effectively blind to anything else. 
● 
Effortful activities interferes with each other. 
● 
Has low efficiency (slow thinking). 
● 
Generally lazy, following the rule of least effort. 
● 
Interactions Between the Two Systems 
When we are awake, both systems are running simultaneously. System 1 runs 
automatically and System 2 is normally running in a low-effort mode, in which only a small 
part of its capacity is used. As described above, System 1 is continuously using its model of 
the world to assess incoming information. It generates suggestions for System 2: 
impressions, intuitions, intentions, and feelings. If endorsed by System 2, impressions and 
intuitions turn into beliefs, and impulses turn into voluntary actions.[14] Most of the time this 
process runs smoothly, and System 2 believes and adopts these suggestions and acts on 
them, which is fine - usually. 
==PAGE:88==
 
87 
When System 1 runs into situations where no apparent answer comes intuitively to 
mind, it calls to System 2 for help. This is what you experienced with 13 x 27. Other 
situations where the two systems works well together, are when System 1 gets surprised. 
When it observes something that does not fit with its model of the world, System 2 is 
activated to search through your memory to make sense of it. This would happen if you saw 
a flying car, heard a cat bark, or saw a gorilla walk across a basketball court. System 2 also 
continuously monitor and controls your own behaviour,[12] which e.g. comes in handy when 
System 1 sends impulses to System 2, that if acted upon, would result in behaviour that is 
not socially acceptable; like always saying the first thing that comes to mind. In other words, 
System 2 has the power to overrule the freewheeling impulses, intuitions, and feelings of 
System 1. 
This division of labour is highly efficient. It minimizes effort, and optimizes 
performance.[12] In general, this constellation works well because System 1 is very good at 
what it does. Its model of the world is usually accurate; and its short-term predictions of the 
future, and the suggestions for System 2 that follows, are ordinarily appropriate. As we shall 
see though, System 1 has biases, and is prone to systematic errors in specific 
circumstances. 
Conflict and illusions 
Figure 3 is a variant of many similar experiments that induces conflict between 
System 1 and System 2. Before reading on, try to say (out loud, or whispering) the colour of 
the words. 
 
Figure 3: Conflict between System 1 and 2.Source: Say the colour of the words 
==PAGE:89==
 
88 
The task itself is a conscious logical task solvable by “programming” System 2 to look for, 
and say the colour of the words. System 1 however, interferes. Recall from earlier that it 
cannot be turned off, and that you cannot refrain from reading and understanding simple 
words. It happens automatically, without conscious control, and is the reason for the 
conflict.[12] This is the effect of the “mental shotgun” mentioned above; we computes more 
than intended. 
 
Now, have a look at Figure 4. 
 
Figure 4: Müller-Lyer. Source: [15] 
At a first glance, nothing abnormal is apparent about this picture. We all see that the bottom 
horizontal line is longer than the one above, and we believe what we see. This, however, is 
the Müller-Lyer illusion, and measurements will confirm that they are equally long. The 
remarkable thing is that even now, when System 2 is aware of this fact, we still see the lines 
as having different length. 
A similar illusion is present at Figure 5, where you are asked: as shown on the 
screen, is the figure on the right larger than the figure on the left? 
==PAGE:90==
 
89 
 
Figure 5: 3-D illusion. Source: [16] 
The obvious and intuitive answer, yes; is wrong. They are of equal size. Remember from 
above that one of the automatic functions of System 1 is to detect whether one object is 
more distant than another. Even though we are looking at a picture on a flat 2-D screen, 
System 1 interprets it as a 3-D scene, and sees the right figure as larger. Again, even after 
we know this, we still get tricked by System 1. 
Is prevention possible? 
Visual illusions as the ones shown above are not the only way to get tricked. 
Illusions of thought - cognitive illusions - also exists, where the emphasis here is your 
thoughts and feelings getting tricked.[12] Knowing that these illusions exist, what can we do to 
overcome them? 
Since System 1 cannot be turned off, errors of intuitive thought are difficult to 
prevent. Firstly, because System 2 may not be aware of the error happening. Secondly, if it 
did, it would require System 2 to step in an overrule System 1, which requires effort and 
attention that may not be available in our limited budget. Constantly monitoring and 
evaluating every suggestion from System 1 would be an impractical and straining way to 
live, due to the slow and inefficient workings of System 2. As Kahneman[17] puts it: the best 
we can do is a compromise: learn to recognize situations in which mistakes are likely and try 
harder to avoid significant mistakes when the stakes are high. In other words; familiarize 
==PAGE:91==
 
90 
yourself with the most common and powerful cognitive biases, so you have a chance of 
preventing their effect when the stakes are high; e.g. when a project manager continuously 
is facing high-risk decisions in a complex dynamic project. 
The Cognitive Reflection Test 
One thing is to train/”program” System 2 to become aware of, and react when cues 
of cognitive biases are present. Another is to figure out whether you are more 
predispositioned to get caught by cognitive biases than others. To investigate this, Shane 
Frederick constructed the cognitive reflection test. This test consists of three questions that 
all have intuitive answers that are both compelling and wrong. It starts with “the bat and ball”-
problem, and is the test you took above. Frederick’s found that people that scores low in this 
test, or tests with a similar type of questions, are more prone to trust the first idea that comes 
to mind. Put in another way, the supervisory function of System 2 is weak or lazy in these 
people; meaning that they are unwilling to invest the effort needed to validate their intuition; 
and are therefore also more susceptible to blindly follow other suggestions from System 
1.[12] This “weak supervisory function of System 2” is also known as “lazy thinking”. Following 
quote sums up the essential: 
“System 1 is impulsive and intuitive; System 2 is capable of reasoning, and it is 
cautious, but at least for some people it is also lazy. We recognize related differences 
among individuals: some people are more like their System 2; others are closer to their 
System 1. This simple test has emerged as one of the better predictors of lazy thinking”.[18] 
So to figure out whether you are more predispositioned to get caught by cognitive 
biases than others; compare your test results with the correct answers below, and ask 
yourself this: are you more like your System 1 or your System 2? 
 
The correct answers are: 5 cents, 5 minutes, and 47 days.

Hypothesis 3a and 3b 
● H3a 
○ Perceived expected profit above the success criteria cut-off value, will 
correlate positively with willingness to start the project. 
● H3b 
○ Perceived expected profit below the success criteria cut-off value, will 
correlate negatively with willingness to start the project. 
 
To test these hypotheses individually with a Chi-square goodness-of-fit test, as was 
done with hypothesis 2a and 2b, would require an expected distribution between no-go and 
go in each of the events of answering below or above. Since this expected distribution is not 
available, the two hypotheses are tested together via a Chi-square test of independence, 
where the focus is on the probability of respectively choosing no-go when below was 
chosen, and choosing go when above was chosen. The Chi-square test of independence is 
done controlling for condition, due to there being a statistically significant association 
between condition and decision, as will be shown later.
