Liposomal Delivery System for the Endogenous Opioid Neuropeptide Endomorphin-1 
IV
Abstract 
Background: Endomorphin-1 (EM1) is an endogenous μ-opioid receptor (MOR) agonist with high 
selectivity and potency, but its clinical translation has been hindered by rapid enzymatic 
degradation and poor blood–brain barrier (BBB) permeability. Liposomal encapsulation offers a 
potential strategy to improve EM1’s stability and bioavailability for systemic use. 
Aim: This thesis aimed to develop and evaluate a liposomal formulation of native EM1 for 
subcutaneous 
administration, 
focusing 
on 
encapsulation 
efficiency, 
physicochemical 
characterization, and in vivo analgesic efficacy in a rat model of pain. 
Methods: Endomorphin-1 was formulated into stealth liposomes by remote loading using an 
ammonium sulphate gradient. Multiple formulation parameters were optimized, including EM1 
concentration, loading temperature, and dialysis conditions. Liposomes were characterized for 
size, polydispersity index (PDI), drug loading, and release kinetics. Antinociceptive effects were 
assessed using the Von Frey test in rats following subcutaneous administration of 0.50 mg/kg 
liposomal formulated EM1. 
Results: The optimized formulation achieved moderate encapsulation efficiency (~45%) and 
consistent and narrow size distribution (~130 nm, PDI <0.06), stable across batches. In vivo, 
liposomal EM1 induced a significant increase in pain threshold, peaking at 60 minutes and 
declining by 24 hours. Free EM1 had minimal effect, confirming the enhanced bioavailability 
provided by encapsulation. In vitro release kinetics did not fully predict in vivo behaviour, 
underscoring the need for more physiologically relevant models. 
Conclusion: This study provides proof-of-concept that native EM1 can be formulated into a viable 
systemic therapy through stealth liposomal encapsulation. The formulation enabled delivery of a 
pharmacologically active dose with a favourable safety profile and reproducible batch 
characteristics. These findings support further development of liposomal neuropeptide delivery 
systems for opioid-based analgesia.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Pain 
1.1.1 A Universal Feeling 
Pain is a fundamental biological signal and a universally experienced phenomenon. It serves a 
protective function by alerting the organism to potential harm or injury. In 2020, the International 
Association for the Study of Pain refined its definition to include both the sensory and emotional 
components of pain, emphasizing its complexity and subjectivity (Raja et al., 2020). However, 
when pain becomes chronic (persisting for more than three months) it is no longer adaptive and 
instead becomes a disease state in itself (Groh et al., 2018). Chronic pain affects over 30% of 
people globally (Cohen et al., 2021), and is associated with significant declines in quality of life, 
increased disability, and comorbidities such as insomnia, anxiety, depression, and 
immunosuppression (Aaron et al., 2025; J. Liu et al., 2024; Rikard et al., 2023; Tanguay-Sabourin 
et al., 2023). Despite its prevalence, chronic pain remains difficult to treat effectively. Existing 
analgesics often provide insufficient relief, carry substantial side effects, or risk dependence and 
addiction, particularly in the case of opioids (Mitsi & Zachariou, 2016). 
To address these challenges, it is essential to first understand the mechanisms that underlie pain 
perception and the various types of pain that patients may experience.  
 
1.1.2 The Pain Signalling Pathway 
Pain begins when specialized sensory neurons (nociceptors) detect thermal, mechanical or 
chemical tissue-damaging (noxious) stimuli exceeding the threshold for activation. Ion channels 
on C-fibre nociceptors opens in response to noxious stimuli, allowing influx of Na+ ions, increasing 
the intracellular voltage and initiating depolarization. Voltage-gated sodium channels then 
propagate the depolarization as an action potential along the afferent fibre, transporting the 
nociception signal along the axon to the terminal. Two main classes of nociceptive fibres carry 
these signals: myelinated Aδ-fibres which mediate the fast sharp, well-localized pain, while 
unmyelinated C-fibres convey the slower burning, dull pain (Dubin & Patapoutian, 2010; Karcz et 
al., 2024).  
In the spinal cord, the first-order nociceptors enter the dorsal horn (spinal grey) and synapse onto 
second-order neurons. At these synapses the action potential is translated to a chemical signal 
in form of neurotransmitters, that are released from dense core vesicles i the first-order neuron 
into the synaptic cleft and continue the signal by binding receptors on the second-order neurons 
in the dorsal-horn. Glutamate is the principal excitatory neurotransmitter and rapidly activates 
postsynaptic on second-order neurons. In addition, the neuropeptides Substance P and CGRP 
are co-released from the nociceptor terminals. Substance P binds its receptors to prolong 
depolarization and promote central sensitization, while CGRP acts on its receptor to further 
enhance excitability (Cao et al., 2024; Karcz et al., 2024).  
The activated second-order neurons cross contralaterally in the spinal cord and ascend in the 
spinothalamic tracts, carrying pain signals to the brain to project the signal in the brain stem and 
thalamus. In the brain, nociceptive signals reach a widely distributed “pain matrix” of cortical and 
subcortical regions. Primary and secondary somatosensory cortices encode the location, intensity 
and quality of the stimulus. Limbic and paralimbic regions process the emotional and motivational 
dimensions of pain. Prefrontal and orbitofrontal cortices integrate pain with context, memory and

decision-making. Together, this network constructs the conscious perception of pain and its 
unpleasantness (Cao et al., 2024; Karcz et al., 2024). 
 
 
Pain can be categorized into nociceptive, neuropathic, or nociplastic types based on distinct 
underlying mechanisms. Nociceptive pain arises from actual or threatened tissue damage, 
neuropathic pain results from a lesion or disease of the nervous system, and nociplastic pain 
stems from altered nociceptive function without clear tissue or nerve injury. Although nociceptive, 
neuropathic, and nociplastic pains can coexist in complex cases, they have distinguishing 
features in their mechanisms and clinical presentation. Differentiating these pain types are 
important because each involves different signalling pathways and thus require different 
treatment strategies (Cao et al., 2024; Nijs et al., 202). 
 
1.1.3 Nociceptive Pain 
Nociceptive pain is the most common pain type and is fundamentally a protective sensation 
triggered by noxious stimuli that cause actual or potential tissue injury. When tissues are damaged 
or inflamed, specialized sensory neurons called nociceptors are activated by chemical, 
mechanical, or thermal stimuli. For example, cell injury leads to release of inflammatory mediators 
(such as prostaglandins, bradykinin, and cytokines) that sensitize and activate nociceptor 
endings, generating pain signals. These signals are transmitted by peripheral nerve fibres (Aδ 
and C fibers) to the dorsal horn of the spinal cord and onward to the brain, where they are 
perceived as pain. This mechanism serves an acute warning function, and the pain prompts 
protective withdrawal and healing behaviours (Karcz et al., 2024). 
Clinically, nociceptive pain is often localized to the site of injury (e.g. a cut, burn, or 
musculoskeletal injury) and typically described as a sharp, throbbing, or aching pain proportional 
to the stimulus. Somatic nociceptive pain (arising from skin, muscle, or bone) tends to be well-
localized and sharp, whereas visceral nociceptive pain (from internal organs) is more diffuse. 
Unlike the other pain types, pure nociceptive pain usually does not involve abnormal neurologic 
symptoms like tingling or numbness, and the pain generally subsides as the underlying tissue 
heals and the inflammatory response resolves (Cao et al., 2024; G. I. Lee & Neumeister, 2020).  
Nociceptive pain responds well to conventional analgesics that target peripheral mediators and 
inflammation. Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are often the first-line treatment for nociceptive 
pain, as they block cyclooxygenase (COX) and reduce prostaglandin production, thereby 
decreasing inflammatory signalling and restoring normal nociceptor sensitivity. For more severe 
nociceptive pain (e.g. postoperative pain or trauma), opioid analgesics or regional anaesthetics 
can be effective by directly inhibiting nociceptive signal transmission (Cao et al., 2024).  
 
1.1.4 Neuropathic Pain 
Neuropathic pain is caused by damage or disease affecting the somatosensory nervous system 
itself. Common causes include nerve trauma, diabetic neuropathy, nerve compression (e.g. 
sciatica), multiple sclerosis, or chemotherapy-induced nerve injury (Cao et al., 2024; Smith, 2023). 
The nerve lesion leads to pathological changes such as deviant electrical activity in the injured 
fibres, abnormal excitability, and structural reorganization of neural connections. For instance, 
injured peripheral nerves may start spontaneous firing and upregulate ion channels that heighten 
sensitivity, simultaneously the normal inhibitory controls in the spinal cord may be lost due to 
damage to interneurons, and glial cells in the CNS become activated, releasing cytokines and 
growth factors that further increase neural excitability. The result is central sensitization, an

amplification of pain signalling within the central nervous system, that persists even after the initial 
nerve injury. This combination of peripheral nerve hyperexcitability and central sensitization 
sustains neuropathic pain, often making it chronic and self-perpetuating (Cao et al., 2024). 
Clinically, neuropathic pain is often described as a burning, tingling, electrical, or shooting pain, 
sometimes accompanied by sensations of pins and needles or numbness in the affected area. 
The pain distribution usually follows to the lesioned nerve pathway (Nijs et al., 2023). 
Characteristics if neuropathic pain include allodynia (pain from normally non-painful stimuli) and 
hyperalgesia (exaggerated pain from mildly painful stimuli), resulting from the lowered thresholds 
of the sensitized neurons (Karcz et al., 2024).  
Neuropathic pain often does not fully respond to standard analgesics. Treatment must target the 
neural dysfunction, thus adjuvant analgesics like antidepressants (SNRIs or tricyclics) and 
anticonvulsants (gabapentinoids) are first-line therapies for neuropathic pain (Cao et al., 2024). 
Neuropathic pain tends to be persistent or recurrent, and it often responds poorly to standard 
analgesics like NSAIDs or opioids (Cao et al., 2024). This is because the driving mechanism is 
nerve dysfunction rather than ongoing tissue inflammation. Instead, treatment of neuropathic pain 
usually requires medications that modulate neural signalling or excitability, such as adjuvant 
analgesics like antidepressants (SNRIs or tricyclics) and anticonvulsants (gabapentinoids). These 
are recommended first-line therapies in many neuropathic pain conditions (Cao et al., 2024). 
Antidepressants can enhance descending inhibition and modulate neurotransmitters involved in 
pain pathways, while anticonvulsants reduce neuronal excitability by blocking calcium channels. 
However, treatment is often not very effective, and the persistence of pain become heavy health 
burdens for patients resulting in neuropathic pain often being accompanied by psychiatric 
disorders, such as depression, anxiety and insomnia (Cao et al., 2024; Smith, 2023). 
 
1.1.5 Nociplastic Pain 
Nociplastic pain is a relatively new category of pain, introduced by IASP in 2016, that describe 
pain that arises from altered nociceptive processing in the nervous system, without clear evidence 
of actual tissue damage or nerve injury (Treede et al., 2019). Like neuropathic pain, central 
sensitization is also a hallmark mechanism for nociplastic pain, however without detectable nerve 
damage. In essence, the nervous system becomes sensitized or dysregulated, generating a pain 
experience out of proportion to any peripheral injury. This type of pain is often chronic and is not 
attributed to ongoing nociceptor activation or to an identifiable lesion in the nervous system. 
Instead, functional changes in how pain signals are processed and modulated are thought to be 
the cause (Cao et al., 2024).  
Nociplastic pain is typically seen in certain chronic pain syndromes where pain cannot be fully 
explained by injury or inflammation such as Fibromyalgia, some forms of chronic low back pain, 
tension-type headaches, and irritable bowel syndrome. These conditions often overlap and have 
a higher prevalence in women (Treede et al., 2019). Clinically, nociplastic pain often presents as 
diffuse or multifocal pain that does not follow discrete nerve distributions. Patients often 
experience amplified pain sensitivity combined with and symptoms such as profound fatigue, poor 
sleep, cognitive difficulties, and mood disturbances (Cao et al., 2024). 
From a treatment perspective, nociplastic pain poses a challenge because typical peripheral 
analgesics (like NSAIDs) often have limited benefit, given the lack of ongoing tissue injury. 
Management therefore focuses on reducing central sensitization and enhancing pain modulation. 
Pharmacologically, certain centrally acting drugs can be useful, for example, SNRIs (duloxetine, 
milnacipran) or tricyclic antidepressants can alleviate fibromyalgia and related pains, presumably 
by boosting descending inhibitory neurotransmitters and normalizing pain processing. Patient

being targeted. For instance, certain neuropeptides are central in nociceptive signalling and 
inflammation, whereas others may influence neuropathic and nociplastic pain pathways (Cao et 
al., 2024).  
 
 
1.2 Neuropeptides 
Neuropeptides are small polypeptide signalling molecules produced and released by neurons 
through the regulated secretory route in dense core vesicles when a neuron is sufficiently 
stimulated to excerpt effects on other neurons or effector cells (Merighi, 2011).  
They vary between 3-100 amino acids in length and function as neurotransmitters or 
neuromodulators. Unlike classical small-molecule neurotransmitters, which act at synapses with 
fast, transient effects, neuropeptides typically bind to G-protein-coupled receptors on target cells 
and induce slower, longer-lasting modulatory effects on neural activity. Many neuropeptides 
diffuse beyond the synapse after release, affecting a broad range of nearby cells. They often co-
exist with classic neurotransmitters in the same neuron and can modulate or augment the 
neurotransmitter’s actions (Hökfelt et al., 2003).  
 
1.2.1 Biosynthesis and Release 
The synthesis of neuropeptides begins in the endoplasmic reticulum, where a signal peptide 
directs the nascent precursor (often ≥90 amino acids long) and often containing multiple copies 
of the neuropeptide sequence, into the secretory pathway. The signal peptide is cleaved off during 
passage through ER before further sorting in Golgi. In the Golgi, specialized endopeptidases 
called prohormone convertases cleave the precursor at pairs of basic residues (e.g. Lys-Arg or 
Arg-Arg) to liberate the smaller peptide intermediates. Carboxypeptidase enzymes then remove 
the basic amino acid tails, and other modifying enzymes add final modifications such as C-
terminal amidation or N-terminal modifications. Finally, the fully processed mature neuropeptides 
are packaged into large dense-core vesicles in the Golgi and transported down the axon to nerve 
terminals where they await the signal for release (J. E. Lee, 2016; Merighi, 2011). 
 
Neuropeptides require high frequency or sustained activation to reach the high Ca2+ influx needed 
to reach and release the dense core vesicles. Once released, neuropeptides diffuse farther than 
classical neurotransmitters because their targets, usually G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs), 
are typically located outside of the synaptic cleft in the peri-synaptic or extra-synaptic regions. 
Since they diffuse further, they are not as quickly reabsorbed or recycled by presynaptic neurons. 
Therefore, their action is terminated mainly by diffusion away and enzymatic degradation by 
extracellular peptidases. Thus, neuropeptide signals has generally slower onset and longer 
duration allowing them to function as neuromodulators, influencing neural circuits over longer 
distances and durations (J. E. Lee, 2016; Merighi, 2011).

1.2.2 Structural Differences 
Shorter neuropeptides (3-10 amino acids) do not maintain a stable secondary structure and are 
usually linear, flexible or assume random coil turns. They therefore rely on peptide bond 
modifications such as amidation and pyroglutamate to enhance stability. Longer peptides (20-40+ 
amino acids) are able to gain stability from secondary structures such as alfa-helixes and beta-
sheets or intramolecular bonds such as disulfide bonds, making them less accessible to 
peptidases. However, even the largest neuropeptides are too small to form tertiary structures. 
Longer neuropeptides can also present multiple contact points to a receptor, potentially 
influencing multiple receptor subtypes or activating complex signalling, whereas short peptides 
often mimic just a single “active core” motif (Christie et al., 2010). 
 
1.2.3 Functional Roles 
Short and long neuropeptides often serve somewhat different physiological roles. Short 
neuropeptides often mediate fast, discrete signalling events, whereas longer neuropeptides tend 
to be involved in more sustained, diffuse modulation of physiological states. Short neuropeptides 
are frequently used for point-to-point signalling or fast neuromodulation in specific circuits. They 
may be released in discrete brain regions or spinal cord synapses to exert a localized effect that 
is tightly coupled to neuron firing. Longer neuropeptides, on the other hand, often act on a broader 
scale. Some are co-released with hormones or function as neurohormones themselves, traveling 
longer distances, and often have endocrine or paracrine roles in addition to their neurotransmitter 
function. Short peptides are less likely to survive long enough to act at distant sites, therefore 
their roles tend to remain local. Longer peptides can integrate signals over longer time frames or 
act in feedback loops (Nässel, 2009; Nässel & Wegener, 2011) 
 
1.2.4 Stability and Half-Life of Neuropeptides 
Neuropeptides generally have short plasma and tissue half-lives as they are prone to degradation 
by proteolytic enzymes and easily cleaved by peptidases in blood and extracellular fluid due to 
their lack of tertiary structures. In vivo most neuropeptides are broken down within minutes or 
even seconds of being released. This rapid degradation is partially credited to the abundance of 
peptidases, which are the primary means of clearing neuropeptides from the extracellular space 
as they are not recycled via reuptake by neurons (Yi et al., 2015).  
The factors influencing their stability include 1) sequence and length, shorter peptides with 
exposed termini are more vulnerable to rapid degradation. Shorter peptides might be destroyed 
by a single protease cut and degraded within few minutes or seconds, while longer peptides might 
require several cuts and persist slightly longer (tens of minutes), still even the most stable 
peptides are degraded in less than an hour (Foley et al., 1979). 2) Protease abundance, different 
compartments differ in their abundance of proteases, and local protease inhibitors can modulate 
degradation rates. 3) Post-translational modifications like C-terminal amidation and N-terminal 
pyroglutamate significantly increase stability by blocking exoprotease cleavage sites (Yi et al., 
2015). Neuropeptides can also shield themselves by binding extracellular matrix components or 
carrier proteins to prolong their half-lives. However, neuropeptides are overall chemically and 
physically unstable in the bloodstream, with fast elimination. This presents a challenge for 
therapeutic use of peptides as they tend to require injection or infusion and frequent dosing i(Yi 
et al., 2015).

1.2.5 Pharmacokinetic Differences 
Shorter and longer neuropeptides also differ in how they are distributed and cleared in the body. 
Due to short neuropeptides (<5 kDa) having very short half-lives, often being eliminated almost 
as fast as they are administered. If they persist, they tend to be filtered by the kidneys and 
excreted rapidly. Longer peptides (several kDa) might not be immediately filtered. Instead, they 
are more likely to be removed by receptor-mediated uptake or by liver metabolism. This can result 
in slightly longer plasma persistence for larger neuropeptides (Nässel, 2009). Moreover, 
modifications that increase a peptide’s effective size or alter its hydrophobicity can shift its 
clearance route. Glycosylation of a peptide makes it more hydrophilic and can reduce uptake by 
the liver, instead favouring renal elimination (Varamini et al., 2013). Similarly, conjugating 
peptides to large carriers (like PEG or albumin) can prolong circulation by avoiding rapid renal 
filtration (Böttger et al., 2017). In practical terms, longer peptides or stabilized peptides can 
achieve longer-lasting effects in vivo, whereas very short peptides often require continuous 
production or infusion to maintain effective levels. Finally, size can affect the ability to cross 
barriers. Small peptides (under ~4–5 amino acids) might cross cell membranes or the blood-brain 
barrier to a limited extent if they are very lipophilic, but generally all neuropeptides have poor 
membrane permeability. Neither short nor long neuropeptides cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB) 
efficiently in their native forms, but shorter ones are sometimes more amenable to chemical 
modification (Varamini et al., 2013). 
 
 
1.2.6 Neuropeptides in Nociception and Antinociception 
One of the most important roles of neuropeptides is the modulation of pain. Different 
neuropeptides either promote nociception (pain transmission) or suppress pain (antinociception).  
 
Substance P (SP) is an 11–amino acid neuropeptide of the tachykinin family that is a well-known 
mediator of pain signals. It is released by primary sensory neurons (nociceptors) in the dorsal 
horn of the spinal cord in response to noxious (painful) stimuli. SP binds to neurokinin-1 receptors 
(NK1) on postsynaptic neurons, causing excitation and transmission of pain impulses to the brain. 
In addition to its central role in nociception, substance P causes neurogenic inflammation in 
peripheral tissues. This dual action makes SP a key player in pain pathways as it not only 
communicates pain centrally but also contributes to the swelling and inflammation that often 
accompany tissue injury. The effects of Substance P are relatively short-lived as it quickly 
degrades in the extracellular space, but during sustained or chronic pain, continuous release of 
SP can lead to prolonged pain sensation and central sensitization. Elevated levels of substance 
P are associated with chronic pain conditions (Karcz et al., 2024; Zieglgänsberger, 2019).  
 
Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide (CGRP) is a 37–amino acid neuropeptide often co-released with 
substance P by nociceptive sensory fibres. It exists in two isoforms (α- and β-CGRP) and is a 
potent vasodilator. CGRP contributes to pain peripherally by causing vasodilation and plasma 
extravasation, and centrally, by modulating pain signal processing in the spinal cord and 
brainstem. It has been implicated in migraine headaches and other pain syndromes. In the spinal 
cord dorsal horn, CGRP can enhance the excitability of pain-transmission neurons (Russell et al., 
2014).  
 
Lastly endogenous opioid peptides (Enkephalins, Endorphins, Dynorphins). The body produces 
several families of opioid neuropeptides that serve as natural pain suppressants (antinociceptive

interneurons in areas like the dorsal horn of the spinal cord to inhibit the release of substance P

receptors (and to a lesser extent μ-opioid receptors). When released, enkephalins hyperpolarize 
pain-transmitting neurons and produce relief from pain. Their effect is potent but very short-lived 
due to rapid degradation (Varamini et al., 2013; Zadina et al., 1997).  
β-Endorphin is a 31–amino acid peptide derived from the precursor POMC (proopiomelanocortin) 
and is a potent agonist of μ-opioid and δ-opioid receptors. β-Endorphin is released in both the 
CNS from hypothalamic neurons and the peripheral circulation from the pituitary gland. It 
produces powerful analgesia, roughly comparable to morphine in effect. It contributes to the 
immediate pain relief experienced in acute stress or injury (Foley et al., 1979). Dynorphins, such 
as dynorphin A (17 aa), mainly target κ-opioid receptors. Dynorphin is found in spinal cord and 
brain regions and can induce analgesia at the spinal level; however, κ-receptor activation can 
also cause dysphoria, and may contribute to the emotional dimension of pain or stress 
(Neugebauer et al., 2020; Stein, 2016).  
All these opioid peptides work by activating opioid receptors, which inhibit neurotransmitter 
release and hyperpolarize neurons in pain pathways. Collectively, the endogenous opioid system 
is a primary mechanism of antinociception in the body, and the target of opioid pain medications 
(Stein, 2016).  
 
 
1.3 The μ-Opioid Receptor (MOR)  
 
1.3.1 The Opioid Receptor Family 
The μ-opioid receptors (MOR), along with δ- and κ-opioid receptors (DOR and KOR), belong to 
the opioid receptor family. They are Class A G protein–coupled receptors (GPCRs) characterized 
by a seven-transmembrane (7TM) helical structure, an extracellular N‐terminus, and an 
intracellular C‐terminus. These receptors primarily couple to inhibitory Gi/o proteins, where 
agonist binding causes inhibition of adenylate cyclase (reducing cAMP levels), opens inward-
rectifying K+ channels, and closes Ca2+ channels, leading to neuronal hyperpolarization (Stein, 
2016). 
 
Opioid receptors are broadly distributed throughout the central and peripheral nervous systems, 
influencing multiple physiological processes such as analgesia, reward mechanisms, stress 
responses, respiratory control, and gastrointestinal motility. Endogenous opioid neuropeptides β-
endorphins, enkephalins, and dynorphins are the primary ligands for MOR, DOR, and KOR 
respectively, and their activation regulates the diverse biological effects attributed to these 
receptors (Sobczak et al., 2014; Stein, 2016) 
 
MOR activation specifically mediates potent analgesia and is involved in reward processing and 
euphoria. MOR stimulation strongly inhibits GABAergic interneurons within reward circuits, 
facilitating dopamine release and positive reinforcement. KOR agonists primarily provide 
analgesia, particularly effective for visceral pain, however also triggering dysphoria by inhibiting 
dopamine release within reward circuits. Lastly, DOR activation produces analgesia with fewer 
side effects such as respiratory depression and gastrointestinal disturbances, and it has shown

potential in managing mood disorders like anxiety and depression, although selective DOR 
agonists have yet to reach clinical application (Reeves et al., 2022). 
 
1.3.2 Receptor Distribution and Functional Distinctions 
The μ-opioid receptor (MOR) is the most clinically significant member of the opioid receptor family 
and serves as the primary therapeutic target for pain relief. Three subtypes of MOR have been 
identified; μ1, μ2, and μ3, originating from alternative splicing of the single OPRM1 gene or distinct 
receptor conformations. To date, structural differences among these subtypes have not been 
clearly defined; instead, their distinctions lie in differential tissue distribution, functional roles, and 
ligand specificity (S. Liu et al., 2021). 
 
The μ1 receptor subtype primarily mediates the analgesic effects of opioids, particularly 
supraspinal analgesia. μ1 receptors are densely localized in brain regions critical for pain 
modulation, such as the periaqueductal grey (PAG) and medullary raphe nuclei. Activation of μ1 
receptors in these regions inhibits local GABAergic interneurons, consequently activating 
descending analgesic pathways. This activation prompts descending neurons to release anti-
nociceptive signals in the spinal cord, where μ1 receptors also reside on primary afferent neurons 
and dorsal horn neurons in the spinal cord. Their activation here inhibits nociceptive 
neurotransmitter release and hyperpolarizes neurons to suppress pain signalling. This results in 
powerful analgesia, both at spinal and supraspinal levels (Jordan et al., 2000; Trescot et al., 
2008). 
 
The μ2 subtype is broadly co-expressed with μ1 receptors in central nervous system regions such 
as the brainstem and spinal dorsal horn. However, in addition to contribute to supraspinal 
analgesia, μ2 is the subtype primarily responsible for adverse opioid effects beyond analgesia. 
Activation of μ2 receptors significantly contributes to(Jordan et al., 2000; Trescot et al., 2008) 
respiratory depression, miosis (pupil constriction), reduced gastrointestinal motility, euphoria, and 
the development of physical dependence. For instance, respiratory depression following 
morphine administration is primarily mediated by μ2 receptor activation in brainstem respiratory 
centres, and opioid-induced constipation is a result from μ2 receptor activity in the gastrointestinal 
tract, slowing intestinal motility (Jordan et al., 2000; Trescot et al., 2008). 
 
The μ3 subtype is pharmacologically distinct, defined pharmacologically as an opiate alkaloid-
selective site that is insensitive to endogenous opioid peptides and primarily activated by opiate 
alkaloids such as morphine. Expression of μ3 receptors is predominantly found outside the 
nervous system, particularly on endothelial cells lining blood vessels and immune cells. Activation 
of μ3 receptors in these cells triggers nitric oxide (NO) release, suggesting roles in vascular 
modulation and immune function, potentially explaining peripheral opioid effects such as 
hypotension and immunosuppression not fully attributable to μ1 or μ2 receptor activation 
(Stefano, 1999). 
 
 
1.4 The Endogenous Opioid Receptor Agonist Endomorphin-1 
Endogenous opioid peptides, including enkephalins, endorphins, and dynorphins, share a 
conserved N-terminal Tyr-Gly-Gly-Phe sequence critical for binding opioid receptors (MOR, DOR, 
and KOR). Enkephalins preferentially bind DOR, dynorphins target KOR, and β-endorphins 
interact with MOR and DOR with comparable affinity (Reeves et al., 2022).

1.4.1 Structure and Receptor Selectivity  
Endomorphin-1 (EM1), discovered by Zadina et al. in 1997, marked the first deviation from this 
classical opioid peptide structure. The tetrapeptide EM1 (Tyr-Pro-Trp-Phe-NH₂) was identified 
through systematic amino acid substitution of a peptide (Tyr-Pro-Trp-Gly-NH₂) with poor affinity 
but high MOR selectivity, Figure 1. Replacing Gly with Phe significantly enhanced affinity and 
selectivity for MOR, leading to EM1. A second peptide, (Tyr-Pro-Phe-Phe-NH₂), exhibited similar 
properties and was named Endomorphin-2 (EM2). Both EM1 and EM2 uniquely deviate from the 
traditional Tyr-Gly-Gly-Phe motif yet demonstrate remarkable potency and MOR selectivity 
(Hackler et al., 1997; Zadina et al., 1997).

The atypical opioid neuropeptides EM1 and EM2 are naturally occurring tetrapeptides and were 
first isolated from bovine brain and later also isolated from human cortex and are believed to be 
human endogenous neuromodulators and ligands for μ-opioid receptors in the brain and spinal 
cord due to their high affinity and selectivity, and concentration in these regions (Hackler et al., 
1997; Zadina et al., 1997).  
 
1.4.2 Distribution of Endomorphins  
EM1 has primarily been found localized in the brain and has not been significantly detected in the 
spinal cord. In contrast, EM2 has predominantly been identified in the spinal cord, and some brain 
regions, however its expression there is lower compared to EM1. This distribution suggests that 
EM1 is primarily involved in supraspinal modulation of pain and possibly in neuroendocrine and 
affective processes regulated by the brain, while EM2 is dominantly involved in the spinal-level 
nociceptive processing, where it may modulate pain signals arising from peripheral stimuli (Martin-
Schild et al., 1998; Q. P. Wang et al., 2003). Consistent with this, EM2 tends to produce more 
potent analgesia when administered intrathecally, whereas EM1 is highly effective when 
administered supraspinal (intracerebral). Both peptides are full μ-agonists, but subtle differences 
in receptor subtype activation have been suggested. EM1 may preferentially activate μ1 receptor-
mediated pathways (implicated in supraspinal analgesia), while EM2 might engage μ2-mediated 
spinal analgesia and peripheral effects (Varamini et al., 2013). Functionally, both EM1 and EM2 
yield robust antinociception with a similar spectrum of opioid effects, and both are being explored 
as templates for new analgesics. Neither endomorphins has shown the kind of severe toxicity or 
organ damage that some synthetic opioids do (Varamini et al., 2013) 
 
 
1.4.3 Analgesic Mechanism  
EM1 activates MOR, engaging Gi/Go-proteins, which leads to inhibition of adenylyl cyclase and 
modulation of ion channels in neurons. The downstream effect is a reduction in neuronal 
excitability and neurotransmitter release in pain pathways. At the cellular level, endomorphins 
cause inhibition of voltage-gated calcium currents and enhancement of potassium efflux, thereby 
reducing synaptic transmission of pain signals (Okada et al., 2002). These actions underlie EM1’s 
potent antinociceptive effects in the central nervous system.  
 
In binding assays, EM1 binds MOR with remarkable affinity (Ki = 0.36 nM) and selectivity (4000- 
and 15,000-fold preference over DOR and KOR, respectively). EM2 binds MOR with similar 
strength (Ki = 0.69 nM), and selectivity (13,000- and 7500-fold preference for DOR and KOR, 
respectively). These peptides are thus regarded as the endogenous ligands with the highest 
known specificity for MOR (Zadina et al., 1997).  
The precursor peptide for EM1 and EM2 has not been identified, still they are considered part of 
the endogenous opioid system due to their accumulation in MOR rich regions of the brain and 
spinal cord and their remarkably high selectivity for the receptor (Varamini et al., 2013).  
 
1.4.4 Analgesic Potency  
Endomorphin-1 (EM1) and Endomorphin-2 (EM2) have demonstrated potent analgesic activity in 
a variety of animal models, including acute, inflammatory, neuropathic, and allodynic pain. In 
many cases, they surpass morphine’s efficacy, particularly when administered centrally, while 
exhibiting a more favorable side effect profile (Czapla et al., 2000; Li et al., 2001; C. L. Wang et

al., 2017; Wilson et al., 2000). EM1 has been shown to be significantly more potent than morphine 
in supraspinal analgesia assays, while EM2, displays enhanced spinal analgesic properties.  
 
Initial studies revealed that EM1 and EM2 produce robust analgesia in both acute and neuropathic 
models with notably fewer side effects than traditional opioids, such as less respiratory 
depression, lower potential for addiction, and reduced tolerance development. However, their 
clinical application has been severely limited by poor pharmacokinetic properties pharmacokinetic 
challenges (how the body absorbs, distributes, metabolizes, and excretes the drug over time, 
determining the drug's onset, duration, intensity of effect, and overall exposure(Jambhekar & 
Breen, 2009). As small, unmodified neuropeptides, endomorphins are rapidly degraded by 
endogenous peptidases, resulting in extremely short plasma half-lives (Czapla et al., 2000), on 
the order of minutes Moreover, their hydrophilic nature prevents effective penetration of the blood-
brain barrier (BBB), and their solubility and systemic stability are poor. Consequently, most 
preclinical studies have relied on central (e.g., intracerebroventricular or intrathecal) 
administration to bypass these limitations and achieve therapeutic levels in the CNS (Czapla et 
al., 2000; Gu et al., 2018; Li et al., 2001; Varamini et al., 2013; Zadina et al., 1997).  
 
Despite these delivery constraints, centrally administered EM1 and EM2 consistently produce 
strong analgesia in rodent models, albeit for limited durations due to rapid metabolism. An 
overview of some of the preclinical studies of EM1 and EM2 is presented in Table 1. However, 
due to the promising findings and potential of this group of endogenous opioids, researchers have 
focused on using EM1 and EM2 as parent structures for developing new compounds with 
improved systemic stability, receptor selectivity, and bioavailability while retaining, or enhancing, 
their analgesic potential (Zadina et al., 2016). Some of the strategies applied to achieve 
compounds with better drug properties include cyclization to minimize  degradation by peptidases 
(Webster & Schmidt, 2020; Zadina et al., 2016, 2024); lipidation to enhance lipophilicity and 
membrane permeability; glycosylation to increase solubility, stability, and alter the 
pharmacodynamics by altering receptor recognition and specificity (Varamini et al., 2012); and 
amino acid substitution to optimize ligand-receptor interactions and enhance selectivity (X. Liu et 
al., 2015; Y. Wang et al., 2015). Many of these analogues have demonstrated strong systemic 
analgesia, often with fewer opioid-related side effects such as tolerance, sedation, and respiratory 
depression. An overview of some of the most promising studies is listed in Table 2 below, and

1.4.5 Pain Models and Routes of Administration 
Endomorphins and their analogues have been assessed in a wide range of preclinical assays, 
including acute nociceptive assays (e.g. tail-flick and hot-plate tests for thermal pain and), 
inflammatory pain models (e.g. paw incision or CFA-induced pain with von Frey filament tests for 
mechanical allodynia and Hargreaves test for thermal hyperalgesia), neuropathic pain models 
(such as spared nerve injury or chronic constriction injury with measurements of mechanical 
allodynia and hyperalgesia), and visceral pain tests (e.g. acetic acid writhing assay) (Csullog et 
al., 2001; Czapla et al., 2000; Horvath et al., 1999; Li et al., 2001; Przewłocka et al., 1999; Soignier 
et al., 2000; L. S. Stone et al., 1997; C. L. Wang et al., 2017; Wilson et al., 2000; Zadina et al., 
1997). In these models, intrathecal or intracerebroventricular administration consistently yielded 
robust analgesic effects. Systemic administration of unmodified endomorphins, however, was

largely ineffective unless very high doses were used, due to poor bioavailability and inadequate 
CNS penetration. 
As a result, analogues with improved pharmacokinetic profiles have been developed and tested 
via routes such as intravenous (i.v.), subcutaneous (s.c.), and even oral administration. For 
instance, glycosylated analogues have demonstrated improved s.c. bioavailability, achieving 
potent analgesia at lower doses than morphine and with significantly longer durations of action. 
In one study, a glycosylated EM1 analogue achieved an equivalent effect to 5.6 mg/kg morphine 
at a dose of only 1.8 mg/kg (s.c.) (Varamini et al., 2012; Zadina et al., 2024). Zadina and 
colleagues tested several endomorphin analogues via i.t., i.v., and s.c. administration in both rats 
and mice, consistently observing longer-lasting analgesia and superior potency compared to 
morphine. 
 
1.4.6 Comparisons with Morphine and Clinical Implications 
In direct comparisons, many endomorphin analogues matched or exceeded morphine in potency 
and duration of action. For example, the metabolically stable analogue A2 showed significantly 
prolonged analgesic effects across multiple models, including neuropathic and inflammatory pain, 
where it outperformed morphine both in terms of efficacy and duration. While morphine typically 
provides around two hours of relief in a thermal nociception model, A2 extended analgesia to over 
five hours at equianalgesic doses. These longer-lasting effects are attributed to enhanced 
metabolic stability and sustained receptor activation by engineered peptides. 
Importantly, these analogues also consistently demonstrated reduced respiratory depression, 
lower tolerance development, and minimal reward behaviour in preclinical assays. This improved 
side-effect profile, combined with enhanced potency, positions endomorphin analogues as 
promising candidates for next-generation analgesics (Zadina et al., 2024).  
 
Although the promising results from central administration studies highlight the analgesic potential 
of EM1, EM2, and their analogues, these methods are not viable for clinical use due to their 
invasiveness. Therefore, significant research efforts have shifted toward optimizing the 
pharmacokinetic properties of these compounds and developing alternative delivery strategies. 
One of the most promising approaches is the encapsulation of endomorphins in nanocarrier 
systems such as liposomes. These systems can protect peptides from enzymatic degradation, 
enhance BBB penetration, and enable sustained release, all of which may overcome the current 
limitations of endomorphin-based therapeutics. The next section explores how liposomal 
formulations may improve the clinical viability of EM1 and EM2 analogues.

1.5 Liposomal Drug Delivery Systems for Neuropeptides 
While extensive research has focused on chemically modifying EM1 and EM2 to improve their 
pharmacokinetic profiles, even the most optimized analogues remain peptides and thus 
susceptible to enzymatic degradation and limited systemic bioavailability. Liposomal formulation 
offers a complementary strategy that can enhance the therapeutic potential of both native and 
modified endomorphins by improving their delivery and stability without fundamentally altering 
their pharmacology. By encapsulating neuropeptides within liposomes, it is possible to shield 
them from enzymatic attack, enhance their absorption and half-life, and even achieve targeted 
delivery to specific tissues (Bozzuto & Molinari, 2015).  
 
Liposomes are spherical vesicles consisting of one or more lipid bilayers surrounding an aqueous 
core, mimicking the structure of cellular membranes. They self-assemble when phospholipids are 
dispersed in aqueous media at concentrations above the critical aggregation concentration and 
temperatures exceeding the lipid’s phase transition temperature. Initially, large multilamellar 
vesicles form, which can be further processed into smaller unilamellar vesicles by methods such 
as extrusion, sonication, or homogenization. Liposomes’ structure allows the encapsulation of 
both hydrophilic drugs (within the aqueous core) and lipophilic drugs (within the lipid bilayer), 
making them highly versatile carriers in pharmaceutical applications, including vaccines and gene 
therapy (Jiang et al., 2024).

1.5.1 Liposome Classification and Structure 
Liposomes are classified based on their size and lamellarity: small unilamellar vesicles (SUVs, 
30–200 nm), large unilamellar vesicles (LUVs, 200–1000 nm), giant unilamellar vesicles (GUVs, 
>1000 nm), multilamellar vesicles (MLVs), and multivesicular vesicles (MVVs). Illustrated in 
Figure 2. SUVs are particularly favoured for clinical applications due to their propensity to circulate 
systemically and accumulate in pathological sites through the enhanced permeability and 
retention (EPR) effect. Their cell membrane resembling structure provide them with high 
biocompatibility, low toxicity, and effective transport of drugs, making them valuable assets in 
drug delivery strategies (Allen & Cullis, 2013; Elizondo et al., 2011; W. Gao et al., 2013).

1.5.2 Lipid Composition and Surface Modification  
The lipid composition of a liposome determines its biophysical properties, including drug loading, 
biodistribution, membrane fluidity, permeability, and stability. Phospholipids with various 
headgroups and acyl chains can be selected to tailor properties such as membrane fluidity, 
surface charge, and lamellar phase behaviour. High-transition temperature (Tm) phospholipids 
like DSPC yields a relatively rigid, less permeable bilayer at body temperature, which helps retain 
encapsulated drugs. Cholesterol, when incorporated into the membrane, modulates fluidity, 
reduces permeability, and enhances stability by integrating between lipid tails. This increases 
membrane rigidity, decreases drug leakage, and enhances resistance to mechanical stress 
during preparation and circulation (Jiang et al., 2024).  
 
Surface charge of the lipid headgroups also influences biodistribution and stability. Zwitterionic 
lipids like phosphatidylcholine mimic cell membranes and tend to be least immunogenic, whereas 
cationic lipids (e.g. DOTAP) can facilitate electrostatic binding of negatively charged genetic 
material but can increase toxicity and uptake by the reticuloendothelial system. Anionic lipids (like 
phosphatidylglycerol or phosphatidylserine) create a negative surface charge which can improve 
colloidal stability (via charge repulsion) and influence biodistribution. Anionic and zwitterionic 
lipids are also closer in character to natural cell membranes compared to strongly cationic lipids 
(Jiang et al., 2024).   
 
PEGylated lipids (often DSPE) with covalently attached polyethylene glycol chains, creates a 
hydrophilic polymer brush on the liposome surface. This PEG coating sterically shields the 
liposome from attack by plasma proteins and uptake by macrophages and can dramatically 
extend circulation time. PEGylated liposomes have demonstrated significantly longer blood 
circulation times compared to their non-PEGylated counterparts, with reports suggesting the half-
lives can exceed several hours. This modification provides substantial pharmacokinetic 
advantages, including reduced immunogenicity and prolonged circulation, thus PEGylated 
liposomes, often called “stealth liposomes”, have become standard for long-circulating 
formulations (Y. Gao et al., 2024; Ramos et al., 2022). One trade-off is that PEG can inhibit 
interactions with target cells. However, since MORs are surface-bound receptors, PEGylation 
does not interfere with endomorphin activity (Tian et al., 2022).  
 
 
1.5.3 Critical Packaging Parameter and Vesicle Formation 
Lipid self-assembly is governed by the critical packing parameter (CPP), defined by the ratio of 
the hydrophobic tail volume to the headgroup area and tail length, illustrated in Figure 3. Lipids 
with a CPP <1 tend to form bilayers and self-assemble to liposomes when they are hydrated 
under suitable thermodynamic conditions, above the lipid phase transition temperature and at 
sufficient concentration (Elizondo et al., 2011).

For a stable liposome bilayer, a CPP of 0.5-1 is ideal. Ensuring the lipid components collectively 
have an appropriate packing parameter is essential for forming stable vesicles. This is often 
achieved by using predominantly cylindrical lipids (PCs, sphingomyelins) and bilayer-preferring 
additives (cholesterol) (Kobierski et al., 2022). 
Liposomes will first assemble as large multilamellar vesicles but can be processed into smaller 
unilamellar vesicles by further processing such as extrusion, sonication and homogenization. The 
specific liposome composition, the type of phospholipids and PEGylated lipids used, and 
proportions of the different components can be tailored to control drug release, targeting, and 
circulation time, depending on the therapeutic need (Elizondo et al., 2011).  
 
1.5.4 Production Methods: Thin-Film Hydration and Extrusion 
Thin-film hydration is a standard laboratory-scale technique for production of liposomes and 
remains widely used. Lipids (and any lipophilic drug to be encapsulated) are dissolved in an 
organic solvent, and the solvent evaporated to leave a thin dry lipid film on the flask wall. An 
aqueous buffer (containing any hydrophilic drug to be encapsulated, if passive loading) is added 
to hydrate the lipids, which spontaneously form multilamellar vesicles (MLVs). This MLV 
suspension can then be processed to reduce the size and lamellarity by extrusion (Elizondo et 
al., 2011).  
High-pressure extrusion forces liposomes through polycarbonate membranes, forming size-
controlled SUVs. It can be performed in an incrementally manner, reducing filter pore size by each 
round (Riccardi et al., 2024). Each pass shears the liposomes, re-forming them into smaller, more 
uniform unilamellar vesicles of roughly the filter pore diameter. Extrusion is effective at producing 
homogeneous liposome populations and is commonly used in industry to achieve liposomes of 
~80–150 nm size, within the optimal size range for systemic parenteral administration (P. Liu et 
al., 2022).  
 
1.5.5 Remote Loading  
Remote loading of hydrophilic drugs is typically performed after liposomes have been formed and 
extruded to achieve the desired size and numbers of bilayers. To enable remote loading, the

liposome suspension is first dialyzed or buffer-exchanged into an isotonic medium (e.g., PBS) to 
establish a transmembrane gradient, commonly pH or ionic, (using e.g. ammonium sulphate). 
Once the gradient is established, the drug is added to the liposome suspension and incubated at 
a temperature above the lipid transition temperature (Tm), increasing membrane fluidity and 
allowing the uncharged form of the drug to diffuse into the liposomal core. There, the drug 
becomes protonated or ion-trapped, depending on the gradient type. After loading, the formulation 
is cooled and dialyzed again to remove unencapsulated drug (Barenholz, 2012; Nambiar et al., 
2024). 
 
Compared to passive loading, remote loading offers significantly higher encapsulation 
efficiencies, often exceeding 80%, particularly for weakly basic small molecules or ionizable 
peptides. In contrast, passive loading yields are typically low (~10–30%), because much of the 
drug remains outside the liposome and is susceptible to degradation or clearance. Remote 
loading also provides better control over drug retention and release kinetics, as drugs can 
precipitate or form complexes inside the vesicle, reducing leakage during circulation. This 
technique has been widely used in clinically approved formulations such as Doxil® and is 
increasingly being adapted for macromolecular therapeutics like peptides (Barenholz, 2012).  
Understanding and optimizing encapsulation efficiency (EE%), defined as the proportion of drug 
successfully entrapped within the liposomes relative to the total amount added, is critical for 
maximizing therapeutic efficacy and minimizing waste. However, achieving high EE% with 
neuropeptides like endomorphins presents distinct formulation challenges due to their 
hydrophilicity, enzymatic lability, and structural instability in aqueous environments. Overcoming 
these barriers often requires careful optimization of parameters such as peptide concentration, 
lipid composition, pH gradients for remote loading, and processing conditions like extrusion 
temperature (Large et al., 2021; Nambiar et al., 2024). 
 
1.5.6 Release Kinetics and Retention 
The release profile of a liposomal formulation is critical for achieving therapeutic efficacy. 
Liposomes typically exhibit a biphasic release pattern, characterized by an initial rapid release of 
surface-associated drug followed by a slower, sustained release of the encapsulated payload. A 
rapid burst may mimic free-drug administration, reducing duration of action, while excessively 
slow release can limit bioavailability at the target site. For neuropeptides, often subject to rapid 
enzymatic degradation and clearance, a sustained or depot-like release is especially valuable, 
extending therapeutic duration and reducing dosing frequency (Large et al., 2021).   
 
To fine-tune drug release, liposomes can be engineered with stimulus-responsive lipids that 
respond to environmental cues. pH-sensitive formulations destabilize in acidic environments, 
such as the tumor microenvironment or endosomal compartments, promoting site-specific 
release. For CNS applications, researchers have explored enzyme-sensitive or thermoresponsive 
formulations that activate once the liposome crosses the blood–brain barrier (BBB), allowing site-
selective release while maintaining systemic stability (Lou & Best, 2020; Samoshina et al., 2011).  
This sustained and protective release profile is particularly advantageous in neuropeptide 
delivery. For example, depot formulations of leuprolide achieve multi-month release following 
intramuscular injection, demonstrating the feasibility of long-term peptide release systems. Even 
prior to release, encapsulation helps preserve bioactivity by shielding peptides like EM1 from 
proteolytic degradation (de Freitas & Soares, 2020).

1.5.7 Release, Distribution, and Targeting of Liposomal Neuropeptides 
However, effective liposomal systems must not only encapsulate and protect the drug but ensure 
the drug is delivered to the right tissue, at the right time, and at a therapeutically effective rate. 
Liposomes offer opportunities for both passive and active targeting. In passive targeting, 
liposomes naturally accumulate in tissues with leaky vasculature, such as tumours, inflamed 
joints, or injured neural tissue, due to the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect. For 
neurological applications, some liposomal systems have been formulated for epithelial or mucosal 
transport, including nose-to-brain delivery via the olfactory and trigeminal nerve pathways (de 
Barros et al., 2021; Katona et al., 2022).  
 
Active targeting enhances specificity by modifying the liposome surface with ligands that bind to 
receptors on target cells. In the context of CNS delivery, researchers have conjugated liposomes 
with transferrin, lactoferrin, insulin, or rabies virus glycoprotein (RVG) peptide to exploit receptor-
mediated transcytosis across the blood–brain barrier (BBB) (Almeida et al., 2020). When 
designing liposomes to cross the BBB, the success of the systems depends heavily on particle 
characteristics such as size (ideally 100–200 nm), surface charge (mildly cationic to enhance 
cellular uptake), and ligand density and orientation. Overcrowding or use of bulky ligands can 
lead to opsonization and rapid clearance by the mononuclear phagocyte system (MPS) (Chen et 
al., 2017; Kamal et al., 2015).   
Surface modifications such as chitosan coating have also shown promise for intranasal delivery 
due to chitosan’s mucoadhesive and cationic properties, which enhance both epithelial 
permeability and cellular uptake (de Barros et al., 2021). For peripheral applications, PEGylation 
remains essential to prolong circulation and reduce immune recognition, especially when CNS 
targeting is not required (Wu et al., 2023).  
 
1.5.8 Clinical Success and Future Promise of Liposomal Drug Delivery 
Since the approval of Doxil® in 1995, the first PEGylated liposomal drug formulation, for the 
treatment of Kaposi’s sarcoma, liposomes have gained widespread clinical acceptance as 
nanocarriers for drug delivery. Several liposomal drugs, including Myocet®, AmBisome®, 
Epaxal®, and DepoDur®, have been approved for intravenous or intramuscular use, offering 
advantages such as prolonged circulation, enhanced tissue targeting, and reduced toxicity (Batist 
et al., 2002; Bovier, 2008; Hartrick & Hartrick, 2008; Patel, 1996; N. R. H. Stone et al., 2016).  
These benefits are largely attributed to formulation parameters such as PEGylation, bilayer 
composition, and particle size. Liposomal vaccines such as Epaxal® and depot formulations have 
demonstrated improved immunogenicity or sustained release (Bozzuto & Molinari, 2015).  
 
Liposomal delivery systems can offer unique advantages for neuropeptide delivery. By improving 
entrapment efficiency, prolonging circulation, and protecting peptides from degradation, these 
systems enhance the pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic profiles of candidates like 
endomorphins. Importantly, sustained release may also mitigate the development of opioid 
tolerance by minimizing peak plasma concentrations and reducing receptor desensitization. 
Furthermore, when paired with targeted delivery strategies, liposomal neuropeptide formulations 
hold the potential to selectively engage specific tissues or receptor populations, expanding their 
therapeutic utility in complex pain conditions (Doyen et al., 2021; Hamid et al., 2024; Pandey et 
al., 2024).

2. Background 
This thesis builds upon the work of previous MSc student Girisa Prabhu, who, under the 
supervision of Associate Professor Andrew Urquhart at the Section of Cell and Drug 
Technologies, DTU HealthTech, synthesized endomorphin-1 (EM1) and several of its analogues 
and identified optimal lipid composition for remote loading into liposomes. The current project 
aimed to replicate and validate the previously achieved encapsulation efficiency (EE%) of EM1 
and advance the research into the preclinical testing phase. A visual overview of the project 
workflow is shown in Figure 4. 
To our knowledge, this is the first preclinical study of liposomal EM-1. Therefore, stealth liposomes 
without active targeting ligands were employed to evaluate the baseline performance of passively 
targeted, long-circulating liposomal EM-1 formulations in vivo.  
This project focuses exclusively on EM1, and neither EM2 or EM analogues were evaluated 
experimentally in this project.  
 
Liposomes were prepared by hydration of a preformed stealth lipid mixture composed of HSPC, 
MPEG-2000-DSPE, and cholesterol (molar ratio 3:1:1) in a 250 mM ammonium sulphate buffer 
(pH 5.2) to form multilamellar vesicles.  
HSPC (hydrogenated soy phosphatidylcholine), a double-chain phospholipid that favors bilayer 
organization, with a CPP around 0.5, supporting stable vesicle formation (Ahmad et al., 2021), 
DSPE-mPEG2000 (lipid-anchored PEG), a bilayer-forming component, with PEG chains 
extending outward. Its CPP aligns with that of HSPC (~0.5), and a PEGylated headgroup provide 
stealth properties to the liposome, reducing protein adsorption and extending circulation time 
(Islam et al., 2023), and Cholesterol which has a CPP > 1, that when incorporated into 
phospholipid membranes increases membrane order and rigidity, filling space between 
phospholipid tails and enhancing stability (Briuglia et al., 2015).  
The lipid mixture was dissolved by heating at 70ºC (above the phase transition temperature (Tm) 
of the predominate lipid HSPC, with magnetic stirring for 3 hours to ensure complete 
solubilization. These vesicles were subsequently homogenized into small unilamellar liposomes 
by high-pressure extrusion, a technique that forces the liposomes through polycarbonate 
membranes of decreasing pore size (2 x 800 nm, 2 x 400 nm, and 2 x 100 nm), thereby ensuring 
uniform particle size and distribution. 
 
Following liposome formation, gradient dialysis was performed to establish a transmembrane 
ammonium sulphate gradient necessary for the remote loading of EM1. This method facilitates 
efficient loading of EM1 into liposomes, relying on differences in ionic strength and pH between 
the internal and external environments of the liposomes. The liposome and EM1 mixture was 
heated to increase bilayer permeability and diffusion of EM1 into the liposomes during the loading 
process. Following remote loading, a second dialysis step was performed to remove unloaded 
EM1. Remote loading offers significant benefits including high encapsulation efficiency, reduced 
drug leakage, and improved stability of the encapsulated drug, ultimately enhancing the 
therapeutic efficacy of the formulation. 
 
At each process step samples were taken and analysed to monitor lipid loss, liposome size 
distribution and EM1 encapsulation efficiency. The main two analytical techniques applied were 
RP-HPLC and DLS.

Reverse-phase High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (RP-HPLC) with Photodiode Array 
detection (PDA) was utilized to quantify the concentrations of cholesterol and EM1. RP-HPLC is 
based on the separation of components due to differential partitioning between a hydrophobic 
stationary phase and a polar mobile phase, allowing precise and accurate determination of 
encapsulation efficiency and drug loading. More hydrophobic analytes interact strongly with the 
stationary phase and elute later, meaning retention time increases with decreasing polarity 
(Kumar et al., 2023). Detection is performed with a photodiode-array (PDA) UV–Vis detector, 
which continuously scans the UV–visible spectrum of the eluent. The PDA outputs an absorbance 
spectrum for each peak, enabling both quantification and spectral identification or purity checks. 
Endomorphin-1 contains aromatic rings and peptide bonds that absorb strongly in the UV range 
(214–280 nm), allowing sensitive PDA detection. In contrast, HSPC and DSPE mPEG2000 has 
no absorbance in this spectrum. Cholesterol with its conjugated double bond in the B-ring of its 
steroid structure has some UV absorbance and thus was used as a maker for the total lipid 
concentration during production (Kumar et al., 2023).  
 
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) was used to measure liposome size and polydispersity index 
(PDI). It measures the size of colloidal particles (such as liposomes) by analysing fluctuations in 
scattered light caused by Brownian motionhoriba.com. A laser illuminates the sample, and the 
time-dependent intensity fluctuations of the scattered light are recorded. The fluctuations are 
related to the particles’ diffusion coefficient, which is converted to a hydrodynamic diameter using 
the Stokes–Einstein equation (Maldonado-Camargo & Rinaldi, 2016). The result is an intensity-
weighted size distribution of the particles. The measurement was performed using Malvern 
Zetasizer, and outputs a Z-average diameter (the intensity-weighted average size) and a 
polydispersity index (PDI) that describes the width of the distribution. The Z-average is a robust 
measure of average size and is supported as the standard “mean size” reported by DLS. PDI 
ranges from 0 (very uniform particles) to 1 (very broad distribution); a PDI < 0.1–0.2 generally 
indicates a monodisperse sample (Skupin-Mrugalska et al., 2021). These size metrics are critical 
quality attributes for liposomes. Monitoring size and PDI helps detect aggregation or instability, 
as the mean size and PDI should remain consistent over time for a stable liposome batch 
(Bhattacharjee, 2016).  
 
In addition to DLS, the Malvern Zetasizer was also used to measure the Zeta potential of the EM-
1 loaded liposomes. Zeta potential (ζ) quantifies the electrostatic potential at the slipping plane of 
a particle in suspension. It measures ζ by applying an electric field and using electrophoretic light 
scattering (ELS) to determine the particle velocity. The particle’s mobility is then converted to ζ 
using models like Smoluchowski’s. Zeta potential reflects the effective surface charge of the 
liposomes and depends on lipid composition and the surrounding medium (Lunardi et al., 2021).  
Strongly charged liposomes (> +/- 20-30 mV) indicate strong electrostatic repulsion. Strong 
positive charges promote cellular uptake and membrane fusion, while the repelling negative 
charges increase stability but can enhance recognition by immune cells, thus shortening 
circulation. Contrary, neutral charged liposomes risk aggregation. Thus, the optimal charge for 
this projects intended application of delivering EM1-loaded liposomes effectively to MOR 
receptors without promoting membrane fusion or cellular uptake is a surface charge of ~ -5 to -
10 mV (Bhattacharjee, 2016). This charge strikes the balance between stability, prolonged 
circulation, and minimal undesired cellular interactions by ensures minimal non-specific cellular 
interactions. Thereby reducing the likelihood of membrane fusion and endocytosis and enhancing 
colloidal stability.

The final optimized liposomal EM1 formulation was evaluated in vivo using a rat pain model, with 
analgesic efficacy and duration assessed via the von Frey test. It is a widely used method to 
assess mechanical nociception in rodents by applying calibrated filaments of increasing force to 
the plantar surface of the paw until a withdrawal response occurs. It allows for quantification of 
mechanical sensitivity or allodynia, making it suitable for evaluating both hyperalgesia states and 
analgesic drug effects. The method is simple, reproducible, and sensitive to changes in 
nociceptive thresholds following treatment. It can be used to measure both normal nociceptive 
pain thresholds and pathological hypersensitivity, such as mechanical hyperalgesia induced by 
injection of inflammatory agents like carrageenan or CFA (Chaplan et al., 1994). To minimize 
animal harm, an acute nociceptive model was chosen for this project.  
Subcutaneous (s.c.) injection was selected to evaluate if the formulation could support systemic 
administration. Since EM1 has not previously demonstrated efficacy via s.c. administration, 
dosing was guided by results from (Zadina et al., 2024)  who tested the s.c. delivery of EM1 
analog A2 in mice at doses ranging from 0.56 to 5.6 mg/kg with effective antinociception.

and 6 for rats) was used to convert mouse doses to equivalent rat doses (Jacob et al., 2022):

Finally, an in vitro release kinetics study was conducted using mini dialysis devices to evaluate

Since one of the primary goals of liposomal drug delivery is prolonged, controlled release of the 
encapsulated therapeutic, characterizing the release profile provides insight into how long the 
drug remains protected within the liposome and how quickly it becomes available at the target 
site. A sustained or delayed release profile can reduce dosing frequency, improve therapeutic 
efficacy, and minimize side effects. Moreover, for centrally acting peptides like EM1, a gradual 
release may help maintain stable plasma and CNS concentrations, potentially reducing the risk 
of tolerance development. These in vitro studies can also predict in vivo pharmacokinetics and 
help optimizing the formulation for maximum therapeutic benefit (Gómez-Lázaro et al., 2024).

The overall aim of this thesis was to develop and evaluate a liposomal formulation of the 
endogenous neuropeptide endomorphin-1 (EM1) for subcutaneous administration and assess its

4. Methods 
4.1 Liposome manufacturing 
Liposomes were prepared by hydrating a stealth lipid mixture of HSPC, MPEG-2000-DSPE and 
Cholesterol (molar ratio 3:1:1) (registered raw material, code nr: LI-111214-1 (Lipoid 
HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE/Cholesterol (3:1:1) in 100mL vial) in 250mM Ammonium Sulphate (pH 
5.2) to a final concentration of 60mM total lipid in 15mL. The lipid mixture was dissolved at 70ºC 
(above Tm) for 3 hours with magnetic stirring to ensure complete solubilization. The resulting 
suspension was size homogenized by high-pressure extrusion using polycarbonate filters in three 
steps (2 x 800nm, 2 x 400nm, and 2 x 100nm) (IsoporeTM Pore size 0.8, 0.4, 0.1μm PC Membrane, 
Filter, 25mm, Merck) to obtain liposomes of desired size and polydispersity. After each extrusion 
step, 20 µL samples were collected for lipid quantification by RP-HPLC and size analysis by DLS 
using Malvern Zetasizer. For HPLC analysis, samples were diluted 1:200 in MeOH, sonicated for 
10 minutes, and vortexed to ensure liposome disruption. For DLS analysis, samples were diluted 
1:100 in Milli-Q water. 
 
4.2 Dynamic Light Scattering and Zeta Potential 
The size distribution and polydispersity index (PDI) of the liposome formulations were measured 
using Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) on a Malvern Zetasizer Nano ZS. Samples were diluted 
1:100 in MilliQ water to reduce particle concentration and avoid multiple scattering and loaded 
into disposable cuvettes. Measurements were performed at 25 °C with a scattering angle of 173°, 
and each sample was measured in triplicate. The intensity-weighted hydrodynamic diameter (Z-
average) and PDI were recorded to assess liposome size and distribution uniformity. 
 
Zeta potential was measured using the same instrument and cuvette setup, with the addition of a 
dip cell electrode in the cyvette. Prior to measurement, samples were diluted 1:50 in phosphate 
buffer (pH 7.4) to maintain consistent ionic strength and conductivity. Measurements were 
conducted at 25 °C in triplicates, and the zeta potential was recorded from the electrophoretic 
mobility. 
4.3 High Pressure Liquid Chromatography  
Analytical Reverse Phase High-Performance Liquid Chromatography with Photodiode Array 
detection (RP-HPLC PDA) was conducted using an XTERRA RP8 C8 column (5 µm particle size, 
4.6 x 150 mm). The mobile phase consisted of premixed solution of 88.5% methanol and 11.5% 
250 mM ammonium phosphate (pH 5.2), delivered under isocratic conditions. The column 
temperature was maintained at 40°C with a run time of 6 minutes per sample. Endomorphin-1 
(EM1) eluted at approximately 1.7 minutes and cholesterol at approximately 5 minutes. Detection 
wavelengths were set at λ = 205 nm for cholesterol and λ = 280 nm for EM1. 
4.3.1 Quantification of Lipid Concentration 
Cholesterol was used as a marker to determine the total lipid concentration of the liposome 
samples and to monitor lipid loss during production. Two calibration curves were constructed; one 
from a six-step 1:2 serial dilution of the stealth mix (0.016–0.500 mg/mL) and another from a 
seven-step 1:2 serial dilution of pure cholesterol (0.002–0.125 mg/mL). All samples were 
analysed using RP-HPLC PDA at λ = 205 nm, and AUC values were plotted against known 
concentrations to generate linear calibration curves. The cholesterol calibration curve was used 
to determine the exact cholesterol concentration of the stealth mix.

4.4 Dialysis 
To prepare the liposomes for remote loading, the suspension was dialyzed in PBS using Slide-A-
Lyzer Dialysis Cassettes (3.5K MWCO) to establish a transmembrane ammonium sulphate 
concentration gradient. The liposome mixture was injected into the cassette using 18 Gauge 
needle and Omniflex-F 1mL syringe, Braun.  Dialysis was initiated at room temperature in PBS 
(pH 7.4)  using a sink volume ratio of 1:50, with buffer changes every hour for four hours. It was 
then continued at 4 °C in PBS (pH 7.8) at a sink volume of 1:150 with buffer exchanges at 8, 16, 
and 28 hours, for a total duration of 32 hours.  
After completion of dialysis the liposome suspension was collected from the Slide-A-Lyzer 
Dialysis Cassette using needle and syringe. A sample of 20uL was taken and diluted 1:200 in 
MeOH for analysis by HPLC to determine post dialysis lipid concentration and lipid loss from 
dialysis, and 1:100 for analysis of liposome size and PdI by DLS. The measured lipid 
concentration was used to determine the amount EM1 needed for remote loading to achieve a 
1:10 drug to lipid ratio. 
 
4.5 Remote Loading 
EM1 was dissolved in pre-warmed PBS (0.5 mg/mL) and mixed at 70 °C until fully solubilized. 
The liposome suspension was added to the EM1 solution at a 1:10 molar ratio (EM1:total lipid), 
based on post gradient dialysis cholesterol quantification. The mixture was incubated at 80 °C for 
1 hour with stirring to facilitate remote loading. After loading, the mixture was cooled to room 
temperature and dialyzed at room temperature for 4 hours in PBS (pH 7.4, 1:100 sink volume) to 
remove unencapsulated EM1. Samples were collected before and after dialysis for HPLC and 
DLS analysis.

5.4 Encapsulation efficiency  
Three experimental parameters were evaluated for their effect on encapsulation efficiency (EE%) 
of EM1: remote loading temperature, number of dialysis cycles, and concentration of the EM1 
solution used for remote loading. Across all tested conditions, the remote loading duration was 
held constant at 1 hour, and all batches was loaded in a EM1 to lipid ratio of 1:10. 
An increase in remote loading temperature from 60 °C to 80 °C led to a modest rise in EE% by 
3.5%, from 30.8% to 34.2%. Similarly, increasing the number of dialysis cycles from two to four 
resulted in a 2.6% increase in EE%, improving from 34.2% to 36.8%. In contrast, the 
concentration of EM1 used for remote loading had a pronounced effect. Doubling the 
concentration from 1 mg/mL to 2 mg/mL caused a substantial decrease in EE% by -20.5%, 
suggesting solubility issues at higher concentrations. Conversely, lowering the EM1 concentration 
to 0.5 mg/mL increased EE% by 12.6%. These results highlight the importance of ensuring 
complete solubilization of EM1 prior to remote loading, as insufficient dissolution likely limits drug 
encapsulation.

4ºC for 12 hours to study if any loading would occur. These loading conditions resulted in 14.1% 
measured encapsulated EM1 (Figure 7).

Liposomal EM1 (0.5 mg/kg) induced a rapid and sustained analgesic effect, peaking at 60 min

To quantify analgesic effect, mechanical pain thresholds were converted into percentage of

11% at 10 minutes, 16% at 60 minutes, and 3% at 24 hours.

observed in any group throughout the study, suggesting that the liposomal formulation was well

6. Discussion 
The aim of this thesis was to develop and evaluate a liposomal formulation of the endogenous 
neuropeptide endomorphin-1 (EM1) and evaluate its analgesic efficacy by subcutaneous (s.c.) 
administration in a rat model for acute mechanical pain. Furthermore, the project aimed to 
reproduce previous achieved liposomal encapsulation efficiency of EM1 and develop scalable 
production method for EM1-loaded liposomes, identify critical parameters for successful 
formulation and evaluate the effect of liposomal formulation on the pharmacokinetic profile of 
EM1.  
 
6.1 Formulation Development: Interpretation of Key Production 
Parameters 
 
6.1.1 Dialysis is the Major Contributor to Lipid Loss 
Lipid loss during production was a critical parameter for evaluating formulation efficiency and 
scalability. Using cholesterol as a marker for total lipid concentration, a cumulative lipid loss of 
~33.0% was observed, with the most significant losses occurring during the dialysis steps. While 
high-pressure extrusion caused minimal lipid loss (<1%), gradient dialysis and free drug removal 
dialysis each contributed to ~32.1% loss, and ~97.6% of the total loss during production. These 
finding are consistent with literature reporting highest lipid depletion during aqueous processing 
steps, especially in systems with repeated buffer exchanges or long incubation periods. The 
observed lipid loss is considered acceptable, not excessive, and reflective of an efficient 
production process (Adamala et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2024; Zucker et al., 2009).   
 
6.1.2 Avoiding Sonication Improved Batch Consistency and Lipid 
Recovery  
Sonication was initially introduced to lipid hydration and mixing protocol when scaling up for in 
vivo studies, but while sonication effectively reduced vesicle size, it introduced several 
complications. Both sonicated batches measured unexpectedly high cholesterol concentrations 
post-gradient dialysis compared to post-extrusion, suggesting analytical inconsistencies or lipid 
precipitation. Additionally, lipid loss during subsequent processing steps was notably higher, 
possibly due to vesicle destabilization from excessive shear stress or incomplete lipid integration. 
In contrast, the third batch, prepared without sonication, using extended hydration, produced 
liposomes with size and PdI values comparable to the original 40 mM formulation. This batch also 
displayed more reliable cholesterol quantification and reduced lipid loss across processing steps. 
Based on these findings, the non-sonicated 60 mM batch was selected for in vivo and in vitro 
analyses due to its reproducibility, stability, and analytical reliability.  
 
Importantly, the increased lipid concentration did not negatively impact high-pressure extrusion 
performance. The resulting liposomes exhibited a mean Z-average of 133.3 nm (StDev=3.94) and 
a PdI of 0.053 (StDev=0.01), nearly identical to the values observed for the initial 40 mM batch 
(132.9 nm (StDev=1.68) and PdI of 0.053 (StDev=0.01), respectively).  
These results indicate the robustness and scalability of the method, and that the extrusion 
protocol used, sequential passes through 800 nm, 400 nm, and 100 nm polycarbonate 
membranes, effectively produced small unilamellar vesicles (SUVs) with tightly controlled size. 
==PAGE:45==
   
 
Liposomal Delivery System for the Endogenous Opioid Neuropeptide Endomorphin-1 
37 
Consistent liposome size and low PdI are critical parameters for ensuring predictable 
pharmacokinetics and biodistribution, especially for passive targeting via the enhanced 
permeability and retention (EPR) effect in vivo. 
Selecting an appropriate liposome size for parenteral administration involves balancing immune 
evasion and drug-loading capacity. Smaller vesicles are more likely to avoid rapid clearance by 
the mononuclear phagocyte system, while larger vesicles can carry greater payloads and support 
more favourable drug-to-lipid ratios. The achieved size of ~130 nm represents a practical 
compromise between these considerations. Moreover, the consistently low PdI (~0.05) indicates 
a narrow size distribution, which is essential for ensuring reproducible pharmacokinetics, 
prolonged circulation time, and uniform tissue distribution in vivo (P. Liu et al., 2022).  
 
6.1.3 EM1 Concentration Is the Key Determinant of Remote Loading 
Efficiency 
Achieving high and consistent encapsulation efficiency (EE%) is critical for the development of 
effective liposomal formulations. In this study, three key parameters were investigated for their 
impact on EM1 encapsulation: remote loading temperature, the number of dialysis cycles used to 
establish the transmembrane pH gradient, and the concentration of the EM1 solution applied 
during loading. 
Remote loading at 80 °C led to a modest improvement in EE% compared to 60°C, yielding a 3.5% 
increase. Similarly, increasing the number of dialysis cycles from two to four enhanced gradient 
strength, resulting in a 2.6% improvement in EE%. These incremental gains suggest that both 
elevated temperature and extended dialysis contribute positively to transmembrane gradient 
formation and peptide loading. 
The most influential parameter was the EM1 concentration. Lowering the EM1 solution 
concentration from 1 mg/mL to 0.5 mg/mL significantly improved EE% by 12.6% (from 35.6% to 
48.2%), likely due to improved solubilization and reduced aggregation at elevated temperatures. 
Conversely, increasing the EM1 concentration to 2 mg/mL reduced EE% by 20.5% (from 35.6 to 
15.1%), potentially reflecting precipitation or limited loading capacity at higher input 
concentrations.  
 
In addition to EE% the drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio is an interesting parameter to evaluate and was 
used as a secondary marker for formulation quality. A lower D/L ratio is considered favourable as 
it indicates that fewer liposomes remained empty and that the drug is more uniformly distributed. 
The batch with the highest EE% correspondingly had the lowest D/L ratio, further validating this 
interpretation. These findings suggest that a lower D/L ratio may be a useful indicator for 
successful remote loading of small peptides in liposomal systems. 
 
Encapsulation of peptides into liposomal systems remains inherently challenging, and 
encapsulation efficiencies (EE%) are generally modest compared to those achieved with small 
molecule drugs. Compounds with favourable physicochemical properties, particularly weakly 
basic small molecules such as doxorubicin, can reach EE% exceeding 90% via remote loading 
methods utilizing ammonium sulphate gradients (Allen & Cullis, 2013). In contrast, peptides often 
exhibit lower EE% due to their larger molecular size, high polarity, and lack of strong ionization 
sites for active trapping during gradient loading (P. Liu et al., 2022).  
Endomorphin-1 (EM1), a tetrapeptide (Tyr-Pro-Trp-Phe-NH₂), contains only a single protonatable 
N-terminal amine, making it a suboptimal candidate for ion-trapping-based loading. As expected, 
the resulting EE% was moderate, however, the formulation successfully delivered a 
==PAGE:46==
   
 
38 
Liposomal Delivery System for the Endogenous Opioid Neuropeptide Endomorphin-1 
pharmacologically active dose sufficient to elicit a measurable analgesic effect, underscoring 
EM1’s high potency (Jallouk et al., 2015).  
In future studies It would be interesting to explore further improvement of EE% by optimizing 
additional formulation parameters not accessed in this project. For instance, reducing the internal 
pH of the ammonium sulphate solution from pH 5.5 to pH 4.0 may enhance the transmembrane 
pH gradient, thereby improving loading efficiency. Additionally, the method of agitation during 
remote loading may influence liposome integrity and diffusion kinetics. In this study, magnetic 
stirring with high speed was employed, which could have introduced excessive shear stress, 
potentially compromising membrane stability and leading to drug leakage. Alternative agitation 
methods such as gentle orbital shaking or low-speed vortexing could reduce shear forces and 
facilitate improved peptide encapsulation (Nambiar et al., 2024). 
 
6.1.4 Liposomal EM1 Induces Analgesia in Acute Pain Model via 
Peripheral Delivery 
The analgesic response observed following subcutaneous administration of 0.50 mg/kg liposomal 
EM1 suggests effective systemic delivery and bioavailability of the encapsulated peptide. The 
time-dependent increase in the percentage maximal possible dose (%MPE), peaking at 60 
minutes, aligns with the anticipated pharmacokinetics of liposomal drug release and supports the 
hypothesis that encapsulation can extend circulation time. 
The early onset of analgesia (24% MPE at 10 minutes) indicates that a fraction of EM1 may have 
been more rapidly released, potentially due to surface-associated or loosely encapsulated 
peptide. The return to near-baseline levels at 24 hours confirms that the formulation did not induce 
delayed hyperalgesia or allodynia, and suggests the effect was transient but pharmacologically 
relevant. 
Rats receiving free EM1 mixed with empty liposomes exhibited only minor transient analgesia, 
with no sustained effect, which is consistent with EM1’s known short plasma half-life (typically 
<10 minutes) due to rapid cleavage by peptidases (Koda et al., 2008). The slight increase in 
%MPE observed at 24 hours in both EM1 groups could reflect inter-animal variability or 
spontaneous changes in mechanical sensitivity, as no clear analgesic pattern was observed in 
the vehicle control group. Importantly, no treatment group showed signs of hypersensitization or 
impaired locomotor function throughout the study, supporting the safety and tolerability of the 
liposomal formulation. 
 
These findings correspond well with the analgesic effects reported for glycosylated, cyclic EM1 
analogues A2 and ZH853, as well as morphine, by (Zadina et al., 2024), which was used as basis 
to plan the dosing in this project. In their study, mice received subcutaneous injections of 1.0–
5.6 mg/kg. They reported that the 1.0 mg/kg dose of A2 (equivalent to ~0.5 mg/kg rat dose 
(present study’s dose)) produced ~60% MPE, while ZH853 achieved 40% and morphine 15% 
MPE at 60 minutes post-injection in hot plate assay. The analgesic effects of A2, ZH853 and 
morphine peaked at 60 minutes and returned to baseline by 180 minutes; however, A2 exhibited 
a dose-dependent prolongation of analgesic duration up to 5 hours (for doses 1.8-5.6 mg/mL), 
while ZH853 and morphine showed no significant change in effect duration across tested doses. 
Considering their similarity in potency with the same dosage at the same timepoint, it is not 
unlikely the liposomal EM1 formulation could follow a similar effect duration.   
 
Notably, endomorphin-1 is an extremely potent and selective MOR agonist under ideal conditions, 
eliciting powerful antinociception after direct central administration (Varamini et al., 2013; Zadina 
==PAGE:47==
   
 
Liposomal Delivery System for the Endogenous Opioid Neuropeptide Endomorphin-1 
39 
et al., 2016, 2024). The challenge has been attaining similar efficacy via peripheral routes due to 
EM1’s poor BBB penetration. This liposomal EM1 formulation partially bridges that gap by 
extending the peptide’s circulation and enhancing its delivery to peripheral MORs, resulting in a 
level of analgesia that aligns with the literature on engineered EM1 analogues (Schmidt et al., 
2011).  
 
In this study, the duration of EM1’s antinociceptive effect in this study was notably prolonged by 
the liposomal carrier, though the absolute analgesic potency is still undetermined, and additional 
measurements of antinociception between 60 min and 24 hours is necessary to identify the 
duration of effect. Nevertheless, the ability of liposomal EM1 to produce significant pain threshold 
elevations in vivo supports the notion that short neuropeptides can achieve meaningful analgesia 
when delivered via a suitable vehicle. 
Further animal studies with increased sampling frequency, and dose-escalation studies, would 
be valuable to characterize the duration and potency of analgesia and to assess whether the 
liposomal formulation can mimic or improve upon the extended effects observed with other EM1 
analogues like A2. 
 
6.1.5 In Vitro Release Kinetics Fail to Reflect In Vivo Pharmacodynamics 
A successful liposomal formulation must not only encapsulate the therapeutic peptide but also 
retain it and release it in a controlled and predictable manner. In vivo, liposomal EM1 
demonstrated a markedly prolonged analgesic effect compared to the free peptide, suggesting 
sustained release and extended systemic availability. 
In contrast, the in vitro release kinetics did not accurately reflect the observed in vivo 
pharmacodynamic profile. The release study, performed in a dialysis-based setup with PBS at 
37 °C under orbital shaking, showed no detectable EM1 in the filtrate during the first 24 hours. 
EM1 was only detected at the 24- and 48-hour timepoints, accompanied by visible turbidity and 
reduced retentate volume. These changes suggest evaporation had dried out the top side of the 
membrane, potentially allowing intact liposomes to pass into the filtrate. This was supported by 
the co-detection of cholesterol and EM1 in the filtrate, indicating liposome leakage rather than 
controlled peptide release. This discrepancy between in vitro and in vivo behaviour highlights the 
limitations of simple PBS-based release models for predicting peptide release from liposomal 
carriers. Despite the shortcomings of the release assay, the minimal release observed under 
physiological pH and isotonic conditions suggests stable encapsulation of EM1 and good storage 
stability. 
 
Interestingly, although the in vitro setup failed to reproduce the time course of analgesia seen in 
vivo (where peak effect occurred at 60 minutes and returned to baseline by 24 hours), the 
formulation’s behaviour may still align with previous findings. For instance, (Schmidt et al., 2011) 
reported that liposomal encapsulation of hydromorphone extended its epidural analgesic effect 
from approximately 2 hours to 72 hours, suggesting that liposomal systems can substantially 
prolong peptide presence in vivo over 24 hours. 
To improve the physiological relevance, future release studies should consider using biorelevant 
media such as plasma or PBS supplemented with serum proteins like albumin. These 
components better simulate in vivo conditions by interacting with the liposomal bilayer and 
promoting peptide release. However, they also introduce challenges, including enzymatic 
degradation of EM1 and the risk of peptide fragmentation, which complicates quantification and 
analysis.

6.1.6 Critical Evaluation of Encapsulation and Its Implications for Dose 
Accuracy 
Lastly, the measured encapsulation of the cold-loaded liposomes gives interesting insights into 
EM1-liposome interactions and efficacy of dialysis. In principle heating liposome suspension up 
to increase membrane permeability is necessary to facilitate drug loading. Thus the measured 
“encapsulated” drug in this batch might very likely not be encapsulated but surface-associated or 
non-loaded drug insufficiently removed by dialysis.  
This thus raises question of actual encapsulation yield of the other “successfully loaded” 
liposomes. If these conditions (12 hours at 4ºC) could yield encapsulation efficiency 
approximately half of the measured encapsulation efficiency of the similarly loaded liposomes (1 
hour at 80ºC), could half of the encapsulated EM1 in these liposomes in reality also be conjugated 
on the outside of the liposomes or insufficiently removed non-loaded EM1. If postulating that 
similarly to the cold-loaded liposomes ~15% of the measured encapsulated EM1 in the 
formulation used for in vivo evaluation also was non-loaded, the dosage would thus be ~30% 
lower and in reality ~0.35 mg/mL instead of 0.50 mg/kg. We would then be in the very lower end 
of the effective dose range, which might explain the conservation and not significant observed 
analgesic effect of the formulation.  
 
Although one of the triplicate batches achieved an encapsulation efficiency (EE%) sufficient to 
reach the planned EM1 dosage concentration of 0.45 mg/mL, its volume was insufficient to treat 
the entire animal group. Therefore, it was combined with the second-best batch, which had 
similarly favourable drug-to-lipid ratio and EE% but lower concentration. To reach the target 
dosing volume and concentration, the pooled formulation was subsequently concentrated. This 
post-loading concentration step, achieved via centrifugation, may represent a practical strategy 
for addressing EM1’s low aqueous solubility. By allowing loading at a lower initial concentration 
and concentrating the suspension afterward, this approach can reduce formulation losses and 
potentially improve encapsulation stability, as EM1 would be less prone to leakage if it’s solubility 
in the surrounding buffer in minimal.  
 
 
6.2 Broader Scientific Meaning of Results  
 
6.2.1 Opportunities and Limitations of Peripheral MOR Targeting 
Given EM1’s poor blood–brain barrier (BBB) permeability and the current liposomal formulation 
not being designed to cross the BBB, its analgesic effects are likely mediated predominantly 
through peripheral μ-opioid receptors (MORs). Intentionally targeting peripheral MORs with non-
BBB-permeable formulations presents both limitations and therapeutic opportunities. While 
peripheral MOR activation can provide effective localized analgesia, particularly for somatic or 
inflammatory pain, it may be inadequate for more complex or visceral pain syndromes that require 
central modulation. Consequently, this restricted distribution could limit the formulation’s utility in 
systemic or neuropathic pain conditions (Dos Santos Rodrigues et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2019; 
JAFAL et al., 2025; Kato et al., 2023).  
A major advantage of peripheral targeting is the avoidance of central opioid-related adverse 
effects, such as respiratory depression, sedation, addiction, and tolerance. By minimizing CNS 
penetration, the formulation may offer safer analgesia with reduced risk of systemic toxicity. 
Peripheral action may support a localized and potentially slower onset of analgesia. While this 
may be adequate for sustained pain relief, it could be suboptimal for rapid relief of acute pain

41 
flares typically managed via CNS-acting opioids. Passive distribution to peripheral MORs may 
necessitate higher dosing to achieve therapeutic levels, potentially increasing the risk of local 
toxicity. However, liposomal encapsulation can improve tissue retention, prolong circulation, and 
enhance local exposure, especially in PNS-rich tissues such as skin, muscle, and joints 
(Benyamin et al., 2008; Stein & Machelska, 2011).  
 
Liposomal EM1 formulations targeting peripheral MOR could offer a promising strategy to reduce 
CNS-mediated side effects while enabling local pain control. However, peripheral MOR activation 
is also associated with its own side effects, including constipation, urinary retention, orthostatic 
hypotension, and impaired immune function. These effects stem from receptor activity in the gut, 
bladder, blood vessels, and immune system. Thus, while targeting peripheral MORs can reduce 
CNS-related opioid side effects like sedation and respiratory depression, it does not eliminate all 
opioid-associated adverse effects (Benyamin et al., 2008; Stein & Machelska, 2011).   
 
6.2.2 Unlocking Central Analgesia: Intranasal Delivery of Liposomal EM1 
While peripheral MOR targeting offers a safer analgesic profile by minimizing CNS side effects, it 
may fall short in treating centrally mediated or visceral pain. To address the shortcomings of EM1’ 
poor BBB permeability, nasal administration of liposomal EM1 presents a strategy to bypass the 
BBB and target CNS without chemically modifying EM1 but rather modifying the liposome. This 
route enables direct nose-to-brain transport via the olfactory and trigeminal nerves, bypassing the 
BBB and allowing rapid onset of action. Liposomal uptake by BBB can be enhanced by facilitating 
mucosal absorption, optimizing liposome size (~100–200 nm), surface charge (weak positive), 
and mucoadhesive coatings (e.g., chitosan or PEG) to improve uptake and residence time in the 
nasal cavity. With these modifications, intranasal delivery may unlock the central analgesic 
potential of EM1 while retaining the safety advantages of non-systemic administration (Gandhi et 
al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Nguyen & Duong, 2025).  
 
The glycosylated, cyclic EM1 analogue A2, recently reported by (Zadina et al., 2024), 
demonstrated good BBB permeability and an extended analgesic duration of approximately five 
hours. This highlights that promising analogues with improved pharmacokinetic profiles compared 
to native EM1 are being developed. However, the complexity of opioid receptor signalling 
presents challenges. The μ-opioid receptor (MOR) shares close structural similarity with other 
opioid receptors, thus subtle differences in ligand affinity and receptor interaction can lead to 
markedly different downstream effects. Although A2 has shown stronger and longer-lasting 
analgesia than morphine at equivalent doses, its lower MOR affinity and selectivity relative to 
EM1 may pose a slightly increased risk of side effects. 
Despite these advances in analogue development, it remains compelling to explore strategies 
that enable central analgesia using native EM1 itself. As an endogenous neuropeptide, EM1 is 
inherently biocompatible and highly selective for MOR, suggesting that it may produce a more 
physiologically aligned and potentially lower-risk analgesic effect when effectively delivered to the 
CNS. Achieving central delivery of native EM1, via formulation strategies such as liposomal 
encapsulation and targeted nasal administration, may thus provide a uniquely specific, side 
effect–sparing approach to opioid-based pain relief. 
6.3 Neuropeptides as Analgesics: Promise, Challenges, and 
Clinical Translation 
Neuropeptides have gained growing attention as promising analgesic agents due to their ability 
to modulate pain through both central and peripheral pathways. Acting through G protein-coupled

42

receptors (GPCRs) such as the μ-opioid receptor (MOR), neuropeptides including endomorphins, 
substance P, neuropeptide Y, and galanin participate in complex nociceptive signaling networks. 
Their intrinsic selectivity and broad distribution across tissues make them attractive candidates 
for the development of safer alternatives to traditional opioids. 
 
6.3.1 Clinical Potential and Pharmacokinetic Barriers for Neuropeptides  
Endomorphins, are of particular interested, with their high MOR selectivity and affinity, producing 
potent analgesic effects with fewer side effects than morphine in several preclinical models 
(Green et al., 2011). Other neuropeptides such neuropeptide W have also demonstrated 
significant antinociceptive activity, in some cases exceeding morphine in efficacy (Green et al., 
2011). Additional candidates like cortistatin and galanin have shown analgesic potential through 
anti-inflammatory pathways, with reduced central side effect profiles (Yeo et al., 2022).  
Despite their promise, neuropeptides face considerable pharmacokinetic limitations. Most native 
peptides are rapidly degraded in plasma, resulting in short half-lives and low systemic 
bioavailability (Yeo et al., 2022). Their hydrophilic nature and susceptibility to enzymatic 
degradation limit their ability to cross the blood–brain barrier (BBB), a key challenge for achieving 
central analgesia. 
To overcome these barriers, numerous chemical and formulation-based strategies have been 
explored. Structural modifications such as cyclization, D-amino acid substitution, lipidation, and 
glycosylation have shown some success in enhancing metabolic stability and BBB permeability 
(X. Liu et al., 2015; Varamini et al., 2012; Y. Wang et al., 2015; Zadina et al., 2016, 2024). In 
parallel, advanced delivery systems like liposomes and nanoparticles are being developed to 
protect neuropeptides from enzymatic degradation and facilitate targeted release in the CNS 
(Blades et al., 2023; Oppong-Damoah et al., 2019; J. P. Wang et al., 2025). Functionalized 
nanocarriers, particularly those modified for mucosal adhesion or receptor targeting, may 
significantly improve drug accumulation in the brain via routes such as intranasal administration. 
 
6.3.2 Endomorphins and the Long Road to Clinical Translation 
Among the opioid peptides, endomorphins (EM-1 and EM-2) are uniquely compelling due to their 
exceptional MOR selectivity and endogenous origin. Theoretically, centrally delivered EM1 could 
offer highly specific analgesia with fewer off-target effects. Yet, nearly 30 years after their 
discovery, neither native endomorphins nor their analogues have reached clinical approval. 
This translational gap stems from several persistent challenges including; 1) metabolic instability: 
endomorphins are rapidly degraded in plasma and gastrointestinal fluids, rendering oral 
administration ineffective and limiting systemic delivery 2) BBB penetration: their poor lipophilicity 
restricts CNS access, and although analogues conjugated with glycosyl groups or cell-penetrating 
peptides show improved brain penetration, these remain in preclinical stages; 3) regulatory and 
market hurdles of inconsistent efficacy, patient variability, and competition from established opioid 
drugs have all slowed clinical investment and progression, as new opioid-based analgesics faces 
heightened regulatory scrutiny and reduced industry interest due to the opioid epidemic, requiring 
stronger safety profiles, more innovative delivery strategies, and clear differentiation from 
conventional opioids to advance clinically and attract investors (X. Liu et al., 2015; Volkow & 
Blanco, 2021).  
 
6.3.3 Drug Delivery Innovations and Future Perspectives  
For peripheral pain conditions such as post-operative or inflammatory pain, non-BBB-permeable 
liposomal EM1 formulations may offer potent localized analgesia with reduced central side effects

such as sedation and reward-related behaviours. This could allow patients to manage pain more 
effectively without experiencing cognitive or motor impairments that interfere with daily activities 
(Jafal et al., 2025; Schmidhammer et al., 2023).  
For neuropathic and nociceptive pain that requires central modulation, future strategies may 
involve combinational delivery systems. Encapsulating EM1 in chitosan-coated liposomes 
conjugated with BBB-targeting ligands could enable CNS-specific delivery, enhancing therapeutic 
efficacy in pain states where central MOR engagement is essential. Additionally, exploring 
liposomal formulations of EM1 analogues offers a promising avenue for improving 
pharmacokinetics. By reducing the rate at which active drug is presented to the receptor, 
encapsulation may mitigate the risk of tolerance development and receptor internalization (Wu et 
al., 2023).  
In particular, the glycosylated, cyclic EM1 analogue A2, reported to provide analgesia for up to 
five hours, could serve as a strong candidate. If liposomal encapsulation were to further prolong 
its duration or reduce dosing frequency, it might represent a valuable treatment option for chronic 
pain with a reduced risk of tolerance and side effects (Zadina et al., 2024).  
 
Finally, successful clinical translation will require more than pharmacological innovation. Given 
the heightened regulatory scrutiny surrounding opioid development in the context of the opioid 
crisis, future analgesics must be designed with safety in mind. Strategies such as limited CNS 
access, abuse-deterrent properties, and exploitation of endogenous opioid selectivity can help 
position EM1-based therapies as part of a new generation of targeted, safer analgesics. When 
combined with smart delivery technologies, these innovations may ultimately enable neuropeptide 
therapeutics to offer effective pain relief with a significantly improved safety profile.

7. Conclusion 
 
This study successfully demonstrated proof of concept that native Endomorphin-1 (EM1) can be 
formulated into a viable systemic therapy through encapsulation in stealth liposomes for 
subcutaneous administration. The analgesic effect observed in rats following injection of the 
liposomal EM1 formulation provides strong evidence that this strategy can aim at overcoming key 
limitations of EM1 connected to its innate neuropeptide stability issues. 
 
In vivo evaluation confirmed that a single s.c. dose of liposomal EM1 produced a measurable and 
time-dependent antinociceptive effect in rats, supporting the formulation’s ability to improve 
analgesic efficacy and demonstrated prolonged duration of action compared to unformulated 
peptide. Encapsulation efficiency was systematically evaluated and reproduced with consistency 
across batches. The formulation achieved an average drug-to-lipid ratio of 1:7 and an 
encapsulation efficiency of approximately 48%, validating and building upon the research of 
previous MSc student Girisa Prabhu. Physicochemical characterization confirmed uniform particle 
size (~130 nm), low polydispersity index, and stable size distribution, indicating robust formulation 
performance.  
 
Together, these findings highlight the therapeutic promise of liposomal encapsulation for 
neuropeptides like EM1, offering a feasible delivery platform that enhances stability and in vivo 
potency. While further optimization is needed to improve loading capacity and assess the 
translational potential, this study provides a solid foundation for continued preclinical development 
of EM1 as a next-generation analgesic.
