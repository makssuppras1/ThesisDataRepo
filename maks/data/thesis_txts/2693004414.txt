Abstract
In this report, I present a technology which can be used for lab automation integrated with a
microﬂuidic device, which has shown to work with hydrophilic in hydrophobic spot patterns
for digital ELISA applications. The goal is to detect Alzheimer’s disease biomarkers present in
patients with pre-clinical Alzheimer’s disease, a stage where no symptoms appear. The report
presents a short review over the different biomarkers and diagnostics tools, which have been
used for pre-clinical Alzheimer’s diagnostics. The lab automation investigated in this thesis
uses 3D printed models integrated with LEGO bricks and LEGO Mindstorm computing. A
set-up was successfully designed, which enabled sampling of one to six samples through the
process of segmented ﬂow. The lab automation was integrated with a microﬂuidic device and
an automated assay was carried out successfully. The integration, however, still needs further
optimisation as not all channels were working at all times and there was still a need for manual
handling of the microscope. Microdroplet array wafers were successfully fabricated and the
characteristics matched previous work on the fabrication [1]. The microdroplet array wafers
were integrated into the microﬂuidic device and droplets could be visualised in brightﬁeld and
by ﬂourensce of resoruiﬁn and Fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC). However, due to issue with
fouling of the surface, cracking of wafers and signal noise the microdroplets arrays was not
implemented in biological automated assay. An automated assay was successfully carried out
on an epoxy modiﬁed glass wafer illustrating the potential of using the system for automated
assays. This was proven that biological assays can be carried out using the lab automation with
a bulk assay enzymatic reaction of horseradish peroxidase (HRP). The system could detect
HRP binding to an epoxy modiﬁed surface and detect different concentrations of substrate.
For Alzheimer’s relevant diagnostic assays it would be necessary to optimise the system and
implement digital ELISA for detection.

Introduction
Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60-80% of all
dementia cases [2, 3]. Dementia affects 47 million people in the world, with a high social cost of 1
trillion USD annually and is said to be one of the greatest health issues of the 21st century [4].
AD was ﬁrst described by Alois Alzheimer in 1906, where it was discovered in an autopsy of a
dement woman that there were two alterations in her brain [5].These alterations were found to be
neuritic plaques caused by an accumulation of Amyloidβ(Aβ) and neuroﬁbrillary tangles (NFT)
formed by hyperphosphorylated tau protein [6]. AD is a neurodegenerative disorder and the
neuritic plaques ultimately lead to synaptic degradation and nerve cell death [8]. These damages
are irreversible, making current treatments based on delaying the progress of AD and on reducing
some of the symptoms [2, 4]. The underlying mechanism for the development of AD has yet to
be proven, however the neuroﬁbrillary plagues, have been seen to inﬂuence disease development
greatly. It is also proposed that proinﬂammatory responses, mitochondrial dysfunction [9] and
oxidative stress-induced apoptosis [10] inﬂuence the development of AD, leading ultimately to

There is a huge potential in discovering AD as early as possible. A wide spread diagnosis of AD
before the symptoms arise, will provide numerous new insights of this complex disease and may
lead to new treatment options which delay the appearance of the ﬁrst symptoms or prevent the
disease all together [11].
Some of the biomarkers involved in AD is thought to arise up to 20 years before the ﬁrst cogni-
tive symptoms appear [2], however the current diagnosis consists of either expensive or highly
invasive procedures. Diagnosis is therefore only carried out on patients with clear indications
and symptoms of AD, however the damage caused by the disease can not be repaired unless di-
agnosis is carried out before symptoms arise. Currently the established diagnostic tools are not
yet suitable screening tools to use on a large symptom free population [12].
It is therefore crucial with early diagnostic tools, which will enable screening on high risk pop-
ulations for the appearance of Alzheimer’s disease. Currently, modern diagnostics face several
difﬁculties, due to that fact that Alzheimer’s pathology is located in the brain and testing often
require a sample from cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF) or neuroimagning (i.e., MRI and PET)[13].
It is possible to detect changes in AD biomarkers 20 years prior to the onset of AD from CSF sam-
ples. A biomarker is evidence of biological, pathogenic or pharmacogenomic changes which can
provide indication of the disease. These can be chemical biomarkers such as protein, chemicals
and miRNA or instrumental biomarkers such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) [14]. Aβ,
tau protein and phosphorylated tau are established biomarkers for AD. If we want more frequent
testing of potential AD patients and by using biomarkers, it is necessary to move away from CSF
samples and toward serum samples. CSF samples are not suitable for general scanning since
they are obtained by lumbar punctures, which cause nausea, severe backaches and weakness and
due to the requirement of skilled and trained personal, leading to a high cost of resources for the
hospitals [14].
In order to accomplish the ideal of early diagnostics, it is necessary to establish a procedure that
uses a sample that is accessible and as non-invasive as possible, in which it is possible to detect
an onset of AD from established biomarkers.
In this project I will investigate and validate a new platform for lab automation, which ideally can
be used to detect biomarkers of Alzheimer’s disease from a blood sample.

1.1
Alzheimers’ Disease
1.1.1
Stages of Alzheimer’s
The symptoms of Alzheimer’s Disease is characterised by three groups of symptoms; Cogni-
tive and executive dysfunction, psychiatric symptoms and in instrumental difﬁculties (difﬁcul-
ties performing everyday task such as complex task as driving a car or simple tasks as putting
on clothing)[3].
Alzheimer’s Disease can be divided into 5 stages by the cognitive dysfunc-
tion symptoms; Pre-clinical stage, prodromal stage, early stage, moderate stage and late stage
of Alzheimer’s disease.
Figure 2: Different stages of Alzheimer’s disease [2, 15].
Of these ﬁve stages, four of them have symptoms of cognitive dysfunction. The symptoms arise
at prodromal stage, which has also been referred to as mild cognitive impairment (MCI) [15]. At
prodromal stage, patients experience impairments affecting memory, verbal and learning abilities
and the stage is often mistaken as normal ageing or stress, however can be distinguish with a
suitable cognitive test [12, 15, 16]. During the early stage of Alzheimer’s disease there are patients
which experience increasing difﬁculties with memory, verbal abilities and learning and some may
have difﬁculties with language, perception and motoric movements. During this stage the patient
can still perform individual tasks with little to no supervision [16]. At moderate stage of the
disease patients will at most parts be hindered in common daily activities such as clothing and
bathing and will mostly be reliant on caregivers. Cognitive functions are also reduced greatly
with reduced vocabulary, loss of reading and writing skills and impaired long-term memory [16].
At the late stage of AD the patient will be completely dependent on caregivers and will experience

loss of verbal language abilities [16].
Interestingly, changes at microscopic scale are seen prior to the onset of symptoms in AD. These
changes are thought to appear up to 20 years before the ﬁrst symptoms and be increasingly ap-
parent as time goes by [2, 17] (ﬁgure 3). At pre-clinical stage an abnormal decrease in CSF Aβ42,
an increase in CSF tau protein can be detected and at prodromal stage there is evidence that sug-

abnormal levels of Aβ similar to the measurements of CSF. PET scans also show a decreased
metabolism of glucose [2]. However, it is important to note that these changes are not present in
all cases of AD, and more thorough research in the underlying mechanisms of AD is necessary,
before it is possible to make a diagnosis prior to the onset of dementia symptoms [2, 19].

1.1.2
Diagnostic parameters.
Currently, diagnosis is performed in combination of assessing cognitive functions and measuring
a few AD related biomarkers such as amyloid-β from CSF samples or PET scans, CSF samples for

tau-protien or Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) scannings. Genetics tests may also be used to
some extent, however, results with a good predictive values are limited to rare autosomal domi-
nant mutations such as mutations in the amyloid precursor protein, presenilin 1, and presenilin
2 (APP, PSEN1 and PSEN2). Common mutations generally still have limited clinical utility and

Previously, diagnostics were primarily or exclusively reliant on the assessment of cognitive func-

functions primary relies on evidence on cognitive defects. It is mostly possible to distinguish the
cause of dementia (such as frontotemporal dementia, Alzheimer’s Disease or other forms of de-

the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) developed by Folstein et al. [22] other tests are also
developed for memory function, executive functions and instrumental functions [12].
However, with the current types of diagnostics it is only possible to conﬁrm AD when the de-
mentia is present [18]. If diagnosis is to be used as a preventive measure for AD it is necessary
to conﬁrm AD in preclinical stages before irreversible symptoms develop. It is therefore required
for a more predictive correlation between biomarkers and AD.
1.2
Biomarkers for Alzheimer’s Disease
Biomarkers are measurable indicators which indicate the state of a biological or pathological con-
dition [23, 24]. An ideal biomarker of AD should detect a fundamental feature of neuropathology
and be validated in conﬁrmed cases. An early and effective detection of biomarkers may also be
beneﬁcial for future research and clinical studies if the biomarker can detect the effect of disease
modifying therapy [25].
The gold standard for AD diagnostics, which among others is recommended by the National on
Aging-Alzheimer’s Association is the use of CSF, PET and MRI [26]. PET is used for amyloid

PET imaging, PET tau imaging and FDG-PET hypometabolism. MRI is used to detect atrophy in
speciﬁc brain regions. CSF is used to quantify the concentration of these biomarkers to test for
abnormalities [27]. CSF sampling is done through lumbar punctures and it is a fairly established
process, however the process can lead to contraindications, can cause nausea, severe backaches
and weakness and require human resources to carry out.
These methods have their limitations, regarding broad population screening. The main issue is
that these methods are highly expensive and invasive, and therefore they are only used to conﬁrm
the diagnosis when there is a high certainty of dementia. [11, 24] The problem is that at this stage
the symptoms of dementia are already appearing and with current treatment the disease can not
be reversed, only the speed of progressing can be slowed. [11]
Blood-based biomarkers may be a solution in order to avoid expensive and invasive diagnostics
[11, 24, 27]. The following biomarker have shown promising potential for diagnosing Alzheimer’s
disease at early stages. The biomarkers with most potential are currently A-β40, A-β42, APP, Tau
protein and Neuroﬁlament light (NfL).
1.2.1
Plasma Aβ
Amyloid-β(Aβ) and soluble peptides forms of Amyloid Precursor Protein (APP) are among the
most relevant biomarkers for AD, as they are highly involved in the disease progression [28].
Amyloid-β originates from APP, a membrane bound protein. APP is cleaved into 3 fragments
(sAPPβ, Aβ and C99) by β-secretase and γ-secretase. sAPPβ is located extracelluar and cleaved
by β-secretase, whereas C99 is located intracelluar and cleaved by γ-secretase. This process sep-
arates Aβ from APP and is released in the extracelluar space in two different sizes Aβ40 and
Aβ42. An abnormal cleavage of APP will lead to Aβ42 tend to aggregate in oligomers forming
neuroﬁbrillary plagues [29], while Aβ40 still is dismantled [29, 30].
Abnormal concentrations of Aβ40, Aβ42 and APP show promising value as biomarkers for AD,
however with some controversies regarding patient to patient variations. Instead, the ratio of
Aβ42 with APP669−711 or Aβ40 show high diagnostic value from plasma samples. [31]. Studies
have shown an inverse association between Aβ42/40 plasma ratios and ﬁbrillary Aβ deposition in
the brain [32].

1.2.2
Plasma Tau
Tau proteins function as a stabilising component, by binding to the microtubules in the cytoskele-
ton of neurons. Tau proteins for AD patients show increased hyperphosphorylation, which leads
to self-assembly of tau proteins. Hyperphosphorylated tau proteins are also called p-tau. The
increased rate of self-assembly leads to neuroﬁbrillary tangles (NfT) [29].
Tau protein is generally a late stage biomarker, which reﬂects the state of damage dementia has
caused. It is quite established for CSF samples with an increased levels of total tau (t-Tau) and
phosphorylated tau (p-Tau) compared to healthy controls [33]. The concentrations of tau protein
are known to gradually increase with age <300pg/ml (21-50 years) to <500 (>71 years). For AD
cases this concentration is signiﬁcantly increased in AD patients of about >450 to >600 pg/ml (in
patients of ages 51-70 years) [14].
Phosphorylated tau (P-Tau) with the speciﬁc epitopes p-tau181,p-tau199,p-tau231, are elevated in
AD, but not for many other neurodegenerative diseases [11, 14, 34]
It is somewhat troublesome to detect tau protein in blood samples, since it is degraded at a higher
degree in blood, due to the presence of proteases [24], but assays have shown an correlation
between increased levels of p-tau and AD-related phenotypes [35].
1.2.3
Other potential biomarkers
Plasma NfL can be used in case of multiplexing or broad screening as it has been observed to be
present in elevated concentration for all neurodegenerative diseases [36]. Changes in plasma NfL
has been observed 10 years before symptoms of AD arise [36].
miRNA’s are being increasingly investigated as biomarker candidates [14], such as AD-related
genes, like APP, PSEN1 and PSEN2, however miRNA still requires further investigation, since
there has been seen inconsistencies when comparing CSF levels to serum levels [37]. An interest-
ing future biomarker target may be neuronal exosomes, which are released from neuronal cells
during the endocytotic processes. Due to their formation pathway, exosomes found in serum have
been seen to contain proteins and miRNA from the parent cell in a similar concentration as CSF.
Exosomes which have a diameter of 30 nm to 100 nm and contain unique membrane proteins,
can be used to isolate and investigate neuronal exosome from a complex sample [38, 39, 40]. Ex-
osomes have been proven to cross the Blood-brain barrier (BBB) with its content remaining intact

neuronal exosomes may solve issues regarding brain disorders as they contain an isolated envi-

1.2.4
General consideration for Blood-based diagnostics
Enzyme linked imunosorbent assay (ELISA) is currently the gold standard for ﬂuid biomarkers in
AD. However, while being able to quantify Aβ and Tau concentration in CSF, where concentration
is relatively high, using ELISA in blood samples is not sensitive enough.
There are several issue related to the use of blood sampels for measuring for the current biomark-
ers, due to the blood-brain barrier. For the biomarkers to be able to enter the blood stream speciﬁc
pathways are required, thus limiting the diffusion of biomarkers from CSF to blood. The volume
of CSF ﬂuid and blood is also signiﬁcantly different and if the concentration is low in CSF ﬂuid, it
is even lower in the blood due to the blood:CSF volume ratio. The concentration of Aβ42 and tau
is 30 to 100 fold lower in plasma than CSF [23, 27, 36, 43, 44].
There are both biological and technical considerations for CNS-related biomarkers. As an exam-
ple, it is necessary to consider that the sample matrix from blood is different from the matrix
from CSF samples. Firstly, the protein content is more complex in blood than in CSF which may
interfere with the chosen assay [45]. Secondly, biomarkers, such as tau, may undergo proteolyic
degradation at a higher degree in blood, which may decrease the sensitivity of the assay [24].
In conclusion, the diagnostics of AD using blood-based biomarkers require highly sensitive and
selective diagnostic tools, in order to handle the complex sample matrix and low concentration of
analyte.

1.3
Alzheimer’s Disease Diagnostics
The assay analysing the biomarkers should have high sensitivity and speciﬁcity, in order to en-
sure that a diseased person is detected and that healthy and diseased people are distinguished. A
good biomarker should have a diagnostic sensitivity and selectivity of at least 80% [25, 46].
The platform should aim to be highly reproducible, simple to handling to avoid handling errors,
non-invasive and inexpensive in order to reach a broad population [25]. In cases where the di-
agnosis suggests that the patient is prone to AD, then the disease will be conﬁrmed with more
time-consuming and invasive protocols. There isn’t that much damage except for the inconve-
nience of the patient. A misclassiﬁction of a patient in pre-clinical stage, will result in the disease
being untreated until it is found by dementia symptoms.
1.3.1
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay
A common diagnostic technique used to quanify biomarkers is Enzyme-linked immunosorbent
assay (ELISA). The assay is from 1970’s and is often used as the gold standard diagnostic protocol
for many biomarkers [47]. ELISA is used to detect antibodies or analytes in a sample. The assay
is designed in a way in which the antibody recognises and binds a sequence in the analytes,
typically proteins or peptides.
The ELISA can broadly be divided into ﬁve different ELISA techniques (ﬁgure 5). A typical sand-
wich ELISA is carried out by immobilising a capture antibody to the bottom of a plastic well plate.
A sample is added to the well-plate with an analyte recognized by the capture antibody. The an-
alyte is ﬁxed to the assay and the sample matrix is discarded through repeated washing steps.
A detection antibody conjugated with an enzyme is added to the assay with a speciﬁc epitope
recognising the analyte. The enzyme enables the catalysis of a speciﬁc substrate, which can be
detected either by a colourimetric change, ﬂuorescent or luminescent signal [47] depending on
the enzyme and substrate. The change in signal is compared to the negative control without the
analyte and can be used to quantify how active the detection enzyme and thus how concentrated
the analyte is in the sample.
Direct ELISA is the simplest and cheapest, but often s puriﬁed samples as there is a risk of other
proteins to interact with the surface. While, a competitive ELISA, sandwich ELISA and double
sandwich ELISA are more complex and expensive, but have a greater speciﬁcity towards the
chosen analyte[47].

1.3.2
Digital ELISA
Digital ELISA (dELISA) is an assay based on the standard ELISA, but where the signal is digital.
The assay consists of numerous compartments, where a signal would either be present or not.
This assay can be used to quantify an analyte if the amount of analytes in a partition normally 0
or 1 [49], unlike with standard ELISA techniques where the signal intensity is measured [47]. The
analytes will arrange in a Poisson distribution and it would therefore be possible to calculate the
concentration from the number of spots that show a signal and the number of spots which do not
show signal [49, 50].

The technique is designed to improve the limit of detection (LOD) compared to the previously
mentioned ELISA (section 1.3.1), so it is possible to quantify analytes normally impossible in a
standard ELISA [49].
dELISA is based on micro-compartmentalising and in this report two versions of dELISA will be
introduced; Bead-based dELISA and Hydrophilic-in-hydrophobic dELISA (HiH-dELISA), whereas
the latter will be investigated for the lab automation.
1.3.3
Digital Counting
dELISA is based on microdroplet arrays (MDAs), where samples are analysed in micro-scale and
partitioned. These partitions are typically encapsulating single molecules which are detected by
labelling them with an enzymes-conjugated detection probe [51]. This principal was ﬁrst intro-
duced in 1961 by Rotman, referred to as digital counting, as a technique where one essentially
counts the number of molecules [51]. Enzyme produces ﬂuorescent products, which due to the
limited volume in the encapsulation result in high local signals [50, 52]. For example if a reaction
with galactosidase in 50 fL of 100 µM substrate, which lead to approx. 5600 molecules of resoruﬁn
within 30 seconds corresponding to about 200 nM in concentration, which is easy to detect on a
ﬂuorescent microscope. In contrast a standard ELISA uses much larger volumes (approx. 100 µL),
which means the amount of resoruﬁn needed for a clear signal is million-fold higher in order to
release the same signal intensity [50, 52]. The principle of digital counting has shown a 1000-fold
greater sensitivity than conventional ELISA techniques [50].
In order for the dELISA quantiﬁcation to work the number of analytes encapsulated in partitions
should be zero or one, which occurs when the concentration of analytes in a sample concentration
is sufﬁciently low. In this case the assay will have a single molecule resolution [52]. It is also
possible to calculate the number of molecules when the array becomes saturated, by assuming
the percentage of partitions follow a Poisson distribution [52].
MDAs were ﬁrst characterised with suspension of droplets through liquid-liquid segmentation
in microﬂuidic channels, but can also be generated on a planar surface. The advantage of MDAs
in planar surface is that the location of the droplets is ﬁxed, which enables speciﬁc partitions. It is
also possible to scale the fabrication of HiH-dELISA wafers, making them suitable for multiplex-
ing [53, 54].

1.3.4
Bead-based digital ELISA
A commercially available technology using bead-based dELISA is SIngle-MOlecule Assay (SIMOA)
with a platform named Simoa Hd-1 Analyzer. The assay is described by Rissin et al. 2010 [49] and
Wilson et al. 2016 [55]. SIMOA is based on immunocomplexes formed on beads. In a Sandwich
ELISA, capture antibodies are immobilised to a magnetic bead. The analyte to bead ratio is small,
typically <1:1, thus the number of beads containing a labeled immunucomplex follows a Poisson
distribution. The formation of immunucomplex is done in reagents tubes outside of the device
and is loaded to the machine, SIMOA Hd-1 Analyser. Once loaded the magnetic beads with a
diameter of 2.7 µm, it will enter femtoliter-sized reaction chambers, with a diameter of 4.5 µm
and depth of 3.25 µm.
The enzyme substrate is added to the wells and the wells are sealed with either rubber gasket or
oil. This will lead to bright spots in the spot array, illustrating a successfully formed immunocom-
plex [49, 52]. The ﬁeld of view from the camera covers thousands of microwells, so a large number
of single molecule signals can be detected. The counting of active and inactive corresponds to the
presence or absence of single enzyme molecules. [49].

Aims
The overall goal of the project is to investigate and validate a new platform for lab automation,
which potentially can be easily accessible for academic, clinical and industrial applications. The ﬁ-
nal format of this platform, is designed to integrate simple LEGO generators, a 3-D printed clamp,
wafers (that can be fabricated outside of cleanroom facilities) and a relatively cheap microscope
platform. These criteria are chosen in order to ensure a cheap, fast and available platform.
In this project I will establish and optimise a protocol for lab automation. I will validate the sta-
bility and reliability of the lab automation.
The lab automation system will be integrated with a microﬂuidic device and this process will be
validated.
I will fabricate wafers patterned with hydrophilic-in-hydrophobic spots for microdroplet array
usage and ﬁnally establish a proof-of-concept for a running assay using the microﬂuidic device
integrated with lab automation.

3.3.2
Peristaltic pump
The peristaltic pump (7c) consists of one LEGO MINDSTORMS® Education EV3 Large Servo
Motor and a 3D printed rotor system. The peristaltic pump works using positive displacement by
rotating a number of rollers in a closed system squeezing a ﬂexible tube against the roller lid. This
creates a vacuum between the rollers in the tubing allowing the movement of ﬂuid/air though
the tubing. Due to the positive displacement mechanics for peristaltic pumps, this pumping type
belongs to a category of pumps called positive displacement (PD) pumps. The pump contains 6
rollers and was closed with a matching lid. Silicon tubing was placed across the 6 rollers, though a
matching indent in the lid. The silicon tubing was changed every couple of experiments (approx 2
weeks of experiments), due to tearing of the tubing. The peristaltic pump is connected to a LEGO
motor with a 8 teeth gear. One round is equal to 360°turn on a small 8 teeth gear, the small gear
is connected to a larger gear of 32 teeth which turns the peristaltic pump rotors. The gear ratio is
1:4, meaning that it takes 4 rounds on the motor to turn the peristaltic rotors one full round.

Micro-droplet array (MDA) wafers were fabricated using a protocol designed earlier in FAST,

The fabrication of micro-droplet arrays follows a standard UV photolithography procedure as il-
lustrated in ﬁgure 16. The process starts by labelling a 500 µm thick 2” borosilicate glass wafer an
ID number using a diamond pen, in order to enable referring to the wafer, while also indicating

which side was up for the remaining wafer processing. The borosiliciate wafer was dehydrated on
a hotplate set to 200°C for at least 30 minutes. The wafer was cooled down to room temperature
and placed in a manual spin coater (SPIN150i-NPP Single Substrate Spin Processor, SPS-Europe
B.V., The Netherlands). Between 1 mL and 2 mL negative photoresist AZ® nLOF 2020 was dis-

on the mask holder, which is illustrated on ﬁgure 16C, the photomask was lightly placed on top

µm and a pitch of 20 µm. The spin-coated wafer was exposed to 365 nm light for 30 seconds with
an exposure intensity of approximately 13 mW/cm2. After exposure the wafer was placed on a
110°C hotplate for post-baking for 2 minutes and afterwards submerged in AZ 726 MIF Devel-

with microscopy AxioObserver Z1 (ZeissTM, Germany) or Dinolite edge AM4115T-GRFBY(Dino-
Lite Europe, Netherlands). The wafer was treated with oxygen plasma with a power of 990 W for
15 min. This was done to remove residuals of uncured resist and increase the concentration of
hydroxyl groups, which improves the following silanization process. The plasma-treated pattern
was submerged in 0.4% (v/v) Trichloro(1H,1H,2H,2H-perﬂuorooctyl)silane (PFOCTS) in Novec
7200. Finally the nLof spots were removed during a lift-off process where the wafer was placed
in a beaker containing NMP and sonicated at 60°C for 20 min. After this process the wafer now
contains a hydrophobic monolayer of PFOCTS with 10nm hydroﬁlic glass spots.

4.1
Characterisation of lab automation.
The goal for the lab automation is ultimately to carry out a dELISA, with only few sample han-
dling steps. Assays can be time-consuming and require trained hands to be carried out, which a
lab automation will prevent and it would be possible to standardise assays using computer gen-

sampled volume was investigated. The LEGO Mindstorm program was designed with varying
rotations of the pump, in order to ﬁnd the optimal setting.

4.2
Microﬂuidic integration
The microﬂuidic device was made using 3D printed parts and a casted PDMS gasket. These parts
are all easily accessible and quick and cost effective to produce. One of the advantages in using
3D printed parts is that it is easy to prototype different parameters to investigate how well the
device works. The microﬂuidic device which was inherited from Arianna Toppi, from the FAST
team, needed to be optimised the prevent cracking of wafers when mounting.
In order to prevent this a PDMS support gasket was designed and casted. The PDMS gasket
distributed the clamping force more evenly across the wafer, to prevent the wafer from cracking.
The wafer did not crack after the implementation of the support gasket, however there were
leakages in the system. The depth of the gasket seat was adjusted to optimised the clamp force in
an effort to provide an adequate sealing of the PDMS channels.
Different combinations of gasket depths were investigated to avoid cracking of wafers and leak-
ages in the microﬂuidics. Wafer holders with different trench depths were printed on Formlabs
3D printer with black resin. The trench depth was prototyped from 0 mm from the surface to
1 mm. The functionality was tested by placing the peristaltic pump after the clamp, creating a
system where ﬂow through the tubes was only possible if the microﬂuidic system was air tight.
The clamping pressure was seen to be uneven for the left, middle and right channels. The edges
often worked quite stable, while the middle channels were more prone to leakages. During an
investigation of the 3D printed models it was observed that the 2.5 mm thin bottom clamp was
bending (appendix 1).
Due to this observation, different thickness of the bottom clamp were prototyped. The clamps
were printed with a thickness of 3 mm, 4 mm and 5 mm, where the 3 mm bottom clamp showed
clear bending, 4 mm showed slight bending and 5 mm did not show visible bending. It was
not possible to use thicker bottom clamps as the working distance of the microscope was shorter
than the thickness of the clamp. The microﬂuidic device showed uneven leakages for all three
thickness, but as expected was more consistent with the 5 mm than the 3 mm. In order to use a
thicker bottom clamp together with 10x objective on the Zeiss AxioObserver Z1 microscope a re-
modelling was needed. The microﬂuidic device was adjusted to contain a 4 mm thick main body
and 3 mm wings, limiting the bending and making imaging possible (see appendix 1 for CAD
design). The 5 mm bottom clamp was not used in order to avoid risk of scratching the objective
lens, which may happen if the bottom clamp is lowered too far in the set-up. Unfortunately, even

Cracking of wafers was prevented by adjusting the gasket height. However, there was always

The support gasket was optimised, however, instead of prototyping trench depths the PDMS
support gasket were casted in different heights, from 0.1mm above the holder surface to 0.7 mm
above. Wafers were still cracking after the switch to aluminium, and this was found to be due to
aluminium residue on the edges which came from the milling process, which caused the wafer
to bend. Fortunately this was easily ﬁxed by polishing the surface with sandpaper. The ﬂuidics
worked without PDMS support gasket, however, there were a few wafers which cracked. This
was ﬁxed by using a PDMS support gasket with a height 1.3 mm (0.3 over the recess holding the
wafer). The current set-up still needs further optimisation as cracking of wafers occasionally still
occurs. It is believed to be due to misplacing the wafer slightly in the recess. As of now, the design
is not as user friendly as intended and more work on the design is therefore needed.

4.5.3
Differences compared to dELISA
A bulk assay was used to prove that a biological assay would work with the lab automation
integrated with the microﬂuidic device. However there were some signiﬁcant differences to the
dELISA making it unsuitable for the diagnosis of pre-clinical AD biomarkers. An assay moving
from the bulk assay to dELISA moves from a digital output to an analogue output, where signal is
dependent. Assays using the bulk assay require a reference negative control, which is subtracted
the enzymatic assay, while a digital assay relies on a droplet count. The output digital ELISA is
not dependent on negative control. The negative control in bulk assay were seen to have high
variance (ﬁgure 37).
One of the major advantages for HiH-MDA is that there is a deﬁned water/air barrier separating
the droplets from the environment. This is not the case in the bulk assay platform, was sample is
in contact with the resin in the clamp and the PDMS channels. This may cause unspeciﬁc binding
or contaminations and therefore large sample to sample variations.
The volume is also one of the main signiﬁcant differences between bulk and droplet platforms. As
the volume is signiﬁcantly higher for the bulk assay than an individual droplet, it requires many
fold more resoruﬁn molecules to give a measurable signal. The volume of the droplets generated
in this project (diameter of 11.73 µm) have a calculated volume of 425 fL assuming the droplet is
a half sphere. Compared to the sample which is carried out with 20 µm and 9µm in the channel,
a 107 fold higher volume.
Bleaching might also effect the bulk assay as the format is exposed to a 574 nm light in a timelapse.
This would not be the case for a droplet assay, however a bleaching effect may reduce the signal
in some droplets impacting the threshold value for accepting a droplet signal.
The bulk assay is expected to be able to function together with different ELISA types (see ﬁgure
5) and with proper optimization it might work with the same preformance as plate reader based
ELISA. However, compared to dELISA, a bulk assay is expected to have a signiﬁcantly higher
limit of detection, making it unsuitable for pre-clinical AD biomarkers. In contrast many of the
limitations would not be present with a dELISA using a hydrophilic-in-hydrophobic spot pattern.
The HiH patterning offers several advantages, it is fairly simple to fabricate and conduct assays
with, the assay produces numerous partitions and it does not require micro-mechanical system
or an oil cover to seal, which other similiar technologies require. However, before HiH-MDA can
be implemented, the surface fouling must be handled, so that droplets can be counted properly.

Future perspectives
The lab automation system can broadly be divided into two parts; An auto-samplinger which
enables segmented sampling from six different samples and a microﬂuidic platform. Due to the
module like set-up it is easy to adjust the different parts. For example the autosampler and pump
can be remodelled to accommodate parallel sampling. The autosampler is ﬂexible and can be in-
tegrated with other platforms than the microﬂuidic device used in this project. The microdroplet
device can be used with wafers modiﬁed with different patterns, such as Fluoracryl 3298-based
microdroplet arrays or PEG-lipid based microdroplet array investigated by Arianna Toppi (un-
published PhD [58]). In conclusion, the platform enables fast prototyping of new assays, fabrica-
tion and automation protocols.
One of the goals that was not accomplished in this project, was to integrate Dinolite edge micro-
scope in the assay. The lab automation and microﬂuidic device would have huge potential, if it
can be proven that a dELISA can be detected using a Dinolite edge microscope or microscope in
similar price range. This would indicate that the application is cheap and accessible and would
be a suitable replacement for expensive and bulky instrumentation as we see with the SIMOA
HD-1 analyser [55]. A successful implementation would be the ﬁrst step for a cheap and portable
automated dELISA kit, which could be used as a point-of-care device or lab automation.
Before the technology can be tested with clinical samples and validated for clinical diagnosics,
there are a few issues found throughout this project, which would need to be investigated.
One of the ﬁrst experiments that should be carried out, is an investigation on the washing re-
quired for a stable assay. Optimal washing for both bulk assays and MDA, would lead to more
reproducible and less variance for more reliable assay. The next step would be to implement HiH-
MDA wafers in the microﬂuidic device. A proof-of-concept of detection with the HiH-MDA has
already been established in work from Arianne Toppi (unpublished PhD thesis [58]). This need
to be reproduced in the lab automation system and it may be relevant to conduct experiments on
ﬂowrates, gasket materials and cleaning protocols to conduct a optimal assay.
The technology has great potential for analyte analysis in the future. Wafer fabrication were
produced at affordable prices and with commonly available lab equipment. The process is also
scalable for commercial application in the future. The lab automation is easy to implement with
low expertise requirements, and can be implemented in a sampling instrument, in case of assay
kit or point-of-care applications.

Conclusion
Detection of Alzheimer’s disease in pre-clinical stage would allow for a bigger time frame for
treatment therapies. This has previously not been relevant since a diagnosis of Alzheimer’s dis-
ease requires the presence of mild cognitive impairments. However, new knowledge of plasma
biomarkers in the pre-clincal stage may pave the way for a new diagnostic strategy for Alzheimer’s
disease. An early diagnostics requires a platform capable of detecting pre-clinical stage biomark-
ers with tools that can be used on routine, with minimal handling of skilled personal. The poten-
tial of using lab automation for the detection of pre-clinical Alzheimer’s disease biomarkers were
investigated in this thesis.
The ﬁrst goal of this project was to establish and optimise a protocol for lab automation. In the
project the stability and characteristics was investigated. The lab automation was successfully
designed to sample from up to six testtubes and the relation between rotations of the peristaltic
pump and sample was calculated. 5.6 µL was sampled from each full rotation on the pump. The
pump was calibrated to move ﬂow to the channels of the microﬂuidic device.
The lab automation was integrated with a microﬂuidic device, enabling the use of patterned 2”
wafers. Gaskets and 3D models were optimised for an even clamping force applied the wafer.
Hydrophilic-in-hydrophobic spot array were fabricated succesfully and integrated in the mi-
croﬂuidic device, however issues of fouling and signal noise prevented integration with the lab
automation. Success droplet arrays were proven possible with the lab automation, visualised in
both brightﬁeld and by ﬂuoresce of resoruﬁn and FITC.
It was proven possible to run an assay using the microﬂuidic device integrated with lab automa-
tion. An assay of HRP immobised to a glass wafer showed that it was possible to detect an enzyme
activity of HRP with HRP conjugated antiTau antibodies immobilised to an epoxy modiﬁed sur-
face, however a high concentration was needed for this. The assays had a linear range between 1
nM and 2.5 nM. It was also possible to distinguish different concentrations of Ampliﬂu Red from
a concentration series. Finally, it was seen that the washing used for assay can be optimised for
further assays. A detection of pre-clinical biomarkers using this platform may be possible, how-
ever, it would be necessary to optimise the protocol and integrate microdroplet array wafers for
a low limit of detection.
