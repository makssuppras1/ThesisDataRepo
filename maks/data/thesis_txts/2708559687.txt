Abstract
The Internet of Things is a buzzword and a hot topic at the moment and a great amount of
research is directed at it. The big areas of interest are privacy, security and energy efficiency. In
this thesis we propose a novel approach of using micro-firewalls in multiple nodes in a Wireless
Sensor Networks instead of the traditional approach of using a firewall at the gateway. Using
the Contiki-NG operating system and the Cooja simulator we perform several experiments to
investigate the benefits of micro-firewalls. We find and report energy savings of up to 20% in
terms of radio transmission and up to 40% in CPU usage, and discuss other benefits in terms
of reduced interference and increase of data throughput. This proves that micro-firewalls are a
worthy solution with significant potential benefits in all kinds of IoT networks.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The Internet of Things (IoT) is more relevant than ever, with an estimated number of 12.3 bil-
lion active endpoints by the end of 2021 according to IoT analytics n.d. Despite the slowdown
caused by the COVID-19 pandemic and the chip shortage, the forecasted number of connected
IoT devices is 27.1 billion by 2025, more than doubling the amount of devices, as seen in Figure
1.1.
Figure 1.1: Number of connected IoT devices. Source IoT analytics n.d.
The Internet of Things can refer to a lot of devices, such as “smart devices” which are electronic
appliances connected to the Internet or even smartphones could be considered part of IoT,
and in this thesis we are mainly looking at microcontrollers connected with sensors. These de-
vices are limited in the amount of RAM and ROM they posses, with the most constrained having
less than 1 KiB and 100 KiB respectively, referred to as Class 0, and the more powerful having
around 50 KiB and 250 KiB respectively, referred to as Class 2, as described by Bormann, Ersue,
and Keränen 2014. Another important aspect about these devices is the energy consumption,
because they are usually battery-poweredandexpectedtolastfora longamount of time before
needing manual intervention.
Such devices are employed in Wireless Sensors Networks (WSN), which consist of a number of
IoTnodesworkingwithsensorstocollectandtransmitdatatoacentrallocation, wherethedata
is stored and used for further analysis. The number of IoT devices, referred to as “nodes”, can
range from a few to hundreds or thousands, and usually they are all connected to each other
in a mesh network. In such a network, there exists one or multiple gateway node that all the
nodes send data to, and which in turn send that data further to its final destination, that being
the cloud or any other place for storing and analysing it. One possible network protocol used
for such networks is RPL, described in Alexander et al. 2012, which creates a tree-like structure,
with the root node being the gateway.

1.2
Background
Despite their immense potential, the IoT devices have a number of issues, one of them being
privacy. Since these devices are usually installed in people’s houses they have access to a lot
of personal information, such as one’s habits or other private data, and because these devices
are on all the time, they can always record and collect this data. And even if the IoT devices
do not have audio or video monitoring capabilities, they still have access to other crucial data,
that when combined with modern machine learning techniques can infer and reveal sensitive
private data. For example, smart light bulbs and thermostats usually do not have any A/V data,
but they can give away more sensitive information, such as sleep patterns or home occupancy.
Most users of IoT devices describe convenience and connectedness as the main reasons for get-
ting them, as examined by Zheng et al. 2018. And even though consumers still worry about the
privacy issues these devices poses, they believe the benefits outweigh the downsides, and also
that the privacy threats are not that big. However, the risks are bigger than most people expect,
as data can be leaked through means of hacking and not only legitimate collection of data. As
presented by Yang et al. 2017, the IoT devices have a large attack surface on a number of layers,
and since these devices are resource constrained, security might not be feasible in some situ-
ations. And as such, private data can be accessed by malicious actors and used for nefarious
actions such as surveillance of unsuspecting users or for blackmailing.
Another less concerning threat is the collection of private data for “personalisation and im-
proved usability”, which is also widely used for targeted advertising. Big Tech companies such
as Facebook and Google rely on users’ data for advertisement, and make billions of dollars ev-
ery year by doing so. To avoid data collection, one can choose to opt out of it, but that is difficult
because every single device that has access to the internet has to be configured to only send
and receive the data needed for it to function, and nothing more. That is why there are more ef-
ficient solutions such as Pi-hole1, which is a network-wide ad-blocker and privacy enhancer. It
works as a local DNS server, blocking requests to known advertisement and tracking domains,
thus making it more difficult, but not impossible, for devices on the network to connect and
send personal data. Of course, this solution is not always working, because devices could use
fixed IP addresses or use their own preferred DNS to bypass Pi-hole. Also, since the content of
the actual data send and received is not examined, devices could send legitimate data to the
same destination as the personal data, and nothing would block it. Still, it is a very efficient way
of ensuring more privacy from IoT devices.
1.3
Problem Statement and Goals
One of the most important factors about these devices is how long it can run before the battery
becomes depleted. To improve that, one could have larger batteries or make the system more
energy efficient, so it consumes less energy. According to Akyildiz et al. 2002, energy expendi-
ture in data processing is much less compared to data communication, and this is why we will
be focusing on reducing the energy used on data communication.
In a tree-like mesh network, the gateway is usually more powerful than the normal nodes, be-
cause it has to connect to the outside internet and run more computationally expensive task
such as a full IP stack and firewall. Firewalls are especially important, not only to prevent an
attack from the outside, but also to protect the data coming from inside the network. In the
event that a node is sending data that it should not, such as personal data or it is simply mal-
functioning, it is usually the gateway’s job to block the data from going anywhere. However,
the problem with that is that the data has already travelled all the way through the network,

summarise, the research questions are:
• What are the benefits of using micro-firewalls instead of firewalls?
• When is it worth to use micro-firewalls?

Literature Review
In this chapter we will do a literature review of related works, in order to have a better under-
standing of the current research in this area. This will include some firewalls prototypes for
WirelessSensorNetworks(WSN),aswellassimilartechnologiesforimprovingsecurityinWSNs.
2.1
Firewall rules
Firewall research has a number of specific focus areas, and one of the main topics concerns the
definition and implementation of firewall rules. Since the firewall rules define the behaviour of
the firewall, then it is crucial to get them right to ensure they achieve what they were intended
to do, and also to do so in an efficient manner. We will present a few papers that are concerned
with the firewall rules.
Onechallengewiththefirewallrulesistheordertheyhavetobeplacedin. Inafirewall, apacket
has to be matched against all the rules, until it either reaches one that matches the conditions,
and if no conditions are matched, then the default action is applied. This means that the order
of the rules also matters, and not only the rules themselves. Ideally, the most used rules would
be the first ones to avoid extra computation by matching more rules than necessary, but it is not
always as simple as that. As shown by Hamed and Al-Shaer 2006, the rule ordering optimization
problem with rule dependency constrains is NP-complete.
In Hamed and Al-Shaer 2006, a novel technique is presented for optimizing firewall filtering
policies depending on the Internet traffic characteristics. Since the problem is NP-complete,
the authors propose a heuristic algorithm that takes an filter list and returns a new optimized
filter list based on the current traffic characteristics.
In Gouda and Liu 2004, the authors propose an algorithm for checking and ensuring the consis-
tency, completeness and compactness of firewall rules. More precisely, given a set of firewall
rules, the algorithm ensures that the rules do not contradict each other, reduces or merges the
rules that have the same effect, all that while ensuring that the overall effect of the firewall has
not changed. This can be applied in our case of using micro-firewalls, when merging or diverg-
ing micro-firewalls from node to node. This way, we could ensure the minimal amount of rules
in order to maximize energy efficiency, while also achieving the desired effect of blocking the
unwanted traffic.
2.2
Power Saving in Wireless Sensors Networks
In Min et al. 2001, and investigation into wireless sensor networks is presented. They focus
specifically on the importance of power aware computation and network communications, and
how the different parts of the hardware and software affect the power costs. It is one of the first
papers to address the topic of power consumption in WSNs, and even though some parts of it
are outdated by now since it originally came out in 2001, the key ideas and concepts remain
relevant even today.
In Pantazis and Vergados 2007, a classification and a comprehensive survey concerning pas-
sive and active power control mechanisms is provided. Our approach of using micro-firewalls
is novel and distinct from the other works, as they mainly focus on the network organisation,

turning off the transceiver when there is no network activity, or improving the efficiency of net-
work protocols at MAC, network or transport layer. We are not doing anything like that, instead
we are minimizing the data that is sent over the network.
In Cardei et al. 2002, a new method for organizing the network in a set of disjoint sets is pro-

2.3
Micro-firewalls in Wireless Sensors Networks
In Hossain and Raghunathan 2010, a lightweight firewall for WSNs is proposed, that allows the
filtering of messages by destination and also by module type, where a module is a predefined
function of the operating system. They implement a proof-of-concept using the SOS operating
system for two cases, namely isolating an intruder in the network, and also for creating a virtual
overlay over the physical overlay of the network. The work is used on top of the related paper
Kothari et al. 2008, which presents a tool for providing fine-grained visibility and control of the
software running on the IoT nodes, but it focuses on the interaction between nodes instead of
the interaction between modules. They focus on the actual firewall implementation, which is
not a part of our research.
In Ma et al. 2006 an algorithm is described for having mobile agents in the WSN that detect
and isolate malicious node from the network. The mobile agents do that by moving to the
neighbour nodes of the malicious node, and cutting of the traffic by rerouteing it through non-
malicious nodes. Our work is different as we do not have special agents in the network, we rely
on broadcasting the firewall rules from the gateway in order to maximise energy efficiency.
In Yi et al. 2012 a solution is presented, where the WSN is split into clusters, and each cluster has
an assigned decision node which is responsible for monitoring and isolating malicious nodes. It
claims to be more energy efficient that traditional solutions because only the nodes in a cluster
need to be aware of the malicious node, instead of the entire network. It is related to our work
because it touches on the energy efficiency of firewalls, but the difference is that we focus on
broadcasting the firewall rules from the gateway, instead of locally between nodes.
2.4
Micro-firewalls in other contexts
In Hwang and Gangadharan 2001 the design experience and research findings of using micro-
firewall inside a network of computer nodes are reported. They use a Policy Manager and Gate-
way Firewall at the introduction point of the Switch Network, in order to supervise and broad-
cast security policy changes to the nodes. Furthermore, they use and compare three methods
for Security-Policy Updates, namely Mobile Agents, CORBA Middleware, and RMI Middleware.
This paper is similar to our research because we also use a Gateway that is responsible for man-
aging the nodes, but the difference is that they focus on nodes that are PCs or servers running
Linux, which are much more powerful than the resource constrained embedded devices that
are used in WSNs. Thus, we cannot use RMI or CORBA, as they would introduce too much over-
head on our devices.
In Jin et al. 2002 Distributed Dynamic Self-immunity Security Architecture is presented, which
consists of a Distributed Micro-Intrusion DetectionSystemand a DistributedMicro-FirewallSys-
tem. However, they only provide the overview of such a system, and do not provide a concrete
use case for the system.

In Bagheri and Shameli-Sendi 2020 a new idea about outsourcing a firewall into multiple micro-
firewalls, anddistributingthemindifferentlocationisdiscussed, butinthecontextofthecloud.
In such this context, there is one main server that serves as the point of entrance to the cloud,
and is connected to a number of “cloudlets”. Just like in our case of a WSN, the gateway is
responsible controlling the internet traffic coming in and out of the cloud, but they focus on
the incoming traffic. The point of decomposing the firewall from the gateway to the cloudlets
is to remove the load on the gateway in case of high traffic. Although their goal is different than
our project, it is similar in the idea of moving firewalls rules from one host to a multitude of
other hosts.
In Chandradeep 2010, a novel approach for protecting groups of computers in an intranet is
presented. Using an Intrusion Detection System, one can detect malicious activity in an inter-
nal network of computers, then use micro-firewalls on the individual machines to protect them
from unwanted traffic inside the network, implemented using the Linux firewall, IPtables. That
way, eveniftheinternalnetworkgetscompromised, thecomputerscanstilldefendthemselves.
This is a similar idea to what we are trying to achieve by blocking traffic inside the network,
but there are different limitations while using personal computers running an operating sys-
tem such as Linux, versus the constrained device used in a WSN. Also, their goal was to protect
computers from future compromised, while we strive to minimise energy usage.
2.5
Summary
Even though there is a lot of research going on into firewalls and into energy efficient WSNs,
our approach of using micro-firewalls is a novel one. As far as we could find, no one has inves-
tigated the use of micro-firewalls for energy saving in WSNs. Because of this, we did not have
much related literature to base our assumptions upon, and we mostly used the literature for
the design of our system. Our main takeaways are the following:
• Energy saving in a WSN can be achieved in a number of ways, because there are a lot of
factors that influence the energy consumption.
• Firewalls can also be optimized in a lot of ways, the most common one is by changing or
rearranging the rules.
• Our research should focus on getting concrete data on how to measure the effectiveness
of the micro-firewall.

3.1.2
Storing and using rules
There are two main types of firewalls, stateless and stateful. Stateless firewalls are the most
basic type, as they only look at every network packet individually, without regard to the traffic
that has happened before. Stateful firewalls, however, keep track of the established connec-
tions in a device, and make a decision on a packet depending on the rule it matches and the
state of the connections in a device. The latter type of firewall is more comprehensive and can
be used for more specific rules and situation, but requires more memory, because all the con-
nection details have to be kept in memory, and also has a higher computational cost because
it has to take more factors into account when making a decision about a packet. So, we will be
only looking at stateless firewalls for our design, for simplicity reasons, but also due to memory
and computational constraints.
With a normal firewall, storing and enforcing the rules is not a problem. Since devices running
a firewall are usually not resource constrained, one can store a large amount of rules without
being concerned about the memory requirements. Also, even though going through all the fire-
wallrulesmighthavehighcomputationalcosts,thisisnotaproblemagain,becauseofpowerful
processors. In our case however, our devices are severely constrained both in terms of memory
and processing power. As mentioned before, the RFC written by Bormann, Ersue, and Keränen
2014 defines three classes of constrained devices:

There are many different ways of storing firewall rules, depending on what part of the traffic is
inspected. The most common firewall rules for an IPv4 host using TCP or UDP are the source
and destination IP address and port. An IPv4 address uses 4 bytes, and a port number is limited
to 2 bytes, thus the total amount needed to keep a firewall rule is 12 bytes. These memory
requirements are defined in the RFC for TCP and UDP, which are found in Transmission Control
Protocol 1981 and User Datagram Protocol 1980 respectively. The same can be done with IPv6
addresses instead of IPv4, but requiring 8 bytes per IP address instead of 4.
Another way to store firewall rules is to look at the media access control address (MAC address)
of the devices instead. This is especially useful for IoT devices, as some microcontrollers have
network cards with built-in firewalls for MAC addresses. Still, even when that is not an option,
the firewall can be implemented in software and the address size is 6 bytes, as defined in 3rd
and Abley 2013.
To better illustrate how many rules a given IoT device support, we can simplify and assume
that a device running an operating system and a given program take 80% of the available RAM,
leaving us with only 20% to store firewalls rules, we would have 1 KiB, 2 KiB and 10 KiB RAM free
for devices of Class 0, 1 and 2 respectively. Thus, the maximum number of rules we could store
depending on the device and rule type is shown in Table 3.2.

3.2
Implementation
3.2.1
Operating System
The first choice we had to make was how to setup and run the system used for testing the effi-
ciency of a micro-firewall. Of course, there was the option to run code on bare metal, without
any operating system on top, but that would require implementing all the things needed for the
system, such as a networking stack, threads, etc., which would basically mean writing an mini
operating system, and that was not the goal. As such, we had to decide between a few popular
options.
RIOTOS,describedinBaccelli, Hahm, etal.2013andBaccelli, Gündoğan, etal.2018isamodern
operating system designed for IoT devices. It implements a microkernel architecture, and it
supports multi-threading, the full set of the C language, as well as C++. It also supports several
networking protocols, including RPL and 6LoWPAN. It has a small code footprint, requiring only
~1.5kB of RAM and ~5kB of ROM.
Zephyr OS, presented in Zephy project n.d., is another operating system designed for IoT de-
vices. It implements a monolithic kernel, and it also provides all the other required features
such as multi-threading, networking, etc. With a basicconfiguration, the Zephyr OS uses ~1.3kB
of RAM, and ~7kB of ROM.
ContikiOS,originallypresentedinSrisathapornphatandShen2002, andthenforkedasContiki-
NGinContiki-NGn.d.,isanoperatingsystemwithanevent-drivenkernel. WewillrefertoContiki-
NG as Contiki for simplicity reasons. The code footprint is ~10kB of RAM and ~100 kB of ROM.
One feature of Contiki-NG that sets it apart from the previous operating systems is the inclu-
sion of Cooja, source Cooja simulator n.d., which is an open source network simulator. It makes
it easy to simulate networks and to deploy code, and it also includes Energest, described in
Dunkels et al. 2007, which is a module for implementing lightweight, software-based energy
estimation approach for IoT devices.
In the end, we chose Contiki because of the inclusion of the Cooja simulator. Even though the
other options were also compelling for a number of reasons, the inclusion of a network sim-
ulator makes it a lot easier to write and deploy code for testing quickly. Using real devices
would imply connecting and setting up the network for every experiment, which would be a
big overhead and would limit the amount of experiments we can run. With Cooja, experiments
can be saved and then modified with little effort, and that also means that versioning control
is possible. Furthermore, since the code written for Cooja is usually not platform specific, the
experiments can be run in the real world if the time frame allows it.
3.2.2
Network Simulation with Cooja
The network simulator Cooja, originally proposed in Osterlind et al. 2006, is a flexible Java-
based simulator designed for simulating networks of sensors. It can run native code for the
platform on which the simulation is run, but it can also emulate a sensor node on the hardware
level. The former option uses less memory and it is easier to configure and deploy, as it does
not have any ROM or RAM limitations, and can be used for prototyping or for checking if the
codecompiles. However, formoreaccurateandrealtests,thehardwareemulationisneeded,to
makesurethat the deployed code can fit in the constraintsof thedevice, and also forsimulating
the energy usage of the mote.
In Eriksson et al. 2009, the accuracy of power profiling using Cooja and its hardware emula-
tor MSPsim is compared against real world experiments. Contiki’s power profiler “essentially
measures the activity time of each hardware component and multiplies it with the current con-

3.2.3
Energest
Energest, originally described in Dunkels et al. 2007, is a software based on-line energy estima-
tionmechanismthatestimatestheenergyconsumptionofasensornode. Themechanismused
is an intentionally simple linear model, which measures how long the specific components of
the board have been running, times the current draw of them. Because of the simplicity of the
model, the mechanism has a small overhead, only adding a few hundred bytes of code and less
than 50 bytes of memory. Furthermore, the processing overhead is only around 0.7%, which
has a negligible energy overhead. Despite this, testing using real nodes, they show that the
software based solution has an accuracy close to the real world.
3.2.4
Firewall mechanism in Contiki
As discussed in the design section about firewall rules, we can not afford to store a lot of rules
because we might run out of memory, and even if we do not, the computational costs overhead
mightbetoobig, slowingdownthenetworktrafficandconsumingmoreenergy. IPv6addresses
are 16 bytes long, and they need to be kept in RAM to ensure the micro-firewall is not too slow,
meaning that we can not have hundreds of rules in the micro-firewall. A better solution is to
keep only the IPv6 subnetwork prefix, and then use only the least significant bytes as the node
identifier, and the number of bytes depends on how big the network is. For example, using an
IPv6 address such as:
fe80:0000:0000:0000:c30c:0000:0000:0001
We can save the prefix of the network, and use only the host ID:
fe80:0000:0000:0000:c30c:0000:0000:0001
where the blue part marks the prefix and the green part marks the host ID. This gives us up to
216 = 128 nodes in our network. Although the IPv6 specification defined in Deering and Hinden
2006 defines that the interface identifier (node ID in our case) to be 64 bits long, we can set a
custom prefix to ensure only the last 16 bits differ from node to node.
And since Cooja’s firewall mechanism functions as a hook function that gets called every time a
packetisincomingoroutgoing, wehavefullfreedomtodecidehowthefirewallrulesarestored.
The most straightforward way for a set of rules would be to have all the IDs stored as unsigned
16bit integers in an single-linked list, that can be updated dynamically by adding and removing
Micro-Firewalls for Efficient IoT Networks
15
==PAGE:24==
rules. This would require 16bits for the node ID, plus 16bits for the pointer, since we are using
16bit microcontrollers. This is not the most memory efficient method, but it is quite simple to
implement and it is robust in the cases where the ID size changes.
The most efficient way to keep the rules in terms of memory would be to only keep 1bit of mem-
ory for every node in the network, since there are only two possibilities for a network packet:
to be processed or to be dropped. And that could be done by having an array of 8bit integers,
where every bit in the integers corresponds to a node ID, i.e. the first integer would determine
the firewall rules for the first 8 nodes, the second integer would be responsible for the next 8
nodes and so on. The reason we use 8bit integers is because one byte is the smallest possible
type in most C implementations. The problem with this approach is the extra complexity, as we
would need 16 integers to store a network with a maximum of 128 nodes, and the network size
would be only configurable with a minimum step size of 8, again because of the limitation of
the smallest type.

Discussion
In this chapter, we will discuss the project, reviewing the idea that went into this thesis, the
results that came out of the experiments we have done and what they mean.
5.1
Pros and cons
As seen from the results in Chapter 4, micro-firewalls prove to be useful in decreasing energy
consumptioninaWSN.Itcanbeseenthatthepositionofthemicro-firewallinthenetworkplays
a significant role in how efficient it is. Intuitively, the closer the micro-firewall is to the node that
needs blocking, the more energy we save. Also, the larger the amount of data the nodes send,
the more worth it is to run a firewall. By trading in some computational power, we can reduce
the network traffic in the WSN, and also consume less energy.
Ifwelook atthe totalenergy saved, itisnotthatmuch, lessthan0.2%inthebestcase. However,
that is because of the MAC protocol used. Since the goal of this thesis was to investigate micro-
firewalls, the lower level networks protocols were outside the scope. And because of that, we
chose carrier sense multiple access (CSMA) as our MAC protocol since it is the easiest one to
work with and is the default in Contiki. Unfortunately this protocol is bad in terms of energy
efficiency, sinetheradiocardhastobeturnedonallthetimetolistenformessages. Thatmeans
every time we should save energy by avoid forwarding a packet, we spend the same amount or
sometimes even more energy by having the radio card in listening mode. This can be seen from
the results in Figure 4.11, there is an almost 20% difference in radio transmission in the nodes
that benefited from a micro-firewall, but less than a percent of total energy savings. If we were
using a more sensible MAC protocol that does not have the radio card on all the time, we would
see similar gains in the total energy saved instead of only the radio transmit.
Andeveninthecaseswherethetotalenergysavingsisnotthatgreat, arestillotherbenefits. For
one, sending less data implies there is less interference in the network meaning that there will
be fewer collisions due to the amount of traffic. Also, there are cases in which the benefits of the
micro-firewall are not only energy based, such as in the case of LoRa or Narrowband Internet of
Thins (NB-IoT).
LoRa is a low-power long-range modulation technique which can be used to create a WSN. One
key aspect about LoRa is that in Europe they have 0.1% and 1.0% duty cycle per day depending
on the channel. Since this is a very small duty cycle rate, it might be beneficial to filter the data
with a micro-firewall and only send useful data. Furthermore, there is a class of LoRa devices
(Class C) that are always listening and are often powered and not energy restricted and in that
case it is always worth it to use a micro-firewall, because the extra computational power spent
on the micro-firewall will result in a large data throughput.
NB-IoT is another low-power radio technology that can be used for WSNs. It focuses on indoor
coverage, low cost, long battery life and high connection density. It relies on cellular services,
and it it is usually priced per the amount of data transferred. Because of this it might be more
economically viable to use micro-firewalls to avoid sending unnecessary data.
However, the micro-firewall might be detrimental to the energy consumption if not configured
properly. As seen from experiment 5, the total energy consumption can be higher with a micro-
firewall, because it requires extra computations for every packet that goes through the node.
In a situation where a node has to process a large amount of traffic, a micro-firewall that does
Micro-Firewalls for Efficient IoT Networks
29
==PAGE:38==
not block any data but has to inspect every packet will end up wasting energy without any ben-
efits. In the same manner, even if a micro-firewall would be useful in some situation, it could
be implemented wrongly or inefficiently, making the computational costs higher than the net-
working costs.
Aswithanyextrafeatureitaddsextracomplexityandcanmakethedevelopmentprocessslower.
In a large network of nodes, keeping track of the network structure and the nodes that need to
blocked is not easy, so it might be easier to only have a firewall at the gateway instead of a num-
ber of micro-firewalls. In this case it is a complexity efficiency trade-off, and as all trade-offs, it
is not always worth it.
Another potential problem is the possibility of false positives. If a node sending useful data
would be categorized wrongly, a micro-firewall would destroy the useful data. This could also
happen if a micro-firewall started malfunctioning in a node that forwards data from multiple
other nodes. In general, since a micro-firewall is limited because of the resources available, it
means it is less flexible than a normal firewall.
5.2
What could be improved
The idea of using a micro-firewall in a WSN is a new concept, which was not explored in the liter-
ature before. The other works which we have looked at in Chapter 2 fall in two major categories,
either using micro-firewalls with devices that are not resource constrained, or using them with
in WSN to isolate malicious nodes. Our work is different because it is focused on resource con-
strained devices and on exploring the benefits of the micro-firewalls rather than using them for
a specific purpose.
And because of this novelty, there are a numbers of choices that went into this project that
could have been done better. Getting familiar with Contiki and setting up the simulation took
longer than expected, reducing the time left for actually performing the experiments and get-
ting more data about the micro-firewalls. Despite the simulations being quite basic, getting
to understand the networking protocols and how to set up the firewall was not trivial. Another
idea that took a significant amount of time but did not happen in the end was on how to update
the rules dynamically. The idea was to have a TCP socket listening on every node, that would
receive commands about activating or deactivating the firewall, adding or removing rules and
so on. However, after a number of failed attempts of implementing that, it was decided to focus
instead of doing more experiments with the current setup, in order to get some kinds of results
at least.
5.3
Future work
One of the first things that could be improved to truly explore the energy benefits of the micro-
firewall would be to use a different MAC protocol. Since this was outside the goal of thesis, we
chosethedefaultprotocol, whichisalsotheeasiesttoworkwith, CSMA.Thenetworkcardbeing
on all the time diminishes the energy savings of the firewall, so a new MAC protocol that takes
this into account would have do be used. CSMA is useful in protocols such as Wi-Fi and Blue-
tooth, which do not take energy into account, but it does not make much sense in an energy-
sensitive application. Contiki supports Time Slotted Channel Hopping (TSCH), which is a MAC
protocol that turns on the network card for listening only during specified intervals. Using TSCH
the energy efficiency of micro-firewalls could be measured more accurately.
Another improvement that can be done is increasing the functionality and configurability of
the firewall. As mentioned above, an attempt was made to do that, but to no avail. If the micro-
30
Micro-Firewalls for Efficient IoT Networks
==PAGE:39==
firewall could be configured to only run when needed, and as close as possible to the node
that needs blocking, then we would see the greatest benefits in terms of energy saving. Fur-
thermore, this would allow us to temporarily block and unblock nodes, which could be used
for reconfiguring the graph of the network, or blocking specific messages based on their tim-
ing. Also, the firewall could be expanded to not only inspect the source and destination of the
packet, but also the content. Since data is usually not encrypted inside a WSN, doing packet
inspection would be fairly trivial and could be used to block data that is wrong or should not
leave the network.
The rule storage of the micro-firewall is yet another area that has a large potential for improve-
ment. In the scenario we talked about, we looked at one of the simplest ways of storing the
rules, in a scenario where we know in advance the network structure and the node’s ID. In a
real world scenario, it is not always possible to know the network structure in advance, and it
is also changing constantly because node keeps connecting and disconnecting, and because
of the interference, the graph of the network changes to adapt to that. So it would be benefi-
cial to keep rules in a smarter way, that can deal with network changes efficiently. Also, if the
micro-firewall would be expanded to include multiple rule types, it would be needed to find an
efficient way of transmitting and storing the roles, to be able to achieve the desired functional-
ity in the network. A way similar to how lossless compression works on more powerful devices
could be used to ensure that the rules have a lower memory footprint, since identifiers such as
IP or MAC addresses have a lot of overlapping data.
Lastly, all the testing could be done on real hardware instead of simulations to get even more
accurate results. Firstly, we would get the most accuracy by physically measuring the power
consumption of the node as opposed to using software solutions such as Energest, which can
only approximate it. Secondly, in the simulation we used a 100% transmission rate which does
not coincide with reality where packets get lost because of distance and other interference. In
a network like the one in our experiments, where we used an unreliable protocol (UDP), losing
a packet would not matter that much since no retransmission would be necessary, but in a re-
liable network with retransmission losing a packet would imply additional energy costs, thus
making the actual energy costs of transmitting a packet higher and making it even more worth
it to block the data early.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to propose the usage of micro-firewalls in WSNs and to investigate
their benefits. The default strategy of blocking packets at the gateway is easy and straightfor-
ward, but using micro-firewalls throughout the network has its advantages and disadvantages
which were examined throughout this thesis.
We have performed several experiments using the Contiki operating system and the Cooja net-
work simulator to analyse different situations. From this experiments we saw that a micro-
firewall is great at reducing the network traffic, and subsequently the amount of energy spent
on radio transmission. We report savings of up to 20% in energy savings in radio transmission,
and up to 40% CPU time reduction by using a micro-firewall for blocking unwanted traffic. We
alsodescribedotherbenefitsnotdirectlyrelatedtoenergyconsumptionthatareobtainedfrom
using micro-firewalls.
However, our results are limited by the MAC protocol we use. Since we use a protocol that is not
designed for energy efficiency, we only see a total energy saving of a few percent at the most.
This could be improved drastically by using an energy aware protocol that would truly highlight
the benefits of the micro-firewalls. That is why in this thesis we only found it beneficial to run a
micro-firewall when it is right next to the source, as otherwise more energy is spent in analysing
every packet than the energy saved by not transmitting the unwanted packages further.
There is a great potential for future work on this thesis and it can grow into a number of direc-
tions. These future paths include switching the MAC protocol, having better firewall rules, more
optimal rule distribution and more accurate testing methods. They can be explored in future
projects and could potentially lead to new ideas and solutions for efficient IoT networks and
devices.
