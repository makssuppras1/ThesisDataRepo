Abstract
Roll­on/Roll­of (RoRo) vessels are a crucial segment of the global shipping industry and this paper
provides an exact method of solving the RoRo Ship Stowage Problem (RSSP) with regards to on
board shifting, using the network solver Flowty. The RSSP variation researched in this paper,
considers a ship with a single deck across a voyage with multiple loading and unloading ports
and a slot based cargo allocation structure. The thesis introduces two models to solve the RSSP,
a network model inspired by the Multi­Commodity Flow Problem (MCFP), and a comparison
model which borrows elements from both MCFP and the Generalized Assignment Problem (GAP).
The Flowty solver utilizes decomposition by splitting the problem into a master­ and multiple
subproblems. To determine the efficiency of the Flowty solver, it is tested on the network model
against the Gurobi solver, across multiple scenarios. These scenarios vary in ship size, number
of cargo types and number of ports. The results showed that Gurobi was efficient at solving the
smallest instances, whereas Flowty proved its efficiency when increasing the problem size. Flowty
was able to solve all constructed scenarios in contradiction to Gurobi, where the computer capacity
was a restriction. Moreover, the comparison model of the thesis quickly solved all scenarios,
however this was due to the model being too simplistic. The Flowty solver was further tested to
determine its limitations. The stress test of Flowty showed promise, however, before being able to
conclude that network optimization solvers such as Flowty are the best way of solving the RSSP,
more research is necessary. Multiple important aspects of the RSSP was not included in the models
of this thesis, which are crucial to consider for real life application. The Flowty solver therefore
needs to be tested with an inclusion of these in order to fully conclude its practical use.

Introduction
The global shipping industry serves as the backbone of international trade, facilitating the move­
ment of goods across continents. Within this sector, Roll­on/Roll­off (RoRo) vessels represent
a crucial segment, specializing in the transportation of wheeled cargo such as cars, trucks, and
trailers. Unlike other cargo vessels, such as bulk carriers or container ships, RoRo vessels enable
seamless drive­on and drive­off operations, eliminating the need for external lifting equipment like
cranes. The RoRo shipping market held a substantial global value of approximately 23.5 billion
USD in 2021, a figure projected to rise significantly to about 35.3 billion USD by 2030 [1].
The RoRo ship stowage problem (RSSP) involves arranging vehicles and cargo within RoRo ships
for efficient transportation. Given the growth in the RoRo shipping market, as well as the in­
creasing complexity and potential economic impact of stowage planning, developing accurate and
efficient optimization models for this problem is of significant interest. Addressing the RSSP can
bring about improved operational efficiency, reduced costs, enhanced safety, and increased prof­
itability for the shipping industry.
Several variations of the RoRo ship stowage problem exist, but fundamentally, the goal of the
problem is usually to either optimize onboard space utilization within RoRo vessels while mini­
mizing cargo movements or reducing the required amount of ballast water needed to balance the
ship. Moreover, these models vary in complexity, ranging from those incorporating multiple decks
to others confined to a single deck but accommodating diverse cargo types, showing the variety
of scenarios addressed within the stowage planning framework. A successful model could poten­
tially yield substantial cost reductions for RoRo shipping companies, as efficient stowage planning
directly translates to operational savings.
In recent years, efforts have been made to solve different variations of the RSSP. However, finding
a solution using an exact method becomes challenging when the problem is scaled to realistic
sizes. To date, no RSSP model that includes the minimization of shifting has been solved through
exact methods. Instead, most literature on the subject focuses on employing different heuristics to
achieve a satisfactory solution.
1.1
Problem Formulation
Even though RoRo shipping makes up a significant amount of the global shipping industry, there
exists only a limited amount of research on the topic. The motivation behind this thesis is to
provide an exact method of solving a specific variation of the RSSP using the network solver
Flowty. Additionally, the thesis aims to demonstrate a method wherein an initially challenging
problem, considered as unsolvable using traditional MIP­solvers, undergoes transformation into a
network model. Subsequently, it employs network solvers utilizing decomposition techniques to
achieve an optimal solution. More specifically, this thesis aims to provide an exact and guaranteed
optimal solution to the RSSP concerning the minimization of shifting on a voyage with multiple
loading and unloading ports, one cargo size and using a slot based layout of the ship. This will
be accomplished by reformulating the problem into a network optimization model and utilizing
the Flowty solver, which applies the Dantzig–Wolfe decomposition algorithm. The Flowty solver

is specifically designed to solve large­scale network optimization problems by dividing them into
sub­ and master problems, thereby enhancing efficiency and scalability.
This goal is formulated as the following research question:
Can the RoRo ship stowage problem be formulated as a network optimization model and be solved
to optimality using the Flowty software? And, to what extent can this model handle practical and
realistic stowage instances in terms of size and complexity?

Literature review
Stowage planning in RoRo shipping has not gained much attention from researchers until recently
as a result of the growing demand for optimised stowage plans. The need for an optimized RoRo
stowage plan is a result of the desire for increased efficiency, reduced turnaround times in ports,
and overall cost­effectiveness. The research of RoRo stowage planning considers multiple aspects
such as time, revenue, safety and environment. Therefore, this literature review aims to explore
past research, emphasizing its relevance in supporting the context and significance of this thesis.
The paper Optimization of stowage plans for RoRo ships by Øvstebo et al. (2011) [2] introduces
the RoRo ship stowage problem (RSSP) with a model that considers a lane representation of each
deck, optional and mandatory cargo, ship stability, and shifting costs. The proposed model is NP­
hard even in its simplest form. The model aims to maximize the revenue from optional cargo
minus the cost incurred when having to move cargo. They create two solution procedures for
comparison. One where they use the commercial MIP solver Xpress and one where they use the
heuristic squeaky wheel optimization (SWO) combined with a local search. From this they find
that the Xpress can solve instances with 5­10 optional cargoes to optimality.
The paper 2D­packing with an application to stowage in Roll­on Roll­off liner shipping by Hansen
et al. (2016) [3] from the International Conference on Computational Logistics, researches a sim­
plification of the general RSSP where the RSSP is seen as 2D packing problem. The problem they
solve is namely the 2­dimensional RoRo ship stowage problem (2DRSSP), where only one deck is
considered. Due to the problem only considering one deck, stability constraints are not included.
Furthermore, they use a grid representation of the deck and have 4 ports where the first 2 ports are
loading ports and the final 2 ports are unloading ports. The paper presents various MIP formu­
lations which aim to maximize the revenue from optional cargo minus the penalty incurred when
shifting cargo. Using a commercial MIP solver, they show that the 2DRSSP only can be solved
for smaller instances, which further motivates the need for alternate ways to solve the problem.
In the paper Stowage Planning with Optimal Ballast Water [4] Jia et al.(2020) focus on an envi­
ronmental perspective. Jia et al. aim to reduce the fuel consumption by minimizing the amount
of ballast water carried. This RSSP is associated to short sea shipping (SSS) as the amount of
ballast water is crucial for short sea RoRo ships where deep sea car carriers as Hansen et al. (2016)
investigate, are more robust to changes in cargo weight. The model this paper solves is the RoRo
ship stowage problem with ballast water (RSSPBW). The problem considers slots instead of a grid
representation where general trailers can use all slots and refrigerated trailers only can be loaded
at designated slots. Furthermore, it is assumed cargoes are homogeneous in size, however they
are different in weight. Moreover, there is a fixed number of decks with weight limitations and all
cargo is mandatory and available at the loading port. Jia et al. end up with a non­linear formulation
that is being linearized. From this they achieve results between 4.4% and 6.7% of savings in fuel
consumption and emissions.
Another paper by Hansen et al. (2020) An adaptive large neighborhood search heuristic for the
planar storage location assignment problem: application to stowage planning for Roll­on Roll­
off ships [5] aims to determine an optimal stowage plan for vehicles stored on a deck such that
the shifting cost is minimized. Hansen et al. (2020) consider the 2DRSSP with both stowage
and shifting cost which is an extension to Hansen et al. (2016) [3]. For this problem all cargo is
mandatory and therefore has to be delivered. Furthermore, the MIP model contains a flow which
Hansen et al. use to model the shifting cost. It is found that a commercial MIP solver can solve
small instances but in order to solve realistically sized instances an adaptive large neighborhood

adaptive search procedure (GRASP) is done followed by several constructors and destructors for

Due to the limited amount of literature on the subject, multiple aspects are still to be researched
which could potentially ease the way of solving the problem. However, several aspects and ways
of modelling the problem have been investigated throughout the years. Some focusing on max­
imizing the revenue, whereas others have focused on environmental aspects such as minimizing
fuel consumption. Both MIP solvers and heuristics have been used to solve the problem. It is
found that MIP models perform well on smaller instances but as the instances increase to a real­
istic size they struggle. Furthermore, heuristics have had great success solving realistically scaled
problems. As mentioned previously, the complexity of the RSSP is vast why most of the papers try
to keep complexity manageable by simplifying the problem. However, the problem with regards
to shifting cost has not been solved to optimality yet. Thus, this thesis aims to investigate whether
the problem can be modelled differently and solved to optimality.

Theory
This chapter introduces fundamental theories essential for addressing the research statement. It
covers the subjects: RoRo ships, key problems like the Multiple Commodity Flow Problem (MCFP)
and the Generalized Assignment Problem (GAP) which are needed to accurately understand and
model the RSSP. Additionally, it acquaints readers with the Flowty solver crucial for solving
network­based optimization problems in this study.
3.1
RoRo shipping
As mentioned briefly in the introduction, RoRo shipping is a significant part of the global shipping
industry and serves a specific role in transporting wheeled goods around the globe. With their sim­
ple ramp­based loading and unloading systems, RoRo vessels excel in quick and cost­effective port
operations, significantly reducing time and expenses associated with traditional cargo handling.
There exist various subcategories of RoRo ships each with their own area of specialization in either
what type of cargo they transport or the way they load and unload their cargo.
Pure car carriers (PCC) and Pure car/truck carriers (PCTC) are RoRo ships that, as the name sug­
gests, specializes in the transportation of cars and/or other vehicle types. These ships are specifi­
cally designed to carry as many vehicles as possible and are large, often containing multiple decks
used for storing vehicles. They usually have an external ramp either in the bow or stern of the
ship for the vehicles to enter and exit and an internal set of ramps, allowing the vehicles to move
between decks.
Figure 3.1: RoRo ship [6]
ROPAX is a ship that can accommodate vehicles as well as passengers. They are sometimes also
referred to as cruise ferries and are mostly used for shorter sea routes. These types of vessels are

particularly popular in northern Europe.
RoLo refers to a roll­on lift­off ship. This type of vessel has decks dedicated to vehicles which is
accessible by ramp and other decks where a crane is needed.
The ConRO (Container­Roll­on/Roll­off) is a ship designed for transporting both vehicles and
containers. Typically, the below deck area primarily accommodates vehicles, while the upper deck
provides space for stacking containers.
Depending on the type and size, RoRo vessels can be used for both short sea travel and longer
deep sea intercontinental routes. This paper focuses on RoRo vessels which only carries one size
of cargo across a voyage with multiple loading and unloading ports.

This section aims to introduce the Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition which is a way of reformulating
a large­scale problem with a certain structure. The key idea behind Dantzig­Wolfe is to change
representation from a set of constraints to a set of extreme points [9]. This is done by decomposing

a subproblem also called the pricing problem. From solving the restricted master problem the
optimal dual solutions are obtained and used in the subproblem to generate new nonbasic columns
with a negative reduced cost. The columns are then added to the RMP [10].

Flowty
Flowty is a prescriptive analytics platform, which is based on column generation and shortest path

network optimization in order to formulate a mathematical model as a network.

network solvers in recent years, this thesis is motivated to assess the efficiency of Flowty and
compare it against traditional solvers as Gurobi.

4.1
The Network
Before going into the mathematical formulation of the model, it is important to design the net­
work behind accurately in order to ensure the problem is modeled correctly. The network will be
formulated as multiple directed graphs G(V, E) consisting of vertices V and edges E.
To make it easier to illustrate, the rest of the section will follow an example of a ship with 4 slots
with the exit ramp being located by slot 2, meaning that every cargo being loaded or unloaded has
to be passed through slot 2, with an exception in the first and final port of the voyage. Since it
is assumed that the ship is empty before starting the voyage and all cargo has to leave the ship at
the end of it, the cargo are moved directly from the supply vertex to the dedicated cargo slot when
loaded or from the dedicated cargo slot to the sink when unloaded. An illustration of the deck
layout can be seen below.

three ports. Cargo type a loads at port 1 and unloads at port 3, b loads at port 2 and unloads at port
3, while c is onboard from port 1 to port 2.

4.2
Shifting
As the overall goal of the model is to reduce shifting and thereby operational time and costs, it is
important to clearly define what it is and which scenarios would initiate it. A shift, in the context
of the RoRo ship stowage problem, usually refers to the movement of a piece of cargo in order to
fit another cargo, which can be labor intensive and costly to do for many pieces of cargo.
In the existing literature of the RSSP, different approaches of modelling the shifting are used. In
the paper 2D­packing with an application to stowage in Roll­on Roll­off liner shipping by Hansen
et al. (2016) [3], the shifting cost is modeled as a function of number of legs the cargo has and the
placement cost of the square. As mentioned in chapter 2, the model is based on a grid representation
of the ship where each square are given an artificial cost which is meant to represent the probability
of shifting occurring in that square. They also consider other aspects, such as the size of the cargo
with the reasoning being that the larger the piece of cargo, the more difficult it is to move. With
this approach it is easy to add multiple factors that can affect the cost of the shift. Another way
to tackle shifting is to use a network based approach where the model uses the flow of cargo to
determine whether a shift is needed or not. It is usually done by modelling paths for each piece of
cargo and identify if another piece is blocking that path, which is the method utilized in the model
presented in this chapter. In this model there is no cost directly associated with the shifts but the
model reduces the number of shifts instead.
For the network defined in the previous section, cargo can only initiate a shift when loaded or
unloaded. Furthermore, it was introduced that 3 types of edges exist. Based on the aforementioned
edge types, a shift can only occur in a vertex if a cargo type has a stationary edge into a given vertex
and the same cargo type also has a stationary edge leaving the same vertex. This is due to the fact
that once a piece of cargo has been assigned the slot it will stay there until it is unloaded. Having
a stationary edge into and out of a vertex is therefore equivalent to the node being occupied. On
the other hand it is still possible for cargo to enter without shifting at a given port if the other cargo
type that had an in­going stationary edge to the vertex is leaving at the given port. The case of a
shift and non­shift is visualised in figure 4.4 below.

With the mathematical formulation defined in section 4.3, this section explains how the model
is defined and implemented in Flowty. Due to the fact that Flowty is a network optimization
solver, the implementation differes from implementing it in Gurobi. Implementing the network
model in Python using the Flowty software, the network graphs firstly have to be defined. Based
on the assumptions of the movement of cargo from section 4.1, logical expressions have been
implemented to generate the possible edges for each cargo type. Having generated the graphs
the optimization model can now be formulated. Firstly the decision variables qY
j , qX
j and sj are
added to the model by using the function addVar. Adding the decision variables to Flowty is a bit
different compared to JuMP. Using Flowty, the function addVar provides the user with multiple
options, among these some are similar to JuMP:
• Define if the variable is included in the objective function or not by setting the parameter
obj to 0 or 1 (When 0 it is not included and vice versa).
• Type of variable as in Julia using JuMP (binary, interger or continous).
• Define the range of the variable as in JuMP. For instance 1:N.
For this model the decision variable sj, which denotes the shifting cost, is added to the objective
function, by setting the parameter obj = 1 for sj .
From this, adding variables to the network model in Flowty is therefore similar to using JuMP in
Julia. However, the big difference lies in the next step which is to formulate the constraints for the
edges.
Flowty functions by the user adding graphs with the feasible edges to the problem. In the case of
the previously defined network model, each cargo type has an unique graph of edges that each are
added to the model. By this, the user has defined each subproblem that Flowty is going to solve and
it therefore takes advantage of the block­angular structure. The graphs are added by the function
addGraph. When adding a graph each edge is added as a decision variable xindex,(i,j),graphindex to
the model. In addition the user has to define different parameters that Flowty offers when adding
the graphs. Among these are:
• If the decision variables of the graph are included in the objective function or not.
• The specific edges the graph should contain, for instance a list that contains all edges.
• The vertex index of the source.
• The vertex index of the sink.
• A lower bound, which is the value that decision variables of x (capacity of the edges) has to
be greater or equal to.
• An upper bound which defines the value of the decision variables x (capacity of the edges)
has to be smaller or equal to.
• Variable type of x, binary, integer or continuous.
In the case of the defined network model, the source vertex is set to 1, the sink node the last node,
the lower bound 0, the upper bound to the demand of the corresponding cargo type of the graph
and the variable type integer.

edges yijk and stationary edges zijk. In Flowty there are no different sets of edges but only one

6
Computational study
The computational study investigates the performance of the two implemented models from chap­
ters 4 and 5. The network model has been solved using Flowty as well as Gurobi, whereas the
assignment model has only been solved using Gurobi. They have been tested on different in­
stances varying in ship size (number of slots), number of ports, number of cargo types and demand
of cargo. In section 6.1 the process of creating the instances is outlined and explained. The results
of running the different instances are presented in section 6.2.
Currently, the Flowty software is only available as a Python package. Consequently, the network
model has been developed in both Julia and Python, while the comparison model is exclusively
implemented in Julia. All computational experiments have been performed on a computer with
processor Intel(R) Core(TM) I7­8565U CPU 1.8 GHz, 16 GB of RAM and running on a Windows
system.
6.1
Test instances
As mentioned previously, the test instances will differ in several aspects. Specifically, they contain
varying ship sizes based on the number of rows and columns. The total available slots onboard are
derived from the product of these two parameters. In addition, the different scenarios will vary in
number of cargo types. As explained in section 4.1, the different cargo types are determined by
the loading and unloading port of the cargo, not the size, as in many other models of the RSSP.
The number of cargo types is being adjusted to see how this affects the performance of the solvers.
Furthermore, the number of cargo types is dependent on the number of ports, which is why the
influence of the number of ports is being investigated as well. The influence is due to 3 ports only
allowing the possibility of 3 cargo types and 4 ports 6 cargo types. Finally, it is also investigated
if the amount of cargo on board (demand) has an influence on the performance.
As no real booking lists have been provided, the data is generated by multiple assumptions and
considerations. The number of cargo types, ports and the ship size all have an influence on the
number of vertices and edges in the model. For this purpose, multiple scenarios have been created.
Firstly 3 ship sizes are created, a small denoted S, medium denoted M and a large denoted L. The
three sizes respectively have the dimensions 10 × 5 (50 slots), 20 × 10 (200 slots) and 25 × 15
(375 slots). In addition to this, the number of visited ports are assumed to be either 3 or 4, as
this will provide a wide range of different cargo types. Thus, the number of cargo types that are
explored are [3,4,5,6]. Which port the cargo types are being loaded and unloaded in is specified in
each test instance section.
Within each scenario two different amounts of demand are tested. This is done by assuming that
two fill rates exists, one of 75% and one of 100%. A fill rate of 100% assumes the ship has to be
filled at maximum capacity at all ports, thus no slots are left empty when leaving a port. Since the
models tested are integer models, a perfect 75% fill rate is infeasible in some scenarios. In those
cases the fill rate is slightly above or below 75%. However, due to how the comparison model is
formulated it is infeasible to achieve the same fill rates. In the comparison model, each slot can
only be assigned a single piece of cargo across the entire voyage. This means that the total amount
of cargo carried on a voyage cannot exceed the total number of slots. For all scenarios the demand
of the comparison model is equal to number of slots, distributed equally across the cargo types.
This is suspected to have a large effect on the computational time required to solve the model. This
will be further discussed in section 7.2. For all test instances a time limit of 3600 seconds has been
set.

7
Discussion and Outlook
In chapters 4 and 5, a comprehensive explanation of the network and comparison models were
provided, which were subsequently validated in the computational study in chapter 6. This chapter
delves into the significant decisions and assumptions made throughout the thesis. It aims to discuss
the reasoning behind these choices and explore alternative routes that could have been taken. In the
first two sections, the considerations associated with the two models will be discussed. Following
this, the next section will provide additional reflection of the computational study. Finally, this
chapter will outline potential future directions for utilizing the Flowty solver.
7.1
Network model
The RSSP is a multifaceted problem and there exists numerous models of the problem, each with
their own approach and considerations. The models presented in this thesis are very simplified
and may therefore have little use in practice as it only considers one cargo size and is slot based.
Depending on the particular type of RoRo ship, they often carry multiple types of cargo in differ­
ent shapes and sizes. One of the largest simplifications made in this thesis was to use a slot based
approach, where all cargo could fit into exactly one slot. Most of the literature on the RSSP re­
garding shifting, uses either a grid or lane based layout of the ship. In a grid based model cargo are
assigned a length and width which allows the model to handle multiple types of cargo and thereby
more closely resembles a real life scenario.
Despite the model being simplified, it was shown in the computational study that Flowty for some
realistically sized instances, could solve the model to optimality. The model could therefore be of
use in real life application if only dealing with one size of cargo. However, it could be interesting
to modify the network model to be based on a grid and thereby include differently sized cargo,
and test if Flowty can handle the increase in complexity. If the case is that it cannot be solved to
optimality with the increased complexity, the simplified model can still possibly be used as a rough
estimate when the RoRo shipping companies make their stowage plan.
There are many other aspects of the RSSP that could be interesting to include in the network
model. Examples are: Multiple decks, dedicated slots for refrigerated cargo and minimization of
ballast water usage. Some of these aspects are crucial to consider in real life scenarios. Therefore,
for the network model to have practical use cases, it must address these aspects to meet certain
standards. Here among, the ballast water system influences both fuel consumption and safety
considerations during the ship’s voyage. The refrigerated cargo is another aspect, which could have
been implemented in the formulated network model if more time was available. Lastly, multiple
decks could have been added to the model, which may result in a more difficult model to solve,
due to the complexity of moving cargo among the decks using an internal ramp system.
For this thesis it is yet unknown whether reformulating the network model could have an influence
on the computational results. With this in mind reformulating the model so it would contain fewer
edges or nodes, could potentially improve the computational time and benefit the results. For
instance the large ship 25 × 15 with 4 ports and 6 cargo types contains 1508 nodes and 12288
edges. In addition, a greater insight of how the Flowty solver works might have allowed for a
more tailored model design.

7.2
Comparison model
The comparison model was included in the thesis to test how the network model with the use of
Flowty performs against another type of model of the RSSP. From chapter 6 it was found that the
comparison model had a computational advantage. This advantage can possibly be attributed to
the fact that in the comparison model a slot could only be used by a single piece of cargo across
the entire voyage, whereas in the network model a slot can be used by multiple pieces of cargo if
available, depending on the number of legs across the voyage. Therefore it would be beneficial to
change the comparison model for it to able to accommodate multiple pieces of cargo in the same
slot and thereby making it able to achieve the same fill rates as the network model. This would
allow for a more fair comparison of the two models.
Furthermore, as shown in section 5.1 the paths for a piece of cargo in the comparison model are
shorter than the network model. Since there are no paths between ports, the number of edges
needed to move the cargo from the source to the sink are reduced depending on the amount of
legs the cargo is onboard the ship. Instead each piece of cargo has two separate paths, a loading
and unloading path. It could be interesting to possibly change the graph of the network model to
a similar graph to test if it improves the solver time. However, having two paths per cargo could
potentially negatively impact the Flowty solver’s performance.
7.3
Computational study
From the computational study, it is clear that the tests should have been run on a computer with a
larger RAM. This would have better demonstrated Gurobi’s performance and, thereby, provided
a more accurate demonstration of Flowty’s performance compared to Gurobi. The computer used
for the tests was equipped with only 16GB of RAM, significantly lower than the RAM capacities
typically found in High­Performance Computers (HPCs) commonly accessible to many companies.
The limited amount of RAM especially affected the Gurobi solver which resulted in the computer
failing to run the larger tests. Seen in chapter 6 it was only possible to run tests of large ships using
the Gurobi solver in scenario 1. This may also have skewed the results of the smaller tests aswell
to favor Flowty which seems to be less affected by RAM restrictions.
For the scenarios, different ship sizes were tested, ranging from 50 to 375 slots excluding the stress
test of Flowty. The particular sizes for the ships were chosen partly to showcase the capabilities
of the solvers and to represent real life RoRo ships. In the paper by Jia et al. (2020) [4] the
model found the optimal solution in an instance with 251 pieces of cargo, which sits in between
the medium and large ship type used in this thesis. However, these sizes only cover certain types
of RoRo vessels as some PCCs can carry up to 9000 vehicles.
The specific distribution of demand across the different cargo types were not considered in this
thesis. The demand distribution were solely based on utilizing all slots on board the ship at each
port. Testing different demand distributions, for example a scenario where the majority of the cargo
were loaded at the first port and unloaded at the last, and vice versa, could have provided additional
insight of the model. The different demand distributions could potentially have an effect on the
computational time.
Evaluating the results, the Flowty solver uses random multi­threading and therefore provides dif­
ferent results and run times. When running the instances it was observed that the solver time varied
a significant amount on the same scenarios. Thus, more test runs for each instance should have
been run in order to determine average results. This would have provided a better representation
of Flowty’s capabilities.

7.4
Flowty
As mentioned in the literature review, all models of the RSSP concerning the minimization of
shifting have been difficult to solve to optimality for realistic sizes and some have been proven
to be NP­hard. Therefore, most of the literature focuses on using approximate methods, such as
heuristics and meta­heuristics, to provide an adequate solution to the problem. This thesis how­
ever, takes an alternative approach by reformulating the model into a network based model and
using a network solver that utilizes decomposition in order to solve it. The computational study
documented that the Flowty solver outperformed the Gurobi solver in terms of solver time for the
RSSP model presented in this thesis. This approach can be applied to a range of diverse prob­
lems beyond the RSSP, thereby further evaluating the efficacy of network solvers such as Flowty.
Numerous models struggle with finding an exact and optimal solution when the problems reach
realistic sizes. Therefore, it would be interesting to test Flowty or other network solvers on other
problems against the approximate methods.

Conclusion
This thesis has researched whether the RoRo ship stowage problem considering minimization of
shifts, can be formulated as a network optimization model and solved to optimality using the Flowty
software. The Flowty software was tested against Gurobi across different scenarios each with
their own combination of the parameters, ship size, cargo type, fill rate and number of ports. In
addition, a simple comparison model was introduced based on a combination of Multi Commodity
Flow Problem and the Generalized Assignment Problem, to provide an alternate way of solving
the RSSP. Gurobi was shown to be efficient at solving small instances of the RSSP, however when
increasing the instance size the solver time increased drastically. For the very largest instances,
the RAM requirement exceeded the capacity. The comparison model solved all instances quickly,
however, due to its inability to achieve the same fill rate as the network model, it was considered
too simplistic.
Flowty was shown to be the most effective solver for the network model when the instances reached
realistic sizes solving all instances within a time limit of 3600 seconds. Furthermore, the stress test
of Flowty showed that, given a time limit of 10800 seconds, it could solve instances that include
a ship with 900 cargo slots with 75% fill rate to optimality and a solution with only 4 shifts with a
100% fill rate. From the computational study it was seen that Flowty is less influenced by the size of
the ship compared to Gurobi. However, the results of Flowty showed an increase in computational
time when adding an extra cargo type which showed that the number of subproblems has a large
influence.
The thesis builds upon the existing research by taking an alternate approach to solving the RSSP.
In most of the papers on the topic, heuristics and meta­heuristics are the preferred solution method,
whereas this paper solved the RSSP to optimality using an exact approach. However, the models
presented in this thesis are simplified compared to most other RSSP models. By being based on
a slot structure the network model can only handle a single size of cargo and leaves out other
important aspects of the RSSP.
In order to truly conclude whether or not the RSSP can be solved to optimality using Flowty,
additional research is needed. The inclusion of other aspects, such as ballast water, different sized
cargo and multiple decks, is needed in order for the model to have any real commercial use. In
addition, the comparison model showed an alternative way of designing the network of the RSSP
containing fewer edges. Investing more time in the comparison model could yield a beneficial way
of formulating the model and incorporate it in Flowty.
To conclude, the thesis showed that the RoRo ship stowage problem can be formulated as a network
optimization model and solved to optimality using the Flowty software. Moreover, Flowty has
shown its potential to solve real life instances of a simplified version of the RSSP. The results of
the test instances imply that Flowty could possibly handle a more complex RSSP model. However,
there are still many aspects and further research that has to be made in order to finally conclude
that Flowty and other network optimization solvers are the most efficient way of solving the RSSP.
