Abstract
The energy use and indoor environment of buildings with a second "skin" and semi-
outdoor space is examined in the present study. Two different building case studies were
chosen, Dome of Visions and EMBRACE. Both buildings were modelled in IDA ICE
simulation software and the simulation results, along with the hand calculations performed,
proved that the heating and cooling loads decreased after the implementation of the shield.
The same effect was noted when the existing HVAC systems of the buildings were im-
plemented in the models. In addition to the simulations, physical measurements were
performed in DoV. The operative temperature measured in the inner zones of the building
was on average below the acceptable limits of EN/DS 15251. The operative temperature in
the semi-outdoor area was constantly higher than the ambient. The relative humidity and
the vertical air temperature difference measured were found to be within the acceptable lim-
its of thermal comfort. Since the thermal environment of a semi-outdoor space cannot be
assessed based on existing standards, an alternative approach was considered, adjusting the
clothing level and PMV range. Based on this, the semi-outdoor space could be comfortably
occupied for at least twice as many hours compared to an outdoor space. It was found that
the shape of the shield and the way it is connected to the building affected the distribution
of those hours throughout the year, while the tolerance level of the occupant had a great
impact on the total amount of hours. After comparing the results of the simulations with
the measurements, the models created were found to perform satisfactorily, considering
the obstacles encountered during the measurement period and the lack of data. Finally,
several scenaria were considered modifying different parameters and investigating the
effect that the shield and the semi-outdoor space have on the energy use.
Keywords: semi-outdoor space, second skin, energy use, indoor environment, shield,
Dome of Visions, EMBRACE

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
It is a worldwide goal to minimize the utilization of fossil fuels, securing stable and
affordable energy supply to the future sustainable societies. Europe has been leading this
effort, establishing targets and setting milestones to be achieved during the process. In
compliance with them, Denmark aims to become independent from fossil fuels and supply
entirely the energy system with renewable energy by 2050 [4].
The accomplishment of this goal requires, among others, implementation of energy savings,
improvement on energy efﬁciency and integration of renewable energy sources. All these
are directly applicable to the building sector, as it is a fact that buildings’ consumption
constitutes 40% of the total energy consumption, and this indicates that there is a great
potential for improvement [71]. For instance, the energy consumption of an existing
building could range between 150-350 kWh/m2 annually depending on the construction
and the operation strategies, while for a building constructed today should be approximately
50 kWh/m2 annually. Future Danish targets indicate that from 2020 a non-residential
building should consume less than 25 kWh/m2 annually, while a residential building less
than 20 kWh/m2 annually [19].
However, the energy savings should not be achieved at the expense of thermal indoor
environment and indoor air quality. Since people spend most of their time indoors [48], the
heating, cooling and ventilation systems, should ensure a satisfactory indoor environment
that does not impair the health or the performance of the occupants. Therefore, it is
important to know the different parameters for the thermal comfort, their impact on the
occupants and their behaviour in connection with the building and the systems. Interna-
tional standards have been established indicating acceptable ranges of these parameters and
measurement methods, in order to evaluate the indoor thermal comfort and air quality [19],
[17], [16]. The ﬁnal goal is to design buildings aesthetically pleasing, energy efﬁcient,
with a healthy and comfortable indoor environment.

Aiming towards this direction, renewable energies should be integrated, systems with
higher efﬁciency should be installed, along with appropriate control systems maintaining
the indoor thermal comfort and air quality in the buildings. Prior to these though, a

The present report, studies an innovative design idea towards this aim; buildings covered

to, for instance, an outdoor garden. This concept could introduce the idea of a more
seasonal living space, which functions in close interaction with the solar radiation. It
could be considered as a different temperature zone in which the thermal comfort of the
occupants would be more tolerant than indoors and also their clothing level could be
adjusted according to every season.
The aim of this project is to investigate the effect that a semi-outdoor space could have
on the energy use and indoor climate of the building, as well as the annual usage potential
of the semi-outdoor space.
Initially a literature review is conducted on existing buildings with a second skin and

Physical measurements have been taken in the buildings, the outcome of which is analysed

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
The aim of this chapter is to conduct a literature review on the existing buildings with
a semi-outdoor space, focusing on the investigations that have been made concerning both
the energy use and the indoor environment of such buildings [51]. The design of such
structures is rather innovative and therefore, the research has been divided into different
categories of structures, each of them resembling in a way the requested construction.
Firstly, semi-outdoor spaces in general have been studied, followed by a review on the
effects that second skin façades have on a building’s energy use and indoor environment.
A combination of these two is the objective, thus studying them separately could provide
valuable information. Moreover, structures called geodesic domes, which are considered
to be a representative example of the requested type of building, are studied.
In order for the thermal comfort to be analysed and assessed, several parameters can
be used. The most widely used parameter is the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV), introduced
by Fanger [24], which is an indoor comfort model to assess thermal conditions with levels
of physiological strain in human subjects. PMV predicts the thermal sensation of a person
based on the six thermal comfort variables, through a heat-balance equation and the results
are based on a scale from -3 to +3 (where -3 is cold, 0 is neutral, +3 is hot). The PMV
index has been partially validated in a variety of indoor contexts [45] and a PMV value
between -1 and +1 is widely considered to be thermally acceptable [24] [6]. This index
is interpreted along with Predicted Percentage Dissatisﬁed (PPD,) which expresses the
percentage of people who are not satisﬁed at a particular thermal environment. Based on
Fanger’s [24] hypothesis that one PMV scale unit corresponds approximately to a change
in temperature of 3oC under mid-range clothing insulation and near-sedentary metabolic
rate, there is an assumption that 1.5oC around the neutral point is considered to be within
thermal comfort limits. Other thermal sensors used for assessment are MRT, PET, ET and
SET.

• Median Radiant Temperature (MRT), is the uniform surface temperature of an
imaginary black enclosure with which one, also assumed black body, exchanges the
same heat by radiation as in the actual environment [6].
• Psychological Equivalent Temperature (PET) is the air temperature in a typical
residential building (Ta = MRT; v = 0.1 m/s e = 12 hPa) in which a person (met =
80 W; 0,9 clo) has the same skin and core temperature as in the actual non-uniform
outdoor environment [30].
• Effective Temperature (ET) is the temperature of a standard environment (RH=50%,
Ta=MRT and v<0.15 m/s) in which a subject would experience the same skin
wetness and mean skin temperature as in the actual environment. Parameters are
limited to low activity and light clothing.
• Standard Effective Temperature (SET) was extended to include a range of activities
and clothing levels with the humidity level of the standard environment dropped to
50%, with Ta= MRT and v <0.15 m/s [26].
Many indoor climate models generally describe thermal discomfort and not stress, so
the adaptation to an environment that differs greatly from neutral conditions has been
doubted. Semi-outdoor spaces have been in the spotlight in recent years, as science has
reached a point, where outdoor micro-climates that were considered non-controlled can
now be partially controlled and unwanted environmental conditions can be obviated. It
has been observed that when it comes to thermal comfort, people are more likely to stress
the accepted climatic conditions they experience in outdoor and semi-outdoor spaces
in relation to indoor environment [59]. Thermal environment is a group of sets and
subsets, with the narrower set being thermal preference. The one wider than that is thermal
comfort conditions, then acceptable thermal conditions, followed by moderately stressful
and then stressful conditions, with the ﬁnal and widest one being the hazardous thermal
environments.
2.1
Semi-outdoor areas
There are different perspectives on what a semi-outdoor space is, and could be divided
into two basic categories. In the ﬁrst category there are generally outdoor spaces. Even
though they are exposed to outdoor weather, these conditions are somewhat moderated by

structures such as transparent roofs or a wall that prevents wind. Semi covered stadia, bus
stations, glass roofs and spaces next to a wall that acts as a windbreak could be included.
In the second category, a fully closed structure could also be considered as a semi-outdoor
space, such as a geodesic dome or a building with a weather shield attached. It allows
natural ventilation through controlled openings, but also fully protects the surrounding
space from snow, wind and rain. This kind of structures is to be further analysed in section
2.3.
There are several studies for the ﬁrst group of semi-outdoor spaces that have given in-
teresting results. A glass roof structure that is exposed to outdoor conditions from all
vertical sides is the simplest example of a semi-outdoor space. The thermal environment
under such a structure has been investigated in Parma Italy [50] and the results showed
that a semi-transparent glass roof can mitigate extreme weather, either cold in winter
or hot in the summer. A small roof inclination is suggested in order to reduce hot air
stagnation. Using an HVAC system and roof evaporative cooling is strongly advised in
order to achieve thermal comfort conditions. Lastly, glass with a high absorptivity in
relation to an evaporative cooling system, can reduce PET temperature up to 6oC under
the glass roof in summer. In addition to that, high solar radiation transitivity roofs are
desirable in winter whilst in summer they can increase the temperature by almost 2oC [50].
Pagliarini et al. showed that the thermal perception of a seated person in such a structure
is very similar to the one of a standing person. However, the effect of solar radiation is
higher on seated individuals [49].
Another example of semi-outdoor spaces is a stadium. A study in stadia in Australia just
before the Olympic games of 2000 showed that the risk of heat stress was much greater in
the shaded upper part of the seated places than it was in the open and unshaded seats. This
was attributed to the limited ventilation and radiant heat from semi-transparent rooﬁng
materials [25]. Something that was also veriﬁed by another study that focused on glass
roofs in Parma, Italy [50]. The suggestions for resolving the issue were to minimize
shading and use an opaque cover on the roof. Also,evaporative cooling can remove the
heat absorbed by the roof before it enters the controlled space [50]. Perry showed that in
USA a fully enclosed and insulated from outdoors sporting stadium, attracts 300% more
attendants than traditional open to outdoor weather ones [65]. Referring to stadia, there
are many suggested ways to modify the micro-climate that is created, based on the climate
zone that the stadium is located in. In moderate climate zones, wind breakers and shading
as well as radiation attenuating devices can be used. In more extreme climates, evaporating
cooling devices such as downdraught cool towers (gravity or fan-forced air that is cooled
by evaporation from a wet medium at the upper-level air intakes) or high pressure mist

2.2
Double skin facades
Moving towards the direction of a sustainable building sector with increased energy
efﬁciency, the application of passive systems to buildings has gained much attention. In
the so called ‘passive buildings’, the indoor environment is not controlled by mechanical
heating and cooling systems, but by the structural and architectural design of the building
and its components [46]. The design strategies of such buildings should be integrated since
the conceptual design phase, by carefully selecting the parameters that critically affect the
performance of the building, such as form, orientation, wall to window ratio, glazing type
and shading, etc. [62]. Amongst others, the double skin façade (DSF) has become a popular
passive solution since 1990’s in Europe and is used for both new and renovated buildings.
A DSF is an envelope construction that contains two transparent surfaces separated by a
cavity, as depicted in ﬁgure 2.3 [7]. The exterior surface is often a hardened single-glazed
pane, while the interior surface is usually an insulating double-glazed unit [54]. Various
Figure 2.3: Double Skin Facades [54]
studies have been performed on the DSF system and its performance, starting from Arons
and Glicksman [3], who have described its characteristics, and demonstrated advantages
and disadvantages. The technical aspects of the design of a DSF system were discussed by
Osterle et al. [47], while Compagno [13] has provided a number of European commercial
buildings which use DSF system. Regarding the building’s energy use, studies have been
performed on the integration of DSF with the building’s HVAC system, demonstrating the
signiﬁcant impact of DSF in reducing building’s heating loads [61]. Moreover, Carriere et
al. explained that the number of glazings reduce the cooling energy, thereby the overall
==PAGE:33==
2.2. DOUBLE SKIN FACADES

energy demand of the building [11]. With regard to materials and colors of the exterior
surface, Blanco et al. has developed a model which shows the behaviour of different
conﬁgurations and the inﬂuence on cooling, heating and lighting loads in the building [8].
In the interior surface clear low-emissivity coating and solar-control glazing could also
be used [5] [9]. The air cavity between the two skins can be naturally or mechanically
ventilated, and the width of the cavity may vary from 0.20 m to more than 2 m. The
air cavity section arrangement (shaft-box window, the corridor façade, the multi-story
DSF and the box-window façade) can inﬂuence the behaviour of the DSF system [54].
Shameri et al. studied daylighting characteristics of several existing DSF structures that
are exposed to different climates (tropical, subtropical and cold temperature climates) [54].
Field measurements accompanied by computer simulations were used to suggest proper
control settings that ensure natural ventilation rates and heating energy savings in double
skin buildings [39].
The incentive for DSF derives from the need to combine the energy efﬁciency, with im-
proved thermal insulation and better natural ventilation, together with the aesthetics of
a transparent façade of modern buildings. On the other hand, there are many challenges
associated with the DSF, as complex thermal and airﬂow phenomena may occur, along
with the sensitivity to the climatic conditions of different geographical regions [52] [60].
There are many advantages involved with DSF, which include wind pressure effect reduc-
tion [54], ﬁre protection and aesthetic view [76]. But more signiﬁcantly, a DSF system
maximizes daylight into the building and contributes to the improved energy performance.
During winter time, the DSF behaves as a buffer zone between the building and the outdoor
conditions, decreasing heat loss, and improving U-values. A representative example is
Yılmaz and Çetinta¸s [76] case study, where it was proved that a single skin facade’s heat
loss was 40% higher than a double skin facade’s heat loss in their case. During summer
time, the solar gain is reduced, namely, the heat load reaching the internal skin is decreased
by the ventilated cavity, as absorbed solar radiation is re-radiated [75]. Yet, when DSF
system is applied, the airﬂow rates and the air temperature in the cavity space should be
carefully controlled, so as to improve the effects of natural ventilation on energy savings
with additional ventilation rates [39].
On the other hand, there are also disadvantages correlated with the implementation of a
DSF system, such as higher construction costs, reduction of available indoor space, and
less room-to-room or ﬂoor-to-ﬂoor sound insulation [54]. Finally, it should be pointed
out that a DSF system is particularly sensitive to factors that can affect its performance,
which include the building’s location, orientation and geometry, as well as the applica-
tion strategies and materials [54]. Different glass types, different transparency ratio and

Geodesic domes
A geodesic dome is a spherical or hemispherical shell structure that is based on a
polyhedron such as the one shown in ﬁgure 2.4. Different kinds of ﬂat geometrical
shapes are connected in a spherical or hemispherical three dimensional structure to form a
geodesic dome. Geodesic domes are lightweight and strong architectural structures [53].
Figure 2.4: Geodesic Dome
The ﬁrst to construct one was Walther Bauersfeld, right after World War I and it was used
as a planetarium. The dome was placed in Jena, Germany and was opened to the public in
July 1926. The term “geodesic” though came 20 years later from R. Buckminster Fuller
who worked with domes for several years and patented many principles on them. He
was also the ﬁrst to introduce to the public the idea of a building into a geodesic dome.
The idea of the dome was appealing to Fuller because of the strength of the structure, the
light weight and the fact that a sphere encloses the greatest volume for the least surface
area. Geodesic Domes were widely used throughout the US and worldwide. One of many
is the Montreal’s biodome which is still standing and houses a museum. In 2000, the
world’s ﬁrst fully sustainable geodesic dome hotel was built. The hotel is situated in Chile
(ﬁgure 2.5) and the dome design was used based on its resistance to strong winds that are
characteristic of the area [68]. Another hotel that used geodesic domes in its design is
Hotel Kakslauttanen, Finland [69]. A dome covering a whole baseball ﬁeld in Cleveland,
USA (Cleveland Browns Stadium) is also in debate. The constructors claim that this will
help minimize the heating costs and give the spectators a comfortable experience all year
round [67]. Geodesic domes can be built with a plethora of materials such as wood, plastic,
concrete or steel. The most widespread construction is a geodesic dome constructed with a

ventilation system, the passive solar gains and a satisfactory insulation on the
building walls, the heating requirements are signiﬁcantly decreased and a uniform
temperature in the building is easy to be maintained.
• It reduces ultraviolet radiation from the sun.
• It minimizes the building’s maintenance, as there are fewer damages to the building
from sun, rain, snow and wind.

• It provides the possibility of growing vegetables and fruits in your own garden no
matter the longitude and the location of the building. Together with that, the plants
found in the semi-outdoor area, provide natural insulation and sound absorption.
• Lastly, the spherical shape of the dome, gives the maximum volume of a building.
Moreover, heating and cooling the home becomes more efﬁcient, due to the fact that
there are no corners where heat may be trapped. The overall air ﬂow in a dome is
substantially better than in a conventionally constructed home.
However, there are also disadvantages correlated with the buildings in a dome [35] [36]:
• It is a challenge to use sewer vents and chimneys through a dome due to its shape.
• Fire escapes and ﬁre safety can be troubling.
• There are areas that residential buildings are obligated to have a certain uniform
look and a dome’s difference can cause problems.
• Air stratiﬁcation and moisture distribution within a dome are usual, and these
conditions tend to quickly degrade wooden framing or interior panelling.
• Sounds, smells, and reﬂected light tend to be transmitted through the entire structure
more easily. However, if planned correctly, this can be a bonus.
• The curved shape of the dome can cause difﬁculties in taking full advantage of the
space that surrounds the building. A curved shaped space can also be difﬁcult to
furnish.
• Water tightening the dome can be a challenge.
• There are no extensive studies on the indoor environment in the semi-outdoor space
and how beneﬁcial or not it can be for human health.

3.1.2
HVAC system
The heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) system of the interior of DoV
and the building within it comprise of only a heating system. There is no cooling system
installed, while the ventilation is only based on natural ventilation strategies.
At the top of DoV there is a detachable roof part which can be controlled by the occupants.
Moreover, around DoV, between the deck and the shield, there are side gaps which are
closed during the heating season and open during the cooling season. Natural ventilation
is achieved by opening the roof part or both the roof part and the side gaps, depending on
the time of the year. It is often the case that external doors are opened for extra ventilation.
Monitoring the operation of DoV, it can be deduced that there is no speciﬁc ventilation
strategy or setpoints established, but it is the responsibility of the occupants to ventilate
DoV naturally so as to achieve comfort conditions.
Regarding the heating system, there are three heating sources. The primary heat source
consists of a 5 kW air-to-water heat pump and an ice-stick-type 3 kW heat pump, while
the auxiliary heating system is a rocket oven. All three sources are connected to a 600 l hot
water tank, from which the hot water is distributed around DoV and the building within it.
The heating system was designed so that heat is delivered both inside the building and
outside in the semi-outdoor area. From the literature review conducted this is a rather
unusual case, as generally the semi-outdoor spaces are not intended to be heated/cooled.
However, since DoV is a prototype with various occupancy utilities, there is a heating
system installed in the semi-outdoor area as well.
The heating system inside the building, comprises of a radiant ﬂoor system embedded in
the ﬂoor deck, water radiators and electric heaters. The radiant ﬂoor system is installed in
the meeting room on the ground ﬂoor. There are two water radiators, one in the meeting
room and one in the ofﬁce room on the 1st ﬂoor. In both rooms there is also an extra
electric heater which is occasionally used according to DoV schedules/occupants needs.
Regarding the heating system in the semi-outdoor area, there are both convectors and a
radiant ﬂoor heating system installed. The radiant ﬂoor system is identical to the one

HVAC system
The HVAC system of the EMBRACE was designed according to the building’s demand,
but at the same time trying to fulﬁl all the requirements of the SDE 14 competition.
Therefore, different systems with several components interacting were chosen to be used
for achieving the heating, cooling and ventilation of EMBRACE. An overview of the
systems’ sources and usages is presented in ﬁgure 3.16.
For the heat production, there are three different heat sources. Solar collectors are
mounted on the top part of the roof, on the weather shield, and produce hot water which
is used both for domestic hot water (DHW) and for space heating (SH). The sufﬁcient
supply of DHW is ensured by active heat recovery from the ventilation system. For this
reason, a compressor (Compact P) is implemented in the mechanical ventilation system.
In winter case, the heat is extracted from the exhaust air, while in summer case, the heat is
extracted from the supply air and then is introduced in the DHW tank. The solar collectors
work in parallel with the active heat recovery system for the production of DHW, which is
introduced into the DHW tank through two spiral heat exchangers, one inside the other.

The internal is connected to the active heat recovery loop, while the external to the solar
collector loop. Finally, the DHW tank is also equipped with an electric heater as a backup

Regarding the space heating, the heat from the solar collectors is mainly utilized during
autumn and spring seasons, when the solar radiation is possible to be sufﬁcient. During
winter or cloudy autumn/spring days, a reversible air-to-water heat pump is installed and
connected to the storage tank. It is worth mentioning that the loops between the heat pump,
the solar collectors and the heat exchangers are circulated by a mixture of water/brine with
glycol, in order to avoid freezing during winter.
The primary cooling system of EMBRACE is nighttime radiative cooling with unglazed
solar collectors, which is an affordable and simple solution. The unglazed solar collectors
are placed on the north side of the deck and are connected to the storage tank. The cold
water of the storage tank is circulated in the radiant ﬂoor system during daytime, cooling
the inner part of the building, and then is stored back in the tank with increased temperature.
During nighttime, the stored water from the tank is circulated in the unglazed collectors.
Thereby, it is cooled down through radiation towards the sky and returned into the tank, so
that it can be used the following day.
For cases that nighttime radiative cooling might be insufﬁcient, the reversible air-to-water
heat pump is to be used for providing the cold water needed.

The system is controlled by Uponor Radio 24V DEM. The control system dynamically
opens/closes the manifold actuators in order to adjust the water ﬂow and provide the power
needed to each zone in order to remain within the temperature setpoints. The pipes in
the radiant ﬂoor and all water pipes in EMBRACE (hot and cold), are PEX 15x2.5 with
thermal conductivity assumed to be 0.35 W/mK. The ones that carry hot water were
insulated with 15 mm PUR foam which has a conductivity of 0.04 W/mK. All insulated
pipes had a heat transfer coefﬁcient of 9 W/m2K along their outer surface.
• Tank information

of 96 l, and the graywater tank of 1000 l. The storage tank for the radiant ﬂoor system
was of a size of 750 l and the radiant ﬂoor pipes had a capacity of approximately 30 l. The
storage tank is heated up to 30oC [27].

CHAPTER 4
Load Calculations
Heating and cooling load calculation is the basis for determining the energy con-
sumption in a building and designing the HVAC and control systems. A load calculation
consists of calculating the heat and mass (moisture) transferred between the air and the
components and surfaces of the building. It can be performed either for a steady-state
condition to obtain the peak load, or for a certain time period with a transient calculation
which considers heat and mass accumulated in the inner zones, in the walls and other
interior surfaces [32].
Load calculations were performed for both buildings, Dome of Visions and EMBRACE,
in order to determine the heating and cooling demand of each one. Each building was
simulated both with and without the shield and the semi-outdoor area, with emphasis given
on the investigation of the effect that they have on the demand of each construction.
At ﬁrst, an estimation was made by hand calculations and following, a detailed analysis
was performed for both buildings with the simulation software IDA ICE.
4.1
Hand calculations
Initially, the heating and cooling load of the buildings were estimated by hand calcula-
tions. Hand calculations are usually performed at the initial part of a design, so they are
based on approximated values and assumptions. However, they provide an overview of the
building’s needs and act as an indicator for the design of the HVAC system.
The heating and cooling loads calculated in this report consist of ventilation losses, inﬁl-
tration losses, transmission losses, solar heat gains and internal heat gains. Each of the
parameters is analysed in the following sections and the basic inputs for each building are
stated in tables 4.1, 4.2 and 4.3 for DoV and 4.6, 4.7 and 4.8 for EMBRACE.

4.2
IDA ICE calculations
After the hand calculations for the loads, more accurate calculations were performed
using the simulation application IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (ICE, version 4.6.2).
IDA ICE is a simulation tool designed to analyse the energy consumption of an entire
thermal envelope and the indoor climate of the individual zones inside. IDA’s interface
provides a range of ﬂexibility, enabling the user to simulate simple cases, but also to design
advanced and complex cases. The simulated buildings can comprise of one or several
zones, an air handling unit and a primary system. Databases including predeﬁned building
components, material data, product data, standards and special systems are available for
the user. Climate data is provided by weather data ﬁles containing information on actual
or synthetic weather. Additional objects or surrounding buildings can be added so that
shading effects are taken into consideration. Wind driven effects on inﬁltration can be also
considered.
Load calculations were performed in IDA ICE using ideal room units. Ideal room units
should be used to condition the zone when no detailed information about an actual room
unit, such as a fan coil or active chilled beam, is available. They have no given physical
location on any room surface and are not connected to the plant of the building. They
do have a maximum capacity parameter, enabling the user to experiment with limited
heating/cooling capacity. However, this parameter should normally be set to a large enough
value to always cover any foreseen need.
The geometry of each building was implemented into the building simulation program.
Ideal room units were applied into the heated zones and the internal gains were inserted in
each zone as presented in section 4.1 . The year was split in heating and cooling season
and the simulations for each season were performed with ideal units (ideal heaters or ideal
coolers). The load calculations were performed as dynamic simulations with the weather
ﬁle Design Reference Year (DRY) for Copenhagen. In each case, the instantaneous peak
load demand in W/m2 and the energy in kWh is presented. The peak load was calculated
as the total load divided by the total indoor heated area. Each of the two buildings examined

4.3
Discussion
DoV and EMBRACE are not directly comparable since the two buildings, thus models,
are completely different construction-wise. Moreover, the shape of the shields and the
way they are connected to each building, critically affect the outcome of the simulations.
Furthermore, DoV is a building that is poorly insulated compared to EMBRACE, with
its U-value for walls and ﬂoors being 0.34 W/m2K and for windows 2.1 W/m2K. This
veriﬁes the fact that the heating and cooling demand in DoV are much higher than the ones
of EMBRACE. Even though they cannot be directly compared, both buildings appear to
be positively affected by the addition of the shield in terms of energy use and peak loads.
In both DoV and EMBRACE, the shield resulted in lower heating and cooling demand.
Regarding the heating load, the effect of the shield in DoV resulted in the reduction of
37%, while in EMBRACE the respective reduction was 3%. This could be attributed
both to the different levels of insulation of the inner buildings and the way the shields are
connected to them.
As far as the cooling load is concerned, a rather different behaviour is observed. As DoV
is completely enclosed in its shield, none of its sides is exposed to the ambient and no
==PAGE:82==
60
CHAPTER 4. LOAD CALCULATIONS
air ﬂows are created. This results in an increase of the cooling load when the shield is
added due to the greenhouse effect. On the other hand, EMBRACE’s cooling load does
not increase when the shield is implemented. The third case examined was the cooling
demand of the building when natural ventilation strategies were implemented. In this case,
DoV managed to eliminate the green house effect and additionally reduce the cooling
load by 62%. This further reduction can be attributed to the reduced solar energy that is
transferred to the inner building due to the presence of the shield. The same effect justiﬁes
the reduction of EMBRACE’s cooling load with natural ventilation. This reduction was
15%, lower than the reduction observed in DoV since not all windows of EMBRACE
are covered by its shield. Regarding the semi-shield model and results, they cannot be
compared with the above, as the shield is not closed and does not create a semi-outdoor
space. This model, was simulated in order to be compared with the physical measurements
taken in EMBRACE.
After the investigation presented, it can be noted that both shield shapes can contribute to
lower the heating and cooling demands of a residential building in Copenhagen.
Finally, considering the heating and cooling loads that were calculated by hand, they proved
to be similar to the outcome of the simulation results for each building. Even though
several assumptions needed to be made, the results for the heating load for EMBRACE
and DoV both for the shielded and the unshielded versions had small deviations from
the results of the simulations, which indicates that most of the assumptions made for the
hand calculations were realistic. Nevertheless, regarding the cooling load, a difference
was observed when the results from the hand calculation of DoV were compared to the
outcome of the simulations. This can be attributed to some assumptions made, especially
the design temperature in the semi-outdoor area and the shading coefﬁcient the windows
of the inner building assumed to have due to the presence of the shield.

temperature range of 18-28oC. The determined coefﬁcients for each sensor have been used
during the analysis of the data measured. Hobo data loggers monitor relative humidity and
air temperature (ﬁgure 6.1 C) ranging between 10-90% with accuracy ±2.5%.

6.3
Occupancy in the semi-outdoor area
One of the main advantages that a semi-outdoor space is claimed to have, is that it
could be used more often and for longer periods than an outside space, i.e. garden. It is
an area that residents could enjoy being outdoors, but with more favourable conditions
than the ambient and protected from rain, wind or snow. It could increase the living space
of a house for a certain amount of time. In order to prove the above and evaluate how
valuable such a space could be for the tenants of the building, the thermal comfort in the
semi-outdoor area of both EMBRACE and DoV was examined. The indicator used was
the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV), which is a value that combines air temperature, mean
radiant temperature, relative humidity, air speed, metabolic rate, and clothing insulation
[24] and can indicate the overall thermal comfort of the occupants. When evaluating an
indoor space there are standards established that deﬁne the acceptable values for the PMV.
However, people’s thermal sensation could be more tolerant when being in a semi-outdoor
area than when being indoors [10]. For example, it would be considered acceptable to
wear a jacket during winter months in semi-outdoor space in order to feel comfortable.
So, the implementation of ﬂexible clothing insulation was decided in order to resemble
this in the model. Normally, based on the design criteria for different types of spaces, the
clothing insulation assumed is 0.5 clo during summer and 1.0 clo during winter. Since in
the present report, a semi-outdoor space is evaluated, it was decided to set the clo value as
more ﬂexible. According to ISO 7730 [17] and based on the clothing category of "daily
wear clothing" the clothing levels of 0.3 clo and 1.5 clo were chosen, which correspond to:
• 0.3 clo: Panties, T-shirt, shorts, light socks, sandals
• 1.5 clo: Underwear with short sleeves and legs, shirt, trousers, vest, jacket, coat,
socks, shoes.
The acceptable range for PMV values in a semi-outdoor area has not been documented yet.
Initially, it was decided to use -0.7<PMV<0.7, which is the range deﬁned as acceptable
Category III in EN/DS 15251 [19] for indoor spaces. Since this range could be identiﬁed
as conservative, two more cases were considered; -1<PMV<1 and -1.5<PMV<1.5.
When a simulation is performed, the software autoadjusts the clo value within the speciﬁed
range according to the instant temperatures. So, the results from the simulation give
instantanous PMV values throughout the year having taken into consideration this clothing
ﬂexibility.
Based on the PMV values obtained for each month, the amount of hours that someone can
comfortably use the semi-outdoor space was determined. For each month several possible

6.4
Discussion
Measurements of operative temperature, air temperature and RH taken in DoV for
approximately three months were used in order to assess the indoor climate in DoV and
the semi-outdoor area. On average, the operative temperature in the inner zones of the
building was observed to be lower than the acceptable range according to EN/DS 15251
for January, February and March. This is attributed to the large temperature ﬂuctuations
occurred due to the large amount of time the building is not conditioned. During April, the
solar heat gains create temperature peaks during daytime, thus the average temperature for
both rooms falls within the acceptable range. In the semi-outdoor area, the temperature
was on average 7 oC lower than the meeting room but constantly 4 oC higher than the
ambient.
Analysing the vertical air temperature difference, all zones have an average difference
below 2 oC, so can be classiﬁed as category A (EN/ISO 7730 [17]) even though many
ﬂuctuations were noted. Regarding RH, it was not controlled so the results of the mea-
surements were an indicator. However, all zones were within the acceptable categories
of EN/DS 15251 [19] .The semi-outdoor area had higher RH values than the inner zones,
while constantly lower than outdoors.
All the previous classiﬁcation was based on already established standards (EN/ISO
7730 [17], EN/DS 15251 [19]), which apply to indoor environment. The semi-outdoor
area can not be evaluated as such and the thermal environment of such an area has not
been widely studied. The thermal satisfaction of an occupant would depend on several
parameters, such as the use of the area, the duration of the occupancy, the clothing level
==PAGE:111==

and the personal expectations that each occupant has of such an area. In the present report
an investigation was performed in order to examine the amount of time occupants can
spent in the semi-outdoor areas of DoV and EMBRACE.
Considering PMV values as an indicator for thermal comfort and by setting adjustable
clothing insulation (0.3<clo<1.5), three PMV acceptable ranges were considered. Based
on these assumptions, the hours that the semi-outdoor space can be occupied was deﬁned
for each building. When considering the moderate PMV range, the semi-outdoor area
of both buildings can be occupied the same amount of hours per year, regardless of the
different construction, material and geometry of the shield.
Nevertheless, the daily time intervals of comfortable occupancy differs between each
building’s semi-outdoor space. DoV’s semi-outdoor area can be comfortably used between
May and September 16 hours a day and in October for 8 hours a day. On the other hand,
EMBRACE can be used for more months a year, including March and April, but the
comfortable hours of each day are limited during June and July (13 hours) in comparison
to DoV.
If a stricter (-0.7<PMV<0.7) or more ﬂexible (-1.5<PMV<1.5) PMV range is considered,
the annual occupancy hours are considerably decreased or increased respectively, with
40% of the total annual hours characterized as comfortable in the semi-outdoor areas if the
PMV values considered are -1.5<PMV<1.5.
Finally, the semi-outdoor area was proven to be beneﬁcial for both buildings and all
PMV ranges. Comparing the moderate PMV range to the outdoor temperature range, the
annual occupancy hours in the semi-outdoor areas are more than double. Considering the
comfortable occupancy hours outdoors, they are distributed in only summer months, in
contrast to the semi-outdoor areas that can be used also in May, September and October at
least.

7.3
Discussion
The comparison between the operative temperature obtained from the measurements
and the simulation indicated that the created model of DoV performs satisfactorily, consid-
ering the several issues encountered during the measurement period. The fact that DoV
does not operate as it was designed to, there are no setpoints in the heating system and the
occupancy level and schedule is not documented created several deviations between the
results from the measurements and the simulation. Regarding the operative temperature in
the meeting room and ofﬁce room, the outcome of the simulations follows a similar trend
to the one of the measurements with the exception of March. During March, the simulation
results give higher values compared to the measurements. This could be attributed to the
fact that in the simulation model there no natural ventilation, so during days with solar
radiation, high temperatures are noted, whereas in the actual operation of DoV the occu-
pants were able to open the windows/doors/roof top if they considered it was necessary.

building by 4% and 8% respectively. As it was expected, the heating load has sharply
increased compared to the default building, due to the low level of insulation. On the other
hand, the cooling load remained approximately the same for the buildings without shield.
Comparing the effect that the shield has on the buildings, one would expect that in the case
of higher U-values the effect would be greater as the building is less insulated, thus more
sensitive to the ambient conditions. This, however, was not proved by the results, since
although the effect on the heating was slightly higher (from 3.1% to 4.1%), the effect on
the cooling load was considerably lower (from 15% to 8%). It could be concluded that the
reduction is not that signiﬁcant, as the other two sides of the building are exposed to the
ambient and the low level of insulation counteracts with the beneﬁts of the attachment of
the shield.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
The scope of this project was to investigate the energy use and indoor climate in houses
with second skin and semi-outdoor space. The DTU solar decathlon house, EMBRACE,
and the prototype building project of NCC, Dome of Visions, were examined throughout
the process of this project. In order to perform the investigation, physical measurements
were conducted in both buildings and dynamic simulation models were created.
Regarding the effect that the addition of a shield has on the energy use of the buildings,
it was proved to be beneﬁcial for both cases. In particular, the heating load was reduced
in both cases, which was not the case for the cooling load. However, when natural
ventilation was implemented in both models, the cooling load was signiﬁcantly decreased.
Nevertheless, a computational ﬂuid dynamic(CFD) analysis is required in order to ensure
that natural ventilation does not create a problematic environment for the occupants. The
impact of the shield on the heating and cooling load of the buildings was of different scale.
This difference was attributed to the different construction, materials of the buildings and
the different shape of the shield and the way they were connected to each building.
When the existing HVAC system of each building was implemented in the respective IDA
ICE models, EMBRACE followed the same trend as the load calculations, though with
higher consumption. On the other hand, no conclusions could be deducted for DoV, due to
special design and diverse operation schedule of the HVAC system .
Indoor climate assessment was performed only for DoV, since sufﬁcient measurements
in EMBRACE were not available. From the evaluation of the indoor climate of DoV based
on the measurements obtained, it was shown that in the indoor rooms, on average were
lower than the acceptable limits. The special occupancy level and schedule, along with the
fact that the building was not conditioned for a considerable amount of hours, justify both
the low temperatures and the many ﬂuctuations observed. According to that, the indoor
environment classiﬁcation indicated that the indoor rooms were within the acceptable

limits only for half the measured time. Nevertheless, the local discomfort evaluated based
on the vertical air temperature difference, as well as the relative humidity measured were
found within the acceptable limits.
Regarding the semi-outdoor area, the temperatures measured were constantly higher
than the ambient temperature. Since a semi-outdoor area could not be considered as
indoor environment and assessed as such, an investigation was performed examining the
comfortable occupancy hours. Based on the results obtained from both buildings, it was
proven that the implementation of the shield offers additional comfortable occupancy
hours, compared to the respective outdoors. Depending on the different shape of the
shield and corresponding geometry of the semi-outdoor space, these additional hours are
distributed differently throughout the year. Considering the tolerance that an individual
occupant may have on the thermal environment, the three different PMV ranges were
established. Based on these ranges, along with the adjusted clothing level, the hours that
an occupant can feel comfortable in a semi-outdoor area can vary signiﬁcantly.
Moreover, a comparison was conducted between the results obtained from measure-
ments and simulations. The analysis indicated that the created models performed satisfac-
torily, considering the obstacles encountered during the measurement period and the lack
of data.
Summing up, during the process of this project the simulation software IDA ICE was
used to create appropriate models which can simulate the semi-outdoor space of DoV
and EMBRACE. Using the measurements conducted, the created models were validated
to a certain degree. However, further investigation is required as several obstacles were
encountered during the measurement period. Furthermore, it was proved that the addition
of a shield on a building reduces both the heating and cooling load, on the condition
that ﬂexible natural ventilation is implemented during the warmer months, thus avoiding
overheating. Moreover, it was investigated and conﬁrmed that the semi-outdoor space in
both building offers additional occupancy hours, which can be characterized as comfortable
with adjustable clothing level. Comparing the overall performance of the two buildings
examined, it was veriﬁed that, both in respect to energy use and to indoor climate, the
geometry of the shield, the way it is attached to the building and the created semi-outdoor
space are of great importance.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the energy use and indoor climate in buildings
with a semi-outdoor space.
In both buildings, after the implementation of the shield, the heating load was decreased
and so was the cooling load when natural ventilation was implemented in the semi-outdoor
area. This was also indicated by the initial hand calculations performed. Moreover, after
implementing the HVAC system for each building, the consumptions appeared to have the
same trends as in the load calculations.
Based on annual simulations that were performed in order to assess the indoor envi-
ronment of each building, all inner zones of EMBRACE were classiﬁed as Category I for
most of the time, whilst the rooms inside DoV were within Category III for at least half of
the time, according to EN/DS 15251.
Regarding the physical measurements conducted in DoV, the results showed that
the average temperatures in the inside rooms were lower than the acceptable limits and
many ﬂuctuations were noted. In the semi-outdoor space the temperature observed was
constantly higher than the ambient; by 4oC on average. In all zones, the vertical air
temperature difference and relative humidity were within the acceptable range, according
to EN/DS ISO 7730 and EN/DS 15251.
In order to assess the thermal environment in the semi-outdoor space, a alternative
approach was followed since there is not an applicable standard established for such a
space. Considering that the thermal sensation of the occupants is more tolerant in a semi-
outdoor space, an adjustable clothing level and three acceptability ranges were considered.
It was proven that the hours that a semi-outdoor space can comfortably be occupied are
considerably higher than the hours that one can stay outdoors. Comparing the two buildings,
the total number of occupancy hours is almost the same, while their different geometry
of the semi-outdoor areas affect the way that these hours are distributed throughout the

Thereafter, based on the comparison between the simulated and measured values of

CHAPTER 11
Future Studies
The investigation of the outcome of this project generated ideas for future studies. In
the present report, the geometry of two different buildings and shields was investigated and
the effect that the geometry had on the energy use and indoor climate was noted. Therefore,
further investigation on different geometries is suggested so as to discover if there could
be an optimal geometry (combination of building and shield geometry).
As mentioned in previous chapters, more data for EMBRACE is required in order to
conduct an indoor environment assessment in the semi-outdoor area.
The models used in this project simulated the existing HVAC system of each building.
However, since the shield affects the energy use of the building inside, an optimization of
the HVAC system could be considered in terms of its components, capacity and operation
schedule.
Based on such an adjusted HVAC system, a supplementary assessment of the indoor
climate of the semi-outdoor space is recommended.
Although a preliminary study of the importance of materials was conducted, further
investigation is needed on how the performance of the shield is affected by the properties
of the materials of the shield, such as transmittance (visible, UV), reﬂectance, emittance,
absorptance, solar heat gain coefﬁcient and insulating value (U-value).
The results showed that natural ventilation is beneﬁcial for the energy use of the build-
ing and the indoor environment of the semi-outdoor space. Nevertheless, a computational
ﬂuid dynamic(CFD) analysis is required in order to ensure that natural ventilation does not
create a problematic environment for the occupants.
During the warmer months of the year overheating occurred in the semi-outdoor area
of both buildings investigated. In the current project only natural ventilation has been
implemented. However, shading could be another way to address overheating.
In the current project two buildings were modelled and simulated in IDA ICE simulation

INTERVIEW ON THE CONCEPT OF DOME OF VISIONS

Interview on the concept of Dome of Visions
Interview of Gert Jespersen, design director in NCC, to Søren Egert
for www.domeofvisions.com
The third room can be both building envelope and the new meeting point
The third room is neither inside or outside – it is inbetween. This could be, for example,
a cover from one house to another. It might be a covered atrium, it might be an additional
envelope, or it could be a whole cover for one or more buildings. In any case, the third
room protects the facade against cold, wind and rain and simultaneously creates a new
space which could be used for a variety of activities. The third room constitutes an added
dimension, especially in terms of energy consumption, and it indicates that such a space
could become more and more popular among both existing and new buildings. According
to the design director in NCC, Gert Jespersen, the third room intensiﬁes the need for critical
thinking on the energy consumption of the older dwellings today. “The construction of
new buildings is at such low level which indicates that energy renovation in the existing
buildings should start been performed in a much higher rate than it does today. Keeping the
today’s low rate of new buildings, would mean that the buildings used today will remain
standing for at least 200 years. And knowing that 40% of the energy consumption takes
place within the buildings, something should be done about the existing buildings. But
also things need to be new and different, focusing on that by an energy renovation not
only lower energy consumption is ensured but also higher quality of living. And here it
is forseen better indoor climate, feedback to each building’s users, social qualities of the
third room, and longer life for buildings, which are essential elements in the toolbox”, says
Gert Jespersen.
Lacking knowledge
The third room, according to Gert Jespersen, would be good for the environment, energy
consumption, our social lives and in general for those things which do not necessarily
have to take place inside. “But basically we know very little about the third room and its
possibilities”, says Gert Jespersen, who is one of the initiators of an actual measurement
project in the Dome of Visions. Here, DTU along with the company IC-meter and NCC
record air temperature, humidity, CO2 concentration, noise level, etc., and it all gets
recorded continuously and are made available to the interested parties. “We want to ﬁnd
out what happens basically on the climatic point of view in the third room. The way we
now calculate the energy, cannot handle the third room. We lack tools and data. Therefore,
we need to develop some methods so that we can effectively measure what is happening in
such a space. We need to establish some standards, so we can actually ﬁgure out what the

third room actually allows for choice in terms of temperature and humidity, and what is
the energy reduction achieved in the building which is built in it”, says Gert Jespersen. He
emphasizes that almost any version of a third room today is able to reduce heat loss from
buildings, simply because solar irradiance ensures higher temperature in the third room

Several parameters
-The Measurements that have began focus on climate. We can online and continuously
measure various conditions inside the third room and constantly comparing it with the
situation outside. In this way we are more qualiﬁed to say which factors should be met, if
we want certain uses of the third room, says Gert Jespersen.
Several aspects?
He emphasizes that measurements alone have limited value. Measurements have to
be inserted into context, to create a model. How do you think the conditions come into
play. What role does the sun exactly? How does this affect many people in the third room
indoor climate? And the plants? I hope, together with DTU, for a model to be created, and
in the long run can we ﬁnd out if the model is correct, or whether to be drawn into several
aspects, notes Gert Jespersen.
Sound barrier
“If we succeed in both model and measurements, we break the sound barrier, metaphor-
ically speaking about the energy renovation in Denmark. There is far too little happening
in the forefront in comparison with the needs that we have. It should be made clear that
the third room adds qualities that residents and businesses will pay for when they are
performing energy renovation. You get the opportunity for a better indoor climate, and
through space that the room creates, there is also an opportunity for a more promising
social life. That message is important”, stresses Gert Jespersen.
New market
-Maybe we can in NCC use the whole question of the third room to get a new market
started. We may be able to get a start, which I will call the ‘renovation bubble’. Currently,
the fact is that only in public buildings, you can really make something out of energy
renovation. Socially, we will all come in any case for energy renovation at some point,
ending Gert Jespersen.
