Abstract
Design of bridges with long and slender spans may introduce problems in the usability of
the bridge, in terms of safe and comfortable vehicle passage. The objective of this thesis
has been to develop a numerical tool for dynamic vehicle-bridge interaction(VBI)-analysis,
with the ambition of analysing the mutual dynamic interaction between a bridge and a
passing train. The project was initiated in cooperation with Ramboll Group A/S, who
participated in setting the framework and input for the VBI-program.
The numerical tool has been developed as an add-on for an existing ﬁnite element(FE)-
software, which must provide the dynamic characteristics of the bridge-structure, to the
VBI-program, by modal expansion.
The vehicle-models were developed as multi-rigid-body systems with spring-damper
suspensions separating the vehicle-body from the wheels. Three vehicle models of different
complexity have been developed. The most advanced has three degrees-of-freedom located
at the vehicle-body, which is attached to four wheels by four independent suspensions.
The system matrices of the vehicles were developed from energy balance equations
and by settling the kinematic equations of the vehicle-body and wheels. Interaction
matrices, dependent on the vehicle location, were created to couple the multi-rigid-body
models to the bridge structure. With moving vehicles, the interaction matrices become
time-dependent, which also entails a time-variation in the coupled system matrices. By
modifying the Newmark time integration procedure to take into account time-dependency
of the system matrices, the dynamic response of the bridge deck and the interacting
vehicles was successfully computed, by the VBI-program.
Through veriﬁcation examples and case studies, the VBI-program was proven versatile
and reliable, and the commercial applications were tested on a design proposal for the
Storstroem Bridge, made by Ramboll Group A/S. The Storstroem Bridge analysis, with
multiple interacting three-dimensional vehicles showed that passenger comfort and safety
was not near being compromised for any realistic train velocity.

Resumé
Design af lange og slanke brospænd kan introducere problemer i broens anvendelighed,
med hensyn til sikker og komfortabel passage af køretøjer. Formålet med denne afhandling
har været at udvikle et numerisk værktøj til dynamisk køretøj-bro-interaktionsanalyse(KBI),
med det formål at analysere den gensidige dynamiske interaktion mellem en bro og et
passerende tog. Projektet blev igangsat i samarbejde med Rambøll Gruppen A/S, der deltog
i fastlæggelsen af rammerne for KBI-programmet og forsynede programmet med input.
Det numeriske analyseprogram er udviklet som en tilføjelse til et eksisterende element-
metodeprogram, der skal levere brokonstruktionens dynamiske egenskaber til KBI-programmet,
ved modal udvidelse.
Køretøjsmodellerne blev skabt som systemer af ﬂere stive komponenter med affjedringsys-
temer til at adskille køretøjets karosseri fra hjulene. Tre køretøjsmodeller af forskellig
kompleksitet er udviklet. Den mest avancerede har tre frihedsgrader, placeret i køretøjets
karosseri, som er fastgjort til ﬁre hjul gennem ﬁre uafhængige affjedringsystemer. System-
matricerne for køretøjerne blev udviklet ud fra energibalanceligninger og ved at fastlægge
de kinematiske ligninger for køretøjets karosseri og hjul. Interaktionsmatricer, afhængige
af køretøjets placering, blev beregnet for at koble køretøjsmodellerne til brokonstruk-
tionen. Med bevægelige køretøjer bliver interaktionsmatricerne tidsafhængige, hvilket
indebærer en tidsvariation i de koblede systemmatricer. Ved at modiﬁcere Newmark-
tidsintegrationsproceduren til at tage højde for tidsafhængigheden af systemmatricerne,
blev det dynamiske respons fra brodækket og de interagerende køretøjer med succes
beregnet af KBI-programmet.
Gennem veriﬁceringseksempler og casestudier, blev KBI-programmet bevist alsidigt og
pålideligt, og de kommercielle applikationer blev testet på et designforslag til Storstrøms-
broen, lavet af Rambøll Gruppen A/S. Casestudiet med Storstrømsbroen, med ﬂere inter-
agerende tredimensionelle køretøjer viste, at passagerers komfort og sikkerhed var langt
fra at blive kompromitteret for realistiske toghastigheder.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
As more advanced design methods of bridge-structures evolve, design solutions, including
longer free spans, are enabled. The design of bridges with long and slender spans is highly
inﬂuenced by the dynamic characteristics of the structure. The primary problems arise as
the low natural frequencies of slender structures coincide with the frequencies of nature
loads, leading to the risk of resonance conditions. Other problems arise in the usability
of the bridge in terms of safe and comfortable vehicle passage. This project looks into
numerical analysis of vehicle-structure interaction of signiﬁcant dynamic nature, more
speciﬁcally high-speed train passage over long-span bridges.
Storstroem Bridge visualisation, source: DISSING+WEITLING architecture [1]

1. Introduction
The project is initiated in cooperation with the consulting engineering group Ramboll
Group A/S, with the objective of developing a numerical tool for dynamic vehicle-bridge
interaction(VBI)-analysis. The purpose of the tool is to capture the mutual dynamic
interaction between a bridge and a passing train.
The dynamic response of a bridge, induced by train passage, is closely approximated, by
representing the train by a series of moving loads. Attainment of the dynamic response of
the moving train is, however, a necessity to assess train passenger comfort and passage
safety criteria. Passenger comfort is assessed in respect to the accelerations within the
passenger cars and passage safety is evaluated based on the magnitude of the contact
forces, between the wheels of the train and the bridge deck. The vehicle accelerations
and contact forces are only accessible from the combined movement of the bridge and the
vehicle, hence establishing the demand for a VBI-analysis.
Numerical procedures for dynamic VBI-analyses have already been developed. A minor-
ity of commercial ﬁnite element(FE)-packages provides VBI-tools, however with very
limited access to the underlying theory and numerical implementation. Not being able
to access the underlying theory and code makes it difﬁcult for the users to investigate if
the VBI-tool performs as promised. This has created the interest for a user-friendly and
well-documented VBI-tool, which can be operated by structural engineers in the industry.
The VBI-tool to be developed, is intended as an add-on for any existing FE-software,
capable of computing the dynamic characteristics of spatial beam-structures. The separate
FE-software should thus provide input for the VBI-tool, describing the dynamic properties
of the bridge-structure, in a compact tensor format. The tool is to be developed in the com-
puting environment MATLAB and customised to an input format, established by Ramboll
Group A/S.
The train-models are to be created within the VBI-program and sought realised as vibrat-
ing multi-rigid-body systems, of different complexities. The dynamic properties of the
train-models are then to be coupled to the system matrices of the bridge-structure, by the
implementation of a location-dependent interaction matrix. The dynamic response of the
coupled system must ﬁnally be obtained by a direct time integration procedure, taking into
account the time-dependent locations of the vehicle models.
The commercial applications of the VBI-tools will subsequently be tested on different
bridge types, including a design proposal for the Storstroem Bridge, made by Ramboll
Group A/S.

CHAPTER 2
Structure by Modal Expansion
The program for dynamic vehicle-bridge interaction is designed as an add-on to any
existing FE-software capable of identifying the vibration modes of bridge-structures with
the path of vehicle motion developed by spatial linear-elastic beam elements. Besides
the bridge deck on which the vehicles pass, the full FE-model may be comprised of
pylons, piers, and cables which neither are parts of the path of vehicle motion. These
elements indeed inﬂuence the overall vibration modes of the structure, but the only vehicle-
structure interaction appears at the bridge deck elements. If the entire FE-model were to
be implemented in the vehicle-bridge interaction program, it would require manual user
input on which nodes are to interact with the vehicle. It is hence desired to extract only
the vehicle interacting nodes of the bridge geometry from the FE-model. The extracted
vehicle interacting part should, however, still possess the same dynamic characteristics
under vehicle passage as the full FE-structure. This, along with a desire for compact input,
has led to the application of what in this thesis is called truncated structure development
by modal expansion. This method relies on relatively few input parameters to extract the
dynamic nature of the full FE-model, even though only a part of the global geometry is
described. Structure development by modal expansion requires a node coordinate array Xin
of the path of vehicle motion, mass normalised mode-shape array Sin describing the same
nodes as the node coordinate array and a vector f with the associated natural frequencies.

Vehicle Model and Coupling of
System Matrices
With the establishment of the modal system matrices governing the dynamic characteristics
of the bridge-structure, the subsequent step is to deﬁne the train-model and how it interacts
with the bridge deck. The vehicle-models are considered as multi-rigid-body systems with
the rigid components connected by spring-damper systems. Coupled system matrices,
containing both the modal system parameters governing the bridge and the system parame-
ters governing the vehicles are established. The associated vehicle-bridge coupling terms,
dependent on the speciﬁc vehicle location, is accounted for by the interaction matrix b,
thoroughly described in Section 3.5.
Three versions of the interaction program are developed, each customised to a vehicle
model of a certain complexity. The vehicle-models are primarily modelled to analyse
the vertical dynamic interaction between vehicle and bridge. The vehicle-models are
accordingly not equipped with the required degrees-of-freedom to describe the horizontal
dynamic interaction. The following assumptions are applied to all vehicles to realise the
multi-rigid-body models:
• All vehicle components are considered vibrating within small displacements.
• Vehicle-body, wheels, and axles are considered rigid components.
• The suspension systems connecting each wheel to the vehicle-body consist of linear
springs and linear viscous dampers.
• Vehicle-wheels are in full contact with the bridge track at all times.
For the sake of simplicity, the coupling of system matrices in the sections to come is based
on the scenario where only a single vehicle interacts with the bridge. The implementation
of multi-vehicle-interaction is described in Section 3.4.

Nh, associated with the horizontal interaction, are however different from those of the
vertical interaction. The horizontal contact force is assumed to act in the local element
2e-direction with a vertical eccentricity eh to the vehicle-body center of mass. The body
mass is distributed to each wheel location, dependent on the location of the center of mass.
The properties that differ from the vertical interaction are illustrated in Appendix A.5.

structure. The numerical solution to the coupled system of equations of motion in (3.6) is

dictated by the time-dependent position of the interacting vehicle, realised numerically by
the inclusion of the interaction matrix b = b(t), in the coupled system matrices. For good

Veriﬁcation examples have to a large extent shown the reliability of the VBI-tool and
case studies have illustrated its primary capabilities. The following section discusses the
limitations of the program, originating from assumptions and choices made throughout
the development. The subsequent section gives a general conclusion on the program

8.1
Limitations of The VBI-tool
The ﬁrst limitations to contemplate, are those arising from early choices on what the
program should be capable of. Three separate programs have been developed, where each
program has one of the three vehicle-models implemented. It is not possible to do an anal-
ysis with different vehicles, that does not possess the same degrees-of-freedom. The user
has to choose one of the three programs and thus, one speciﬁc vehicle model, dependent on
the necessary complexity of the analysis. For simplicity, some parameters of the analysis
are chosen constant through time. The vehicle velocity vt is constant and applied to all
vehicles in the same analysis. The horizontal and vertical eccentricities ey and eh, from the
bridge shear center to each vehicle center of mass is likewise assumed constant through
time. These assumptions are deemed rather realistic, however, if desired, both the vehicle
velocity and the eccentricities can be changed to time- and location-dependent parameters
by relatively few adjustments to the VBI-program.
Other potentially inﬂuential aspects in the interaction and vehicle modelling are disre-
garded, due to the chosen limits to the complexity of the VBI-tool. This includes relevant
components in the vehicle modelling, vehicle-vehicle coupling, and implementation of
61
==PAGE:87==
8.1. Limitations of The VBI-tool
track irregularities. These disregarded aspects are brieﬂy discussed in Section 8.3, con-
cerning further related studies.
8.1.1
Application of External Loads
In the current state of the VBI-program, external loads are only applicable to the degrees-of-
freedom associated with the bridge deck. These loads are applied in geometric coordinates
in the VBI-driver and transformed to the modal coordinate representation in the main
analysis function MainProgram.m. With a long-span bridge including pylons, piers,
and cables, it could nevertheless be relevant to apply loads on these elements. Seismic
loads, for instance, should be applied to the base of the piers and pylons. This is currently
not possible, as the input geometry and mode-shapes only include the degrees-of-freedom
associated with the bridge deck. Placing loads on the excluded degrees-of-freedom is
possible if the user constructs the modal loads from the full matrix of mode-shapes S, in a
separate program, and provides the modal loads as input for the VBI-program. It would
thus only require suppression of the load vector in the VBI-driver and the transformation
to the modal coordinate representation in MainProgram.m.
8.1.2
Limitations of FE-input
Some assumptions made in the modelling of the interaction matrix b can directly result
in the VBI-program misinterpreting the contact forces. The consequences of these as-
sumptions are hence important to emphasise, in order for the user to operate the program
correctly. What characterise these potentially compromising properties is that they are
applied for modelling simplicity and due to the limited format of the FE-input. The
background and potential consequences of the assumptions are presented in the following,
which concerns the resulting limitations on the FE-input.
The spatial beam model can have whatever geometry desired, as long only the path of
vehicle motion is extracted as FE-input. However, due to the way of interaction matrix
implementation, demands exist to where in the modelling space, the bridge is located and
how the local element coordinate systems are orientated. A central assumption made for
the VBI-analysis is that the gravitational load always acts in the global 3-direction. The
application of this assumption limits the modelling of the FE-input, as vertical must always
correspond to the direction of the global 3-axis in the modelling space. The assumption
of the gravitational load acting in the global 3-direction is applied when the contact force
Fw is dissolved into components in the local element coordinate system, when creating
the vertical interaction matrix b. The underlying theory is presented in Section 3.5. The
vertical and longitudinal force components F3 and F1, illustrated in Figure 8.1a, depend
62
==PAGE:88==
8.1. Limitations of The VBI-tool
on the element inclination angle θ12. The inclination angle is deﬁned as the element
inclination to the global 1-2-plane, as the gravitational load is assumed to act perpendicular
to that. In the creation of the vector of domain load components h in (3.42) and the
displacement interpolation matrix N in (3.45), an additional assumption is made for the
orientation of the individual local element coordinate systems. The assumption is that a
force in the global 3-direction only generates vertical and longitudinal force components in

coordinate system is rotated about the local 1e-axis, the direction of the contact force Fw
would be misinterpreted. As the force components depend only on the inclination to the
global 1-2-plane, the contact force would be interpreted as acting with an inclination to the
global 3-direction, as illustrated in Figure 8.1b.

8.2. Conclusion
8.2
Conclusion
The thesis objective was to develop a numerical tool for dynamic train-bridge interaction
analysis, for the assessment of passenger comfort and safety. By representing the input
bridge-structure by modal expansion, the VBI-program can be applied as an add-on for an
existing FE-software. The exclusion of nodes, not associated with the bridge deck, allows
for a user-friendly input format, where the train-models can only interact with the intended
nodes. Three train-models of different complexity were created and implemented in three
different versions of the VBI-tool. The train-models have been successfully coupled to the
modal system matrices of FE-bridge-structures, by use of location-dependent interaction
matrices. These matrices control the distribution of contact forces and vehicle system
parameters to the coupled system. The interaction matrices are implemented in the coupled
system matrices and the movement of the coupled trains, thus introduce time-varying
matrices. By modifying the Newmark time integration procedure to take into account the
time-dependency of the matrices, the dynamic response of bridge and interacting vehicles
has been successfully computed. Through veriﬁcation examples and case studies, the
VBI-program has been proven reliable and capable of describing the vertical dynamic
interaction between the train-models and the bridge deck. Still, with room for further
development of the VBI-tool, it meets the objectives, established by the chosen complexity
of the interaction model. The commercial capabilities cover both comfort and safety
assessments of vehicle passage over bridges. These capabilities have been tested on
a project proposal of the Storstroem Bridge. For the chosen vehicle-setup, the analysis
showed that passenger comfort and safety was not near being compromised for any realistic
train velocity.

8.3. Further Studies
8.3
Further Studies
This closing section gives four potential additions to the present vehicle-bridge interaction
program, along with brief comments on what the additional implementations would imply.
8.3.1
Track Irregularities
Track irregularities are deviations from ideal rail geometry and can arise from various
events, such as manufacturing imperfections, installation errors, and joint dislocations [19].
It is considered a very inﬂuential factor in the dynamic interaction of vehicle-bridge
systems [20]. From parametric studies in [19] track irregularities are shown to have a
signiﬁcant inﬂuence on especially the vehicle-body accelerations and contact forces, which
makes it an important aspect when evaluating comfort and safety. In [20] and [21] the track
irregularities are proposed implemented by adding irregularity functions to the kinematic
relations between the wheels and the bridge. The kinematic equations in (3.8) hence imply
that the position-dependent irregularity function may be implementable in the interaction
matrix b, in further studies.
8.3.2
Coupling with Adjacent Vehicles
For the current interaction program, the coupling of adjacent train-wagons by a longitudinal
suspension system would be irrelevant, as all vehicles are travelling with the same velocity.
This ensures that the distance between each vehicle is constant through time, which
means that no dynamic interaction can occur between them. However, if the longitudinal
movement of each of the train-wagons depends on the movement of the adjacent vehicles,
longitudinal coupling and thus longitudinal vehicle-body degrees-of-freedom would be
necessary. Movement dependent on adjacent vehicles is imaginable by considering a
power car with an engine, "pulling" the passenger wagons behind. The implementation
of longitudinal vehicle coupling implies an extension of the system matrices by the
longitudinal degrees-of-freedom, where coupling terms occur between adjacent vehicles.
8.3.3
Additional Rigid Components in Vehicle Models
For the present vehicle-models, vehicle-body and wheels are the only rigid components
implemented. A more realistic train model would also consist of bogies, which should be
modelled as rigid components between the vehicle-body and the wheels. This requires
a primary suspension system, connecting the bogies to the wheels, and a secondary
suspension system, connecting the bogies to the vehicle-body. The bogies could potentially
be modelled with similar degrees-of-freedom as the vehicle-body. In the implementation
of the additional degrees-of-freedom, the bogies would require a considerable extension of
the kinematic equations of the vehicle, as the movement of the bogies should be related to
both wheels and vehicle-body.
65
==PAGE:91==
8.3. Further Studies
8.3.4
Horizontal Interaction
The present vehicles are primarily modelled to assess the vertical dynamic vehicle-bridge
interaction. In order to properly analyse the risk of derailment, horizontal dynamic
interaction is needed to compare vertical and horizontal contact forces. Horizontal in-
teraction is implemented by adding two degrees-of-freedom to the vehicle-body of the
three-dimensional vehicle, illustrated in Figure 3.1. The required degrees-of-freedom
are a translational degree-of-freedom in the y-axis and a rotational degree-of-freedom
about the z-axis. From similar energy considerations to those of the vertical coupling, the
horizontal vehicle interaction contribution to the coupled system matrices can be deduced.
The present horizontal interaction matrix bh, would need to be modiﬁed, to take into
account the horizontal dynamic interaction.

A.8.1
Remarks
A few remarks on the driver ﬁle.
• Above is an input example of ﬁve three-dimensional vehicles passing the Storstroem
Bridge model.
• Adaption of input: The parameter y in line 50 is supposed to scale the mode-shapes
so that they are given in meter. If the mode-shape input is already in meter, then
y = 0. If the mode-shape input is in millimeter then y = 3. There has been some
discrepancies with the mode-shape input for the Storstroem Bridge model and it is
assumed that these have been written in micrometer which results in y = 6.
• In line 109 the vector initial is deﬁned. initial contains the distances from the front
wheels of each vehicle to the ﬁrst bridge node. initial thus holds the distances xin as
described in Chapter 5. The length of initial should correspond to the number of
desired vehicles in the analysis.
• In line 115 and 116 two velocity parameters are deﬁned. When using ANALYSIS =
1, vt is used and when using ANALYSIS = 2, vtpar is used.
• From line 289 in the driver, the plot and animation functions are located along with
the option of scaling different parameters of the animation. By setting plotﬁg in line
296 or 301 equal to 1, standard response ﬁgures will show. Setting them equal to
any other number will cancel them. The same applies for animation in line 317.
• Note that not all parameters are given the same names in the input driver ﬁle, as in
this report. All parameters in the driver ﬁle and in all sub-functions are however
described in detail in the program.
