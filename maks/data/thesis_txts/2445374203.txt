Summary
In this thesis we study the problem of traﬃc sign classiﬁcation. Traﬃc sign
recognition is an important problem to solve in order for autonomous vehicles
and human drivers to understand each other and predict each others actions. A
major issue in traﬃc sign recognition is the design diﬀerences between countries,
which might seem irrelevant for a human but pose signiﬁcant challenges for a
machine. These causes classiﬁers to generalize poorly on datasets from other
countries, and therefore state-of-the-art traﬃc sign recognition systems are still
country speciﬁc.
We brieﬂy discuss the theory behind convolutional neural networks, generative
adversarial networks, domain adaptation, and how they can be used to clas-
sify images. We give an overview of previous work done on publicly available
datasets. We conduct classiﬁcation experiments with state-of-the-art classiﬁer
on several publicly available datasets and study the cross dataset performance.
We extend the classiﬁer to perform domain adaptation between the datasets, by
making it domain invariant through mixing of the features. We ﬁnd that domain
adaptation causes the classiﬁers to predict the classes with the highest prior on
the training sets, and generally does not improve the cross dataset performance.

og menneskelige chauﬀører kan forstå hinanden og forudsige hinandens hand-
linger. Et væsentligt problem i genkendelse af traﬁkskilte er designforskellene
mellem lande, som kan virke irrelevante for et menneske, men kan være et stort
problem for en computer model. Disse medfører, at modeller generaliserer dårligt
på datasæt fra andre lande, og derfor er state-of-the-art genkendelsessystemer
stadig speciﬁkke for hvert land.
Vi diskuterer kort teorien bag convolutional neurale netværk, generative adver-
sarial netværk, domain adaptation og hvordan de kan bruges til at klassiﬁcere
billeder. Vi giver et overblik over tidligere forskning udført på oﬀentligt tilgæn-
gelige datasæt. Vi gennemfører eksperimenter med state-of-the-art modeller på
ﬂere oﬀentligt tilgængelige datasæt og studerer modellernes præcision på tværs
af datasættene. Vi udvider modellerne til at udføre domain adaptation mellem
datasættene ved at blande variablene og gøre dem invariant overfor domænet.
Vi ﬁnder, at domain adaptation medfører, at modellerne forudsiger klasserne
med den højeste fremkomst på træningssættene, og det generelt ikke forbedrer

Chapter 1
Introduction
The 2007 DARPA challenge [BIS09] served as a important milestone in the ﬁeld
of autonomous vehicles. Of the 11 autonomous vehicles in the ﬁnals, 6 were
successfully able to navigate an urban environment to reach the ﬁnish line, with
the fastest vehicle ﬁnishing with an average speed of 22.5km/h. Since then the
ﬁeld of autonomous vehicles have received a large amount of attention.
We
have seen a gradual increase in vehicle automation with a diverse pamphlet of
drivers assistance programs, but we are yet to see a fully autonomous car as
a commercial product. However, major tech-companies like Waymo (Google),
Uber, Lyft, and Tesla as well as traditional car manufacturing companies are all
making strides in the ﬁeld, and expect to lunch commercial fully autonomous
vehicles in the beginning of the next decade [Aut]. Tesla founder and CEO,
Elon Musk, have stated, he expects almost all new cars to be fully autonomous
in just 10 years.
My guess is that in probably 10 years it will be very unusual for cars to be built
that are not fully autonomous.[Elo]
However, other are less optimistic.
CEO of Toyota Research Institute, Gill
Pratt, stated in June of 2017 that no market participant is close to achieving
true full autonomy,
... a wonderful goal but none of us in the automobile or IT industries are close
to achieving true Level 5 autonomy.[Gil]
==PAGE:10==
2
and roboticist Rodney Brooks predicts that by 2031 "a major city bans parking
and cars with drivers from a non-trivial portion of a city" and in 2045 "the
majority of US cities have the majority of their downtown under such rules"
[Rod]. As such it seems fully autonomous vehicles and human drivers must be
able to co-exist for a long time. This means autonomous vehicles cannot rely on
communication between vehicles, but need to monitor and predict the intention
of human drivers and behave in ways expected by human drivers.
For this
purpose detection and recognition of traﬃc sign are very important problem to
solve.
Traﬃc sign classiﬁcation is a diﬃcult multi-class classiﬁcation problem. There is
a large number of diﬀerent traﬃc sign categories, and problems such as diﬀerent
weather conditions, lighting variations, partial occlusions, diﬀerent traﬃc signs
perspectives, motion-blurring, and deliberate vandalism make classiﬁcation even
more diﬃcult [EEF+18]. To help solve this problem several public databases of
traﬃc sign have been created with the most used on being the German Traf-
ﬁc Sign Recognition Benchmark [SSSI12]. Using these datasets we have seen
several deep classiﬁers using convolutional neural networks (CNN) capable of
outperforming human recognition [AGAGSM18], [CMMS12]. However, due to
the lack of traﬃc sign standardization current traﬃc sign recognition systems
are still country speciﬁc. State-of-the-art deep classiﬁcation networks requires
large amounts of labeled training data. Acquiring this labeled data for all coun-
tries in the world would be an very slow and expensive process. Thus arise
the need for state-of-the-art classiﬁcation models that can achieve a low cross
country generalization error.
There exist two main design standards for traﬃc signs. The Manual on Uni-
form Traﬃc Control Devices (MUTCD) [SoC] in use in America and the Vienna
Convention on Road Signs and Signals [Vie] used in Europe. The Vienna Con-
vention on Road Signs and Signals was created with the goal to universally
deﬁne and visually describe traﬃc signs. However, currently only 68 countries
have signed the treaty, and even between these countries there are still minor
visual variations between countries. These minor diﬀerences might be irrelevant
for a human, but pose signiﬁcant challenges for a machine [MTM12b]. Both the
European Road Assessment Program (EuroRAP) [Eur] and Austroads [Aus]
have made studies on problems facing traﬃc sign recognition and recommends
a standardization of traﬃc signs.
Ganin and Ajakan et al. suggest using adversarial training to train neural net-
works capable of performing domain adaptation [GUA+16], [GL15], and show
it can be used to classify traﬃc signs from the German Traﬃc Sign Recognition
Benchmark when trained on synthetically created traﬃc signs [MKCK13]. In
this thesis we extend the idea of Ganin and Ajakan. We investigate the per-
formance of state-of-the-art traﬃc sign recognition algorithms on datasets from

2.1.2
Spatial Pooling Layers
After a convolution layer, a neural network is usually followed by a pooling
layer. The role of this layer is to improve the computational eﬃciency of the
network such the following layers will have fewer inputs to process.
This is
especially important when the following layer is a fully connected layer where
the number of parameters depends on the input size. The most popular pooling
function is max-pooling [SMB10]. The output of the max-pooling function is
the maximum in a rectangular kernel around the input pixel. By moving this
kernel more than one pixel at a time it is possible to downsample the input.
The amount the kernel is moved each time is called the stride. An example of
max pooling with downsampling can be seen in ﬁgure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Maxpooling with downsampling using a kernel of size 3 and a
stride of 2. Every output neuron takes the maximum value within
its receptive ﬁeld.
Another beneﬁt of pooling is that the model becomes invariant to small trans-
lations. Imagine shifting all inputs in ﬁgure 2.3 right once, and all the outputs
would still be the same. This is very useful when we want to determine the
existence of certain features in an image, but do not care much for their exact
location.

make CNNs invariant to rotation, scaling, skewing, and cropping. In theory
such transformation eliminates the need for hand-crafted data augmentation and
dataset extensions. Spatial transformers perform a diﬀerential transformation

algorithm.

2.3
Regularization
Very large neural networks have millions of parameters and can memorize very
large datasets data. During training, their accuracy on the training set typi-
cally converges towards 100%, while it degrades on the test set. This is called
overﬁtting, and arise because the models start to ﬁt small details only present
in individual data points or the measurement error in the training dataset. The
test data might then be wrongly classiﬁed, because these small details is not
present or the data points have diﬀerent measurement errors. We already saw
how parameter sharing in convolution networks acts as an regularization method
by decreasing the number of parameters, and the ﬂexibility of the models.
2.3.1
L2 regularization
One of the simplest and common approaches to overcome overﬁtting is to intro-
duce a weight decay term to your loss function (2.14). This term penalize the
size of the parameters, and drives the parameter values closer to the origin. This
prevents the network from modeling the training data exactly and therefore help
generalize to new examples. The loss function then become
J(w) = α
2 wT w + J(w)
(2.27)
Taking the derivative with respect to the parameters w the new update formula
becomes
wt+1 = (1 −λα)w −λ∇wJ(w)
(2.28)
The update formula now includes a term which reduces the weight vector by
a constant amount during each step. By making a quadratic approximation of
the loss function and drawing contours of the loss function around the unreg-
ularized minimum w∗as in ﬁgure 2.5 we can see how L2 regularization works.
Along directions where the eigenvalues of the hessian of the loss function is large
the eﬀect of the regularization is small, and vice versa. The directions which
parameters greatly reduces the loss function are kept nearly the same, but di-
rections along which the loss function stays nearly constant are forced towards
zero. This is an example of bias-variance trade oﬀ.

2.3.2
Early Stopping
As mentioned previously when training deep networks we often start to see the
accuracy on the test set begins to decrease with more training. Early stopping is
a simple technique to stop training when the maximum accuracy on a validation
set is observed. We periodically evaluate the performance on the validation set
during training, and store the parameters if the performance improved. If the
performance does not improve during a preset patience period, we stop training
and return the stored best model.
Bishop [Bis95] shows that early stopping applied to a quadratic model is similar
to L2 regularization, where the quantity (λ · τ)−1, λ being the learning rate and
τ the number of optimization steps, serves the role as weight decay parameter α.
He thus argues that early stopping limits the optimization procedure to a volume
around the initial parameter value w0 similar to how L2 regularization causes
the minimum of the total loss (2.27) closer to the origin in ﬁgure 2.5. Early
stopping has an advantage over L2 regularization in the fact that the stopping
point is chosen automatically by at a particularly good point by evaluating the
generalization on a validation set, and not set as a hyperparameter.

2.3.3
Dropout
Dropout [SHK+14] is a computationally very cheap and very eﬀective regular-
ization technique of deep neural networks. Dropout trains the ensemble of all
subnetworks that can be formed by removing non output units from a parent
network, and acts like a model averaging over all possible subnetworks. To train
with dropout, each time we load a minibatch we randomly sample a diﬀerent bi-
nary mask, µ, to apply to all input and hidden units in the network. The binary
mask has the eﬀect of turning oﬀrandom activations in the network by setting
them to zero. The sampled subnetwork is then trained for one step using the
usual forward-pass, backwards-pass and learning update. Since all sampled net-
works are subnetworks of the same parent network the parameter sharing among
the subnetworks causes the remaining networks to converge towards good set-
tings of the parameters. The parameter sharing of dropout training makes it
possible to represent an exponential amount of models with a limited amount
of memory.
As in other ensemble methods we compute the arithmetic mean over all trained
models to do inference. For dropout this quickly becomes expensive to evaluate
due to the exponentially increasing number of subnetworks. Instead a better
approach is to use the normalized geometric mean.

investigated features are grey scale pixel values of 28 × 28 images, pyramid of

The ﬁrst adversarial based neural network domain adaptation was done by
Ganin and Ajakan et al.
[GUA+16], [GL15].
Ganin and Ajakans approach
is to modify the feature representation of a neural network in an adversarial
manner by training two classiﬁers. One classiﬁer predict the class labels and
the other classiﬁer attempts to predict the domain of samples. The label clas-
siﬁer is trained to be able to predict class labels using samples from the source
domain and the domain classiﬁer is trained to be able to predict the domain
of all samples both from the source and the target domain. The deep feature
extractor is trained such that the label classiﬁer is capable of predicting the class
labels of samples, but the domains are indistinguishable in the feature space.
That means it is trained such that the domain classiﬁer is unable to predict
the domain of samples. Thus the feature extractor is trained to ﬁnd separable
features for the label classiﬁer but clustered when used by the domain classiﬁer.

Experiments
We will base the classiﬁcation networks of our experiments on the state-of-the-
art networks determined by Garcia et. al [AGAGSM18]. During the prepro-
cessing stage, images are sampled to an image size of 48 × 48 pixels, and both
global normalization and local contrast normalization with Gaussians kernels
[JKL09] are computed. The eﬀect of this normalization is shown in ﬁgure 5.1.
We use the negative loglikelihood (2.14) as our objective function, and we use
stochastic gradient decent as our optimization algorithm. We use a mini-batch
size of 50 samples per iteration, and set an initial learning rate of λ = 0.01. We
add an exponential decay to learning rate reducing it by a factor of 0.9 after
each epoch. All experiments are conducted using the Pytorch v. 0.4.1 package
for Python 3.6.
Using a validation set of 10% of the available training data, we investigate a
few possible modiﬁcations to the proposed procedure. The proposed possible
changes are: Increasing the input image size from 48×48 to 186×186, inclusion
of dropout layers, and inclusion of weight decay L2 regularization.
We use
the procedure proposed in [AGAGSM18] as the base, and select the optimal
strategy one at a time using the randomly selected validation set. In order to
ensure the best possible generalization across datasets, we would like the value
of hyper-parameters to be the same for all dataset.
In testing the optimal image size of the input, we sampled each image to either
48 × 48 or 186 × 186 during preprocessing.
We also test the importance of
making images square by zero-padding along the shorter dimension before being
re-sized to 186 × 186. The image size of 48 × 48 used the network architecture

5.3
Unsupervised Adversarial Training
We now turn to the domain adversarial networks as described in section 2.5.
For the class labelling part of the network we use the architecture from table
4.2. As the domain classiﬁer we use two fully connected layers with 100 hidden
neurons and 2 output neurons.
We will begin by considering unsupervised domain adaption. We train on an-
notated samples from the source domain and unannotated samples from the
target domain. The loss function presented in (2.50) serves as the objective
function. One issue when sampling from two datasets is the large diﬀerence in
dataset sizes between the German GTSRB dataset and the other datasets. In
[GUA+16] they use the same number of samples from both datasets in every
iteration, and limit the epoch length using the smaller of the two datasets. In
order to utilize all samples in the larger datasets we instead limit the epoch
length using the larger of the datasets and resample the smaller dataset after
a full pass of all images. As such, images in the smaller dataset maybe used
multiple times in an epoch but in a random order. Again we use a mini-batch
size of 50 samples per iteration, set an initial learning rate of λ = 0.01, and
add an exponential decay to learning rate reducing it by a factor of 0.9 after
each epoch. We experienced a problem with exploding gradients in the the ﬁnal
convolution layer before the split into domain and labeling branches. This was
solved by limiting updates of the domain branch to only every third iteration
and removing dropout layers.
Ganin and Ajakan et al. suggest initialising the domain tuning parameter to
zero and gradually increase it to the ﬁnal value of αp = 1 using the formula,
αp =
2
1 + e−10·p −1
(5.1)
where p denotes the training progress and is increased linearly from 0 to 1.
This should help the domain classiﬁer be less sensitive to noisy signals in the
early training stages. The networks are trained for 50 epochs, and use the same
learning rate and batch size as in the source version.

Chapter 6
Future Work and
Conclusion
In this thesis we study the domain shift between signs from diﬀerent countries,
and investigate the ability to train classiﬁcation models using domain adaptive
neural networks capable of achieving a low generalization error when train-
ing and testing on diﬀerent countries. We train a state-of-the-art classiﬁer on
all datasets and ﬁnd that it does achieve state-of-the-art performance on all
datasets. However, we ﬁnd that the set of optimal features for classiﬁcation
diﬀers greatly between the 2 main design standards, the Vienna Convention on
Road Signs and Signal in Europe and the Manual on Uniform Traﬃc Control
Devices in the US. Even within these standards small regional diﬀerences gen-
erate a shift in the optimal feature set and cause low generalizability across
country borders.
Our approached assumed the creation of a domain invariant feature set through
the mixing of features across domains would increase cross country generaliza-
tion.
However, we ﬁnd that a domain invariant feature set is generally not
suitable for classiﬁcation. By careful optimization of the domain tuning pa-
rameter, it is possible in some cases to improve the cross domain classiﬁcation
accuracy using unsupervised domain adaptation.
An optimized value of the
domain tuning parameter can cause the domain invariant feature set to intro-
duce higher variation in the posterior predictive distributions between networks.
Therefore, we suspect the performance might be improved by ensemble methods
combining multiple networks in a committee.
==PAGE:97==
89
In semi-supervised domain adaptation we ﬁnd that mixing of features can be
used to improve performance, when there is low number of labeled samples in the
target domain. An alternative approach would be to create new labeled samples
from the target domain using conditional generative adversarial networks, and
use these new samples for training. The recently proposed BAGAN algorithm
[MSI+18] have shown ability to produce synthetic images similar to the GTSRB
dataset, which are suitable for training a classiﬁer. Domain adaptive networks
using generative adversarial networks to transform images between domains
have improved performance when adapting classiﬁers from synthetic images to
the GTSRB dataset. As such these GAN based domain adaptive network might
also be used to transform real life traﬃc signs between domains.
