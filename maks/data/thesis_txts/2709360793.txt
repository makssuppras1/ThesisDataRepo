ABSTRACT  
Human papillomavirus (HPV) is the main causative agent of cervical cancer, a disease that approximately 
500,000 people are diagnosed with and 300,000 lose their life to every year. This is despite the fact that 
prophylactic vaccines have been available for more than 15 years. These vaccines are based on virus-like 
particles (VLPs); assembled particles of the structural protein L1 from HPV. While the present HPV vaccines 
are effective in protecting against infection and preventing cervical cancer, this positive effect is not 
distributed equally on a global scale. The high prices of the HPV vaccines make them unavailable to low- and 
middle-income countries, and that leaves a clear mark on the statistics. Most cases of cervical cancer and 
consequently, deaths, occur in these nations. To change this and make an already successful solution 
accessible to everyone, the price of the HPV vaccines needs to be lowered. The high price for the vaccine is 
often pointed out to primarily be caused by the expensive production organisms, and therefore, plants have 
been proposed as alternative production hosts.  
In this project, the moss species Physcomitrium patens was investigated for the production of HPV-16 L1 
virus-like particles (VLPs). This was a proof-of-concept project, and multiple L1 constructs were designed for 
localization to different cellular compartments. Two variations of the L1 protein were successfully 
synthesized by P. patens in this work, one full length L1 that theoretically enters the nucleus, and one 
truncated L1∆22 that in theory remains in the cytoplasm. In total, seven P. patens transformants were 
obtained, three L1 producers and four L1∆22 producers. Moreover, 7 and 12 P. patens transformants were 
confirmed to harbour the genes encoding L1 and L1∆22, respectively, and even more transformants are soon 
to be screened for presence of the genes. Therefore, more L1- and L1∆22 producers are expected to be 
confirmed. While L1 and L1∆22 ends up in the nucleus and the cytoplasm, another L1 construct localizing to 
the chloroplasts will be generated in the future. For this, L1 will be fused with a chloroplast transit peptide 
(CTP). In this project, five putative CTPs native to P. patens were tested for in vivo protein transfer to the 
chloroplasts. Four of these were verified as being functional CTPs and can be used in P. patens for future 
projects.  
This project proved that successful synthesis of L1 protein is possible in the moss P. patens, and these results 
can be used for further research of optimized protein yield and VLP assembly in this species, perhaps with 
the outcome of a vaccine more accessible to everyone.

BACKGROUND 
 
4.1 
HPV is the main causal agent of cervical cancer worldwide 
Cervical cancer is a widespread tragedy, often caused by the sexually transmitted human papillomavirus 
(HPV)1. In 2018, there were 530,000 cases of cervical cancer and 311,000 deaths worldwide1. Besides the 
cervix, cancer caused by HPV can develop in several organs, such as the vulva, vagina, penis, anus, and even 
cause head and neck cancer2,3. HPV initially cause both cutaneous and mucosal infections, and most infected 
people naturally recover from infection within one or two years without ever showing symptoms2,3. The ones 
that don’t recover develop a persistent infection with the virus, which over a course of many years can evolve 
to become tumours4. This happens when viral proteins that promote cell growth and suppress 
downregulation of proliferation are dysregulated2. The carcinogenesis is not a benefit to the virus itself, but 
it can be an unfortunate consequence, as 96% of all cases of cervical cancer are estimated to be caused by 
HPV2,3. 
HPV is a non-enveloped double stranded DNA virus1. It consist of six early regulatory proteins and two late 
structural proteins, L1 and L2, encoded in a 8000 bp genome1. The virus infects the epithelial cells in the skin 
as well as the oral- and anogenital mucosa by entering through an abrasion (Figure 1)3. Here, the basal 
epithelial cells are infected by HPV; a non-lytic infection that never becomes systemic2. Instead, the virus life 
cycle follows, and is dependent on, the differentiation of the keratinocyte cells in the epithelial layer2. After 
infection of the basal epithelial cells, the early genes are replicated at a low copy number of 50-100 copies 
per cell2. Only when the cells have moved to the upper layers of the epithelium and are terminally 
differentiated, the replication of viral genes accelerates to more than 1000 copies per cell, the late genes of 
L1 and L2 are expressed, and the virion assembles2. When squames of epithelial cells are shed from the 
surface layer, the virus particles are released and spread to infect new cells2. This dependence on cell 
differentiation makes culturing of the virus in laboratory settings very difficult5. The change from virus 
infection to cancer in the mucosal tissue is regulated by the viral oncogenes E5, E6 and E72. These genes 
encode proteins responsible for forcing the cells into the S phase to access the host replication machinery, 
but this dysregulation of cell proliferation can in some instances lead to the creation of immortal tumour 
cells2. Only some types of HPV are associated with the risk of developing cancer, caused by abnormal 
expression of E6 and E72.  These genes are consistently expressed in malignant tissue6.

The categorisation of HPV into types is based on the genotype of their L1 proteins2. A >10% divergence in 
DNA sequence constitutes a new type, which is numbered after order of discovery2. More than 200 HPV types 
have been identified, whereof only some infect genital mucosa and few are thought to be carcinogenic2. In 
the case of cervical cancer 96-99% of all cases contain HPV, and the two types HPV-16 and HPV-18 have been 
attributed to 50-60% and 20% of all these cases, respectively2,3. Besides HPV-16 and -18, there are 10 more 
types categorised as high-risk carcinogenic, while even more types are assigned the risk status “probably 
carcinogenic” or “possibly carcinogenic”3.  
Therefore, all current prophylactic vaccines against HPV target both HPV-16 and -18. The Cervarix® vaccine 
only protects against these two types, while Gardasil® protects against two additional types, HPV-11 and -6 
causing genital warts. All four are also included on Gardasil 9®, in addition to the carcinogenic HPV-31, -33, -
45, -52 and -581. The new Cecolin® also only protects against HPV-16 and -18. The discovery that human 
papillomaviruses cause cervical cancer was awarded with a Nobel price to German virologist Harald zur 
Hausen in 20087. Hausen conducted six years of research at Freiburg University, where HPV-16 was 
discovered for the first time from tumour biopsies and connected to the development of cervical cancer8. 
This work led to the development of vaccines against HPV to protect against the related cancer7.  Because 
the proliferation of HPV requires differentiation of epithelial cells, vaccine development in vitro was not 
possible before the development of methods for recombinant protein synthesis, making production of virus-
like particles possible5.

4.2 
Virus-like particles as a vaccine against HPV: benefits and current limitations 
The current vaccines against HPV are all based on the technology of virus-like particles (VLPs). VLPs are built 
from viral structural proteins, but they lack the genomic material that native viruses contain inside the 
particle and are incapable of replication9. Thereby, VLPs mimic the shell of viruses, but cannot perform the 
infectious lifecycle of the original virus10. However, the VLPs still retain the immunogenicity of the virus 
particle, since the antigenic structure and the dense repetitiveness of the epitopes are conserved10. It is 
crucial for effective and safe vaccines that they cannot cause disease under any circumstances, while still 
eliciting a strong immune response when injected10. For this reason, the use of live virus in a vaccine is often 
avoided. There is the risk when using a live attenuated virus vaccine, engineered to cause no or very mild 
symptoms, that the inactivating mutations will change and the virus revert back to being infections, as was 
seen with the oral polio vaccine11. Therefore, subunit vaccines only containing free proteins from the 
pathogen are safer, and VLPs share this safety. Moreover, VLPs have improved immunogenicity by presenting 
the antigen in a multivalent conformation, thereby enhancing the recognition by the immune system12. 
Compared to nucleic acid vaccines, VLPs only needs to be refrigerated, not frozen, which eases the 
distribution13,14. Several vaccines on the market are based on VLPs. Besides the HPV vaccines, these include 
hepatitis B vaccines, hepatitis E vaccines, and influenza virus A vaccines9. 
The VLPs against HPV can form solely based on the L1 major structural protein, and this is used in the HPV 
vaccines currently on the market1. L1 is a 56 kDa protein that self-assembles into capsomeres consisting of 
five L1 monomers, 72 of which in turn assemble into the full immunogenic VLPs (Figure 2)10. The VLPs of 
these viruses are approximately 55 nm in diameter, and while the native virus also contains the minor capsid 
protein L2 in the surface structure, this protein is not necessary for the particle to assemble and serve as a 
vaccine10.  
 
Figure 2: Simplified structure of the HPV L1 VLP (top) and the native HPV virus (bottom). As opposed to the VLP, the 
native virus also contains the structural protein L2 in the capsid, as well as genomic material in the cavity of the 
particle. Picture from Schiller et al. (2015)13. 
 
The HPV vaccines are very effective, and countries that have implemented the vaccine have reduced cases 
of HPV infections, genital warts, and cervical cancer in their populations. A recent study from the United 
Kingdom evaluated the HPV immunisation program with the final statement that the programme has “almost 
eliminated cervical cancer in women born since 1st of September 1995”15. Despite the undoubted success of

the vaccine, there are some challenges hindering worldwide reduction in the cases of HPV and cervical 
cancer. The biggest hindrance is the access to the vaccines for low- and middle income countries (LMICs), 
whereof only 8% have introduced the vaccine nationally2,3. More than 86% of all cases of cervical cancer and 
88% of the related deaths occurs in these countries2. With the high number of annual cases and deaths 
attributed to HPV induced cervical cancer worldwide, and most of these cases occurring in the LMICs, there 
is a strong need for solving the issue of limited use of the vaccines in these countries. The main reason for 
the lack of distribution is reported to be the high price of the vaccine2,13.  The high cost is often attributed to 
the complex production systems of yeast and insect cells16. Therefore, plants have been investigated as a 
cheaper, more sustainable alternative for production of HPV VLPs among other alternatives such as the VLPs 
from Cecolin® produced in E. coli10,16. 
 
4.3 
The benefit of plants as production organisms  
The most common production organisms for VLPs in general are bacteria, yeasts, and insect cells17. Bacteria 
are efficient at producing non-enveloped VLPs, but they come with a few drawbacks17. They are not able to 
make mammalian-like glycosylation on the proteins, a post-translational modification that can be important 
for appropriate immune recognition17,9. Different glycosylation patterns may also affect the half-life and 
stability of proteins when used in vivo18. Moreover, the VLPs produced by bacteria can lack the correct folding 
from proper disulphide bonds, protein solubility may be lowered, and there is a risk of endotoxins 
contaminating the product17. The problem of endotoxins is avoided by using eukaryotic expression systems, 
such as plants, yeast or mammalian cells17. In these organisms, more suitable post-translational 
modifications, especially glycosylation and phosphorylation, are also generated, although in yeast they are 
still fairly dissimilar to human glycosylation17. The downsides of eukaryotic hosts are that these expression 
systems are more complicated, more costly, and the yield lower than for bacterial hosts17. Despite this, 
Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells are being increasingly used for production of pharmaceutical proteins as 
they can achieve protein yields up to 10 g/L, can be grown in suspension, and are relatively resilient18. Other 
vaccine production systems include chicken eggs and insect cells, which enable production of vaccines within 
6-8 weeks, but require costly media18.  
Plants as production hosts come with many of the advantages as mammalian cell systems including similar 
protein folding, assembly and glycosylation, but with the additional benefit of being cheaper, at least when 
considering biomass production18. They therefore hold great promise as effective biopharmaceutical 
factories. The costs of downstream processing is often identical to mammalian systems, while site and batch 
costs are lower for plants18. For biologics in general, it has been estimated that the cost of production using 
plants are only 68% of the cost when using mammalian cells18. Moreover, glycosylation and other post-
translational modifications can be engineered in plants to represent the human analogues and thereby 
improve downstream processing and the half-life of proteins18. Another benefit of plant-produced 
pharmaceuticals is that plants do not come with the risk of endotoxins as Gram-negative bacteria do, and 
neither the risk of mammalian pathogens that would make an insufficiently purified product unsafe10. A rapid 
production of new molecules can be obtained by transient expression in plants, making these a useful host 
for producing pharmaceuticals where a quick response is needed when demand increases10. This can be done 
with low costs10. For this purpose, Nicotiana benthamiana has been broadly used as a plant production host 
for recombinant proteins because of its well-studied agroinfiltration and the many virus vectors that can 
infect the plant for expression of recombinant proteins10. A downside to N. benthamiana is the many

Despite the successful plant-based production of many compounds, few plant-produced pharmaceuticals 
have reached the market20. Elelyso, produced in carrot cell cultures by Protalix, was approved for treatment

company, a COVID-19 vaccine approved for use in Canada23. The number of commercial plant-produced 
pharmaceuticals are thereby increasing, and vaccines are no exception.  
HPV VLPs have successfully been produced in plants such as Nicotiana spp., potato plants, Arabidopsis 
thaliana, and tomato plants16,24,25,26. While there are many perks of protein production in these, we will apply 
the approach of HPV-16 L1 VLP synthesis to a more minimalistic and scalable plant: the moss Physcomitrium 
patens. 
 
4.4 
Successful production of proteins can be obtained in Physcomitrium patens  
Known under the common name “spreading earthmoss”, Physcomitrium patens is a bryophyte belonging to 
the Funariaceae family27. Since its discovery, the plant has changed name several times by being placed in 
different genera27. For a long time, Physcomitrella, was the genera to which the moss was thought to belong, 
but a thorough phylogenomic analysis determined that the plant should be classified as Physcomitrium 
patens in 201927,28. The name Physcomitrella patens, informally just Physcomitrella, is still widespread, as the 
moss has been referred to by this name in research for decades28.  
P. patens has been used for production of a large array of products, such as the therapeutic proteins vascular 
endothelial growth factor (VEGF), monoclonal antibodies, erythropoietin (EPO), and complement factor H29. 
Valuable metabolites include the antimalarial drug artemisinin, taxadiene, fragrant compounds, and 
cosmetic active ingredients20. A chimeric multi-epitope protein from the human immunodeficiency virus 
(HIV) has also been produced in P. patens for the exploration of vaccine production in this plant30. One unique 
feature of P. patens is the exceptionally high frequency of homologous recombination occurring in this moss 
compared to vascular plants and other higher eukaryotic cells, and combined with the dominant haploid 
state of moss cells, this makes P. patens an attractive target for bioengineering20.  
The use of P. patens as a cell factory has further advantages besides the genomic properties that makes it 
easy to engineer. It can be easily cultivated using different approaches, such as Petri dishes, Erlenmeyer 
flasks, or bioreactors, all using a simple medium without the need for carbon sources, antibiotics or growth 
factors31. By controlling the temperature and light conditions, the cells can be maintained in an asexual state 
with no production of spores31. The tissue in the suspension cultures is protonema, consisting of filamentous 
haploid cells29. The organism can be kept in the protonema state by regular mechanical disruption of the 
cultures, thereby maintaining a culture of small individual cells and optimizing nutrient uptake29. Undisrupted 
growth of the plant cells will lead them to develop into gametophores, the adult plant with leaf-like, shoot-
like and root-like structures29. Notable for moss-based production of desirable compounds are the 
photobioreactors, making the production scalable and tightly controlled20. The homogenous cell suspensions

4.5 
Expression of HPV-16 L1 in previous studies  
While there are no reports on HPV-16 L1 previously being expressed in P. patens, successful expression of 
these VLPs in other organism have been obtained and improved.   
In an early study by Jochmus et al. (1999), it was found that a 34 amino acid deletion in the C-terminal of the 
HPV-16 L1 protein resulted in a more effective assembly of the particles compared to wild type L1 in insect 
cells34.  
In 2003, the HPV-16 L1 VLPs were expressed in plants by Biemelt et al. (2003) and Varsani et al. (2003)35,36. 
The productions in tobacco plants, Nicotiana tabacum, and potato plants, Solanum tuberosum, by Biemelt et 
al. (2003) yielded amounts of L1 protein of 0.2-0.5% of the total soluble protein, approximately 12 µg/g fresh 
weight35,37. The highest concentrations of the protein was obtained in their study when a human codon-
optimized version of the gene was used, as opposed to the original gene sequence or when optimized for 
potato plants35. Varsani et al. (2003) used a native sequence of the gene, but investigated the differences 
between VLP formation of full length L1 protein and a C-terminal truncated version36. This protein was less 
truncated than the one generated in the Jochmus et al. (1999) studies, as only 22 amino acids were omitted36. 
These amino acids were considered the nuclear localization signal (NLS) of the L1 protein36. NLSs are short 
signal sequences in proteins localized to the nucleus38. The signal is recognized by nuclear transporters that 
bind the protein and facilitate its transfer through the nuclear pore complex38. When inside the nucleus, the 
protein retains its NLS38. By deleting the NLS in L1, the protein will theoretically remain in the cytoplasm after 
synthesis, whereas the full L1 protein translocates to the nucleus. In the study, low yields were obtained of 
only 2-4 ng/g fresh weight for both the full length and truncated protein36. In contrast to the study by Jochmus 
et al. (1999), the truncated proteins did not seem to effectively form full size particles in these experiments, 
but only capsomeres, which nevertheless are equally immunogenic36.  
The yield of transgenic L1 protein expressed in plants was increased markedly by Maclean et al. (2007) 37. In 
this study, several expression parameters were tested for optimization in Nicotiana spp.37. It was found that 
the highest concentration of L1 protein was produced when the gene encoding L1 was codon-optimized for 
humans, similar to the findings of Biemelt et al. (2003)37. The human codon-optimization was compared to 
the native L1 protein from the HPV-16 virus and to a plant codon-optimized version37. Surprisingly, the plant-
optimization resulted in the lowest concentration of L1 of all three codon variations37. It was argued by the 
authors that this may be a result of inhibitory sequences in the 5’ end of the L1 gene that hamper 
transcription37. Previously, it has been shown that mutating the first 514 nucleotides of the gene could 
==PAGE:13==
Maria Caroline Weilguny 
Master Thesis (30 ECTS) 
Technical University of Denmark 
s163708 
15.03.2022 
University of Freiburg 
12 of 80 
 
increase the L1 concentration, and this could explain the higher titre of L1 generated by the human codon-
optimized gene37. The human codon-optimization may disrupt the inhibitory sequences, allowing an 
increased transcription of the L1 gene, while the plant codon-optimized version may increase the inhibition37. 
The differences in L1 concentration could not be explained by variations in the G+C content or the use of 
rarer codons37. 
In the study, the yield of L1 based on cellular localization was also tested37. L1 proteins were expressed as 
the full native amino acid sequence or expressed with N-terminal transit peptides targeting them to the 
endoplasmic reticulum (ER) or the chloroplasts37. Here, the highest L1 concentration was obtained when the 
protein localized to the chloroplasts37.  
Overall, the synthesis of L1 protein when expressed from the human codon-optimized gene and targeted to 
the chloroplast reached the highest protein levels37. The authors reached yields of up to 887 µg/g fresh 
weight. Transmission electron microscopy of the protein extract revealed that the L1 proteins assembled into 
full VLPs, which were then used to successfully elicit immune responses in mice37. Thereby, this study 
demonstrated that high yields of HPV-16 VLPs can effectively be obtained in plants.  
The localization of L1 to the chloroplasts was obtained by N-terminally fusing the protein to a chloroplast 
transit peptide (CTP) 37. CTPs are heterogeneous amino acid sequences found in the N-terminal of precursors 
of nuclear-encoded plastid-targeted proteins39. The nuclear-encoded proteins are translated in the cytosol, 
whereafter the CTP sequence binds to the receptors of translocons in the chloroplast membrane39. This 
facilitates internalization of the protein into the chloroplast, a process completed by the removal of the CTP 
by a peptidase39. It has been found that deletion of these CTPs from their native proteins will retain the 
protein in the cytoplasm, while fusing a CTP to a nonplastid protein will result in the relocalization of the 
protein into the chloroplast39. This mechanism was utilized in the chloroplast targeting of the L1 protein.  
The benefit of chloroplast targeting was also shown in another, independent study expressing L1 in Nicotiana 
benthamiana40. Here, a plant-optimized gene was used, but increased yield obtained from localization to the 
chloroplast was again observed40. Both here, and in the study by Maclean et al. (2007), the best performing 
constructs contained both the CTP sequence as well as the NLS, compared to removal of the NLS on the 
chloroplast targeted protein37,40. 
Since Maclean et al. (2007), even higher yields of the L1 protein have been obtained in plants. Based on the 
findings of increased concentration of the protein when targeted to the chloroplasts, Millán et al. (2008) 
expressed the native protein in chloroplast DNA in Nicotiana tabacum19. Here, 3 mg/g fresh weight was 
obtained19.  
In the present project, the experiences from these former studies on HPV-16 L1 expression in plants were 
utilized for nuclear transformation of P. patens with the gene, leading to protein production.

5.2 
METHODS FOR CLONING 
 
5.2.1 Constructing pL1: Optimization of the L1 gene 
A human-codon optimized version of the L1 gene, hL1 (HL1; GenBank accession no. DQ067889) generated 
by Maclean et al. (2007)37, was further optimized for P. patens. For this, physCO44, an online P. patens codon 
optimization tool, was used. Hereafter, alternative splice sites of the motif AGGT44 as removed manually by 
changing the codons that participated in these motifs to synonymous codons. Using the online tool 
psRNATarget45 with the library of published miRNAs from “Physcomitrella patens (280 published miRNA)” 
and the default settings in Schema V2 (2017 release), one miRNA binding site was predicted and its target 
sequence in the gene was altered manually with synonymous codons. Analysis by the tool again predicted 
no miRNA binding when using the default settings.  
 
5.2.2 Construction of pL1 and pL1Δ22 vectors (plasmids 1997 & 1998) 
For the transformation of P. patens, a backbone containing ampicillin resistance as well as hygromycin 
resistance (hptII) was used. The plasmid 1546 to be used as the backbone contained another gene, MFHR1 
along with a signal peptide and a HIS tag, flanked by a PpActin5 promoter and a CaMV35S terminator. This 
gene was cut out using the restriction enyzmes FastDigest XhoI and FastDigest BamHI (Thermo Scientific) for 
replacement with the pL1- or pL1Δ22 coding sequence between the promoter and terminator.

The pL1 gene was ordered from Genewiz (Leipzig, Germany) in their pUC-GW-Amp plasmid containing 
ampicillin resistance. From this plasmid, pL1 and the truncated pL1Δ22 was amplified by Phusion™ High-
Fidelity DNA Polymerase (Thermo Scientific) using primers with overhangs containing XhoI and BamHI

The digested pL1- and pL1Δ22 genes were ligated into the digested 1546 backbone using T4 DNA ligase 
(Thermo Scientific) and a 2:1 ratio of insert:vector. They were then used for transformation of E. coli.

in Table 5-14) confirmed the intact expression cassette of pL1/pL1Δ22 genes with promoter and terminator, 
as well as the hptII gene flanked by promoter and terminator. The new plasmid consisting of the 1546 
backbone with pL1 replacing the MFHR1 gene was assigned the number 1997, while the plasmid with pL1Δ22 
was assigned 1998 in the AG Reski plasmid stock and for the project further on.  
Plasmids maps can be found in Appendix III and Appendix IV. 
 
5.2.3 Linearization of pL1 and pL1Δ22 constructs for transformation into P. patens 
Expression constructs containing pL1 and pL1Δ22 as well as the hygromycin resistance gene were linearized 
by double digestion of p1997 and p1998 with restriction enzymes HindIII and EcoRI (Thermo Scientific) for 
24-48 hours and assessed by gel electrophoresis for complete digestion. The digestions were precipitated 
and sterilized (section 5.2.4) before being used for transformation of P. patens. 
 
5.2.4 Sterilization and precipitation of DNA for P. patens transformation 
The purified DNA to be used for transformation of P. patens was added 1/10 (v/v) 3M sodium acetate (pH 
5.2) and 2.5 times (v/v) of >95% ethanol of -20°C. It was vigorously mixed and incubated at -20°C overnight. 
The sample was then centrifuged at 14,000 rpm for 30 min at 4°C and the supernatant was removed. 
Thereafter, the sample was vigorously mixed with 1 ml 70% ethanol of -20°C and it was centrifuged at 14,000 
rpm for 10 min at 4°C. Under sterile conditions, the supernatant was removed, and the pellet left to air dry 
for 30 minutes in the sterile work bench. 100 µl of sterile water was then added, and the samples were 
incubated at 37°C for 30 min while intermittently mixing. The samples were then mixed and stored at -20°C 
before being used for transformation.

5.2.6 Construction of CTP-eGFP vectors (plasmids 2038-2042) 
Plasmid 1840 from the AG Reski plasmid stock contains a linker and an eGFP gene flanked by an Actin5 
promoter and a NOS terminator. Via Gibson cloning, the CTP candidates were inserted in front of the linker. 
As the linker began with a methionine, this was removed by using primers excluding this amino acid when 
amplifying the vector. Because of the large size of the plasmid, it was amplified in two fragments containing 
a 47 bp overlap for fusion via Gibson cloning. Primers are listed in Table 5-10 and Table 5-11 section 5.1.3. 
The CTP candidates were amplified from P. patens cDNA with primers containing overhangs matching each 
end of the linearized p1840 plasmid. The overhangs were chosen to have a melting temperature of >55°C. 
All amplified fragments were extracted from gel and used for Gibson cloning in an insert:vector ratio of 4:1. 
Thereby, each Gibson reaction contained three fragments to be assembled: two parts of the backbone and 
one CTP. The reaction mixtures were used for transformation of competent E. coli cells, which were assessed 
with colony PCR. For positive colonies, the bacteria were cultured, and the plasmids purified and sent in for 
sequencing to confirm a complete gene cassette of promoter, CTP, linker, eGFP and terminator.  
Plasmid maps can be found in Appendix V - Appendix X.

extraction. A 2 mm leaf was frozen for 30 min in dilution buffer and then crushed using glass beads and a 
tissue-lyzer at 15 Hz for 1 min. The extractions were then centrifuged and the supernatant used as template 
in PCR reactions.

5.3.1 Cultivation of E. coli 
E. coli bacteria were grown on solid LBAmp(50) agar or in liquid LBAmp(50) at 37°C. To multiply and isolate plasmids, 
transformed colonies were lightly touched with pipette tips, and the tips ejected into the medium. For 
smaller amounts of plasmid, transformed E. coli was inoculated in 4 ml LBAmp(50) and incubated at 37°C at 140 
rpm shaking for 15-16 hours. Were larger amounts of plasmid needed, the bacteria would be inoculated in 
250 ml LBAmp(50) and incubated under the same conditions for 20 hours.  
 
5.3.2 Cultivation of P. patens 
Physcomitrium patens was cultivated in 180 ml liquid Knop ME pH 5.8 medium. It was kept at 22°C, while 
shaking at 125 rpm, and under a light cycle of 16h day/8h night at a light intensity of 55 µmol/m2. To sustain 
the protonema state of the plants, the culture was dispersed under sterile conditions by blending it with an 
Ultra-Turrax (IKA, Staufen) at 19,000 rpm for 1 min once every week. Sterile controls were taken from the 
Ultra-Turrax on LB-, TSA- and Knop ME solid medium to assess if the cultures were contaminated. The 
protonema was placed on a 100 µm sieve to separate it from the medium, and it was transferred to fresh 
Knop ME medium.  
 
5.3.3 Transformation of P. patens  
Leading up to the transformation of P. patens, the moss culture had been grown in Knop ME pH 4.5 for at 
least one week and was blended 5-7 days prior to the transformation. One day before transformation, the 
culture was transferred to fresh Knop ME pH 4.5 without blending.  
To prepare the protoplasts for transformation, the moss culture was filtered through a 100 µm protoplast 
sieve. An appropriate amount of material was then placed on a sterile petri dish, where 10 ml 2% Driselase 
solution and 10 ml 0.5 M mannitol solution were added. The sealed petri dish was then incubated in the dark 
under aluminium foil for 1.5 hours on a rotary shaker at 30 rpm. The cell solution was handled very carefully 
and only pipetted with glass pipettes with a wide opening or cut pipette tips to avoid shearing of the cell 
membranes. The moss suspension was passed through another 100 µm protoplast sieve. The petri dish was 
rinsed with 3 ml 0.5 M mannitol solution that was also pipetted onto the sieve. The filtrate was then passed 
through a 50 µm protoplast sieve, and the previous sieve box also rinsed with 3 ml 0.5 M mannitol solution. 
The filtrated protoplasts were filled into glass tubes and centrifuged at 500 rpm for 10 min (brakes and 
acceleration set to 3). The supernatant was removed and all pelleted protoplasts resuspended in 10 ml 0.5

volume of 10 ml 0.5 M mannitol solution distributed between all tubes. The gently resuspended protoplast 
solutions were then combined into one tube. A Fuchs-Rosenthal counting chamber was used to count the 
protoplasts from this solution and the volume of 3M medium to add for a concentration of 1.2 x 106

5.3.4 Growth and selection of P. patens transformants

5.4.3 Western Blot analysis 
A gel from SDS-PAGE analysis was used for protein transfer to a membrane (Amersham PVDF blotting 
membrane, Cytvia). The gel was incubated in cathode buffer for 15 minutes. The membrane was soaked in 
100% methanol for 15 seconds and then in ultrapure water (>18 MΩ) for 2 minutes. Afterwards, it was placed 
in anode II buffer for at least 5 minutes. Two filter papers (Whatman®) were soaked in anode I buffer, one in 
anode II buffer, and three in cathode buffer. On an anode electrode plate, a stack containing the following 
order from the bottom was assembled: 2x filter papers from anode I buffer, 1x filter paper from anode II 
buffer, the membrane from anode II buffer, the gel from the cathode buffer, and 3x filter papers from the 
cathode buffer. Air bubbles were removed. A cathode electrode plate was put on top, and the plates turned 
on at 1.5 mA/cm2 (81 mA for a standard gel) for one hour with a maximum limit of 25 V. Hereafter, the gel 
was stained with Coomassie Blue to visualize any proteins left on the gel after transfer to the membrane.  
The membrane was incubated in 4% blocking solution on a rotary shaker for one hour at RT or at 4°C 
overnight. It was then washed in TBST three times, each time incubating for 5 minutes on a rotary shaker. 
After being washed, the membrane was incubated with the primary antibody (Anti-HPV16 L1 antibody 
[CamVir 1] (ab69), Abcam) in a 1:10,000 dilution in 2% blocking solution on a rotary shaker at RT for one hour 
or at 4°C overnight. The membrane was then washed three times as done previously and then incubated in 
the secondary antibody (Amersham ECL Mouse IgG, HRP-linked whole Ab) in a dilution of 1:25,000 in 2% 
blocking solution for one hour on a rotary shaker. It was washed three times again before a 5-minute 
incubation with a 1:1 mixture of the reagents (luminol and peroxide) in a detection reagent kit (Amersham 
ECL Prime Western Blotting Detection Reagent, GE Healthcare). The membrane was from then on kept out 
of the light in a film cassette and developed on film (Amersham Hyperfilm ECL, GE Healthcare) at different 
exposure times.

5.4.4 Sample preparation for mass spectrometry analysis 
The preparation of samples for mass spectrometry was performed in a sterile hood, and ultrapure water (>18 
MΩ) as well as MS-grade pipette tips, tubes, and chemicals were used to prevent protein contaminations in 
the samples. The preparations are based on method 3 from Goodman et al. (2018)53. 
From an SDS-PAGE gel stained with Coomassie Blue (section 5.4.2) sections containing proteins of interest 
were cut out with a sterile scalpel by laying the gel on a sterile glass plate. The gel cut-outs were sliced into 
very small pieces and collected in tubes. They were prepared by adding 15 µl reducing agent to cover the gel 
pieces. The samples were then incubated at 1200 rpm shaking for 30 min at RT. The supernatant was 
removed, and 100 µl destaining solution added. The samples were then incubated at 1200 rpm shaking for 
10 min at RT and the supernatant removed. This was done two times to completely destain. 100 µl 100% ACN 
was added to dehydrate the gel pieces, and the samples were incubated at 1200 rpm shaking for 5 min at RT 
before removing the supernatant. To evaporate any remaining ACN, the samples were vacuum dried. The 
dried gel pieces in each sample were added 30 µl trypsin solution to soak in the enzyme. This solution was 
prepared by mixing 10 µl trypsin stock solution with 90 µl 100 mM ABC. The samples were incubated at 37°C 
overnight. Hereafter, the supernatant was transferred to a fresh reaction tube and 20 µl 5 % FA was added 
to the gel pieces. They were incubated for 10 min at RT and 12000 rpm. They were then centrifuged, and the 
supernatant pooled with the supernatant of the enzymatic digest. This was done 3 times in total. The solution 
was then desalted and purified using C18-tips (Thermo Fisher). They were equilibrated with 100 µl 0.1 % FA 
and washed with 100 µl of a solution consisting of 80 % ACN in 0.1 % FA. 200 µl 0.1 % FA was then used to 
remove residual CAN from the tips. The resulting peptide solution was added to a C18 membrane. It was 
washed 2 time in 100 µl 0.1 % FA. The elution from the tips were then performed with 100 µl of a solution of 
30 % ACN in 0.1 % FA. Finally, the samples were vacuum dried.  
 
5.4.5 Mass spectrometry analysis 
Tryptic peptides were separated on a UltiMateTM 3000 RSLCnano (Dionex LC Packings/Thermo Fisher 
Scientific, Dreieich, Germany) equipped with two C18 pre-columns (μPAC™ trapping column, PharmaFluidics)

performed in data dependent acquisition mode (DDA) on a Orbitrap Q Exactive plus (Thermo Fisher Scientific,

range was 375-1,700 m/z at a resolution of 70,000 Thomson (at m/z=400) on the MS1 level and 35,000

RESULTS 
To obtain the goal of the project, P. patens previously transformed with a version of the HPV-16 L1 gene, 
hL1∆34, was analysed. Moreover, new lines with an HPV-16 L1 encoding gene, pL1, were designed, used for 
transfection of P. patens, and analysed for synthesis of the protein. Finally, putative chloroplast transit 
peptides were tested in P. patens for functional localization to the plastids with the purpose of fusing these 
to pL1 constructs in the future.  
 
6.1 
hL1Δ34 TRANSFORMANTS 
In previous work performed by Alexander Niederau, P. patens was transformed with an expression cassette 
for hL1∆34, encoding a truncated version of the L1 protein (see Figure 3). This gene was based on the human 
codon-optimized gene, hL1, formerly shown to induce high yields of the L1 protein in Nicotiana 
benthamiana37. The success of this version of the gene in the Nicotiana plants was therefore tested in 
Physcomitrella. Here, the hL1∆34 gene was used for transformation, encoding a truncated L1 protein with 
the nuclear localization signal (NLS) removed by deleting 34 amino acids in the C-terminal, as done by 
Jochmus et al. (1999)34. The removal of 34 amino acids was also recommended in personal communications 
with Prof. Ed Rybicki experienced in plant expression of HPV-16 VLPs.

Phire Hot Start II DNA polymerase has a much lower proofreading ability than a high-fidelity DNA

to be sequenced, a high accuracy is needed. While Phire Hot Start II DNA polymerase has a fidelity 2 times 
the fidelity of the Taq polymerase, Phusion High-Fidelity DNA polymerase is 52 times more accurate57. 
Another amplification of the entire gene cassette (Figure 6) was therefore performed using the Phusion High-
Fidelity DNA polymerase. A higher reaction volume was also used to gain more DNA for cloning into the pJet 
vector. A higher insert:vector ratio could improve the success of the cloning reaction. The amplicons were 
again purified and cloned into pJet vectors and transformed into E. coli. The same primers amplifying part of 
the hL1Δ34 gene was used, but again, with no results. Figure 7 also represents these experiments.

6.2 
pL1 TRANSFORMANTS 
The new lines of L1 producing P. patens to be generated during this project were transformed with a version 
of the L1 gene named pL1. This gene was optimized for expression in P. patens and contained a new promoter 
and terminator. Moreover, the in vivo assembly was not used, but instead, the gene cassette was assembled 
beforehand, and this linearized fragment was used for transfection of the moss cells. It was not targeted to 
a specific gene in P. patens via homologous recombination, but instead, an untargeted approach was applied 
to obtain random integration at multiples sites. These changes were implemented to avoid the hurdles of 
the hL1∆34 transformants. By doing this, it would minimize the risk of the protein not being synthesized in 
detectable amounts or being synthesized in alternatively spliced versions.  
 
6.2.1 pL1 gene constructs and experimental strategy 
Optimization of hL1 to generate the pL1 gene   
The pL1 gene was optimized based on a human codon-optimized L1 gene (hL1) from Maclean et al. (2007), 
where this codon version of the gene resulted in the highest concentrations of L1 in Nicotiana spp.37. The 
need for optimization became apparent when in silico analyses (below) showed that the hL1 gene contained 
alternative splice sites in P. patens as well as one miRNA-binding site. The hL1 gene was therefore optimized 
in the present thesis for expression in P. patens by performing codon-optimization for P. patens, by removing 
alternative splice sites specific for P. patens, and by removing one detected miRNA binding site. The sequence 
of the original hL1 gene can be found in Appendix I, while Appendix II contains the sequence of pL1.  
The P. patens codon-optimization resulted in the change of nine codons in the gene, and the GC content 
increased from 62.25% to 62.85%.  
The motif AGGT had been discovered to be a common alternative splice site in P. patens44, and five of these 
motifs were present in the positive-sense strand of the gene. The codons containing these bases were 
replaced manually with synonymous codons based on different nucleotides. Thereby, no AGGT motifs would 
remain in the transcript.  
Finally, one P. patens specific miRNA was predicted to bind and cause cleavage of the mRNA. The binding site 
for the miRNA was altered by manually changing the codons where the miRNA would bind the strongest. 
Three codons were changed, encoding glutamine, phenylalanine, and an arginine. The gene was analysed by 
the tool again to confirm that the miRNA was no longer predicted to bind.  
After the modifications above, the pL1 gene had been generated.

6.3 
TEST OF CHLOROPLAST TRANSIT PEPTIDES IN P. PATENS 
For future production of L1 VLPs in P. patens, it may be advantageous to localize the synthesized protein to 
the chloroplasts, as this resulted in higher yield of L1 in N. benthamiana37,40. Therefore, different putative 
chloroplast transit peptides (CTPs) native to P. patens were tested in this project for their ability to localize a 
target protein to the chloroplasts in vivo. The test was conducted by fusing CTP candidates to the fluorescent 
protein eGFP. The constructs were then expressed transiently in P. patens protoplasts. Thereby, the 
localization of the fluorescent signal in the cells would give a qualitative indication if the CTPs target a protein 
to the chloroplast.  
 
6.3.1 Selection of CTP candidates 
To determine which peptides to test for chloroplast localization, P. patens proteomics data from previous 
mass spectrometry datasets46,47,48,49 were analysed (Sebastian Hoernstein, unpublished). The in silico tool 
TargetP-2.060 was used to predict the cleavage site from the amino acid sequence, and the resulting N-
terminal amino acid remaining after predicted cleavage was compared to the N-terminal amino acid 
observed in the proteomics data. For the proteins where this amino acid was the same, the cleavage 
predictions made by TargetP-2.0 was thereby confirmed by the experimental data. For six proteins, the 
predictions agreed with the proteins observed in experimental data (Sebastian Hoernstein, unpublished). 
These six proteins were the initial candidates for the CTP test in this project. The sequences of the CTP 
candidates and the proteins they originate from can be found in Appendix XI.  
In the present project, the sequences of the putative CTPs were then in silico fused to the N-terminal of eGFP 
and L1 and these sequences were analysed with TargetP-2.0, see Table 6-1. The tool returns the predicted 
cleavage site in the protein and a score for which cell compartment the protein most likely localizes to. One 
predicted CTP ended with a methionine (CTPc22), which can introduce an alternative translation initiation 
site, resulting in the downstream RNA being translated without the CTP fused to it. Therefore, an alternative 
version of this CTP, named CTPc22noMet, without the terminal methionine, was included in the test of CTPs.  
The in silico analysis showed that for one CTP candidate, it was predicted that the cleavage site would change 
when fused to the eGFP and L1 proteins, and as a result, amino acids from the CTP would be left in the N-
terminal of the proteins (Table 6-1, protein Pp3c4_8470V3.1). This candidate was therefore ruled out, as the 
additional amino acid could potentially change the properties of L1. Another CTP candidate was predicted to 
be cleaved differently when fused to eGFP, but not L1 (Table 6-1, CTPc5). It would leave one amino acid in 
the N-terminal of eGFP. Therefore, this transit peptide could still be used for localization of L1. The score of 
the predictions for the CTP candidates were high for all except for one CTP (Table 6-1, protein 
Pp3c10_10490V3.1). Here, the scores were below 0.5 for the prediction of localization to the chloroplast, 
and therefore, this CTP was also ruled out. Five CTP candidates remained to be tested for their localization 
of eGFP to the chloroplasts in P. patens.

DISCUSSION  
 
7.1 
Lack of L1 detection in hL1∆34 transformants led to improvements in pL1 transformants 
Transformation of P. patens with hL1∆34, a human codon-optimized and truncated version of the L1 gene, 
did not yield detectable L1∆34 protein in a Western Blot analysis using anti-HPV16 L1 antibody (Figure 4). 
The analysis did not contain a positive control of purified L1 protein, and it is therefore not possible to 
conclude if the lack of detection was a result of non-functional antibody, or the absence of L1∆34 protein. In 
the Western Blot analysis, signals were detected from proteins of a size of ~45 kDa in all samples, including 
the WT extract. This could indicate that there is some unspecific binding of the antibody to native proteins in 
P. patens.  
Several possibilities could explain the lack of detection of the L1∆34 protein. The gene was shown to be 
present in the transformants (Figure 5), but it could not be shown that the gene cassette was intact (section 
6.1.2). The problem could therefore lie in the expression of the gene. It was not investigated in what 
constellation the gene was present in the cells, as attempts at amplifying the gene cassette resulted in 
fragments that were amplified from only the reverse primer binding to the terminator. This is suggested by 
the sequencing results, which showed the HSP terminator was present in both ends of the amplicon. 
Moreover, the hL1∆34 gene itself was not present in the amplicons, as is clear from the PCR of the E. coli pJet 
transformants, which showed no amplicon when using a hL1 specific primer pair (Figure 7).  Besides the 
sequencing results revealing a damaged expression cassette, it was also discovered that the hL1 gene 
contained alternative splice sites and a miRNA binding site in P. patens, which could further complicate 
synthesis of a complete L1 protein. These findings from the hL1∆34 transformants were therefore used to 
improve the pL1 constructs and change the transformation strategy.  
 
7.2 
Protein synthesis and possible degradation by pL1 and pL1∆22 transformants 
The pL1 and pL1∆22 genes were optimized for expression in P. patens by codon-optimization of the hL1 gene 
(Maclean et al. (2007)37) and removal of alternative splice sites and a miRNA binding site (section 6.2.1). Upon 
random integration into the P. patens genome for high copy numbers of the genes, both L1 and L1∆22 were 
detected in protein extract from the transformants (section 6.2.5). The presence of the proteins was further 
confirmed by mass spectrometry analysis (section 6.2.6).  
Besides L1 and L1∆22 proteins being detected in the Western Blot analysis, proteins of smaller and bigger 
sizes were also detected by the anti-HPV16 L1 antibody. For all transformants, bands indicating proteins of a 
size of ~45 kDa were visible (Figure 18). This raises the question if these bands are caused by binding to the 
same protein as the ~45 kDa bands in the L1∆34 Western Blot analysis (Figure 4). When a sample containing 
this band from a L1∆22 transformant was analysed with mass spectrometry analysis, it was revealed that 
peptides unique for the L1 protein were present. It therefore seems most likely that this is degraded or 
alternatively spliced L1∆22 protein, and for the L1 transformants, the proteins of the same sizes must be 
caused by the same type of degradation or alternative splicing of L1. Since the ~45 kDa bands in the analysis 
of the L1∆34 transformants were also present in the WT protein extract, they cannot be caused by binding 
to degraded L1∆34. It is possible that there is both unspecific binding of the antibody to a protein of this size,

as well as a L1 derivate of the same size, also detected by the antibodies. Proteins of the size of ~45 kDa were 
also detected by anti-HPV16 L1 antibody in the Western Blot analysis of the L1 positive control when mixed 
with P. patens protein extract (Figure 17). Since there was no negative control, it was not possible to 
determine if the detection was caused by unspecific binding of the antibody, or if the proteins were 
degradation products of L1 arising when the protein came into contact with proteases in the cell extract, or 
both.  
Another type of degradation product confirmed by mass spectrometry analysis was the proteins of ~23 kDa 
detected only in the L1∆22 transformants. Since these degraded proteins were not detected in the L1 
transformants, they are likely to be caused by proteases in the cytoplasm not present in the nucleus. 
Proteases are pH dependent, so different proteases often reside in different cellular compartments optimal 
for their activity61. Because of the proteases, accumulation of recombinant protein in the cytosol is often not 
efficient, and this compartment is generally considered unsuitable for production of recombinant proteins61. 
An alternative explanation of the small 23 kDa fragments could be that the removal of the NLS in the L1∆22 
proteins makes the altered C-terminal accessible for proteases, while the NLS protects the L1 protein from 
this degradation, regardless of cellular location. Mapping of the measured peptides in the sample to L1∆22 
shows that peptides near both the C- and N-terminal end are present (Appendix XIII). Instead, peptides 
detected in the other samples mapping to the centre of the protein are not measured in this sample. It is 
probable that an endopeptidase cleaves the protein somewhere in the centre, leaving two degradation 
products of roughly the same size to be present in this band on the gel.  
Moreover, it has been found that deletions can occur in episomal DNA, caused by recombination of short 
direct repeats in the DNA sequence62. This could be another cause of alternative versions of recombinant 
protein, especially for in-frame deletions, but for the proteins detected in Western Blot analysis (Figure 18), 
this is most likely not the case. For deletions inside the gene, size differences between the proteins would 
remain. In the analysis, smaller proteins that are detected in both L1- and L1∆22 transformants seem to be 
of the same size.  
Ultimately, the nature of the degradation can only be determined by further experiments. 
 
7.3 
Recombinant protein yield in pL1 and pL1∆22 transformants 
The successful production of L1 and L1∆22 by the P. patens transformants will in future projects be followed 
by yield measurements to determine if accumulation in the nucleus, the cytoplasm or the chloroplast result 
in the highest titres of recombinant protein. Yield measurements will also allow identification of the highest 
producing transformant for each of the pL1 constructs. In this project, no quantitative measures of the yields 
were obtained, but very rough estimations were calculated from the Western Blot analysis (Figure 18). 30 ng 
L1 positive control was loaded onto the gel, and by assessing the intensity of the bands for L1- and L1∆22 
protein, at least the same amount of recombinant protein is present in all transformants. The only exception 
may be the L1 producer 6B where the band is fainter. A conservative estimate based on the amount of fresh 
weight used in protein extraction, and assuming the same amount of protein as in the positive control, results 
in an estimated productivity of at least 13 µg protein/g FW for all samples. This is however, a very rough 
estimate. Most bands in the analysis were significantly more intense than the positive control, therefore 
most likely contains a lot more protein. The yield could further increase optimized production conditions in 
bioreactors or from screening of more transformants, possibly identifying a higher producing line.

Meanwhile, the yield is expected to decrease to some extent over time when episomally replicating 
constructs are lost in the absence of the hygromycin selection62. When P. patens is transformed with linear 
DNA constructs, some will integrate into the genome, while other constructs will remain as 
extrachromosomal DNA62. As with stably integrated DNA, the genes will be expressed and replicated, but 
when there is no selection pressure, the episomal DNA will be lost with time62. When the episomal 
recombinant constructs are lost, fewer copies of the gene will be present in the cells, and this will result in 
lower yields51. Removal of some of the episomal constructs was probably already obtained by growth of the 
transformants for two weeks on medium without selection pressure in between the two hygromycin 
selection steps. Still, to effectively lose all episomal expression of recombinant genes, transformants are 
often cultured for one year51. 
Large variations in yield when expressing the HPV-16 L1 protein in plants have been reported in other studies. 
In transiently transformed Nicotiana benthamiana 0.02–0.037 µg/g FW was reported, and the same authors 
had obtained 0.002-0.004 µg/g FW in transgenic Nicotiana tabacum63,36. At the other end of the spectrum, 
high yields of 533 µg/g FW were obtained in transiently transformed N. benthamiana when using a human 
codon-optimized version of L1 targeted to the chloroplast37. This version of the gene was the starting point 
for generating the pL1 genes in the present project. In the N. benthamiana studies, L1, comparable to the L1 
protein in this project, amounted to ~379 µg/g FW37. In transgenic N. tabaccum plants, the authors obtained 
even higher yields of up to 887 µg/g FW when the protein was targeted to the chloroplasts, but variation 
between different leaves in the same transformant was high37. The reported quantifications of L1 yields are 
all based on ELISA measurements, making them more precise than the rough estimate in this project.  
Besides gene optimization, the relatively efficient L1- and L1∆22 protein production in this project can likely 
be attributed to the adoption of the strategy used by Ruiz-Molina et al. (2021)51. By using the same expression 
cassette as the authors of this study, only replacing the MFHR1 gene with pL1 and pL1∆22, random 
integration and strong expression of the genes in P. patens were implemented. In the study on MFHR1 
expression, yields of up to 20-30 µg/g FW were initially obtained in shake flasks, while addition of auxin (NAA) 
increased the yield to ~60 µg/g FW when measured 7 days after auxin addition.  Auxin was already applied 
to enhance protein production in the L1- and L1∆22 transformants, but further experiments on the culturing 
conditions and the optimal effect of auxin on the expression is likely to increase protein yield further.  
The addition of auxin to the transformant cultures was based on the experiences in the study of MFHR1 
expression under different culturing conditions. The study indicated that auxin has an indirect upregulating 
effect on the PpActin5 promoter used for both MFHR1- and pL1- and pL1∆22 expression51. Therefore, it 
seems probable that the hormone would increase pL1- and pL1∆22 expression as it does MFHR1. To assess 
the effect of auxin on yields of L1- and L1∆22, quantitative measurements of the proteins would have to be 
conducted on transformants cultivated with and without auxin supplement.   
 
7.4 
Chloroplast localization of L1 in P. patens  
With the prospect of increased L1 yield, given that the results in Nicotiana species37 are also applicable to P. 
patens, the protein will be fused to a chloroplast transit peptide (CTP) in the future. In this project, four CTPs 
native to P. patens were verified for functional localization of eGFP to the chloroplasts (section 6.3.3). These 
were the four named CTPc5, CTPc10, CTPc22 and CTPc22noMet.

It became evident that the last methionine in CTPc22 did not introduce an alternative translation initiation 
site, as the localization of eGFP to the chloroplasts were successful for both CTPc22 and CTPc22noMet. Based 
on the assessment in the stereo microscope, there was no apparent difference in the efficiency of the two 
CTPs (Figure 22).  
CTPs from proteins in P. patens have already been identified for the two protein isoforms FtsZ1-1 and FtsZ1-
264,65. It was discovered that fusion of amino acid 1-93 of FtsZ1-1 or amino acid 1-107 of FtsZ1-2 could localize 
GFP to the chloroplasts64,65. When analysing the full protein sequence of the proteins with the prediction tool 
TargetP-2.0, the tool predicted localization to the chloroplasts with a high score for FtsZ1-1, but the cleavage 
sites differed from the 93 amino acid sequence used in the study64. The CTP was predicted to be shorter than 
93 amino acids, only 46 residues long and, moreover, when fusing either the reported CTP or the TargetP-
2.0 predicted CTP to L1, the predicted cleavage sites changed. For the usage of the reported CTP, since it was 
predicted to be shorter by TargetP-2.0, the predicted cleavage would leave 47 amino acids in the N-terminal 
of L1. On the other hand, if the predicted CTP sequence of 46 amino acids was used, the predicted cleavage 
sites would also change, cutting one amino acid inside L1. For FtsZ1-2 TargetP-2.0 did not successfully predict 
it to localize to the chloroplast and therefore did not predict a cleavage site. Ultimately, the CTPs from the 
FtsZ proteins were not used since no experimental data had confirmed the precise cleavage sites. For the six 
CTP candidates considered in this project (section 5.2.5) the predicted cleavage sites by TargetP-2.0 had been 
confirmed by datasets from mass spectrometry analyses. Therefore, fusion of these sequences to L1 would 
most likely confer the lowest risk of altering the L1 protein.  
The explanation of higher yields attained when the protein accumulated in the chloroplasts could be the 
protection from proteases, that the CTP sequence stabilizes the mRNA or the protein while in the cytoplasm, 
or that isolation of L1 protein in the chloroplasts protects the rest of the cell from the effects of 
hyperaccumulation37,66. The chloroplast environment also allows disulphide binding and folding of complex 
structures66. The highest yields of ~3 mg/g FW HPV-16 L1 protein produced in plants were achieved by 
transformation of the chloroplast DNA in tobacco plants19. This may be caused by a combination of the 
increased stability of the protein in the organelle, as well as the high copy number of the transgene attainable 
from this transformation target19. 
The increased yield of L1 protein when targeted the chloroplasts in Nicotiana species was observed in two 
independent studies when the NLS sequence was retained on the protein as opposed to when the NLS was 
removed37,40. Having two localization signals on a peptide intuitively seems counterproductive, and in the 
studies, the cellular localization of the proteins was not investigated directly to determine if the recombinant 
proteins end up in the nucleus or plastids. An indirect indication can be found in a Western Blot analysis from 
one of the studies, where full length L1 protein and CTP-fused full length L1 protein were detected to be of 
the same size37. Therefore, the CTP signal must have been cleaved off, indicating that the L1 protein has 
entered the chloroplasts for this construct. Generally, dual targeting of proteins encoded by one gene but 
localizing to two cell compartments, is not uncommon in plants67. Most of these proteins are mitochondrial 
and plastidial though, containing N-terminal transit peptides that are ambiguous and target both 
organelles67. Fewer proteins are dually targeted to the mitochondria or chloroplast and to the nucleus, 
thereby having both an N-terminal transit peptide as well as a C-terminal NLS67. For the ones that do, it is 
common that the N-terminal signal is dominant over the NLS67. Proteins with dual localizations to the 
mitochondria and nucleus are mostly only observed in the nucleus when the N-terminal signal is absent from 
alternative splicing, alternative translation initiation, or masked from recognition by an N-terminal amino 
acid extension67. Based on these observations in plants, it is not surprising that the CTP in the CTP-L1 studies 
is dominant over the NLS. Meanwhile, the possibility of the L1 protein relocalizing to the nucleus after

chloroplast import cannot be ruled out. In Arabidopsis thaliana the protein HEMERA/pTAC12 has been shown 
to be imported into the plastid via a CTP, whereafter the CTP is cleaved off and the protein matured, and it 
is then transferred to the nucleus68. In general, the destination of a dually targeted protein may be 
determined by the affinities to the different receptors69. Alternatively, folding of the protein may make one 
of the signal peptides inaccessible to its receptor69. Why higher yields are obtained when the NLS is conserved 
on the CTP-L1 construct remains a mystery, but one possibility could be protection from the degradation that 
is observed in this project (Figure 18) when the NLS is present as opposed to deleted. 
 
7.5 
Further experiments for successful production of HPV-16 VLPs in P. patens 
In this project, successful transformation of P. patens to express HPV-16 L1 and L1∆22 was achieved. Further, 
four CTPs able to localize eGFP to the chloroplasts were verified. The next step will be to create stable 
transformants for expression of the fusion product CTPc5-L1. If the production of the protein in P. patens is 
similar to Nicotiana spp., this construct will result in the highest yield compared to L1 and L1∆22. Whether 
this is the case can be determined by quantification of the proteins by sandwich ELISA. Another important 
assessment will be transmission electron microscopy (TEM) to visualize if the proteins form full VLPs or only 
capsomeres. Moreover, TEM in situ on whole cells would reveal if the targeting of the L1 proteins worked as 
intended and if they are present in the desired cell compartments. For HPV-16 L1 VLPs expressed in tobacco 
leaf chloroplasts, they can be observed in the stroma and around grana stacks as small dense particles about 
the size of the height of one or two thylakoids19. Purification of the VLPs before visualization can be beneficial 
if the yield is low70. Purification has been attained by sucrose cushion sedimentation, density gradient 
ultrafiltration, and chromatography techniques such as size exclusion chromatography19,40. Efficient VLP 
purification has been described using ultracentrifugation of a double sucrose cushion and a density gradient, 
separated by a dialysis and concentration step70.  
A sedimentation analysis could be used to further investigate the proportion of fully assembled VLPs37,19. In 
other studies, sucrose-gradient sedimentation analysis has been used to compare the sedimentation 
coefficient of plant produced L1 to insect cell derived HPV-16 L1 VLPs37. The fractions collected from the 
sedimentation analysis contains L1 aggregates of different masses and the analysis can be used to assess how 
well the protein assemble into the full particle or rather into smaller aggregates37.  
Analyses investigating the truncated proteins detected in Western Blot analysis (Figure 18) for L1 and L1∆22 
can be conducted in various ways. In the case that these shortened version of the proteins arise from 
alternative splicing of the mRNA, this will be clear from mRNA analyses. Northern Blot analysis or reverse 
transcriptase PCR followed by gel electrophoresis and sequencing can be utilized44. Another probable cause 
for smaller variations of the L1 proteins is degradation by proteases. Engineering approaches to avoid 
degradation of the protein will often be by knockout of the protease gene or by co-expression of a protease 
inhibitor, but the absence of this enzyme may affect the cell negatively71. Alternatively, the target for 
cleavage within the recombinant protein can be modified by replacing the residues as long as the properties 
of the protein are not lost71.  
Glycosylation of the L1 protein is also an important aspect to uncover, as it can affect the immunogenicity of 
the protein72. For example, an N-linked glycosylation on the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) surface 
envelope glycoprotein gp120 was hindering antibody from binding to an epitope on this protein72. When the 
motif leading to the glycosylation was altered, the glycosylated oligosaccharide chain was no longer present,

and the antibody recognition of the protein was increased72. A study expressing the HPV-16 L1 protein in the 
animal CV-1 cells and human keratinocyte HaCaT cells found a 66 kDa version of the protein that was 
determined to be N-glycosylated with a high-mannose type oligosaccharide structure73. All 4 predicted N-
glycosylation sites on the L1 protein appeared to be glycosylated and a weak hydrophobic region in the N-
terminal of L1 was determining for the glycosylation, albeit only a fraction of all L1 in the cell was actually 
modified73. Besides localizing strongly to the nucleus, L1 also show some staining in the ER, but it is not 
secreted73. The authors conclude that glycosylated L1 is retained in the ER and cytoplasm and not targeted 
the secretory pathway or the nucleus, where most of the L1 otherwise reside73. The importance of 
glycosylation for immunogenicity of HPV-16 L1 VLPs must be determined to maximize the effect of a vaccine. 
Since the N-glycosylation in the ER is conserved among eukaryotes, and L1 proteins do not move to the Golgi 
Apparatus, there is no apparent need for cells engineered for humanized glycosylation74. With regard to VLP 
assembly, glycosylation is also considered to not play a significant role, as the L1 proteins are natively 
localized to the nucleus for assembly, and VLPs are possible to produce in E. coli. 
Besides the intracellular expression of L1 considered in this project, another aim for localization of the L1 
protein could be secretion via the ER and Golgi apparatus into the medium. Secretion is highly beneficial for 
production with liquid cultures of P. patens, since the downstream processing of the product becomes easier 
by purification from the simple growth medium compared to when disruption of the cells is necessary32. 
Moreover, the naturally secreted proteins from P. patens are fully characterized and do not display protease 
activity32.  Thereby, a secretion of L1 into the extracellular medium would contribute to further lower the 
price of HPV vaccine production. Secretory signal peptides endogenous to P. patens have already been 
identified and shown to efficiently facilitate secretion of heterologous proteins when N-terminally fused to 
them75. As with the CTPs, secretion signals are also cleaved off when the protein enters the ER, so signal 
peptides would not interfere with VLP formation75. Nevertheless, the precise cleavage site for the secretion 
signals would also need to be experimentally verified. It is not easy to find studies on secretion of L1 protein 
into extracellular medium and VLP assembly, but research has shown that purified HPV-16 L1 protein can 
form VLPs in vitro when produced from a baculovirus expression vector system76. Similarly, secretion of HPV-
16 L1 by P. patens into the medium and subsequent purification and in vitro VLP assembly could be a 
possibility.  
Obtaining efficient production of HPV VLPs in P. patens could be a starting point to develop vaccines against 
more complicated viruses. Chimeric HPV L1 VLPs have been investigated for vaccination against multiple HPV 
antigens in one VLP, as well as vaccination against other pathogens34,77,78. For example, a chimeric VLP 
expressed in the yeast Pichia pastoris consist of a fusion of the HPV-16 L1 protein and the HIV epitope P18-
I10 and is being developed with the aim of combining protection against HPV and HIV in one vaccine78.  
While there are still many aspects to uncover and possibilities to consider before an HPV vaccine is 
successfully produced in P. patens, this project represents the first step on the way: synthesis of the main 
component, the L1 protein.

CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK  
In this thesis, recombinant HPV-16 L1 and L1∆22 protein were successfully produced in Physcomitrium 
patens. This was obtained by optimizing the genes for expression in the moss species, by utilizing a strong 
promoter, and by using an approach of random integration of the fully assembled gene cassette into the 
nuclear genome. Three transformants were confirmed for L1 production, while four were shown to produce 
L1∆22, and more producers are expected be confirmed from screenings of the transformed moss that 
survived the selection process. Moreover, this project verified four chloroplast transit peptides (CTPs) native 
to P. patens for their ability to localize protein to the plastids. This will enable the targeting of L1 to 
chloroplasts in P. patens in the future. In upcoming projects assessment of VLP formation will also be 
conducted, as particle formation is important for the use as a vaccine.  
Already, plants show great promise for VLP production, as several vaccines are in clinical trials. Some of these 
are produced by the Canadian company Medicago that produces VLPs in Nicotiana benthamiana. Their 
quadrivalent influenza vaccine has finished phase III clinical trials and their COVID-19 vaccine has recently 
been approved for use by the Canadian authorities79,23. Thereby, the first plant-produced vaccine has been 
approved for human use80. This proves that there is room for plant-based vaccines, specifically VLPs, on the 
market. With just one vaccine approved so far, there are many opportunities for sustainable vaccine 
production awaiting. The need for more accessible vaccines should be met with efficient green production 
hosts, P. patens being an obvious candidate.
