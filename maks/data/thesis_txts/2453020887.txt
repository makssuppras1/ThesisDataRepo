 Resume
Connection of power electronic supplies to the utility introduce harmonic currents which result in 
numbers of problems, such as waveform distortion, heating, EMI and reduce reliability issue of 
the utility. Therefore, the harmonic currents need to be restricted to a limit which is defined by an 
international standard such as IEC61000-3-2. Thus, power factor correction (PFC) converters 
should be implemented to fulfil these requirements in the standards.  
Conventionally, the PWM converters have been the primary candidate for PFC applications, which 
they can provide higher power factor with simple control techniques. However, these converters 
suffer from many drawbacks such as low power conversion density, high switching losses, and 
electromagnetic interference (EMI) problems. Accordingly, they operate at low switching 
frequencies resulting in bulky passive components. In contrary, the resonant converters can 
minimize these drawbacks, since they have many advantages such as high-frequency operation; 
low EMI; fast dynamic response; high energy density; and low losses. 
In this thesis, an overview of the previous researches which have been published proposing 
solutions for PFC converters was presented and classified. This has led to investigating the 
adoption of resonant converters into PFC applications, since the literature reveals that the resonant 
converters have inherent power factor capability. Thus, the resonant converters were studied to 
select the topologies suitable for this application. The Class-E converter was chosen as it is one of 
the high-frequency, high-efficient and low noise converters family due to its soft-switching 
capability. However, this converter suffers from high switching stress, which limits the input and 
voltage applications. Class-E and Class-E derived topologies were investigated to be adopted for 
PFC application. These two topologies were modelled, analyzed, and designed using two different 
mathematical approaches, namely, analytical approach and state-space approach. The later gives 
more precise results, does not need any design conditions, and is the mainstay of design of the 
controller. 
The Class-E converter had a good performance with a power factor 0.996 was achieved 
inherently. The results completely meet the utility-line input current harmonic contents of the 
lighting equipment standard IEC 61000-3-2 Class-C with THD 8.8 per cent without any input filter 
and without a controller. However, the efficiency of the converter was about 88.2 per cent. In 
contrary, the Class-E derived proposed circuit showed low performance compared with Class-E, 
as the power factor was 0.932 with higher THD reached 21percent without input filter. However, 
the efficiency of the converter was 94 per cent, which is higher than that in Class-E topology. 
Finally, bridgeless topologies of the Class-E and Class-E derived were proposed, designed and 
simulated.

 Chapter 1
                                                       Introduc
 
The large-scale deployment of power electronics in the electrical power grid has led to some 
challenges such as harmonics and low power factor, which boosted the demand for criteria to meet 
these challenges or at least limit them. As a result, several international standards have been 
published, which specifically limits the magnitude of harmonics and improve the power factor. 
The International Electrotechnical Committee (IEC), for instance, published an international 
standard IEC 61000-3-2 which prescribes the maximum amplitude of the line frequency harmonics 
up to the 40th harmonic current. These standards are applicable to equipment with rated current 
less than 16 A per phase and supplied from low voltage lines at 50 or 60Hz [1]. To comply with 
these standards, power factor correction (PFC) needs to be employed.  
Single-phase converters consist of two stages, namely an AC-DC rectifier followed by an 
energy storage capacitor and a DC-DC converter, as shown in Fig. 1.1. The front-end stage AC-
DC converter rectifies the AC voltage to a relatively smooth DC voltage. The capacitor filters the 
double-the-line frequency component. While the DC-DC converter provides the current and 
voltage levels that apply to the load with or without galvanic isolation. This work focusses on the 
front-end power factor correction rectifier for the single-phase offline converter. 
 
Figure 1.1: Offline converter structure.   1.

the capacitor voltage resulting in a short duration pulsating current occurring at the peak of the 
voltage waveform, as illustrated in Fig 1.2(b). Although the fundamental component of the current

Two approaches that can be followed to reduce the current harmonic, specifically passive 
and active ones.  The passive approaches are simpler, yet they are bulky passive components which 
are not able to regulate the output voltage. In these approaches, a large inductor can be inserted in 
series with the capacitor. The resulting  LC filter is limiting the harmonics,  restricts the conducting 
electromagnetic interference (EMI) and smooths the dc output voltage. However, the capacitor and 
the inductor can resonate near the harmonic frequencies resulting in degrading the input AC 
current. Thus, the active approaches are more prevalent in implementation of PFC application. In 
the active approaches, controlled switches are used to correct the input power factor. The PFC 
circuit is inserted between the storage capacitor and the full bridge rectifier, as shown in Fig 1.3(a), 
results in a current waveform mimics the voltage waveform as shown in Fig 1.3(b). This PFC 
circuit which is basically a boost converter, in this case, suppresses the harmonic content of the 
converter, as illustrated in Fig 2(c), and the converter emulates a pure resistor.

Besides the boost converter, other pulse-width-modulation (PWM) converters are used 
for PFC applications. For instance, SEPIC, Cúk, and combination of a buck and a boost 
converter can be used for these applications [3]. These converters have simple topologies, 
straightforward design procedure, higher power factor and a simple controller. However, they 
suffer from a number of disadvantages such as high switching losses which limits the switching 
frequency and resulting in low power conversion density. In addition, switching mode operating 
in the PWM results in quasi-square switching current and/or voltage waveforms, which have 
high-frequency harmonic components and cause EMI problem. 
Replacing the PWM converters with resonant converters can minimize these drawbacks. 
These topologies have many advantages over their PWM counterparts, such as soft switching; 
high-frequency operation; low EMI; fast dynamic response; high energy density; and low losses 
[3-5]. Thus, if the resonant power factor correction circuits can be designed to operate under zero 
current and/or zero-voltage switching, then switching frequency can be increased resulting in 
reducing the size of the passive components and thus increase the power density, higher loop-gain 
bandwidths and faster response [6].

However, the resonant converters have some drawbacks such as the switches are subjected 
to higher forward currents and reverse voltages than they would encounter in non-resonant 
counterparts of the same power [7]. In addition, the circuits, architectures, and control currently 
present the biggest hurdle to achieve the advantages of the high-frequency operation for a wide 
operating range [8]. In addition, the complexity of the controller can be another drawback. Finally, 
as the passive resonant components may take many cycles to store or release resonant, this may 
result in large-signal during the transient conditions [8]. 
Thus, the main aim of this project is to investigate the capability of adoption resonant converters 
into PFC applications. 
2. Project Objectives
 
The main objectives and contribution of this project are summarized in the following: 
 
• To research and review some of the existing PFC topologies. 
• To choose the right two resonant topologies to be adapted to the AC-DC PFC applications.  
• To model the two converters using two different mathematical approaches. 
• To design the converters for a set of specifications. 
• To verify the functionality of the proposed topologies through simulation. 
• To realize the concept through experimental verification.

 Chapter 2
            State-of-the-Art in the PFC T
 
Connection of power electronic supplies to the utility introduce harmonic currents which result in 
numbers of problems, such as voltage distortion, heating, EMI and reduce the reliability of the 
utility [9]. 
For decades, a significant number of researches have been published proposing solutions 
for PFC converters. For simplicity, these solutions can be classified into two main groups, namely, 
PWM based PFC (PPFC) converters and soft-switched PFC (SPFC) converters. This chapter 
cursorily presents a review for active approaches in the literature. 1.
 PWM PFC Converters
In this PFC topologies, the diode bridge rectifier is combined with a PWM DC-DC converter 
followed by a large capacitor for filtering and energy storage. A comprehensive review of PPFC 
converters configurations carried out in [10], and they were categorised into two main 
classifications, namely topology-based classification and converter-based classification, as shown 
in Fig 2.1.  
Boost converters are the most famous among PWM DC-DC converters, which traditionally 
used for PFC applications, because of its simplicity and economical for the single-phase input 
power supplies [11]. However, it has some drawbacks such as complexity of control, high 
conduction and switching losses, complexity in implementing isolation, high startup inrush 
current, absence of current protection in overload conditions, and the restriction on the output 
voltage [9, 12-13]. In contrary, Buck converters suffer from crossover distortion as the input 
current does not flow as long as the input voltage lower than the output voltage. Thus, it is not 
ideal for power factor corrector applications [9, 14]. The buck-boost, SEPIC, Cuk and Flyback 
converters are good candidates since they perform both step-up and step-down characteristics [14-
15] although they suffer from high voltage stress, an inverting output, and bulky passive 
components [13, 15-16]. 
A family of bridgeless PFC converters have been proposed to improve the efficiency by 
replacing two of the line frequency diode by high-frequency switch [17]. This family of converters 
was classified into four different groups, as shown in Fig 2.2, and it is discussed in detail in [18].

 Soft-switched PFC Converters
SPFC converters can be classified further into soft-switched PWM for PFC applications (SP-
PFC) converters and Resonant PFC (RPF) converters, as shown in Fig 2.3. In the first group, the 
soft switching can be provided for the aforementioned conventional PWM converters through 
auxiliary circuits, control techniques, or a combination of them. While in the second group, soft 
switching is achieved inherently by adopting resonant based approaches.   
 
Figure 2.3: Classification of soft-switched PFC converters. 
a) SP-PFCConverters
Soft-switching commutation and pulse width modulation can be achieved simultaneously through 
three different approaches, as illustrated in Fig. 2.3.  
In the first group, an auxiliary circuit is used to achieve the soft-switching. For instance, 
some of the soft-switching is achieved by employing the active snubbers solution, which is also 
called zero voltage-transition (ZVT), in which an additional switch is added to a passive snubber 
[25]. In [26-28], extra passive components and semiconductor switch were added to replace the 
conventional hard-switching with a ZVS-PWM. While [29] proposes a boost PFC converter 
employing a ZCS PWM switch instead of ZVS to minimise the conduction loss and the current 
stress of the main switch.

Although, the previous ZVS and ZCS PWM techniques can only improve the switching 
losses, albeit the significant conduction loss cannot be solved [29]. However, both the soft-
switching condition can be achieved, and conduction losses can be minimised simultaneously. 
The auxiliary circuit is applied to PFC converters to gain the soft-switching while the conduction 
loss is reduced by using techniques such as bridgeless, parallel PFC, and interleaving topologies 
[24-25, 31-32]. Nevertheless, this solution increases the complexity of the design and the control, 
mainly if the circuit is operated at higher switching frequencies. 
Relying on auxiliary components alone may result in losing efficiency outside a narrow 
operating range and/or power [33-34]. Thus, converters can be controlled to achieve both ZVS, 
as well as power factor correction [33]. By adopting the control solution, the converter achieves 
ZVS across wide voltage and power ranges by a minimal amount of circulating current [34]. In 
[35], a current control technique is used in buck-boost converters to achieve ZVS conditions in 
the switches where the switching frequency can be kept constant over the wide load changes. 
However, operating at a constant switching frequency usually, need more significant bulky 
passive components [13]. 
In other solutions, a hybrid combination of auxiliary circuit and control technique to 
achieve soft-switching. A combination of ZVT and current mode control are combined to 
achieve ZVS in a buck converter [36]. While, in [37], the ZVT method is applied with the fixed 
switching frequency to the boost-boost, and it is controlled by the ZVT-PWM controller to 
achieve the soft-switching operation. 
b) Resonant Converters
In these converters, the concept of resonant inverters is combined with PFC into one stage with 
sharing semiconductor device, which makes it attractive for commercial applications [38].  These 
converters can be divided further into two main groups: ZVS- and ZCS RPFC converters. 
A single-stage high power factor electronic ballast combined with a class E rectifier is 
introduced in [39]. In this topology, a Class-E current-driven rectifier with low voltage derivative 
is used for current shaping and operates under the ZVS condition. The Class-DE rectifiers are also 
used as power factor correction [38, 40]. In this topology, the Class-DE current driven rectifier 
with low voltage derivative is employed to operate under the ZVS condition. Both Class E and 
Class-DE for PFC applications can provide a systematic and straightforward analysis, a cheaper 
solution for commercial applications, a higher switching frequency range, a lower line harmonics 
distortion, and inherent PFC capability [38]. However, the Class-E in this application, for instance, 
has a bulky transformer [41] while Class-DE needs additional passive components for ZVS and 
load matching.  
The ZVS RPFC converters suffer from high conduction losses in the near the zero-
crossing of the utility-line input voltage [38, 42]. Thus, the ZCS RPF converters are the 
alternative. These converters have higher efficiency than their ZVS RPFC counterparts since the 
no current flow through the switch near the zero-crossing of line voltage. In [42], a family of 
circuit diagrams of the ZCS RPFCs without considering an EMI filter are presented.

 3.
 Summary
For decades, the PWM converters were dominating the PFC applications due to their high 
efficiency and power quality [6]. Nevertheless, operating in switching mode is challenging, 
specifically, when the high-frequency switching occurs at the higher current or high voltage levels 
resulting in high power dissipation. Thus, it limits the switching frequency and hence resulting in 
bulky size for the passive components. Moreover, because of the quasi-square switching current 
and/or voltage waveforms, these converters cause EMI problem. On the other hand, the bridgeless 
version of PPFC has many components, sophisticated control, and noise interference issues [24]. 
Soft-switching techniques are adopting to solve these problems and to enable operating at 
higher frequencies, and thus increases the dynamic response and increases the energy density. 
Table 2.1shows the trend of using RPFC converters for low power range. In contrary, SP-PFC 
converters still dominate the higher power PFC applications. Moreover, SP-PFC converters need 
extra components, sophisticated control, or a combination of them to gain soft-switching. On the 
other hand, most PPFC converters operate at low switching frequencies and need extra matching 
circuits. 
To the best of the author knowledge, no literature was conducted to adopt Class-E resonant

a) Voltage-Source Series-Resonant Converters
In these topologies, the output power can be controlled by controlling the switching frequency in 
which the voltage increases as the operating frequency approach the resonant frequency. This 
family of converters is divided further into:  
I. 
Series-loaded Resonant Converters (SRC) 
This type, in which resonant elements are connected in series with the load, has a simple 
topology, constant characteristic impedance, and constant resonant frequency over a wide range 
of loads [44]. It is preferred for low output applications [45], due to high peak current, particularly, 
if they are operated in discontinuous conduction mode (DCM). In this operation mode, SRC 
supplies constant current under load variation, which gives inherent overload and short-circuits 
protection [45]. Moreover, the function of switching frequency and charging time is linear, in the 
DCM, which makes the design of the circuit easier [46].  However, its efficiency drops 
progressively under light loads [45]. Furthermore, the operation for the no-load condition is 
impossible as the switching frequency will be infinite; as a result, no-load regulation is needed 
[47]. 
II. 
Parallel-loaded Resonant Converters (PRC) 
In this topology, the resonant tank is connected in parallel with the load. Compared with SRC 
counterparts, it is more suitable for multiple outlets applications as well as can operate as step-up 
or step-down converter with short circuit protection capability [45], as the PRC behaves as a 
voltage source to the secondary side. Due to the characteristics of operating regions, the light load

Conversely to the voltage-source parallel-resonant converters, the output voltage in these

voltage should lag the square-wave input current. This can be achieved by the capacitive vars effect 
which must be supplied to the converter, although this is only possible when the switching 
frequency is higher than the resonant frequency which contradicts the preferred operation 
condition [44]. 
c) Class-E and Subclass E ResonantConverters 
Class-E converter is one of the high-frequency, high-efficient and low noise converters family due 
to its soft-switching capability [1,74-75]. As in this topology, the zero-voltage switching (ZVS) or 
zero-current switching (ZCS) can be achieved based on its topology.  
 
Class-E inverters, which can operate at ZVS or ZCS condition, are the most efficient inverters 
available so far [1]. The ZVS operation condition is highly dependent on the effective resistance 
(i.e. the resistance is seen by the transistor). When the resistance is at its optimum value, both ZVS 
and ZVDS can be achieved. In this case, the switch is turned on when the switch voltage and its 
slope are zero and the current increase gradually, thus the antiparallel diode remains off, and the 
Class-E inverter is operating under the optimum operating condition. While the effective resistance 
is less than its optimum value, it is operated at suboptimum operation condition. In this case, the 
ZVS is still achieved while the slope of the switch voltage is negative at the turn off which results 
in part of negative the switch current flows in the antiparallel diode cause automatic turn-on of the 
switch [50]. Finally, when the resistance is higher than the optimum value, the ZVS cannot be 
achieved, and the switch current experiences a high spike at turn on. As a result, the effective 
resistance needs to vary from its optimum values to zero. However, the load resistance may vary 
from a minimum value to infinity. The first way to solve the mismatch between the effective 
resistance and the load resistance is through using an inductive impedance inverter [51].  The 
second approach to solve the load sensitivity is through relying on tuning saturable reactors to 
achieve ZVS regardless of variations in the load [52]. Another approach is through adding 
capacitances to the inductive coupling to form an impedance-transformation circuit [53]. In [54], 
the magnetizing inductances of the Class-E isolation transformer version are used to satisfy the 
==PAGE:24==
 
14 | P a g e  
 
ZVS, without the need of any extra component. Another approach can be adopted which is the 
load modulation approach where the resonant frequencies and characteristic impedances of the 
resonant networks in the circuit are adjusted to provide flexible operation and maintain ZVS 
switching at a constant switch duty ratio [55]. 
In the Class-E ZCS inverter, the switch is turned off when the current reaches zero resulting 
in zero turns off switching loss. This inverter can also achieve ZCDS condition if the design 
condition is fulfilled for optimum operation condition [56]. 
The only difference between Class-E ZVS and Class-E ZCS is the external capacitor, 
which is parallel to the switch, does not exist in the ZCS ones. However, with the absence of the 
parallel capacitor and as the switch turns on at nonzero switch voltage, this causes energy 
disputation in the switch. As a result, the switching frequency in Class-E ZVS inverters is lower 
than that of Class-E ZVS inverter [1]. Moreover, the external parallel capacitor absorbs the 
parasitic capacitances of the switch and inductors in the circuit.  
The Class-E rectifiers can achieve ZCS and low current-derivative switching [56]. A 
capacitor can be added in parallel to the diode, to reduce the peak diode current and low voltage-
derivative switching [57]. There are two types of Class-E rectifiers, namely half-wave or full-wave 
rectifier, can be used [1]. In the half-wave ones, the diode turns on and off at low voltage derivative 
switching condition resulting in low noise and switching losses, yet these rectifiers suffer higher 
conduction losses [58]. On the other hand, the full-wave Class-E rectifier has lower conduction 
loss than the half-wave counterparts as well as low losses in the filter capacitor resulting in 
reducing its size [59].  
Class E rectifier is a counterpart of class E inverter, where the current and voltage 
waveforms in the rectifier are the reversal image of the waveforms in the inverter, as shown in Fig 
3.3 [59]. Thus, the class E inverter can be connected to class E rectifier to build what is known as 
resonant DC-DC converter [1, 53, 61-62]. Since the rectifier is driven by an AC voltage source 
and thus, it can be isolated from the source by high-frequency transformer [60]. This converter can 
operate, under ZVS condition in both inverter and rectifier part, with load ranges from full load to 
open circuit as well as safe operation under short circuit at the output [53-54]. However, the switch 
in class E experience high voltage stress, which reaches theoretically about 3.6 times the input 
voltage under 50 per cent operation condition [1]. In practical, the high voltage stress on the switch 
could be higher than that value (i.e. 3.6) due to the nonlinearity characteristic of the shunt capacitor 
[63, 65].

the peak voltage a bit but increases the third harmonic switching frequency AC current resulting

uses a small input inductor participating in the resonance. Although it suffers from high losses 
because of the high harmonic current.

 2.
 Bridgeless Class-E Converter
In the circuit shown in Fig. 5.1, the two diodes of the bridge rectifier are on each input voltage 
cycle, which causes voltage drop and energy dissipations. To enhance the efficiency and reduce 
the voltage drop, the rectifier bridge needs to be removed. This can be accomplished by an 
additional circuit to be operated when the input voltage in the negative cycle, as illustrated in Fig 
5.5 [92].    
The principle of operation in this topology is straightforward. When the input voltage is 
larger than zero (i.e. during the positive half cycle of the input voltage) the upper coupling circuit 
is working just like a normal DC-DC resonant converter, while the bottom coupling circuit is 
inactive as depicted in Fig 5.6 (a). During the negative halfwave of the input voltage, on the other 
hand, the lower coupling of the circuit is active, and the upper one is inactive, as illustrated in Fig. 
5.6 (b). In this manner, one of the coupling circuits is inactive during each half cycle resulting in 
many advantages, such as lower temperature rising, smaller components, higher power density, 
and symmetrical input current flow resulting in low harmonic content, compared with the 
conventional topology shown in Fig 5.1 [92].

 Chapter 7
                                                       Conclus
 
This chapter presents a discussion and an overview of the two converters which are presented in 
this thesis. In addition, it includes suggestion and future works with respect to the proposed 
topologies. 1.
 Discussion
The results show that the two topologies have power factor capability inherently. However, the 
Class-E circuit gave a higher power factor and lower harmonic distortions. The capacitor in the 
resonant tank of this circuit also provides DC isolation between the input and output. Although 
the circuit has low efficiency compared with PWM converters counterparts, the circuit can be 
operated with soft switching (i.e. it can be operated at a higher frequency) and hence it achieves 
low EMI; fast dynamic response; high energy density; and low losses. Moreover, Table 7.1 
shows that the Class-E has a simple architecture, and it does not need an additional controller to 
achieve PFC or an additional filter to filter out the harmonic currents as compared with other 
resonant converters for power factor applications. Finally, most of the resonant converters in the 
literature they are used for low power factor applications, which ranges from 36W to 52W, while 
these two circuits are proposed to higher power applications.  
On the other hand, the Class-E derived circuit showed higher efficiency, albeit it has poor 
PF and THD. In addition, the circuit has no DC isolation between the input and the output. Thus, 
a large capacitor needs to be inserted to achieve the isolation without affecting the resonance 
behaviour of the circuit. 2.
 Overview
In this work, the investigation of adoption resonant converters, namely Class-E and Class-E 
derived, into PFC applications were carried out. The two topologies were modelled, designed, and 
analyzed by two mathematical approaches. Based on the analysis and experimental results, the 
work reveals the following: 
• State-space approach gives more precise results; solves numerically algebraic equations of 
the transient condition and switching condition simultaneously; does not need any design 
conditions and is the mainstay of design of the controller.  
• The two topologies achieved the PFC functionality inherently, with operation base on soft-
switching, which allows for high-frequency design. 
•  The Class-E converter showed good performance with a power factor 0.996 was achieved 
inherently. The efficiency of the converter was 88.2 per cent with THD 8.8 per cent without 
any input filter and still the results completely meet the utility-line input current harmonic 
contents of the lighting equipment standard IEC 61000-3-2 Class-C. 
==PAGE:64==
 
54 | P a g e  
 
• In the Class-E converter, the switching frequency was varied to achieve the output load 
variation; meanwhile, the duty ratio was adjusted to achieve the ZVS condition. 
•  The Class-E converter suffers from higher voltage stress across the switch. 
• The Class-E derived showed low performance compared with Class-E, as the power factor 
was 0.932, which achieved inherently, with higher THD reached 21percent without input 
filter. However, the efficiency of the converter was 94 per cent, which is higher than that 
in Class-E topology. 
• To enhance the efficiency and reduce the voltage drop, bridgeless PFC topologies were 
proposed. The topologies consolidated two symmetrical circuits, in which the only one of 
them operates each half cycle of the line voltage. 
• The bridgeless converters have higher efficiency and two quadrant operation on the AC 
side. 3.
 Future works
The research focused mainly on proof-the-concept of the Class-E and Class-E derived 
converters from the topology aspect, so far. However, the design needs to be optimized and 
controlled. Thus, the suggestions can be classified into two main areas. 
a) Topologies Modification
As the prototypes have inherent power factor capabilities with the ability to consistently achieve 
ZVS at across the entire power load range. The converters need to be redesigned at a higher 
switching frequency. Thus, the parasitic components need to be taken into consideration in the 
design and analytical procedure. In addition, the main factor to design inductors is the core material 
selection. Thus, an investigation and comparison should be carried out to select the right one. The 
efficiency improvements can be achieved if the bridge rectifier is eliminated, as it was proposed 
in the bridgeless PFC converters. Furthermore, the efficiency of the current-driven class D rectifier 
can be degraded due to energy dissipation caused by the inherent parasitic capacitances of the 
diodes. These capacitances may result in losing the ZCS at higher frequencies. Thus, if the two 
diodes can be replaced by two switches, the rectifier can be derived by the higher-frequency 
voltage source, and bidirectional power transfer can be achieved in this case. Finally, the Class-E 
ZCS can be implemented to achieve higher efficiency and compared the results with Class-E ZVS 
topology. 
b) Topologies Control
The Class-E topology loses the ZVS capability at lower voltages, which can be solved by duty 
ratio control. While the output power can be adjusted through frequency modulation. Class-E 
derived, on the over hand, can be controlled by burst mode operation if it is redesigned to operate 
at higher switching frequencies.
