Abstract
Detecting defects on photovoltaic modules is a difficult task that at the same time is cru-
cial for the panel to work correctly. Defects on the solar cell can greatly influence its
performance. Detecting defects is mostly done manually which is also problematic for
many reasons like the number of images to analyze, the difficulty to localize the defect
and mark it, labeling, and so on. In this paper, we present a new approach to this prob-
lem. We will approach the problem with Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) in an
unsupervised manner. More specifically we are going to introduce a fast-AnoGAN net-
work to this problem. We will test the binary classification capabilities of this method on
two different datasets of electroluminescence images of PV modules. One of them con-
tains monocrystalline cell images and the other polycrystalline cell images. We will use
six different methods to classify the images into healthy or defective and evaluate their
performance.

Introduction
Sustainable energy is a topic that grows substantially every year, it’s the best way to get
energy while producing almost no pollution. In Q1 2020 renewable energy in global elec-
tricity generation reached 28% contribution [1]. The Danish Government set an ambitious
goal of powering the whole country on renewable energy sources by 2050 [2]. Solar pan-
els are one of the most popular and best ways to do that, so indeed they will contribute
to reaching fossil independence. It’s worth noting that in 2021 wind and solar energy
themselves came 10% in global energy production. In Denmark in 2020 solar panels
generated 1.2 TWh which gives approximately 4% of the country’s energy generation [3].
The amount of energy produced/converted by each solar panel is not only dependent on
the weather/amount of sun that it’s exposed to but also on its physical state such as the
condition of its surface. In general solar panels don’t require a lot of attention, but still,
regular maintenance is required. Currently, maintenance of them is not as efficient as it
could be. In the age of automation, the maintenance work done mostly manually could
be improved by introducing automation. Doing so would make the maintenance not only
more efficient and cheaper but also the results would be more consistent. This is why
in this project we will be focused on exploring one of the possible solutions to automate
the maintenance process. To be more precise we will analyze the electroluminescence
images of photovoltaic panels captured by drones in order to detect the defects in the
cells.
Figure 1.1: Solar Farm [4]
There are some approaches to introducing deep learning as a solution to this problem.
They are usually based on supervised learning which might be problematic, due to the
labeling of the images. Defects on photovoltaic panels appear in some areas of the im-
age. Those areas very often don’t resemble any specific shape (for example often it’s
a crack that randomly spreads on the cell), Let’s not forget that there are many types
of defects that might appear on the panel and some of them are harder to identify than
others, especially on the polycrystalline cells that have random patterns on their surface

some of the defects are not that easily spottable. Additionally, we have to take into ac-
count different types of panels. When we work with monocrystalline panels it is easier
to identify the defect due to the fact that cells in this panel should be fairly identical with
uniform background, whereas, in polycrystalline panels, cells have patterns that increase
the difficulty of identification. Those factors make it hard for people who are responsible

1.1
Our approach
One of the most important factors is to make defect detection as automated as possible.
After going through relevant papers we decided to try to solve this task by applying the
solution presented in [5], we choose this method because the model looked interesting,
their problem was technically similar to the one presented in this project, and the perfor-
mance of their solution ended up being the best from all the methods they tested. This
means that we will train a generative adversarial network (GAN) and encoder on healthy
data (data without defects) and then use it with both healthy and defected images. The
use of the encoder is important when we want to perform these actions in real-time, in-
stead of in a series of iterations that are time-consuming as it is done in [6]. The encoder
is used to map a real image to a latent space point, which is later used by the generator
to generate a corresponding fake image. By comparing the real image with the gener-
ated one we hope to be able to extract the defect from the real image (if it’s a defected
image). In the end, using this method, allows us to train the networks on healthy data,
without the need to annotate the defected images. The only data that is going to be used
in the training has to only be separated from the whole dataset by identifying whether it is
healthy or not, this process is way simpler since it doesn’t require classifying the defect or
localizing it. In the training healthy data is provided without any labels which makes this
method unsupervised. After the training, we will use 6 different methods of classification
to classify the images into healthy and defective.
1.2
Objective
The objective of this paper is to explore the potential of f-anogan by using it on datasets
containing electroluminescence images of solar cells with and without defects. Perform
different experiments by changing the architectures of the networks as well as using dif-
ferent or differently preprocessed input.
1.3
Most Relevant Findings
In all of the experiments, the models learn to generate a visually similar representation of
the real images. The generated images never contained a defect, which is good for this
method.
The classification methods were performing better always for the monocrystalline dataset.
In the end, we managed to achieve better results for 4 out of 5 methods using the f-
AnoGAN models than for the 6th method that wasn’t using the models in the case of the
monocrystalline dataset. In case of polycrystalline for all 5 methods using the f-AnoGAN
models we got better results than for the 6th one that wasn’t using the models.
The best AUC values are 0.854 for monocrystalline and 0.602 for polycrystalline dataset
during the regular experiments, but in additionally conducted experiments we managed
to achieve an AUC value of 0.69 for the polycrystalline dataset.

2.2
Monocrystalline vs polycrystalline cells
The main difference between those two technologies is the crystal purity of a cell. Monocrys-
talline cells are manufactured from a single crystal of silicon, whereas polycrystalline are
manufactured from many crystals of silicon melted together.
This also affects their performance. In general monocrystalline cells perform better having
efficiency between 16-24%, while polycrystalline efficiency varies between 14-20% [10].
2.3
Convolutional Neural Network
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is one of the most fundamental deep learning algo-
rithms that is used to extract features from images and classify them. Other than that it
also performs exceptionally well with audio inputs. CNNs are based on how the human
brain works. To be more specific their architecture resembles the way how visual cortex
works.
CNNs are pretty simple in their architecture and they consist of 3 main types of layers:
• Convolutional layer - as the name suggests this block is the most characteristic block
for CNN. It requires a few components that are input data, a filter, and a feature map
[11].
– Input data is data representing an image (or other types of input). For example,
if an image is an RGB image of width w and height h, then the input is of size
3 x w x h.
– A filter is a matrix of weights that moves across the image to distinguish if a
feature is present in the part of the image.
– Feature map results from applying a filter on the image. The feature map’s size
depends on a few variables but it’s smaller or equal to the input data.
In this process, we can change a number of filters which will increase the depth
of the result, a stride that is by how many pixels we will move the filter which will
increase the size of the output, and lastly, padding means adding a frame of zero
values to the image so that the filter could cover more, which increases the size of
the output.
• Pooling layer - this layer is responsible for the dimensional reduction of its input data
(downsampling). Similar to the convolutional layer, this layer also uses a filter that
moves across the image, but this time the filter doesn’t contain weights. We have 2
most popular filters for this layer:
– Max pooling is the most popular method that uses a filter that outputs the max-
imal value from the area.
– Average pooling on the other hand returns the average value from the area.
• Fully-connected layer is a layer where each input node (neuron) is connected to
each output node with some weight. These layers are usually used as the last
layers in CNNs, which are meant for classification purposes based on the feature
maps acquired by the previous layers.
CNNs are often used partially as parts of some other architectures, usually as feature
extractors.

2.4
Unsupervised learning
In deep learning we have 3 main types of learning:
• supervised learning - learning with labels
• unsupervised learning - learning without labels
• reinforcement learning - learning based on an environment [12]
In this paper, we use an unsupervised learning approach, since one of the problems that
we want to address is related to labeling itself. As mentioned in previous sections labeling
the defects on solar cells is a problematic task due to the fact that each expert can perceive
defects in a different way, for example by highlighting the area of the defect in a different
way than others (being less or more precise).
Unsupervised learning is a type of learning that doesn’t need labels in a dataset. In this
method, it is often said that it is self-organized which means that models on their own learn
to recognize patterns and learn from them [13]. These algorithms on their own analysis
and cluster data that is not labeled.
Unsupervised learning has many applications, in our case, we will use these methods to
train both the Generative adversarial network and the encoder.
2.5
Generative adversarial network
A generative adversarial network (GAN) is a type of network that is a composition of two
separate networks competing with each other. It’s a fairly new method presented in [14].
Two networks are called Generator and Discriminator. It is common to explain this method
as sort of a game, where one team’s gain is another team’s loss [15]. One of the best
explanations that I met is presented in paper [14]. They compare the generator to a team
of counterfeiters trying to produce non-detectable fake currency. A discriminator on the
other hand is a team of police trying to detect a counterfeiter’s currency. The competition
between those two makes both agents improve in their tasks. When the generator is being
punished/awarded by losing/winning in the current learning stage it improves in producing
the fake data, whereas the discriminator in the same manner learns how to distinguish
the generated data from the real data.
2.5.1
Why GAN?
After doing some research on how to solve the problem presented in this project we
choose to use GAN in our solution. GANs are networks commonly used in unsupervised
learning problems. They are used to generate data similar to the data used in the learn-
ing process. We decided to use that because the initial situation was similar to the one
presented in the paper [5] and we considered this solution as one that can also suit our
problem, and on top of that according to the paper, f-AnoGAN outperformed alternative
approaches (analyzed by them). In this paper, they had to find defects in the images of
the retinas to potentially detect disease. In our case, we have to detect defects on solar
cells, which for that method is not much different.
2.5.2
Latent space
Before going further with an explanation of the GAN training it is worth explaining what
latent space is.
Latent space often described in machine learning as z-space is a multi-dimensional space,
where points represent an item and its features, in the way that two similar items will have
their representing points close to each other in latent space [16]. One of the advantages

of latent space is that its dimensionality of it is largely reduced compared to a raw image
[17].
In the GAN network, latent space plays an important role in the generating process. Since
a point in a latent space can represent an image, the points are used as input for the
generator. The generator based on these points produces images.
2.5.3
Discriminator training
The discriminator outputs 2 losses used to train both of the competitors. In the case
of the discriminator, the training process is simple because the discriminator is directly
connected to its loss which means that we can use it directly to train [18]. To train the
discriminator we follow the steps:
1. Discriminating the real from fake data
2. Discriminator loss judges the decision
3. Backpropagation - Discriminator updates its weight based on the loss

Generator training
The generator produces images based on random input from latent space (choosing a
random point in latent space).
As you can see in the figure below (fig 2.3) the Generator is not directly connected to its
loss, which makes the generator’s training a bit more complex. In the case of Generator
training, we have to backpropagate through the discriminator as well [19]. To train the
generator we follow the steps below:
1. Choose a random point in the latent space and feed it to the generator
2. Generator produces fake data based on the input
3. Discriminator classifies real and fake data
4. Calculate generator loss
5. Backpropagation - obtain the gradients by backpropagating through both the dis-
criminator and the generator
6. Update the generator’s weights

2.5.5
GAN training
As we explained above GAN training consist of two different training. So to train it properly
we have to alternate between them so that when we train the discriminator, the generator
stays constant so that the discriminator can learn the generator’s unwanted behaviors and
identify them during the classification process [20]. Similarly, during the generator training,
the discriminator has to stay constant, but in this case, additionally, the discriminator takes
part in the generator’s training process which means that changing it during training would
be even more destructive/nonsensical.
In our experiments we decided to alternate between those training in the following way:
1. Train discriminator for 4 training steps
2. Train generator for 1 training step
3. Until finished repeat steps 1 and step 2

2.5.6
Wasserstein Gradient Penalty Loss
The loss function chosen by us to train the GAN network is Wasserstein Gradient Penalty
Loss (WGAN-GP Loss). The original Wasserstein method offers training stability but in
some cases, it still fails to converge or generates poor samples [21]. Using Gradient
Penalty constraint instead of gradient clipping allows for easier optimization and conver-
gence. For further explanation of the method, we suggest paper [22].
2.6
Encoder
In paper [6] the solution they present is iteration based. So to generate an image similar
to the real image they need a point in the latent space that represents the real image. In
order to find it, they iterate. First, they take a random point in the latent space, and then
with each iteration, they are getting closer to the representing point. So in the end this
method is quite time-consuming and can’t be done in real-time.
In [5] they address this issue by adding an encoder to the composition. The encoder is
supposed to map the real image into the latent space to find its representing point and
then feed this point to the generator. In the figure 2.4 we present the architecture of the
fast-AnoGAN network.
Figure 2.4: Fast-AnoGAN Architecture
Autoencoder is a type of neural network that is used to find an efficient way of coding
unlabelled data [23]. It consists of 2 parts encoder and decoder. The encoder learns to
represent the input data in a dimensionally reduced version and the decoder learns to
recreate the input based on the code provided by the encoder. In the case of f-AnoGAN
encoder learns to represent the real sample image as a point in the latent space, and the
role of the decoder is played by the generator that tries to generate an image as closely
resembling the real input image.
2.6.1
Encoder training
The Encoder training is scheduled after the training of the generator and the discrimina-
tor. In the paper [5], they introduce 3 different ways of training the encoder ziz, izi, and
izif. They differ on the loss function and in all of them the only training component is the
encoder, the generator is already trained and stays fixed this time.
• ziz training is based on the mean squared error of the random point in the latent
space and latent space point produced by encoding the image generated based on
this random point:
zizloss = 1/d||z −E(G(z))||2
Where d is a number of dimensions in the latent space and z is the random point
from the latent space.
8

==PAGE:16==
The main problem with this method is that although the encoder is now exactly where
it should be aiming with its coding, it is being provided with only generated images
and never sees the real input images.
• izi training in comparsion with ziz training incorporates real images and follows the
typical structure of autoencoder trainings, where encoder is followed by decoder
G(E(x)). This time is based on mean squared error of the real input image and the
generated image based on the encoding of the real input image:
iziloss = 1/n||x −G(E(x))||2
Where n is the number of pixels in the image, x is a real input image, and || · ||2 is a
sum of squared pixel-wise residuals of pixel values of the images.
The problem with this approach is that the true mapping of the input images to the
latent space is unknown.
• izif training is basically an izi training with an additional component in the loss func-
tion. This time we are not only going to use the generator and the encoder but also
we will introduce the discriminator to the equation.
Adding the discriminator will help with finding points in the latent space that actually
looks closer to what real images look like. In this case same as with the generator,
the discriminator is also fixed.
izifloss = 1/n||x −G(E(x))||2 + κ||f(x) −f(G(E(x)))||2
Where κ is a weighting factor of the second component of the equation (in our ex-
periments it’s set to 1.0) and f(·) is the result of the discriminator.
Calculated loss is going to be a reference to as ”anomaly score”.
This method is being recommended in the [5] paper, due to the fact that it consists of
both differences found in the image space and differences found in the latent space.
We decided to use this method in all of the experiments that we had conducted.

In this chapter, we will introduce the datasets that we used during the training as well as

Datasets of electroluminescent images of sollar cells are not easy to find. Most of them

3.1
SPIE2019 dataset
SPIE2019 dataset was acquired using drones with CMOS (complementary metal-oxide
semiconductor) sensors in the outdoor conditions in the evening. The dataset was created
from 982 electroluminescence images of polycrystalline photovoltaic modules. Those
images were automatically split into cells using x and y axes projections to detect cell
boundaries. After automatic blur detection, they got 753 images of modules or over 47
thousand images of cells. In this project, we got provided with over 36 thousand cell
images from this dataset.
Additionally, the dataset got evaluated by experts in PV fault detection. They labeled each
image as:
• healthy
• finger failure
• crack of type A
• crack of type B
• crack of type C
Types (A, B, C) of crack represent the severity level of a crack. But in this project since
we train the networks in an unsupervised manner we are only interested in healthy and
defective labels, we don’t need to differentiate the type of defect.
SPIE2019 dataset presents electroluminescence images of solar cells acquired by drones.
The data is provided in 6 series that in total give us 36483 images of solar cells, although
not all the images are going to be used in the experiments. The provided data is already
preprocessed to some extent. Drones were manually flown over solar fields and they
recorded videos of solar panels. Images got obtained from those videos. So the main
problem was due to the lack of automation, the videos are chaotic which makes the pro-
cess of getting the images of cells more complicated. The preprocessing that has been
done before is mostly about acquiring a centered image of a cell.

Algorithm 1: Cropping to square algorithm
Where crop takes 4 parameters representing box to crop – a 4-tuple defining the left,
upper, right, and lower pixel coordinate.
Algorithm 1 allows us to crop from the middle of the image maximal possible square

that we know that we will keep the symmetry of the picture which in our case is really
important. Additionally, due to the fact that images are already filtered by the dimension
size, we know that the cropped image will be at least 280 pixels per 280 pixels.
Dark images
The process of acquiring the images from the drone footage is as you can see quite
problematic. The last issue that we addressed is the differences in the average color of the
images. That most likely comes from a low EL signal in the cells (potential degradation).

In this chapter, we will introduce the experiments, the methods as well as the results of

4.1
Introduction to the experiments
The goal of the experiments that are presented in the next section is to test different
methods and try to find which one is working the best. We could split the experiments into
two phases:
1. pre-experiment phase - in this phase we were testing which variables or architec-
tures might improve the performance of the model.
2. experiment phase - in this phase we had already chosen which values we want to
change and we tested every combination of those variables to find the best perform-
ing models.
To put it simply the first phase was more of testing what could be changed and to what
extent to improve the performance. Whereas during the proper experimental phase we
had already established what is the most crucial and test all the combinations of those
values, this phase was more organized.
In the end, we will evaluate only the result of the proper experimental phase.
4.2

In this phase as mentioned above, we were checking how changing some values, adding
layers, and changing layers are influencing the results. This phase took over a month in

• different architectures of the networks (Generator, Discriminator and Encoder)
– adding/removing layers
– changing the size of layers
– learning rate
– size of the latent space
– decay of the gradient
• number of epochs
• datasets to train
– size of the image
– different filtering - this applied only to the SPIE2019 dataset where we change
the threshold on the average color to filter the darkest images

4.3.3
results
In this section, we will present the results of the conducted experiments. We decided to
evaluate the results using 6 different methods.
• Anomaly score - loss presented in the encoder training section.
• Peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) - is a ratio between the maximum possible power
of a signal and the power of distorting noise that affects the quality of its represen-
tation [28]. It’s going to be used as an image similarity metric.
• Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) - is another image similarity metric. The difference
between SSIM and PSNR is that PSNR estimates absolute error while SSIM cal-
culates perceived image quality. SSIM compares local patterns of pixel intensities
that have been normalized for luminance and contrast [29].
• OTSU method - is automatic image thresholding, that groups pixels in the image
into 2 classes, foreground, and background, by finding a threshold value [30]. In
comparison with previous methods, otsu takes just one image as an input. As a
score, we used the ratio of foreground pixels to all the pixels. We used the Otsu
method in 3 different ways:
– Otsu on the subtracted image (real image - generated image).
– Otsu with Gaussian blur on the subtracted image - Gaussian blur added to
reduce the noise.
– Otsu on the real image - we decided to use it as a reference value to compare
the results of binary classification of the methods using the model with a method
that doesn’t use the model. Since this method uses real images it doesn’t use
a model at all.
To evaluate the results we plotted receiver operating characteristic curves (ROC curves),
which can show a diagnostic ability of a binary classifier as its discrimination threshold
varies, [31], and calculated the area under the curve (AUC) that is a quantitive measure
of model performance.

Smoothness of latent space
In general, relatively close points in the latent space should generate images that are
visually similar to each other. We will call this feature latent space smoothness.
We checked the smoothness of the latent space in 2 methods
1. 2 random points - in this method we take 2 random points z1 and z2 from latent
space, next we calculate a vector by which we will be moving:
v = (z1 −z2)/num_samples
where num_samples is the number of images we want to generate. By adding v to
the point we will get the next point that is on the line defined by z1 and z2. In this
way, we can find a list of num_samples points starting in z1 ending in z2, where every
two neighboring points are apart from each other by v. By visually comparing if the
transition between the images is ”smooth” we decide if the latent space is smooth
2. 2 random real images - in this method we take 2 random real images, that we feed
to the encoder to get the z1 and z2 points and later we follow the same steps as in
the 1st method.

Future work
To further analyze this method of solving the problem we suggest to try conducting more
experiments with different architectures, by making the architecture more complex it could
be possible to generate images that are closer to the real image. Especially working
on improving the generator performance could be beneficial since in all the experiments
conducted in this project it wasn’t able to generate images with a high level of detail, which
especially in the case of polycrystalline datasets seems to be crucial for this method.
Getting more detailed images that are close to the real image and subtracting them from
each other would lead to better extraction of the defect.
Another important aspect to analyze would be splitting the images and conceivably trying
some further preprocessing of the images. In the additional experiment phase, it proved to
have a huge potential since the results were improved by 9%. This most probably means
that further improving the results is still possible. Splitting the images in a ”smarter” way
could be also advised. For example, it could be done by detecting the horizontal/vertical
lines that are present on PV cells and splitting the images according to those lines.
Due to the fact that the time for conducting experiments greatly exceeded the expected
time we weren’t able to work on adding an unsupervised classifier to classify the detected
defects which were included in the initial idea. So lastly we suggest adding an unsuper-
vised classifier as the last element of the pipeline to cluster the images which is the primal
idea of this solution. Depending on the method supervised classifier could be used but
it would limit the defect classes to only the ones previously acknowledged. To avoid it if
an unsupervised classifier would be incorporated, the model could split the images into
classes that are not limited by the number, and whenever a new type of defect would
appear it could be potentially recognized and classified.

Conclusion
We conducted experiments using 2 different datasets - monocrystalline and polycrys-
talline. The experiments were supposed to check the results of training different models
on different datasets. Models were trained to generate a healthy image representing a
real image (either healthy or defected) and later by the use of different methods we were
testing binary classification results.
First, in the pre-experiment phase, we decided what we want to consider as a variable
and how we want to change it in the next phase. After that in the experiment phase, we
conducted all planned experiments and analyze the results. The results for the monocrys-
talline dataset were significantly better than for polycrystalline. For every classification
method that uses the model, except for SSIM, we reached better performance for the
method that doesn’t use the model (OTSU on the real images). The highest AUC val-
ues are 0.854 by Otsu on subtracted image for the monocrystalline dataset and 0.602 by
Anomaly score for the polycrystalline dataset. Models every time learned to generate an
image similar to the real image, and always subtracting a generated image from a cor-
responding real image gave the impression of normalizing the image (in terms of color)
which was also proven by generated histograms were histogram of subtracted image re-
sembled Gaussian distribution with a peak around mid-range (127 in this case).
Additionally for every experiment visually checking the smoothness of the latent spaces
confirmed the smoothness of each of them.
Lastly, in order to check the possibility of improvement for the polycrystalline dataset, we
tried splitting the images into halves and quarters in the additional experiment phase.
Splitting the image in quarters didn’t result in an improvement. However, horizontally
splitting the images into halves yields better results by 9% which in the end resulted in an
AUC value higher by almost 17% from the method that doesn’t use the f-AnoGAN model.
