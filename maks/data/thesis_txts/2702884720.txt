Summary
Freeing laborers from dangerous and boring work makes people start thinking about
autonomous driving in mine. The perception system, as the base of the autonomous
system, earns a spotlight from researchers. For accomplishing the autonomous systems’
assignments, the perception system needs to detect objects in mine. In this project, we
focus on the detection task using laser scanners.
We present a dataset that is collected in mine for the lidar object detection task. Based
on the permission of mine operators, we designed 4 different commonly seen scenarios
in mine. In the dataset, we are interested in the following types of objects: Trucks, cars,
auxiliaries, excavators, pedestrians, and other. We follow the KITTI dataset’s formation
in labeling and evaluation.
We also present an anchor-free lidar object detection model. The anchor-free method
outperforms our baseline model, Point Pillars, and is friendly for implementing on em-
bedding systems. For solving the problem that large objects shade their centers, we
employ deformable convolution in the extraction procedure. For regressing the location
of objects, we predict the offsets instead of the location directly. But for regression of
the centers’ height of objects, we have no idea but to use brute force. For the dramatic
variant scale information, we predict the logarithmic of objects. For the heading angle,
we adopt the trigonometric function.
After experiments on both the public dataset, KITTI, and our private dataset, we present
the advantage of our models on both datasets. At the end of the thesis, we review our
project, analyzing the limitations and the possible future works for improvement.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Freeing people from tedious and heavy load working environments are always one of
the reasons to develop the technology. Now, people who live in cities can possess the
facility of advanced technologies: 5G, Autonomous driving systems, etc. People who are
working in a mine, however, have to work in a dusty, ear-deaf, and even live-threatening
environment. This difference makes people start thinking: how to make mine operations
intelligent, or can we make the operation autonomous?
There are mainly two types of mines in the world: open-pit mines and non-open mines
[Wik21b].
The main difference between them is the way to transfer minerals, while
under-ground mines use tunnels to carry the thing we want to the surface or the factory
directly, open-cast mines use gigantic heavy-load trucks for a similar job. In this project,
we will focus on the open-cast mines.
These trucks might be the largest source of dust, noise, and life-threatening objects
in the mines due to the following reasons. Because the trucks could be found every-
where in mines, which means the trucks’ noise is the biggest source of noise and means
the truck contributes the most ranged dust in the mine. According to the data from
wiki [kom21], a dump truck could reach 15.60 m, 9.19 m, and 7.37 m in length, width,
and height, respectively. The height of the truck and the cockpit’s position show that
the blind area of the truck is large. In other words, drivers could not see objects that are
relatively small in a relative distance, such as people in 5 meters. Furthermore, the data
indicates that the capacity of the truck is over 200 tons. Because of the capacity and
the weight of the truck, the driver would not have a feeling that he just overwhelmed an
object. In conclusion, the trucks are important parts of mine operations that we want
to free people from the dangerous environment.
Making the trucks automated is a solution to our task. Since the trucks are driven
by themselves, there would be no more people working in this environment. In addition,
because the truck can run without people, they could continue working all day alone,
which boosts productivity. Meanwhile, because of the automated system, trucks could
process their tasks neatly. In total, making trucks automated is thus a solution for our
ultimate target. And the perception system is the basement of the whole auto-pilot
system.

1 Introduction
There are mainly three tasks in the field of computer vision: classification, detection,
and segmentation. In our project, we need to find the existence and locations of objects
in a scene. More specifically, we need to detect those common objects in mine: e.g.,
dump trucks, pickup trucks, cars, auxiliaries, excavators, and pedestrians. Therefore,
our task is the detection task.
Although there are various sensors that could be used for detecting objects, this pa-
per will focus on the lidar object detection system. And in the following sections of the
chapter, we will first give a naive description of the point cloud, a problem statement
about object detection using lidar, our motivations, and our contributions.
1.2
Object Detection
Object detection is a kind of method that allows computers can identify the classifica-
tion and exact location of an object in a given scene. In other words, object detection
outputs a set of bounding boxes around these detected objects, allowing us to locate the
objects we want to detect.
Compared with classification, which only presents the label of the given data, the detec-
tion not only gives the types of objects but also outputs the location. For example, for
an image that contains a car, the classification method outputs the label of the image:
”Car”, while the detection gives you the exact type and location.
Object detection methods can be parted into two parts: Machine Learning(ML) meth-
ods and none-ML methods. For non-ML methods, it is necessary to extract features
using [Wil94; VJ01; Low99] first, and using some tools like SVM, k-NN, and k-Means
do the classification. On the other hand, none-ML methods, like deep learning, can
achieve end-to-end detection without choosing features[Ren+16; Red+16].
1.3
Point Cloud: record the world in 3D
Point Cloud is a kind of data that could present the information of scenes in 3D. Com-
pared with 2D images, lidar renders rich geometry information: shape, distance, and
scale information, which helps us recognize the world.
3D point clouds have different presentation forms: RGB-D images, raw point cloud,
voxel point cloud, and mesh. An example about commonly used presentation forms are
demonstrated in Figure.1.1
Raw points cloud is a set of points defined by a given 3D coordination [gro21]. It could
provide the most precise object geometry information without discretization. And it

• Point cloud is irregular and unordered. Although Lidars scan scenes regu-

relations between the points are invariant after we transform the points. In sum-
mary, points’ geometric properties will not be changed by our perception.
• Point cloud has latent topological structures. Point clouds do not provide
topology information directly. However, since the geometric information of a point
is recorded based on a certain coordination, for example, Cartesian coordinates, the
topological relationship between a point with its nearby points could be modeled by
certain algorithms. It indicates that the point cloud could be handled by topology.

Volumes are another presentation form of point clouds. It could be seen as a cloud with
some modifications. And this presentation is very different from the raw point clouds.
Mainly properties of volume presentation are shown in the below paragraphs:
• Volumes are structured data. Although the raw point cloud is irregular and
unordered, there are some approaches to transform this unstructured data in an
ordered fashion. By employing gridding methods, the cloud data are transformed
into grid-based volume data, which are structured and can be saved orderly. In
short, volume presentations are easy to be processed due to the structured data
organization.
• Volumes presentation could save computation resources. By gridding the
cloud, a large amount of point cloud information can be compressed in input grids.
This compression reduces the amount of data that needs to be processed and thus
saves time and memory consumption. In conclusion, volume data have advantages
in processing.
• Volumes share the same geometric and topology properties with the
point presentation. Gridding methods do not violate the raw points’ geometric
and topology properties. In fact, gridding methods are a sort of regularization,
and thus the geometric and topology properties are saved. In summary, we can
model the volume data in the same way as we model the point cloud.
1.4
Problem Statement
We focus on the object detection problem during the project in the field of autonomous
driving in mine. For solving the problem, we will collect a dataset for training the sys-
tems, and will propose a neural network for accomplishing the detection task.
In this project, we are interested in a list of objects: vehicles, pedestrians, and other
obstacles. A detailed list for illustrating our selections is shown below:
• Trucks: The most common vehicle run over the mine. Due to the size and weight
of a truck, any crush it is deadly.
• Cars: The second common vehicle we can see in mine. Different from other vehicles
in mine, the size of cars are small but as the same important as other vehicles.
• Excavator: Static but important one in mine. Trucks need to detect the position
of excavators and drive to the loading yard.
• Auxiliary: Auxiliaries are slow but common in mine. Trucks need to detect auxil-
iaries and making the choice whether stop or slow down the speed.

1.5
Motivations
Autonomous driving in a mine is urgent since it frees people from dangerous labor en-
vironments. People working in the mine have to bear the noise produced by machines,
breathe dusty air ranged by vehicles, and be cautious about potential accidents: e.g.,
fallen rocks. Autonomous driving just wipes the existence of people in mine, making
labor no longer suffer the working environment and dramatically lowering the accident
rate. Moreover, autonomous driving in the mine will be an experiment field of city self-
driving naturally. Because of the nature of the mine, vehicles exist in the mine sparsely.
And because of the implementation of 5G stations, the V2X network could be tested
and achieved. These two things constructed the environment simpler but similar to the
one in the city. Based on these two reasons, we argue that autonomous driving in mine
is necessary and helpful for future autonomous driving research.
Within the system of autonomous driving, the perception system could be the first
and the most important part. As people cannot drive cars blindly, losing the percep-
tion system for an autonomous system is unacceptable. There are several vision sensors
available and implemented in dump trucks, but the lidar is important enough for us
to investigate. Cameras can receive rich information from the environment, and some
of them could reach very distances. However, they cannot collect distance information
directly. Although stereo camera modules solve the distance problem by calculating
photograph methods, the modules suffer the problem that the time consumption for
processing a frame is high. What is worse, both monocular cameras and stereo cameras
cannot solve the dark environment problem which cannot be solved. Some people would
argue that the effort of vehicles’ light in the dark, we claim another weakness of the
camera system. Even with the most sophisticated exposure system, human eyes would
be blinded by a light appearing suddenly. Let alone to say the camera system. However,
lidar would be another story, since the principle of gathering surrounding information
is different, lidar could see in the dark and stay sensitive when a strong light appeared.
Furthermore, lidar could gain accurate distance information directly, and point clouds
are enough for detection tasks in mine.
Detecting objects using point cloud still has much work to be done. Since the price
of lidar became affordable, laboratories and companies started researching methods.
Many methods blow out into researchers’ eyes. However, because of the hardness of
point cloud processing, there are still works that need to be done for tackling the object

1.6
Contributions
Based on the aforementioned related works, we propose a new object detection model
using lidar point cloud for this project:
• We utilize 2D convolution and 3D convolution for feature extraction stage and
compare the performance of them.
• We present 3D object detection anchor-based methods and tackling prediction
problems.
• We apply the attention mechanism convolution to address the vacuum center-point
problem.
• We consider the sparse of the road scenes and using a data augmentation for a
higher training eﬀiciency.
• We demonstrate that our work outperforms the baseline Point Pillars in our work.

2.2
Lidar and Point Cloud Processing
2.2.1
Lidar
Lidar, often called: 3D laser scanning, is a sensor that does scan the surrounding world
using light. Lidar is an active sensor, which means it generates energy for measuring
the environment where is placed. Although there are different types and platforms of
lidar [Wik21a], they share the same measuring principle, that is time of flight(ToF).
The principle of the ToF is fundamental. We pulse a beam of light and receive the
reflection of the beam, time from the period, calculate the distance from lidar to the
object. We present [RCr21] for a simple illustration in Figure 2.9.
The measuring procedure is simple and effective. That makes the lidar provides rich
geometric information with high accuracy. Objects’ surface information: locations and
reflections are thus extracted by lidars. Besides, lidars are activate sensors, they generate
beams for scanning. And therefore, they can accomplish the job in a dark environment.
However, lidars are not the perfect sensor.
Because of the limited power, they can-
not scan a very far distance. Also, lidar cannot scan low-reflection objects well. These
two situations result in blank areas in scanning. In addition, lidars render massive point
clouds, which is a challenge for point cloud processing.

Feature Process
Points we receive from the device contain 3D locations and sometimes contain reflections.
However, this is hard for us to process with these data, mathematical tools need more
attributes for carrying the job. Feature processing is to gain higher dimensions data
from a single point. Feature processing is usually done by two approaches: handcrafted
[Rac16] and learned [SWL19; Shi+20a; HHW10; GLU12; Ge+20a], which is usually
conducted by machine learning methods and called feature learning. The latter term is
also named feature extraction. Considering the massive number of points, even gridded,
lots of resources for computation are needed. Convolutions are widely used in the field
of computer vision, this method extracts information of an object based on kernels. By
setting the size of the kernel and the stride of the convolution process, we can down-
sample the given data without using sampling methods, which would introduce the
unbalanced sampling problem.

2.3.1
Point-based VS Voxel-based
Point-based
[Snovel19_CVPR]is the first work that uses points directly in the object detection task.
It takes raw points as input and produces the locations of predicted objects. The Point-
RCNN is composed of two stages. While the first stage produces lots of redundancy
proposals, the second stage refines the proposals and scores them. The model starts
with a semantic segmentation to separate the background and objects, by predicting
the labels of points. Then for points labeled as an object, also called foreground, the
model generates a bunch of predicted bundle boxes and uses methods to remove the
redundancies. In the second stage, the predictions are transited and rotated into their
canonical coordinations, using pooling methods to refine the prediction results and to
score the outputs. Recently, transformer becomes a new hot method in point cloud
learning [FRK21; Guo+20; WCY21; ML20; HKX21]. [MGJ21] is novel method that use
transformer[Vas+17] in 3D object detection. [Qi+19] propose a method based on vote
for selecting objects to detect.
Voxel-based
Because of the structured property of voxel presentation, lots of works are done [Shi+20a;
ZT17; Ali+19; YML18; Wan+20; YZK20; Lan+19]. Points are formatted into grids
and these models use the convolution method processing the gridded data and output
predictions. Gridding methods naturally introduce information loss in the feature pro-
cess. [ZT17] first maps the unordered point cloud into structured voxel space. [YML18]
proposed a novel 3D convolution named sparse convolution for alleviating the intensive
computations. [Shi+20a] inspired by the [YML18], replacing the feature extraction mod-
ule for saving computational resources. Furthermore, [Shi+20b] utilizes the advantages
of point-based method[Qi+17b] and the voxel method[YML18], propose a new network
and achieves the state-of-the art. On the other hand, [Ali+19] projects the 3D point
cloud into three different planes and uses modified YOLO[Red+16; RF16; RF18] net-
works accomplishing the 3D object detection task. [Lan+19] choose another approach,
it first grids the point could using pillars and projects the pillars into 2D pseudo images,
transforming 3D object detection methods into 2D object detection methods. [WJ19]
turns to image for seeking power, it first detects objects on images and projects the
result to a 3D point cloud for regressing the true position of the object.
However, the literature and our experiment results show that the information loss does

Anchor-base
Anchors often refer to prior knowledge, which is first introduced in Faster-RCNN [Ren+16].
It exploits the general information of a set of objects and predicts the location, scale,
and type of the given object. Anchor-based methods could regress the location and scale
even if the object is occluded. And thus, lots of lidar-based object detection models are
anchor-based models [SWL19; Zen+18; Ali+19; YML18; Shi+20b; Lan+19; Shi+20a].
These networks learn a set of coeﬀicients and produce the prediction answer. But, the
anchor-based method drawbacks are obvious. Every anchor needs to be calculated and
thus would greater the time consumption. Also, another anchor-based methods’ draw-
back is to identify obstacles. Obstacles vary from type to type, e.g. stones, animals,
walls, etc. Although these objects can be named obstacles in general, their different
shapes make the design of anchors a problem.

3.2.5
Post Process
In our project, we try to reduce the usage of the NMS method for saving computation
resources and time. Because the NMS method needs to calculate IoU among all bundle
boxes and sort these boxes based on an index, and because computation resources are
stressed in our vehicle-based chips, we need to find a new method for saving these re-
sources. In our project, we modified the work of [Ge+20a] for saving time.
Filtering bundle boxes using max pooling operations are easy for implementation and
saving computing resources. However, the pooling would blur the exact location of data
in feature maps. More specifically, the shape of the feature map is changed if we ap-
ply the max-pooling method directly without padding. But if we pad the feature map,
the maximum scored prediction’s exact location cannot be found. We here present our
method, named: argmax.
Just like the name, the argmax takes the feature as input and produces the local max-
imum scored predictions location on the feature map. It avoids the blur issue when we
apply the max-pooling method directly and achieves the same eﬀiciency. The pseudo-
code of the argmax method can be found here.

Implementation Performance
We deploy our model on a Xavier for evaluating the performance of the network. We
maintain the same software environment but change the hardware from our server to the
Xavier system. In Table 4.6, our result demonstrates that the performance of hardware
has a direct influence on the performance of the model. We think this performance gap
can be explained by two reasons: 1, the power of the Xavier is lower than the GPU on
server. 2, Our current use toolbox cannot perform Xavier best.
Indeed, the Xavier is designed for autonomous systems.
It is designed as a mobile
device and needs to fit the requirements of low energy consumption. Therefore, the
Xavier trades off with computing power and electric consumption. It comprises some
computing performance for meeting the energy requirement. This makes the inference
processing slower than we experiment on the server.
Our currently use toolbox cannot squeeze the power of Xavier is another reason. Mmde-
tection3D is a powerful, easy-to-use, and versatile toolbox in the field of research. Re-
searchers feel convenient developing themselves models and conduct experiments. How-
ever, the toolbox is not designed for mobile devices, it wraps too many features and
is computationally intensive. For servers, the price for using the box is affordable, but
might be too expensive for the Xavier. Besides, most people choose to translate their
models into TensorRT when implemented for gaining a higher processing speed. Ten-
sorRT is a framework developed by Nvidia for model inferencing on their machines. The
mmdetection3d does not support export models as the Onnx framework or the TensorRT
framework. As a result, we cannot squeeze the power of Xavier, and this is a future work
to be done.

5.1
Conclusion
In this thesis, we propose a data set for object detection in mine and propose an anchor-
free lidar object detection method. After many experiments, our model demonstrates
this performance and beats the competitor, Point Pillar, in both accuracy and speed.
In addition, because our Center-Based Detection neural network does not require prior
knowledge, we can quickly implement our model from a dataset to a new dataset.
We first have a brief introduction to different methods for 3D representations of the
point cloud, and their processing. Based on our literature study, we investigate differ-
ent point cloud representations and how they can be processed. We find that the voxel
methods suit us the best since others are suffering from different issues: computationally
expensive, discarded to points’ distances.
In the preliminary chapter, we check two different voxel methods. Analyzing how they
work and what are their potential influences. We find that polar coordinates are natural
for lidar information representation but blend long ranges’ information. The Cartesian
coordinates, on the other hand, do not suffer the issue but are dragged by the vacuum
problem. We then have a look at three major datasets for autonomous driving, and
summary the features of these datasets. After that, we move to the scenario in mine,
finding the mine is very different from the city. Finally, we study the model of Point
Pillars and think the anchor-based method is not suitable for embedded systems.
We travel to the testing area for collecting a new dataset.
We design four different
scenarios for collecting data.
In our model, we have different feature extraction ap-
proaches. In 3D feature extraction, we adapt sparse convolution for saving computation
resources.
For 2D convolution, we leverage the spatial attention method for solving
the shading problem introduced by the large objects. We propose our Center-Based
detection method and multi-head detection strategy for achieving large-scale detection
problems with anchors.
Throughout experiments, we analyzed our proposed dataset. We perform experiments
on both the KITTI dataset and the proposed dataset. The results demonstrate Center-
Based method competes with the Point-Pillar and achieves better in our method. The
deformable convolution focus on those irregular objects; the detector head produces accu-

tasks; Our proposed alternative NMS method pushes the speed inference further.
5.2
Limitations
There are limitations to our work. For each limitation, we would give our suggestions:
• Center-Based Detection has shown the compatible performance to Point Pillars.
However, it cannot beat the current SOTA models. We believe the problem is the
center’s height regression. Since we only use simple brute force as our solution, we
believe there is another way for improving. A solution could be the new feature
extraction method.
Our experiments show that 3D convolution has a greater
capacity for extracting height information. We encourage readers to try to find a
better method for extracting 3D information.
• In this project, we collect the dataset by emulating the daily scenarios in mine.
However, we know that emulating can never be the truth. We encourage readers
to collect the dataset in real scenarios.
We acknowledge these limitations and we hope readers can solve them in the future.
