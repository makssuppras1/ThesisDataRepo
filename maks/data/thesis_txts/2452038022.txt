Quantiﬁcation of emissions and losses of chemicals during plastic waste recycling
by Luca LONARDO
The global plastic consumption continues to increase, and plastic recycling is highlighted as crucial to
promoting Circular Economy. Plastic is synthetic or semi-synthetic material made with one or more
polymers usually mixed with chemical additives (such as plasticizers, ﬂame retardants, pigments
etc.) to enhance the properties of the ﬁnal product. Thus, if recycling processes are not designed
to remove chemical additives, potentially hazardous substances used as chemical additives might
increase or decrease in concentrations during mechanical recycling. Hence, the need for collecting
quantitative data about the fate of chemical additives during mechanical plastic recycling.
The overall aim of this study is to quantify the emissions and losses of chemicals during mechan-
ical plastic recycling.
The following speciﬁc objectives are included: (i) selection of polymers to investigate; (ii) selection
of chemical additives to investigate; (iii) construction and implementation of a lab scale experimen-
tal recycling process; (iv) quantiﬁcation of chemical additives during recycling; (v) data analysis to
evaluate the fate of chemicals during recycling.
Samples of six selected polymers, including three virgin plastic materials (PP, PS and PET) and
three recycled plastic materials (HDPE, PP and PS) were analysed for the fate of nine selected chemi-
cal additives, including ﬁve metals (Ti, Cr, Cd, Sb and Pb), two brominated ﬂame retardants (TBBPA
and HBCD) and two phthalates (DiBP and DEHP), during mechanical plastic recycling.
The results have shown that the same chemical additive can have different behaviour in different
polymers, but also within the same type of polymer. Exception is given to few metals: Cr, which
slightly increased its content during plastic recycling in all the polymer types in which was quanti-
ﬁed; Cd and Pb, which kept almost constant concentrations during recycling in all the polymer types
in which were quantiﬁed; Ti, which exhibited the same drop in concentration during the last recy-
cling cycle of the recycled samples of HDPE and PP. Indeed in recycled PP, Ti content passed from
7711.74 µ/g to 6916.29 µ/g.
Furthermore, also the type of polymer might inﬂuence the fate of chemical additives. Indeed,
recycled PS showed an increase in concentrations of both analysed phthalates, both BFRs and also
for Ti and Cr.
However, although some small variations in concentrations, chemical additives during mechan-
ical plastic recycling does not exhibited signiﬁcant emissions or losses. Moreover, the quantiﬁcation
of chemical additives in this study underlined the fact that the samples of recycled plastic contain
higher concentrations of chemical additives than virgin plastic materials.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Plastic material is one of the major innovations of the past century. Since the introduction in the
marketplace in 1950, plastic has been adopted for many applications that previously relied only on
traditional materials such as steel, wood and glass.
Due to the wide use of plastic, global plastic production has increased constantly through the
years. Figure 1.1 shows the production volume in million metric tons of plastic worldwide and in
Europe from 1950 to 2017.
FIGURE 1.1: Global and European plastic production from 1950 to 2017 (Statista, 2018).
In 2017 the world plastic production had risen to 348 million metric tons. Europe alone produced
64.4 million metric tons, while China is the largest producers of plastic in the world with almost 90
million metric tons per year (Statista, 2018). Furthermore, according to different forecasts (Grand-
viewresearch, 2019; Geyer, Jambeck, and Law, 2017), plastic production will keep increasing in the
next years.
Plastic is synthetic or semi-synthetic material made with one or more polymers (such as polypropy-
lene (PP),polyethylene (PE), polyvinyl chloride (PVC), polystyrene (PS) etc.) usually mixed with
some chemical additives (such as plasticizers, ﬂame retardants, pigments etc.). Chemical additives
are required to improve the properties of the ﬁnite product,for instance, some polymers such as PVC
cannot be used without chemical additives.
The wide use of plastic generates a large amount of plastic waste. The unsustainable disposal of
this waste material is the cause of environmental concern. At the end-of-life, if collected, plastic may
ends up in landﬁll or undergo through plastic recycling or energy recovery.
Lately, to promote Circular Economy, instead of accumulate plastic waste in landﬁll alternative
solution are preferred. In Europe, indeed, in the last 10 years recycling has increased by almost 80%
==PAGE:15==
Chapter 1. Introduction
2
while landﬁll decreased by 43% (“Plastics - The fact 2017” 2018). Moreover, for the ﬁrst time in 2016
plastic recycling has overcome landﬁll (“Plastics - The fact 2017” 2018). The decreasing of landﬁll is
also due to the increasing of energy recovery. Indeed, only in Europe energy recovery has increased
by 61% in the last decade (“Plastics - The fact 2017” 2018).
Despite the increasing interest in the whole recycling sector in the last years, plastic recycling is
still a challenge. Indeed, plastic recycling might be more complex than what it seems to be, especially
due to the complexity of plastic waste.
Plastic waste is a mix of different polymers, such as PP, PE, PVC, different chemical additives and
others impurities such as food remains, metals, liquids etc. Thus, recycling processes will include a
sorting step to differentiate the different polymers and a cleaning steps to remove impurities. While
cleaning steps are efﬁcient to remove a lot of impurities, they are not design to remove chemical
additives. Thus, the concentration of chemicals during plastic recycling either remains the same or
will increase or decrease by increasing the number of times that the plastic material is recycled. The
increase in concentrations of chemical additives during recycling could be due to different factors
such as either the need for more chemical additives at each recycling cycle or as a consequence of the
degradation of the polymer, which will reduce the polymer size and thereby increase the concentra-
tion of chemical additives. On the other hand, the decrease in concentrations could be caused by the
migration or release of chemical additives into the environment during the recycling process.
The unknown fate of chemical additives during plastic recycling may lead to lower quality of re-
cycled plastic, risking to introduce unwanted and potentially toxic chemicals in our product. There-
fore, a greater understanding of the fate of chemicals during recycling may increase the recycling
rates and lead to safer plastic recycling.
Recent studies regarding the fate of chemicals during plastic recycling have tried to highlight the
problem. In particular there have been studies regarding the fate of: metals (Eriksen et al., 2018;
G¨otze et al., 2016); phthalates (Pivnenko et al., 2016); brominated ﬂame retardants (BFRs) (Pivnenko
et al., 2017). Also, a more generic overview of the problem is given in Hahladakis et al., 2017. A
more detailed description of the results of this works is discussed in section 2.4. Furthermore, a
master thesis on the same topic has already been developed in the department of DTU Environment
(Norden, 2019).
However, even though there are some available studies in the literature, there is still a lack of
knowledge about the behaviour of chemical additives in plastic during recycling, indeed, important
quantitative information is still missing.
The overall aim of this study is to quantify the emissions and losses of chemicals during mechan-
ical plastic recycling.
The following speciﬁc objectives are included: (i) selection of polymers to investigate; (ii) selection
of chemical additives to investigate; (iii) construction and implementation of a lab scale experimen-
tal recycling process; (iv) quantiﬁcation of chemical additives during recycling; (v) data analysis to
evaluate the fate of chemicals during recycling.
The analyzed polymers in this project are three virgin materials (PP, PS and Polyethylene tereph-
thalate (PET)) and three recycled materials (PP, PS and High-density polyethylene (HDPE)).
Focus is then placed on selected potentially hazardous chemical additives:
• Metals: Titanium (Ti), Chromium (Cr), Cadmium (Cd), Antimony (Sb) and Lead (Pb);
• BFRs: Tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA) and Hexabromocyclododecane (HBCD);
• Phthalate: Di-iso-butyl phthalate (DiBP) and Bis(2-ethylhexyl)phthalate (DEHP).
Additionally, the results of this work might be relevant to stakeholders interested in improving
the circular economy. The results could be just the beginning of a collection of more data in order to
prove or deny a common fate of chemical additives during mechanical plastic recycling, with a view
to ultimately improve the quality of recycled plastic and reduce the use of feedstock material.

Theoretical background
2.1
Plastic material
Plastic is a low-cost, lightweight, primarily fossil based material that is outstandingly durable and
easy to manufacture even into complex shapes. Indeed, the word plastic comes from the Greek plas-
tikos, meaning shapeable. Plastic material is mainly composed of polymers. Polymers are very large
molecules (macromolecules) that are built up of smaller units called monomers, the word polymer
also comes from Greek meaning many parts (Painter and Coleman, 2009).
Polymers in turn, can be divided in two main categories (Painter and Coleman, 2009):
• homopolymers, polymers containing only a single type of repeat unit (eg. PVC)
• copolymers, polymers containing two or more types of repeat units (eg. PP copolymer)
FIGURE 2.1: Different types of polymers: 1) homopolymer 2) copolymer.
Figure 2.1 show two different polymer chains, the homopolymer one is made only with monomers
of type A, while the copolymer chain is made of monomers of type A and B. The arrangement of the
different monomers along the chain determine if the copolymer will be of the alternating, random,
block or graft type.
Polymer can be either natural such as Cellulose, a long chain macromelucule composed of carbon,
hydrogen and oxygen or synthetic such us PET, also composed of the same atoms (Painter and Cole-
man, 2009). The difference in the two molecules is given by the different arrangement of the atoms
along the polymer chains. The word plastic usually refers to synthetic or semi-synthetic polymer.
In general, plastic is the material (polymers plus chemical additives) while polymer is the molecule
(Painter and Coleman, 2009).
Another important classiﬁcation is based on the polymer structures (Painter and Coleman, 2009):
• crystalline polymers have a highly ordered molecular structure, these polymers are strong and
rigid with a deﬁned melting point;
• amorphous polymers have a random molecular structure, they are usually ﬂexible and elastic.
Amorphous polymer do not have a deﬁned melting point, instead, they gradually get soften as
temperature rises.
==PAGE:17==
Chapter 2. Theoretical background
4
Usually, polymers do not have 100% crystalline structure, thus, polymers with more crystalline re-
gions are called semicrystalline polymers, while polymers that have no crystalline regions are called
amorphous polymers (Painter and Coleman, 2009). The two types of structure can also be recognised
by their colour. Crystalline structures tend to reﬂect light, thus the polymer will appear opaque. In-
stead, amorphous polymers are transparent. As the percentage of crystallinity increases the polymer
becomes progressively less clear (Painter and Coleman, 2009).
The chemistry involved in making polymers is called polymerization, it involves different chem-
ical mechanisms that depend on the nature of the monomers that will be strung together. Most
polymers fall into one of the two broad polymerization categories: condensation or addition(Painter
and Coleman, 2009).
Condensation, so called because it involves the splitting out of a molecule of water, it is also
known as step-growth polymerization. The reaction involves monomers with functional groups such
as amines and acids. Figure 2.2 shows the polycondensation reaction that involves the production of
PET.
FIGURE 2.2: Polycondensation reaction in the production of PET (Wikipedia, 2019a).
Addition, also known as chain polymerization, involves monomers with double bonds, such as
ethylene or propene. Figure 2.3 shows polypropylene synthesis by chain polymerization.
FIGURE 2.3: Addition reaction in the production of PP (Denniston et al., 2017).
Both processes, most of the times use catalysis processes to increase the rate of polymerization by
adding substances known as a catalyst, such as the use of antimony (Sb) in PET production.
A further classiﬁcation of plastic material is usually made among thermoplastics and thermosets
(Rodriguez, 2018).
• Thermoplastics are linear and branched chains, with very limited cross-linking, if any. The main
characteristic of this type of plastic is that is capable of being molded and remolded repeatedly
since does not undergo chemical change in its composition during manufacturing. (Rodriguez,
2018). Thermoplastics are ductile material that get soften when heated. PP, HDPE, PS, PET and
PVC are all example of thermoplastics.
• Thermosets are network polymers with heavily cross-linked chains. During thermosetting man-
ufacturing a chemical reaction occurs that is irreversible, thus, this plastic can melt and take
shape only once (Rodriguez, 2018). Thermosets are hard and brittle materials that do not get
soften when heated up, but instead they decompose. Example of thermosets are polyurethane,
epoxies and polyester resin.

2.1.1
Processing and fabrication of plastic materials
Once the plastic is made, it is usually sold in the form of pellets to the manufacturing industries. The
pellets, also referred as raw materials, are then processed into usable shapes through fabrication or
conversion (Rodriguez, 2018). An example of fabrication could be the conversion of PET pellets into
plastic bottles. Fabrication can be divided in 4 main steps (Rodriguez, 2018):
==PAGE:19==
Chapter 2. Theoretical background
6
• Compounding: is the ﬁrst step in most plastic fabrication, it consists of mixing together dif-
ferent raw materials (such as pellets of PP, PS and PE) in proportions according to the speciﬁc
recipe that is followed (Rodriguez, 2018). At this stage more chemical additives can be added
if required;
• Forming: it consist in shaping the compounded plastic into various forms. Forming involves
different steps such as melting, shaping, and solidifying (Rodriguez, 2018). Forming can be car-
ried out with different technologies such as extrusion, injection molding, blow molding, com-
pression molding, foaming, thermoforming, cold molding, rotational molding etc. (Rodriguez,
2018). The two most commonly used procedures are extrusion and injection moulding, due to
the ﬂexibility and easy to use of the processes;
• Fibre reinforcement: is the process of introducing the reinforcements into the polymer matrix.
Polymer-matrix composites are for example plastic-based materials where a continuous poly-
meric phase (the matrix) is bound together with another phase (the reinforcement) of short or
continuous ﬁbers (see section 2.2.4) (Rodriguez, 2018).
• Finishing: is the last step of fabrication. It consist in manufacturing processes such as joining,
machining and coating (Rodriguez, 2018). Examples of joining are welding or gluing. Machin-
ing has the task of reﬁning the plastic product by drilling, sawing, turning, sanding, and other
operations. Since colour may be added in the form of pigment or dye in plastic (see section
2.2.2 below), surface coating here referred to application that have protective or decorative pur-
poses (Rodriguez, 2018). For example in the automotive industries, the bumpers produced by
injection molding can be painted to match the rest of the car body (Rodriguez, 2018).
Compounding, fabrication and ﬁnishing are used in all types of plastic production, while ﬁbre rein-
forcement is used only in polymer composites manufacturing.
2.2
Chemical additives in plastic
In many plastic materials the polymer is only one of the constituents. Indeed, during manufactur-
ing, usually before thermally forming the melted plastic into pellets (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017),
the polymer is mixed with chemical additives to improve the properties of the ﬁnite product, or to
improve the production process itself, such as the use of chemical catalysts in polymer production.
Chemical additives are classiﬁed according to the functional properties that they give or enhance
in the ﬁnal plastic product. Among these additives are plasticisers, antioxidants, ﬂame retardants,
ﬁllers, colorants, stabilisers, lubricants, etc. (Hahladakis et al., 2017; Rodriguez, 2018).
Some of the chemical additives used in plastic, if released, are cause of potential environmental
and human health risks. Although pure polymer is non-toxic for humans, since cannot digest or
absorb it, some chemical additives are hazardous substances that can be easily absorbed and damage
the human body (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017).
Related to plastic, chemical additives are usually divided into four main categories (Hahladakis
et al., 2017; Hansen et al., 2013):
• Functional additives (plasticisers, ﬂame retardants, stabilisers, antistatic agents, slip agents, cur-
ing agents, blowing agents, biocides, etc.)
• Colorants (soluble azocolorants, pigments etc.)
• Fillers (mica, talc, kaolin, barium sulphate, clay, calcium carbonate)
• Reinforcements (glass ﬁbres, carbon ﬁbres etc.).
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 2. Theoretical background
7
Besides these four main categories, also the residual or unreacted monomers and oligomers from the poly-
merization process are considered as chemical additives in many literature.
The majority of the chemical additives are not chemically bound to the plastic polymer, exception
is given to metals and some ﬂame retardants that are instead solidly bond to the polymer and are
becoming part of the polymer chain (Hahladakis et al., 2017) .As a general rule, the chemical addi-
tives that are not chemically bond to the polymer will migrate more easily through the plastic, as
consequence the release into the environment is more probable (see section 2.4).
Next sections will give a more detailed description of the different types of chemical additives.
2.2.1
Functional additives
Functional additives are so called because they have the purpose to modify the plastic properties by
giving desirable and beneﬁcial functions. Table 2.1 displays a description of the most common func-
tional additives in plastics, showing the typical amount in which they are used, the main substances
and also gives some extra comments.
A more detailed description of the most used functional additives follows.
• Plasticisers:
typically used in plastic to reduce the modulus, increase the ﬂexibility and soften the material,
plasticisers are low-molecular-weight elements that are used in high weight fractions (Andrady
and Rajapakse, 2017). Mainly used in PVC, the concentration of plasticisers in some products
can rise up to the 70% of the weight fractions of the ﬁnite product (Andrady and Rajapakse,
2017). The most used plasticizers are phthalates, these chemical additives, if ingested, tend to
bio-accumulate in living organisms (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017). The main concern is re-
lated to small animals where the body concentrations reached via ingestion can become very
high, especially in marine environment where ﬁshes can get exposed to phthalates by ingest-
ing small plastic debris (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017). In humans the exposure to phthalates,
taken from contaminated food or water, can compromise the male reproductive function (An-
drady and Rajapakse, 2017). Nowadays USA, European Union, and Japan have restrictions on
the use of some phthalates, especially in toys manufacturing (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017).
• Flame retardants:
are mixed with the polymer to control and reduce ﬂame propagation. Flame retardants are
used in relatively high-volume concentration (usually 10-20 %w/w), they are indispensable
in construction, transportation, textile industries and also in common household plastic (e.g.
TV, computer, etc.) (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017). Flame retardants can be either reactive
(chemically bound in the material), such as TBBPA, or additive (not chemically bound in the
material), such us the majority of remaining BFRs (Hansen et al., 2013).
• Stabilisers (antioxidants, UV stabilisers, heat stabilisers):
stabilisers are added to plastic to reduce the effects of aging, they are typically used in low
concentration (0.1 - 3 %w/w) (Rodriguez, 2018; Hahladakis et al., 2017). Indeed, when poly-
mers are exposed to heat, radiation, or mechanical shear, reactive free radicals can be gener-
ated and cause degradation of the polymer (Hahladakis et al., 2017). Antioxidants are used
to prevent oxidation of the polymer when it is exposed to ultraviolet (UV) light or processed
at high temperatures (Hahladakis et al., 2017). UV stabilisers are added only to plastic made
for outdoor applications (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017). Heat stabilisers are used to prevent
decomposition of the polymer during manufacturing where temperatures can rise well above
180 [oC], temperatures that some polymer cannot stand (Hahladakis et al., 2017). In PVC the
addition of heat stabilisers is indispensable in order to reduce dehydrohalogenation (loss of
hydrogen chloride [HCl]) that can occur when the polymer is expose to manufacturing tem-
peratures (Rodriguez, 2018). Usually, stabilizers are macro-molecular, thus they do not migrate
==PAGE:21==

easily. Nevertheless, some chemical additives used as stabilisers are hazardous substances that
can be dangerous even in low concentrations, especially if the application involves continu-

used as a UV stabilizer, are carcinogenic substances (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017).

2.2.3
Fillers
Fillers are principally used to improve mechanical properties of the plastic, and also to lower the
overall cost of the material by increase the bulk of the ﬁnal plastic (Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017).
The effect of the ﬁllers is to improve properties such as creep and strength, also acoustic, electrical
and thermal properties are positively impacted by these chemical additives (Andrady and Rajapakse,
2017). Fillers are used in a relatively high amount in plastics, the typical amount is up to 50 %
of wight fraction (Hahladakis et al., 2017; Hansen et al., 2013). Usually, chemical additives used as
ﬁllers are compounds with low-molecular-weight which can be better disperse in the polymer matrix
(Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017). The most common used substances are: calcium carbonate, talk,
clay, zinc oxide, glimmer, metal powder, wood powder, asbest, barium sulphate, glass microspheres,
silicious earth. In normal condition, ﬁllers do not migrate easily and they are also insoluble in water
(Andrady and Rajapakse, 2017).
2.2.4
Reinforcements
Reinforcements, as the name suggests, are used to reinforce the mechanical properties of plastic such
as tensile strength, ﬂexural strength and stiffness of the material (Rodriguez, 2018). The reinforce-
ment effect is obtained by introducing ﬁbre-based materials into the polymer matrix. Since this pro-
cess involves the manufacturing of plastic composite materials, even though reinforcements give
desirable functions such as functional additives, they are considered separately by the latter. The
most used reinforcements are glass ﬁbers, carbon ﬁbers and aramide ﬁbers (Hansen et al., 2013). The
typical amount of reinforcements in plastic is 15 to 30 % w/w, the highest concentrations are achieved
by glass ﬁbers, due to the high density of glass (Hahladakis et al., 2017; Hansen et al., 2013)
2.2.5
Residual or unreacted monomers and oligomers
As already explained at the beginning of chapter 2, polymers are build up of small molecules called
monomers. During the polymerization process some monomers do not take part at the reaction,
these residual or unreacted monomers might then be present in the ﬁnal plastic (Hahladakis et al.,
2017). They have a typical concentrations of 0-2 % w/w (Hansen et al., 2013). Even though they
are not added in plastic on purpose, some literature consider the residual or unreacted monomers
and oligomers as chemical additives. These residues are very low molecular weight elements, thus
tend to migrate quite easily (Hahladakis et al., 2017). If the concentration of residual or unreacted
monomers and oligomers reach a certain limit potential human health risks could arise (Hahladakis
et al., 2017). Examples of residual or unreacted monomers and oligomers are: residual of styrene in
PS; residual of bisphenol A (BPA) in polycarbonate, epoxy resin and unsaturated polyester resin; the
unreacted monomers of vinyl chloride in PVC; unreacted acrylamide in polyacrylamide; oligomers
of formaldehyde; residual of nonylphenol in phenol/formaldehyde reins and in epoxy resins; un-
reacted octylphenol in phenol/formaldehyde reins; 4-(1,1,3,3-tetramethylbutyl)phenol used in poly-
mers preparetions (Hansen et al., 2013).

2.3.1
Plastic recycling
In the recycling sector is common to divide the plastic waste in two main categories:
– post-industrial (PI) waste, also called preconsumer waste, which is generated during manufac-
turing and never makes to the costumer, is usually clean and with known composition;
– post-consumer (PC) waste which is generated at the end-of-life (EoL) of the product, is usually
a mix of different plastic of unknown composition and it can be potentially contaminated by
organic impurity (such as food remains) or non-polymer inorganic impurity (such as metals),
which makes recycling a complex task (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017).
Depending on the country, PC plastic waste is collected separately or not from all the other waste,
while PI plastic waste often is processed in-company or is dealt business-to-business (Ragaert, Delva,
and Geemb, 2017).
Recyclable materials are identiﬁed through Resin identiﬁcation code (RIC), also known as SPI
code, from the Society of the Plastics Industry (SPI) which established the classiﬁcation system in
1988 (ASTM International, 2018). RIC code is a symbol appearing on plastic products which identify
the type of polymer out of which the product is made (ASTM International, 2018). It is usually a
triangle type symbol with a number and, not always, the polymer name. Each type of recyclable
polymer has an associated RIC number (see Appendix A).
Plastic materials can be recycled through two different processes, thermoplastic can be recycled
either mechanically or chemically, while thermoset plastic, since can not be re-melted, can be recycled
only chemically.
▷Mechanical recycling
Mechanical recycling is the most used and preferred recycling method, is cost effective and easy to
perform compared to chemical recycling (Rao, Sultana, and Kota, 2017). Usually mechanical recy-
cling involves the following steps (Rao, Sultana, and Kota, 2017; Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017):
• Collection: the plastic waste is collected from the consumer;
• Separation and sorting: plastic waste is separated based on shape, colour or polymer types;
• Washing: this step is required to remove organic or inorganic impurity;
• Drying: the washed plastic waste is dried before entering in the next step;
• Grinding: the wasted plastic product is reduced to ﬂakes;
• Compounding: ﬂakes are mixed according to a speciﬁc request;
• Forming: waste plastic ﬂakes are formed through extrusion, injection moulding or other tech-
niques (see section 2.1);
• Pelletising: the material is reduced into granulate/pellet.
==PAGE:25==

According to the origin of the plastic waste, these steps can occur in a different order, more then
one time or not at all (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017). For example, PI plastic waste does not
require collection if is in-company recycling, or sorting since usually is a mono-stream material with
well-known composition, or washing since is usually a clean material (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb,
2017). On the other hand, PC plastic waste requires collection from the end user, sorting (e.g. selection
of PET from the whole plastic waste) and washing to remove the impurities (Ragaert, Delva, and
Geemb, 2017).
The most used forming technique in mechanical recycling is extrusion, since it make easy to
shape the recycled plastic into forms that can be easily pelletised to be then used to manufacture
other products. Figure 2.7 shows the longitudinal section of a screw extruder during the forming of
thermoplastic polymers.
FIGURE 2.7: Longitudinal section of a screw extruder of thermoplastic polymers (Rodriguez,

The process is relatively simple, plastic pellets/ﬂakes are placed into the hopper which is con-
nected to the barrel of the extruder, here the pellets/ﬂakes are gradually melted by a combination of
mechanical energy generated by a turning screw and by heaters placed along the barrel (Rodriguez,
2018). The working temperatures depend on the type of plastic, they usually are in a range from
180oC to 350oC. The molten plastic is then forced through a die which forms a continuous extrudate
shape with a constant cross-section similar to the one of the die oriﬁce (Rodriguez, 2018).
The second most used method to form plastic during recycling is the injection molding. Figure

In the section on the left, plastic pellets/ﬂakes are fed into the hopper and melt, the process
is similar to the one described for the extruder. In the central section, when a sufﬁcient amount
of plastic is melted, the screw moves forward and inject the molten plastic through a gate into the

While extrusion is used to shape continuous plastic proﬁles, injection molding is used to shape
plastic pieces of small medium size.
Although it is widely used, mechanical recycling can have different challenges. First, the recy-
clable plastic waste may be contaminated by non plastics materials or by different polymers, making
sorting a difﬁcult task. Moreover, even the same polymer type has variations with different molecu-
lar weights such as LDPE used to produce plastic bags and HDPE used for manufacture bottle caps
(Rodriguez, 2018). Sorting is, indeed, an important step in mechanical recycling, since different type
of polymers are complicated to be processed together for two main reasons. Firstly, different poly-
mers have different melting points and processing temperatures, mixed plastic waste will require to
be processed at the temperature of the polymer with the highest melting point, this will overheats
and degrades the polymer with the lower melting point (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017). The
second reason is that some polymers are immiscible, meaning than after melting, during solidiﬁca-
tion they will show phase separation, which reduces the ﬁnal properties of the material (Ragaert,
Delva, and Geemb, 2017). However, some immiscible polymers can also be processed together to
form polymer blends (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017), such as polystyrene/poly-butadiene and
polycarbonate/acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (Khan, Chohan, and Mazumder, 2018). Usually, plas-
tic waste that cannot be separated by polymer type is mostly used to produce plastic “lumber” for
ﬂooring (Rodriguez, 2018).
A further issue is the mixing together of plastic with different colours and different chemical
additives, which reduce the possible applications for the recycled material.
Beside these, the main problem to face during mechanical recycling is the degradation of the
polymers . Polymer degradation is an undesirable and irreversible change in the polymer proper-
ties, such as tensile strength, color, shape, etc., due to several different factors such as heat, light
or chemicals (Hawkins, 1984). During mechanical recycling two types of polymer degradation can
occur: thermal-mechanical degradation, it takes place during reprocessing the plastic, due to heat and
shear; lifetime degradation, due to photo oxidation process caused by combination of heat, light, oxy-
gen, moisture, radiation and stress during the lifetime of the plastic (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb,
2017). Even if the ﬁrst one happens in the absence of oxygen while the second one is boosted by oxy-
gen, both types of degradation ends up with very similar structural changes in the polymer, such us
chain scission and chain branching (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017). Polymer degradation usually
involves reductions in the molecular weight of the polymer (Speight, 2011). The effects of thermal-
mechanical degradation can be reduced by adding chemical additives, such as heat stabilisers and
ﬁllers (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017).
For all the foregoing reasons, recycled plastic has certain disadvantages in comparison to virgin
plastic. Thus, most recycled thermoplastics are used in the manufacturing of less demanding prod-
ucts (Hahladakis et al., 2017; Rodriguez, 2018). Although, some plastic can be recycled to produce the
same type of product, for example plastic bottles in PET can be recycled to manufacture new plastic
bottles. When plastic waste is used as a substitute to virgin plastic in manufacturing, the process is
called primary recycling, while the use of recycled plastic in manufacture less demanding products is
called secondary recycling (Hahladakis et al., 2017)

▷Chemical recycling
Chemical recycling is a less used recycling technique, which has started to grow interest especially
because it has a high potential in circular economy improvement. The idea is to break down waste
plastic into small molecules to produce monomers or petrochemical feedstocks (Ragaert, Delva, and
Geemb, 2017). Chemical recycling can be used to reprocess either thermoplastics and thermosets, it
also allowed to recycle heterogeneous and contaminated plastic waste that is complicated to sort.
Chemical recycling includes different techniques such as: gasiﬁcation, pyrolysis, ﬂuid-catalysed
cracking and hydrocracking (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017). These techniques are used to re-
process plastic waste that can not be mechanically recycled such as mixed PP/PE/PS that cannot
be sorted. Usually, the mixed plastic is heated up at high temperatures (400 - 500 oC) in absence
of oxygen to break down the long polymer chains into small molecules. The thermal-chemical de-
composition yields into there fraction: gas, liquid ans solid residue (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb,
2017). During chemical recycling also chemical additives and other impurities are reduced into basic
chemical elements (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017).
Material such as PET, PUR and nylon can be recycled through chemical recycling. Indeed, PET
can be completely depolymerised into its monomers (i.e. trephthalic acid, dimethyl terephthalate,
bis(hydroxylethylene) terephthalate and ethylene glycol) (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017). More-
over, a European project DEMETO has the mission of improving chemical recycling on the largest
scale to close the life cycle of plastic, putting effort especially in PET chemical recycling techniques
(Polymer Comply Europe, 2017).
2.3.2
Energy Recovery
In developed countries, most of the plastic waste is used for energy recovery (Hahladakis et al., 2017).
Indeed, in Europe the 41.6% of plastics waste is destined for energy recovery (“Plastics - The fact
2017” 2018). Energy recovery is mostly used for power generation since the energy content of plastic
is comparable with heating oil (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017), respectively ∼40 MJ/kg and ∼43
MJ/kg (Wasielewski and Siudyga, 2013). However, the incineration of plastics cause environmental
concern, and it requires advanced pollution control measures (Ragaert, Delva, and Geemb, 2017).
Indeed, uncontrolled combustion of plastic waste is responsible of the emissions of hazardous sub-
stances, such as acid gases and dioxins (Hahladakis et al., 2017). Energy recovery also contributes
to global warming since involves combustion of fossil-based materials and related releases of CO2
(Hahladakis et al., 2017).
2.4
Fate of chemical additives in plastic waste recycling
Some substances used as chemical additives may be dangerous for human health and for the envi-
ronment. Brominated ﬂame retardants, phthalates, heavy metals, etc. are all hazardous substances
(Hansen et al., 2013). The main concern about the use of chemical additives in plastic is related to the
ability of these substances to migrate and be released into the environment. The risk is indeed due to
the eventual exposure of these hazardous substances.
Molecules can move through space by essentially three mechanisms of mass transfer, such as
diffusion due to a molar concentration gradient, migration due to an electric ﬁeld, and convection due
the kinematic velocity (Perez, 2016). In this work the mass transfer of chemical additives through
plastic will be generically deﬁned as migration. Chemical additives may migrate and be released
from plastic products into the environment in any phase of the plastic life (Hahladakis et al., 2017).
Most concern is related to those chemical additives that are not chemically bound to the polymer
chain and are easily able to migrate. Indeed, chemical additives in plastic can be present with two
types of bounds. Chemical additives can be chemically solid bounded or simply dissolved in the
==PAGE:28==
Chapter 2. Theoretical background
15
polymer matrix. Usually, metals are solidly bounded within the polymer while organic chemical
additives are just dissolved in the polymer matrix. (Hansen et al., 2013).
The potential release of chemical additives from plastic is related to many factors such as com-
position, size, and initial concentration of the chemical additives, it also gets affected by type and
thickness of the plastic and type of bound (Hahladakis et al., 2017). Among all these factors, the con-
centration of chemical additives in plastic is the one that mostly could get affected during recycling.
Since recycling involves melting down the plastic and exposing to relatively high temperature also
the chemical additives, it is relevant to understand what happens to chemical additives during the
process. Indeed, the concentration may remains the same or will increase or decrease. Unfortunately
there is a knowledge gap regarding the fate of chemical additives in plastic during recycling. How-
ever, in recent years, due to the strong concern about plastic pollution and the growing interest in
improving the circular economy, the fate of chemical additives in plastic has became subject of study.
Previous works have shown that samples of recycled plastic from household waste contain higher
concentrations of metals than virgin plastic (Pivnenko et al., 2015; Eriksen et al., 2018; Oyen et al.,
2015). Even though, in these studies, the concentrations did not exceed the legal limit for metals con-
tent in plastic, the elevated metal concentrations might effect the quality of the recycled plastic and
reduce its possible applications (Eriksen et al., 2018). Metals content in plastic waste was also subject
of study in G¨otze et al., 2016, where are also summarized the content of different metals in different
types of plastic waste according to the available values found in the literature.
Moreover, studies on ﬂame retardants have shown that these chemical additives can remain in
plastic materials after recycling (Pivnenko et al., 2017; Oyen et al., 2015). Flame retardant such as
Tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA) was found even in plastic subjected to four recycling process (Piv-
nenko et al., 2017). A study on the presence of phthalates in recycled plastics shows that these chem-
ical additives tend to accumulate in the recycled plastic, the work also argues that the source of the
plastic waste (PC or PI) is to be taken in consideration to understand the fate of chemical additives in
plastic recycling (Pivnenko et al., 2016).
Furthermore, since recycling involves the use of elevated temperatures, at which chemical addi-
tives might be released into the environment, causing possible toxic workplace exposure (Hahladakis
et al., 2017), understanding the behaviour of chemical additives during recycling will also help to
have a more safe workplace. Thus, to improve the quality of recycled plastic and increase the recy-
cling rate, the fate of chemical additives during recycling need further research.

FTIR spectroscopy
Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) was carried out in order to ensure that the nature
of polymer materials was indeed the one shown on the TDSs. Tests were performed at DTU envi-
ronment lab, on an Agilent 4300 Handheld FTIR. Each spectrum was obtained with a resolution of 8
cm−1. After cleaning the FTIR lens, the plastic pellet was put on the machine and the analysis was
performed.
The machine automatically takes the spectrum of the plastic sample and it compares with all the
spectra available in its library. In the results, the spectrum of the plastic sample is compared with the
one that better match with the currently analyzed sample. Furthermore, the machine gives a short list
of all possible spectra that can match, which are associated with a quality value that goes from 0, the
lowest probability that the two spectra are of the same type of material, to 1, the highest probability
that the two spectra are of the same type of material. Quality values were approximated to the third
signiﬁcant digit.

PETv is the only material, among the analysed one in this work, that requires a slightly different pro-

and Giles, 2013). PETv was dried at 150 oC for 6 hours in the dryer silo, as speciﬁed in the TDS (see
appendix E).
The proﬁle temperatures for mechanically recycling PETv was set according to the TDS (appendix
E) T=[250-260-270-280] oC. Initially 0.5 kg of PETv were used to clean the extruder. When the PETv

be recycled again. The collection of the samples for the chemical analysis was done after the drying.
Also PETv recycling simulation ended by feeding 0.5 kg of PPp,v into the hopper and then extruded
to remove all the PETv leftover.
During recycling, PETv showed a change in the colour, from a white material it became com-

talline or amorphous at room temperature (see section 2.1). Usually, PET is sold in crystalline struc-

to high temperature and relatively fast cooling, PET structure change from crystalline to amorphous,
and the material become transparent. By putting the PET in the dryer silo at 150 oC for 6h, the mate-
rial has the time and the temperature to reorder its structure and become crystalline, assuming again
the white colour. Figure 3.4 shows the four PETv samples, the second picture from the left shows

3.4
XRF spectroscopy
X-ray ﬂuorescence (XRF) was carried out on the initial plastic samples (i.e. PPv 1.0, PSv 1.0, PETv
1.0, HDPEr 1.0, PPr 1.0, PSr 1.0) to have an idea about their content of inorganic chemicals. XRF is
a non-destructive analytical technique rapid to perform that can be used to determine the chemical
composition of a wide variety of material, such as polymers, rocks, minerals and liquids (Beckhoff
et al., 2006)
XRF spectroscopy was performed at DTU Mek lab, on a Oxford Instruments Handheld XRF (X-MET
7500 Series) with a limit of detection of 1 parts per million (ppm). Plastic samples were placed into a
speciﬁc plastic container, pressed and put under the XRF lens. For each type of plastic, three different
measurements were carried out. The ﬁnal values obtained were an average of the three different mea-
surements. Furthermore, Oxford Instruments Handheld XRF showed each result with an uncertainty
value regarding the instrument. The ﬁnal combined uncertainty was calculated using summation in
quadrature, which is also known as root sum of the squares, between the instrument uncertainties
and the standard deviation on the sum of the three measurement according to De Chiffre et al., 2015.
The purpose of the analysis was to have an idea of the concentrations of some metals, thereby
allowing for better measurements later. In particular, was important to evaluate the possible presence
of Bromine (Br). Since Br is one of the main constituents of BRFs, its presence into the plastic samples
could be a sign of possible presence of BFRs (Guzzonato, Puype, and Harrad, 2016; Sharkey et al.,
2018). Indeed, chemical analysis to quantify the amount of BFRs are quite expensive and could have
been the possibility that the plastic samples had no BRFs.
Assuming a worst-case scenario, the all Br content has been considered to be part of the BRFs, and
by knowing the chemical formula of TBBPA and HBCD was possible to estimate their concentrations
in the plastic samples. The chemical formula of TBBPA is C15H12Br4O2, with a molar mass of 544.9
g/mol. The molecule contains 320.0 g/mol of Br, which is the 58.7 % of the whole molecule. The
estimated TBBPA concentration has been calculated dividing the Br content of the plastic sample
by the Br percentage content in the TBBPA molecule, all multiplied by one hundred. The same
calculation has been done also for HBCD, which chemical formula is C12H18Br6 and molar mass
641.7 g/mol. The molecule of HBCD contains 480.0 g/mol of Br, which accounts for the 74.8 % of the
whole molecule.

3.5.2
Sample preparation and chemicals extraction
The chemicals extraction from the plastic samples was carried out in DTU Environment lab, two
different methods were used, i.e. Acid digestion for metals extraction and Solvent extraction for organic
extraction. All plastic samples were subjected to both methods. The description of the two methods
follows.
1. Acid digestion was used for metals extraction. It consists in using concentrated acids to totally
destroy or dissolve organic as well as inorganic components, in order to bring into solution the
whole sample. Solution that can be subsequently subjected to analytical techniques to deter-
mine chemical concentrations. In this speciﬁc process, 0.1 g of plastic material were poured
into modiﬁed polytetraﬂuoroethylene (PTFE-TFM) vessels and mixed with 6 ml of Nitric acid
68% and 3 ml of Sulfuric acid 99.999%. The PTFE-TFM vessels were insert into speciﬁc ce-
ramic vessels, closed with pressure lids, and placed into the microwave carousel with a pres-
sure/temperature sensor. The carousel was put into a microwave reaction system for 120 min.
The used microwave was an Anton Paar Multiwave 3000. The ﬁrst 15 min were used to warming
up the samples and the last 15 min to cool down them, while the remaining 90 min were used
to hold the samples at 170 oC. When the process was done, the samples were poured into virgin
PP disposable digestion tubes and 1 ml of Hydroﬂouric acid 40% was added to the samples.
After 2h at room temperature, 6 ml of Boric acid 10% were also added. The sample tubes were
then left in a heating plate at 80 oC for 3h. Finally, the sample tubes were ﬁlled up until 50 ml
with Milli-Q water;
2. Solvent extraction was used for the extraction of organic chemical additives, i.e. Phthalates
and BFRs. This process does not involve the dissolution or destruction of the samples. For
this method, 1 g of plastic material was poured into PTFE-TFM vessels and mixed with 10 ml
of Methanol and 10 ml of n-Hexane. This type of microwave digestion required the use of
additional magnetic stirrers to support digestion of ﬂoating samples. Also in this process, the
PTFE-TFM vessels were insert into the ceramic vessels, closed with pressure lids, and placed
into the microwave carousel with a pressure/temperature sensor. The carousel was put into the
microwave reaction system for 70 min. For this process, 10 min were used for warming up, 10
min for cooling down and 50 min to hold the sample at 100 oC. The samples were then poured
into virgin PP disposable digestion tubes.

3.5.3
ICP-MS analysis
Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) is a widely used method to analyse the total
metal content in waste plastic, it is also used to analyse metal migration from plastic into different
kind of food (Eriksen et al., 2018).
ICP-MS analysis was performed at DTU Environment lab. As ﬁrst step, Yttrium and Scandium
were added directly into the samples as internal standards. The used instrument, an Agilent 7700x
ICP-MS, was equipped with a platinum-tipped skimmer and sample cones, a double-pass Scott spray
chamber operating at 2 oC and a Micromist spray chamber. Platinum-tipped cones were used instead
of standard Nickel-tipped cones since the latter cannot stand the high acid content and the amount
of organic material in the samples. All samples were analysed in helium collision mode. The ﬂow
of helium was 5 mL/min, while carrier gas ﬂow was 0.34 mL/min and dilution gas ﬂow was 0.59
mL/min. The depth of the sample cone was 8 mm. The metal concentration was calculated in µg/l
on 50 ml of sample. The conversion in µg/g was done according to the plastic weight used in each
sample during the acid digestion, see appendix H.
ICP-MS analysis was performed on all plastic samples, since for all the six different type of poly-
mers there were four samples, the total number of samples to be analysed was 24. For each analysed
samples, ICP-MS result will be the average of three values, since the same injection will be analysed
three times.
3.5.4
LC-QQQ analysis
High Performance Liquid Chromatography – Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometry (LC-QQQ) is the
method used in this work to quantify the two selected BFRs, i.e. Tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA)
and α-,β-, and γ-Hexabromocyclododecane (α-,β-, and γ-HBCD).
After the solvent extraction was ﬁnished, a mixture of deuterated 13C12 −α-HBCD and 13C12-
TBBPA was added as an internal standard to all samples.
LC-QQQ method consist in pumping a mobile phase under high pressure into a column con-
taining a stationary phase. Here the different compounds present in the sample mix with either the
stationary or mobile phase. With time, the different compounds ﬂow out of the column and can be
quantiﬁed with mass spectrometry (MS). Chromatographic separation was achieved on a 3.0 x 100
mm, 1.8 micron reversed phase column (Zorbax Eclipse Plus C18 HD, Agilent Technologies) with 5
millimolar (mM) ammonium formate as aqueous phase (A) and 10:90 Water:Methanol with 5 mM
ammonium formate as mobile phase (B). Detection was achieved in Multiple Reaction Monitoring
(MRM), the source temperature was set to 100 oC with 7 l/min nebulizer gas ﬂow ,while the shear
gas temperature was 300 oC at 7 l/min. The collision energy was set to 35 V and the fragmentor at
135 V. The dwell time was set to 200 ms. The BFRs concentration was calculated in µg/l on 2 ml of
sample. The conversion in µg/g was done according to the plastic weight used in each sample during
the solvent extraction, see appendix I.
LC-QQQ analysis was performed on all the 24 plastic samples.

3.5.5
GC–MS analysis
Phthalates quantiﬁcation was performed through Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC-MS).
After solvent extraction, the samples were subjected to solid-phase extraction (SPE) clean-up using
Alumina-N columns (Supelco, St. Louis, USA), conditioned with ethyl acetate. Prior to GC–MS
analysis, the samples were reduced to approx 500 µl through evaporation. In each of the samples
was added a mix of DMP-d4 and DEHP-d4 as internal standard. Chromatographic separation was
achieved on an Agilent 6890 gas chromatograph. Helium was used as a carrier gas with a 1 ml/min
constant ﬂow. The samples were injected in splitless mode, with the sample inlet held at 300oC. The
samples were held at 300 oC for 12 min. Detection was achieved on an Agilent 5975C triple-axis
mass-selective detector operated in selected ion-monitoring (SIM) mode, with the MS source at 230
oC and the quadrupole at 150 oC. The phthalates concentration was calculated in µg/l on 2 ml of sam-
ple. The conversion in µg/g was done according to the plastic weight used in each sample during the
solvent extraction, see appendix I.
GC–MS analysis was performed on all the polymer types, moreover the 24 samples were run
three times for a total of 72 samples, in order to have three replicate values of the same samples. The
ﬁnal result is indeed an average of the three values coming from the three different measurements.
3.6
Data analysis
The data generated from the analytical analysis was base on equipment with speciﬁc limit of detec-
tion (LOD). Concentration below LOD means that the concentration of the speciﬁc chemicals was
undetectable, thus the speciﬁc chemical additives could also be present into the plastic samples but
in small undetectable amount. In the chemical analysis, the LOD for each of the chemical additives
were calculated as an average of the analysed blank samples plus three standard deviations of the
blank. The blanks samples, which also have been through the microwave digestion procedure to-
gether with the plastic samples, consisted only of the chemicals used for the extractions. For the
statistical analysis, undetected values were replaced with 65% of the LOD, according to (Martın-
Fernandez, Barceló-Vidal, and Pawlowsky-Glahn, 2003; Eriksen et al., 2018; Pivnenko et al., 2016).
Moreover, chemical additives where more than 50% of the samples showed concentrations below the
LOD were excluded from the statistical analysed.
ICP-MS results showed metals concentration which was calculated as a mean value of the same
injection analysed 3 times, showing for this reason a standard deviation (SD) on the mean value. ICP-
MS results are displayed through a chart showing the mean value with a blue dot and its standard
deviation with a light blue rectangle.
LC-QQQ results showed only one value for each sample. Data are graphically displayed with a
column chart, furthermore, in the graph is displayed also the trend during recycling cycles through
a dotted blue line.
GC–MS analysis was performed on 72 samples, which generated a large amount of data enough
to perform statistical analysis. The results of the GC–MS analysis have been analysed with statistical
ANOVA test, in order to investigate the variations in the concentrations of chemical additives during
the different recycling cycles. Indeed, ANOVA method allowed to compare more than two groups.
Single factor ANOVA test was performed on excel. Before starting the statistical analysis a Null
Hypotheses H0 has to be deﬁned. Usually, the null hypothesis for an ANOVA is that there is no
signiﬁcant difference among the groups, meaning that there is no difference in the concentration of
chemical additives between the recycling cycles. The null hypothesis is always true unless it gets
contradicted. In the speciﬁc case the H0 has been stated as follows:

µ1.1 is the data correlated to the plastic sample recycled one time;
µ1.2 is the data correlated to the plastic sample recycled two times;
µ1.3 is the data correlated to the plastic sample recycled three times.
The results of ANOVA analysis are the F-critic and the F-value with the associated probability
value (p-value). In general, if p-value is smaller than 0.05 the null hypothesis can be rejected. More-
over, F-value has to be higher then F-critic to reject the null hypothesis. If the null hypothesis is
rejected, one concludes that the means of the groups are not equal, or at least one of the means is dif-
ferent. However, the ANOVA does not tell where the difference lies. If the null hypothesis is rejected,
ANOVA test has to be performed between each pair of groups to conﬁrm where the differences oc-
curred between groups. Furthermore, box plots are provided in the results section to visualize the
difference between concentrations of phthalates.

Chapter 5
Data analysis and discussion
The following chapter is dedicated to the discussion of the analytical results. Since the choice of
analysing the plastic materials for these speciﬁc substances used as chemical additives was made in
accordance with table 3.1, all the results are compared with data given in Hansen et al., 2013 , which
was the source of the table. Exceptions are given to Ti and Sb since the decision of quantifying their
content was not dictated by Hansen et al., 2013. Moreover, for the phthalates are also presented the
statistical results of the ANOVA method. The following section are divided by the different type of
chemical additives.
5.1
Fate of Metals during mechanical recycling of plastic waste
In this project has been analysed the fate of ﬁve different metals (Ti, Cr, Cd, Sb and Pb) during plastic
waste mechanical recycling. In general, the concentrations of these metals did not showed signiﬁcant
change during the plastic recycling simulation. Next subsections are divided by types of metals. The
metal content quantiﬁed in this work is analysed and compared also with three previous works
which have also analysed metals content in plastic waste, namely G¨otze et al., 2016; Eriksen et al.,
2018 and Norden, 2019.
5.1.1
Titanium
As described in 3.5.1, Ti is not a hazardous element, however titanium dioxide, which is used in
plastic manufacture, has a low order of toxicity. Currently, there are not LOC available for Ti in the
literature.
In this work, Ti is the only metal that was detectable in all analyzed different types of plastic
materials. All the recycled plastic materials, i.e. HDPEr, PPr and PSr, showed high concentrations of
Ti, values much bigger compared to the data in Eriksen et al., 2018. In HDPEr and PPr, Ti concentra-
tions were almost constant during the ﬁrst two recycling cycles, while then decreased almost of 10
% during the third recycling cycle. Behaviour that might be explained by the polymers degradation
(see 2.3.1) during the last recycling cycle. Polymer degradation cause a reduction of the molecu-
lar weight, which mean short polymer chain and as consequence more freedom for the metal to
move and migrate. Thus, Ti either migrated into the water bath during the cooling down of the
still high-temperature plastic ﬁlament, or, if still solidly bound with the polymer components, could
have evaporated together with the volatile constituents1 of the polymer. However, without a thermo-
gravimetric analysis (TGA)2 of the polymers, it is not possible to predict the right molecular weight
reduction, which during extrusion is inﬂuenced by many factor such as temperature, pressure, time,
speed etc. (Farahanchi and Sobkowicz, 2017). Also, without a chemical analysis of the water used
1Volatile constituents in polymers can be: hydrocarbons, residues of unreacted monomers, residues of solvents which
are used for the production of the polymer, plasticizers, antioxidants, degradation products etc. (Berezkin, Alishoyev, and
Nemirovskaya, 1977)
2TGA is an experimental technique in which the mass of a sample is measured as a function of sample temperature over
time (Bottom, 2008).
==PAGE:58==
Chapter 5. Data analysis and discussion
45
into the water bath is not possible to assert that the metal migrated from the polymer to the water.
The highest content of Ti was quantiﬁed in PSr, with the highest peak in PSr 1.2 with 9762.68 µg/g.
The virgin materials, i.e. PPv, PSv and PETv, showed much smaller concentrations of Ti. The content
of Ti in PPv and PETv was of the same order of magnitude as reported in the analysed samples by
Norden, 2019. Ti was detected only in one PETv sample, i.e. PETv 1.2, while the value in PPv 1.1
might be simply an outlier since it was differing greatly from the Ti content in the other PPv samples.
PSv exhibited Ti content only in PSv 1.1 and PSv 1.2, with smaller values compared to data in Norden,
2019.
Ti content was also quantiﬁed with XRF analysis (see table 4.2), which was performed only on the
initial samples (i.e PPv 1.0, PSv 1.0, PETv 1.0, HDPEr 1.0, PPr 1.0 and PSr 1.0). XRF analysis showed
different results compared to ICP-MS quantiﬁcation, although the order of magnitude was the same
in both type of analysis.
Apart from some similarities, Ti fate inside the analysed plastic had different behaviours. Also
the same types of polymer showed different behaviours, especially PSv and PSr, where, in the ﬁrst
one the concentration decreased while in the second one it increased during the recycling cycles.
5.1.2
Chromium
Chromium itself is not considered a hazardous element, though some chromium (VI) compounds
used in plastic manufacture, such as chromium trioxide, sodium dichromate and potassium dichro-
mate are classiﬁed as dangerous substances by ECHA. These substances are fatal if inhaled, toxic
if swallowed, carcinogenic, toxic in contact with skin, cause damage to organs through prolonged
or repeated exposure, are suspected of damaging fertility or the unborn child and are very toxic to
aquatic life (European Union, 2006). The LOC given by EU RoHS 2 (Directive 2011/65/EU), 2019 is
1000 µg/g in EEE, which refers to chromium (VI) compounds and not to only Cr element.
Except for PPv, traces of Cr were detectable in all the other analysed polymer types. In PPr, the
initial sample PPr 1.0 is the only analysed PP sample which showed a concentration of Cr of 0.59
µg/g. The results obtained in this work about Cr content in PP was not in accordance with values
showed in G¨otze et al., 2016; Eriksen et al., 2018. Indeed, in both papers analysed PP waste samples
exhibited Cr contents of 48.9 ± 9 µg/g (G¨otze et al., 2016) and ∼3 µg/g (Eriksen et al., 2018). Both
PSv and PSr samples exhibited traces of Cr. The values were in within the same order of magnitude
as reported in PS waste samples by G¨otze et al., 2016; Eriksen et al., 2018. Cr content in HDPEr was
in accordance with the values found in Eriksen et al., 2018, while in G¨otze et al., 2016 Cr content in
HDPE waste samples was one order of magnitude bigger. PETv showed traces of Cr during the last
two recycling cycle. Moreover, as Hansen et al., 2013 suggested Cr was present in all the different
types of analysed polymers.
XRF analysis did not found any content of Cr in any of the analysed plastic samples. The reason
might be due to the small content of Cr, even though HDPEr 1.0 showed a concentration of 3.95 ±
0.58 µg/g which was high enough to be quantiﬁed through XRF spectroscopy.
Cr seems to have the same fate in all analysed plastic samples, except for the two PP samples.
However, even though the metal concentrations seems to increase linearly, see ﬁgures 4.9b, 4.15a,
4.10a and 4.11b, the variations in Cr concentrations are very small. The increase in concentration
might suggest that while volatile constituents of the polymer evaporate during the extrusion, the
metal kept solidly bound with the polymer matrix.
5.1.3
Cadmium
According to ECHA classiﬁcation in REACH registrations, cadmium is a hazardous substance. This
metal is fatal if inhaled, is very toxic to aquatic life, may cause cancer, causes damage to organs
through prolonged or repeated exposure, is suspected of causing genetic defects, is suspected of
damaging fertility or the unborn child and catches ﬁre spontaneously if exposed to air (European
==PAGE:59==

46
Union, 2006). According to European restriction, the concentration of Cd inside products should be
below 100 µg/g, exception is given to recycled PVC where the concentration can achieve a maximum
value of 1000 µg/g.
In this work, Cd was detectable only in two types of plastic, namely HDPEr and PSr. In both
types of plastic, the metal concentration was almost constant during the recycling simulation. The
results are not in accordance with Hansen et al., 2013, where Cd is suggested to be a substance used
as chemical additives also in PET and PP (see table 3.1), which did not show any Cd content. In
HDPEr, Cd content was on average 2.32 ± 0.06 µg/g, which is below the LOC allowed by ECHA.
However, the value was not in accordance with G¨otze et al., 2016, where HDPE do not have any Cd
content. In PSr, Cd content on average was 11.76 ± 0.50 µg/g, which also is below the LOC allowed
by ECHA. Moreover, Cd content was a bit higher compared to the value reported in plastic waste
samples by G¨otze et al., 2016 and Eriksen et al., 2018. PETv, PPv and PPr did not exhibit any trace
of Cd. Concentration of Cd in PETv, were below LOD also in the plastic waste samples reported in
G¨otze et al., 2016. On the other hand, PP waste samples in G¨otze et al., 2016 and Eriksen et al., 2018
showed a small content of Cd, which was not the case of the two PP analysed in this work, i.e. PPv
and PPr.
Cd, same as Cr, was not quantiﬁed with XRF analysis, even though PSr 1.0 showed a concentra-
tion of 11.30 ± 0.55 µg/g which was high enough to be quantiﬁed through XRF spectroscopy.
In both types of plastic materials, i.e. HDPEr and PPv, Cd showed a slight increase in the concen-
trations during the recycling cycles. However, the SDs were overlapping in all the cases, thus we can
assert that Cd did not show signiﬁcative variations in concentrations during recycling simulation.
5.1.4
Antimony
As reported in section 3.5.1, some antimony compounds used in plastic industry are known to be
toxic. The LOC for Sb in REACH regulation is 50000 µg/g.
Sb was the metal with the highest content in PETv, with an average value of 290.40 ± 2,30 µg/g
during the recycling simulation. Sb is known to be used in PET polymerization, the concentrations
were in within the same order of magnitude as reported in G¨otze et al., 2016, Norden, 2019 and
Eriksen et al., 2018. HDPEr exhibited the highest concentrations of Sb, among the analysed types of
plastic, 521.68 ± 22,63 µg/g on average. The metal content increased during the ﬁrst recycling cycle
to then decrease linearly during the last two cycles. The concentrations quantiﬁed in this work in
HDPEr were far away from the values reported in G¨otze et al., 2016, 2.9 ± 1.9 µg/g (G¨otze et al.,
2016) vs 521.68 ± 22.62 µg/g quantiﬁed in this work . Since the sources of the plastic waste used to
manufacture HDPEr is unknown, was not possible to deduce from where the metal entered in the
polymer cycle. In HDPEr, Sb could have been used as stabiliser or as halogenated ﬂame retardant
activating. PPv did not show any Sb content, while PPr exhibited an average content of 0.97 ± 0.32
µg/g. The quantiﬁed content of Sb in PPr was in accordance wit the data reported in G¨otze et al.,
2016; Norden, 2019. PSv also had no detectable concentrations of Sb. On the other hand, PSr showed
an average content of 21.75 ± 1,26 µg/g, which also was in within the same order of magnitude as
reported in G¨otze et al., 2016; Norden, 2019.
Sb content, quantiﬁed with XRF spectroscopy, was in disagreement with ICP-MS results. Only
in HDPEr the result was quite similar, while for the other samples XRF spectroscopy showed an Sb
content that was not found with ICP-MS analysis. However, Sb concentrations quantiﬁed with XRF
are associated with high values of uncertainty (see table 4.2), suggesting a possible source of error.
In all types of analysed plastic, Sb did not have signiﬁcant variations in concentrations during
the different recycling cycles, even though in PPr Sb concentrations slightly increased through the
recycling simulation. Moreover, Sb content was always below the LOC.

5.1.5
Lead
Lead is classiﬁed as dangerous element in classiﬁcation provided by companies to ECHA in REACH
registrations. Pb causes damage to organs through prolonged or repeated exposure, is very toxic to
aquatic life, may cause cancer and may cause harm to breast-fed children (European Union, 2006).
The LOC depends on the speciﬁc used compound, but articles or accessible parts thereof that may be
placed in the mouth by children should have a Pb content below 500 µg/g (European Union, 2006),
while Lead content in EEE shold be below 1000 µg/g (EU RoHS 2 (Directive 2011/65/EU), 2019).
In this work, Pb was detectable only in two types of polymer, i.e. HDPEr and PSr. Indeed, also for
Pb, the results are not in accordance with Hansen et al., 2013, where Pb is suggested to be a substance
used as chemical additives also in PET and PP (see table 3.1), which did not show any Pb content.
In HDPEr the concentrations of Pb during the recycling cycle were almost constant with an average
content of 10.70 ± 0.54 µg/g. Also in PSr the concentrations of Pb were almost constant through
the recycling simulation, with an average content of 4.90 ± 0.22 µg/g. In both plastic materials, the
contents were in within the same order of magnitude as reported in HDPE and PS waste samples by
G¨otze et al., 2016. PP waste samples analysed in G¨otze et al., 2016 and Eriksen et al., 2018 showed a
Pb content that was not found in both PP samples analysed in this work, i.e PPv and PPr. While, also
in G¨otze et al., 2016 and Eriksen et al., 2018 Pb in PET samples was undetectable or just above LOD
as the case of PETv in this work.
Quantiﬁcation of Pb with XRF spectroscopy showed results that were in within the same order of
magnitude as the ones quantiﬁed with ICP-MS method.
Pb content never exceeded the LOC and did not exhibit any signiﬁcant variations in concentra-
tions during the recycling simulation run in this work.
5.2
Fate of Brominated ﬂame retardants during mechanical recycling of
plastic waste
Tetrabromobisphenol A and Hexabromocyclododecane diastereomers are the two BFRs analysed in
this work. The fate of TBBPA and α, β and γ-HBCD during mechanical plastic recycling simulation
is discussed in the following sections. REACH regulation give a LOC that is the same for all BFRs,
which is 1000 µg/g. LOC was respected in all analysed samples for both types of BFRs. The results of
the BRFs content are in disagreement with Hansen et al., 2013, where BFRs are non considered to be
chemical additives used in PET and PS, which both showed traces of BFRs in this work. Moreover,
the content of the speciﬁc BFRs is compared with Pivnenko et al., 2017, in which BFRs content in
plastic waste has already been analysed.
5.2.1
Tetrabromobisphenol A

causing cancer (European Union, 2006).
In this work, traces of TBBPA were found in all analysed types of polymer. In all types of polymer,
the concentration of TBBPA, quantiﬁed with the LC-QQQ analysis, were in within the same order of
magnitude as reported for the analysed plastic waste samples by Pivnenko et al., 2017.

recycling cycles (see ﬁgure 4.20a), in PPv the concentrations increased during the ﬁrst two recycling
cycles to then decrease of almost 94% during the last recycling cycle (see ﬁgure 4.16). The drop of

5.4
Implications for mechanical plastics recycling
The quantiﬁcation of chemical additives in this study underlines the fact that the samples of recycled
plastic, i.e. HDPEr, PPr and PSr, contained higher concentrations of chemical additives than virgin
plastic, i.e. PPv, PSv and PETv. This is due to the fact that plastic recycling processes are not designed
to remove chemical additives. Hence, chemical additives contained in one type of plastic products
can remain in the materials after recycling and thereby be spread through the mechanical plastic recy-
cling processes, causing a re-introduction of unwanted chemicals into newly manufactured products.
This potentially affects the quality and applicability of recycled plastic.

The failure to remove chemical additives during mechanical recycling not only affects the quality
of recycled plastic but might affect also the manufacturability of the plastic materials. Indeed, it is no
coincidence that all the recycled plastic materials (i.e. HDPEr, PPr and PSr), which also exhibited the
highest chemical additives content, exhibited a strange behaviour during the recycling simulation,
making the process more challenging.
Moreover, the chemical additives which showed a decrease in concentration, have potentially
evaporated or migrated into the water bath, causing environmental and human health risk. The
greatest risk could be caused to the manufacturing operators, who will be exposed to unwanted
hazardous substances.
Finally, polymer degradation might also play a role in the fate of chemical additives during me-
chanical recycling. Indeed, the breakage of chemical bonds that cause a reduction in the molecular
weight during the polymer degradation might enhance the migration and release of the chemical
additives. Indeed, either the act of breaking bonds and as well the new short polymer chains might
increase the freedom of chemical additives inside the polymer matrix.
5.4.1
Possible solutions to improve mechanical plastic recycling
Nowadays, there are already different suggestions to improve either the quality and the quantity
of recycled plastic. Has been proved that design for recycling initiatives can potentially increase up
to 23% the currently recycled plastic rate (Eriksen, 2019). The initiatives are proposing of design
plastic product with only one polymer type and avoid colours that make difﬁcult the sorting step,
such as black plastic. While a way to increase the quality of recycled plastic could be to introduce a
sorting step upstream. For instance, if food packaging would be sorted from the remaining plastic
waste in the household, up to 30% of the source-separated plastic waste could be recycled into high-
quality recycled plastic (Eriksen, 2019). Such initiative would avoid the spread of unwanted chemical
additives in recycled products and it considerably encourages primary recycling. However, this will
make the collection more challenging and expensive. A further solution to improve both quality
and quantity of recycled plastic is also presented in Eriksen, 2019, it suggests to regulate the use
of polymer types with speciﬁc products. Which means, for example, to use only PS or PP for food
packaging, PET for plastic bottles and so forth.
Moreover, EU legislation should force the polymer industry to specify which chemical additives
are used, specifying the used substance and concentration. Indeed, the TDSs of the analysed plastic
samples did not show the presence of chemical additives found in this work. A possible solution
could be to create a recycling code that is associated with the chemical additives in the same way
that RIC codes are already associated with the polymer types. This would prevent the spread of
unwanted chemical additives in recycled plastic.
Finally, it should be invested more in researching for safest substances that can be used as a
substitute for hazardous chemical additives, which would deﬁnitely solve the problem upstream.
5.5
Sources of uncertainty and future works
Quantify the fate of chemical additives during mechanical plastic recycling proved to be a very chal-
lenging project. Too many factors might have inﬂuenced the results.
==PAGE:65==
Chapter 5. Data analysis and discussion
52
During the recycling simulation, even though the extruder was open and fully cleaned, small
residues of plastic from previous use could have contaminated the analysed plastic samples. More-
over, the chemical analysis was not performed on a large number of samples. This did not generate a
large set of data that could have made possible to perform statistical analysis, as well as the exclusion
or identiﬁcation of outlier values.
A personal suggestion for future works would be to run the recycling simulation on a small scale
extruder to avoid possible contamination since small equipment is easy to clean. Furthermore, small
equipment would allow keeping track of the amount of polymer that is feed into the hopper and
the one coming out, which would avoid possible loss of information during the recycling process.
Indeed, this will make possible to quantify the possible loss of the polymer during the process and
relate it to the loss or accumulation of chemical additives. For the same reason, it should be inves-
tigated also the degradation of the polymers during the recycling process. It also strongly recom-
mended having at least ﬁve different replicates for each sample, in order to have enough data for run
statistical analysis. Lastly, it would be interesting to analyse the water contained into the water bath
of the extrusion process in order to quantify possible losses of chemical additives due to the contact
of the still high-temperature plastic with the water.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Six different plastic materials, including three virgin plastic materials (i.e. PPv, PSv and PETv) and
three recycled plastic materials (i.e. HDPEr, PPr and PSr) were analysed for the fate of nine selected
chemical additives, including ﬁve metals (i.e. Ti, Cr, Cd, Sb and Pb), two BFRs (i.e. TBBPA and
HBCD) and two phthalates (i.e. DiBP and DEHP), during mechanical plastic recycling.
The results have shown that the same chemical additive can have different behaviour in different
polymers, but also within the same type of polymer. Exception is given to few metals: Cr, which
slightly increased its content during plastic recycling in all the polymer types in which was quan-
tiﬁed; Cd and Pb, which kept almost constant concentrations during recycling in all the polymer
types in which were quantiﬁed; Ti, which exhibited the same drop in concentration during the last
recycling cycle in HDPEr and PPr. Furthermore, also the type of polymer might inﬂuence the fate of
chemical additives. Indeed, PSr showed an increase in concentrations of both analysed phthalates,
both BFRs and also for Ti and Cr.
However, although some small variations in concentrations, chemical additives during mechan-
ical plastic recycling does not exhibited signiﬁcant emissions or losses. Thus, the different chemical
additives present in one type of plastic during mechanical recycling will be introduced into the new
recycled material, reducing indeed the quality of the recycled plastic. This was also conﬁrmed from
the fact that the analyzed recycling samples exhibited the highest content of chemical additives.
However, in all the analysed plastic samples the LOD given by ECHA in REACH regulation was
always respected, with values much smaller than the critical ones.
The work also underlined that XRF spectroscopy gives just a rough idea of metals content. In-
deed, chemical quantiﬁcation of metals through ICP-MS analysis showed different concentrations.
This was also due to the fact that ICP-MS gives the average content of the metal in the samples,
whereas XRF gives the concentration in the spot where the measurement is done. Hence, if the metal
is not uniformly distributed, one with XRF may hit the spot with low/high concentrations, this ex-
plains the difference with ICP-MS.
The analysed plastic samples had TDSs which did not show the presence of chemical additives
found in this work. In my opinion, EU legislation should force the polymer industry to specify
which chemical additives are used, specifying the used substance and concentration. The further
step would then be to create a recycling code that is associated with the chemical additives in the
same way nowadays RIC code are already associated with the polymer type. This would avoid the
spread of unwanted chemical additives in recycled plastic.
However, the ideal solution would be to do not use hazardous substances as chemical additives
in the ﬁrst place. Governments should invest in researching for safest substances that can be used as
a substitute for these currently used toxic ones.

Processing; 
 
PET is hygroscopic, and since moisture adversely affects the IV during melt processing of the polymer, 
it must be dried prior to molding. Undried PET pellets can contain up to 0.3% (3000 ppm) of moisture. 
After drying the moisture content typically is below 50 ppm. 
 
In the drying process, there are four variables that should be considered: 
 
 
Drying temperature 
 
 
150 - 170°C 
 
Residence time in the dryer silo  
6 hours 
 
Dew Point of Drying Air   
 
< - 20°C 
 
Airflow Rate  
 
 
 
2.2 m3/h/kg PET 
 
PET has a relatively high melting point of around 245°C.  Processing PET pellets on an extruder or 
injection moulding machine requires temperature setting of 30 – 40°C  above the melting point.   
A typical temperature profile (from hopper to nozzle) is: 295 – 285 – 280°C and hot-runners can be set 
at 275 to 285°C.  Temperatures above 300°C should be avoided. 
The melt residence time should be limited to < 2 minutes to avoid discoloration.  During machine stops 
the temperature setting should be reduced to avoid the material to degrade or decompose.
