Abstract 
In this project the focus has been on transferring plasmids from E. coli MG1655 in pork meat. It has been 
investigated at 3 and 13°C for 14 days and at 13 and 36°C for 3 days. No trans conjugants were found in the 
meat that had been at 3 and 13°C, but were seen after 1 day at 36°C. For the meat experiments the 
concentration of TetA and UidA genes were followed by Q-PCR. The results from the Q-PCR were not valid 
but indicated that from the 14 days experiment the concentration of TetA was decreasing at both 
temperatures and UidA was decreasing at 13°C but more constant at 3°C.  
A growth experiment showed that E. coli and S. marcescens could grow down to 13° but at 3°C the 
concentration was constant. It was also tested when E. coli MG1655 could transfer the plasmid pKJK10 to E. 
coli MT102 by conjugation at 3, 13, 22 and 36°C. Trans conjugants were seen down to 13°C. The strains were 
grown at different temperatures to see if that had an effect, but the results showed that the important 
temperature, is the temperature the conjugation should occur at and not what temperature the strains were 
grown at. Due to the results the transfer of plasmids does not occur in meat at 13°C or below, and further 
research is therefore needed to find out when it occurs.

Introduction 
Antimicrobial resistance is a growing problem in the world. In EU 25,000 dies yearly due to infections from 
resistant bacteria [1]. The growing resistance comes from a big use of antibiotics in treatment of humans and 
animals. In Denmark 12 million pigs were sent out of the country to be slaughtered in 2015. It is assumed 
that over 63-70% of the pigs were carrying Methicillin Resistance Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)[2].  
Comparing of resistant bacteria isolated in animals and humans showed that there was a limited detection 
of identical clones but identical resistance genes and plasmids were detected [3]. This could indicate that the 
resistance is not spread by vertical transmission but more likely by horizontal transmission.  
It is important to know how the transmission of resistance genes occurs and where in the slaughtering 
process it can happen to be able to stop it. For this project it has been tried to see if transmission of resistance 
genes can happen in pork meat during slaughtering of pigs at 3 to 13°C by horizontal transmission, and also 
at what temperatures horizontal transmission can occur.  
 
Project aim 
The project aim is to find out if antimicrobial resistance is possible to occur by conjugation in meat from 
Escherichia coli (E. coli), and which bacteria that can receive the plasmids in meat. To get a picture of that, it 
is in this project tested when the bacteria can grow, at which temperatures it is able to make conjugation 
also depending on what temperature it has been growing at, and a meat experiment to see what bacteria 
gets a Gfp-marked plasmid by API and how the concentration of E. coli and the plasmid is changing over days 
in the meat with Q-PCR and plate spreading.

Theory 
The amount of used antimicrobials in the industry is huge. Until 1999 growth promoters were used but after 
2000 it has been illegal to use in the industry. After the use of growth promoters stopped, the amount of 
prescribed veterinary antimicrobials increased and was at is highest in 2009. From 2010 to now the amount 
of prescribed veterinary antimicrobials looks more constant. In 2012 were Pigs the main consumer of the 
antimicrobial consumption with 75% of the total. Tetracycline is the antibiotic that is used the most in the 
pig industry. Therefore the amount of Tetracycline (Tet) resistant bacteria in pork meat is high. In 2008 was 
more than 30% of the isolates taken from healthy pigs resistant to Tet and in diagnostic pigs more than 60% 
of the isolates was resistant to Tet[4]. 
 
Antibiotic resistance 
Antibiotics have many different modes of action depending on which antibiotic it is. In general the modes of 
action can be on the cell wall, cell membrane, protein synthesis, Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) 
synthesis/metabolism and Ribonucleic acid (RNA) synthesis [5]. All these parts in the cell are lethal for the 
cell. Therefore, bacteria can acquire antibiotic resistance to survive. A bacterium can acquire resistance by 
even mutation or acquire it from other bacteria by transformation, conjugation or transduction [6].  
Natural vs. acquired resistance and mechanisms 
Natural resistance to an antibiotic is when a bacterium does not have the target for the antibiotic, and the 
antibiotic can therefore not harm the bacterium.  
Acquired resistance: There are different ways to acquire resistance. It can be because the bacterium mutates 
or the bacterium can get DNA for resistance from another bacterium by transformation, conjugation or 
transduction. The bacteria strain used as donor in this project has a plasmid with the genes for Tetracyklin 
which it is able to give by conjugation to other bacteria [6].  
Mutation is the most common mechanism of antibiotic resistance [7]. This is used to make the two strains, 
used as recipient in this project, Rifampicin and Naldixin resistant.  
A mutation could make the bacterium resistant by point mutations in the target genes/influx pumps, which 
would lead to the antibiotics not being able to attach to the target and the bacteria will therefore not be 
harmed by the antibiotics [6]. An Illustration can be seen on Figure 1. 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
 
Figure 1 – Illustration on point mutation in the target genes/influx pumps [6], on the left is without the point mutation and the antibiotic

Bacteria can also acquire resistance by getting antibiotic resistance genes from other bacteria. This make the 
bacteria able to destroy the antibiotic by enzymatic cleavage, make a target protection so the antibiotic 
cannot attach or code for an efflux pump which can pump out the antibiotics and therefore not harm the 
bacterium [6]. An illustration of this can be seen on Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2 – Illustration on mechanisms of required resistance [6]. 
 
The overall mechanism of antibiotic resistance is seen in Figure 3. There are five different mechanisms to 
prevent the antibiotic in harming the bacteria: Reduced permeability, active efflux, altered metabolism, 
enzymatic modification or degradation and alternation of the drug target [5]. 
 
Reduced permeability: When the permeability is reduced, the antibiotics are not able to enter the 
cells. 
 
Active efflux: The active efflux pumps the antibiotics out and thereby prevents its action on the cell 
 
Altered metabolism: An altered metabolism can have the effect that the antibiotic cannot disturb 
the metabolism as it did before and are thereby not effective.  
 
Enzymatic modification: An enzymatic modification or degradation of the antibiotic, would mean 
that the antibiotic would not be able to bind to the site as it should or the antibiotic will be degraded. 
 
Alternation of drug target: A mutation in the target side, thereby the antibiotic cannot bind to the 
target. [5].

Which mechanism that is best against an antibiotic depends on what mode of action the antibiotic has, for 
example, if the mode of action is on the cell wall or cell membrane it does not help to have a mechanism 
against it inside the cell. 
 
Figure 3 – Illustration on the mechanisms of antibiotic resistance [5] 
 
Plasmids 
Plasmids are often circular double stranded DNA that can vary in size from 1 kbp to more than 1 Mbp. 
Plasmids must carry genes that make them able to replicate but only some carry the genes for conjugation 
and are called conjugative plasmids. The plasmids that cannot conjugate them self are called non-
conjugative. Non-conjugative plasmids can be transferred to another host if it can use the transfer machinery 
from another conjugative plasmid and is then known to be mobilizable [8]. 
Plasmids do not carry essential genes for the host but can carry genes that make the host able to survive in 
different environments such as genes for resistance. In a host there can be many different plasmids present, 
but two plasmids that are closely related genetically cannot be maintained in the same cell. This is called that 
the plasmids are incompatible. If two incompatible plasmids are in the same host, they will be divided at cell 
replication. There are a number of incompatibility (Inc) groups. Plasmids that are in the same Inc group 
cannot be in the same host, but can be in a host with other plasmids from other Inc groups. The connection 
of the plasmids in one Inc group is that they are sharing a common mechanism for regulating their replication 
[8].  The genes in a conjugative plasmid are often organized in functional groups as seen on Figure 4.

Possible ways of transferring plasmids 
Plasmids can be transferred between two bacteria by even conjugation or transformation. 
Conjugation is a way of transferring genetic material between two bacteria. Conjugation can only occur when 
two bacteria are in contact. The bacterium having the genes that it wants to transfer also need to be possible 
to make Sex pili. The genes for making sex pili are usually on the plasmid that will be transferred, so the 
receiver can transfer the plasmid to new bacteria. The receiver then has the plasmid and can duplicate and 
transfer it later. [10]. An illustration of how conjugation occurs can be seen on Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5 – Illustration of how conjugation occur  [10]

The other way a bacterium can get a plasmid is by transformation. Transformation is when cells die and their 
DNA is free in the surroundings, and another cell takes in part of their DNA for example a plasmid. If it is not 
a plasmid the cell also needs to take the DNA in as a part of its chromosome. An illustration of this can be 
seen on Figure 6. 
 
Figure 6 – Illustration of how transformation occur [11] 
 
pKJK 10 
In this project a conjugative plasmid, pKJK10 was used for all the plasmid transfer experiments. The plasmid 
pKJK10 was constructed from pKJK5 by insertion of a gene cassette consisting of resistance genes encoding 
for Kanamycin, Streptomycin and Tetracycline resistance and the Gfp gene encoding the green fluorescent 
protein (GFP)[12] The product made from the genes for LacI in a bacteria works as a repressor for the Gfp 
expression, which means that the bacteria only will be fluorescent if it does not make the product from the 
genes for LacI, and it will then be easy to see if the plasmid has been transferred to other bacteria than its 
host. pKJK5 is a 54,383 bp plasmid that was recovered from a soil environment. From sequencing, the 
backbone genes it seemed similar to other IncP-1 plasmids that are containing a Tra1 and Tra2 region and is 
therefore classified as an IncP-1 plasmid [13]. An illustration of pKJK5 can be seen on Figure 7. IncP-1 plasmids 
can be transferred and replicate in all Gram-negative bacteria. They are known to be efficient to transfer 
resistance between different strains [14].

The principle of Q-PCR 
In this study the concentration of TetA and UidA genes were wanted to follow during the experiment. The 
TetA gene codes for resistance to Tetracycline and is interesting to follow because more than 30% of isolates 
from pigs are resistant to Tetracycline [4]. There are several genes coding for different ways of resistance to 
Tetracycline. Many of them are coding for efflux, so the antibiotics are pumped out of the cell, but some are 
coding for Ribosomal protection and Enzymatic. It is chosen to look after the TetA  gene that is coding for an 
efflux [15]. It is known that the plasmid is carrying the genes for TetA.  
The UidA gene is interesting because it only is E. coli and Shigella spp. that are carrying the gene. It is therefore 
easy to follow the concentration of E. coli by following the concentration of the UidA gene [16]. 
For this study Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (Q-PCR) was used instead of polymerase chain reaction 
(PCR), because Q-PCR gives the possibility of getting a quantitative result of how much of the DNA there is. 
This makes it possible to follow the concentration during an experiment and not only know if the DNA is 
present or not. 
To run Q-PCR instead of PCR the DNA needs to be marked by a fluorescent reporter. This can even be done 
nonspecific by using for example SYBR Green. It can also be done more specific by using Fluorophore-Labeled 
Primers or a Target-Specific probe. A Target specific probe can only be used for one specific method, but it is 
also certain that there only will be results if the target is present. When using unspecific marking as SYBR 
==PAGE:16==

Green, it can be used for all methods but there can be problems about melting temperatures, which can 
make the detection more unclear [17].  
For this project a TaqMan probe was used. A TaqMan probe consists of a reporter and quencher. During the 
annealing step the TagMan binds together onto the single stranded DNA and is designed to bind after the 
primer. When the reporter and quencher are attached the reporter will not be fluorescents, but as soon they 
are apart there will be fluorescent. During the extension the probe will partially be displaced and the reporter 
is cleaved by DNA polymerase, and the free reporter will fluoresces [18]. An illustration of this can be seen 
on Figure 8.

Growth experiment 
A growth experiment was made to see what temperatures E. coli and S. marcescens could growth at and how 
fast. The results should be used to know how many days the strains should be at different temperatures, 
before trying to make conjugation. 
E. coli and S. marcescens was grown overnight on Luria Broth (LB) plates at 36°C from a stock culture that 
had been at -80°C. Around 2-3 colonies from each strain was inoculated in 10 ml LB with either 16 µg/ml Tet 
or 25 µg/ml Rif and 25 µg/ml Nal as antibiotics, to ensure the growth only consist of antibiotic resistant 
bacteria. They were incubated at 36°C overnight.  
OD600 was measured on the bacteria cultured to adjust the number. Dilutions of 107, 105 and 103 Colony 
forming unit (CFU)/ml were made using the fact that OD600 = 1 correspond to approximately 8 · 108 CFU/ml 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
[19]. The diluted cultures were incubated at 3, 13, 22 and 36°C. At 22 and 36°C only the dilution with 
107CFU/ml was incubated. During the growth experiment OD600 was measured on the 107 CFU/ml. For the 
other concentrations at 3 and 13°C, spot tests were made to detect the CFU/ml. At 36°C the OD600 was tested 
on day 0 (4.5 hours after the beginning) and on day 1. For 22°C, OD600 was tested on day 1 and 2. The 
concentrations at 3 and 13°C were tested on day 1, 3 and 5. A flowchart of the experiment can be seen in 
Appendix IV. 
 
Conjugation experiment at different temperatures 
To test the influence of the growth temperature of the donor and recipient, and see the influence of the 
temperature where the conjugation should occur, this experiment was made. For this experiment E. coli 
MG1655 was used as donor and E. coli MT102 and S. marcescens W225 were used as recipients. 
E. coli and S. marcescens were grown overnight on LB plates at 36°C from a stock culture that had been at -
80°C. Around 2-3 colonies from each strain was inoculated in 10 ml LB with either 16 µg/ml Tet or 25 µg/ml 
Rif and 25 µg/ml Nal as antibiotics, to ensure the growth only consist of antibiotic resistant bacteria and 
incubated at 36°C overnight. 
OD600 was measured on the bacteria, and dilutions were made to get 107 and 103 CFU/ml. The strains were 
incubated at 3, 13, 22 and 36°C. At 3°C only the dilution with 107CFU/ml was incubated. For the other 
temperatures, it was only 103 CFU/ml that was incubated. The concentrations at 3 and 13°C were incubated 
for 7 days, and the concentrations at 22 and 36°C were incubated for 1 day. OD600 was tested on the bacteria 
to know the concentrations. 
From each concentration from all temperatures 2 ml was centrifuged at 5000 g for 5 min. The supernatant 
was removed and the pellet was dissolved in 0.9% NaCl water. A dilution was made in 0.9% NaCl water to get 
106 CFU/ml. 
Filters were placed on LB plates and 50 µl donor and 50 µl recipient were pipetted on the filters and mixed. 
The plates were incubated at four different temperatures to see what temperature the conjugation can 
appear at. The temperatures chosen were 3, 13, 22 and 36°C. Table 1 shows at which temperatures the donor 
and the recipient have grown and at which temperature the conjugation should happen. The plates that were 
incubated at 3 and 13°C stood for 3 days at the given temperature and the plates that were incubated at 22 
and 36°C stood for 1 day at the given temperature. The purpose of the difference in days was to maximize 
the possibility for the conjugation to happen.

Meat experiment 
To see if E. coli MG1655 could transfer the plasmid pKJK10 in meat at 3 and 13°C, a 14 days experiment was 
set up. 
E. coli MG1655 was grown overnight on a LB plate at 36°C from a stock culture that had been at -80°C. Around 
2-3 colonies were inoculated in 10 ml LB with 16 µg/ml Tet, to ensure the growth only consist of E. coli with 
the plasmid that should be conjugated, and incubated at 36°C overnight. 
OD600 was tested on E. coli, and diluted to get 107 CFU/ml. To two times 450 g pork meat, 4.5 ml of the diluted 
E. coli was added (gives a concentration of 105 CFU/g in the meat). The meat was incubated at 3 and 13°C.  
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
On day 0, 1, 3, 6, 8, 10 and 14 pork meat was taken out from both temperatures to be tested. When tested, 
5 g of the meat from each temperature was weighed and then mixed with 45 ml 0.9% NaCl water and 
massaged for 2 min. From the fluid, 10 ml was kept in a 10 ml tube and 1 ml was pipetted to an eppendorf 
tube. From the 10 ml tube 10 fold dilutions were made, and the concentration of E. coli was tested on 
MacConkey plates and searching for trans conjugants on Minimal agar plates. Enumeration of total bacterial 
count was tested on the two plates without antibiotic and searching for trans conjugants was done using the 
two types of plates with 16 µg/ml Tet. For the trans conjugants and the bacteria that has been a problem by 
over growing plates, an API test was made.  
The 10 ml tube and the eppendorf tube are kept at -20°C until further use (the eppendorf tube is used for Q-
PCR). A flowchart of the experiment can be seen in Appendix VI. 
 
Meat experiment used to Flow Cytometry 
To see if E. coli MG1655 could transfer the plasmid pKJK10 in meat at 13 and 36°C, a 3 days experiment was 
set up where it also was tested by Flow Cytometry if there were any trans conjugants. 
E. coli MG1655 was grown overnight on a LB plate at 36°C from a stock culture that had been at -80°C. Around 
2-3 colonies were inoculated in 10 ml LB with 16 µg/ml Tet, to ensure the growth only consist of E. coli with 
the plasmid that should be conjugated, and incubated at 36°C overnight. 
OD600 was measured on E. coli, and diluted to 108 and 106 CFU/ml. Pork meat was weighed off with 5 g in 
each, eight times. One of them was a negative control and was incubated at 36°C. 
To three times 5 g pork meat 500 µl of the dilution with 106 CFU/ml was added and incubated at 13°C. To 
three times 5 g pork meat 500 µl of the dilution with 106 CFU/ml was added and incubated at 36°C. The last 
meat sample was a positive control and therefore added 500 µl of the dilution with 108 CFU/ml and incubated 
at 36°C. The rest of the overnight culture was incubated at 36°C, and plated out on MacConkey after 3 days, 
to check that the E. coli MG1655 did not mutate and were lighting in UV light itself. The meat was taken out 
for testing on day 0, 1 and 3. The controls were first taken out on day 3. For all days one from each 
temperature was taken out. To each meat sample of 5 gram, 45 ml of 0.9% NaCl water was added and 
massaged for 2 min. Exactly 1 ml suspension was pipetted into an Eppendorf tube and stored at -20°C until 
use (used for Q-PCR). The rest of the liquid was filtered through a coffee filter and poured in a 50 ml tube. 
The concentrations of E. coli were checked on MacConkey and MacConkey with 16 µg/ml Tet. The search for 
trans conjugants was made on Minimal or LB and Minimal with 16 µg/ml Tet or LB with 16 µg/ml Tet and 50 
µg/ml Kan. On day 0 and 1 Minimal plates were used, for day 3 LB plates were used. For the trans conjugants

For the meat experiments a Q-PCR was run on all samples to test for the resistance gene for Tetracycline 
(TetA) and to test for the gene for E. coli (UidA). First the samples needed to be cleaned for meat. Two 
different DNA purification methods have been used during this project. First a kit called Qiagen Tissue and 
Blood was used. The used method is described in Appendix XI. Because of problems with Qiagen Tissue and 
Blood kit, it was also tried to use another method that is based on boiling lysis. It is a method that has been 
used on pork tenderloin earlier with success [20]. Both methods were made on overnight cultures, but also 
on meat to be able to make standard curves for further use. The procedure for the standards from Qiagen 
Tissue and Blood kit can be seen in Appendix IX, and the procedure for the standards from boiling lysis can 
be seen in Appendix X.   
When the samples were cleaned and ready for Q-PCR they were tested for the genes UidA and TetA. For TetA

Further possible work on this project 
Improvements for this project could be to work on the set up for Q-PCR, which could make it more durable 
and possible to trust, and look some more on the extraction/purification of the DNA. It could be that there 
have to be used two different cleaning methods depending on if it is plasmid or chromosome DNA that should 
be tested on. It was seen that Qiagen was good for chromosome DNA, but not for plasmid DNA. 
It was also seen in this project that conjugation was not detected in meat at 3 and 13°C. It can be due to the 
selection when using specific plates. It could therefore be useful to work further on the Flow Cytometry, to 
see if there are any bacteria in meat that receive the plasmid, but just not present on the plates. If the results 
stills show that there are not any trans conjugants, it can be said that conjugation does not occur at 13°C or 
under. Then the slaughtering process should be examined to see where else it could occur, where the 
temperatures are higher. 
It could also be an idea to make the meat experiment again and be aware that the plates for E. coli should be 
incubated at 37°C which make it grow faster and is then possible to outcompete most of the other bacteria, 
which makes it possible to follow the decrease or increase of E. coli during the 14 days. 
It could be interesting to try the conjugation experiment with one of the bacteria that earlier in a project for 
the same group has been seen to be able to receive the pKJK10 plasmid at low temperatures in meat. The 
plasmid should then be removed, and then it is known that the specific strain can receive the plasmid. This 
could also give an idea of whether it can happen at 13°C or maybe even at lower temperatures.  
 
Summative discussion 
It has been seen in a study [27]  that the concentration of E. coli and Tet resistant E. coli is decreasing during 
the slaughtering process. After cooling there is no Tet resistant E. coli left. If a conjugation with Tet resistance 
should happen during the slaughtering process from E. coli it therefore has to happen before the cooling. 
This is also in coherent with the results from this project, were it was seen that it does not happen at 13°C or 
under. When looking at the results from the article and the project, the focus on where the transfer happens 
should be before the cooling, where the temperatures also are higher [27]. 
In this study, it was seen that the concentration of Tet resistant E. coli is decreasing in the meat during 14 
days both at 3 and 13°C. The concentration of E. coli has not been easy to follow due to problems with other 
bacteria, but it was seen that the concentration at 3°C was nearly constant. Some of the E. coli are therefore 
losing its plasmid during the 14 days. When looking at the total Tet resistant bacteria on Minimal agar it 
seems to increase in the meat, this could be because of conjugation, but when looking for trans conjugants 
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
there were not found any. The concentration where also increasing in the controls, so it is probably just due 
to growth of Tet resistant bacteria. The reason why E. coli lose the plasmid could be because it is not 
necessary in the meat due to the lack of Tetracycline, and is therefore just a burden to carry at the low 
temperatures.   
It would be expected that the prevalence of antibiotic resistance is higher in conventional pigs than in organic, 
but a study [28] showed that there is no significant difference. It depended more on where in Europe the 
pigs were from. The prevalence was higher in southern Europe than in northern Europe. It could be 
interesting to see what the reason for the difference between the countries is. Is it the antibiotics that are 
used, or is it the difference in the slaughterhouses. Further investigation on this would make it easier to know 
where it is best to do an effort to decrease the prevalence of antibiotic resistance [28]. 
In general there need to be more investigation on where transfer of antibiotic resistance genes occurs and 
what makes a difference for the prevalence of the resistance. This would make it easier to know where to go 
in to defeat the prevalence and where it will have the biggest effect.

Conclusion 
For this project it can be concluded that E. coli MG1655, E. coli MT102 and S. marcescens W225 can grow 
down to 13°C. S. marcescens W225 cannot receive the IncP plasmid pKJK10 that E. coli MG1655 is trying to 
transfer, and the results were therefore all negative. For E. coli MT102 it was able to receive the plasmid 
down to 13°C. 
No trans conjugants were seen in meat at 3 and 13°C when standing for 14 days, but at 37°C several trans 
conjugants were detected already after 1 day, and one of them were analyzed to be a Pseudomonas. It can 
therefore be concluded that E. coli MG1655 is able to make conjugation in meat, it just does not happen at 
13°C or under. 
The results from Q-PCR are not very trustable, and there should therefore be worked on the method for 
extracting and purification of the DNA. 
Over all it can be concluded that conjugation of the plasmid does not occur in meat at 13°C and under.

Escherichia coli MG1655:lacIq-Km with Gfp-marked conjugative IncP plasmid (pKJK10)

Spot test is made for the three bacteria at the concentration 101,102,103 and 104 CFU/ml
One of each of the three concentrations for each bacterium is incubated at 3, 13, 22 and 36°C
Spot test is made for the bacteria standing at 3 and 13°C, to see if they are killed or survived, at the same 
days as OD600 is measured.  
For the 13°C, the following concentration is spotted: 102,103,104 and 105 CFU/ml 
For the 3°C, the following  concentration is spotted: 103,105 and 107 CFU/ml

36°C: 0,1

Appendix XII – Procedure for boiling lysis

The supernatant is decanted and the pellet is diluted in 50µl TE-buffer
The dilution is boiled for 10 min at 95°C
After boiling it is centrifuged for 1 min at 3000g and freezed at -20°C until use
