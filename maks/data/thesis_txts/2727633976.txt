Abstract
In the face of increasing demands for high data rates and low data latency, 5G, the latest
generation of communication technology standardized by the 3rd Generation Partnership
Project (3GPP), has become a cutting­edge technology in the field of wireless commu­
nications. Massive Multiple­Input and Multiple­Output (MIMO) has been introduced as
a key technology due to its ability to fully utilize spatial resources, while Orthogonal Fre­
quency Division Multiplexing (OFDM), which is immune to frequency selective fading, has
also been introduced to support the large­scale capacity and connectivity of 5G. In ad­
dition, beamforming plays a crucial role through shaping and directing the transmitted
signals towards desired users, resulting in mitigating interference by exploiting the spatial
dimensions provided by multiple antennas.
At the same time, the introduction of 5G technology also brings a series of problems, such
as how to handle the increased complexity introduced by the interaction between multiple
antennas. The key to solving all these problems is the accurate mastery of Channel
State Information (CSI), which also requires the research and improvement of channel
estimation and equalization techniques.
Channel estimation is the process of estimating channel characteristics to reduce the im­
pact of channel impairments such as fading and noise, which are essential for the receiv­
ing end to receive the signal accurately. This thesis focuses on two pilot­based channel
estimation algorithms, Least Squares (LS) and Minimum Mean Square Error (MMSE).
Equalization is the process of correcting the distorted signal so that the true value of the
transmitted signal can be restored as much as possible. This thesis focuses on the linear
feedforward algorithm MMSE and the nonlinear feedback algorithm Least Mean Squares
(LMS). In the LMS algorithm, optimal performance is obtained by constantly adjusting the
number of decision feedbacks.
In practical scenarios, analyzing an algorithm involves not only performance, computa­
tional complexity and latency also play vital roles. This thesis analyzes and compares
different channel estimation and equalization algorithms from the above three perspec­
tives in both Single­Input and Single­Output (SISO) and Multiple­Input and Multiple­Output
(MIMO) systems and proposes two combinations of channel estimation and equalization
algorithms with the aim of finding a balance between throughput and both computational
complexity and latency for better implementation in practical applications.

Introduction
In this era of rapid information development, the field of mobile wireless communication
technology is rapidly moving into a new phase of advancement. Following the transfor­
mative revolution and evolution witnessed in the 4th and 5th generations, the emergence
of 5th generation mobile communication technology marks a major milestone in wireless
communication. Its arrival signifies a substantial leap in the overall quality of communi­
cation services. With the explosion of mobile data traffic overwhelming the capabilities of
4G technology, the primary objective of 5G technology is to improve both services and
the technology itself. The goal of the 5G network is to improve upon the existing architec­
ture and establish a new generation of broadband mobile communication technology that
exhibits remarkable attributes such as high speed, low latency, and extensive connectiv­
ity[1].
1.1
Introduction to 5G Mobile Communication System
1.1.1
5G Key Technologies
The advancement of each successive generation of communication systems brings along
with it new technologies, often built upon existing ones, while also incorporating full uti­
lization and innovation. The introduction of key technologies in 5G communication serves
as the foundation for achieving optimization goals within the current communication land­
scape. These technologies play a crucial role in enabling fast communication services in
the new era, among which massive Multiple­Input and Multiple­Output (MIMO), Millime­
ter Wave (mmWave) communication, and beamforming have become current research
hotspots.
• massive MIMO
Following the introduction of 5G in the realm of wireless communication, one key
technology that stands out is undoubtedly massive MIMO. The term ”massive” refers
to the significantly larger number of antennas, reaching hundreds or even thou­
sands, surpassing the antenna count in 4G systems. Building on the foundation
of 4G MIMO technology, massive MIMO incorporates dozens or even hundreds of
additional antennas. This enables the deployment of a greater number of wireless
devices within the same spatial area, reducing network load while offering advan­
tages such as high gain, robustness against interference, and improved spectral
efficiency through the utilization of multiple antennas[2]. In order to enhance the
quality of user services within 5G networks, massive MIMO technology plays an
unparalleled role, and its achievements can be attributed to the following factors.
• mmWave Communication
As the term suggests, electromagnetic waves with a wavelength in the millimeter
range are referred to as mmWave. These waves have emerged as a promising
choice for next­generation communication systems. Typically operating within a
frequency range of 30 GHz to 300 GHz[3], mmWave offers expanded spectral band­
width, enabling ultra­high­speed wireless data transmission.
The primary objectives of 5G communication systems encompass enhancing data
rates (up to 20 Gbps) and providing capacity with ultra­low latency (1 millisecond).
Additionally, 5G aims to achieve high reliability, remarkable flexibility, and improved
device­to­device communication. These goals have led to the exploration of the

• Beamforming
Beamforming leverages multiple antennas to focus radio waves in specific direc­
tions, thereby creating narrow beams that enhance signal strength and quality. This
is achieved through two main techniques: analog beamforming and digital beam­
forming. Analog beamforming utilizes phase shifts in the antenna array to form
directional beams, while digital beamforming employs complex signal processing
algorithms to control the amplitude and phase of individual antenna elements.
The adoption of beamforming in 5G networks brings a plethora of benefits. First and
foremost, it enhances spectral efficiency by allowing simultaneous communication
with multiple users in different directions. Beamforming also mitigates the effects
of interference and multipath fading, resulting in improved signal reliability and cov­
erage extension. Additionally, it enables efficient utilization of network resources,
reduces power consumption, and enhances overall network capacity.
While beamforming offers immense potential, it also poses certain challenges. These
include beamforming algorithm optimization and hardware implementation complex­
ities. Researchers and engineers are actively exploring innovative solutions to ad­
dress these challenges and unlock the full potential of beamforming in 5G and be­
yond. Future research directions include adaptive beamforming, hybrid beamform­
ing architectures, and beamforming in dynamic and mobile scenarios[4].
Beamforming is a pivotal technology in 5G networks, revolutionizing wireless com­
munication by enabling highly focused and efficient signal transmission. Its abil­
ity to enhance coverage, capacity, and reliability makes it a critical component for
meeting the demanding requirements of modern communication systems. As the
deployment of 5G networks progresses, further advancements and optimizations in
beamforming techniques are expected to shape the future of wireless communica­
tions.

demands for ultra­high data transmission rates and substantial data volumes. 5G serves

less communication technology. It goes beyond being a mere infrastructure project and

5G Challenges
The development of any new advancement is inevitably accompanied by new challenges.

1.2
Research Significance
Based on the previous section, we can deduce that many of the key technologies in 5G
are advancements built upon the foundations of previous systems. Notably, massive
MIMO technology represents an enhanced approach that addresses the limitations of
existing MIMO technologies that have reached their performance saturation point. This
technique demonstrates potential in terms of improving system performance and enhanc­
ing resource efficiency, thanks to the implementation of a multi­antenna configuration at
the base station. However, the substantial increase in the number of antennas also in­
troduces a range of qualitative changes, thereby presenting new challenges for channel
estimation and equalization techniques within the 5G system.
The fundamental technologies of massive MIMO encompass channel modeling, channel
estimation, precoding design, and the acquisition of Channel State Information (CSI)[2].
Massive MIMO systems maximize the utilization of spatial resources by employing multi­
ple antennas at the transmitter. However, they encounter challenges in mitigating the ad­
verse effects of various fading phenomena, including frequency selective fading and mul­
tipath fading. To overcome these challenges, Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplex­
ing (OFDM) technology is employed as a multi­carrier narrowband transmission method.
OFDM divides the original channel into mutually orthogonal sub­channels, facilitating the
conversion of data streams from serial to parallel. By transmitting data over narrowband
sub­channels, the signal’s transmission rate is reduced. The orthogonality between these
sub­channels significantly minimizes interference and enables efficient utilization of the
available spectrum resources. Additionally, beamforming plays a crucial role in enhancing
the performance of MIMO­OFDM systems. Beamforming, a signal processing technique,
directs transmitted energy toward the intended receiver while suppressing interference.

By adjusting the amplitude and phase of the signals on each antenna element, construc­
tive interference is achieved at the receiver, while destructive interference is created in
undesired directions. The combination of massive MIMO’s ability to provide substantial
gains and high capacity with OFDM’s capacity to mitigate fading greatly enhances system
performance. Massive MIMO enhances spatial multiplexing gain and increases system
capacity, while OFDM mitigates the adverse effects of multipath and frequency selective
fading. Furthermore, beamforming optimizes signal transmission in specific directions,
further improving signal quality and reducing interference.
However, in order to fully exploit the aforementioned advantages of the system, accurate
acquisition of CSI at the transmitting end is a crucial prerequisite. Channel estimation
technology serves as the means to extract CSI and forms the foundation of signal detec­
tion. Despite the popularity and adoption of OFDM and beamforming techniques in current
standards, it is important to note that their combined benefits may not completely compen­
sate for severe performance degradation caused by the inherent instability and non­ideal
characteristics of the transmission environment. Several factors increase the complexity
of receiver design, necessitating channel estimation for capturing relevant system param­
eters. The accuracy of the channel estimation results directly governs the performance
of the entire signal processing system. The system’s operation can be categorized into
two modes: time and frequency. In a Time Division Duplexing (TDD) system, uplink CSI
can be obtained based on previously placed pilots. Reciprocity allows for the direct ac­
quisition of downlink CSI without introducing additional pilot resources[6]. Conversely, in
a Frequency Division Duplexing (FDD) system, the absence of reciprocity between uplink
and downlink necessitates the use of pilot signals for channel estimation in both direc­
tions. However, for massive MIMO systems, limited by the number of antennas, pilot
signals become a resource­intensive requirement. Consequently, seeking new channel
estimation algorithms that can achieve effective estimation results with a limited number
of pilots represents a valuable direction for future optimization in massive MIMO systems.
Equalization technologies target unstable channel characteristics. Due to the presence
of noise and the random nature of channel properties, the received signal waveform often
becomes distorted. The purpose of equalization is to reverse the distortion caused by the
channel, making it a crucial step in establishing a resilient wireless communication sys­
tem. Equalization involves compensating for the entire transmission channel. It requires
designing different structures to accommodate various channel characteristics and mit­
igate the issue of Inter Symbol Interference (ISI) that arises during communication[7].
Adaptive equalizers play a significant role in this process by continuously and automat­
ically adjusting internal parameters based on output results. This adaptive adjustment
enables the equalizer to effectively adapt to the current channel conditions, ensuring real­
time optimization and achieving an ideal compensation effect. It is important to note that
accurate CSI is a prerequisite for the effective performance of equalizers, and the two
aspects mutually reinforce each other in achieving the desired results.

formance of channel estimation and equalization methods has become increasingly chal­

losses associated with the channel, numerous viable algorithms for channel estimation

1.3.1
Channel Estimation
In a massive MIMO system, whether it operates in TDD or FDD mode, the large number
of antennas presents significant challenges related to pilot management. Additionally,
the utilization of mmWave technology results in a low Signal­to­Noise Ratio (SNR), fur­
ther complicating the task of channel estimation. Currently, channel estimation methods
in wireless communication systems can be broadly classified into three main categories:
blind channel estimation, non­blind channel estimation, and semi­blind channel estima­
tion, which combines aspects of the former two. The distinction between blind and non­
blind methods lies in the incorporation of reference signals within the overall estimation
algorithm.
While blind channel estimation eliminates the need for pilot symbols and overhead con­
cerns, it relies on estimating unknown channel parameters using the statistical character­
istics of the received and transmitted signals. However, this approach is time­consuming
and prone to instability due to the involvement of unknown information. Given these limi­
tations, non­blind channel estimation has gained popularity as a more favorable algorithm
for channel estimation. Non­blind channel estimation requires the insertion of predefined
reference symbols in the transmitted signal to detect the unknown characteristics of the
channel. Notably, two representative algorithms in this category are the Least Squares
(LS) algorithm and the Minimum Mean Square Error (MMSE) algorithm[8]. The LS al­
gorithm is more susceptible to channel noise, leading to suboptimal estimation results.
Conversely, although the MMSE algorithm comes with higher complexity, it significantly
enhances the accuracy of channel estimation.
In conclusion, the majority of channel estimation algorithms face challenges such as
significant pilot overhead and complex optimization requirements. Moreover, as next­
generation wireless communication systems demand high­quality services, the need for
accurate channel estimation has become increasingly stringent. Therefore, there is am­
ple opportunity for further research and improvement in channel estimation algorithms to
meet these evolving requirements effectively.
1.3.2
Equalization
In wireless communication systems, the transmitted signal encounters various types of
losses, with ISI being particularly detrimental. This interference leads to significant shifts
and distortions in the signal’s amplitude and phase, resulting in a notable increase in the
system’s Bit Error Rate (BER); therefore, mitigating its effects is crucial for maintaining a
stable level of communication quality.
Equalization at the receiving end is an effective approach to mitigate the effects of ISI. By
incorporating an adjustment mechanism within the communication system, the received
signal can be corrected through equalization. Equalization technology can be broadly
categorized into two types: frequency­domain equalization and time­domain equaliza­
tion, each targeting different aspects of signal correction. Frequency­domain equaliza­
tion compensates for channel amplitude frequency and group delay characteristics using
frequency­based properties. It leverages the spectral properties of the received signal to
equalize channel distortions and mitigate ISI. On the other hand, time­domain equaliza­
tion corrects channel characteristics from a time­response perspective. It focuses on the
received signal and applies appropriate equalization techniques to combat ISI and im­
prove overall signal quality. With the rapid advancement of digital signal processing and
the development of ultra­large­scale communication systems, frequency­domain equal­
ization has emerged as the predominant method for equalization. Its ability to handle

complex channel conditions and effectively mitigate ISI makes it well­suited for modern
communication systems.
Equalization can be categorized as linear or nonlinear based on the operations they em­
ploy and the presence of feedback or feedforward mechanisms. Linear equalizers typi­
cally utilize linear processing operations and may or may not incorporate feedback. Non­
linear equalizers, including both feedback and feedforward types, employ non­linear oper­
ations to enhance the equalization process. Linear equalizers, such as Zero Forcing (ZF)
and MMSE[9], have a relatively simple structure. They are commonly used in scenarios
where the level of ISI and channel fading is not severe. However, their performance may
be limited in challenging channel environments. Nonlinear equalizers, such as Decision
Feedback Equalizer (DFE) and Maximum­Likelihood Sequence Estimation (MLSE)[10],
are capable of maintaining good performance even in the presence of severe channel
impairments. These algorithms leverage feedback mechanisms to improve equalization
by considering past symbol decisions. However, the increased complexity of nonlinear
equalizers must be carefully managed to avoid potential instability caused by transmission
delays.
Given that the channel is a dynamic and ever­changing environment characterized by
fading and interference, the equalizer needs to adapt to these characteristics to achieve
effective compensation. This necessity has driven the development of adaptive equaliza­
tion technology, which enables the equalizer to dynamically update its parameters based
on real­time channel conditions. Through this adaptive process, the equalizer automati­
cally adjusts to changes in the channel, ensuring optimal performance. By continuously
adapting to the evolving channel, adaptive equalization mitigates distortions caused by
the varying channel conditions. It aims to restore the distorted portion of the received
signal, thereby reducing the BER within a narrower range.
1.4
Chapter Arrangement
The content of this thesis is organized into six chapters, and the topic focuses on channel
estimation and equalization technology. Theoretical derivation, performance simulation
verification, computational complexity and latency analysis, and practical application sug­
gestions are carried out for different algorithms in these two technologies.
The specific thesis structure is arranged as follows:
The first chapter is the introduction. The background of the thesis, which is on a 5G wire­
less communication system, including its key technologies and challenges, is introduced.
In order to address these issues, channel estimation and equalization technologies are

Massive MIMO technology capitalizes on scarce spatial resources, offering the poten­

it brings about qualitative improvements. The presence of multiple antennas enables
better resistance against fading and noise, significantly enhancing anti­interference ca­
pabilities. In the 5G era, massive MIMO technology represents a significant technological
breakthrough, enabling more dependable transmission and faster data rates without com­
promising bandwidth or power consumption.

2.1.2
OFDM technology
OFDM Technology Principle
OFDM is a modulation technique that employs multiple carriers[14]. It has gained widespread
adoption in wireless communication systems due to its ability to combat frequency­selective
fading channels. The underlying principle of this technology involves ”splitting” and ”slow­
ing down” the transmission signal. This is achieved through the conversion of the sig­
nal from serial to parallel using several subcarriers, enabling parallel transmission while
reducing the overall transmission rate of subsignals. Notably, the subcarriers possess ex­
cellent orthogonal characteristics, which helps mitigate interference among them. Through
comprehensive analysis, it is evident that OFDM technology is a highly viable modulation
technique that effectively counters fading in the channel by utilizing orthogonal subcarri­
ers.

2.1.3
Massive MIMO­OFDM System
The impact of multipath effects and frequency­selective fading on overall communication
cannot be underestimated. While massive MIMO technology can leverage the benefits of
multipath effects to mitigate their negative consequences and address related challenges,
it falls short when it comes to frequency­selective fading. On the other hand, OFDM tech­
nology excels at mitigating frequency­selective fading by decomposing the channel into
several narrow­band sub­channels, effectively converting it to flat fading[16]. However,
OFDM technology requires a larger bandwidth ratio due to the increased number of sub­
carriers needed to enhance the transmission rate of the communication system. Massive
MIMO technology, by increasing the number of antennas, enhances channel capacity
within the same bandwidth resources and significantly improves the spectral efficiency of
the entire system. This makes it highly complementary to OFDM technology. Therefore,
the combination of massive MIMO and OFDM systems becomes imperative.
In a massive MIMO­OFDM system, the data transmitted across numerous antennas un­
dergoes a series of signal processing steps, including serial and parallel conversion, fol­
lowed by modulation using OFDM. Upon reaching the receiving end, the signal is demodu­
lated using OFDM. This integration allows massive MIMO technology to achieve superior
transmission performance on flat channels, with both technologies mutually enhancing
each other’s capabilities.

Channel Estimation
In any communication system, the transmission of signals relies on channels. In the case
of time­varying wireless channels, channel estimation becomes crucial to ensure accu­
rate reception and recovery of the original signal at the receiver. The channel estimation
algorithm involves studying the input signal through mathematical analysis to derive rel­
evant channel parameters. Typically, estimation algorithms are categorized as non­blind
estimation, semi­blind estimation, and blind estimation. Non­blind channel estimation al­
gorithms are widely adopted in many communication systems due to their real­time effec­
tiveness and high accuracy in reconstructing the input signal. This study aims to simulate
and compare two prominent non­blind channel estimation algorithms to gain deeper in­
sights into their performance, advantages, and disadvantages.
3.1
Pilot Design for Channel Estimation
The effectiveness of channel estimation is influenced by the quantity and positioning of
pilots. To enhance channel estimation, several methods for pilot placement are intro­
duced. In a massive MIMO­OFDM system, under ideal channel conditions, L positions
can be freely chosen as pilots within the channel, allowing us to subsequently obtain the
estimated channel value. In general, the positions of the pilot symbols are equally spaced.
3.1.1
Pilot Classification
Fading channels are the most common in practical applications. Given the higher com­
plexity in the time domain, this article focuses on introducing the technique of inserting
pilot symbols in the frequency domain. Various insertion methods lead to different pilot
structures, and the choice of pilot structures directly impacts the accuracy of channel es­
timation. Three primary types of pilot structures are identified: block pilots, comb pilots,
and two­dimensional pilots. In the illustrations, data subcarriers are depicted as white
circles, while pilot subcarriers are represented as black circles.
From the schematic diagram depicted in Figure 3.1, it is evident that the pilot informa­
tion exhibits continuity in the frequency domain while being discrete and periodically dis­
tributed in the time domain. The block pilot’s distribution characteristics render it par­
ticularly advantageous in scenarios involving frequency selective fading, making it well­
suited for channels with relatively gradual variations over time, known as slow fading
channels. With the block pilot, there is no need for interpolation to obtain channel infor­
mation, thereby reducing system complexity.
Based on the schematic diagram shown in Figure 3.2, it can be inferred that the comb
pilot structure exhibits a contrasting pattern to that of the block pilot, making them com­
plementary. The comb pilot structure demonstrates continuity in the time domain while
being discrete and periodic in the frequency domain, thereby offering distinct advantages
in scenarios involving time­selective fading. Due to its continuous nature and tracking ca­
pability, the comb pilot structure is better suited for fast­fading channels. However, when
opting for the comb pilot structure, it is essential to ensure that the subcarriers of the
channel remain unchanged in frequency. Furthermore, utilizing the comb pilot structure
necessitates interpolation at the receiving end to acquire channel information, resulting in
increased system complexity.

3.2.2
Spline Interpolation
Spline interpolation is a more sophisticated interpolation method that aims to provide
a smoother and more accurate estimation of values between data points. It employs
piecewise­defined polynomial functions to interpolate the data. The method divides the
data into smaller segments and fits separate polynomials to each segment, ensuring a
continuous and smooth transition between neighboring segments. This allows spline in­
terpolation to capture more intricate variations in the data and provide a more accurate
representation of the underlying trend. The most commonly used type of spline interpola­
tion is cubic spline interpolation, where cubic polynomials are used to interpolate the data
points.
Cubic spline interpolation is a type of spline interpolation that utilizes cubic polynomials
to estimate values between data points. It offers a higher degree of smoothness and
accuracy compared to linear interpolation. In cubic spline interpolation, the data is divided
into smaller segments, typically between adjacent data points. Within each segment, a
cubic polynomial function is constructed to fit the data points and ensure continuity at the
boundaries. The cubic polynomial has the form:

To construct the cubic spline, several conditions are imposed on the polynomial. These

• Interpolation: The spline must pass through all the given data points to ensure that

• Continuity: The spline and its derivatives must be continuous at the boundaries of

4
Equalization
In a wireless communication system, the quality of the transmitted signal received at the
destination can be severely degraded due to the unstable nature of the wireless channel.
Moreover, the transmitted signal encounters various detrimental factors, such as ISI and
Doppler frequency shift, which often impose limitations on data throughput. To achieve
high­quality wireless communication services and enable higher data transfer rates, it is
crucial to mitigate the losses induced by the channel. The main technical challenge lies
in counteracting the signal distortion caused by the transmission channel between the
wireless transceivers. Equalization serves as the primary technology to combat the effects
of ISI. Therefore, a wireless communication system requires an equalizer to compensate
for channel influence and restore the originally transmitted data. Currently, numerous
equalizers have been proposed and implemented, capable of alleviating ISI and even
adapting to the time­varying characteristics of wireless mobile channels. By employing
these equalizers, the reliability of the entire system can be significantly improved.
4.1
Equalization Technology
The fundamental principle of equalization involves processing the signal arriving at the
receiving end after channel transmission. This processing is essentially the opposite of
the channel’s characteristics and can be seen as a form of reverse filtering. The purpose of
equalization is to counteract signal distortion caused by imperfect channels and ultimately
restore the received signal’s accuracy. This section will provide an introduction to the
underlying theories of equalization, including the operational principles of equalizers, their
categorization, and their respective characteristics.

4.1.1
Equalization Classification
Frequency Domain Equalization and Time Domain Equalization
The equalization technique plays a crucial role in compensating for the attenuation caused
by the transmission channel, thereby enhancing the overall communication quality. In
digital signal transmission systems, equalization techniques can be categorized into two
domains based on their processing approach[20].
26
Channel Estimation and Equalization for a 5G Channel
==PAGE:39==
Frequency domain equalization involves compensating for the frequency characteristics
of the communication system using filters. By applying frequency domain equalization,
the system aims to achieve a distortion­free state.
On the other hand, time domain equalization focuses on correcting the distorted signal
waveform in the time domain. This correction aims to eliminate ISI and enable the entire
system to achieve an ISI­free state, thereby improving the system’s impulse response[21].
Linear Equalization and Nonlinear Equalization
Equalization techniques can be categorized into two main types: linear equalization and
non­linear equalization. This categorization is based on whether the output of the equal­
izer is utilized as a feedback mechanism for further adjustment. It is important to note
that there are also non­linear feedforward equalizers that employ non­linear processing
techniques.
Linear equalization is an equalization that operates as a linear function of the received
samples. It means that the signal vector at the receiving end is processed linearly to
obtain the output value of the signal. The output of the equalizer is not used in the whole
equalization process and the overall architecture is relatively simple. Commonly used
algorithms are ZF and MMSE.
Non­linear equalization involves the use of non­linear processing techniques to compen­
sate for channel distortions. Unlike linear equalization, non­linear equalizers sometimes
consider the output of the equalizer as feedback to further adjust the equalization process
when determining the output value of the signal. Commonly used non­linear equalizers
include DFE and MLSE. It is important to note that there are also non­linear feedforward
equalizers that employ non­linear processing without utilizing feedback. These non­linear
feedforward equalizers leverage advanced signal processing techniques, such as neural
networks, to capture and compensate for complex channel distortions.

4.3
Adaptive Equalization Algorithm
Linear equalizers, despite their benefits in simpler implementation and low computational
complexity, have limitations when it comes to effectively mitigating severe channel distor­
tions and accommodating dynamic channel conditions due to their fixed tap coefficients.
Consequently, in communication scenarios with poor channel quality, the use of an equal­
izer capable of tracking time­varying channels becomes crucial. Nonlinear equalizers,
with their feedback filtering modules, address this limitation of linear equalizers. How­
ever, the presence of feedback introduces the issue of error propagation, where devia­
tions in previously calculated results continue to impact subsequent decisions. Consider­
ing these factors, an adaptive filter emerges as a more promising option. Adaptive filters
enhance overall equalization performance by dynamically adjusting the tap coefficients of
the equalizer in real­time.
4.3.1
Adaptive Equalizer Model
In wireless communication systems, it is crucial for equalization to effectively handle dis­
torted signals in various challenging environments. However, the wireless channel is
characterized by its variability and instability, constantly undergoing random fluctuations.
Therefore, an exceptional equalizer must possess real­time capabilities and be able to
adjust its coefficients promptly. An equalizer that meets these requirements is commonly
referred to as an adaptive equalizer[22].
Adaptive equalizers, in contrast to traditional linear equalizers, exhibit a notable advan­
tage by acquiring real­time estimates of channel statistics. By continuously monitoring
the received signals, adaptive equalizers are able to dynamically update their parame­
ters and adapt to the ever­changing conditions of the communication environment. This
adaptability enables them to effectively compensate for the uncertainties introduced by
unknown and time­varying channels, ensuring reliable and robust communication.

4.3.2
Decision Feedback Equalizer
Among various adaptive equalizers, DFE is one of the most widely used adaptive equaliz­
ers in digital communication systems to mitigate the effects of ISI[23]. The DFE operates
by utilizing feedback from the previous decisions to cancel the interference caused by
previously transmitted symbols. It adapts its tap weights based on the error between the
received signal and the desired signal, optimizing the equalization performance over time.
This feedback mechanism helps to improve the accuracy of symbol detection and mitigate
the distortions introduced by the channel.
The DFE is commonly used in communication systems where ISI is a significant concern,
such as wireless channels with multipath fading or high­speed data transmission scenar­
ios. Its ability to effectively cancel out the interference caused by previous symbols makes
it a valuable tool for improving the overall system performance and mitigating the impact
of channel distortions.

5.2.3
Hardware Suggestion
In the discussion of the MMSE equalization algorithm, SVD is introduced as a decom­
position matrix in order to reduce the computational complexity. However, SVD can be
challenging due to requirements for its accuracy and memory in hardware systems. For­
tunately, there are alternative methods that can be used as replacements for SVD. One
of the commonly employed techniques is QR decomposition.
QR decomposition, based on the Gram­Schmidt process, decomposes a matrix into an
orthogonal matrix Q and an upper triangular matrix R[27]. It provides a computationally
efficient way to factorize a matrix, and it has been widely adopted in hardware imple­
mentations for various applications. One specific algorithm for QR decomposition is the
Householder QR algorithm, which offers numerical stability and high performance[28].

5.4
Practical Application Suggestions
In practical applications, the choice of channel estimation and equalization algorithms
depends on the specific scenario and requirements. Different approaches to channel
estimation and equalization offer varying trade­offs between performance, computational
complexity, and latency. The MMSE channel estimation algorithm is known to deliver
superior performance by considering noise and interference, but it comes at the cost of
Channel Estimation and Equalization for a 5G Channel
43
==PAGE:56==
higher computational complexity. On the other hand, the LS channel estimation algorithm
offers lower complexity but may exhibit reduced performance in the presence of noise and
interference. Similarly, the LMS equalization algorithm provides improved performance
compared to MMSE equalization, but it also involves much higher latency.
For instance, in scenarios where high accuracy is crucial and computational resources
are abundant, the MMSE channel estimation algorithm can be a suitable choice. It ex­
cels in scenarios with severe interference or dense multipath propagation, such as urban
environments or highly congested wireless networks. Conversely, in scenarios where
computational resources are limited or real­time processing is essential, LS channel esti­
mation may be more appropriate. LS channel estimation performs well in scenarios with
relatively low interference and simpler channel conditions, where the additional compu­
tational complexity of MMSE channel estimation is not necessary. It finds applications in
scenarios like rural areas with less congested wireless networks or cases where real­time
processing and low­latency communication are critical, such as live video streaming or
gaming.
In this case, two combinations of channel estimation and equalization algorithms are pro­
posed. To ensure the balance among performance, computational complexity, and la­
tency, one approach uses MMSE channel estimation and MMSE equalization, and the
other approach uses LS channel estimation and LMS equalization. In this way, the per­

6.1
Conclusion
With the rapid improvement of wireless service quality and the rapid increase in demand
for wireless mobile devices, 5G has built a wireless communication environment with
unimpeded communication and ultra­fast data transmission. At the same time, there are
some challenges, such as large spectrum resource requirements and high system energy
consumption, which have become severe problems for 5G. Therefore, if the 5G wireless
communication system wants to become a universal network communication technology,
it needs effective channel estimation and equalization technology to do so.
As we all know, due to the characteristics of the wireless channel, the signal arriving at
the receiving end is usually distorted. In order to restore the original data transmitted, the
channel must be effectively estimated and compensated on the receiving end. These two
technologies have become one of the main challenges for 5G communication systems.
Therefore, this thesis conducts comprehensive research on different channel estimation
and equalization algorithms, including theoretical derivation, simulation verification, com­
putational complexity evaluation, latency analysis, and practical application suggestions.
The main work can be summarized as follows:
First, the current 5G communication system is introduced in detail, including the key tech­
nologies introduced compared with the previous communication systems and the new
challenges brought by various new technologies to 5G.
Secondly, different pilot design and interpolation algorithms and two pilot­based channel
estimation algorithms, LS and MMSE, are introduced, and theoretical analysis, formula
derivation, and comparison of the advantages and disadvantages of these two channel
estimation algorithms are carried out.
Next, the working principle, classification, and characteristics of different equalizers are
introduced. Then two different equalization algorithms, MMSE and LMS, are introduced,
and theoretical analysis, formula derivation, and comparison of advantages and disad­
vantages are carried out.
Finally, all the channel estimation and equalization algorithms mentioned above are com­
prehensively analyzed and compared from three perspectives: performance, computa­
tional complexity, and latency. In terms of channel estimation algorithms, the performance
of the LS is lower than that of the MMSE, but the computational complexity of the LS is
lower than that of the MMSE in both SISO and MIMO systems. In terms of equalization
algorithms, the performance of MMSE is lower than that of LMS, but the latency of the
MMSE is so much lower than that of the LMS in both SISO and MIMO systems. In the
same channel estimation or equalization algorithm, the performance of MIMO is gener­
ally higher than that of SISO and the computational complexity and latency also increase
accordingly. In addition, in order to balance performance, computational complexity, and
latency when analyzing an algorithm, two methods that are more suitable for practical
applications are proposed. The first is a combination of LS channel estimation and LMS
equalization, and the second is an MMSE channel estimation and MMSE equalization
combination. The performance of these two combinations is very close, the first method
has higher latency and the second method has higher computational complexity. Either
Channel Estimation and Equalization for a 5G Channel
47
==PAGE:60==
method can be implemented in the practical application based on the specific require­
ments.
6.2
Future Work
There are three specific aspects that require further attention in the thesis.
Firstly, the performance of different algorithms in this thesis is all simulated on a MIMO
system rather than a massive MIMO system. This is because the current limitations of the
MATLAB simulation software restrict the exploration of massive MIMO, as it only allows
for up to four spatial stream layers. To truly investigate the potential of massive MIMO,
it is essential to overcome these limitations and conduct simulations with a larger num­
ber of spatial stream layers, enabling a more accurate representation of massive MIMO
characteristics.
Secondly, both of the channel estimation algorithms introduced in this thesis heavily rely
on pilot resources, which leads to limited spectral efficiency and lower throughput. To
address this issue, it is crucial to delve deeper into developing more optimized channel
estimation algorithms that strike a balance between accuracy and resource utilization.
Exploring innovative approaches, such as compressive sensing[29], will be valuable in
enhancing the performance of channel estimation.
Furthermore, the implementation of the DFE poses challenges due to the need to test
different decision feedback intervals under varying channel conditions. To tackle this, fur­
ther investigation should involve conducting comprehensive experiments to determine the
optimal decision feedback interval for different channel scenarios. This empirical analysis
will provide insights into the behavior of the DFE and facilitate the development of adap­
tive strategies for determining the decision feedback interval based on real­time channel
conditions.
In addition to these focal points, there are other avenues for future work worth consid­
ering. For instance, exploring advanced signal processing techniques, such as hybrid
beamforming[4], can contribute to enhancing the performance of massive MIMO systems.
By addressing these aspects, future work will advance the field of massive MIMO in my
thesis by overcoming limitations in current simulations, optimizing channel estimation al­
gorithms, refining the implementation of DFE, and exploring new technologies to unlock
the full potential of massive MIMO systems in practical applications.
