Abstract
As the presence of technology continuously increases, it becomes more important for
elementary education to cover the subject of technological understanding. An attempt
to educate students in elementary schools all over Denmark was done by the Danish
Ministry of Education in 2018-2021 with the experiment "Tekforsøget". They found
that the subject was relevant but they were unable to crack the code of how to design
fitting material. This thesis is another attempt at designing an educational resource,
taking the findings of Tekforsøget into account. The goal was to make an educational
game that was easy for teachers to apply, thanks to an accompanying course plan and
the features of the game, and motivating for students to engage with. The main method
used to guide the design of the educational resource was the double diamond model.
Through desk research, a semi-structured interview, and analysis of the state-of-the-
art tools that teachers use to teach technological understanding, key components of
the educational resource were identified. To develop these key components the Scrum
framework, the motivators and design principles explained by Laine and Lindberg, and
the Godot engine were used. The development of the resource was split into four sprints,
validating the current increment of the resource at the end of each sprint. Testing the
product through questionnaires, user tests, feedback, and statistical evaluation, the
results showed that the educational game was motivating for the students and that the
lessons were manageable for the teacher to facilitate. Furthermore there were indications
that it was possible to prepare a 90 minute lesson in less than 45 minutes, and that
the resource had a positive impact on the student’s understanding of "if-then-else"
statements. However, no significant impact was proven. Overall, this thesis contributes
to the field of teaching technological understanding, through the design, development
and validation of an educational game and course plan.

Introduction
Between 2018 and 2021, the Danish Ministry of Education conducted an experiment
called Tekforsøget, involving 46 elementary schools across Denmark [Tek]. The purpose
of this experiment was to address a significant gap in elementary education: the subject
of technological understanding. This gap is a problem because technology has become
a big part of society and several parts of life require a certain level of technological
understanding.
As part of Tekforsøget, they introduced a four way partition for teaching technological
understanding. This partition consists of: Digital Empowerment, Digital Design and
Design Processes, Computational Thinking, and Technological Agency. These topics
were taught both as a stand-alone subject and integrated into existing subjects. Eval-
uation of the experiment revealed that both students and teachers found the subject
matter relevant and engaging. However, teachers also reported two major challenges:
the extensive time required to prepare teaching materials and the difficulty of provid-
ing students with meaningful, constructive feedback due to the lack of clearly defined
learning objectives [Con].
The goal of this Master’s Thesis is to address these challenges by designing an edu-
cational resource that is both easy to apply and provides concrete learning objectives.
The resource focuses on computational thinking in particular, with an emphasis on the
concept of "if-then-else" statements. The aim is to create a resource that helps teachers
prepare lessons within the allotted time and motivates students to engage with the ed-
ucational game. We will achieve this engagement by following the a framework created
for educational game design [LL20]
Using an iterative design process involving user testing and feedback, we will develop
and validate an educational game and an accompanying course plan. The overall goal
is to investigate how such an educational resource can be designed to support learning
outcomes and be practical to implement in a teaching setting.

Methodology
2.1
The Double Diamond
The report will be structured based on the Double Diamond Model [Hum]. The model
is composed of two diamonds representing the problem space and the solution space,
respectively. Both of these diamonds are split into two phases resulting in the four phases
Discover, Define, Develop and Deliver. The Discover and Develop phases are diverging
phases and the Define and Deliver phases are converging, resulting in a design process
that switches between diverging and converging. We will work iteratively throughout our
project, which means that we will jump back and forth between the Develop and Deliver
phases of the solution diamond. The solution diamond in this project is encompassed
in the Scrum framework. This is described in section 2.2.
The Discover phase diverges to explore the problem space by expanding upon existing
knowledge on the subject and discover problems regarding the subject. We will conduct
desk research and a semi-structured interview to further explore the problem space.
This phase will be documented in section 4.
The Define phase converges towards a final problem statement by scoping the gained
knowledge.
Based on our research in the discover phase, we will decide the design
specifications of our product. This phase will be documented in section 5.
The Develop phases diverge by exploring the solution space to develop solutions for the
identified problem. We will do this through brainstorming, user story mapping and risk
assessment. These phases will be documented in section 6, 7, 8 and 9.
The Deliver phases converge by validating the product. The validation will be performed
through user testing and statistical evaluation. These phases will also be documented
in section 6, 7, 8 and 9.

2.2
Scrum
As for our development framework, we will utilize Scrum [SS]. Scrum is an agile and
lightweight framework that is designed to support teams develop their product iter-
atively. It is structured around clearly defined roles, events, and artifacts that help
organize the work and enables the team to continuously improve their product over
time. The Scrum team consists of three distinct roles; "Product Owner", "Scrum Mas-
ter" and the "Development Team". The product owner represents the stakeholders and
acts as a bridge between the stakeholders and the development team, ensuring that
the team is always working on the most valuable features by defining the goals and re-
quirements of the product. The Scrum Master establishes Scrum in the team. It is the
Scrum Master who ensures that everyone understands Scrum theory and practice and
performs as effectively as possible. The Development Team is responsible for building
the product and delivering a working increment at the end of each sprint.
In Scrum, the sprint is defined as the container for all other events. A sprint is a time-
boxed iteration, which typically lasts 1-4 weeks. Each sprint begins with a planning
session called the "Sprint Planning" where goals for the current iteration are set. Then
each day of a sprint has what is called "Daily Scrum". Daily Scrum is a short stand-
up where the team plans the work and focus of the day. At the end of each sprint two
events are held; a "Sprint Review" and a "Sprint Retrospective". In the sprint review the
3
==PAGE:11==
27/6 - 2025
sprint’s results are presented to the stakeholders and the progress towards the product
goal is discussed. In the sprint retrospective the scrum team inspects how well the last
sprint went and plans what to do in order to increase quality and effectiveness. The
sprint retrospective is the final event of a sprint and defines the end of the sprint.
The artifacts of Scrum are the product backlog, the sprint backlog and the increment.
The product backlog is a prioritized list of everything that might be needed to improve
the product. This backlog is refined throughout the time span of the product where
the items in the backlog are broken down into more precise items. Items that can be
implemented by the team within one sprint are considered ready for selection in a sprint
planning event. The sprint backlog is a subset of the product backlog that the team
focuses on completing during the current sprint, as well as a plan for how the increment
is delivered. Like the product backlog the sprint backlog is updated during the sprint
as the team learns more about the product. The increment is the output created during
a sprint. An item cannot be part of an increment without meeting its "Definition of
Done". When an item from the product backlog is considered done in regards to the
definition of done, an increment is created. It is possible to create multiple increments
during a sprint, however they have to work together and with all prior increments.
We will adapt the Scrum framework to suit our two-person team by sharing each of
the three roles’ responsibilities collaboratively.
This means that both of us have to
contribute with creating goals for the product, creating tasks for the product backlog
that will allow us to reach said goals, and complete these tasks through development.
We will work in sprints varying from 2-4 weeks. To ideate features for our product,
brainstorm how to test our increments and produce the items and goals for our product
backlog and sprint backlogs, we will make a Miro board. To keep track of both our
product backlog as well as our sprint backlogs we will use ClickUp [Click], which is a
cloud-based project management tool used to organize projects. With Clickup we can
create multiple KanBan boards, one for each sprint, as well as an overall calender for
our project to keep track of deadlines. At the start of each sprint we will refine our
product backlog and add new goals to it. We will follow this up with a sprint planning
session, where we select the tasks that we want to complete during that sprint. For our
daily Scrums we will discuss what tasks we want to work on individually that day. We
will develop some of the tasks through pair programming because we have to make sure
that we agree on the implementation. At the end of each sprint we will combine our
increments through GitHub and ensure that the increments work as intended. We will
then test the product and discuss the results, representing the sprint review and the
sprint retrospective. The tests of the sprints will consist of different methods based on
the goal of that sprint. This includes surveys, user-tests, questionnaires, user interviews
and statistical evaluation. Based on the results and feedback, we will plan what to
improve and what features to add. These points will be added as items and goals in the

Background
3.1
Tekforsøget
Tekforsøget [Tek] was an extensive experiment regarding the implementation of teaching
technological understanding in the Danish elementary school. They taught technolog-
ical understanding through a four way partition: Digital empowerment, digital design
and design processes, computational thinking, and technological agency. They defined
digital empowerment as being able to examine the possibilities and consequences of
digital artifacts in a critical, reflective and constructive way. Digital design and design
processes was defined as the students’ abilities to plan and execute iterative design pro-
cesses, taking future use contexts into account. Computational thinking concerned the
students’ abilities to decode and describe phenomena and processes from their every-
day life, academic contexts, and in digital artifacts, through algorithms and models.
Finally, technological agency was the topic regarding how computers work, how com-
puters communicate through networks, security against hackers as well as programming.
Tekforsøget introduced technological understanding as a subject on its own as well as in-
tegrated it in already existing subjects. They did this by creating an exhaustive quantity
of lesson plans with associated educational resources. The experiment was performed
by multiple university colleges together with 46 danish elementary schools from 2018 to
2021.
From the definitions of the four topics of technological understanding we decided that
we wanted to focus our work on computational thinking. In the preparation of the
project we reached out to two middle school science teachers from Thomasskolen in
Skovlunde. Therefore we knew that we would be working with middle school students.
Because of this the relevant results of Tekforsøget regarding the students’ development
in computational thinking was that of middle school students. We also disregarded the
results regarding whether technological understanding fit in other subjects in elementary
school and disregarded the students self-assessment results. The percentages presented
below are the amount of teachers who agreed with the statements to a high degree or a
very high degree.
Tekforsøget [Con] found that in regards to the educational staff’s experience of the
subject only 31% agreed that the topics of technological understanding were under-
standable for them. 19% agreed that the topics of technological understanding were
translatable to concrete teaching and 21% agreed that it was possible to translate the
topics to concrete teaching. Only 20% agreed that the subject fit the students’ level of
understanding.
In regards to the educational staff’s assessment of the course plans and materials 54%
agreed that they could use them to plan their teachings in technological understanding
6
==PAGE:14==
27/6 - 2025
and 47% agreed that the materials made it possible for them to teach technological
understanding in agreement with the description of the subject. 51% agreed that these
course plans contributed to improve the students’ technological understanding, however
only 31% agreed that they could assess the overall teaching of technological understand-
ing.
In regards to the educational staff’s self-assessment regarding their own competences
and knowledge in technological understanding 38% agreed that they could complete
their teaching in technological understanding. 36% agreed that they were able to plan
the teaching in technological understanding and only 27% agreed that they were able
to evaluate the teaching in technological understanding.
In regards to the middle school students’ motivation when working with technological
understanding 44% of the educational staff agreed that the students were motivated.
The results of the middle school students’ improvements in the computational thinking
tasks showed that they scored 11 out of 25 in 2019 and 13 out of 25 in 2020. Tekforsøget
deemed this a statistically significant difference with a p-value of p < 0.05, where
N = 361.
From these results, Tekforsøget concluded that the teachers struggled to give proper
feedback to the students because of their own lack of expertise regarding the topics and
because the goals of the tasks presented were too open-ended. For some of the topics
this also resulted in the teachers not being able to help all the students who needed
help. In addition to this, the time the teachers spent preparing the lessons exceeded
the allotted time. Lastly, Tekforsøget found indications towards motivation being an
important factor for the students’ learning of the subjects, however they could not
conclude that motivation caused learning.

4.1
Motivation
Knowing the importance of motivation from the results of Tekforsøget, we wanted to
know how to motivate students to engage with our educational game.
For this we
searched for a framework to guide us through the development of our educational game.
To find such a framework we searched for "design motivating educational game" on
Google Scholar. This resulted more than 600.000 articles. To reduce this number, we
assessed that the search algorithm would show the most relevant articles first. For this

4.3
Zone of Proximal Development
The theory of ZPD [Vyg78] explained the that there are three zones of challenges. The
first zone where a learner can complete the tasks without any help, the second zone
where a learner can complete the tasks with external intervention and the third zone
where the learner simply cannot complete the tasks. Vygotsky argued that to develop
ones knowledge and skills, a learner must complete the tasks in the second zone, which
he named the Zone of Proximal Development. According to Vygotsky’s theory, when a
learner completes tasks in their ZPD they will eventually be able to complete the tasks
individually, having raised their ZPD. An illustration of the theory can be seen in figure
2 below.
9
==PAGE:17==
27/6 - 2025
Figure 2: An illustration of the Zone of Proximal Development
The theory of ZPD is also mentioned in the article "Motivation and its Relationship to
the Design of Educational Games" [Bix06], which we read when researching motivation
in section 4.1. In this article they explained that following from the theory of ZPD, de-
signers of educational games had to be aware of the challenge level of the tasks presented
in the game. The designer should ensure that the tasks are within the player’s ZPD,
meaning that the game should provide the external intervention needed for the player
to succeed. By designing the game with built-in guidance we would be able to reduce
the amount of help the teacher needed to provide. This would reduce the workload of
the teacher as well as increase the player engagement, since the students would not have
to wait for the teacher to help them, should the task prove too challenging.

5.1
Motivators
One the main reasons we chose to design the material as an educational video game
was the fact that video games are associated with fun. This in itself should motivate
the students. The framework designed by Laine and Lindberg [LL20] provided us with
explicit motivators and design principles to consider when designing our educational
game. Based on the results of Tekforsøget, the requests of the teachers and our analysis
of the State-of-the-Art tools, we decided which motivator classes to use in the design of
our own educational game.
During the semi-structured interview F, the teachers both mentioned the theory of the
Zone of Proximal Development and their own experience that if challenges are too hard
the students will quickly lose interest. This made the challenge class an obvious choice.
This class refers to the challenges introduced in the game being suitable for the player’s
skill level. Presenting the player with such challenges can improve their self-esteem. It
is tightly connected to the classes feedback and rules & goals which are explained below.
Different players will perceive challenges differently but making the challenges feel fair
will be a strong motivator.
As the main goal of our game is to improve the player’s knowledge, the next obvious
choice of motivator class was the competence class. This class refers to achievements,
advancements and knowledge improvement. In many games competence is perceived as
reaching a higher level. Suitable challenges are important for developing such compe-
tence, as these will also motivate players to improve and reach the goals set for them by
the game, their teacher and themselves. Completing the later, more challenging, levels
will let the player feel that their problem solving skills and knowledge have improved.
Based on the choices of the first two classes, the rules & goals class was our next
inclusion. This class refers to the importance of making the rules clear and the goals
achievable to the player. The rules are the boundary set for the player, determining
how they can engage with challenges and reach the goals. The goals should be clear and
worth striving for as this will let them motivate the player. We would also be aware of
the relationship of rules and goals in order to make sure they supported each other and
caused neither contradictions nor confusion.
The feedback class is an important link between challenge and rules & goals. This class
concerns how providing clear and relevant feedback on the challenges performed by the
player can be a great motivator. To show the player that they completed a challenge
according to its rules, clear feedback is needed. It is also important to help the player
realize what went wrong in the case of failure. This along with clear rules and goals
alleviates one of the main problems identified in Tekforsøget, namely, that teachers
14
==PAGE:22==
27/6 - 2025
found it difficult to provide feedback for their students.
In combination with ZPD, social interaction is extremely important when it comes to
knowledge development. Based on this we wanted the built-in guidance to represent a
social interaction, as well as emphasize the fact that we did not attempt to replace the
teacher, but rather assist them. This led to us choosing the fantasy class. This class
refers to the use of role-playing, narrative and visual experiences to motivate the player.
By introducing a character to be the guide of the game, and by providing a narrative
that the player can partake in, we can facilitate an engaged learning experience.
One of the main concerns the teachers had with the State-of-the-Art tools were that
many of them had overwhelming and complex controls.
Based on this we included
the control class. This class refers to the inputs of the player and their influence on
the game. Rather than directly motivating the player, this class was more related to
avoiding frustration and discouragement. In order for the player to focus on completing
challenges and achieving their goals, the controls should be a tool to get there and not
another obstacle they need to overcome.

Final Problem Statement
As our contribution, we designed, developed and validated an educational resource,
consisting of an educational video game and a supporting course plan. The goal of the
resource was to teach students the concept of "if-then-else" statements.
Based on our research and our design specifications, we formulated the following in-

• Research question (RQ1): How might we design the educational game such

explained by Laine and Lindberg [LL20] will motivate the students, as measured
by how fun, frustrating, and challenging they find the game.
• Research question (RQ2): How might we design the resource such that it is

In particular we want to test if a succinct course plan, that defines learning ob-
jectives, specific goals for the tasks of the game, and a summary of the solutions
will allow teachers to prepare for a 90 minute lesson within 45 minutes.
• Research question (RQ3): How might we design the educational game to keep
the amount of help the students need at a manageable level?

In particular we want to test if built-in guidance and an appropriately scaling
challenge level, will keep the amount of assistance the teacher needs to provide at

• Research question (RQ4): How might we design the educational game such

Sprint 1
The purpose of this sprint was to develop a viable initial concept. We wanted to make
sure that the students would be willing to play the game, meaning that we wanted them
to be motivated. For this reason we developed a demo consisting of three levels, which
represented the core mechanics and motivators of the game. To develop the demo we
needed a game engine.
It takes time to create a game engine so in order to have as much time as possible
to develop, we chose to use an existing game engine. The game engine we picked was
Godot.
Godot is a free and open-source 2D and 3D game engine which excels at user-friendliness
and cross-platform compatibility, allowing games to be exported to multiple platforms,
including Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, iOS, and web browsers [Godot]. Godot
has a built-in logging system where it creates a log from everything being printed to
the console. Godot uses a node-based system where nodes are structured in a hierarchy
and more specialized nodes inherit from the base nodes. Nodes can be organized and
grouped into scenes which can be instantiated, inherited from, nested inside each other,
and reused in other scenes. Nodes communicate through signals which can transmit
data across objects. The game loop is controlled by a scene tree, which both manages
the nodes inside of scenes, the scenes themselves, and connections between scenes. The
scene tree is a hierarchy of scenes and nodes and the whole scene tree represents the
game. Scenes are designed through the scene editor, a screenshot of which can be seen
in figure 6 below.
19
==PAGE:27==
27/6 - 2025
Figure 6: Godot’s scene editor. Nodes are listed on the left, the scene is shown in the
middle and node properties are shown on the right.
Coding in Godot can be done in the built-in scripting language called GDScript, which
is inspired by Python and designed to integrate seamlessly with the engine. However,
Godot also supports programming languages such as C++ and C#. We used GDScript
as our programming language of choice.
One of the main reasons why we chose Godot as our game engine was that it was user-
friendly and we valued this highly because we had not done much game development
before this project. The built-in logging system and the simplicity of exporting ones
game would save us time that we could instead use for developing and testing game
features. Since the game engine was very popular it was easy to get help either through
forums or Godot’s official Discord server whenever problems arose.
We utilized Godot’s node signals and scene nesting to loosely couple game elements
such as UI boxes, buttons, and game components, reducing the amount of hard-coded
dependencies. This also made it possible to use smaller scenes as building blocks for
bigger scenes increasing the flexibility and reusability of game components. We used
scene instancing to automatically generate game components based on the scene scripts.
We utilized inheritance to enable scripts to inherit from bases which made it simpler to
add and modify multiple scenes. As for the UI we utilized abstraction to simplify the
UI and hide computational tasks that only required essential user input. The general
development principle we followed throughout the development of the educational game
was Keep It Simple Stupid (KISS). It was important when working with limited time
that we kept the code as simple as possible and avoided complex functions as these

For the initial concept our primary goal was to create an educational game that players
would not only learn from but also genuinely want to engage with. Simply presenting
educational content would not suffice. We needed to make the experience enjoyable and

To brainstorm and ideate we made a brain writing board in Miro. Brain writing is

the same time frame. In our brain writing session we had multiple categories concerning
choices that we needed to make for our game, such as art style, mechanics, game type
etc. We did two rounds of five minutes each, while discussing the ideas after each round.
We then voted for our favorite ideas in each of the categories. After voting we expanded
upon the top ideas and ended up with more refined concepts. Our brain writing board
can be seen in figure 7.

To guide our design decisions for the initial concept we used the framework by Laine
and Lindberg [LL20], which we expanded on in section 5.1. This framework was useful

learn which features motivated players and which caused frustration.
We designed the game to be played on school computers, meaning laptops provided by
the schools. We learned from the semi-structured interview that most schools either
have this or require students to have their own computers. In terms of DP 6 (input
modalities) we wanted the game to be playable entirely with the use of mouse, track
pad or touch screen, to keep the controls simple and easy to use. In order to follow DP
8 (Consistent controls) we wanted to get the controls right from the start. This meant
that we designed the two main interfaces, one using arrows and the other using drop-
down menus. The arrows should be familiar to any child who has played a few video
games and we hoped that drop-down menus would be familiar as well, thus following DP
9 (Familiar controls). In order to follow DP 10 (Simple interactions) we chose for the
cars to drive on their own and simply use the arrows to choose their final destinations,
as this would reduce the complexity of controlling cars in the arrow levels. To follow
DP 5 (Repeatable challenges), we added a restart and home button to let the player
retry levels.

==PAGE:32==

By simplifying the controls as much as possible we shifted the focus of the challenge from
a skillful performance to designing a solution to the problem. The next important step
was to make sure that the player understood what problem they were trying to solve,
which lead to DP 25 (Clear goals). We did not want players to have to read the rules of a
level and be unsure about what they had to do. We wanted the challenge to be creating
a solution, not simply understanding the rules. We also used DP 31 (Representation
of learning content). By embedding the learning content directly into the rules and
problems we kept the technical language to a minimum, only using "if", "then", and
"else" since these are the exact terms we wanted to teach the player. Together, the clear
goals and simple controls let us focus on DP 29 (cognitive challenges), by challenging
the players problem solving skills and not their ability to read or click fast.
Since this was the initial concept it was important to gauge what level of challenge we
should aim for in order to best motivate players. DP 1 (Varying difficulty) reminded
us that different players would require different difficulties to be entertained the most,
so we aimed for the first three levels to reflect this. We tried to make the second level
harder than the first, to let the test show us if we knew what would be difficult. We still
wanted the tasks to be simple, to accommodate for DP 2 (Simple tasks). Therefore, the
main difference between the levels was that the rules of the first level were based on the
color of the car to make it immediately visible. The rules of the second level were based
on its relative position on the screen to require more thought. This new way of thinking
about the cars was an attempt to follow DP 4 (New challenges). However, this DP was
hard to evaluate in such a short demo. The shift from arrows to drop-down menus was
the second main change we used to motivate the player. To take DP 3 (Enough time)
into account we also wanted players to have enough time to complete the levels, such
that they could succeed and feel victorious. Since the game was designed to be used in
a school setting, there would be a natural time limit. However, as designers we made
sure that there were no intrinsic time limits.
To present the player with the rules, the goals and the feedback, we designed the char-
acter "Hjælpe Jens" as a helpful supervisor showing the player the ropes in their new
job of parking cars to appease the unnamed boss. Doing this follows DP 50 (Providing
a fantasy context). The goal of this was to engage the player and give them a reason
to perform well, namely impressing "Hjælpe Jens" and the boss. Since two of the three
levels introduced new controls, we prioritized space in the UI to explain the controls
with text as seen in figure 10. This was done to follow DP 21 (Tutorials and introduc-
tions). In terms of DP 22 (Immediate feedback), the text box showed the player when
a level was complete, and when the player had made an error.

6.2.1
Tests
The goal of the initial concept was to design a demo that could motivate players through
the features of the game. In the testing of this first version of our game, we focused on
the motivators mentioned above, as well as some questions we wanted answered before
moving on with the first iteration.
• In terms of control we tested if it was intuitive and easy to use the controls to
navigate the game scenes. We also tested if the player could apply a solution to
the problem once they understood the problem.
• In terms of competence we tested if the gameplay, narrative, and visual elements,
supported the learning content of the game.
• In terms of rules & goals we tested if the problems were formulated in a way that
was easily understandable.
• In terms of challenge we tested to see if the problems were fittingly challenging
for the players.
• In terms of feedback we tested if all the information was conveyed properly to the
player, such that they knew what was going on, and how to solve the levels.
• In terms of fantasy we did not specifically test whether the participants liked the
narrative, as we thought it would be too time consuming to change direction away
from the parking lot theme.
To address this we made and conducted a UX target table test and a think-aloud test.
The UX target table [HP18] is a quantitative test where a table is designed and then used
to test a program. The table contains the user class, meaning the user who performs
the tasks and answers the questions posed in the table. Each row of the table has its
26
==PAGE:34==
27/6 - 2025
own high level UX goal to be tested. A measure is then chosen to determine how well
the program fulfills the UX goal. A measuring instrument is also chosen. This can be
a task or a question for the user. A metric of what is being measured, such as the time
it takes to perform the task, must also be chosen for the measure in order to evaluate
it properly. Finally a baseline and a target level is determined. The baseline will often
be based on a previous iteration. The target is the value that determines a success.
In our case we had no previous versions, so we omitted the baseline level and instead
determined a target level by performing the tasks on our own to see how quickly it could
be done. We knew the mechanics and the solutions and we wanted to test "ease of first
time use", so we added some extra time and errors and ended with the target levels
seen in table 1.
To test the game using the table, we contacted a few friends and asked them to par-
ticipate. We had 6 participants do the UX target table test. We had them share their
screen in order to track and time their progress. We then went through the table and
asked them to perform the tasks one at a time. Finally we asked them three questions
and noted any additional remarks they had.
During and after completing the tasks of the table, we noted any feedback that the
participants shared.
The Think Aloud Data Collection Technique [HP18] is a qualitative validation technique
where a participant completes tasks while they express their thoughts and impressions
out loud. This lets developers better understand how the task is perceived and how the
user feels during the task in regards to the UX design, their expectations, likes and dis-
likes. The technique is simple to use and only requires the participant to verbalize their
thoughts. The technique is especially useful when evaluating user task performance,
however, it is also useful when participants walk through a prototype or assist with a
UX inspection.
We conducted two think-aloud tests with two female participants in their mid to late
twenties. The participants walked through the game by completing four tasks: Start
the game and play the first level, solve the second level, solve the third level, and exit
the game. We asked the participants to talk about how they felt during the tasks and
express any confusion or confusing features in the game.

6.2.3
Discussion
In the think-aloud tests the participants did not say every thought and feeling that
came to their mind. The reason for this might be that the participants were not used to
this way of expressing themselves and that we were not clear enough in our instructions
as to how the participants should communicate during the test. This could have been
mitigated with a test-run before the actual test so the participants could get used to
thinking out loud [HP18].
Along with the average amount of errors the participants made completing levels 1-3
being significantly higher than what we had hoped for, it was clear to us that the rules
of the levels were too ambiguous and the problems too difficult for first time users. This
was in conflict with DP 2 and 25 and we added to the backlog that we should simplify
the tasks and make clearer goals.
The lack of feedback was a flaw in the design and following DP 22. To alleviate this
problem, we added the goal of improving feedback to the product backlog. We also
wanted to let the player keep track of how far they had come through a given level.
We used several different kinds of text boxes, some with buttons and some without.
This was in conflict with DP 8, since it was inconsistent and thus confused some players.
Reducing the amount of different places we added text would also force us to reduce
the amount of text and make it more concise. The consensus about the text being too
vast and poorly phrased made it obvious that we had more work to do in this regard.
In terms of control we added arrow key support and an undo button to the product
backlog. The arrow keys would allow players to use their preferred input in accordance
with DP 6.
The undo button was an important feature since having to restart an
entire level because of a single mistake in the end, might cause frustration and loss of
engagement.
Even though one participant expressed the concern that the theme of a parking game
29
==PAGE:37==
27/6 - 2025
might not be a hit with middle school students, we stuck with the choice due to lack of
time to explore further options. We did however take into consideration that "Hjælpe
Jens" could be presented in a better way, by improving his introduction, relating to DP
50.
Even though the UX target table showed a decent score on the question of visual appeal,
we received many comments that it needed improvement. The high score could be the
result of having our friends as participants.
Based on our testing and observations we added the following goals to the product
backlog.
• Fix the bug where control of the final car is not relieved after parking it.
• Fix the bug where the an error message is show as the final car is on its way to
park.
• Improve the drop downs to reduce confusion.
• Add arrow key support.
• Add an undo button to reduce back tracking.
• Remove the settings button or give it a purpose.
• Reduce amount of different boxes with overlapping functions.
• Improve text in terms of amount, ambiguity and readability.
• Improve feedback when parking cars correctly.
• Improve feedback when completing and failing levels.
• Improve visuals by reducing blurriness

To test the first iteration of the game, we visited one of the fourth grade classes at

In order to get data from the students’ gameplay we used file logging. As mentioned
earlier file logging is built into Godot such that everything that gets printed to the
console during gameplay, whether it is error messages or self-made print statements,
also gets written in a log file. We organized our logs as comma separated value files

"ID", "Time", "Event" and "Value". The ID was only generated and written in a file

our built-in guidance.
We also had in mind to create a formula that could calculate a difficulty score for each

In order for players to feel motivated and engaged, their experience is at least as impor-
tant as what the logs show. To gain more insight in the students’ experience, playing our
game, we looked into different methods of testing games and educational material with
39
==PAGE:47==

children. This lead us to the article by Mari [Mar24] which concerns designing question-
naires for young students in order to evaluate teachers. They developed a framework
comprising five tasks to go through when designing questionnaires for children. The
five tasks include understanding the children, formulating the objectives of the ques-
tionnaire, developing the questions, designing the administration strategy and piloting
the questionnaire.
The semi-structured interview let us learn about the children from their teachers. We
knew that the class was generally quiet and good at focusing, this allowed us to design
enough questions to get the answers we needed. Before we developed the questions,
we formulated the objective: Were the students motivated and were the tips helpful?
With this in mind we developed the questions drawing inspiration from The System
Usability Scale (SUS) [HP18]. Since we designed the questionnaire for children, we did
not use SUS directly due to its generic design and difficult language. The questions were
designed such that each new question would alternate between positive and negative
wording to force participants to consider their answer. Each question was presented as
a statement, which the participant then had to rate based on how much they agreed with
it. In order to avoid neutral answers we designed it such that there were only 4 possible
answers: "Strongly disagree", "Disagree", "Agree" and "Strongly agree". This way we
would force any opinion to be visible even if they only slightly agreed or disagreed.
In terms of administration strategy, we made the questions as simple as possible and
provided images of the UI, when possible, to clarify what was meant by the questions.
We also made the fewest questions that would still get the required answers. This was
to keep item response rate as high as possible. Mari stated that children aged 6 years
and above are prone to answer in ways that are more socially acceptable. To alleviate
this, we assured the students several times during the testing session, that the tests were
for testing and improving the game and not to test the students. For piloting the test
we did not have access to any children in the target group. We had a few acquaintances
go through the questions to make sure others would be able to understand them. The
questionnaire can be seen in appendix D.2.
We conducted a user interview with the teacher to ask a few follow up questions. These
questions were planned in advance with two distinct purposes. Firstly to support the
logs and our personal observations in the class room. Secondly to ask some questions
in order to improve the usability of the game in terms of time and supporting material,
such as a written course plan or a walk through.

The main sources of inspiration for developing the course plan were the existing proto-

The goal of this course plan is to allow teachers to prepare a lesson using the game
within the allotted preparation time. In most danish schools this means 1 minute of
preparation time per 2 minutes of the lesson. Since the science lessons of the school we

Since the data was not normally distributed we made a Mann Whitney U Test [MW47]
to verify whether the difference between the before and after tests was significant. A
Mann Whitney U Test, also known as a Wilcoxon rank-sum test [Wil92], is a non-
parametric statistical significance test, that compares two independent samples that
are not normally distributed. In our case the test was one-tailed, since we assumed an
improvement. The test computes a U-statistic, which represents the number of times
a score in one group precedes a score in the other group when all scores are ranked
together.
From the third grade we got a U-statistic of U = 232. This meant that out of all
361 possible Before–After comparisons (19 × 19), there were 232 cases where the after
result scored higher than the before result. Since 232 is more than half of 361, there

students, especially the third grade students, needed more time to complete the game,

Tekforsøgets results was based on a whole year of teaching where our results were based

different concepts within the subject of computational thinking, where we only tested
the concept of "if-then-else" statements, which was deemed especially challenging in our
semi-structured interview.

on. Therefore we do not know whether the before test is harder than the after test or

Discussion
10.1
Research question 1
We wanted to design an educational game that motivated students, such that they were
interested in the learning contents of the game. From the results of the questionnaire in
the second sprint, we found all of the students either agreed or strongly agreed that they
had fun playing the game. Less than 1
4 of the students agreed that they were frustrated
after failing a level. At least 4
5 of the students were challenged during the game, but not
so soon or so much, that they lost interest, as all but one student completed all but one
of the completable levels. This suggests that designing the educational game using the
framework presented by Laine and Lindberg [LL20], will motivate the students. This
is consistent with prior research by Capecchi [Cap+24], which supports our findings.
Based on the success of the second sprint we did not perform further tests regarding
the motivation of the students, however we kept following the framework in subsequent
sprints. The observations we made in the final sprint supports our findings that the
game was motivating and the students were engaged.
10.2
Research question 2
We wanted our educational resource to both consist of an educational video game and
a course plan, to help teachers prepare their lessons using the game within the allotted
time. From the results of the third sprint, we found that it was possible to prepare a 45
minute lesson in 20 to 25 minutes, using the first iteration of the educational resource.
In the final sprint, we increased the amount of levels in the game by 12 and added color
levels and "else" drop-down menus. We were unable to test the final version of the
course plan, and based on this we are unable to conclude, whether it is possible for a
teacher with no prior experience with the educational resource, to prepare a 90 minute
lesson within 45 minutes. However, the results of the second sprint indicate that the
added material should be enough for a 90 minute lesson, and the results of the third
sprint indicate that, the preparation time should not exceed 45 minutes, even with the
added material. In contrast, Tekforsøget [Con] concluded that teachers were unable to
prepare their lessons within the allotted time, using their course plans and educational
materials, and only 36% agreed to a high degree or very high degree that they were even
able to plan the teaching. Our tests were limited by the fact that we were unable to
find valid candidates to test this particular research question. Because of this and the
fact that we do not have clear results, the indications that the preparation time should
not exceed 45 minutes should be considered cautiously.

==PAGE:73==

10.3
Research question 3
We wanted to design an educational game that kept the help the students needed at
a manageable level for the teacher. From the results in the second and final sprint
we saw that the provided tips were used very little. However, in the second sprint all
but one student completed all but one of the completable levels, and the teacher was
neither stressed nor overwhelmed, by the amount of help the students needed. In the
final sprint, 15 students completed 22 levels in both grades. The teacher spent 1.25
minutes, of the 30 minutes, helping the fourth grade and stated that he was not busy
at all and had plenty of time to help each student sufficiently. The teacher spent 18.16
minutes, of the 30 minutes, helping the third grade students and stated that he did not
feel overwhelmed by the amount of help the students needed and that he had time to
help each student sufficiently. This indicates that the challenge level and the built-in
guidance is enough to keep the amount of help the students need at a manageable level,
even though the tips were not used as much as anticipated. In contrast to our findings,
Tekforsøget [Con] concluded that teachers were not always able to help all the students
that needed help.
10.4
Research question 4
We wanted to design an educational game that had a significant impact on the students’
understanding of the concept of "if-then-else" statements. From the results of the final
sprint, we saw that there were indications towards that the game had a positive impact
on the scores of the third grade students. However, neither of the two grades showed
a statistically significant improvement after the test session. This suggests, that the
game does not have a significant impact on the students’ understanding of "if-then-
else" statements. One explanation for this could be the fact that the students did not
spent enough time playing the educational and reflecting afterwards, for the game to
have the intended impact. Another explanation could be the indications found in the
second sprint, that the challenges of the game were too easy for the students because
of the minuscule use of tips. This would mean that the challenges were not in the ZPD
of the students, which further suggests that they did not raise their ZPD and expand
their knowledge. In contrast, Tekforsøget [Con] found that the middle school students
they tested showed a significant improvement in their computational thinking. If we
had been able to perform a within-subject test we would have been able to provide more
convincing results.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to design, develop and validate an educational resource,
consisting of a motivating educational game and a succinct course plan, that would
allow Danish middle school science teachers to teach their students the concept of "if-
then-else" statements. The teachers should be able to prepare their lessons within the
allotted time and they should be able to guide their students when they needed help.
Through desk research, conducting a semi-structured interview, analyzing the state-of-
the-art tools, performing user tests, and statistical evaluation, we designed, developed
and validated a educational resource.
The results of this thesis implicate that basing the design of an educational game on
motivators and design principles, such as the ones presented by Laine and Lindberg
[LL20], makes it more likely that students will want to engage with the end product.
We saw subtle indications that a course plan which provides clear learning objectives,
succinct descriptions and a summary of solutions, enables teachers to prepare a 90
minute lesson in 45 minutes or less. However, we could not conclude on these findings,
based of a lack of valid candidates for testing. Furthermore, we found that designing
the game with fitting challenge levels and built-in guidance will make it manageable for
teachers to assist any students who need help during the lesson. When testing whether
the resource had a significant impact on the students’ understanding of the concept
of "if-then-else" statements, we found indications that there might be an impact, but
it was not statistically significant. Focusing so heavily on the resource being simple,
our results suggest, that we exaggerated and designed an educational game that is not
challenging enough for the students to reach the learning objectives.
In summary, this master’s thesis is a step in the right direction towards designing moti-
vating educational resources, that are simple for teachers to prepare, while being man-
ageable to apply in a classroom setting, when teaching specific concepts of computational
thinking.

Forløbsbeskrivelse 
Forløbet er beregnet til elever, der ikke har arbejdet med programmering før. Ved hjælp af 
spillet Parkeringspladsen skal eleverne danne sig en forståelse af, hvordan 
“hvis-så-ellers”-instruktioner virker, og hvordan de selv kan konstruere instruktioner ud fra 
egne observationer og tanker. 
Spillet og banerne deri bruges som opgaver for eleverne, hvor de, ud fra et regelsæt, skal 
parkere bilerne på de rigtige pladser. Der er forskellige typer af baner, som eleverne skal 
igennem. I den første type bane parkerer eleverne bilerne via pile på skærmen eller ved 
hjælp af tastaturet. I den anden type bane skal eleverne konstruere "hvis"-delen af 
“hvis-så”-instruktioner, som bilerne skal parkere efter. I den sidste type bane skal eleverne 
konstruere både "hvis"- og "så"-delen af “hvis-så”-instruktionerne. I løbet af spillet 
introduceres også "ellers"-udtrykket, der sørger for, at der altid er en instruktion til de biler, 
som ikke er inkluderet i et af "hvis'erne". 
Efter eleverne har gennemført spillet og reflekteret over det gennem fx fremlæggelser eller 
diskussion, er målet, at de opfylder de konkrete læringsmål.

Reflektion 
Det kan være en fordel at gennemgå enkelte baner på et smartboard i plenum, hvis der er 
behov for en fælles introduktion. Her får eleverne mulighed for at dele deres idéer til mulige 
løsninger og forholde sig til andres tanker. 
En idé til efterfølgende refleksion er, at eleverne kan fremlægge og gennemgå deres tanker 
bag løsningen af en bane, efter at de har løst den på egen hånd. En anden form for 
refleksion kunne være, at eleverne selv skal beskrive dagligdagshændelser som 
“hvis-så”-instruktioner – for eksempel: “Hvis det regner, så tager man regntøj på” eller “Hvis 
jeg har en saks i hånden, så må jeg ikke løbe”. 
Spillet 
Spillet foregår på en parkeringsplads, hvor biler skal parkeres efter en række regler, 
udarbejdet af en ukendt chef. Når bilerne er parkeret korrekt, vises et grønt pop-up på 
skærmen, hvorefter eleven kan gå videre til næste bane. Hvis bilerne derimod bliver parkeret 
forkert, vises et rødt pop-up med en beskrivelse af den fejl, der er skyld i, at det gik galt. 
Herefter må eleven prøve at klare banen igen.​
Der er tre forskellige typer baner: pilebaner, “hvis”-baner og “hvis-så”-baner. Hver af disse 
typer har to forskellige slags regler. De første baner af hver type har tekstbaserede regler, 
mens de sidste har farvede parkeringspladser, hvor eleverne selv skal deducere reglerne ud 
fra farverne. 
Pile baner (1-01 til 1-09) 
De indledende baner i spillet har fokus på at introducere spilleren for de begreber og 
koncepter, der benyttes gennem hele spillet.  
Her parkeres bilerne enkeltvis med pilene i UI boksen eller med piletasterne.  
 
UI boksen, som eleven parkerer bilerne med, er vist nedenunder. 
 
Læringsmål 
Målene med pile banerne er at blive bekendt med spillets rammer og at kunne læse og 
forstå reglerne, der præsenteres.​

Forløbsbeskrivelse 
Forløbet er beregnet til elever, der ikke har arbejdet med programmering før. Ved hjælp af 
spillet “Parkeringspladsen” skal eleverne danne sig en forståelse for, hvordan “hvis-så-ellers” 
instruktioner virker, og hvordan de selv kan konstruere instruktioner udfra egne 
observationer og tanker.  
 
Spillet og banerne deri bruges som opgaver for eleverne, hvor de udfra regelsæt skal 
parkere bilerne på de rigtige pladser. Der er forskellige typer af baner, som eleverne skal 
igennem. Den første type bane parkerer eleverne bilerne via pile på skærmen eller via 
tastaturet. I den anden type bane skal eleverne konstruere "hvis" delen af “hvis-så” 
instruktioner, som bilerne skal parkere efter. I den sidste type bane skal eleverne både 
konstruere "hvis" og "så" delen af “hvis-så” instruktionerne. I løbet af spillet introduceres 
også "ellers" udtrykket, der sørger for, at der altid er en instruktion til de biler, som ikke er 
inkluderet i et af "hvis'erne".  
 
Efter eleverne har gennemført spillet og reflekteret over det gennem fx. fremlæggelser eller 
diskusion er håbet at de opfylder de konkrete læringsmål 
Rammer og praktiske forhold 
Klassetrin 
●​ Forløbet er lavet til 3. og 4. klassetrin. 
Varighed 
●​ Forløbet varer ca. 2 lektioner/undervisningsgange, hvor hovedaktiviteten i begge 
lektioner er at spille spillet. 
Materialer 
●​ Projektor/smartboard 
●​ Computere 
●​ Spillet "Parkeringspladsen" 
Lokaler 
●​ Der arbejdes i klasselokalet, eller hvor der normalt arbejdes individuelt med 
computere. 
Videnspersoner 
●​ Der er ikke behov for tredjeparter eller eksterne eksperter.

Reflektion 
Det kan være en fordel at gennemgå enkelte baner på et smartboard i plenum, hvis der er 
behov for en fælles introduktion. Her er det muligt for eleverne at dele deres idéer til mulige 
løsninger og forholde sig til de andres tanker. 
 
En idé til efterfølgende reflektion er, at eleverne kan fremlægge og gennemgå deres tanker 
bag deres løsning til en bane efter at have løst den på egen hånd. 
Spillet 
Spillet foregår på en parkeringsplads, hvor biler skal parkeres efter en række regler 
udarbejdet af en ukendt chef. Når bilerne er parkeret korrekt, vil der være et grønt pop-up på 
skærmen, hvorefter eleven kan gå videre til næste bane. Hvis bilerne derimod bliver parkeret 
forkert, vil der være et rødt pop-up på skærmen, hvorefter eleven må prøve at klare banen 
igen. 
Der er 3 forskellige typer baner: Pile baner, “hvis” baner og “hvis-så” baner. 
Pile baner (1-6) 
De indledende baner i spillet har fokus på at introducere spilleren for de begreber og 
koncepter, der benyttes gennem hele spillet.  
Her parkeres bilerne enkeltvis med pilene i UI boksen eller med piletasterne.  
 
UI boksen, som eleven parkerer bilerne med, er vist nedenunder. 
 
Læringsmål 
Målene med pile banerne er at blive bekendt med spillets rammer og at kunne læse og 
forstå reglerne, der præsenteres.​
 
“Hvis” baner (7-15) 
I "hvis" banerne skal eleverne løse banerne ved at færdiggøre "hvis" delen af en “hvis-så” 
instruktion. Dette gøres ved at vælge et element i drop-down menuerne i UI boksen. Når der 
XXXIX
==PAGE:114==
27/6 - 2025
Emil Wraae Carlsen & Kristoffer Magnus Overgaard 
er valgt et element, kan eleven trykke på startknappen i UI boksen for at sætte bilerne til at 
parkere i forhold til elevens “hvis-så” instruktioner.  
 
UI boksen, som eleven parkerer bilerne med, er vist nedenunder. 
 
Læringsmål 
Målene i "hvis" banerne er at opbygge en grundforståelse for, hvordan en hvis-så instruktion 
er opbygget, og hvordan man kan ændre den og dens virkning. Derudover bygger vi videre 
på elevernes forståelse af reglerne med mere komplekse regler, hvorfra eleven skal 
deducere betingelserne ("hvis"-delen) af en “hvis-så” instruktion. 
 
“Hvis-så” baner (16-18) 
I "hvis-så" banerne skal eleverne selv udfylde "så" delen af instruktionen. Dette er en 
forøgelse af kreativ frihed, som giver eleven mere kontrol over udformningen af deres 
instruktioner. Med denne udvidelse konstruerer eleverne “hvis-så” instruktioner, ligesom man 
ville gøre det i et sprog som Scratch. 
 
UI boksen, som eleven parkerer bilerne med, er vist nedenunder. 
 
Læringsmål 
Målene med disse baner er, at eleverne ved hjælp af “hvis-så” instruktioner kan løse gradvist 
mere komplekse problemer. Derudover bygger vi videre på elevernes forståelse af reglerne 
med mere komplekse regler, hvorfra eleven skal deducere både betingelserne ("hvis"-delen) 
og konsekvenserne ("så"-delen) af en “hvis-så” instruktion.

Interview noter 
●​ Hvordan oplevedes lektionen? 
○​ Der var ro, de fokuserede på spillet 
○​ De havde det sjovt 
○​ Det blev spillet som et spil og ikke som et læringsspil (positivt) 
○​ Viden om at det er et læringsspil sænker motivationen 
○​ Flere gav eksplicit feedback om at de synes det var sjovt 
○​ Tilfredsstillende at gå fra manuel styring til automatisk styrede biler 
 
●​ Virkede børnene underholdt/motiverede? 
○​ Ja, bestemt 
●​ Elevernes humør? 
○​ Godt 
●​ Behovet for hjælp? 
○​ De fleste kunne selv 
○​ De skulle lige huske at læse reglerne for hver bane 
○​ En god idé at skifte parkeringsplads efter hver bane 
●​ Elevernes engagement? 
○​ Meget engagerede, ikke megen uro 
●​ Føltes børnene fokuserede eller ufokuserede, og kan det have noget at gøre med at 
det er nyt og spændende? 
○​ Fokuserede, både pga spillet fangede dem men også fordi det er nyt og 
spændende 
●​ Hvordan passede det tidsmæssigt. Hvad kunne du tænke dig? 
○​ Antal lektioner? 
■​ Det passer ret godt til 1 lektion 
○​ Tid pr lektion? 
■​ Ikke fuld lektion men i hvert fald 45 minutter 
●​ Hvad kan vi gøre for at tilpasse tiden? 
○​ Flere levels eller et endless level for at holde eleverne fokuserede 
●​ Hvad har du brug for ift. forberedelse? 
○​ Beskrivelser af alle baner/samlet beskrivelse? 
■​ Læringsmål 
●​ Behøver ikke være 18 læringsmål til 18 baner 
■​ Løsning til hver bane 
○​ Video vejledning kunne overvejes 
○​ Det skal være nemt at gå til, så ikke alt for meget med mange instrukser og 
sådan. Det er fint for underviseren at se banen for første gang sammen med 
eleverne, programmet selv siger mere end en stor pdf siger. Kort instruktion, 
det er det her vi laver. De første 5 baner handler om det her, de næste 
handler om det her 
○​ Facitliste bagerst i PDF’en kunne være rar at have. 
○​ Snip af UI boxen i stedet for banerne
