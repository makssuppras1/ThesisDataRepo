Abstract 
Originating in Scandinavian building tradition, and serving as storey partitions in dwellings, a 
suspended floor construction of timber joists, in Danish named “træbjælkelag” has been the 
predominant building component for horizontal storey partitions, specifically in Denmark, 
through nearly 400 years. Defined, -in a certain layout, by the second Danish general building 
code classified as BD60, equal to a resistance to fire of 60 minutes (REI 60). 
Though this structures fire rating properties often is questioned by fire safety professionals 
and has been for many years, its classification of BD60 remains. The timber joist structure is 
found classified with even notable variations and still in use to a considerable extent. 
The derivation of the classification, BD60, for the timber joist structure specifically, is sought 
for, but not found to be described in the literature. Terminating the use will not be feasible nor 
sustainable, but data on the structures fire resistance capabilities, would reveal if further fire 
protection measures should be considered, when renovating buildings with these structures. 
By subjecting a representative test specimen to the standard temperature-time curve, ISO 
834-1 standard temperature-time curve1, the aim is to collect a dataset for future reference 
and assess the timber joist structures capabilities when subjected to fire. 
The findings from the performed test, shows that fire protrudes the unloaded tested specimen, 
prior to 60 minutes.  
The heat transfer through the timber joist structure and the impactful contribution of the cavities 
i.e. the structures ´thermal fire endurance´ was an interesting discovery. Equally significant is 
that the test revealed smoke as an issue of great concern to be emphasized and addressed.

Introduction 
Throughout time the method of constructing the timber joist structure has changed to 
accommodate the demands in the concerning building regulations throughout time. In the 
Danish building legislation, the timber joist structure is firstly found described with fire rating 
properties, by the second general building code, covering the entire country of Denmark. 
“Bygningsreglement for Købstæderne og landet 1966”2, which states the timber joist structure 
to have a resistance to fire of 60 minutes, -BD60, in a certain defined layout.  
Up until then, the multi component building element is only found described by its layout and 
given criteria for each component’s properties. BD60 is one of the past Danish national 
classification terms, and still a valid classification of the present. The particular fire resistance 
class, is classified according to national Danish standard DS 1052.13 and used solely in 
Denmark, prior to the European classes of reaction to fire performance was implemented by 
2002. The fire resistance performance criteria for the classification of BD60 is comparable to 
the standardized European performance criteria of EI60, in relation to EN 13501-24.  
The derivation of the classification, BD60, for the timber joist structure specifically, is sought 
for, but not found to be described in the literature. Neither has interviews with professionals, 
practicing in the late 1960th and onwards, led to any further knowledge of actual testing 
conducted.  
The earliest source is found to be, publications done by Dansk Brandværns-Komité, being, 
Brandtekniske meddelelser, Fortryk Nr.-5 in 1933; Brandtekniske meddelelser, Fortryk Nr. 26 
in 1935; Brandtekniske meddelelser Nr.57 in 1937; Brandtekniske meddelelser Nr.7 Forslag II 
8 1939 in 1939. These publications was supplemented by Brandværnshåndbogen af 1929-319 
and Brandværnshåndbogen af 194010, stating that; “Such a floor separation is, if not fireproof, 
nevertheless very resistant and burns through rather slowly, where it is not a particularly 
violent fire”. 9 & 10 
Brandsikring, Bygningsmæssige foranstaltninger11 mentioning actual tests carried out by 
”statsprøveanstalten” and the author,  Børge Johannes Rambøll, herein mentions knowledge 
of numerous accounts of actual fires penetrating a BD 60 classified storey partition, and by 
these failing significantly prior, compared to results found in the test lab. The results from these 
tests have been searched for, through the Danish national archive, but not obtained, by this 
proven to be a very time-consuming task.  
Vidar Stenstads PhD, Eldre murgårdar og brann12 mentions the Norwegian structure similar 
to the timber joist structure, “Stubbloft” as having a fire resistance of 40-60 minutes, as well 
as a test conducted in 1953 for “Stubbloft” exposed to the ISO 83413, resulting in termination 
of the test at t=29min. 
Brandtekniske fejl og mangler i bygninger14 mentions the timber joist structure with an 
assumed fire resistance of approximately one hour in reference to “normalbrandprøven” 
(standard temperature-time curve), with no further reference. Since the globally acknowledged 
temperature-time curve was introduced in 1917 and published by National Bureau of 
Standards – NBS in 1921, it is reasonable to assume that “normalbrandprøven” correlates to 
this, known today as ISO 834-1 standard temperature-time curve1.

Scope and extent of application 
Due to growth in the population and people relocating to larger cities, the increased demand 
for housing, led to the building of more than 100.000 new apartments in Copenhagen, in the 
early 1900th, according to Information om bygnings-bevaring15. To further establish the extend, 
of the use of timber joist structures in present time, and only considering flats situated in 
Copenhagen, an estimation using data from Table 23 from SBI rapport 25316 stating that 
approximately 360,000 flats, was erected prior to 1960 and still in function by 1995, when the 
report was published. The flat sizes concerned in the study, range from 32-227m2, with an 
average of 73m2. The 73m2 per flat, is found as a reasonable average in a general perspective, 
corresponding to a typical two-bedroom apartment. Considered up against the 360,000 flats, 
it results in 26,280,000m2 or 26.3km2 storey partitions, in the greater Copenhagen area alone.

by 1960, whereof 270,000 was established prior to 1950. These are distributed as shown in

Building statute of March 29th, 1939 for Copenhagen 
Since the instatement of the first building statute, concerning all of Copenhagen, Bygningslov 
for Staden Kjøbenhavn og dens Forstæder20 (1856), the timber joist structure, has been 
revised by the following building statutes, Bygningslov for Staden Kjøbenhavn18 (1871), 
Bygningslov for Staden Kjøbenhavn af 12. April 188921 and up until Byggelov af 29. Marts 
193919 being the latest of the statutes concerning only Copenhagen.  
The offset is chosen as §38 of Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919, but concludes for the most part, 
buildings erected in accordance with the statues above, since all state a great resemblance in 
the matter of storey partitions and regarding the general layout of the timber joist structure.

Throughout all these statutes, the insertion of a stomped clay substrate off minimum 2 inches 
thickness is equally described, but the extend of the implementation has been extended over 
time, ruling out some exceptions described in the earliest statutes, leading to Byggelov af 29. 
Marts 193919 stating that all storey partitions, regardless of use, shall be fitted with the insertion 
of a stomped clay substrate, hereby including storey partitions, adjacent to addicts and lofts. 
"It was not until Copenhagen's Building Bylaws of 1939 that the requirement for clay substrate 
became completely precise: »In all wooden joist layers, including sub-bases and taper joists, 
clay substrate must be placed«". Københavnsk Etageboligbyggeri 1850-190022 
Components in the timber joist structure  
The general layout as depicted in Figure 1, are further explained below by each component. 
 
Figure 1 - Timber joist structure layout. 
1. Flooring 
The flooring by type, is defined in Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919 as, minimum 22mm hardwood 
or 31mm softwood, relating to a span between the joists of 1m c/c. According to Byggebogen 
bd. 123, softwood flooring of 22mm can be used when the joist´ has a maximum distance, of 
0.75m, c/c. Husbygningslære II - tømrerarbejde24 describes 5/4” thick by 6-7” wide boards for 
the main living areas and perhaps narrower boards for adjacent rooms. These are attached to 
the joist´s by 3 ½” headless nails. 
2. Clay substrate 
The statue, vedtægt om ildebrand af 1. juni 154925 is the first known description of an added 
clay substrate for storey partitions in dwellings. Insertion of stomped clay substrate is first seen 
described in the Copenhagen building legislation, as a demand, after a greater fire in 1795. In 
the, Placat af 4. Juli 179526 (law) it was stated that a clay substrate had to be placed within 
the structure, or as an alternative, bricks could be laid out, to the same extent of clay substrate. 
The origin of the usage of clay, is described as more customary practice due to its availability, 
rather than ascending from the clays properties as such, nor by intent from the legislators. The 
clay being dug out of the construction pit and stored for usage on site, meant that only the 
labour cost of digging and hand-carrying the clay, added to the total cost of construction. By 
1939 the guilds of the craftsmanships are described as being very influential on the regulatory 
methods of constructing and the low cost of clay usage is described as the primary incentive 
for its use.

layer, laid out with a water content of maximum 8 percent of off the dry weight.

dried out powdery state. In SBI rapport 14222 the clay substrate is said to be laid out, in the

the clay. It’s a common finding during renovations, to discover all sorts of debris, within the

af 29. Marts 193919. 
3. Wood board insertion 
The insertion of wood boards for upholding the clay substrate can be done in two ways, as 
described in Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919, as well as Byggebogen bd. 123, Vejledning I 
husbygningskunst30, Husbygningslære II - tømrerarbejde24 among others. The wood boards 
are commonly described to have a 1” thickness and are typically 4” wide. They are to be laid 
out with no gaps, and resting in the groove, or on the lath. These are not found described as 
being nailed to the construction.

for the wood board to rest in. The height of the groove is found described as 1” and the depth 
is found described as 1” or 5/4”, but often depicted with far less depth. Carrying out the groove 
has been very time-consuming; thus it is described as the most common practice due to the 
low labour cost. For joist´, less than 7”*7”, is described the placement of an lath, for the wood

4. Joists 
In SBI rapport 14222, pg. 106, its described that through the 1700th to the early 1900th the 
declarations in the Danish building regulatory has knowingly described its minimum demands 
of height and width of the Joist´, with regards to the Swedish inch being smaller than the 
Danish. Following the Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919, an idealized Swedish inch of 2.5mm, was 
introduced.  
This means that measures described in the earliest building regulatory´ would be of less height 
and width than in present terms, since Joists are mostly seen as worked Swedish lumber. An 
example would be, Swedish measure is equal to 0.946 of the Danish inches of that time, 9” x 
0.946 = 8.514”, or the official Danish inch measure as of Meterloven af 4. Maj 190731 
26.1545mm * 0.946 = 25mm. 
Husbygningslære II - tømrerarbejde24 describes that all other trades, -besides carpentry, in 
the Danish building industry, shifted to the meter system by the introduction of Meterloven af 
4. Maj 190731, and the use of the Swedish inch was discontinued for timber products. Measure 
of inches in this thesis, is all regarding the idealized Swedish inch of 25mm. 
The maximum distance between the joists was set to 1 ½ alen ≈ 1.0m. In Vejledning I 
husbygningskunst30, this is described as 3 feet / 36 inches c/c = 0.93m. Byggebogen bd. 123 
states that the distance is dependent on the flooring, within a varying span of 0.75-1m c/c. 
Husbygningslære II - tømrerarbejde24 defines the criteria set by Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919, 
as the mean distance being 1m c/c, but allows for variations within 0,95-1.05m c/c.

5. Wood board for ceiling 
The wood boards for the ceiling are generally described as ¾” or 21mm, though it is found 
described as 1” in Husbygningslære II - tømrerarbejde24, based on solidity. Byggebogen bd. 
123 tells that they are to be nailed on to the joist, by 90mm nails, 2 per each board intersecting 
the joist. 
6. Straw spread 
In Husbygningslære - Murarbejde27 The straw material is described as weaved straws, 
weaved with a galvanized steel thread for every 150mm, and nailed to the underlaying wood 
boards by large headed nails, placed alternately on each side of the steel thread, for every 
100-150mm.  
The straw material is placed perpendicular to the wood boards. The butt joints are to overlap 
by 30-50mm. Further durability can be obtained, by placing yet another layer of straws 
perpendicular to the first layer. This is not done on the specimen for testing since the object of 
interest is the described minimum structure for obtaining the classification of 60 minutes. 
7. Ceiling plaster 
The ceiling is plastered with a mortar plaster, based on a 0-4mm chalk-mortar mixed with a 
small amount of cement in a ratio of 1:12. In Husbygningslære - Murarbejde27 the process is 
described as follows: Firstly, a thin layer of chalk-mortar is applied, by throwing the chalk-
mortar onto the structure, to secure a firm adhesion. Whenever this layer is fully dried, the 
second and full layer of mortar is applied. Lastly a thin layer of 0-1mm chalk-mortar is applied 
for finishing purposes.

History of storey partitions role in fire safety in a general perspective. 
Throughout the world there has been a lot of great fires through time, with severe damages to 
the concerning societies. The severity of these fires was for one part, due to the general 
building stocks, lack of fireproofing. The last big catastrophic fire in Copenhagen was due to 
the bombardment in 1807. The storey partitions are a crucial element of hindering a fire in a 
dwelling from propagating upwards internally in a building, and thereby is a key element in 
supporting a safe egress for the inhabitants of a building in general, and in same way 
supporting firefighters ableness to fight down a fire, before it gets too big to manage. 
In the 1850th and onward, a great work of fireproofing building components where done. This 
work especially originated from the industrialization in mainly Great Britain, as well as France 
and United States according to, SBI rapport 14222. In SBI rapport 14222 its described that by 
the early 1800th, floor constructions using steel, cast-iron and concrete began emerging in 
USA, France, Great Britain, and Germany, and strongly advancing in the 1850, by the steel 
manufacturing processes making huge progresses, towards both casting and extrusion. This 
led to the timber joist structure was sought replaced, with the British originated invention “Jack

Statement of intent 
Storey partitions in Danish buildings, erected in the years from the 1800, all up to the 1950´, 
was predominantly a building component, -named “træbjælkelag”, consisting of spanning 
wood Joist´, serving as supporting structure, for the ceiling, flooring, and acoustic separation. 
Træbjælkelag, is determined as having a fire resistance of 60 minutes and is stated classified 
as BD60III. The actual time of fire resistance given by the træbjælkelag, is questioned, by its 
thinly distributed layer of 12mm, non-flammable or non-combustible materials, serving as the 
protective layer for the combustible parts, being the main structural construction as well as the 
acoustic separation and the actual flooring.  
The træbjælkelag´ small portion of non-combustible materials is of far less mass than storey 
partitions, seen implemented later in present time, all being classified according to either a 
Danish national-, or European -standard test scheme.  
Though the given classification is described in a broad array of literatureIV, the classification is 
neither a result of fire testing nor calculations and remains unproven. Amongst professionals 
it is said, to have been defined, on behalf of mutual understanding, between legislators and 
firefighting professionals, in and around the 1960´V, meaning that regulations account for them 
by postulation.  
 
 
III BD (branddrøj) is one of the Danish terms for classification of fire resistance prior to 2002, where 
these were supplemented by the common European terms. BD is equal to the European classification 
of REI, but is allowing a construction to be fully or partially made of combustible materials. BD translates 
to linger or able to sustain fire.

Theory 
The EU has the following directives, -which among other, covers the means of fire safety in 
renovations. Byggevaredirektivet 89/106/EØF66 (CPD) states in “ANNEX 1, Essential 
requirements, Clause 2, Safety in case of fire”, that; 
“The construction works must be designed and built in such a way that in the event of an 
outbreak of fire:  
• 
the load-bearing capacity of the construction can be assumed for a specific period of time,  
• 
the generation and spread of fire and smoke within the works are limited,  
• 
the spread of the fire to neighboring construction works is limited,  
• 
occupants can leave the works or be rescued by other means,  
• 
the safety of rescue teams is taken into consideration….” 
This was later replaced by Byggevareforordningen 305/2011/EF67 (CPR), 1st. of July 2013, 
where same requirements are stated as basic requirements, also in Clause 2, in ANNEX 1, 
and is also found as prescriptive requirements, reproduced in the Danish Building regulatory, 
currently BR1832 §82 Clause 1-5.  
DS/EN 1995-1-2/AC68, is referred to by 89/106/EØF66, later 305/2011/EF67, this determines 
the method of calculating fire resistance for floors with timber beams, as part of the general 
Eurocode series for structural components EN 1990 Basis of Structural Design69. DS/EN 
1991-1-270 describes the same exposure to fire capabilities as EN 1363-171 
If the timber joist structure can be, or already is proven to accommodate these requirements, 
the structure is fitted for its purpose as a compartmentation. If it is found to be of less fire 
resisting properties, it will still be a legitimate compartmentation for usage in every already 
established dwellings, by this being erected according to the concerning building regulation of 
the time, but nevertheless the safety level could be questioned and in case of renovation the 
need for further improvements would arguably need some concern. 
Applicability and usage of ISO 834 standard fire 
The DS_ISO 834-1_199913 fire-resistance test [ISO 834], referred to by EN 1363-1, Fire 
resistance test – Part 1: General requirement71 the applied temperature-time curve, is equal 
to the temperature-time heating curve, specified in ASTM E119 Standard test methods for fire 
tests of building construction and materials72, First published in 1921 by NBS, National Bureau 
of Standards. According to The historical narrative of the standard temperature-time heating 
curve for structures73 it derrives from works performed in the late 1800th and early 1900th, in 
both Europe and Americas. 
“The standard fire test is a common and globally applied fire resistance metric. Its advantage 
lies in its simplicity, convenience in repeatability, and the fact that it has been used for more 
than a century. It therefore has a wealth of experience for testing performance of structures 
and building products. This allows the construction industry to move at a fast pace based on 
precedent. Historically however, questions have been posed by practitioners and researchers 
on the applicability of the standard temperature–time heating furnace test as a single 
qualification metric for structural fire resistance design purposes. Questions were primarily 
derived from the unrealistic nature of the standard temperature–time curve as a fire and the 
potential limitations to this.”73

Though it is said to have been developed with the scope to assess concrete structures, it is 
used for structures of combustible parts as well. “The standard fire curve is still largely used 
in the performance-based design of timber structures.” Design fires for use in fire safety 
engineering74. In this controlled environment of the furnace, the burning will never be suffering 
from oxygen starvation, in design fire terms, like described in Enclosure Fire Dynamics75 2.2.2. 
This would conclude to a rapid growth period, followed by a fully developed fire throughout the 
entirety of the test. This is very unlike most, -small room, compartment fires, by these being 
controlled by the oxygen supply, -or lack hereof, its often seen to dampen a fire, or by the 
opposite, being the fuel controlled, free-burn examples given in Design fires for use in fire 
safety engineering74 with a more moderate Growth rate, than compared to the ISO 83413 fire, 
followed by a Flashover (peak) and then Decay a phase.  
All this to conclude the standard temperature-time curve as being the conservative or most 
severe as a benchmark, surpassing the neglect of the decay phase, which on the contrary, 
too is hazardous for structures by the in-depth thermal penetration. The main difference in 
using this same temperature-time curve between both combustible and non-combustible test 
specimens is that fuel added by the combustible test specimen itself is deducted from fuel 
added to the furnace. By being globally recognised, it would be hard to replace, though other 
metrics could seem a better fit for combustible structures.

Conduction through the test specimen 
Though the EI (BD) criteria for the timber joist structure is the main objective for this thesis, 
and thereby examining if temperatures exceeding 140˚C above ambient, is reached within 60 
minutes by its unexposed side (upper side). The internal heat transportation or conduction 
reflects a failure criterion of the three combined layers and two air cavities respectively and 
knowledge hereof is relevant to monitor, as data basis in a future aim to further improve the 
fire resistance capabilities of the timber joist structure when renovating buildings with these 
structures.  
“Rule 1: The "thermal" fire endurance of a construction consisting of a number of parallel layers 
is greater than the sum of the "thermal" fire endurances characteristic of the individual layers 
when exposed separately to fire.”82 
Conduction is a molecular energy transport by redistribution of thermal energy by its transport 
and exchange in a medium (material). The thermal resistance 𝑅 is defined by each material 
in relation to the thickness of the layer as 𝑅= 𝑋i/𝑘𝑖 where Xi  is the thickness of the layer and 
𝑘𝑖 is the materials heat transfer coefficient. The thermal properties of a material changes by 
its temperature, the change for specific heat transfer in wood can be assessed by DS/EN 
1995-1-2/AC68 table B.2 which also specifies the changes of the heat transfer for charred 
wood. The specific heat transfer for a material is further influenced by the density of the 
material by a higher density is equal to a lower air content and /or moisture, thus a high water 
content would equal a lower heat transfer, by the water content would need to be evaporated 
by a higher or prolonged specific heat. The gain from moisture contents in fire endurance, is 
stated as high as 8% for each % by volume, by Ten Rules of Fire Endurance Rating82. 
“Rule 8: The presence of moisture, if it does not result in explosive spalling, increases the fire 
endurance.”82 
EN 1363-1 Fire resistance test – Part 1: General requirement71 describes that materials used 
in test specimen, shall be representative of the use in practice as one of the design criterions, 
meaning that all materials in the construction shall reach its equilibrium prior to testing, which 
correlates well with the thermal resistance changing due to the above mentioned parameters.

Joists exposed to fire  
The combustion rate [βn] for the joist would likely be influenced by the common shrinkage 
cracks in wooden structural members. This subject is one of many, covered in Etagehuse med 
bærende konstruktioner af træ, brandtekniske forhold87. The combustion rate is stated to 
differentiate 10-20% between glulam wood and cut construction wood due to the shrinkage 
cracks, but being accounted for in DS 41388, later DS/EN 1995-1-289. Large shrinkage cracks 
are common in old timber placed in constructions for decades, since the timber dehydrates to 
its state of equilibrium at around 11% moisture content. Træ og brand - Anvendelse af 
brandbeskyttelsesmaling ved restaurering af trækonstruktioner90 shows an example of the 
combustion rate for old timber with a moisture content of 11%, as βn = 0.78mm/min, and 
compares this to a combustion rate of βn = 0.63mm/min for timber with a moisture content of 
17%. Shrinkage cracks are not considered a substantial deficiency in old cut timber, by the 
great cross-sectional area. Modern construction wood is due to its optimized cross-sectional 
area more likely to suffer deflections due to tangential shrinkage which occurs with less 
probability of consequential shrinkage cracks.

A contribution and a well-known mean to improve the fire resistance of the timber joist 
structure, is by improving the “membrane” layer on the fire exposed side, commonly done by 
mounting layers of gypsum boards in addition to the mortar plaster. The gypsum boards will 
prolong the heating (i.e. pyrolysis and charring) of the wooden members in the structure, for a 
certain time. DS/EN 1995-1-268 describes a calculation method for determining this 
“membrane” layer.  
The ableness of compensating the first “membrane” layers poor fire protecting performance, 
by improving the following layers is questioned by Failure times of gypsum boards93 which 
also defines a method to define failure of gypsum boards, (i.e. fall-off). “Gypsum is a non-
combustible material and gives no contribution to fire. Its high water content provides up to 
90% of the fire-resistance protection of gypsum boards.” 93 
Based of test carried out on gypsum plaster boards, all in compliance with EN 520 Gypsum 
plasterboards – Definitions, requirements and test methods94 The journal, Failure times of 
gypsum boards93 concludes that that; “the values in EN 1995-1-268 are non-conservative. Full 
scale tests in database show that failure of gypsum board type A occurs often earlier 
compared to design values according to EN 1995-1-2:2004”. 68

The data for failure times for two- or three-layer claddings for floors is deemed insufficient. In 
case of two layers, these are most likely to fail at the same time, as fall-off will occur, 
concurrently as the gypsum plaster dehydrates, and at this instance the charring of the joist 
commences. "Charring of protected wooden members can start before the failure of cladding. 
Charring rate of wood is much slower in this stage compared to charring of unprotected wood. 
After the claddings fall off the charring increases to much higher rate than the charring rate of 
initially unprotected wood. Therefore the failure time of gypsum boards is an important 
property of cladding in design of wooden structures in fire.” 93 
The Danish BYG-ERFA has published the technical guideline Brandsikring og lydisolering af 
træetageadskillelser 47, this recommends the commonly recognized mean for an increased 
fire resistance for the timber joist structure, by mounting gypsum boards to the soffit of the 
structure. All of these recommended layouts are based on gypsum boards mounted without 
cavities behind the gypsum boards.

Method 
By building a replica of the timber joist structure with a layout as described by Byggelov af 29. 
Marts 193919 and testing the test specimen according to DS/EN 1365-2, Fire resistance tests 
for loadbearing elements – Part 2: Floors and roofs98, the aim is to establish a data-set for 
better determining the fire resistance capabilities for the timber joist structure as a hole, but 
also gaining knowledge of the capabilities for each single layer in structure, to withstand fire 
exposure. The present DS/EN 1365-298 is chosen over the less severe, prior Danish standard 
DS 105150 as the present testing standard is found more beneficiary for the data collecting for 
future utilization hereof. 
Minding the economic aspect of fire testing, the test is performed in smaller scale and without 
load applied to the structure. The test carried out is therefore, not considered as a fully 
accredited test, though all other parameters will be carried out in compliance with EN 1363-1 
Fire resistance test – Part 1: General requirement71VI, in conjunction with the test facility 
chosen, -DBI99, being an accredited test facility in relation to these test paradigms. The E & I 
criteria will be determined, for this specific test specimen on behalf of the test. 
The R criteria, being the loadbearing capabilities of the structure, will be assessed by 
calculating the loadbearing capabilities of the residual cross section of each joist, by samples 
of each joist, acquired immediately after the test is terminated. The loadbearing capabilities 
will be determined according to the maximum span, in relation to the specific joist cross 
sectional area, as stated by Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919.

Smoke development 
The smoke development penetrating the test specimen and on to the unexposed side, 
discovered during the test, must be addressed as a deciding issue, when determining the 
general safety level of buildings, with these structures implemented. Neither the test paradigm, 
DS/EN 1363-171 nor DS/EN 1365-298 includes matters of smoke spread, in the case of smoke 
temperatures below the general threshold of 140˚C (180˚C) and the cotton pad not catching 
fire.  
The past Danish standard DS 1051.150 describes in section 6.2.4, that significant propagation 
of smoke, through the test specimen, to the unexposed side should be assessed as a 
determinant risk for defining the untenability criteria, but still it is not a criteria for failure of the 
test specimen. Since only the classification is stated and not the issue of smoke propagation 
is accounted for in any of the known literature, no one would be able to address this criterion 
of concern as part of the risk assessment for a given building. By the time spend building and 
the care and attention to the details, the test specimen is considered equal to or better than 
the similar structures in the general built environment, still the smoke development 
commenced at t=17min and increased throughout the duration of the test. The smoke is 
considered to rapidly create a hazardous environment for adjacent compartmentations, why 
matters of safe egress for neighbouring units, is of great importance to address.  
Smoke detectors, connected in series across adjacent dwellings, could be implemented to 
upgrade the safety, but these are not usually fitted connected in series between adjacent 
compartmentations and would most likely introduce a lot of less fortunate implications by false 
alarms, from everyday living as cooking and so forth. In Danish context stand-alone smoke 
detectors are of today mandatory by BR1853 for dwellings, but for these older established 
buildings, only when the extend of renovation subjects the requirement of a building permit. 
The use of smoke detectors is found as both a cheap and important improvement, which 
quickly could be implemented by the inhabitants in each of these dwellings. Any additional 
smoke detector supplemented in these housings could add to the general fire safety level.

Discussion 
As stated in Ten Rules of Fire Endurance Rating82 “Fire endurance tests alone cannot supply 
all the data needed for intelligent appraisal of fire-resistant ratings of building elements.”82 
Bearing in mind that the furnace is fuelled by Propane. The Propane will consume most 
available oxygen in the furnace. The propane is supplied to the furnace as premixed with 
oxygen. To ensure a complete combustion of the propane, an overhead oxygen level within 
4-8% and above the 4% threshold as per DS/EN1363-171. This lower fuel/oxygen ratio is 
secured throughout the duration of the test, leaving the “free burn” of the test specimen in a 
state of oxygen depletion. Being a combustible test specimen, the combustion of the test 
specimen, are contributing to the temperature-time curve, and the technician would have to 
correct the supply of propane accordingly to maintain the temperature-time curve. 
["It is well known that we cannot expect to get exhaustive, generally valid information about a 
natural phenomenon's multiplicity of interacting factors through a complicated and 
unmanageable experiment that "»corresponds to reality«"] Studier over træbrand100 
If this test was performed as a full-scale test, the data would most likely be somewhat different. 
The larger furnace used for full-scale tests, would present a significant larger turbulence in the 
furnace itself, as well as in the cavities of the test specimen, leading to a faster burn rate of 
the combustible members of the test specimen. A “free burn” experiment, supplemented with 
a proper fuel load density could seem the better fit and more accurate method to determine 
the fire rating probabilities of the timber joist structure and structures alike. 
If the test was performed according to the past Danish standard DS 1051.150, the use of 
sheeted thermocouples would have resulted in slowed heating curve as these would react 
slower to the heating and further the standard allows for a greater deviation in temperature 
from the temperature-time curve of up to ±200˚C by the test specimen being combustible. The 
outwards pressure profile is as well less severe by being 10Pa (±2Pa) and half, compared to 
the used 20Pa (±3Pa). The slower heating, possible lower mean temperature, and lower 
outwards pressure in combination, leaves the timber joist structure with a higher probability of 
sustaining the duration of the test.

Conclusion 
The statement of intent was based on my knowledge when this work commenced. It tells my 
narrative and findings at the time, by the timber joist structure being “empirically classified”, 
meaning not actual being tested. The work has concluded that this partial thesis by the timber 
joist structure being empirically classified, remains unproven. 
The termination at t=58min represents the specific result for the specific test specimen in a 
furnace smaller than the furnace size to be used in a formal classification test. The furnace 
size is of importance since the turbulence in a larger scale furnace is significantly higher than 
in the smaller furnaces. The turbulence would stress the test specimen further and create a 
greater turbulence inside the cavities as found in the performed test. This concluding that the 
test specimen would need to show an excessive durability of 60 in the smaller furnace to 
withstand a duration of 60 minutes in the full-scale test scenario, regarding the E and I criteria. 
Further the test was conducted without any load applied. Applied load during a fire test is 
stated to have a significant effect upon the test specimens performance by DS/EN1363-171.  
The timber joist structure in this particular layout, is by the test result concluded not to be able 
to withstand 60 minutes of exposure to the standard temperature-time curve, ISO 83413 
Though the c/c distance was differing in the built test specimen from the specifications given 
in Byggelov af 29. Marts 193919 and thus a shorter c/c distance might prolong the fire 
performance of the structure, this is found of less significance and found not to be pivotal to 
the outcome, particularly since the test was performed without any applied load. 
Thereby concluding that it is very unlikely that the timber joist structure would meet the criteria 
for a classification of either BD 60 or EI 60 if tested in full-scale according to ISO 83413.  
Besides this and unrelated to the direct topic of this thesis, being the discovered issue, that a 
lot of smoke was observed pervading through the timber joist structure. The build quality and 
compactness of this specific test specimen is assessed as surpassing the general built 
environment, which would deem the timber joist structures in the general built environment 
less able to withhold and confine smoke. There is no threshold requirement regarding smoke 
in the test standard, but it is one of the main criterions in Byggevareforordningen 
305/2011/EF67. The timber joist structure is found as not being in compliance within the 
intended main tenability criteria for smoke as stated in Byggevareforordningen 305/2011/EF67.

Outlook 
In Brandsikring, Bygningsmæssige foranstaltninger11, Børge Johannes Rambøll, mentions 
knowledge of results found in the test lab at ”statsprøveanstalten”. Finding these results would 
disclose a dataset, to compare up against the findings of this thesis, and is found to be of great 
importance to better asses the properties of the timber joist structure, in a future work. 
Since Brandsikring, Bygningsmæssige foranstaltninger11, was published in 1951 and Børge 
Johannes Rambøll, -being an esteemed professional, mentioning knowledge of numerous 
accounts of actual fires penetrating BD 60 classified storey partitions, by these failing 
significantly prior. Why would the use and implementation of this building component be 
continued in its use, and even added fifteen years later, in the first Danish building legislation 
concerning the whole country, Bygningsreglement for købstæderne og landet2, and 
successively up until present time, while in the same fifteen-year period, fire safety 
professionals were raising concerns to the legislators of the general building stocks need for 
improvements on fireproofing? 
By the timber joist structure not fully meeting the criterion given in Byggevareforordningen 
305/2011/EF67 Clause 2, in ANNEX 1 and specifically for Denmark, BR1832 §82 Clause 4, a 
goal for further investigation could be determining the feasibility of improving the current fire 
safety level of the approximated 20km² timber joist structure, compared to the societal and 
legislative acceptance of uncertainties in general.
