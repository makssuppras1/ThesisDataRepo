Abstract 
 
A recent examination of the annual number of hydrocarbon (HC) release incidents in the offshore UK 
sector shows a downward trend. The declining trend in HC release rates raises the issue of whether all 
underlying causes of failures improve at the same rate or whether the decline in release rates was the 
result of the improvement of a specific underlying cause alone or a fraction of them. The identification of 
a pattern is very important, as it will help in future decision-making over risk assessments concerning 
offshore installations.  
Thus, in the present project the aim was to analyze the incidents reported in the Hydrocarbon Releases 
Database (HCRD) and to determine whether over the years there was a significant shift in the underlying 
causes of HC releases. Mainly, to determine whether over the years, a) management, meaning safety rules 
and regulations and staff’s competence and compliance, and b) equipment-building tactics and methods, 
improve at a different rate.  
In order to investigate the issue raised and analyze the underlying causes of HC releases, the following 
points are fulfilled: 
 
Assessment of the existing taxonomy in regards to whether it can uniquely categorize the various 
HCRD incidents; establishment of a new taxonomy since the existing one was deemed 
dysfunctional. 
 
Categorization of the HCRD incidents based on the new taxonomy and analysis of the categories 
by using the appropriate statistical method. 
 
Ensuring the robustness of the taxonomy by varying it, and comparing the results of the varying 
taxonomies; conclusion on whether the detected trend is legitimate 
 A very important aspect of the present project is to interpret and analyze in detail the various HCRD 
entries. This is achieved by studying both the HCRD and the existing reporting forms.  
Furthermore, an understanding of various possible failure modes is crucial in order to assess the existing 
taxonomy; it is deduced that the existing taxonomy does not allow a unique classification of incidents that 
are caused by more than one failure mode. A new taxonomy, that allows a distinct classification, is 
established; this new taxonomy is implemented on the HCRD and the various release incidents are re-
classified. Finally, the results of the HCRD based on the new taxonomy are statistically analyzed by the 
method of regression analysis.  
Indeed, a noticeable trend points to a significant improvement in safety regulations and staff’s 
compliance. Hence, it is concluded that the improvement in reliability was mainly due to human and 
organizational factors. Finally, different variations of the new taxonomy confirm the noticed pattern.  
As a future research subject, the possibility of improvement of equipment-building tactics and methods 
is proposed.

Chapter 1 – Introduction 
 
1.1 Background of the study 
The energy sector has a strong presence in the North Sea and commercial extraction of offshore oil and 
gas and related activities constitute the single most important economic factor in the area [1]. 
Unfortunately, offshore oil rigs activities entail numerous hazards. While all hazards need to be considered 
in a risk assessment, a large part of the attention will normally be on hydrocarbon loss of containment 
events, as such accidents can potentially have grave consequences to the life and health of workers, 
pollution of the environment, direct and indirect economic losses and deterioration of the security of the 
energy supply [2] [3].  
Furthermore, reliability and security of the energy supply are of critical socio-economic importance, and 
safety at sea is one of the main concerns for offshore industries in general [1]. Thus, it is of great 
significance to control the hazards and risks, and this begins at the design stage of a platform and extends 
to numerous regulations concerning the operation and maintenance of an offshore installation [4].  
However, plenty of these regulations were developed after disastrous accidents had already taken place, 
like the Piper Alpha disaster in July 1988. This disaster shocked the world and changed offshore safety 
management and legislation in the North Sea beyond recognition [5]. 
Specifically, at the time of the Piper Alpha disaster, the main sources of hydrocarbon leaks data either 
covered major accidents involving substantial losses and stoppages of over 24 hours, or they only covered 
failures and breakdowns with no link to the consequences of the failure. As a result, there was a scarcity 
of information [6]. 
Lord Cullen, in his report concerning the investigation over the Piper Alpha disaster, stated: “I am 
convinced that learning from accidents and incidents is an important way of improving safety 
performance. […] I consider it would be useful if there was a systematic means by which what could be 
learnt from incidents and near misses could be shared by all operators.” [7]. Hence, to improve 
performance, failures need to be anticipated so that predictive maintenance can occur, whether that is 
servicing or replacement [8]. 
This led in the Health and Safety Executive, Offshore Safety Division (HSE-OSD) to take from the 
Department of Energy the responsibility for offshore safety in the UK sector in April 1991 and, in order to 
gather the required data on hydrocarbon leaks, spills and ignitions, the HSE-OSD issued two new forms 
for the reporting of all offshore incidents (reporting forms: OIR/9B and OIR12). The first completed forms 
were received in early October 1992, so the start date for all data in the Hydrocarbon Releases Database 
(HCRD) was decided to be the 1st of October 1992 [9]. 
Since its creation, the HCRD has been used by many researchers in order to develop release frequency 
data for standard equipment types [10]. Finding a trend in the data can help anticipate failures as 
determining the underlying causes of incidents can help prevent future undesirable events [8] [10]. 
Nowadays, the HCRD has become the standard source of release frequencies for offshore quantitative 
risk assessment (QRA) [11]. 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
A recent examination of the annual number of incidents in the HCRD reveals a downward trend [2]. 
However, the population of equipment has generally grown over the same time period, so the frequency 
of incidents based on equipment appears to have been falling at an even faster rate [2]. Further recent 
analysis, as presented during a Fire and Blast Information Group (FABIG) seminar, indeed shows that there 
are declining trends in release rates. 
 
1.2 Research objectives 
The declining trend in hydrocarbon (HC) release rates raised the issue of whether all underlying causes of 
failures improve at the same rate or whether the declining trend in HC release rates was the result of the 
improvement of a specific underlying cause alone or a fraction of them. Identifying a pattern will help in 
decision-making over future risk assessments of offshore facilities. Hence, it was crucial to identify 
whether the trend observed in the total release rates was accompanied by a trend in the relative 
contribution of specific underlying causes; especially distinguishing between causes related to design and 
age on one hand, and maintenance and management of human factors on the other hand. 
Thus, in the present project the aim was to analyze the incidents reported in the HCRD, and to determine 
whether over the years there was a significant shift in the underlying causes of HC releases. Mainly, to 
determine whether over the years, a) management, meaning safety rules and regulations and staff’s 
competence and compliance, and b) equipment-building tactics and methods, improve at a different rate.  
In order to investigate the issue raised and analyze the underlying causes of HC releases, the following 
objectives should be fulfilled:  
 
Assessment of the existing taxonomy in regards to whether it can uniquely categorize the various 
HCRD incidents; establishment of a new taxonomy if the existing one is unsuccessful. 
 
Categorization of the HCRD based on the taxonomy. 
 
Analysis of the categories by using the appropriate statistical method; detection of possible trend.  
 
Ensuring the robustness of the taxonomy by varying it, and comparing the results of the varying 
taxonomies. 
 
Conclusion on whether the detected trend is legitimate. 
 
Examination of the improvement of the category which appears to improve at the slowest rate. 
 
1.3 Overview of the research methodology 
The process followed in order to achieve the objectives of the present project is presented in Figure 1.1. 
The first and most important step was to understand in depth all the possible entries of the HCRD, 
especially the part that referred to the failure modes that resulted in the release incident. In order to do 
that, not only the database itself, but also all the reporting forms issued from the creation of the HCRD 
until today, were studied in great detail. A crucial part of this step was to research over the history of the 
guidance concerning the database; due to the fact that numerous regulations have been issued during 
the operation years of the HCRD, the incidents reported throughout all these years were not consistent.

10 
 
 
Figure 1.1: Methodology followed in present project 
 
The issue of the consistency of the data was then raised. As a result, two different datasets were decided 
to be used in the present project. The first dataset was basically the HCRD itself as it included all accidental 
releases ever reported (“All” dataset), and the second dataset included only the incidents that would be 
considered worthwhile to report by current regulations (“Reportable” dataset). Thus, the first dataset had 
the advantage of including a significantly greater number of incidents, whereas the second dataset had 
the advantage of consistency. 
In the meantime, the taxonomy used in the HCRD was assessed in regards to whether it could uniquely 
categorize the various HCRD incidents. Since it was concluded that the existing taxonomy was 
dysfunctional regarding managing a unique categorization for every incident, a new taxonomy was 
created for this purpose. Thus, a newly-introduced taxonomy – named “Basic taxonomy” – was 
established, and it was based on all the so far issued incident reporting forms; more specifically, the newly-
introduced taxonomy was based on the reporting method of the various failure modes. In order to 
perfectly define the taxonomy, diagrams of logic gates were created for all categories. 
The new taxonomy was used to re-classify the incidents of the two datasets in regards to their failure 
modes. Still, for a few data years, an issue of a big percentage of unidentified incidents was raised. Since 
these data years had the potential to negatively impact the results of the present research, certain actions 
were taken in order to correctly purify the database from incidents that were impossible to categorize.  
Afterwards, two different approaches to data visualisation were used in an effort to easily comprehend 
the results. Diagrams and pie charts were created in order to present how the categories changed 
throughout the years. 
Due to the fact that the population data were not included in the present study, it was impossible to 
conclude whether there was indeed an improvement of the various categories by the diagrams of the 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
categories alone. Thus, the figures of the ratio of each category divided by the identified annual incidents 
were presented. This particular step was very important as it had the potential to make apparent a 
possible shift in the causes of incidents. 
Indeed, since a particular pattern appeared to emerge, an appropriate statistical method (regression 
analysis) was used to test the results. 
Furthermore, wanting to test the robustness of the Basic taxonomy, two more taxonomies – which were 
varying versions of the Basic taxonomy – were created (Taxonomy L and Taxonomy S). More specifically, 
Taxonomy L managed to categorize plenty of incidents that the Basic taxonomy had left unidentified, thus 
significantly reducing the number of annual unidentified incidents; whereas Taxonomy S had stricter 
definitions for the categories, resulting in increasing the number of annual unidentified incidents. 
Eventually, the incidents of the two datasets were again categorized by these two new taxonomies, and 
the new results were analysed. Hence, for both new taxonomies, all above steps were repeated. Finally, 
the robustness of the Basic taxonomy was confirmed by the fact that the results of the three taxonomies 
were consistent. As a result, the detected trend was considered legitimate. 
Finally, the issue of whether the category with the slowest improvement rate, had indeed improved or 
not, was raised. Thus, the absolute annual number of incidents of this category was statistically tested by 
the method of regression analysis. 
 
1.4 Structure 
The present project is structured in a rather simple form.  
In chapter 2, due to the fact that an in-depth understanding of the HCRD was necessary for the completion 
of this project, an in-detail presentation of the history of the HCRD and of the possible HCRD entries is 
provided. 
In chapter 3 the two datasets are created.  
In chapter 4, since it was a significant part of the methodology, the theory behind regression analysis is 
presented.  
Chapter 5 can be regarded as the core of this project as it introduces a new taxonomy that was used to 
uniquely categorize all HCRD incidents. Furthermore, the results of the two datasets based on this newly-
introduced taxonomy, are provided and analyzed. Moreover, the robustness of the new taxonomy is 
examined and the final results are presented. 
Finally, Chapter 6 discusses conclusions and recommendations for future work.

Chapter 2 – History and entries of the HCRD 
 
2.1 Detailed history of the HCRD 
When the Piper Alpha disaster occurred, the majority of data sources about offshore accidents had as a 
criterion that “dangerous occurrences” were the stoppages that exceeded 24h. As a result, “near misses” 
(i.e. hydrocarbon leaks and ignitions that didn’t have as an outcome such a stoppage) were not reported 
and were thus missing from the gathered data [9]. 
In order to address this issue, Lord Cullen, in his investigation over the Piper Alpha disaster report, 
recommended that: “The regulatory body should be responsible for maintaining a database with regard 
to hydrocarbon leaks, spills and ignitions in the Industry and for the benefit of the Industry” (Cullen 
Recommendation 39) [9]. 
In April 1991, the HSE-OSD took over the responsibility for offshore safety in the UK sector from the 
Department of Energy and, as the new Regulatory Body, it also assumed responsibility for the setting up 
of the HCRD in line with the Cullen Recommendation 39 [6]. 
The first step towards the creation of the database was to review the existing definitions for dangerous 
occurrences. The definition concerning hydrocarbon releases was: "Any release of petroleum 
hydrocarbon resulting in the stoppage of plant; the suspension of work; a flash fire; a continuous fire; an 
explosion; the operation of a smoke, flame, fire or gas detector at or above the lowest action point; or 
any specific action to prevent the possibility of a fire or an explosion; and/or any release resulting in or 
having the potential to cause death or serious injury to any person" [6]. 
Afterwards, the HSE-OSD issued two new reporting forms: i) the OIR/9B (presented in Appendix 1) in 
which the basic incident data were reported and which was a legal requirement by the Reporting of 
Incidents Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences (RIDDOR) to be submitted in case of a HC release incident 
and ii) the OIR12 (presented in Appendix 2) in which the supplementary data were reported and of which 
submission was voluntary. Nonetheless, despite the submission being voluntary, the offshore industry, on 
the whole, complied [12]. As mentioned before, the first completed forms were received by HSE-OSD in 
early October 1992 and thus, the start date of the HCRD was decided to be the 1st of October 1992. 
Relatively soon though, it became apparent that there wasn’t enough guidance as to what was considered 
reportable or not. Thus, the HSE provided the earliest clarification to the requirements under RIDDOR in 
OTO 96 956 “Revised guidance on Reporting of Offshore Hydrocarbon Releases” (UK HSE, 1996) [13]. Still, 
the criterion that a release was reportable if it had the potential to cause death or major injury was 
subjective enough to result in inconsistency in the severity of events which operators considered 
reportable [12]. 
This resulted in further guidance from the Oil & Gas UK (O&GUK), in collaboration with the HSE (O&GUK, 
2008). In “Supplementary Guidance for Reporting Hydrocarbon Releases, Revision 1”, the first actual mass 
limitations for which releases were considered reportable under RIDDOR were provided and can be found 
in Table 2.1 [14].

Table 2.1: Limitations for reportability of an incident [14] 
 
 
This new guideline managed to be quite objective, however there were still inconsistencies in what was 
reported, so the limitations were revised again in 2014 (O&GUK, 2014).  
At the same time, as a direct result of the Deepwater Horizon oil spill (April, 2010), the “Commission 
implementing regulation (EU) No 1112/2014” (EU, 2014) was introduced by the European Union and, 
along with it, a new reporting form (presented in Appendix 3). In 1112/2014, the EU regulation introduced 
the terms “fugitive” and “non-fugitive” emissions in order to represent releases due to different failure 
mechanisms [15].  
Fugitive emissions are defined as “releases arising from loss of tightness from hydrocarbon containment 
equipment such as valves, flanges and other connections, pressure relief devices, process drains, open-
ended valves, pump and compressor seal systems, agitator seals, and access door seals”.  
Non-fugitive emissions are defined as “releases due to causes such as corrosion pinholes or cracks in 
process containment systems”.  
The EU reportable limits are: 
 
For the fugitive gas emissions the limit is: ≥ 3 kg/h and ≥ 1 kg released. 
 
For the non-fugitive gas emissions the limit is: ≥ 1 kg released. 
 
For the fugitive equivalent liquid emissions except methanol the limit is: ≥ 100 kg/h and ≥ 60 kg 
released (for the fugitive equivalent methanol emissions the limit is: ≥ 80 kg/h and ≥ 60 kg 
released). 
 
For the non-fugitive equivalent liquid emissions the limit is: ≥ 60 kg/h released.

Finally, the most recent revision of mass limitations for the reportability of an incident under RIDDOR was

2.2 Entries in “Cause of release” section of the HCRD 
As mentioned in previous chapters, the main objective of this report was to identify a possible trend in 
the underlying causes of HC releases in the offshore UK sector. The first step towards this direction was 
to create a taxonomy that would enable the grouping of the incidents with relevant causes.  
In order to do that, it was essential to study the reporting method of the causes of the releases. So, the 
possible choices of the OIR12, the 1112/2014 and the ROGI reporting forms were analyzed.

Chapter 3 – The “All” and “Reportable” datasets 
 
3.1 Creation of the “All” and “Reportable” datasets 
As mentioned before, HSE has provided in their website, the HCRD in the form of two files, the hrs1992-

4.2 Two-sided hypothesis test 
After finding the equation of the regression line (equation 1), it is also important to decide whether the 
slope of the line, α1 is statistically significant. Since there is only one independent variable (Variable x) and 
only one assumed dependent variable (Variable y), a t test can be used in order to examine whether there 
is indeed a relationship between the two variables or not.  
Hence, the effort is to disprove the null hypothesis, Ho that usually states that the slope is in reality equal 
to zero: 
Ho: α 1 = 0 
As a result, the alternate hypothesis, H1 is: 
H1: α1 ≠ 0 
This translates to a two-tailed t test and, for a significance level of α=0.05, half of α (0.05/2=0.025) is used 
to test the statistical significance in the positive direction (if slope α1 is positive) and half of α is used to 
test the statistical significance in the negative direction (if slope α1 is negative). Hence, the slope is 
considered statistically significantly different from zero, if the t value of the slope is in the top 0.025 of the 
t distribution, or in the bottom 0.025 of the t distribution (Figure 4.2).

Chapter 5 – The newly-introduced Basic taxonomy 
 
5.1 Assessment of the existing taxonomy 
After understanding the various possible entries of the HCRD, and generating the two datasets that were 
analyzed in the present project (the “All” and “Reportable” datasets), it was finally possible to assess the 
existing taxonomy in regards to whether it could uniquely categorize each incident.  
Although it would be easy to adopt the existing taxonomy used in the HCRD and simply have four 
categories named ”Design cause”, “Equipment failure”, “Operational failure” and “Procedural failure”, in 
reality, failures rarely occur in this well-defined manner. The major issue is that very often, more than one 
kind of failure is responsible for a release: In plenty of cases, a combination of two or more different, but 
interchangeable reasons is responsible for a release, and it is sometimes difficult to pinpoint the initiating 
factor of the failure. For example, some failures, classified as corrosion failures, are due to overstressing 
the pipeline in corrosive environment [22]. Due to this fact, the existing taxonomy used in the HCRD was 
considered unsuccessful in uniquely categorizing each incident, and thus, the need of a new taxonomy 
emerged. Hence, the newly-introduced taxonomy used in this project needed to be quite more 
complicated than the simple categorization used in the reporting forms. 
Furthermore, after a careful analysis of the possible entries in the OIR12, 1112/2014 and ROGI reporting 
forms, and also of the work sheet (in Excel format) that was provided by DNV GL, it appeared that the 
“Operational failure” column (Column E) was not answering to the question “What was the cause of the 
release?”, but rather answering to the question “What happened exactly?”. Examples of this issue are the 
“Left open”, “Opened when containing HC”, “Incorrectly fitted”, “Dropped object” or “Other impact” 
entries. These entries are definitely useful and help in the understanding of the release incident, however 
they don’t really help in the identification of the cause of this result. For this reason, it was assessed that 
the primary focus should be on the “Design Cause”, “Equipment Failure” and “Procedural failure” columns 
(Columns C, D and F respectively). 
 
5.2 Presentation of the Basic taxonomy 
Before presenting the newly-introduced Basic taxonomy, it was important to make a certain assumption. 
Since the reporting of an incident, up to a point, depends on the personal knowledge and notions of the 
reporting personnel, it is possible that incidents were and still are reported subjectively. Thus, for this 
project to be possible to be conducted, the assumption was that personal subjectivity did not interfere 
with the reporting. 
Since the main objective of the present project was to understand whether the equipment or the 
management of the installation is more important on deciding which risk assessment data to use, the 
“Equipment Failure” column was decided to be considered the central one for the newly-introduced 
“Basic taxonomy”. Thus, for starters, the new taxonomy was based on whether there was an equipment 
failure or not. This decision led to two mega-categories: The “YESEQUIP” if there was an equipment failure, 
and the “NOEQUIP” if there was not an equipment failure. 
The mega-category “YESEQUIP” basically presents which were the reasons that led to the equipment 
failure and it consists of the newly-introduced categories: “DES2EQUIP”, “PROC2EQUIP”, “BOTH2EQUIP”

Nevertheless, even though the regression lines of the figures pointed to a certain conclusion, it was still 
necessary to decide whether the slopes of these regression lines were statistically significant. For this 
reason, the method of regression analysis was used. However, regression analysis can be either a two-
sided or a one-sided test. So, the issue of which of the two should be used was raised. 
It is true that a one-sided test – besides testing whether a slope is different than zero – also tests whether 
the sign of the slope is positive or negative; and this could have been more appropriate for the present 
cases, where the signs of the slopes were of interest as well. However, since other slopes were positive 
and other slopes were negative, testing every category with a one-sided test, would demand stating the 
two hypotheses (the null hypothesis and the alternate hypothesis) for every category separately; and this 
had the potential of creating great confusion. 
In order to avoid this unnecessary confusion, and state one null and one alternate hypothesis that 
corresponded to all categories, it was decided that all slopes would be tested with a two-sided test. 
Besides, the two-sided test still provides a confidence region for the slope, thus the sign of the slope can 
be indirectly confirmed by the slope’s confidence region; for a significance level of α=0.05, there’s a 95% 
confidence that the slope of the regression line, lies within the confidence region. 
To conclude, the two-sided test was preferred against the one-sided test, for both reasons of simplicity 
and adequacy. 
So, for all the slopes, the null hypothesis was that the slope was in reality equal to zero:  
Ho: α 1 = 0 
And the alternate hypothesis was that the slope was different from zero:  
H1: α 1 ≠ 0

5.5 Robustness of Basic taxonomy and total results 
A major issue that hasn’t been discussed so far is the robustness of the Basic taxonomy. The results appear 
to be sensible, but how sensitive are they in changes of taxonomy? For this reason, it was important to 
research over the robustness of the created Basic taxonomy. Hence, two more taxonomies were created 
(Taxonomy L and Taxonomy S) and the HCRD was further analyzed based on those two taxonomies as well 
(Appendix 6).  
The idea behind the Taxonomy L was to create a “looser” version of the Basic taxonomy that, unlike the 
Basic taxonomy, would include the discarded data years (2007 and 2012 and onwards). This was achieved 
by accepting entries that were noted as simply “OTHER” in the “Equipment failure” column. This simple 
approach managed to drop all annual percentages of unidentified incidents to less than 25% for both 
datasets. 
Similarly, Taxonomy S was a “stricter” variation of the Basic taxonomy, by discarding incidents that the 
Basic taxonomy had accepted. This was achieved by taking the “Operational failure” column into account, 
just as much as the “Design cause” and “Procedural failure” columns were taken into account. Hence, the 
“Operational failure” column, was included in the logical gates diagrams of all eight categories, instead of 
only being included in the logical gates diagram of the OPER category. 
The results of all three taxonomies were in relative agreement, meaning the same categories appeared to 
be changing in statistical significance in comparison to the rest. Therefore, it was considered that the Basic 
taxonomy was robust and could withstand changes.

Chapter 6 – Conclusions and recommendations 
 
Recently, after an analysis of the database concerning the HC release incidents occurring in the UK 
offshore sector (HCRD), it was noted that there has been a significant decrease in the annual number of 
HC leaks in the past two decades. This raised the following question: Do all underlying causes improve at 
the same rate or is the declining of release rates caused mainly by the improvement of a specific 
underlying cause or a fraction of them? It is quite possible that, over the years, procedures, design, 
operation and equipment have improved at a different rate. 
Identifying a trend in underlying causes of releases in the Hydrocarbon Release System is very important 
as it will positively influence decision-making over future risk assessments of offshore facilities. So, in an 
effort to investigate whether, a) management, meaning safety rules and regulations and staff’s 
competence and compliance, and b) equipment-building tactics and methods, improve at a different rate, 
the present project was executed.  
However, for the present project to be possible to be completed, a certain assumption was necessary. 
Since incident reporting depends on the personal knowledge and notions of the reporting personnel, it is 
possible that incidents were reported subjectively. Thus, the assumption that personal subjectivity didn’t 
interfere with the reporting, was made. 
So, the first step of the present research was probably the most important one, as an in-depth 
understanding of the possible entries of the HCRD was essential. Hence, the history of the HCRD, the HCRD 
itself, and also all the so far issued reporting forms, were studied in great detail.  
Afterwards, two sets of data were exported by the HCRD. The first dataset (the “All” dataset) included all 
4528 accidental releases reported between the years 1993-2014. Moreover, in an effort to acquire a 
better-defined dataset, a second dataset was generated by the first dataset. The “Reportable” dataset 
included only a part of the “All” dataset as it included only what is considered worthy to report according 
to present mass limitations (Figure 2.1). As a result, the “Reportable” dataset contained a significantly 
smaller amount of incidents (3277), but had the great advantage of consistency. 
Next, the taxonomy used in the HCRD, was evaluated in regards to whether it could uniquely classify a HC 
release incident. Since the existing taxonomy was assessed as dysfunctional in this regard, a new 
taxonomy (“Basic taxonomy”) was created. The most important aspect of this taxonomy was that, first 
and foremost, a distinction was made between the incidents that had occurred due to an equipment 
failure, and the incidents that had occurred due to other reasons. However, due to the fact that in plenty 
of cases a combination of two or more failures is responsible for a release, initiating causes leading to an 
equipment failure were also taken into account for the creation of the taxonomy. 
This new taxonomy was used in order to categorize the accidental HC release incidents of the two 
datasets. It was then revealed that there was a big percentage of unidentified incidents in recent years. 
This raised an issue of uncertainty as a big percentage of unidentified incidents had the potential to 
interfere with the results of the present research. So, in an effort to purify the database, these data years 
were rejected. 
==PAGE:73==
73 
 
It was now possible to acquire the first results of the two datasets based on this newly-introduced 
taxonomy. These first results hinted to an improvement of all categories throughout the years and thus, 
a more detailed analysis was in demand.  
By dividing the number of annual incidents of each category to the number of annual identified incidents, 
the annual ratio of each category was calculated. Figures of all categories for both sets of data were 
created and were analyzed by the method of regression analysis. 
Indeed, this approach gave notable results, especially given the fact that the results of the two datasets 
appeared to be in relative agreement. The first important finding was that the equipment appeared to be 
improving at a significantly slower rate in comparison to design and procedures. The second most 
important finding was that the release incidents caused by failures in procedures appeared to be 
improving at the fastest rate. Hence, the so far results pointed to an improvement in management. Finally, 
the improvement of design was considered as probable to be contributing in the improvement of 
reliability, however this finding wasn’t considered definite. 
In order to verify the results and test the robustness of the Basic taxonomy, two more new taxonomies 
were created. The idea behind Taxonomy L was to identify a greater number of incidents, whereas the 
idea behind taxonomy S was to make a very strict taxonomy that rejected any incident that had even the 
slightest potential of uncertainty in its classification. Both new taxonomies provided more or less the same 
results as the Basic taxonomy in regards to equipment and procedures, thus ensuring the robustness of 
the Basic taxonomy. However, the finding that maybe improvements in design also contributed in the 
improvement of the reliability of offshore installations was rejected by these two new taxonomies. 
Finally, after the conclusion that the decrease in release rates has mainly occurred due to management 
factors, the issue of whether equipment as well has contributed to the improvement of reliability, even if 
at a slower rate, was raised. For this reason, the absolute number of incidents due to equipment failures 
was analyzed. Unfortunately, due to a great discrepancy between the results of the two datasets, no 
concrete conclusions could be made. 
However, knowing whether equipment has indeed improved or not throughout the years is very 
important, as such knowledge would help in decision-making over risk assessments of offshore facilities. 
Thus, in the future, it is of particular interest to research over possible improvement of the equipment. 
Furthermore, the present research has managed to identify a significant improvement in offshore 
procedures, however whether that was due to improvement of safety rules and regulations, or 
improvement of the staff’s competence and compliance, remains unknown. Such information can be vital 
and it is certainly valuable to research over in the future.

Appendix 5 – “1 in 4” 
 
The idea behind the “1 in 4” limitation was relatively simple. The aim was to reject as few years as possible, 
but, at the same time, decide on a limit strict enough so as to not have unidentified incidents interfering 
with the results; a big percentage of unidentified incidents had the potential to disturb the results of the 
present research, especially due to the fact that the vast majority of entries that didn’t allow the proper 
identification of the incidents were in the “Equipment failure” column of the HCRD, which was decided to 
be considered the central one and the basis of the two mega-categories. 
It was mainly for this reason that the Taxonomy L was created. As is also explained in Appendix 6, the idea 
behind the Taxonomy L was to include the years rejected by the Basic taxonomy. The results of Taxonomy 
L helped decide over a proper limitation of percentage of unidentified years.  
In the figure below (Figure A5.1), the ratio results of the YESEQUIP mega-category can be seen for both 
the Basic taxonomy as well as for the Taxonomy L (for the “All” dataset). Along with those results, the 
ratio of annual unidentified incidents for the Basic taxonomy can be seen.

Appendix 6 – Taxonomy L and Taxonomy S 
 
In an effort to confirm the results of the Basic taxonomy and to investigate the taxonomy’s robustness to 
changes, two more taxonomies were implemented on the two datasets. Both taxonomies were based on 
the same logic presented in Tables 5.1 and 5.2, except the one was “looser” (Taxonomy L) and the other 
was “stricter” (Taxonomy S). 
 
Taxonomy L 
The idea behind the Taxonomy L was to include the discarded by the Basic taxonomy data years (2007 
and 2012 and onwards). After a careful analysis of the HCRD entries in these years, it was noted they were 
mainly rejected due to many entries in the EQUIP category that didn’t allow the incidents’ identification. 
In particular, there were plenty of the following entries: “OTHER”, “OTHER – NOT KNOWN”, “OTHER – 
AWAITING INVESTIGATION”, etc. Since the entries “OTHER – NOT KNOWN” and “OTHER – AWAITING 
INVESTIGATION” are betraying that, in reality, it is unknown whether there was indeed an equipment 
failure or not, they were again decided to be discarded. Au contraire, assuming the fact that there was 
indeed an equipment failure, but the reporting personnel just didn’t provide specification for it, it was 
decided that all entries that contained simply “OTHER” in the EQUIPMENT column would be included.  
This particular difference was the only difference between the Basic taxonomy and Taxonomy L, meaning 
that this new taxonomy was practically the same as the Basic taxonomy with the exception that in all four 
categories of the YESEQUIP mega-category (EQUIP, DES2EQUIP, PROC2EQUIP and BOTH2EQUIP), the 
entry “OTHER” in the EQUIPMENT column was accepted as an equipment failure. By definition, the “DES”, 
“PROC”, “BOTH” and “OPER” categories were kept constant between the two taxonomies. 
In the Tables A6.1 and A6.2 below, the results of Taxonomy L are shown. It is clear that the aim to reduce 
the number of unidentified incidents was achieved. For the previously rejected years (2007 and 2012 and 
onwards) the average ratio of the unidentified incidents dropped to 14.7% from the previous 37.1% for 
the “All” dataset and, for the “Reportable” dataset, the average ratio of the unidentified incidents 
dropped to 15.6% from the previous 38.3%.

By simply looking at the ratio figures above (Figure A6.30 up to A6.55), it is noticed that the results 
provided by the two taxonomies (Basic taxonomy and Taxonomy L) are mainly in agreement. However, 
there are some differences as well. 
For the “All” dataset, the categories, bigger categories and mega-categories that have provided similar 
results – and more specifically, ratios that appear to be declining – are the DES2EQUIP, BOTH2EQUIP, 
OPER, DES, PROC, BOTH, DES*2, PROC*2, BOTH*2 and NOEQUIP. Furthermore, the ratios of the EQUIP 
and the YESEQUIP categories are again increasing. However, Taxonomy L gives for the PROC2EQUIP 
category an increasing throughout the time ratio. 
Similar results are provided by the “Reportable” dataset analyzed with Taxonomy L as well; the results of 
the two taxonomies are mainly matching, but the PROC2EQUIP category ratio is again increasing. 
Furthermore, the ratios of BOTH and BOTH*2 categories are also increasing by using Taxonomy L to the 
contrary of the Basic taxonomy. 
However, for all the newly increasing ratios, it is important to see if the findings are statistically significant 
or not. The same of course is true for the rest ratios. It is though very likely that the ratio of the EQUIP 
category will again be statistically significantly increasing for both datasets, as the points are rather orderly 
placed around the regression line (Figures A6.30 and A6.43). 
So, in order to conduct the necessary tests for all the slopes, the null hypothesis was that the slopes were 
in reality equal to zero: Ho: α1 = 0 
And the alternate hypothesis was that the slopes were different from zero: H1: α 1 ≠ 0 
In the tables below (Tables A6.3 and A6.4), for the various categories, bigger categories and mega-
categories, their slopes, the lower and upper limit of the slopes, their t-values and their P values are 
presented. For a significance level of 0.05 and for 20 degrees of freedom (22 years minus 2), the tcrit is 
equal to 2.086. The resulting P values of the categories that are statistically significantly different than 
zero (less than 0.05) are marked with red.

As can be seen by the Tables, for both datasets, the positive slopes of the ratios of the PROC2EQUIP, BOTH

statistically significantly different from zero. Hence, with Taxonomy L as well, the results acquired show 
that the equipment-building methods improve at the slowest rate (EQUIP and YESEQUIP ratios increase), 
whereas procedures and management appear to be improving at the fastest rate (PROC and NOEQUIP 
ratios decrease). 
The interesting thing to note here is that, with Taxonomy L, it was possible to identify the slope of PROC 
as statistically significantly different than zero for both datasets – something that the Basic taxonomy 
didn’t give as a result, as it identified the slope of PROC as statistically significant only in the case of the 
“All” dataset.  
There is one more difference between the results of the two taxonomies, as the negative slope of the 
OPER category appears to be statistically significantly different than zero for both datasets in the case of 
Taxonomy L. Instead, the Basic taxonomy didn’t identify the slope of the OPER category as statistically 
significant for either dataset. However, this doesn’t contradict the conclusions made based on the results 
of the Basic taxonomy, as the operation of an installation is part of the overall management. 
So, in total, for the Taxonomy L too, it appears that the improvement in reliability mainly happened due 
to reasons not related to equipment and rather, it happened due to improved procedures and overall 
management.

Taxonomy S 
The two taxonomies used so far (Basic taxonomy and Taxonomy L) have generally ignored the 
“Operational failure” column. As a result, the issue of whether there were confusing combinations of 
causal failure entries was raised, as for example a combination of a “failure related to design” entry in the 
“Design cause” column with a “dropped object” entry in the “Operational failure” column and with a “no 
procedural failure” in the “Procedural failure” column.  
As a principle, it was accepted that the combinations of entries in the HCRD were sensible, however, in 
any case, the Taxonomy S – meaning, the “stricter” variation of the Basic taxonomy – was as considering 
of the “Operational failure” column as of the “Design cause” and “Procedural failure” columns. To 
conclude, the idea behind Taxonomy S was to address the possibility of a confusing combination of 
entries.

In general, the results acquired from the Taxonomy S were in agreement with the results acquired from 
the Basic taxonomy. Mainly, the category EQUIP was increasing its ratio, whereas the category PROC was 
decreasing its ratio. This resulted in an increase of the YESEQUIP mega-category against the decrease of 
the NOEQUIP mega-category.  
However, a few minor differences with regards to the Basic taxonomy also appeared. Mainly, and only for 
the “Reportable” dataset, the categories DES and BOTH appeared to be increasing their ratios through 
time, along with bigger category BOTH*2.  
In order to confirm or reject the results of Taxonomy S, regression analysis was again used. It was likely 
that the results would be confirmed for the categories EQUIP and PROC, and for the mega-categories 
YESEQUIP and NOEQUIP, since the ratio points were rather orderly placed around the produced regression 
lines. Instead, the results that deviated from the results of the Basic taxonomy (DES and BOTH) were not 
expected to be statistically significant. 
So, again, the null hypothesis was that the slopes were in reality equal to zero: Ho: α 1 = 0 
And the alternate hypothesis was that the slopes were different from zero: H1: α 1 ≠ 0 
For the various categories, bigger categories and mega-categories, their slopes, the lower and upper limit 
of the slopes, their t-values and their P values are presented in the tables below (Tables A6.7 and A6.8). 
For a significance level of 0.05 and for 16 and 15 degrees of freedom (18 years minus 2 for the “All” dataset 
and 17 minus 2 for the “Reportable” dataset), the tcrit is equal to 2.120 and 2.132, respectively. The 
resulting P values of the categories that were less than 0.05 are marked with red in order to signal their 
statistical significance.

Again, even if Taxonomy S was quite different from the previous two, it gave approximately the same 
results regarding the statistical significance: For the “All” dataset, for the slopes of EQUIP, PROC, PROC*2 
and for the slopes of the two mega-categories, there was strong statistical evidence that they were not 
zero. Instead, for the “Reportable” dataset, the non-zero slopes were limited to EQUIP and the two mega-
categories.  
Furthermore, the findings regarding the positive slopes of DES, BOTH and BOTH*2 categories could be 
ignored, since the P values were so far from 0.05.  
To conclude, with Taxonomy S too, it appeared that the improvement in reliability was the result of factors 
not related to equipment and, more specifically, it appeared that the improvement in reliability was the 
result of improvement in procedures.
