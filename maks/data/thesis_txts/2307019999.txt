Abstract  
 
Energy savings represent one of the highest achievements of the European Union. This need 
derives from the willingness to reduce the Greenhouse Gases (GHG) emissions. In fact, production 
of energy represents one of the main causes of emission. 
 
It has been calculated that 40% of primary energy used in Europe is need to satisfy the energy 
demand coming from building sector. In this context, new regulations (ISO 50001) suggest the 
implementation of Energy Management Systems (EnMS). It is believed that those systems have 
big potential in improving energy efficiency in buildings. In fact, they can help to identify problems 
of energy waste, and, later on they will help the development of different strategies when 
connected to Smart Grid such as balancing demand and control and optimization of costs related. 
 
This study proposes a heating consumption forecasting technique to be implemented in EnMS. 
The data used to create the model are real historical data collected with 15 minutes timeframe 
from two offices buildings and one production factory. The model is created by using clustering 
method to gather the data and successively linear regression to interpolate the information. 
Results show an NRMSE of 0.10 and 0.16 respectively for office buildings and production factory.  
An application of the forecasting model is presented to detect possible systems faults, enabling 
energy manager to take action in order to avoid energy waste. The Fault Detection Diagnostic 
(FDD) is operated throughout the investigation of one day ahead forecast. 
 
Finally, a deeper analysis investigates possible costs and energy saving obtained by studying real 
operation of the heating system. The analysis is conducted by monthly investigation of heating 
consumption. In particular weekly profiles are compared with weekend ones. The results show 
potential saving up to 300% in the coldest months.

1 Introduction 
 
Climate change is one of the greatest threats facing the planet. Effects such as glaciers melting, 
shifting of plants and animals ranges, flowering of trees start sooner and earlier breaking up of ice 
on rivers and lakes are daily observed phenomena [1]. In addition, raising of temperature leads to 
scarcity of drinkable water, food scarcity and desertification, transforming Earth in unsuitable 
place for the developing of our species and not only. 
 
It is broadly known that major leaders of this phenomena are Greenhouse Gases (GHG). The 
concentration in the air of these gasses has been registered increasing since 1980 [2] and it is 
expected to continue to increase during the following years. The main emission sources are fossil 
fuels used during combustion for energy production.  
Among energy users, building sector represents one of the main consumers [3]. For example it is 
calculated that energy used in buildings in Europe accounts for 40% of the total consumption. [4]. 
 
In this context, many countries decided to react throughout the implementation of new 
regulations. In particular, the goal is to first reducing the emission until stopping them.    
European Union defined its own climate and energy package that involves different steps [5]: 
x 
20% CO2  emission reduction respect 1990 by 2020 
x 
40% CO2  emission reduction respect 1990 by 2040 
x 
80% CO2  emission reduction respect 1990 by 2050 
Denmark set even more ambition targets, aiming to be fossil fuel independent by 2020 [6]. 
 
Specifically, for buildings application, ISO 50001 was recently introduced. Its objective is to 
improve buildings energy performance and to identify savings potential. In particular, this 
standard suggests the installation of Energy Management Systems (EnMS) able to record energy 
consumption of the entire building. In this context the figure of energy manager becomes really 
important. They need to analyze the data in order to perform actions aiming to ensure energy 
savings.  
 
Furthermore, future buildings’ vision is moving toward the idea of smart buildings. This implies 
the introduction of smart systems aiming to optimize in every moment the energy relation 
between demand and supply and costs related.  
Energy consumption forecasting models will assume a central role because they will allow to 
evaluate different strategies helping the optimization of energy usage and costs.  
 
1.1 Motivation 
 
Energy Management Systems are having a big spread inside the energy management market. In 
particular, Facility Management companies have big interest in installing energy analysis tools to 
deliver energy savings services to their customers. 
In this context, energy manager figure is becoming more and more important, they need to 
control buildings’ performance and delivered energy saving strategies based on historical 
consumption data.  
In addition, the integration of smart cities concept and smart buildings open new scenarios in 
which historical building’s behavior information could be used to develop new strategies.

However, it is not always easy to determine how to use all the collected information. In fact, smart 
meters, used by Energy Management System software, are able to gather enormous quantity of

building’s behavior. However, a deeper analysis could provide with more outputs.  
In this context, possible example of how to analyze the gathered data would help energy

Furthermore, the implementation of forecasting models will have central role for the

1.2 Objectives 
 
The overall goal of this study aims to develop an energy forecasting model that can easily be 
implemented in Energy Management Systems software. The application of such a model can be 
several with different scopes. In particular, in this thesis it is tried to propose the predictive model 
to detect faults detection in the system aiming to help energy managers to reach energy saving. 
  
In addition, different historical consumption data analyses are investigated in order to support 
energy managers with several possible surveys aiming to detect energy savings. 
Based on that, a cost analysis I proposed to evaluate the economic aspect. 
 
The final outcome is a method that can easily be implemented in Energy Management System and 
helpful for energy managers.

2 Background 
 
Since after the industrial revolution (1760-1840), CO2 and GHG emissions have increased in the 
atmosphere [2] leading to the so-called greenhouse gas (GHG) effects [7].   
In the last decades, European countries established several targets to face the environmental 
problem. One of this is “2020 climate & energy package”, which sets a boundary of 20% in 
emission reduction compare with the level in 1990. In addition, it pushes for 20% integration of 
renewables in the EU portfolio and 20% improvement in energy efficiency [8]. 
In this context building sector plays an important role. People spend the majority of their lifetime 
inside buildings, which implies a large demand of energy in order to satisfy occupational activities 
as well as indoor climate comfort [9]. In particular, in most of the cases, high indoor climate 
performances are achieved by high-energy consumption. However, this result contrasts the goal 
of reduction in energy consumption. According with the International Energy Agency (IEA), 
buildings are responsible for 32% of the total final energy consumption, meaning around 40% in 
terms of primary energy consumption [10].  However, in 2013 the Global Buildings Performance 
Network claims that “it is technologically feasible to reach the total energy required for providing 
thermal comfort in buildings by nearly one third by 2050, compared to 2005 levels, despite an 
increase in floor area of nearly 130%” [11]. 
 
During the last decades big efforts have been done from researchers in order to develop new 
technologies and methods to increase the energy performance of the buildings, hence reduce the 
energy consumption. The main actions that have been investigated until now are building 
renovation and energy management by monitoring facilities’ consumptions.  
The first one is an expenses action that aims in elements renovation such as wall, roof materials, 
windows type as well as renovation of facilities systems such as ventilation, heating and air 
conditioning.  
The second solution has been developed mainly in the last years thank to technologies 
development. It focuses on energy consumption optimization by using so called Energy 
Management systems (EnMS). This solution involves very low or no investment costs [12].  
 
2.1 Building energy renovation 
 
Energy renovation often consists in improving building’s body’s materials. This means to add extra 
insulation layers to external walls and roof.  
Moreover, building renovation includes facilities systems substitution. 
The Building Performance Institute Europe, in its report “A guide to developing strategies for 
building energy renovation”, describes several benefits rising from improving the energy 
performance of buildings by operating building renovation actions. The study reports energy 
savings of 20-30% up to 60% with deep renovation [13].  
 
2.2 Energy Management Systems 
 
Energy Management Systems are energy monitoring tools that allow users to achieve possible 
savings by energy management. In fact, using these systems enables to track energy consumption, 
detect system´s faults and forecasting consumption in order to optimize the entire building 
performance.  
The implementation of these tools has been pushed by the introduction of ISO 50001. This 
standard aims to provide every type of organizations with an international recognized structure 
for implementing an energy management system (EnMS).

In order to evaluate the effective benefits that energy management can achieve, several European

In addition, EnMSs are considered even more interesting since they require less personnel, time 
and financial resources than other energy saving solution [15]. 
 
Application examples 
 
In the last years, many companies decided to invest in these systems. 
One EnMS application example can be found in one of Arden Wessels’s works. In his investigation 
Wessels summarized different study cases of energy savings achieved by Toyota SA Company 
through the implementation of Energy Management System (EnMS) [41].  
 
EnMSs have been applied also in gold mines. H.G. Brand at al. describes how the implementation 
of these systems lead to a potential savings. The idea was to install an EnMS software in one gold 
mine and use the information from the system to perform different projects to reduce the energy 
consumption. The system identified five possible projects related to the energy eater of the mine´s 
gold extraction procedure such as cooling, pumping, ventilation and compressed air. The final 
savings was calculated to be 10.66 MW [16].  
 
In the article “Saving energy with management systems”, the author Juliane Branzel describes 
how Germany breweries companies used EnMS to save energy out of their energy eater 
processes. In particular, in this German case, breweries were able to create the prerequisites to 
achieve tax benefits by increasing the energy performance of their buildings according with ISO 
50001 [15].  
2.2.1 Applications 
 
As stated above, Energy Management Systems deal with problems related with how building’s 
facilities are operated. Such a context is difficult to study. In fact, buildings’ systems are run by 
people who have different perception and needs. Being able to predict people’s behavior 
represent a big resource topic all around the world. In 2009, Mahdavi et al. studied the interaction 
between users’ presence and the building. It was conclude how difficult is to predict the general 
behavior of a single person. However, looking at different buildings and group of people in a long 
term can generate a certain reference pattern.  Furthermore, it was pointed out the strong impact 
that users have on the energy consumption based on their behavior. [17] 
 
Pang Xiufeng et al. evaluated the difference performance of a building from the design condition 
(simulation) and the practice operation. What they concluded is that in most commercial buildings 
do not perform in practice as intended in the design process. The reasons can be different: faulty 
in construction, deterioration over the time but also incorrectly configured control systems and 
inappropriate operation procedures [18].  In particular, Energy Management Systems can solve

Fault Detection and Diagnostic 
 
In order to face the inappropriate buildings operation problems a lot of research has been done 
in Fault Detection and Diagnosis (FDD). The aim is to reduce most of the buildings’ problems 
throughout the systems’ faults study.  
Typical problems addressed by FDD in existing buildings are: scheduling problems, simultaneous 
heating and cooling, faulty controls, deactivated or falsely set controls, lack of sensor calibration, 
set-points manual reset to compensate for inadequate system operation [12]. 
 
In one of  his article, Tzivanidis shows and energy savings of 10% by working on the thermostat 
operation mode and cooling power in terms of time [19].  
 
In the article “Building operation and energy performance: Monitoring, analysis and optimization 
toolkit” Andrea Costa et al. propose a novel integrated toolkit designed to assist the energy 
managers. The new approach is mainly divided in three steps: 
 
1. Structured performance definition using Building information Model (BIM) methods and 
technology 
2. Performance monitoring and analysis including transforming the measured data into 
useful information for FDD activities 
3. Evaluation of different building operation strategies based on calibrated whole building 
energy simulation models 
 
The crucial part of the entire method is the second step where the energy manager needs to be 
able to analysis the building/system performances in order to identify possible system’s faults, 
hence possible energy savings. The method developed by the European research project Building 
EQ defines analysis algorithm based on standard visualization techniques that facilitate 
identification of possible pattern. In particular, the method used mainly time series plot, scatter 
plots carpet and box plot that can easily provide clear characteristic patterns. 
The last step is also important, BES model is used to satisfy the hypothesis for the application of 
the Key Factors methodology. This approach was developed in 2008 and it is an energy manager’s 
supporting tool that aims to find the optimal solution to satisfy both energy consumption and 
indoor climate quality. These results are achieved by using a model that can represent the building 
as close as possible. To this calibrated representation, Key Factors variation is performed in order 
to find different operation strategies. Those factors are parameters that influence the 
environmental and energy performance of the building such as temperature set point, lightening 
levels set points etc. In this way the energy manager can have different operation strategies to 
evaluate [12].

Forecasting 
 
Being able to forecast energy consumption in buildings is considered a hot topic. In fact, people 
interested in such information are experts operating in different fields such as energy traders, 
Facility Management companies and, recently, smart cities developer.

For the first ones, energy forecast assumes a central role because allows them to optimizes their 
profit by matching demand and supply in the electricity market. In this context many methods 
have been developed to estimate energy consumption of groups of users. The main studied facility 
in this sector is electricity [22], [23]. 
Moreover, forecasting consumption could help facility managers in analyzing buildings’ 
performances. In fact, a predictive model would be able to give in advance an idea of which would 
be the expected building behavior under certain inputs. Subsequently, building analysts could 
compare the real consumption with the expected and determine whether any operation faults 
occur.    
 
As mentioned above, in this sector several researches have been studied to develop software 
based on mathematical models unable to forecast energy consumption of buildings. 
However, the existing researches need improvement in three aspects [10]:

events [26].

2.3 Users behavior 
 
Other studies focus on users’ behavior forecast. In fact, it has been assessed that the way users 
behave can highly influence the total energy consumption [17]. 
In 2009, Mahdavi et al. studied the interaction between users’ presence and the building. The 
infrastructures were placed in Austria. The aim was to analyze the user control actions pertaining 
to building systems while considering the indoor and outdoor environmental conditions under 
which those actions occurred. 
The study pointed out that such interactions are difficult to predict at a single person level. 
However, looking at different buildings and group of people in a long term, it is possible to define 
a certain pattern. In particular, this pattern was seen to be highly related to indoor and outdoor 
condition, thus based on those data it is possible to provide a reliable basis for occupancy and 
control action models in performance simulation applications. Such information can support an 
energy manager to find an optimal solution and reduce the energy consumption [17].

2.4 Future 
 
In the last years a big effort from researchers and companies have been done to develop smart 
meters and engineering software such as EnMS system. According with Nelson Fumo and M.A. 
Rafe Biaswas [9] the future of residential energy consumption prediction will move toward to the 
analysis of individual dwellings leaving behind models that use data from a sample to describe the 
population. The same concept can be applied to office buildings. Where the specific analysis of 
the building can better forecast the energy consumption providing with a specific model solution. 
 
Furthermore, cities are developing towards the concept of smart cities where every type of real 
estates will be connected to smart grids. In this context being able of forecast energy consumption 
in single buildings will be even more interesting and crucial. In fact, an automatic analysis between 
forecasted energy consumption and prices would give the possibility to the user to get the 
cheapest price by accumulating during the cheap hours and using during the expensive period. 
The forecast of the consumption will also allow to determine the storage power capacity.

3 Methodology  
 
Nowadays energy consumption analysis is becoming more and more important for both economic 
and technical reasons.  
The aim of this study is to find a reliable energy-consumption forecasting model that can easily be 
implemented in Energy Management System (EnMS) software. In addition, energy consumption 
usage and costs related are investigated in order to provide energy analysis methods for energy 
managers.  
Many researches have been studied around this topic. However, none of them, at the knowledge 
of the author, seems to be practically introduced in the industry of EnMS.  
 
In particular, this thesis aims to analyze single buildings (offices and production factory). 
In order to be able to do so, a heavy effort has been done in the preliminary work. Throughout 
the literature review the EnMS’s state of art has been assessed and different forecasting models 
with their applications has been evaluated to identify the optimal solution. 
 
This section will present the main point on which the thesis has been developed. In particular, it 
gives an idea of the different steps that leaded to the application of the final forecasting model.

3.4 Clustering 
 
Throughout the literature it was seen that grouping input data based on similar pattern is a widely 
used technique in data investigation sector. In particular, clustering methods are commonly used 
to analyze energy consumption. For example, clustering is used to identify different costumer 
categories in electricity market based on load profile [33],[22]. 
 
Furthermore, clustering can be used in forecasting energy consumption in buildings. One example 
is provided  by Vladimir Cherkassky et al. who studied a clustering based model to predict 
electricity consumption in commercial buildings [28]. 
 
The goal is to gather the usage data in order to successively apply linear relation enable to describe 
the consumption behavior. In this way, it will be possible to develop an energy consumption 
prediction formulation. 
 
Clustered groups can be shaped following two techniques: 
 
1. Mathematical algorithms 
2. Observation criteria 
 
The first method uses clustering techniques based on mathematical algorithm such as [22]:

x

x

x 
Modified follow the leader

are groups in which elements are gathered accordingly with the selected algorithm’s criteria.  
One example can be using some distance criteria such as Euclidean norm [22].

3.7.1 Monthly mean peak consumption values

The aim of this analysis is to investigate the mean value during ramp-up hours in order to compare 
the obtained results for weekdays and weekends. From the investigation it would be possible to 
determine whether the heating operation schedule has to be optimized.  
Furthermore, comparing the mean peak consumption between the different buildings allows 
assessing the overall building performance. 
The analyzed mean values were calculated using the geometric mean formula (21). From 
literature, this type of average value is suggested when dealing with bench of data obtained by 
fluctuating trends with possibility of noise peaks. In fact, geometric mean has the property of not 
being greatly influenced by outlier values. On the contrary, arithmetic mean is the most common 
calculation when averages are needed. It is often used to report central tendency of the data set. 
However, this type of calculation is highly influenced by outliers.

developed previously. In particular it calculates the geometric mean of values gathered in ramp-

3.7.2 Monthly base load 
 
The buildings’ base load was calculated in order to evaluate the minimum heating consumption 
to keep a certain temperature inside the building. This assessment represents a good indicator of 
estimating the expected heating consumption during non-occupied hours and during weekend 
days. In fact, since there are not people working in the office, it does not make sense to hit it up 
at 20-21°C. However, a minimum temperature should be kept constant in order to facilitate a 
rapid fulfillment of the required temperature when necessary (before users arrive at work). 
The base load was evaluated looking at the historical heating consumption data available for each 
heating month. The calculation was performed for both week and weekend days.  
The base load was established by calculating the geometric mean (formula (21)) of the 
consumption registered during “Non-occupied” hours.

3.9 Sensitivity 
 
The sensitivity analysis is an important tool enabling researches to deeply understand the 
analyzed data. In fact, investigating samples with sensitivity studies allows the possibility to verify 
the robustness of the model to same inputs data variation and to search for errors in the model 
by encountering unexpected relationships between inputs and outputs. In addition, it gives better 
understanding between relationships between variables.  
In this study two investigations were performed in order to deeply analyze the buildings’ 
performances. 
3.9.1 Input data 
 
As stated in Chapter 3.2 “enerlutec” provided heating consumption data only for 6 months. In the 
sensitivity analysis it was tried to increase the number of input consumption data in order to 
investigate the improvements of the forecast quality. In fact a bigger number of set data would 
allow to consider more building’s behavior information. In particular, it would help to reduce the 
“over-fitting” problem. This phenomena occurs when the fitting line is calculated among few input 
information [37]. It is characterized by high R2 values (close to 1). However, when the linear

4.5.1 Assumption 
 
In all investigations it was assumed a heating consumption equal to the base load during weekend 
days. In fact, the building should be heated at the minimum level to be able to keep a minimum 
acceptable temperature. This will allow to rapidly reaching the desired comfortable temperature 
once people are expected to occupy the edifice again. Every type of consumption higher than the 
base load was considered not necessary and as a consequence wasting energy leading to potential 
savings.

4.6.2 Variation in heating price 
 
In the previous calculations the heating cost was considered to be constant at 0.07 CHF/kWh. 
However, it can happen that heating suppliers provide different tariffs for peaks heating 
consumption. The criteria could be time of the day or high heating consumption values.  
In this analysis the consequences of having different cost for peak consumption and normal 
consumption were estimated. 
Peak consumption is defined to be the difference between the registered consumption in “Ramp-
up” and the mean value of “Occupied” hours.  
 
Increase the peak price leads to an overall increment of the total heating cost. Therefore, it would 
be interesting to eliminate the peak consumption. Peaks are used to face the non-operation of 
the heating system during the night. Hence, they can be eliminated by keeping a constant 
consumption during all day.  
In particular, the investigation aims to identify the peak price that makes more convenient to keep 
a constant consumption instead of having peaks in the early morning.

5 Discussion 
 
This Investigation assumes an important role inside the study of EnMS applications. In fact, from 
literature it was seen that there are not many application examples of this technology in office 
buildings [15], [16], [41].  
It has to be considered that only relevant results will be taken into account. 
5.1 Weather data 
 
All the investigations performed in this study assume that the weather data are highly reliable and 
collected directly on site. However, historical weather data of outdoor temperature have been 
collected from a weather station located 14 km away from Bade city. It can be easily expected 
that this imprecision has big impact on the final results. In order to solve the problem, the 
implementation of thermometers measuring outside temperature on site will provide with more 
precise data. 
In addition, the used weather station did not provide with information regarding solar radiation. 
Such variable has big impact on energy consumption. In fact, variation in solar radiation influences 
solar gain leading to lower required energy during the winter and higher electricity consumption 
in the summer. Adding this information to the forecasting model could increase its reliability.   
 
5.2 Occupants presence 
 
In this work it was experienced the difficulty of considering people’s effects on energy 
consumption. From literature [17],[29] it is known that people’ behavior has big impact on energy 
consumption. However, it is problematic to understand how people affect the energy usage when 
using EnMS. In fact, it is difficult to keep track about information such as: daily number of 
occupants inside a room, number of windows opening and duration. Being able to collect such 
information will allow to create a much more complex mathematical relation that can improve 
reliability of the forecast. However, the problem could be partially solved if a thermometer placed 
inside the room could collect inside hourly temperature and thermostat set point.

In fact, results from the analysis allowed to identify possible setting mistakes in both heating and 
electric systems. 
 
In particular, comparing electricity consumption profile with findings from literature, it was 
possible to recognize similar patterns.

forecasting model. In addition to this, from literature it was seen that a lot has been study 
regarding forecasting of electricity consumption for both single and group buildings, therefore, it 
was considered interesting to focus on heating consumption [22], [23], [24], [25], [28]. 
5.4 Clustering 
 
As it was stated for the preliminary analysis, even cluster formation had a massive influence on 
the final results. In fact, the forecasting model is highly related to the data included in the clusters. 
 
In this study it was decided to use “Observation” clusters formation method. This means that a 
big effort is required to the energy manager. In fact, he is the responsible to investigate possible 
consumption pattern in order to classify the consumption. The importance of this role was also 
seen in literature [12].

5.7 Further analysis 
 
Further analysis investigation has been developed in order to detect possible energy saving by 
looking at the heating schedule and possible anomalies of the heating system. 
The studies were thought to be a general survey made by an energy manager aiming to analysis 
the building’s performance. 
5.7.1 Monthly based load 
 
A big limitation of using EnMSs is that the results are highly dependent on the historical data. 
Therefore, the estimated value could differ from the calculation operated by buildings’ 
performance software. However, the calculation performed in this study can be consider a reliable 
representation of the real value due to the heating consumption profile. 
 
The overall analysis of the monthly base load’s results shows a small difference between the load 
registered during the weekends and the weekdays. Such a result is the expected one. In fact, in 
both case it is the minimum consumption to avoid the temperature to drop below a certain 
temperature. Therefore, since the conditions for weekends and week days are the same during 
“Non-occupied” hours, the results should be the same. 
5.7.2 Monthly mean consumption 
 
Results from the monthly mean consumption during “Occupied” hours show an interesting 
outcome. In fact, it is seen that the two office buildings and the production site (during November 
and December) are heated during the weekends as occupants are at work. However, with such a 
schedule, the consumption during the non-working days should result to be higher during the 
weekends due to the absence of internal gains. On the contrary it is only slightly different.  
Such a result could be due to the fact that the number of data collected to calculate the average 
is lower for the weekend days than the week days. This problem can be solved by using a weight 
average. 
Another reason could be that the number of radiators running are lowers during the weekends. 
In fact, as seen from literature it is difficult to predict and record occupants’ behavior [17]. Most 
of the buildings use thermostat controllers inside rooms, others use to have the thermostat 
integrated in radiators. However, in both cases people have the possibility to operate on the 
heating system changing heating schedule. Therefore, they might close some radiators and leave 
others running. 
5.7.3 Monthly extra consumption 
 
It has to be considered that the monthly extra consumption calculated in this study is highly 
dependent on the assumption of how it was defined. In fact, the extra consumption during 
weekends was defined to be the difference between the hourly real consumption and the base 
load due to the assumption of constant base load during the weekends.  
The general trend was seen to be higher extra consumption during cold months and lower during 
warmer months. 
 
It is known that the assumptions considered, can strongly influence the results. However, having 
data only from the EnMS tool without being able to simulate alternative building’s performance

data would increase the information of how the building behaves under certain weather input. In

5.8.2 Variation in heating price  
 
The variation in heating price has been performed in order to evaluate a possible strategy in case 
of different price between peak and non-peak consumption. This situation is experienced with 
electricity facility but it is expected to become more reality with the future introduction of district 
heating.  In fact, having a hot water supply would push the heating delivery to develop a market 
similar to the electricity one.  
It has to be considered the having a constant consumption all day long as a proposal is considered 
to be an extreme solution. Anyway, it could represent a possible solution to base on the case 
study. Another examination could be to investigate the optimal number of hour of constant 
consumption to compensate the turn off during the night. In this way the peaks will still be 
reduced by a constant consumption but the number of hours will be lower. 
 
In addition, it has to be considered that in this study the peak consumption has been defined as 
the difference between the hourly consumption during “Ramp-up” period and the mean 
consumption value during “Occupied” hours. This means that the limit for peak consumption vary 
every month. However, in real life the limit is fixed and based on the contract with the retailer.  
 
From the analysis it has been see that Building 0694 and Building 0900 show opposite behavior. 
In fact, for the first one, having a constant consumption is always cheaper than having a peak. On 
the contrary, for Building 0900 having a constant consumption results to be always more 
expensive.

6 Conclusion 
 
The study performed in this thesis aimed to investigate possible potential uses of energy 
consumption data collected by EnMSs. In particular, a daily consumption-forecasting model was 
developed together with a possible application. In addition, a deep analysis of usage energy was 
performed as a sample for detecting savings and system’s operation schedule. 
 
The investigations were initially performed using an office building as a reference case (Building 
0342). Successively, the analyses were applied to another office building (Building 0694) and a 
production site (Building 0900). This allowed testing the investigation reliability and applicability 
for the same type of building but also others. 
 
From the study it was seen that a preliminary analysis obtained by simply plotting energy 
consumption over the time and Energy Signature represent an optimal starting point to 
understand buildings’ dependency between the variables. In particular, the outputs underline a 
strong dependency of heating consumption between outdoor temperature and daily working 
hours. These results allowed the author to develop energy savings analysis and consumption 
forecast model on heating facility usage.    
 
The heating consumption forecasting model was obtained by applying linear relation method 
among data collected inside different groups.  “Observation” criteria was used in order to cluster 
the data. Results showed that the model represent a reliable method for daily prediction of 
heating consumption. In particular, the reference case showed NMAE and NRMSE mean values 
among the test period of respectively 0.07 and 0.10. Well performing results were obtained also 
for Building 0694 with a mean NMAE value of 0.13 and NRMSE of 0.16. The forecasting model was 
found to be applicable and well performing for production site as well with 0.16 NMAE and 0.18 
NRMSE.  
Furthermore, it was observed that increasing the information in the input data pool would lead 
to a reduction of the forecast’s error up to maximum 5 % (Building 0900).  
 
Being able to forecast energy consumption represents an interesting topic in several contexts. In 
this study it was used the “One day ahead” forecast for Fault Detection Diagnostic (FDD).  The 
results showed that the method can be used to alarm energy manager regarding possible 
unexpected behavior of the building. At that point they can decide to operate together with the 
technician to detect system’s fault. 
 
Deeper analysis of heating consumption proposed in the study, displayed the important role of 
system’s operational schedule. 
In fact, it was seen that in all the selected buildings the operation schedule was not correctly 
balanced leading to heat up the areas when not necessary. In particular, the phenomena was 
observed comparing different consumption pattern during the week with other during the 
weekends. It was investigated that a correct operation and control of the heating planning would 
reduce energy consumption and energy cost. It was conclude that the savings can be higher than 
100% percent during coldest months. 
In conclusion, using a constant consumption pattern was seen to be a possible cheaper solution 
than peak consumption profile depending on heating tariffs. In fact, based on the building and 
cost of the peak consumption, eliminate peak energy would allow to reduce the heating costs.
