ABSTRACT 
The challenge of feeding a rapidly increasing world population based on an agricultural system 
threatened by pests is a major global problem. Agrochemicals such as pesticides frequently fail 
in protection of crops, and are furthermore involved in a range of adverse effects. This problem 
calls for more effective and sustainable solutions. The utilisation of naturally present micro-
organisms for this purpose has become a promising alternative. However, more research is 
required to develop reliable and effective commercially available biological control agents 
(BCAs). 
The purpose of this study has been to identify optimal growth conditions and media for the 
isolation and characterisation of bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas from soil, as an 
overwhelming amount of results speak in favour of using this genus as a BCA. By selecting 
fluorescent colonies growing on Pseudomonas Base Agar an exclusive library of Pseudomonas 
species was created, verified by 16S rRNA sequencing. This library was subsequently examined 
for the presence of biocontrol traits and antagonistic activity towards five phytopathogenic 
bacteria in a high-throughput setup. Some of the most efficient antagonistic strains were 
furthermore chosen for whole-genome sequencing which could confirm some of the already 
detected biocontrol traits, as well as reveal additional biocontrol-related genes.  
The procedures developed in this study can be used for future large-scale screenings of 
bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas. In addition, the effective antagonistic strains already 
isolated can be further examined to determine their potential as commercial BCAs.

fluorescerende Pseudomonas i stor skala. Derudover kan de effektive antagonistiske stammer 
allerede isoleret blive undersøgt videre for at udforske deres potentiale som kommercielle 
BKAer.

1     INTRODUCTION 
The agronomical challenge of supplying the growing world population with food, combined 
with the pressure of protecting crops and food products from pests is of great concern. 
Pathogens account for half of the yield losses due to pests, and the difficulties of controlling and 
eradicating phytopathogens makes this a relevant problem (Haas & Défago 2005). 
Agrochemicals, including pesticides, have been a common solution to this problem, however 
the range of adverse effects caused by these chemicals are driving the search for more 
environmentally friendly approaches to control plant diseases (Walsh et al. 2001). One possible 
approach is the use of plant-growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR).  
The academic interest for using microorganisms in the protection of plants developed in the 
early 1970’s. PGPRs are bacteria found at the root surfaces that can improve growth of plants, 
directly or indirectly (Ahmad et al. 2008). Specific bacteria are able to contribute to direct 
growth promotion by providing the plant with plant growth promoting substances, such as 
phytohormones, or facilitate the uptake of plant nutrients from the environment. Indirect 
mechanisms of plant growth involve attenuation or complete suppression of deleterious effects 
caused by one or more phytopathogens (Ahmad et al. 2008). The type of PGPRs possessing the 
latter abilities are known as biological control agents (BCAs).  
It is consequently of great interest to identify effective BCAs for use as commercial agricultural 
applications worldwide.  One of the most studied genera of PGPRs are Pseudomonas. Due to 
their catabolic versatility, excellent root-colonising abilities, and the capacity to produce an 
arsenal of antimicrobials, fluorescent pseudomonads have proven to be efficient BCAs (Walsh 
et al. 2001). However, there are some challenges of using BCAs as commercial products. 
Although impressive results have been obtained in the laboratory and under greenhouse 
conditions, conflicting results have been observed for BCAs in the field (Ojiambo & Scherm 
2006; Haas & Défago 2005). One way to contribute is to gain more knowledge about the 
rhizosphere biodiversity, and obtain a deeper understanding of how the members of these 
communities interact.  
 
2     AIM OF STUDY 
The aim of this study was to isolate and characterise bioactive Pseudomonas from soil. Although 
this is not a novel concept, it is pioneer work within this lab, with the purpose of creating a solid 
basis for future research. The optimal conditions and media for growth and isolation of 
fluorescent Pseudomonas from various soil samples were characterised prior to identification 
of important biocontrol mechanisms possessed by the isolates. In addition, bioactivity was 
quantitatively explored using bioassays in an agar plate setup. The purpose was further to 
design high throughput screening methods for isolation and characterisation, with the aim of 
using these for future large-scale experiments.

3     THE GLOBAL AGRICULTURAL CHALLENGE 
The world population is growing immensely fast with a doubling of the human population from 
1960 to 2003 (Oerke 2006). This requires agricultural productivity and a sustainable 
production to meet the growing food demand. Another challenge seen in agriculture are yield 
losses caused by pests; pathogens, animals and weeds, which are combined responsible for up 
to 40% loss of total agricultural productivity, where pathogens account for half of this amount. 
Furthermore, post-harvest losses due to pathogens, which are not included in this number, 
contributes to further damages and loss of food products (Savary et al. 2012; Bebber et al. 
2014).  
Soil-borne pathogens belong to several different phyla, including bacteria, fungi and nematodes 
(Haas & Défago 2005). The pathogens reside in the soil for brief or extended periods where 
they survive on plant residues or as resting organisms until root exudates reach their location 
and thus allow them to grow. By penetrating the roots, the pathogens can escape competition 
with other microorganisms, where they remain until the host plant is dead, or they can move 
outside the plant to infect other parts of the root (Haas & Défago 2005). Whereas weeds can be 
controlled with a 75 % efficacy, only 32 % efficiency is reached for pathogen control in 
agriculture (Oerke 2006). Soil-borne pathogens can have devastating effects on crops, and they 
are notoriously difficult to control. Actions such as crop rotation and resistant bred plants have 
proven insufficient to control important plant pathogens (Haas & Défago 2005). 
Protection from pests is a necessity to meet the ever-growing human demand for food quantity 
and quality. A method used abundantly since the early 1960’s are pesticides. Ideally, the 
pesticide must be lethal exclusively to the target organism and not to any other non-target 
species, which unfortunately, is not the current situation (Aktar et al. 2009). It has been 
estimated that more than 26 million people worldwide suffer from non-fatal pesticide 
poisonings (Pimentel & Burgess 2014). Of all annually poisonings there is about 3 million 
hospitalised cases, 220,000 fatalities and 750,000 chronic illnesses, with chronic effects 
including irreversible neurological, respiratory and reproductive effects as well as cancer 
(Pimentel & Burgess 2014). In the environment, pesticides are frequently detected in surface 
and groundwater, and soil erosion guides pesticides into rivers and lakes, further 
contaminating fish and aquatic systems. Moreover, pesticides have a negative impact on 
wildlife populations, affecting habitat, growth and reproductive systems (Pimentel & Burgess 
2014).  
Not only do these chemicals pose a potential risk to humans and our environment, they also 
interfere with the ecosystem of the soil (Aktar et al. 2009). An important aspect is the effect 
pesticides have on soil microorganisms, which was formulated by Dr. Elaine Ingham, “If we lose 
both bacteria and fungi, then the soil degrades. Overuse of chemical fertilizers and pesticides 
have effects on the soil organisms that are similar to human overuse of antibiotics. 
Indiscriminate use of chemicals might work for a few years, but after awhile, there aren’t 
==PAGE:10==
Page | 9  
 
enough beneficial soil organisms to hold on to the nutrients” (Santos & Flores 1995). Microbes 
are key players in the soil ecosystems as they break down organic matter, recycle chemical 
elements and make nitrogen available for plants. In addition to plant pathogens, pesticides also 
affect natural beneficial microorganisms and hence the biodiversity in the soil. This 
occasionally results in major pest outbreaks due to the destruction of their natural enemies, 
with the outcome of a vicious circle where even more expensive pesticide treatments are 
required to sustain crop yields (Pimentel & Burgess 2014). In the end, all of the indirect costs 
of pesticide use are likely to counterbalance its economical profitability.  
Agriculture is facing a major global challenge trying to feed the world population in a time with 
increasing energy prices, reduction in land and resources, globalisation and climate changes 
(Chaparro et al. 2012). As pesticides are not a sustainable solution to pests, new pest control 
programs should maximise the benefits, while at the same time minimising the health, 
environmental and social costs. Today there is an increasing public awareness of ecological 
concerns and a trend toward environmental values calling for sustainable solutions. This 
awareness is motivating researchers to create biotechnological approaches to provide 
nonchemical sustainable pest management.  
 
4     THE RHIZOSPHERE 
The largest reservoirs of biodiversity are found in soils, where the rhizosphere probably 
represents the most dynamic and complex habitat on Earth (Hinsinger et al. 2009). The 
rhizosphere is the zone of soil surrounding the plant roots, where microbe populations are 
influenced by root activities. Soil is a nutrient-depleted environment, consequently, 
microorganisms aggregate near nutrient sources, such as plants, where they create microbial 
hotspots (Effmert et al. 2012). Compared to unplanted soil, the microbial activities are 10-1000 
times higher in the rhizosphere (Haas & Défago 2005; Hinsinger et al. 2009).   
The rhizosphere is an important area for metabolism, and this complex food web consists of 
several organisms, including bacteria, fungi, oomycetes, nematodes, protozoa, algae, viruses, 
archaea and arthropods, with bacteria being the most abundant organism (Mendes et al. 2013; 
Sivasakthi et al. 2014). The astonishing number of microorganisms surrounding plant roots can 
reach cell densities far greater than the number of plant cells. These communities of 
microorganisms are referred to as the plants microbiome, with the number of microbial genes 
immensely outnumbering that of the plants (Mendes et al. 2013). As for humans and the human 
microbiota, the plants have coevolved symbiotic relations with their microbiome. Plants 
deposit and invest a significant proportion of their photosynthetically fixed carbon into the 
rhizosphere and thus in the maintenance of their microbiota, including the exudation of sugars, 
amino acids, organic acids, and mucilage. In return, the rhizosphere microbiota provide specific 
functions and traits, such as improved root architecture, enhanced nutrient uptake, and 
protection against pathogens (Mendes et al. 2013; Pieterse et al. 2016).  
==PAGE:11==
Page | 10  
 
It was proposed by Cook et al. that plants selectively stimulate and support microbial 
rhizosphere populations as a defence strategy (Cook et al. 1995). Thus, by selective exudation, 
the plant can attract specific microorganisms with traits that are beneficial to plant growth and 
health. Over the years, a convincing amount of evidence has shown that microorganisms are 
able to suppress infection of plants, (Duffy & Défago 1999; Raaijmakers et al. 1999; Zhou et al. 
2012; Sang & Kim 2014; Michelsen et al. 2015; Latz et al. 2016). The geographic areas where 
the incidence of severity of disease is lower than expected for the prevailing surroundings have 
become known as disease-suppressive soil. In The Glossary of Plant-Pathological Terms 
(Shurtleff & Averre 1997), disease-suppressive soils are defined as “Soils in which certain 
diseases are suppressed due to the presence in the soil of microorganisms antagonistic to the 
pathogen or pathogens.” This means that, even though pathogens are present in the soil and the 
host plant is susceptible to the disease, the plant does not suffer from the disease, or that disease 
severity is reduced. The microbial basis of several disease-suppressive soils has been 
established through mainly two research strategies. First, heat treatment or other antimicrobial 
treatments of the disease-suppressive soil has resulted in destruction of the suppressiveness, 
and secondly, suppressiveness can be transferable to conducive soils, which are soils that 
allows the development of disease (Weller et al. 2002; Haas & Défago 2005). Thus there is 
strong evidence that disease suppression is the result of the presence of certain rhizobacteria 
with antagonistic activities.  
However, there are not merely beneficial microorganisms being able to colonise the plant roots. 
Root exudates also attract detrimental organisms such as phytopathogens, which can harm 
plants as well as the beneficial microbiota (Chaparro et al. 2012). The discovery of disease-
suppressive soil more than 70 years ago resulted in an idea of using natural microorganisms in 
the control of plant diseases (Haas & Défago 2005). The thought of using living creatures that 
are self-sustainable started to develop, which would reduce the need for repeated applications, 
avoid the problem of pathogens evolving resistance, while at the same time provide an 
environmentally friendly and social sustainable solution to the pest problem (Chaparro et al. 
2012). Several bacterial species isolated from various disease-suppressive soils have shown to 
be beneficial for plant health and growth (Voisard et al. 1989; Raaijmakers & Weller 1998; 
Michelsen & Stougaard 2011), and bacteria with such traits are known as plant growth-
promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR). PGPR may promote direct or indirect growth of the plant. 
Direct plant growth is caused by the bacterium providing the plant with growth promoting 
substances or facilitate nutrient uptake from the environment, such as nitrogen fixation and 
solubilisation of minerals. Indirect growth promotion however, improves plant growth through 
biocontrol where the plant pathogen is antagonised. These PGPRs are known as biopesticides 
or biological control agents (BCAs).

5     BIOLOGICAL CONTROL AGENTS – PLANT PROBIOTICS 
In one gram of soil it is estimated that the number of bacterial cells can exceed 1010, with the 
number of species varying from 103 to 105. The rhizosphere is a densely and diverse habitat for 
microorganisms where several types of interactions take place, including mutualism, 
commensalism, cooperation, antagonism, competition and coexistence (Effmert et al. 2012). 
Soil-borne, non-pathogenic bacteria with the ability to antagonise phytopathogens to prevent 
plant diseases is a promising alternative to harmful pesticides. Some biological control agents 
are able to present broad antagonism toward plant pathogens, whereas others can be 
pathogen-specific, or even specific to a certain type of soil or plant species (Hernández-León et 
al. 2015).  
Biological control agents can be considered as the plants frontline defence against soilborne 
pathogens (Cook et al. 1995). Thus, plant growth is indirectly supported through improvement 
of growth restricting conditions towards plant pathogens. The main mechanisms of this 
protection includes antibiosis, competition for nutrients, trace elements and space, parasitism, 
interference with quorum sensing and thus virulence, and induced systemic resistance 
(Mendes et al. 2013). Based on these traits, biological control agents can be described as plant 
probiotics, where the common function for plant and gastrointestinal probiotics is a beneficial 
microflora reducing a range of diseases caused by pathogens (Picard & Bosco 2007).  
In antibiosis the antagonist is able to produce low-molecular-weight compounds that are 
deleterious to the growth or metabolism of other microorganisms, namely antibiotics. 
Furthermore, metabolites such as volatiles and enzymes produced by biocontrol bacteria have 
shown to be important in the suppression of plant pathogens (Raaijmakers et al. 2002; Haas & 
Défago 2005; Raaijmakers & Mazzola 2012). Most BCAs produce multiple antibiotics, and many 
of these exhibit broad-spectrum activity, thus a range of pathogens can be targeted. There is an 
overwhelming number of studies contributing to the evidence that antibiotics are important 
for the suppression of plant pathogens by antagonistic bacteria. Mainly four methods have been 
used to determine the importance of antibiotics in biocontrol (Raaijmakers et al. 2002). One 
approach is to use culture filtrates or purified antibiotics to determine if these provide similar 
levels of control as achieved by the producing biocontrol strain (Nakayama et al. 1999). Second, 
mutagenesis and thus inactivation of antibiotic production should result in a reduced ability of 
the biocontrol strain to control the pathogen. Mutagenesis was successfully used to 
demonstrate the effect phenazine had on take-all, a major disease of wheat. Pseudomonas 
fluorescens 2-79 mutants defective in phenazine synthesis were non-inhibitory to the pathogen. 
When restoring the phenazine production in these mutants, suppression could once more be 
observed, indicating an important role of this antibiotic in disease suppression (Thomashow & 
Weller 1988).  A third approach include enhancement of antibiotic production in the producing 
biocontrol strain (Ligon et al. 2000). Last, antibiotic biosynthetic genes can be introduced into 
heterologous, non-producing strains followed by evaluation of their ability to control plant 
pathogens. This was performed by Fenton et al, where a plasmid containing a genomic fragment 
==PAGE:13==
Page | 12  
 
required for production of 2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG) was transferred into the DAPG-
nonproducing Pseudomonas strain M114. Efficient production of DAPG was observed together 
with enhanced antagonism towards the plant pathogen P. ultimum compared to the parent 
strain M114 (Fenton et al. 1992).  
Another possible mechanism in plant protection is competition. This includes competition for 
nutrients, trace elements and suitable niches on a root surface. It is thus required that the 
biological control agent is able to effectively scavenge and utilise the available nutrients to be 
able to constitute a significant proportion of the rhizosphere (O’Sullivan & O’Gara 1992). 
Successful root colonisation is therefore a key trait of biocontrol agents, and it has been 
estimated that BCAs represent 0.1-1% of the culturable aerobic rhizobacterial population (Haas 
& Défago 2005). 
Recent studies suggest that the preferred lifestyle of microorganisms living in soil 
environments is within a biofilm (Pandin et al. 2017). Bacteria are highly capable of forming 
biofilms on crops and in the rhizosphere, which is not surprising as this mode of life can confer 
a diversification of cell types and increased tolerance to environmental stress (Pandin et al. 
2017). Furthermore, it has been shown that biofilm formation by biological control agents can 
be induced by plant root exudates, exposure to antimicrobials, or stress (Selin et al. 2010; Sang 
& Kim 2014; Chi et al. 2015). Biofilm formation can be an important asset in biocontrol with the 
following beneficial biocontrol properties: i) antagonism by niche exclusion due to spatial and 
nutritional competition, ii) microbial communication/interference, iii) production of 
antimicrobials by biofilm members, iv) stress tolerance, and v) direct effects on plant 
physiology (Pandin et al. 2017). Specific signalling molecules, autoinducers, are used to sense 
and communicate with surrounding microorganisms, a regulatory response called quorum 
sensing (QS). QS is important in regulation of a variety of phenotypes in bacteria, including 
biofilm formation, toxin production, virulence factors, and motility, which are essential for 
successful establishment of a pathogenic relationship with their plant host. Quorum sensing 
interference (QSI) compounds are molecules that mimic the structure of autoinducers and/or 
function as compounds that are antagonistic to the autoinducer molecules, thus repress 
quorum sensing (González & Keshavan 2006). This can be accomplished through interference 
with receptor proteins (Ji et al. 1997) or by hydrolysis of signalling molecules (Dong et al. 2007). 
These are highly conserved enzymes, typically with a broad range that targets long or short 
acyl-chain molecules (Vaz Jauri & Kinkel 2014).  
The phenomenon of induced systemic resistance (ISR) is the ability of microorganisms to 
induce innate defence mechanisms in the plant, thus making it more qualified to defend itself 
towards a broad range of pathogens (Haas & Défago 2005). ISR positive plants are able to react 
much faster and stronger to pathogen attacks. This response is believed to be elicited by stimuli 
such as volatiles, QS signalling molecules and antibiotics produced by biocontrol agents, 
compounds also involved in antagonism (Jain & Das 2016; Pandin et al. 2017). The effect of ISR 
on the plant is seen through various mechanisms: growth promotion, physiological tolerance, 
==PAGE:14==
Page | 13  
 
induction of cell wall reinforcement, and increase in the production of defence enzymes, 
antioxidants and pathogenesis-related proteins (Jain & Das 2016).  
 
 
Figure 1 – Interactions between plants, beneficial bacteria and pathogens in soil. The plant excretes 
exudates which attracts plant-growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) with biological control abilities. 
These bacteria are able to elicit induced systemic resistance (ISR) in the plant, making it more capable 
of defending itself against phytopathogens. Furthermore, the bacteria are able to antagonise plant 
pathogens, which also are attracted by the nutritious environment offered by the plant (Haas & Défago 
2005).  
 
However, antagonism and competition are not the only key traits a BCA should possess. 
Qualities important in biocontrol include rapid growth in vitro in relation to mass production, 
rapid utilisation of seed and root exudates, efficient colonisation and growth in the rhizosphere 
and/or in the interior of the plant, production of a wide range of bioactive metabolites, the 
ability to aggressively compete with other microorganisms, and adaptation to environmental 
stresses (Weller 2007). These mechanisms are well documented for rhizobacteria belonging to 
Firmicutes and Proteobacteria, with some of the most studied genera being Bacillus and 
Pseudomonas (Mendes et al. 2013).

5.1     Fluorescent Pseudomonas 
Pseudomonas is typically rod-shaped, gram-negative, aerobic, motile, non-endospore forming 
bacteria belonging to the family γ-proteobacteria. This genus is considered the most diverse 
and ecologically significant group of bacteria on Earth (Yadav et al. 2014). They are found in a 
large variety of environments, including freshwater, seawater, soil, plants and humans, which 
reflects their high degree of physiological and genetic adaptability. After sequencing complete 
genomes of Pseudomonas species, it was found that only 25% to 35% of each individual genome 
consisted of core genes, which confirms their diversity (Silby et al. 2011; Baltrus et al. 2011; 
Loper et al. 2012). The genus Pseudomonas comprise over 100 species, where more than 50 of 
these species belong to the group of fluorescent Pseudomonas (Loper et al. 2012). The large size 
of Pseudomonas genomes most probably reflects their abilities to encounter a range of 
ecological niches on a regular basis (Spiers et al. 2000).  
Fluorescent Pseudomonas can be distinguished from other pseudomonads by their ability to 
produce an extracellular fluorescent pigment. Within this group, well-known plant pathogens, 
such as P. syringae and P. aeruginosa, an opportunistic human pathogen, can be found. Others 
are able to form commensal relationships with plants, a quality of great interest. Fluorescent 
Pseudomonas is one of the most promising groups of BCAs, as they possess many of the 
characteristics necessary to protect plants against pathogens.

5.2     Biocontrol Related Characteristics of Fluorescent Pseudomonas 
The ability to successfully colonise the root surface is a crucial factor for effective biocontrol of 
plant pathogens. Considering the complex environment of the rhizosphere a vast array of genes 
and traits have shown to be important for colonisation. These include cell surface structures, 
flagella, enzymes, and chemotaxis (Sang & Kim 2014). Once attached to a root, microcolonies of 
fluorescent Pseudomonas form within a few days, with cell densities typically ranging from 103 
to 107 colony forming units (CFU) cm-1 of root (Haas & Défago 2005). 
It is important that the bacterium is able to recognise and adhere to the plant roots, a process 
stimulated by root exudates composed of organic molecules such as carbonic acids, amino acids 
and sugars. Chemotaxis is an important trait for colonisation, and this has been demonstrated 
for P. aeruginosa, where it was found that root exudates altered gene expression of genes 
involved in chemotaxis (Mark et al. 2005). Motility has shown to be of utmost importance for 
attachment and colonisation, as motility allow cells to move towards nutrients such as root 
exudates (Turnbull et al. 2001). Furthermore, increased biofilm formation, enhanced motility, 
as well as reduced sensitivity to environmental stress has shown to promote colonisation to 
plant roots (Sang & Kim 2014). After recognition, a stable plant-bacterium interaction requires 
attachment of bacterial cells to the roots. For this purpose, bacteria can use cell surface proteins 
such as fimbriae, flagella, pili or O-antigen (Berg & Smalla 2009).  
Members of fluorescent Pseudomonas are well-known biological control agents with the 
capacity to produce a wide range of secondary metabolites. This characteristic contributes to 
plant protection and enables the bacteria to adapt rapidly to changing environments. The 
antimicrobial arsenal include hydrogen cyanide (HCN), pyoluteorin, phenazines, pyrrolnitrin, 
siderophores, cyclic lipopetides, and DAPG, as well as enzymes such as proteases, cellulases, 
chitinase and β-glucanase (Weller 2007; Hernández-León et al. 2015). Antibiotic producers are 
readily isolated from soils that are naturally suppressive to different plant diseases, suggesting 
that they play an important part in the natural biological control that takes place in these soils. 
P. fluorescens isolated from disease suppressive soil in Greenland where found to control 
Rhizoctonia solani by producing the two lipopetides, nunamycin and nunapeptin (Michelsen et 
al. 2015). Another study reported that suppressiveness to take-all in wheat was lost when DAPG 
producing fluorescent Pseudomonas spp. were eliminated. Furthermore, conducive soil gained 
similar levels of suppressiveness to take-all as natural suppressive soil when DAPG producing 
fluorescent Pseudomonas strains were introduced (Raaijmakers & Weller 1998). The majority 
of fluorescent Pseudomonas produce a combination of various antimicrobial compounds, and 
for some biocontrol agents, several antibiotics may account for the suppression of specific or 
multiple plant diseases. Furthermore, many of the antibiotics have a broad-spectrum activity. 
DAPG produced by a range of fluorescent Pseudomonas targets a variety of plant pathogenic 
fungi, as well as bacteria, nematodes, and protists (Raaijmakers et al. 2002).   
Secondary metabolites can be distinguished based on physiochemical properties: soluble 
secondary metabolites and volatile compounds. The antibiotics belong to the soluble secondary 
==PAGE:17==
Page | 16  
 
metabolites, which are soluble in water and exhibit stronger bioactivity, but are limited by short 
distances. Volatiles are small molecules that easily evaporate and diffuse through air- and 
water-filled pores. This property is ideal for long distance cooperation or competition (Tyc et 
al. 2016). Soil microorganisms produce large amounts of highly diverse volatiles, and the broad 
functionality of these are seen in: i) bioconversion, ii) physiological processes, such as 
nitrification, iii) electron acceptors and donors in metabolic reactions, iv) quorum sensing and 
quorum quenching, v) defence compounds, and vi) communication signalling (Effmert et al. 
2012). Volatiles play an important role in long distance interactions in microbial communities, 
where they function as infochemicals in intra- and interspecies communication, or they can act 
as antimicrobials and thus function as a biocontrol trait. Volatiles produced in the rhizosphere 
have in several studies been confirmed to possess antifungal qualities. The fungicidal nature of 
volatiles released from Pseudomonas sp. was seen as sclerotia and ascospore suppression by up 
to 90%, suggesting a potential role for volatiles in biological control (Fernando et al. 2005).

Identification and isolation of specific ISR elicitors is a difficult process. This can further be 
complicated by the fact that the ISR response to a given BCA strain depends on the plant species 
(Haas & Défago 2005). Others suggest that substances involved in ISR are partly the same as 
those involved in microbial antagonism, including siderophores, antibiotics, QS signalling 
molecules, and volatiles (Jain & Das 2016). In a previous report, it was suggested that a 
==PAGE:18==
Page | 17  
 
siderophore was involved in the induction of ISR by selected P. fluorescens strains (Leeman et 
al. 1996). 
The complex environment in the soil confers several challenges for its microbial inhabitants, 
being exposed to different kinds of stresses of both biotic and abiotic origin, including 
antibiotics, temperature and pH. Two-component signal transduction systems enable 
microorganisms to respond to environmental changes. Pseudomonas species isolated from soil 
possess a range of two-component signal transduction proteins, allowing rapid responses to 
environmental fluctuations (Heeb & Haas 2001; Humair et al. 2010). A study performed by 
Garbeva and colleagues demonstrated that in the meeting with other bacteria, several genes 
involved in two-component signal transduction systems was found to be upregulated in P. 
fluorescens Pf0-1 (Garbeva et al. 2011). Genes coding for stress response and detoxification 
proteins showed increased expression in the rhizosphere in a transcriptional profiling study 
analysing P. putida during their interaction with maize roots (Matilla et al. 2007). It was 
emphasised that stress adaptation, in addition to nutrient acquisition, were two selective forces 
important for colonisation in the rhizosphere (Matilla et al. 2007). 
 
5.3     Pseudomonas at the Genomic Level  
The immense spectrum of ecological, metabolic, and biochemical characteristics of fluorescent 
Pseudomonas extends to the genomic sequence level. The genome sizes of bacteria belonging to 
the Pseudomonas genus vary greatly, ranging from 3.7 Mbp for P. strutzeri to 7.1 Mbp for P. 
aeruginosa, whereas P. putida KT2440 has a genome size of 6.1 Mbp (Spiers et al. 2000). There 
exist a large number of unique protein-coding genes in Pseudomonas genomes, compared to 
closely related isolates. This is reflected in P. fluorescens, where 22-27% of the coding 
sequences (CDS) were classified as unique genes (Silby et al. 2009). Thus, in addition to a large 
genome, which provides a broad repertoire of functional abilities, this genus is furthermore 
highly adaptable and flexible due to their genome-specific genes (Silby et al. 2011).  
Mobile genetic elements, such as phages, plasmids, transposons and genomic islands, have been 
found in all Pseudomonas spp. in which this has been investigated and these are important 
elements contributing to the large genomic diversity (Silby et al. 2011; Loper et al. 2012). These 
elements are most known for their contribution to increased pathogenicity, fitness and 
antibiotic resistance. Furthermore, there is evidence that mobile genetic elements play a role 
in colonisation and survival in new ecological niches (Mavrodi et al. 2009). Investigation of 
mobile genetic elements in P. fluorescens Pf-5 revealed several prophage regions and genomic 
islands, which showed similarities to genomes of other Pseudomonas spp., but also included 
regions unique to the Pf-5 genome with the abilities to contribute to the fitness of the bacterium 
in the rhizosphere (Mavrodi et al. 2009).  
Loper and his co-workers discovered an enormous heterogeneity within the group  of 
fluorescent Pseudomonas (Loper et al. 2012). The core genome of the ten strains investigated 
consisted of only 1491 genes, which represents less than 35% of any of the individual genomes. 
==PAGE:19==
Page | 18  
 
A range of 300 to 900 genes were unique to each strain, and together with the large pan-genome 
of 13,872 protein-coding genes, these results emphasise the high level of diversity within this 
group of bacteria. Furthermore, this study substantiate the strain-specificity of biological 
control traits in Pseudomonas spp., as exclusive occurrence of many of these traits were 
observed in specific strains or sub-clades. Most of the genes relevant for biocontrol are likely 
acquired through horizontal gene transfer, however, only a fraction of these genes were located 
on clearly defined mobile genetic elements. On the other hand, these loci have characteristics 
suggesting recent acquisition, such as atypical GC content, and it was suggested that horizontal 
gene transfer and integration through repetitive sequences could be the basis of their origin 
within these strains (Loper et al. 2012). 
 
5.4     Digging Into the Antagonistic Mechanisms of Fluorescent Pseudomonas 
Antibiotic-producing fluorescent Pseudomonas are frequently isolated from naturally 
suppressive soils, indicating an important role of these antibiotics in disease control 
(Raaijmakers et al. 1997). Several studies have revealed that the antibiotics can be detected in 
the rhizosphere, and that they do play a central role in the suppression of plant pathogens 
(Thomashow & Weller 1988; Raaijmakers & Weller 1998; Duffy & Défago 1999; Haas & Keel 
2003).  
 
5.4.1     Phenzine-1-Carboxylic Acid 
Phenazines are a large group of nitrogen-containing heterocyclic pigments, with the structure 
of phenazine-1-carboxylic acid (PCA) illustrated in Figure 4. These compounds are produced 
by marine- and soil bacteria, including members of Pseudomonas. Almost all phenazines have 
broad-spectrum activity against bacteria and fungi, which can be explained by accumulation of 
toxic superoxide radicals in the target cells (Mavrodi et al. 1998). Individual strains of 
Pseudomonas spp. differ in number and types of phenazines produced, and phenazine 
production is further strongly influenced by growth conditions (Dwivedi & Johri 2003; Huang 
et al. 2009).  
An important member of the phenazines is phenazine-1-carboxylic acid. PCA is a pigmented 
antibiotic playing a crucial role in disease suppression against important fungal 
phytopathogens. Production of PCA by fluorescent Pseudomonas spp. effectively suppressed 
take-all of wheat caused by two isolates of Gaeumannomyces graminis var. triciti (Mazzola 
1995). It has been verified that the antibiotic is naturally produced in situ (Thomashow et al. 
1990; Mavrodi et al. 2012). In a study made to determine the susceptibility of a group of 
phytopathogenic fungi towards PCA produced by Pseudomonas chlororaphis, it was discovered 
that each fungal species showed different sensitivity to PCA. All 25 pathogenic fungi were partly 
or completely inhibited by PCA, except for Rhizoctonia solani (Puopolo et al. 2013). Studies 
==PAGE:20==
Page | 19  
 
furthermore imply that PCA also might be toxic to plants, which is an important consideration 
for application of PCA positive BCAs (Toohey et al. 1965; Slininger et al. 1996).  
 
 
 
Abundant numbers of phenazine producing Pseudomonas were found to thrive under arid 
conditions, suggesting soil moisture to be a major abiotic factor in the development of a 
phenazine-producing microbial community (Mavrodi et al. 2012). Moreover, it has been 
proposed that phenazine biosynthesis is regulated by quorum sensing (Mavrodi et al. 2012). 
Results also suggest that pH is important for the antibiotic activity of PCA (Ownley et al. 1992). 
 
5.4.2     2,4-DiAcetylPhloroGlucinol 
DAPG, also known as Phl, is a broad-spectrum antibiotic able to target a range of organisms, 
including fungi, bacteria, viruses, protists and helminths (Haas & Keel 2003; Brazelton et al. 
2008). This diverse collection of sensitive organisms suggests that DAPG targets basic cellular 
processes. In fungi, DAPG is able to cause alterations of the plasma membrane, vacuolisation, 
and the disintegration of cell contents, which indicate that the antibiotic can interfere with the 
maintenance of membrane integrity (Brazelton et al. 2008). Difference in DAPG sensitivity 
between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria suggests that cell wall structure might play 
an important role in DAPG uptake or responsiveness (Kamei & Isnansetyo 2003). DAPG 
functions as an antimicrobial at high concentrations, whereas at lower concentrations it might 
act as a signal molecule influencing the gene expression of biocontrol traits in BCAs. 
Furthermore, it can act as a signal molecule for plants, with its ability to stimulate ISR, root 
exudation and enhance branching of the plant roots (Bakker et al. 2007; Brazelton et al. 2008; 
Combes-Meynet et al. 2011).

DAPG-deficient mutants together with the demonstration of production of the antibiotic in the 
rhizosphere (Bonsall et al. 1997; Keel et al. 1992; Raaijmakers et al. 1999). Furthermore, DAPG 
producing fluorescent Pseudomonas are frequently isolated from disease suppressive soils, 
originating from different geographic locations worldwide. In a study made to determine the 
frequency of antibiotic-producing Pseudomonas spp. in natural environments, it was observed 
that DAPG-producing fluorescent Pseudomonas were isolated from all three take-all-
suppressive soils but not from their complementary take-all-conducive soils (Raaijmakers et al. 
1997). Similar results have also been observed for other plant species and their diseases, 
including black root rot of tobacco (Keel et al. 1996) and Fusarium wilt of tomato (Tamietti et 
al. 1993). These results points towards an important role for DAPG in natural suppressiveness 
to various plant diseases. However, it should be mentioned that DAPG might have a toxic effect 
on plants. Keel et al. examined the effect DAPG exerts on various crops, and in the case of 
tobacco, DAPG was more toxic to growth of the plant compared to the phytopathogen of the 
plant (Keel et al. 1992).

PhlF from the phlA promoter (Schnider-Keel et al. 2000). The term phlD+ fluorescent 
Pseudomonas is used interchangeably with DAPG producers, since detection of phlD is 
correlated with the ability to produce the antibiotic. PhlD plays a key role in the two 
biosynthetic routes of DAPG, encoding a polyketide synthase that aids in the biosynthesis of

traits in A. brasiliense involved in colonisation on roots indicating that the antibiotic can act as 
a signal by which beneficial pseudomonads may stimulate plant beneficial activities of other 
BCAs (Combes-Meynet et al. 2011).

repressor of DAPG in the strain CHAO (Schnider-Keel et al. 2000). Glucose and zinc has been 
identified to stimulate DAPG production in CHAO in a strain-specific manner (Duffy & Défago 
1999). In the same study, it was found that inorganic phosphate reduced the production of 
DAPG (Duffy & Défago 1999).

5.4.3     Hydrogen cyanide 
Cyanide is mostly present as hydrogen cyanide when physiological conditions reach a pH of 
seven. A variety of organisms are able to produce hydrogen cyanide, including fungi, algae, 
plants and bacteria. In plants, cyanogenesis is believed to act in defence against herbivores and 
pathogens (Blumer & Haas 2000b). Bacterial HCN production is restricted to the two phyla, 
proteobacteria and cyanobacteria, which includes fluorescent pseudomonads (Blumer & Haas 
2000b; Kremer & Souissi 2001).  
Hydrogen cyanide is a broad-spectrum antibiotic involved in biocontrol by many fluorescent 
Pseudomonas. It works by inhibiting metalloenzymes, such as cytochrome c oxidase, an 
important component of the respiratory chain in many organisms. The producing organisms 
have developed mechanisms to protect themselves, such as the cyanide-insensitive cytochrome 
oxidase produced by P. aeruginosa during cyanogenesis. Non-producing organisms also 
possess defence mechanisms towards HCN, with cyanide detoxification being a widespread 
phenomenon in nature involving enzymatic degradation or conjugation with acceptor 
compounds (Blumer & Haas 2000b).  
The relationship between HCN production in vitro and biocontrol ability has been investigated, 
where a positive correlation could be observed (Ramette et al. 2003). In P. fluorescens CHAO, 
HCN played an important role in the disease suppression of tomato crown and root rot caused 
by Fusarium. A mutant with abolished production of HCN showed significantly impaired 
biocontrol relative to the wild-type, and restoration of HCN production re-established the 
ability of the mutant to control the phytopathogen (Duffy et al. 2004). The first study to provide 
evidence for the role of HCN in disease suppression showed that a cyanide-negative mutant was 
less effective in protecting a tobacco plant against black root rot caused by the fungal pathogen 
Thielaviopsis basicola, than its P. fluorescens CHAO parental strain. Complementation of the hcn 
genes into the mutant restored its ability to supress disease. In addition, in another P. 
fluorescens strain not producing HCN, the hcn genes were inserted, which resulted in improved 
ability to supress the fungal pathogen (Voisard et al. 1989). This biocontrol trait also 
demonstrated to be important for protection of wheat against economically important wheat-
pathogenic fungi (Flaishman et al. 1996).  
 
HCN biosynthetic genes are comprised of a cluster of three genes, hcnABC, which is thought to 
form an operon encoding the HCN synthase (Blumer & Haas 2000b). Maximal production of 
HCN is observed at low oxygen levels and high cell densities during the transition from the 
exponential to the stationary growth phase, followed by a rapid loss of HCN synthase activity 
(Blumer & Haas 2000b; Pessi & Haas 2001). The production of HCN requires the presence of 
oxygen, however in limiting amounts, as an oxygen-sensing protein, anaerobic regulator of 
==PAGE:24==
Page | 23  
 
arginine deiminase and nitrate reductase (ANR), is necessary for production. ANR is a dimeric 
protein, which activates under low oxygen supply. The promoter of the HCN genes contains an 
ANR box where ANR binds and activates the target promoter (Blumer & Haas 2000a). Iron has 
a stimulatory effect on cyanogenesis. It is believed that ANR is an iron sensor that is dependent 
on iron for proper assembly and thus activation (Blumer & Haas 2000a). Collectively, oxygen 
and iron availability determine the activity of ANR (Blumer & Haas 2000a).  
Even though HCN production by bacteria is traditionally considered a plant-growth-promoting 
trait, results indicate that HCN can be phytotoxic. It is able to block photosynthetic electron 
transport, inhibit nitrate assimilation and carbohydrate metabolism in plants. A study 
characterising the effects of HCN on seedling root growth of various plants showed that HCN 
significantly reduced growth of weeds like lettuce, barnyardgrass, and green foxtail (Kremer & 
Souissi 2001). Another group furthermore demonstrated that HCN produced by fluorescent 
Pseudomonas spp. resulted in physiological changes that caused plant death of the weed 
Arabidopsis thaliana (Blom et al. 2011). However, not only weeds are targeted by HCN. Bakker 
and Schippers revealed that HCN was involved in inhibition of energy metabolism in potato, 
thus suppressing the growth of this important crop (Bakker & Schippers 1987). Consequently, 
HCN production can have contrasting effects on plants, being able to act both plant-growth 
promoting and phytotoxic. A new hypothesis for the role of HCN in the rhizosphere suggest that 
the main contribution of this volatile is sequestration of metals and thus an indirect increase of 
nutrient availability, which is beneficial for the rhizobacteria and their plant hosts (Rijavec & 
Lapanje 2016).  
 
5.4.4     Extracellular proteases 
Proteases are enzymes naturally found in all living organisms. These enzymes catalyse 
hydrolysis of peptide bonds in proteins, and are also recognised by the names proteinases or 
proteolytic enzymes. Proteases are ubiquitous in nature, where soils represent an ideal habitat 
for many extracellular enzyme-producing bacteria (Sharma et al. 2015). 
In bioactive fungi, proteases have shown to be important for antagonistic activities towards 
phytopathogens (Segers et al. 1994; Bonants et al. 1995; Elad & Kapat 1999). This trait has also 
been observed for various biocontrol agents of bacterial origin. Screening of bioactive bacteria 
towards phytopathogenic fungi resulted in the isolation of Enterobacter agglomerans 
producing proteases with chitinolytic activites. Chitin is an important fungal cell wall 
component, and bacteria possessing degrading enzymes targeting this component were able to 
suppress disease caused by Rhizoctonia solani (Chernin et al. 1995). 
Proteases produced by pseudomonads have also shown to contribute to biocontrol activities. 
Saddiqui and co-workers present evidence for the extracellular protease, AprA, produced by P. 
fluorescens CHAO contributing to biocontrol of nematodes. Culture supernatants of CHAO 
==PAGE:25==
Page | 24  
 
inhibited egg hatching and caused mortality of juvenile nematodes, and addition of a protease 
inhibitor to the culture supernatant markedly reduced these actions (Siddiqui et al. 2005). 
 
5.4.5     Siderophores 
All living organisms, with a few exceptions, require iron for growth and survival. Even though 
iron is one of the most abundant elements in the Earth’s crust, the bioavailability of iron is 
limited by the low solubility of ferric iron (Fe3+), which is the predominant state of iron in 
oxidised and aerated soils (O’Sullivan & O’Gara 1992). Under iron limiting conditions, some 
microorganisms, including fluorescent Pseudomonas, are able to produce and secrete low 
molecular weight, high-affinity iron-binding ligands (Sivasakthi et al. 2014; Jain & Das 2016). 
These compounds are named siderophores and function in the solubilisation, transport, and 
storage of iron  (Tyc et al. 2016). Siderophores sequester ferric iron, and this complex is 
consequently taken up by the microbial cell through specific recognition by membrane 
receptors (Bakker et al. 2007). The biosynthesis of siderophores is triggered in response to iron 
limitation (Cabaj & Kosakowska 2009).  
Siderophores function as a biocontrol trait by making iron unavailable to phytopathogens. This 
mechanism is based on the pathogen not being able to produce siderophores, or the 
siderophore produced is of lower affinity than that of the beneficial microorganism (Jain & Das 
2016). This principle was demonstrated for the first time by Kloepper and co-workers in 1980, 
where the isolated fluorescent siderophore from a Pseudomonas strain mimicked the biological 
action of the producing strain towards the phytopathogen Erwinia carotovora (Kloepper et al. 
1980). Plant growth promotion is also an advantage provided by siderophores, as they are able 
to directly supply the plant with iron (Jain & Das 2016). Some plants possess receptors for 
siderophores being able to take up iron directly in the form of an iron-siderophore complex. 
The importance of siderophore production by Pseudomonas was observed as enhanced rootlet 
elongation of cucumber and spinach seedlings in a study investigating Pseudomonas strains 
differing in their production of siderophores (De Bellis & Ercolani 2001). Moreover, 
siderophores have been suggested to play a role in ISR (Meziane et al. 2005). 
There is a large diversity of siderophores based on structure, affinity and stability.  A common 
feature of siderophores is that they all form octahedral complexes with ferric iron, illustrated 
in Figure 7. Frequently, siderophores are grouped in two main groups: hydroxamate- and

are much more stable and thus potentially more significant in the rhizosphere (O’Sullivan &

6     THE AMBIVALENT ASPECT OF POSSESSING MULTIPLE PLANT- 
        GROWTH PROMOTING TRAITS  
 
The diversity of biocontrol abilities among fluorescent pseudomonads is huge, and it is not 
unusual that BCAs display several different biological control mechanisms (Loper et al. 2012; 
Bruto et al. 2014). It is hypothesised that accumulation of genes contributing to plant beneficial 
traits has been selected by the interaction of these bacteria with plants, and based on this, it 
could be expected that PGPR traits might be identified by their particular assortment of genes 
contributing to plant beneficial functions (Bruto et al. 2014). Furthermore, it is believed that a 
strain displaying several plant-beneficial properties provides more benefits for the plant, with 
the following reasoning; i) the effects of the different mechanisms are added up quantitatively, 
or ii) it ensure that at least one mode of action is expressed in particular environmental 
conditions.  
In several studies, the most effective PGPRs have proven to be those possessing multiple plant-
beneficial properties (Rana et al. 2011; Sarma et al. 2014; Almario et al. 2014). Furthermore, 
the removal of one mechanism often reduces the overall plant-beneficial effects (Thomashow 
& Weller 1988). Despite the immense amount of research trying to identify the mode of action 
of these plant-beneficial bacteria, no strict definition of the molecular feature has been 
characterised, thus the PGPR status is not well defined. Nevertheless, certain genes important 
for plant-bacteria cooperation have been identified, and several studies have looked into the 
co-occurrence of these genes in various bacterial isolates to achieve a more fundamental insight 
into the potential associations of plant-beneficial traits in PGPRs.   
A common approach in identifying effective PGPR strains is screening isolates for either activity 
towards a phytopathogen or for a range of plant-growth promoting (PGP) traits of both direct 
and indirect functions. Four P. fluorescens strains were identified based on their antagonism 
toward the phytopathogen Botritis cinerea. Following, the strains were found to possess a wide 
range of antagonistic and plant-growth promoting activities, which was suggested to explain 
the high degree of biocontrol due to synergistic interactions of multiple traits (Hernández-León 
et al. 2015). Sarma and co-workers isolated and screened 120 morphologically distinct 
fluorescent pseudomonads in the rhizosphere of green gram. Out of these, 19% showed 
antagonistic activity towards phytopathogenic fungi, where a significant variation in the 
production of a wide range of metabolites and enzymes was observed. The isolate possessing 
the largest assortment of antagonistic, PGP and stress tolerant traits was P. aeruginosa, which 
also was the most efficient isolate in suppressing disease of four different fungal 
phytopathogens (Sarma et al. 2014). These results substantiates the hypothesis that multiple 
antagonistic traits contribute to better suppression of disease.  
Another possible method of finding effective PGPR strains is to screen for isolates based on 
their ability to enhance growth of plants. This was performed by Rana et al. who selected 10 
isolates out of 100 based on their ability to enhance seedling length and germination of wheat. 
==PAGE:28==
Page | 27  
 
The selected isolates were further tested for their PGP abilities, and it was found that the three 
isolates possessing the greater number of PGPR traits gave rise to maximum enhancement of 
weight and plant height. It was hypothesised that these results was a consequence of better 
survival due to their ability to utilise a larger number of carbon and nitrogen substrates (Rana 
et al. 2011). Furthermore, the wide range of attributes might make these three isolates more 
adaptable to diverse environments, which is an important trait if the PGPR is to be used in 
various soils and climatic conditions.  
The comparison of PGP genes in bacteria representing different taxonomic (Alpha-, Beta-, and 
Gammaproteobacteria) as well as ecological status (PGPR, endophytes/symbionts, 
saprophytes, plant pathogens and animal pathogens) was examined to determine if these genes 
are selected for in PGPR due to their interactions with plants. Genomes of 23 plant-beneficial 
bacteria were investigated and compared to 279 bacteria of another ecological status (Bruto et 
al. 2014). PGP genes restricted to PGPR were not found, except for phlABC, which was only 
present in three Pseudomonas PGPR. However, the number of genes contributing to plant 
beneficial functions increased in the order of animal pathogens, phytopathogen, saprophytes, 
endophytes/symbionts and PGPR, indicating that the accumulation of these genes might be an 
intrinsic PGPR characteristic. It was suggested that PGP genes are selected for in plant-
associated environments and counterselected in other environments. Furthermore, they found 
that PGP genes were more likely to be conserved in closely-related species, indicating that the 
potential to become a PGPR strain may rely on ancestral features (Bruto et al. 2014).  
Although multiple PGP traits appears to be a general feature and an advantage of effective PGPR 
strains, there are some contrasting evidence to this observation. Vacheron et al. examined if 
PGPR strains exhibiting multiple plant-beneficial properties were better adapted to the 
rhizosphere by testing if these strains would preferentially be selected by the plant roots 
(Vacheron et al. 2016). Properties involved in enhanced plant nutrition, plant hormone 
modulation, or pathogen inhibition were analysed in fluorescent Pseudomonas isolated from 
maize rhizosphere or bulk soils. In contrast to the expectations, it was found that maize 
preferentially selected strains with low numbers of plant-beneficial properties (up to five). The 
Pseudomonas possessing high numbers of plant-beneficial properties (>5) were not prevalent 
in the maize rhizosphere as they were outcompeted by rhizobacteria with lower numbers (1-
5) of properties. This suggests that there is a trade-off between rhizosphere prevalence and the 
feature of possessing a large number of PGP properties. Furthermore, they propose that the 
plant might attract a diversity of Pseudomonas spp. that together possess the different 
properties, which collectively will contribute to plant health (Vacheron et al. 2016). These 
results favour co-inoculation of commercial PGPR strains, where a functional complementarity 
between the plant-beneficial actions will enhance the positive effects on the host plant.

7     CHALLENGES WITH FLUORESCENT PSEUDOMONAS AS  
        BIOLOGICAL CONTROL AGENTS 
 
There is an increasing global market for use of biological control agents in agriculture. The 
consumer demand for pesticide-free food, and stringent environmental regulations has 
resulted in a steady increase in the use of biopesticides by 10% every year. In 2015, more than 
200 products of microbial biopesticides were available on the US market (Kumar & Singh 2015; 
GreenCape 2016).  
However, there are still some obstacles to overcome for biological control agents to reach is full 
commercial potential. One of the main impediments for use of BCAs has been their inconsistent 
performance in situ. A wealth of biotic and abiotic factors impact performance of the introduced 
BCA strains. One of the most important factors is the ability of the bacterial strain to colonise 
the root, as a threshold in density must be reached in order for the BCA to be effective. 
Consequently, factors reducing colonisation also reduces the biocontrol ability of the BCA 
(Fuente et al. 2015). Furthermore, successful colonisation does not necessarily equal a 
metabolically active biological control agent. The production of secondary metabolites is under 
strict control. Inconsistent production or inactivation in situ of these metabolites can be caused 
by small environmental changes, such as fluctuations in temperature, pH and water 
composition of the soil, exemplified by the antibiotic PCA being inactive under acidic conditions 
(Raaijmakers et al. 2002).  
A quantitative study examining the overall biocontrol effectiveness in field conditions was 
conducted in 2006. The study included 53 published reports between 2000 and 2005, 
investigating the suppressiveness of biological control agents with the aim of trying to identify 
general factors that influence the success or failure of biological control in plant pathology 
(Ojiambo & Scherm 2006). The results indicated that only moderate effectiveness of BCAs are 
obtained on average. Some of the factors examined was effectiveness of greenhouse versus 
field, utilisation of fungal versus bacterial BCAs, and conditions of low, medium or high disease 
pressure, however no significant differences between these factors were confirmed. One factor 
that did significantly increase disease suppression was the application of BCAs with short 
generation times that produce large numbers of short-lived offspring. Several factors were not 
investigated in this study, such as the combination of multiple biological control agents 
(Ojiambo & Scherm 2006).  
The problems are not restricted to performance in the field, but also extends to development of 
commercially viable products. Fluorescent pseudomonads lose viability when stored for longer 
periods, which provides difficulties in providing concentrations high enough for effective 
biocontrol (Walsh et al. 2001; Haas & Défago 2005). Compared to BCAs of the genera Bacillus, 
pseudomonads have a major weakness in its inability to produce resting spores, as spores 
protect from stresses during formulation and storage. However, progress has been made within 
this area, with products such as biopolymer formulations protecting the cells during storage 
and distribution (Stockwell & Stack 2007; Berg 2009). Still, more research is required for 
optimal production, formulation and delivery of commercialised biological control agents.  
==PAGE:30==
Page | 29  
 
Furthermore, the toxicological risks must be mapped for the BCA and its metabolites before 
being approved for use in the field. The impact of the BCA on non-target organisms, such as the 
effect on human health and the environment must be assessed, which requires a stringent 
testing procedure. If the BCA is genetically modified even more detailed studies are demanded 
before they can be approved for commercial use (Walsh et al. 2001). Engineering biocontrol 
strains has been one approach in trying to achieve more reliable biocontrol performance in situ. 
However, the overexpression of various antibiotic genes has not contributed to convincing 
results. Two engineered derivatives of P. putida WCS358 were modified to constitutively 
produce DAPG and PCA. Although this alteration resulted in increased suppression of various 
phytopathogens compared to the parental strain in vitro, it did not suppress the symptoms of 
take-all in wheat and there were no improved results in relation to plant yield compared to the 
wild-type (Bakker et al. 2002).  
Haas and Keel has furthermore expressed their concern of antibiotics produced by fluorescent 
Pseudomonas being too broadly toxic and not acting specifically enough towards the 
phytopathogen of interest (Haas & Keel 2003). There is evidence for the deleterious effects on 
crops in addition to the phytopathogen when high doses of antibiotics such as DAPG, HCN and 
phenazines are present (Toohey et al. 1965; Bakker & Schippers 1987; Keel et al. 1992; 
Murhofer et al. 1995; Slininger et al. 1996). They suggest a strategy might be to identify 
Pseudomonas biocontrol strains producing antibiotics that specifically inhibit the 
phytopathogen of importance (Haas & Keel 2003). Other suggestions include using mixtures of 
biocontrol agents to create synergistic effects in disease suppression.  
 
7.1     Possible Solutions 
The motivation for using a cocktail of biological control agents is to increase the disease-
suppressive effects, which can occur in various ways; i) the combination of biocontrol strains 
exhibiting different disease-suppressive mechanisms can complement each other, and ii) the 
application of biocontrol strains known to stimulate each other in the expression of relevant 
biocontrol genes can enhance disease suppression towards phytopathogens. The sensitivity of 
biocontrol mechanisms to fluctuations in environmental conditions is an important limitation 
to successful exploitation of BCAs in the field. The application of biocontrol cocktails increase 
the likelihood that biocontrol genes are expressed, thus resulting in more effective plant 
protection.  
Boer et al. performed an experiment combining two biocontrol strains possessing multiple 
antagonistic traits, with the hypothesis that these traits in combination would contribute to a 
higher level of protection against Fusarium wilt targeting radish. P. putida WCS358 and P. 
putida RE8 were chosen based on their ability to produce pseudobactin and induce systemic 
resistance respectively, with both strains being effective in suppression of Fusarium wilt. The 
combination of these strains did enhance disease-suppression compared to single strains, 
which was suggested to be due to the combination of their antagonistic mechanisms (De Boer 
et al. 2003). 
==PAGE:31==
Page | 30  
 
This strategy was also tested for biological control agents of more diverse origin. The yeast 
Pichia guilermondii  and the bacterium Bacillus mycoides were tested for suppression of the 
pathogen Botrytic cinerea on strawberry (Guetsky et al. 2002). The differences in antagonistic 
mechanisms were viewed as inhibition of conidial germination, and breakage and destruction 
of conidia of the pathogen, provided by P. guilermondii and B. mycoides respectively. The 
mixture of these two BCAs resulted in even more severe conidial destruction. It was shown that 
P. guilermondii competed with the pathogen for important nutrients, whereas the bacterium 
induced systemic resistance in the host. Furthermore, both BCAs were able to secrete inhibitory 
compounds. The combination of these activities most likely contributed to the reduced disease 
severity observed when the bacterial and fungal biocontrol agents were combined (Guetsky et 
al. 2002). 
An experiment inoculation three biological control agents of bacterial origin was conducted 
under greenhouse conditions to determine their combined role towards multiple cucumber 
pathogens. The general trend observed was greater suppression and increased consistency of 
significant disease suppression by the use of strain mixtures compared to experiments using 
single BCA strains. Field trials conducted for two seasons showed that seed treatment with the 
three-strain mixture greatly improved plant growth and reduced disease to a level statistically 
equivalent to a synthetic pesticide (Raupach & Kloepper 1998). 
It was demonstrated that two nonrelated pseudomonads were able to stimulate each other in 
the expression of the antimicrobial compound DAPG (Maurhofer et al. 2004). This approach of 
using strains that stimulate each other in expression of important biocontrol genes might be 
one successful strategy to improve efficacy of BCAs in the field. The combination of P. 
fluorescens CHAO and P. fluorescens Q2-87 resulted in enhanced DAPG biosynthesis, an 
antibiotic used by both strains in disease suppression of take-all of wheat. However, when 
applied to a gnotobiotic take-all assay, the strain mixture only slightly conferred more 
suppression to take-all compared to the individual strains (Maurhofer et al. 2004). It was 
suggested for future approaches to select naturally derived PGPR strains of diverse genotypes, 
which could not only enhance DAPG production, but also contribute with improved growth, 
activity and competitiveness in the rhizosphere. Furthermore, the host plant and the soil 
environment are factors affecting production of secondary metabolites important for 
antagonism, and these factors should also be considered.  
Lutz et al. studied the interactions between biological control agents using the reporter gene 
constructs phl’-‘lacZ in P. fluorescens CHAO, and ech42-goxA or nag1-goxA fusions in 
Trichoderma atroviride, to monitor their production of DAPG and chitinases respectively (Lutz 
et al. 2004). It was found that DAPG enhanced nag1, but not ech42-goxA expression. 
Furthermore, an unidentified substance produced by CHAO repressed expression of both 
chitinases. Mixture of the two microorganisms contributed to enhanced expression of phlA in 
CHAO. These results indicate that both negative and positive effects on expression of important 
biocontrol genes can occur in mixtures of BCAs (Lutz et al. 2004).  
==PAGE:32==
Page | 31  
 
Other strategies to create more stable commercial biopesticides is to add nutrients when 
introducing a new BCA. Evidence suggests that carbon sources and minerals are important 
factors in the biosynthesis of antimicrobial compounds produced by Pseudomonas BCAs. 
Minerals and carbon sources are furthermore an easy and economical way of improving 
biocontrol of both introduced bacteria as well as indigenous bacteria (Duffy & Défago 1999). 
Seeds are normally pelleted and dried for storage purposes, a procedure frequently effecting 
the survival and thus biocontrol efficacy of Pseudomonas. Addition of nutrients to seed pellets 
has proven to be positive for survival of introduced BCAs. (Moenne-Loccoz et al. 1999).  
As for human beings, changes in the microbiome composition of plants could cause 
susceptibility to stress and disease. It has been proposed that complete profiling of the crop 
microbiome and a detailed analysis of how introduced microbes will influence this community 
will be the key to understand the effect they have on growth physiology and yield performance 
of plants (Sruthilaxmi & Babu 2017). Microbiome blueprints for individual crop species will be 
useful for optimising a microbiome in relation to choosing the right biofertilisers and biocontrol 
microbes. This would provide an opportunity to select biocontrol strains that interact positively 
with the already existing microbiome, and to exploit strains who’s biocontrol genes will not be 
silenced by the other residents (Sruthilaxmi & Babu 2017). De La Fuente et al. found that 
competition among closely related BCA strains sharing the same biocontrol traits and niches 
are more severe than competition with other microorganisms. Consequently, introduction of 
BCAs that are closely related to microorganisms already existing in the rhizosphere is not 
recommended, as displacement of the BCA might be a consequence of this (Fuente et al. 2015). 
Creating a microbial blueprint of the rhizosphere before introducing a BCA would thus be useful 
to avoid this issue.  
Furthermore, a relationship between biologically active fluorescent Pseudomonas and their 
genomic group has been observed. DAPG-producing fluorescent Pseudomonas spp. of a specific 
genotype preferentially colonise the rhizosphere of certain crop plants (Raaijmakers & Weller 
2001). This knowledge can be used to select BCAs superior in their abilities to suppress disease 
and in their ecological competence (Picard & Bosco 2007). Another important aspect in 
selection of BCAs is the plant genotype. Plants specifically selects particular fluorescent 
Pseudomonas strains, thus the knowledge of the population of fluorescent pseudomonads 
indigenous to a soil would contribute to knowledge on which strains would be effective BCAs 
in that particular environment (Picard & Bosco 2007).

8.7     Whole Genome Sequencing 
Based on morphology, phenotypic and bioassay experiments, eight bacterial strains where 
chosen for Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS):  
P3K1 - P3K3 - P3K4 - P9K1 - P9K3 - P11K4 - P12K1 - P12K3  
The selected isolates were grown up in 10 mL LB medium for two days, whereby 0.5 mL was 
used for chromosomal DNA extraction. Genomic DNA (gDNA) was isolated by the use of 
Wizard®Genomic DNA Purification Kit (Promega). Extracted gDNA was preserved in 100 μL 
DNA preservation buffer, from which 2 μL were used for gel electrophoresis, 1 μL was used to 
measure A260/280 on Nanodrop and 1 μL was used to measure DNA concentration on Qubit 
(Thermo Fisher Scientific). 4 ng/μL gDNA were delivered for WGS with MiSeq, Illumina at DTU 
Biosustain NGS Lab (Technical University of Denmark).  
Paired-end reads of 2x150 bp were generated. These sequences were trimmed, filtered and de 
novo assembled using CLC Genomics Workbench (QIAGEN Bioinformatics). The ends of the 
sequences were trimmed with the limit of 0.05. Contigs smaller than 200 bp or with coverage 
below 10x were removed. Several contigs were BLASTed to find a reference genome which 
could be used to align and thus rearrange the contigs into a draft genome, using Mauve (the 
Darling lab, computational (meta)genomics). Subsequently, Emboss union was used for joining 
the contigs into one continuous sequences, which was used for annotation of the genome. 
Prokka (Victorian Bioinformatics Consortium) was the software tool used for predicting 
proteins and their putative functions. Following, the draft genome was used for comparing the 
sequences to the public databases PATRIC, which automatically compared the sequences to the 
virulence databases Victors, and VFDB, and to databases for antibiotic resistance, CARD and 
ARDB. Furthermore, the genome sequences were submitted to antiSMASH to predict secondary 
metabolite biosynthetic gene clusters. In addition to these databases, NapDos was also used for 
detection of secondary metabolite genes.  
The genomes were visualised with the genome browser Artemis (Sanger Institute). In addition, 
this tool was used for searching of genes relevant for biocontrol. Furthermore, the Genome-To-
Genome Distance Calculator (GGDC)(Auch et al. 2010) was used for taxonomic identification of 
the sequenced genomes at a species level. The seven genomes were individually uploaded to 
the server, together with accession numbers of the genomes identified through BLASTing the 
three housekeeping genes 16S rRNA, rpoD, and recA. In BLAST, top hits with complete genomes 
were selected.

9     RESULTS 
 
 
9.1     Isolation of Pseudomonas from Soil 
Isolation of fluorescent Pseudomonas is not a novel concept, with several approaches already 
existing (King et al. 1954; Sands & Rovira 1970; Gould et al. 1985; Krueger & Sheikh 1987). 
However, for this lab it is pioneer work, where new methods are to be implemented with the 
aim of doing future high throughput screenings of bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas.  
Different media and temperatures were tested in the isolation of Pseudomonas species from the 
12 soil samples listed in Table 1. Incubation of plates was performed at either room 
temperature or at 30 °C. A higher amount of fluorescent colonies were detected at room 
temperature, consequently incubation at room temperature was chosen as standard. Soil 
samples were diluted at 10-folds down to 10-5, where growth and controllable fluorescent 
colonies were most frequently isolated from plates of 10-2 to 10-4 dilutions. Furthermore, 
several different media were tested, including Pseudomonas Isolation Agar (PIA; modified, see 
attached recipe in Appendix C), KB+++, and PAB++.  
The first round of isolation was performed on PIA and KB+++ agar plates. When comparing the 
results, KB+++ agar plates more frequently gave rise to fluorescent colonies. However, both 
media gave rise to growth of unwanted fungi, thus an antifungal media was required. KB+++ 
medium with the addition of the fungicide cycloheximide (100 μg/mL) was tested. It inhibited 
fungal growth successfully and intensely fluorescent colonies were detected. However, due to 
less prevalent growth on these plates as well as the toxicity of the fungicide, another media was 
considered. PAB medium added chloramphenicol and CFC Pseudomonas supplement was 
examined, as the fungicide included in this supplement is far less toxic to humans than 
cycloheximide. This combination resulted in good bacterial growth and fungal inhibition after 
two days of incubation. The fluorescent frequency and intensity was slightly reduced on PAB++ 
plates compared to KB+++ plates, thus selected colonies were restreaked onto KB+++ plates 
without fungicide. It required a longer incubation time on KB+++ plates, but after three days, 
predominantly, fluorescent colonies could be observed.  
Freeze stocks were made to preserve the soil samples, however, when isolating from these 
samples the results were not equal to isolation from undiluted soil samples stored at 4 °C. 
Isolation from freeze stocks resulted in a total lack of growth on plates from soil sample P10. 
Furthermore, culturing of soil sample P8 gave rise to homologous nonfluorescent colonies. 
Consequently, no fluorescent colonies were isolated from these two soil samples.  
Accordingly, it is recommended that undiluted soil samples stored at 4 °C are serially diluted 
down to 10-4 and plated out on PAB++ agar plates. Incubation should take place at room 
temperature and in darkness for two days before fluorescent colonies are restreaked on KB+++ 
plates. The last set of plates should be incubated for three days in darkness and at room 
temperature. The final and optimal setup is illustrated in Appendix H, Figure 43.

9.4     Library Construction for High Throughput Experiments  
The making of the Pseudomonas Library for high throughput experiments turned out to be a 
complicated process. At first, the isolates were directly inoculated in 200 μL LB medium in a 
96 well microtiter plate and incubated for one to two days at 30 °C with shaking. This resulted 
in a very uneven growth pattern, where OD600 fluctuated from 0.2 to 1.1 (see Appendix A, 
Table 14), furthermore the shaking most likely resulted in contamination in the form of 
==PAGE:45==
Page | 44  
 
mixing of the different isolates, as condensed medium were seen on the lid. Thus, a 
homogenised culture could not be guaranteed. When plated out from the freeze stock library 
the colonies were growing very uneven, where growth could hardly be detected among some 
of the isolates.  
With the attempt to prevent contamination, the next step was using a Breathe-Easy membrane 
during incubation and shaking. However, when the membrane was to be removed, the 
condensed medium attached to the membrane was splashing and contaminating the wells. The 
final and chosen Pseudomonas Library construction was made by growing all of the isolates 
individually in 10 mL LB tubes prior to pipetting 200 μL into each well of the plate in the 
preferred pattern. This resulted in a more even growth of colonies and prevented 
contamination.  
 
9.5     Phenotypic Characterisation 
To detect for effective biological control agents, the selected isolates were tested for the 
presence of various antagonistic traits in vitro, including siderophore, extracellular protease 
and hydrogen cyanide production.  
 
9.5.1     A high percentage of the isolates produce siderophores 
Siderophores are important in highly competitive environments and can be used to suppress 
growth of competing microorganisms (Kloepper et al. 1980; De Bellis & Ercolani 2001). A huge 
variety of different siderophores exist, thus a universal assay able to detect all forms was 
employed. The Chrome Azurol S (CAS) assay uses CAS and hexadecyltrimethylammonium 
bromide (HDTMA) as indicators, where CAS and HDTMA confers a tight complex with ferric 
iron, which produces a blue colour. When a siderophore is added it will remove the iron from 
the complex, and the colour will change from blue to orange.

9.7     Bioassay 
This experiment was set out to determine if the Pseudomonas isolates could antagonise 
different types of plant pathogenic bacteria. Although several of the isolates possess different 
biological control qualities in form of siderophores, extracellular proteases and antibiotics, it is 
not a certainty these factors will contribute with bioactivity, or at all be expressed during 
different environments. It should be mentioned that this experiment was not performed on a 
qualitative basis, but rather a quantitative basis to identify isolates able to antagonise a range 
of bacterial phytopathogens.

Various bioassay experiments resulted in one preferred design. The first bioassay experiment 
was performed by using 0.5% LB top-agar with the pathogen culture, whereby the 
Pseudomonas Library was replica plated. This was not successful as an uneven lawn of the 
pathogen could be seen, as well as chaotic growth of the library. The Pseudomonas Library was

9.7.2     A diverse variety of bioactivity among the Pseudomonas isolates 
The Pseudomonas isolates were tested for their abilities to inhibit five different bacterial 
phytopathogens. The different isolates varied greatly in their abilities to inhibit the pathogens, 
as some isolates were able to inhibit all five, whereas others did not inhibit a single pathogen. 
Furthermore, the isolates showed various degrees of antagonism towards the different 
pathogens individually, shown by dissimilarities in inhibition zones (see Appendix E, Table 19-
22 for measured inhibition zones).  
However, some general traits could be observed. Overall, the isolates grew better together with 
X. campestris, as the colonies of the isolates in general were bigger when growing together with 
this pathogen compared to the other pathogens (see Appendix E, Table 19-22 for colony size). 
Furthermore, X. campestris was the pathogenic strain being inhibited by the highest amount of 
isolates (23 isolates/75%), as seen by the presence of yellow pillars in Figure 18.  
The isolates had most difficulties in growing together with the pathogen P. carotovora which 
was observed by smaller Pseudomonas colonies or no visible growth at all, compared to growth 
together with the other pathogens. This pattern was also seen in relation to inhibition zones, 
where only seven isolates (23%) were able to inhibit this pathogen. Few inhibition zones were 
further observed for the two pathogens P. atrosepticum and D. solani, where only six (19%) and 
eight (26%) Pseudomonas isolates respectively were able to supress growth in various degrees.

9.7.3     No clear correlation between phenotypes and bioactivity 
Four of the seven isolates not showing any inhibition had protease activity. Furthermore, all 
seven showed competent siderophore activity. Three of the seven isolates were mucoid on LB 
medium. Finally, none of the bioactive incompetent isolates were positive for HCN or contained 
any of the antibiotic genes.  
However, there were also potent bioactive isolates showing only protease and siderophore 
activity. Furthermore, about half of the competent antagonists were mucoid on LB. Thus, based 
on these results, these three factors (mucoidy, protease and siderophore activity) alone cannot 
be used to predict bioactivity.  
There were only two strains giving positive results in the HCN experiment, where P9K1 
effectively inhibited all five pathogens, whereas P12K3 only suppressed the growth of two 
pathogens. Furthermore, P9K1 was the only isolate encoding the genes for DAPG, and this 
isolate was one of a few being able to inhibit all five pathogens. Thus, there is a possibility for a 
correlation between DAPG, and good bioactivity.

NapDos is a software tool for the detection of genes involved in secondary metabolism.

GGDC (Auch et al. 2010) was used for DNA-DNA hybridisation, which is the gold standard for 
taxonomically defining bacterial species. The server calculates intergenomic distances and 
reports on the differences in GC content. Differences in percent genomic GC content between 
distinct species cannot be larger than 1 within the same species. Based on this criteria, only one 
isolate gave rise to relevant results; P12K1. In addition, DNA-DNA hybridisation (DDH) values 
should be higher than 70% to be considered as the same species. The highest value obtained

Pseudomonas brenneri. This correlates with the results obtained from BLASTing the 16S rRNA

P3K4) got the result NaN, meaning either that there were no matches for the reference genomes 
used, the genomes were not successfully downloaded, or that the accession numbers used were

10     DISCUSSION 
 
10.1     Evaluation of Growth Media  
The isolation of fluorescent Pseudomonas is based on their ability to produce siderophores that 
fluorescence under UV light. The ability of these bacteria to excrete the fluorescent pigments 
varies in relation to what synthetic media they are grown on. Several combinations have been 
tested through time (Burton et al. 1947; King et al. 1948; Palumbo 1973), and certain 
compounds appear to be more important in the production of siderophores, including sodium, 
potassium, magnesium, phosphate and sulphates (Georgia & Poe 1931; Bhattacharya 2011). 
Furthermore, limiting availability of iron is probably the most essential nutritional condition 
for successful excretion of fluorescent pigments (Totter & Moseley 1953). In addition to 
isolation of fluorescent pseudomonads, isolation of a high diversity of species within this genus 
is desired. Media rich in nutrients are generally used for this purpose, and it should be 
mentioned that different media select for different species (Picard & Bosco 2007) 
We started isolating Pseudomonas spp. by using both PIA and KB+++ agar plates. Comparisons 
of these two media showed a higher number of fluorescent colonies present on KB+++ agar 
plates. Considering that both media contain compounds important for pigment production 
(magnesium chloride and potassium sulphide in PIA, and dipotassium hydrogen phosphate and 
magnesium sulphate in KB (HiMedia 2011; HiMedia 2015)) it is not surprising that fluorescent 
colonies were observed on both types of plates. PIA is a medium used in the selection of 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa specifically, whereas KB is a general medium used for non-selective 
isolation of Pseudomonas spp. isolated from a variety of environments (HiMedia 2011; HiMedia 
2015). Consequently, this can be the explanation for a higher frequency of fluorescent colonies 
being detected on KB+++, as this medium probably allows for growth of a bigger variety of 
species. The growth of different Pseudomonas spp on four different media was examined by 
Krueger and Sheikh, and results showed that the highest amount of Pseudomonas species failing 
to grow was seen on PIA plates (Krueger & Sheikh 1987). Although KB medium was not 
included in the mentioned experiment, it is an indication of the low coverage of strains being 
able to grow on PIA plates.   
Probably the most frequently used medium in the detection of fluorescent Pseudomonas is 
King’s Medium B (King et al. 1954). However, KB is a non-selective isolation medium, which 
could result in antagonism by other growing bacteria, thus a decrease in the diversity of 
fluorescent Pseudomonas could be a consequence (Sands & Rovira 1970). Species of the 
Pseudomonas genus have few nutritional requirements in common, but high intrinsic resistance 
to antibiotics on the other hand is a prevalent feature which can be used as a selective factor 
(Gould et al. 1985). Thus, selective media containing antibiotics have frequently been used to 
eliminate other microorganisms, and in a study where three antibiotics, targeting both bacteria 
and fungi (penicillin, novobiocin and cycloheximide), were added to KB medium, high 
selectivity of fluorescent pseudomonads was observed compared to KB medium without 
==PAGE:65==
Page | 64  
 
(Sands & Rovira 1970). This is in accordance with our results. KB medium with the addition of 
ampicillin and chloramphenicol, both targeting bacteria, was tested and this resulted in growth 
of fluorescent colonies, however there was also a presence of fungal colonies on these plates. 
The addition of the fungicide cycloheximide effectively eradicated the presence of these fungi. 
However, with the knowledge of the toxicity of cycloheximide, it is not optimal to work with.  
Consequently, we tested Pseudomonas agar base, which originally was made for isolation of 
Pseudomonas spp. from meat and meat products. However, the large variety of nutrients 
present in this medium permits growth of a diverse range of Pseudomonas spp. Furthermore, 
the presence of magnesium chloride and potassium sulphate favours the production of 
pigments, which is the basis of selection in this study. Indeed, fluorescent colonies could be 
observed on this medium. The reason for choosing this medium was due to its CFC 
Pseudomonas supplement, which contains antibiotics targeting both bacteria and fungi. The 
introduction of these compounds successfully prevented fungal growth, however, non-
fluorescent bacteria not belonging to the Pseudomonas genus also grew on these plates, which 
was confirmed by 16S rRNA sequencing of two non-fluorescent white colonies both belonging 
to the Acinetobacter genus. These results are consistent with results observed in a study 
comparing four different Pseudomonas selective media. Growth of nonpseudomonads was most 
frequently observed on PAB-CFC plates compared to PIA, Pseudomonas selection isolation agar 
and GH medium, where Acinetobacter was one of those genera being able to grow on PAB-CFC 
plates (Krueger & Sheikh 1987). In a study made by Sands and Rovira it was suggested that 
detection of fewer fluorescent pseudomonads was due to inhibition by other organisms present 
(Sands & Rovira 1970). If the presence of these nonpseudomonads have resulted in antagonism 
of fluorescent pseudomonads on our PAB plates are not known. However, it could be an 
explanation for the lower fluorescent intensity seen for PAB++ plates compared to KB+++ plates.  
 
10.2     Colony Morphologies 
Our results show that fluorescence is more frequent and intense at high cell densities. This can 
partly be explained by the presence of more cells, thus an increase in fluorescent intensity. 
However, a study has also shown that quorum-sensing, and thus cell density, is able to affect 
pyoverdine biosynthesis. Even though cell densities are not an essential signal for pyoverdine 
expression it does enhance the production of this siderophore (Stintzi et al. 1998).  
Stress signals triggers the formation of biofilms, which will provide the bacteria with an 
increased ability to survive in a harsh environments (Kaplan 2011; De la Fuente-Núñez et al. 
2013). It was detected that our isolates more frequently formed mucoid colonies on media 
where antibiotics were added, such as KB+++ and PAB++, compared to LB medium. Although 
Pseudomonas are generally known to be intrinsically resistant to several antibiotics, it cannot 
be excluded that some of the strains isolated are more sensitive than others, and thus the 
==PAGE:66==
Page | 65  
 
addition of antibiotics might trigger the mucoid state as a defence mechanism towards 
environmental stress.  
Furthermore, we detected a higher amount of fluorescent colonies when incubated at room 
temperature compared to 30 °C. A study made to determine the optimal conditions for 
siderophore production in Pseudomonas found that the highest levels of pyoverdine were 
detected at 20-25 °C (Jankiewicz 2006), which correlates well with our findings. Furthermore 
it was found that the highest amount of pyoverdine was determined after 72 hours for 
Pseudomonas spp. plated on KB medium (Jankiewicz 2006). These results were also seen when 
our isolates were cultivated on KB+++ plates.   
 
10.3     Factors Influencing Microbial Diversity in Soil 
Two of the most important factors in relation to microbial diversity and abundancy is the plant 
species and soil pH (Berg & Smalla 2009). Plants are able to select specific microbial 
populations by secreting specific root exudates, which varies from plant to plant (Berg & Smalla 
2009). Several studies have shown that pH is a key factor in regulation of the microbiome 
composition in the rhizosphere (Mendes et al. 2013). In this study, soil pH was measured with 
the intention of determining its affect on the microbial community in the different types of soil. 
As we could not identify our isolates at a species level, soil pH and its influence on Pseudomonas 
diversity could not be evaluated. However, we did observe a variation on microbial 
heterogeneity in the various soil samples. It is speculated if pH could be the explanation for this.  
In a study performed by Fierer and Jackson, it was found that factors such as site temperature, 
latitude and geographic distance did not affect the composition and diversity of bacterial 
communities in 98 soils collected from across North and South America (Fierer & Jackson 
2006). Rather, soil pH was the best predictor of bacterial diversity and richness, with the lowest 
levels of diversity and richness observed in acidic soils (Fierer & Jackson 2006). Several studies 
substantiate the role of pH in bacterial diversity and abundancy in soil (Cookson et al. 2007; 
Rousk et al. 2010). The great influence of pH on the bacterial composition is likely due to the 
narrow pH ranges required for optimal growth of bacteria (Rousk et al. 2010).  
During isolation of Pseudomonas from soil, no growth was observed for soil sample P10 when 
grown on PAB plates. The low pH of 4 measured for this soil sample could be a plausible 
explanation for the lack of growth. The P10 sample was diluted and plated out on different kind 
of plates, including LB and KB, where a single fungal-like colony was the only detection of 
growth, which was seen on KB medium in a 10-1 and 10-2 dilution. A study found that fungal 
diversity and richness was only weakly affected by pH (Rousk et al. 2010), which could be the 
explanation for the presence of fungi and not bacteria.  
Another theory which could explain the lack of growth from soil sample P10, and in addition 
explain why no fluorescent colonies could be observed from soil sample P8, is that these 
==PAGE:67==
Page | 66  
 
samples consist of bulk soil rather than rhizosphere soil. In bulk soil bacterial growth is limited 
by the restricted availability of nutrients (Garbeva et al. 2014). This results in a lower 
magnitude of bacterial population density in the bulk soil compared to the rhizosphere where 
the plant release a high amount of nutrients in the form of root exudates (Matilla et al. 2007; 
García-Salamanca et al. 2013). Several studies have furthermore shown that Pseudomonas are 
more abundant in the rhizosphere compared to bulk soil (García-Salamanca et al. 2013; 
Vacheron et al. 2016). Sample P8 and P10 were collected from a fallow organic field and from a 
forest, respectively, which could mean that bulk soil has been isolated, where lower amounts of 
fluorescent Pseudomonas are present. Furthermore, both of these soil samples possessed a 
much smaller microbial diversity compared to the other soil samples, which underpin the 
theory of bulk soil being isolated in the case of soil sample P8 and P10.  
Another consideration to be made, is that our soil samples were collected during the winter-
time. Root exudation changes during plant growth development, thus the sampling time might 
have an influence on microbial diversity and abundancy (Berg & Smalla 2009). A study 
examining how soil microbial communities react to seasonal changes showed that the number 
of culturable bacteria peaked in April, with smaller peaks observed in September, and a 
microbial biomass peaking in July (Girvan et al. 2004). Furthermore, a study investigating the 
seasonal shifts in rhizosphere microbial populations was performed for three different plants 
(Houlden et al. 2008). Shifts in the microbial community was seen as the plants matured. Shifts 
in microbial communities have also shown to be affected by plant species, which was explained 
by differences in root exudation (Houlden et al. 2008). Accordingly, there could be a 
consequence for sampling during the winter-time when less or no root exudates are secreted.   
Moreover, soil sample P8-P12 were cultivated from freeze stocks. It is possible that freezing 
could influence the composition and population density of the samples, especially the fungi, as 
the preservation method used was intended for bacteria. However, the small microbial 
diversity was only seen for soil sample P8 and P10. Furthermore, studies indicate that storage 
at -80 °C does not affect the viability of fungi (Pasarell & Mcginnis 1992; Lee et al. 2007).  
Another observation made in our study was that non-agricultural soils in general displayed a 
lower pH compared to agricultural soils. This is in accordance with another study discovering 
that forest soil had a lower pH compared to agricultural soils (Cookson et al. 2007). Cookson et 
al. explain this difference in pH as a result of difference in vegetation type and treatment of the 
soil, such as fertilising and liming. Termination of the use of fertilisers and liming has resulted 
in a reduction of soil pH (Bardgett et al. 1996).

10.4     16S rRNA Sequence Unqualified for Pseudomonas Species Identification 
The universal primer pair used for characterisation of Pseudomonas species in this study was 
selected first and foremost to determine the bacterial origin of the selected isolates. It did 
indeed show that all 31 isolates were of bacterial origin, and in addition they were all classified 
within the genera of Pseudomonas. Identification at the species level gave rise to more 
conflicting results, with different results from forward and reverse primer, as well as obtaining 
several species in the top hits with equal queries and identities. Investigations of the 
effectiveness of the 16S rRNA gene in characterisation of bacteria has provided evidence for 
successful identification at the genus levels (>90%), however, in regard to species level it has 
proven to be less effective (65% to 83%) (Janda & Abbott 2007). Unsuccessful identification is 
often caused by species sharing similar and/or identical 16S rRNA sequences, which is likely 
the problem encountered in this study, where several species appeared with similar queries 
and identities. A primer targeting a gene more specific to Pseudomonas or with a higher 
evolutional rate should thus be implemented.  
However, the primer pair was able to confirm that the methods and procedures applied in the 
isolation of Pseudomonas from soil were effective. Fluorescent Pseudomonas were isolated and 
characterised from a variety of soils residing different crops and vegetation. The presence of 
Pseudomonas species in this vide range of soil types is not surprising as this genus is highly 
adaptable to fluctuating environmental conditions and known to inhabit a variety of 
environments (Silby et al. 2011).  
 
10.5     All Isolates Produce Siderophores 
It was observed in our studies that all isolates except for P12K3 produced siderophores on CAS 
agar. These results are comparable to results from another study, having found that 96% (23 
out of 24) of their isolates produced siderophores with varying degree of production (Sarma et 
al. 2014). We also noticed a varying degree of production in the form of various clearing zones 
ranging from 1 mm to 10 mm.   
After sequencing of P12K3, it was found that the genome encoded genes and biosynthetic 
clusters for pyoverdine production. It was considered if the lack of clearing zone on CAS agar 
plates was due to the massive growth of the isolate, with the results of overgrowing its own 
clearing zone. The reason for this massive growth is hypothesised to be due to culturing on a 
plate which was not completely dry. Analysis of the genome of P12K3 further resulted in 
identification of various motility genes, including the twitching motility genes pilG and pilH, 
which could have been the cause of the massive and uneven growth observed. Based on this 
theory, we unofficially tested isolate P12K3 once more on CAS plates, which were inspected for 
humidity before being streaked. As the pictures in Appendix B, Figure 26 and 27 reveal, P12K3 
is fully capable of producing siderophores. Consequently, all of the isolated Pseudomonas spp. 
in this study are siderophore producers.

10.6     Genome Analysis Revealed Protease Genes in Protease Negative Isolate 
In the case of extracellular protease producers, about half of our isolates were able to create 
clearing zones on skim milk plates. Two studies investigating the biocontrol mechanisms of 
various biocontrol pseudomonads found that all of their isolates displayed protease activity, 
studies comprising four (Hernández-León et al. 2015) and 87 Pseudomonas species (Wang et 
al. 2001). As reported in another study (Kothary et al. 2007), it could be that the isolates not 
creating a clearing zone do not quantitatively produce enough protease to degrade the casein. 
When investigating the seven genomes sequenced, it was found that all contained the gene 
aprA, which encodes an extracellular protease, and out of these seven isolates, P3K1 did not 
produce extracellular protease during the protease assay. This finding substantiates the 
possibility that not all isolates produced significant amount of protease to create clearing zones. 
However, a false positive protein prediction could also be the explanation for the presence of 
aprA in the P3K1 genome.  
Moreover, we found that 48 hours incubation was required for one of the isolates in order to 
produce extracellular proteases. Optimal conditions for proteinase production by P. aeruginosa 
was determined after 48 hours, and additionally, 37 °C was the optimal temperature (Raj et al. 
2012). The low amount of protease producing strains identified in our study, compared to the 
studies of Hernández-Léon et al. and Wang et al. could therefore be the result of a lower 
temperature used (room temperature, approximately 20 °C) compared to those in the other 
two studies (28 °C and 27 °C respectively). It would thus be interesting to try incubation under 
these conditions and compare the results with those obtained previously.  
 
10.7     Uncertainties of the Hydrogen Cyanide Assay 
There were some conflicting results in relation to the HCN experiment. However, the two 
positive controls P. aeruginosa PAO1 and P. protegens CHAO did show production of HCN, 
whereas the negative control P. putida KT2440 did not. The only two isolates producing HCN in 
two individual experiments were P9K1 and P12K3. Other isolates showed indications of blue 
marks on the filter paper however, these results were not consistent.  
Screening rhizobacteria for plant growth promoting activities, including HCN, revealed that 
88% of the Pseudomonas isolates were positive for HCN production (Ahmad et al. 2008). This 
number is significantly higher than our 6%. Furthermore, a different study revealed that all of 
their Pseudomonas strains of different ecological status, except for phytopathogens, possessed 
the HCN genes (Bruto et al. 2014). Out of 20 Pseudomonas isolates, 11 were positive for HCN in 
an experiment determining the role of HCN in the rhizosphere (Rijavec & Lapanje 2016). In 
general, it appears that a higher percentage of Pseudomonas isolated from soil are HCN 
producers compared to our results. All of the mentioned studies used different methods for 
identification of HCN, such as plate assays, colorimetric assays and PCR, which indicates that 
the method employed in this study is not optimal.  
==PAGE:70==
Page | 69  
 
The reason for our low percentage of HCN positive isolates could possibly be due to the 
experiment itself. It is known that low oxygen levels are required for activity of the 
transcription factor ANR, which positively regulates HCN biosynthesis (Haas & Défago 2005). 
The use of parafilm to seal the microtiter plates was only tested in the last experiment, thus it 
could be a possibility that too much oxygen has been present for optimal production of HCN. 
The microtiter plates were incubated for 72 hours, which could also be a factor influencing the 
results. Voisard et al. performed the same experiment, however with an incubation time of only 
16 to 24 hours (Voisard et al. 1989). Furthermore, the contamination in some of the 
experiments made it difficult to evaluate if the blue colour from the different wells were due to 
mixing of different isolates. The culture should not come into contact with the cyanide detection 
paper as this will interfere with the cyanide detection reagents (Filloux & Ramos 2014), and 
this could also be a reasonable explanation for the low presence of HCN positive isolates, as it 
was observed that the paper was wet in several of the experiments. Consequently, it is 
recommended that conditions are adjusted and more care should be taken if continuing using 
the same setup.  
 
10.8     New Primer Required for Phenazine-1-Carboxylic Acid Detection 
Not a single isolate tested positive for PCA in this study. Not even our positive control P. 
aeruginosa PAO1 gave rise to a band on the gel from electrophoresis. According to the 
databases NCBI (NCBI Resource Coordinators 2016) and Pseudomonas Genome Database 
(Winsor et al. 2016) the genes required for PCA synthesis is present in PAO1. Furthermore, P. 
aeruginosa PAO1 is described as the most studied phenazine-producing bacterium by Cui et al. 
and its possession of the two seven-gene operons phz1 (phzA1B1C1D1E1F1G1) and phz2 
(phzA1B1C1D1E1F1G1) makes it able to produce PCA (Cui et al. 2016).  
The primer pair PCA2a/PCA3b used in this study to detect for PCA was designed by 
Raaijmakers et al., and were developed from a sequence within phzC and phzD, respectively 
(Raaijmakers et al. 1997). Investigation of the specificity of the primer showed that all 
phenazine-producing strains gave rise to a fragment of the expected size, except for the P. 
aeruginosa strains, including PAO1. This is in accordance with our results, which is a reasonable 
explanation for the lack of fragment observed for PAO1.  
Furthermore, Raaijmakers et al. investigated the PCA-producing population densities of 
fluorescent Pseudomonas derived from seven different soils, and not a single PCA-producing 
fluorescent pseudomonad was detected in any of the soils (Raaijmakers et al. 1997). This can 
mean two things; either the primer pair does not allow for detection of a wide enough variety 
of phenazine-producing fluorescent pseudomonads, or PCA-producing Pseudomonas are not 
that common in natural soils, thus PCA production might not be a significantly important trait 
in rhizosphere bacteria. However, phenazines have been described as one of the most common 
antibiotics produced by Pseudomonas biocontrol agents (Weller 2007), whereas a study 
==PAGE:71==
Page | 70  
 
performed by Garrido-Sanz et al. found that phenazines are a common trait in strains belonging 
to P. corrugata and that only occasional occurrences appear in other P. fluorescens groups 
(Garrido-Sanz et al. 2016). Accordingly, our results indicate either that no PCA producing 
fluorescent Pseudomonas were present in the collected soil samples, or that the primer pair 
used was not suitable for detection of these bacteria. Therefore, a different PCA primer pair or 
assay should be examined to verify that our soil samples does not possess PCA-producing 
pseudomonads.  
 
10.9     DAPG is Important for Biocontrol 
The identification of DAPG is less complicated compared to PCA detection, as phlD, one of the 
six biosynthetic genes in the Phl biosynthetic cluster, is conserved among DAPG producers of 
worldwide origin (Raaijmakers et al. 1997). P9K1 was the only isolate producing fragments of 
the correct size during PCR using two different primers in the detection of DAPG. Both primer 
pairs, B2Bf/BPR4 and Phl2a/Phl2b, are phlD-specific (Raaijmakers et al. 1997; McSpadden 
Gardener et al. 2001). Due to similar results obtained for both primer pairs, it should be 
considered whether one of them should be chosen as a standard. B2Bf/BPR4 is documented to 
amplify phlD from a variety of DAPG producing pseudomonads representing all 13 genotypes 
(McSpadden Gardener et al. 2001). Consequently, this primer pair is suitable for detecting a 
wide range of DAPG-producing Pseudomonas from natural environments and can thus be 
recommended as a first choice.  
Examination of an immense range of biocontrol fluorescent pseudomonads showed 30% 
occurrence of Phl+ strains among 549 in total (Rezzonico et al. 2007). This is a tenfold higher 
occurrence than observed in our study. However, the selection of rhizosphere-competent 
Pseudomonas might have a great influence on this percentage, whereas in our study a random 
diversity of soil pseudomonads was selected for. Raaijmakers et al. found that a large 
population of DAPG producing fluorescent pseudomonads could be isolated from suppressive 
soils, whereas none were isolated from the complementary conducive soils (Raaijmakers et al. 
1997). Although we do not have any information about the suppressiveness of the soils 
collected, these results do indicate that DAPG is important for suppression of disease, and thus 
P9K1 stands out as a potential BCA. It was found that all three PGPR Pseudomonas strains 
investigated by Bruto et al. possessed the DAPG operon, whereas Pseudomonas strains of other 
ecological type, e.g. saprophytes and phytopathogens, did not (Bruto et al. 2014). We do not 
know exactly in which ecological category our isolates belong to, but these results further 
substantiates the importance of DAPG in protection of plants, and thus the potential of P9K1 as 
a PGPR.  
Full genomes of 93 strains belonging to the P. fluorescens group were examined for correlation 
between distinct traits and specific species. The biosynthetic cluster of DAPG was only present 
in 13 strains in total, where nine of these belonged to strains within the P. corrugata group, and 
==PAGE:72==
Page | 71  
 
the reaming four belonged to the P. protegens group (Garrido-Sanz et al. 2016). These results 
correlates well with our findings, with the only DAPG-positive isolate, P9K1, most likely 
belonging to the P. protegens group based on 16S rRNA sequences derived from both PCR and 
genome sequencing.  
The relationship between 87 DAPG producing pseudomonads and their biocontrol traits has 
been investigated (Wang et al. 2001). Interesting results showed that all 87 strains had a co-
occurrence of the biocontrol traits DAPG and extracellular proteases as well as DAPG and HCN. 
Our only isolate positive for DAPG did produce both extracellular protease and HCN. With rare 
exceptions, DAPG producers are also HCN producers(Haas & Défago 2005). Evidence from a 
range of studies have shown a co-occurrence of the genes phlABCD and hcnABC (Keel et al. 1996; 
Wang et al. 2001; Duffy et al. 2004; Rezzonico et al. 2007; Bruto et al. 2014). This indicates an 
evolutionary linkage of the two compounds, and a synergistic relationship between DAPG and 
HCN in biocontrol (Haas & Défago 2005). The occurrence of both of these gene clusters has been 
associated with superior biocontrol performance compared to HCN producers not able to 
produce DAPG (Rezzonico et al. 2007), which is in accordance with our results, having observed 
Phl+HCN+ P9K1 to be a brilliant antagonist, whereas isolate P12K3 which only produced HCN 
performed less efficient in antagonism of various phytopathogens.  
 
10.10     Optimal Bioassay Setup 
The aim of our bioassays has been to identify bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas in a 
quantitative manner. The experimental setup has involved bacteria-bacteria interactions using 
an agar plate system. Although these bioassays are rather artificial, they do allow an analysis of 
the bioactive potential of the isolates.  
Different setups of the bioassay experiment was tested, where the top-agar bioassay totally 
failed. Semi-solid plate assays containing 0.4-0.8% agar are normally used for studying motility 
in bacteria (Morales-Soto et al. 2015), and the top-agar used in this study contained 0.5% agar. 
Consequently, the massive and chaotic growth observed on these plates can most likely be 
explained by swarming or twitching motility. Pseudomonas is known for swarming and their 
ability to swarm can take place on a wide variety of media compositions (Morales-Soto et al. 
2015). Furthermore, analysis of the sequenced genomes revealed that all seven isolates 
possessed several genes involved in motility.  
Considering liquid cultures versus colonies used in bioassay replica plating, inhibition zones 
were clearly more frequent in the latter setup. An explanation could be that the colonies 
growing on non-competitive LB plates prior to being confronted with pathogenic bacteria gives 
these isolates better conditions to grow, compared to the liquid cultures which have to establish 
themselves in a competitive environment. Furthermore, it is easier to observe and compare 
growth of colonies versus liquid cultures, and thus determine if lack of growth in bioassay is 
due to pathogen or just low concentrations of the isolates.

10.11     Biocontrol by Pseudomonas Isolates 
Both for the liquid assay and the colony assay it was observed that Pseudomonas colonies were 
much smaller on plates containing P. carotovora compared to the other pathogens, a tendency 
that was slightly reduced after 48 hours. The reason for this difference is not known, but an 
explanation could be that this pathogen grows faster than the other phytopathogens and thus 
the increased concentration could contribute to more competition for nutrients. Furthermore, 
the Pseudomonas isolates abilities to inhibit the growth of the pathogenic bacteria differed 
among the pathogen species. Similar results have been documented in a study examining 
Pseudomonas BCAs biocontrol efficacy against various strains of Pectobacterium and Dickeya 
spp. (Krzyzanowska et al. 2012). Among the five pathogens, D. solani was the bacterium being 
least affected by our Pseudomonas isolates in relation to inhibition. The low susceptibility of D. 
solani to antimicrobial metabolites secreted by antagonistic bacteria probably accounts for the 
low frequency of inhibition zones observed in this study (Czajkowski et al. 2013). 
The response of these bioassays have been studied in a one-to-one conformation. In natural 
environments bacteria are more likely to encounter several different competitors 
simultaneously, and in these situations, the production of broad-spectrum antibiotics might be 
of great advantage. Bioactive traits identified in this study all target a wide range of pathogens, 
and it was clear to see that isolates possessing a wide range of traits were more prominent in 
inhibition of various phytopathogenic bacteria. Consequently, a wide arsenal of bioactive traits 
do improve the suppressive abilities of the isolates. This is also the result of several other 
studies (Bruto et al. 2014; Sarma et al. 2014). With the exception of PCA, which no isolate tested 
positive for, P9K1 possessed all of the other bioactive traits, and it demonstrated to be the most 
bioactive isolate. 
Furthermore, these assays revealed that there were no direct correlation between the 
possession of siderophores and extracellular proteases, and bioactivity. Siderophores was a 
trait observed for all isolate, whereas proteases was observed for about half of them. This does 
not exclude the importance of siderophores and proteases in antagonistic interactions, as the 
full potential of these traits might be caused by synergistic interactions with other bioactive 
traits.  
Only a small fraction of bioactive traits was screened for in this study, and no qualitative 
analysis was made in relation to determining which trait actually contributed to antagonism. 
Isolate P3K1 and P3K2, being two out of three isolates inhibiting all phytopathogens, only 
possessed one biocontrol trait; siderophore production. Although P9K1, the third isolate being 
able to suppress all five phytopathogens, possessed all bioactive traits, it is not known if the 
metabolites are at all produced in the bioassays. Consequently, it is not possible to determine 
which bioactive traits are most important for phytopathogenic suppression.  
Two of the most potent BCAs were chosen for another bioassay. P9K1 and P3K1 were 
separately grown in a community of microorganisms derived from various soil samples to 
==PAGE:74==
Page | 73  
 
determine their colonising and antagonistic abilities in an environment more related to the 
surroundings they originate from. Both isolates had no difficulties growing in the six various 
communities, however, for P3K1 it was observed that a few colonies were able to inhibit growth 
of this isolate. Furthermore, P9K1 was also observed to be a better antagonist than P3K1 as a 
smaller number of colonies were able to grow on these plates. The possession of the broad-
spectrum antibiotic DAPG might be an important asset in suppression and colonisation by 
P9K1. Studies have indeed demonstrated that PCR detection of DAPG genes of Pseudomonas 
strains correlates with their in vitro DAPG production and root colonisation abilities (Ramette 
et al. 2001; Landa et al. 2002; Picard & Bosco 2007). Both Pseudomonas isolates were 
surprisingly effective at suppressing fungal growth. These results are of great interest, as the 
most economically important plant pathogens are of fungal origin.  
 
10.12     Quality of Whole Genome Sequencing 
As expected, the size of the assembled genomes of the eight Pseudomonas isolates varied from 
approximately 6 Mbp to 7 Mbp, which is the average size for fluorescent Pseudomonas genomes, 
with the exception of isolate P9K3. The large genome size of this genome is likely to be caused 
by a contamination, as the genome size is almost twice the size of an average Pseudomonas 
genome.  
GC content describes the guanine and cytosine content of a biological sequence, and it generally 
range between 25% and 75% for bacterial genomes (Mann & Chen 2010). Analysis of the GC 
content can provide information about the evolutional role in the shaping of the genome 
content. It has been suggested that changes in GC content over time are due to responses to 
environmental conditions, which confers an advantage to the organism. This is observed for 
free-living bacteria, which have a higher average GC content. The average GC content of P. 
putida KT2440 and P. fluorescens Pf0-1 is 61.5% and 60.5% respectively (Mann & Chen 2010), 
which correlates well with the GC contents of the Pseudomonas spp. investigated in this study, 
varying from 59.5% for isolate P12K3 to 63.3% in isolate P9K1.  
 
10.13     Identification of Biocontrol Genes 
Motility has shown to be important for colonisation, as motile strains can outcompete others 
by rapidly covering the root surface (Pandin et al. 2017). This trait is thus important for 
biocontrol in situ. PATRIC and Artemis predicted the presence of motility genes in all genomes, 
including genes for flagella, pili and chemotaxis. Furthermore, extracellular polysaccharides 
such as alginate has shown to be important for adherence and thus colonisation in the 
rhizosphere (Bianciotto et al. 2001), and genes for alginate production was found to be present 
in all genomes.  
==PAGE:75==
Page | 74  
 
Various genes for biofilm production was identified in all Pseudomonas genomes. The biofilm 
mode of life plays an important role in the rhizosphere as it manages some of the beneficial 
effects observed for BCAs. Secondary metabolite production is often upregulated in the sessile 
mode compared to the planktonic mode, and alginate contributes by preventing dilution of the 
antimicrobials into the environment. Another important aspect of biofilms is that they can 
contribute with increased tolerance to stress (Pandin et al. 2017). Biofilms therefore provide 
protection and increased antagonistic activity of BCAs.  
Biocontrol agents are not the only antimicrobial-producing organisms in the soil. Members of 
the rhizosphere, such as pathogenic bacteria are also able to produce antibiotics which are used 
to eradicate other members (Lugtenberg et al. 2017). Consequently, antibiotic resistance 
systems can act as a defence mechanism. All seven genomes contained a range of antibiotic 
resistance mechanisms, including multidrug efflux pumps. The ability to survive in the harsh 
soil environments is therefore strengthened by the presence of these genes, which is important 
for successful biocontrol.  
Another interesting finding was the presence of VgrG genes in all of the genomes. VgrG proteins 
are part of type VI secretion systems (T6SS), more specifically the tip complex, and a deletion 
of this gene results in abolished T6SS secretion (Liang et al. 2015). Putative toxins are often 
found to be encoded in the vicinity of vgrG genes (Hachani et al. 2014), thus upstream and 
downstream genes of vgrG were examined for the different genomes in Artemis, which can be 
seen in Figure 42, Appendix G. A wealth of hypothetical proteins were generally found upstream 
and downstream of the genes. Furthermore, several genes coding for proteins involved in 
membrane channels and efflux pumps were also identified. Another finding was the presence 
of rhsAB in the vicinity of some of the vgrG genes (Figure 42C and D, Appendix G). The rhs genes 
are a family of enigmatic composite genes, where several have been identified as toxins (Kung 
et al. 2012). The finding of rhsAB in the vicinity of some of our genomes is an indication of a 
T6SS being present. As these toxins lack recognisable secretion signal sequences, they are 
dependent on a secretion system. It has been found that rhs genes are linked to T6SS in many 
bacteria, which deliver the toxins into both eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells (Koskiniemi et al. 
2013). Consequently, the presence of such a system could benefit in antagonism and improve 
plant growth, which has been demonstrated for the biocontrol agent P. putida (Bernal et al. 
2017). However, it should be noticed that this secretion system is also used by phytopathogens 
(Trantas et al. 2015). 
Aryl polyenes are a class of bacterial pigments which has shown to protect against 
photooxidative damage and lipid peroxidation (Schöner et al. 2016). Biosynthetic gene clusters 
encoding enzymes for the biosynthesis of aryl polyenes were present in all seven genomes. 
Another biosynthetic gene cluster common to all genomes was pyoverdine. This is not 
unexpected as all colonies did show fluorescence, and pyoverdine is thus likely the source. The 
use of antiSMASH further detected the presence of two compounds toxic to plants in four out 
of the seven genomes. Mangotoxin, first identified in the plant pathogen Pseudomonas syringae, 
causes apical necrosis of mango trees (Cazorla et al. 2003). The toxin targets the biosynthetic 
==PAGE:76==
Page | 75  
 
pathway of arginine by inhibiting the key enzyme ornithine N-acetyl transferase (Carrión et al. 
2012), but the toxin has also shown antimicrobial activity against pathogenic microorganism 
found on plant tissue (Cazorla et al. 2003). Coronatine is yet another phytotoxin produced by 
P. syringae. This is a non-host specific toxin causing diffuse chlorosis in a range of plant species 
(Geng et al. 2014). In this study we are screening for bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas which 
in the end should contribute to plant growth. The presence of plant toxins in these isolates is 
an important wake-up call. Although the isolates possess traits characteristics for biocontrol 
agents, this is not equivalent to being a PGPR. On the other hand, even though the clusters are 
present in the genome, it is no equal to expression in situ. Another notice it that, these are only 
predictions, and thus there could be a presence of both false positive and false negative genes 
in these analysis.  
Based on antiSMASH, P9K1 did not show presence of any phytotoxins. On the contrary, it 
possessed several biocontrol gene clusters, including alginate, orfamide, pyoluteorin, 
pyrrolnitrin and DAPG. Orfamides produced by P. protegens have shown antifungal and 
insecticidal activity (Jang et al. 2013; Ma et al. 2016). Furthermore, the two compounds 
pyoluteorin and pyrrolnitrin contribute to strong antifungal activity (Nowak-Thompson et al. 
1999; Hammer et al. 1997), thus the collection of all these compounds makes P9K1 able to 
target a wide range of plant pathogens, which is likely the reason for the effective biocontrol 
activity observed for this isolate in antagonism of both bacteria and fungi.  
 
10.14     Species Characterisation of Sequenced Genomes 
The GGDC has proved to be currently the most accurate method to replace in silico DNA-DNA 
hybridisation in determining bacterial species (Garrido-Sanz et al. 2016). The web server was 
used to characterise the seven sequenced genomes, however with unsuccessful results. Only 
one genome gave rise to DDH value above 70%, which is required for identification at the 
species level. P12K1 was identified as Pseudomonas brenneri, which is consistent with the 
results obtained from BLASTing the sequences 16S rRNA and rpoD from the genome. However, 
the 16S rRNA analysis based on PCR characterised P12K1 as Pseudomonas proteolytica, which 
underpins the lack of resolution of the 16S rRNA primers used. Three of the genomes had no 
matches. A previous test with one of these isolates using GGDC did give rise to results, implying 
that the reference genomes have not been successfully uploaded. Furthermore, the remaining 
genomes did not give rise to any relevant DDH values, which suggests that not enough reference 
genomes have been applied.

11     CONCLUSION  
In conclusion, we have successfully isolated bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas from soil. The 
optimal conditions and media for growth and identification of fluorescent pseudomonads was 
determined. Isolation was most successful on PAB plates added chloramphenicol and CFC 
Pseudomonas supplement, to supress growth of unwanted fungi and other bacterial genera. 
Furthermore, incubation was optimal at room temperature, and 48 hours was required for 
fluorescent colonies to occur. KB+++ was an efficient medium for restreaking of the fluorescent 
colonies, as this medium enhanced the fluorescence intensity, which was used in verification of 
this trait.  
The isolates were examined for the presence of various biocontrol traits, and when comparing 
these to the in vitro biocontrol activity towards five phytopathogenic bacteria, no clear 
connection could be observed between specific traits and successful suppression. However, 
P9K1, the only isolate encoding genes for DAPG, did show enhanced qualities in several 
bioassay experiments compared to the other isolates, indicating that DAPG might be an 
important asset in biocontrol. A high throughput setup has worked well in characterisation of 
biocontrol traits and antagonistic activity. However, there are still some adjustments to be 
made in optimisation of the setup, which will be introduced in the next chapter.  
The most efficient antagonistic strains were chosen for further studies. Whole-genome 
sequencing was performed, which could confirm some of the already detected biocontrol traits, 
as well as reveal additional biocontrol-related genes within the selected genomes.  
The procedures developed in this study can be used for future large-scale screenings of 
bioactive fluorescent Pseudomonas. In addition, the effective antagonistic strains already 
isolated can be further examined to determine their potential as commercial BCAs, with 
recommended experiments being presented below.

12     FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
Although several different growth media and conditions was tested for in this study, it would 
be interesting to perform isolation on nutrient-limiting media. Traditional media used for 
isolation of Pseudomonas are normally rich in nutrients. However, soil is in general an 
oligotrophic environment, and a nutrient-poor medium selective exclusively for Pseudomonas 
spp., NAA 1:100 has been developed (Aagot et al. 2001). A unique diversity of Pseudomonas 
groups were represented on this medium. Consequently, it could be relevant testing this 
medium in addition to the other media to obtain a larger diversity of fluorescent Pseudomonas.  
Another aspect in relation to selective media is to add iron-binding agents to the medium to 
remove metal ions. This has shown to significantly increase the ability of Pseudomonas to 
produce fluorescent pigments (Lamichhane & Varvaro 2013), which would make it easier to 
detect for fluorescent colonies, and likely obtain a higher diversity of fluorescent Pseudomonas.  
One of the factors known to influence the rhizosphere composition is the growth stage of the 
plant (Sruthilaxmi & Babu 2017). With this in mind, soil samples should be collected during a 
seasonal time lapse to obtain a higher diversity of Pseudomonas spp., and to provide 
information about which season is optimal for sampling bioactive Pseudomonas. With the desire 
of sampling soil from the rhizosphere, as higher microbial populations resides in this 
environment compared to bulk soil, plants should be dug out of the soil, whereby the soil 
remaining on the plant roots is collected for further investigation (Berg & Smalla 2009). 
Furthermore, a greater number of fluorescent colonies should be isolated from each soil 
sample, which will make it statistically easier to determine the microbial characteristics of each 
soil sample in relation to Pseudomonas diversity and prevalence of biocontrol activity.  
For a high-throughput approach, all isolates were grown up in individual test tubes prior to 
being transferred into a 96-well microtiter plate. The cultivation of each and every isolate is not 
a high-throughput solution. It was not attempted to make a freeze stock of the microtiter plates 
prepared from individual test tubes, therefore such an initiative is recommended. 
Characterisation of our isolates was successful on a genus level, whereas species level could not 
be identified. The analysis of 16S rRNA gene sequence is a widely used tool for phylogenetic 
studies. However, it does not discriminate adequately to permit identification of Pseudomonas 
at a species level, due to the extremely slow rate of evolution taking place in this gene 
(Yamamoto et al. 2000). Other genes evolving much faster, such as gyrB, rpoB and rpoD are able 
to provide a higher resolution than the analysis of the 16S rRNA gene sequences (Yamamoto et 
al. 2000). A specific rpoD primer highly selective for species of the genus Pseudomonas has been 
developed (Mulet et al. 2009). The primer pair PsEG30F/PsEG790R, producing a fragment of 
760 nucleotides in length, is recommended as a substitution for the universal 16S rRNA primer 
pair used in this study, due to its abilities to distinguish between Pseudomonas species.  
 
==PAGE:79==
Page | 78  
 
Adjustments of the HCN experiment is required to obtain more reliable results. Incubation for 
24 hours have proven optimal for HCN production (Lorck 1948) consequently, the 72 hours 
incubation period used in this study needs to be adjusted. Furthermore, isolates are 
recommended to be transferred in a similar pattern as the Pseudomonas Library to prevent 
contamination, however still in triplicates. The plates must to be sealed properly with parafilm, 
as too much oxygen downregulates HCN biosynthesis (Haas & Défago 2005). It is moreover 
important that the cyanide detection paper is tightly sealed to the microtiter plate to obtain 
discrete spots, and that the culture do not come into contact with the cyanide detection paper 
as this will interfere with the cyanide detection reagents (Filloux & Ramos 2014). The improved 
experimental setup is illustrated in Appendix H, Figure 45.  
The primer pair detecting for PCA was not able to amplify the expected fragment from DNA of 
our positive control P. aeruginosa PAO1. Implementation of a new PCA primer is therefore 
recommended. Based on the key enzyme PhzE in phenazine biosynthesis, the corresponding 
gene phzE has been used for construction of the primer pair phzEf/phzEr (Schneemann et al. 
2011). This primer pair is recommended based on its ability to detect genes of a large variety 
of different phenazines in a great variety of bacteria, due to the conservation of the gene 
(Schneemann et al. 2011). 
Other phenotypes of importance for BCAs are biofilm production in relation to colonisation 
(Hernández-León et al. 2015). Assays detecting these traits would be a good contribution in 
screening of bioactive Pseudomonas, as it would increase the chance of selecting isolates 
appropriate for in planta experiments and commercialisation. Biofilm formation can be studied 
in a microtiter plate (Filloux & Ramos 2014), which would be optimal for large scale screenings.  
To better mimic the soil environment, the bioassays should be performed on nutrient-limiting 
media. The fact that microorganisms growing in nutrient-rich media generally use their 
metabolic energy to grow rather than for production of secondary metabolites speaks in favour 
of using nutrient-poor media, as this type of media could results in strong competition and thus 
production of secondary metabolites (Haas & Keel 2003; Lugtenberg et al. 2017).  
To further investigate the biocontrol potential of the isolates, it would be really interesting to 
include phytopathogenic fungi in the bioassays, as these contribute to great economical losses 
within agriculture. Important plant pathogenic fungi include Fusarium species and Botrytis 
cinerea due to their broad host range (Dean et al. 2012).  
Bacterial cocktails of BCAs is suggested to be one of the solutions to improve efficacy of 
biocontrol agents in the field. By mixing the right species of bacteria, an induction in biocontrol 
can be achieved, either by the biocontrol traits being added up, or through synergistic 
interactions resulting in induced expression of biocontrol genes. It would be interesting to see 
if such a response can be obtained with the strains isolated in this study. Another approach to 
increase the effectiveness of BCAs in the field is to add amendments that promote either growth 
or biocontrol of the BCAs, such as molecules triggering QS (Pandin et al. 2017) or zinc to 
enhance DAPG production (Sarma et al. 2014). One way to examine this would be to make a 
==PAGE:80==
Page | 79  
 
bioassay setup with and without amendments to determine their effect in biocontrol of 
phytopathogens.  
In relation to the sequenced isolates, it would be relevant to identify the taxonomy of these 
strains. The GGDC should be tested once more with a larger and more diverse range of reference 
genomes. Moreover, it would be interesting to generate a phylogenetic tree to determine the 
evolutionary relationship among the isolates and reference genomes. In relation to biocontrol 
genes and plant-growth promoting traits, it would be of interest to further explore the 
sequenced genomes. Genes involved in secretion systems and quorum sensing interference 
molecules would be relevant for identification, but also genes involved in stimulation of plant-
growth, such as nif (nitrogen fixation) and pqq (contribute to mineral phosphate 
solubilisation)(Bruto et al. 2014). It will also be of importance to confirm these as non-
phytopathogens, as some of the strains did possess plant toxins.

Hydrogen Cyanide Detection Protocol  
Ingredients + equipment 
Cyanide detection solution: 10 mg copper(II)ethylacetoacetate and 10 mg 4,4’-methylenebis (N,N-
dimethylaniline) are dissolved in 2 mL chloroform 
3 MM Whatman filter paper 
96-Well round-bottom microtiter plates 
Breathe-Easy sealing membranes + plate roller 
 
Preparation of filter paper: 
1. Cut the filter paper into 8x12 cm sheets 
2. Wet filter paper with 2 mL cyanide detection solution by pipetting onto the paper in a fume 
hood 
3. Allow the sheets to dry for 15 min in the hood 
Preparation of samples: 
1. Grow the bacterial strains in a 96-well microtiter plate in triplicates for two days 
2. Remove the microtiter plate lid and cover the plate with a cyanide detection paper sheet, 
which is labelled with a pencil in the top left corner of the sheet with “A1” 
3. Seal the detection paper tightly in place using a Breathe-Easy membrane and a plate roller 
4. Incubate plates at 30 °C without shaking for 48 hours  
5. After incubation, remove filter paper  
 
  
 
 
 
The hydrogen cyanide will turn the detection paper blue.
