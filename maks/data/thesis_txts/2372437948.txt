Abstract
In this thesis the use of the waste product from making industrial instant coffee has been
investigated for the purpose of using it as a building material. Because it is a waste product
it is not used for anything, thus a lot can be gained from repurposing it. It is known that
when dried the coffee becomes hard making it interesting to use as construction material.
The coffee has a very high water content which makes the material shrink a lot when it is
dried. This also causes the material to crack, which means it is difﬁcult to get a coherent
product and thus it is not very suitable as a construction product. Different ways of drying
where cracks could be avoided where investigated, but no good way was found. The most
promising results were when the coffee was applied pressure while drying, but no ﬁnal
setup was found with the given possibilities.
It was attempted to lower the water content in the coffee for further use. It was possible
when drying at a low temperature, but it took a very long time. The coffee was dried,
crushed and mixed with water in order to control the water content, but this showed that
the coffee was not reversible as the strength were not the same when dried again. It was
discovered it is possible to lower the water content by using a microwave. The coffee had
to be stirred in order not to harden and to get water away from every part.
Because no good way for the use of the coffee as a stand alone construction material
was found, it was attempted to mix it with clay for the purpose of use as unﬁred bricks.
Many different combinations were produced with dry and wet materials. It was found
that the best mixture was made of wet clay and wet coffee with a water content of around
60%. This combination gave a low shrinkage while still having a good texture that the
not show cracking. When the unﬁred bricks had a coffee content over 15% it showed that
the strength decreased.
The coffee greatly improved the sensitivity to water already with 5% coffee and it im-
proved when more was added. Results showed that the plastic and liquid limits were
much higher for the coffee than for the clay, meaning the water content had to be higher

When 5% coffee was added the bricks showed an improvement in compression strength

I denne rapport undersøges affaldsproduktet fra industriel instant kaffe med formålet at
bruge det som bygningsmateriale. Fordi det er et affaldsprodukt bliver det ikke brugt til
noget og dermed kan der vindes meget ved at genbruge det. Det vides at kaffen bliver
hårdt når den bliver tørret, hvilket gør det interesant som bygningsmateriale. Kaffen har
et meget højt vandindhold hvilket gør at det krymper meget når det bliver tørret. Dette
gør også at materialet revner, hvilket betyder det er besværligt at bruge som bygningsma-
teriale. Forskellige metoder af tørring blev undersøgt med øje for at undgå revner, men
ingen god metode blev fundet. Den mest lovende metode var når tryk blev tilføjet un-
der tørringen, men ingen endelig metode blev fundet med de givne muligheder. Det blev
forsøgt at mindske vandindholdet i kaffen for videre brug. Det var muligt ved udtørring
med lav temperatur, men det tog meget lang tid. Kaffen blev tørret, knust og blandet
med vand for at kontrolere vandindholdet, men det viste at kaffen ikke var reversabelt da
styrken ikke var den samme da det blev tørret igen. Det blev opdaget det var muligt at
mindske vandindholdet i en mikroovn. Der skulle røres i kaffen for at det ikke blev hårdt
og vand forsvandt over det hele. Fordi der ikke blev fundet nogen god måde at bruge kaf-
fen som et selvstændigt byggemateriale, blev det forsøgt at blande det med ler til brug i
ubrændte mursten. Mange forskellige kombinationer blev prøvet med både tørre og våde
materialer. Det blev konstateret, at den bedste blanding bestod af vådt ler og vådt kaffe
med et vandindhold på omkring 60%. Denne kombination gav en lav krympning, mens

et kaffeindhold på over 15% viste det sig, at styrken faldt.

højere for kaffen end for ler, hvilket betyder, at vandindholdet måtte være højere, når der

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
We live in a world where the population becomes larger each year. Not only will there
be a higher need of food, but there will also be an increase of waste which takes a lot of
space. A proper sorting of waste has come into focus the later years, seen in the daily
life from the many different garbage bins, now counting 10 different types of sorting in
Copenhagen.
1.1
Industrial coffee waste
The industrial sector also contributes to the high amount of waste. An example is when
instant coffee is produced. The following section contains information from (Pedersen,
2017). This generates a waste product because only 50% of the coffee bean is utilized.
Brewing coffee at home as low as 25 % is utilized, but because of the large scale in which
instant coffee is produced it still leaves around 400 000 t of waste a year globally which
needs to be discarded.
In theory only 75 % can be utilized leaving e.g. shells left. A new process is developed
by GEA Process Engineering A/S which utilizes 70 % of the bean has been developed,
the last few percent are hard to utilize and does not pay of ﬁnancially. Even though 20 %
more of the bean is utilized with the method it still produces a lot of waste and with the
industry seeming to be growing more waste will still be produced in the years to come.
The industrial coffee waste produced with the new method can be seen on ﬁgure 1.1 and
looks similar to cake frosting. It looks very different from the waste known from the
home-brewed coffee, but there is a difference of around 50 % in utilization and therefore
much of the solid material is used leaving it much more liquid.
When drying the new coffee waste it was discovered that it became hard and insoluble
making it a candidate for other use than waste, e.g. as a building material.

1.2
Unﬁred bricks
Unﬁred bricks have been used for a long time, not speciﬁcally in the shape of a brick, but
as a plaster e.g in old timber frame constructions as seen in ﬁgure 1.2. One reason for the
use of unﬁred bricks were the lack of wood, making it difﬁcult to make ﬁred bricks.
Figure 1.2: Old timber framed house.
The beneﬁt of unﬁred bricks is that the production costs are lower because they are not
ﬁred at high temperatures. The bricks also weighs more than ﬁred bricks, making them
extremely suitable for indoor environments as they can regulate the climate by absorbing
the moisture in a room.
Bricks are still used widely for construction, but the clay resource is not inﬁnite and there-
fore it is investigated whether or not some of the clay can be replaced by the industrial
coffee waste and whether it has any useful effects.

2.1.1
Industrial coffee waste
The Industrial coffee waste (ICW) was provided by GEA Process Engineering A/S which
is the developer of the new process.
Because the product is considered as waste only the chemical content is known. It consists
of roughly 40% lipids, 10% minerals, 5% lignin and 45% melanoidines. (Dan-Nielsen,
2016)
From company supervisor, Anders, it was learned:
• The standard when drying coffee is at 80◦C for 16 hours
• After drying the ICW was very hard and difﬁcult to break
• After drying the ICW had become insoluble
With this in mind the industrial coffee waste was investigated.

2.3
Screening of industrial coffee waste
An aim was to investigate whether the ICW can be used as a building material, preferable
in its pure form, for example as a brick. Building materials generally have very speciﬁc
and strict demands for dimensions and it is therefore important being able to make speciﬁc
dimensions with the ICW.
2.3.1
Drying temperature
It was investigated whether the drying temperature had an impact on the hardness.
Four different temperatures were investigated as well as room temperature. The samples
were all put in small beakers as on ﬁgure 2.4, three for each temperature which were
dried for 20 hours. The samples at room temperature were placed in a fume cupboard.
The water content was calculated and the hardness was evaluated after drying.
20 hours were chosen instead of the usual 16 hours, due to the early mornings or late
evenings when drying for 16 hours.

2.3. SCREENING OF INDUSTRIAL COFFEE WASTE
2.3.1.1
Water evaporation
Measurements of the weight at different times were conducted in order to determine how
fast the water was evaporating at room temperature. A small Petri dish was ﬁlled with
coffee and was weighted at time zero and then again at times 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30,
35 and 113 minutes.
2.3.2
Water solubility
In the outline for these investigations was the insolubility of the ICW after drying amongst
the important characteristics. This was examined by putting some pieces of dried coffee
in water observing if the water got coloured, meaning the coffee had begun to dissolve.
2.3.3
Mould on coffee
The raw ICW is kept in a fridge, because it was said from GEA that mould will appear on
the coffee otherwise.
By putting petri dishes with coffee in an exicator with silica gel to absorb the moisture it
was investigated if this was true.
2.3.4
Drying
Different ways of drying the ICW were investigated to see if the cracking could be
avoided.
2.3.4.1
Drying in microwave
It was attempted to dry the ICW in a microwave. Some coffee were put in a microwave
which was turned on.
2.3.4.2
Slow drying
If the coffee is dried at a low temperature before hardening at a higher temperature, then
the center might have shrunk enough to not cause cracking. Therefore some samples were
ﬁrst dried at 30◦C for 5 hours and then dried at 80◦C for 20 hours and compared to others
only dried at 80◦C.
2.3.4.3
Pressure + heat
It might be possible the dry the ICW while applying pressure. The desired effect is that
while the water vaporizes it is possible to apply a pressure on the top compressing the
ICW while it still contains its shape and only the height changes.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The industrial coffee waste had high water content around 85% which made it difﬁcult
to work with. The water caused large cracking when dried and small bits were obtained
rather than one large piece. No alternative method for drying was found where the crack-
ing could be avoided and it was concluded that the industrial coffee waste could not be
used as a stand alone material.
In order to mix the industrial coffee waste with other things different methods for lowering
the water content were examined. It was found that the water content could be lowered
when drying at low temperatures, but it took a long time. The coffee could be microwaved
and thereby lower the water content, which were much faster. One had to be careful and
stir regularly else the coffee would begin to harden at certain places.
With water content in the coffee lowered by microwaving it was investigated whether it
could be used in unﬁred bricks. Many compositions were investigated by making small
pellets using both wet and dry clay and coffee.
Pellets made of dry clay and wet coffee showed promising results, but it was difﬁcult to
mix the clay in and because pellets made of wet materials showed the same promising
results, it was decided to use the wet clay and wet coffee with a lowered water content.
It was found that when having a coffee content above 10% the pellets could easily be
broken and it was still possible at 10% coffee content.
A water content around 60% in the coffee was determined to be the best as the pellets
were too dry below this level and a shrinkage as low as possible in the mixture was
desired which was connected to the water content.
When adding coffee to the clay the texture did not show the same consistency to water as
for the clean clay with the coffee having a play dough texture at 70% water. Atterberg’s
limits were therefore determined showing the plastic and liquid limit was at 12 and 21%
for the clay while it for the coffee was 58 and 78%. This meant the water content in the
coffee could not be lowered much more than 60% before it was unworkable.

CHAPTER 4. CONCLUSION
When looking at compression strength for unﬁred bricks the coffee improved the strength
with a content of 5%. When 10% coffee was added the bricks had many cracks and the
strength was not improved, though not worse. When the bricks had a higher water content
the strength decreased in both cases. Drying temperature had a small impact on strength,
but this was most likely because less cracks had emerged when drying below 105◦C. The
drying time had a larger impact, meaning that the bricks dried for 48 hours compared to
24 hours showed generally higher strengths. This was no matter to moulding method or
rate setting for the compression machine, which had vary little impact.
The optimal brick composition was determined to be with a coffee content at 5% where
the water content of the industrial coffee waste had been lowered to around 60%, which
is approximately the plastic limit. The bricks had to by dried at a low temperature at ﬁrst
and the longer time at the high temperature, the higher strength.
4.1
Further work
The ICW stabilizes the unﬁred bricks making them more resistant to water, but it should
be investigated how the coffee effects the moisture stabilizing abilities.
It might be possible to apply pressure while drying, but a special device which presses
while also allowing the steam to get out, but the coffee inside, will have to be used.
For ﬁred bricks a heating/cooling curve is used and because the bricks had less cracks
when put at room temperature before drying at 105◦C such a curve could be used for the
coffee brick as well. But because the coffee becomes hard at around 80-105◦C it would
be good to investigate just at what temperature the coffee begins to harden. This could
mean the coffee bricks could be dried lower than that temperature ﬁrst in order to get
rid of some of the water and then hardened at 105◦C and thereby avoiding many of the
cracks.

Laboratory test: Atterberg limits 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
PLASTIC LIMIT wP (%) 
1.1 
ASSUMPTIONS 
For this laboratory test, larger “stones” have to be removed. For time reasons in this class the material 
will be dried in a microwave. It should be noted that the standards prescribe full oven drying at 105C 
(see Laboratoriehåndbogen, dgf-bulletin 15). Full drying means that after 4 additional hours drying, 
the change should not exceed 0,1%. 
1.2 
EQUIPMENT 
- 4 microwave-proof containers 
- Mixing bowl 
- Balance with accuracy 0,1 % of the measured mass or better 
- Rolling board 
- Microwave 
- Metal wire with diameter 3 mm 
1.3 
PROCEDURE 
In addition to the plastic limit, the natural water content is measured.  
1) Take about 100g material from the sample and knead it thoroughly in the bowl. 
2) Remove particles with diameter >0.42mm. 
3) Take the mass and ID number of the 4 microwave-proof containers. Write the number and 
mass on the labsheet (top part). 
4) The material is divided in 4 parts. 1 to determine the natural water content and 3 for the 
plastic limit.  
5) Weight one of the samples and record the mass on the labsheet. 
6) Dry in the microwave the natural water content sample for 10 minutes. 
7) One portion at a time is rolled on the board until it has a diameter of 3mm over its entire 
length. 
8) To get an even water content throughout the sample, the material is kneaded again and then 
a new thread with d = 3 mm is rolled 
9) Continue until the thread starts crumbling just when the diameter is 3mm.  
10) Weigh the specimen in the container and record it on the lab sheet. 
11) Dry the container with the specimen in the microwave oven for 10 minutes. 
12) Weigh the dried specimen in the container and record it on the labsheet. 
13) Repeat points 7 to 12 with the other specimens (in total 3 times). 
1.4 
CALCULATIONS 
The water content is calculated as the water weight (moist sample weight minus dry sample weight) 
divided the mass of the dry sample and it is given in %.  
The plastic limit is the average of the three values obtained.

LIQUID LIMIT  wL (%) 
2.1 
ASSUMPTIONS 
Same as in 1.1 
2.2 
EQUIPMENT 
- 
Casagrande-apparatus including various tools 
- 
5 microwave-proof containers 
- 
Mixing bowl 
- 
Balance as in 1.2 
- 
Microwave 
- 
Stopwatch 
- 
Hair dryer or heat gun 
- 
Distilled water 
- 
Spoon 
- 
Knife 
2.3 
PROCEDURE 
1) 
Take about 200g material from the sample and add water to obtain the consistency of soft 
butter. 
2) 
Remove particles with diameter >0.42mm. 
3) 
Take the mass and ID number of the 5 microwave-proof containers. Write the number and 
mass on labsheet L3.1. (lower part). 
4) 
Knead the sample thoroughly in the mixing bowl. 
5) 
Take a part of the sample to place it in the CAsagrande cup. Smooth the sample surface and 
check that its thickness does not exceed 10mm. 
6) 
Using the Casagrande grooving tool create a cut perpendicular to the cup surface. 
7) 
Rotate the handle so that the cup blows approximately twice per second until the soil on the 
two halves of the cut has flown together and is touching across a 12.5mm (½”). 
8) 
Record the number of blows reading on the device on the labsheet. A specimen of about 10g 
is taken where the two halves are touching. 
9) 
Weigh the specimen in the container and record it on the labsheet. 
10) Dry the container with the specimen in the microwave oven for 10 minutes. 
11) Weigh the dried specimen in the container and record it on the labsheet. 
12) Clean and dry the Casagrande cup and knife. 
13) If more than 25 blows were recorded water is added, otherwise the remaining portion of the 
sample is air dried or dried with a hot air gun. 
14) Repeat points 4 to 13 4 or 5 times. Each time change the water content of the sample. 
The best results are obtained by adding water. The number of blows should be between 10-50 to have 
a linear trend. Ideally there should be 2 data points above and data 2 points below 25 blows.  
2.4 
CALCULATIONS 
The water content is calculated as in 1.4. The liquid limit can be obtained by plotting the 
corresponding values of water content and blows in the logarithmic chart in the labsheet L3.1. 
The points should lie on a straight line and the water content corresponding to 25 blows is the liquid 
limit.
