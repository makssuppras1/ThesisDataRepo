Abstract 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa opportunistic pathogen is in dire need of a fast yet precise genome editing 
tool. This technique should also include a way to select the desired genotypes with non-selectable 
phenotypes. For this purpose, we have adapted a CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering protocol 
optimized in Pseudomonas putida.  ssDNA recombineering allows easy modification (deletions, 
insertions and mismatches) by means of a ssDNA-binding protein (or recombinase) and a 
recombineering oligonucleotide. The CRISPR-Cas9 system is used as a counterselection tool 
against the wild-type cells that do not incorporate the desired mutation. For this, we used the 
CRISPR-Cas9 system from Streptococcus pyogenes and designed spacers that target the wild-type 
genome of the bacteria. Using P. aeruginosa PAO1 reference strain, we have characterized the 
recombineering efficiency of an array of recombinases to be used in ssDNA recombineering. We 
have introduced different mismatches and deletions in PAO1 genome by targeting several genes 
with recombineering. We have also shown the efficiency of CRISPR-directed cleavage. Using 
CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection, we were able to introduce deletions in P. aeruginosa PAO1 strain 
in two different genes. Our results show the potential of CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering as 
a genome editing tool in P. aeruginosa, and the possibility to expand it to other more clinically 
relevant P. aeruginosa strains.

INTRODUCTION 
Relevance of Pseudomonas aeruginosa 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is an opportunistic pathogen that has become one of the most common 
causes of nosocomial infections and infections in immune-compromised patients. This Gram-
negative bacterium is highly adaptable, growing in soil and marine habitats, and infecting both plant 
and animal tissues. It also produces virulence factors and exhibits intrinsic resistance to antibiotics 
and disinfectants, as well as the ability to form biofilms1,2. Treatment of P. aeruginosa is complicated 
and can become life-threatening, due to its adaptation and survival mechanisms, and its ability to 
acquire antibiotic resistance, which combines with its own naturally occurring intrinsic resistance. 
Moreover, there is a rising trend of multi-drug-resistant (MDR) strains to a diverse set of clinical 
antibiotics (b-lactams, aminoglycosides, carbapenems and fluoroquinolones)3,4.  
 
The main laboratory model system of P. aeruginosa evolution and infection is the reference strain 
PAO1. The complete genome sequence of PAO1, assembled in 2000, has a length of 6.3 million 
base pairs (Mbp). The study identified 5570 Open Reading Frames (ORFs), of which only 6.7% of 
them had a known function in P. aeruginosa. This number has increased to 21.4%, according to the 
Pseudomonas Genome Database5. These percentages refer to the number of genes classified as 
Class 1, which is defined as “function experimentally demonstrated in P. aeruginosa”, as opposed 
to Classes 2-4, which rely on gene similarity and/or sequence homology5–7. As a model organism 
and because of its clinical relevance, a deeper experimental characterization of the PAO1 genome 
would benefit the scientific community and the patients’ lives.  
 
The study of P. aeruginosa samples collected over time from the lungs of Cystic Fibrosis (CF) 
patients offers a unique insight of evolution and host adaptation8. As opposed to long-term in vitro 
evolution experiments9,10, CF lungs are a highly complex environment for bacteria, making the 
longitudinal study of these populations more thorough11,12. CF is a recessive genetic disease that 
affects the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR) gene, which ultimately 
results in thickened mucus that cilia are unable to clear from the lungs and that inhaled 
microorganisms can more easily colonize and infect13,14.  P. aeruginosa is the main cause of 
morbidity and mortality in CF patients. Colonization begins in an intermittent way, eventually 
developing into chronic infection and leading to pulmonary failure and death12.  P. aeruginosa 
infection of CF lungs can be inhibited by intensive antibiotic treatment and an inflammatory response 
from the immune system in the early stages of the infection. However P. aeruginosa infection 
eventually becomes chronic and this transition is not fully understood15,16.

2 
 
Whole-genome sequencing of clinical isolates offers a new insight to infection microbiology, as it 
allows the study of pathogen evolution inside the patients. Using a systems biology approach, a 
comparison of these isolates genomes identified convergent evolution of P. aeruginosa adaptation 
to CF patients. A selection of pathoadaptive genes were repeatedly targeted across different P. 
aeruginosa lineages. While some of the targeted genes were linked to antibiotic resistance or major 
regulators that shifted the pathogen lifestyle, 12 of them were hypothetical proteins where mutations 
have unknown or unclear effects. Furthermore, an individual pathoadaptive gene was usually 
targeted by distinct mutations in each patient rather than the same specific mutation, which likely 
resulted in variant impact on function and phenotype. The study of mutations that contribute to host 
adaptation is highly relevant as it can potentially reveal novel therapeutic strategies for chronic 
infections by unravelling how host adaptation works. Characterization of these mutations in clinical 
isolates is usually very complex because of the accumulation of multiple single nucleotide 
polymorphisms (SNPs) and insertions and deletions (indels) in their genomes, which may or may 
not have an additive effect on the final phenotype of the clinical strain. Some clinical isolates have 
more than 600 mutations, making it difficult to correlate the mutation with the phenotype. One 
research strategy to characterize mutations is to move those mutations of interest to a reference 
genetic background, such as P. aeruginosa PAO1. Another option is to revert mutations in clinical 
isolates17–21. Unfortunately, despite great efforts in the Pseudomonas scientific community, genome 
editing is far more complex than in E. coli. The focus of this project is to find an efficient, fast, simple 
and reliable genome editing technique to engineer at will any mutation into both the reference strain 
P. aeruginosa PAO1 strain, and P. aeruginosa clinical strains. 
 
Genome editing techniques in Pseudomonas aeruginosa 
As mentioned above, the study of the effect of single mutations in longitudinal clinical isolates is 
complicated because of the accumulation of mutations throughout time. Moving these mutations into 
a laboratory strain with a reference genetic background is possible by genome editing techniques 
that have been available for a long time and are continuously updated. Standard and optimized 
protocols for Pseudomonas, such allelic replacement22, typically include the use of antibiotic 
resistance cassettes.  
 
Allelic exchange is a method that allows genome editing (including gene deletions, insertions and 
mismatches) in P. aeruginosa. For this technique, an allelic exchange vector is required. This 
plasmid contains a ColE1 origin of replication, which makes it a suicide vector in Pseudomonas, an 
antibiotic resistance cassette, counter-selection markers, and a copy of the gene of interest carrying 
the desired mutation flanked by homologous DNA sequences to the recipient chromosome. To 
introduce the mutation (insertions and mismatches), the protocol uses splicing-by-overlap extension 
(SOE)-PCR. Allelic replacement relies on two homologous recombination events, which rely on 400-
==PAGE:8==
 
3 
 
500 bp homology sequences flanking the mutation. The first event consists of the insertion of the 
plasmid into the chromosome using antibiotic selection. Different antibiotics can be used as there 
are multiple vectors available. The second crossover is rarer and is isolated by counterselection22. 
sacB gene from Bacillus subtilis is often used as a counter-selection marker, but there are other 
alternatives23,24. This protocol produces strains with precise, unscarred and unmarked mutations, 
thanks to homologous recombination and the double crossover event, respectively.  
 
Nonetheless, allelic exchange has its disadvantages. Firstly, this protocol involves multiple PCR 
steps to amplify the mutant allele and the flanking homology regions, which is done separately. The 
amplified sequences are cloned together in the allelic exchange vector. This could become a 
bottleneck, and even terminate the experiment, if the gene is in a region which is difficult to amplify. 
Secondly, it is time consuming, as it takes at least 2 weeks of laboratory work to produce one mutant 
strain. To shorten this protocol, one can discard counter-selection by using only positive selection. 
This produces marked mutants bearing antibiotic resistance cassettes, which can be problematic to 
work with, especially when studying mutations from clinical isolates that either link to antibiotic 
resistance mechanisms or occur in the background of elevated resistance in the adapted isolate. 
The antibiotic resistance cassette can be removed – leaving a small scar – by exogenous 
recombinases25,26, but this option again extends the protocol procedure time. Lastly, P. aeruginosa 
is intrinsically resistant to several types of antibiotics, thus antibiotic selection is limited27. 
 
Recombineering 
(recombination-mediated 
genetic 
engineering) 
is 
another 
homologous 
recombination-based in vivo method28. Recombineering allows for precise modifications that include 
insertions, deletions and mismatches. Traditionally, in E. coli this technique is based on 
bacteriophage recombination systems, such as Reda and Redb (from l phage)29,30 or RecET (from 
Rac prophage)31,32. Recombineering proteins can be encoded in the bacterial genome as phages or 
expressed from a plasmid. Recombineering in E. coli has been used for a long time and optimized 
for each system. The homologous region is provided by linear DNA – either single stranded (ss) or 
double stranded (ds) – which can be synthetic (i.e. bought from a commercial house) or a PCR-
product. The optimization of E. coli protocols has shortened the length of homology regions from 
approximately initial 1000 bp33 to 30-70 bp34,35.  
 
Homologous recombination with ssDNA, or ssDNA recombineering, is the most promising approach 
so far in bacteria. It requires the presence of a ssDNA-binding protein (alternatively named 
recombinases), such as Redb or RecT. This protein binds to the oligonucleotide, effectively 
protecting it from exonuclease degradation. Extensive research suggests a model where ssDNA 
recombineering takes place in the replication fork. Thus, when chromosome regions become 
transiently single-stranded during replication, the oligonucleotide bound to the ssDNA-binding 
==PAGE:9==

protein will come across its homology region, thus binding and invading the lagging strand during 
replication. The ssDNA-binding protein promotes annealing between the recombineering 
oligonucleotide and the corresponding complementary sequence (Figure 1A). Mutagenesis occurs 
by incorporation of the recombineering oligonucleotide into the chromosome combined with 
recombination. It is important to note the impact of strand bias in recombineering efficiency. Strand 
bias is dependent on replication direction. When ssDNA oligonucleotide binds to the lagging strand, 
DNA polymerase III (Pol III) does not recognize it as the normal RNA/DNA pairing, thus the enzyme 
dissociates and moves forward to the next RNA primer. Next, DNA polymerase I (Pol I) starts 
replication in the lagging strand, substituting Pol III. Pol I and DNA ligase promote a recombination 
event between the already annealed ssDNA oligonucleotide and the chromosome. Oligonucleotides 
binding to the lagging strand of the chromosome, as represented in Figure 1A, have been shown 
repeatedly to be more efficient than those binding to the leading strand. This is due to the 
replacement of Okazaki fragments by ssDNA oligonucleotide (in the homology region), increasing 
the chances of recombination, as it can bind more frequently than in the leading strand. 
Oligonucleotides are stably inherited in the subsequent rounds of replication28,36–38. Thus, ssDNA 
oligonucleotides should always be designed to bind to the lagging strand. To determine which is the 
lagging strand of the gene of interest, first we have to determine the replichore in which it is located. 
This is determined by the position of the dif motif, which is different for every species of 
proteobacteria39.  
 
In Figure 1B, different oligonucleotides are depicted depending on the type of mutation we wish to 
produce. For this project, we decided to use 45-50 bp of homology flanking the mutation (deletion, 
mismatch or insertion) site because of its performance in P. putida recombineering experiments40,41. 
It has also been shown repeatedly that the DNA mismatch repair (MMR) system plays a role in 
recombineering, by identifying mismatches between the ssDNA annealing to the chromosome. This 
limits the efficiency of recombineering by repairing the mismatch42. Oligonucleotide design should 
avoid the inclusion of mismatches that are easily detected by the mismatch repair system (G-G, G-
T and A-A; high-sensitivity mismatches) as opposed to those that are poorly recognized (C-T, A-C, 
and A-G, T-T, C-C; low-sensitivity mismatches)43. 
 
In comparison to allelic replacement, recombineering has several advantages. Firstly, 
recombineering only involves one recombination step. Secondly, the homology size required for 
recombination is smaller – from 400-500 bp to 45 bp – which reduces the expense of recombineering 
oligonucleotides given that we also avoid multiple PCR steps. Consequently, the length of the 
protocol is considerably shortened from 2 weeks to 5 days (including the verification the mutation 
presence). However, despite its many advantages, recombineering as a genome editing technique

has barely been used in other species than E. coli. It is believed that this system can be exported to 
many other species28,38. It has been successfully applied in other Gram-negative bacteria such as 
P. putida44, Salmonella enterica45 and Pseudomonas syringae46, and in Gram-positive such as 
Lactobacillus reuteri and Lactococcus lactis47, using Red system, a P. syringae system similar to 
RecET proteins, and RecET, respectively. Also, dsDNA recombineering with Red recombination 
system has been tested in P. aeruginosa. One research group did so by using homology regions of 
400-600 bp48. Another research identified that 50 bp could be enough for this procedure, but they 
still required sacB counterselection, extending the duration of the whole protocol49. These two 
protocols are not a real improvement to genome editing in P. aeruginosa because they include the 
disadvantages mentioned above from traditional allelic replacement: antibiotic resistance cassette, 
long homology regions and PCR protocols for dsDNA synthesis48,49.  
 
ssDNA recombineering has also been recently optimized in P. putida40,41. A series of new ssDNA-
binding proteins (referred as ‘recombinases’ from now onwards) have been tested in P. putida 
platform strain EM42. All of them are homologous to Redb (or b protein) from the Red recombinase 
system. In this strain, a total of 90-100 bp of homology is sufficient to modify the genome in various 
ways: insertions, deletions and mismatches40,41,50,51. The recombinase is expressed in a plasmid 
containing the Standard European Vector Architecture (SEVA) with xylS-Pm expression system52. 
xylS-Pm expression system has been used and optimized repeatedly since its construction. This

Bearing in mind the successful implementation of ssDNA recombineering in P. putida and after 
comparing the characteristics of allelic exchange technique versus recombineering, we propose that 
ssDNA recombineering holds the best chance to perform efficient and time-competitive genome 
editing in P. aeruginosa. In our previous research, recombineering experiments showed very similar 
efficiency values for P. aeruginosa PAO1 (see Results section, Table 3 and Figure 4). 
 
The CRISPR-Cas system as a genome editing tool 
Recombineering has one discouraging bottleneck, which is the lack of counterselection. This not a 
problem if the mutations produce a selectable phenotype, such as certain missense mutation in rpsL 
which causes streptomycin (Sm) resistance. However, this is a serious issue if the mutation we seek 
to produce has no obvious phenotype. Considering that the main scope of this project is to modify 
the P. aeruginosa genome at will, choosing recombineering over allelic replacement appears unwise 
if mutations must be restrained to already characterized genes. Luckily, biotechnology is an ever-
growing field, in which basic research translates into countless applications. The Clustered Regularly 
Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeat (CRISPR) sequences and CRISPR associated (Cas) genes, 
named together the CRISPR-Cas system, are the most recent example. In this project we use the 
CRISPR-Cas9 system as a counterselection tool in combination with ssDNA recombineering. Cells 
that do not introduce the mutation through recombineering are targeted by Cas9 and thus eliminated, 
as explained below.  
 
CRISPR-Cas is an adaptive immune system for bacteria and archaea, which had been under study 
for several decades until it was introduced as a new genome-editing tool55. CRISPR-Cas systems 
are grouped into Class I (which is divided into types I, III, and IV) or Class II (which is divided into 
types II, V, and VI). Class 2 systems use only one Cas protein, while Class 1 uses a complex of 
several Cas proteins56. Because of this, genome engineering protocols use Class 2 systems. For 
this project, we will focus on the archetypal Streptococcus pyogenes CRISPR-Cas9 system. This 
CRISPR-Cas9 system has been simplified by elimination of other cas genes (cas1, cas2 and csn2)57. 
The CRISPR array consist of several spacers, which are small DNA sequences typically of 
exogenous origin, separated by direct repeats of DNA of similar length. The immune memory is 
provided by these spacers, which are derived from foreign genomes (such as phages or plasmids 
from other bacteria or archaea) and inserted in CRISPR arrays55. The whole CRISPR array is 
==PAGE:12==
 
7 
 
transcribed into a long precursor-crRNA (pre-crRNA), which is composed of spacers and direct 
repeats58,59. Another important element is the trans-activating CRISPR tracrRNA, a non-coding RNA 
of Class II CRISPR-Cas systems that couples with Cas9, the CRISPR-associated endonuclease 
which is responsible for DNA cleavage by double-strand break (DBS). The tracrRNA:Cas9 complex 
binds to the pre-crRNA to generate crRNAs, which contain approximately 20-nt of the spacer and 
22-nt of the repeat. The mature crRNA:tracrRNA forms a ribonucleoprotein with Cas9, and together 
they look for matching spacer sequences, next to a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM). This DNA 
element is only present in the foreign DNA molecule, not in the host genome. This PAM sequence 
depends on the Cas9 protein and is usually 2-5bp long. In S. pyogenes, the PAM sequence is 5’-
NGG-3’. The presence of the PAM region next to the spacer matched sequence allows for precise 
DBS55,57,60,61. The whole process is illustrated in Figure 2 in a simplified representation.

In prokaryotes, the CRISPR-Cas9 system has been used before to achieve mark-less mutations for 
genome editing: E. coli, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Lactobacillus reuteri, Clostridium cellulolyticum, 
and several Streptomyces strains57,62–66. All this research is focused on the same premise: to use 
CRISPR-Cas to enhance genome editing by cleaving the wild-type genome, and at the same time 
drastically reducing the population of wild-type cells that do not incorporate the mutation57. These 
two facts are very appealing for P. aeruginosa genome editing, in particular the possibility to use the 
CRISPR-Cas9 system as a counterselection tool, in addition to increasing ssDNA recombineering 
efficiency. Because the focus of this project is to develop a more efficient genome editing protocol 
than previous ones, we decided to implement a CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection system, which has 
recently been optimized in P. putida EM42 to boost recombineering efficiency67.

OBJECTIVES 
The focus of this project is to implement and optimize a CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering in 
reference strain P. aeruginosa PAO1. To pursue this objective, we used the same plasmids as in P. 
putida EM4267, as represented in Figure 3. The project is divided in two main parts.  
 
First, we selected the most efficient recombinase (Table 3) for P. aeruginosa PAO1 using the plasmid 
shown in Figure 3. For this, we tested several genes and different mutations within those genes. We 
also showed that it is possible to introduce clinical mutations through recombineering in P. 
aeruginosa PAO1. 
 
Secondly, we implemented CRISPR-Cas as a counterselection tool using the plasmid in Figure 3B 
for expression of CRISPR-Cas9 system and the plasmid in Figure 3C for the spacer that targets 
wild-type sequence counterselection. For this, we first tested the efficiency of genome cleavage in 
P. aeruginosa with the CRISPR-Cas9 system. Then, we showed CRISPR-Cas9-assisted 
recombineering works in P. aeruginosa. Finally, we were able to introduce clinical mutations through 
CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering in P. aeruginosa PAO1.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
Media and growth conditions 
Overnight planktonic cultures of both E. coli and P. aeruginosa were grown in Luria-Bertani (LB, 
Sigma-Aldrich) broth at 37°C with 250 rpm shaking. Strains carrying the temperature-sensitive 
promoter were grown at 30°C. For recombineering experiments, strains were grown when indicated 
in glycerol-free terrific broth (TB) (24 g l—1 yeast extract, 12 g l-1 tryptone, 12.54 g l-1 K2HPO4, 2.31 
g l-1 KH2PO4) at 37°C with 200 rpm shaking. For recombinase induction, 1 mM 3-methyl benzoate 
(3-MB) was added in the xylS-Pm expression system. Alternatively, the temperature was raised to 
42°C for 30 minutes in the thermo-inducible system. For solid media, LB agar was added to a final 
concentration of 20 g l-1. Pseudomonas Isolation Agar (PIA, Sigma-Aldrich) was prepared according 
to manufacturer instructions. The following antibiotics were added where indicated to the media: 25 
and 100 mg l-1 chloramphenicol (Cm) for E. coli and P. aeruginosa respectively, 0.25 mg l-1 
ciprofloxacin (Cip), 10 and 15 mg l-1 gentamicin (Gm) for E. coli and P. aeruginosa respectively, and 
25 and 100 mg l-1 streptomycin (Sm) for E. coli and P. aeruginosa respectively. Minimal medium 
contained 20 mM citrate as the sole carbon source, M9 salts (1x), MgSO4 x 7 H2O (2 mM) and was 
supplemented when indicated with 250 mg l-1 of 5-fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA) and 20 mg l-1 uracil. For 
solid media, 1.6 g l-1 agar was added. Moreover, when indicated, 100 mM MgSO4 and 300 mM 
sucrose were used in this project. All reagents and components were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich.

General DNA manipulation, plasmid construction, and DNA transformation 
DNA manipulation was done following standard protocols. Plasmidic DNA extraction was done using 
NucleoSpin Plasmid EasyPure (Macherey-Nagel, M-N).  The following enzymes were used for 
restriction mapping of plasmids or cloning: BcuI, PacI, EcoRI, and BsaI. For DNA ligation we used 
T4 DNA ligase and its corresponding buffer. All enzymes were acquired from ThermoScientific 
manufacturer. For DNA amplification we used OneTaq® 2X Master Mix with Standard Buffer (New 
England Biolabs, NEB). We used 1% agarose gels for DNA visualization. DNA purification was done 
with NucleoSpin Gel and PCR Clean-up (M-N).  
==PAGE:21==

For spacer annealing we mixed equimolar amounts of each oligonucleotide and heated the mix up 
to 95°C for 5 minutes, and then let it cool down at room temperature. After the spacers were 
annealed, they were cloned in pSEVA331-CRISPR vector, already digested with BsaI. Sequencing 
was done with Mix2Seq kits (Eurofins Genomics). In particular, the presence of the spacers was 
verified by sequencing with primer PS1.  
 
DNA transformation was done by triparental mating. For this, the recipient P. aeruginosa PAO1 
strain, the helper strain carrying the pRK600 plasmid and the E. coli strain harboring the mobilizable 
plasmid of interest, were used. We mixed equal amounts of each from an overnight culture and we 
washed them twice with LB media. We re-suspended in a total volume of 50 µl. The final volume 
was plated in LB agar plates and incubated at 37°C for 6-16 hours. Cells were collected and re-
suspended in 10 mM MgSO4. Dilutions were plated into PIA agar plates with the selective antibiotics 
and incubated overnight at 37°C. To verify the presence of the plasmids, colonies were checked by 
colony PCR and diagnostic restriction digest. 
 
Assays for recombineering efficiency 
The recombineering protocol was adapted from P. putida40,41. Strains P. aeruginosa 
PAO1/pSEVA658-Ssr/Rec11 were grown overnight in LB-Gm15 at 37°C with shaking (250 rpm). As 
a control we used a strain transformed with empty vector pSEVA651. Overnight cultures were diluted 
to OD600=0.1 in 20 ml of fresh LB-Gm15 and regrown to OD600=0.4-0.5. Then, recombinase 
expression was induced in cells by adding 1 mM 3-MB and incubating for 30 minutes. Cells were 
collected to prepare competent cells by centrifuging at 5000 rpm for 5 minutes at room temperature 
(RT). After pelleting the cells, they were washed twice with 2 ml of 300 mM sucrose and re-
suspended in 200 µl of the same solution73. Aliquots of 100 µl of competent cells were mixed with 1 
µl of 100 mM recombineering oligonucleotide. These mixes were then electroporated at 2.5 kV in 2-
mm gap width cuvettes (Bio-Rad Laboratories) using a Micropulser apparatus (Bio-Rad 
Laboratories). The control strain was electroporated with and without the oligonucleotide to 
determine background recombineering. Electroporated cells were inoculated into 5 ml of TB without 
antibiotics and incubated overnight at 37°C with shaking (250 rpm). The next day, serial dilutions 
were prepared and plated in LB and LB with selective agent (i.e. streptomycin) solid agar. Plates 
were incubated at 37°C for 1 day. We counted all the colonies to calculate recombineering frequency 
dividing the total number of colonies from the LB with the selected agent by the number of colonies 
from the LB plates. This ratio was then normalized to 109 cells. To confirm the presence of the correct 
mutation instead of spontaneous mutations, we selected 5-20 colonies to amplify the gene of 
interest. To verify the presence of the mutation, we sent the PCR products for sequencing. This 
protocol was used for rpsL/gyrA/gyrB/rpoN-targeted recombineering. Alternatively, a similar 
==PAGE:22==

recombineering protocol was design for mutations that reduce the growth rate of bacteria (i.e. pyrF 
knockout)40. The protocol is the same for recombinase induction and preparation of competent cells. 
The only difference with the procedure described above is the recovery period. After electroporation 
with the recombineering oligonucleotide, cells are transferred into 20 ml of fresh LB and incubated 
for 6 hours at 37°C with shaking (250 rpm). This protocol was used for pyrF-targeted recombineering. 
There is a schematic representation of the two recombineering protocols in Figure S1 
(Supplementary information). 
 
For recombineering experiments using the thermo-inducible expression system, bacteria were 
always grown at 30°C instead of 37°C. For the induction of the recombinase expression, temperature 
was increased to 42°C for 30 minutes. 
 
Interference assays 
The interference assay protocol was adapted from P. putida67. P. aeruginosa PAO1/pSEVA421-
cas9-tracrRNA was grown overnight in LB-Sm100 at 37°C with shaking (250 rpm). Competent cells 
were prepared as described in the previous section. Cells were electroporated with empty 
pSEVA331-CRISPR331 as a control or with pSEVA331-CRISPR331-‘spacer’. The ‘spacer’ term 
refers to the crRNA targeting the gene of interest, designed as described in the previous section. 
Electroporation was done as described before. After electroporation, cells were transferred to 3 ml 
of fresh LB without antibiotics and grown for 2 hours at 37°C with shaking (250 rpm). Serial dilutions 
were prepared and plated the appropriate dilutions in LB-Sm (for total cell count) and LB-Sm-Cm 
plates agar plates (for co-transformants). The survival rate was determined from the transformation 
efficiency. Transformation efficiency was calculated by dividing the number of colonies of co-
transformants by the total number of colonies. Transformation efficiency of the empty control plasmid 
(pSEVA-331-CRISPR) was normalized to 109 cells and used to estimate the 100% survival rate. 
Transformation efficiency of bacteria transformed with the spacer was also normalized to 109 and its 
survival rate was inferred. The survival rate determined the efficiency of interference of each spacer.

Preliminary results suggest the potential of recombineering in P. aeruginosa 
Previous results obtained during a special course before the beginning of this master thesis project 
showed the potential of recombineering in reference strain P. aeruginosa PAO1. In this preliminary 
work, we tested the recombineering efficiency of the gene rpsL with 5 recombinases, which are 
shown in Table 3. The rpsL gene encodes the 30S ribosomal protein S12. A recombineering 
oligonucleotide was used to introduce a single nucleotide change in rpsL (Figure 4A). This single 
mutation causes an amino acid change (K43T) that induces streptomycin resistance in 
Pseudomonas.

Recombineering of rpsL test workflow was established in P. putida EM4241, and our results (Figure

For that reason, we analyzed recombineering performance for the recombinases shown in Table 3. 
All of them, except Rec11, have already been published and were obtained from Víctor de Lorenzo’s 
laboratory at ‘Centro Nacional de Biotecnología’ (CSIC). The recombinases used in their study were

Pseudomonas putida DT-T1E41. For our research, we identified Rec11, native to a P. aeruginosa 
strain, with a protein BLAST (BLASTp), by searching for homologues to a ssDNA-binding protein 
found in Pseudomonas aeruginosa MTB-1. This is the phylogenetically closest P. aeruginosa strain 
to P. putida DT-T1E.

Selection of the most efficient recombinase by targeting new genes 
To confirm which recombinase among the selected ones – Ssr and Rec11 – was most efficient, we 
targeted the pyrF gene for recombineering (Figure 5A). pyrF encodes an orotidine 5'-phosphate 
decarboxylase, a protein involved in de novo synthesis of nucleotides pyrimidine and uridine

significant difference (p-value greater than 0.05) between the controls and Rec11. These results 
suggest that Ssr is more efficient in recombineering of deletions than Rec11, but characterization of 
further deletions versus mismatches in other systems is necessary to confirm this. 
 
Moreover, the use of pyrF to test recombinase efficiency was not as effective as in P. putida. Indeed, 
we observed up to three different types of colonies in minimal media agar plates. All three types 
were confirmed by colony PCR to be P. aeruginosa PAO1. We did not observe any correlation 
between colony type and mutation of pyrF. In P. putida, the ∆pyrF genotype is associated with a 
smaller colony size than the wild-type. These results suggest that pyrF does not work as a flawless 
test for recombinases in P. aeruginosa as in P. putida. Therefore, we decided to test recombineering 
on more genes to determine which recombinase is the most efficient.  
 
Implementation of recombineering with clinically important mutations 
Recombineering of rpsL versus pyrF indicated a range of recombinase efficiencies in P. aeruginosa 
PAO1 which could be gene or mutation-type dependent. Therefore, we decided to use 
recombineering on other genes to further characterize the importance of recombinase choice and 
associated dependencies on mutation versus gene. Previous results described in this thesis showed 
a high recombineering efficiency when introducing a single nucleotide mismatch in PAO1 (Figure 
4B). To verify the full potential of recombineering of the PAO1 strain, we extended our 
recombineering tests to infection-relevant mutations from clinical isolates with complex phenotypic 
outcomes. 
 
gyrB encodes subunit B of DNA gyrase, an enzyme that when mutated is held to confer resistance 
to quinolones in P. aeruginosa74, E. coli75, and other pathogenic bacteria such as Mycobacterium

function inferred by homology. In a recent work from our group (Bartell et al., under review), 
nonsynonymous mutations in gyrB and gyrA (Figure 6A and 6C) were present in clinical isolates with 
varying levels of ciprofloxacin (Cip) tolerance. Furthermore, an unexpected association with 
increased adhesion was found for gyrB mutants and strains with a combination of gyrA/B mutations, 
but not in gyrA mutant strains. Therefore, we decided to move these mutations into a PAO1 genetic 
background to analyze their effects more thoroughly and in isolation from other potentially 
compounding mutations in the adapted clinical strains (Bartell et al., under review).

Introduction of clinical mutations with CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering 
Previous results showed that CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering is possible in P. aeruginosa 
PAO1. We decided to introduce the following mutations found in clinical isolates to confirm that it is 
possible to target other genes with different mutations in addition to the deletion tested in pyrF. To 
do so, we decided to modify PAO1 again with the nucleotide change G1405T in gyrB (Figure 6A) 
using CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection. This mutation is relevant because we used it previously to 
measure recombineering efficiency for Ssr and Rec11. We were also interested in trying to introduce 
a 3-nt insertion in gyrA (Figure 6C) in PAO1 because we were not able to do so using only 
recombineering.  
 
Secondly, we wanted to work with a complete new set of genes to verify that CRISPR-Cas9-assisted 
recombineering can be used to modify any gene and to introduce mutations that link to complex and 
difficult to select phenotypes. Novel patterns of sequential mutations in the retS/gacAS/rsmA 
pathoadaptive regulatory system have recently been identified (Bartell at al., under review). This set 
of genes is involved in the molecular infection-switch of P. aeruginosa in CF patients. They conform 
a regulatory pathway in which retS codifies a sensor kinase/response regulator hybrid, gacS codifies 
another kinase/response regulator hybrid, gacA encodes a response regulator, and rsmA encodes 
a final major regulator. These elements are thought to regulate the transition from acute to chronic

mutation of upstream retS induces a shift to a chronic infection phenotype by ultimately inhibiting 
rsmA activity via the de-repression of gacAS, while mutation of gacA/S increases rsmA activity and 
induces a shift towards an acute infection phenotype. However, genetic and phenotypic analysis 
recently highlighted a surprising mutation sequence in three CF patients where retS mutations 
appearin the early years of infection to induce a chronic infection phenotype but are followed by 
subsequent mutation of gacS/gacA, which appears to revert an adapted strain back towards acute 
infection traits17,81. Work from our research group identified several combinations of mutations in the 
retS/gacS/gacA pathway (Bartell et al., under review). We selected two significant combinations – 
one in retS/gacS/ and another in retS/gacA – found in one or more patients, which are represented 
in Figure 10. Following the time line of appearance of these mutations in the clinical strains, we 
decided to target retS first, and secondly gacS/gacA.

Thermo-inducible promoter could increase recombineering efficiency 
We considered the possibility to optimize recombineering, and eventually CRISPR-Cas9-assisted 
recombineering, by changing the expression system of the recombinase. A heat inducible 
expression system, based on the pL/pR promoters from l phage in E. coli, has been used for 
production of recombinant proteins. The main advantage of it is that it is a very efficiently regulated 
expression system82. This tightly regulation is hypothesized to improve recombineering efficiency by 
reducing Ssr toxic effect, which has been shown in P. putida41. The system consists of the pL 
promoter that transcribes the gene of interest – Ssr in this case – and the mutant thermolabile cI 
repressor, which also is part of the l phage from E. coli. The repressor is inactivated when 
temperature rises above 37°C82. We thank Víctor de Lorenzo and his group for giving us the plasmid 
with the thermo-inducible promoter and Ssr (pSEVA651-cIpL-Ssr) to test its efficiency in P. 
aeruginosa PAO1. We tested the recombineering efficiency of this system in rpsL, pyrF, and gyrB 
(Figure 12). The mutations to be introduced were the same as described in the previous sections. 
When we compared these results to the original ones with the 3-MB-inducible system (Figure 4B vs 
Figure 12A; Figure 5B vs Figure 12B; Figure 6B vs Figure 12C), we did not observe significant 
differences in Ssr efficiency. This result prevented us of cloning our other recombinase – Rec11 – 
with this expression system. To complete the characterization of this expression system, we tried it 
also in CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering for pyrF deletion. We did not obtain any positive 
candidates in these experiments. Altogether, our results suggest that cIpL thermo-inducible 
expression system is not more efficient than the 3-MB-inducible one in P. aeruginosa.

DISCUSSION 
P. aeruginosa is a highly relevant bacteria in infection microbiology. The Pseudomonas scientific 
community would benefit greatly from a simple, fast and efficient genome editing tool for P. 
aeruginosa. In this project we have adapted a new genome editing method for P. aeruginosa. This 
project was divided into two main objectives: 1) to optimize recombineering in P. aeruginosa PAO1, 
and 2) to implement the CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection tool. We have characterized an array of 
new recombinases and improved recombineering efficiencies via protocol optimization. We have 
managed to engineer mismatches and deletions through recombineering in the following genes: 
rpsL, pyrF, gyrA, and gyrB. rpoN-targeted recombineering was not successful. We have also 
introduced mutations in pyrF and retS using CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering, but not in gyrA 
nor gyrB. All things considered, this project has upgraded the genetic programming of P. aeruginosa 
as well as expanded the possibilities of research with this new genome editing technique in P. 
aeruginosa.  
 
To our knowledge, this is the first time ssDNA recombineering of gene deletions and introduction of 
mismatches has been achieved in P. aeruginosa. In this project we have achieved very similar 
recombineering efficiencies as in P. putida for long deletions and low-sensitivity mismatches40,41. Our 
results showed that rpsL-targeted recombineering can be used as a standardized test for measuring 
different recombinases efficiencies in P. aeruginosa. We observed a very similar efficiency 
compared to P. putida. However, the difference in efficiency between the controls and the 
recombinases was smaller in P. aeruginosa than in P. putida 41. This can be caused by adaptive 
resistance83 or phenotypic tolerance84,85 to antibiotics, or spontaneous mutations.  
 
pyrF-targeted recombineering efficiency was lower in comparison to rpsL recombineering efficiency. 
This difference could be explained by the use of different selection agents and media than in rpsL-
targeted recombineering. Recombineering efficiency could also be affected by the difference in 
generation time (~0.7 h) between LB and minimal medium with citrate as sole carbon source86. The 
difference in efficiency between controls and the recombinases was smaller in P. aeruginosa when 
compared to P. putida40. This can be due to the stressful conditions of use of minimal media and 5-
FOA as a selective agent, which could produce more spontaneous mutants. However, the 
recombineering efficiency was higher in P. aeruginosa than in P. putida41. We also observed different 
morphotypes when selecting pyrF mutants in the recombineering experiments. Morphotype diversity 
has been shown in stressful environments such as CF lungs87. This could be correlated with the 
metabolic adaptation that P. aeruginosa undergo during CF pulmonary infection, which also varies 
between and within patients88. Minimal media could be a stress factor for P. aeruginosa, thus 
promoting morphotype diversity. Because of this, we used pyrF-targeted recombineering as an 
==PAGE:41==
 
36 
 
indicator system. Our results suggest that Ssr can be more efficient in producing deletions than 
Rec11 (Figure 5B). Ssr was also observed to be more efficient for introducing deletions in P. putida 
(Aparicio et al., manuscript in preparation). However, further deletions versus mismatches tests in 
other systems are needed to confirm this. 
 
We showed that gyrB-targeted recombineering can be used as an alternative in measuring 
recombinases efficiency in P. aeruginosa. We observed again a smaller difference in efficiency 
between the control and the recombinases than in rpsL-targeted recombineering. This can be 
explained by the use of a different antibiotic for selection (ciprofloxacin instead of streptomycin), 
which have another target in the cell and thus a different effect (DNA gyrase instead of ribosome)89,90. 
Our results suggest that Rec11 is more efficient for recombineering mismatches than Ssr in P. 
aeruginosa. 
 
rpoN-targeted recombineering was not successful in P. aeruginosa so far. We have several theories 
as to why the experiments targeting three different mutations failed (full rpoN deletion, mismatch, 
and short deletion in rpoN, Figure 7) failed. In the case of the single nucleotide mutation, the failure 
to introduce this mismatch can be explained by the MMR system, which has been known to inhibit 
recombineering or reduce drastically its efficiency34,42. The MMR system is more sensitive to certain 
mismatches, such as the C-T mismatch introduced by the oligo Rec-rpoN-C112T43. This could 
explain the low-to-none efficiency of this recombineering experiment. Moreover, these mutations are 
also associated with slow growth (Mojsoska et al., manuscript in preparation) in contrast to mutations 
of DNA gyrase. There is an alternative recombineering protocol (see Material and methods section; 
Supplementary information, Figure S1) with a shortened recovery time that favors slow growing 
bacteria. We plan to test this alternative protocol for rpoN mutations in the future. This shorter 
incubation period allows bacteria to introduce the mutation which causes slow growth, and at the 
same time reduces the risk of a fast-growing population to overcome them. We also had no evidence 
that these particular mutations directly produced Cip resistance, they were simply associated with 
resistant clinical strains (Mojsoska et al., manuscript in preparation). Cip resistance of clinical isolates 
containing those mutations can be a result of a combination of different mutations in other genes 
which are no longer present once the mutation is moved into the PAO1 background and therefore 
renders selection with Cip futile. Thus, it might be not possible to select these mutations with Cip. 
Instead, we could use CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection. 
 
We cannot conclude anything about the use of recombineering to include insertions or high-
sensitivity mismatches, which easily-recognized by the MMR system. In the future, it would be 
interesting to verify that we can also introduce small insertions in P. aeruginosa with a similar 
efficiency to the rest of modifications. If Ssr and Rec11 are not efficient enough for these 
==PAGE:42==
 
37 
 
modifications, we could also characterize a new set of recombinases, which were already tested in 
P. putida41. Furthermore, we will assess the possibility to transiently66,91–93 or permanently42,94 
suppress the MMR system. This way, we would be able to introduce high-sensitivity mismatches in 
P. aeruginosa genome.  
 
To further assess recombineering efficiency, we tested a thermo-inducible expression system. When 
we compared it to the 3-MB-inducible system, the recombineering efficiency results were very 
similar. Interestingly, the thermo-inducible expression system does increase recombineering 
efficiency in P. putida EM42 (Aparicio et al., manuscript in preparation). This difference could be 
explained by the fact that bacteria with the thermo-inducible system were always grown at 30°C, 
which is the optimal temperature for P. putida growth95, but not for P. aeruginosa96. Our results 
suggest that the 3-MB is an appropriate expression system for recombineering alone and also for 
CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering. The expression system does not appear to be a conflicting 
element for the recombineering efficiency of the system. 
 
The interference assays showed that CRISPR-Cas9-directed cleavage of P. aeruginosa genome 
causes cell death. Our results suggest that it is always important to test the efficiency of spacers 
with interference assays. The CRISPR-Cas9 system has effectively been used for counterselection 
in combination with recombineering to introduce small and large deletions in the P. aeruginosa PAO1 
genome. So far, we have successfully introduced two types of deletions (long and short) in two 
different genes in PAO1 (pyrF, Figure 5A; retS, Figure 10A). Our preliminary results suggest that 
Ssr is a more efficient recombinase in combination with the CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection tool. 
Nonetheless, characterization of different mutations (mismatches and insertions) in pyrF and other 
gene systems is necessary to confirm this hypothesis. Modification and/or optimization of CRISPR-
Cas9-assisted recombineering could be required. 
 
CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection tool can be optimized by modification of the plasmid carrying the 
CRISPR array (pSEVA331-CRISPR, Figure 3C). We hypothesize that expression of more than one 
spacer targeting the same mutation and gene could improve the efficiency of counterselection. Two 
or more spacers would be cloned between the DRs in the CRISPR plasmid. Thus, after maturation 
more crRNA:tracrRNA:Cas9 complexes would be available to bind and cleave the genome. This 
could increase the efficiency of CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection.  
 
Multiplexed recombineering in combination with CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection can also be 
achieved66,67. Spacers targeting the different genes are cloned in the same plasmid, and this plasmid 
is transformed with as many recombineering oligonucleotides as targeted mutations. In the future, 
we would like to test if multiplex recombineering is possible in P. aeruginosa. To do so, we will 
==PAGE:43==
 
38 
 
continue to work with one of the retS/gacS mutations (Figure 10A). Our results showed already that 
it is possible to obtain a small deletion in retS. We will try to introduce the second mutation in gacS. 
If successful, then we will introduce both of the mutations at the same time by CRISPR-Cas9-
assisted recombineering. The use of CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering for multiplexing 
mutations in P. aeruginosa would be a very interesting result as it would further expand the 
possibilities of P. aeruginosa genome editing by effectively shortening the protocol. 
 
During this project, we cured the high-copy number plasmid (Figure 3A) used in recombineering. In 
contrast, in P. putida they used 10 cycles of overnight culture to cure the three plasmids used in 
CRISR-Cas9-recombineering67. We did not try curing all of them in P. aeruginosa. We hypothesize 
that curing the other two plasmids, containing the Cas9 and tracrRNA, and spacer for 
counterselection (Figure 3B and Figure 3C, respectively), is feasible in a reasonable amount of time 
by using LB without any antibiotic pressure. Both of them are medium-copy number plasmid. If in 
the future we observe that plasmid curation is complicated, we can explore the alternative of using 
self-killing plasmids. We can take advantage of the CRISPR-Cas9 system and include spacers that 
target the origin of replication of the plasmids we want to eliminate. The expression of this spacers 
would only be induced after the events of CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering have taken place.  
 
With our research, we have showed that CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering works in P. 
aeruginosa PAO1. First, we tested the efficiency of different recombinases in ssDNA recombineering 
of this bacterium. We proved that we can use recombineering to modify several genes. We showed 
the efficiency of CRISPR-Cas9 in cleaving the genome of P. aeruginosa PAO1. Finally, we showed 
that CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering is a simple, easy and precise genome editing tool in P. 
aeruginosa PAO1. Moreover, our results suggest the potential of this tool and open the door to 
expand it to clinical strains. We will focus our efforts in implementing this system in other P. 
aeruginosa strains, such as PA14 and clinical isolates of CF patients from our collection, which are 
more clinically relevant. These strains are much less laboratory-adapted, thus engineering them is 
a highly relevant challenge.

CONCLUSIONS  
1.- rpsL-, pyrF- and gyrB-targeted recombineering can be used to test different recombinases 
efficiencies in P. aeruginosa PAO1. 
2.- It is possible to engineer P. aeruginosa PAO1 with mismatches and deletions with recombineering 
in different genes. 
3.- Rec11 is the most efficient recombinase to engineer mismatches, whereas Ssr is the most 
efficient recombinase to introduce deletions in P. aeruginosa PAO1. 
4.- The CRISPR-Cas9 system can be used to cause specific genome cleavage in P. aeruginosa 
PAO1. 
5.- CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering is a simple, fast and efficient genome editing tool in P. 
aeruginosa PAO1. 
6.- Ssr has a higher editing efficiency than Rec11 for CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering.

FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
For our understanding, this project has expanded the possibilities of genome editing in P. 
aeruginosa. We believe that we can move forward in the research and characterization of P. 
aeruginosa infection model by tackling several issues. 
 
In P. putida, all mutations (short and long deletions, small insertions, and low-sensitivity mismatches) 
had very similar recombineering efficiency, except the high-sensitivity mismatches40. The MMR 
system is known to reduce recombineering efficiency in high-sensitivity mismatches more than for 
low-sensitivity mismatches42,43. In E. coli, transient66,91–93 and permanent42,94 suppression of the MMR 
system has been shown to increase ssDNA recombineering efficiency. We are not interested in a 
permanent suppression of the MMR system for P. aeruginosa as this can create unwanted and off-
target mutations. The MMR system consists of three proteins: MutS, MutL, and MutH, which are part 
of the DNA Adenine Methylase (Dam)-directed mismatch repair system. Dam is the protein in charge 
of genome methylation. The methylated strand indicates which is the original and correct base in a 
mismatch. Overexpression of Dam makes the whole genome methylated, thus impairing the MMR 
system and rendering a mutS– phenotype97. Moreover, a dominant-negative mutator allele of mutL, 
such as mutL E32K, has also shown increased efficiency in recombineering. The overproduction of 
this allele, which is inactive and cannot be complemented by the native version of mutL impairs the 
MMR system92,93,98. Considering the increased efficiency of recombineering when using one of the 
MMR system manipulation strategies explained above, a future strategy will be to use one of them 
in P. aeruginosa. Thus, we could potentially engineer any type of mutation in P. aeruginosa.  
 
Despite the many possibilities to optimize and expand the CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering 
toolbox, we have to acknowledge an important limitation, which is the presence of a PAM motif next 
to the mutation. The efficiency of counterselection can be reduced if the PAM motif is far away, or 
maybe CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection is not even possible due to the lack of a PAM motif nearby. 
This issue can be solved by the use of Cas9 orthologues or engineering Cas9 proteins, which have 
a different PAM motif than S. pyogenes Cas9 endonuclease. Modifications of Cas9 include resizing 
of cas9, making the enzyme more robust and reliable to avoid cleavage of sequences that are similar 
to the protospacer, and alteration of the PAM motif99–101. Thanks to the almost infinite possibilities of 
CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing, new improvements and adaptations are constantly being published. 
We can use these advantages to keep optimizing CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering and 
overcome limitations that may come in the future. 
 
In this project we have established that it is possible to engineer P. aeruginosa PAO1 reference 
strain through CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering. We want to move forward and try genome 
==PAGE:46==
 
41 
 
editing of more clinically relevant P. aeruginosa strains. P. aeruginosa PAO1 is the most common 
reference strain. PAO1 has evolved into sub-clones due to genome diversity worldwide among 
laboratory strains. In a P. aeruginosa structure of population structure study, PA14 strain was shown 
to be the one of the most frequent genotypes, but not PAO1102. The genotype’s variability of PAO1, 
which can jeopardize the reproducibility of research, make P. aeruginosa PA14 an attractive 
alternative as a laboratory strain. PA14 is a virulent strain due to the presence of pathogenicity 
islands which are not present in PAO1102–104. Virulence factors that are present in P. aeruginosa 
PA14 are not required for PAO1 pathogenicity, as it has been shown that virulence in P. aeruginosa 
strains is multifactorial and combinatorial105. Nonetheless, this enhanced virulence might offer a 
more realistic picture of what happens in reality, i.e. CF lungs. 
 
CF clinical isolates offer another point of view for P. aeruginosa research. We are also very interested 
in implementing CRISPR-Cas9-assisted recombineering. We do not know if it is possible to engineer 
all the clinical isolates, as the more adapted they are to CF lungs the more difficult it is to handle 
them in laboratory settings. Recently, several genome editing techniques have been developed for 
clinical isolates of other pathogens, such as Acinetobacter baumannii106, Candida parapsilosis107, 
Staphylococcus aureus108,109, and an E. coli pathogenic strain from urinary catheter110. Most of them 
rely on the combination of CRISPR-Cas9 and recombineering with varying efficiencies. An 
interesting optimization that we can implement from their research is to reduce the number of 
plasmids we use in our protocol. This is an advantage when working with clinical strains that have a 
varied antibiotic profile. In the future, we could engineer a single plasmid containing the recombinase 
expression system and the cas9 and tracrRNA genes. Furthermore, this would simplify the protocol 
in general.
