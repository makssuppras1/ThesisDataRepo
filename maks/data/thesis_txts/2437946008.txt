Abstract 
ii 
 
Abstract 
Management and monitoring of water systems is important to help decision-makers and authorities 
to plan and secure an integrated water resource management approach. Regional hydraulic models 
can provide time series of water levels and discharge rates used to study and understand regional 
climate variability. Recent years of rapid development and availability of remote sensing date has 
shown to be a valuable source for hydrological models as it can provide inputs to the model, especially 
valuable for ungauged catchments. 
This study explores the use of remote sensing data to inform a lumped conceptual hydrological model 
of the Semliki River Catchment in Eastern Africa. It is performed as part of a greater GlobWetland 
Africa initiative launched by ESA and Ramsar focussing on management and monitoring of wetlands.  
For this study a Muskingum routing model was used as a river model, driven by outputs from a 
Budyko type rainfall- runoff model. Remote sensing data and digital elevations models were used to 
extract the catchment hydrology and construct a hydrological model of the Semliki River Catchment. 
Precipitation and temperature products were used as input to the model which was calibrated against 
few ground observations, using an optimisation approach of flow duration and climatology. Altimetry 
data was used to validate the simulated water balance of the catchment which includes three lakes. 
The model returns simulated river discharges and changes in lake storage, that can be used to support 
future water resource management in the area. 
The results of the study showed promising use of remote sensing data for the modelling the discharges 
and water balance for the Semliki River catchment. Model limitations were highly linked to the lack 
of reliable network of ground stations both in availability and quality. While remote sensing was 
found applicable and further limits the need of ground data, a limited number of reliable ground 
discharge stations is still necessary to setup a good model structure and calibrate model parameters.

University of Denmark (DTU), under the supervision of Professor Peter Bauer-Gottwein and co

This thesis focuses on hydrological modelling of the Semliki River Catchment in Eastern Africa using

demonstration study as part of the GlobWetland Africa initiative launched by the Ramsar Convention

1 Introduction 
Integrating the management of water, land and people is one of the major challenges of the 21st 
century (Ramsar, 2013). The growing effects of climate change and population growth are affecting 
water resources across the globe. Changes in precipitation patterns will change ecologically important 
attributes of flow properties, which will affect many rivers and wetlands (IPCC, 2014c).  
In Africa ecosystems are already being affected by climate change, and predictions show that climate 
change will impose an increased stress on water resources in Africa, which will be subjected to high 
variability in space and time (IPCC, 2014a). Management and monitoring of water systems is 
important to help decision-makers plan and build an integrated approach to adaptation. However, 
many places in Africa are poorly gauged, and these data gaps constrain an integrated water resource 
management (IWRM) approach. The prediction of streamflow in ungauged catchments is important 
for water resource management, but is also a scientific challenge for hydrologists (Zhang et al., 2008).  
Over the last two decades, alongside the rapid development and availability of remote sensing 
techniques for hydrology, satellite observation data has shown to be a valuable source of observation 
of land surface hydrological fluxes and state variability (Tang et al., 2009). Remote sensing gives 
new possibilities for river catchment modelling, and provides additional and alternative forcing, and 
constraints to enable timely and reliable simulations and forecasting (Li et al., 2016). Remote sensing 
can provide gridded data products in near real time with a broad geographical special coverage and 
high spatial resolution (Tang et al., 2009).  
This study explores the use of remote sensing data to inform a lumped conceptual hydrological model 
of the Semliki River Catchment in Eastern Africa. Precipitation and temperature products are used as 
input to the model which is calibrated against few ground observations of flow and lake level. 
Altimetry data was used to validate the simulated water balance of the lakes with the catchment 
boundaries. The model returns simulated river discharges and lake levels that can be used to support 
future water resource management in the area. 
 Background – GlobWetland Africa 
The African wetlands hold some of the ecosystems in the world with the largest biological diversity. 
They are experiencing an increased pressure due to human activity including settlement and drainage 
for agriculture (DHI GRAS, 2018). The main challenge with monitoring and management of the 
wetlands in Africa is the lack of data from the areas and lack of institutional capacity.  
Sentinel, the new generation of satellite missions from The European Space Agency (ESA) provides 
global monitoring with free open source data, that provides enhanced possibilities for wetland 
monitoring and management. Together, the Ramsar Secretariat and the ESA have launched 
GlobWetland Africa (GW-A) to embrace the possibilities of using earth observation (EO) for 
management and monitoring of African wetlands. The project is initiated to facilitate the exploitation 
of satellite data for conservation and to provide stakeholders with tools and methods to better fulfil 
their commitments towards the Ramsar Convention of wetlands. The Ramsar Convention of wetlands

is an intergovernmental treaty established in 1971 to provide a framework for conservation and wise 
use of wetlands worldwide, achieved through local and national actions together with international 
cooperation (Ramsar and UNESCO, 1971).  
The Semliki River was chosen as a pilot site along with 60 other GW-A projects across the African 
continent, three of which also investigates river hydrology. The project aims to investigate and model 
the river catchment hydrology of the area using remote sensing data. The simplistic model is an 
attempt to understand the relationship between the water balance in a catchment and the climate 
conditions. The model is designed as a tool for stakeholders toward a better management and 
monitoring of wetlands in the catchment area.    
 Objectives 
To protect wetlands and biodiversity the integrated management of water and land is essential:  
“Recognizing that everything is interconnected is part of the solution” (Djoghlaf, 2009). The overall 
objective of this project is to gain a better understanding and foundation of the river catchment 
hydrology in the Semliki River catchment, to help improve water management and inform 
stakeholders with information of the catchment hydrology.  
There is a continuous development in the modelling of watershed hydrology and flow predictions, 
especially in areas such as Africa where basins are poorly gauged and data is scarce (Onema, 
Taigbenu and Ndiritu, 2012). This study was performed to further explore the possibilities and 
limitations of using free and public available remote sensing data for hydrological modelling of river 
discharges. The main objective of this study is to setup a hydrological rainfall-runoff model of the 
Semliki River catchment using remote sensing as forcing data. The objective was obtained through 
the following research objectives: 
➢ Developing a watershed delineation of the catchment using remote sensing data and identify 
points of interest. 
 
➢ Setting up a lumped conceptual model for the catchment, using the Budyko framework for 
the water balance and Muskingum routing to simulate river discharge.  
 
➢ Incorporating lake water balances and including snow melt and accumulation processes in the 
model.  
 
➢ Validate and calibrate the model through a designed calibration strategy based on model 
parameters, available data and hydrological properties of the catchment.  
 
➢ Investigate how lake water levels can be acquired through remote sensing and used to validate 
model results.  
 
➢ Assess the overall model performance and the applicability of integrating remote sensing into 
hydrological models for poorly ungauged areas.

2 Study Area 
The Semliki River catchment is located in the equatorial Nile catchment, between DR Congo and 
Uganda as shown in Figure 1. The catchment is part of the upper drainage to the White Nile and 20% 
of the discharge in the White Nile originates from the Semliki catchment (Onema, Taigbenu and 
Ndiritu, 2012). The catchment is characterised by its lakes, Lake Albert, Lake Edward and Lake 
George and the significant Rwenzori mountain range. The elevation in the basin ranges from 600 m 
to 5111 m above sea level. The flora and fauna of the area holds the unique and distinct ecosystems 
of the Albertine Rift (Onema and Taigbenu, 2009). The area has a long history of trans-border 
conflicts and since the 1990’s the region has suffered from civil war with cross-border military and 
civil conflicts (Raeymaekers and Jourdan, 2009). These issues are reflected in the data availability 
from hydrological field measures and studies. Thus, the area is considered a sparsely gauged 
catchment with scarce hydrological data. 
 
Figure 1: Catchment outline and geographical location of the Semliki River catchment.   
To obtain knowledge and understanding of the study area reports and documents from the local 
governments were reviewed. Further, a comprehensive literature research was performed using the 
scientific articles database - Web of Science (Clarivative Analytics, 2018). The research was aimed 
towards a general knowledge of the catchment and to review previous relevant studies from the area. 
The literature research and data regarding the catchment, showed implications of the conflicts in DR 
Congo and the British colonisation of Uganda, as most data and records, both recent and historical, 
were found for the Ugandan part of the catchment.

 Semliki River 
The Semliki River originates in the southern part of the catchment, where it drains the basins of Lake 
Edward and Lake George. From this point, it meanders its way 140 km through the Albertine rift 
where it drains into Lake Albert in the North. For the first 40 km, the river runs through the densely 
forested Semuliki National Park. For the following 100 km, it runs through the Semliki flats 
dominated by grassland (NEMA, 2009). In the Semliki Flats, the banks of the river suffer from 
erosion, partly due to livestock activity and increased snowmelt in the Rwenzoris. On the Ugandan 
side of the river some areas have experienced up to 10 m annual erosion and in some areas along the 
river, the river width has doubled the last 10 years (NEMA, 2009). The eroded banks of the river have 
caused years of silt deposit in Lake Albert causing the delta front to expand into the lake (NEMA, 
2009). In the lower reaches of the river the change in course of the meandering river has in recent 
years raised concerns in Uganda, as the river in the last third of the stretch forms the boundary 
between DR Congo and Uganda. In some places, the border has shifted almost 1 km and local farmers 
are losing their land (Randerson, 2010). For the first two thirds the Semliki River runs through DR 
Congo.  
 Rwenzori Mountains 
The catchment is known for the iconic Rwenzori Mountains. The range is around 120 km long and 
65 km wide with the three significant peaks of Baker, Speke and Stanley and the highest point 
reaching 5111 m a.m.s.l (Whittow, 1960). The catchment is located close to the equator and therefore 
receives a high amount of solar radiation. However, it is the area in Uganda with the lowest amount 
of sunshine, due to frequent cloud covers and prolonged light rain (NEMA, 2009). The Rwenzori 
Mountains are considered as one of the largest and most significant water catchments in Uganda 
(Ramsar, 2008). The streams originating from the mountains feed the ecologically important Lake 
George and Lake Edward and are a significant source of the White Nile feeding though the Semliki 
River and Lake Albert (Ramsar, 2008). 
The range experiences two rainy seasons, the first from March to May and the second from August 
to December. However, the dry periods are only moderately dry as the Rwenzoris are influenced by 
moisture advection from the middle of the continent during the dry months (Georges and Kaser, 
2003). For some altitudes and temperatures, precipitation over the mountains will fall as snow. The 
mean annual 0°C altitude of the Rwenzoris is located somewhere between 4600 and 4700 m a.m.s.l 
(Georges and Kaser, 2003). 
In recent years there has been a decline in the size of the permanent Rwenzori ice cap and it is 
predicted to disappear within the next decade (Taylor et al., 2006; NEMA, 2009). The glaciers extent 
has gone from 2.01 ± 0.56 km2 in 1987 to 0.96 ± 0.34 km2 in 2003 (Taylor et al., 2006). Presumably, 
the main reason is changes in climate and the consequential rise in air temperatures. A study by 
Georges and Kaser (2003) found a correlation between increased incoming shortwave radiation and 
glacier surface in East Africa, in the end of the 19th century. Over the last century the area has 
experienced a decrease in precipitation and an increase in air temperature. This has not only caused 
less accumulation of precipitation, but also less cloud protection against shortwave radiation.

 Major Lakes of the Catchment 
The studied catchment includes three lakes, all part of the African great lake system. The Semliki 
River runs from Lake Edward in the south to Lake Albert in the north.  
Lake George, with a surface area of about 250 km2, is the smallest of the three lakes in the catchment 
and is located in the southeastern part of the catchment, where it connectes and discharges into Lake 
Edward, through the 32 km long Kazinga Channel. The lake is surrounded by wetlands and the lake 
itself can be considered as a wetland since the average depth of Lake George is around 2.4 m (Mafabi, 
2000).  
The border of DR Congo and Uganda runs though Lake Edward, the second biggest lake in the 
catchment. The 2325 km2 large lake lies within UNESCO Heritage site and the national parks Virunga 
National Park (Congo) and Queen Elizabeth National Park (Uganda). There are very few settlements 
around the lake. However, conflicts emerged in the area around the lake in 2014 due to oil dispute 
(Gettleman, 2014).  
The largest lake of the catchment, Lake Albert, has a surface area of 5,400 km2 with a surface 
elevation of 619 m. a.m.s.l (Bastawesy, Gabr and White, 2013). The lake is around 160 km long and 
30 km wide and has a maximum depth of 51 m. The Semliki River drains into Lake Albert in the 
southern, whilst Lake Albert connects the Kyoga Nile with the White Nile in the most northern point 
of the lake as seen on Figure 2.  
The lake and the wetlands in the catchment serve as natural reservoirs, resulting in higher evaporation 
losses over the open waters. However, they also attenuate the otherwise high seasonality in river 
flows, caused by the two rainy seasons (MWE, 2013).

 Wetlands and Ramsar Protective Sites 
Around 13% of Uganda is covered by wetlands which 
are either permanent or seasonal (NEMA, 2009). Within 
the catchment two main wetlands are present as indicated 
in Figure 3. The Lake George system wetland is the 
oldest Ramsar site in Uganda. It was designated in 1988 
due to the important protection of flora and fauna. The 
wetland area is dominated by papyrus swamps and 
wetland grass and hosts more than 150 bird species 
(Mafabi, 2000). Another seasonal wetland is situated at 
the southern banks of Lake Albert where the Semliki 
River drains into the lake. In the northern end of the lake, 
at the inflow from Kyoga Nile, is a delta area, which is 
also assigned as a Ramsar site (Murchison Falls – Albert 
Delta Wetland system).   
The Rwenzori Mountains located in the centre of the 
catchment were listed as a Ramsar site in 2009, covering 
an area of 99,500 ha. In the late 80’s an international 
concern arose regarding the environmental protection of 
the Rwenzoris. The mountains support vulnerable and 
endangered species living in and around the Rwenzoris.  It supports one of the most important bird 
communities in Uganda with 217 species recorded (Ramsar, 2008).       
 Catchment Characteristics and Climate  
The catchment experiences a tropical climate with a 
high annual rainfall of high seasonal variability 
(Bastawesy, Gabr and White, 2013). It has an annual 
average rainfall of 1125 mm mainly falling during the 
two rainy season from March to May and again from 
October to December (Onema and Taigbenu, 2009). 
Temperatures in the catchment are moderate 
throughout the year due to its location close to the 
equator and on the elevated African plateau.  
The catchment is located in the east African Rift valley 
with its characteristic curving through the landscape as 
seen on Figure 4. Here, the DEM clearly shows 
significant elevation differences in the catchment with 
the lakes and the Semliki River at altitudes 600-1000 
m.a.m.s.l compared to the Rwenzoris ranging up in the 
landscape.

The physiography of the catchment is the result of deposits of several generations of sedimentary 
cover, erosion, volcanic activity and rift faulting (MWE, 2013). Hence, the geology of the catchment 
is very heterogeneous and includes e.g. Rift valley sediments, gneisses and argillites (See Figure 38 
and Figure 39 in Appendix D) The southern parts of the catchment around Lakes Edward and George 
show evidence of volcanic activity over the last 5000 year, where volcanic rocks alongside cones and 
crates are found in the landscape. 
 Climate Change 
The intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has highlighted glacier retreat and Great 
Lakes warming as directly observed changes in the region related to anthropogenic climate change 
(IPCC, 2014a). Climate change and the related effects on snowmelt and continued loss in glacier ice,  
imply a change in the observed streamflow, together with a shift of peak discharge (IPCC, 2014b). 
Inter-annual fluctuations of the Nile flows are closely linked to El Niño-Southern Oscillations 
(ENSO) climatic events (Bastawesy, Gabr and White, 2013). Recent years have shown a more 
frequent and stronger returns of ENSO, which in Eastern Africa is associated with enhanced rainfall, 
that is correlated with the short rainy period (September – December) (Phillips and McIntyre, 2000).  
In the last years, East Africa has experienced major floodings as a result of ENSO – the latest towards 
the end of 2015.  
In 2013 the Directory of Water Recourses Management in Uganda published a National Water 
Resources Assessment report and in that context, climate change and the possible impacts were 
assessed for Uganda (MWE, 2013). The possible climate changes were based on results from a new 
Regional Climate model from the UK Met Office, based on multiple model projections (DHI, 2013). 
The predictions were modelled for the period 2020 to 2049. The model projections showed a strong 
agreement of temperature increase in all months of the year, showing the largest expected increase in 
April to June (2-3 °C) and the lowest changes in November to March (0.75 – 1.75 °C). The potential 
evapotranspiration showed an increase of 4-16% in the months from April to June. Changes in 
precipitation showed no consensus in the model predictions regarding the direction of change. 
However, the main tendency was a minor decrease in rainfall for the months from April to June, 
whilst an increase of precipitation might be expected from November to January. The water level in 
the major lakes were expected to have a minor decrease, associated with an expected decrease in flow 
between -4 to -8%. However, model predictions for flow in the Semliki river ranged from -19 to 
+19% (DHI, 2013). 
In addition to future predictions of climate change the population of DR Congo and Uganda are 
expected to grow at some of the highest rates in the world. Uganda’s population increased from 4.8 
million in 1948 to 29.6 million in 2008 and is predicted to reach about 100 million in 2050 (NEMA, 
2009). Similarly, the population of DR Congo is expected to reach around 190 million in 2050, 
compared to a population of 81 million in 2017 (Worldometers, 2018).  The increase in population is 
likely to have an impact on the water resources in the catchment due to greater extraction, pollution 
and land cover changes.

 Previous Studies 
The Semliki River catchment is one of the most upstream parts of the great African Nile system. As 
the longest river in the world and a river catchment that covers 10 countries, the major Nile system 
has been the subject of numerous studies. These studies include publications from the Nile Basin 
Initiative, an intergovernmental partnership between 10 Nile basin countries (NBI, 2018). Other 
studies of the Nile Basin includes Bastawesy, Gabr, and White (2013) and Butts et al. (2016). More 
specific studies concerning the Semliki River and the surrounding catchments are; Kileshye Onema 
and Taigbenu (2009), Kileshye Onema, Taigbenu, and Ndiritu (2012), Zaake et al. (2011) and Earle 
and Rukundo (2012), which are described in the following sections. 
The study by Bastawesy, Gabr, and White (2013) used remote sensing data as a compliment to ground 
measures in the process of developing a distributed hydrological model of the Ugandan Lakes and 
the Upper White Nile Basin. Here 36 individual catchments were monitored and gauged for periods 
from 1967 to 1992. The study raises concerns about the sparse and incomplete record of hydrological 
data, which was a challenge for hydrological modelling. The study estimated annual discharges 
between the different interconnected basins and examined potential management options for both 
storing water in the catchment and lowering the huge evaporation losses in the system from all the 
lakes and swamps, which cover more than 80 000 km2 in the Upper White Nile catchment. The studies 
showed an estimated annual flow of 22·109 m3 discharging from the Kyoga swamps into Lake Albert 
and a discharge of 25· 109 m3 from Lake Albert further into the Sudd wetlands of Sudan (Bastawesy, 
Gabr and White, 2013).  
The Directorate of Water Resources Management in Uganda performed a water availability study of 
what they determine as the Western catchment management zone, including most areas of the Semliki 
river catchment (Zaake et al., 2011) . The study included a hydrological model of the area constructed 
in Mike Basin. An overall water balance of the management zone was conducted, including checking 
of data, preparation of water balances and estimation of flow statistics. 
Publications by Onema and Taigbenu (2009) and Onema, Taigbenu and Ndiritu (2012) presented a 
study of the Semliki river with a catchment boundary between Lake Edward and Lake Albert. A 
relationship between the normalised difference vegetation index (NDVI) and rainfall was analysed 
together with flow predictions of the river. They constructed a model of 21 sub-catchments based on 
physiographic variables, which were further divided into two groups of high and low elevations for 
the flow predictions. Most of the landscape attributes and rainfall products were acquired and 
generated from remote sensing data.  Long-term mean monthly discharge was predicted using linear 
and non-linear regression models, and a problem statement based on flow duration curves (FDCs). 
They concluded that the best predictions were obtained with the non-linear models. For the NDVI-
rainfall relationship, weak significant correlations were obtained for 12 of the sub-catchments using 
a 1-month time steps. However, by introducing a 1-month lag the results showed a high and 
significant correlation between NDVI and rainfall for 17 out of the 21 sub-catchments for the 1-month 
time step. When using an annual time step no significant relationship between NDVI and rainfall 
were found.

 Hydrological Model Framework 
Hydrological models are extremely important and are used by scientists and stakeholders to develop 
strategies for water resource management and planning. Hydrological modelling is the art of 
obtaining an understanding of the catchment behaviour. It is a mathematical approach to model and 
understand the hydrological reality as much as possible. The construction of a model structure is a 
fine balance of making it as simple as possible but as complex as necessary. Hydrological modelling 
is used to help understand and quantify the relationship between the catchment inputs, states and 
outputs at a spatial and temporal scale.  The basic assumptions behind hydrological modelling are as 
follows, where the modelling process of the system response Q is a result of a hydrological model m 
with inputs X, model parameters ϱ, as well as initial and boundary conditions S0 (Hrachowitz, 2017).

There are two main types of hydrological models, which are either distributed, physically based 
models or lumped conceptual models. Physical models are based on the Naiver Stokes Equation and 
include a full description of forces acting on a grid cell. These models often require high inputs of 
data and knowledge of the catchment specific processes. Lumped-Conceptual models are build up as 
aggregated representations of real system with different storage units. When choosing a model 
framework, the complexity of the model and the number of parameters should be supported by the 
data accuracy and availability (Zhang et al., 2008). Thus, for this study a conceptual lumped rainfall-
runoff model was selected and coupled with linear reservoir model and a one-dimensional routing 
model due to the limited availability of detailed ground data, both in time and space. The main model

3.1.2 Nash Cascade  
The Nash Cascade is implemented in the rainfall-runoff model to enable the model to capture the 
timing and peak of the observed discharge. The characteristics of an instantaneous unit hydrograph 
(IUH) is the timing of the peak and the peak value (Krajewski, 2014). The concept of Nash is used to 
transform the effective rainfall into a direct runoff hydrograph. The Nash Cascade is performed by 
routing an instantaneous unit inflow through a cascade of N linear reservoirs in series with a retention 
parameter k for each one. Using two model parameters this method is used to obtain the correlation 
between unit hydrographs and the characteristics of the catchment (Nash, 1957).  
The Nash Cascade is added to the model to handle tributary processes in the sub-catchment and which 
the direct runoff to the river to better simulate the observed discharge.

3.1.6 DEM Hydro-Processing 
The main catchment watershed delineation was drawn using DEM products to extract different 
geographical parameters of the drainage catchment. To obtain a correct drainage network a sink fill

defined with the upstream flow contributing areas.

runoff model and the Qout is add directly as drainage to the lakes without running through the routing 
model as no rivers are included for these areas.  
 
 Model Calibration 
The aim of a model calibration is to find a set of model 
parameter, that together giver the best possible match 
between the simulated and observed discharges.  Model 
calibration is a practice of parameter tuning and 
evaluation. The procedure of model calibration and 
evaluation is described in Figure 10. Once the model is 
calibrated and the simulations fit the observed data to a 
satisfactory degree, then the model can be tested on a 
validation period. 
The case specific model is made up of eight model 
parameters to be calibrated. All parameters are used to 
reflect the catchment characteristics and replicate how 
forcing inputs are translated into discharge. All 
parameters must be measured or calibrated and as many 
of these parameters are difficult to estimate without 
further fieldwork and measurements, all parameters are 
calibrated for this case study.  
For calibration, the catchment is divided into different zones. Within each zone all sub-catchments 
are given the same set of parameters. The calibration zones are a trade-off between complexity and 
simplicity and are mainly established based on acquired knowledge of small scale catchment 
properties, computer computation times, and the possibilities for calibration and validation, which 
limited by the availability of ground observations.  
For this study four calibration zone are defined. The calibration zone shown in Figure 11 are divided 
geographically in relation to the three lakes and the main Semliki River. Ideally, more ground 
measurement, especially for the Semliki river could have resulted in an additional breakdown of 
calibration zones.

The modelling period of this study runs from 1999 to 2017. The first year is used as a warmup period 
for the model hence, model calibration is first started from year 2000. For calibration the model is 
wrapped into a “black box model” consisting of an initialisation of the model with forcing and 
evaluation data followed by a calibration class that runs the model and adapts the output to be 
compared with evaluation data.    
The calibration scheme used for the study is an automatic calibration achieved using the python 
package SPOTPY. SPOTPY is a python tool that facilitates computational optimisation techniques 
that can be used for calibration, uncertainty and sensitivity analysis (Houska, 2015). The tool is simple 
and flexible and allows the user to test different algorithms without the need of complex coding.  
SPOTPY contains multiple optimisation algorithms. For this study the evolutionary algorithm       
SCE-UA (the Shuffled Complex Evolution method developed at the university of Arizona) was used. 
The SCE-UA is within the hydrologic community considered to be an effective and efficient 
optimisation technique which is specially designed to deal with complications encountered in 
conceptual watershed model calibration (Duan, Sorooshian and Gupta, 1994) .The algorithm samples 
a number of random points within a given parameter set. Each parameter sets performance is then 
assessed and ranked. Hereafter, the parameter sets are partitioned into a chosen number of complexes 
which are shuffled and evolves towards a global optimum through information sharing between the

3.2.1 Objective Function 
Objective functions aggregate the model errors over time between observed and simulated data. There 
are different objective functions that are used in hydrological modelling depending on the modelled 
variable e.g. groundwater and streamflow. Furthermore, different objective functions put more weight 
on fitting certain parts of the hydrograph, e.g. high flows or low flows. Different objective functions 
are also linked to the method of calibration. For this study the model is not calibrated on the basis of 
fitting a hydrograph with daily streamflow. Instead, the model is calibrated against long-term and 
seasonal averages of flow patterns using the two methods of climatology benchmark and flow 
duration curve (FDC) and their associated objective functions.  
Climatology Benchmark 
The climatology benchmark is a long-term average of daily flows. The benchmark provides 
information about the seasonality and helps to increase the stability of the simulated discharge. It 
basically assumes that averaged over a long time period, seasonal flow patterns will be similar. For 
the purpose of comparing simulated climatology to the benchmark of observed data a weighted root 
mean square error (RMSE) is applied as an objective function.

 Software Modelling Environments  
Throughout this study different model environments and programs were used to obtain the final 
model results. The watershed delineation was constructed in the geographical information system 
(GIS) program called Quantum GIS (QGIS). QGIS is an open-source cross-platform program where 
spatial information can be processed.  
Part of the GW-A initiative has been to develop open-source software that can be used by partners to 
support training of African water authorities. The GW-A toolbox is designed as a resource of 
integrating EO processing and GIS analysis to produce products like wetland information and 
mapping etc. (DHI GRAS, 2018).  The GW-A toolbox was used for this study and downloaded as a 
plugin for QGIS. The toolbox includes several aspects that are useful for hydrological modelling. A 
model development workflow (MDWF) was used to generate model and climate files, later exported 
and read into python. The GW-A toolbox also uses the hydrological modelling tool Terrain Analysis 
Using Digital Elevation Models (TauDEM). The TauDEM tool is used for extraction and analysis of 
hydrological information acquired from the topography using the DEM (Tarboton, 2006). 
The main model consists of scripts written in the open-source programming language Python. For 
this study Python 2.7 was used and the model was edited and run through the Spyder environment. 
The model scripts used for this study was originally written in MATLAB by Pernille Engelbredt 
Krogh in 2010 on the basis on Zhang et al.’s (2008) study and method. Further adaptions to the model 
were added by Christian Milzow in 2010. In 2016 all scripts were adapted and written into Python by 
Cécile Kittel (Kittel, 2016). For this study, specifications and adjustments to the scrips were made to 
include the conditions and hydrological processes specific to the Semliki River catchment, such as 
snow melt and lake storages. Already model incorporated snowmelt process were slightly modified, 
whilst modules of lake storage and lake behaviours were scripted specifically for this study.  
To help keeping track of changes in the model files the version control system Git was used. The 
main repository is available on GitHub and managed by Cécile M. M. Kittel. The model was then 
adjusted and managed locally on the authors computer using the GitBash interface to branch, merge 
and pull files from/with the original master model.

4 Data 
Missing and non-homogeneous climatological data are a global concern for hydrology-related studies 
(Woldesenbet et al., 2017). However, the global monitoring of the hydrological cycle has strongly 
improved with the vast amount of data that is made available from satellite-based remote sensing 
(Michailovsky, Milzow and Bauer-Gottwein, 2013). This section describes the climate forcing used 
to drive the hydrological model of this project. Data quality, variability and uncertainties will be 
presented. As for the EO data, the specific data acquired, space missions and data post-processing are 
described. Furthermore, locally obtained data are presented including ground measurements of 
discharge, precipitation and lake water levels, which have generously been provided by DWRM in 
Uganda.  
 Remote Sensing 
In ungauged areas the lack of ground measures like discharges, precipitation and evapotranspiration 
is a great limitation to obtaining a successful hydrological model. Lack of ground data has pushed 
more models to be forced with remote sensing data (Xu, Li and Tolson, 2014). EO are not only a 
necessity, as the only data source but it also brings along advantages compared to prior models solely 
based on measured time series. While data from gauging stations are often more precise then satellite-
based estimates, they however, only measure values and magnitude at the specific location they are 
installed. In contrast, remote sensing data can provide continuously spatial distributed data.   
4.1.1 Digital Elevation Model 
The digital elevation map gives the contours of the catchment and is used to make the catchment 
delineation and flow patterns. The DEM used for this project is the Shuttle Radar Topography 
Mission (SRTM) where four tiles of 5° were downloaded and merged to obtain a DEM raster covering 
the catchment. The SRTM DEM comes at a resolution of 90 m.

Previous studies using the similar model methodology for hydrological modelling of the river

products gave the best model results including better estimations of discharge magnitudes.  
Based on the results of previous studies TRMM was chosen as the satellite-based precipitation 
product for this study. The TRMM mission was launched in November 1997 and is a joined project 
between NASA and the Japanese space agency, JAXA (NASA, 2007). The mission measures 
precipitation of a spatial resolution of 0.25° x 0.25° within the latitude coverage of 60°N-S (since 
2000 before 50° N-S). The satellite provides precipitation observations using several space-borne 
instruments including, the first precipitation radar in space, a TRMM microwave imager and a visible 
infrared scanner (NASA, 2007). The combination of these instruments helps to understand 
precipitation processes, structure and climatology. When TRMM was launched it was intended as 
experimental mission focusing on tropical rainfall climatology. However, over it’s lifespan it has 
evolved into one of the primary satellites for analysing precipitation characteristics on a time scale of 
3-hr and beyond (NASA, 2017).   
The precipitation product used for this study is the TRMM-3B42, a daily raster product with 
accumulated rainfall in mm/day based on three-hourly measurements. The mission was turned of and 
terminated in spring 2015 however, the TRMM-3B42 will continue to be produced through early 
2018 based on products of the Global Precipitation Measurement mission (GPM) launched in 2013 
(Huffman, 2015).  
4.1.3 Temperature 
Estimations of evapotranspiration are calculated based on inputs of temperature data provided by the 
European Centre for Medium Range Weather Forecast (ECMRWF). Daily values of maximum and 
minimum temperatures were downloaded from the ERA-Interim reanalysis product. The product’s 
dataset archived track back to 1979 and presently data is released for download with a delay of 1-2 
months (Berrisford et al., 2009).  
4.1.4 Altimetry Lake Water Levels 
Remotely sensed time series of lake water levels in the catchment were obtained from radar altimetry. 
Radar altimetry from space consists of a vertical range measurement between the given satellite and 
a reflecting water body. The distance is calculated from the travel time of an emitted pulse to return 
to the satellite antenna. 
Two remote sensing altimetry products were used and compared. One time series of altimetry 
products were produced by Hydroweb and distributed by Theia, with support from CNES and Legos 
(Rosmorduc, 2016). The Hydroweb project database holds water levels from around 100 lakes around 
the world. The time series are based on merged altimetry data from the missions: ERS-1 &2, Envisat, 
Jason-1 and GFO, with a revisit time of 10 to 35 days (Rosmorduc, 2016).  
The Second altimetry product is from AltWater (http://altwater.dtu.space) using altimetry data from 
the CryoSat-2 mission launched in 2010 (Nielsen et al., 2018).

5 Results and Discussion 
This chapter presents the results obtained in the process of preparing a hydrological model of the 
Semliki River Catchment, including model calibration and validation. Firstly, the catchment 
delineation and setup are presented. Secondly, remotely sensed TRMM precipitation is compared to 
ground measures, before presenting the main model results from the lumped conceptual model. Here,

presenting a regionalised strategy for the model parameters. Further, the lake water balance for Lake

the catchment, in order to assess how well the TRMM rainfall data corresponds with data from rainfall 
stations on the ground.  
Overlapping data periods were identified and compared. The accumulated rainfall was calculated for 
the three rainfall stations, and the TRMM cells they are located within. These are then plotted against 
each other to create double-mass curves. The results for each of the three stations are shown in Figure 
18. 
For all three stations Figure 18 shows that the TRMM observations from the cells where the rainfall 
stations are located gives less precipitation than the measured precipitation from the rainfall stations. 
The slopes of the curves are 0.93, 0.83 and 0.88 respectively for Masindi, Kijura and Katiba. Thus, 
the rainfall from the TRMM data from the cells are about 7-12% lower than the rainfall recorded at 
the rainfall stations. This is a relatively small difference and does not necessarily indicate that the 
TRMM data underestimates the actual rainfall. Instead there can be good reasons for this difference.  
It should be noted that while the TRMM data represents an average rainfall in the cell, taking 
variations within the cells into considerations, then the rainfall at the rainfall stations is instead 
measured at a specific point. One very likely reason that the rainfall measured at the stations are 
slightly higher than the TRMM data is that all three stations seems to be located in the wetter part of 
their respective TRMM cells (c.f. Figure 17). This is most obvious for Kijura, which also is the station 
for which TRMM is lowest compared to that measured at the rainfall station. Thus, based on Figure 
18 it cannot directly be concluded that the TRMM data should be over- or underestimating the actual 
rainfall. Instead it provide some confidence that the TRMM data used are close to the actual rainfall. 
Figure 18 also indicate three double-mass curves, which are quite different for the three stations.   
For Masindi, which has the longest and most complete record of data (Jan 99 to April 2010) there is 
an almost 1:1 correspondence for the first part of the observation period, while it only start to deviate 
during the last years. For Kijura, the TRMM data gives a consistently lower rainfall as compared to 
what is measured at the rainfall station.  
For Katiba, the total cumulative rainfall from TRMM is also lower than the observed. However, if 
the first for period is excluded, the rainfall from TRMM data and ground data are almost similar (cf. 
the parallel curves). The measured data for the first period where the graph looks different might be 
due the measurement error or simply just some extreme events at the location of the rainfall station. 
To obtain a deeper understanding of the difference between the ground observations and TRMM data, 
monthly statistics were calculated. Masindi is the only station assessed to have a long enough time 
series of data measures, to perform representative monthly statistics. The results are calculated based 
on 9 to 12 monthly sums. The found monthly averages are plotted in Figure 19 together with the 
standard deviated.

5.3.3 Climatology Benchmark 
A part from FDC, a climatology benchmark was also used to optimize the calibration of the model

catchment’s dual-cycle rainfall regime, with rainy seasons from March to May and again from August

problems with matching the timing of the rainy season. Mpanga simulates the peak of the first rainy

has a delay on the simulated rains for the second half of the year.

 Regionalisation of Model Parameters 
After finishing the initial calibrations of the separate calibrations zones and the evaluation of model 
performance, one main realisation was drawn. The parameter-sets from one station in a calibration 
zone does not necessarily give a nice fit when directly transfer to other sub-catchments within the 
same defined calibration zone. As discussed in the previous section this may, among other things, be 
due to variations in geophysical properties and precipitation regimes between the catchments. 
In this study the Semliki catchment was only sub-divided into four established calibration zones due 
to lack of observed data for calibration and validation. When looking at the overall catchment 
variations of annual precipitation and the heterogeneous geophysical properties, it is a very rough 
assumption that sub-catchment within the same calibrations zones are likely to have matching 
parameter-sets. The validation results for the calibrated zones conclude that this rough assumption 
does not hold. If more stations with observed discharge had been available to calibrate against, the 
catchment could have been divided in to a larger number of smaller and more homogeneous 
calibration zones. This was, however, not possible for the given data period. 
This realisation lead to a regionalised strategy, moving away from the initial method of assigning the 
same model parameter-set to all sub-catchments within the established calibration zones.  
As the limited available data did not allow for a better justification of dividing the catchment into 
suitable zone with common properties, an approach of averaging was instead used. Recognizing the 
limitations and lack of better approaches/methods within the scope of this study, an averaged 
parameters-set was created based on the three sets found for three of the calibration zones. This 
parameter-set given in Table 6 was then assigned all the sub-catchments. Surely, the already 
calibrated sub-catchments and their upstream sub-catchments were given the same parameters sets as 
the result of the best fit. A complete table with all the final assigned parameter-values for each sub- 
is found in Table 16 in Appendix H

5.5.3 Estimation of Rainfall (P) over Lake Albert 
The estimated rainfall over Lake Albert is calculated the same ways as for all the catchments. It is 
obtained from gridded climate data from TRMM and read using zonal statistics to calculate the mean 
precipitation over the lake in mm/day. This is then spread over the area of the lake to obtain and 
calculated as m3/s. The mean precipitation for Lake Albert was found to be 165.9 m3/s. 
5.5.4 Estimation of Open Water Evaporation (Eo) from Lake Albert 
The estimated open water evaporation for Lake Albert is calculated using the Hargraves method 
assuming that the actual evapotranspiration is equal to the potential evapotranspiration. Further an 
empirical factor of 1.1 is multiplied. At the end of this study it was discovered that the developed 
coding to estimate the open water evaporation was subject to an unit- or other error somewhere that 
was unfortunately not easily found and fixed for before the end of this study. The problem was found 
as modelled evaporation values resulted in a mean of 13.5 m3/s, corresponding to about 0.22 mm/d, 
which is way too little. To still be able to give an estimate of the water balance for Lake Albert mean 
estimated monthly values of open water evaporation was used. This data was found in the National 
Water Resource Assessment for Uganda (MWE, 2013), which probably provide the best available 
estimates.

5.5.6 Model Performance and Uncertainty for Lake Albert Water Balance 
With all the calculated or obtained inflows and outflows, the simulated change in storage can be 
compared to the observed water heights for Lake Albert to assess how well the model performs in 
capturing the actual changes observed for Lake Albert over for the time period 2000 to 2017. The 
model simulates the lake water balance as volumes, whereas observed data are available as lake water 
levels. Based on the available lookup tables of Height-Area-Volume curve for Lake Albert a function 
was estimated to convert the simulated volumes to simulated changes in water level to be compare 
that with the observed water levels.  
The relationship between water levels and volumes in Lake Albert and the function to convert 
between the two is found in Figure 45 in Appendix I. The corresponding observed and simulated 
water levels for Lake Albert are shown in Figure 31. It shows that the simulated water level slowly 
increases in the first half of the period and decreases again during the second half of the period and 
eventually ends again at a height close to the initial start value. The observed heights instead follow 
a steadier water level with decreases for the years 2007 to 2012. However, the simulated water levels 
capture some of the inter-annual fluctuation similar to the observed.

For this study a snow module was included in the runoff model to simulate snow storage and 
snowmelt processes. The four snow parameter described in section 3.1.5 where fixed based on 
literature findings. DEM products were used to produce elevation bands for three sub-catchments 
covering the high altitudes of the Rwenzories (c.f. Table 12, Table 13 and Table 14 in Appendix C).

as for other catchments around the world due to small differences in temperature throughout the year.

 Model Application and Further Studies 
The model constructed in this study gives an overall insight on the hydrological cycle and the climate 
variability in the Semliki River catchment. The model gives rough estimate of discharges in the 
catchment, not giving any specific importance to matching peak flows or baseflows. Depending on 
future usage of the model, adjustments can be made to match the questions asked in relation to water 
management. If floods, hence peak flows are of concern then objectives functions emphasising this 
matter, should be given more weighing in the objectives functions. 
The model does not have enough detail to be used for water management within smaller regions of 
the catchment. Particularly, not before the performance of using the method of regionalisation is 
further assed through validations with new discharge measures around the catchment. The estimated 
discharge for specific sub-catchments are therefore highly uncertain. However, discharge simulations 
for the calibrated sub-catchments seems promising and could potentially be used by local stakeholder 
in that area.  
There are many uncertainties related to the model, many which are also linked to the availability of 
observed data and missing years of discharge inputs for the period. It is expected that a few more 
gauging stations would highly increase the quality of the model, allowing for a better division of 
calibration zones and possibilities of validating the model results. If a more detailed model structure 
is constructed with new information, the method of using remote sensing products to force the model 
is evaluated to have good potentials.  
The model can be used to run simulations of future climate predictions to get a rough idea of how this 
may change the hydrological variability and patterns in the catchment.  
For future work and optimisation of the model, the following first steps of adjustments and 
exploitations are suggested:  
➢ Finding and fixing the error still present for the python script to calculate correct estimates of 
open water evaporation of the lakes. 
 
➢ No water balance was presented for Lake Edward due to the uncertainties of lake parameters 
for lake George which could not be calculated due to lack of measurements. However, it could 
be interesting to try and calibrate the two parameter sets for Lake Edward and George based 
on discharge measures at the Semliki station. Yet, this would still be liked to high uncertainties 
due to the average parameter-set assigned for the catchment were the Semliki station is 
located. 
 
➢ For this study TRMM products were used as precipitation input to the model. For further 
investigation it could be interesting to assess the performance of using data from the Global 
Precipitation Measurement mission (GPM) which has a better spatial resolution. 
 
For this project remote sensing products of precipitation, temperature and altimetry was used. 
However, other catchment characteristics have great potential to further be included in the model 
using remote sensing. Other studies e.g. included the total water storage from GRACE to help 
calibrate the model (Kittel et al., 2018). GRACE is a gravity mission that measures spatial-temporal 
==PAGE:69==
Results and Discussion 
59 
 
variations of land water storages. However, due to it’s coarse resolution of above 450 km the 
catchment size of this study was evaluated to small, and GRACE product were therefor not included 
in this study due to resolution limitations. Listed below are suggestions and further possibilities of 
using remote sensing products to further optimise this model in further studies along with suggestions 
on remote sensing applicability for hydrological modelling at other study sites:  
➢ In this study available HVA- curves were used for the water balance of the lakes. However, 
for other catchments, lacking such information, the combination of altimetry measures and 
SAR images of lake extents could be used to develop a height-area curve for lakes.  
 
➢ Different mission of microwave soil moisture monitoring has been launched. These could 
potentially be included in the calibration of model parameters. Further, a study by  Li et al., 
(2016) highlights the potential of using soil moisture products for flood forecasting, as soil 
moisture is an important initial condition for rainfall flood forecasting.  
 
➢ It was not possible to validate the use of the snow module in this study due to limitations of 
data. However, if better date were available remote sensing could potentially be used to 
calibrate snow parameters e.g. using SAR images to map changes in snow cover.  
 
➢ Lastly remote sensing could be used to obtain better information used for a future optimised 
regionalisation strategy, using NDVI remote sensing or backscatter values from SAR 
Polarimetry to produce a map of land use classification (Freeman, Member and Durden, 1998 
and Onema and Taigbenu, 2009).

6 Conclusion 
This study demonstrates the use of remote sensing data as forcing inputs to a hydrological model of 
the sparsely gauged Semliki River catchment. 
A digital elevation map was successfully used to develop a watershed delineation of the catchment, 
and map out the main river network for this study. A lumped conceptual hydrological was based on 
the rainfall-runoff method by Budyko and a Muskingum river routing framework. The model was 
calibrated against available discharge measurements, using a method of climatology and FDC. This 
method captured annual climate variations well, and simulated discharge results nicely captured the 
low flows and the magnitudes of peaks to a satisfactory degree.  
Large heterogeneities in geophysical catchment properties and limited available data made it hard to 
perform satisfactory results for the split station validation of the model parameters. Consequently, a 
simple regionalisation strategy was decided upon, for assigning model parameter for the sub-
catchments with no measures available for calibration.  
For this study a simulated mean yearly water balance was presented for the biggest lake in the 
catchment (Lake Albert). It was highlighted that differences between simulated and observed water 
levels, are highly linked to the data gaps and general quality of available measures, along with 
uncertainties of estimated outflows from the lake. Altimetry water levels from two different remote 
sensing products were further compared with ground observations showing very promising results in 
capturing the fluctuations in lake storage.  
Results of comparing the TRMM precipitation product with ground observations showed a general 
good agreement, and although the TRMM data showed fewer days with no rainfall, the frequency 
distribution of different daily rainfall events were found similar.  
Model limitations were highly linked to the lack of a reliable network of ground stations both in 
availability and quality.  Therefore, the use of remote sensing data was found very applicable as 
forcing input to the hydrological model, as resent ground measures of precipitation and 
evapotranspiration are hardly existing. However, as remote sensing can limit the need for ground 
data, it is concluded that a limited number of reliable ground stations is necessary to setup a good 
model structure and calibrate model parameters.
