Abstract
As the demand for renewable energy sources continues to grow, offshore wind energy
plays an evermore important role. The design of substructures and foundations of off-
shore wind turbines are indeed governed by wave loads. Extensive research has been
carried out within the ﬁeld of wave loads on monopiles, however, the local forces of the
wave loads have not been addressed to the same extent. This project aims to determine
the local wave forces along a monopile and analyse the relation between wave kinematics
and wave loads. It is achieved through two types of experiment, where one experiment
measures the forces and another experiment determines the corresponding wave kinemat-
ics.
A small scale model of a monopile is located on a sloping bottom in a wave ﬂume. The
monopile comprise of 7 cylindrical sections connected by 6 force transducers, enabling
force measurements on each of the affected sections. Various tests with regular waves
have been conducted and it is found that the maximum local force on the top sections are
encountered later than the maximum total force on the monopile. Also, the total force
is in general overestimated when adopting wave kinematics from Wheeler stretching and
stream function theory.
Additionally, particle image velocimetry (PIV) experiments are carried out to deter-
mine the wave kinematics for the undisturbed wave. Comparing obtained acceleration
proﬁles have revealed that acceleration proﬁles found from PIV resembles the shape of
stream function acceleration proﬁle, although they are typically smaller. Furthermore,
applying PIV kinematics underestimates the local forces on the two bottom sections of
the monopile model to a greater extent than those determined from Wheeler stretching
and stream function kinematics.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The world population has increased rapidly over the last decades and will continue to do
so for many years to come, which inevitably increases the demand for multiple resources,
including energy resources. As fossil fuels are limited and polluting and causes the global
temperature to rise, renewable energy sources play an ever more important role in the sup-
ply of energy. One of the fastest growing renewable energy sources in the world is wind
power generated from wind turbines.
Denmark is a pioneer country within the ﬁeld of wind power, and in 2012 it was po-
litically determined that wind power by 2020 should cover 50% of the Danish electricity
consumption. Offshore wind turbines generate signiﬁcantly more power than onshore
wind turbines, due to the larger dimensions and more favourable wind conditions. An
offshore wind farm does not have to meet the same requirements with regard to neigh-
bours, sound discomfort etc. as onshore wind farms do, and the wind speed is faster and
more steady offshore, yielding higher energy production.
Offshore wind turbines need to be extremely robust structures owing to severe wind
and wave climates and high costs. High safety factors are applied in the design process
since offshore wind turbines indeed are classiﬁed as important and sensitive structures
where maintenance is not as straightforward as for land based structures. Obviously,
these standards for the offshore wind farms lead to high costs, which is said to be the
main disadvantage regarding offshore wind turbines. The demand for cost reductions re-
lated to offshore wind farms is necessary in order to further expand.
The work presented in this thesis contributes to the Danish DeRisk project. It is a joint
research project involving 9 partners across different companies and universities. The
DeRisk project aims to bring down the costs related to offshore wind energy by providing
new design methods with reduced risk and uncertainty in predicting extreme wave loads.
For more information on the DeRisk project, please see (The DeRisk Project, 2015).

1.1
Project Scope
Wave loads are one of the design-giving criteria in regard to offshore wind turbine sub-
structures. For many years, the wave loads exerted on a monopile foundation have been
well estimated since a vast number of experiments have been conducted within that ﬁeld.
However, it is still sought to extend the knowledge on the relation between wave kinemat-
ics and wave loads on a monopile in order to reduce the uncertainty and provide better
estimates of the local forces. An example of a monopile foundation is shown on Figure
1.1 where also the transition piece of the wind turbine is attached.
Figure 1.1: Monopile foundation and transition piece. Figure from (Grab CAD, 2013).
This project aims to analyse the relation between wave kinematics and wave loads on a
monopile and determine local wave loads vertically. It is achieved by conducting two
types of experiments with different set-ups, in which one experiment studies local forces
on a monopile and the other experiment investigates wave kinematics.
First experiment to be conducted is force measurements performed on a small scale
monopile model. The monopile model is located in a wave ﬂume equipped with a sloping
bottom to provide the shoaling effect. It is segmented and consists of 7 sections kept in
place by 6 strain gauge load cells, thus enabling measurements of the total force on each
section. The exerted wave loads will be determined on each of the affected sections for 20
different wave conditions. Based on this vast amount of force data, the sectional variation
of the force is investigated. It will be assessed how well the obtained forces compare to
forces predicted by kinematics from stream function wave theory and Wheeler stretching.

Second experiment involves particle image velocimetry (PIV) which is performed

Related Works
Wave loads on marine structures have been studied for decades, especially the impact
loads from breaking waves on cylindrical marine structures. There are many different
approaches when dealing with this topic. Chella, Tørum, and Myrhaug (2012) performed
an extensive literature review on the topic of breaking waves and the associated effects on
offshore wind turbine substructures
A study with similar experimental facilities to the force measurements in this project
was carried out by Xu et al. (2013). They had a wave ﬂume with a sloping bottom and a
segmented monopile where the total force was measured on each segment. Here it was
found that the vertical force distribution appears parabolic along the loaded length. The
force was measured with pressure sensors and not load cells as in this thesis. Roos, Swan,
and Haver (2010) investigated the wave impact on cylindrical columns of a gravity-based
structure, where force sensors was placed vertically from SWL and to the top. A vertical
force distribution was not given, but it was found that the largest loads are often not re-
lated to the steepest or tallest waves.
The relation between wave kinematics obtained through PIV and wave loads was re-
cently investigated by Scharnke, Lindeboom, and Duz (2017). The study analysed how
the wave-in-deck impact load was related to breaking wave kinematics determined from
PIV experiments by conducting two individual types of experiment. One experiment de-
termined the wave-in-deck impact load while another experiment investigated the PIV
kinematics. Reasonable load predictions was obtained.

Linear wave theory is the simplest way to describe a wave, and assumes a potential ﬂow
and a sinusoidal surface elevation. In practical engineering it is of great signiﬁcance
to be capable of estimating the wave kinematics in the entire water column including
the kinematics above still water level (SWL). Two common theories to obtain this, is
the stream function wave theory and linear wave theory applying the Wheeler stretching
method.

Flow Regimes
The force coefﬁcients CD and CM depend on the ﬂow regime. Flow regimes around
a cylinder are governed by two important parameters, namely the Keulegan-Carpenter
number KC and the Reynolds number Re.
The KC number is given by
KC = UmT
D
(2.19)

eter of the cylinder. When KC is very small, the ﬂow may not even separate behind the
cylinder, while separation is likely to occur for large KC numbers, potentially leading to
vortex shedding. Generally, vortex shedding occurs for KC ≥7. Another rule of thumb
is that 0 < KC ≪20 −30 is the inertia-dominated regime, while KC > 20 −30 is the
drag-dominated regime (Sumer and Fredsøe, 2006).

Load Cells
The applied load cells in this experiment are strain gauge load cells with a Wheatstone
bridge conﬁguration. When a load cell is subjected to a given force, the strain gauges
in the Wheatstone bridge either stretches or contracts. If the load cell is connected to an
electrical system, this stretching or contraction will give rise to a change in the electrical
resistance of the strain gauge, thus enabling measurements. A photograph of the applied
load cell is shown on Figure 3.9.

Validation of the Monopile Model
When the monopile model is assembled, the model needs to be validated. The structure of
the monopile makes it exposed to multiple sources of error, so even though the load cells
are calibrated and capable of measuring given forces, the assembling of the monopile
can potentially lead to contact between the components. The monopile is not completely
rigid, and small ﬂexibilities in the model may cause the different components to interact
and consequently transfer loading between one another. Especially contact between the
PVC blocks is of crucial importance to prevent.
In order to investigate if there is an internal load transferring taking place between the
components besides the load cells, the model is initially validated outside the wave ﬂume.
This is only carried out once and will not be repeated prior to the experiments. Further-
more, a model validation outside the wave ﬂume is essential as it is not possible to load
the bottom section of the monopile when it is placed inside the wave ﬂume simply be-
cause it is too close to the bottom.
The monopile is placed horizontally on channel struts between two tables and fastened
with clamps as shown on Figure 3.11.
Figure 3.11: Validation of the monopile outside the wave ﬂume.

model validation it is sought to resemble the wave loads affecting the monopile. Each
section is therefore loaded by tying ﬁve weights around the monopile, suspending them

experiments. A force measurement is once again conducted. Next, measurements are

Experimental Procedure
20 experiments have been carried out with regular waves by following an identical ap-
proach in terms of test execution and data processing.
Data acquisition in an experiment involves data from 6 strain gauge load cells and 6
wave gauges which are simultaneously logged at a sample rate of 1000 Hz. Previous tests
have shown that sampling at 1000 Hz is sufﬁcient to obtain the correct surface elevation.
The calibration curves from the load cells in Appendix A are applied to convert the mea-
sured voltage from the strain gauge into shear forces. By means of (2.30), the load on
each section is determined from the shear forces in the load cells.
Reference measurements are performed prior to an experiment and after an experi-
ment to serve two purposes. They are compared to each other to see if any displacements
have taken place in the monopile, and they are subtracted from the logged signal during
the experiment, as they correspond to no applied force.
After an experiment, disturbances will be present in the wave ﬂume due to the pre-

CHAPTER 4
Particle Image Velocimetry
Experiments
This chapter contains a description of the experiments concerning particle image ve-
locimetry (PIV) which are the second of two types of experiments conducted in this
project. An outline of the experimental set-up is given in the ﬁrst section. Next, the
experimental procedure is given. The section is divided into two subsections where one
subsection considers the procedure during the experiment and presents the given test con-
ditions. The other subsection focuses on the procedure after the experiment, namely how
the data is processed. Concluding remarks complete the chapter.
PIV is a whole-ﬂow-ﬁeld technique to obtain the instantaneous velocity ﬁeld in a cross-
section of a ﬂow. Seeded particles are added to a ﬂuid with properties enabling them
to follow the ﬂow completely. A laser sheet originating from a double pulsed laser illu-
minates the particles in the seeded ﬂow. The motion of the particles are captured by a
camera taking two pictures of the measurement area in a rapid succession, thus making
it possible to obtain a displacement ﬁeld. As the time increment between the images are
known, the velocity ﬁeld is established by dividing the displacement ﬁeld with the time
increment.

the case for stream function waves. It is thus not straightforward to present the exact same
phases for the wave obtained from stream function as the measured wave, especially as
the waves get longer and higher. The offset between the forces found from stream func-
tion and Wheeler stretching is therefore potentially a result of a phase difference rather
than having different force magnitudes.

6.3
Breaking Waves
The topics related to kinematics of breaking waves are studied extensively as it governs
the design of various marine structures, including monopiles. One of the objectives of this
project is to pave the way for the investigation of the loads exerted from breaking waves
on a monopile, and a natural extension of the research so far is therefore to proceed with
breaking waves.
A force measurement has been conducted with wave conditions enabling the wave to
break at the location of the monopile. The test was performed to study how the experi-
mental set-up would respond to these circumstances.
Figure 6.8 shows the surface elevation of the breaking wave and the corresponding
force exerted at the monopile sections. The blue line indicates the surface elevation and
the black lines are forces, where the indices on the y-axis refer to the different monopile
section. All sections are affected by the wave load due to the large wave height. Section 4
is observed to undergo a somewhat slamming load from the wave, resembling an impulse
load.
In the force measurements presented in section 5.1, the noise from the forces and the
surface elevation was removed by cutting off the highest frequencies. However, in this
test analyses of data have revealed that cutting off the highest frequencies can lead to
important data being lost due to the rapid exertion of the wave load and the response of
the monopile.
From Figure 6.8 disturbances in the forces are observed after undergoing the slam-
ming load. This indicates that the wave impact excites the natural frequency to a small
extent. The disturbances could also suggest that the structure of the monopile is simply
too ﬂexible for this type of loading.
The current experimental set-up regarding force measurements are not recommended
if breaking waves are to be investigated. It appears that the natural frequency is excited
as the wave hit the monopile. However, the kinematics of breaking waves could still be
studied with PIV.

6.4
Concluding Remarks
It has been demonstrated that the potential of the present project contains various inter-
esting aspects that is worth pursuing. Recommendations have been given for each of the
three aspects of future work.
When performing PIV on irregular waves, one particular wave in the time series ought
to be investigated by generating the same time series multiple times in order to reduce the
uncertainty. Forces obtained from CFD simulations will serve as a good tool to discuss the
experimental results, but a horizontal mesh at the cylinder is required. Breaking waves is
a natural continuation of the present work. However, it has been assessed that the current
monopile model is either too ﬂexible or the natural frequency is excited by the break-
ing wave. A new structure of the monopile therefore needs to be developed in order to
measure the impact forces from breaking waves.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A small scale monopile model has been developed to successfully measure local wave
forces from 20 regular wave tests with different wave conditions. The force measure-
ments have shown that the monopile sections at SWL and above SWL encounter their
maximum force after the bottom sections of the monopile. In the conducted tests, it is
found that the maximum total force on the monopile is in general overestimated by Mori-
son’s equation when applying wave kinematics from stream function theory and Wheeler
stretching, especially as the wave height and wave steepness increase. Furthermore, the
local wave force on section 3 of the monopile is likely to be more overestimated than the
wave forces exerted on the bottom sections.
Velocity ﬁelds have been established from PIV experiments for 6 regular wave tests
with different wave conditions, thus velocity and acceleration proﬁles are obtained. It has
been found that acceleration proﬁles from PIV either has smaller or identical values to
those predicted by stream function theory and the Wheeler stretching method. However,
despite a smaller magnitude of the accelerations, the shape of the acceleration proﬁle re-
sembles the shape of that obtained from stream function theory for all the investigated
tests.
As a consequence of the smaller accelerations, the forces obtained from PIV kinemat-
ics tend to underestimate the experimentally measured forces on the bottom sections of
the monopile.
As a ﬁnal remark, it is mentioned that a good experimental methodology has been de-
veloped to undertake tests with breaking regular waves. However, a different and more
robust set-up of the monopile model is in that case proposed.

APPENDIX D
Load Cell Error in the Experiment
This appendix contains the difference between the reference measurements in all 20 ex-
periments with regular waves.
Prior to the generation of waves a measurement is performed, hence no external force
is applied. After the generation of waves has begun and the water is settled in the wave
ﬂume, a measurement is once again performed. These measurements are termed ref-
erence measurements as they are subtracted from the obtained signals during the force
measurements. The static calibration of the monopile revealed that unintended displace-
ments were likely to occur. It is therefore investigated how the monopile responds to
dynamic loading.
Table D.1 lists the difference between the test performed before and after wave gen-
eration for all 6 load cells to all 20 tests.
It is seen that the monopile responds well to the dynamic loading, as there are no large
deviations between the two measurements. However, signiﬁcant deviations occur for load
cell 5 in some of the tests.
The data that is processed and analysed are taken from the last two minutes of the
conducted experiment. Therefore, the reference measurement carried out after the exper-
iment is applied in the computations. It is thereby assessed that the deviation on load cell
5 will not yield problems or misleading results.

APPENDIX E
Calibration of Wave Gauges
The wave gauges need to be calibrated in an interval of 0.2 m as this is the order of the
largest wave height in this project. As described in section 3.1, the sloping bottom limits
the wave gauge from being lowered sufﬁciently and it is thus limited from measuring in
the desired interval. This challenge is dealt with by initially rising the water level 0.15 m
higher than the water level applied in the experiments. Subsequently two measurements
are conducted with a vertical spacing of 0.05 m. The water level is then lowered 0.15 m
and measured with millimetre precision by a length measuring instrument. The instru-
ment is ﬁxed to Unistrut channel struts which are mounted to an aluminium proﬁle where
also the wave gauge is attached as shown on Figure E.1.

between the conductivity of the rods and the water depth can be established based on the
