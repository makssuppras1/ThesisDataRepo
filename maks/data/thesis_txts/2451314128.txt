Abstract
As eye tracker devices are becoming cheaper and simpler to use, eye
tracking is applied to diﬀerent research ﬁelds more.
The data ob-
tained from an eye tracker device can be statiscally analyzed to detect
events such as ﬁxations and saccades. Using detected events, diﬀerent
visualization techniques are established to create attention maps and
scanpaths for examining various aspects of the data.
Existing analysis algorithms are based on collected data which indi-
cates that these algorithms are run after collecting the data. This
thesis investigates diﬀerent adaption of existing algorithms for apply-
ing analysis techniques on the ﬂy based on a stream of an eye tracking
data. It also explores adaption of existing visualization techniques to
create attention maps and scanpaths on the ﬂy. End goal of thesis
is to provide an online eye tracking data analyzing and visualizing
pipeline.

Chapter 1
Introduction
History of eye tracking goes back to 1870s when scientists had to use naked eye
observations to track eye movements. Louis Emile Javal, a French ophthalmolo-
gist noticed that readers do not have ﬂuent eye movements whilst reading a text.
Instead they have quick movements (saccades) with short pauses (ﬁxations) [2].
Edmund Huey, an American psychologist also worked on Javal’s studies in
1908. Huey built the ﬁrst eye tracking device which had a lens that was attached
to a pointer. Pointer changed its position according to the movements of the eye
[3].
Guy Thomas Buswell, an American educational psychologist have used ﬁrst
uninterrupted eye tracking device and discovered that eye movements between
oral and silent reading diﬀer signiﬁcantly [4].
After 1970, due to developments in eye trackers, devices became more accu-
rate. Eye tracking devices improved to seperate eye movements from head move-
ments. As they became more accessible, eye tracking experiments began to be
applied to diﬀerent research ﬁelds more and more. For researchers from diﬀerent
ﬁelds to be able to understand eye tracking data easily, visualization represents
a key point in eye tracking process. Eye tracking data can be visualized as raw
1
==PAGE:16==
or one can detect oculomotor events from the data and visualize them. Diﬀerent
ﬁelds, applications and experiments requires diﬀerent visualization techniques.
A great amount of research indicates that eye tracking data can be used in
many diﬀerent areas. Many publishing investigated diﬀerent techniques to ana-
lyze eye tracking data, detect oculomotor events and visualize them. Therefore,
the focus of this thesis is to improve these techniques and algorithms in order to
analyze and visualize eye tracking data in real time based on a stream of data.
This thesis presents an overview of diﬀerent studies in analyzing and visualiz-
ing eye tracking data. Descriptions of diﬀerent terms that is used to explain eye
tracking data are also provided. Detailed information on state of the art of this
thesis is given in Chapter 2.
Following the state of the art chapter, a new algorithm, S-MDBSCAN is
provided in Chapter 3 in order to detect ﬁxations and saccades in real time.
Tests and results of the algorithm in comparison to other well known algorithms
are also provided.
Furthermore, Chapter 4 details techniques chosen to visualize detected events
in real time. Chapter 4 provides two visualizations on static stimulus: scanpath
and heat map. An experiment is also designed to test suggested visualization
techniques and results of the experiment with detailed information concludes
Chapter 4.
Chapter 5 describes the system architecture of the pipeline for analyzing and
visualizing eye tracking data in real time. It also includes the implementation
details of suggested algorithms and technologies used.
This thesis is concluded in Chapter 6 where the solution and its main in-
novative features are summarized. Application scenarios of the pipeline is also
mentioned in Chapter 6.
Finally, this thesis includes a discussion of potential future work in relation

Chapter 2
State of the Art
Summary
In order to get further into this thesis project, one shall investigate what has been
done so far. This chapter gives a background about analysis and visualization
of eye tracking data, it answers questions like: What is a ﬁxation? What is a
saccade? What algorithms has been developed in order to detect oculomotor
events? What properties do these algorithms carry? What type of methods can
be used to visualize analyzed eye tracking data?
2.1
Terminology
Stimulus Stimulus is deﬁned as visual content that is presented to the eye.
Stimulus can vary as static or dynamic and 2D or 3D.
Fixation A ﬁxation is an aggregation of gaze points. Gaze points are aggregated
based on a speciﬁed area and time-span. The aggregation area is usually about
20 to 50 pixels, the time-span between 100 and 300 ms [5]. Diﬀerent metrics
can be used in order to express ﬁxations such as ﬁxation position and ﬁxation
4
==PAGE:19==
2.2 Eye Tracking
duration.
Saccade Saccades describe a rapid eye movement from one ﬁxation to another.
They typically last about 30 to 80 ms and are the fastest movement the human
body can perform. During this timespan visual information is suppressed [5]. Dif-
ferent metrics can be used in order to express saccades such as saccadic duration
and saccadic velocity.
Scanpath The term scanpath is today used to very concretely describe how
the eye physically moves through space, typically but not exclusively for one
participant [5]. This means that the term scanpath can also be described as the
sequence of events (i.e. ﬁxations and saccades).
Attention map Attention maps consist of ﬁxations over time which is very often
visualized in the form of a heat map [5]. Attention maps deﬁne the areas of the
stimulus which have attracted more attention than other areas.
Areas of interest (AOI) Areas of interests (AOIs) deﬁne regions in the stimulus
that the researcher is interested in gathering data about: did the participant look
where expected and what were the properties of their eye movements in the
area looked at [5]? AOIs can be deﬁned before or after the eye tracking data is
collected.
2.2
Eye Tracking
Eye tracking devices record gaze coordinates as raw data. Even though data
extracted from an eye tracking device may vary, every eye tracking device provides
time stamp and gaze coordinates of left and right eye.
After that, ﬁxations and saccades can be detected from gaze points in order
to measure participants’ focused regions of the stimulus. Then if needed, AOIs
can be deﬁned in order to focus on speciﬁc areas of the stimulus.

IVT Velocity-threshold ﬁxation identiﬁcation (IVT) is a velocity-based method
that separates ﬁxation and saccade points based on their point-to-point velocities.
The velocity proﬁles of saccadic eye movements show essentially two distributions
of velocities: low velocities for ﬁxations (i.e., <100 deg/sec), and high velocities
(i.e., >300 deg/sec) for saccades [6].
IVT algorithm starts with determining
velocities between each point. Then according to the threshold, it classiﬁes the
point as a ﬁxation or a saccade. IVT pseudocode is shown in Table 2.2.

IHMM Hidden Markov model ﬁxation identiﬁcation (IHMM) uses probabilistic
analysis to determine the most likely identiﬁcations for a given data point. Hidden
Markov models (HMMs) are probabilistic ﬁnite state machines and IHMM uses
a two-state HMM in which the states represent the velocity distributions for
saccade and ﬁxation points [6]. IHMM calculates likelihood of each data point
with a decoding process. IHMM then uses dynamic programming to label data
points to ﬁxations or saccades in a way that maximizes the probability of a correct
assignment conforming to the HMM. IHMM pseudocode is shown in Table 2.3.

IMST MST identiﬁcation (IMST) is based on minimum spanning trees (MSTs)
that is, a tree connecting a set of points such that the total length of the trees
line segments is minimized [6]. IMST algorithm constructs a tree of the data then
it enforces certain thresholds on the data points at the edges of the tree in order
to determine saccades.
CDT The Fixation Dispersion Algorithm based on Covariance (CDT) by Veneri
et al. [7] is an improvement of the ﬁxation dispersion algorithm based on F-tests
(FDT) [8] previously developed by the same authors [9]. They have improved
their algorithm with using co-variance calculations on gaze coordinates.
EM The algorithm used by Engbert and Mergenthaler [10] is a further develop-
ment of the algorithm [11] used earlier by Engbert and Kliegl [9]. This velocity-
based algorithm uses an adaptive velocity threshold for detecting saccades.
BIT The idea to determine whether a peak in velocity is due to a real movement
or a noise by looking if both eyes have the same velocity peak at the same time
is taken advantage by the Binocular-Individual Threshold (BIT) algorithm [12].
MDBSCAN MDBSCAN introduced by Wang and Shic [13]. It is a modiﬁed
version of density based clustering algorithm DBSCAN. DBSCAN algorithm uses
the minimum distance and minimum number of points as thresholds. MDBSCAN

2.4
Visualization
Visualization of eye tracking data explains distinct attributes of ﬁxations, sac-
cades and scan paths. Various visualization techniques open up ways to analyze
diﬀerent aspects of eye tracking data.
2.4.1
Visualization Categories
Academic publications on eye tracking data visualization can be classiﬁed by
diﬀerent visualization categories. In this subsection, some of those categories are
described and leading publications are classiﬁed accordingly in Table 2.5.
Point-based and AOI-based Stimulus This category is the only stimulus
related category mentioned in Section 2.4.1. Point-based stimulus is focused on
the movement of the gaze. It targets spatial distribution of the gaze position.
When a speciﬁc analysis of the eye tracking data needed, point-based approach
is not always enough. Thus, deﬁned AOIs of the stimulus can be used.
Temporal, Spatial and Spatio-Temporal Visualizations A temporal di-
mension is a dimension of time so this visualization category shows time line in
an axis. On the other hand, a spatial dimension is a dimension of coordinates
9
==PAGE:24==
2.4 Visualization
in space.
Latter dimension, spatio-temporal dimension is the combination of
previous two.
2D and 3D Visualizations As this category self explains itself; commonly, 2D
stimuli is represented using 2D visualizations. However, one can add another
dimension such as time in order to represent 2D stimuli using 3D visualizations.
In order to prevent data loss caused by representing 3D eye tracking data using
2D visualizations, 3D visualizations are used to represent 3D eye tracking data.
In-context and Not-in-context Visualizations Stimulus and visualization
are linked with one another in in-context visualizations. This can be done by
including stimulus behind the visualization as the background. Not-in-context
visualization techniques do not include stimulus at all in the visualization.
Single User and Multiple Users Visualizations Single user visualization
represents only one users data in the visualization. On the other hand, multiple
users visualization techniques allow inspection the eye movements of multiple
participants.
This way, one can analyze group behavior.
One problem with
multiple users visualizations is visual clutter since it may be confusing to represent
too much user data. Diﬀerent optimization techniques such as bundling of lines
can be used in order to overcome this problem [14].
Static and Animated Visualizations Static visualization techniques are usu-
ally used with static stimulus. For dynamic stimulus, AOIs can be used to visu-
alize data in static visualization. Animated visualization techniques sequentially
presents data time to time. One can combine in-context visualization and ani-
mated visualization techniques by overlaying visualization over the stimulus.
Interactive and Not-interactive Visualizations Non-interactive visualiza-
tions do not allow users to interact with the visualization. On the other hand,
users can zoom in or out, ﬁlter diﬀerent parts of the data and replay over time
with interactive visualizations.

Chapter 3
Stream - Modiﬁed DBSCAN
Summary
This chapter provides modiﬁcations considered on top of existing algorithms dis-
cussed in Section 2.3 in order to analyze eye tracking data in real time and
discusses experimental results of proposed algorithm.
3.1
Algorithm
In order to analyze a stream of raw eye tracking data in real time, modiﬁcations
has been done to MDBSCAN algorithm in Section 2.3. There are several reasons
why MDBSCAN is selected; since velocity-based algorithms can be susceptible to
noise, a dispersion-based algorithm is more suitable for analyzing stream of eye
tracking data and since DBSCAN is very successful at separating clusters of high
density versus clusters of low density it can easily detect ﬁxations considering the
fact that ﬁxations are high density clusters.
Just as with the MDBSCAN algorithm, S-MDBSCAN algorithm also requires
two parameters:
12
==PAGE:27==
3.1 Algorithm
• min pts: Minimum number of points for an eye ﬁxation. In the DBSCAN
algorithm, a point is a core point if at least min pts are within distance ϵ
to that point.
• ϵ: Distance threshold that checked for deciding a point is a core point or
not.
Algorithm takes one data point at a time since it happens in real time. This
point consists of x and y coordinates and the timestamp. When a new point is
added to the analysis, there are two possibilities for the latest ocumolotor event:
a ﬁxation or a saccade. If it was a ﬁxation, then algorithm checks if the new
point ﬁts in the ﬁxation or not. This check is done by measuring the distance
between the query point and last ﬁxation’s center point. This check diﬀers from
MDBSCAN, reasoning behind this adaptation is complexity. If one to use a high
frequency eye tracking device (300Hz), a data point would be generated in every
3ms. In order not to cause any lags, instead of checking all points before the
query point, S-MDBSCAN checks the distance between the query point and the
last ﬁxation’s center point. Center point changes as points are added to it. If the
distance between the query point and last ﬁxation’s center point is less than the
distance threshold (ϵ), then query point is added to the ﬁxation. Else, ﬁxation
ends, query point is labeled as a saccade and it is added to the current window.
Use of current window is explained in the next paragraph.
If the last ocumolotor event was not a ﬁxation i.e. it was a saccade, algorithm
checks if the new point can be added to current window of point where there is a
possibility that they can form a ﬁxation or not. This check is again by measuring
the distance between the query point and current window’s start point. If the
distance is less than the duration threshold (ϵ), then the query point is a part of
the current window. In order to label the current window as a ﬁxation, number
of points is checked in the current window. If the current window has more or
13
==PAGE:28==
3.1 Algorithm
equal number of points than the min pts, then the current window is a ﬁxation.
All points in the current window are labelled as a ﬁxation. If the distance is not
less than the duration threshold (ϵ), then this means that the current window
cannot be ﬁxation. But the current window might still contain a possible part
of a ﬁxation.
So, while the current window is not fully empty, ﬁrst point in
the current window is removed until all the points in the current window has a
distance to the ﬁrst point that is less than ϵ.

4.3
Heat map
As Table 4.3 suggests, if time is not included as a dimension in the eye tracking
data it can be visualized as heat maps for static stimulus because in contrast
to scan paths, order of looking is not important for heat maps. Unlike static
stimulus, scene changes constantly in dynamic stimulus so time has to be included
as a dimension, therefore heat maps are not applicable for dynamic stimulus.
Heat maps can be constructed in two ways. One can either use raw data
samples or use ﬁxations as input for creating heat maps.
Raw data samples
are used when ﬁxation detection algorithm is not reliable. This may be caused
because algorithm settings are not set properly or simply algorithm is not good
enough. However, when heat maps are constructed using raw data samples as
input, each data point is a part of the heat map with same temperature because
each data point always has the same duration.
When raw data samples are processed and analyzed, ﬁxations can be used
as input for creating heat maps. Saccades are eliminated from the heat map if
analyzed data is used which makes the visualization more clean i.e. noise free.
If saccades were not eliminated, when many saccades clutter it would create a
heated point in the heat map. This would deﬁnitely misguide viewer on where
the attention is. In a real time setting where heat maps are generated on the ﬂy,
it is important to decrease computational processing. By using analyzed ﬁxations
instead of raw data points as input for heat maps, volume of the data that needs
to be drawn is signiﬁcantly decreased.
When ﬁxations are used as input, heat maps can be constructed again in
two ways. One can either construct unscaled or scaled heat maps. Scaled heat
maps are duration based which means that ﬁxations have temperatures relative
to their duration. On the opposite, in unscaled heat maps each ﬁxation have the
same temperature. Constructing scaled heat maps are similar to constructing

5.3.4.3
Sequence Diagram
Figure 5.10 illustrates the sequence diagram for visualization process, which is
the ﬂow of this component.
Process is initiated with AIOHTTP server of Analysis module connecting and
emitting start signal to socket server. Received message is broadcasted and is
picked up by socket client. This signal triggers sketch class to setup conﬁgura-
tions for drawing. After this process is done, visualization starts by AIOHTTP
server sending analyzed data points to socket server one by one which are broad-
casted by socket server. Socket client listening to socket receives data points and
draws them. After transmission of the analyzed eye tracking data is completed,
AIOHTTP server disconnects from Node.js server.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The focus of this thesis was to create a pipeline for analysis and visualization
of eye tracking data. The work which has been done in this thesis concluded a
prototype pipeline for real time analysis and visualization of eye tracking data.
Chapter 3 presented a new algorithm for detecting ocumulotor events in real
time from eye tracking data. Tests done on the newly proposed algorithm in
Chapter 3 showed that algorithm works successfully comparing to already existing
post hoc algorithms. Tests also revealed that proposed algorithm is resistant to
noise and high frequencies.
Chapter 4 discussed two visualization techniques and their adaptions in order
to create scanpaths and heat maps in real time. An experiment has conducted in
order to see how proposed visualization techniques work and where they can be
used. Experimental results suggested that real time visualization of eye tracking
data allowed experimenters to observe attention of the subject at an instant or
throughout the experiment. Results also showed that proposed visualization tech-
niques of eye tracking data lets experimenter to give feedback and/or directions
to subjects in real time.
Chapter 5 explained the complete pipeline, its architecture, software design
and implementation. Architecture consists of three modules: Real time playback
62
==PAGE:77==
module, Analysis module and Visualization module.
Three modules serve as
micro services connected to each other and are easy to replace with diﬀerent
modules for future work.
In conclusion, this thesis provides algorithms and techniques to analyze and
visualize eye tracking data in real time whilst creating a pipeline which starts raw
eye tracking data and ends with real time visualization.

Chapter 7
Future Work
As already discussed in Subsection 5.1.3, a future improvement may be adding a
new module, namely Video stream playback module which would allow real time
visualization of dynamic stimulus.
Visualization of eye tracking data has various diﬀerent aspect that can be
implemented into this pipeline. One can add analysis and visualization of areas
of interests to mark certain parts of the stimulus.
There is no user interface implemented for Real time playback module so
a future improvement might include implementation of a user interface for this
module.
This prototype of the visualization has a dull user interface so a future im-
provement might include adding slider for replay, zooming in or out and conﬁg-
urable settings visualizations.
