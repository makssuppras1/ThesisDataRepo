Abstract
Concrete is one of the most widely used structural materials nowadays. Despite its
many advantages in terms of compressive strength and low price, it still has a very
signiﬁcant shortcoming when tensional stresses are applied. In bearing structures
this problem was solved by using reinforcing steel rebars and more recently by ad-
dition of discontinuous ﬁbres into the concrete mix. These short ﬁbres dispersed in
the matrix serve as crack-bridging elements once load is applied and concrete cracks.
In order for such a crack to further widen, the ﬁbres have to be pulled-out of the
respective sides of the crack. This mechanism improves ﬂexural stiﬀness of concrete
and helps to avoid brittle modes of failure.
Since these kind of composites have emerged, many diﬀerent ways of describing
their behaviour were used.
However, a convenient approach employed by many
researchers is a single ﬁbre pull-out test. Unfortunately, over the years of research
no standard has been developed for how to perform such test but literature still oﬀers
useful guidelines. Another issue with steel ﬁbre reinforced concrete is its durability.
The ﬁbres are fully activated when the matrix cracks and this fact means that
they are left exposed, however autogenous healing of concrete could help reduce
these eﬀects. CT scanning might be useful when studying interfacial transition zone
between ﬁbre and matrix.
In order to study autogenous healing of concrete eﬀect on steel ﬁbre reinforced
concrete in the context of single ﬁbre pull-out, ﬁve testing series were cast with three
diﬀerent hooked ﬁbre types. The last casting series was cast with the ﬁbre’s hooks
cut oﬀin order to create a straight ﬁbre to prove the mechanical anchorage eﬀect of
said hook. Three diﬀerent test setups were used, ﬁrst was a standard compression-
tension testing machine and supporting equipment, second was the same device but
modiﬁed to allow for the use of Aramis image analysis software and the design of
the third test setup, which was a small testing rig, ﬁtting inside Ct scanner, was
another objective of this work.
Pull-out test results are presented as pull-out load versus displacement curves
and required energy. Results of the pull-out testing of ﬁrst and ﬁfth series have
shown that we were successfully able to prepare, carry out and analyze single steel
ﬁbre pull-out from concrete. They show good agreement with the studied literature
and also their comparison has proved the mechanical anchorage eﬀect of the hook.
From the investigated ﬁbre types ArcelorMittal has shown the best performance,
followed by very similar results for KrampeHarex and the worst was Bekaert.
To study the eﬀect of autogenous healing the second, third and fourth series
were ﬁrst pre-pulled in order to introduce some damage to interfacial transition zone
between ﬁbre and matrix. Second series was then put in hot lime water to start the
healing processes with the expected result of adhesional bond restoration. Samples
from third and fourth series were left in air at room temperature with the assumption

of no healing. Repeated pull-out testing had contradictory results among ﬁbre types
meaning that results for ArcelorMittal and to certain extent also Bekaert supported
the idea that the healing products form stronger ITZ than original, but no such
conclusion could be made from results for KrampeHarex however, the adhesional
bond was restored in all cases. The most important ﬁnding was that the assumption
of no healing in air was incorrect, as these samples have shown better performance
than both healed and standard samples.
Error in data collection from Instron 6022 was observed where the results from
the image analysis showed considerably huge diﬀerence in the data regarding the
displacement of the ﬁbre.
Custom made setup built to reproduce pull-out tests
wasn’t functioning properly before the end of this research. It however, ﬁt the CT
scanner and we assume that after the issue with the setup is solved a CT scan of a
pull-out test will be possible.
Results from CT scanning of the samples were very limited due to the time
constraints for this projects. Only a fraction of the acquired data was processed,
but it allows to identify cracks in interfacial transition zone. Important information
has been identiﬁed for the scanning time, radiation intensity and overall handling
of the samples in the scanner.
Certain limitations were found in this research and its results, these should be
further investigated. Especially, focus should be put into the Aramis analysis to
determine a more correct way of displacement measurement during pull-out test.
Also not enough evidence was found to draw any precise conclusion for the eﬀect of
healing and further research should be done, where the eﬀect of healing in air will
also be investigated. Further development of the small testing rig is necessary which
would help also the study of healing eﬀect through the possibility of CT scanning
of real-time test.

Introduction
Recent utilization of structural concrete lead to a demand for material with improved
ﬂexural stiﬀness. One of the very common and practical ways how to achieve this is
by addition of steel ﬁbres. These ﬁbres bridge cracks resulting from tension loading
and their random distribution allows for multiple crack development. Research and
practice has proven that the use of straight ﬁbres is not as eﬀective as using ﬁbres
with hook shaped ends. These provide mechanical anchorage in addition to bond
and frictional resistance.
In order to describe the behaviour of such a composite it is necessary to under-
stand the pull-out processes of steel ﬁbres from concrete and how are they aﬀected
by diﬀerent variables. Over the years of research few test setups and analytical mod-
els have been developed, but there is not much consistency and comparison tends to
be diﬃcult, as even for a single test setup the scatter of results is high. Moreover,
the damage to the concrete matrix used in tests hasn’t been thoroughly evaluated.
In general there is an agreement that the pull-out process has two phases. The
ﬁrst is debonding of the interface between ﬁbre and matrix. The second is repre-
sented by the frictional resistance of the interface as the ﬁbre is being pulled out.
This is often called residual pull-out resistance. In the case of ﬁbres with hooked
shaped ends, the hook must ﬁrst be straightened, resulting in extra damage. The
cracks which result from this kind of test are important for SLS evaluation and
could aﬀect the durability of concrete. Therefore investigation of the ability of con-
crete to autogenously heal such damage should be performed. CT scanning oﬀers
the possibility to study the structure of the interfacial transition zone ITZ and the
development of cracks inside the matrix.
1.1
Aim and objectives
The Aim of this project is to study the damage caused to the concrete matrix and
ﬁbre-matrix interface during the ﬁbre pull-out and evaluate the ability of concrete
to autogenously heal this damage.
In order to achieve this, following objectives are set:
1. Find a consistent approach for performing a single ﬁbre pull-out test which
would coincide with previous research and literature.

2.1
Introduction to Fibre Reinforced Cementitious
Composites
Recent utilization of structural concrete has led to a demand for material with
improved ﬂexural stiﬀness and thus the addition of ﬁbres into concrete has started.
These work in such a way, that they allow for the development of multiple cracks
and then bridge the two sides of a crack together and thus prevent brittle mode of
failure. This means that when the load increases and the crack widens, the ﬁbres
are being pulled-out. The described mechanism is especially true for discontinuous
ﬁbres [24].
Figure 2.1: Crack bridging eﬀect of ﬁbres [28]
First, the ﬁbres that could be found in the nature were used, which lead to
the advent of cements reinforced with asbestos ﬁbres in 1900. Nowadays not only
cement, but also other materials are being reinforced, such as plastics, ceramics and
epoxies. Diﬀerent kinds of ﬁbres are used: steel and glass, polypropylene, nylon or
carbon and Kevlar (aramid) [16].
The ﬁbres that were ﬁrst used were smooth and straight, however since the
1950-60, this has changed as the eﬀectiveness of mechanical bonding which is much
higher than the adhesional/frictional bond has been proven. From then on, ﬁbres
with various geometries, deformed ends, hooks and also crimped ﬁbres were used
[16]. These kinds of deformations are mainly assumed to work through two diﬀerent
4
==PAGE:19==
CHAPTER 2. STATE OF THE ART
mechanisms. First energy is required for the plastic deformation when the ﬁbre is
being pulled-out, and second, the inﬂuence sphere(space inside the matrix which is
aﬀected by the ﬁbre being pulled-out) of this kind of ﬁbre is greater than that of a
straight one and thus stressing and cracking goes deeper into the matrix [25].
Figure 2.2: Diﬀerent ﬁbre geometries. From left: Hooked, Flat-end, Crimped.([8])
Steel ﬁbres are very commonly used due to relative ease of creating deformed
shapes during the production process. They are either made of carbon steels or
alloy steels (stainless) where corrosion resistance is required. The usual lengths for
the industrially produced ﬁbres are between 12-70 mm. Their tensile strength Rm
ranges from 345-2400 MPa. Fibres are often designated by their aspect ratio lf/df,
which is the ratio of their length to their diameter [25].
Concrete to which these kinds of steel ﬁbres are incorporated is called Steel Fibre
Reinforced Concrete (SFRC). Currently most of SFRCs that are produced have low
ﬁbre volumes (0.5-2%). The main negative eﬀect of adding the ﬁbres is in changing
of the packing density of aggregates and thus the workability which can be easily
measured by the decrease of slump during the slump test. Also, when the weight
percentage of ﬁbres is nearing 2% of the total weight of the mixture the amount of
ﬁnes in the mixture has to be increased [25].
There is a wide variety of factors that govern mechanical properties of SFRC.
They can be however, grouped into three distinct groups as Fibre, Matrix and
Specimen. Fibre: type, geometry, aspect ratio, volume, orientation, distribution
Matrix: strength and maximum aggregate size Specimen: size, geometry, method
of preparation, loading rate However, it can be said that both the ﬂexural stiﬀness
and fracture energy are increased for SFRC when compared to plain concrete [16].
2.2
Single Fibre Pull-out Test
As it has been mentioned earlier the preferred mode of failure for ﬁbre reinforced
composites is by pulling-out the ﬁbres bridging the crack in the matrix. This mode
results in highest required energy, which is only increased for ﬁbres that also provide
mechanical anchorage e.g. hooked, crimped etc. [25]
In order to be able to design structures that utilize the crack-bridging mecha-
nism of ﬁbre reinforced composites certain parameters have to be experimentally
evaluated. Most importantly the shear stresses that result as the interface between
ﬁbre and matrix is being damaged during pull-out [7].

2.2.1.1
Pull-out rate eﬀect
One variable that can inﬂuence the results of a pull-out test is the rate of load
applied. The range of pull-out rates that have been used is very wide, as seen in
Table 2.1. And only a few of studies have investigated its eﬀect [32][1][17].
This issue has been thoroughly studied by [32] where the quasi-static(0.021 mm•
sec−1) and seismic load rates(25.4 mm • sec−1) were applied to diﬀerent ﬁbre types
embedded in very-high strength concrete matrices. It was found that increase in
load rate will also increase peak pull-out load and energy. Also, various ﬁbre types
have displayed diﬀerent rate sensitivities. Especially for the ﬂattened end ﬁbre which
changed its mode of failure from complete pull-out to rupture when the seismic load
rate was applied [32].
In [1] however, the increase of the pull-out rate also changed the mode of failure
for crimped ﬁbres embedded in more brittle matrices(containing silica fume) from
fracture to complete pull-out. On the other hand in the case of the same ﬁbres, but
matrices with higher w/c ratios, it led to ﬁbre fracture.
Another study [17] was also focused on load rate sensitivity but applied a wider
variety of load rates(0.0178 to 17.8 mm • sec−1 ).
It was shown that the rate
sensitivity depends on other variables such as: ﬁbre type, volume fraction and matrix
strength. Hooked-end ﬁbres showed no load rate dependence, while twisted ﬁbres
did. More importantly however, it was shown that the rate sensitivity increases with
increased matrix strength.

The diﬀerence can be easily explained when the pull-out mechanism is explained.
Hooked-end ﬁbres only utilize a small portion of the embedded length to create
mechanical anchorage, on the other hand in the case of crimped ﬁbres, the whole
length is activated in this mechanism. For crimped ﬁbres there was found higher
pull-out work, but lower peak load than for hooked-end ﬁbres.
The results for crimped ﬁbres have shown that if the ﬁbres have higher embed-
ded length they are more likely to reach premature ﬁbre failure conditions, but no
investigation of the pull-out energy was done, only peak loads were reported [31].
2.2.2.2
Fibre tensile strength
Ultimate tensile strength Rm of the ﬁbre governs the ﬁbre fracture. Most of the
ﬁbres now commercially used have strength well over 1000 MPa which in the case
of ﬁbres aligned with the load direction is suﬃcient.
However, the ﬁbre fracture often occurs before reaching the ultimate tensile
strength which can be attributed to an additional shear stresses at the point where
they enter the matrix [19]. The stresses are resulting from bending moments applied
to the ﬁbre due to its inclination angle. According to [26] these stresses provide a
mechanism that favours slip between the crystals of steel and thus reduce the tensile
strength.
2.2.2.3
Fibre inclination
Random orientation of the ﬁbres in real composites makes the eﬀect of inclination
angle in relation to the load direction an important variable to investigate, however
the results found in literature are contradictory.
When the inclination angle was varied from 0 to 60 degrees by a 15 degree
increment the results show that the peak loads and energy absorbed by ﬁbres with a
non-zero inclination in relation to the load direction are lower than those which are
aligned with the load direction. Also, the peak loads for aligned ﬁbres were found
to happen at lower slip values. This can be explained by the mechanism described
in previous section and also by the fact that the crushing of matrix at the corner
where ﬁbres enter allows for ﬁbre straightening and thus increased slip [26].
In a more detailed study where ﬁbre orientation is varied between 0 and 60
degrees, but with a 10 degree increment. It was concluded that both peak loads and
toughness are increased with a non-zero inclination, however, with a limit which is
found to be at approximately 20 degrees. This limit is explained by the reduction
of ultimate strength due to steel crystals slippage which leads to premature ﬁbre
fracture [19].
Most recently this limit has been found to be between 15 and 40 degrees in
[20], if the concrete compressive strength and ﬁbre type were kept the same. To
avoid ﬁbre rupture only high strength matrices and ﬁbres were used. Again, for
higher inclination angles spalling of matrix occurred at the ﬁbre exit point. An
angle of around 30 degrees were reported to provide the highest pull-out loads in
[11]. Spalling of concrete is illustrated in Figure 2.8.

2.2.2.4
Embedded length
Random distribution of ﬁbres and the development of cracks in FRC result in ﬁ-
bres bridging the cracks with diﬀerent embedded lengths on each side of the crack.
Therefore the investigation of the inﬂuence of embedded length is necessary.
This is especially true for straight ﬁbres, as they only transfer stresses by adhe-
sional and interfacial frictional forces over the length of the ﬁbre. It was concluded
in [10] that increasing the ﬁbre embedded length for straight ﬁbres while other pa-
rameters remained the same resulted in increase of peak load and pull-out work
proportional to the increase of are of ITZ.
In the case of hooked-ended ﬁbres the eﬀect of embedded length is not so sig-
niﬁcant at its greater values. In the ﬁndings of [9] a linear relationship between the
embedded length and pull-out work for both straight and hooked ﬁbres was found.
Moreover, the peak pull-out load was found to increase by 26% when the embedded
length was increased from 10 to 30 mm. However, as shown in [19] the hook can
only fully straighten and thus develop its maximal peak load and toughness if the
embedded length exceeds the length of the hooked-end. Otherwise the eﬀect of a
higher embedded length only aﬀects residual frictional resistance.
2.2.3
Eﬀect of matrix variables
FRC that are produced contain not only high variability of ﬁbres but also use many
diﬀerent concrete mix designs and therefore it is important to investigate how this
aﬀects the pull-out resistance of steel ﬁbres. As in the case of conventional steel
reinforcement the micro-structure of the interfacial zone between steel and concrete
is diﬀerent and weaker than that of the bulk paste [16]. Adhesional bond which is
formed in this zone is responsible for the mechanical transfer of stresses in the ﬁrst
phase of pull-out and is damaged in the process [25]. Two main variables that were so
far studied are the compressive strength and curing processes used. This only makes
sense as both higher compressive strength and certain curing methods(autoclaving,
steam curing) lead to a denser micro-structure.

2.2.3.1
Matrix strength
The easiest way how to distinguish concrete mixes is by comparing their compressive
strength fc. Compressive strengths used in previous research vary greatly from 20
to 120 MPa. The general agreement is that with the increase in matrix strength
the bond characteristics are improved [11][1][19][10][15][32]. For very high strength
concretes the peak-pull out load can exceed a value required to break the ﬁbre
leading to rupture[1], [26] [2].
For hooked ﬁbres the relation between the peak
pull-out load and compressive strength of concrete is even more apparent, because
the stress transfer is reliant on bearing at the deformed locations, where it almost
completely relies on the compressive strength of concrete[1].
2.2.3.2
Curing process
Standard curing process used for concrete specimens was employed by most of the
researchers performing pull-out tests of ﬁbres from concrete matrices. By standard
here is meant leaving the specimens in the moulds for 24 hours after casting and
covering by plastic foil followed by demoulding and submersion into water for 28
days.
The variable curing conditions for diﬀerent researchers are represented in
table 2.1.
When other types of matrices were used, such as SIFCON(slurry inﬁltrated con-
crete), VHSC(very high strength concrete) or RPC(reactive powder concrete) other
curing methods such as steam curing and autoclaving were performed. As found by
[2] autoclaving decreased the strength of the concrete, but it has greatly increased
the pull-out load(by up to 72%). This is explained by the fact that congestion of
hydration products occurs when the samples are exposed to higher temperatures
during the autoclaving process. Also microstructural investigations was performed
to further support this argument.. However a decrease of the debonding toughness
happened, due to ﬁbre rupture resulting from high peak pull-out loads which ex-
ceed the tensile strength of the ﬁbre. Similar results were found by [9]. This was
especially true for RPC mixtures with low w/c ratios.

Conclusions
This study was focused on performing a single steel ﬁbre pull-out from concrete test
and studying autogenous healing of concrete through the means of pull-out testing
and CT scanning. Since there is currently no legal standard for single ﬁbre pull-out
test, extensive literature review was carried out in order to determine a suitable
approach.
Three diﬀerent types of hooked steel ﬁbres were investigated in this study, desig-
nated by their producer’s name as ArcelorMittal, Bekaert and KrampeHarex. Five
testing series were cast, each having 30 samples, 10 for every ﬁbre type meaning
150 samples in total. Three test setups were used, ﬁrst being standard tension-
compression testing machine and supporting equipment, second being the very same
machine, but slightly modiﬁed to allow for Aramis image analysis and third being
small testing rig, which design was also a part of this study. First series and last
series were used for standard testing of hooked and straight ﬁbres. The remaining
three series were used to study the eﬀect of healing.
Supplementary material characteristics tests were also carried out. From them
we can say that we have achieved uniform compressive strength of concrete fc of 68
MPa across all testing series. The importance of this fact is easily explained by fc
being the variable that has the biggest inﬂuence on pull-out test results, but since
its eﬀect wasn’t the objective of this study we wanted to negate it. The ultimate
tensile strength Rm of ﬁbre material has proven that the values supplied by their
respective manufacturers are up to the standards and were used to calculate the
forces necessary to break the ﬁbres in order to ﬁnd the upper limit for pull-out load.
From the results presented and discussed in the previous chapter we can say
that we were able to successfully prepare, carry-out and analyze single steel ﬁbre
pull-out from concrete test. Results in the form load vs displacement curves show
good agreement with results found in Literature. For the ﬁrst series which contained
hooked ﬁbres the normalised pull-out load Fn was found found to be the highest for
ArcelorMittal, followed with a very similar perforamnce by KrampeHarex and the
worst performance was observed for Bekaert. The same conclusion is drawn from
comparing normalised required energy En.
Testing of the straight ﬁbres which were present in ﬁfth casting series, has in the
terms of normalised pull-out load Fn and normalised required energy also proven
that ArcelorMittal ﬁbre performs the best, whereas Bekaert and KrampeHarex have
very similar behaviour. When comparing the results for hooked ﬁbres and straight
ﬁbres for single ﬁbre types in all accounts we ﬁnd the superiority of hooked ﬁbres,
63
==PAGE:78==
CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSIONS
with the value of Fn at 0.3 mm displacement being 3 times higher and value of En
2 times higher for hooked ﬁbre than for straight ﬁbre for all three ﬁbre types.
To study the eﬀect of healing second casting series was pre-pulled in order to
introduce damage to interfacial transition zone between ﬁbre and matrix and sub-
sequently cured in water for 28 days. Samples from third and fourth casting series
were used as a proof series, meaning they were pre-pulled but left in the air for 28
days with the assumption of no healing taking place. Results from repeated pull-out
testing were then compared for both series and also with results for ﬁrst series.
We have obtained contradictory results across the diﬀerent ﬁbre types. Results
for ArcelorMittal and to certain extent also for Bekaert are supporting the argument
that healing produces stronger ITZ than original, however results for KrampeHarex
ﬁbre do not provide any base for such conclusion, only to that the adhesional bond
was restored. Moreover, the results of tests where no healing was assumed show
even stronger ITZ than healed samples, meaning that the assumption of no healing
when the samples were left in air was wrong and healing took place.
An image analysis was performed on test series three and four to proof-test the
results acquired from all pull-out tests. It was observed that the displacement d
measured by Instron 6022 was considerably diﬀerent from displacements measured
by the image analysis. It was concluded that a variable inﬂuencing the results is
present between the ﬁbre and the ramp of the device and recommended to use image
analysis on all future pull-out tests.
Custom built setup was created with an intention to reproduce the regular pull-
out tests while lowering the overall dimensions of the device so that it would be
possible to use this setup inside a CT scanner. This goal was partially achieved.
While the setup ﬁt inside the CT scanner it was not possible to proof-test the device
by making a pull-out test since the stepper motor used as a drive train wasn’t
functioning properly. It is assumed that this issue is caused by a driver board which
didn’t supply enough voltage to the stepper motor and therefore the motor wasn’t
able to pull the ﬁbre as desired, more precisely its eﬀectivity was only 10% - motor
pulled only 50 N.
Results from the CT scanning are very limited due to time constraints in this
project. The data we were able to acquire and process have shown the possibility
to identify cracks in the ITZ. However many limitations were identiﬁed, mostly
regarding the time it would take to perform a full investigation of diﬀerent stages
of pull-out. On the other hand, important information about radiation intensity
and overall handling of samples and CT scanner which can serve as a good basis for
further research were found out.

Further research
In this study an attempt to investigate autogenous healing of concrete eﬀect on pull-
out test and thus on ﬁbre reinforced concrete in general was performed, however
certain limitations were found and in order to better understand and improve the
ﬁndings of this thesis recommendations for further research are made.
Firstly, it was proven that results from RPT can contain signiﬁcant error in the
displacement data which should be taken into account in the future research. The use
of image analysis is highly recommended for every pull-out test performed on Instron
6022. Other options such as the use of linear displacement sensors or correction of
64

results from Instron 6022 are optional but not completely precise regarding this
issue. The use of displacement sensors would not assume potential displacement
error in the zone where ﬁbre is gripped by a clamp. Correction of the results based
on the linear dependence between the pull-out load F and slip diﬀerence ds shows
satisfactory results but is only applicable in the elastic region for every curve since
after elastic region on tested samples no dependance between F and ds was observed.
Secondly, since the assumption of no healing in air was proven to be incorrect a
research that would study the microscopic and also chemical structure of ITZ could
better evaluate the actual processes taking place in ITZ during healing and thus
determine the extent of healing both in air and in water. This can be done with the
reference to pull-out testing in which CT scanning could be especially helpful.
Thirdly, custom built setup needs to be improved. It is recommended that the
ﬁrst step is to upgrade the driver board since the one which was used was overheating
and not supplying the stepper motor with enough voltage. After the stepper motor
will be able to successfully perform a pull-out test calibration of the linear load cell
should be proof tested more precisely. Using this setup in a CT scanner should
result in several 3D scans of a particular sample during phases of the pull-out test.
It is recommended to disconnect the setup before every scan. The reason for this
is mainly the fact the it is not possible to ﬁt everything to the CT scanner (PC,
power supply). Another option could be extension of all cables coming to the custom
setup. This way it would be possible to use a hole in a CT scanner and leave the
setup connected during the test.

This topic was extensively researched in [29]. Here a rectangular cross-section de-

crossing a section is proposed. Most important part is the theoretical calculation
of an orientation factor. It is supplemented by ﬁbre counting on 107 Rilem beam
specimens with a notch and the comparison between expected and counted number
of ﬁbres was proven to be good.
