Abstract
Interesting series of papers were published in the late 1980s and early 1990s by a
group led by a prominent scientist in the field of theoretical chemistry. The series
suggested that the main pathway for catalytic dissociation of molecular nitrogen
were due to tunneling. This can be considered highly unusual as heavy atoms
like nitrogen generally have very low tunneling probabilities even at energies very
close to the barrier. The work was supported by simulations and molecular beam
experiments. Later analysis suggested that the dissociation was not due to tun-
neling but rather momentum spread in the highly localized nitrogen bond. This
work attempts to reproduce the results presented back in the 1990s and analyse
the real cause of dissociation. The methods include the complete description of
the potential energy surface used in the original work. A full one-dimensional anal-
ysis along the reaction coordinate is also conducted to see agreement between the
one-dimensional model and the two-dimensional model. This includes zero-point
energy along the reaction coordinate with semi-classical estimates of the trans-
mission probabilities and wave-packet propagation along the reaction coordinate.
Lastly the two-dimensional results are reproduced and the trajectories towards dis-
sociation analysed leading towards strong indications of momentum spread being
the culprit.

1.1
Tunneling in chemical reactions
Chemical kinetics describe the rate at which a chemical reaction happens. For a
chemical reaction
aA + bB ‚ÜícC + dD
(1.1)
Has an integrated rate law
rate = k[A]x[B]y
(1.2)
Where the coeÔ¨Äicients x and y are determined by the reaction mechanism and k is
the rate constant. Numerous theories of varying complexity have been developed
to theoretically predict the rate constant. One of the more prominent theories is
Transisition state theory (TST), a theory that assumes that a chemical reaction
follows a minimum energy path from reactant to product via an activated complex
located at the saddle point or barrier [7]. TST is however a strictly thermody-
namical formulation with basic assumptions such as the ones stated above. As
such TST does not consider strictly quantum mechanical effects such as quantum
mechanical tunneling.
Tunneling is a strictly quantum mechanical effect where a particle has a non-zero
probability of crossing a potential energy barrier of height Vm at its maximum while
having energy E = V + T < Vm. This is strictly forbidden by classical mechanics
but a natural consequence of the wave nature of matter and the probabilistic nature
of quantum mechanics. Indeed there are several instances of tunneling in chemical
reactions especially involving lighter atoms such as hydrogen. This present work is
investigating an interesting result which indicates that the primary mechanism for
molecular nitrogen catalytic dissociation is quantum tunneling. This is specifically
surprising given the high mass of molecular nitrogen. In particular further analy-
sis will reveal that another strictly quantum phenomenon, specifically relevant to
molecular nitrogen is present.

2.1
Literature
Back in 1989 R. Kosloff and his collaborators published a series of papers indicating
that one of the primary mechanism for catalytic dissociation of molecular nitrogen
was quantum tunneling. This claim was supported by wave-packet propagation on
an empirical PES and in good agreement with molecular beam experiment. Later
on, other scientists discovered alternative reaction mechanisms for catalytic disso-
ciation of molecular nitrogen, indicating that the conclusion Kosloff et. al reached
might be misplaced.
Kosloff and his collaborators first paper of the series was based upon an empirical
PES describing the dissociation of molecular nitrogen on the catalytic Re(0001)
surface. [3] The PES was severely reduced in dimensionality whereas a real PES
would have six degrees of freedom for molecular nitrogen, electronic degrees of
freedom and those of the metal, this PES had two degrees of freedom, the height
between the surface and nitrogen‚Äôs centre of mass (translational coordinate,z) and
the vibrational coordinate (r).
The empirical PES was based upon model po-
tentials and known spectroscopic parameters such as the vibrational frequency of
molecular nitrogen and the barrier height at the saddle point and three free pa-
rameters to get the desired topology (for further details on the PES see section
x). The computational study was performed by modelling molecular beams as
propagating wave-packets on the PES and measuring the dissociation rate as a
function of the incident kinetic energy of the wave-packet. Since the wave-packet
had energy less than the barrier height any crossing of the barrier would be clas-
sified as a tunneling event. The results from the computational were in very good
agreement with the molecular beam experiments performed by the same team,
indicating that the reduced dimensionality of the PES was taking into account
the most significant factors and that the main mechanism of dissociation at low
collision energies was due to tunneling.
Kosloff and his collaborators published several more papers on the subject further
adjusting the PES and obtaining more elaborate experimental data on the subject
including temperature dependence. The common factor throughought the series
was the agreement between a crudely approximated PES and experiment which
indicated towards tunneling being the mechanism. [10][15][18][17]
In 2000 Niels Henrikssen and collaborators published a paper analysing the re-
sults of Kosloff et. al[8]. The objective of the analysis was to understand how
the considerably heavy nitrogen molecule could tunnel through a significant bar-

rier considering that heavy molecules usually don‚Äôt exhibit that behavior. Their
analysis started with a one dimensional model of the reaction path and estimated
tunneling rates which were many orders of magnitude below the calculated rate
in two-dimensional simulations. However the results in the two-dimensional case
were reproduced indicating that tunneling was at play in higher dimensions. The
pair however suggested an alternative route, instead of the molecular nitrogen
tunneling through the barrier they proposed that momentum spread in the vibra-
tional coordinate was the main mechanism, this was supported by simple models
showing that the probability of having momentum suÔ¨Äicient to cross the barrier
was comparable to the dissociation rate predicted by two-dimensional simulations.
In the early 2000s Jens N√łrskov and collaborators started DFT analysis molecu-

The study suggested that at low collision energies the molecular nitrogen used an
alternative pathway at step sites resulting in significantly reduced barrier heights.
The conclusion of his study was that a range of barrier heights existed on the
catalytic surface fully explaining the high dissociation rate of the molecular beam
at low collision energies. Further studies including a full six-dimensional simula-
tion on a DFT produced PES suggested that tunneling was insignificant for the
dissociation of nitrogen [4][14][20].
2.2
Objectives
The purpose of this work is to investigate the dynamics of the system that Kosloff
et al. presented back in 1989. It seems accepted within the literature that tun-
neling does not play a significant role in the catalytic dissociation of molecular
nitrogen, however it is still a mystery as to why the results indicated towards that.
A Potential energy surface using the same model potential as is present in the
original work is constructed and from that a thorough one dimensional analysis is
conducted. This will entail both semi-classical methods estimating the tunneling
probabilities on the reaction path as well as one dimensional wave-packet propa-
gation along the reaction path. Furthermore an attempt to reproduce the original
two-dimensional rates in order to investigate what trajectory the wave-packet takes
towards the reactant space. The main interest of this work is seeing if there is gen-
uine tunneling happening in the two-dimensional case or if it is due to the high
momentum spread in the vibrational coordinate of molecular nitrogen.

3.2.2
Practical implementation, mass scaled coordinates
From a practical standpoint transforming all of the expressions associated with the
adiabatic surface is unnecessary. Using it to ones advantage that the output of the
calculated surface is a grid, the surface can be calculated in the non-transformed
(z,r) coordinates and then afterwards the coordinates can be re-scaled by the mass
factor associated with them. This works since the relevant index in the grid will
not change. However the new contour will be stretched by the respective mass
factor. So to summarize applying the mass-scaled coordinates:
Calculate the PES in non-scaled (z,r) coordinates
Scale the coordinates by their mass factor
Produce the contour.

Conclusion
Starting with the conclusion that Kosloff et al. reached on the main pathway of cat-
alytic dissociation of molecular nitrogen at low collision energies being tunneling.
This conclusion was supported by wavepacket simulations and in agreement with
experiment. The large discrepancy however between the one-dimensional model
and the two-dimensional model indicated towards an alternative pathway however.
The comprehensive one-dimensional work in this work has included a full descrip-
tion of the reaction coordinate including its zero-point energy and tunneling rates
through that barrier and being in disagreement with the higher dimensional model.
One dimensional wave-packet propagation along the reaction coordinate further
reaÔ¨Äirmed this disagreement. However the dissociation probabilities presented by
Kosloff et al were in agreement with the two-dimensional simulations done in this
work. Further analysis did however show that, due to the large momentum spread
in the vibrational coordinate of molecular nitrogen, the wave-packet diverged from
the minimum energy path and took a trajectory over a higher barrier than the
saddle point. The conclusion that can be drawn from this work is that molecular
nitrogen will choose a trajectory that is not along the minimum energy path due
to high momentum spread and can therefore exceed predictions from classical rate
constant theories. The dissociation is still due to a strictly quantum phenomenon,
the uncertainity principle in this case.
