Abstract 
 
High-Temperature Polymer Electrolyte Membrane (HTPEM) fuel cells are 
electrochemical devices that convert chemical energy from fuels into electrical energy. 
Lifetime is one of the main issues HTPEM fuel cells confront, and continuous research aims 
to improve the state of the art. This study seeks to investigate HTPEM fuel cells' durability by 
performing accelerated stress testing (AST). More specifically, this research focuses on the 
effects and causes of acid loss as a major durability mechanism. This thesis project has industry 
cooperation from Blue World Technologies, which provided membrane electrode assemblies 
(MEA) for AST. Valuable insights are generated by investigating the effects of temperature, 
air lambda, and current density using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), 
polarization, and durability curves. Three main experiments were conducted, one in which 
temperature varies while keeping other parameters constant, another varying the air lambda, 
and finally, the last varying current density. Acid loss mechanisms such as evaporation and 
dehydration as a function of temperature and stoichiometry are analyzed, as well as acid 
migration as a function of current density. All experiments are conducted following the same 
protocols and equipment. 
Analysis of the temperature effects revealed that MEA break-in periods for 160°C 
procedures are longer than those for 190°C protocols. The fuel cells' increased temperature also 
revealed a convincing sign of acid evaporation. As the temperature increased, the voltage loss 
increased, and the ohmic resistance loss increased. When analyzing the effects caused by air 
lambda at 190°C, the decrease in output voltage at air lambda 20 was the worst in comparison 
to air lambda 10 and 5, revealing that as air lambda is increased, the decreasing rate of the 
output voltage is higher. The expected outcome was also observed at 160°C, where air lambda 
5 had a slower degradation rate than air lambda 10 and the ohmic resistance loss increased more 
as air lambda was raised. The clearest trend in this thesis was the experiment analyzing the 
current density effects. The observed trends showed that the ohmic resistance loss increased 
faster and the output voltage decreased faster with increasing current density when all other 
parameters remained constant. Furthermore, the kinetic resistance loss, the current density at 
zero overpotential, and the Tafel slope for all MEAs in all experiments remained constant and 
had a stable trend throughout the procedures.

1.1 Thesis Motivation  
From the beginning of civilization to modern times, the progress of human society has 
been fundamentally tied to our ability to harness and utilize energy. Yet, to this day, fossil fuels 
have been used to meet energy needs, escalating air pollution and releasing greenhouse gases 
like CO2 [1]. Therefore, effectively managing CO2 emissions is one of the developed nations' 
most prominent issues [1], [2]. To address this problem, the Kyoto Protocol was adopted in 
1997 by the United Nations to combat climate change. As per the protocol, participating nations 
must declare goals for lowering their greenhouse gas emissions and formulate and implement 
plans to achieve them. The agreement also suggests that countries must take immediate steps 
such as developing renewable energy sources, carbon capture and storage, and adopting 
advanced low-CO2 emission energy systems [1], [3]. These challenges may promote the 
development of cleaner and more efficient energy sources. In 2014, Denmark announced the 
aim of a 40% decline in CO2 emissions from 1990 to 2020 and that by 2050, the Danish energy 
sector would be 100% renewable [4]. This will require drastic adjustments at all ranks. 
Numerous applications are supposed to be used to reduce emissions. In the present context, 
fuel cells have emerged as a versatile energy technology, gaining significant attention, and are 
zero-emission energy conversion devices. They provide solutions for three UN sustainable 
development goals, which include affordable and clean energy, sustainable cities and 
communities, and climate action. However, like any other energy source, fuel cells face critical 
challenges. One of the primary issues is the degradation within the cell affecting its durability 
and performance [5].

1.2 What are Fuel Cells? 
Fuel cells are electrochemical devices that instantly and constantly convert chemical 
energy from fuels into electrical energy. These fuels include chemical compounds such as 
hydrogen, methanol, ethanol, or natural gas that undergo redox reactions with oxygen, an 
oxidizing agent, supplying a clean, environmentally friendly method of generating electricity 
[6]. The electrolyte used in fuel cells is typically used to categorize fuel cells. The temperature 
of operation associated with each fuel cell provides the basis for categorization by high and 
low temperature. Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cells (PEMFC), Direct Methanol Fuel 
Cells (DMFC), Alkaline Fuel Cells (AFC), and Phosphoric Acid Fuel Cells (PAFC) are 
members of the low-temperature category up to 200℃. The Molten Carbonate Fuel Cell 
(MCFC) and the Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) are two high-temperature fuel cells functioning 
between 500°C and 1000°C [7]. 
Figure 1.1 presents an overview of several fuel cell technologies with possible 
applications. This study concentrates on phosphoric acid-doped polybenzimidazole (PBI) 
membranes in HTPEM fuel cells supplied by air at the cathode and hydrogen at the anode, as 
phosphoric acid-doped PBI is a proton-transporting polymeric substance, allowing the 
membrane to work as an electrolyte. 
 
Figure 1.1 Schematic of different fuel cell systems. The schematic shows that in the anode, 
fuel is oxidized, and at the cathode, oxygen is reduced forming water. The electrolyte is the 
present ionic species used in the reaction [8]. 
==PAGE:10==

In an HTPEM fuel cell, as presented in Figure 1.1, there must be no contact between the 
fuel on the anodic side and oxygen on the cathodic side; otherwise, hydrogen will chemically 
combust in oxygen. Like a battery, an electrolyte with contacting porous electrode structures 
separates the reactants and keeps them from chemically reacting [9]. Protons (H+) and 
electrons are separated and produced via the electrocatalytic oxidation of hydrogen at the 
anode, as seen in Figure 1.1, and the electrolyte does not permit electrons to pass through, only 
ions. Instead, the electrons are repelled from the anode and toward the cathode through an 
external circuit, while the protons go through the electrolyte to the cathode. The electrons and 
protons will react with oxygen being fuelled into the cathode to produce water. Thus, this 
process creates a flow of electricity, as shown in Figure 1.1 [6], [9].  
A fuel cell's most important components are ionic conductive electrolytes, anodic and 
cathodic electrodes. The membrane-electrode assembly (MEA) is the term used to describe 
when these three components are assembled [6]. The electrolyte serves a variety of purposes. 
As briefly mentioned earlier, its primary purpose is to separate the fuel from the oxidant by 
acting as a barrier to molecular permeability. As an ion conductor, it allows ions to move 
through it. Furthermore, the electrolyte divides the entire cell reaction into two half-cell 
reactions[6], [9]. The reactions are visually explained in Figure 1.2. The following reactions

1.4 High-Temperature PEM Fuel Cells 
HTPEM fuel cells are typically compared to Low-Temperature PEMFC (LT-PEMFC). 
The most common type of LTPEMFC is based on perfluorosulfonic acid (PFSA) membranes 
like Nafion®. This technology has many upsides, such as the membrane possessing high 
conductivity, chemical stability, flexibility, durability, and strength, but it only works when 
hydrated. Due to this, the fuel cell temperature must not exceed a temperature of 80°C so that 
the high water content is not disrupted, leading to several challenges with watering systems, 
control of thermal systems, and low tolerance to oxidant impurity [13]. HTPEM fuel cells use 
polybenzimidazole membranes doped in phosphoric acid and can operate at temperatures of 
120 to 200°C. Furthermore, its polymer electrolyte membrane helps the electrochemical 
reactions to produce energy from a fuel, such as hydrogen, and an oxidant, such as air [6]. 
 
1.4.1 Polybenzimidazole (PBI) and phosphoric acid (PA) 
 
Growing interest has been shown in HTPEM fuel cells that use polymer membranes 
doped with phosphoric acid (PA) and polybenzimidazole (PBI). Polybenzimidazole (PBI) 
membranes doped with phosphoric acid were introduced in 1995 and can function at

1.4.2 Comparison of LTPEM vs. HTPEM fuel cells 
 
The LTPEM fuel cell is the benchmark to whom HTPEM fuel cells are typically 
compared. There are, however, a few notable variances. In PEM FC, the cathode side has low 
electrochemical kinetics due to the exchange current density in the oxygen reduction 
reaction being smaller than the exchange current density in the hydrogen oxidation reaction. 
Thus, defining its overall reaction rate since the overpotential at the cathode is responsible for 
the cell voltage loss. Therefore, the cell voltage loss is caused by the overpotential at the 
cathode. Furthermore, a major advantage of HTPEM fuel cells is that it can withstand larger 
levels CO than LTPEM fuel cells [17]. The Nafion membranes in LTPEM fuel cells are limited 
to a temperature of max 80℃ and the platinum catalyst is of high attraction to CO. At lower 
temperatures, CO oxidation kinetics on the surface of catalysts in lowered, so LTPEM fuel 
cells can only withstand a maximum of 110 ppm CO concentration before poisoning the 
catalyst [17]. In addition, HTPEMs can be utilized to generate power from reformate gases that 
contain a high amount of hydrogen without complications as it is capable to withstand 
impurities, up to 3-5% more in the concentration of CO, and is cheaper since it does not require 
cooling [17]. Appropriate heat control is required to ensure the best efficiency and longevity 
of the fuel cell. If the temperature is too high, the electrodes and electrolyte can undergo 
dehydration, and flooding will happen if the temperature is too low. HTPEM fuel cells have a 
large difference in temperature than the environment it is in, taking away the need for a cooling 
system, making it easier to transport [17]. On the other hand, the main issue that must be solved 
in HTPEM fuel cells is acknowledged to be durability. All fuel cell components degrade in 
performance throughout the operation and can cause a voltage drop and even the breakdown 
of the whole system [10].

1.4.3 Components of HTPEM Fuel cell 
 
The HTPEM fuel cells consist of a phosphoric acid-doped PBI MEA inserted between 
the catalyst and gas diffusion layers. The MEA is fitted within the test hardware between two 
PTFE gaskets and two graphite bipolar plates with O-rings. Current collectors are positioned 
at each side and joined by stainless steel endplates, which are screwed together using eight 
bolts. The components can be visualized in Figure 1.6. 
 
 
Figure 1.6 Exploded component schematic of HTPEM single fuel cell [18]. 
 
The membrane is manufactured using a material made with polymers that conducts ion, 
but not electricity, and is resistance against heat and chemicals. The membrane material 
includes H+ ions in motion. The electrode is conducting and is designed such that the fuel 
supplied is in contact with the catalyst and the electrolyte at the same time. The catalyst layer 
is made up of carbon particles that are coated with platinum nanoparticles that conduct 
electrons and can resist heat [19]. The catalysts are present on both the anode and the cathode, 
but as the reaction rate at the cathode is significantly slower than the reaction rate at the anode, 
the platinum loading is more prominent on the cathode [10]. The gas diffusion layer is a layer 
that can conduct electrons and is made of permeable carbon materials. Gasses and fuels are 
==PAGE:17==
 
 15 
able to flow through the gas diffusion layer to the catalyst. It discharges water and helps with 
the conductivity between the catalyst and bipolar plates.  
 
1.5 Master’s Thesis Objectives and Problem formulation 
With the discovery of the phosphoric acid (PA) doped polybenzimidazole (PBI) 
electrolyte in 1995 by Wainright et al. [14], many studies have been conducted on developing 
and implementing the technology into HTPEM fuel cells. Throughout the last decades, studies 
have been conducted using different protocols and methods. However, the objective has been 
the same: to perfect this technology by improving durability, enhancing performance levels, 
and better understanding variable dependencies. 
With this being said, the aim of the research conducted in this master’s thesis is to 
examine the HTPEM fuel cell degradation behavior due to acid loss when testing Dapozol® 
21cm2 MEAs with constant production parameters at different one-cell AST protocols. The 
electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) characterization procedure is crucial to this 
endeavor. So, this thesis uses EIS to examine the efficiency of HTPEM fuel cells, which will 
be analysed conjunctly with polarisation curves. The aim is to understand how different 
protocols and variables affect the degradation of phosphoric acid within the cell. Determining 
these factors will help further research and enable improvements in the functionality of 
HTPEM fuel cells. Parameters like temperature, stoichiometry, and current density will be 
investigated. 
Further objectives include identifying durability issues for HTPEM fuel cell, 
demonstrating the ability for project planning, learning lab skills, identifying safety issues for 
fuel cell research, developing protocols for the project that can result in better alternative 
procedures, and learning research disciplines.

Chapter 2: 
Degradation and Acid Loss  
The technology of the HTPEM fuel cell and its most recent developments in acid loss and 
degradation are discussed and explained in this section. 
2.1 Degradation mechanisms and protocols 
Fuel cells with PBI membranes have shown excellent performance results in several 
studies, showing a durability of 17,000 hours of operation [20]. However, the rate at which a 
fuel cell degrades corresponds to the conditions it runs under. Therefore, finding optimal 
operating conditions and strategies is essential. The degradation of HTPEM fuel cells with PBI 
membranes is mainly caused by unwanted reactions and corrosive environments, which are 
affected by the operational cycling of high to low cell voltages and fluctuations in humidity 
and temperature [20]. Literature studies show that the main issues are in catalyst agglomeration, 
which affects the total catalyst active area, and the phosphoric acid leaking from within the 
cell, affecting its conductivity [21], [22].  
High potentials are the main cause of catalyst degradation. When running a fuel cell at 
the OCV, the platinum (Pt) particle agglomeration is increased due to Pt oxidation and carbon 
corrosion within the cell [20]. Furthermore, agglomeration is caused by coalescence of Pt 
nanoparticles from the carbon corrosions [23]. In a 244.5 h durability test, Qi et al.[24] 
discovered that running an HTPEM fuel cell at OCV for an extended period resulted in OCV 
decrease and efficiency loss. As evidenced by X-ray diffraction, the decrease in efficiency was 
linked to the growth in the size of the Platinum crystallite. 
Literature on the durability and degradation of HTPEM fuel cells mainly focuses on 
comparing steady and cycling operations [20]. A specific protocol procedure is developed to 
test a specific degradation mechanism because many degradation mechanisms occur 
simultaneously, making it hard to differentiate the causes. For example, load cycling with 
constant temperature and hydrogen flow has proven to be the leading cause of catalyst 
degradation [20], [23]. 
Taccani et al. [20] explain that conducting Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) 
tests benefit understanding degradation in the MEA. The increased hydrophilic properties of 
the cathode, which reduces the acid inside the fuel cell, is caused by carbon corrosion in 
addition to catalyst aggregation [20]. A decrease in acid leads to lower conductivity levels in 
the electrode and a decrease in the TPB zone. The TPB zone is crucial for designing and 
==PAGE:19==
 
 17 
optimizing electrochemical devices such as fuel cells. It is the zone at which the electrolyte 
phase, catalysts, and gas pores converge. Increasing the TPB zone's coverage area can speed 
up electrochemical reactions and enhance the device's functionality [25]. 
2.2 Acid Loss 
2.2.1 Acid doping level and ohmic Loss 
HTPEM fuel cells are always directly compared to LTPEM fuel cells. Concerning 
ohmic resistance, HTPEM fuel cells have comparatively greater ohmic resistance than its 
competitor. Ohmic loss is a decrease in voltage brought on by a resistance in the conductive 
properties of the cell's membrane and electrodes [6]. The conductivity of protons in phosphoric 
acid-doped PBI membranes is determined by the acid doping level (ADL). ADL, in this case, 
is referred to as the amount of acid absorbed by the membrane to create highly proton 
conductive sites, and it is defined as the acid molecule's molar number per PBI unit [26]. At 
the start of this technology, low molecular weight PBI was used, and the ADL was 5-6 H3PO4 
per polymer repeat unit (PRU). The ADL nowadays is 11–12 H3PO4 per PRU using high 
molecular weight polymers [27]. Furthermore, it was researched that the lower level of ADL, 
the higher the ohmic and charge transfer resistance, which causes a voltage drop [28]. The 
increase in ADL over the years does result in a higher proton conductivity within the 
membrane; however, it affects the membrane's ability to withstand mechanical stresses and 
strain [16]. A study conducted on the excess phosphoric acid impact on HTPEM fuel cells by 
Matar et al. [29] concluded that significant performance degradations were recorded when 
utilizing a phosphoric acid-doped PBI-based MEA from a commercial source, as presented on 
Figure 2.1.  
 
==PAGE:20==
 
 18 
Figure 2.1 Comparison of voltage over time of MEAs with and without acid pre-treatment. 
The experiment was conducted at 140℃ and 200 mA/cm2 current density for both MEAs. [29] 
 
The molecules from the acid have the potential to reduce the membrane's robustness 
while raising its gas permeability, as the acid lowers the polymer's intermolecular forces 
because the space between the polymer backbones is lengthened [30]. Therefore, obtaining a 
precise ADL is highly important when assessing membrane production.  
 
2.2.2 Acid dehydration 
Acid dehydration is a frequent occurrence in HTPEM fuel cells that can result in the loss 
of proton conductivity and decreased fuel cell efficiency. The water molecules in the fuel cell 
environment can evaporate at high operating temperatures and low relative humidity, depriving 
the acid molecules of the hydration they require to transmit protons. Dehydration and the 
formation of aggregates or clusters in the acid molecules, as a result, may decrease their 
mobility and proton conductivity [31]. 
Despite phosphoric acid's favorable intrinsic thermodynamic properties, it will dehydrate 
at high temperatures due to condensation, yielding water. However, it is not desirable to have 
a combination of acid and water in the cell because it could lead to adverse changes in the 
==PAGE:21==
 
 19 
internal conductivity of the membrane and changes in phase equilibrium. However, it is 
difficult to solve since water is created in the cell's reaction [27]. The phosphoric acid's 
dehydration fluctuates depending on the temperature, current density, and reactant 
stoichiometry, leading to variations in concentration and dispersion within the MEA. 
Therefore, it is appropriate to anticipate that such changes may impact how quickly the MEA 
ages [32]. 
Phosphoric acid condensation is successfully controlled for fuel cells using reformatted 
fuel, which frequently contains a sizable portion of leftover water. Nevertheless, condensation 
can happen if the fuel cell is run on dry hydrogen fuel with a low lambda, mainly when 
operating at OCV since the cathode is dry [27]. The membrane may contract and lose its 
mechanical stability due to acid dehydration, resulting in mechanical issues [33]. Zhang et al. 
[34] found that hydrating HTPEM fuel cells can positively affect performance, but establishing 
a good ratio of water to phosphoric acid is difficult. When water is added to fuels, ohmic 
resistance loss is improved, and the conductivity at the anode increases.  
Furthermore, acid dehydration can change the conductivity of the cell due to the formation 
of water-acid compositions [16]. When phosphoric acid is dehydrated at high temperatures, it

2.2.3 Acid Evaporation 
Another way of acid loss that results in decreased fuel cell efficiency and durability is acid 
evaporation. Water molecules comprise most of the gas phase above a phosphoric acid (PA) 
mixture. In other words, water is predominant in the vapor phase, even if other substances may 
be found in the mixture. The amount of acid in the vapor phase has been calculated to be 
roughly 0.7 μbar at fuel cells operating at 160 °C and 3.7 μbar at 190 °C [16], as seen in Figure 
2.2. The evaporation mechanism predominates in acid loss from HTPEM fuel cells. The acid 
loss may be quantified through the residual from the acid evaporated in gasses. The acid 
evaporation rate rises five-fold from 160°C to 190°C and can be estimated using the acid vapor 
pressure and corresponding temperature [16]. The acid loss from phosphoric acid evaporation 
is not insignificant when considering the substantial airflow over a few years. The acid loss

appears minimal at a load value of 200 mA/cm2 and low stoichiometry at 150–160 °C [27]. 
However, the increase in acid vapor pressure has a substantial temperature-dependent pattern, 
proving that evaporation is the primary process causing acid to be lost [35]. The acid loss 
caused by evaporation suggests that the temperature at which the cell operates and gas lambda 
have a significant effect [36]. In addition, Oono et al. [28] also states that phosphoric acid 
evaporation increases as temperature increases, where a cell running at 150°C has an acid 
evaporation loss of 0.6 μgm-2s-1 compared to 1.8 and 9 μgm-2s-1 for cells operating at 170°C 
and 190°C, respectively.

2.2.4 Acid mobility 
When assembling an MEA, the phosphoric acid moves from the membrane onto the 
catalyst layers [37]. Therefore, break-in periods are essential for HTPEM PBI fuel cells as it 
takes a few days for the fuel cell to reach an operational steady state [38]. In addition, the 
catalyst layer's microporous characteristics are essential to the break-in process as phosphoric 
acid is drawn into the electrode from the membrane due to capillary forces [39]. The phosphoric 
acid is believed to be redistributed in the MEA when operating in the break-in period, which 
==PAGE:23==
 
 21 
was found to be roughly two weeks in Kwon et al. [40] research. Therefore, two weeks are 
needed before the cell reaches a state and operates at a stable voltage. The same study found 
that when cells operate at high current densities, the highest voltage is achieved somewhere in 
the middle of the breaking procedure. On the other hand, when using lower current density 
protocols, the voltage level will gradually increase during the period. In addition, at the end of 
the break-in periods for high current densities, mass-transfers limitations start to affect the cell. 
It is thought that some of the phosphoric acid inside the electrodes begins to obstruct the pores 
of the catalytic layer since the electrodes do not include proton-conducting polymers [40]. 
Furthermore, the ability of the phosphoric acid to move around inside the membrane 
increases acid loss [41]. Research done by Yu et al. [35] and Wannek et al. [37] found that 
phosphoric acid loss was much higher as the current density increased. The acid loss will 
decrease the conductivity within the membrane, increasing the cell's resistance. At protocols 
with a current density higher than 200 A cm−2, phosphoric acid can infiltrate the anode side's 
catalyst and gas diffusion layers [42]. A study by Eberhardt et al. [43] showed that the mobility 
of phosphoric when moving from the cathode to the anode occurred when the protocol current 
density was high. In contrast, when the protocol current density was low, it went the opposite 
way from the anode to the cathode, proven to be linked to the electrochemical pumping 
phenomena. Hence, current density favors phosphoric acid migration. 
2.2.5 Acid operation lifetime 
The lifetime of HTPEM fuel cells is affected by the cell's functioning temperature and the 
phosphoric acid inside the MEA [32]. According to reports, the carbon support and the polymer 
membrane will degrade more quickly at high temperatures [28]. In addition, phosphoric acid 
increases the acidity of the MEA, harshening performance conditions [44]. HTPEM fuel cells 
with protocols that include 160 °C with a max current density of 600 mA/cm2, have enough 
acid to last 50000 hours [27]. When protocols include a higher temperature like 190°C and 
increased gas flow rates, 90% of the phosphoric acid is lost, and the operation lifetime falls to 
2830 hours [45]. Considering the MEAs are gradually nearing their equilibrium, and a portion 
of surplus acid is dissipated first, the recorded acid loss rate is often greater early than later, as 
seen on Figure 2.3.

2.2.6 Acid possible improvements 
Studies such as Chen et al.[47] investigated the effects of decreasing the specific 
resistance of HTPEM fuel cells by making membranes thinner. However, the thinner the 
membrane, the less acid is available. In addition, the limited phosphoric acid may move into 
the electrodes, decreasing the durability. A way to counter the problem would be to utilize 
membranes with high ADL or phosphoric acid pre-imbibed electrodes [27]. In reference to 
lifetime degradation caused by excess phosphoric acid within the membrane, a pre-treatment 
developed by Matar et al. [29], can improve lifetime, as discussed previously. 
Washing excess phosphoric acid occurs when water encounters it. Therefore, water 
condensation during the operation should be avoided at all moments for the cell's protection. 
In a study conducted by Lee et al. [48] the membrane capacity to hold acidic chemicals using 
specific polymers that have been altered with quaternary ammonium (QA). This change has 
been demonstrated to result in pairings of ions known as QA-H2PO4 that improve phosphoric 
acid retention. Furthermore, when exposed to various humidity levels in an inert environment, 
these modified membranes retained acidic compounds better than other PBI membranes.

The characterization of HTPEM fuel cells is accomplished in this thesis by performing 
and analyzing polarization curves, EIS, and durability curves. An overview of each diagnostic 
tool is provided. 
3.1 Polarization Curves 
The most common electrochemical method for assessing HTPEM fuel cells is to graph 
the cell voltage against the current density using an array of fixed operating parameters, as seen 
on Figure 3.1 [6], [49]. Information about the entire fuel cell can be obtained from this method 
[50]. It provides data on the efficiency and if there is any decrease in performance when it is 
running. Plotting the current density versus the cell voltage can get a constant-state polarization 
curve. Specific variables, including the effects of gas stoichiometry (lambda), flow rate, 
and temperature on cell efficiency, can be identified and evaluated methodically by analyzing 
such curves [49]. 
 
Figure 3.1 Diagram and analysis of a theoretical polarization curve showing the relevant 
overpotentials and areas [49]. 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 25 
There are three main zones on the optimal polarization curve for an HTPEM fuel cell 
running on hydrogen and air [49]. The first zone is at low current densities, where the oxygen 
reduction reaction's (ORR) slow kinetics is the main cause for the fuel cell voltage to drop 
dramatically [51]. The second zone is in the mid-range of current density, where the ohmic 
resistance causes a considerable potential loss that primarily stems from obstructions to the 
transport of electrons across the electrode [49]. As a result, the voltage decreases inversely and 
linearly with the current density at mid-ranges. Finally, the cell voltage significantly declines 
in the third and last zone because of the mass transport limit across the gas diffusion and 
electrocatalyst layers [51]. 
Many mathematical formulas were introduced to simulate the polarization curves. In 
addition, modeling investigations have been conducted to clarify the electrochemical processes 
of PEM fuel cells. The relationship between current density and cell potential up until the mass 
transport effects, where the mechanism is affected by ohmic and activation loss, was derived 
by Ticianelli et al. in 1988 [52]. The derived equation is shown below: 
 
𝐸= 𝐶−𝑏log(𝑖) −𝑅𝑖 (18) 
 
Where 𝐸 is the cell potential, 𝐶 is a constant, 𝑏 is the Tafel slope, 𝑅 is the resistance 
responsible for the linear slope in polarization curves, and 𝑖 is the current density [52]. This 
equation assumes that mass transport is negligible. 
 
3.2  Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) 
EIS is a non-destructive technique that offers valuable information on the performance 
of a fuel cell and its components without disrupting the system's stability. EIS is an appropriate 
and effective diagnostic testing approach for fuel cells. However, the real benefit of this 
technique is its capacity to separate the distinct contributions of the mass transport resistances 
and the interfacial charge transfer resistances in the diffusion layer and catalyst layer [53]. To 
execute EIS, voltage signals must be applied to the fuel cell at various frequencies. The 
impedance at each frequency is then determined by comparing the emitted to the response 
signals [54]. The reaction by the fuel cell's alternative current (AC) delivers further knowledge 
about the system under investigation. By conducting a frequency-dependent impedance test, it 
is possible to describe the system's physical properties of the electrochemical setup with an 
==PAGE:28==
 
 26 
equivalent circuit (EC). Then, it is feasible to distinguish between the individual voltage losses 
of the various components (namely the anode, cathode, electrolyte, and possibly even the 
diffusion processes) to determine which limiting processes affect the fuel cell’s performance 
[50]. Many studies have used EIS to optimize the development of membrane electrode 
assembly (MEA) [50], [53], [55], [56]. Since the experiment conducted in this study uses pure 
hydrogen in the anode, then the impedance at the cathode mainly monopolizes the PEM fuel 
cell impedance [49], [56]. The following figure shows the standard spectrum of a PEM fuel 
cell:

4.2 The MEA  
The MEAs provided by Blue World Technologies for this thesis are Dapozol® 5x5cm2 
with a thickness of about 560 μm. The loading of Platinum in the PtCo cathode catalyst is 1.02 
mgPt/cm2, while the loading of Platinum in the Pt anode catalyst is 0.9 mgPt/cm2. The PBI 
membrane was doped in 12 mol of phosphoric acid per PBI mol, is about 70 μm doped-
thickness, and was placed between a pair of electrodes with a surface area of 21 cm2. The 
electrodes were manufactured using a slot die coating technology to add a catalyst layer to the 
gas diffusion layer, followed by a 2-hour heat treatment at 220℃. A polymeric reinforcement, 
Kapton of 50 μm, was added by compression of the membrane at a high temperature between 
the electrodes. Two 200 μm thick aluminum plates were used to regulate the compression ratio. 
4.3 Fuel Cell Assembly 
Each HTPEM fuel cell was put together with the various components layered together, 
as shown in Figure 4.2. The MEA is the core of the fuel cell in the middle of the setup, while 
the remaining parts are distributed in symmetrical pairings on each side of the MEA. The 
assembly starts with an aluminum endplate (1), followed by a rubber gasket (2) between the 
current collector (3) and the endplate. The rubber gasket is used to isolate the electrodes from 
the endplate. Then a Papyex flexible graphite paper of 500 μm thickness (4) is placed between 
the current collector and the bipolar plate (5). After, a PTFE gasket of 150 μm thickness (6) is 
placed between the bipolar plates and the MEA (7). The same procedure is mirrored on the 
other side of the MEA. Finally, the cell is clamped together using eight bolts (8).

6.1.5 Temperature Effects: Summary and Discussion 
 
The break-in period for fuel cells operating at 190°C took less time to reach stability than 
those operating at 160°C. The increasing trend in the ohmic resistance loss across time and the 
decreasing trend in the change in voltage indicates that when the temperature rises, the ohmic 
resistance loss increases, and the output total voltage decreases. This agrees with Søndergaard 
et al.’s [36] research, where it was found that fuel cells operating at higher temperatures will 
degrade faster. This trend is most likely caused by electrolyte dehydration and acid evaporation. 
The viscosity of the electrolyte rises with dehydration, making it more difficult for molecules 
of PA to migrate. In addition, the degree of acid deprotonation by water will also be reduced 
when there is less water, preventing electrolyte ions from forming. As a result, the overall 
conductivity will be lower [36]. Furthermore, Aili et al. [16] determined that for fuel cells 
running at 160°C and 190°C, there is around 0.7 μbar and 3.7 μbar of acid in the vapor phase, 
respectively, supporting that more acid is lost at higher temperatures. Furthermore, Oono et al. 
[28] found that a cell running at 150℃ reached its maximum voltage output after several 
hundred hours and is only at a 7% max voltage output after two years. Another cell running at 
170℃ reached its maximum voltage output a few hundred hours and was terminated at 6400 
hours. Finally, another cell running at 190℃ instantly reached maximum voltage output and 
was terminated after 1220 hours of operation.  
When analyzing the kinetic resistance loss obtained from impedance tests, a stable and 
constant trend is found for all MEAs, suggesting no increase in kinetic resistance loss within 
the cell. When analyzing the polarization curves, there is no significant change in resistance. 
The exchange current density at zero overpotential and Tafel slope are constant throughout the 
twenty-seven days, suggesting no kinetic change in the electrochemical process. Therefore, this 
further supports that most resistance found within the cell is ohmic resistance, most likely 
caused by acid loss. Eberhardt et al. [45] used a starting acid concentration of 36 mgH3PO4/cm2 
and reached the finding that just HTPEM fuel cells performing at 160°C with a maximum 
current density of 600 mA/cm2 could maintain a sufficient acid supply for the targeted lifespan 
of 50000 hours. Because of acid loss, that lifespan is complicated to accomplish at greater 
temperatures or current densities. Upon reaching a 70%–80% acid loss, a substantial change in 
ohmic resistance could be taking place, and it was found that 90% of the acid evaporated within 
2830 hours when protocols of 190°C and high flow rates were used [36], [45]. Thus, overall, 
==PAGE:58==
 
 56 
the acid loss brought on by the evaporation mechanism indicates that the cell's operating 
temperature is a key factor [16].

7.1.4 Lambda Effects: Summary and Discussion  
 
The difference between the change in ohmic resistance loss and the difference in 
stoichiometry between the MEAs is consistent if the results of C0-40 are compared to either 
C0-30 or C0-27. As air lambda is increased, the larger the increase in ohmic resistance loss. 
Similarly, the difference between the change in voltage output and the difference in 
stoichiometry between the MEAs is consistent. As air lambda is increased, the larger the 
decrease in voltage output. These results are supported by Søndergaard et al.’s [36] research 
stating that as gas-flow rates increase, the degradation rate also increases. The most likely cause 
would be acid evaporation. It is hypothesized that low air lambda results in slower evaporation 
and electrode activation. On the other hand, increased air lambda ought to accelerate the acid's 
evaporation throughout its lifetime [36]. Furthermore, Galbiati et al.'s [32] research states that 
an increased stoichiometry helps remove more water, dehydrating the MEA and carrying the 
acid vapor out of the cell, which increases the degradation rate. Furthermore, when kinetic 
resistance data from impedance testing are analyzed, a stable and constant trend is discovered 
for all MEAs, indicating no increase in kinetic resistance loss within the cell. There is no 
significant change in resistance calculated from polarization curves over time. The exchange 
current density at zero overpotential and Tafel slope are constant throughout the twenty-seven 
days, suggesting no kinetic change in the electrochemical process for all the MEAs. This 
further supports that the degradation is caused by acid loss via evaporation or dehydration. 
Unfortunately, the results obtained in this thesis aren’t defining enough. Experiments would 
have to be conducted again for C0-30 and C0-27, and for a longer period of time for C0-28 and 
C0-26. Thus, the conclusion on acid loss is mostly based on theory.

8.1.4 Current Density Effects: Summary and Discussion 
 
The disparity between the MEAs' differences in current density and change in ohmic 
resistance loss is consistent. The increase in ohmic resistance loss is proportional to the increase 
in current density. The difference between the MEAs' current density and the change in output 
voltage is also consistent. The magnitude of the decline in voltage output increases with 
increasing current density. The trend for this experiment is very evident and supports the 
argument that larger current densities increase the mobility of electrolyte ions, which will 
increase humidity [32], [36]. Consequently, PA will accumulate in the anode due to 
the phosphate anions that relocate and react with the protons present in the anode. Becker et al. 
[31] found that the ohmic resistance loss decreases near the anode when phosphoric acid builds 
up in the anode and reduces in the cathode. In contrast, it considerably rises on the cathode 
side. This results in acid loss in the cell and increased cell degradation because of evaporation 
and diffusion [36]. When current density is higher than 200 mA/cm2, PA has also been found 
to permeate the anode side's catalyst and gas diffusion layers [42]. Yu et al. [35] and Wannek 
et al. [37] also found that degradation due to phosphoric acid loss increases as the current 
density increases. Eberhardt et al. [43] demonstrated that the electrochemical pumping 
phenomenon was associated with the movement of phosphorous to the anode from the cathode 
occurring at high current densities. Thus, migration of phosphoric acid is favored by current 
density. Furthermore, impedance testing data on kinetic resistance loss shows a consistent and 
constant trend for all MEAs, showing that the kinetic resistance loss inside the cell has not been 
affected much. In addition, the resistance obtained from polarization curves increases faster the 
higher the current density. Tafel slope and the exchange current density at zero overpotential 
remain constant over the twenty-seven days, indicating that there has been no kinetic change 
in the electrochemical process for any of the MEAs.  
In addition, to support the claim that the current density is the main mechanism affecting 
the fuel cell’s performance in this experiment, a graphical comparison was made between 
MEAs C0-42 and C0-40, shown in Figure 8.4. Both cells operate at 190℃ and the same airflow

Chapter 9: 
Conclusion and Future Work 
Concluding remarks, main findings, discussions, and suggestions for future complimentary 
studies are presented in this section. 
 
With the growing interest in HTPEM fuel cells comes more responsibility in understanding 
and improving this technology. This thesis focused on better understanding how different 
parameters affect acid loss and the durability of a phosphoric acid-doped PBI membrane 
HTPEM fuel cell. Three experiments were conducted where three parameters were studied: the 
effects of temperature, air lambda (stoichiometry), and current density. These experiments 
included analyzing data from polarization curves, ohmic and kinetic resistance losses, and the 
drop in voltage output over time. Blue World Technologies provided the MEAs used, called 
Dapozol®, and all MEAs were from the same batch for improved consistency. In order to 
perform those investigations, different diagnostic tools were used: electrochemical impedance 
spectroscopy (EIS), polarization curves, and durability curves. 
In Results (II), Temperature Effects, it was found that MEA break-in periods are more 
extended for 160℃ protocols than for 190℃ protocols, which influenced many results in this 
thesis since each cell was run for twenty-seven days. Nonetheless, increasing the temperature 
of the fuel cells showed a strong indication of acid evaporation. Furthermore, as the 
temperature was increased, the ohmic resistance loss increased at a faster rate, and the voltage 
output decreased at a faster rate. Additionally, the kinetic resistance loss was stable and in a 
constant trend throughout all temperature protocols. Lastly, the resistance calculated from 
polarization curves, the current density at zero overpotential, and the Tafel slope were constant 
and did not take much effect during the procedure, suggesting that the electrochemical kinetics 
of the fuel cell were not affected much.  
In Results (III), Lambda Effects, the expected trend was that as air lambda was 
increased while other protocols were kept constant, more degradation would be measured. 
However, this was different for this report. At 190℃, air lambda 5 showed a higher degradation 
in output voltage than air lambda 10; however, at air lambda 20, the degradation was the largest. 
At 160℃, the expected result was seen, and air lambda 5 showed a lower degradation rate than 
air lambda 10. In contrast, as air lambda was increased, the ohmic resistance loss increased at 
a faster rate. Whereas the kinetic resistance loss was stable and in a constant trend throughout 
all air lambda values, similar to experiment 1. The resistance derived from polarization curves, 
==PAGE:74==
 
 72 
the current density at zero overpotential, and the Tafel slope remained steady and had no impact 
during the process, indicating that the electrochemical kinetics of the fuel cell was not 
significantly impacted.  
Results (IV), Current Density Effects, had the most evident trend out of all experiments 
in this thesis. The increase in current density showed a great indication of acid loss due to acid 
mobility. Furthermore, the observed trends were that as current density increases, while all 
other parameters are kept constant, the ohmic resistance loss increases faster while the output 
voltage decreases faster. The protocol at 800 mA/cm2 reached the end of its life on day 19, 
while others lasted over 27 days. However, the kinetic resistance loss was constant and barely 
changed throughout the experiments, suggesting that its ohmic part mainly affected the total 
resistance. In addition, the resistance from polarization curves also increased faster as current 
density was increased; however, the current density at zero overpotential and the Tafel slope 
remained steady throughout the twenty-seven days, suggesting no kinetic change within the 
fuel cell.  
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and acid titration tests were not conducted due 
to the time constraints of this thesis. Therefore, future work for this thesis includes conducting 
SEM and acid titration for selected MEAs to support results further. In addition, all experiments 
should be repeated, especially for experiment 2, to ensure all trends are consistent. Moreover, 
a solution must be found for the different break-in stability periods. Lastly, an extra temperature 
investigation should be added for the Temperature Effects experiment, either at 180℃ or 
200℃. In addition, Søndergaard et al. [36] also state that in regards to acid evaporation, a better 
study to conduct would be to measure the temperature of the MEA itself (in-situ) rather than 
the temperature at the end plate because the temperature is greater in the MEA. Moreover, more 
protocol comparisons should be included in the flow rate effect Figure 8.4 to help emphasize 
the trend, such as an MEA running at 190℃ at 400 mA/cm2 and air lambda 10. Lastly, to 
improve the trends seen in this report, running the fuel cells for longer than 27 days when 
repeating experiments would help enforce the measured trends and provide more precise 
results.
