Abstract
The problem that will be investigated in this thesis is how to do segmentation with
deep learning and uncertainty quantiﬁcation on a confocal microscopy pancreas
dataset with ambiguous images due to noise.
Firstly, a learning process of how to segment images with deep learning, use PyTorch
and the HPC cluster has been done. Then, the investigation of how to quantify
uncertainty of segmentation has led to the methods of the Ensemble U-Net, Dropout
U-Net and Probabilistic U-Net.
The methods were implemented and validated by several test measures such as the
Dice coeﬃcient, sensitivity and speciﬁcity. The segmentation of a lung abnormality
data set gave good results, whereas the task of quantifying the uncertainty of the
segmentations by the Ensemble U-Net and the Dropout U-Net was more tedious.
It has been concluded that most of the networks needed considerably more training in
order to get the segmentation of the images and corresponding uncertainty
quantiﬁcation that we wished for.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The main problem that will be investigated in this thesis is how to do uncertainty
quantiﬁcation on a confocal microscopy pancreas dataset with ambiguous images due
to noise. The biologists who ﬁlmed the videos for the dataset, which we segment as
2D image slices, are interested in quantitatively conﬁrming that the topological events
happening during development of the tubular structure is related to diﬀerentiation of
beta cells nearby, which requires a binary segmentation of the tubes. However, the
images are very noisy as the images are recorded with a low signal to noise ratio,
creating a diﬃcult segmentation task. Moreover, the annotation of the images is
tedious, even for a trained expert. Since it is a diﬃcult task to annotate 3D images,
the annotation will be faulty. This is why we want to know the uncertainty of the
segmentations. Knowing the conﬁdence with which we can trust the segmentation
output is vital for the decision making.
This project started with having little to no prior knowledge of the concepts of, let
alone having never worked with, neural networks, image segmentation in deep
learning, uncertainty quantiﬁcation, Pytorch, Unix commands and High Performance
Computing cluster service from DTU. Therefore, we will at ﬁrst take a look at the
very basics of the a neural networks works, which loss functions has been used
throughout the project and how testing scores for the neural network are computed.
A Encoder-Decoder Segmentation Net and U-Net is implemented for image
segmentation, in a learning-by-doing manner, to practically understand the concepts
of image segmentation with deep learning. The U-Net is run on the Confocal
Microscopy Pancreas dataset, while both networks are run on a lung abnormality
dataset. We take the lung abnormality dataset, also referred to as the LIDC-IDRI
dataset, into consideration as it is fairly easy to segment, and thereby a good dataset
in order to get familiar with the methods.
In order to solve the problem with the uncertainty of the segmentations, a variety of
uncertainty quantiﬁcation methods are investigated. We look into the Ensemble
U-Net, the Dropout U-Net and the Probabilistic U-Net. The Dropout U-Net has been
chosen to be implemented and validated for the Confocal Microscopy Pancreas
dataset in order to solve the issue with uncertainty quantiﬁcation.

Datasets
For this project, two data-sets has been taking into consideration, when working with
the networks. The ﬁrst dataset we are going to describe is the LIDC-IDRI, which is
lung CT scans with four annotations diﬀerent annotation from four experts. The
reason why we use this dataset is to reproduce the results from the A Probabilistic

This dataset contains more ambiguous images and is hard to segment and annotate.
Therefore we are much interested in doing uncertainty quantiﬁcation of the
segmentations for this dataset.
2.1
LIDC-IDRI Dataset
In this project we use the LIDC-IDRI dataset. The dataset contains 1018 lung CT
scans from 1010 patients with annotations of lesions by four experts. Independently,
each expert manually segmented what they detected and considered to be abnormal.
After annotating their own CT scans, the radiologists where shown anonymized
annotations of the others, and were allowed to make adjustments to their own masks
[1].
Figure 1: The LIDC-IDRI dataset. The ﬁrst column shows the input image, and the remaining columns the
ground truth segmentations provided by the 4 graders.
In the dataset, The CT scans have been resampled into 0.5mm × 0.5mm in-plane
resolution and cropped 2D images (128 × 128) centered at the locations that the
experts segmented as lesions. Figure 1 shows an example of the lesion
centered-images and the masks provided by the 4 radiologists.

2.2
Confocal Microscopy Pancreas Dataset
We study the problem of segmenting tubular structures in the pancreas using the
confocal microscopy pancreas dataset [2]. The images are recorded with low signal to
noise ratio, creating a challenging segmentation task, even for human experts due to
the noise in the images [2]. The dataset is conﬁdential and it is only uploaded to the
DTU server. Therefore, all code has been run on the High Performance Computing
cluster service.
We need a binary segmentation of the images in order to quantitatively conﬁrm
topological events happening during development of the tubular structure, in which
may be related to diﬀerentiation of beta cells nearby. The developmental biologists
want to observe that during embryonic development, the pancreatic tubes grow from
a web-like network with loops to a tree-like structure without loops [2].
The dataset consists of 2D image slices extracted from 3D image stacks, which are
obtained live from a confocal microscopy. The live imaging follows the development of
the pancreas of mouse embryos imaged for 40-48 hours [2]. In Figure 2, two time
points for the mouse embryos are depicted next to the two manual segmentations of
an expert. To get a better look at the images, the time points are depicted with a
maximum intensity projection.
Figure 2: The Confocal Microscopy Pancreas Dataset. Left column: The original images for two time points
with a maximum intensity projection are depicted. Right column: The manual segmentations by the expert
for the two time points.
The images have a resolution of 1024 × 1024 × N, where N varies between 27 and 40.
This dataset contains 6 time-points and by stacking these a dataset with a total of
174 images with corresponding 174 annotations is obtained. We split the dataset into
a test set, training set and validation set each holding 88, 43 and 43 images
respectively.

The Neural Network
One of the project goals is to understand the basic principles of image segmentation
with deep learning. In the following we will describe the basics of the neural network
and further describe how the convolutional neural network works in order to set the
scene for the neural network methods used in the project.
3.1
The Basics of the Neural Network
This section is dedicated to explain the very basics of the neural network. We start
from scratch by explaining how a neural network work from forward propagation,
through training and onto backpropagation. In between we explain which loss
function is used and how the testing has been conducted.
3.1.1
Forward Propagation and Equations
The artiﬁcial neural network consists of a number of neurons. A neuron is a
mathematical function, which takes several numbers as inputs. For each input xd
with d = 1, . . . , D being the index of the input, the neuron assigns a weight wd. These
weights are what makes a neuron unique. They are ﬁxed during testing, but change
during training in order to tune the network, This will be discussed in later sections
(section 3.1.3, 3.1.4 3.1.5). The simplest model consists of one neuron and is called a
Perceptron, see ﬁgure 3.
Figure 3: Single Perceptron (proposed by Frank Rosenblatt in 1957).
We are now going to consider a modiﬁed version of the Perceptron. As already
mentioned, neurons are functions. These functions are linear combinations of weights
and inputs with some non-linear function on top of it. The linear functions can be
formulated as
y(x, w) = w0 + w1x1 + . . . + wDxD,
(3.1)
i.e. we take the inputs and multiply them by the corresponding weights and sum
everything together. The result is a number. Then we apply some non-linearity,
usually the rectiﬁed linear unit (ReLU)
ReLU(x) = max(x, 0).
(3.2)
This non-linear function applied on the linear neuron is called an activation function.
Hence the ﬁnal formula for the neuron will be
y(x, w) = max(0, w0 + w1x1 + . . . + wDxD).
(3.3)

3.1.3
Network Training
When we train the network, we want to minimize the loss function in order to get
more accurate network. We start oﬀthe training of the neural network by initializing
the weights randomly, which will obviously not yield good results. Thus, in the
process of training we start with a bad performing neural network and, hopefully,
wind up with a neural network with high accuracy and low loss.
We use the adaptive learning rate optimization algorithm, Adam, which has been
designed speciﬁcally for training deep neural networks [6], to minimize the loss. The
Adam optimizer uses the squared gradients of the loss to scale the learning rate and
takes advantage of the momentum by using the moving average of the gradient. The
optimizer is as mentioned an adaptive leaning rate method, which means that it
computes individual rates for diﬀerent parameters [6]. It uses the ﬁrst and the second
moment of the gradient to adapt the learning rate for each weight of the neural
network. The N’th moment of a random variable is given by
Mn = E[Xn].
(3.18)
We consider the gradient of the loss function of the neural network as a random
variable. The neural network is usually evaluated on the same small random batch of
the data, as it requires less memory and the network usually trains faster with
mini-batches. A mini-batch is a batch size which is smaller than the full dataset. The
batch size deﬁnes the number of samples that will be propagated through the
network. A disadvantage of splitting the data into batches can be loss of accuracy
when estimating the gradient, as the network will not have as much data to take into
account.

3.1.4
Error Backpropagation
We want to eﬃciently evaluate the gradient of the error function with respect to the
weights and biases. This is done by a local message scheme in which information is
sent alternately forward and backward through the network, and is known as error
backpropagation.
A step - forward and backward - in the network is called an epoch. The number of
epochs decides how many times we want to update the network. An alternative to
setting a number of epochs is early stopping, which stops the training of the network,
when the validation error stops improving at some given patience. The validation
error is computed by the loss (described in section 3.1.2) of the model given the
validation set.
When the gradients are computed, we adjust the weights by subtracting a small
quantity proportional to the gradient, and then reset the gradients to zero. We set
the gradients to zero in order to recompute everything again until the number of
epochs or the patience of the early stopping stops the training.
3.1.5
Test
In order to be able to evaluate how well our semantic segmentation models perform,
we use the following validation indices: pixel accuracy, Intersection over Union (IoU),

3.2
The Convolutional Neural Network
One simple approach to image segmentation could be to treat the image as the input
to a fully connected network, such as in Figure 4. Given a suﬃciently large training
set, the fully connected network would in principle yield a good solution to this
problem and learn the invariances by example [3]. However, this approach ignores the
key property of images, which is that nearby pixels are more strongly correlated than
distant pixels. This is why we introduce the convolutional neural networks (CNN).
The CNN exploit this property by extracting local features that depend only on small
subregions of the image. The information from the local features can then be merged
in later stages in order to detect higher-order features and provide information about
the image as a whole [3].
Figure 8: Illustration of part of the convolutional neural network. The diagram shows a layer of convolution
units followed by a layer of sub-sampling units [3].
The structure of the CNN is depicted in Figure 8. In the convolutional layer, the
units are organized into feature maps where the units in the feature maps each take
inputs only from a small subregion of the image and are constrained to share the
same weight values. Input values from a patch are linearly combined using the weight
and the bias, and the result is transformed by a sigmoidal nonlinearity using (3.7).
We can think of the units as feature detectors, and thus all of the units in a feature
map detect the same pattern, but at diﬀerent locations in the input image. As we
often have to detect multiple feature in order to have an eﬀective model, there will
generally be multiple feature maps in the convolutional layer, each having its own set
of weight and bias [3].

3.2
The Convolutional Neural Network
The outputs of the convolutional layer form the inputs for the sub-sampling (also
called pooling) layer of the network. There is a plane of units in the pooling for each
of feature map in the convolutional layer, and each unit takes input from a small
receptive ﬁeld in the corresponding feature map of the convolutional layer [3]. In this
project max-pooling is used as sub-sampling. Max-pooling is a pooling operation that
calculates the maximum value for patches of a feature map, and uses it to create a
down-sampled feature map. It has the practical advantages, that it (i) improves
boundary delineation, (ii) reduces the number of parameters enabling end-to-end
training and (iii) is easily translated to other architectures [10].
As we will see later, in practical architecture there are several pairs of convolutional
and pooling layers in a network. At each stage there is a larger degree of invariance to
input transformations compared to the previous layer. The gradual reduction in
resolution is compensated by an increasing number of features as there may be several
feature maps in a given convolutional layer for each plane of units in the previous
sub-sampling layer [3]. The ﬁnal layer of the network is typically a fully connected,
fully adaptive layer with a soft-max output nonlinearity [3, 11, 12].
The convolutional neural network can be trained by error minimization using
backpropagation to evaluate the gradient of the error function [3].

4.1
Encoder-Decoder Segmentation Net
The encoder-decoder segmentation network (E-N SegNet) is used to model
appearance, shape and spatial relationship between classes, while having the ability
to deﬁne objects based on their shape despite their small size. This is done by a
convolutional encoder-decoder architecture for image segmentation [10].
The motivation of the E-N SegNet comes from the need to map low resolution
features to input resolution for pixel-wise classiﬁcation. Also the mapping must
produce features which are useful for accurate boundary localization and also eﬃcient
in pixel-wise semantic segmentation.
The architecture of the E-D Segmentation net has an encoder part and a
corresponding decoder part followed by a ﬁnal pixel-wise soft-max classiﬁcation layer.
This is illustrated in ﬁgure 9. The encoder network has 8 convolutional layers
designed for object classiﬁcation corresponding to ﬁrst 8 layers in a convolutional
network described in section (3.2). Each encoder layer has a corresponding decoder
layer and hence the decoder network has 8 layers. The ﬁnal decoder output is fed to a
binary class soft-max to produce class probabilities for each pixel independently.
Figure 9: Illustration of the Encoder-Decoder Segmentation Network architecture. The encoder produces
down-sample the image by convolution and max-pooling, and the decoder then upsamples its input to produce
a feature map. Then a convolution with trainable ﬁlter bank is performed to upsample the output using the
feature map, and at the end a soft-max functional is applied.

4.2
U-Net
Each encoder in an encoder network performs a convolution in order to produce
feature maps. Then an element-wise rectiﬁed linear non-linearity (ReLU) is applied.
Following that a max-pooling with a 2 × 2 non-overlapping window is performed and
the resulting output is sub-sampled by a factor of 2 [10]. A window deﬁnes the region
that the network looks at, so in this case, it looks at 4 pixels at the time. When it is
non-overlapping, it does not take the same pixels into consideration. The max-pooling
is used to achieve translation invariance over small spatial shifts in the input image,
i.e. if an object in the image shifts position, the network should still be able to detect
it [10]. While we can obtain more translation invariance for a more robust
classiﬁcation by several layers of max-pooling and sub-sampling, it also causes loss of
spatial resolution of the feature maps. Hence, increasingly lossy boundary detail is
not good for image segmentation, since the detection of boundaries is vital [10].
A bottleneck layer is added to reduce the number of feature maps in the network,
which otherwise tend to increase in each layer. This is done by 1 × 1 convolutions
with fewer output channels than input channels.
Then, the appropriate decoder in the decoder network upsamples its input feature
maps from the corresponding encoder feature maps. The feature representation at the
output of the ﬁnal decoder is fed into a soft-max classiﬁer. The output of the
soft-max classiﬁer is a 2 channel image of probabilities, and the predicted
segmentation corresponds to the class with maximum probability at each pixel [10].
4.2
U-Net
The U-Net architecture is build upon the architecture from the encoder-decoder
segmentation network from the previous section. An important modiﬁcation is that
we also have a large number of feature channels in the upsampling part, which allow
the network to propagate context information to higher resolution layers [13]. Hence,
the encoder is more or less symmetric to the decoder and yields a u-shaped
architecture, as illustrated in Figure 10.
The network does not contain any fully connected layers and only uses the valid part
of each convolution. This means that the segmentation map will only contain the
pixels, where the full context is available in the input image [13].
Figure 10: The architecture of the U-Net. The U-Net consists of decoder and encoder part with skip connec-
tions in order to retain the full context available from the input image.

4.3
Ensemble U-Net
The architecture of the U-net consists of an encoder and an a decoder part. The
encoder part follows the same architecture, as discussed in section 4.1. It consists of
the repeated application of two convolutions, each followed by a rectiﬁed linear unit,
and a 2 × 2 max-pooling operation with a stride 2 for downsampling. A bottleneck is
added to lower the number of features, before we start the decoder network. In every
step of the decoder network we upsample and convolute the feature map with each
followed by a ReLU. At the ﬁnal layer, a soft-max classiﬁer is added [13]. All of this is
done with skip-connections in order to retain the contexts information from lower
resolutions to higher resolutions in the encoder-decoder framework.

4.2. However, as the name states, it is an ensemble of U-Nets. We run the Ensemble
U-Net m times, and take the mean and standard deviation of the output from the
last layer. Hence, we have that the deterministic variant of the U-Net is the 1-sample
of the Ensemble U-Net.

in order to measure the uncertainty of a segmentation, the dropout U-net is

(4.1)

posterior
KL(q(W)||p(W|X, Y )).

Mi [14]. The dropout probability is ﬁxed at pi = 0.5 as a standard probability of

objective function has the eﬀect of minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence term
[14]. However, in order to prevent class imbalance in our experiments, the focal loss

DISCUSSION
8
Discussion
In this project we have worked with two datasets; The Lung abnormality dataset
(LIDC-IDRI) and the confocal microscopy pancreas dataset, respectively. The
LIDC-IDRI dataset has mainly been used to learn how the diﬀerent methods for
segmentation and uncertainty quantiﬁcation works, whereas the microscopy pancreas
dataset by the task of segmenting has been used to help biologists quantitatively
conﬁrm topological events happening during development of tubular structures. The
microscopy dataset is very noisy, and even for experts, hard to annotate. That is why
we need methods to quantify the uncertainty of the segmentations we compute.
The ﬁrst task has been to understand how to segment images using deep learning.
This has been done with a Encoder-Decoder Segmentation Net and a U-Net. The
Encoder-Decoder Segmentation net does it fairly well. We obtain a sensitivity, a
speciﬁcity and an accuracy of 0.993541 and a Dice coeﬃcient very close to 1.
The U-Net, however, is not performing as well as expected. Visually we can observe
from the ﬁve examples in ﬁgure 18, that the U-Net does not segment the LIDC-IDRI
well. The test measures agree to this fact, as the sensitivity is 0.514248 and speciﬁcity
is 0.056144. However, the U-Net has only been trained for 600 epochs, and might not
have that time enough to converge.
The same goes for the U-Net segmentation of the Microscopy Pancreas data set as it
has only been trained for 600 epochs. Most remarkable is, that it was a speciﬁcity of
0.0, however given the tediousness of the data, it should be trained much more!
Next we have been looking at ways to quantify the uncertainty when segmenting
images. We need uncertainty to know the conﬁdence with which we can trust that the
segmentation output is important for decision making. For example, for the pancreas
dataset, we want to be sure that it is a topological event we observe - and not noise,
and in which case, what the uncertainty of this is.
The ﬁrst method that was taking into consideration, was the Ensemble U-Net. The
Ensemble U-Net is described in section 4.3, but in short we have the mean of the
prediction of each of the m U-Nets. The uncertainty is calculated by the standard
deviation of the predictions. A complication has been, that we have to run the U-Net
m times, and as we have already seen in the discussion of the U-Net, the U-Net needs
lots of training. The Ensemble has been run with m = 4 times of U-Nets trained by
400 epochs each, with the diﬀerent U-Nets being for the four diﬀerent labels
annotated by the experts. Looking at Figure 20 and Table 4, the Ensemble U-Net, as
well at the U-Nets need more training.
The Dropout U-Net is also able to measure the uncertainty of the segmentation and
is the next method we will discuss. The Dropout U-Net drops nodes in a U-Net with
a probability of 50% but also needs to be trained m times. The dropout U-Net has
been trained with m = 4 and only 300 times each. This results in a accuracy of
0.507875 and a dice coeﬃcient of 0.668884. The dice coeﬃcient is too low to be
acceptable as it gives a measure of the similarity between the predicted segmentation
and the ground and should be close to 1. Again it appears that the network, like the
others, needs substantially more training. We note that the accuracy is not always a
good measure for images with high imbalance in foreground and background.

However, that is the reason why we expect it to be high, and deﬁnitely not as low as
we have seen it for the results presented in this report.
The results from this project has has been aﬀected by time constraints. If time was
not an issue, the networks could have been trained signiﬁcantly longer resulting in

and at ﬁrst, it was assumed that an early stopping at around a patience of 10 would

trained with 335 epochs, which has been shown not to be enough.

CONCLUSION
9
Conclusion
In this project we have been through a process of learning how to segment images
with deep learning and investigating a variety of methods for uncertainty
quantiﬁcation of these segmentations.
Two datasets have been looked into, the lung abnormality data set and the more
tedious confocal microscopy pancreas dataset. The segmentation of the lung
abnormality data set with the Encoder-Decoder Segmentation Net was assessed to be
successful based on the measurements such as the Dice coeﬃcient, sensitivity and
speciﬁcity. On the other hand, the other investigated networks (U-Net, Ensemble
U-Net and Dropout U-Net) was not segmented as successfully.
When it comes to uncertainty quantiﬁcation of the segmentations, a clear result was
not found, since the networks behind need signiﬁcantly more training than carried out
in this thesis. The test measures spiked in the wrong direction and the predictions as
well as the uncertainties did not show meaningful results.
Thus, it is concluded that taking the time to train the networks is of utmost
importance for getting good results when working with deep learning.
