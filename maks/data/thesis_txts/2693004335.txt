Abstract
People living with type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) are forced to take the right amount of insulin
every day, in order to maintain a normal blood glucose level, thus avoiding hypo- and hyperglycaemia
respectively. Normally, the glucose level will be controlled by the person taking a blood glucose
measurement from the ﬁnger using a needle, test strips and an apparatus (ﬁnger prick test). By in-
troducing participants from 12 Swedish diabetes clinics to a continuous glucose measuring devise
(CGM) as well as a connected NovoPen®, it is investigated if it becomes easier for the participants to
be in glycaemic control. Participants’ CGM records blood sugar measurements every 5 or 15 minutes,
while their connected pen records all insulin injections. In collaboration with medical doctors, the
individual participant is educated in the participant’s diabetes, based on the data collected from re-
spectively the connected NovoPen® and the CGM.
In this study, CGM data is set up as having a compositional data structure, such that it becomes
possible to set up two different linear mixed effects models. These are set up to examine whether
participants spend more time in Time-in-Range (TIR), also known as the optimal blood sugar inter-
val (3.9-10.0 mmol/L), from baseline to follow-up. The participants’ development is measured over
approx. 6 months, corresponding to 5 visits at a medical doctor. Both models show that the parti-
cipants improves markedly when it comes to time spent in TIR, ending up spending almost 3 hours
more in TIR in one day (2.73 hours longer in the ﬁrst model and 2.88 hours longer in the other model)
compared to baseline.
In addition, it was seen that the number of hours the participants spend in respectively hypo- and
hyperglycaemia were reduced. The study also investigates how complex continuous biological data
can be handled, as well as the advantages and disadvantages of using a compositional data structure.

Introduction
The aim of this report is to conduct a statistical analysis of time-in-range from continuous glucose
monitoring (CGM) data. This is in order to for example investigate, whether there is an increased
time in range, after introducing an Ambulatory Glucose Proﬁle (AGP) report to the patient diagnosed
with diabetes.
Diabetes, also known as diabetes mellitus, is a chronic disease that affects more than 442 million
people around the world, according to the World Health Organization (WHO) [21]. Patients with
diabetes are not only dependent on subcutaneous administration of insulin, but also on taking the
right amount of insulin several times each day. The amount of insulin people with diabetes needs, is
depending on their food and drink intake, i.e. their blood glucose level. For a patient with diabetes it
is crucial to keep a blood glucose level as close as possible to what is seen in people without diabetes,
so the patient with diabetes does not get sequalaes or in worst case die. In order to do so, patients
need to know their blood glucose level, which is traditionally measured by a small blood sample
taken from the ﬁnger by the use of needles, test strips and an apparatus (ﬁnger prick test).
Scientists’ goal are to improve tools and medications for people with diabetes, such that it becomes
easier for the patients to handle their diabetes. By introducing the CGM, it might be easier for the
patients to keep track of their blood glucose level throughout the day, in a faster and easier way than
by doing a ﬁnger prick test. One of the key beneﬁts of a CGM is that it help patients living with
diabetes to achieve their target time spend within an acceptable range, and by doing this it becomes
easier for the patients to avoid time spend in respectively hyper- and hypoglycemia states through-
out a day [1]. It is generally established that a CGM tends to improve glycaemic control, especially
in young patients with type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) [9]. The special edge of this study is, that the
participants in the study are given both a CGM and a connected NovoPen®. From the CGM, con-
tinuous blood glucose measurements are obtained at the same time, as insulin dose data is extracted
from the connected insulin pen. Therefore, the data used in this study have both CGM and insulin
pen data available at the same time. Moreover, this study is a fundamentally new way of modelling
CGM data, and the aim of this report is therefore also to illustrate the approach on study data from
Novo Nordisk.
Since the type of data from the CGM consist of several blood glucose measurements at several
timestamps throughout a day for different participants, it is not straightforward to ﬁnd a suitable
method of modelling such data. One of the problems, among other things, is that the observations
in the data set from the same participant are highly correlated. Furthermore, the number of observa-
tions will vary from participant to participant, depending on how often they carry their CGM. One
way to handle this type of data statistically, is to set up the data as compositional data. This is done
by summarising the CGM data as time-in-range data. How to do this will be described in the follow-
ing sections. When the data is set up as compositional data, it becomes possible to use multivariate
statistical methods normally used for statistical analyses, which were not appropriate to use directly
on CGM data.

4.1
Continuous glucose monitor
The measuring of glucose several times a day can be distressing for people living with diabetes. The
technology behind glucose measurements has evolved and a so-called CGM has been developed.
This monitor continuously measures the patient’s glucose level in real time, meaning that the patient
is no longer forced to measure blood glucose several times a day, and it is therefore being used by
an increasing number of people with T1DM and people with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) [1].
A CGM consists of a small electrode placed under the skin on the body. Furthermore, it saves all
the glucose measurements until the data is being send and uploaded to a receiver. Depending on the
type of monitor, blood glucose measurements are measured every ﬁfth or ﬁfteenth minute. The blood
glucose measurements are typically measured in millimoles per litre (mmol/L). At the moment two
types of CGM systems exists, both of which are approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration
(FDA);

The CGM monitor have been used for people with T1DM and T2DM since the ﬁrst device was ap-

people with diabetes. Therefore, in 2019 an article was published summarizing how individuals as
well as HCP’s can make better use of CGM data. The article was based on results that were pub-
lished and discussed at a congress for Advanced Technologies & Treatments for Diabetes (ATTD) in
February 2019 [3].

patients blood glucose level, and compare it with the insulin injections over time. This gives the pa-
tient a basis for a meaningful discussion with the medical doctor, regarding possible adjustments in

The optimal glucose range that a patient should be in is the Time in Range (TIR), meaning that the pa-
tient has a blood glucose level between 3.9 mmol/L and 10.1 mmol/L. For people without diabetes,
the blood glucose level should be within the range of 4-8 mmol/L. Furthermore, it is extremely prob-
lematic if a patient is in a hypo (low blood glucose level) for too long, since it often requires medical
attention and can have physiological impacts. When the blood glucose is below 3.0, the brain has
only a little glucose available, meaning that it does not function properly anymore. Being in a hypo
level is therefore a critical condition where the patient might faint.
It is not as critical to be in hyper state (high blood glucose level) as it is to be in hypo state in the
short term. However, there are a number of adverse effects that can come from having been in hyper
for too long. Adverse effects such as eye-, kidney- and heart- diseases comes due to being too long
time in hyper state. However, it is not life-threatening in the same way as being in a hypo state is.
The aim is therefore to educate the patient to take the right amount of insulin at the right time, such
that the patient’s blood glucose level is in the optimal range (TIR) most of the time. Hopefully, the
CGM can be a help for the patients in their diabetes management, and help them improve their hy-
poglycaemia awareness.
As written earlier, to educate the patient, they are provided with an AGP report based on their per-
sonal blood glucose measurements from the CGM device. This report consists of a bar plot of the time
spend in the different ranges, and corresponding glucose statistics and targets in percentage. This
gives the patient an overview of how many hours the patient is in the ﬁve different ranges through-
out the report period (typically 14 days). Furthermore, plots of the patient’s daily glucose proﬁle and
a summarized ambulatory glucose proﬁle plot of all glucose values from the report period is given.

Statistical methods
The data used in this report is, as described in the previous sections, data collected from CGMs from
81 participants living with T1DM. One way to handle this type of data, is to handle it as compositional
data. A more in-depth explanation of how CGM data can be converted to have a compositional data
structure can be found in the analysis section (section 7). In this section, the theory behind what
compositional data is, how compositional data is modelled as well as how to deal with zero-values
will be described and discussed.
6.1
Compositional data
Compositional data is a speciﬁc data structure, where elements provides portions of a total. This type
of data can occur in many different types of ﬁndings, such as portions of chemical elements, concen-
tration of cell types in a patient’s blood, percentage of time spent in glycaemic ranges etc. Common
to all of these examples are that they have different components, which deﬁnes the different individual
parts of a composition. Each of these components has an amount, corresponding to the importance of
the components of the whole. These amounts can be measured in different units, such as time, per-
centage, concentration etc. In relation to that, the terms total and portion can be introduced. The total
describes the total of all amounts, while the portions deﬁnes the individual amounts divided by the
total amount. In that way, we ensure that the individual components sums up to a constant deﬁned
as c. c is typically equal to 1 if data is about proportions, 100 if data should be handling percentages
or 106 if data is about masses and should be handled as parts per million (ppm) [19].

vector can be written as;

of Aitchison [2]. However, these methods are rarely used in the ﬁeld, and therefore needs to be
modiﬁed, such that they can be used in the case where CGM data is handled as compositional data.
6.2
Transformations of compositional data
In 1982 the statistician John Aitchison made a general framework on how to handle compositional
data in a meaningful way. Some of the principles he deﬁned were scaling invariance, perturbation
invariance and subcompositional coherence [19]. In that way, he deﬁned these well-known algebra-
ic/geometric operators on R, and replaced those with operations that could be used in compositional
geometry. Furthermore, Aitchison proved that the compositional structure that he had deﬁned, was
isometrically equivalent to RD−1. This means, that it is possible to convert any compositional stat-
istical program with D parts, into a classical multivariate problem with real vectors of (D-1) coordin-
ates. This was later on called The principle of working with coordinates by Pawlowsky-Glahn [19]. With
Aitchison’s transformations, it is possible to build a (D-1) dimensional Euclidean space structure on
a simplex, and since an Euclidean space is equivalent to a real space, it became possible to translate
any real vectors to composition vectors [19]. The equivalence between an Euclidean space and a real
vector space is achieved through an isometry. An isometry is per deﬁnition a map, that takes ele-
ments from one space, and map them to another space, but where angles and distances are kept.
Aitchison deﬁned several transformations that could be used to transform data from the simplex
to the real space. Three of the most applied isometric transformations are respectively the centered
log ratio (clr) transformation, the additive log ratio (alr) transformation and the isometric log ratio (ilr)
transformation. The ﬁrst introduction of a transformation deﬁned by Aitchison, was the transforma-
tion written as the log-ratio between each component, relative to the last component, which follows
a Multivariate Normal distribution. This was later on deﬁned as the alr transformation [16].

formation of compositions in SD, transform them to coordinates in RD−1, do statistical analyses and
then transform the results back to SD, from where they will be interpreted. If interpretation of the
transformed coordinates are needed, the ilr transformation is not recommended.
If the advantages and disadvantages of the different transformation methods are weighed against
each other, the alr method seems to be most beneﬁcial in this particular study. The models that will
be set up later on will have compositions as the dependent variables, and therefore the challenge
with selection of divisor will not affect the results itself. Moreover, in this study it has been decided
that it is more important to be able to interpret the transformed coordinates, than to not be able to. As
seen for the alr transformation, it is easier to interpret ﬁnal analysis results since they are in relation
to one of the components, compared to e.g. the ilr transformation where the transformed coordinates
might involve many (or all) parts of a composition[19].
There exist more transformation methods than the ones deﬁned above, since none of them are per-
fect. The aim is to transform compositional data from the D-dimensional simplex space to the (D-1)-
dimensional real space to be able to perform multivariate statistical methods on compositional data.
However, to make these transformations, no matter what type of transformation is chosen, the log-
arithm of each component must be taken. Since the logarithm to zero is not deﬁned, a way to handle
zero values has to be found. The goal is therefore to replace the zeros by a certain value that has the
least effect on the ﬁnal analysis results.
6.3
Ways of handling zeros, missing values and outliers
Zeros, missing values and outliers are challenging when it comes to plotting, analysing and model-
ling compositional data. The methods that is typically used for handling this type of data depends
on every single component, as discussed in the previous section. Furthermore, one of the constraints
for compositional vectors is that xi > 0. Thus, if a compositional data set have one or more zeros, they
17
==PAGE:25==
ANNE-BETH AMSTRUP
Master thesis
need to be handled in some way. There are several reasons why zero values occur in compositional
data. The different zero- and missing- values can be categorised in ﬁve different categories [19];
• Missing (completely) at random
A missing value is said to be missing completely at random if there are no further meaning of
why it is missing. This is the least problematic form of missing values and it happens now and
then in data sets.
• Missing not at random
The opposite of a missing complete at random is a missing not at random. This occurs if the
chance of having a missing value depends on the actual value missed. If, for example, a measur-
ing instrument consistently cannot measure high values, and therefore return a missing value,
then the missing values are probably due to incomplete measurements.
• Structural zero
It is said that zero values are structural if the part that is measured, is not properly deﬁned.
The zero values obtained for a compositional data part would therefore be consistent. This
could occur if several chemical components are measured, but some of the components are
incompatible.
• Below detection limit
A zero or missing value would be characterised as a value below detection limit i.e. if a laborat-
ory equipment cannot distinguish between a value and a true zero. In most cases these values
will often be returned as Below Detection Limit (BDL).
To handle this, the BDL could be replaced by a fraction of the known detection limit. However,
this might not be the best idea when handling compositional data, since all the other composi-
tional parts will change proportion. Therefore, often a BDL-value will be encoded by a 0.
• True zero A zero-value is said to be a true zero if a part is not present at all. The typically hap-
pens when data is count data and not measurement data. This type of zero value is not because
data is missing or due to the laboratory equipment not being able to detect a value. Thus, it is
assumed that if the number of observations increase, the number of zeros for a compositional
part will decrease.
It is important to state which type of zeros a compositional data set consists of, since different ways
of handling the different types of zeros exist. A way to handle the problem with the zeros, no matter
what type of zero it is, is by imputation or in other words by informed guesses. Yet, there exist different
types of imputation methods. Before the different imputation methods are described, let x deﬁne a
compositional vector and r deﬁne a replacement vector of x. Furthermore, let δ deﬁne a small ﬁxed
value that can be used as a replacement value for zero values.

To get conﬁdence intervals (CI) for the parameter estimates, the bootstrap method will be used. If the
CI’s were found directly in the RD−1 domain, after estimating the parameters, it would become difﬁ-
cult to back-transform the upper and lower quantiles, since these are not compositions and therefore
do not have to comply with the same conditions as apply to compositions. For-example, the lower
and upper quantiles do not have to sum to c, when they are transformed back to SD, and it is therefore
difﬁcult to transform them correctly. Instead, by using the bootstrap method, a number of outcomes
are simulated from the ﬁtted distribution in RD−1, and back-transformed to SD. In practice, this is

7
Statistical Analysis
As introduced in the data section (section 5), the data that will be used in this report is CGM data
collected from 81 participants with T1DM. There are various good reasons why CGM data should be
handled as compositional data. Compositional data is multivariate by nature, since it consists of at
least two components, otherwise it would not make sense to think of a total or an amount. However,
there are several issues when setting up multivariate analyses on this particular type of data. First of
all, it would be incorrect to set up initial univariate analyses, since each component cannot be ana-
lysed isolated from the rest. Furthermore, several assumptions for using multivariate methods will
not be satisﬁed, when using these methods directly on compositional data. E.g. it is inaccurate to as-
sume that each component alone follow a Gaussian distribution, since each component consists of a
bounded range of values. Nor would it make sense to assume that the components are independent,
since the time spend in one range depends on time spend in the other ranges [19].
To be able to set up appropriate multivariate analyses for compositional data, the data has to be trans-
formed from the simplex space of dimension D, to the Euclidean space of dimension D-1. Thus, the
analyses on the Education space is on itself meaningless without knowing the total. The goal is there-
fore to set up a multivariate linear mixed effects model in RD−1, and then transform the parameter
estimates from the model back to SD, to get something meaningful. In the following subsections, the
CGM data will be set up as compositional data. Furthermore, different ways of handling potential
zero values will be discussed and analysed. At the end, a multivariate linear mixed effect models
will be set up, and the results from this will be discussed.
7.1

It makes sense to think of CGM data as multivariate, since each participant spend an amount of time
in one of the ﬁve glycaemic ranges throughout a day, as deﬁned at the congress for ATTD. This can
be thought of as a ﬁve-dimensional response variable in a model. However, since these ﬁve ranges
cannot be assumed to be independent of each other, nor can it be assumed that each of them alone
follow a Gaussian distribution, the normal multivariate methods cannot be used directly. Thus, each
composition is bounded between 0 and c. c is in this case set to be equal to 1. An illustration of the
problem can be seen below, where histograms are plotted for each of the ﬁve ranges;

7.3
Handle zero values
If a patient has a zero value in one of the ﬁve ranges, this is not due to incomplete measurements or
random missing values. Each component denotes how much time a participant spend in one of the
ﬁve different glycaemic ranges, through a certain time period. This type of data could therefore be
thought of as count data, since the time in the ranges is counted in seconds. However, it is reasonable
to treat zero-values in CGM data as true zeros.
One of the main problems when dealing with compositional data is, that every zero value needs
to be imputed. Otherwise it will not be possible to make one of the logarithmic transformations,
which further allows us to set up multivariate statistical models. Nevertheless, there does not exist
many theories on how to handle true zeros in compositional data. We therefore had to rethink how to
handle these. Even though the zero values in CGM data are true zeros, one could wonder, that if the
CGM had measured the blood glucose level every minute a day, there might not have been as many
zero values in the data. Thus it can be argued that the zero values are actually BDL values. At the
moment, CGM’s measure the glucose level every ﬁfth or ﬁfteenth minute, depending on the brand
of the monitor. If there had been a measurement every single minute a day, there might have been
fewer zero values in the ﬁnal data.

7.3.1
Imputation of the aggregated 14 day compositional data set
As mentioned in section 6.3.1 there are some disadvantages of the additive replacement method,
compared to the multiplicative replacement method. However, the EM-algorithm is expected to be
the best method for imputation, since the algorithm maximizes the expected likelihood function,
such that it converges iteratively to the maximum likelihood estimate, and thereby converges against
a true replacement value for each of the compositions, depending only on the observed observations
in the data set. This method should therefore not be near as sensitive to an initial start guess of δ, as
the other two methods would be.
Since some of the CGM’s measure the glucose level every ﬁfteen minute, while other measure every
ﬁfth minute, it was decided to test δ values from one to four minutes, to avoid having to split the
data up even further. The AR and MR method were set up using the equations from section 6.3.1.
For setting up the EM algorithm for imputation of missing values, the lrEM function in R was used.
This function uses the exact same method as described in section 6.3.2. It imputes coordinates rep-
resented compositional data with BDL values, by using the information of the covariance structure.
The function takes the data set as input, as well as a unique label of the unobserved values, and a
detection threshold. The unique label of unobserved values are in this case values = 0.0, while the
detection threshold is deﬁned as respectively δ1min,...,δ4min, as for the two other methods. It took 11
iterations for the function to return a data set without any zero values. An illustration of the new

Multivariate linear mixed effects model - aggregated 14 day composi-
tional data
CGM data is correlated data, since it is longitudinal measurements for the same participants. Fur-
thermore, multiple response variables are collected in the form of the ﬁve glycaemic ranges deﬁned
by ATTD [3]. Therefore, the CGM data can be deﬁned as multivariate longitudinal data. For this
type of data, the aim is often to analyse if there have been any changes in the response over time,
which is also the case in this study [6]. The participants in this study are assumed to be randomly
selected from a larger population. However, since there exists several observations from the same
participants, the observations within a particular group are likely to be correlated. This correlation
should be handled in the analysis [7]. A way to do this, is by setting up a linear mixed effect model,
where there are added random effects that account for variation in the intercept parameter, across the
individual participants in the study. Relative to the linear regression model, which only contain ﬁxed
effects that reﬂect the mean intercept and the mean slopes, the linear mixed effects model contains
both ﬁxed and random effects.
The aim of this study is to investigate, if there are a change in TIR over time, for the participants
in the study, after they have received a CGM and a connected insulin pen. As a starting model, it can
be assumed that the mean for the visit numbers are µjk for j = 0,...,ni and k={HypoL1, HypoL2, TIR,
HyperL1}. However, since we have deﬁned a certain mean structure where all visits ≥5 are equal,
we impose the restriction that µj1k = µj2k for j1, j2 ≥5. Thus, the ﬁxed effect in the model will be the
aggregated visit number, where all original visits ≥5 are deﬁned as visit 5, to get this certain mean
structure. Moreover, the variable does not vary across participants, and it will be possible to estimate
the parameters of this ﬁxed effect. These parameter estimates will then describe the mean, of the time
in each of the ranges, at a certain visit for each of the response coordinates. Nevertheless, the random
effect in the model will be the participants, and by that we allow variation across the participants.

8.1
Model setup
The simplest multivariate LMM that can be implemented, is the one where the four Y-coordinates
are assumed to be independent of each other. It is a good model to start out with, even though it is
incorrect to assume independence, since all the ranges are related to each other, and thereby also de-
pends on each other. It is more correct to assume that the four Y-coordinates depends on each other,
such that for the variance-covariance matrix of Y, the non-diagonal elements are no longer equal to
zero-matrices, as illustrated in equation 8.4.
For setting up a multivariate LMM in R, the lme function from the lme4 package was used. It ﬁts
a linear-mixed-effects model, where it is allowed to have within-group errors that are correlated and
have unequal variance. However, to be able to use the lme function, the transformed CGM com-
positional data needed to be stacked in R, since that data is multivariate, which lme cannot handle.

Multivariate linear mixed effects model - aggregated 1-14 day compos-
itional data
Throughout this study, it was chosen to set up a model with aggregated 14 day data at ﬁrst, due to
standard clinical practice. Yet, it is more statistically correct to summarise data on daily basis, since it
allows a more detailed modelling that takes the imbalances in data into account. Thus, a model with
aggregated 1-14 day observations will be set up and modelled in this section.
By creating this model, the goal is to deal with some of the disadvantages in the aggregated 14
day compositional model. The main disadvantage in the aggregated 14 day model was that several
participants were removed from data due to the cleaning decisions. As it was shown, several par-
ticipants were removed since they did not meet the inclusion criterion of having at least 70% data
coverage every day and at least 70% coverage over a 14-day period after each visit. Furthermore, the
model set up for the aggregated 14 day compositional data does not take the different days after a
visit into account, due to the way data is summarised. Instead, the day element is indirectly grouped
within the visit effect. In other words, the error term ϵijk for participant i, at visit j for Y-coordinate
k, contains both the visit variation as well as the residual variation, and thereby random day CGM
observations for every visit. Furthermore, it was assumed that the ϵijk’s were independent of each
other.

Discussion and perspectivation
The aim of this study was to investigate if people living with T1DM became better at taking care
of their diabetes after receiving a CGM, a connected insulin pen and education from HCPs. To in-
vestigate this, CGM data from several participants were collected, after which it was summarised in
such a way, that it became possible to see how much time each participant had spent in each of the 5
glycaemic ranges deﬁned by ATTD [3].
10.1
Discussion of models and results
Due to different guidelines from medical doctors and statisticians respectively, data were summar-
ised in two different ways. In one data set, 14-day blocks were deﬁned, known as the aggregated
14 day data set, while in the other data set, 1-day blocks were deﬁned, known as the aggregated
1-14 day data set. Both ways having advantages and disadvantages. In addition, it was decided that
the two data sets should be cleaned differently, so that there were stricter inclusion criteria for the
aggregated 14 day data set than for the aggregated 1-14 day data set. This had the consequence that
less patients were included in the aggregated 14 day data set, than in the aggregated 1-14 day data
set. It also meant, that it was no longer possible to compare the two models directly, since the data
on which the models are based, are cleaned in two different ways. Nevertheless, it was seen for both
models that the participants on average spent more time in TIR. Time spent in TIR was a success
criteria for being able to conclude whether the participants gained a better control over their blood
glucose and thereby their diabetes.
In addition, participants spent less time in hypo as well as hyper, which was also what we were
hoping to see. Moreover, it was found that the percentage change from baseline to Visit 5+ for TIR
in the model with aggregated 14 day data where around 23.7%, which corresponds to an increase
of about 2.73 hours per day. In the model with aggregated 1-14 day data, this number was slightly
higher, since there were 26.8% change in TIR from baseline to Visit 5+, which corresponds to around
2.88h more a day in TIR. What can also be concluded from both models are, that the time in Hyper
L2 was reduced by 2.30h in the 14 day block model, and by 2.29h in the 1 day block model.
Although the results from both models for the percentage change from baseline to Visit 5+ give
roughly the same results, the results for the parameter estimates are better in the model with 14 day
blocks, than in the model with 1 day blocks. In the same way, the baseline is also better for one
model than for the other. Now, take for example the average TIR at baseline for the two models. The
result here is that on average the patients spend 47.7% of their time in TIR in the 14 day block model,
whereas the baseline for the 1 day block model was 44.8%. Similarly, patients end up using 59.0% of
their time in TIR at Visit 5+ in the 14 day block model, but only 51.8% in the 1 day block model.
There are several reasons why the parameter estimates differ in this way. First of all, recall that to be
included in the 14 day block data set, the participants must have at least 70% data coverage a day, in
a 14 day period. In this 14 day period, the participants must also have at least 70% data coverage, at
the request of guidelines from ATTD [3], which corresponds to around 10 days data coverage on a
14 day period. In this way, only participants who wore their CGM continuously were included. It is

therefore conceivable that these participants are already very well-regulated regarding their diabetes
and that giving them a CGM is just a little extra help. In the same way, it can be discussed whether all
the participants who are not as well-regulated with their diabetes are unnecessarily excluded from
the study, since they did not wear their CGM often enough. This would make sense in terms of the
inclusion criteria that have been reduced for the model with 1 day blocks. Here, participants only
needed to have 70% data coverage per day for a 14-day period after each visit. It was therefore also
seen, that more participants were included in this model compared to the other model, and that sev-
eral of these only had observations from 1 day after a visit.
There were both advantages and disadvantages of splitting data into respectively 14 day blocks and
1 day blocks, as well as setting up a model for each. One of the reasons to set up 14 day blocks data
was, that it is good clinical practice. It is therefore easier for HCPs to understand and further use
the results from the compressed data. In addition, this data structure provides a good overview of
participants’ diabetes patterns, because data is compressed. Besides that, we saw that data contained
fewer zero values as it was summarised over a 14 day period, which also meant that all imputation
methods could be easily applied on this data. Some of the disadvantages of summarising data in
this way, however, was that it was not nearly as statistically detailed. We saw that ϵ and the visit
effect merged, so that we had to assume that there was no timely aspect of the observations within
each visit. It also meant that the days were assumed not to be correlated, which is not a reasonable
assumption. In addition, it also meant that it was not possible to handle the imbalances in the data.
Similarly, there were also advantages and disadvantages by summarising data in 1 day blocks. Many
of the disadvantages we saw by summarising data in 14 day blocks, had been turned into beneﬁts by
summarising data in 1 day blocks. However, this meant that new disadvantages arose from this.
First of all, it is more statistically correct to take day into account, since it allows a more detailed
modelling. By doing this, it is possible to handle the imbalances seen in the data. Furthermore, by
reducing the inclusion criteria, we are allowing more data observations in the data set. This implies
that we allow more participants with few daily observations to be included, which could mean that
the parameter estimates found are actually more realistic. However, by adding day with an expo-
nential correlation structure to the model, the model did not work since it became too complex. The
complexity of the model also became a problem when the zero values in the data needed to be im-
puted. Here it was seen that the EM algorithm could not converge correctly, and therefore did not
return an estimated covariance matrix, which should later on have been used to ﬁnd a conditional
mean. This conditional mean should have been used to impute missing values. Instead only the MR
method could be used.
Furthermore, the large amount of zero values entailed that the ﬁnal parameter estimates were more
affected of the given threshold δ, than it was seen to be the case for the model with 14 days block data.
In general, a number of decisions were made on the basis of good clinical practice, and not on the
basis of theoretical statistical practice. For example, it was decided to look at the development from
baseline to Visit 5+, instead of the development from baseline to each of the participants original
number of visits. However, the original visits were indirectly a part of both models in the form of a

10.2
Discussion of method
An important part of this study was also to investigate whether the compositional data structure
could be used to analyse complex continuous biological data, as CGM data is. In most literature, the
compositional data structure, as well as the methods that can be used to handle this structure, is to
be found within the world of geology. Here, most examples are regarding rocks and similar things
that are present in soil samples. But since CGM data also consists of elements/ranges in relation to
each other, it would make good sense to set it up in a similar way. Moreover, there is no international
consensus on how CGM data should be handled and analysed.
As it was described in the ﬁrst sections, the CGM data split into the ﬁve glycaemic ranges are not
Gaussian distributed nor independent. Therefore, it was not possible to perform normal multivari-
ate analyses, since the conditions for this had not been met. It was found, that a way to handle this
was by setting up CGM data with a compositional data structure. This was done in such a way that
for every participant at every day, a percentage of time spent in each of the ﬁve ranges was calculated
in relation to the time spend in the remaining ranges. In order to be able to use normal multivariate
methods, data had to be transformed by one of the log-ratio transformation methods. Here we saw
the ﬁrst and biggest challenge in setting up CGM data as compositional data, namely the problem
with zero values. In searching literature, it was difﬁcult to ﬁnd methods as well as similar issues with
data that contained several true zero values.
However, it was possible to ﬁnd methods that could be used to handle BDL values. Here it turned
out that especially the MR method was easy to use and gave reasonable results compared to the more
complex EM algorithm method. Another challenge in using compositional data structure is that all
analyses and results are made and obtained in RD−1, which means that everything must be trans-
formed back to the original domain SD, to get the desired format. Thus, when ﬁnding conﬁdence
intervals for the parameter estimates, this has to be done by the bootstrap method, to get results in
SD.
With that in mind, most advantages outweigh the disadvantages, and it makes good sense to set
up CGM data as compositional data, since it enables using normal multivariate methods. Since the
problem that gives the most challenges in setting up CGM data as compositional data, is the presence
of true zeros, another method will be discussed.

Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, theory behind the compositional data structure as well as how to apply stat-
istical analysis on compositional data have been explained. This was done in order to investigate if
CGM data can be handled as compositional data, and if an appropriate statistical analysis of TIR can
be set up. To do this, CGM data was set up with a compositional data structure by summarising data
in the ﬁve glycaemic ranges deﬁned by ATTD [3].
The study shows that the dialog between the participants living with T1DM and the HCPs is import-
ant for the participants to help them obtain glycaemic control, and improve their treatment decisions.
Furthermore, CGM and pen data gives HCPs a better basis to being able to plan and help the indi-
vidual patient on the way. In both models set up, it was experienced that the participants on average
spent more time in TIR and less time in Hypo L2 as well as Hyper L2. These improvements were
seen already after 5 visits at a HCP. It is particularly remarkable that in both models the time spent
in Hypo and Hyper L1 does not increase.
Even though the connected pen data were not a part of the analysis in this study, the participants
were still evaluating past dosing data from their connected pen with a medical doctor at each HCP
visit. This may also have an indirect effect on the results seen from the two models. By introducing
participants to their previous dose data, it may be possible to improve their timing of their insulin
injection such that they maintain glucose control, and thereby end up spending more TIR.
Setting up trials is always problematic. The data used for this study is collected from single-arm
study with continuous enrolment from 12 Swedish diabetic clinics. It can therefore not be concluded
that the results seen from the two models are only due to giving participants a CGM and a connec-
ted pen, since other unknown factors can play a role. In addition, strict inclusion criteria were set
up, which means that 71.17% of patients were excluded from the aggregated 14 day data set. In the
aggregated 1-14 day data set 61.21% of the participants were excluded from the analysis.
Since the participants did not have the same amount of data available, both data sets were unbal-
anced. To handle this, linear mixed effects models were set up, in order to include participants as
a random effect. By setting up linear mixed models with random intercept, each participants got
their own baseline level (visit 0), which could be analysed in relation to a follow-up level (visit 5+).
However, there were fewer observations available at visit 5+ than at visit 0, which means that the
estimates found at visit 5+ only rely on the few participants with observations here.
This gave rise to another problem, namely that it was not possible to set up an ordinal model dir-
ectly on data without handling the unbalances in the data collected. However, there were many
advantages of setting up CGM data as compositional data as it addresses the issue of data not being
Gaussian distributed, as well as the issue of data being unbalanced.
Due to the way the data is enrolled, there is a large variation between the number of HCP visits
and the time between the visits for the participants. For future work it would be preferable to have a

larger study population under more controlled conditions, which could possibly reduce any bias and
imbalance in data. It would then be possible to set up an ordinal model and reduce model complexity,
