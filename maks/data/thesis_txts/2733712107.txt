Abstract
In the green energy system, alkaline electrolysis utilizes electricity generated from inter­
mittent solar or wind sources to produce hydrogen. Enhancing operating conditions, such
as temperature, pressure, and alkali concentration, can boost current density for improved
efficiency. However, these harsh conditions often lead to increased corrosion of cell metal
components. Given limited research on high­temperature alkaline electrolyzers (150­200
°C), this study focuses on analyzing corrosion products to infer corrosion behavior.
The findings indicate that higher nickel content in the metal enhances corrosion resis­
tance. Stainless steel is predominantly susceptible to corrosion cracking. A uniform dis­
tribution of Fe3O4 spinel (containing Ni or Cr) or a dense layer of Co3O4 protects the metal
matrix. Coating stainless steel with nickel or cobalt can be a cost­effective measure, and
heat treatment can improve coating density.
The impact of reducing and oxidizing atmospheres on corrosion is noteworthy, with more
severe corrosion observed in oxidizing atmospheres, emphasizing the need for further
research.

Introduction
This chapter is to present the fundamental principles of alkaline electrolyzers, covering
components, reaction conditions, and primarily focusing on metal corrosion within reduc­
ing and oxidizing environments.
1.1
Principles of alkaline electrolysis cells
Water electrolysis is one of the most promising methods for green hydrogen generation
via Power­to­Gas technologies in the future sustainable energy system [1]. Alkaline water
electrolysis, in particular, highlights among other electrolysis technologies since it is the
one with greater maturity and widespread commercialization [2].
Water in alkaline solution can be electrolyzed using electricity from renewable sources
following Equation 1.1 to produce hydrogen at the cathode and oxygen at the anode,
separated by an aqueous electrolyte, as shown in Figure 1.1.

1.1. Principles of alkaline electrolysis cells
Initially, alkaline water electrolyzers employed a traditional assembly with a specified dis­
tance between electrodes (Figure 1.1 a), but this was subsequently replaced by the zero­
gap assembly, wherein porous electrodes are directly pressed onto the separator to min­
imize ohmic losses attributed to the electrolyte and to improve gas separation properties
(Figure 1.1 b). Compared to sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide’s higher product gas
purity and lower resistance in the electrolyte results in minimal cell polarization, making it
the preferred choice (25−30 wt %) in most commercial water electrolyzers [6, 7]. Porous
materials like Asbestos/Zirfon/Nickel coated perforated stainless steel or dense anion ex­
change membranes (AEM) can be used as the separator, shown in Figure 1.1 c [8]. AEMs
consist of polymers with fixed cationic side groups that are paired with anions, which be­
come mobile when dissociated in the presence of water [4]. As illustrated in Figure 1.1
d, the porous gas diffusion layer, consisting of nickel mesh/foam, establishes electrical
connecting between the electrodes and the bipolar current collector, helping evenly gas
distribution to mitigate electrode corrosion [3, 5]. The metal components from both in­
terconnects (i.e., bipolar plates or current collectors) and electrodes undergo exposure
within alkaline environments under oxidizing or reducing atmospheres.
Compared to the alternative commercialized electrolysis technology, polymer electrolyte
membrane (PEM) electrolyzers, alkaline electrolyzers operate at lower current densities
(0.2–0.8 A/cm2) but larger cell areas, longer system lifetimes, and reduced costs, mak­
ing them well­suited for large­scale hydrogen production [9]. Alkaline water electrolysis
cells typically operate within the temperature range of 70­90 °C under pressures of less
than 30 bar [8]. Elevated temperatures of alkaline electrolyzers can improve the current
density (beyond 1 Acm−2) and overall efficiency, which is not easy to achieve in PEM sys­
tems because of membrane stability problems [10]. Additionally, as the saturated steam
pressure rises in correspondence with increased temperatures, elevating the pressure
can not only keep the electrolyte in the liquid phase but also enhance the apparent ef­
fectiveness of electrolysis by reducing ohmic loss through decreased gas bubble volume
and enhanced kinetics of electrode processes [11]. As for the electrolyte, increasing con­
centration is associated with a reduction in gas contamination, while the concentration
yielding the highest conductivity exhibits a temperature­dependent increase, reaching a
maximum of 30 wt% KOH at 30 °C (0.69 Scm−1) and approximately 45 wt% at 200 °C
(2.9 Scm−1) [10, 12]. In general, there is promising potential for high temperature alkaline
electrolysis corresponding to high pressure and concentration [13].
Electrode materials should have good corrosion resistance, high conductivity, and strong
catalytic activity. Nickel electrodes, despite displaying a higher hydrogen evolution over­
potential compared to noble metal electrodes, are favored in commercial alkaline elec­
trolyzers due to their lower cost, and additional cost reductions can be achieved by em­
ploying nickel­plated steel or high­nickel alloys [14]. For high­temperature alkaline elec­
trolysis (HT­AE), alloys like Ti−Ni, Ni−Co, and Ni−Ir, Ni−Ru, Ni−Mo show lower overpoten­
tials than pure nickel, and while Raney Ni, i.e., Ni leached out Al or Zn is commonly used
for its high surface area, alternatives including platinum on carbon and advanced materi­
als like spinel and perovskites (Co3O4, NiCo2O4, LaNiO3, and La0.5Sr0.5CoO3) have been
explored [6]. Adding Ti and Mo to Raney Ni stabilized the cathode catalyst for over 8000
h at 160 °C, while Co and Fe improved cathode performance at high temperatures. For
anodes, porous nickel structures were unstable beyond 160 °C, indicating potential use
of mixed oxide coatings like Ni, Fe or Co oxides [10].

1.2
Literature review on corrosion in alkaline environment
Frequently, corrosion induced by alkalies results in pitting and localized attacks, as ca­
thodic films tend to form, concentrating the attack on vulnerable anodic areas [15]. In hot
concentrated caustic settings, research primarily targets stainless steels and nickel alloys,
exploring general corrosion and stress corrosion cracking (SCC). It has been found that
these two types of corrosion are much more severe in the presence of O2 compared to
Ar or H2 environments [10].
Stainless steel is categorized into austenitic, ferritic, martensitic, duplex, and precipitation­
hardening types. From the perspective of constituent elements, austenitic stainless steels
with face­centered cubic (FCC) crystal structure exhibit superior corrosion resistance due
to the chromium forming a passive oxide layer, and the added nickel enhances the stability
of this film, distinguishing them from body­centered cubic (BCC) martensitic or ferritic
steels with minimal nickel concentration [16].
Ni exhibits superior corrosion resistance compared to Fe but falls short of Cu’s corrosion
resistance in reducing environments. However, Ni maintains its advantageous austenitic
and FCC crystal structure up to its melting point, preventing transitions from ductility
to brittleness. Ni­based alloys, known for enhanced resistance to alloying elements in
solid solutions and good metallurgical stability, shown in Table 1.1, have been developed
with diverse additives to ensure beneficial engineering properties without concerns about
harmful metallurgical changes during manufacturing or thermal processes [15]. The incor­
poration of Cr into Ni–Fe alloys enhances the electrocatalytic efficiency for the hydrogen
evolution reaction (HER) compared to the Ni–40Fe electrode, with the synergistic effect
attributed to the modification of electron density states in d orbitals through alloying with
Cr [17]. MONEL alloy 400 resists caustic soda but is prone to stress­corrosion crack­
ing in strong alkalis at high temperatures. Its corrosion rates increase in concentrated
caustic soda and potash. INCOLOY alloys 800, 825, 020, and 25­6MO may experience
stress­corrosion cracking in hot concentrated alkalis. INCONEL alloys 622, 625, C­276,
and 686 are suitable for halide­contaminated environments, while INCONEL alloy 600
is preferred to Nickel 201 for high­temperature caustic applications with sulfur [15]. The
following corrosion studies of Ni and high­Ni alloys and stainless steels will be introduced.

1.2. Literature review on corrosion in alkaline environment
1.2.1
Reducing environment
Ni and Ni alloys
For Ni and Ni alloys, the corrosion rate is < 0.1 µm/year which is almost no corrosion. In
the early 1980s, Garat et al. made experiments on Nickel 200 and Nickel 201, revealing
that cathodic loading of nickel resulted in a significant reduction in ductility and structure
hardening, especially at high temperatures of 130 and 180 °C. Hydrogen embrittlement
behaves in an intergranular form without the involvement of an impurity segregation mech­
anism [18, 19]. The susceptibility to gaseous hydrogen embrittlement rose with increasing
temperature, but the presence of low quantities of O2 in H2 obtained through electrolysis
could inhibit the embrittlement process. Additional investigations indicated that the pres­
ence of weld, reduction in grain size, the existence of a high dislocation density, or the
initial cold working state could intensify the embrittlement [19].
Later in 1993, Gras et al. found minimal weight losses of Ni­Fe­Cr alloys (Alloy 600,
690 and 800). In KOH solutions saturated with H2, the open circuit potentials of diverse
materials closely align with the reversible hydrogen electrode potential, despite slight Fe
dissolution at the initial stage [20]. Flis et al. investigated Ni alloys containing 10–99 wt%
Fe in 25 wt% KOH at 80 °C, finding that high Fe content led to pitting corrosion during
hydrogen evolution, shown in Figure 1.2. The corrosion products around pits primarily
comprised iron oxides, and the pitting corrosion was attributed to nobler potentials at
hydrogen gas bubbles due to ohmic potential drop [21]. Addition of Mo in Ni can improve
electrochemical activity due to the larger and rougher electrode surface area but also to
the higher current exchange. As the Mo content increases in nickel (Mo+Cr) alloys, the
corrosion rate in boiling 50w% NaOH decreases. Nady et al. found that the corrosion
resistance of Ni–Cr–Mo–Fe alloys surpasses that of Ni–Cr–Mo alloy, making it suitable
for hydrogen generation in alkaline electrolytes [22].
Figure 1.2: SEM surface images of pitting in 25 wt.% KOH at 80 °C for (a) Fe and (b)
10Ni­Fe cathodes in 7 days [21].
Stainless steels
Exposure to hydrogen induces ductility loss and embrittlement in austenitic stainless steels
with low Ni content, because of deformation martensite during cold working [23]. Addition­
ally, duplex grades, containing approximately 50% ferrite, are prone to losing mechani­
cal properties when subjected to high­pressure hydrogen environments [24]. Gras et al.
conducted tests on austenitic stainless steels from the AISI 300 series, specifically type
304L, 310 and 310L. Among them, only 304L exhibited a consistent corrosion rate of 10
µm/year at 150 °C, while others were < 0.1 µm/year at 120 °C, 150 °C and 180 °C.
Stress corrosion tests revealed that 304L experienced transgranular cracking at all three

1.2. Literature review on corrosion in alkaline environment
Summary
From an electrochemical standpoint, cathodic metal elements in a hydrogen­reducing at­
mosphere only have the possibility to undergo cathodic precipitation, so the corrosion is
mainly hydrogen embrittlement or pitting affected by Fe [27]. Ni exhibits corrosion resis­
tance in alkaline environments, forming a protective layer of oxide or hydroxide on the
surface; however, stress corrosion cracking (SCC) may occur when the nickel content
falls below 20% [20]. The introduction of Cr helps mitigate the dissolution of Fe or Mo
in alkaline solutions [22]. Under hydrogen conditions, it is possible to form amorphous
spinel layers and polyhedral spinel outer layers. Spinel can be iron­rich, chromium­rich,
or nickel­rich.
1.2.2
Oxidizing environment
Ni and Ni alloys
In 1983 Garat et al. tested Nickel 200 and Nickel 201 in 40 wt% KOH at 30 bar and found
the corrosion rate of 10­20 µm/year at 130 °C and 30­45 µm/year at 180 °C, caused by
segregation of sulfur component (shown in Figure 1.5 a), accompanied by the production
of NiO, Ni (OH)2, and exclusively NiO in high temperature, respectively. No SCC occurred
because of the high value of nickel stacking fault energy [19]. In 1993 Gras et al. found
Alloy 800 followed parabolic corrosion law and behaved best among Alloy 400, 600, 690,
shown in Figure 1.5 c, due to the formation of NiFe2O4 passive layer. SCC only occurred
in Alloy 800 at highest temperature of 180 °C, which is intergranular cracks due to lower
Ni content and silicate ions in KOH, while at 120, 150 and 180 °C, these ions effectively
shielded pure Ni, preventing general corrosion without stress (Figure 1.5 b) [20]. Corro­
sion rates at 180 °C of Ni differed between the two earlier studies, which were attributed
to differences in impurities in KOH and pressure. Alloy 600 and 690 exhibited break­away
uniform corrosion behavior, characterized by a constant and relatively low corrosion rate
during the initial 2000 h of testing. Subsequently, the break­away corrosion point typically
occurs around the 2000­hour mark, leading to a notable increase in corrosion rates after
that (Figure 1.5 c). Lohmann et al. suggested that Alloy 800L (low­carbon version of alloy
800) is a promising material for HT­AE up to 200 °C, while Alloys 59, K­500, 625, and 825
demonstrate overall stability under alkaline conditions, even at elevated temperatures,
and Alloys 400, 690, and 800 are suitable for temperatures up to 150 °C [10].

Metal cations are transported outward along grain boundaries, with oxygen moving in­
ward, leading to the formation of oxides within existing scales and generating compres­
sive stresses. Although a higher grain boundary density suggests reduced protection
against corrosion, during the initial scale formation, it is advantageous on the alloy side
as it facilitates the enrichment of essential alloy elements for protective scale formation
[29]. Soloveichik et al. determined that 310 has the lowest initial corrosion rate among
304, 304L and 316 while 304L has the lowest long­term corrosion rate at around 90 °C
[16]. In Figure 1.8 a, plates and rhombohedra are typical for hematite and needles are
goethite, while outer layer of rhombohedral particles in Figure 1.8 b are Fe2O3 hematite
and needle­shaped islands are goethite based on crystal habit. Main phase of Figure 1.8
b and d are Ni(OH)2/NiO(OH), despite some hematite and NiFe2O4 in 316 [29].
Figure 1.8: SEM images of (a) carbon steel, (b) 316, (c) S327X0, (d) Alloy 625 exposed
at 80 °C in 25 wt% KOH, 30 bar O2[29].
Then in 2021, Tjelta et al. carried out electrochemical investigation through Tafel plots at
80 °C on 316L and found in strong alkali and oxidizing potentials, NiFe2O4 spinel is ther­
modynamically stable, while at lower potentials, Cr2O3 also becomes stable, suggesting
potential chromia presence at the electrode surface based on polarization history, with
subsequent trans­passive dissolution (CrO42 – ) during anodic polarization [30]. The pas­
sive Cr2O3 layer was proved by Hamidah et al. in 2.25 wt% KOH at 50 C between 304,
316 and Cu alloy. Although a thin, compact Cu2O film formed in pH 10 and a protec­
tive monoclinic cupric oxide (CuO) for pH 12, 13, Cu alloys behaved less resistant than
stainless steels [31].
In 2019, Todoroki et al. conducted the constant current density electrolysis (CCE) to
simulate working status on 316 at 30 mA/cm2 in 1 M KOH (approximately 5.61 wt%) at
75 °C, shown in Figure 1.9. Before electrolysis, the 5 nm passivation film is Fe and Cr
oxides. During the CCE test, Fe­Mn particlelike and rodlike oxide particles, appeared on
the 10 nm Ni/Fe hydroxide layers, are dissolved under strong oxidizing conditions during
electrolysis firstly. The buffer layer observed during CCE­2h grows to 30 nm for CCE­5h,
accompanied by the emergence of nanofibers with a diameter of 3 nm and a length of 25
nm. Subsequently, the nanofibers increase in diameter to 5 nm and extend to a 50 nm
upper layer for CCE­5h, ultimately reducing to a thickness of 40 nm with aggregations
of Ni–Fe hydroxide nanofibers [32]. At high current densities (400 mA/cm2) for practical
hydrogen production, the nanofiber structure persists despite the thickness of oxide layer
becomes smaller [32].

1.3. Motivation and objectives of the study
Summary
Under oxidizing conditions, Ni and Cr are the most important alloying elements. Commer­
cially pure Ni is the most resistant material to both general corrosion and to environmen­
tally assisted cracking or stress corrosion cracking (SCC). Anodic dissolution seems to be
the governing mechanism for caustic cracking [34]. Nickel oxide serves as a diffusion bar­
rier alongside nickel hydroxide, and elevated temperatures facilitate the decomposition of
nickel hydroxide into nickel oxide. Utilizing spinel as a protective layer proves advanta­
geous, especially in hot alkali environments, and it surpasses the stability of Cr2O3 over
time. The introduction of impurity elements can result in localized corrosion, particularly
with higher iron content, leading to the dissolution of iron and chromium and the formation
of a rough surface. Corrosion is more severe in the presence of oxygen compared to hy­
drogen, potentially leading to intergranular cracks. The corrosion rate tends to increase
with higher temperatures, particularly impacting ferritic and duplex stainless steels in an
oxygen­rich environment. In general, nickel alloys exhibit superior corrosion resistance
compared to stainless steel. There is ongoing consideration for the development of a
stable coating containing nickel to enhance corrosion resistance.
1.3
Motivation and objectives of the study
This project aims to characterize the corrosion behavior of various metallic components
(pure metals, alloys or stainless steels) at the intended high temperature and pressure
alkaline electrolysis conditions (40 wt% KOH, 150­200 °C, > 30 bar) in O2 or H2 environ­
ment. The corrosion experiments have been conducted in previous projects, including
the corrosion rates, cross plane conductivity measurements and microscope examination
of initial samples.
The focus of the current project will thus be to characterize the pre­corroded components
via post­mortem microstructural analysis using SEM&EDX or XRD etc. The obtained
knowledge will be useful for understanding the corrosion behavior and for developing
counter­acting measures pursued in other projects.

2.3
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and
energy­dispersive X­ray (EDX) spectroscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) utilizes a focused high­energy electron beam to
generate signals from electron­sample interactions that provide insights into the exter­
nal morphology, chemical composition, crystal structure, and material orientation of solid
specimens, shown in Figure 2.2 a.
Primary imaging in scanning electron microscopy relies on signals from secondary elec­
trons (SE) and backscattered electrons (BSE). SE imaging captures low­energy sec­
ondary electrons (<50 eV) emitted from the specimen’s conduction or valence bands
through inelastic scattering. Originating within a few nanometers beneath the surface, SE
signals offer sub­nanometer resolution, providing highly localized imaging of the sample
surface. Conversely, BSEs are higher­energy electrons resulting from elastic scattering,
emerging from deeper sample regions [35]. While BSE images have lower lateral resolu­
tion than SE images, they are valuable for subsurface imaging, offering information about
features beneath the specimen surface, making it useful in topographical analysis. BSE
signals, influenced by the atomic number (Z) of the sample, detect contrast, making them
effective in distinguishing areas with different chemical compositions. Heavy elements
with higher atomic numbers exhibit stronger backscattering, appearing brighter in BSE
images. The newly developed electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) technique can
identify the crystal structure [36].
14
==PAGE:23==
2.4. X­ray diffraction (XRD)
Energy­dispersive X­ray spectroscopy (EDX or EDS) utilizes electrons excited near the
nucleus, prompting more distant electrons to lower their energy levels to occupy the cre­
ated ”holes.” The emission of distinct X­ray frequencies during this process allows for the
qualitative and quantitative analysis of the near­surface composition of a sample. Due to
the unique nuclear charge of each element, the energies of core shells and the spacing
between them vary. While not every peak in an element’s spectrum is exclusive, there
are sufficient characteristic peaks to determine the composition of the sample with ade­
quate resolving power [37]. In this work, 15 kV is employed for elements with high atomic
number.
As it can be seen in Figure 2.2 b, the Hitachi TM3000 is a tabletop scanning electron
microscope (SEM) designed for ease of use and versatility in observing uncoated speci­
mens, with magnification range of 15X to 30,0000X. The Zeiss Ultra 55 is a high­resolution
scanning electron microscope (SEM) that utilizes GEMINI® technology, which can offer
1.0 nm @ 15 kV with maximum 900,000x magnification. The working distance should be
around 8.5 mm when measuring EDX and the resolution is 1 µm.
For sample preparation, the metal sheet is affixed to the sample holder using conductive
carbon tape to enable the observation of surface oxides. And for cross­section observa­
tion, the sample is mounted in epoxy which involves mixing epoxy components, embed­
ding the metal sample, curing, sectioning and applying a conductive coating.

Exterior look of TM3000 and Zeiss Ultra.

X­ray diffraction (XRD)
X­rays, high­energy electromagnetic waves with a wavelength between 10−3 and 101 nm,
are produced by accelerating electrons generated from heating a tungsten filament in
a vacuum through a high potential field, directing them to a target, and inducing X­ray
emission, commonly achieved through sealed tubes, rotating anodes, or synchrotron ra­
diation sources, shown in Figure 2.3 a. The generation of X­rays involves two effects:
the deceleration of electrons resulting in a continuous distribution of wavelength and the
ionization of atoms, causing characteristic radiation with energy depending on electron
shell transitions and the material’s properties.
In Rayleigh scattering, also called elastic (coherent) scattering, the energy of the scattered
wave remains unchanged, preserving its phase relationship with the incident wave, caus­

3.2. Oxidizing environment
3.1.3
Summary
In a high­temperature, high­pressure hydrogen atmosphere, an investigation was con­
ducted on pure nickel without corrosion and four stainless steel samples exhibiting cor­
rosion, characterized by small pits and cracks. The pits displayed lower oxygen content,
and an amorphous oxide layer covered the entire sample surface. Although all stainless
steel samples generated spinel particles of varied sizes and distributions on their outer
surfaces, none of them contained nickel. Chromium in the oxide layer exhibited higher
concentration at the interface between the oxide and the metal. The presence of oxygen
was linked to the diffusion of the KOH solution.

3.2.3
Summary
In the oxidizing atmosphere, pure nickel has a relatively dense amorphous oxide layer as
both the base metal and the coating. Since nickel oxide and nickel hydroxide only act
as transport barriers and are not as corrosion­resistant as spinel, sulfamate Ni coating at
304L has better corrosion resistance than pure nickel due to spinel formation.
Cracks occur in all stainless steel substrates, with the exception of 304L, which is due
to the formation of a dense layer of spinel particles on the surface of 304L as protection.
The oxide layer on the surface of metal 441 and SAF 3207HD is discontinuous and un­
even, with localized corrosion. The most severe corrosion in stainless steel occurs on
the Nanoflex, where a small amount of spinel is formed, but a large number of cracks
are produced. It can be seen that the inner layer is a dense amorphous oxide layer as a
barrier, and the outer layer is a layer of evenly distributed spinel paricles, which seems to
be the most corrosion­resistant method.
All nickel alloys form a dense oxide layer on the surface. Under the influence of other
elements such as Fe, the oxide layer exhibits different forms in different layers, while the
outer layer is needle­like nickel hydroxide. 41Cu has the best corrosion resistance, which
is attributed to the formation of evenly distributed spinel particles in the inner oxide layer.
With the exception of the electroless nickel coating, the other nickel­containing coatings
exhibit separation from the substrate, while the cobalt­containing coating remains unsep­
arated. In the case of nickel­containing coatings, the introduction of Sn and Pd causes se­
vere corrosion, accompanied by the phenomenon of oxide layer detachment, even though
a dense oxide layer forms on the surface of Ni­Sn on 304L. There is no delamination for
the dense oxide layer on the surface of electroless Ni and sulfamate Ni, where the different
oxide shapes are directly related to their nickel content. For cobalt­containing coatings,
cobalt alone will experience more severe general corrosion, while Ce­containing cobalt
coatings can slow down the corrosion rate. Heat treatment results in a dense and stable
protective layer of cobalt oxide on the outermost surface of Ce/Co HT on 441, which has
the best corrosion resistance except for a small amount of local corrosion.

Conclusion and outlook
In reducing environment, Ni, Ni alloys (Sanicro 625, Sanicro 41Cu, Sanicro 75X), and
electroless or sulfamate Ni coated on stainless steel 304 were identified as suitable base
materials, showing a corrosion rate < 0.001 mm/year at approx. 200 °C in 40 wt% KOH
at 30­50 bar pressure in an atmosphere of 5% H2 in N2/Ar. Two of the Ni alloys (Sanicro
41Cu, Sanicro 75X) and sulfamate Ni coated on stainless steel 304 demonstrated an
acceptable corrosion rate in an oxidizing atmosphere of 5% O2 in Ar at approx. 200 °C
in 40 wt% KOH at 30­50 bar pressure. The high temperature treated (HT) Ce/Co HT on
441 showed the best corrosion resistance amongst realized coatings, apart from localized
corrosion in places where the coating has pores or cracks.
Strategies to reduce corrosion rates are primarily associated with nickel content and have
a connection to molybdenum, shown in Figure 4.1. In Ni or nickel alloys or stainless steels,
the corrosion rate typically diminishes with an increase in nickel or molybdenum content.
Figure 4.1: Influence of a) Ni content, b) Mo content on the corrosion rate of samples
under 5% H2 or 5% O2 in Ar in 40 wt% KOH at 30­50 bar.
In oxidizing conditions, our results align qualitatively with Raúl B. Rebak’s findings, indi­
cating that Ni demonstrates greater resistance than Ni alloy 625 [34, 48]. However, our
observed corrosion rates are approximately 10 times and 2 times higher, likely due to the
elevated temperature in our testing. In contrast to DECHEMA’s trend, our study reveals
enhanced corrosion resistance for 41Cu compared to 625, even with a 50 °C higher tem­
perature, showcasing a 7 times lower corrosion rate for 41Cu [49], which could be due to
the formation of spinel particles of 41Cu.
The Ce/Co coating subjected to high­temperature treatment exhibits significant cracking
under hydrogen, yet it provides effective protection in an oxygen environment. Nanoflex
generates spinel particles in both hydrogen and oxygen, with a higher particle count in
hydrogen; however, numerous cracks are observed. Alloy 441 demonstrates superior
corrosion resistance, with cracks concentrated in the oxide layer, yet localized corrosion
occurs under oxygen. On the surface of 304L, a substantial amount of spinel is produced.
The absence of a thick oxide layer under hydrogen results in deep cracks extending into
the metal matrix. The oxide layer of pure nickel in oxygen exhibits a layered and coarse
structure.
65
==PAGE:74==
Nickel is very stable under hydrogen conditions but has corrosion under oxygen with the
existence of both NiO and Ni(OH)2. None of the spinels in the samples tested in H2 con­
tain nickel, while samples with significant spinel on the surface under O2 including Ce/Co
on 441, 41Cu, Nanoflex, and 304L, which can form nickel­containing spinels. Cracks
were observed in all oxide layers, and laterally extended cracks appeared in the nickel
alloy oxide layer under O2. The inner layer of stainless steel has a uniformly distributed
amorphous inner oxide layer, while the oxide layer of nickel alloys have a variety of crys­
talline compositions. The corrosion under the reducing atmosphere is mainly hydrogen
embrittlement and stress corrosion cracking, and the corrosion type under the oxidizing
atmosphere is mainly general corrosion. The most corrosion­resistant structures are the
inner layer of continuous oxide, plus the outer layer of homogeneous spinel or dense
cobalt oxide (in the same elemental ratio as spinel).
For reducing environment, considering the embrittlement always appears in intergranu­
lar form without involving an impurity segregation mechanism, further observation using
transmission electron microscopy (TEM) or electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) meth­
ods can be implemented to determine whether hydrogen embrittlement occurs by crack
location. Since the interaction volume of EDX under current setup is 1 µm3, the needle­
like oxides in nickel alloys in oxidizing environment are difficult to measure. Subsequent
tests should mainly include TEM and X­ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) to accu­
rately determine the valence state and phase composition of the elements in different
areas in oxide layer. The specific phase composition of the amorphous oxide layer still
needs to be further measured using TEM.
