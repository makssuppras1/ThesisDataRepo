Abstract
With global warming effects increasing there has been increasing efforts on global and national
scales for climate mitigation strategies. On a national level, Norway has defined climate targets
which aim to decrease greenhouse gas emissions by 50-55% in 2030 and 90-95% in 2050 compared
to 1990 levels [1]. Hence Norway aims to become a low-emission society by 2050.
The Research Centre on Zero Emission Neighbourhoods in Smart Cities (FME ZEN), focuses on
research for how to decarbonize neighbourhoods which is in line with Norway’s climate targets. A
large focus for decarbonizing neighbourhoods is on local energy generation energy efficiency and
energy flexibility considering the buildings, energy system and mobility within the neighbourhood.
The FME ZEN uses pilot projects across Norway as test areas for solutions which potentially
can contribute to the establishment of zero emission neighbourhoods. Campus Evenstad is one of
FME ZEN pilot projects and used as a case study in this assessment. This assessment focuses on
the potential for decarbonization of an energy system within a zero emission neighbourhood. The
climate change impacts arising from each technology currently implemented at Campus Evenstad
as well as the combined energy system are evaluated through the use of life cycle assessment.
Additionally a scenario analysis is carried out in order to evaluate the effects of possible changes
in the system.
The assessment found that the total climate change impacts arising from the current energy system
was 0.024 kg CO2 −eq per kWh and thereby 35,159 kg CO2-eq per year. Where 74.9% of the
climate change impacts were related to the operational energy use of the energy system. Through
the scenario analysis, the scenario with the most potential showed a decrease in climate change
impacts by 12% by altering the energy contribution and efficiencies of the technologies currently
implemented at Campus Evenstad. As the operational energy use was the largest burden on the
system, solutions to reduce, especially the use of wood chips in the system are essential.
In order for Campus Evenstad to reach net zero emissions from the energy system and thereby in
the neighbourhood as a whole, new solutions are essential for further investigation. As concluded
in the assessment, the current state of the energy system will not be able to reach net zero
emissions. However with continuous reductions and considering synergy within a neighbourhood,
steps can be taken towards a successful zero emission neighbourhood. Through the utilization of
renewable energies and solutions for reductions in greenhouse gas emissions, Campus Evenstad is
providing knowledge on steps to take towards a low-emission society. Introducing zero emission
neighbourhoods into Norway, does not only have the potential to work towards the Norwegian
climate targets but also reduce energy sources in neighbouring countries with higher climate
impacts through the export of Norwegian electricity.

Sammenfatning
Med stigende globale opvarmningseffekter har der været en stigende indsats på global og national
skala for klimabegrænsningsstrategier. På nationalt plan har Norge defineret klimamål, som sigter
mod at reducere drivhusgasemissionerne med 50-55% i 2030 og 90-95% i 2050 sammenlignet med
1990-niveauerne [1]. Dermed arbejder Norge mod at blive et lavemissionssamfund i 2050.
Forskningscenteret for nuludslips-områder i smarte byer (FME ZEN) fokuserer på forskning i,
hvordan man dekarboniserer kvarterer. Målet ved forskningscenteret er i overensstemmelse med
Norges klimamål. Et stort fokus for dekarbonisering af kvarterer er på lokal energiproduktion,
energieffektivitet og energifleksibilitet. Disse mål tager hensyn til bygningerne, energisystemet og
transport indenfor et kvarter.
FME ZEN bruger pilotprojekter på tværs af Norge som testområder for løsninger, der potentielt
kan bidrage til etableringen af nuludslips-område. Campus Evenstad er et af de FME ZEN pilot-
projekter og bruges som casestudie igennem denne analyse. Denne analyse fokuserer på potentialet
for dekarbonisering af et energisystem i et nuludslips-område. De klimaændringspåvirkninger, der
opstår fra hver teknologi, der i øjeblikket er i brug på Campus Evenstad, samt det kombinerede
energisystem evalueres ved brug af livscyklusvurdering. Derudover udføres en scenarieanalyse for
at vurdere effekterne af mulige ændringer i systemet mod at have nuludslip.
Analysen af det nuværende energisystemet på Campus Evenstad fandt den samlede klimapåvirkninger
til at være 0,024 kg CO2-ækvivalenter per kWh og dermed 35.159 kg CO2-ækvivalenter om året.
Hvor 74,9% af klimaændringspåvirkningerne var relateret til energisystemets operationelle energi-
forbrug. Gennem scenarieanalysen viste det sig af ved en ændring af energibidraget og effek-
tiviteten på de teknologier der allerede er implementeret på Campus Evenstad, kunne klimaæn-
dringspåvirkningerne reduceres med 12%
For at Campus Evenstad kan nå netto nuludslip fra energisystemet og dermed i nabolaget som hel-
hed, er nye løsninger afgørende for yderligere undersøgelser. Som konkluderet i vurderingen vil den
nuværende tilstand af energisystemet ikke kunne nå netto nul-emissioner. Men med kontinuerlige
reduktioner og synergi i et kvarter, kan der tages skridt hen imod et vellykket nuludslips-område.
Gennem udnyttelse af vedvarende energi og løsninger til reduktion af drivhusgasemissioner giver
Campus Evenstad viden om, hvilke skridt der skal tages hen imod et lavemissionssamfund. At
indføre nul-emissionskvarterer i Norge har ikke kun potentialet til at arbejde mod de norske kli-
mamål, men også reducere energikilder i nabolande med større klimapåvirkning gennem eksport
af norsk elektricitet.

Preface
This project is carried out as the master’s thesis, accounting for 30 ECTS, for the Nordic Master in
Environmental Engineering with a specialisation in Residual Resources Engineering and Industrial
Ecology. The master’s thesis is written by Sofie Nørgreen in the Spring of 2023, over a span of
22 weeks from the 16th of January to 12th of June. As a student of the joint Nordic Master
programme, the master’s thesis was written in collaboration with both the Department of Energy
and Process Engineering at the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) and
DTU Sustain at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). While the project writing took place
while studying the second year at NTNU. The supervisors of the thesis are Valentina Bisinella
(Associate Professor, DTU Sustain) and Juudit Ottelin (Associate Professor, NTNU Industrial
Ecology Programme).
Additionally the master’s thesis was carried out in collaboration with
SINTEF where Marianne Kjendseth Wiik (from the Department of Buildings and Installations,
SINTEF) supervised and supported the project work.
The main purpose of the master’s thesis was to understand and calculate the climate change impact
arising from the energy system of a zero emission neighbourhood case study, Campus Evenstad.
SINTEF is one of the research partners in the Research Centre on Zero Emission Neighbourhoods
in Smart Cities, where Campus Evenstad is used as a pilot project for the development of zero
emission neighbourhoods and used to test various solutions, especially within energy generation.
A life cycle assessment was performed for the energy system of Campus Evenstad using the life
cycle assessment program EASETECH. The key learnings from the case study are then used to
discuss the potential of developing zero emission neighbourhoods as a means of supporting the
goal of achieving a low carbon society.
I would like to thank everyone who has supported me through the process of writing the mas-
ter’s thesis. Firstly a huge thanks to my supervisors, Marianne, Valentina and Juudit, for their
guidance, advice, input and inspiration throughout the entire process. With a special thanks to
Valentina, her extensive knowledge within the field and support through many projects together
has motivated and inspired me throughout the years. I would also like to thank my family, friends,
classmates and rugby team who have listened to me and helped keep my motivation up through-
out the entire process. I would also like to thank SINTEF, Statsbygg and employees and Campus
Evenstad for allowing me to work with Campus Evenstad as a case study and providing time and
data, as this project would not have been possible without them. Lastly an additional thanks to
the companies, Volter and Osby Parca, who took their time to provide data in order for me to
complete a thorough life cycle assessment of the specific products.

INTRODUCTION

1
Introduction
Climate mitigation strategies are increasing in importance as effects from global warming continue
to increase due to anthropogenic activities. An effort to globally limit climate change effects is the
Paris Agreement which was put into effect in 2016 as a global framework aiming to limit global
warming to an increase of 1.5° by 2050 in relation to 1990 levels [2]. As part of the Paris Agreement,
Norway along with the EU agreed to reduce their greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 40 percent
by 2030 compared to 1990 levels [1]. The Norwegian Ministry of Climate and Environment has
further implemented a Climate Change Act which has a climate target to reduce GHG emissions
by at least 50-55% in 2030 compared to 1990 levels and 90-95% in 2050 compared to 1990 levels
[1]. With these climate targets, Norway is aiming at becoming a low-emission society by 2050 [1].
In order to reach the defined climate targets, holistic climate mitigation strategies are essential.
Norway has already put efforts and measures into reducing GHG emissions, however compared to
1990 Norway has in 2021 reached a reduction of 4.5%. The national energy supply and heating in
other industries and households, accounts respectively for 3.5% and 1.0% of Norway’s total GHG
emissions [3]. However with the drastic technological developments since 1990 levels, energy de-
mand has seen a significant increase and thereby an increase in GHG emissions is seen. Compared
to 1990 levels, the GHG emissions in 2021 from energy supply has increased by 405.6% in Norway
while for heating there has been a decrease of 80.5% [3]. With the increasing usage, developments
in low-emission and renewable energy technologies are crucial.
To successfully reach a reduction of 50-55% by 2030 along with increasing emissions from energy
usage, drastic measures are needed on a national scale in Norway and there is thereby an interest
in technologies and projects which are pushing this agenda. One of the research centres in Norway
with the focus on establishing a low-emission society and neighbourhoods is the Research Centre
on Zero Emission Neighbourhoods in Smart Cities (FME ZEN). The FME ZEN states that net
zero emission neighbourhoods can play an important role in decarbonising the European electricity
and heat systems through the use of renewable and low-carbon energy systems [4, 5]. The locally
produced electricity and heat produced at neighbourhood scale can be exported to other regions
as well as free up the use of Norwegian hydropower for other purposes [5]. By developing and
implementing these solutions for establishing net zero emission neighbourhoods (ZEN), it would
greatly contribute to the low-carbon society Norway aims to be.
Part of the FME ZEN on the energy usage in neighbourhoods and strives to increase overall energy
efficiency through renewable and low-carbon energy sources while sustaining flexible operations
and providing economic sustainability [6]. Local energy generation, energy efficiency and energy
flexibility are key factors to consider to successfully achieve and optimize the energy on a neigh-
bourhood scale. To assess these factors while considering the neighbourhood as a whole, a holistic
approach is essential to consider the optimal trade-offs between material use and efficiency, energy
efficiency as well as the environmental impacts of the energy system.

Objective
In order to support the research on decarbonization of energy systems and climate mitigation
efforts, this report will research and evaluate the development of energy systems within zero
emission neighbourhoods through the use of a case study. As part of the research at FME ZEN,
pilot projects are testing new solutions for developing zero emission neighbourhoods at test areas
across Norway. Campus Evenstad is one of the pilot projects where energy is one of the primary
focus areas. At Campus Evenstad the focus is testing combinations of different energy and power
technologies while maintaining a low or net zero emission of greenhouse gases [6].
As a way of assessing the energy system of Campus Evenstad through a holistic approach, a life
cycle perspective is used to evaluate each of the energy technologies along with the energy system

questions:

2. How can LCA be used to examine the opportunities of reaching net zero emissions for an
energy system at neighbourhood scale and what are the associated uncertainties thereof?
3. To what extent does the case study successfully achieve or which measures are necessary to
reach a zero emission ambition level?

2.2
Zero Emission Buildings
In Norway the building sector accounts for 34% of the final consumption of energy. The govern-
ment has set targets to reduce the energy use in existing buildings by 10 TWh by 2030 compared
to 2015 levels [7]. These targets focusing on minimisation of energy losses can be achieved through
renovation of the existing buildings and by building new buildings following low energy use stan-
dards. A zero emission building (ZEB) is defined as a building with a very high energy performance
and where the low-energy need is covered by renewable energy sources [12].
Previously performed life cycle assessments on buildings have shown that the operational stage
accounts for around 80-90% of the total GHG emissions from the building’s life span [13]. However
with the focus shifting to more energy efficient buildings, the material supply is becoming a
considerable contributor to the GHG emissions from a building’s life span [14]. Hence the GHG
emissions from various life cycle stages of a building should also be taken into account and not
solely the operational energy use. The Research Centre on Zero Emission Buildings (FME ZEB)
has defined various ambition levels for zero emission buildings. The FME ZEB is located in Norway
and lead by the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) and the SINTEF
community (an independent research organization). A ZEB is defined as a building generating
enough renewable energy to compensate for the greenhouse gas emissions produced over the life
span of the building [15]. While the generated greenhouse gas emissions through the life span
of the building are dependent on the various life cycle stages taken into account. Figure 2 (on
the following page) displays which life cycle stages are included for the specific defined ambition
levels.
The ambition levels are based on all the life cycle processes included through the Norwegian
Standard for the assessment of environmental performance of buildings, Norwegian Standard (NS)
15978. The lowest ambition level a zero emission building can achieve is the ZEB - O/EQ ambition
level, which only included net zero emissions from the operation energy use, while excluding the
operational energy of electrical equipment. While the highest achievement for a ZEB is to have
net zero emissions when considering the complete life span from production stage to end of life
processing, the ZEB-COMPLETE ambition level. [16]

2.3
Zero Emission Neighbourhoods
A zero emission neighbourhood expands the system boundaries in comparison to a ZEB, to account
for buildings, the entire energy system and mobility within a confined area.
The goal of the
Research Centre of Zero Emission Neighbourhoods in Smart Cities, FME ZEN is to "enable the
transition to a low carbon society by developing sustainable neighbourhoods with zero greenhouse
gas emissions" [17]. The research centre is like FME ZEB also lead by NTNU and the SINTEF
community. Along with other research partners, FME ZEN currently has 8 pilot projects placed
throughout Norway for testing new solutions and technologies for the development of ZENs. The
following definition of ZENs is defined by FME ZEN in order to have an overarching guideline
principle.
"In the ZEN research centre, a neighbourhood is defined as a group of interconnected
buildings with associated infrastructure, located within a confined geographical area. A
net zero emission neighbourhood aims to reduce and compensate its direct and indirect
greenhouse gas emissions towards zero over the analysis period, in line with a chosen
ambition level. The neighbourhood should focus the following:
a. Plan, design and operate buildings and associated infrastructure towards mini-
mized life cycle GHG emissions and compensating remaining GHG emissions to
6
==PAGE:24==
2

Spring 2023
obtain a net zero emission neighbourhood.
b. Become highly energy efficient and powered by a high share of new renewable
energy.
c. Manage energy flows (within and between buildings) and exchanges with the sur-
rounding energy system in a flexible way.
d. Promote sustainable transport patterns and smart mobility systems.
e. Plan, design and operate with respect to economic sustainability, by minimising
total life cycle costs to achieve affordable zero emission neighbourhoods and choose
cost optimal GHG emission reduction strategies.
f. Plan and locate amenities in the neighbourhood to provide good urban form and
land use and stimulate sustainable behaviour." [17]
The six criteria which the ZENs are evaluated on through the definition are; GHG emissions,
energy, power, urban form and land use, mobility and economy [17]. When evaluating the perfor-
mance of a ZEN, the analysis period and ambition level are determining factors [17]. The ambition
levels for ZEBs, presented in Figure 2 can also be applied to ZENs by taking the same life cycle
stages into account for the ambition level. Additionally, in order to assess the performance of the
pilot projects with respect to achieving the definition, assessment criteria and key performance
indicators (KPIs) are defined for each project. The KPIs are defined on the basis of which of the
six criteria are the most relevant to the projects being carried out at the pilot area [17].
Energy flexibility should be considered when developing and to successfully maintain a ZEN
through seasonal and environmental variations. Technologies within energy storage are able to
manage the peaks and loads to the energy system. By ensuring energy flexibility it maintains the
users’ needs and comfort within a neighbourhood by giving the ability to manage the demand,
storage and local generation when there are external changes to the energy system. [18]

2.5
Energy technologies relevant to the development of ZENs
As zero emission neighbourhoods are still in the research phase there are no defined guidelines on
how to successfully achieve a ZEN [17]. However through the definition of ZENs and overarching
guideline principle defined by FME ZEN (see Section 2.3), a key component when designing ZENs
are renewable energies. By implementing renewable energy sources, it is a part of maintaining
low or net zero GHG emissions and creating a self-sustaining operation of neighbourhoods [17].
Renewable energies ensure that the energy generation of a ZEN can work towards net zero emis-
sions. In addition to the energy generation, energy storage and flexibility technologies are also
essential in the development of ZENs [17]. An overview of the most relevant technologies for
energy generation and energy storage and flexibility for the development of ZENs are provided in
the following sections, however is not limited to the mentioned technologies.
2.5.1
Energy generation
Solar energy has so far been the most commonly used energy source used to achieve net zero
emissions for the operational phase of ZEB and ZEN projects [26]. Utilizing solar energy can be
done through both PV systems generating electricity as well as solar collectors which generate heat.
The benefit of using solar energy technologies is that they only require an input of sunlight as the
operational energy use and thereby achieve zero emissions in the operational phase [27]. However
for both PV systems and solar collectors the environmental burdens are related to the resource
use in the products [27]. Through previous studies on life cycle assessments of photovoltaic cells,
the emission factor for energy generated by a PV system is commonly in the range of 0.011 to
0.037 kg CO2-eq per kWh electricity produced [28, 29]. While for solar collectors the emission
factor for energy generation is on average 0.04 kg CO2-eq per kWh and ranges from 0.015 to 0.150
kg CO2-eq per kWh [30].
Small-scale combined heat and power units are also commonly installed as an input to the local
heating grid at neighbourhood scale [15]. CHP units offer the primary product thermal energy
to the grid while also supplementing electricity to the local electricity grid. CHP units and PV
systems are often used in complement to each other, as solar energy has the highest production
in the summer months with more sunlight while the CHP unit is primarily used in colder winter
months to generate heating. A CHP unit generates energy through the combustion of a fuel source
such as; vegetable oil, bioethanol, heating oil, natural gas, coal, biomass, biogas or municipal waste
[27]. However for the focus of CHP units relevant to implement into ZENs, the fuel source would
only be derived from the renewable energy sources. The chosen fuel source will also effect the GHG

previously performed life cycle assessments for CHP units based on an energy input of wood chips,
the emission factor was found to commonly be in the range of 0.028 to 0.070 kg CO2-eq per kWh
energy generated [31, 28, 6].
Fuel cells are a co-generation technology, where similar to the CHP unit, an output of electricity
and power is generated. Fuel cells are still under development, despite showing promising potential
the fuel cells are not yet largely introduced into the market. However there is a future potential
of introducing fuel cells into ZENs, as they require an input of fuel such as hydrogen, methane,
methanol or natural gas and produces electricity and heat at high efficiencies. [27]
Heat pumps or boiler systems are also an available technology to be applied into ZENs. Heat
pumps and electric boilers transform an input of electricity into thermal energy for the neighbour-
hood [27]. While other energy sources are also found as input to boilers such as biomass, which
have a combustion of a biomass energy source and transforms into thermal energy. Similar to the
CHP unit, the carbon intensity of the technology is highly dependent on the energy source and
with regards to ZENs, the focus is on utilizing renewable energy sources [27]. Life cycle assessment
have been performed on heat pumps and the emission factor for energy generation is on average
0.15 kg CO2-eq per kWh and ranges from 0.065 to 0.28 kg CO2-eq per kWh [30]. Similarly, life
cycle assessments performed for electric boilers have emission factors ranging from 0.132 to 0.208
kg CO2-eq per kWh energy generated [21, 32] while biomass boilers using an input of wood chips
have shown to have emission factors ranging from 0.009 to 0.02 kg CO2-eq per kWh [33, 21].
Other on-site renewable energy sources such as small-scale wind power or small-scale hydro power
are possible to implement into ZENs. However as both are highly dependent on natural conditions
of the neighbourhood location, cities and urban neighbourhood areas are often not suited for local
electricity generation from small-scale wind and hydro power due to space limitations [27].
2.5.2
Energy storage and flexibility
Energy storage and flexibility is an essential factor in a ZEN to ensure the users’ energy demands
are met through all seasons when there can be variations in energy supply from the renewable
energies [18]. Technologies can be implemented in the neighbourhoods in order to ensure a flexible
supply when there are peaks and loads in the neighbourhood. As mobility is also taken into account
through the ZEN criteria, the use of electric vehicles is also significant to the operational phase
of a neighbourhood. Various types of stationary batteries can be installed and act as an energy
storage to ensure energy flexibility as well as providing an energy supply to electric vehicles [27].
Mobile batteries, in the electric vehicles, can also provide system services through concepts such
as Vehicle-to-Grid, Vehicle-to-Building and Vehicle-to-Home. The concepts refer to the service
that a mobile battery in the vehicles can provide generated energy to either the electrical grid,
buildings or to homes. By utilizing energy generated in the mobile batteries, this also provides a
form of energy storage and flexibility. [27]
Thermal storage is also a potential form of energy storage which can be utilized in zero emission
neighbourhoods. The potential technologies providing thermal storage can be borehole thermal
energy storage, accumulator tanks with water or utilizing thermal mass of building materials [15].

System Definition
In order to evaluate and compare products, technologies or systems in terms of environmental
impacts, a life cycle assessment can be carried out. A life cycle assessment evaluates the environ-
mental impacts over the complete life cycle of a product or product system through the inputs
and outputs [34]. Hence when evaluating and comparing products or systems to each other, a life
cycle assessment can be carried out ensuring the same processes are considered in the life cycles
to be comparable.
As this project is carried out in collaboration with NTNU, SINTEF and Statsbygg, all based in
Norway, a Norwegian Standard (NS) was used as the primary guideline for the greenhouse gas
calculations of the energy system at Campus Evenstad. However a Norwegian Standard has not
yet been developed specifically for the greenhouse gas calculations or life cycle assessment of energy
systems hence the NS 3720: Method for greenhouse gas calculations for buildings (referred to as
NS:3720 throughout the report) was used as the guideline. Along with this, the general guidelines
for life cycle assessments created by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO),
ISO 14040 Environmental management - Life cycle assessment — Principles and framework and
ISO 14044 Environmental management - Life cycle assessment — Requirements and guidelines
were also taken into consideration. Conducting an LCA can be divided into four main steps; 1)
Defining the goal and scope, 2) Inventory analysis, 3) Impact assessment, and 4) Interpretation
[34]. This report focuses on the energy system of the case study, Campus Evenstad. The exact
system is described in the following sections through the goal and scope of the LCA along with
the system boundaries of the energy system at Campus Evenstad.
3.1
Goal and scope
The initial steps of performing an LCA is to define the goal and scope of the assessment which
determines the outcome of the study. The goal defines the intended application, reason for con-
ducting the study and the intended audience. The goal will inform the reader the purpose of the
study and to whom the results will be relevant for. From there the scope defines the specifications
and details of the study ensuring the goal is fulfilled. [34]
Campus Evenstad is aiming to reach net zero emissions for the neighbourhood, which includes
buildings, energy system and mobility. Projects focusing on establishing zero emissions buildings
have previously been carried out at Campus Evenstad. As Campus Evenstad aims at becoming
a zero emission neighbourhood, the potential of transforming the energy system into having net
zero emissions is now the next step taken into consideration.
Hence the goal of this project
is to determine the current climate change impact occurring from the energy system at Campus
Evenstad and evaluate the potential of reducing the emissions in the future. The intended audience
of the project is the involved partners of the zero-emission neighbourhood Campus Evenstad which
includes SINTEF, Statsbygg as well as the users themselves at Campus Evenstad. The application
of results of the project could potentially be used as initial research for alterations to the current
energy system in order to reduce the climate change impact at Campus Evenstad.
The primary objective of the project is to assess the climate change impacts of the complete energy
system at Campus Evenstad. The climate change impacts are evaluated by performing a life cycle
assessment of the energy carriers along with a scenario analysis to assess various pathways for

Life Cycle Inventory
Once the system is thoroughly described through the goal and scope, the life cycle inventory (LCI)
analysis is the following part of performing an LCA. The LCI phase focuses on the data collection
and quantification of the relevant inputs and outputs of the defined product system [34]. Data is
acquired for each unit process within the defined system boundaries. The types of data which are
collected through the process to quantify a unit process are; 1) energy inputs, raw material inputs
and other physical inputs, 2) products, co-products and waste, 3) emissions to air, discharges to
water and soil, and 4) other environmental aspects [34]. Once the primary part of data is collected
the data calculation process takes place in order to generate results of the inventory specific to the
defined system. Thus the data calculation procedures include validation of data collected, relating
the data to unit processes, and relating the data to the functional unit [34]. Due to limitations
and uncertainties of available data for the exact product system being studied, assumptions will
have to be made throughout the process to create the best representation of the product system.
The life cycle inventory will be gathered separately for the energy generation technologies at
Campus Evenstad along with the data related to energy production from the total energy system
at Campus Evenstad. As the same system boundaries are defined for each technology, the data
will be presented with respect to the life cycle stages included in the NS:3720 (presented in Figure
6).The assumptions necessary to complete the life cycle inventory are described throughout the
corresponding sections. The life cycle inventory for each technology will be presented in the total
units over the reference study period. The values for the life cycle inventory scaled to the functional
unit of 1 kWh energy generation can be found in the supplementary information, Appendix B:
Excel.

Life Cycle Impact Assessment
Through the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) the significance of potential environmental im-
pacts from the product system are evaluated based on the LCI results [34]. The life cycle impact
assessment is divided into three mandatory elements along with some optional elements to include
in the LCIA. The three mandatory elements is; 1) the selection of impact categories, category
indicators and characterization models, 2) classification and 3) characterization. The optional
elements includes normalization, grouping and weighting of results [34].
The initial steps of an LCIA is selecting which impact categories are relevant for the study.
The relevant impact categories is dependent on the defined goal and scope. For example some
impact categories are related to pollution while some are indicators for resource use. Hence the
desired and results should be taken into account when choosing impact categories to give the
best representation of the study. This also applies when selecting the category indicators and
characterization models. Either midpoint or endpoint impact indicators can be chosen in this
step. The impact indicators are a way of presenting results and thereby the presentation and
audience should be considered throughout this step. [58]
Classification includes assigning the components of the LCI to different impact categories. The
LCI results are organized by the potential of environmental impact they can cause. Hence, the
LCI results are classified into one or multiple impact categories [58]. The following mandatory
element of the LCIA, is the characterization process which consists of converting the LCI results to
common and comparable units for an impact category. The different types of substances emitted
for an impact category are all converted into one common unit by using characterization factors.
The characterization factor describes the impact potential of one substance in terms of a reference
substance. Then one reference substance is used to describe the impact of a total impact category.
[58]. Following the mandatory elements, optional elements such as normalization, grouping and
weighting or results can be incorporate into the LCIA. These can be included depending on the
desired presentation of results and audience of the study [34].
For this assessment, the EASETECH software is used to model the life cycle assessment of the
energy system at Campus Evenstad. EASETECH is an LCA software developed by the Tech-
nical University of Denmark which models environmental technologies with a strong focus on
waste technologies [59]. Processes from the Ecoinvent database are imported into EASETECH
to calculate the complete LCA.
5.1
Allocation
The allocation method chosen for an LCA influences which sub-processes are considered within
the life cycle of the processes. An attributional LCA focuses on the impacts from flows defined
in a specific system, in contrast to a consequential LCA which aims to account for how a system
will respond to a change in flows, for example due to possible decision changes [60]. The LCA of
the energy system at Campus Evenstad only focuses on the impacts from the flows defined within
the specific system and thereby an attributional LCA is performed.
As a whole life cycle assessment is calculated for each of the energy technologies at Campus
Evenstad with defined system boundaries and life cycle stages chosen with respect to the NS:3720,
55
==PAGE:73==
5
LIFE CYCLE IMPACT ASSESSMENT
Spring 2023
then the allocation, cut-off by classification system model for attributional LCAs is chosen in
Ecoinvent. The allocation, cut-off by classification has the cut-off of environmental impacts within
the activities at the point before waste treatment processes [60]. This indicates that all the by-
products of an activity are classified as recyclable and the waste treatment processes of each
activity can be considered based on the assumptions in the assessment [60]. As the life cycle
assessment of the energy system at Campus Evenstad considers waste treatment processes then
this is the chosen allocation method to avoid accounting for the same processes multiple times.
5.2
LCIA method
The methodology for calculating LCAs can differ depending on the chosen life cycle impact as-
sessment method. For this assessment the ReCiPe 2016 method is chosen. ReCipE 2016 is one
the most commonly used impact assessment methods and is one of the most recent and updated.
The ReCiPe method has 18 defined midpoint indicators addressing various environmental impact
categories [61]. In the ReCipE 2016 method, the endpoint impact indicators aggregate the results
from the midpoint indicators of an LCA into three categories; damage to human health, damage
to ecosystems, and damage to resource availability [61].
The complete LCIA method considered in EASETECH is ReCiPe 2016 - hierarchist, midpoint,
without LT. This expresses that the ReCiPe 2016 impact assessment method is chosen and only
considering the midpoint indicators. Furthermore a hierarchist perspective is considered, which
is a perspective that mainly considers authority, consensus and regulation as well as a scientific
consensus [61]. Hence the hierarchist perspective can be applicable to common society which is
regulated through governments, laws and authority. The hierarchist perspective also considers a
short-term time perspective, hence no long-term (LT) effects are considered in the LCIA method.
The LCIA method considers the global warming potential over a 100 year time perspective (GWP
100) [61].
5.3
Selection of life cycle impact assessment elements
The impact categories, classification and characterization models are selected with the consider-
ation of the goal and scope of this assessment of the energy system at Campus Evenstad. As
the main goal of Campus Evenstad is to successfully become a net ZEN, only the climate change
impact category will be considered as it considers the emissions which are in focus in ZENs.
The climate change impact category accounts for the potential global warming due to emissions
of greenhouse gases to the air and considers emissions arising from fossil resources, bio-based
resources and land use change[62] For the climate change impact category, all the greenhouse
gases (CO2, CH4, NO2, etc) influencing climate change impact are represented in terms of kg
CO2-equivalents (CO2-eq) [61]. As the climate change is the only impact category considered for
the energy system at Campus Evenstad, the impact indicator of this assessment is represented
in terms of midpoint impacts. If considering endpoint indicators, global warming would have an
influence on both damage to human health and damage to ecosystems endpoint impact indicators.

5.4
Allocation of emissions for co-generation units
The allocation of emissions when it comes to co-generation processes is a continuous question.
The allocation of the total emissions onto the by-products of a process can be based the physical
flows such as the mass or energy or the economical value a product supplies to the process [60].
A CHP unit generates two outputs, electricity and heat, through the operational phase. Hence
an allocation of emissions based on the exergy, the electricity and heat generation, is commonly
chosen. As this assessment focuses on the energy generation of the various energy technologies,
then the allocation of emissions is decided to be based on the output of exergy [63].
The CHP unit installed at Campus Evenstad has an energy output of 100 kW of heat and 40 kW
of electricity. The share of emissions allocated to heat generation is the heat capacity divided by
the total capacity such as:
Allocation of heat = Heat Capacity
Total Capacity = 100kW
140kW = 71.4%
(1)
Similarly the allocation of emissions related to the electricity output from the CHP unit is calcu-
lated as:
Allocation of electricity = Electricity Capacity
Total Capacity
= 40kW
140kW = 28.6%
(2)
Once the total emissions from the energy generation from the CHP unit have been calculated, the
emissions are distributed specifically to heat and electricity. To be able to compare the emissions
related to heat and electricity generation to other the other technologies it is assumed that 71.4%
of the total emissions of the CHP unit arise from heat generation and 28.6% arise from electricity
generation.
5.5
Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis is performed to assess which parameters are the most sensitive in a model and
thereby can potentially have the largest influence on the uncertainties in the model. The NS:3720
explaining the Method for greenhouse gas calculations for buildings states that a sensitivity analysis
is essential as part of the presentation of results and discussion. As uncertainties are associated
with the data gathered, a sensitivity analysis must be carried out for the parameters with the
largest contributions to the product system’s GHG emissions. The sensitivity analysis is performed
to identify how changes in key parameters will affect the final results and thereby contributes to
building an evaluation of the parameters in the system. The robustness of the results are analyzed
in the sensitivity analysis by considering data quality and assumptions made throughout the LCI
phase. In addition, the compliance between the goal and the defined system boundaries are also
investigated along with the compliance between the developed scenarios. The outcome of the
sensitivity analysis ensures the uncertainties are minimized for the parameters which have the
largest influence on the system. [33]
A sensitivity ratio (SR) is calculated as part of the sensitivity analysis.
The sensitivity ratio
gives an indication to the relative sensitivity of a parameter. It thereby indicates the sensitivity

5.6
Scenario analysis
A scenario analysis can be carried out in addition to an LCA as additional support to determine
the best cause of action for a system with respect to a time frame. A scenario analysis is used
as a method for predicting possible outcomes of a situation, assuming different trends will be
continued in the future [64]. The scenario analysis can be used in order to optimize variables
and combinations of different scenarios in a system.
The scenarios are determined based on
the intended goal of the study. Hence along with performing an individual LCA for each energy
generating technologies at Campus Evenstad and the energy system as a whole, a scenario analysis
is also performed for the entire energy system. The scenario analysis is carried out in order to
model different alternatives and compositions of the energy system at Campus Evenstad over
time. Hence three scenarios are formed for the energy system of Campus Evenstad to see how the
system will respond to the changes. The scenarios are developed by keeping the ZEN criteria in
mind (see Section 2.3) and ways to work towards them within Campus Evenstad.
Scenario 1: Intended prioritization of technologies
Using the description of the intended prioritization of heat generation.
Electric boiler should
only be used as a backup heat source when necessary. The scenario will include utilizing the full
efficiency of the CHP unit in contrast the the current efficiency of 80% while the rest of the heat
to the thermal heating grid of Campus Evenstad will be supplied by the biomass boiler and the
electric boiler will be excluded.
Scenario 2: Increasing share of local electricity generation
Increasing the capacity of local electricity generation at Campus Evenstad to become more self-
sufficient and decrease the climate change impact arising from operational energy use. The scenario
will include increasing the efficiency and utilizing the full capacity of the CHP unit along with an
additional installation of a new PV system. The CHP unit will use the estimated full capacity
of 400,000 kWh and use the full efficiency of 100% compared to the current setting of 80%.
Additionally the it is assumed that there is capacity at Campus Evenstad to install a new PV
system, with a capacity of 10 kW which generates an additional 1000 kWh annually. The additional
PV generation is based on rough estimates in order to determine the viability of the solution.

Due to the significant use of wood chips for the energy system of Campus Evenstad which has

Throughout this section the results from the analysis of the case study, the energy system at
Campus Evenstad will be discussed and thoroughly analyzed.
From there the results of the

7.1
Impacts arising from the energy system at Campus Evenstad
Taking the results, presented through Section 6.1 and 6.3, into consideration the impacts arising
from the combined energy system of Campus Evenstad can be discussed with respect to different
perspectives. The climate change impacts arising from each of the life cycle modules and the
contributing technologies thereof.
The total climate change impacts arising from the annual
production of energy from the current energy mix at Campus Evenstad is discussed with respect
to the energy contribution of each energy carrier. Additionally a broader perspective is applied to
the impacts arising from the energy system through a consideration of multiple impact categories
through a life cycle impact assessment.
7.1.1
Climate change impacts across the life cycle modules
The total climate change impact related to the annual generation of energy from the combined
energy system at Campus Evenstad came to a result of 35,159 kg CO2-eq per year. To pinpoint
the biggest burdens to the climate change impact, the distribution of emissions across the life
cycle modules is analyzed (see Table 77). With the current energy mix of the energy system, the
raw material supply, replacement of components throughout the study period and the operational
energy use modules accounts for a total of 95.5% of the total emissions.
The operational energy use alone accounts for 74.9% of the total emissions and thereby being
the largest share. In order to identify which technologies have the biggest impacts across the life
cycle stages, the climate change impacts arising throughout the life cycle stages divided across
the technologies of the energy system at Campus Evenstad are presented in Figure 12. For the
operational energy use the biomass boiler accounts for a 49% of the climate change impacts,
followed by the contribution of imported electricity with 28% and the CHP unit with 18%.
In comparison to all the life cycle modules over the lifetime, the Raw material supply module and
the Replacement module also account for a significant share, respectively for 10.2% and 10.4% of
the climate change impacts. In Figure 12, the PV system has the largest share of contributing
impacts for the raw material supply and replacement of components, due to the significant material
supply required for the production of photovoltaic cells.

Total climate change impacts of energy system at Campus Evenstad

7.1.3
LCIA Results
A life cycle impact assessment was also performed to have a full environmental profile of the energy
system at Campus Evenstad. Due to the globally large focus on reducing GHG emissions, climate
change impact is often the impact category in focus. Also as the goal of Campus Evenstad is to
achieve a net zero emission status with respect to GHG emissions, then the focus is mainly climate
change impact throughout this assessment. However in order to avoid trade-offs in a system a life
cycle impact assessment including other impact categories is also essential in an environmental
assessment.
Through the life cycle impact assessment it was shown that climate change impact is not one
of the most critical impact categories, as presented from the normalized impact results in Table
80. The normalized impact results can be compared across impact categories as each impact is
report in terms of person equivalents (PE) The annual normalized impact of the energy system
for climate change is 4.2 PE. While in comparison the three impact categories which have the
highest impact are Water use, Land use and Human toxicity: cancer, with respective impacts of
2626.0 PE, 161.6 PE and 87.7 PE. Out of the 18 impact categories, climate change impact is the
impact category with the 10th highest impact. This indicates that other impact categories should
be considered in addition to the climate change impact of the energy system of Campus Evenstad,
especially the water use, land use and human toxicity with cancerous effects.

However in order to limit drastic trade-offs for the energy system the contribution of each of

of other impact categories should be taken into consideration in addition to the climate change

7.2
Sensitivity analysis of the model
A sensitivity analysis is performed as a means of determining which parameters can have the
largest influence on the climate change impact of the energy system, through the use sensitivity
ratios. The parameters which are determined to be most sensitive can be used as support for
various solutions to decrease the climate change impact of the energy system. Furthermore the
sensitive ratios can be used as a way to limit uncertainties in data, hence there should be focus
on decreasing the uncertainty for the parameters with the highest sensitivity ratios to ensure a
sound model.
The sensitivity ratios for the 15 most influential parameters on the climate change impact of the
combined energy system is presented in Table 79. The parameter with the highest sensitivity to
the model, a ratio of 0.409 is the input of wood chips to the biomass boiler, hence an increase in
input of wood chips would have the largest increase on the total climate change impact. Even
though the energy generation of the biomass boiler also has an influential sensitivity ratio of -
0.206, being the 5th most sensitive then an increase in generation would have a lesser impact on
the climate change impact than the respective increase in use of wood chips. Similarly the energy
generation from the CHP unit and the respective use of wood chips are also very significant for
the combined energy system, with respective ratios of -0.171 and 0.136, ranking 6th and 7th most
influential. However here an increase in the use of the CHP unit could be suggested as an increase
in the energy generation would have a larger decrease on the total climate change impact than an
increase in the use wood chips would increase the climate change impact.
The total energy use of the campus is a significant parameter influencing the climate change
impact, having the second highest SR of -0.312.

technological development it would be a recommendation to work towards reducing the climate

7.4
Transformations for Campus Evenstad towards a ZEN goal
New solutions and transformations are necessary if Campus Evenstad wants to successfully reach
a status of net zero emissions within the neighbourhood. The primary ambition level of a zero
emission status, previously defined for zero emission buildings, is to achieve net zero emissions from
the operational energy use of the neighbourhood. Hence the energy system is a significant factor
which limits this potential. The scenario analysis was used as a means of testing solutions for
the energy system of Campus Evenstad determine the potential burdens or benefits the scenarios
would have on the climate change impact. By combining the scenario analysis with the acquired
knowledge from the results of the climate change impact of the current energy system, suggestions
for steps towards a ZEN status are highlighted for Campus Evenstad.
1. Altering the prioritization of the current technologies
The analysis of the combined energy system is based on the energy mix in 2022. However as stated
in ZEN Reports, the technologies supplying heat to the local heating grid at Campus Evenstad
should have the CHP unit as the first priority and the biomass boiler supplying the remaining
heating demand. The electric boiler should only be used as a backup heat source in contrast to
the current supply of 16% of the total heat demand. Additionally, the CHP unit is currently not
operating at full capacity nor efficiency. The capacity of the CHP unit is estimated to be 400,000
kWh [6], while the CHP unit is currently generating 90,000 kWh and runs at an efficiency of 80%.

In Scenario 1 of the scenario analysis, the energy mix was altered to these conditions, following
the prioritization and running the full capacity and efficiency of Campus Evenstad. By doing so
the climate change impact of combined energy system at Campus Evenstad decreased by a total
of 12%. Furthermore by introducing these changes, the energy efficiency increases significantly for
CHP unit, as presented in Figure 15. Thereby there is a strong suggestion for Campus Evenstad
when considering reducing climate change impacts, as it does not require any additional inputs
to the system but only a different prioritization of the current technologies already available.
2. Increasing share of locally generated energy
A target for a zero emission neighbourhood is to have a share of local energy generation. One of
the criteria states that ZENs should "become highly energy efficient and powered by a high share of
new renewable energy", while also working towards the criteria to obtain net zero emissions [17].
Despite Campus Evenstad already being powered by a large share of local renewable energies with
the use of the CHP unit, biomass boiler, solar collectors and PV system, then there is still a large
amount of imported electricity. The imported electricity still accounts for 87.5% of the electricity
demand and overall 42.8% of the energy demand. Hence in order to work towards to criteria of
ZENs, the share of local renewable energies at Campus Evenstad should be increased. Also by
using imported electricity, it limits the goal of achieving net zero emissions and a ZEB-O ambition
level as there will always be associated emissions. Hence a net zero balance is not attainable as
the energy system will have associated emissions and the there is not a large enough capacity for
export of electricity.
In order to reduce the share of imported electricity, Scenario 2 explores the impacts of implement-
ing a new PV system. The effects of increasing the electricity generation from the PV system by
1000 kWh annually was explored along with the increase in efficiency of the CHP unit in Scenario
2. The alterations resulted in a decrease in 12.0% of climate change impacts, however this was due
to the change in generation from the CHP unit. Similarly in Scenario 1 where the prioritization
were changed there was also an decrease in climate change impacts by 12.0%. The difference
between the two scenario when implementing the additional PV system was an increase by 58 kg
CO2-eq which is 0.002% of the total climate change impact. Hence the additional implementa-
tion of the PV system had a negligible effect on the climate change impact of the energy system.
While the operational energy use of the energy system did slightly decrease an additional 0.2%
from Scenario 1 to Scenario 2 from the just the PV system.
As the PV system does not decrease the overall climate change impact, the monetary costs for
installation and implementation and the resource use of a new PV system may not currently be
a feasible solution. However as photovoltaic cells are in a great technological development, the
implementation of a new PV system may be considered in some years at Campus Evenstad, to
reduce the operational energy use.
However initially other solutions should be considered for
further analysis as a means of increasing the share of locally generated renewable energy and
reducing the operational energy use.

Campus Evenstad is currently one of the most self-sufficient campuses in Norway, in addition to the
local heat and electricity generation they also produce food through the livestock and agriculture.
Hence readily available waste products, food waste and manure, can be transported into biogas.
A solution to decrease the climate change impact arising from the use of wood chips would be

7.5
Comparison to other LCA studies
The results from this study can be held up against previous LCA studies performed at Campus
Evenstad along with general LCA studies on each of the individual technologies used at Campus
Evenstad. FME ZEN has previously conducted studies on the climate change impacts arising from
the technologies at Campus Evenstad. However with recent developments of the energy system,
this assessment provides new information.
A study carried out by Backe et. al, where the focus was only on the operational energy use, it
was estimated that the CHP unit emitted 0.028 kg CO2-eq per kWh [6]. While in this assessment,
the complete life cycle was taken into account leading to an emission factor of 0.076 kg CO2-eq
per kWh where 0.053 kg CO2-eq per kWh was related to the operational energy use. By also
considering all the processes related to the production and transportation of the wood chips and
not only direct emissions, this led to an impact of 0.053 kg CO2-eq per kWh in the current system
and an optimized impact of 0.016 kg CO2-eq per kWh for the optimized use of CHP in the scenario
analysis. Both of these emission factors for the use of wood chips are higher than the suggested
0.004-0.015 kg CO2-eq per kWh by the report A Norwegian ZEB Definition Guideline [16].
Similarily the use of wood chips also led to a higher emission factor for the use of the biomass
boiler. The biomass boiler had an emission factor of 0.028 kg CO2-eq per kWh. While in other
LCA studies often ranges from 0.009 to 0.020 kg CO2-eq per kWh [33, 21]. Again the emission
factor from this assessment shows a slightly higher climate change impact than previous studies.

The climate change impacts arising from the PV system of Campus Evenstad were calculated
to be 0.69 kg CO2-eq per kWh, with no required operational energy use. A previous studied
conducted on the same PV cells as used in the first PV system installed at Campus Evenstad, had
an emission factor of 0.045 kg CO2-eq per kWh [46]. While other previous studies on general PV

The solar collectors installed at Campus Evenstad were modelled to have an impact of 0.035 kg
CO2 per kWh. As LCAs on solar collectors from previous studies showed a large range from 0.015
to 0.15 kg CO2 per kWh, the solar collectors at Campus Evenstad were within the range.
Life cycle assessments performed on electric boilers have a typical emission factor ranging from
0.132 to 0.208 kg CO2-eq per kWh [21, 32]. While the assessment of the electric boiler at Campus
Evenstad resulted in an emission factor of 0.014 kg CO2 per kWh. For the electric boiler the
emission factor is comparable and in the low range of emission factors from other studies. As the
operational energy use of the electric boiler is dependent on the Norwegian electrical grid with a
low climate change impact, this maintained a low emission factor for the overall electric boiler.
Despite the emission factors for the CHP unit, biomass boiler and PV system being generally
higher than the emission factors found from previous studies, the assessment of the energy system
at Campus Evenstad is still relevant. As the emission factors are highly dependent on the system
boundaries and processes included as part of the followed standard, then the technologies can
be compared within the system. As it is a comparative study and system boundaries are kept
consistent through the assessment, the results from the energy system of Campus Evenstad can
be applied. However extra consideration should be taken when comparing to other LCA studies,
as the emission factors are dependent on a reference study period of 50 years and not the expected
lifetime of the individual technologies.
7.6
The benefits of introducing ZENs in Norway
The case study Campus Evenstad is one of the pilot projects by the Research Centre on Zero
Emission Neighbourhoods for research on how to develop ZENs specifically considering Norway.
The case study is specific to Norway and thus the potential and benefits from introducing ZENs
across Norway can be evaluated. In Norway the benefits for introducing ZENs across the nation is
slightly limited, as the Norwegian electricity mix is based on 98% renewable energies, mainly from
hydropower [7]. Due to the large share of renewable energy and one of the electricity mixes with
the lowest climate change impact globally [11], it requires significant technologies to compare with
the electricity mix. Hence as also presented throughout this assessment, the imported electricity
has a lower emission factor than the the emission factor of each of the studied technologies.
However if considering the implementation of ZENs into countries with a higher impact from the
electricity mix, a much larger potential could be seen.
However despite the limited potential from displacing the national electrical grid, there are larger
potentials when widening the view to neighbouring countries. Norway is currently one of the
countries in Europe with the largest electricity export [7]. Hence by displacing a larger share
of energy within Norway through the implementation of ZENs, it is possible to increase the

7.7
Limitations
When performing assessments and modelling with large amounts of data, there are always asso-
ciated uncertainties and limitations. The primary limitations to the overall assessment of climate
change impacts arising from the energy system is the reference study period and the input data
specific to 2022 which was defined by the system boundaries. In addition there was limited avail-
ability on specific data to the case study for the technologies and thereby the model necessitates
assumptions. By defining the limitations, further work beyond the thesis could focus on reducing
the uncertainties thereof.
As the functional unit of the energy system is based on a reference study period of 50 years the
assessment assumes that the technologies at Campus Evenstad will remain over the study period
and thereby no technological developments will occur over.
An associated assumption is also
using the energy generation data specific to 2022 as it is assumed to be a representative year for
Campus Evenstad. These are significant assumption to make, as over a study period of 50 years it
is very likely that technological developments will happen within the field of renewable energies.
It is also unlikely that the energy mix will continue to remain the same, however through the
scenario analysis this limitation is minimized as the energy mix and solutions for developments
of the energy system are assessed. Additionally in order to evaluate the system and thereby the
necessary limitations, the emission factor results were held up against other LCA studies. Since
available data was limited for the products specific to the case, assumptions were essential to
make throughout the life cycle inventory. This is also a limitation to the results as some data is
dependent on assumptions and data from other life cycle assessments.
The general methodology which was applied to the life cycle assessment was based on the Norwe-
gian Standard 3720 "Method greenhouse gas calculations for buildings". A limitation is associated
with the methodology as it is not specific to the greenhouse gas calculations for an energy system.
In the NS:3720, the module for operational water use is not considered and thereby the impacts
related to water use for each of the technologies was not considered. However when expanding
the system boundaries to climate change impacts arising from a whole neighbourhood, it might
be more significant to account for water use.

7.8
Further work and recommendations
Through the assessment of the energy system at Campus Evenstad, recommendations for the
development of the energy system towards net zero emissions can be provided. Despite the large
share of renewable energies already implemented at Campus Evenstad, there is still a long way
for Campus Evenstad to reach the primary ZEB-O ambition level for the energy system and as
a neighbourhood. Hence more in-depth assessments should be performed for the potential trans-
formation solutions recommended for the energy system. The most significant recommendation
for Campus Evenstad is to alter the prioritization of the energy contribution of each technology.
The CHP should run at full efficiency and capacity and the biomass boiler should supply the
remaining heat to the campus, while the electric boiler is only used as a backup source. This
would have an expected reduction of 12% for the climate change impacts of the energy system.
When considering the recommendation of altering the prioritization, a more in-depth analysis of
monthly data should be completed in order to take peaks and loads of the system throughout the
year into account.
As analyzed and discussed through the scenario analysis, the use of waste products is also a way
to reduce the climate change impacts of an energy system. By utilizing waste products, there
are avoided emissions from the waste treatment of the waste products and an avoided production
from the initial fuel energy which is displaced. Despite the small change in associated greenhouse
gas emissions, the potential of utilizing the generated food waste and manure for energy should
be considered and further assessed. The recommendation is in line with the ZEN way of thinking
as it promotes synergy between the processes taking place at a neighbourhood.
A general recommendation for the testing of new solutions at Campus Evenstad and other ZEN
case studies is to not only consider the greenhouse gas emissions but also consider the trade-offs to
other impact categories which arise when implementing these solutions. As a small reduction in
the climate change impact may lead to a larger increase in another impact category and thereby
the solution will become more burdensome on a broader scale.
This assessment was limited to largely focusing on climate change impact while considering the
trade-offs to other environmental impact categories. However for further work the scenarios and
solutions which are recommended to Campus Evenstad should also have an analysis of the mon-
etary costs. Even though Campus Evenstad is test area for new solutions, monetary costs are
an essential aspect when considering neighbourhoods on a broader scale. Hence a business case
should also be considered for the feasibility of the financial investment required for the suggested
solutions.
Lastly for further work the key findings from this assessment should be applied to different scales
and to various case studies. As well as performing assessments on more aspects of the neighbour-
hood together, such as including mobility, energy storage, etc. To further work with different case
studies and possibly model at different scales, the key findings with respect to Campus Even-
stad can potentially be used as background for recommendations to the development of ZENs in
general.

8
Conclusion
The goal of the assessment was to understand the contributions of an energy system to the
potential establishment of a zero emission neighbourhood. Campus Evenstad was used as a case
study for a neighbourhood which has already begun efforts towards becoming a zero emission
neighbourhood.
Along with literature research and in-depth analysis of the energy system of
Campus Evenstad was performed in order to address the research questions. The key points to
each of the research questions are summarized.
The technologies and energy strategies which are beneficial for the development of an energy
system within a zero emission neighbourhood were analyzed throughout this report to support
Research Question 1.
Literature research on the energy technologies which are beneficial for
development of ZENs provided background knowledge for the assessment. This showed the use
of renewable energies and local energy generation is the key factor for an energy system within a
ZEN. The relevant technologies were similar to the ones already implemented at Campus Evenstad,
including a combined heat and power unit, a biomass boiler a PV system and solar collectors.
While Campus Evenstad is working on limiting their use of the electric boiler and imported
electricity which supplies the remaining energy.
The gathered information about the technologies and energy system of Campus Evenstad was then
analyzed through a life cycle assessment and a scenario analysis of the energy system to support
Research Question 2. The climate change impacts were calculated for each of the technologies
within the energy system along with the energy system as a whole. The annual climate change
impact arising from the 35,159 kg CO2-eq. A sensitivity analysis was carried out as a means of
evaluating the uncertainties in the model. In addition a qualitative analysis of the limitations and
comparison to previous LCA studies on the technologies was used to assess the energy system. As
the aim of an energy system within a zero emission neighbourhood is to reach a net zero emissions,
initially within the operational energy use. Through the climate change impact assessment, the
largest burdens to the system are determined and can be used as focus areas when considering
solutions for reduction in climate change impacts.
The largest burden to the current energy
system at Campus Evenstad was the use of wood chips for both the biomass boiler and CHP
unit. The efficiencies of each technology were also assessed to compare the energy contribution to
contribution to GHG emissions. In the current system it was determined that the CHP unit had
the most burdensome efficiency.
The energy system of Campus Evenstad was evaluated with respect to defined ambition levels.
A scenario analysis was performed as a way to assess possible solutions for decreasing the impact
and working towards a zero emission ambition level.
As defined in Research Question 3, the
potential measures to reach net zero emissions are evaluated and discussed as a part of the scenario
analysis. Currently the best solution for Campus Evenstad would be to alter the prioritization of
the technologies. In the scenario the efficiency of the CHP unit is used to its full capacity and
the remaining heat demand of the local heating grid is supplied by the biomass boiler and the
electric boiler is only used as backup. By implementing these changes to the energy system there
is a potential decrease of 12% in the climate change impacts. Despite the potential to decrease
climate change impacts, extensive measures and further research is necessary for the energy system
of Campus Evenstad to successfully reach a status of net zero emissions as the operational energy
use of the energy system is the largest contributor to the climate change impacts.
