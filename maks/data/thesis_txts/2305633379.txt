Abstract
Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus are opportunistic pathogens often found
in the airways of patients with the genetic disease cystic ﬁbrosis. P. aeruginosa and S. aureus can
interact in the airways of cystic ﬁbrosis patients, leading to changes in virulence and survivabil-
ity.
In this study interactions between P. aeruginosa and S. aureus isolated from cystic ﬁbrosis pa-
tients are investigated. Phenotypic changes were observed when interacting different clinical
strains of P. aeruginosa and S. aureus from a cystic ﬁbrosis patient CF236, isolated at three time
points, over a year. Phenotypic changes were shown to vary between bacteria isolated with a
half year in between, as only the latter S. aureus strains were affected by P. aeruginosa pheno-
typically.
P. aeruginosa and S. aureus from patient CF236 were found to affect each other when grown as
co-cultures. P. aeruginosa affected the growth rate of S. aureus, while S. aureus affected the com-
position of P. aeruginosa in both a planktonic and a bioﬁlm environment.
A new proteomics strategy was used to investigate differences in protein expression between a
P. aeruginosa DK2 and a S. aureus strain from the patient CF236 when grown closely together, as
both show interesting phenotypic changes when interacting. The proteomic strategy was able
to identify two statistically relevant down-regulations of proteins in S. aureus, FHS and AtpD.
To further identify possible protein regulations responsible for the phenotypic changes, the pro-
teomic data was screened for proteins not identiﬁed by statistics. Several protein regulations
were found to potentially play a role in the phenotypic changes and growth regulations ob-
served in S. aureus. Regulations of AtpA, AtpD, AtpG, Ldh2 and FHS could suggest that S. aureus
shifts towards fermentation metabolism, as a response to inhibition of its electron transport
chain. Regulation of the cell division proteins Atl and FtsZ, could explain the increases in growth
rates observed. Pigmentation of S. aureus when interacting with P. aeruginosa DK2, could be ex-
plained by an up-regulation of protein SarA, that positively regulates staphyloxanthin.
This study provides some new insights on interactions between P. aeruginosa and S. aureus in
a patient co-infected with both bacteria over a one year period. The whole picture of why the
changes occur in P. aeruginosa and S. aureus when interacting is still uncertain, but this study
provides possible explanations to some of the changes occurring in the interacting bacteria.

Introduction
1.1
Cystic Fibrosis
Cystic ﬁbrosis (CF) is an autosomal recessive inherited disease, caused by one or more than 1500
possible mutations in the cystic ﬁbrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR) gene,
leading to a malfunction or loss-of-function. The disease has an incidence of clinical disease of
1 in 2500 births [1].
CFTR functions as a AMP-regulated chloride ion channel, across epithelial cell surfaces in the
lungs, and is important for regulating mucociliary clearance of micro organisms [1]. This leads
to dehydration of the epithelia mucus layer, making it difﬁcult to get rid of by normal clearance
and coughing. The thick mucus layer also provides a great growth media for pathogens to col-
onize [2]. The over exposure to microbes leads to a continuous recruitment of inﬂammatory
defense mediators, especially polymorphonuclear leukocytes (PMNs) and antibodies. This will
over time cause tissue damage, and a decrease in lung function [1, 2] (See Fig. 1).
Figure 1: Schematic illustration of the epithelium layer in non-CF and CF patients. (A) shows the epithelium
layer of a healthy person. The cilia of the epithelia cells can easily move microbes, and clear the lungs. (B) shows
the epithelium layer of a CF patient. Here the cilia cannot remove microbes, due to the dehydrated and thick
mucus layer, over time leading to chronic infections. The inﬂammatory defense mediators caused by infection,
will over time lead to tissue damage and reduced lung function. Figure modiﬁed from Folkesson et al. 2012 [1].
When CF patients get infected by pathogens, the current treatment is various intensive anti-
biotic usage. This delays the infection, but also leads to increased antibiotic resistance in the
pathogens. The treatment has increased the median of expected life span of CF patients from
approximately 8 years in 1974 [1] to over 40 years today [3].
1.2
Microbial diversity in Cystic Fibrosis
The microbial diversity in CF patients is wide. Up to 158 different bacterial families have been
detected in CF lungs [4]. The most occurring pathogens in the CF lung are Pseudomonas aeru-
ginosa, Staphylococcus aureus and Haemophilus inﬂuenzae [1, 3].
==PAGE:8==
7
The prevalence of pathogens in the CF lungs also differs according to patient age [4]. The bac-
terial diversity in CF patients seems to expand in the ﬁrst 10 years of age, after which a decline
in diversity is seen, as dominant species settle in the lungs [4].
Figure 2 shows the correlation between patient age and occurrence of different common CF bac-
teria. It shows that P. aeruginosa, most often found in adult CF patients [3], mostly infect CF pa-
tients after 6-10 years, and after approx. 25 years becomes the most dominant pathogen. It also
shows how S. aureus starts as the most dominant pathogen, but after P. aeruginosa starts adapt-
ing in the lung, a decline in S. aureus occurrence is seen, as P. aeruginosa starts out-competing
S. aureus [5]. Other common CF bacteria are also seen differing in occurrence, according to pa-
tient age, and show that the bacterial diversity in CF lungs change with age.
Figure 2: Occurrence of several common pathogens in CF airways, as a function of patient age. The X-axis
indicates the age of patients while the Y-axis indicates the percentage of infected patients. Plots are shown for P.
aeruginosa including multi-drug resistant pseudomonas aeruginosa, H. inﬂuenzae, Burkholderia cepacia complex,
S. aureus incl MRSA, MRSA, Achromobacter, Stenotrophomonas maltophilia and MDR-PA. The graphs show that
for young CF patients the dominant pathogen is S. aureus. As the patients age, P. aeruginosa becomes the
dominant pathogen, while the percentage of patients infected by S. aureus decline. Figure obtained from Cystic
Fibrosis Foundation [3].
Bacterial species may also differ according to regions in the CF lungs. Jorth et al. (2015) [6]
found that isolates of P. aeruginosa from dissected CF lungs showed regional diversity through
the lungs. They found that single linages of P. aeruginosa had evolved differently, and showed
differences in requirements for nutrition, responses to host defenses, antibiotic resistance and
virulence. This shows that different selective pressures through the lungs may drive interspecies
diversity, and that there is limited bacterial mixing in the lungs.
1.3
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is an gram-negative, rod-shaped bacterium involved in a wide range
of infectious diseases. P. aeruginosa is highly adaptive to its environment, and normally inhab-
==PAGE:9==
8
its water and soil, but can also survive in other environments, including the human respiratory
system and wounds. P. aeruginosa can cause a range of acute and chronic infections in immun-
osuppressed persons, including burn wound infections and chronic CF infections [7].
P. aeruginosa produces a wide range of virulence factors, both cell-associated and extracellu-
lar [8]. Most of P. aeruginosa’s extracellular virulence factors are controlled by the Pseudomonas
quorum sensing system (QS), composed of two acyl homoserine lacton (AHL) systems, las and
rhl [8]. P. aeruginosa uses the QS signals to sense cell density, and coordinate growth and vir-
ulence production.
1.3.1
Adaption and Evolution of Pseudomonas aeruginosa in the CF lungs
Chronic infections in CF are associated with changes in phenotypic characteristics and genetic
mutations in the chronically infecting bacteria [1]. When P aeruginosa chronically infect CF
patients, loss-of-function mutations in the lasR gene is often seen [9]. The lasR gene is in-
volved in the synthesis of the QS signal molecule 3,4-dihydroxy-2-heptylquinoline (PQS) from
4-hydroxy-2-heptylquinoline (HHQ), and a loss-of-function mutation leads to accumulation of
HHQ, though PQS is still produced at a lower rate [10]. Chronically infecting P aeruginosa often
grow slower than other P aeruginosa [1], and often loose a lot of its virulence factors [11].
The Copenhagen CF center has a strain collection of microbes found in a large number of CF
patients, dating from 1973 till present day [1]. They have found two frequently encountered
clone types, DK1 and DK2, both dominant in large groups of patients. Both clone types have
been isolated in patients since 1973, and serves as important strains for studying evolutionary
changes in bacteria infecting CF patients.
Damkiær et al. 2013 [12] studied the phenotypical evolution of the DK2 strain from early oppor-
tunistic pathogen to host-speciﬁc pathogen in CF, by introducing four gene mutations seen in
the DK2 strains into the P. aeruginosa PAO1, both stepwise and combined. The mutations were
in the genes mucA (G430∆), algT (A55G), rpoN (T1256C) and lasR (deletion). The genes are in-
volved in three regulatory networks, the MucA/AlgT(σ22), the RpoN (σ5) and the LasR. They
saw that the phenotype of the PAO1 mutants showed similarities to the DK2 strains, showing
that P aeruginosa undergoes several mutations to ensure its long term survival in the CF lungs.
1.4
Staphylococcus aureus
Staphylococcus aureus is a gram-positive cocci. It is highly adaptive to its surroundings, and
can be found in a wide array of environments. It is often found as a commensal bacteria on
==PAGE:10==
9
human skin and mucous membranes, but can also survive in blood and different tissues [13],
including the CF lung. S. aureus can cause a wide range of diseases [13], and is known to acquire
resistance towards antibiotics by mutations or horizontal gene transfer, leading to new multi
resistance strains [14]. Methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA) is an increasing problem world-
wide, as many MRSA’s are multi resistant, and only susceptible to glycopeptide antibiotics like
vancomycin. [15]. Both increased MIC [16, 17] and resistance [18, 19] towards vancomycin have
recently been seen, increasing the global threat of MRSA.
1.5
Quorum sensing
Quorum sensing (QS) systems are a process in which bacteria can communicate and control
their growth, by extracellular signaling molecules. QS is regulated by cell-density, as the QS mo-
lecules accumulate at higher densities, leading to correlated growth and production virulence
factors.
1.5.1
The P. aeruginosa Quorum Sensing System
The QS system of P. aeruginosa is composed of a large and complex hierarchy (Fig 3), and is as
previously stated composed of the las and rhl systems. The transcriptional regulator LasR is
transcribed by one of multiple transcription factors. LasR bound with the QS signal molecule
3-oxo-C12-HSL, induces genes controlled by the las system, including rhlR and rhlI [20, 21] as
well as production of the QS signal molecule PQS [10]. PQS induces rhlI, which leads to the pro-
duction of the QS signal molecule C4-HSL, that activates the regulator RhlR. RhlR bound with
C4-HSL induce genes controlled by the rhl system [20]. The LasR-3-oxo-C12-HSL, RhlR-C4-HSL
and PqsR-PQS complexes are all involved in regulation of virulens factors in P. aeruginosa [21].
In 1998 Davis et al. [22] found that P. aeruginosa uses QS molecules to regulate its bioﬁlm devel-
opment. By comparing the average thickness and density of wt PAO1 bioﬁlm against lasI and
rhll mutants, they found that the lasI mutant formed thiner bioﬁlm, with no mini colonies and
water channels, where as the rhll mutant seemed to grow similar to the wt. It was found that the
thin bioﬁlm formation of lasI was due to a loss of the QS signal 3-oxo-C12-HSL. When measur-
ing the EPC level in both the wt and lasI mutant, no signiﬁcant difference were seen, meaning
that the same amount of bioﬁlm was produced in the wt and mutant, with the mutant cells more
tightly packed. This indicates that 3-oxo-C12-HSL has a role in how tight the cells are packed,
but no effect on the amount of bioﬁlm produced.
Favre-Bonté et al. 2003 [23] also found that the PAO1 lasI mutant produces the same amount of
bioﬁlm as the wt. They also found that a PAO1 rhll mutant had a large reduction in produced
bioﬁlm compared to the wt, indicating that C4-HSL is involved in production of bioﬁlm.

1.5.2
The S. aureus Quorum Sensing System
The S. aureus QS system is composed of a two-component system called the accessory gene
regulator (agr) [13, 24] (Fig 4). The agr system generates two transcription units (RNAII and
RNAIII), that are expressed by promoters P1 and P2. P2 expresses the agrACDB two component
system [25]. agrD produce an autoinducing peptide (AIP) signal molecule, that is modiﬁed and
secreted by AgrB to the extracellular environment, where it is detected by AgrC. AgrC then auto-
phosphorylates and activates AgrA. AgrA then activates the two agr promoters P2 and P3 [26,
13]. P3 transcribes effector molecule RNA III, regulating numerous genes, including controlling
several virulence factors [27].

1.6
Bioﬁlm
Bioﬁlms are aggregated bacterial communities, enclosed by a self produced extracellular mat-
rix. Forming bioﬁlm gives the bacteria several beneﬁts, opposed to planktonic state. One beneﬁt
can be protection against environmental factors such as increased tolerance towards antibiot-
ics [28].
Cells in bioﬁlm can change their growth state to stationary, protecting them against antibiotics
such as beta-lactams that are most effective against rapid growing cells [29, 30], making the an-
tibiotics less effective against the bacteria.
The formation of bioﬁlm happens over several stages (Fig 5). First the cells attach reversibly to
the surface, either continuing formation of bioﬁlm, or returning to planktonic state. This stage
is followed by irreversible attachment, after which the cells begin to form micro colonies. These
colonies then start to mature, and produce extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) and the
cells start to aggregate, resulting in bioﬁlm. After the formation of bioﬁlm, cells can detach from
==PAGE:13==
12
the bioﬁlm, and disperse as planktonic cells. These cells can then possibly start a new bioﬁlm
formation at a new location. Bioﬁlm dispersion can happen actively by the cells, or passively by
environmental forces [31].
Figure 5: Phases of bioﬁlm formation. The formation and dispersal of bioﬁlm is shown. First the planktonic
bacteria reversibly attaches to the surface (left). Then the cells attach irreversibly to the surface. The bacteria then
starts forming microcolonies and start to mature and form bioﬁlm. Lastly the bioﬁlm can disperse cells returning
them to planktonic life. Figure obtained and modiﬁed from M. Toyofuku et al. 2016 [31].
P. aeruginosa QS molecules 3-oxo-C12-HSL and C4-HSL are involved in bioﬁlm formation. They
have been found in CF sputum with the same ratio as laboratory bioﬁlms [32], indicating that P.
aeruginosa forms bioﬁlm in the CF lungs.
S. aureus can also form bioﬁlm in the CF airways, and a study by A. Molina et al. 2008, found
that MRSA clones were also able to form bioﬁlm in the CF lungs [33], making them even harder
to eradicate.
1.7
Polymicrobial interactions
Since Robert Koch published his famous postulates in 1890, stating that a pure culture of a
pathogen isolated from a diseased host should cause disease in a healthy host, the research
in pathogenesis has mostly focused on studying single microorganisms in vitro or in in vivo
models of infection. In nature most microbes live in multi species environments, where bac-
terial interactions form the distribution and activities within the population. This knowledge
has helped put more research into looking at microbiological interactions, leading to the realiz-
ation that not all infections are caused by a single pathogen, but rather multiple pathogens can
together cause an infection [34].
Microorganisms living in a community can form synergistic relationships, giving them a com-
petitive advantage when growing together. Different types of interactions can affect the synergy
between species (Fig. 6).
==PAGE:14==
13
Figure 6: Polymicrobial interaction mechanisms. On the left, different types of interactions, and examples. On
the right, the illustrations show how one microbe interacts with another microbe, and examples of responses from
the interaction. Figure modiﬁed from Murray et al. 2014 [34]
Interaction by metabolic cross-feeding happens when metabolic end products from one mi-
croorganism is consumed by another microorganism. This can lead to increase in growth, anti-
biotic resistance or evasion of immune responses from the host [34].
Interactions by signaling systems (eg. quorum sensing), involves low molecular weight mo-
lecules used by microorganisms as signals to communicate with other microorganisms, and
regulate behavior. Interactions by signal can lead to increased virulence and increase in bioﬁlm
production [34].
Microorganisms can also interact by direct contact. This is mediated by adhesins, that recognize
speciﬁc receptors on the bacterial surface and facilitate binding, leading to co-aggregation. This
effect is important for bioﬁlm formation, and can lead to better invasion of host tissue [34].
1.7.1
P. aeruginosa polymicrobial interactions in Cystic Fibrosis
A study by Sibley et al. 2008 [35], showed that some bacteria isolated from CF sputum enhanced
virulence when co-infected with P. aeruginosa PAO1 in a Drosophila model, compared to infec-
tions with only PAO1. Some strains showed virulence properties, and were able to cause infec-
tion when isolated alone. These strains showed increased motility when co-infected with PAO1.
Other strains showed no virulence when isolated, but still showed an increase in motility with
PAO1. Duan et al. 2003 [36] also showed that a Streptococcus isolated from CF sputum greatly en-
hanced the virulence of PAO1 in an agar bead infectious model in rat lungs. They suggested that
==PAGE:15==
14
the interactions could result from interspecies communication, including the QS Autoinducer-2
(AI-2) mediated signaling.
There is evidence suggesting that P. aeruginosa senses and uses peptidoglycan from gram-positive
bacteria to enhance virulence against both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. This gives P. aeruginosa
enhanced host killing, but also allows P. aeruginosa to change the composition of the com-
munity, by killing competing gram-positive bacteria [37].
1.7.2
P. aeruginosa and S. aureus in Cystic Fibrosis
In the CF lungs, P. aeruginosa normally out competes S. aureus through excretion of exoproducts
and competition for oxygen, leading to inhibition of the S. aureus electron transport chain [5].
To survive the inhibitory products, S. aureus may adapt through a small-colony variant (SCV)
phenotype, with defective mutations in the electron transport chain, causing them to shift from
respiratory metabolism to fermentation [38]. These SCV’s show increased survival in the pres-
ence of P. aeruginosa.
P. aeruginosa has been shown to be able to utilize iron released from S. aureus upon lysis [39],
and consume lactate produced by S. aureus when inhibited by P. aeruginosa [5], giving P. aeru-
ginosa additional competitive advantages.
Early-infecting P. aeruginosa have been shown to signiﬁcantly inhibit the growth of S. aureus,
while P. aeruginosa adapted to the CF airway had a reduced or no effect on S. aureus [40], in-
dicating that while P. aeruginosa adapts to the CF lungs, it loses some of its inhibitory functions
towards S. aureus.
A study by Michelsen et al. 2014 [41], found that in vitro co-culturing clinical P. aeruginosa
DK2 isolates with S. aureus JE2 showed synergistic interactions. S. aureus showed no inhibition
from P. aeruginosa, and P. aeruginosa showed enhanced growth activity, either by an increased
growth rate or increased cell density. P. aeruginosa also showed an increased tolerance towards
certain antibiotics when grown with S. aureus supernatant. Investigations of antibiotic resist-
ance showed that P. aeruginosa had adapted small colony variant (SCV) characteristics. A later
study by Michelsen et al. 2015 [42], found that the pqsABCDE operon was up-regulated in the
P. aeruginosa DK strain, when interacting with S. aureus JE2. The operon is involved in the syn-
thesis of HAQ molecules, and they concluded that the HAQ molecules played an important role
in the enhanced growth activity of P. aeruginosa.
Understanding how interactions between P. aeruginosa and S. aureus in the CF lung works is
==PAGE:16==
15
important, as synergistic interactions between two of the most occurring pathogens in CF can
cause a great threat to the patients.
1.7.3
Other Polymicrobial interactions
Polymicrobial interactions are huge problems in health care, not only in the CF lungs. The fol-
lowing section will focus on some polymicrobial interactions outside the CF lungs, highlighting
the importance of research in polymicrobial interactions.
Polymicrobial infections have been seen in wounds, where the MRSA strain USA300 has been
found able to coexist synergistic in vivo with P. aeruginosa isolated from wounds. When co-
infected, an increase in wound healing time was observed [43].
Diabetic ulcers are also sites for polymicrobial infections. Different bacteria have been sugges-
ted to form functional equivalent pathogroups. These groups enhance virulence compared to
mono-culturing the bacteria [44].
Intravenous catheters, extensively used in health care, are associated with increased risk of in-
fection. Numerous bacterial species have been found on catheters, and polymicrobial bioﬁlms
have been found to exist on most catheters [45]. The two bacteria Proteus mirabilis and Provid-
encia stuartii, have been found to increase incidents of urolithiasis and bacteremia when co-
cultured in a mouse model of urinary track infection (UTI) using catheters [46].
A study in UTI showed that a Streptococcus increased the concentration of uropathogenic Es-
cherichia coli during acute infection, and reduced host inﬂammatory responses, despite the
Streptococcus being rapidly out-competed [47].
Polymicrobial infections are also seen in Otitis media, a common ear infection in children,
caused by opportunistic bacteria such as Haemophilus inﬂuenzae and Moraxella catarrhalis.
Co-infection with H. inﬂuenzae and M. catarrhalis have shown to increase bioﬁlm antibiotic res-
istance and host clearance, by interspecies QS, originating from H. inﬂuenzae AI-2 signals [48].
The studies shown here are just a few of many cases of polymicrobial infections in various en-
vironments. The cases show that polymicrobial infections are a serious problem to the human
health, and that it is a ﬁeld of great scientiﬁc importance.
1.8
Proteomics
Proteomics is the study of genetic and cellular functions on a protein level. Mass-spectrum (MS)
based proteomics is the golden method for analyzing complex protein samples. The method
can be divided into several steps. First the proteins are released from the cells by lysis, followed
==PAGE:17==
16
by puriﬁcation and denaturation. The proteins are then enzymatically digested, often by tryp-
sin, resulting in the proteins being degraded to peptides with C-terminally protonated amino
acids. This step is important, as the MS is less sensitive to whole proteins. The peptides are
then run through a liquid chromatography-tandem–mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS), where a
high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) separates the peptides by adsorption. The
peptides are then read on an MS, at different time points, based on the separation. The MS pri-
oritizes the peptides and sends them to MS/MS, where the peptides are isolated based on ion
charge and fractioned through energetic collision with gas. An MS/MS spectrum is then read,
and peptide sequences are found. The peptide sequences are then matched against a sequence
database, to ﬁnd the original proteins [49]. By using LC-MS/MS both protein hits and concen-
trations are found.
Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization (MALDI) time of ﬂight (TOF) is often used to ensure
that no polymers are in the sample prior to LC-MS/MS. MALDI-TOF works by sublimating and
ionizing a sample in a crystal matrix, using laser pulses. MALDI-TOF is also used for analyzing
simple peptide mixtures [49].
Normally proteomics is done on a single organism grown in liquid media, to get a high yield of
cells. The proteomics is only done on a single organism, as co-culturing with another organism
can mask proteins and give false positives.
Previous studies have looked at co-culturing P. aeruginosa and S. aureus. Kluge et al. 2012 [50]
co-cultured P. aeruginosa, S. aureus and Burkholderia cepacia in liquid media, and analyzed the
mix-culture with simple proteomic strategies and LC-MS/MS. They identiﬁed proteins as being
species speciﬁc, by comparing a 2D-PAGE of the mixed culture to 2D-PAGE’s of mono-cultures,
by gel positions. This made them able to identify and analyze some, but not all, species speciﬁc
proteins.
No strategy of co-culturing two organisms and separating them before LC-MS/MS have previ-
ously been made. In this study a new method for separating two interacting organisms and
measuring their protein expression with proteomics is proposed.

1.9
Aim of Project
The aim of this project is to characterize interactions between bacteria isolated from cystic
ﬁbrosis patients, both phenotypically and on a proteomic level.
Interactions between different P. aeruginosa and S. aureus from the same patient will be valid-
ated by co-culturing them on solid media, and measuring different aspects of their growth in
liquid media, both isolated and co-cultured.
A new proteomic strategy will be used in trying to validate the changes in protein levels when a
speciﬁc P. aeruginosa DK2 and a S. aureus isolated from a CF patient are co-cultured or grown
alone, as the P. aeruginosa has previously shown synergy towards a reference S. aureus.
The results from proteomics will be used to show if the new method can be applied as either
a method to screen bacteria for interesting regulations, or as a method to validate all protein
regulations in interacting bacteria.

2.2
Bacterial isolation
New bacterial strains found in this study, were isolated from sputum samples from the CF pa-
tient 236, on 8/12-2014, 5/5-2015 and 18/11-2015. Sputum samples were obtained from mucus
coughed out by the patient. All sputum samples were taken by the Cystic Fibrosis Center at
Rigshospitalet Denmark. All sputum samples were also analyzed by the Department of Clinical
Microbiology at Rigshospitalet Denmark prior to shipping to DTU, to visually identify patho-
gens and estimates of their numbers.
Their analysis found that all three samples contained P. aeruginosa and S. aureus. The 2014
sample contained many S. aureus and few P. aeruginosa. The two 2015 samples both contained
many S. aureus and P. aeruginosa.
All bacterial strains were cultivated on Luria broth (LB) agar plates or in 10ml liquid LB medium
(shaken at 200rpm), and incubated at 37°C, unless otherwise speciﬁed.
A small part of the sputum samples was mixed in 0.5ml 0.9% NaOH, and from that a serial di-
lution was made. 100 µl from each dilution was plated on both LB and Pseudomonas Isola-
tion Agar (PIA) plates, and incubated for 2 days. Different bacterial strains found were then
restreaked on either LB or PIA plates, and incubated for 1-3 days, until clear colonies were seen.
Single colonies from the restreaked plated were incubated in liquid LB for 1-2 days, and 1ml of
==PAGE:21==
20
the cultivated sample was mixed with 0.5ml 50% glycerol, and saved as freeze stock at -80°C.
Freeze stock bacteria were streaked on appropriate agar plates, and incubated for 1-2 days.
2.3
Bacterial identiﬁcation
To identify the bacterial strains isolated from the sputum samples, two different PCR tests were
done, one targeting the tuf genes in S. aureus as done by Delgado, S. et al. 2009 [54] and one
targeting the acsA gene in P. aeruginosa as done by Curran, B. et al. 2004 [53]. The primer pair
tuf-g and tuf-e resulted in a 530 bp fragment identifying S. aureus, and the acsA primer pair
resulted in a 842 bp fragment identifying P. aeruginosa. The primer tuf-e, was also used with
tuf-a and tuf-g, as done by Delgado, S. et al. 2009 [54], resulting in a 370 bp fragment identifying
Staphylococcus epidermidis. For protocols see Appendix A.
2.4
Interactions assays
To test for phenotypical differences between interacting bacteria, two different strategies were
used. Cross-streaking and spot assays.
2.4.1
Cross-streaks
To test if different P. aeruginosa and S. aureus affected each other when grown together, cross-
streaks were used.
For the bacterial interaction cross-assay isolated strains from patient CF236, as well as PAO1,
JE2 and DK2-P24M2-2003 were used. Over night (ON) cultures were made for each of the strains
that where tested. Two strains were tested for interactions at a time, and 5 µl of one of the strains
was spotted on a LB plate, and a small amount of the ON culture was streaked across the plate,
and brieﬂy dried. 5 µl of the other strain was then spotted isolated from the previous spot, and
a small amount of the ON culture was streaked through the dried streak from the other strain
(See Fig 7). This created an interaction zone where the bacteria grew together. The plates were
incubated for 3-5 days at 37°C, and phenotypic changes were observed.
Figure 7: Illustration of the cross streak method. Two different ON of bacteria are both isolated as a spot, and
crossed through each other, creating an interaction zone.

2.4.2
Spot assays
To see phenotypical effects different P. aeruginosa and S. aureus had on each other when grown
close to each other and with some distance, a spot assay was used. For the bacterial interaction
spot assay, strains isolated from patient CF236 and PAO1, JE2 and DK2-P24M2-2003 were used.
Two strains were tested for interactions at a time. Four spots of 5 µl ON culture from the ﬁrst
strain was spotted on a LB plate, one in the bottom corner and three at the top, and left to
dry. Four 5 µl spots of the second ON culture was spotted on the plate, one isolated in the
bottom corner, and the rest close to the top dried spots, with increasing distance (See Fig 8).
The plates were incubated at 37°C, for 3-5 days, and phenotypic differences in interacting strains
were identiﬁed.

Groth curves

Proteomics
Bacterial interactions between DK2-P24M2-2003 and CF236-Sa-1 were tested using MALDI-
TOF and LC-MS/MS, using a new bacterial interaction strategy. ON cultures of the interacting
bacteria were spotted on 0.45 µm HA ﬁlter paper on LB media. Each bacteria were spotted alone
and next to the other bacteria (See Fig. 10). The plates were grown for 3 days at 37°C before re-

isolated and close to each other. Numbers indicate the ﬁnal four samples.

The puriﬁed proteins were tested using MALDI-TOF before running LC-MS/MS. 1 µl puriﬁed
protein were loaded to an 600 µM AnchorChip target, and validated using MALDI.
LC-MS/MS was run on a Thermo Scientiﬁc Q-Exactive Orbital MS coupled to a Proxeon nLC
EASY 1000, by the Protein and Immune Systems Biology group.

3.1
Strain identiﬁcation by PCR
To identify S. aureus and P. aeruginosa strains in patient CF236, PCR strategies were used. From
the sputum samples from patient CF236, 23 colonies from the 2014 sample (12 from LB, 11 from
PIA), 19 from the early 2015 sample (9 LB, 10 PIA) and 12 from the late 2015 sample (all LB),
were isolated and saved to freeze stock. Based on phenotype, 9 strains from the 2014 sample,
8 from the early 2015 sample and 12 from the late 2015 sample, were selected and identiﬁed as
S. aureus, P. aeruginosa or unknown strains, using PCR to detect either the tuf gene in S. aureus
or the acs gene in P. aeruginosa. The strains P24M2, PAO1 and JE2 were all used as positive or
negative controls, to assure that the PCR reactions worked correctly.

Identiﬁcation of S. aureus
The primer pair tuf-a, tuf-e and tuf-g were used to identify S. aureus strains from patient CF236.

the primers tuf-e and tuf-g, this was also done as an extra control, to see if S. epidermidis was
also present in the patient. All gel images can be seen in Appendix B.

more S. aureus strains might exist. As CF236-Sa-1 still showed a band, and the negative controls
showed none, the positive results are still trusted. Results of PCR identiﬁcation can be seen in
Table 2.
No positive control for S. epidermidis were included, so it is possible that false negatives exists.
This is ignored as only S. aureus is used for further experiments.

3.2
Interactions assays
Two interaction assays were made to see how different P. aeruginosa and S. aureus affected each
other, when grown together or close together on LB agar.
3.2.1
Cross-streaks
Cross-streaks were done to see how different P. aeruginosa and S. aureus strains reacted pheno-
typically, when grown on top of each other. This created an interaction zone, where phenotyp-
ical changes could be observed. Figure 13 shows an example of the cross-streaks. In the ﬁgure
P24M2 (vertical) and JE2 (horizontal) is crossed. A small inhibitory effect can be seen on JE2,
while no change can be seen on P24M2.
Figure 13: Example of Cross streak. Cross streak between P24M2 (vertical) and JE2 (horizontal). The dotted box
indicate the interaction zone. Black arrows show negative phenotypical changes in JE2.
In general the cross-streaks only showed small to no effects on the strains. As a result, they were
==PAGE:29==
28
difﬁcult to interpret, compared to the spot assay. Therefore the spot assays became the main
source for seeing phenotypical changes between P. aeruginosa and S. aureus.
3.2.2
Spot assays
A spot assay was done to see the effect P. aeruginosa and S. aureus had on each other when
grown closely together. Bacteria from the 2014 sample and the early 2015 sample from patient
CD236, were spotted next to each other and isolated. Phenotypic changes were evaluated from
-3 (negative effect) to +3 (positive effect). A table of all spot interaction results can be seen in
Appendix C.

D shows PA-2015-2 isolated. E shows sa-2015-4 isolated. Phenotypical changes can clearly be seen in sa-2015-4
when grown close to PA-2015-2. Changes in color in PA-2015-2, was due to light pollution in the microscope room.
Figure 14 shows an example of how the spot interactions looked. In the example PA-2015-2 and
sa-2015-4 are spotted against each other. No changes seems to happen to PA-2015-2, while sa-
2015-4 changes phenotypically. sa-2015-4 becomes yellow, and a clear inhibitory effect can be
seen close to the interaction site. The bacterial spot also seems thinner through the whole spot,
compared to the isolated spot.
In general spot assays of P. aeruginosa and S. aureus from the 2014 sample seemed to only have
small inhibitory effects on the S. aureus strains. Phenotypically both P. aeruginosa and S. aureus
from 2014 had no changes in the appearance of the interacting spots compared to the isolated
spots.

got a yellowish pigmentation when grown close to P. aeruginosa.

strategies were used. Growth and competition was measured by CFU in shaking ﬂasks and
bioﬁlm formation on biomass carriers. Growth was also measured as growth rates in liquid
cultures.

3.3.3
Liquid culture interaction assay
To test if co-culturing S. aureus and P. aeruginosa had an effect on the total growth rate, com-
pared to growing S. aureus isolated, growth curves were made as minimum triplicates on a Cyta-
tion 5 imaging reader. Co-cultures were either made by mixing S. aureus and P. aeruginosa with
the same initial OD600 (normal), or by increasing P. aeruginosa’s initial OD600 to 0.05 (OD). An
average of the growth curves for each sample was used to calculate growth rates with the pro-
gram GrowthRates 2.1 by Barry G. Hall.
As previously shown clinical P. aeruginosa is partly or totally out-competed by S. aureus. From
those results it is estimated that the growth rate when co-culturing clinical S. aureus and P. aer-
uginosa, will be based on S. aureus.
The growth rate experiment worked poorly with mono-cultures of P. aeruginosa, so no growth
rates were calculated for P. aeruginosa.
To evaluate if the differences in growth rates between S. aureus mono-cultures and co-cultures
of S. aureus and P. aeruginosa are statistically different, unpaired student’s t-tests were per-
formed on growth rates from each growth curve. The student’s t-test tells if two data sets are stat-
istically different from each other. The t-test is performed on http://www.physics.csbsju.edu/stats/t-
test.html. The data sets are evaluated as statistically different if p<0.05.

3.3.4
Biomass carriers
To test S. aureus and P. aeruginosa’s ability to grow together over time in bioﬁlm, a biomass car-
rier experiment was setup. Mono-cultures and co-cultures where evaluated by CFU to test the
ability of the bacteria to form bioﬁlm on a new biomass carrier daily.
The experiment was setup using the bacteria pairs; PA-2014-pBAD-lacZ + sa-2014-5 and PA-
2015-1-pBAD-lacZ + sa-2015-1 (Fig. 25).
Figure 25 shows the CFU counts on day 3 and day 7 for mono-cultures and co-cultures in logar-
ithmic scale. Each strain was measured both as mono-culture and in the co-culture. 80γ X-gal +
0.3% arabinose agar plates were used to differentiate the P. aeruginosa and S. aureus by turning
the P. aeruginosa strains blue.
==PAGE:40==
39

3 (blue) and day 7 (red) in a logarithmic scale. At the bottom, the CFU strain is shown, and above two boxes,

The 2014 sample pair showed that sa-2014-5 was able to grow and form bioﬁlm approx. equally
both as mono-culture and co-culture with PA-2014. PA-2014 was also able to grow as mono-
culture, but in reduced numbers compared to sa-2014-5. When co-cultured with sa-2014-5,
PA-2014 had very few CFU’s on day 3, and by day 7 PA-2014 was out-competed.
The 2015 sample pair showed that sa-2015-1 was able to grow and form bioﬁlm both in mono-
culture and co-culture. PA-2015-1 was also able to grow and form bioﬁlm when grown as mono-
culture after 7 days, while poor CFU’s was observed at day 3. When co-cultured with sa-2015-1,
PA-2015-1 only had few CFU’s at day 3, and was out-competed after day 7.
The results show that clinical P. aeruginosa and S. aureus are both able to form bioﬁlm, and that
they can co-exist in a bioﬁlm in vitro on a short scale, while P. aeruginosa is out-competed on a
longer scale.
sa-2015-1 mono and PA-2015-1 mono both showed a high increase in CFU’s from day 3 to 7. The
increase might suggest selection of hyper bioﬁlm strains. To test this hypothesis, a new carrier
experiment was performed.
3.3.5
Selection for hyper biomass strains
To test if the increase of sa-2015-1 and PA-2015-1 CFU was due to a selection of strains with a
higher tendency of producing bioﬁlm, a new experiment was made. A colony from each strain
on the day 7 CFU count plates, were used to recreate the biomass carrier experiment till the day
3 CFU. If a selection of hyper bioﬁlm producers was the case, the new CFU counts would be
==PAGE:41==

Discussion
4.1
Experimental representation of the Cystic Fibrosis airway
Clinical samples from patient CF236 are isolated from sputum. The samples are made by sampling
mucus coughed up by the patient. These samples only show bacteria in mucus accessible by the
patients coughing. The samples may therefore not represent the whole microbiome population
in the patients lungs, as bacteria can infect different areas of the airways. The bacteria found in
the sputum might also not normally interact in the lungs.
All experiments done in this study is done in vitro under the assumption that the bacteria found
in sputum, also interacts in the CF airway. This might not fully represent the environment and
interactions in the CF lungs, but the experiments give a view of how the bacteria interacts under
laboratory conditions. This is used to estimate how the bacteria might interact in the CF air-
way, as interaction studies can be both difﬁcult and unethical to perform on the patient. Animal
models can be used, but are often done after in vitro studies have been done in the laboratory.
4.2
P. aeruginosa affects S. aureus when grown closely together
The spot assay method was chosen as the only method to see interactions between bacteria.
The other method of studying interactions, the cross-streak method, showed smaller variations
in phenotypic changes, and often S. aureus became dominant as it grew faster than P. aeru-
ginosa, and no real interaction zone was produced. Therefore the results from the spot assay
was chosen instead of the results from the cross-streaks.
Growing P. aeruginosa and S. aureus as spots on solid agar showed that the bacteria expressed
different interactions. The clinical samples from 2014 only had small effects on S. aureus in form
of inhibition, while no effects were seen on P. aeruginosa. In bacteria from the 2015 sample,
bigger effects were seen on S. aureus. S. aureus was more clearly inhibited, and phenotypical
changes as yellow pigmentation and thinner growth were seen, while no effects were still seen
on P. aeruginosa from 2015.
The results clearly showed that there are differences in the interactions seen in bacteria from
2014 and 2015. From the 2015 sample three P. aeruginosa strains were identiﬁed and used for
spot testing. They all showed the same characteristics when isolated and interacting with S.
aureus, and all had the same phenotypic and inhibitory effects on S. aureus. This suggest that
the three isolated strains belong to the same clone type. The P. aeruginosa from the 2014 looked
like the strains from 2015, but had only little effect on S. aureus.
All S. aureus also shared the same phenotype when isolated, so as a control to see if the changes
in interactions where due to P. aeruginosa or if other S. aureus strains infected the patients in
==PAGE:46==
45
2015, a spot test between PA-2015-2 and sa-2014-3 was made. sa-2014-3 showed the same phen-
otypical changes as the S. aureus from 2015. This shows that there are differences between the
P. aeruginosa in the 2014 and 2015 samples.
4.3
S. aureus out-competes P. aeruginosa in liquid media
When growing different pairs of P. aeruginosa and S. aureus together in liquid media, two cases
were seen. In the case where a WT P. aeruginosa (PAO1) is co-cultured with a clinical S. aureus
(sa-2015-1), the S. aureus was completely out-competed after 24 hours. In the other case where
clinical P. aeruginosa’s were co-cultured with clinical S. aureus, the opposite picture was seen.
After 24 hours both P. aeruginosa’s (PA-2014, PA-2015-1) were either extensively inhibited or out-
competed.
As previously mentioned clinical P. aeruginosa have been shown to grow slower and produce less
virulence factors than non-clinical P. aeruginosa, due to mutations [1, 11]. This might explain the
reduction in P. aeruginosa CFU’s when grown together with S. aureus, as S. aureus grows faster,
and settle in the environment before P. aeruginosa can colonize. The clinical P. aeruginosa also
produce less virulence factors against S. aureus, giving S. aureus better opportunities to colonize
in the media. The reduction of P. aeruginosa CFU could therefore be a result of competition on
nutritions.
While the picture might be different in the CF lungs, the results show that clinical S. aureus is
better at colonizing in vitro than clinical P. aeruginosa.
The biomass carrier experiment showed that clinical P. aeruginosa was out-competed over time
in a co-culture bioﬁlm, but was able to grow and form bioﬁlm as mono-culture. The results
showed that the P. aeruginosa from 2014 formed approx. 90% less CFU’s in mono-culture than
clinical S. aureus on day 3, and 80% less on day 7. The P. aeruginosa from 2015 was able to form
approx. equally the same CFU’s in mono-culture as clinical S. aureus on day 7, while PA-2015-
1 formed much less CFU on day 3 than sa-2015-1. Both co-cultured P. aeruginosa’s made less
CFU’s on day 3 than the mono-cultures, and on day 7 no CFU were found for both of them.
Phenotypic results from this study suggest that S. aureus has no inhibitory effects against P. aer-
uginosa, indicating that other mechanisms may cause the eradication of P. aeruginosa in co-
culture bioﬁlms.
The co-culture of PA-2014 and sa-2014-5, resulted in a CFU count of 30 PA-2014 CFU’s on day
3. This results is unbelievably low, and more PA-2014 CFU’s are thought to exist in the sample.
An explanation could be that PA-2014 has difﬁculties growing together with sa-2014-5 on solid
media, as sa-2014-5 colonies appeared approx a day before PA-2014. This might suggest that
==PAGE:47==
46
when S. aureus colonizes on the media, a reduction in space and nutrition inhibits P. aeruginosa
from growing sufﬁcient colonies.
Both sa-2015-1 and PA-2015-1 had large differences in CFU’s between day 3 and 7 in mono-
cultures. The CFU on day 7 for sa-2015-1 was approx. 8 times higher than day 3, and the CFU
on day 7 for PA-2015-1 was almost 700 times higher than day 3. An explanation could be that
by letting bacteria in bioﬁlm be the main colonizing agents, a selection of bacteria with a higher
tendency of forming bioﬁlm, might be selected for. This could explain why the day 3 count gave
less CFU’s, as the cells on day 3 had a lower habit of producing bioﬁlm.
A repetition of the experiment with colonies of sa-2015-1 and PA-2015-1 found on solid media
on the last CFU count, showed that the new sa-2015-1 CFU looked like the original day 3, mean-
ing that no increase in bioﬁlm production could be seen. PA-2015-1 showed even higher values
on the new CFU count, suggesting that a selection of hyper bioﬁlm P. aeruginosa strains may be
selected for.
4.4
P. aeruginosa affects the growth of S. aureus
Growth rates of co-cultured P. aeruginosa and S. aureus was estimated to mainly be a result of
the growth of S. aureus. This assumption was made as a result of the inhibition of P. aeruginosa
CFU’s seen in the results. The assumption is also based on the growth curves seen for P. aeru-
ginosa. These curves suggested that P. aeruginosa had difﬁculties growing in the imaging reader,
as only little growth and large standard deviations were observed for all P. aeruginosa strains.
From these observations the growth rate differences are estimated to result from S. aureus af-
fected by extracellular molecules from P. aeruginosa.
The growth rate experiments showed that P. aeruginosa affected the growth of S. aureus. Both
samples from 2014 and early 2015 had an effect on the growth rate of S. aureus when co-cultured
with a higher start OD600 for P. aeruginosa. In the P. aeruginosa from 2014, the increase in growth
rate time of S. aureus approx. 70-84%. Early 2015 P. aeruginosa increased growth rates by 24-
52%. The results clearly show that the growth of S. aureus is inhibited by P. aeruginosa.
Co-culture between sa-2014-5 and PA-2014 with equal start OD600 had a statistically signiﬁcant
growth rate time increase of approx. 30%. No statistical differences were seen in the samples
from early 2015 co-culture samples with equal start OD600. The growth rates for strains from
late 2015 samples, had low time increases when co-cultured, but all except one data sets were
still statistically different. The growth rate triplicates from late 2015 had a lower standard devi-
ation, compared to strains from 2014 and 2015, indicating that the sample replicates grew more
steadily, and might explain why most increases in late 2015 were statistically accepted.
==PAGE:48==
47
Repeating the experiments with a standard strain, used on all plates and in multiple places on
the plates, could show if the increase in standard deviation was due to uncertainties in the plate
or if the bacteria did in fact grow with more variation in 2014 and early 2015 strains. This could
potentially tell if P. aeruginosa is starting to out-compete S. aureus in the CF patient, and be-
coming the dominant pathogen.
4.5
P. aeruginosa and S. aureus affect each other in co-culture
The two CFU experiments showed that P. aeruginosa from patient CF236 performed bad when
co-cultured with S. aureus from patient CF236. Experiments with PAO1 showed that only clin-
ical P. aeruginosa lost to S. aureus. Growth experiments showed that P. aeruginosa negatively
affected the growth of S. aureus. When grown closely together no effect could be seen on P.
aeruginosa, while S. aureus showed various phenotypic changes. All these results show that P.
aeruginosa and S. aureus affects each other when co-cultured.
When P. aeruginosa ﬁrst infected patient CF236 in 2014 till the last sputum sample in 2015, no
decline is seen in S. aureus. This suggests several things. The P. aeruginosa could rapidly under-
goes mutations reducing it’s growth and virulence towards S. aureus.
The P. aeruginosa and S. aureus could infect different areas of the lungs during the mutations,
explaining the unaffected S. aureus.
The P. aeruginosa could also previously have infected CF patients, and therefore already have
adapted to the CF lungs. This theory ﬁts with the reduced growth of P. aeruginosa when co-
cultured with S. aureus, for both 2014 and 2015 samples.
The growth rates for late 2015 strains suggest that P. aeruginosa affects S. aureus more, when
both have the same start density. This could indicate that P. aeruginosa is starting to suppress
S. aureus, and becoming the dominant pathogen in the patient. Future sputum samples from
patient CF236 will tell if this is in fact the case.
The differences in growth between the samples can either suggest that the P. aeruginosa found
in the 2014 sample, evolves in the lungs, and changes its afﬁnity towards S. aureus. It could also
mean that new P. aeruginosa strains infect the patient over time.
To test which of the theory’s is correct, or if both theories are correct, and multiple P. aeruginosa
strains infect the patient at certain time points, sequencing the strains could show if multiple
P. aeruginosa’s exists in the three time samples. This could also show the clone type of the P.
aeruginosa at the time points, and give a better image of the infection.

4.6
Proteomic analysis of DK2-P24M2-2003 and CF236-Sa
The proteomic data of the two clinical bacteria, showed that a total of 319 proteins from P24M2
and 553 proteins from CF236-Sa could be identiﬁed using LC-MS/MS. The optimal protein hits
from LC-MS/MS is 50% of the reference proteome, 5563 for P. aeruginosa PAO1 and 2889 for S.
aureus NCTC 8325. In this study the cover rate was only 5.7% for P. aeruginosa and 19% for S.
aureus, meaning there is still improvements needed for the new method to fully work, and give a
full picture of the protein expressions happening in the organisms. The main task of proteomics
in this study was to get the new method to work, and show that it is possible to get two organ-
isms to interact and later separate them. This goal was accomplished, but it still leaves room for
future optimizations.
When growing the bacteria as spots next to each other, and using the whole spot for quantifying
the difference in protein expression, only a part of the interacting spot might have differences
in expression. By using the whole spot, the protein expression is given as an average of all cells
in the spots, and might not give the best image of protein expression when interacting bacteria.
The proteomic results show that differences in expression can be seen between isolated and in-
teracting bacteria, but the differences might be higher if only the interacting zones in the spots
were used. This possesses a problem, as selecting only part of a spot, makes the data less quant-
itative, as the interaction zone differs in the spots, and the amount to take from the isolated
spots will be less clear.
The time use for a proteomic experiment, from start to ﬁnish, is over a week. Therefore the time
for getting the method good enough for running LC-MS/MS was limited, and only a single ex-
periment including quadruplicates could be done within the time limit of this thesis. Therefore
some factors, eg. incubation time, in the experiments were chosen based on assumptions, to
how the biggest protein yield were obtained.
The spots used for proteomics were incubated for 3 days on LB. The incubation time was based
on the time frame for pheotypical changes to appear in the spot assay experiments. The protein
yield might be higher with other incubation times, and measuring protein content in spots with
various incubation times, might lead to deeper LC-MS/MS runs.
As the method of growing the bacteria as spots next to each other was new, no previous pro-
tocol was available for appropriate lyse if the cells by sonication. Therefore various tests were
made with sample sonication, to ﬁnd a program with a high protein yield. Equal samples were
sonicated using different amplitudes and pulse intervals/breaks. The sonicated samples were
measured on a nanodrop, to get an idea of the amount of released proteins. The nanodrop has
==PAGE:50==
49
the disadvantage that it can also falsely measure DNA as proteins, giving a too high protein read.
It was estimated that if high concentrations of DNA were present in the samples, high concen-
trations of proteins would be present, as the cells must have been lysed for DNA to to be in the
samples.
To see if the cells were in fact lysed, and to make sure that the proteins used for LC-MS/MS were
a picture of the whole cell, and not just membrane proteins released by sonication, two samples,
only one sonicated, were compared in microscope. No whole cells could be seen in the sonic-
ated sample, that looked much different from the non-sonicated sample. This showed that the
sonication worked as intended.
The proteomic experiment with triplicates for P24M2 and quadruplicates for CF236-Sa, showed
that only two regulations in protein expressions could be found statistically signiﬁcant in CF236-
Sa, while no regulations were found signiﬁcant in P24M2. Therefore the multiple samples where
analyzed in pairs of interacting and isolated bacteria, to see if any tendencies could be seen in
the data. This analysis has no statistical value, but can be used to screen the samples and ﬁnd
interesting proteins.
Some variations can be seen in the fold change for P24M2 and CF236-Sa. In some cases where
more than one fold change can be found for a protein expression, the fold changes are both
positive and negative. This is estimated to result from insufﬁcient concentrations of the affected
proteins. While this report allows statistics to be ignored to some extend, to see trend in the data,
the pairs with contradictory data sets are simply to uncertain for predictions of tendencies, and
are as a result ignored.
4.6.1
CF236-Sa
In the spot assay, CF236-Sa had orange pigmentation when interacting with P24M2, while be-
ing white when isolated. In one of the four replicates, an fold increase of 1.7 was found in
the transcriptional regulator protein SarA. This protein has been found to positively regulate
crtM, an important enzyme for the biosynthesis of the S. aureus virulence factor and pigment-
ation agent staphyloxanthin [65]. This suggest that the staphyloxanthin biosynthesis pathway
is up-regulated when CF236-Sa interacts with P24M2, explaining the orange pigmentation in S.
aureus. The other proteins involved in the pathway are not detected by the LC-MS/MS, and by
optimizing the method, future results could show if the pathway is in fact up-regulated.
The general trend in CF236-Sa shows that several stress responses are regulated, as proteins in-
volved in stress are seen up-regulated (eg. Putative universal stress protein SAOUHSC_01819).
Generally reactions needing ATP and ADP are seen down-regulated (eg. Succinyl-CoA ligase

[ADP-forming] subunit alpha and Formate–tetrahydrofolate ligase). An explanation can be that
some the proteins involved in producing ATP from ADP are also seen down-regulated. The neg-
ative regulation of the protein AtpD was identiﬁed as statistically signiﬁcant. This protein is in-

AtpA [59] and AtpG [60], was also found down-regulated when looking for trends in the data,

This ﬁts the previous mentioned literature, stating that P. aeruginosa affects the electron trans-

defenses, and that CF236-Sa shifts towards fermentation.

as the proteins needed for division is expressed in reduced numbers. This ﬁnding could explain
why increased growth rates are observed when co-culturing P. aeruginosa and S. aureus.
Overall the proteomics data for CF236-Sa suggests that CF236-Sa is under stress from P24M2,
and therefore regulates several stress responses. When comparing with the spot assays, the pro-
teomic data also suggest that CF236-Sa is inhibited by P24M2 when grown closely together.

Future Research
This thesis has looked into interactions between clinical P. aeruginosa and S. aureus from CF
patients, both on a scale of growth, and in expression of proteins. The ﬁndings in this project
show that P. aeruginosa and S. aureus affects each other when grown together, but this has also
lead to further questions on how P. aeruginosa and S. aureus co-infect.
The effects S. aureus has on P24M2 should be further studied, to understand why colonies of
P24M2 thickens, and what consequences the interactions can have on the health of CF patients.
More cases where CF patients are infected by both P. aeruginosa and S. aureus simultaneously
should be studied, to further increase the knowledge of interactions between the bacteria, and
to increase our understand of what leads to synergy between the species, like P24M2 and S.
aureus.
Interaction studies using biomass carriers should also be considered. The experimental setup
used in this thesis gives rise to questions as to how the experiment could be optimized to better
allow multi species bioﬁlm interactions. As the clinical S. aureus used in this study grew much
faster than P. aeruginosa, a clear disadvantage were given to P. aeruginosa from the beginnings.
in vivo studies of CF co-infections could also be relevant to study, as to see how the bacteria
interact in a CF lung model.
More work should be put into optimizing the protein yield in the proteomics, so a broader pic-
ture of the protein regulations is seen. This could be done by increasing the amount of bacterial
culture in each sample. Each step of the process should be evaluated by protein yield, ex by gel
electrophoresis, and optimized to the highest yield.
The precision of the method should also be evaluated, ex by adding a known concentration of
a contaminant or protein, and measuring the ﬁnal concentration. This could give a picture of
how precise the method is.
Getting a higher protein yield could conﬁrm if the pqsABCDE operon is up-regulated in P24M2
when interacting with CF236-SA. It could also conﬁrm if the staphyloxanthin biosynthesis path-
way is up-regulated, or if other pathways contribute to the pigmentation of S. aureus when in-
teracting with P24M2.

Conclusion
Co-infections between P. aeruginosa and S. aureus can happen in the CF lungs. When P. aer-
uginosa adapts to the CF lungs, it undergoes several loss of function mutations, leading to re-
duced virulence towards S. aureus.
Clinical P. aeruginosa taken over a time period from a single CF patient (CF236), have been
shown to affect the growth rate of S. aureus from the same patient. The same S. aureus strains
have been shown to affect the composition of P. aeruginosa both in liquid culture and in bioﬁlm.
Phenotypical testing of interactions between P. aeruginosa and S. aureus taken in 2014 and 2015
in CF236, showed that P. aeruginosa affected S. aureus more in the latter sample. No effect were
observed on P. aeruginosa, while clear inhibition could be seen on the 2015 S. aureus strain in-
teractions. From these results it is concluded that P. aeruginosa and S. aureus interact and affects
each other when grown together.
Based on phenotypical changes, interactions between a DK2 P. aeruginosa strain and a S. aureus
from patient CF236, was chosen for proteomic analysis.
A new method for separating interacting bacteria prior to LC-MS/MS was proposed in this study.
The new method was proven able to ﬁnd two signiﬁcantly regulated proteins FHS and AtpD in
S. aureus. Though the method could ﬁnd regulated proteins, more work still needs to be done
in optimizing the method, before it can be fully applied as an experimental analysis.
The method also possessed the potential of being used as a way of screening for interesting pro-
teins in interacting bacteria. Several potentially interesting protein regulations were found in
S. aureus using this tactic. These proteins were used as potential explanations to phenotypic
effects seen in S. aureus spots. Proteins AtpD, AtpA and AtpG involved in ATP production were
found reduced and Ldh2, involved in fermentation of lactate from pyruvate, was up-regulated
when S. aureus interacted with a DK2 P. aeruginosa, indicating that the energy transport chain
in S. aureus is inhibisted by P. aeruginosa and that S. aureus shifted metabolism from respiration
towards fermentation. The enzyme FHS, using ATP to catalyze its reaction, was found down-
regulated, suggesting that enzymes using ATP are down-regulated by S. aureus as a result of the
inhibition of the energy transport chain. Two proteins involved in cell division, atl and FtsZ,
where both down-regulated, and might be part of the explanation to why P. aeruginosa affects
the growth rate of S. aureus. The protein SarA was found up-regulated, and might regulate the
orange pigmentation seen in interacting S. aureus.
Whether these protein play an important role in the phenotypic changes in S. aureus is still
uncertain, but it is concluded that proteomics strategies could potentially explain some of the
phenotypic changes observed.

A.4
Growth curves in Cytation 5 imaging reader
1) Make ON cultures of strains, by incubating 1 colony in liquid LB at 37°C.
2) Dilute mono-culture strains to OD600=0.01 in 1 ml LB.
3) Dilute co-culture strains to OD600=0.01 each in 1 ml LB.
4) Add 200 ml 3 times or more, to a 96 well micro titter plate. Remember space for a blank sample
(LB).
5) (optional) CMLAB BarSeal foil is put over the wells to avoid evaporation.
6) Plates are run on a BioTek® Cytation 5 imaging reader, with continuous shaking at 37°C, and
OD600 measure every 10 minutes.

tion of growth curve and the best correlation coefﬁcient (R).

A.7.2
Protein precipitation
1) Add 1.2 ml acetone 100% to sonicated protein mixture.
2) Incubate at -20°C ON.
3) Centrifuge 10.000 rpm for 5 minutes at 4 °C. Discharge supernatant.
4) Air-dry 5 minutes.
A.7.3
In-solution trypsin digestion for pellets of precipitated protein
Glatter et al. 2012 [55]
For 10 µg protein:
1) Dissolve protein pellet in 20 µl 8M Urea, 50mM Tris-HCl ph 8.0
2) Add 2 µl 450mM DTT
3) Incubate 45 minutes at room temperature
4) Add 4 µl fresh 500mM IAA
5) Incubate dark for 1 hour at room temperature
6) Add 74 µl 50mM Tris-HCl ph 8.0
7) Add 2 µl 0.1 µg/µl Trypsin
8) Incubate ON at 37°C
9) Stop reaction with 2 µl 10% TFA

Rappsilber et al. 2007 [56]
1) Punch out two C18 ﬁlters and place at the end of DL-10 diamond tower pack 10 µl tip, for

2) Add 20 µl 60% ACN, 0.1% TFA to each tip, and spin approximately 20 seconds at 4000 rpm*
3) Add 20 µl 0.1% TFA to each tip, and spin approximately 20 seconds at 4000 rpm*
4) Add 100 µl sample and spin*
5) Add 40 µl 0.1% TFA and spin*

8) Add 1 µl 5% Formic Acid
9) Add 6 µl H2O
