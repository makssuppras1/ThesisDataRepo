Abstract
The atmosphere of the Earth is constantly bombarded by cosmic rays. This leads to an increase
in atmospheric ionization. Evidence show an increased aerosol nucleation when ions are present
due to a lowered Gibbs free energy barrier, leading to the supersaturated gas condensing earlier.
To investigate the ion induced and neutral nucleation, an electrode (with a potential up to 10
kV) has been ﬁtted to a 1 m3 reaction chamber at DTU, creating an electric ﬁeld inside the cham-
ber when turned on, this electrode is expected to remove ions present in the chamber.
Two types of experiments were performed, one varying the concentration of condensable gas, one
varying the electric ﬁeld. A total 236 experiments were performed during a 5 month project du-
ration.
It was found that the ion induced nucleation is more dominant at low nucleation rates (under 0.1
cm−3s−1), with about 75 % of the total nucleation being ion induced. At high nucleation rates
(over 3 cm−3s−1) the ion induced nucleation is less dominant. At high nucleation rates the ion
induced nucleation was found to be 45 %. The CERN, CLOUD experiment [Dun+16] found re-
ductions of 60 % and 28 % at low - high nucleation rates respectively when eliminating ions.
By Varying the electric ﬁeld inside the reaction chamber a sudden ratio change between the neu-
tral and total nucleation was found at voltages from 65 V to 100 V. In this voltage region the ion
induced nucleation increases signiﬁcantly. At this electric ﬁeld intensity the ion lifetime is much
shorter than the ion recombination time this result is therefore surprising.
Unknown underlying mechanisms might be present.

1
Introduction
In this project we wish to describe the aerosol nucleation at low sulphuric acid (H2SO4) levels.
We intend to remove/minimize (depending on the measurement series) the ionization present in
the experiment chamber such that only the neutral nucleation is left.
As UV radiation hits the atmosphere and the ozone layer of the Earth, the ozone molecules (O3)
resonate (peaking at ca. 255 nm). In this process ozone molecules are photolysed producing O2.
The free O atoms react with water vapor (H2O) present in the air forming OH radicals. The
highly reactive OH radicals combines with pollutant sulfuric dioxide (SO2) forming HSO3.
HSO3 combines with O2 leading to SO3 and H2O. The Sulfuric trioxide then reacts with water
vapor leading to the formation of Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) [Sor09].
When enough of these sulfuric acid particles are clustered together, they are called aerosols. In
general aerosols are liquid or solid particles in suspension in air (or in gas). The diameter of
secondary aerosols (which we investigate in this project) is typically < 1µm. Primary aerosols
coming from e.g. sea-spray and dust are typically larger (> 1µm).
The nucleation the formation of aerosols. Speciﬁcly it is the exact moment where the energy
barrier for stable clusters is crossed. The aerosols then grow either by condensation or by
coagulation with other aerosols.
The ionized particles, which are removed/minimized in this project, comes from Cosmic rays
constantly bombarding the atmosphere leaving ionized particles behind. This ionization leads to
an increase in chemical reactivity and aerosol nucleation.
Cosmic rays consist that primarily of high energetic protons coming either from the sun or from
outer space as remnants from supernovae explosions. These highly energetic protons can be
incredibly toxic to the human body leading to mutations in the DNA. This could lead to sickness
or even death if enough of this radiation is absorbed by the body.
Fortunately we have both the magnetic ﬁeld and atmosphere acting as a shield, protecting us
from most of the harmful particles.
As the protons collide with the particles atmosphere the incoming particles produce a shower of
secondary particles leaving ionization behind.

The experiment chamber used to perform the experiments is a cylindrical stainless steel tank
1.125m long and a radius of 0.53m giving a volume of 1 m3.
Into the center of the chamber an electrode is inserted. By applying a voltage to this electrode,
particles of opposite charge (opposite of the electrode voltage) are attracted to the electrode and
thereby removed from the chamber.
Particles having the same charge as the electrode will be accelerated towards the chamber walls.
The grounded chamber wall acts as a second conductor attracting ions with the same charge as
the electrode. Thereby some (or all) of the ionization is removed in the chamber after some time,
depending on the voltage potential applied to the electrode
If the electrode voltage is high the ion lifetime is small. If the lifetime is lower than the ion
recombination time we can perform measurements where only the neutral nucleation is present.
The electrode and Teﬂon insulator was constructed previous to these experiments. To ﬁt this
electrode to the experiment chamber (see section 3 for experiment setup) a blind ﬂange was
constructed that tightly grips the Teﬂon insulator allowing us to insert the ﬂange to the
experiment.
Furthermore a secure connection between the electrode and a high voltage supply had to be
established to ensure personal safety while people are physically present in the laboratory.

2.1
Types of aerosols
Aerosol are particles either liquid or solid suspended in air.
In this project the type of aerosol we are interested in is H2SO4 as it nucleates into an aerosol
but many types of aerosols is found.
Soot, pollen, sea salt particles, mineral dust, asbestos ﬁbres, ﬂy ash are some of these. The size of
these aerosols range from 1 nm to over 1 µm.
Depending on altitude and pollution the aerosol concentrations vary from under 10 particles at
20 km altitude to 105 cm−3 in urban areas [CUR06] (2 cm−3s−1 at ground level peaking at
approximately 40 cm−3s−1 at an altitude of 12-13 km according to [Baz08], corresponding to the
top of the Troposphere). Most of these aerosols are small.
The aerosols may be deﬁned in two main groups. Primary particles and secondaries.
Primary particles comes directly from a source e.g. salt particles coming from sea spray, ash from
volcano eruptions or pollen from plants and trees.
Secondaries (which we are interested in) are produced in the atmosphere.
The secondary particles is produced as UV photons sent by the sun collides with the ozone layer
The ozone molecule are photolysed into O2. The free oxygen atom combines with water becoming
H2O2, Hydrogen Peroxide, which is unstable quickly decaying into OH radicals.
The very reactive OH radicals reacts with the pollutant pollutant Sulfur dioxide SO2 present in
the atmosphere forming HOSO2.
After this process the O2 molecules combine with the newly formed HOSO2 molecules which is
also unstable decaying into SO3, Sulfur Trioxide, and HO2, Hydroperoxyl.
Ultimately the SO3 molecules combine with water leading to the formation of Sulfuric acid,
H2SO4, one type of aerosols.
In the reaction chain explained in sec 1 it is evident that several elements must be present in
order to form these H2SO4 aerosols.
Ozone must be present in the atmosphere in order to start the reaction. If the ozone somehow
vanish from the atmosphere the cloud formation process would change ultimately leading to the
4

cloud coverage changing structure.
Secondly, atmospheric water vapor is critical as H2O reacts with the free oxygen atom and reacts
with the Sulfuric Trioxide molecule, furthermore water vapor condenses onto the aerosol
combining into a cloud if the aerosol density manages to grow large enough.
The process described above is shown more schematically below [Sor09]

O + H2O −→OH + OH
OH + SO2 = HOSO2
HOSO2 + O2 −→SO3 + HO2

SO3 + H2O + M −→H2SO4 + M

2.2
Nucleation
The vapour pressure of H2SO4 is low and is lowered further by the presence of water. Therefore
the H2SO4 easily becomes supersaturated. The supersaturation means that the there is an
abundance of H2SO4, and the gas is more likely to condense.
The H2SO4 particles may collide with other H2SO4 particles (coagulate) forming a cluster.
When this cluster is large enough (the critical cluster size or nucleation barrier), it becomes an
aerosol.
To become an aerosol the clustered particles must overcome the thermodynamic potential , ∆G
(Gibbs free energy). The supersaturated gas prefers to go through a phase transition from gas to
condense since the Gibbs free energy would be minimized due to a lower chemical potential.

In the beginning of the growth phase the surface tension has large signiﬁcance, later as the radius

The phase transition from gas to condensing into larger particles costs energy.
In the beginning of the process this surface tension is larger than the energy the gas gain by

Classical aerosol formation predicts a level of binary homogeneous nucleation H2SO4 and H2O
much lower than the observed nucleation in the lower atmosphere.
Ammonia (NH3) has therefore been investigated as a factor to accelerate the aerosol formation
process this is called ternary homogeneous nucleation.
Ammonia might stabilize the the molecular cluster earlier than seen in the binary nucleation. As
the clusters stabilize earlier, the critical size of the cluster decrease hence the thermodynamic
barrier is lowered [Yu00].
This theory, though, underestimates the formation of new particles up to a factor of 10.
Another underlying mechanism must therefore be present, the signiﬁcance of ions in the
nucleation process is therefore investigated
Manningen et al. 2010 [Man10] found by performing experiments on 12 diﬀerent sites in Europa
that the ion inﬂuence on the total nucleation was typically in the range of 1-30%. Increasing as
the total formation rate decrease.

2.3
Clouds
The cloud coverage on Earth has huge impact on the climate on Earth, several eﬀects takes place.
As the cloud coverage increase, shortwave heat radiation is absorbed and reﬂected [Ped12], as
secondary radiation from the sun, is kept in the atmosphere.
However, the high albedo in white clouds reﬂects a large portion of the suns radiation leading to
clouds having a net negative forcing of - 27.7 Wm−2 [MS00] resulting in a net cooling eﬀect on
the Earths climate, suggesting that if the cloud increase, the Earth will cool.
Due to this it is extremely interesting to gain a better understanding of the cloud formation on
Earth.
This could in the long run increase the understanding of global climate eﬀects such as global
warming and the consequences thereof.
Furthermore, an increase in the knowledge could help exo-planet scientists understand the
mechanisms of cloud formation on planets alien to our own in the search of life in the universe.

2.7
Nucleation Rate
The nucleation rate at which the nucleation is taking place the the critical cluster size. At other
sizes this is called the formation rate. The nucleation rate is enhanced by the presence of ions.
[Sve07] describes the formation of new H2SO4 aerosols being reduced by 20% applying an electric
ﬁeld of 6.000V m−1, and 50 % at 12.000V m−1. Notice that this eﬀect is heavily dependent on
other parameters, primarily the gas concentration (H2SO4concentration)
As the UV light is turned on the process of ozone photolysis lead to the formation of H2SO4.
18
==PAGE:33==
This process takes time, therefore we expect to see a delay from the turning on of the UV lights
until the ﬁrst aerosols appear.
The small aerosols (that survive possible loss factors) grow larger and begin to appear in the
PSM (see section 3.2) that detects aerosols of 1.4 nm size.
At ﬁrst the number of new aerosols is expected to show an exponential increase as a function of
time. The increase in aerosols will then stabilize converging towards some upper production limit
(equilibrium: production = loss) if the UV radiation is present for long enough.
After the ﬁrst exponential increase, a region of linearly increasing aerosol count is entered. This
region can be used to ﬁnd the production rate (nucleation rate) by applying a linear ﬁt to this
almost perfectly linear region. The slope of this ﬁt is then the nucleation rate.
When the UV lights are turned oﬀthe peak count will appear after a time delay. After the peak
is reached the aerosol decay begin since the production rate is zero.

2.8
Growth Rate
After the initial nucleation a growth phase is entered where H2SO4 is condensed onto the aerosol
or where two aerosol coagulate into one large aerosols.
The growth rate can be investigated using both the PSM and the CPC. The PSM measures
aerosol down to the size of 1 nm and the CPC measures aerosols of 3 nm we can ﬁnd the growth
rate of aerosols inside the chamber using the time diﬀerence between the detection in each of the
instruments.
Losses occur in this growth process. Not all 1 nm aerosols grow to the CPC detectable 3 nm due
to losses either by the pressure relieving outlets, or by particles getting trap on the sides of the
chamber.
Therefore the decision is made to analyse the time diﬀerence when the aerosol counts has reached
50% in the PSM and CPC respectively.
This growth rate might increase the knowledge of the size of possible losses present in the
chamber.

3.2
PSM
The Particle Size Magniﬁer (PSM, Airmodus A10) [A10] is able to detect particles as low as 1nm
using a dual-stage detection technique.
The dual-stage means that the particles are ﬁrst passed through the PSM where the small
particles are grown to a larger size by condensing Diethylen-Glycol ((HOCH2CH2)2O) onto the
aerosols. Then the enhanced aerosols passes through the Ultraﬁne Condensation Particle Counter
24
==PAGE:39==
(UCPC 3776, TSI) [TSIb] where the particles are grown further by heating buthanol and
applying the buthanol to the incoming airﬂow from the PSM. The airﬂow is then passing through
a cooler allowing the buthanol to condense onto the aerosols. The aerosols are this way grown to
a size of approximately 10 nm (from a size of 1-2 nm) which allows the UCPC to detect the
aerosols optically.
Figure 13: Setup of PSM, UCPC, CPC and humidiﬁer. Air ﬂows from the chamber through
the black tube splitting in two, one to the PSM (black box, right) and one to the CPC (grey box,
left). From the PSM the black cable ﬂows up to the UCPC (grey, above the PSM) after the initial
growth stage. Above the UCPC is a container of Buthanol. The humidiﬁer (steel top, bottom left)
send humid air through a tube into the chamber. Note that the UCPC is not attached to its PSM.
The black tube seen in the left side of the PSM is connected to the UCPC when running.
Note that the 1 nm cutoﬀis at a lower detection rate than the one we are working with. A larger
detection rate means a higher cutoﬀdiameter. The cutoﬀdiameter (is the diameter where 50 %
of incoming particles are measured) is the size measurable by the instrument. There is therefore a
trade oﬀwhen calibrating the instrument, higher detection rate or the ability to trace smaller
molecule clusters.

In this project the cutoﬀdiameter is approximately 1.4 nm.
From one of the chamber outlets (2.5cm long inlet/outlet) an airﬂow of 2.5 L/min ﬂows through
a short tube 0.5m of length. This airﬂow corresponds to a PSM calibration with a cutoﬀ
diameter of 1.4nm with a detection eﬃciency of 50%. The higher the incoming airﬂow, the lower
the cutoﬀdiameter. This eﬃciency increases to upwards of 80% as the incoming particles
increase in size.
From the PSM another short tube connects the PSM to the Ultraﬁne Condensation Particle

The UCPC is detecting the particles optically, by further condensating buthanol onto the

The UCPC as well as CPC, has the same working principle. The airﬂow passes through a heated

To minimize the chance of misunderstandings between the CPC and the UCPC.
The dual-stage PSM plus UCPC is called ’PSM’ from this point on.

A humidiﬁer (Lauda ecoline Re 306) is connected to the chamber keeping the relative humidity

RH is given by the ratio of partial presseure of water to the equilibrium of water at a given
temperature. The humidiﬁer temperature is set at 18 degrees Celsius during the experiments.
The humidity in the chamber is dependant on the temperature set, higher temperature, higher
humidity.
18 degrees Celsius leads to a relative humidity of 36.5% in the chamber at an airﬂow into the
chamber of 6 L/min. The relative humidity is of great importance in the aerosol formation
further explained in section 2.1 as H2O molecules directly aﬀects by increasing the production
rate of H2SO4. In reality water molecules are almost always abundant in the nucleation process.
We wish to keep relative humidity constant at a stable level that allows for a healthy nucleation
inside the chamber.
The humidiﬁer also has a temperature control, this temperature is kept constant as close to 22.5
degrees Celsius as possible. Very minute changes in temperature changes might occur when
people are physically present in the laboratory due to the body heat released. This should though
only result in changes on the scale of a few hundreds of a degree.

The error bars on the points shown in the ﬁgures containing ratio plots are calculated using the
rule of error propagation. In this case the two values used to construct the ratio (Joff and Jon) is
a multiplicative expression where each have their respective standard deviation. The standard
deviation is for both Joff and Jon) found using drift-corrected data.
Assuming that we under given conditions, collect data from 3 experiments, then 3 nucleation
rates are found. The 3 nucleation rates are used to construct the average nucleation at the given
parameters.
The standard deviation is constructed from the vector of these 3 nucleation rates. If the three
nucleation rates are close to each other, the deviation will be small.

Summary and Conclusions
In this project we have carried out an investigation of aerosol nucleation under controlled condi-
tions in a large sealed reaction chamber subjected to an intense electric ﬁeld.
It is found that aerosol nucleation is greatly reduced in presence of a large electric ﬁeld.
In the experiments an electrode potential of 10 kV was applied. Under this condition, the nucle-
ation was reduced by 26 % - 83 % depending on the parameters in the chamber.
A 10 kV electric potential in the center of the chamber removes ions much faster than the recom-
bination time of ions, the presence of ionization while this potential is applied is therefore consid-
ered removed. Basically. only the neutral nucleation will take place under these conditions.
It was found that the nucleation was greatly reduced (by 71 - 74.5 %) at low nucleation levels
(nucleation rates below 0.5 cm−3s−1).
At high nucleation rates (above 1 cm−3s−1) the electric ﬁeld presence seems to reduce the total
nucleation to a lesser extend.
Four measurements series were made under high nucleation conditions with a 10 kV ﬁeld, show-
ing a reduction in nucleation ranging from 26 % - 64 %.
These ﬁndings agree with [Dun+16] (estimating ions are responsible for 28 % of the total nucle-
ation), though our measurements ﬁnd a higher ion inﬂuence. This suggests a correlation between
ion induced nucleation and the amount of nucleation taking place.
The dominance of ions at low production conditions was suggested in [Ped12]. Our measurements
support this as we ﬁnd that the total nucleation aﬀected much more by the electric ﬁeld at low
gas concentrations than a high gas concentrations in the chamber.
The second type of observations focused on reducing the ionization in the chamber rather than
eliminating it, by varying the electrode potential and applying voltages.
During these experiments electric ﬁeld intensity was varied by changing the electrode voltage.
Two measurements series were taken, one at high electrode voltages using a negative power sup-
ply, and one at low electrode voltages using a positive power supply. The total voltage range in-
vestigated during these measurements span from 10 V to 10 kV. A critical voltage zone were ob-
served at voltages in the region from 65 V - 100 V.
In this zone the nucleation suddenly reduced signiﬁcantly. Theory of ion recombination cannot

explain this.
An experiment at 80 V supported this and indicated that the criticality is found in this voltage
range.
A possible explanation (not yet been proven) for this found critical region could be that, the
ions attracted by the electrode form a screen around the electrode minimizing the electric ﬁeld
is the chamber. The wak electric ﬁeld increase the ion lifetime (that is reduced by the electric
ﬁeld) of the ions placed outside the screen. This increase in lifetime might explain the nucleation
behaviour observed during the experiments in this project. This is a very interesting result and
might induce future research on this topic.
To my knowledge no previous articles have investigated this phenomena.
If more time had been available, a top priority would have been to re-do all experiments under
the exact same conditions to check for stability and reproduceability.
Furthermore, incorporating a working mass spectrometer in the experiment would provide quan-

the chamber would be known allowing for a better understanding of the processes occuring in the
