Abstract
Supported by records that link the existence of motor impairments in familiar high-risk
for schizophrenia and bipolar disorders, the sulci variability is explored to gain a further
understanding of the morphology of the brain under these contexts.
Two techniques
were employed to capture the variability of the central sulci via a non-linear reduction of
dimensionality, with Isomap, and via direct geometrical measures. The impact of being
at familial high-risk for schizophrenia and bipolar disorders and the impact of motor
performances in the morphology of the central sulci brain is explored in the central sulcus.
Geometrical measures on the sulcus volumes have shown a significant effect of the balance
movement score in the superior-medial region of the sulcal depth. Features extracted with
the Isomap technique have shown an effect of an epsilon-like shape in the central sulcus
related to high aiming and catching movement scores.
As far as it is known, the effect of familial high-risk children for schizophrenia or bipolar
disorders was for the first time explored in this study. No strong significant effects were
found related to this respect.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Schizophrenia and Bipolar disorders affect people all over the world. Several factors play
a role in the development of these disorders; however, genetic and environmental factors
have a major influence. Some children at familial high risk for schizophrenia show devel-
opmental deficits in cognitive, motor and social factors. How these factors translate into
the development of the brain is important for both early diagnosis and the understand-
ing of the mechanisms behind these disorders. Among the early symptoms reported in
these disorders, impairments of fine motor and coordination skills have been overlooked
in development over the years. To further explore the morphological impact of motor
skills in the brain of familial at-risk children for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, the
central sulcus is explored. In the brain, the central sulcus is hypothesized to be related
to motor performance, as this sulcus separates the primary motor cortex, responsible for
planning/executing voluntary movements and the somatosensory cortex, responsible for
processing sensory information from the environment.
In this master thesis project, structural magnetic resonance data from children at familial
high risk for schizophrenia or bipolar disorders will be analyzed, as part of an ongoing
longitudinal study - the Danish High-Risk and Resilience Study. This report is building on
top of studies reported in this Cohort by Burton et al. 2017, which has found a significant
correlation between familial at-risk for Schizophrenia and impairments in dexterity scores.
In particular, the central sulcus shape will be studied using non-linear geometrical analysis
and direct geometrical measures, as well as direct physical measures, to test relevant
features that will aid in establishing potential relationships between subject groups and
subject motor performances.
Aims
The project objectives can be divided into physiological and technical ones. Included in
the physiological aims are:
• Gaining a better understanding of the brain variability, in the central sulcus region
in children;
• Exploring the relationship between the central sulcus shape variability and familial
at-risk for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder;
• Exploring the relationship between the shape variations and clinical motor perfor-
mance;
1
==PAGE:12==
In the technical aspect of the project, the aims include:
• Manipulate clinical structural magnetic resonance imaging;
• Brain morphological shape analysis in terms of morphological geometrical properties
and group clustering;
• Statistical analysis that considers physiological constraints;
Hypothesis
Hypothesis 1. The central sulcus shape is correlated to motor performance.
Hypothesis 2. Group differences are correlated with the shape of the central sulcus.

Chapter 2
Physiological Background
2.1
Psychiatric Disorders
Psychiatric disorders have been present throughout human history and have caused sig-
nificant challenges for individuals globally.
It is estimated that approximately 10-20%
of the world’s population is affected by some form of psychiatric disorder (Kessler et al.
2005). The formal recognition of psychiatric disorders began in the late XIX century (s).
These disorders are characterized by disturbances in thought, emotion, and behaviour,
which can significantly impair daily functioning and quality of life (American Psychiatric
Association 2013).
In this project, schizophrenia and bipolar disorders are explored, both of which share var-
ious clinical elements, such as risk factors, high heritability, unknown pathogenesis, onset,
and association with premature mortality, among others.
2.1.1
Schizophrenia
Schizophrenia is a chronic mental disorder, accounting for a worldwide prevalence of about
1% (Saha et al. 2005). Diagnosed with clinical assessment, schizophrenia is characterized
by positive and negative symptoms, depending on whether the symptom is character-
ized by an excess/distortion of normal functions or by a reduction of normal functions.
Common positive symptoms are hallucinations (sensory experiences from no external stim-
ulus), delusions (false beliefs), disorganized thinking/speech, and grossly disorganized or
catatonic (lack of rigid movements). The negative symptoms include social withdrawal,
decreased motivation and cognitive deficits in working memory and executive function
McCutcheon, Reis Marques, and Howes 2020.
2.1.1.1
Onset and clinical development
The typical age where schizophrenia is diagnosed is around early adulthood, but symp-
toms might arise earlier. Subtle motor and cognitive impairments arise before the first
psychotic episode. Before this, a slow increase in all symptoms fronts can be seen dur-
ing late adolescence and early adulthood. A general description of the clinical course of
schizophrenia is shown in figure 2.2).
2.1.1.2
Risk Factors and associated Neurobiology
Two components that can explain the development of schizophrenia: heritability and en-
vironmental. Heritability is estimated at approximately 80% based on population studies.
Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have found that 100 loci contribute to a genetic

variance associated with schizophrenia (Sullivan, Kendler, and Neale 2003) (Ripke et al.
2014).
Molecular pathways that could be involved in the development of the disorder were as-
sociated with abnormal regulation of the postsynaptic membrane, synaptic transmission,
and voltage-gated potassium channels (Ursini et al. 2018). Another study showed that
risk-related genes are related to specific neuron populations, such as the hippocampal
pyramidal cells, medium spiny neurons, and cortical interneurons. Postmortem studies

complement and microglial (gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA)-ergic and glutamatergic)
systems (Osimo et al. 2019) (Lewis, Hashimoto, and Volk 2005).
The output of cortical pyramidal cells, which are primarily excitatory neurons, is moder-
ated by inhibitory neurons that release the neurotransmitter GABA. The interaction be-
tween excitation and inhibition generates oscillations in a certain frequency (around 40Hz)
that characterize patterns of neural activity in the brain. In individuals with schizophre-
nia, these networks appear to be impaired in a way that disrupts the normal functioning of
the networks affecting cognition and negative symptoms: loss of pyramidal cell dendritic
spines leads to less excitation to GABAergic, which leads to a decrease in inhibitory pro-
cesses on pyramidal cells (firing of these pyramidal neurons are central in the generation
of the 40Hz frequency).
Some studies within twin populations show, however, divergent clinical outcomes. The
environmental factor can play a role in these cases, as well as in cases with no prior
genetic factors. In fact, prenatal and obstetric complications, including malnutrition and
infections, have been associated with the risk of developing schizophrenia (Bramon et al.

2005) (Hulshoff Pol et al. 2000). The combination of hereditary and prenatal adversities
has been shown to increase the overall risk by a factor of 5 (Ursini et al. 2018). Other risk
factors include belonging to an ethnic minority, urban living, first-generation migration,
childhood trauma, and cannabis use.
Stress generation factors prompt the dopamine
system to boost its response. If there are cortical deficits (responsible for higher-order
functions like planning, decision-making and regulatory control) the regulation of this
increase in dopamine is impaired, especially in the striatum (where rewards and motivation
are processed). Over time, this can lead to inappropriate assignment of importance or
”salience” to otherwise irrelevant stimuli, resulting in a positive feedback loop of psychotic
symptoms (since having psychosis also generates stress) Osimo et al. 2019.
The physiological and morphological changes reported are
• large ventricles (Pearlson and Marsh 1999), more specifcally the third ventricle (Boos
et al. 2007)
• reduction of brain volumes, that might include whole brain (Haijma et al. 2013),
hemispheric volume or region-wise changes (such as reported for temporal lobe vol-
ume reductions)(DeLisi et al. 2006)
• decrease in white matter volume (Haijma et al. 2013) (DeLisi et al. 2006)
• changes in gray matter volume, which some studies indicate a whole brain reduction
(Haijma et al. 2013)(Boos et al. 2007), others localize specifically region-wise in the
frontal and temporal lobes, cingulate and insular cortex, and the thalamus (Ellison-

• decrease in hippocampus volume (Boos et al. 2007)
• decreased sulcus depth in the frontal lobe (Wei et al. 2022)
• large reductions of caudate nucleus and thalamus volumes (Haijma et al. 2013)
2.1.1.3
Motor Behaviour Impairments in Schizophrenia
The presence of abnormal motor behavior in schizophrenia has been reported in classical
literature as a characteristic feature of the disorder, as it is manifested in childhood and in
never-medicated patients. Motor symptoms range greatly, encompassing different aspects
of motor dysfunction. Symptoms include abnormal involuntary movements, parkinsonism
(rigidity, tremor, slowness of movement), neurological soft signs (poor coordination or
motor persistence) and catatonia (rigidity and freezing of position).
Brain structure neuroimaging studies related to motor symptoms remain low. However,
structural and functional abnormalities have been shown in several brain regions, such
as in pre-motor and motor cortices, basal ganglia, thalamus, and the connecting white
matter tracts (Walther and Strik 2012).
Schizophrenia has also been associated with significantly less motor activity than the
healthy controls, in a study that used actigraphy to record the motor activity of patients
with schizophrenia and major depression and healthy controls (Berle et al. 2010).
2.1.2
Bipolar Disorders
Bipolar disorders are characterized by significant mood swings, including features of ma-
nia/hypomania and depression. Bipolar disorders can be segregated into two categories.
Bipolar I disorder accounts for around 0.6-1.0% of global lifetime prevalence and the di-
agnosis includes the presence of at least one syndromal manic episode, i.e., a period of
5
==PAGE:16==
abnormally elevated mood and high energy, often including impulsive behaviour, decreased
need for sleep, and grandiose ideas. In bipolar II Disorder (0.4-1.1% of global prevalence),
a combination of hypomanic and depression states is necessary for the diagnosis of this
variation. The hypomanic states are less severe cases of manic states. When individuals
experience major depressive episodes, symptoms related to intense sadness or hopeless-
ness, loss of interest in activities, and other depressive states are often present (Merikangas
et al. 2011)Grande et al. 2016.
2.1.2.1
Onset and clinical development
Much like in schizophrenia, the great majority of the disorder’s onset is around early
adulthood, with symptoms manifestation gradually manifesting. However, in bipolar a
trimodal age‐at‐onset distribution has been reported across 14 studies, with frequent onset
at around a mean of 17, 26 and 41.9 years of age, being around 17 years the most frequent
onset peak (Bolton et al. 2021).

whereas bipolar II disorder is characterized by at least one hypomanic and one depressive
episode (part b). Mixed features can occur in both disorders (Image credits to ’Bipolar
disorders’ by Vieta et al. 2018 )
2.1.2.2
Risk Factors and associated Neurobiology
As in schizophrenia, bipolar disorder is highly heritable (approximately 70%). Addition-
ally, bipolar I disorder shares a lot of the same disorder-related loci as schizophrenia,
while bipolar II disorder with major depressive disorder. Recent research has identified
approximately 30 genetic variants associated with bipolar disorders, which are involved
in several biological processes, including signal transmission, the glutamatergic system
(critical for excitatory signalling), hormone regulation, immune pathways, and histone
pathways. These changes can lead to hyperexcitability and dendritic spine loss, hormones
that influence brain development and function deregulation, neuroinflammation and affect
gene expression and thus influence neuronal development and plasticity (Staal et al. 2000).
6
==PAGE:17==
The combination of these genetic factors and the environment contributes to the disease
development. Genetic predispositions interact with environmental triggers, such as early
life stress, trauma, and substance use, which are also common risk factors for schizophrenia.
As a consequence, increased cortisol levels, neurotoxicity (leading to cell damage) and
hyperexcitability related to substance use.
This interplay can lead to impaired circuitry and brain matter change, such as frontotem-
poral grey matter loss, axonal damage, default-mode network deactivation failure (inabil-
ity to properly deactivate a network of brain regions) and disturbed emotional processing.
(Hibar et al. 2017).
The physiological and morphological changes reported are progressive and include:
• grey matter loss, specifically observed in the left pars opercularis, which is located
in the frontal lobe involved in language processing and motor functions; in the left
fusiform gyrus, located in the temporal lobe and relevant for facial recognition and
processing of complex visual information; in the left rostral middle frontal cortex,
located in the frontal lobe associated with higher cognitive functions such as decision-
making and executive control and in the hippocampus (critical for memory formation
and spatial navigation, as well as implicated in mood regulation and stress response);
Hibar et al. 2017
• white matter integrity loss in the corpus callosum, corona radiata, cingulum bundle,
superior longitudinal fasciculus and the thalamic radiation, suggesting demyelina-
tion;
• resting state connectivity alterations in the default mode network, a network involved
in self-referential thinking and emotion regulation (Meda et al. 2014).
2.1.2.3
Motor Behaviour in Bipolar Disorders
Abnormal motor behaviour has been presented as a symptom of Bipolar Disorder (BP)
throughout the literature.
Kang et al. 2018 evaluated the activity and energy variables during gait and sit-to-walk of
individuals with BP. This study found that during the hypomania stage, individuals with
BP moved faster and produced the largest amount of force during gait and sit-to-walk,
compared to both healthy individuals and individuals with BP during either a depressed
state or an euthymic state.
De Crescenzo et al. 2017 evaluated thirteen studies comparing the motor activity of BP
patients and healthy controls and found a general decrease in mean activity.
2.2
Central Sulcus
2.2.1
Central Sulcus and Motor Function
Traditionally, the brain cortex has been divided into regions or lobes that differ in mor-
phology and function. The central sulcus separates two regions related to motor function:
the motor cortex and the somatosensory cortex. The motor cortex, linked to voluntary
movement, is connected to spinal motor neurons and through them sends signals down
the motor tracks to the body. It is located anterior to the central sulcus, in the precentral
gyrus, frontal lobe. The somatosensory cortex receives tactile and other sensory informa-
tion that is carried through ascending pathways from the body periphery to this cortex
region, in the postcentral gyrus, parietal lobe [Eric R. Kandel in Principles of Neural
7
==PAGE:18==
Science, Fifth Edition] (Konstantopoulos and Giakoumettis 2023). In 1937, Wilder Pen-
field (Penfield and Boldrey 1937) discovered that different body parts are prompted when
neurons in the cortical region surrounding the central sulcus are electrically stimulated.
According to this mapping (figure 2.4), all body parts were linked to different portions of
the cortex, and in different scales. The lower body is mediated by neurons in the medial
brain, followed by the hands and fingers, mediated by a big portion of the neurons, and
the face in the most lateral side of the cortex. Interestingly, the size of the cortical body
representations along this central line is proportional to the density of innervation, hence
the high fineness of sensitivity/control in the fingers or lips [Eric R. Kandel in Principles
of Neural Science, Fifth Edition].
Figure 2.3: Somatotopical organization of the primary somatosensory cortex (A) and pri-
mary motor cortex (B) with respective homunculus representation, indicating the cortical
size that each body part mediates. (Image credits to Eric R. Kandel in Principles of Neural
Science, Fifth Edition) who adapted from Peneld and Rasmussen 1950.)
2.2.2
Central Sulcus Description
The central sulcus is used as a great reference point in the brain, due to its important
positioning and its characteristic shape. In its mid-area, a characteristic curve is relatively
preserved as an omega or epsilon shape, in the axial view, or hook shape in the sagittal
view. This curvy omega shape is commonly designated the ”hand-knob”, as it resembles
a door knob and the location corresponds to the hand in the somatotopic organization of
the motor cortex (Yousry et al. 1997).
The sulci depth is in function of the location of the sulcus, from the most medial and
dorsal brain area to the most lateral and ventral. Common forms of the sulcus have been
studied in terms of top and bottom length, thickness, depth and profile.
2.2.2.1
Depth
The depth is one of the most described features of the shape of the central sulcus. It is
a linear length from the surface of the brain to the most nuclear part of the brain, along
the space that separates the precentral from the postcentral gyrus.
Using Magnetic Resonance (MR) and manual measurements along the anterior-posterior
commissure plane, Amunts, Schlaug, et al. 1996 showed depth that in right-handers, the
left central sulcus is deeper than the right, and the opposite for the left-handers. Later,
Amunts, Jaè Ncke B, et al. 1998 refined their studies and showed that right-hander males
exhibit a deeper central sulcus on the left side than on the right. Left-handers show less
significance in asymmetry than right-handers. No interhemispheric asymmetry was seen

3.1
Shape Analysis
In neuroimaging analysis, three-dimensional objects can be represented in various forms.
These objects can be described by vertices with coordinates in a 3D coordinate system.
When edges connect these vertices to form faces, the resulting structure is known as a
mesh. Alternatively, if the vertices are not associated with specific coordinates in a 3D
space, they can be referred to as nodes. When these nodes are connected by edges, the
structure can be referred to as a graph.
3.1.1
Dimensionality Reduction - PCA
Dimensionality reduction techniques assume that a high-dimensional space (n-dimensional
manifold) has an intrinsic dimensionality lower than n with which data can be explained.
principle component analysis (PCA) is a linear dimensionality reduction technique that
defines the linear mapping of x 7→y as Y = AX, where Y represents the projected low-
dimensional data, A is the projection matrix, and X the data in high-dimensional space to
be projected. X is typically defined as a matrix of data, where each element represents one
experimental point. PCA does this by attempting to find the most descriptive features of
the data, i.e., it finds directions through which the variation is maximum. These directions
are orthogonal to each other so that each represents a unique feature/variation of the data.
The algorithm of classical PCA is:
Step 1: Subtract the mean of each experiment to centre the data at zero, B = X −X
Step 2: Compute the covariance matrix: C =
1
n−1B⊤B
Step 3: Decompose C into eigenvalues and eigenvectors such that CV = V D, where V
are the eigenvectors and D the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues
Step 4: Sort the eigenvectors by the eigenvalues in descending order to prioritize the prin-
cipal components
The algorithm returns hierarchical coordinate systems that represent the statistical vari-
ations in the dataset. The principal components are defined with ”loadings,” which are
weights associated with each experimental variable. These loadings indicate the contribu-
tion of each variable to the principal component, allowing for a clearer interpretation of
the data’s underlying structure (Tenenbaum, De Silva, and Langford 2000).

3.1.2
Dimensionality Reduction - Isomap
The isometric feature mapping (Isomap) is a non-linear dimensionality reduction algorithm
that seeks to preserve the geodesic distances between all points in a high-dimensional
space. Isomap aims to capture the spatial patterns of a high-dimensional manifold. If
such a manifold can be parametrized by spatial features of the data, it can be reduced
in dimensionality in a way that explains the data according to these features.
Given
a set of volumes unordered by any feature type, the Isomap algorithm allows for the
ordering of these volumes according to spatial features found in the manifold. It returns
the coordinates that correspond to the degrees of freedom of each volume in each low-
dimensional space.
Rather than relying on Euclidean distances, which may overlook non-linear variations
within the data (as is the case with PCA), Isomap aims to preserve the intrinsic geometry of
the data by utilizing geodesic distances between points within the manifold. The geodesic
distance is the shortest path distance along the data and can be computed eﬀiciently by
considering the data as a graph, in which each node is a data point and the edges represent
the distance between neighbouring nodes.
The Isomap algorithm can be described in 3 steps.
Step 1: Construct the neighbourhood graph
Step 2: Calculate all pair’s shortest paths
Step 3: Construct low-dimensional embedding
In the first step, the neighbourhood graph, G, is built considering all data in the input
space X. The graph can be built either by setting a radius ϵ around each point i, where
all points j under this radius would be connected to i by an edge; or by setting K finite
number of nearest neighbours around i and connecting the points under this condition
with an edge. In both cases, a weighted graph is created with edges of weight dx(I, j).
In the second step, the geodesic distances dM(I, j) are calculated between all pairs of points
along the manifold M by computing the shortest path distance dG(i, j) in the recently
created G.

3.1.3
Floyd-Warshall
The Floyd-Warshall algorithm finds the shortest paths between all pairs of nodes in a
graph. It starts by initializing a matrix D, where D[i][j] is the weight of the edge from
vertex i to vertex j. If there is no direct edge available, it is set to infinity. However, if
i equals j, then D[i][j] is set to 0 because the shortest path from a vortex to itself is 0
(Thomas H. Cormen 2009).
Afterwards, this matrix is iteratively updated. Each vertex k is considered an intermediate
point, and the algorithm tries to improve the shortest path from vertex i to vertex j by
going through k.
For every pair of vertices i and j, the algorithm checks if the path
i →k →j is shorter than the current known path i →j.
The formula can be defined as:
D[i][j] = min(D[i][j], D[i][k] + D[k][j])

ANOVA Test
The ANOVA, or ANalysis Of Variance, is a statistical method used to test for differences
in variation between populations and is especially useful for comparison of more than 2
groups. Given a variable to model with one group factor, the ANOVA test is said to be
one-way. Given that this effect has p groups, the null hypothesis is that the group means
are equal:
H0 : µ1 = µ2 = … = µp
The comparison of the F-Statistic to the critical F-value with certain degrees of freedom
and at a chosen significance level will provide insights into the rejection or not of the null
hypothesis.

and the mean sum of squares within groups (MSSW), as F =

3.2.3
Permutation Test
Permutation tests are non-parametric methods that test the null hypothesis by generating
the distribution of the test statistic through random permutations of the data. It can be
coupled with several other statistical tests and does not rely on assumptions about the
distribution of the data, making it flexible and robust to use (Helwig 2019).
The null hypothesis is that there are no differences between the compared distributions.
The data is permuted to break any existing associations between variables, which generates
datasets that represent the null hypothesis. For each permutation, the test statistic is
recalculated. The p-value is calculated by comparing the observed test statistic to the
distribution of test statistics obtained from the permuted datasets.
The p-value represents the proportion of permuted test statistics that are as extreme or
more extreme than the observed test statistic, indicating the likelihood of observing such
a result under the null hypothesis.

The Mann-Whitney U test is a statistical method that is used to determine whether

In machine learning, the performance is evaluated using cross-validation, where a dataset

remaining subsets are used to validate the model. This allows for the evaluation of the

Brain Structure with MRI
The brain structures are assessed in this report by means of Magnetic Resonance Imaging
(MRI), which is a non-invasive imaging technology that utilizes the principles of nuclear
magnetic resonance to image the body. By magnetizing the body with a great homo-
geneous steady field (B0), some particles will be magnetized and aligned to it. When
radio-frequency (RF) pulses are emitted, disturbance of the greater alignment is created
and the particles will be additionally magnetized. Upon turning off the RF pulse, these
particles will relax towards the homogeneous field and will emit RF signals, that are cap-
tured by a receiver coil. The intensity and timing of these signals provide information
about the amount of particles magnetized in a particular area of the brain. The particles
to be magnetized are typically protons of hydrogen and as the brain is constituted by
tissues with different water densities, the signals received will allow for the mapping of the
brain.
The relaxation of the protons when realigning to B0 can be decomposed into a longitudi-
nal and a transverse component. T1-weighted images are obtained from the longitudinal
relaxation of the spin, which will emit a higher signal in low-water density regions such
as bone and fat (bright contrast); and a lower signal in regions such as the ventricles
containing blood (dark contrast).

4.3
Characterization of the Central Sulcus with Isomap
The characterization of the sulci shape can be done by taking direct and indirect ap-
proaches. In the direct approaches, features such as length and depth are taken from each
individual sulci. In the indirect approaches, features are retrieved from the variation of
shape in the group populations.
Direct features can be taken with prior knowledge of what to search for, however indirect
features that look into broad variations of shape can tell us what to look for in our specific
data set, and can possibly point are direct measures to investigate. This is exactly the
approach taken in this project. First, the shape is generally characterized by the principle
variations detected with the use of Isomap, and secondly, the variations, as well as support
from literature, are going to inform the direct feature extraction.
4.3.1
Isomap
The Isomap algorithm is used to characterize the biggest variations in the shape of the
central sulcus. Taking inspiration from work done by Z. Y. Sun et al. 2016 and Vareilles
et al. 2022, prior to feeding the extracted segmented central sulci into the Isomap algo-
rithm, a square NxN distance matrix is built, with N number of sulci. The distance
matrix contains in each element the distance from each sulcus to the rest. The sulci are
3-dimensional volumes that, though aligned to the same Talairach space, have slightly
different positioning that will bias the distance calculation. Additionally, the left and the
right sulci should be flipped towards one another, so that the distances calculated are, as
far as possible, only shape-related.
For each pair of sulci:
Step 1: Alignment of sulcus i to sulcus j;
if sulcus j lays on the opposition hemisphere, flip j
Step 2: Calculate the Euclidean distance Di,j between i and j
The distance measures from each sulci pair once aligned are calculated with the Euclidean
measure.
This measure, contrarian to the Euclidean, accounts for all vertices of both
sulci, even with different numbers of vertices. After the matrix of distances D is built,
the distances from sulci in the left hemisphere are aligned to the right, and the other way
around. To account for the best alignment convergence possible, the pairs of sulci whose
distance is minimum are chosen. This matrix should have zeros in the main diagonal and
non-zeros in the remaining elements.
The Isomap is computed with input the distance matrix D. The algorithm starts with
computing the neighbors by means of the brute-force algorithm to obtain the k nearest
neighbors to each element of the matrix. The number of neighbors is calculated from the
analysis of the reconstructed error. Following the neighbor’s construction, a neighborhood
graph is built and the distance along the shortest path is computed with the Floyd-
Warshall algorithm. The distances in this case are geodesic. Then, the kernel-PCA is
performed on the resulting matrix, with a number of dimensions d calculated from the
reconstruction error as well.
4.3.2
Alignment Algorithm
The alignment technique is crucial to building the dissimilarity matrix and is fundamental
for successfully reducing the number of dimensions.
The algorithm used to align the sulci iterates over the distance measured between each
sulcus and iteratively performs a rigid alignment between the sulci. The sulci are rep-

vertices contained in each sulci. The parameters to set initially are the amount of iter-

mean distance between cloud points.
The algorithm iterates the transformation from one sulci, Sreference to the Smoving, fol-
lowing the procedure:
Step 1: Calculate the closest point.
- Compute the squared Euclidean distance between each point in Smoving and all
points in Sreference.
- Then compute the closest coordinate points in Sreference to each point in Smoving.
Step 2: Compute Transformation.
- Compute the centroids (mean positions) of both the Smoving points and their
corresponding closest points in the Sreference.
- Then center the points around the origin.
- Use Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to solve for the rotation matrix that
best aligns the centred points.
- Compute the translation vector by aligning the centroids after rotation.
Step 3: Apply the transformation and Store the cumulative rotation and translation vec-
tors
Step 4: Repeat all steps if the improvement after translation is higher than ϵ or if the
maximum number of interactions is reached.

4.3.6
Comparison of Isomap Components in Familial High-Risk
From each component obtained, the sulci from different groups were separated.
The
separated groups were tested for whether they came from the same distribution, using
Kruskal–Wallis test. Following it, if any group differences emerge from an Isomap compo-
nent, pairwise Mann–Whitney tests with Bonferroni correction were performed. Testing
the different groups in their embedded component form means testing for their relative
coordinate positions along each component. Suppose a certain component encodes for a
shape pattern related to the elongation of the hand-knob, for example. In that case, sulci
with a more elongated hand-knob should be assigned a value (or coordinate) close to one
extremity of the component extension. Kruskal–Wallis is a non-parametric measure used
here to test for differences in the distribution of the coordinate values from schizophrenia
(SZ), bipolar disorder (BP), and control.
The asymmetry of the central sulci is taken into account, hence this analysis is performed
separately for the left and the right hemispheres.
Statistical significance is considered at the level of 0.05. Multiple comparison correction
leads to a statistical threshold of 0.025.
4.3.7
Prediction of Motor Performance with Isomap Components
To evaluate if the motor performance is associated with the shape of the sulci, the compo-
nent coordinates are used to attempt to classify motor performance scores obtained from
Movement ABC-2. The classification should indicate whether the sulci shape is a good
prediction for motor performance. In a more detailed analysis, the coeﬀicients retrieved
from the classification will indicate the relevance of each component to the classification.
4.3.7.1
Classification classes
The motor performance scores Movement ABC-2 are used to create the classes for the
classification. Two classes are formed from the rank scores, based on the degree of motor
performance. According to the authors of this battery assessment (Hutchins et al. 2013),
the motor scores can be divided into three categories: low or equal to the 5th percentile
(the motor performance is considered poor), equal or above the 16th (performance is
considered adequate) and borderline performance in between in the in-between extremes.
The classes used to classify motor performance are the ones that entail the two ends
of the spectrum. However, very few participants fit under the adequate motor outcome
category in the >=16th percentile. For dexterity, only 2 participants (4 sulci) have fit
this threshold; the same for the aiming and catching scores; and for the balance scores no
participants fit the criteria. Hence, the threshold is lowered to include the 13th percentile
and above.
Since the data pool will be reduced significantly, the analysis is carried out without sepa-
rating the left and right hemispheres.
4.3.7.2
Classification method
The classification is performed with SVC, in its linear form to allow for more clear in-
terpretation of results.
As the classes are imbalanced, the class_weight='balanced'
parameter is used. This method adjusts the weights of the classes inversely proportional
to their frequencies in the input data. Consequently, the classifier assigns a higher penalty
to misclassifications of the minority class and a lower penalty to misclassifications of the
majority class, thereby mitigating the bias towards the majority class. The regularization
parameter C controls for the trade-off between maximizing the margin and minimizing
classification errors. It was optimized using Grid Search with a coss-validation of 10-fold
and maximizing accuracy. The model is submitted to a second cross-validation of 10-fold
shuffled, with the found optimized value of C. For all validation folds, the accuracy, the
22
==PAGE:33==
F1-score, and the Area Under the Receiving Operator Curve (ROC AUC) are computed.
The ROC AUC curve is also plotted.
4.3.7.3
Classification coeﬀicient relevance
During the second cross-validation, the coeﬀicients obtained in each classification loop are
stored. The coeﬀicients are the weight vector, normal to the decision boundary (hyper-
plane).
The decision boundary in linear Support Vector Classifier (SVC) is given by:
w · x + b = 0
where w is the weight vector, x is the Isomap coordinates, and b is the intercept.
The coeﬀicients w are 10, for each Isomap component:
w = [w1, w2, . . . , w10]
Each component is weighted with 10 coeﬀicients that are weighted according to their
significance in the classification task. The magnitude and consistency of the coeﬀicient
values are observed in a box plot. The relevant coeﬀicients and their relationship with the
Isomap patterns found are discussed.

Both depth and profile are measured in all sulci.

The depth and profile curves were generated for all participants, individually. A sample

All samples were plotted and outliers were removed in two blocks of 50 parameter points

The sulci were rejected if at least one of the blocks was one standard deviation deviated
from the average.
4.4.3

The depth and sulci are analyzed to their full extent to identify areas with variation
explained by the factors of familial high-risk (schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, control),

dexterity, aiming and catching, and balance scores.

Chapter 6
Discussion
Two techniques were used to evaluate the sulcal shape in the context of familial high-risk
for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder and motor performance.
6.1
Isomap
6.1.1
Alignment with ICP
Alignment between pairwise sulci is an extremely important part of the Isomap procedures
endured since it directly impacts the dissimilarity measure between sulci.
The choice of a rigid alignment that maintains the sulci shape differences and eliminates
as much space between the sulci was proven to work well. However, the metric with which
this is stated is rather ineﬀicient and subjective and holds a lot of space for error. The
results were considered good if the alignment was possible to the point that visually it
seemed like the biggest common shapes were aligned. This measure would be proven good
enough if the Isomap embedding visualizations would make sense. This metric should be
improved in further studies.
6.1.2
Choice of dissimilarity measure
The ideal dissimilarity measure would be the distance between sulci, independently of
the alignment, but since sulci have different sizes, the dissimilarity measure has to be
calculated as the distance measured after alignment.
Otherwise, a Procustus measure
would be perhaps ideal, where the distributions would be taken into account, not the
geometrical measures.
The Euclidean distance was chosen as the dissimilarity distance measure and was computed
the average of the closest distances between sulci. This solution was able to capture well
the distances.
6.1.3
Choice of k-neighbors
The k-neighbors seem to not show much difference in the choice between k=50 and K=12,
k=20, k=30 or k=70, especially in components 1 to 7.
Very strong positive and negative correlations were found in these initial components.
Furthermore, very strong negative values were thought to not demonstrate any impact on
the interpretation of these components. It would be expected that the variation identified
by the components correlated very negatively would represent the opposite direction of
the variation detected.
As a post hoc analysis, the Isomap embedding was computed for k=12 neighbors, shown
in figure 6.1. The visualization confirms what is expected. By setting k=50, the large
42
==PAGE:53==
amplitude in the hand-knob is shown on the right side of the component space; and with
with k=12, the high amplitude in the hand-knob is shown on the left side of the component
space.
k=12 and k=50 show very similar results in the components associated with the highest
variability (initial ones), and the reconstruction error plot in figure 5.4 shows an exponen-
tial error decrease as the number of neighbors increases. This suggests a good choice of
k=50.
However, k=50 and k=70 are very highly correlated. Considering the reconstruction error
plot, this suggests that for even higher values of k, the Isomap embedding would be the
same as the one presented with k=50. This means that the graph used to calculate the
geodesic distances along the manifold produces similar embeddings whether it is highly
connected or not.
In this way, the data might vary linearly, instead of non-linearly, as hypothesized at the
start of the Isomap analysis.
Figure 6.1: Component 1, k=12
6.1.4
Component results
The Isomap components have shown some evidence of their meaning since the results
obtained in the first component were also found in previous records.
The first component appears to reflect the variation in the hand-knob, which appears with
great magnitude on one side of the axis, and with little magnitude on the other side. On
this side, a doubled knob is also seen. These observations were also seen by Zhong Yi Sun
et al. 2012 and Vareilles et al. 2022.
6.1.5
Classification with CSV
The classification task was performed to assess whether the Isomap features could classify
sulci shapes associated with the respective movement scores.
The classification power
observed was rather limited, with AUC mean values denoting 0.62+/-15, 0.28+/-0.14,
and 0.62+/-0.13, for dexterity, balance, and aiming and catching scores.
The variations between 10 folds of cross-validation reveal instability and inconsistency. A
variation range of around 30 percentual points is seen across all classifications in the AUC
scores, indicating a lack of consistency.
Moreover, this variation shows that some subsets of data can generate much better results
than others, flagging a possible issue with the data used. In fact, a strong class imbalance
was denoted in the dexterity and balance classification tasks (but not in the aiming and
catching scores).
43
==PAGE:54==
The F1-scores reveal similar results to the average test accuracy (0.5934, 0.446, 0.543, dex-
terity, balance, and aiming and catching, respectively) which indicates that the precision
and recall are similarly balanced at a low range.
The average accuracy for the three metrics is 0.722, 0.650 and 0.691, for dexterity, balance,
and aiming and catching. These indicate the ratio with which the model correctly classifies
and indicates a reasonably good learning capability. The test accuracy shows decreased
average results (around 0.12 for all), which indicates that the model is overfitting the data
in the training process and further flags perhaps a lack of generalization.
The overfitting was addressed in the model with the use of GridSearch to compute the
optimized C, which was retrieved with C= 0.031, C=0.068, and C=3.174 (dexterity, bal-
ance, and aiming and catching). A larger C in the last score indicates that the hyperbole
was closely fitting the values.
Though techniques were employed to avoid overfitting (as C tuning, as well as setting the
model to weigh the class sizes), the data was generally poorly classified. Further techniques
could have been employed to increase the performance of the model, however, the goal
of this report does not lie in the eﬀiciency of the model, but in its meaning. It is very
possible that the nature of the data is simply not prone to the classification attempted.
In this regard, Isomap embeddings, i.e., the shape features of the CS do not seem to offer,
as a whole, enough power for the classification of the motor scores.
Nonetheless, a detailed exploration of the role of each component in the classification
was carried out. In that regard, some coeﬀicients, i.e. the weight that each component
was attributed in the classification, showed consistency that might be worth exploring,
especially in the cases where the mean AUC is above 0.6, which is the case of the dexterity
and aim and catching scores. These are further discussed, alongside the direct feature
results in the following sections.
6.2
Direct Features
6.2.1
Parameterization
The parameterization submitted to each sulcus is an advanced automated technique, that
does not require machine learning. It is specialized in the parameterization of mesh shapes
with elongation in two directions (in this case, the direction associated with length and
the one associated with depth), as it takes the Fiedler vector, associated with the longest
variation of a Laplacian matrix, and subsequent orthogonal vectors.
A drawback associated with the parameterization is related to the sensitivity to noise.
This could be the case of noisy sulci volumes that are a result of poor segmentations. As
each segmentation volume has around 2000 vertices and the parameterization is done on
100 points, each iso-line will only account for an average of around 15x2 points, which
is very low (the points are averaged around the isolines so they can contribute to 1-2
iso-parametric lines). The impact of parameterization on uncorrected sulci was observed
when parametrizing the sulci with folds included (not shown in this report).
The parameterization of each sulcus was deemed generally successful after visual inspection
(A.11. And after the removal of 7 outliers based on standard deviations from the mean
points in the first and second halves of the sulci.
Additionally, the curve averages follow the results found in other studies. Cykowski et al.
2008 implemented for the first time this sulcal parameterization. Towards the superior/-
dorsal side Cykowski et al. 2008 reported a superior peak on the averaged sulcal depth,
44
==PAGE:55==
at the mark of 1/3 of the length, which was observed. The second peak at the 2/3 length
mark was reported with a higher magnitude than the superior peak, which was also ob-
served. A local minimum in between the peaks was additionally observed and replicated
in this report. The same magnitude of sulcation, in millimetres, is also reported here.
6.3
Features Interpretation
The depth and profile curves were generated for all subjects, and divided into left and
right hemispheres due to shown asymmetric evidence (Cykowski et al. 2008). The curves
were averaged and permutation tests with ANCOVA were performed on different windows
along the depth extent.
6.3.1
Morphological and Functional Relationship
In the context of depth measurements, central sulcus asymmetry is reported in several
handedness studies. These studies aim at drawing a link between functional motor per-
formance and morphological characteristics of the brain. It is presumed that the asym-
metry in the somatotopic hand region could be related to increased connectivity of neural
circuits in the dominant hand compared to the non-dominant.
Sulcal asymmetry was
found in right-handers, such that the depth of the central sulcus (CS) was more pro-
nounced on the left hemisphere in right-handers, rather than in left-handers (Cykowski
et al. 2008)(Amunts, Schlaug, et al. 1996)(B. Sun et al. 2015). The central sulcus depth
was also found to be larger in humans than in chimpanzees (Hopkins, O. Coulon, and
Mangin 2010).
However, in a study with 20 right-handed musicians, controls showed a more pronounced
left-larger-than-right asymmetry in the central sulcus than in musicians (Li et al. 2010).
Conflicting studies have reported results that support and disapprove of the relationship
between physical function and brain morphology, which is important to note in further
interpretation of the data.
6.3.2
Familial High-Risk Differences
Around position 30 of the parametrized sulci, the CS shows variance related to FHR in
the depth and profile features. This variance is only significant before permutation.
In the right hemisphere, the sulci of children at risk for schizophrenia was associated with
the Isomap component 8. This component is rather diﬀicult to interpret visually. It is
a component that represents a lower relevance in variation than the first components,
associated with the first eigenvalues and vectors. In this sense, the fault might not be in
the visualization, but in the content of it. Much more distinct averaged sulci are observed
in the initial components. In this one, the sulci seem to be averaging on a lot of different,
non-meaningful shapes, that originate a non-distinct average.
Given that the Isomap components capture the main variabilities in the shape of the
central sulci, then familial high-risk groups show no significant relevant variations in the
sulcation, by employment of the Kruskal–Wallis test. No significance was also found in
previous whole-brain studies.
6.3.3
Motor Performance Relevance to the Shape of the Central Sulcus
6.3.3.1
Dexterity Scores
The Isomap components showed a small predictive capability of the dexterity scores as
a whole, suggesting a limited relationship between dexterity scores and the shape of the
sulci. No particular component emerged as a consistent and relevant factor leading to
the prediction results. Vareilles et al. 2022 has also studied the Isomap components of
45
==PAGE:56==
the central sulcus, and explored dexterity with a predictive SVC model on children aged
5. They have indicated a poor dexterity outcome mostly linked to a higher, deeper, and
shorter length right hand-knob. The link with these features emerges from component
4, which matches component 4 in this study. However, no particular relationship with
component 4 was found here.
The fact that no particular component was associated with a classification that has a mean
AUC of 0.62 +/-0.15 might suggest that no Isomap features are related to the dexterity
scores.
In the direct measures, dexterity scores did not show a significant effect in shape via
the permutation test. Prior to permutation, poor significance was found with the AN-
COVA test at the center of the hand-knob area. The Isomap component encoding for
the hand-knob amplitude variation, component 1, has not shown a significant difference
in distributions between the two classes of motor scores (Mann-Whitney U, statistic =
1166.0, p-value = 0.1446).
6.3.3.2
Balance Scores
The shape of the sulci encoded by the Isomap components shows no predictive power on the
balance Movement ABC-2 scores. However, the balance scores have shown a significant
effect after perturbation with ANCOVA in the first peak of the depth profile, on the
superior/dorsal direction.
The depth is a feature diﬀicult to evaluate in the Isomap components, so no comparison
can be made.
6.3.3.3
Aiming and Catching Scores
The Isomap component number 6 indicates significant differences between the central sulci
of participants whose motor performance was low versus participants with adequate motor
performance. Along the extension of this component, higher motor performance in the
aiming and catching task was shown to be associated with a more foldy, epsilon shape
central sulcus than lower performance. In the direct features, the aiming and catching
scores showed an effect (although poor and did not resist multiple comparisons) in the
central section of the CS depth shape, in between the depth peaks. The depth is a feature
that provides poor capacity for visualization, especially in component 6 of the Isomap
embeddings.
6.4
Limitations
Even though manual segmentations are the golden standard, they add bias to the data.
Segmentation quality and removal of outliers in the Isomap analysis are factors that might
impede interpretation and statistical significance to be shown. Further limitations can be
found.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
To analyze the shape of the central sulci, two techniques were employed that captured
variability of the central sulci via a non-linear reduction of dimensionality, with Isomap,
and via direct geometrical measures.
Supported by records that link the existence of
motor impairments in familiar high-risk for schizophrenia and bipolar disorders, the sulci
variability is explored to gain a further understanding of the morphology of the brain under
these contexts. The impact of being at familial high risk for schizophrenia and bipolar
disorders and the impact of motor performances in the morphology of the central sulci
brain is explored in the central sulcus. In this sense, the relationship between function and
morphology is also explored with the Isomap and direct geometrical measures techniques.
Geometrical measures on the sulcus volumes have shown a significant effect on the balance
movement score in the superior-medial region of the sulcal depth. Features extracted with
the Isomap technique have shown an effect of an epsilon-like shape in the central sulcus
related to high aiming and catching movement scores, but further studies need to be
performed to check for this observation.
As far as it is known, the effect of familial high-risk children for schizophrenia or bipolar
disorders was for the first time explored in this study. No strong significant effects were
found related to this respect.
Further studies ought to be conducted, as both familial high-risk children for schizophrenia
and bipolar disorders have their onset in early adulthood, some years after the age studied
in this report. In this way, genetic factors and early environmental factors related to the
sulcal shape in these groups are not observed in this study.
