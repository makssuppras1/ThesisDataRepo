Abstract
Industrial upscaling of engineered tissues for in vitro testing is become a growing de­
mand. However, the cost of producing a large number of devices can be a significant
challenge. Unlike glass, polymers as substrate for engineered tissue platform offer com­
patibility with industrial upscaling processes, making them an attractive substrate choice
for cost­effective production of engineered tissues and test devices on a larger scale. This
master thesis project is aimed to explore the feasibility of direct 3D printing on industrially
used polymer using Stereolithography (SLA). Howerever direct 3D printing on polymer
is still a challenge, Therefore, the primary focus is on manufacturing microstructures on
polymer surfaces to enhance adhesion for direct 3D printing. The study employed stere­
olithography which offers the highest 3D printing resolution as the printing method and
hot­embossing technique to create microstructures on cyclic olefincopolymers (COC).
The research began with a comprehensive literature review to understand the goals of
surface structuring of polymers and enhancing surface adhesion through chemical, me­
chanical, and plasma treatments. COC and Polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) were the
examined substrates in this study. The test structures (2x2x2 mm3 cube) were printed on
substrates using poly(ethylene glycol) diacrylate with two molecular weight of 700 g.mol­1
and 250 g.mol­1 (PEGDA250/PEGDA700) through streiolitography 3D printing. The adhe­
sion strength of the printed test structures on the microstructured polymer surfaces was
evaluated using microscopy and mechanical testing. Analysis of the results demonstrated
that the design of microstructures significantly impacts the adhesion strength of the sur­
face, including parameters such as channel depth, channel side wall angle, and shape of
protrusions. The study confirmed the printability of well­defined structured polymers us­
ing SLA and demonstrated comparable adhesion strength to glass substrates. However,
challenges were encountered during the embossing of the structures to the substrate,
attributed to tool precision and suboptimal temperature conditions during hot emboss­
ing. Further research could focus on optimizing the uniformity of imprinted structures and
addressing the challenges encountered during the hot embossing process.

Introduction
1.1
Background
In recent decades, biomedical research has seen significant growth in fields such as cell
biology, drug development, and tissue engineering [1]. The growing demand for engi­
neered tissues for in vitro testing has created a need for industrial upscaling, driven by
the pharmaceutical, biotechnology, and academic sectors. To meet this increasing de­
mand, large­scale production is necessary to fulfill the requirements of these sectors.
However, the manufacturing process involves complex fabrication techniques, special­
ized materials, and stringent quality control measures, resulting in significant production
costs, especially when large numbers of devices are required.
Among the available techniques, 3D printing is commonly used to shape the cell­loaded
scaffold into molds made of harder polymer materials in scaffold­based tissue engineering
[2].The selection of suitable materials and techniques for 3D printing depends on factors
such as intended applications and desired models [1]. Stereolithographic 3D printing of­
fers high­resolution printing of complex structures using flexible, cell­compliant materials
[2].This project aims to investigate the substrate employed in scaffold­based tissue engi­
neering manufacturing.
While stereolithography offers several advantages, faces limitations when using indus­
trially used polymer materials like COC and polystyrene as substrates. These thermo­
plastic polymers (TP) exhibit low surface adhesion with the photopolymer resins utilized
in stereolithography. However, direct 3D printing on polymer surfaces is highly desirable
for applications such as tissue engineering platforms and organ­on­chip devices. Glass
has been a commonly used substrate material due to its high transparency, flatness, and
chemical resistance. However, the switch from glass to polymer substrates has been
gaining popularity due to due to their compatibility with industrial upscaling processes
and cost­effective production of engineered tissues and test devices.
The use of polymer substrates offers integration flexibility between different polymers with­
out the need for gluing, addressing concerns related to toxicity and stability. Additionally,
polymer substrates eliminate the requirement for additional components like holders for
optical analysis, simplifying the process and reducing the potential errors and enhanc­
ing efficiency, However, achieving strong adhesion between the printed structure and the
polymer substrate is critical. Surface treatments are essential to enhance adhesion of
polymer materials for direct 3D printing, ensuring successful outcomes in these applica­
tions.
3D Scaffold­based Tissue Engineering Platform
Tissue engineering involves a complex process of generating tissue structures. This in­
cludes selecting and isolating suitable primary cells, culturing and differentiating them,
and seeding them scaffolds that have been designed to support the formation of 3D tis­
sue structures resembling the natural extracellular matrix. However, designing scaffolds
with the desired properties, such as biocompatibility, non­brittleness, and nutrient support,
presents challenges. Thus, selecting appropriate materials, processing techniques, and
surface properties are essential for creating effective tissue engineering platforms [3].
Recent tissue engineering research has shifted towards developing 3D tissue culture plat­
forms, which promote better cell differentiation and tissue formation than traditional 2D

is a growing interest in developing new tissue engineering platforms that incorporate 3D

Thermoplastic Polymers and Their Properties
Polymers are known to have low surface energy and adhesion strength. This is primarily
attributed to weak layers that result from impurities during polymerization, low molecular
weight tails at the surface, additives like antioxidants and slip agents, external process­
ing aids such as mold release agents, and post­fabrication contaminants [4]. Polymeric
materials possible to be used as substrate such as Cyclic Olefincopolymers (COC) and
Polystyrene. COC is a transparent and amorphous material composed of cyclic olefin
monomers norbornene (NB) with linear olefins (ethene). Currently, COC is a registered
trademark of and manufactured by Topas Advanced Polymers. TOPAS is an abbreviation
for thermoplastic olefin polymer of amorphous structure [5, 6]. Polystyrene is a synthetic
polymer made from monomers of the aromatic hydrocarbon styrene. It is used in a wide
range of products, including lab ware such as petri dishes and tissue culture trays [6].
Among various thermoplastic materials, Cyclic olefin copolymer (COC) has higher heat
resistance than polyethylene and polypropylene, allowing it to undergo steam and dry heat
sterilization. It also exhibits excellent resistance to various solvents, allowing it to with­
stand ethanol sterilization. On the other hand, polystyrene has lower heat and chemical

resistance compared to COC, but it still performs well in ethanol sterilization. Both COC
and polystyrene offer good optical transparency for optical analysis applications. They
can be used for mechanical surface modification through various fabrication techniques
[7]. These techniques enable the creation of tailored surface features and patterns, en­
hancing surface adhesion and in some applications interactions with cells [6]. Additionally,
different studies shows chemical modification is applicable on COC and polystyrene [5,
8, 9].
Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) is a transparent thermoplastic and synthetic polymer
derived from methyl methacrylate. It shares similar properties to COC and polystyrene,
such as heat stability, optical transparency, and solvent resistance [10, 11]. However, in
this study, PMMA is primarily used for research purposes rather than industrial substrate
applications. The main focus of comparing PMMA with COC lies in their structural differ­
ences, particularly in terms of surface polarity. This motivates the investigation of both
materials to explore their potential in the context of the research objectives.
Native COC surfaces exhibit hydrophobic properties, resulting in water repellency with
contact angles typically ranging from 90° to 100°. This characteristic makes COCs suit­
able for applications requiring water vapor barriers. However, it poses challenges when
using them as substrates for 3D printing, especially with resins containing aqueous sol­
vents. COCs have exceptionally low water absorption levels, less than 0.01%, which is
approximately five times lower than PDMS and 10 −30 times lower than PMMA [5, 12].
Thermoplastic polymers such as PMMA and COC are predominantly composed of hydro­
carbons, with PMMA containing additional elements like oxygen and nitrogen, resulting
in a higher polarity compared to COC. These polymers are characterized by low surface
energy, leading to hydrophobic or weakly hydrophilic surfaces. Nevertheless, the surface
energy can be enhanced to improve surface wettability and bond strength. This can be
achieved through mechanisms such as enhanced mechanical interlocking or formation of
chemical bond at the interface [13].
Enhancing Surface Adhesion
Adhesion refers to the process where interatomic and intermolecular interactions take
place at the interface between two different surfaces. Describing the mechanism of ad­
hesion in simple terms is challenging due to its complexity and evolving nature. Sev­
eral theories, such as mechanical interlocking, electrostatic and chemical adhesion, have
been proposed to explain the mechanisms involved. It is likely that a combination of these
mechanisms contributes to the bonding observed in various adhesive systems [14].
Adhesion can occur through different types of chemical bonding, encompassing primary
chemical bonds such as covalent, ionic, and metallic bonds, as well as secondary bonds
like Van der Waals forces and hydrogen bonds. These bonding interactions between
molecules or atoms play a vital role in facilitating adhesion between surfaces. Primary
chemical bonds involve the sharing, transfer, or attraction of electrons, resulting in strong
and long­lasting adhesion. Secondary bonds, although weaker, also contribute to adhe­
sion through intermolecular forces [15]. Mechanical interlocking takes place when the
adhesive penetrates into the pores present on the surface of the substrate. This inter­
locking mechanism creates a physical barrier that hinders the propagation of cracks at
the interface. Additionally, it enhances the surface area, resulting in greater contact be­
tween the adhesive and the substrate [15]. Figure 1.2 shows and example of chemical
bonding and mechanical interlocking between two material.

Surface adhesion can be improved through various treatments including mechanical,
chemical, and energetic treatments. Mechanical treatments are particularly effective when
there is mechanical interlocking between the adhesive and adherend. Chemical treat­
ments, such as solvent cleaning, promote the formation of covalent chemical bonds at
the interface. Energetic treatments, such as plasma treatment, remove organic contam­
inants, induce oxidation processes, and enhance the polarity and wettability of surfaces
[4].
The mentioned treatments have a significant impact on the adhesion between the sub­
strate and the resin used for 3D printing. As an example the platform (see figure 1.1)
fabricated on chemically modified cover glasses. Through chemical modification of the
glass surface, covalent bonding is achieved with the printed material, ensuring strong
adhesion and preventing detachment during the printing process as well as subsequent
handling [2]. Chemical treatments have been also investigated for enhancing surface ad­
hesion of polymers, such as PMMA. One approach involves chemical modification using
acids and oxidizers, which increases the surface polarity of the polymers, leading to im­
proved adhesion [14]. Same study showed that PMMA can be treated by soaking it in a
hydrochloric acid solution. This treatment promotes the formation of highly polar carboxyl
groups on the PMMA surface, resulting in a substantial increase in adhesive strength, up
to twice as strong as untreated samples [14].
Plasma activation is a commonly used method for modifying the surface properties of
polymer substrates, which can be used beside with other treatments. By exposing the
substrate to energetic ions, electrons, or UV photons, plasma generates reactive free
radicals that disrupt chemical bonds on the material’s surface. This results in the forma­
tion of desired charged surface groups and an overall increase in surface energy. Plasma
activation has shown effectiveness in modifying various polymers, including elastomers
like PDMS and thermoplastics such as PMMA and COC [13]. Plasma treatments offer an
effective means of enhancing bond strength between substrates, even with short expo­
sure times. These treatments allow for surface property modification without altering the
overall properties of the polymer. The formation of oxygen­containing functional groups,
such as hydroxyl groups and carboxyl groups, on the polymer surface during plasma
treatment improves surface wetting and enhances adhesion with other materials [14].
Surface treatments such as plasma treatments are typically used to improve adhesion,
but these methods are not always effective in the long term. Chemical treatments, such
as the use of methyl methacrylate monomer and exposure to ultraviolet light, have been
shown to improve adhesive strength but can also be problematic due to the use of harmful
chemicals [17].

Poly(ethylene glycol) diacrylate (PEGDA)
Hydrogels are 3D networks of crosslinked hydrophilic polymer chains that can mimic the
extracellular environment of cells and tissues. Hydrogels have broad applications in re­
generative medicine, including gene and cell therapies, as well as tissue engineering. Hy­
drogels can be categorized as natural (e.g., alginate, gelatin, collagen) or synthetic (e.g.,
poly(lactic­co­glycolic acid), poly(ethylene glycol), poly(ethylene glycol) diacrylate) [1, 18].
Natural polymers provide biological cues for cellular behaviors but often have poor me­
chanical properties and batch­to­batch variations. Synthetic polymers like poly(ethylene
glycol) offer flexibility in tailoring physical and chemical properties for specific applications
by controlling composition, molecular weight, and crosslinking density [1]. PEGDA is a
widely used printing material in various applications due to its advantageous properties,
including customizable mechanical properties, ease of use, and compatibility with cells
[2, 19]. Given its common usage and favorable attributes, we chose to use PEGDA in the
research context of our study.

Theory
1.2.1
Stereolithography
3D printing methods use computer­aided design (CAD) models to fabricate highly complex
3D objects by adding material layer­by­layer. This process minimizes material waste and
can be used with a variety of materials [20].

Stereolithography (SLA) is a 3D printing technologies that fall under the vat photopoly­
merization category, it encompasses both Digital Light Processing (DLP), Liquid Crystal
Display (LCD). The common feature of these image display techniques is that they use
light to cure a photopolymer resin, but they differ in the light source and projection meth­
ods. SLA is a popular choice that uses spatially selective light exposure to cure polymer
resins, resulting in layer­by­layer object formation. SLA is a prevalent vat photopolymer­
ization technology that uses photosensitive thermoset polymers in liquid form. Meanwhile,
DLP uses a digital projector as a light source to cure a layer of resin. LCD, on the other
hand, uses an LCD display module to project specific light patterns [21].
Stereolithographic 3D printing is a process that involves using a liquid resin solution con­
taining monomers, photoinitiator, and absorber, which undergoes light­induced chemical
polymerization to form a solid structure.This solidification process is spatially controlled by
the light source in the XY plane. Light pattern is obtained through computer­aided design
(CAD) of a 3D object, sliced into a Z­stack image series of XY slices. A digitally controlled
stage moves the printed object in the Z direction, allowing for printing of subsequent lay­
ers to create a 3D structure [2]. In the printer used in this project, a set of digital mirror
device (DMD) is used to project light at 365 nm according to entire cross­sectional area of
the image. The vat containing the print resin is placed above the projector, and the DMD
pixel pattern is projected onto the bottom of the vat at a 1 : 1 scale.
Figure 1.4: A) Stereolithographic process illustrated schematically. Using DMD projection,
the desire pattern is projected on the bottom of the vat to create PEGDA network [2]. B)
3D object is sliced into a series of flat patterns. These patterns are then printed layer by
layer, with each layer separated by a defined distance known as the slicing thickness [21].
SLA 3D printing has parameters that impact accuracy, cost, and build time. Using a
smaller slicing thickness in the stereolithographic 3D printing process can improve the
accuracy and quality of the structure, but it also increases the fabrication time due to the
need for more slices. The layer height ranges between 25 and 100 microns [21].
1.2.2
Polymerization
SLA resins are composed of three essential elements, namely, monomer, photoinitiator
and photoabsorber. In addition, they may contain non­reactive substances such as a dilu­
ent or solvent to reduce the viscosity of the resin and a photoabsorber to control the depth
of light penetration. When the photoinitiator is activated by UV light, it produces reactive
species that polymerize the monomers, leading to the formation of a solid crosslinked
polymer network [1, 22].

To achieve the best Z­resolution for each slicing thickness in stereolithographic 3D print­
ing, it is possible to modify the absorber concentration. The slicing thickness can be
adjusted within the range of 1 µm to 1000 µm, depending on the application and choice of
absorber concentration. By changing the absorber concentration and the exposure time,
the penetration depth of the light can be controlled to improve the binding within the lay­
ers and between layers and substrate. Therefore better adherence to substrate can be
obtained by lowering the absorber concentration at the expense of Z­resolution [2].
Furthermore, PEGDA mechanical properties can be adjusted to make it either more flex­
ible or more rigid by altering its polymer concentration. This versatility makes it a suitable
material for tissue engineering, as many tissue engineering applications necessitate the
ability to stretch or deform the chip platform without causing damage [2]. This study uti­
lized two types of PEGDA with different molecular weights, 700 g.mol­1 and 250 g.mol­1, as
well as two different absorber concentrations for the latter material. In the case of PEGDA
700, water is added to the material, whereas PEGDA 250 is used in its pure form.
1.2.3
Hot Embossing Process
Hot embossing is a widely used technique for replicating micro and nanostructures onto
thermoplastic polymer substrates. Two types of hot embossing techniques, namely con­
ventional/ single­stage and Roller to Roller (R2R), have been developed. In the conven­
tional technique, two plates sandwich the master and workpiece, which are then heated
above the glass transition temperature (Tg) of the workpiece. Subsequently, pressure
is applied to the assembly [23]. The heating of the system can be carried out through
two modes, namely isothermal hot embossing, where both the master and the polymer
substrate are heated to an equal temperature, and non­isothermal hot embossing, where
the master and the polymer are heated to different temperatures [23]. In contrast, in R2R
hot embossing, one of the rollers contains the micropattern, and the other roller serves
as the supporter. The workpiece is heated from the master roller above Tg, similar to the
conventional method [23].
The hot embossing process comprises three steps, namely molding, cooling, and unmold­
ing. In the molding step, the polymer is heated above Tg, and a pre­force is applied. In
the cooling step, the temperature is decreased to below Tg, and in the unmolding step,
the polymer is demolded from the master [23].
In this study, the conventional hot embossing technique was used to replicate microstruc­
tures. In this process as it is shown in figure 1.6a the temperature increases gradually from
room temperature to a temperature above polymer glass transition temperature in a spe­
cific amount of time (t1), while maintaining a constant preload pressure (Pp). Once it gets
close to the embossing temperature, the pressure is increased to the embossing pressure

Thermoplastic Polymer for Hot Embossing and Its Behaviour
Thermoplastic polymers (TP) are widely used in the manufacturing of micro­ patterns
through hot embossing. This is primarily due to their advantages such as affordability, low
molding temperatures, lightweight nature, excellent chemical and thermal resistance, as
well as optical transparency. TP can be classified into two groups: amorphous polymers
and semi­ crystalline polymers, with each group further categorized into three types based
on their thermal stability: standard, technical, and high­performance polymers. These
polymers have viscoelastic behaviours. The modulus of the polymer workpiece changes
with temperature and time and is used to explain the deformation behavior. Their defor­
mation is categorized into three different states: glassy, rubbery, and flow, depending on
the temperature and time period of deformation [23, 24].
During hot embossing, the workpiece is heated beyond its glass transition temperature
(Tg) but below its melting temperature (Tm) to avoid any defects. The embossing process
is carried out in a rubbery state where where its molecules exhibit significant vibrational
motion and thermal energy is sufficient to overcome the potential barrier for the rotational
and translational movement of polymer molecules. This recoverable deformation in the
rubbery state significantly affects the accuracy of the embossed part. Gaining insights into
the polymer workpiece’s behavior during hot embossing is essential for achieving precise
and accurate micro­pattern fabrication. It is particularly important to avoid reaching a vis­
cous flow state, as the resulting deformation becomes permanent and irreversible, unlike
the deformations observed in a rubbery state [23, 24]. In this study, COC was used for
hot embossing the microstructure on it with (Tg) 142◦C.
Fabrication Process for Structured Silicon Masters
The production of microstructures typically involves several fabrication steps, including
photolithography, etching, and post­processing such as bonding, dicing, and packaging.
Among these steps, etching plays a crucial role and various approaches have been de­
veloped to address its complexity. One widely used method for microstructure fabrication
8
Stereolithographic 3D Printing on Polymers for Advanced Tissue Culture
==PAGE:16==
is deep reactive ion etching (DRIE), which combines physical and chemical etching tech­
niques. A significant breakthrough in DRIE came with the introduction of the Bosch pro­
cess by Laemer and Schilp in 1996. The Bosch process, named after their employer, has
become a key process in the silicon industry. In the Bosch process, a time­multiplexed al­
ternating sequence of passivation and etching is employed. During the passivation step,
a thin polymer layer, typically made of (C4F8), is deposited onto the substrate. It acts as a
barrier against the etching gas. In the subsequent etching step, both the horizontal pas­
sivation layer and the underlying silicon are etched. This process takes advantage of the
accelerated ions directed towards the target, resulting in significantly higher vertical etch
rates. As a result, high aspect ratio trenches can be formed. The Bosch process in DRIE
has revolutionized microstructure fabrication, enabling the creation of complex and high
aspect ratio structures. Its ability to combine passivation and etching steps has greatly
improved the precision and efficiency of the etching process, making it a fundamental
technique in the silicon industry [26].
Figure 1.7: Fabrication process of structured silicon masters: A photoresist layer is ap­
plied before lithography, followed by the Bosch process involving passivation and etching
cycles for precise pattern formation [27].
There is a trade­off with the Bosch process. The Bosch process always produces square
walls and channels with almost a vertical sidewall slope, having a slight relief angle aided
stamp release after embossing. However, the walls produced by this method are not
perfectly smooth. The alternating etch and deposition steps in the process result in sub­
micron rippling or roughness on the walls. While this rippling is inherent to the process,
it is generally at a scale that does not significantly affect the overall functionality of the
microstructures [28].

Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy
The basic principle of reflection mode CLSM (confocal laser scanning microscopy) is il­
lustrated in Figure 1.8 . In this method, laser or other light source illuminates a pinhole,
and the light that emerges from the pinhole is focused by an objective lens to a spot on
the sample placed in the focal plane. The reflected light from this spot is partially reflected
by a beam splitter towards the detector pinhole [29]. Light originating from regions of the
sample outside the focal plane, such as those above or below it, does not reach the detec­
tor pinhole and remains undetected. The objective lens aligns the illuminating pinhole and
the detector pinhole in the focal plane, creating a confocal configuration. This confocality
ensures that only light from the focal plane contributes to the image formation, leading to
sharp images with enhanced depth resolution. This property of selective rejection of light

Adhesion Measurement Force Setup
The adhesion measurement system used in this study is a custom­built setup, consists
of several components, including a step motor, a scale, actuator, and a MATLAB script
for control and data acquisition. A step motor is a type of electric motor that converts
electrical pulses into precise mechanical motion. The adhesion force test is a used tech­
nique to characterize the mechanical behaviour of the printed object on the substrate. It
consists of the measurement of the maximum force needed to detach the object located
perpendicularly to the surface of substrate.
The stepped displacements applied by the electric motor are applied to the cube using an
actuator. The home­made test setup is designed to have the actuator push the cube down
wards. Figure 1.9 shows a schematic illustration of the test setup, where the cube and
substrate are placed in a holder. The holder then is placed on a scale. The displacement
applied by the electric motor is the input to our test and the weight measured by the scale
is the output data in our test setup.

Silicon Master Cleaning Method for Polymer Residue Removal
The main objective of the work process was to remove polymer residues from a silicon
master after first and second attempt of hot embossing. The n­Heptane was heated to
a temperature of 78 °C, which is 20 °C below its boiling point. The heating took place in
a glass petri dish. The silicon wafer was completely immersed in the n­Heptane for one
hour. The petri dish was covered with a matching lid to minimize evaporation. To prevent
the n­Heptane condensate from contacting the heating plate, the petri dish with its lid was
placed inside a larger petri dish. Following the heptane cleaning step, three additional
petri dishes containing Ethanol, Acetone, and Milli­Q water were used. Using tweezers,
the silicon wafer was dipped into Acetone for a few seconds, followed by dipping it into
Ethanol for a few seconds, and then into Milli­Q water. All four petri dishes were located
inside a fume hood to ensure safety. To dry silicon wafer an air gun was used.

3
Result and Discussion
The initial phase of this project involved conducting experiments on Methacrylate and
plasma­treated glass substrates, which are commonly used as typical substrates for 3D
printing. These experiments were carried out to establish a baseline for comparison with
thermoplastic polymer substrates. Furthermore, similar experiments were performed on
structured COC to explore the potential of mechanical treatment in enhancing the adhe­
sion between the resin and substrate. It is worth noting that the investigation of PMMA
primarily serves research purposes rather than its usage in industrial applications. The
main focus of comparing PMMA with COC lies in their inherent structural differences, par­
ticularly in terms of surface polarity. This motivated the exploration of both materials to
gain insights aligned with the research objectives.
In addition to the aforementioned experiments, 3D printing was conducted on plasma­
treated substrates and, in some cases, even on untreated substrates. It is important to
acknowledge that these experiments were primarily intended for observation purposes,
given the well­established fact that the adhesion between the test structure and substrate
is not optimal. Consequently, the experimental design was centered around observation
rather than comprehensive analysis.
The idea of utilizing two absorber concentrations stemmed from the need to print on poly­
mers, which do not possess completely flat surfaces like glass. To ensure a higher safety
margin in the printing process, it was decided to employ both a half absorber (0.75 mg/mL)
and a full absorber (1.5 mg/mL) concentration. Specifically, for COC structures with grat­
ing patterns featuring channels on the surface, the objective was to ensure effective resin
penetration into the channels. Another rationale behind employing different absorber con­
centrations was to investigate their impact on adhesion force. Higher absorber concen­
trations result in increased photon absorption, leading to a reduced number of photons
reaching the interface. Consequently, the chemical bonds between the substrate and the
bulk material of PEGDA are fewer, resulting in lower adhesion forces.

Figure 3.11 illustrates the molecular structure of trimethylsilyl propyl methacrylate (TM­
SPMA), which serves as a silane coupling agent for glass. In the presence of water,
trimethylsilyl undergoes a reaction to form silanol, which acts as the active agent. These
silanol groups can then undergo condensation with the naturally occurring silanol groups
on the glass surface. The condensation reaction involves the removal of a water molecule
(H2O) from the silanol group and the hydroxyl group on the glass surface. The remaining
oxygen atoms from both groups form a strong chemical bond, known as a siloxane bond
(Si­O­Si). This bond anchors the silane agent to the glass surface, creating a stable and
durable coating [31, 39].

The hot embossing process used to create grating structures on a 1 mm COC disk posed
several challenges that needed to be addressed in our study. These challenges included
the deformation of the COC disk and substrates, ensuring successful replication of the
desired microstructure, and avoiding deformation or breakage of the microstructures [40].
Previous studies have shown that among the three parameters of pressure, time, and
temperature, temperature has a greater influence on the final outcome [41]. Therefore,
our project focused on investigating how changes in temperature specifically affect the
three challenges mentioned above.
During the imprinting process, the polymer gradually fills the gap between the substrate
and the mold while temperature and pressure remained constant. To illustrate this flow
behavior of the polymer during embossing, schematic diagram is illustrated in figure 3.25
[42].
Figure 3.25: Schematic of polymer flow before and during embossing period [42].
When evaluating different temperature conditions, it was observed that the channel widths
and depths were not fully formed when the process temperature was around the glass
transition temperature (Tg)(second set) figure 3.23b, figure 3.20, compared to other tem­
peratures. This discrepancy may be attributed to the inadequate flow of COC to properly
fill and shape the channels when the temperature is close to Tg [42].
For temperatures higher than Tg, the demolding temperature becomes crucial. Remov­
ing the mold at temperatures close to Tg leads to incomplete formation of channel shapes
and depths. This can be attributed to the high­temperature, liquid­like flow of COC, where
the polymer remains soft (above Tg) and can rebound or flow even after the applied load
is removed. When the load is removed during cooling, the material does not solidify im­
mediately, resulting in a loss of channel shape [43, 44]. To achieve better cooling and
strengthen the polymer while achieving fine shape of grating structure, a gradually de­
creasing demolding temperature (Tdeemb=110◦) was employed [40, 43, 44].

However, excessively low demolding temperatures can cause plastic deformation and
breakage of protrusions inside the silicon master. This deformation occurs when the poly­
mer experiences high stress resulting from the different coefficients of thermal expansion
between the polymer and the silicon master [40].
Our results revealed that the imprint temperature has a significant impact on the accuracy
of the imprinted structure. As we increased the temperature above the glass transition
temperature (Tg), we achieved better replication of the desired structure. Specifically, we
observed that the well define replications were obtained at a temperature of 162◦C with a
process pressure of 6 bar. However, at higher temperatures, we encountered deformation
of the polymer disk, leading to COC disk bending towards the center of the disk (figure
3.24).
This behavior can be attributed to the notable difference in the coefficient of thermal ex­
pansion between the silicon (Si) master ((2.6–3.3) x 10−6) and the COC (0.6 x 10−4 k−1)
[5, 37]. When temperature is above Tg, both the Si master and the polymer are in spa­
tial equilibrium. During the cooling phase, the polymer solidifies and tends to shrink more
than the Si master. However, since the formed protrusions are anchored inside the master
holes, residual stresses develop, which can ultimately result in protrusion breakage. This
phenomenon becomes more pronounced with increasing polymer thickness, as three­
dimensional effects come into play [40, 13]. Some studies have suggested using two dis­
tinct temperatures to address this issue. However, in our setup, we opted to reduce the
size of the disk instead of employing multiple temperatures to prevent clogging. Nonethe­
less, the effect of thermal bonding persisted, especially when we increased the imprint
temperature while keeping the cooling temperature too low. The significant difference
in temperature between the heating and cooling phases had an impact on the overall
process [40].

The presence of a corrugated interface between the cube and substrate enhances the
attachment strength at the adhesion surface, resulting in a higher detachment force re­
quired to separate them. This increase in detachment force can be attributed to two main
factors. Firstly, the larger adhesion surface area between the cube and substrate leads to
an increase in the overall resistance at the adhesion surface in form of bending and shear
effects. This increased resistance contributes to the higher detachment force. Secondly,
the engagement of the corrugations at the interface creates a mechanical interlocking,
effectively interlocking the cube and substrate which have a role in holding the cube and
the substrate together and increasing the total detachment force.

and stressed as shown in figure 3.33 which is a magnified segment of the corrugated
surface between the cube and the substrate. Under the actuator force, the horizontal
surfaces of the teeth on both cube and the substrate apply a pressure on each other
perpendicular to the surface.

Matsuzaki et al. conducted a study, comparing vertical wall angle and different undercut
angles, has shown that an increase in the undercut angle results in enhanced adhesion
strength. This observation aligns with our own findings, as we have observed a similar
interpretation in terms of effect of wall angle on adhesion [17]. Increasing the undercut
angle facilitates enhanced mechanical interlocking, resulting in improved adhesion on the
surface. However, imprinting such structures onto rigid materials, challenges arise due
to the presence of mechanical interlocking between the master and the substrate. As
mentioned in introduction, these applications are typically limited to elastomers capable
of undergoing significant deformations [17]. Therefore, we sought to fabricate structures
with wall angles closer to vertical. Variation in the results across a range of acute angles
to more vertical angles, we observed superior printing and higher adhesion strength with
the more vertical wall structures.
A comparison between the adhesion of COC (without embossed structure) and PMMA
reveals that they exhibit different surface adhesion characteristics. While PMMA demon­
strates better adhesion, as evidenced by successful printing of cubes on untreated PMMA
(table 3.4), COC shows weak adhesion strength. This difference can be attributed to
the higher polarity of PMMA compared to COC. PMMA has the ability to swell, allowing
PEGDA250 to partially penetrate its surface and establish mechanical anchoring between
the printed polymer chains and the existing polymer chains. In contrast, COC behaves
differently due to its resistance to swelling and requires a strong solvent to dissolve or
even induce swelling.

Conclusion
This research focused on the effective manufacturing of microstructures on polymer sur­
faces for enhanced adhesion in direct 3D printing applications. The fabrication of grating
micropatterns was achieved using the Compact NanoImprint (CNI) tool, followed by the
imprinting process. Accordingly, investigating the impact of different temperatures on the
precise replication of microstructures on the substrate was conducted. Through experi­
mentation, it was observed that the highest temperature of 162◦C yielded the most well­
defined structures during the final imprinting trial. However, further analysis is required
due to the challenges posed by thermal stresses resulting from the different thermal ex­
pansion coefficients of silicon and COC. Silicon residue found on the COC substrate hin­
dered its usability for subsequent 3D printing applications. The variations in depth and
wall angle, along with the different shapes (rounded and rectangular), allowed for the ex­
amination of 3D printing on these structured COC surfaces. The results indicated that
well­defined structures exhibited better adhesion surfaces, while poor structures showed
weaker adhesion. The adhesion force tests and 3D printing outcomes supported the no­
tion that well­defined structures exhibited stronger adhesion compared to other treated
types of COC and PMMA substrates. However, it cannot be definitively claimed that
COC structures have superior adhesion compared to Methacrylate and plasma­treated
glass, as there were variations in structure and partial detachment observed in most of
the substrates, although full detachment did not occur in some cases. Additionally, vary­
ing the concentration of the PEGDA absorber did not reveal any noticeable differences in
adhesion strength based on qualitative observations. However, to make more statistically
significant claims, further analysis and a larger sample size would be necessary.
To further enhance the experimental findings and provide a comprehensive analysis, sev­
eral aspects can be elaborated upon. Firstly, optimization of the adhesion measurement
setup should be considered, particularly in terms of improving the scale limitation. This
would also involve integrating visual inspection alongside force­displacement graph ac­
quisition, as both are crucial for accurately interpreting the results, especially when exam­
ining structured substrates. Regarding hot embossing, additional efforts should be dedi­
cated to refining the replication process. This could be achieved by further optimizing the
imprinting parameters in finer detail, aiming to improve the overall replication accuracy.
Furthermore, for a thorough analysis of the structure and its performance in 3D printing,
it is essential to examine both the detached cube and substrate using microscopy. This
examination can help identify whether the breakage occurred cohesively or adhesively
and provide insights into the penetration of the cured resin into the channels by analyzing
the cross­section of the cube. Overall, exploring new structure designs with wider chan­
nel width could facilitate better resin penetration and increasing the depth of the channels
may enhance adhesion. It would also be interesting to explore alternative pattern shapes
and evaluate their impact on adhesion performance. Additionally, considering the interest
in using PEGDA 700, further investigations should be conducted. Adjusting parameters
such as increasing the exposure time, reducing the absorber concentration, and modifying
the water concentration could potentially improve adhesion for PEGDA 700. Exploring the
viscosity of the resin and its influence on penetration into the channels would also provide
valuable insights. It is crucial to highlight that achieving precise and high­quality structure
fabrication on the polymer substrate remains a pivotal aspect of these experiments.
