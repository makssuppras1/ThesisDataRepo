Abstract
Climate change is accelerating biodiversity loss in marine ecosystems, increasing the
need for robust monitoring tools. In response, regulatory frameworks are tightening to
protect oceanic biodiversity (Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025). Among emerg­
ing approaches, environmental DNA (eDNA) analysis has shown great promise (An­
druszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). However, critical gaps remain in understanding the
transport, persistence, and degradation of eDNA in complex marine environments (Pastor­
Rollan et al. 2024).
This study investigates the persistence and distribution of eDNA in Fehmarn Belt by a
stone reef site called Rock works. A framework for future eDNA sampling is proposed,
combining hydrodynamic modeling with biological parameters using the MIKE software
by DHI. Two model experiments were conducted: a forward particle tracking model to
simulate eDNA distribution, and a backtracking model to estimate the origin of eDNA
particles. Results indicated that eDNA distribution is highly sensitive to hydrodynamic
conditions, depth, and decay rates. The backtracking model further demonstrated that the
probability area eDNA originated from increased with decreasing decay rates, but con­
sistent hydrodynamic patterns with high start concentrations were observed across sce­
narios. The simulations also revealed that sampling sites located closer to the coastline
gave a more accurate and representative signal of local biodiversity. Finally, the study
provides recommendations for future eDNA monitoring and highlights the importance of
integrating hydrodynamic considerations into fieldwork planning.

1
Introduction
Environmental DNA (eDNA) is a rapidly developing field that has seen substantial growth
since 2020 (Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025). At present, there are still large knowl­
edge gaps in eDNA ecology, and its transport through the marine environment (Barnes and
Clare R. Turner 2016; Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). Currently, hydrodynamics
are often overlooked when sampling is performed. This study will focus on Fehmarn Belt,
where a submerged tunnel from Denmark to Germany is currently being constructed. DHI
(formerly known as Danish Hydraulic Institute) has been conducting environmental mon­
itoring, and in connection with that, a new stone reef has been established, called Rock
works. To assess the stone reef’s biodiversity, eDNA sampling has been conducted in the
marine environment in front of the stone reef. This report will combine hydrodynamic
modelling with eDNA results from three different species, that could be potential inhabi­
tants of the stone reef.
This report aims to create a framework that can be used to track eDNA movement and
persistence, and finally be a tool when conducting fieldwork, to assist in the fieldwork
planning. To create the framework, a better understanding of how eDNA is transported
within marine environments is explored by analyzing the effects of current conditions and
eDNA degradation rates.
The following research questions will be investigated.
• To what extent do factors like current dynamics, depth, and eDNA decay rates
influence the transport distance of eDNA?
• How precisely can the geographic origin of an eDNA sample be estimated?
The study will investigate the research question by conducting two models. The first
model will be used to test the sensitivity of eDNA distribution to changing degradation
rates, changing current conditions, and at different depths. The second model will be
compared with real eDNA sampling results to see whether the eDNA signals received
could be from Rock works. Afterwards, it will be tested where the eDNA could originate
from. The models will be conducted using the MIKE software by DHI.
These results will hopefully lead to a better understanding of eDNA distribution in the
marine environment and create recommendations for future sampling.

Background
2.1
eDNA in environmental monitoring
The demand for biodiversity monitoring is on the rise as a result of the global impact of
climate change on the oceans and a general decline in biodiversity. Both globally and
locally, stricter regulations are being enforced to protect and monitor oceanic biodiversity
(Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025). Historically, monitoring has primarily depended
on manual detection methods, which are limited in temporal and spatial coverage (Hen­
dricks et al. 2023). Traditional monitoring techniques involved visual surveys by divers,
the use of remotely operated vehicles (ROVs), seabed trawling, seabed sediment sampling,
tagging of individual species, and fishing, which are often harmful to the ecosystem and
time­intensive (Andruszkiewicz, Sassoubre, and Boehm 2017).
To accommodate the growing and essential need for biological data, innovative technolo­
gies are being developed to enhance ocean monitoring, with eDNA analysis being a new
emerging tool (Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). Environmental DNA (eDNA) anal­
ysis is described as a cost­effective and non­invasive approach for assessing species com­
position in aquatic environments (T. S. Jo 2023). This method enables data collection
over a larger spatial scale than conventional techniques, with greater efficiency and the
ability to identify rare species that traditional methods may overlook (Scriver, Ammon,
et al. 2024).
Research in eDNA as a tool has been steadily rising over recent decades. Figure 2.1
illustrates the significant increase in eDNA datasets from 2020 to the present, demon­
strating increased activity in eDNA monitoring. Notably, 65% of eDNA datasets come
from freshwater ecosystems, while only 25% are from marine environments (Altermatt,
Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025).

To fully leverage the advantages of eDNA, it is crucial to gain an improved understanding
of its interactions with the marine environment. eDNA is currently for use in monitoring
invasive species, tracking aquatic life, managing fisheries, and conducting biodiversity as­
sessments (Scriver, Ammon, et al. 2024; Ellis et al. 2022; Andruszkiewicz, Yamahara, et
al. 2020; Simões et al. 2025; Toshiaki Jo and Yamanaka 2022). Enhancing our understand­
ing of how biotic and abiotic factors influence the fate of eDNA is vital for developing
a comprehensive global framework for its applications and methodologies (Bowers et al.
2021; Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025).
2.1.1
What is eDNA?
Environmental DNA refers to genetic material that can be extracted from soil, water and
air (Barnes and Clare R. Turner 2016). It is genetic material released into environments by
organisms (Strickler, Fremier, and Goldberg 2015). It exists as cellular DNA from intact
cells or as extracellular DNA from cells that have died naturally or been destroyed (Taber­
let et al. 2012) (Verdier et al. 2024). eDNA is used for species detection or biodiversity
assessment from a specific location.
eDNA is primarily detected through two genetic approaches. The approaches are called
Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR), which is used for identifying single species, and metabar­
coding for assessing communities. PCR is used for single species detection, and will target
one specific gene (Scriver, Ammon, et al. 2024). It can be done by two analytical tech­
niques, quantitative PCR (qPCR) and digital PCR (dPCR). Digital PCR is a newer method
that can quantify the absolute number of eDNA sequences, in low concentrations (Tsang
et al. 2021). Metabarcoding involves amplifying common gene regions across multiple
species, which will give information about the taxonomy in the present community (Bow­

ers et al. 2021). Metabarcoding has also been used to understand spatial and temporal
patterns across an ecosystem, since it can detect multiple species at once compared to
PCR (Djurhuus et al. 2020). Results are reported as copies per liter for PCR or reads for
metabarcoding, which represent the number of eDNA copies after amplification (Alter­
matt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025).
The size and type of eDNA particles vary significantly, complicating the quantification
process. Smaller fragments can dissolve, interact with other substances and/or altering
their makeup, whereas larger fragments may settle, based on their density and the sur­
rounding waters’ hydrodynamics. These differences influence how environmental factors
affect them and their potential travel distance (Wilcox et al. 2016; Shogren et al. 2016).
According to Allan, DiBenedetto, Lavery, et al. 2021 large fragments are on the size of 10
s of µm to 1 mm, whereas small fragments are from 0.1 µm to 10 s of µm. They consider
the small fragments as neutrally buoyant.
This research adopts the method of (Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019), considering
neutrally buoyant particles that behave passively, due to existing uncertainties in measur­
ing settling rates. Although eDNA is detectable in various environments like marine water,
freshwater, air, sediments, and ancient materials, this study concentrates exclusively on
marine contexts (Lamb et al. 2022; Harrison, Sunday, and Rogers 2019).
2.1.2
Current challenges in eDNA application
As stated above, only 25 % of the eDNA studies conducted have been done in marine
environments, and fewer on the fate and transport of eDNA in the marine environment
(Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025; Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). This
means that there are currently knowledge gaps in understanding how eDNA acts in the
marine environment. It is crucial to create a better understanding of eDNA movement and
persistence, before creating a combined consensus on how eDNA monitoring should be
conducted globally (Bowers et al. 2021; Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024).
A comprehensive review of eDNA monitoring and modeling concluded that currently
three challenges emerge to improve eDNA methods in monitoring: Knowledge of the
persistence, transport, and state of eDNA (Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024).
Firstly, there is the persistence of eDNA. This refers to how eDNA is affected by abiotic
and biotic factors and how long it will be present in the marine environment. Many papers
still highlight big uncertainties in understanding the persistence of eDNA (Scriver, Zaiko,
et al. 2023). Secondly, Transport. eDNA is expected to be highly influenced by oceano­
graphic drivers (Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024). Therefore, understanding the hydrodynamics
will help in understanding the persistence of eDNA in the environment. Lastly, the state
of eDNA. eDNA state has been proven to change depending on species, and change its

reaction to environmental conditions (Brandão­Dias et al. 2025; T.S. Jo 2025). However,
knowledge is still lacking. eDNA state will not be an area in focus for this paper since the

it would be relevant to investigate how to quantify different eDNA states.
2.2
Transport of eDNA
One of the key challenges in the interpretation of eDNA is the transport of eDNA. eDNA
particles in the marine environment are carried by currents, and thus eDNA could poten­
tially be detected far away from its source. Some of the earliest studies on eDNA transport
were done in rivers and streams, which found eDNA great variations in eDNA transport.
One of the earlier studies found that eDNA could travel maximum 10 km from the source
(Deiner and Altermatt 2014). A newer study found distances but up to 17 km away from
the source (Snyder et al. 2023). Whereas one study found it could travel up to 300 km
in distance from the source (Pont 2024). However, the focus in the riverine environment
was primarily on the travel distance for an eDNA particle, whereas for the marine envi­
ronment, the question is not only how far a particle can travel, but also where the source
originates. This will add a layer of complexity in a very complex marine environment.
eDNA is highly influenced by ocean currents. An understanding of the oceanographic
drivers is therefore critically important for mapping the movement and transport of eDNA
(Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024) (Jeunen et al. 2020).
Transport in the marine environment can be divided into horizontal and vertical move­
ments. Horizontal current direction influences the transport of eDNA, particularly when
treated as a passive particle. One study found that eDNA can degrade by 90–95% within
the first 1–3 km of transport, although detectable traces may still travel distances of up to
10­30 km overall (Ellis et al. 2022). Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019 reported that with
a decay rate of 0.055 h−1, approximately 30% of eDNA decayed before reaching a distance
of 5 km in their simulation. They also found that at decay rates equal to or greater than
0.26 h−1, eDNA would decay completely before being advected 5 km, whereas at rates
equal to or below 0.029 h−1, eDNA would consistently be transported farther than 5 km
before degrading. These findings highlight the uncertainty in horizontal eDNA transport,
which is strongly influenced by both current dynamics and decay rate.
Secondly, there is the vertical movement of water masses. Vertical movement involves
processes such as current intensity, wave action, and freshwater discharge, all of which
can result in increased vertical mixing (Allan, DiBenedetto, Lavery, et al. 2021). Jeunen
et al. 2020 investigated eDNA signals from different habitats in three areas, each with a
depth range of up to 4 meters. Information on the habitats was previously known, allowing

the researchers to validate the accuracy of the eDNA signals. The study showed that with
increased vertical mixing, the eDNA signals were distributed more widely throughout the
water column. In contrast, under conditions of low vertical mixing, the eDNA signals re­
mained localized around the expected habitat. This highlights the effect of water column
stratification and mixing intensity in shaping the vertical distribution of eDNA particles.
The study successfully detected habitat­specific eDNA signals when combined with in­
formation on vertical mixing, which also helped explain outliers in the eDNA data. An­
druszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019 also emphasized the importance of incorporating knowl­
edge of the thermocline to better understand the vertical distribution of eDNA. Their re­
sults showed that eDNA dispersal patterns changed significantly in correlation with shifts
in thermocline depth. A study that focused on the mesopelagic ocean (200 ­ 1000 m) also
discovered that the depth at which eDNA is detected is closely correlated to the depth at
which the organism sheds eDNA, indicating that physical processes do not have a strong
effect on vertical movement. However, the study also concluded that in shallow areas,
eDNA will be more affected by physical processes, compared to deep waters. (Allan,
DiBenedetto, Lavery, et al. 2021)
Combining vertical and horizontal movement of eDNA signals enhances our understand­
ing of its distribution and improves the ability to locate habitats, particularly when ocean
currents are well understood. Biophysical models are therefore essential, as they integrate
oceanographic knowledge with biological factors to accurately estimate eDNA transport
and decay rates (Scriver, Zaiko, et al. 2023).
2.3
Persistence of eDNA in the aquatic environment
To successfully track eDNA, it is essential to understand the factors impacting the per­
sistence and detection of eDNA (Strickler, Fremier, and Goldberg 2015). In the previous
section, it was described how eDNA can be influenced by physical movement. How­
ever, the detectability of eDNA is also highly dependent on biotic factors (Sassoubre et al.
2016; Harrison, Sunday, and Rogers 2019; Mauvisseau et al. 2022; Snyder et al. 2023). To
understand eDNA in the marine environment, it is therefore important to identify the ecol­
ogy of eDNA, which is defined as origin, transport, state and fate of the genetic material
(Barnes and Clare R. Turner 2016).
2.3.1
Decay rate
Decay rate can be defined as: the disappearance of eDNA due to physical, chemical, or
biological processes, and does not include settling under the influence of gravity. (An­
druszkiewicz, Sassoubre, and Boehm 2017).
There is a growing consensus that environmental conditions should be considered when

understanding persistence of eDNA (Mauvisseau et al. 2022). Temperature is the most
well­documented factor that influences the decay of eDNA. Increasing temperature leads
to a higher decay rate, resulting in lower persistence in the aquatic environment (Scriver,
Zaiko, et al. 2023; Harrison, Sunday, and Rogers 2019; Mauvisseau et al. 2022; Pastor­
Rollan et al. 2024). Temperature has a strong correlation with microbial activities, which
has also proven to increase the decay rate. Microbial activity will enhance when temper­
atures increase due to enzymatic and metabolic processes (Harrison, Sunday, and Rogers
2019; Lamb et al. 2022; Salter 2018). A study by Snyder et al. 2023 tested how biofilm
presence would affect eDNA removal. Biofilm is the presence of an aggregated microbial
community (Flemming and Wingender 2010). The test showed that, across particle sizes,
biofilm enhances the eDNA removal, highlighting the effect microbial activity can have
on decay. The study also looked into the effects of light on biofilm populations, which
were also proven to affect eDNA removal. However, they also stated that the effect that
light has on the removal could have been the increase in temperature (Snyder et al. 2023).
The same experiment was conducted two years later, which then proved that light did not
have an effect (Brandão­Dias et al. 2025). This aligns with Andruszkiewicz, Sassoubre,
and Boehm 2017 who tested how sunlight affects eDNA decay, and found that sunlight is
not the main driver of eDNA decay.
In conclusion, temperature has been proven to be the dominating factor for eDNA degra­
dation, where other factors have not shown the same strong correlation (Strickler, Fremier,
and Goldberg 2015; Scriver, Zaiko, et al. 2023).
2.3.2
Shedding, and particle size
eDNA is shed into the environment by the host organism and can be released in various
forms and states. The shedding rate is, similar to decay rates, affected by abiotic and biotic
factors. Species­specific traits can impact the amount of eDNA released into the environ­
ment and the size of the eDNA (Harrison, Sunday, and Rogers 2019; T.S. Jo 2025). The
life stage of the species can also affect the amount of eDNA released, with juveniles shed­
ding more eDNA than adults (Maruyama et al. 2014). Factors such as water temperature
and stress can also impact the amount of eDNA released (Sassoubre et al. 2016).
The type of eDNA that is released is heterogeneous and diverse (Scriver, Zaiko, et al.
2023). The particle size is important when quantifying eDNA and understanding its move­
ment in the marine environment. The particle size can vary from < 0.2 µm to > 180 µm
(Wilcox et al. 2016; Cameron R. Turner et al. 2014; Barnes, Chadderton, et al. 2021). The
size of the particle will affect its buoyancy in the water, with larger particles settling and
smaller particles being dispersed and diluted, and the size can have a considerable impact
on the spatial distribution of eDNA (Verdier et al. 2024). The particle size has also been

2.4
Modeling approaches
Incorporating hydrodynamic models with eDNA persistence can be an important tool for
visualizing the spatial distribution of eDNA and persistence in the marine environment
(Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). There is an emerging research field, where proba­
bility maps of eDNA in the marine environment are being combined with biological data
from eDNA characteristics and oceanographic data (Scriver, Zaiko, et al. 2023). Creating
a framework with biophysical models for eDNA monitoring would be a significant help
for biodiversity assessment and monitoring invasive species. However, more information
on eDNA ecology is needed to create a sustainable method (Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al.
2019; Fukaya et al. 2021)
Two mathematical methods commonly used in biophysical modelling are the Eulerian
and Lagrangian approaches. The methods are numerically different, but both models can
predict eDNA distribution in the environment.
The Eulerian method uses a tracer based approach, often used for concentration modelling
when moving over a grid of ocean cells. This approach will incorporate hydrodynamic
components to calculate concentration levels per grid cell (Ellis et al. 2022). To use the
Eulerian model, it is important to know the biomass concentration in the environment, or
how to quantify the concentration from copies/l. This is still an ongoing issue in eDNA
sampling, and therefore, using the Eulerian approach can have some issues.
The second method is a Lagrangian approach, which is an agent­based method, that tracks
and moves individual particles across the marine environment. The particles will be
moved based on the hydrodynamic conditions. This model does not incorporate con­
centration as the Eulerian approach does, but how the singular particle is affected by the
conditions in the environment (Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024). This model could therefore
be more relevant when the goal is to determine source and origin. With this approach, it
would also be possible to have multiple fractions of eDNA, since this can affect the spatial
distribution of eDNA (Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019; Brandão­Dias et al. 2025).

3.1.1
Hydrodynamical conditions
The Fehmarn belt is in the narrow part of the Belt Sea, where moving fronts of water
masses are transported through. The Belt Sea is a transitional area between the Baltic Sea
and the North Sea, with water from the Baltic Sea having salinity levels at 8 PSU and water
from Kattegat having salinity levels at 25 PSU, and the North Sea having salinity levels at
35 PSU. The Kattegat and North Sea water masses will be denser due to the high salinity
levels and be situated in the lower part of the water column. The water from the Baltic will
have low­salinity levels and a lower density, meaning it will be located in the upper part of
the water column. This will result in a stratification between the two water masses. This
will particularly occur in the summer, whereas in the winter the stratification will be less

3.2
Literature study
A literature study was previously conducted that investigated the eDNA degradation rate.
The focus was to find the average decay rate and see if any had successfully incorporated
environmental parameters in the eDNA degradation. From this study, the following will
be extracted for this project: decay rates and temperature coefficient. The temperature
coefficient was calculated by an Akaike criterion test (AIC). The AIC test is used to quan­
tify the impact the individual factors have on the overall model. AIC utilizes maximum
likelihood estimates of the parameters from each model to determine the model with the
minimum AIC value (Akaike 1974). This method helps identify which parameters sig­
nificantly influence the eDNA degradation rate, and provides a relative measure of model
quality. The resulting decay rates and AIC comparisons are presented in the appendix (see
Table A.1, Figure A.2 and Table A.2)
The decay rates varied between 0.02 h−1 to 0.3 h−1, with some outliers above that point.
For this project, it will be categorized as a first­order differential decay equation, since it is
the most commonly used, and since PCR method can’t distinguish between eDNA states
yet (Mauvisseau et al. 2022). The temperature coefficient used in this study is 0.003 h−1,
based on the results from the literature study (see Table A.2). The AIC test found that
eDNA decay rates vary with temperature at a rate between 0.0025 h−1 and 0.0035 h−1 per
C◦. Therefore, a midpoint value of 0.003 h−1 was chosen as a representative coefficient
for this project when incorporating temperature.

4.1.4
Summary of Model Experiment 1 results
In Model Experiment 1, it can be seen how factors such as current dynamics, depth, and the
decay of eDNA impact the distance eDNA travels. The findings indicate that as the decay
rate of eDNA decreases, the travel distance increases, since the particles remain present
in the marine environment for longer periods and can thus travel farther. The study also
confirmed that eDNA travel distance lessens with increased depth due to reduced current
speeds. Another hypothesis examined was whether current speed had a more significant
impact on eDNA transport than degradation rate variations. Though definitive conclu­
sions are challenging, it has been noted that changes in current speed alter the eDNA
travel distance even when the degradation rate remains constant. This suggests that cur­
rent intensity is likely a more dominant factor in controlling eDNA travel distance than
the rate at which it degrades.

Discussion
5.1
Decay rate does affect eDNA travel distance
A low decay rate will result in a longer eDNA travel distance than a high decay rate. This
was possible to see on Figures 4.5 ­ 4.10, where the maximum possible eDNA transport
distance increased with decreasing decay rate. This is even clearer in Figure 4.21, where
the total probability area of eDNA origin is shown across degradation rates, with a pattern
that decreasing rates lead to an increasing probability area. For this study, it was calcu­
lated that for a degradation rate of 0.09 h−1 (normal) in April at 1 meter depth, 2.66% of
the particles were degraded before reaching 5 km distance, whereas in August at 1 meter
depth, 23.83% of the particles were degraded before 5 km. At 5 meters depth, 56.38%
and 53.59% of the eDNA particles were degraded before 5 km. Andruszkiewicz, Koseff,
et al. 2019 demonstrated by a Lagrangian particle model using a decay rate of 0.055h−1
that 30% of the eDNA had decayed before reaching 5 km. The amount of degraded par­
ticles would also increase or decrease proportionally with an increase or decrease in the
degradation rate. Although the decay rate of Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019 is lower
than the normal rate used for this study, the results show a faster removal of particles.
This indicates that other factors should be considered when modeling eDNA particles in
the marine environment.
Figure 4.18 shows the lowest eDNA decay rate, and Figure 4.19 illustrates the lowest
degradation with a +6 C◦temperature increase. The figures show that when adding a tem­
perature coefficient, the probability area of eDNA origin decreases 2.8 times. Indicating
that changing water temperature will impact the eDNA probability area. At the current
moment, no studies have been found that have tried to incorporate a temperature coeffi­
cient into eDNA particle modelling. However, what can be concluded from this study is
that areas with big variation in water temperature, as areas with strong seasonality, should
be aware of what effect changing temperatures can have on eDNA degradation and eDNA
ecology.
5.2
Hydrodynamic conditions are important for eDNA
travel distance
Model Experiment 1 demonstrated that eDNA transport distance will change depending
on the current conditions. If strong currents occur, the distance will become much greater
than under normal conditions. In April, at a 1 meter release depth for the normal degra­
Modelling eDNA transport in the marine environment
37
==PAGE:48==
dation rate, the maximum distance was approximately 15.5 km. For August, which had
storm conditions also at a 1­meter release point for a normal degradation rate, the max­
imum distance was 53.5 km. This correlates to the travel distance in August being 3.53
times longer than the travel distance in April. This shows how sensitive eDNA particles
can be to hydrodynamic conditions in the marine environment and should be understood
before investigating eDNA ecology. In Model experiment 2, a week was extracted that
had similar current speed and directions to October 3rd and 4th(when the samples were
taken), but also days with opposite current direction and stronger speeds, to gain hydrody­
namic variation in eDNA distribution. Figure 4.20 ­ 4.18 illustrates how the eDNA area
increased with decreasing decay rate. However, what was also clear was that the high­
est mass concentrations across the three simulations were, to some degree, similar. All
had high mass concentration at the sample sites and along the shore. Figure 4.18 has a
further spread due to the rate of 0.02 h−1, but the overall patterns are similar. This indi­
cates that hydrodynamic conditions will, to some degree, have a stronger effect on eDNA
distribution than the degradation rate. Ellis et al. 2022 demonstrated by using an Eule­
rian transport model that with decay rates of 0.035 h−1 and 0.041 h−1 the eDNA would
typically concentrate 10 ­ 20 km away from the source point before dropping below the
limit of detection (LoD), but if a strong along­shore current would occur, it could be up
to 50 ­ 60 km. Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024 also conducted an Eulerian model using the same
degradation rates as Ellis et al. 2022 and found the highest concentrations were located 5 ­
10 km from the source, and could be found up to 20 km away from the sites. These results
are similar, but not completely alike, to the study of Ellis et al. 2022. This shows how the
same degradation rate can have different distance results depending on the geographical
location, and how hydrodynamics in the area can affect the travel distance.
Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024 also did a backtracking model based on a Lagrangian particle
approach. They found that the highest concentrations of eDNA were found 15 km from
the sample site, which was similar to Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019 results. Even
more importantly, they concluded that the area from which eDNA could originate was
very dependent on the hydrodynamic conditions. A six hour difference in eDNA being
released to the environment could significantly impact where the eDNA is found. This is
similar to the results from this study. Figure 4.18 is based on different current directions
and speeds, and it shows that when the direction is moving SE towards the Baltic, the
eDNA particles can originate from further out in the open water, compared to when the
current is NW. However, as seen on Figures 4.1 ­ 4.4 across directions and depths, the
eDNA particles will be situated relatively close to the coast, indicating a strong effect
by along­shore currents. Ellis et al. 2022 also demonstrated how the along­shore current
would impact the dispersion to a higher degree than the cross­sectional currents, which
38

also seems to be the case in this study.
The area to the right of sample locations and Figures 4.20 ­ 4.18 all have high accumula­
tion. If this is compared to current conditions, there is often a low current speed compared
to the surrounding area (see Figure A.9). This means that if particles are transported to­
wards or away from this area, there is a likelihood that the eDNA particles will be present
in the low current area for a longer time. This also means that when there is an area with
low current speed or circulating currents, the decay rate could be disregarded to some
degree, since the particles would be present for a longer time. Again, it amplifies the
importance of understanding current conditions around a sample site.
Overall, it can be concluded that the decay rate does impact the distance to which the
eDNA can travel and originate, but decay rate is not the only factor influencing eDNA
persistence. Hydrodynamics has a strong impact on the spread and travel distance as
well. Therefore, a good understanding of the hydrodynamic conditions is needed to fully
understand the persistence of eDNA. Finally, eDNA decay rate is important, but to some
degree, hydrodynamic conditions will be more significant in understanding the spread and
travel of eDNA particles.
5.3
Does the eDNA sampling at Rock works likely
represent the biodiversity?
The sample location by Rock works was constructed based on near/far concept in cross­
shore directions. The model results showed that only the location closest to the coast would
have eDNA signals from Rock works, when simulated in days with similar conditions to
the 3rd and 4th of October 2024 (see Figures 4.12 ­ 4.17).
The hydrodynamics showed that when the dominating current direction is northwest, then
having samples located on the east side of Rock works does not make sense. This is be­
cause the eDNA particles from Rock works will be transported in the current direction,
which is northwest. Both Ellis et al. 2022 and Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024 detected strong
coastal currents to impact the eDNA distribution, and that it should be considered at sam­
pling. The same happens to be the case at Fehmarn. Therefore, for future sampling at
this location, the eDNA sampling should be conducted in such a way that it follows the
current direction. Ellis et al. 2022 also reported that to maximize the sampling strategy, it
should be carried out within 100 meters of the source. With all the results combined, the
conclusion is that the sampling should be as local as possible for habitat detection.
Determining whether the eDNA sampling at Rock works effectively capture the biodiver­
sity is challenging. However, it was evident that the sampling sites with the fewest eDNA
reads were located the furthest away. This highlights the importance of positioning eDNA

sampling sites close to the target area, and downstream of current flows that carry eDNA
particles.
5.4
How reliable is the model?
All the hydrodynamics data have been validated by DHI, which is used for many projects
as Fehmern A/S. This means it has been thoroughly validated before being put to use. The
pathways of the particles can therefore be trusted. The model shows similar patterns to pre­
vious studies with particles being dominated by an along­shore current transport (Pastor­
Rollan et al. 2024; Ellis et al. 2022; Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). The particle
distribution was also compared to the current direction in the hydrodynamic model after
the simulations had been run. Therefore, it can be assumed that the particle distribution
is reliable. The biggest uncertainty in terms of particle distribution is the backtracking
model. When hydrodynamics data are reversed, the model can sometimes have difficul­
ties with abrupt changes in the hydrodynamics (Breivik et al. 2025). A further sensitivity
test of the dispersion before and after reversing the hydrodynamics is recommended for
future modeling attempts. The dispersion values used are assumed to be reliable since it is
the recommended values used by DHI, and are used in many projects globally. However,
a dispersion sensitivity test should also be conducted for both the forward and backward
models.
Another important aspect for future modeling is to understand the dilution of eDNA and
incorporate the limit of detection (LoD) into the simulations. This was not done for this
study since the eDNA concentration was a relative measurement. Ellis et al. 2022 incor­
porates dilution in their study, which showed eDNA detectability would be almost none
after 3 km from the source site. Comparing this result to Figures 4.18, 4.20, and 4.19, there
is a very large area where very little eDNA is detected. Perhaps if more investigation on
dilution were done, this whole area would be under the limit of detection, which means
that eDNA can not be detected in that area.
One aspect that should be investigated more thoroughly is settling and sedimentation.
This was also handled differently from Model Experiment 1 to Model Experiment 2. In
Model Experiment 1, sedimentation was enabled, allowing particles to settle to the bottom
when they reached it. For Model Experiment 2, sedimentation was disabled, and when
the particles reached the bottom, they would be resuspended. This was done to get the
full extent of the particle’s movement before it degraded. However, other studies have
incorporated sedimentation, so perhaps for the next backtracking model this should be
considered (Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019).

5.5
Future guidelines for eDNA sampling
The objective of this study was to gain insight into the persistence of eDNA and to de­
velop an improved framework for eDNA sampling and modeling. The findings revealed
that sampling sites nearer to the shoreline offer the most accurate representation of biodi­
versity. Conversely, as the distance from the coast increases, the likelihood of capturing
a representative biodiversity assessment declines.
eDNA distribution is also highly affected by an along­shore current, which transports the
particles southeast or northwest, depending on the current direction. It was shown that
a lower degradation rate would increase the probability area from which eDNA could
originate, but that there is a hydrodynamic pattern in which the highest accumulation of
eDNA mass can be found, regardless of the rate.
Therefore, if the goal is to investigate biodiversity or monitor invasive species, this study
has proven that a thorough understanding of hydrodynamics and water temperature is cru­
cial and can be a significant tool in understanding and interpreting the eDNA results.
For future eDNA sampling and monitoring following recommendations are given.
• Storm conditions can increase eDNA transport distances by a factor of approx.
3 compared to normal conditions, highlighting the importance of hydrodynam­
ics in eDNA distribution. The results demonstrated the importance of how chang­
ing currents can affect the distance to which eDNA particles can travel. Particu­
larly in coastal areas with along­shore currents, eDNA distribution can be affected
greatly. Evaluation of hydrodynamics before conducting eDNA sampling or mon­
itoring should therefore be considered. Based on the results from this study, it is
recommended to incorporate hydrodynamics into eDNA monitoring.
• Adding a +6 C◦temperature increase reduces the probability area of eDNA by
a factor of approx. 2. Water temperature can impact the eDNA persistence. By
adding a temperature coefficient, it was illustrated how eDNA distribution could

increase or decrease with decay rate. This demonstrates how a shift in temperature
in the marine environment would impact the area in which eDNA can be distributed.
Areas with seasonality as Denmark, should therefore consider the impact, changing
seasons can have on eDNA transport and origin.
• eDNA distribution is site­specific. This study and previous studies demonstrate
how eDNA distribution will be highly influenced by the specific hydrodynamic and
biotic conditions at the site. This study finds that eDNA distribution and travel
distance cannot be generalized across sites. However, the framework developed in
this study for visualising eDNA distribution can be applied to other sites, and reveal
how eDNA particles move within specific marine environments. A more compre­
hensive understanding of the influences of hydrodynamics and water temperature
will further enhance the effectiveness of eDNA monitoring and interpretation.
5.6
Limitations and future research
To improve and enhance the accuracy of these results, a few adjustments could be made.
In terms of model simulations, releasing particles with different sizes and different degra­
dation rates could increase the accuracy of eDNA distribution in the models. This could
help with capturing the biodiversity, since eDNA particles can have different sizes and
states depending on the species. In the study of T.S. Jo 2025, particle size distribution
was compared across different species. The results showed that the size of the eDNA
particle would change across species. The size of the eDNA particle will impact how the
particle moves throughout the marine environment (Brandão­Dias et al. 2025). This is
important information when simulating particle movement. In terms of degradation rates,
Pastor­Rollan et al. 2024 found that little change was happening between species­specific
decay rates, and therefore, one overall rate could be sufficient. Oppositely, Mauvisseau
et al. 2022 recommends that the state of eDNA is considered in terms of degradation rate,
since it can impact how it is affected by environmental factors (Barnes, Chadderton, et al.
2021). Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of the state of eDNA is needed to de­
velop a stronger modeling framework and account for the diverse biodiversity patterns in
specific sites.

6
Conclusion
This study provides valuable insights into how eDNA distribution in the Fehmarn Belt re­
gion is influenced by hydrodynamic conditions and degradation rates. The results showed
that eDNA travel distance increased with decreasing decay rates and strong oceanic cur­
rents, whereas particles released at deeper depths or high decay rates decreased the eDNA
travel distance. The backtracking model demonstrated that the potential origin area of
eDNA increased as the decay rate decreased. Despite varying decay rates, a consistent
hydrodynamic pattern emerged, revealing persistent areas with high eDNA concentra­
tions. The findings highlight the importance of sampling eDNA as close as possible to the
target site. Sampling in hydrodynamically unsuitable areas or too far from the source may
result in detecting signals from non­representative or irrelevant species.
This study has introduced a new framework that incorporates hydrodynamic modeling
into eDNA monitoring and sampling strategies. By integrating hydrodynamic informa­
tion, researchers can enhance both the efficiency of field sampling and the accuracy of
data interpretation. Additionally, these results are contributing to the growing body of
knowledge on eDNA persistence and transport in marine environments.

B
DRAFT
Modelling eDNA transport in the marine
environment: Investigating the influence of
hydrodynamics and decay rates
Abstract — Climate change is accelerating Climate change is accelerating biodiversity loss in ma­
rine ecosystems, increasing the need for robust monitoring tools. In response, regulatory frame­
works are tightening to protect oceanic biodiversity (Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al. 2025).
Among emerging approaches, environmental DNA (eDNA) analysis has shown great promise (An­
druszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). However, critical gaps remain in understanding the transport,
persistence, and degradation of eDNA in dynamic marine environments. This study investigates
the persistence and distribution of eDNA at Fehmarn Belt by a stone reef site called Rock works.
A framework for future eDNA sampling is proposed, combining hydrodynamic modeling with bi­
ological parameters using the MIKE software by DHI. Two model experiments were conducted: a
forward particle tracking model to simulate eDNA distribution, and a backtracking model to esti­
mate the origin of eDNA particles. Results indicated that eDNA distribution is highly sensitive to
hydrodynamic conditions, depth, and decay rates. The backtracking model further demonstrated
that the probability area eDNA could originate from increased with decreasing decay rates, but
consistent hydrodynamic patterns with high start concentrations were observed across scenarios.
The simulations also revealed that sampling sites located closer to the coastline gave a more accu­
rate and representative signal of local biodiversity. Finally, the study provides recommendations
for future eDNA monitoring and highlights the importance of integrating hydrodynamic consider­
ations into fieldwork planning.
B.1
Introduction
Environmental DNA (eDNA) is a rapidly de­
veloping field that has seen substantial growth
since 2020 (Altermatt, Couton, Carraro, et al.
2025). At present there are still large knowl­
edge gaps in eDNA ecology, and its transport
through the marine environment (Barnes and
Clare R. Turner 2016; Andruszkiewicz, Koseff,
et al. 2019).
To accommodate the growing and essential need
for biological data, innovative technologies are
being developed to enhance ocean monitoring,
with eDNA analysis being a new emerging tool
(Andruszkiewicz, Koseff, et al. 2019). Envi­
ronmental DNA (eDNA) enables data collec­
tion over a larger spatial scale than conven­
tional techniques, with greater efficiency and
the ability to identify rare species that traditional
methods may overlook (Scriver, Ammon, et al.
2024).
60

==PAGE:71==
This paper will hypothesize that eDNA parti­
cles have longer travel distances at the surface
level and with decreasing degradation level. It
is also expected to change behavior with current
conditions. Further, it is hypothesized that the
eDNA signal from the sample locations will be
from Rock works, and it will be tested where
the eDNA will originate by a langrigarian back­
tracking model. The research question is fol­
lowing:
To what extent do factors current dynamics,
depth, and eDNA decay rates influence the
transport distance of eDNA? How precisely can
the geographic origin of an eDNA sample be es­
timated?
B.2
Methods
B.2.1
Case study
This study will focus on Fehmarn Belt where
DHI (formerly known as Danish Hydraulic
Institute) has been conducting environmental
monitoring, and in connection with that, a new
stone reef has been established, called Rock
works. To assess the stone reef’s biodiversity,
eDNA sampling has been conducted in the ma­
rine environment in front of the stone reef.
The Fehmarn belt is situated between Lolland
(Denmark) and Fehmarn (Germany). The lo­
cation of the sampling can be seen in figure
B.1. This, together with high­resolution hydro­
dynamic data makes it a perfect case to test the
transport of eDNA.
Figure B.1: eDNA sample sites collected the
third and fourth of October. Colours symbolizes
depth.
The Fehmarn belt is in the narrow part of the
Belt sea where moving fronts of water masses
are transported through. The Belt sea is a transi­
tional area between the Baltic Sea and the North
Sea. The Kattegat and North Sea water masses
will be denser due to the high salinity levels and
therefore be situated in the lower part of the wa­
ter column. Whereas water from the Baltic will
have low­salinity levels and have a lower den­
sity. This will result in stratification between
the two water masses. This will particularly oc­
cur in the summer, when the stratification is less
pronounced due to strong winds and convection,
resulting in the mixing of water masses (Jakob­
sen and Trébuchet 2000).
B.2.2
Hydrodynamical model
The hydrodynamical information used was the
DHI 3D hydrodynamics (HD) model. The HD
data from DHI was validated by comparing
measured and modelled current speed and di­
rection from two stations in the Fehmarn belt.
To distribute the particles when releasing a dis­
persion factors was added horizontally (1 m/s)
and vertically (0.00001 m/s). These are the rec­
ommended values by MIKE (DHI 2025). The
dispersion factor will activate a random spread­
ing of the particles by each timestep. This is
Modelling eDNA transport in the marine environment
61
==PAGE:72==
a common procedure when conducting simula­
tions with multiple particles.
The hydrodynamic information is inserted into a
Eulerian framework in MIKE software, and the
HD information will be written into the underly­
ing mesh. The mesh is also constructed by DHI.
The mesh sizes range between 150 meters by the
coast and up to 20000 meters in the open water.
The resolution is highest around Fehmarn belt,
both on the Danish and German side.
B.2.3
Biophysical model
The models are done in ABM lab by MIKE soft­

B.2.4
Model Experiment 1
The purpose of Model Experiment 1 was to test
the sensitivity of eDNA decay rates and how
the distance would change with different current
conditions. The purpose of this test is to under­
stand the patterns of eDNA in this specific ma­
rine environment.
Three degradation rates will be tested, which are
average, maximum, and minimum values from
the literature study A.2. The rates are: low: 0.02
h−1, normal: 0.09 h−1 and high: 0.3 h−1.

Model Set­up
The simulation releases 10 particles at every

when below 0.1 in mass.

B.4
Discussion
B.4.1
Decay rate does affect
eDNA travel distance
A low decay rate will result in a longer eDNA
travel distance than a high decay rate.
This
was possible to see on Figures B.6, where the
maximum possible eDNA transport distance in­
creased with decreasing decay rate.
B.4.2
Hydrodynamic conditions
are important for eDNA
travel distance
Model Experiment 1 demonstrated that eDNA
transport distance will change depending on the
current conditions. If strong currents occur, the
distance will become much greater than under
normal conditions. In April, at a 1 meter re­
lease depth for the normal degradation rate, the
maximum distance was approximately 15.5 km.
For August, which had storm conditions also at
a 1­meter release point for a normal degrada­
tion rate, the maximum distance was 53.5 km.
This correlates to the travel distance in August
being 3.53 times longer than the travel distance
in April. This shows how sensitive eDNA par­
ticles can be to hydrodynamic conditions in the
marine environment and should be understood
before investigating eDNA ecology. In Model
experiment 2, a week was extracted that had
similar current speed and directions to October
3rd and 4th(when the samples were taken), but
also days with opposite current direction and
stronger speeds, to gain hydrodynamic variation
in eDNA distribution. Figure B.7 ­ 4.18 illus­
trates how the eDNA area increased with de­
creasing decay rate. However, what was also
clear was that the highest mass concentrations
across the three simulations were, to some de­
gree, similar. All had high mass concentration
at the sample sites and along the shore. Figure
B.8 has a further spread due to the rate of 0.02
h−1, but the overall patterns are similar. This
indicates that hydrodynamic conditions will, to
some degree, have a stronger effect on eDNA
distribution than the degradation rate. Ellis et al.
2022 demonstrated by using an Eulerian trans­
port model that with decay rates of 0.035 h−1
and 0.041 h−1 the eDNA would typically con­
centrate 10 ­ 20 km away from the source point
before dropping below the limit of detection
(LoD), but if a strong along shore current would
occur, it could be up to 50 ­ 60 km. Pastor­
Rollan et al. 2024 also conducted an Eulerian
model using the same degradation rates as El­
lis et al. 2022 and found the highest concentra­
tions were located 5 ­ 10 km from the source,
and could be found up to 20 km away from the
sites. These results are similar, but not com­
pletely alike, too the study of Ellis et al. 2022.
This shows how the same degradation rate can
have different distance results depending on the
geographical location, and how hydrodynamics
in the area can affect the travel distance distance.
B.4.3
Future guidelines for
eDNA sampling
This study aimed to improve understanding of
eDNA persistence and develop a more effec­
tive sampling and modeling framework.
Re­
sults show that nearshore sampling provides
the most accurate biodiversity representation,
while accuracy decreases with distance from the
coast. eDNA distribution is strongly influenced
by alongshore currents, and lower degradation
rates expand the potential source area. How­
ever, eDNA tends to accumulate in specific hy­
drodynamic zones, regardless of degradation.
To effectively assess biodiversity or monitor in­
vasive species, understanding hydrodynamics

and water temperature is essential. Based on
these findings, the following recommendations
are proposed for future eDNA sampling and
monitoring.
Storm conditions can increase eDNA trans­
port distances by a factor of approx. 3 com­
pared to normal conditions, highlighting the
importance of hydrodynamics in eDNA dis­
tribution.
This study demonstrated the im­
portance of how changing currents can af­
fect the distance to which eDNA particles can
travel. Particularly in coastal areas with along­
shore currents eDNA distribution can be af­
fected greatly.
Evaluation of hydrodynamics
before conducting eDNA sampling or monitor­
ing should therefore be considered. Based on
the results from this study, it is recommended
to incorporate hydrodynamics into eDNA mon­
itoring.
Adding a +6 C◦temperature increase reduces
the probability area of eDNA by a factor of
approx. 2. Water temperature can impact the
eDNA persistence. By adding a temperature co­
efficient, it was illustrated how eDNA distribu­
tion could increase or decrease with decay rate.
This demonstrates how a shift in temperature in
the marine environment would impact the area
in which eDNA can be distributed. Areas with
seasonality as Denmark, should therefore con­
sider the impact changing seasons can have on
eDNA transport and origin.
eDNA distribution is site­specific This study
and previous studies demonstrate how eDNA
distribution will be highly influenced by the spe­
cific hydrodynamic and biotic conditions at the
site.
These findings indicate that eDNA dis­
tribution cannot be broadly generalized across
sites.
However, the framework developed in
this study for understanding eDNA distribution
can be applied to other locations. A more com­
prehensive understanding of the influences of
hydrodynamics and water temperature will fur­
ther enhance the effectiveness of eDNA moni­
toring and interpretation.
B.5
Conclusion
This study provides valuable insights into how
eDNA distribution in the Fehmarn Belt region
is influenced by hydrodynamic conditions and
degradation rates.
The results showed that
eDNA travel distance increased with decreas­
ing decay rates and strong oceanic currents,
whereas particles released at deeper depths or
high decay rates decreased the eDNA travel dis­
tance.
The backtracking model demonstrated
that the potential origin area of eDNA increased
as the decay rate decreased.
Despite varying
decay rates, a consistent hydrodynamic pattern
emerged, revealing persistent areas with high
eDNA concentrations. The findings highlight
the importance of sampling eDNA as close as
possible to the target site. Sampling in hydro­
dynamically unsuitable areas or too far from the
source may result in detecting signals from non­
representative or irrelevant species.
This study has introduced a new framework
that incorporates hydrodynamic modeling into
eDNA monitoring and sampling strategies.
By integrating hydrodynamic information, re­
searchers can enhance both the efficiency of
field sampling and the accuracy of data inter­
pretation. Additionally, these results are con­
tributing to the growing body of knowledge on
eDNA persistence and transport in marine envi­
ronments.
