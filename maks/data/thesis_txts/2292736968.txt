VIII!
Abstract 
 
This Master Thesis is the result of an intensive five-months period dedicated to 
editing this text, working as an intern in an architectural and engineering design 
studio in Turin, Italy, named “Building Engineering”. 
! 
 
 
 
 
The aim of the project is to make an overall analysis and suggest a new complete 
design proposal regarding the refurbishment of a historic existing seven-storey 
baroque building in the centre of Turin, Via Lagrange 12 Italy. The project is 
called “Lagrange12”. 
The new design proposal focuses mostly on the re-designing of the top storeys of 
the existing building, proposing an innovative, prefabricated, faster, cheaper and 
lighter solution. The aim is to give a different, more energy efficient and more 
cost-effective solution than the one proposed by the design studio, which could 
hypothetically work in the real world, meanwhile providing the highest possible 
quality of the final result. Therefore, the cutting-edge technology of X-Lam or 
CLT, Cross Laminated Timber, glued wood panels is used. This solution is 
proved to be better than a traditional building method, with regard to costs, times, 
safety and energy performance. Meanwhile, all the aspects concerning the 
properties and the applicability of this technology are taken into account and 
described, giving a reasonable explanation for every characteristic and every 
chosen solution. Moreover, a full detailed collection of drawings and details is 
created to clarify better the specifications and the feasibility of this project.  
The report is divided in five main parts: 
1. Introduction to the project and preliminary study; 
2. New design proposal: architecture, structure, systems, feasibility; 
3. Technical drawings: joints and connections; 
4. Drawings: plans, elevations, sections, building phase construction; 
5. Appendixes. 
A collection of all the files, related to this project, are attached to a USB pen drive

IX!
Sommario 
!
La seguente tesi di laurea magistrale in Ingegneria Edile è il risultato di un intenso 
periodo di cinque mesi dedicati alla realizzazione di una proposta progettuale, 
ottenuta frequentando un importante studio di progettazione architettonica e 
ingegneristica della città di Torino, Italia, la Building Engineering.  
L'obiettivo del progetto è di proporre un'analisi generale su un intervento di 
ristrutturazione edilizia e di suggerire una nuova proposta progettuale completa e 
alternativa riguardo alla ristrutturazione di un edificio barocco di sette piani, nel 
centro di Torino, in Via Lagrange 12. Il progetto prende il nome di "Lagrange12". 
La nuova proposta progettuale è incentrata principalmente sulla riprogettazione 
degli ultimi due piani dell'edificio esistente e il rifacimento del tetto piano, 
proponendo una soluzione innovativa per una sopraelevazione fatta con elementi 
prefabbricati in legno, in modo più rapido, più economico e creando una struttura 
più leggera, con elevate requisiti antisismici. L'obiettivo è di proporre un progetto 
alternativo rispetto ai metodi di costruzione tradizionali, ma che sia al contempo 
di alta qualità, efficiente dal punto di vista energetico ed economicamente 
vantaggioso. La soluzione proposta è stata analizzata in modo tale da confermare 
la sua reale fattibilità, affiancandosi come possibile alternativa a quella proposta 
dallo stesso studio di progettazione.  
La tecnologia scelta è quella dell’X-Lam, pannelli di legno massiccio a strati 
incrociati, incollati fra di loro e resi a pannelli prefabbricati, prodotti in fabbrica e 
poi assemblati in cantiere. Questa soluzione è stata così dimostrata essere migliore 
rispetto ai metodi di costruzione tradizionale, per quanto riguarda i costi, i tempi, 
la sicurezza e le prestazioni energetiche. Nel contempo, tutti gli aspetti riguardanti 
le proprietà del materiale e l'applicabilità di questa tecnologia sono stati presi in 
considerazione, descrivendo le sue caratteristiche e spiegando i motivi delle 
soluzioni scelte. Inoltre, una raccolta dettagliata di tavole e dettagli tecnici è stata 
realizzata per chiarirne meglio le specifiche e la fattibilità. La seguente tesi è 
suddivisa in cinque parti principali: 
1. Introduzione al progetto e studio preliminare dell’edificio esistente; 
2.  Nuova proposta progettuale: architettura, strutture, sistemi e fattibilità; 
3.  Disegni tecnici e nodi: soluzioni per giunzioni e collegamenti; 
4. Disegni: piante, prospetti, sezioni, assemblaggio, cantiere; 
5. Appendici.

1 Purpose of the project  
 
The aim of the project is to make an overall analysis regarding the refurbishment 
of a historic existing seven-storey baroque building in the centre of Turin, Via 
Lagrange 12, Italy. The project is called “Lagrange12”. 
 
! 
Figure 1 – Urban setting of refurbishment intervention: Lagrange12 
This master thesis is the result of five months of hard work, carried out during an 
internship in the architectural and engineering studio called “Building 
Engineering”, located in Turin. The scope of this project is to describe the main 
building works that occur during a large renovation design project, in which 
many elements and factors constantly influence the final result and propose a new 
and innovative solution for the rebuilding of the upper part of the building, 
designing a solution that could be perfectly integrated with the existing one, but 
that could have a better energy performance, structural stability and be 
lighter, cheaper and faster to carry out, saving money and time. The project fits 
inside the existing and on-going one and the aim was to solve some of the design 
==PAGE:15==
2!
problems that occurred during the design phase. Moreover, a complete design 
study was developed, basing it on a possible solution for the rebuilding of the 
top two storeys of the building. The aim was to give a different solution, beside 
the one proposed by the design studio, which could hypothetically work in the real 
world. The following diagram summarises the flow of this Master Thesis. 
 
Figure 2 – Flow diagram developed  
The first part of the work focused on the current real estate situation in Italy in 
order to understand the condition of the property heritage present in the territory; 
then a close investigative analysis was conducted on the building itself trying to 
understand its history, urban aspects and its condition at the preliminary phase, 
both architecturally and structurally, clarifying the works that needs to be done.  
In the second part, the new design proposal based on the cutting-edge technology 
of wood X-Lam panels is described accurately: the new architectural concept is 
introduced, proposing the design of four new apartments. Then the structural 
system is analysed proving that the new structure is lighter and therefore the mass 
of the building is reduced, obtaining benefits also from a seismic point of view. 
Additionally, structural pre-dimensional analyses and detailed calculations of the 
full structure are conducted to prove its load-bearing characteristics. Next, a 
detailed study about all the aspects concerning a design was analysed through 
detailed drawings, technical solutions, fire behaviour and building construction 
phase, concluding finally with a budget and timetable analysis to clarify the real 
feasibility of the new design proposal. 
To conclude, an energy and environmental investigation was edited to 
understand better the environmental impact on the planet of the new addition over

moreover, a solution of improvement of the existing envelope was proposed 
based on a detailed analysis conducted using the Glazer-Method. Moreover, the

determined precisely. The building was analysed in detail, trying to solve some of 
the most intriguing matters, from the foundation up to the roof. 
The project is based on the recovery of a seven storey baroque building, with two 
levels below the ground floor and its renovation for residential use, according to 
its original destination, adding also a commercial area on the ground and first 
floor. This choice was led by the central and strategic position of the building and 
by the new commercial trend that has spread all over the area. Originally, the 
building was subdivided into nine units, distributed from the first to the sixth floor 
and three commercial units, placed on the ground and the first floor. As an added 
service and value, the basement will be restored and used as a cellar by the 
commercial activities to stock goods or as a commercial showroom. In the current 
courtyard, a brand new underground three-storey parking lot, accessible by a car 
lift, will be built. The new renovation aims to create the perfect mix between 
architectural values and new functional needs, arising from the new scope. 
Meanwhile, the designers have been trying to protect and enhance the neoclassical 
style, which is still readable in many parts of the building.

2 Current Real Estate Situation 
!
This analysis is basically focused on the building renovation market and on the 
situation of the Italian building condition. [19] 
Old buildings that need to be renovated compose most of the European Building 
Industry market. Moreover, these buildings represent a fundamental role for their 
importance, 
considering 
their 
historic, 
socio-cultural, 
economical 
and 
environmental value. Most of these buildings need a full renovation, especially 
from an energetic point of view. They need a strong intervention to ensure the 
stability of the structures, the rehabilitation from a socio-cultural aspect, but 
especially in order to meet the energy requirements and standards of performance 
that the new or renovated buildings must fulfil. 
The current Italian landscape is really rich with real estate, but on the other hand it 
is under-developed. In fact, the critical situation of the Italian building stock 
emerged during the Green Economy Meeting in 2012, when it was shown up that 
Italy has the oldest and most under-renovated stock in Europe; considering that 
5% of the overall buildings are in urgent need of renovation, while 40% of the 
buildings need extraordinary maintenance interventions. Consequently, this 
condition of the building market is clearly dependent on the nature of Italy itself, 
because Italy and its evolution are profoundly connected to its history, which has 
created during the centuries a huge number of historic settlements and architecture 
masterpieces.  
Analysing the situation of the Italian building stock, it can be said that it is 
composed by 55% of buildings that are older than 40 years; moreover, if we 
consider the medium sized cities the percentage goes up to 70%, while for big 
cities it reaches the peak of 76%. In the last decade, considering the renovation 
field, an increase of 15% has been observed in the investments in the renovation 
sector. This can be related to different factors that helped to foster and advocate 
the investments: first of all, the presence of old and crumbling buildings on the 
territory, then the possibility to personalize and modernize the buildings, the 
increased subsidies and policies of tax deduction (between 36% and 55%) offered 
by the government for the refurbishing and renovation of the building heritage and 
finally the need to fulfil the European regulations, for instance concerning 
electrical systems and heating technologies. Moreover, a developed and diffused 
awareness of the environment and the green energy sources has spread into the

many people to renovate their own.

3 Special concern for historic residential buildings 
 
The renovation of the building heritage is really important and a fundamental 
aspect for Italy due to the fact that the refurbishment of aged buildings allows the 
country to save resources, to reduce the land consumption, to give new life to the 
important past and to integrate new technologies into old building, with the aim of 
energy savings.  
The ability of the designer is to let the footprints of the past relive, to reconfigure 
the interior and to find them a specific new social function, which must be 
coherent with the features of the building and the urban integration. The 
refurbishment of historic buildings represents the preservation of their unique 
authenticity and a specific place, society and epoch; moreover, they incorporate 
the construction art, the knowledge combined with the techniques and the 
technologies. The elements that make a historic construction authentic refer to 
surroundings, external appearance, general composition, architectural details, 
interior organization, simultaneously taking into account the structural setting and 
its details. To sum up, a building is built to respond to the beneficiary’s 
requirements, making it more a social commitment rather than a self-serving one. 
The restoration must restore the functionalities of the architecture, its aesthetic 
and the characterizing details, combined with the needs of structural stability, 
serviceability, fire safety and durability. Each of these performance parameters is 
set within a certain design value that usually decreases during the service lifetime 
of the building, as shown in Diagram 1. The diagram shows that there is a 
difference between the theoretical degradation and the real degradation process, 
characterized by repeated cycles of decay and repair to the building. [26]

building functionality beyond its limits of service life.

The rehabilitation of historic building is required when there is the need to 
modernize the systems, improve the functionality or make functional 
modifications. Meanwhile, damage and deficiencies can interfere with the 
serviceability life of the construction and they can overlap with the generally 
worn-out condition of the building. At this point, if the conditions are not any 
more sustainable, it needs to undergo rehabilitation.   
The renovation needs to be undertaken carefully, considering the functionality of 
the new refurbishment as well as the structural reliability, which concerns 
resistance, stability, serviceability, durability and maintenance of the building. 
First of all, the preservation of the authenticity of historic construction needs 
ensure the respect of architectural style, geometrical proportions and urban 
requirements. At the same time, the structural interventions need to assure the 
compatibility of the new structures with the original one, while setting an 
appropriate degree of reliability with the original frame of the existing structure. 
Therefore, the interventions need to maintain the original forms, while performing 
the necessary repairs and strengthening, repair the faults with new materials 
compatible with the original ones both physically and chemically.

To sum up, the refurbishment and the sustainable retrofitting of the historic 
buildings need to take in consideration many different aspects [44,45]:   
3.1 Whole Building Strategy 
 
• These buildings are made of a solid masonry framework, which is able to 
bear high loads; in fact most of the walls have a considerable thickness 
and most of them are bearing walls. Additionally, the walls guarantee a 
great and durable performance both structurally and energetic, providing 
an important thermal inertia, especially in the envelope walls. The seismic 
aspect is fundamental, especially in a sensitive environment such as Italy. 
• Before any type of intervention the structure it is necessary to evaluate 
whether it stands in good condition and if it can hold the new features that 
will be added. If there are any risky defaults of structural parts, like 
foundations, floors or bearing walls, they must be repaired using the best 
possible appropriate technologies. The structural additions must be 
evaluated in a wider context, considering the feasibility of the design, the 
necessity of intervention and their costs. 
• A fundamental aspect of a renovation is related to the maintenance of the 
historic pattern of the building. Generally, old buildings are constrained 
by strict laws that do not allow any changes to the facade features, from 
colours to materials, from style to finishing. Therefore, it is crucial to 
restore every part of existing building, without altering the original style. 
• Audit of the existing structures, cavities, shafts and technical 
compartments that were used since before the renovation and a valuation 
of those spaces have to be made about the installation of the new technical 
equipment for energy savings. 
• The redesign of the building layout depends on the final destination of the 
building. The redistribution of the spaces has to be considered to make the 
building more liveable and to propose new living solutions that could fulfil 
the modern-day needs. 
Finally, the transformation of the existing building needs to consider also:  
• The fulfilment of safety and security standards, regarding the structural 
affordability, the durability of the constructions and the fire resistance of 
the critical areas, like stairs and underground parking lot. These need to be 
==PAGE:24==
11
designed so that they are consistent with the current standard of 
performance, avoiding any alteration of the building and its spaces, in 
order to attain final approval by local authorities. [26] 
3.2 Sustainability and energy-efficient strategy  
 
Due to the fact that existing buildings represent the largest part of the built 
environment, it is important to improve their sustainability and the energy 
performance of the old existing buildings during their retrofitting. Meanwhile, 
another goal is to create a better performance building by applying an integrated, 
whole building design process. It has to answer to all the design objectives, 
creating a building that is more functional, less costly to operate and more 
liveable by the users, creating a more comfortable and healthier environment. 
The improvement of the indoor environmental quality is most effective on the 
health and the productivity of the occupants and it is also enriched by the decrease 
of the moisture penetrations and the reduction of the mould.  During the retrofit 
and the refurbishment of the building, it also really important to upgrade space 
distribution, accessibility, safety and security of the building. In this way, 
therefore, the operation costs and the environmental impacts will be reduced and 
the durability of the building will be increased. Inspired by an article by Richard 
Paradis on the Whole Building Design Guide website [43] about the way how to 
retrofit the existing buildings in a sustainable and high energy performance way, a 
list with the most relevant possible strategies and recommendations, related to a 
refurbishment intervention on historic buildings, is outlined below [44,45] [26]: 
 
• Redesign and upgrade all the systems to minimize the energy 
consumption and improve the performance of the building.  
• Optimize the recycling or reuse of demolished materials and 
construction waste, minimizing the wasted quantities.  
• Energy audit, evaluate the existing systems and the performance of the 
building and its envelope. Solve the problems of leaks, faults and 
inefficiencies that will lead to an increase in costs. This process is 
designed to understand the existing condition of the building, identifying 
the major building systems and the areas that need to be improved. 
==PAGE:25==
12
• Determine the air tightness of the building envelope, replacing leaky 
windows, gaps around the vents, pipe faults and moisture intrusion.  
• Discover and remove hazardous material such as: asbestos, 
polychlorinated biphenyls (PCB) and lead paints. 
• Redesign and upgrade daylight, ventilation, lighting and heating, after a 
detailed study of the occupancy pattern, to enhance the quality of life of 
the occupants. 
• Redesign the envelope and the interior, proposing new and innovative 
solutions for the construction partitions, with the aim of guaranteeing the 
fulfilment of the requirements for the internal acoustic and thermal 
comfort. 
• Determine the best way to heat and refresh the indoor environment, 
deciding if natural ventilation is an important feature to be integrated in 
the project.  
• Investigate the renewable energy and alternative solutions that would cut 
out, as far as possible, dependency on the fossil fuel-based energy. Some 
of the possible alternatives could be: use of a heat-exchanger, use of more 
electricity-based systems and the creation of a geothermal plant to provide 
heat for the hot water and for the heating system. 
• Replace of existing windows with new high-performance windows, 
selected specifically for the present climate and the type of exposure of the 
building. Special films or glasses can be added for more security or an 
exterior to interior noise reduction system can enrich the windows’ 
protection. 
• Evaluate the addition of a green-roof, if it can be a sustainable and cost-
effective solution to cut the energy consumptions. 
• Update the systems as appropriate to maintain a balance between the new 
needs for energy and technology, with meters that control the consumption 
in real time; but at the same time maintaining the original character of the 
building. 
• Use the on-going renovation to adopt sustainable operations, install 
green cleaning technologies and use methods that reduce the costs of the 
services.

5 Historic synopsis of the case building 
 
The building is placed in the New town created with the southern urban expansion 
of Turin ordered by the Duke of Savoy Carlo Emanuele I, who started this great 
master plan in 1619. It was accomplished over three centuries. Piazza San Carlo 
represents the connection between old medieval city, reshaped during the first half 
of the 1600, and New Town. Figures below show building itself and its position

The expansion of this part of the city was carried out during the XVII century, 
Baroque period, as well as the palace, as part of the well-known Theatrum 
Sabaudie; a brilliant master plan wanted ordered by the royal family of Savoy all 
over the region called Piemonte, of which Turin was the capital. Many documents 
and important drawings, since the 1682, represent the “L” shape of the building. 
The present state of the building was heavily influenced by neoclassical additions. 
In fact, these changes and major transformations were made at the beginning of 
the XIX century, in particular in 1824, when the building was renovated, 
transforming it from an old pre-existing factory of XVII century into what we can 
enjoy today.  
During the Second World War, it was severely damaged by the bombing that 
caused the collapse of the upper part of the building, but during later years it was 
renovated and eventually became an impressive testimony of the neoclassical 
architecture in Turin. In 1953, the Municipality bought the building from a saving 
bank fund with the aim of realizing the urban restoration of the city centre, 
especially considering the enlargement of Via Roma and the refurbishment of the 
buildings all along the main street.

7.1 Recovery of the exterior and interior facades  
 
The historic analysis, conducted prior to the drafting of the project, led to the 
discovery of the original design of the building’s facade and to the

neoclassical value of the building can be seen on the two facades, facing Via

ground floor, readable by the comparison with the original drawings and by a 
direct analysis of the individual elements. In order to preserve and enhance the 
facades on the street, a stratigraphic analysis will be needed to revive the original 
colours of recesses and projections and to restore the decoration of pilaster strips 
and window frames, which must be recuperated and/or maintained. Lagrange12 is 
distributed on seven different levels, without counting the existing basement, used 
as cellars and storage rooms. In an overall analysis, the building presents a well-
preserved facade, apart from some little collapse of the finishing, while it also 
presents vaulted ceilings on the ground and first floors and in the basement, and 
on the upper storeys the original load-bearing walls have been conserved. The 
elevations are constituted by a stone-clad basement area, corresponding to the 
ground floor, enriched with a decorative smooth ashlar-work, positioned on the

The historic buildings of the seventeenth century are mostly built in solid brick, 
arranged in a specific way. The technique used created thick load bearing walls 
that could hold the heavy weight of the whole structure. In fact, the building

Regarding the mechanical properties, the masonry presents regular texture in 
horizontal rows of bricks and mortar joints that are subjected to compression. The 
break shows a set of vertical cracks relatively more frequent in the central part. It 
is typical and representative in masonry brick blocks joined by regular horizontal 
mortar joints. The cause of the break is the different deformability of the 
constituents. The mortar, more deformable, is located in a state of confinement 
between the layers of bricks which produces a state of triaxial compression; by 
reaction on the faces of the bricks, which are adherent to it, arises a state of forces 
(plane) of biaxial traction, which causes the break, as load increases. 
The vertical cracks, present on both vertical planes, are due to the collapse of the 
blocks in the plan for traction.

pillars, which progressively form due to separation from the wall or the buckling, 
combining compressive and bending stress. Moreover, the shear forces can be 
applied to inclined and weaker parts. 
These are the experimental cases, when those kind of problems occur: 
• The walls are made of mortar with a particularly weak break; therefore they 
break at a higher stress value respect to the strength of the mortar. 
• The performance of the masonry wall can be increased by the presence of 
particular strong bricks, with a great behaviour if subjected to tension. 
• The core walls or walls made with round gravel in the inner part have a 
different way to collapse.

• Resistance (especially tension) and geometry of the brick; 
• Strength and stiffness of the mortar (the more the rigidity increases, the

the one of the brick);  
• Deformational characteristics of brick and mortar; 
• Thickness of the joints (which affects the state of confinement); 
• Capacity of water absorption of brick and of water retention of mortar; 
• Adhesion between mortar and brick; 
• Manufacturing of the masonry

8.2.1 Barrel vaults and flexural problem 
 
The most common vaults used in historic buildings are the barrel vault and the 
lunetted barrel vault shown in Figure 37 below. At Lagrange12 the barrel vault in 
particular, is widely used, especially in the basement of the building. [2,25] 
Figure 37 - Barrel vault (left) and lunetted barrel vault (right). 
Characteristics 
With regard to the vaults design, the most important aspect of the construction of 
the vaults was the realization of a suitable filling, capable of ensuring, on the one 
hand, the bending stiffness of the vault and, on the other hand, the least possible 
burden on supporting structures. In fact, the abutment is made of masonry and it is 
generally constructed simultaneously with the vaulted structure and, from a 
structural point of view, it is located on the lower sides. Moreover, it may still be 
considered part of the piers. Differently, the filling consists of loose material, 
often the one excavated and it is placed above the arc. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 38 - Schematic drawing of the abutment and the filling of the vault 
Usually, the material of the filling is a conglomerate of poorer quality than the one 
used in the arc, but it ensures the absorption of the stresses that the arc alone 
would not be able to resist. [27,28,29] 
==PAGE:56==
43
From the constructive point of view, the arrangement of the bricks for each of 
the spindles can be arranged in different textures: longitudinal rows, transversal, 
longitudinal and transversal and herringbone brick disposition. 
The easiest method is represented by the longitudinal row disposition, in Figure 
39 (a) below, placing the brick courses parallel to the lines of support.  Another 
method was used when the span of the vault was longer and slightly lowered; 
therefore the bricks were placed at 45° with respect to the plan of the vault. The 
bricks were placed starting simultaneously from the four corners and proceeding 
symmetrically towards the centre as it can be seen in Figure 39 (b) below. This 
type of arrangement of the rows identifies an attempt by builders to shift part of 
the thrust not only on the longitudinal walls, but also on the head walls.  
 
Figure 39 - Equipment for barrel vaults: equipment in longitudinal rows (a); 
equipment with bricks at 45 ° to the sides of the plant (b). [2,27,28,29] 
 
Usual structural disorders 
The masonry vaults are frequently treated as shells, where the flexural regime 
is secondary to that membrane. This is true because often, in old buildings, in the 
extrados of the vaults there is a heavy, loose filling, generally consisting of 
discarded material from the building site, able to fit the curve of the vault and able 
to bring down the flexural regime which might arise in one masonry vault. Such 
filler, however, while contributing to limit bending of the arch, may often be too 
burdensome for the vaulted structure, due to changes in the destination of use with 
a consequent increase of variable loads or following horizontal stresses caused by 
earthquakes. Therefore, this could lead to an excessive increase in loads and 
horizontal thrust on piers, resulting in bulging of the lower part of the vaulted 
structure. In fact, the filling, placed above the arch or the vault, leads to an 
==PAGE:57==
44
increase in the vertical load on the structure, often causing a considerable thrust 
on the piers; however, its function is not only linked to the creation of a horizontal 
plane on which the finishing for the floor is placed, rather, it is primarily to limit 
the development of flexural stresses. 
Solutions 
Currently, in the restoration of historic buildings it is therefore common to replace 
this inconsistent filling with light and cohesive material or with stiffening 
masonry arcs, called frenelli, shown in Figure 40, which function as diaphragms 
able to let the arch to work in compression even in unfavourable load conditions 
such as those of concentrated or asymmetrical load. 
 
Figure 40 – Example of frenelli applied to the barrel vaults 
The use of “frenelli”, unlike the soil or other materials normally used as filling, on 
one hand allows lightening the structure by reducing the overhead burden on the 
vault and on the other allows providing a sufficiently stiff structure to be able to 
absorb the bending of the bow. Finally, in the Figure below, the most common 
methods to restore a vault architecturally and structurally are shown.

8.2.2 Pavilion vaults 
 
Characteristics 
The pavilion vaults made in masonry are a frequent type of vault, very common in 
historic buildings and they can be found in the project “Lagrange12” on the 
ceilings of the basement and the first two storeys. The pavilion vaults were used 
extensively starting from the sixteenth century as a cover for the most 
representative building environments. [2] 
From geometric point of view, pavilion vaults are generated by the intersection of 
two or more barrel vaults. If you were supposed to cut a barrel vault with two 
diagonal vertical planes, four shares are identified AA' and BB': Parts A and A' 
are called nails, while parts B and B' are called spindles, as shown in Figure 42.  
 
 
 
Figure 42 - Isometric view of a barrel vault with pointed nails, and spindles. 
 
Four or more spindles form the pavilion vault, which has the ability to unload 
uniformly the vertical and horizontal stresses on the perimeter walls due to its 
geometrical conformation. However, it is evident that the volume and thus the 
weight of the pavilion vaults, is greater than the other types of vaults set on the 
same plant. To overcome this problem, in the past, a more complex variation like 
the barrel vault with pavilion heads was used. An example of this type of vault 
can be seen in Figure 43. Additionally, for the covering of elongated, rectangular 
rooms builders used a cylindrical barrel vault joined to the heads with two flaps 
semi cylindrical. 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 43 - Barrel vault with heads pavilion [2] 
==PAGE:59==
46
From the constructive point of view, the pavilion vault is made with the methods 
and equipment typical of the barrel vault, repeated in the two directions of the 
axes. To achieve a pavilion vault, it was necessary to establish the line of the 
intrados of the vault, which was then deduced from the curvature of the edges. To 
build a vault of this type, wooden ribs were necessary, that reproduced wholly or 
in part, the surface of the intrados of the vaults and on which it was then built the 
same vault, placing wooden half-ribs to reproduce the diagonals and the arcs.  
 
Figure 44 - Axonometry and carpentry for the armour of a pavilion vault [2] 
The arrangement of the material for each of the spindles was identical to the one 
for the barrel vaults. The edges, in the pavilion vaults, were built simultaneously 
and with the same material of the spindles. However, the edges being the weak 
points of the structure, it was often preferred to form rows perpendicular to the 
diagonals, able to create a greater connection in those areas, instead of 
longitudinal courses distribution, as in Figure 45. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 45 - Diagram of perpendicular rows to diagonals (left), longitudinal (right) 
 
Usual structural disorders and solutions 
Pavilion vaults, like all vaults, exert excessive pressures on the support of the 
vault and cause the displacement of the external walls, especially in the case of 
large vaults with scarce vertical loads on the piers, as for instance with the 
presence of the vaults on the upper floors of the buildings. In addition to the 
problem related to the push against the support of the vault, pavilion vaults often 
have major damage along the diagonals. In pavilion vaults, the interactions 
==PAGE:60==
47
between the arcs are of fundamental importance for the determination of the stress 
to which they are subjected and for the evaluation of the thrust that they exert on 
the perimeter walls. Therefore, in the pavilion vaults, as in all vaulted roofs, the 
collapses and damage are often caused by thrust at the base of the vaults, which, if 
excessive, can lead to the relative displacement of perimeter walls. In fact, it is 
important to maintain the stability of the structure, so that the forces that the vault 
exerts on the walls of the sphere, combined with the vertical loads, are not such as 
to load the piers with tensile forces. If this condition is not satisfied, the formation 
of lesions at the base of the piers and the rotation of the same can cause a 
lowering in the key of the vault, with the consequent variation in the shape of the 
original structure and the formation of hinges on the top and on the sides of the 
vault. The cracks tend to follow the generatrices and appear in the keystone, as 
shown in the Figure 46 below.  
Figure 46 - Collapse mechanism caused by the dismissal of support 
 
Moreover, usually pavilion vaults are subjected to structural damages along the 
diagonal. The reasons the cracks appear along the diagonals are related to: 
• Collapse of support walls, caused by excessive horizontal thrust of the vault;  
• Cracks are generated by the tensile stresses perpendicular to the diagonals;  
• Disruptions related to the decay of the materials used; in particularly, the 
deterioration of mortar, due to the disintegrating action of moisture, freeze-
thaw or chemical actions, can reduce the mechanical properties. Therefore, the 
quality of the contact and the capacity of the tensile strength are reduced and 
the coefficient of friction between the segments can be greatly reduced 
compared to masonry.

10.1 X-LAM 
 
Born in Germany in the nineties and developed over the last fifteen years, X-Lam 
or CLT, Cross Laminated Timber technology, has characterized an incredible 
revolution in the building sector allowing designers to build safe, ecological, 
modular and lighter buildings. This revolution has deeply changed the way of 
conceiving bearing structures and surfaces and it has turned against the more 
traditional and established methods of frame or truss construction, like “platform 
frame”, that was never widely used in Italy.  
CLT or X-Lam is very stable dimensionally and structurally speaking and 
basically it basically consists of several fir wood layers boards joined 
crosswise, at 90th, and glued together on the wide faces with special 
formaldehyde-free polyurethane adhesive. Analysing the cross section of a CLT 
module, it can be said that it consists of at least three or more layers of wood 
board, placed one on top of the others with an orthogonally diverse orientation to 
the following layer as shown in Figure 51 below.  [4] 
Figure 51 - Stratigraphy of the X-Lam panel (left) and an example of a 5-layer 
module (right) 
The cutting-edge quality of X-Lam consisted in passing from a linear and 
unidirectional elements, like beams and pillars or uprights and crosspieces, to a 
structurally bidirectional element, which works both as a plate (slab) and as a 
slab (wall) and that could work perfectly also as a shelf. To sum up, the most 
valuable characteristic of these compressed fir wood panels is their capacity for 
absorbing stress in any direction. The wood itself has an anisotropic behaviour, 
for this reason localized effects of accumulation of tension rise; differently the X-
Lam distribute shear stress, bending moment, normal force in the direction of its 
fibres where the resistance is maximum optimizing the response of the material. 
==PAGE:66==
53
Therefore, the result is a homogeneous distribution of the loads on the entire 
perimeter of the plate. Similarly, the vertical panels behave in the same way.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 52 – Structural plate behaviour of the X-Lam slab 
This technological improvement came directly from the users’ need to build 
quickly and accurately and with a reduction of the overall costs of the 
construction itself. Finally, this led to the affirmation of the concept of 
“prefabrication”.  This solution represented an extremely cutting-edge turnkey for 
the building industry, allowing the total customization of the building, tailored to 
the needs of the customer, manufactured and delivered to the building site ready 
to be put in place. Therefore, the cross-laminated timber panels or X-Lam 
perfectly suit the trend for innovative prefabricated technological solutions. 
The basic elements of a multi-storey wood are therefore: [21]

Due to this characteristic, CLT can span in the two different directions, due to the 
fact it becomes a rigid panel and therefore it can span vertically, between columns 
and horizontally, between beams, avoiding any type of additional structural 
support. Therefore, CLT modules can be used for floors, walls or roofs in multi-

storey buildings, both as a total CLT-made structure and as an addition and joint 
between a new structure and an existing one, as in my study case on the building 
Lagrange12. In fact, the panels suit perfectly the needs of a renovation or an 
extension of an existing structure, due to the fact it does not give any type of 
problem, architecturally, structurally or from a thermal point of view and 
moreover, it can be erected as quickly as a tilt-up concrete system. The 
prefabricated modules manufactured off-site are delivered to the building site, 
hoisted into place and bolted together with different types of connections, 
especially bolted steel plates. Additionally, these metal plates are of minor 
importance in terms of weight in the order of 5-10 ‰ with respect to the total 
amount of wood used, however their performance is absolutely essential to 
transfer the forces between the elements. Furthermore, the use of large-sized solid 
wooden panels allows all the openings in the panels, like doors or windows, to be 
used for a load-bearing purpose, if necessary.  
Finally, we must emphasise the totally environment-friendly characteristics of 
CLT, due to the fact the wood comes only from sustainable forests; moreover, the 
timber can be produced near the building site or in neighbouring countries, 
reducing transportation and CO2 emissions. The environmental footprint is also 
related to the transportation of modules and it is endorsed and improved by the 
possibility of using both 2D and 3D methods of transportation thanks to the 
flexibility of the modules, in their sizes and modularity. Finally, this technology 
allows creating a structure, which can be perfectly disassembled and taken apart 
in the future, if not needed anymore. For more details on CLT, check Appendix E. 
10.2 Reasons to choose X-Lam in Lagrange12  
!
The design of the two upper storeys of the Lagrange12 building is a box 
structure in which the walls and the floors are rigid diaphragms mechanically 
linked together using metal joints. In fact, the building actually bases its own 
structural efficiency on this box-behaviour, characterized by the continuity of 
material and the uniform distribution of mechanical stresses throughout the 
materials used. This system has been chosen rather than the typical frame 
construction; due to the fact the X-Lam system produces more benefits, with 
respect to more widely used systems, such as concrete-brick hollow-cores. Figure 
53, below, shows a summary of the main features.

For instance, on the one hand, it is true that X-Lam system needs a larger amount 
of material, due to its full horizontal section, on the other hand, however the wood 
used is mere waste from other manufacturing processes and therefore it has a 
lower quality compared to the wood used in the construction realized with 
uprights and crosspieces of the “platform frame”, but the final result is of high 
quality anyway. Moreover, X-Lam is the most environmentally friendly solution, 
because it uses the general manufacturing wood waste, which could not be reused 
in other way. This characteristic makes the panel solution basically economically 
equal to the other solution; additionally, it gives to X-Lam that extra structural 
benefit with a higher load-bearing capacity compared to the other system, based 
on an equally dimensioned horizontal section. Moreover, the X-Lam offers such 
great values that it can be totally equalized to a concrete panel, but with the 
advantage that a wall has an extremely better thermal performance, while using 
about half of thickness of a normal “brick+insulation” wall. Thus, it is possible to: 
• Enlarge the interior spaces (between 5% and 10%), due to the reduced 
wall thickness; 
• Reduce the use of material; 
• Reduce the weights loading on the structure; 
• Create a lighter and slimmer structure.

10.2.1 Mechanical Strength 
!
The X-Lam panel has a great mass-performance ratio with good dimensional 
stability and good isotropy in the plane. Moreover, it brings a ductile and flexible 
method to the design of the structures, once the check of the connections between 
the modules is done. For instance, the choice of X-Lam for the elevation of a 
structure on an existing old brick building, like Lagrange12, will avoid any need 
for structural strengthening of the existing structure, due to the fact the wood 
panels are three times lighter than a normal brick wall. Moreover, dimensional 
stability and stiffness are main features of the X-Lam system. The structure is able 
to absorb and transmit to the foundations stresses and forces that come from any 
direction. Particularly, it is suitable against seismic activity, due to its ability to 
withstand the loads, which are divided in several directions. 
10.2.2 Energy Efficiency 
!
X-Lam is an excellent thermal, electrical and acoustic insulation material that 
creates an elevated living comfort. The conductivity of the wood panels (U = 0.12 
to 0.13 W/mK) allows the panel itself to act as an insulating material and with 
good thermal inertia, with a thinner thickness of the wall itself; additionally, the 
total energy performance of the envelope is improved by substituting the existing 
worn-out windows with new ones with a U-value of 1.5 W/mK. 
Damping and elasticity qualities and the multiple-layer principle of the materials 
used contributes to give a better acoustic insulation performance, therefore X-
Lam walls work better than the massive construction methods as a barrier against 
noise propagation. The transmission of noise and vibration is avoided by putting 
sealing stripes between the construction elements and fibrous insulation materials 
into walls and floors. 
The X-Lam multi-layer system is also sufficient to provide the perfect 
airtightness; this is fundamental to avoid thermal losses, condensation growth, 
draughts and any acoustic bridge. To ensure a good airtightness, an inner layer of 
sealing is placed against the vapour diffusion. Externally, a taping sealant joint 
==PAGE:70==
57
cover guarantees the airtightness between the construction elements; moreover, 
plastered walls ensure the tightness against wind.  
Finally, the building made of X-Lam panels is basically free of thermal bridges 
and very easy to seal with external insulating coats that however have very 
restrained equal transmittance. Moreover, X-Lam is a good hygroscopic material, 
capable of absorbing humidity variations of the environment. Shrinking, swelling 
and variations in the stress state of the wood, caused by stabilized humidity in the 
wall, are completely irrelevant longitudinally, and almost nil transversally, due to 
presence of extra coatings and insulation. Mould and parasites are avoided. 
10.2.3 Appearance 
!
The system has the quality of matching with steel, glass, bricks and other building 
materials and fits to the surrounding urban context, without architectural 
inconsistencies. The X-Lam system has also the right versatility in terms of 
combination of the structure to the interior and exterior finishing (plaster) that is 
fundamental in such historic building as Lagrange12. 
10.2.4 Fire Safety  
!
The structure design of the panel itself has a great performance against fire 
spread; in fact its thickness, made of layers, it allows fire control (wood panels: 
REI 30-90), thanks to its property of carbonizing rather then burning. Therefore, a 
limited loss of resistance in the panel due to heat and a total absence of toxic 
smoke leaks are registered. The endurance of the wood panels exposed to fire is 
improved by applying non-flammable insulation materials and plasterboard plates. 
10.2.5 Sustainability

10.6 Structural pre-analysis  
 
The aim of the following calculation is to define the right type of panel and verify 
its resistance and deformation. The first step is to pre-dimension the structural 
elements of the new X-Lam structure, slabs and walls, respectively. The pre-
dimensioning process starts by estimating the thickness of slab and its constant 
loads and imposed loads. Pre-analysis has been conducted using the design 
details, which perfectly defines the stratification of the partition, set in Chapter 11 
and the structural tables, given by the producer KLH (Appendix F), which 
supplies all the possible type of panels and their strengths, depending on the span. 
It is important to state that the tabled values are already given, taking into account 
the weight of KLH element; therefore, constant load and imposed load need to be 
calculated and added to the load to find out the right type of panel, which can 
varies among thickness, number of layers and fire requirements. The first case 
was edited and described in details as an extensive case, while calculations for the 
other partitions have been just reported following, since the same process was 
used.

10.7 Seismic behaviour 
 
Unlike the vertical actions that may affect only a portion of the structure and some 
building elements, the horizontal seismic action is an action that involves the 
structure as a whole and therefore the continuity of the connections between the 
different portions of the structure is particularly important and must be effective 
both in tension and compression. [7] 
The new two-storey add-on made of cross-laminated bearing panels can be 
considered similar to a box positioned on the existing structure and connected to 
its main partitions. The added walls and floors behave as diaphragms made of 
solid wood panels, which are linked by mechanical connections. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 82 – Seismic forces acting on X-Lam prefab top addition on Lagrange12 
In order to guarantee the behaviour of the entire box-like structure is necessary to 
avoid failure of the structural geometry, locally or globally; in other words, it is 
fundamental that the structure remains tight and connected as a box. For this 
purpose, some connections between the various structural elements must be 
designed with adequate reserves of over-strength in a way that the structure 
always behaves in an elastic way, avoiding excessive deformations or breaks. The 
connections between the elements help to dissipate the energy transferred by a 
possible earthquake. The needed connections are:  
A. Connection between floor slab modules in order to ensure the almost 
complete absence of relative sliding and to guarantee the hypothesis of 
rigid diaphragm; 
B. Connection between the floor, a rigid diaphragm and the 
underlying walls, rigidly connected; 
==PAGE:114==
101
C.  Connection between vertical walls, in particular in the corners of the 
building, so stability of the wall themselves and the one of the whole 
box-structure is guaranteed. 
Connections, marked red in Figure 83, need to have an over-resistance in building 
constituted by modules, with the aim to maintain the box structural model.  
Figure 83 – Connections with elastic behaviour 
Differently, the connections, described in Chapter 10.8, that are designed to 
dissipate the seismic energy through a ductile behaviour, but not provided with 
enough over-resistance, are: 
D. Connections between vertical wall-panels; 
E. Connections at the base of the walls against shear forces; 
F.  “Hold-down” connection at the beginning and at the end of each wall 
and next to the openings. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 84 - Connections with ductile behaviour 
Additionally, the idea to create for the top addition on Lagrange12 a structure 
made of modules, connected together by vertical joints gives important benefits to 
==PAGE:115==
102
the structure from a seismic point of view. In fact, the additional X-Lam structure 
is made of many smaller modules connected between them, instead of huge prefab 
modules. This building method is extremely important seismically, due to the fact 
that a single big panel of X-Lam has a lower capability to dissipate the energy of a 
seismic action, therefore it is less ductile; on the contrary, the same wall, 
constituted by several panels of smaller dimensions, has a better ability to 
dissipate the forces thanks to the vertical joint. This is the reason way prefab 
modules are used. Therefore, the seismic force acting on panels is softened and 
the actions that arise along panels’ joint are controlled through screw connections. 
Different walls’ behaviours are simplified in Figure 85, below.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 85 – Different ways to dissipate seismic actions 
In the same way, the steel joints have an important role and they need to be 
designed with a resistance great enough to resist to the horizontal actions. The 
final scope is to create a box-structure able to resist to the loads and able to 
dissipate the horizontal actions in its frame and its connections, as a unique body. 
Moreover, the horizontal forces acting on the structure create rollover and sliding 
effects, which need to be prevented respectively with the dowels (Hold-down 
system) and angular brackets. These specific calculations are not pertinent with 
the scope of this thesis so they will not be designed, but it is important to 
remember the need for this type of investigation if further studies would be made.

10.7.1.1 Assumptions and values

that is glued wood panels; therefore the structure factor of the X-Lam is set to 
q=2. This parameter was chosen to reduce the forces obtained from linear analysis 
taking into account the non-linear response of the structure as a function of the 
material, the structural system and the adopted design process.  
Even though, the q-value of the X-Lam has a medium ductility, a lower value, 
equal to q= 1,5, which is the dissipative values of a typical brick wall, is chosen. 
This choice is made just to run an unfavourable analysis and prove that the 
structure would resist to an earthquake way more if it were made of X-Lam; in 
fact, the chosen q-value is lower than the X-Lam one and therefore the structure is 
treated as a more rigid one, having a lower ability to dissipate the energy.

10.7.3  Seismic analysis – Lagrange12 
 
The aim is to design an additional structure that could have a seismic acceleration 
lowered as much as possible. The scope is to have a final overall mass lower than 
the one made of brick and concrete, therefore seismic forces acting on each floor 
must be reduced respect to previous existing structure. Ground acceleration 
cannot be modified, while masses can be reduced and so this helps to create a 
lighter and less seismic sensible structure.  
The most common procedure to understand the behaviour of a structure and its 
acceleration during a seismic event is a simplified method based on the elastic 
design spectrum system. In fact, it is a curve that determines the seismic design 
forces; moreover the graph gives the maximum response acceleration demand of 
structures with different natural periods. An example of the elastic design 
spectrum system is shown in Figure 87, below.  
The graph has on the y-axis the spectral acceleration Sd, while on the x-axis the 
vibration period, T, is set. The aim is to reach the lowest acceleration applied to 
the structure; the graph below shows that the best condition to have a low spectral 
acceleration Sd are the line A-B or C-D of the curve.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 87 – Typical elastic design spectrum system 
Every segment in Figure 87 is different:  
A. A-B: it is a short segment, in which period of oscillation (T) is low and 
spectral acceleration increases rapidly. Structure is very rigid and it 
swings with a high frequency (= low T); 
==PAGE:121==
108
B. B-C: it is an horizontal segment in which acceleration remains constant 
and maximum, even if period increases; 
C. C-D-E: curve constantly descends. The higher the period of oscillation 
(T), the lower the frequency of swinging of the building, the lower the 
acceleration applied to the building, the lower the forces applied to each 
storey.  For this reason, the structure results more flexible and elastic. 
 
An ideal way to design would be to find the lowest value of acceleration Sd, which 
can have a high period T and therefore in the descend segment C-D.

structure and the concrete base; moreover, this sheath needs to flip on the concrete 
base structure and not onto the wood wall to avoid moisture traps, as shown in 
Figure 97. Sometimes, another layer of rubber is placed in addition to the sheath 
between the wall and concrete base; this added layer is placed not only with the

acoustic damper.

The connection between floor and walls must play a dual role: prevent both lifting 
(traction forces) and sliding (shear forces) of walls respect to the base under the 
effect of horizontal actions, like wind or earthquake, applying on vertical plan of 
the wall, and in general, on the whole building. Lifting and rollover are usually 
contrasted with the elongated angular steel plates, commonly called “hold down” 
systems, as seen in Figure 98. The holds down brackets are connected to wood 
walls with nails or screws and to concrete base with steel threaded bars inserted in 
holes sealed with cement mortar. These must be positioned in correspondence of 
extreme limits of walls and next to openings. Nails and connection screws to the 
wall have diameters that vary from 3 to 6 mm and threaded rods from 12 to 18 
mm depending on the type of hold-down brackets and applied loads. 
Moreover, sliding is countered with steel angular brackets connected with nails 
or screws to the walls and always with steel threaded bars (always diameter 12-18 
mm), when the wall is directly connected to the concrete curb or X-Lam slabs;

10.9 X-Lam fire behaviour  
 
The X-Lam is a wood based material as described before, but it perfectly resists 
against the fire exposition, despite the general and diffuse idea wood is 
dangerously weak. Actually, the X-Lam panels are certified by the regulation of 
resistance to fire, ONORM EN 1365-2, up to REI 90, depending of course by the 
number and thickness of the layers constituting the wall. [9] 
!
!
!
!
!
 
 
Figure 108 – Fire resistance of the X-Lam panels 
Moreover, the X-Lam has been studied and many tests with direct fuel-fire 
exposure have been carried out, showing that its mechanical properties are 
maintained, without altering the main structure and behaving similarly to masonry 
bearing walls and much better that concrete partitions, which fire resistance 
depends only on the thickness of the concrete layer. In fact, analysing the 
structural X-Lam behaviour exposed to fire, several benefits are found:  
• Slow burning and carbonization goes from the outside inwards; 
• Un-burnt timber preserves the structural efficiency, dangerous bending 
and deformations, typical of steel, are avoided despite high temperature; 
• Breakage never occurs, if structure has been designed to withstand the 
loads; 
Therefore, as a conclusion, the loss of efficiency of the wooden structure exposed 
to fire happens by reduction of the useful cross-section, while not by physical-
mechanical degradation.

10.10 Building construction phase 
 
The X-Lam prefab panels are produced in a factory to exploit the industrialization 
process that is a fundamental factor to ensure a reduction of the costs. In fact, one 
of the advantages, as said in the previous paragraph, is that panels can be 
prefabricated by CNC-milling. The panels are delivered to the building site with 
windows, doors cuts-out, slots and their customized dimensions, just ready to be 
installed. This process simplifies the building construction, reduces the costs and 
simplifies on-site controls. Prefabrication of modules assures a fast assembly 
process and it allows having slots and shafts ready for the technical installation 
passages.  Following, other advantages of CLT are enlisted:  
• “Simplification: modules and panels; 
• Economic benefit: cheaper, clear and defined prices; 
• Logistic: better scheduling;  
• Time saving; 
• More safety for workers: controlled process.” [42] 
In the following paragraphs, the main aspect of transportation, assembly and 
mounting are analysed, based on Lagrange12 project.   
10.10.1Transportation 
 
The modules are prefabricated in the factory and their sizes fit the transport 
limitations. In fact, the transportable sizes do not exceed 12x3x3 metres, due to 
the fact that bigger delivery can encounter many difficulties, like the location of 
the building in the historical city centre, characterized by a narrow street and 
environmental boundaries. The modules are loaded, horizontally or vertically, 
onto the trucks and delivered to the building site. To transport the panels, an 
articulated truck is required. Each truck can be loaded with a maximum of 25 t 
and it can transport elements with a maximum length of 13.6 m (up to 16m also), 
maximum height of 3 m and maximum width of 2.5 m. The panels are positioned 
inside the truck supported by wooden skids (75x75mm or 95x95mm); these skids 
have also the function of simplifying the unloading of the modules, when cranes 
or forklifts are used to lift the panels and place them in the right position. Figure

completed before the arrival of the prefab modules and the start of construction. 
Since the assembly procedure involves high costs, despite the use of a reduced

Assembly needs to be carried out only by high-specialized and well-trained

same time creates a dependence on specialized wood workers, which a traditional 
construction company usually does not have while only X-Lam producers have.

panel, according to the structural bearing capacity of every single panel.

deterioration of the installed elements caused by moisture and weathering.

10.11.1System: Ventilation 
 
The ventilation of the indoor environment is a fundamental aspect in a total 
renovation context; in fact the new apartments need to be ventilated correctly to 
create the best climate conditions for the dwellers. It is important to state a 
complete calculation of the ventilation diffusion was not carried out, but the aim 
was to give a reasonably complete overall analysis. 
First, the condition of the existing old brick structure needs to be considered. In 
fact, old buildings used to have a natural ventilation system mainly thanks to the 
widespread and poor airtightness of the building, for instance around the windows 
and through the walls. The situation changes when such a innovative material and 
technical solution as X-Lam is used; in fact, the considerable properties of the 
cross laminated panels, combined with the addition of insulation, provides the 
building with a high rate of airtightness, therefore the building is well insulated 
and protected from the external agents. On the other hand, perfect airtightness is 
not always an advantage, because it is then necessary to provide air inside the 
indoor environment to ensure sufficient internal ventilation. The X-Lam 
prefabricated panels make it possible to perfectly integrate the ventilation systems 
due to the fact the structure is really flexible; for instance holes and slots can be 
made in the structure, through the walls and ventilation shafts can be installed 
attaching them to the main frame. Therefore, different solutions for the ventilation 
have been considered proposed in the following pages. 
Basically, two solutions are most suitable for providing ventilation to the rooms 
and these can be combined with the X-Lam frame solution. Both the solutions 
have been designed to provide and extract air from the most important rooms, 
such as bathrooms and kitchens, where the highest concentration of moisture 
develops. Moreover, the two systems supply fresh air inside the rooms and take 
out the stale air. In any case, it is assumed that the air, which is inserted inside an 
apartment, for instance in hallways, will spread all over the apartment, without 
creating different air compartments. Thus, chosen solutions have been: 
1. Ceiling ventilation: intake and output hidden behind a false ceiling. 
The system has been designed with two types of canals, intake (blue) and output 
(red), which pass under the false ceiling. Respectively, the system supplies low-
flow fresh air through air vents into the wide spaces, like living rooms and

10.13 X-Lam and costs  
 
X-Lam is a sustainable and cheap alternative to steel and concrete constructions. 
The costs effectiveness about the use of X-Lam prefabricated panels is given by 
the possibility to cut the construction times and by the intrinsic properties of the 
panels, which act like insulating layers, therefore reducing costs for extra 
insulation. The alleged prejudice that the adoption of prefab wood structures is 
more expensive than a traditional building technique is not true. In fact, we 
following paragraphs prove that the use of X-Lam is perfectly competitive with 
a traditional way to build, with bricks and reinforced concrete.  
In Italy, the average price of X-Lam is even lower than traditional constructions, 
especially if the construction costs, the costs of scheduled maintenance and the 
operation related to the entire life cycle of the building are considered.  Moreover, 
the time required for the realization of a structure made of X-Lam and one made 
of bricks and reinforced concrete is very different. In fact, the choice of X-Lam 
reduces the time of construction by 40% to 50%, respect to a traditional 
construction, saving on labour costs, financial charges, business management and 
with the impressive advantages of being able to have the building ready for sale, 
lease or residence in advance respect to a traditional construction, without any 
compromises on architectural design and construction quality.

10.14.1Sustainability in building construction 
!
Nowadays environmental issues are becoming more and more mainstreams in the 
building design and the need to create greener and more sustainable buildings has 
shifted from an individual choice to something absolutely essential. In addition, 
the building sector, according to the “World Business Council for Sustainable 
Development”[43] of European commission, consumes an extremely high rate of 
the worldwide energy, estimated at being between 30-40 per cent of the overall 
energy consumption of resources and energies. Therefore, it is essential to be 
responsible and consider the building’s environmental impact over its entire life 
cycle, before designing or building any building. This reduction in the impact of 
building sector activities needs an appropriate evaluation method based on the 
principles of the Life Cycle Thinking approach, which makes it possible to 
estimate effects of different design choices from an environmental point of view.  
Thus, LCA, Life Cycle Assessment, represent the most suitable methodological 
approach to prove the whole sustainability of the building processes, construction 
and products. The LCA method is a scientific method valued by the EU, which 
confirmed it as life-cycle standardized method, born to assess environmental 
performance of buildings (ISO 14040 and 14044:1997). The aim is to define a 
standard for sustainable construction works, able to perform a whole high-quality 
evaluation concerning environmental performance and costs, related also to the 
influence that they have to the human health and comfort. [19] 
==PAGE:174==
161
10.14.2Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a standardized relative assessment method, 
which removes part of the uncertainty and it was developed during the Sixties for 
the industrial sector with the aim of evaluating the environmental impact of the 
production line. “It quantifies all relevant emissions and resources consumed and 
the related environmental and health impacts and resource depletion issues that 
are associated with any goods or services” [19]. Recently, it has been applied to 
the building sector as a support method to improve the precision of environmental 
design throughout an objective estimation of the potential impacts of a building’s 
construction and its potential savings. Thus, the aim is to assess and compare 
inputs, outputs and potential environmental impacts of products and services over 
their life cycle. This way of evaluating the sustainability of products and systems 
is increasingly growing and is well recognized as the best way to assess the 
environmental impact of a building process. This method makes it possible to 
investigate the potential environmental impact of different products, materials and 
building processes, helping them, through effective and efficient consumption and 
production, to be more sustainable. Therefore, their environmental impact is 
controlled through two fundamental principles of the industrial production: 
“Cradle to Gate” from the raw extraction of the material, through its production to 
its application and “Cradle to Grave” principle, from its use and maintenance until 
its disposal at the end of life, as shown in Figure 148. [19][35][36][37] [41]

10.18.2Glaser method and hygrometric control 
 
Glaser Method [3] is a tool used to calculate diffusion of water through the 
building. Moreover, it is used to perform an approximated analysis of vapour 
pressure in a flat structure and to calculate risk of interstitial and surface 
condensation, which worsen up the thermal conductivity performance of a 
building envelope. Condensation is generated directly by contact between cold 
surfaces and air moisture, which is transformed into water vapour. Therefore, 
condensation can be [3]: 
• Surface condensation: it occurs when the surface temperature of the 
building component is lower than the dew point temperature of the air-
water mixture with which it comes in contact; 
• Interstitial condensation: it occurs when partial vapour pressure, which 
is the pressure difference between two environments separated by the 
building component, reaches saturated vapour pressure, which is the 
pressure in function of considered surface temperature. It depends on 
thermal resistance and vapour permeability of each layer.  
Condensation is influenced by:

In addition, the variation of the content of interstitial water reduces also 
mechanical properties of the materials that become more susceptible to the 
thermal changes and they can undergo dilatations, efflorescence (winter) and salt 
crystallization (summer) on plasters, mortars and masonry. 
10.18.3Limitations of the Glaser-Method 
 
The method is a simplified method, which is not based on real situation; in fact 
several limitations of this method can be enlisted [3]. Finally, monthly climate 
data are considered and heat exchange is not taken into account. However, the 
following factors can produce an underestimation of risk of condensation and an 
overestimation of time to re-evaporation. Limitations of the method are 
summarized in Figure 155 below:  
 
 
Figure 155 – Glazer Method limitations 
==PAGE:185==
172
To prevent condensation, the water vapour content, expressed as the ratio between 
the masses of moist and dry air at a specific temperature, must be below a certain 
limit value set by the normative. The condensation, which occurs inside an 
element at the end of the heating period, must always evaporate before the 
beginning of the next heating season. 
The tendency of the vapour pressure can be found making an approximated 
analysis, conducted with the help of two different types of tools: 
• Analysis with the Glaser method; 
• Finite difference methods. 
In this case, the Glaser method was used, due to the fact that it is a simplified 
method and rather than the finite differences method, which requires a significant 
amount of climate data and thermo physical properties of the materials, that are 
currently only available in the research field. [3] [39,40] 
 
This simplified analysis [3] is done considering a steady state of heat transfer and 
vapour diffusion, combined with the use of tabulated values of design conditions, 
regarding indoor relative humidity and relative humidity. For this purpose, it is 
necessary to know the surface temperatures of the structures in contact with 
exterior and interior air and the temperature trends within the structure elements. 
Consequently, the saturation vapour pressures for every material layer within the 
structure are calculated, based on the temperatures, and then the vapour pressures 
are calculated. The results are then illustrated in trend graphs that compare the 
trends of the vapour pressures with the saturation pressures, in function of the air 
temperatures and the vapour permeability of materials.  
The formation of interstitial condensation occurs when the line that represents the 
variation of the partial pressures is tangent or crosses the line of the saturation 
pressures. Respectively, in the first case there is a risk of condensation, while in 
the second case there is certainty of condensation. The maximum quantities of 
condensation are set by the normative for the different materials, in order that the 
water vapour formed during the heating period can evaporate before the beginning 
of the next heating season.

Results of the Glazer-Method analysis'
 
This analysis clarified that the best choice for the renovation would be the 
solution with the internal insulation and the vapour barrier, CASE III. This 
solution would be effective from an energy point of view, improving the 
resistance against the condensation and meanwhile the thermal efficiency of the 
wall. In fact, the advantages of this method are: 
• No ruining of the external, historic facade, placing the insulation inside; 
• Simpler placing and mounting; 
• Great behaviour against the moisture flow; 
• Great thermal energy performance; 
• Slight reduction of living space, next to the external perimeter (< 3% max); 
• Reasonable expense. 
The energy performance is an important aspect. The Italian standards requires for 
this type of wall only a U-value of 0.27 W/m2 K, while the solution proposed for 
CASE III would reach 0.313 W/m2 K, reached with only 65 mm of insulation; 
compared to 0.877 W/m2 K of the simple wall without any type of layers. 
Anyway, this solution can be considered satisfying with regard to thermal 
improvement and reduced thickness of the added insulation layer, which will 
worth from an economical point of view and it will not reduce in any case the 
interior space. 
In conclusion, a little layer of insulation would improve more than 50% the 
exiting U-value and consequently the total energy performance of the building, 
while the internal living area will be modified less than 3% of the existing square 
meters, so the space will still be liveable and flexible to define the interior spaces, 
without taking too much living space with the new cladding. This solution should 
be adapted to the different walls that constitute the building, depending on their 
thickness. Additionally, this coverage should need to be valuated also from an 
economical point of view, understanding if the investment would worth the 
addition. Lastly, it is important to point out again that the Glazer-method is 
always on the safe side and it has some limitations and it could show worst 
scenarios than in reality. Therefore, this method is just an optimal approach to 
observe the worst-case scenario.

Conclusion 
!
The aim of this master thesis was to propose a different and innovative solution to 
renovate the upper storey of the baroque building, while maintaining the historical 
pattern. Therefore, there is nothing better than the idea to use wood to design 
addition to an existing building, because there is nothing more natural and less 
impact than wood. The Cross Laminated Timber technology was proved to be 
durable, sustainable and flexible. The X-Lam technology perfectly fits to the 
needs of the design, satisfying the architectural needs and the structural ones, 
while providing all the qualities that a building should fulfil to be considered 
liveable. Moreover, the concept of prefabrication was made part of this project 
with the aim to create a structure, which could be easy and fast assembled with 
short set-up time; additionally, the prefab idea allowed to have a clearly defined 
and simple scheme structure, based on the structural setting of the existing 
building underneath. Thus, the structure was proved to be lighter than concrete or 
brick and more energy efficient, being able to create a healthy indoor climate. In 
addition, wood is an excellent reusable material and it can be recycled repeatedly.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:198==
185!
The environmental impact was reduced as much as possible and the process was 
made sustainable and eco-friendly construction method with positive CO2 
balance, thanks to the fact that the top-addition can be considered as a volume 
which has a low impact to be produced and it can be dismantled and recycled in 
the future if it will not be needed anymore and at the same time the durability of 
the wood is considered to be equal to the one of traditional building materials. 
 
The idea of using wood as a construction material is still underdeveloped in Italy 
and most of the building companies seem to be reluctant to this innovation and 
look askance the change. This solution could rise up discussion and doubts about 
the reliability and feasibility of such a project; in fact wood is allegedly, but 
wrongly, considered as a weak material for several reasons, as fire safety and 
endurance. On the contrary, this project proved that this solution is simpler, faster 
and safer than a traditional construction method because it is made only of bolted 
element, which are first connected with steel plates and anchorages, then sealed 
with special sheaths and finally covered with insulation and external and internal 
finishing. Moreover, this constructive system guaranties the structural stability 
and strength the building needs, also providing excellent fire safety characteristics 
and earthquake-proof capability. The speed of assembly guarantees a cost-
effective investment and consequently a reduction of the construction time by 
40% to 50%, becoming competitive with the traditional building techniques. 
Designers collaborates directly with the producer and they set exact costs at the 
beginning, which will be kept unchanged until the structure is mounted and ready 
to be handed over to the owner.  
Despite all the positive aspects of the construction method described in this thesis, 
some further analysis and investigation could be carried out. For instance, the 
architectural project could be revised, reduced or changed depending on the 
designers and clients needs; meanwhile, the structural calculation can be revised 
and integrated in a more complex dynamic analysis to try to clarify how the 
building will react against seismic actions, wind and suction. Furthermore, a 
detailed energy analysis could be run to demonstrate the energy efficiency and the 
sustainability of the new top-addition. Finally, a revision of the costs and the 
timetable could be done directly with the help of the producer to find out more 
cost-efficient solutions.

Appendix A - Historic study on urban evolution of Turin 
 
To better understand the background of the city’s evolution and the historic period 
of the case building, Lagrange12, it is important to describe the development of 
the city through the centuries and the expansion and the changes over two 
thousand years. The names of the buildings and squares, mentioned in the 
following pages, refer mostly to the general Urban Plan shown on page X. 
Ancient city - Historical period: 28 a.C – 476 d.C. 
 
The city of Turin has always been a city under strong development. In ancient 
times, the Romans founded the city around 28 B.C. and they called it Augusta 
Taurinorum, due to the fact that it was founded at the hands of the emperor 
Augusto. It was born as a military camp for the army and even nowadays it is 
possible to indentify the grid matrix of streets and squares that characterised the 
old Roman encampments. The old planimetry of the military garrison town was a 
rectangular plan of about 760m per 680m, almost perfectly square. The Figure 
below shows the old camp and its position respect to Piazza Castello.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure A1 – Bird’s eye view drawing by F. Corni [47] 
==PAGE:256==
194!
Medieval city - Historical period: 476 d.C. - 1500 
 
After the fall of the Roman Empire in the 476 d.C., the city continued to expand 
slowly throughout the Middle Age until the 1500s, under the French domain, as a 
countship of the French descent of Savoy family. Basically, during the fourteenth 
and fifteenth centuries, the city reorganized itself into different quarters, partly 
losing the geometric regularity of the Roman plan and it distributed better the 
urban spaces better, also adding a fortification around it. These maps also show a 
little amphitheatre that stood next to one of the main old gates of the city Porta 
Nuova; it was later demolished and the site is now occupied by Piazza San Carlo. 
[47]

Modern city - Historical period: 1500 - 1600 
 
Even before returning to the possession of the city, in 1561, the Duke Emanuele 
Filiberto of Savoy took two ‘revolutionary’ decisions decided to transfer the 
capital of his state from Chambery, in Savoy, France to Turin and he ordered that 
the entire official acts of the duchy should be written in Italian. Both these 
decisions were descended from the military, political and social vulnerability of 
the capital, being too close to the kingdom of France. Emanuele Filiberto realized 
that the only hope for expansion of its domains was towards Italy. As soon as the 
Duke obtained the city of Turin, he decided to make it more defensible; therefore 
a pentagonal citadel located in the west corner of the city was realized to better  
protect the most vulnerable part of the city. The construction started in 1563 and it 
ended in 1577. The choice of Emanuele Filiberto to move the Court from 
Chambery to Turin in 1583 positively and considerably influenced the future of 
the city. This shift was made after the treaty of Chateau-Cambresis that gave back 
the possession of his dominions, and at the end of long war between France and 
Spain. This choice fostered the beginning of the long history of the Savoy capital. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure A4 – Urban plan of the fortified town 
==PAGE:258==

Modern city - Historical period: Fist Expansion (1620 – 1673)  
 
Carlo Emanuele I, the son of Emanuele Filiberto, lanched the first urban 
transformation starting with the well-known master plan of high-level 
construction in the region, called Theatrum Sabaudie. Under his reign, the first 
expansion of the city was carried out, to the south, with the construction of Via 
Roma, leading from Piazza Castello to Porta Nuova and the birth of the current 
Piazza San Carlo. The period of peace, between 1601 and 1613, allowed Carlo 
Emanuele to transform the heart of the city that became the place of absolutist-
dynastic power, with landscaping of the Royal Palace grounds. In 1620 Carlo 
Emanuele I began to work on the south expansion of the city, called Città Nuova,  
New City. The project presented by Carlo di Castellamonte, royal architect, was 
chosen. It included the development of the modern city around a large central 
square, Piazza Reale, currently Piazza San Carlo, with the role of market, 
surrounded by porches to accommodate workshops and with the streets that kept 
the chessboard structure of road network. Even the defensive wall underwent 
some changes related mainly to the displacement of the gates: the expansion of 
the city involved the demolition of the gate called Marmorea, which was replaced 
by the Porta Nuova at the end of the homonymous street Via Nuova, nowadays 
called Via Roma. The ancient defensive walls and the old gates to the city were 
moved and changed due to the expansion of the city. [47]  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure A5 – Urban plan of the expanded and fortified town 
==PAGE:259==

Modern city - Historical period: Second Expansion (1673 – 1700) 
 
On October 23, 1673 Duke Carlo Emanuele II, son of the duke Carlo Emanuele I, 
started the second expansion of the city in south-east direction, which this time 
included an expansion within the walls of Turin area towards the River Po; not 
surprisingly, the main artery of the new section, called Contrada di Po, was 
baptized, precisely, Via Po. This new arterial road was uncommon, due to the fact 
it ran in diagonal, instead of respecting the orthogonal scheme of the city. The 
boundaries of the new area ran from Piazza Castello, the Royal Square and 
Palazzo Madama and Palazzo Reale, the royal palaces up to the future Piazza 
Vittorio Veneto, which was dedicated to Carlo Emanuele II. The eastern 
expansion was designed and supervised by Amedeo di Castellamonte, son of 
Carlo, the ducal architect. He adhered to the principle of uniformity of the facades 
that had already characterized the southern expansion. 
The boundaries of this new area ran from Via Accademia delle Scienze to Via San 
Francesco da Paola, and then touched the current Piazza Cavour, Via Maria 
Vittoria, Piazza Vittorio Veneto, finally joining with the Giardini Reali, Royal 
Gardens.  
 
 
Figure A6 – Urban plan of the expanded and fortified town 
==PAGE:260==
198!
 
Modern city - Historical period: Third Expansion (1700 – 1770) 
 
After the siege of the French in 1706, which lasted 117 days, the city experienced 
a period of economic and demographic expansion. In 1719 Turin had 56,000 
inhabitants, but the population was expected to increase due to the historic and 
political environment. Vittorio Amedeo II, became, after the Treaty of Utrecht, 
King of Sicily, a title which was then modified, in accordance with the Treaty of 
London of 1718, to King of the Kingdom of Sardinia. The Architect Filippo 
Juvarra supervised fior the project of the new western extension of the city, also 
including an intense fortification of the area. The artery of the third extension was 
Via del Carmine; also in this case the centre of enlargement is a square, what 
today is called Piazza Savoia, intersection with Via della Consolata. This district 
was planned for a military use, and then it was transformed into a new residential 
and commercial district after the end of the war, stretching along Via Garibald, 
which was renovated, overhauled and enlarged to host new commercial activities 
and a more lively quarter. 
 
Figure A7 – Urban plan of the expanded and fortified town 
 
 
==PAGE:261==
199!
 
Contemporary city - Historical period: 1800-Nowadays 
 
The first decade of the XIX century is characterized mainly by the French regime 
that presided over the city after a war with the Savoy. Therefore, Turin became 
part of the French State. During the French domination, the city was reorganized 
and fortified on the northern side. After this period, the Savoy again took the 
control of the city and realized a plan of expansion to the south, along the river 
Po, realizing the current Piazza Vittorio (1840) and Corso Vittorio and the 
quarters around it. Finally, Turin overcame harsh times through the two World 
Wars, always expanding its confines and enlarging its boundaries in a regular and 
consistent way, to become the city as we know it nowadays. 
Below, the urban evolution of Turin from 1865 until 1970 is show. Each map 
shows two different stages the grow: 
 
            1865-1901                           1901-1921                             1921-1971 
Figure A8 – Urban expansion of the city of Turin the XIX and XX centuries 
 
During the last twenty years a major urban project of requalification of the urban 
areas close to the city centre has been undertaken. Two districts represent the most 
important requalification plan: Borgo San Salvario and Porta Palazzo, 
respectively the southern quarter and the northern quarter, with respect to the city 
centre. These represent the two main multi-ethnic districts of the city, at opposite 
sides of the centre of Turin. The two districts have a strong multicultural identity, 
rooted in the early waves of migration affecting Turin in the twentieth century and 
that exploded in the nineties of the last century, with the establishment of new 
==PAGE:262==
200!
communities from different parts of the world. The aim was to solve the urban, 
social and commercial decay, by improving the condition of life and work of the 
two districts. 
 
Figure A9 – City plan with urban districts 
The improvement of these neighbouring districts strongly influenced a lot the 
requalification of the city centre, which was no longer just the old part of the city, 
the haunt of bank clerks and shoppers, but also a lively quarter. Many old and 
central buildings have been renovated and new parking lots have been 
constructed. This has encouraged the return of residents to the city centre. The 
urban plan included also the creation of pedestrian areas and cycling lanes, which 
helped to create a more lively and liveable district. 
In recent years, Via Lagrange was the subject of a careful renovation that has 
affected the area surrounding the street and its buildings, with the aim of 
promoting its inclusion within the system of pedestrian city. Meanwhile, Via 
Lagrange became increasingly prestigious, as a shopping precinct and as an 
elegant address for the new dwellers. In conclusion, “Lagrange12” fits perfectly 
into this social and environmental movement of requalification of the street, 
reinventing itself as one of the main arteries of the old city centre, proposing high-
class shops, food stores and apartments.
