Abstract
The popularity of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles has experienced a solid increase
in the past decade. With the gained popularity it is starting to ’¼ünd its place as
an integral part of our every-day lives. In recent years suggestions and commer-
cial products deploy Unmanned Aerial Vehicles in, increasingly, mission critical
tasks. Emergency response, ’¼üre watch, or acting as a delivery service for critical
goods in remote areas. The Unmanned Aerial Vehicle is capable of complet-
ing tasks deemed too trivial, or too dangerous for humans.
As it always is
with cyber security, increases in certain assets will see the incentive for attack
that asset increase. This dares us ask the question; how is Unmanned Aerial
Vehicles geared to handle common security threats? In this project we will anal-
yse and discuss the security of low-capacity devices. We will propose solutions
for implementing a secure and authenticated drone communication protocol for
two market segments: recreational and commercial usage. We identify a light-
weight marshalling library, MAVLink, which we will extend with e’¼ācient cryp-
tographic primitives. In the end, we conclude that implementing a secure and
authenticated drone communication protocol is possible, and that by tweaking
the security parameters we can ’¼ünd suitable solutions for both commercial and
recreational usage.

The history of drones, or Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), is hardly an exclusively
positive one. In fact, for years UAVs were synonymous with the anonymous killer
occupying the sky in con’¼éict driven areas. While recent years have seen little e’¼Ćort
made towards limiting its presence in military applications, UAVs the past decade
has seen UAVs receive an increased amount of interest. The commercial and private
market is the main reason for this. Today, the UAV-concept is a billion dollar industry:
commercial usage of UAVs has an estimated market value of $2 billion ŌĆō expected
to reach $10 billion spanning the next 10 years [1] ŌĆō and consumer UAVs shipments
are expected to grow from 15.8 million units in 2017 to 23.8 millions in 2018 [2].

concepts and real-life applications [5].

1.1
The Security of Unmanned Aerial Systems
The Unmanned Aerial Vehicles recent surge in popularity is not insigni’¼ücant: demand
is skyrocketing, bringing many new players and investors with it. The technology has
been improving in recent years as a result. The combination of popularity and techno-
logical improvements has seen UAVs introduced to human every-day life, performing
tasks previously handled by humans. This development is positive in a number of
ways: it may be able to deliver (critical) goods to remote areas, or perform emergency
response faster than its human counter part. The bene’¼üts are rather obvious. It can,
however, pose a number of security threats. Crashes and failures are serious buz-
zwords when associated with the transport of life-essential goods, or travels through
crowded areas at high velocities. The con’¼üdence we place in UAVs to successfully
complete critical tasks boils down to the quality of their implementation, and their
ability to handle malicious third-parties. The security of Unmanned Aircraft Systems
is critical to entrust them with essential tasks. The questions is, how well are UAVs
currently equipped to handle common security threats?
Examples already exist illustrating the simplicity of executing attacks on di’¼Ćerent
Unmanned Aircraft Systems. In 2015 a team published an attack targeting the seed
used to plan the frequency hopping in a UAS [6]. This were only made possible by the
fact that the underlying radio link relied on no security layer. Learning the planned
frequencies allowed them to seize complete control of the Unmanned Aerial Vehicle.
Projects like SkyJack [7], developed by Samy Kamkar, and Virus-copter [8] both
provide open-source automated solutions for hijacking UAVs from speci’¼üc vendors.

Unfortunately these solutions seems a poor ’¼üt to Unmanned Aircraft Systems: Wi-

While large companies may be able to a’¼Ćord it, it is often not feasible in smaller

1.2
Related Work
The issue of lacking con’¼üdentiality and/or authenticity in Unmanned Aircraft System
applications is well-known, and proposals of how to achieve it already exist. Bertino
et al. proposed a "Certi’¼ücateless Signcryption Tag Key Encapsulation Mechanism",
which supports authenticated key agreement, non-repudiation, and user revocation
in one-to-one (and later many-to-many in a follow-up publication) communication
scenarios using Certi’¼ücateless Public Key Cryptography (CL-PKC) [9, 10]. Butcher
et al. proposed adding RC5-encryption in ECB mode to the Micro Air Vehicle Com-
munication Protocol (MAVLink), performed by the radio transceivers (i.e. encrypting
already generated MAVLink-messages). The goal was to obtain con’¼üdential MAVLink
communication ŌĆō which they achieved with some success [11]. The messages were en-
crypted, but with a limited, and often repeating, message pool, ECB made it easy to
distinguish messages. The encryption mode also resulted in a system highly suscep-
tible to replay-attacks.
Meier et al. (the creators of MAVLink) suggested adding encryption and authenti-
cation to MAVLink. They suggested a broadcasted secret master key exchange setup.
To acheive their goals they extended the library with AES-GCM Authenticated En-
cryption (AE) [12]. They suggested for the key negotiation to take place through a
wired UART communication link. Their proposal su’¼Ćered from many weaknesses and
’¼éaws, which we will use as a point of reference later in this documentation.

1.3
Project Description
The need for con’¼üdentiality and authenticity in Unmanned Aircraft Systems is ar-
guably there. In order for us to trust UASs to perform the tasks and ideas considered
up until this section, we need to ensure that they are "cryptographically" up to the
task. The goal of this project is, thus, to design and implement a protocol for estab-
lishing a secure and authenticated channel in UASs. The Unmanned Aerial Vehicles
in these systems exists in many shapes and sizes: some are in possession of compu-
tationally powerful CPUs, and others may struggle with its limited resources. The
proposed CL-PKC solution (Bertino et al.) is, while e’¼ācient, meant for, at least,
medium-capacity drones running on 32/64-bit processors. The aim of this project
is to investigate and ’¼ünd solutions suitable for low-capacity UAVs running on 32-bit
MCUs. The project will follow the Dolev-Yao model. This model assumes an attacker
with complete control of the communication medium, allowing her to intercept, drop,
manipulate, and forge messages. The attacker is only limited by the constraints of
the utilised cryptographic measures. The overall scope of (approach to) this project
is to
ŌĆó ’¼ünd suitable solutions for authenticating and encrypting drone communication;
ŌĆó consider ways to securely exchange cryptographic keys;
ŌĆó design and implement possible solutions; and
ŌĆó test, evaluate, and compare these in a suitable environment.
The communication between a drone and a GCS is entirely up to the protocol
implemented by the vendor. Some may implement their own set of instructions and
responses, and others may use more generic protocols. Investigating drone commu-
nication protocols that already exist may allow us to choose one, and apply our
solution to it. The project will focus on UAVs made for private, and, to some extend,
commercial usage. The security of military grade UAVs is disregarded completely.
The security of such a system is already extensively researched; its ’¼ürmware is rarely
open-source, and the resources of the hardware is hardly what you would classify
as low-capacity. The scope of this project will solely evolve around protocol secu-
rity. Vulnerabilities in the software, physical attacks, and exploitation of individual
sensors (e.g. GPS-spoo’¼üng) wonŌĆÖt a’¼Ćect our considerations in any sense. Based on
our current knowledge and speci’¼üed scope we are ready to compile a list of initial
requirements. The protocol implemented should
ŌĆó provide a secure and authenticated communication channel;
ŌĆó allow methods for key negotiation;
ŌĆó be build on top of an existing drone communication protocol;
ŌĆó be easy to use for users with little, to no, experience in cryptography; and
ŌĆó work securely/e’¼āciently on UAVs with limited resources.

Implementing security is much more than just throwing cryptographic primitives at

the entire communication link. Knowing just one single key would allow the adversary

like this fails to deliver forward secrecy. The adversary only has to recover one key

achieve forward secrecy in our system, we have to ensure that a recovered key only
breaks recovered keys session. Ultimately, each key exchange needs to be secured by
more than just encryption. Public-key Cryptography has a quality like that. If the
exchanged keys were public keys, learning them would not compromise the session
wherein they were exchanged. The following section will explore the PKC-based key
exchange methods of Di’¼āe-Hellman (DH) and ECDH, and discuss how they might

3.2.2
Mitigating Padding Oracle Attacks in CBC
The previous Cryptography subsection brie’¼éy discussed the correlation between the
padding of CBC and padding oracle attacks. While this type of attack is made pos-
sible by the padding requirement of CBC, it really is an implementation issue. The
adversary facilitating the attack is completely reliant on some part of a system to act
as an oracle. This can be the server. In the attack, the adversary will, initially, ma-
nipulate the last byte of the second-to-last cipher block. The XOR operations of CBC
will see this manipulation a’¼Ćect the last byte of the ’¼ünal plaintext block. The adver-
sary will provide the modi’¼üed ciphertext to the oracle-server, and use the response
from the server to determine the next step. The server will perform a decryption of
the modi’¼üed ciphertext, and check if the last plaintext block is correctly padded. If
not, it will respond with an error, correct padding wonŌĆÖt yield a response. In the
’¼ürst case the adversary will guess a new value for that byte and try again, the latter
makes the adversary move on to the next byte. A successful attack would allow the
adversary to coax the server into decrypting the entire message. This is what is know
as a chosen-ciphertext-attack.
The Horton Principle: Authenticate what is meant, not what is said.
There are three possible combinations of encryption and authentication, whereas
we will consider two of them. The Horton principle suggest an Authenticate-then-
Encrypt approach. In this, the plaintext is authenticated, and the plaintext+tag is
then encrypted. This could be vulnerable to padding oracle attacks; there is no way to
authenticate the ciphertext, and detect changes. If we authenticate the ciphertext the
server would immediately detect the ciphertext manipulation. The second possible
approach, Encrypt-then-Authenticate, thus mitigate chosen-ciphertext attacks
like the padding oracle attack. The Encrypt-then-Authenticate approach has
an additional e’¼āciency bene’¼üt. In our system, the authentication, from Chaskey, is
by far the most e’¼ācient operation. In the Encrypt-then-Authenticate approach
we get to authenticate the ciphertext, before performing a computationally expensive
decryption of a, potentially, corrupted/modi’¼üed ciphertext. In a low-capacity setting
like ours, this could save our UAV from wasting already scarce resources. We thus
formally select the Encrypt-then-Authenticate approach for all later proposals.

4.1
Transport Layer Protocols
Up until now we have been operating on the top-most layer of the OSI-reference
model ŌĆō the application layer. This has been su’¼ācient for proposing solutions based
on achieving di’¼Ćerent goals, while assuming that the layers below handles the en-
capsulation correctly. The issue we face now is that the "MAVLink communication
session" based solutions actually rely on a very strict set of protocol requirements. If
these requirements are not met by the underlying transport layer, or us, the solution
wonŌĆÖt work. The following subsections will explore the root of these requirements.
Finally, we will analyse how the MAVLink v2.0 CTR solution is potentially improved
by choosing a second protocol option for the transport layer.
4.1.1
Communication Sessions with TCP
The session-based solutions will break without reliable and sequential delivery. With-
out it we can encounter two scenarios. Messages could arrive out of order or with a
substantial delay ŌĆō which is commonly known. If the transport protocol fails to ensure
ordered sequential delivery, it would have to be the job of the application protocol.
That could result in a signi’¼ücant growth of the MAVLink-devices workload. Remem-
ber, the ciphertext of a MAVLink-message, c = (ci, ci+1, . . . , cn), is decrypted using
the last block of the previous ciphertext, ciŌłÆ1, as the IV. The MAVLink-library does
little to marshal the sequencing by default. Ensuring that messages are ordered is a
task we will have to place on the MAVLink-device implementation. This will force
the device to handle a large bu’¼Ćer that stores numerous messages, and potentially
stall until a missing link arrives. This assumes that the missing link actually arrives
at all.
Waiting for a message is ine’¼ācient and computationally demanding, but if a mes-
sage fails to arrive at all, it will break the communication link. We wonŌĆÖt suggest how
this could be solved manually. Doing so would require a rewrite of the MAVLink-
library, which would con’¼éict with our goals. Leaving this for the individual UAS
developer teams is not an option. Placing such a burden on the developing team
is not the greatest invitation to using our library extension. In that last case we
might as well not propose the solution at all. Finally, it would be super’¼éuous to

MAVLink-library. The best we can do is to clearly state the requirement ŌĆō and hope

Final Statements
From following the ’¼éow as described in the introduction, we now arrive at the ’¼ünal

portant issues, notes, and solutions gathered from the previous sections. It will do

6.1
Discussion
The outcome of the work we have done so far has yielded three viable proposals.
Before we assess them based on the requirements we want to exclude the MAVLink
v2.0 CBC proposal. While it is a working solution, it simply does not bring anything
unique to the table. We wanted two distinct solutions, one for commercial usage, and
one for recreational usage. The excluded solution does not suit these segments better
than any of the other two solutions. This leaves us with the solutions:
1. MAVLink v1.0 CBC
2. MAVLink v2.0 CTR
The CBC solution proposed a special IV session which gained the advantages of
using CBC with a random 128-bit IV. This however, made it unsuitable for anything
other than TCP communication. This makes it un’¼üt for real-time piloting (or manual
control) which subsequently makes it a poor ’¼üt for recreational use. The strengths of
this model, is the security. The longer tags makes the authentication more resilient
against collision attacks. The nature of the TCP is really only a hindrance during
manual control. UDP, however, is really unsuitable for mission, and waypoint based
navigation. These implements a communication ’¼éow, where the GCS only sends out
a few commands, each of which are important. Waypoint navigation is common in
commercial systems like delivery services. The increased security of MAVLink v1.0
CBC and the TCP message reliability makes it the perfect match for the commercial
segment.
The MAVLink v2.0 CTR provides something di’¼Ćerent. It provides a minimal com-
munication overhead, a full payload, and a message stream that is ’¼üts free-piloting.
We discussed how lowering security parameters could be justi’¼üed, if it helped limiting
restrictions on the underlying systems operability. Where the other proposal ’¼üts the
commercial segment, this proposal ’¼üts the recreational use. The only small concern
we had, was the initially 17 byte larger header of the underlying MAVLink v2.0 li-
brary. We put this concern to rest later, when we discovered how the combination of
TCP-overhead, padding and larger tags would see our MAVLink v1.0 proposal grow
to a larger average size.

Future Work
Throughout the report we had to make assumptions or disregard some aspects that

secure and authenticated channel for UAV communication for low-capacity devices.

market segments. The number of UAVs is estimated to keep following current trends.
As we see those UAVs continue to take on more tasks we might see future start-ups
joining the race. We are con’¼üdent that something like our solutions could provide a
solid base to those startups.
The solutions we propose requires little to no knowledge in cryptography. We are
con’¼üdent that startups that might not have an entire security team at their disposal,
would be capable of using our security extension with only a little introduction to
basic concepts. We are also con’¼üdent in its potential use on the recreational market,
where users either access the system through a general GCS, or where developers
build their own system.

Motivation
The surge in popularity of UAVs is staggering, and the technology itself is ever
improving.
The commercial, and private, usage of UAVs sees an increasing
number of them perform critical tasks in crowded areas. The bene’¼üts associ-
ated with such a development is rather obvious. This development can, however,
also pose a number of security threats. Crashes and failures are serious buz-

The con’¼üdence we place in UAVs to successfully and safely complete critical

Unmanned Aerial Vehicles are often controlled remotely, using a Ground
Control Station (GCS), or autonomously by pre-planned ’¼éight missions. The
communication between GCSs to UAVs are wireless, and often achieved through
a WIFI, Bluetooth, or Telemetry link. A default wireless link implements no
inherent security layer, leaving that task to the programmers planning to utilize
the link. If no layer is implemented it leaves the communication link vulnerable
to an array of attacks (such as eavesdropping, denial of service, manipulation)
leaving the service vulnerable to all concerns mentioned in the paragraph above.
The issues surrounding secure communication links is a highly researched topic,
with a vast number of available solutions.
The hardware that powers your
product is often the deciding factor when opting for a speci’¼üc solution. To UAVs
the hardware is extremely vendor speci’¼üc; some may run on 64-bit processors,
and others 32-bit micro-controllers. This could seriously limit the number of
viable solutions for obtaining a secure communication and authenticated channel
between GCSs and UAVs.
Examples already exist clearly showing the ease of performing di’¼Ćerent at-
tacks on UAVs. In 2015 a team published an attack targeting the seed used
to plan the frequency hopping in a radio link [6]. With the underlying proto-
col not relying on any other layer of security, this would allow them to cease
full access of the device. Projects like SkyJack [7], developed by Samy Kamkar,
and Virus-copter [8] both provide open-source automated solutions for hijacking
UAVs from speci’¼üc vendors.

The scope of this thesis is to design and propose a solution for achieving a secure
and authenticated communication link between a drone and its control station
through cryptographic measures. We aim to achieve this by
ŌĆó investigating current solutions;
ŌĆó analysing di’¼Ćerent drone communication protocols; and
ŌĆó designing a solution based on the ’¼ündings.
The project will focus on UAVs made for commercial or private use. Military

an obvious concern in such devices we disregard them due to the facts that: itŌĆÖs
already a highly research area, the solutions and the ’¼ürmware on the drones are
rarely open source, and the hardware most likely provide computational power

o’¼Ćered to the two other branches of UAVs. The investigation may build on:
