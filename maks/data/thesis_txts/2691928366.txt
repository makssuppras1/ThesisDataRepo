Summary
When robots become a ubiquitous part of modern life, it is essential for diﬀerent
robot types to work together, in ad-hoc collaboration. This requires Theory of
Mind, the ability to attribute plans and intentions to other agents. We design
such a system Multi-Agent Collaboration (MAC), capable of complex ad-hoc
collaboration and heavily compare to an existing system Bayesian Delegation
(BD). MAC is designed around the concept of a collaboration which assigns
sets of agents to sets of tasks, allowing for complex collaboration. We introduce
a notion of a handoﬀagent which allows us to generate and reason about a
diverse set of plans. Unlike traditional goal recognition which recognises the
most probable plan, we ﬁnd best plans, and use goal recognition to verify that
it is probable, that other agents will collaborate on said best plans. We fur-
thermore introduce a probability that an agent is idle, which is based on the
lack of meaningful progress on other tasks. The system is tested on a cook-
ing inspired environment with hierarchical tasks as well as documenting certain
complex collaboration which the system has shown to be capable of.

Chapter 1
Introduction
As robots become a ubiquitous part of modern life, it is no longer enough that
robots are capable of navigating an environment and solving tasks alone, they
must also be able to collaborate. Not only must they collaborate with other
robots, they must also collaborate with humans. This adds signiﬁcant complex-
ity to the tasks to be completed, especially when the robots do not know what
agents they will need to collaborate with a priori. It requires signiﬁcant prior
coordination between robotic agents to facilitate communication of plans at all,
and it is very slow to communicate plans between robots and humans. Therefore
we investigate scenarios in which the agents must be able to implicitly coordi-
nate plans, not allowing any explicit communication. In order to achieve this,
the agents need Theory of Mind, the ability to attribute plans and intentions to
other agents [PW78].
More precisely, the agents must deploy and recognise strategies for pursuing
goals; divide-and-conquer where each agent is pursuing their own goal, single
task collaboration where multiple agents are collaborating on one task, and
multi task collaboration where multiple agents are concurrently collaborating
on multiple tasks.
We investigate an environment with hierarchical tasks which are not spatio-
temporally distributed. Thereby the agents must coordinate and collaborate on
both levels; what tasks are being pursued, and that low level navigational actions
==PAGE:10==
2

do not conﬂict. More concretely we use a cooking inspired environment with
partially ordered hierarchical tasks in which agents must pick up and combine
ingredients to form dishes [WWE+21]. Through out the thesis we will heavily
compare to the Bayesian Delegation (BD) system [WWE+21] which is capable
of divide-and-conquer and single task collaboration. In this thesis we develop
a competing system, Multi-Agent Collaboration (MAC) based on collaboration
on multiple rather than single tasks, and online goal mirroring. We manage
to achieve a system capable of complex collaboration, including the mentioned
multi task collaboration, which improves the performance of a number of other
agents in ad-hoc collaboration compared to the agents in self-play. Further-
more MAC achieves the best self-play score, and is the only agent of the ones
tested, capable of solving all the proposed tasks in the presence of an idle agent.
Thereby showing a more robust recognition of the other agent’s actual goal.
The system is tested on a concrete environment, but is designed for general
multi-agent environments with a similar task structure, with the exception of
the search engine. Assumptions on the environment will be stated as needed,
and alternatives to the domain speciﬁc aspects of the system will be discussed.
The main contributions of this thesis are a notion of idle probability for agents
(the probability that an agent is not pursuing any tasks), the ability for multiple
agents to collaborate on multiple tasks concurrently, and the composition of the
overall system.
The thesis is structured as follows. In Chapter 1 we introduce the environment
used as a running example, and for benchmarks.
In Chapter 2 we give an
introduction to the general ﬁeld of activity, goal and plan recognition, making
our way to the challenges of multi-agent plan recognition and the epistemics
of agents inferring each others plans. We then provide a short description of
BD in Chapter 3 as well as a discussion on the advantages and disadvantages
of the approach and how they compare to MAC. In Chapter 4 we describe the
developed system, MAC, in plain text, and provid pseudo-code for the most
important parts of the system in appendix. We then discuss the central design
choices and compare them to BD. In Chapter 5 we provide benchmarks for the
system in self-play, and ad-hoc play with the systems used in [WWE+21], as well
as the mentioned Still agent and a restricted variant of MAC. In Chapter 6 we
discuss some interesting areas of future work. Finally we conclude in Chapter
7.

1.1
Environment
We now summarize the main dynamics of the environment as described in
[WWE+21] (see Section A.1 for full details), as well as introduce conventions
which will be used to visualise and describe the environment through out the
thesis. The environment is inspired by cooking tasks, as has been done in a
number of works ([CSH+19], [SWL+19], [WWE+21]), but the one used here is
the exact one used in [WWE+21].
An instance of the environment speciﬁes an initial state, a goal state, and a
number of partially ordered hierarchical sub-tasks with deﬁned preconditions
and postconditions. Each sub-task has two items as precondition and one item
as the post condition, with the intuition that the two items are combined into
one. One or both of the precondition items may be movable. The environment
contains two types of unmovable objects, chopping stations and delivery sta-
tions, as well as three movable base items, lettuce, tomato and plate, and their
combinations. See Figures 1.1a and 1.1b for the full list of items and sub-tasks
(recipes) respectively. All goal states specify a number of items which must be
placed on a delivery station.
The agents can move in the four cardinal directions and cannot occupy the same
cells. The individual actions of the agents for a time step are executed one at a
time, and must be valid both at the beginning of the step, and when executed.
Items can be picked up, placed down and combined only by interacting with a
counter. The environment is fully observable and deterministic.

Chapter 2
Theory
Decentralised multi-agent systems have a distinct program controlling each
agent, where either all agents are of the same type, labeled self-play for that
agent type, or the agents may be diﬀerent types, labeled ad-hoc play. Tradi-
tionally decentralised multi-agent systems have focused on either cooperative
self-play in which multiple agents of the same type are collaborating towards a
joint goal, or zero sum games (ad-hoc or self-play) where the agents are actively
working against each other.
In the cooperative self-play scenario one agent may calculate a plan and com-
municate it to the rest of the agents, or they may pre-calculate a policy which
exploit that they are all running the same program. However this approach does
not work in ad-hoc play where the agents do not know what program the other
agents are running and therefore whether communication is possible at all. The
agents therefore need a way to implicitly coordinate with each other.
In zero sum games an agent will try to minimise the opponent’s score, while
trying to maximize her own, i.e. the classical min-max search. It is a reasonable
assumption that the opponent will be playing optimally (or as optimal as pos-
sible), and the agent therefore need to minimise the worst case score. Thereby
any deviation of the opponent’s behaviour from the expected will only increase
the ﬁnal score, since the agent expected the worst case. As argued by [CSH+19]
this is inherently diﬀerent from the cooperative case, as any deviation from the

plate, and then go back and chop the lettuce (Figure 2.1c). Therefore choosing

the given options, even in cooperative play.

2.1 Goal and Plan Recognition

2.1
Goal and Plan Recognition
Goal and plan recognition have been used interchangeably in the literature, and
there is not a consensus on exact deﬁnitions [MKG21]. We will use the terms
interchangeably and provide a common deﬁnition from the literature relevant
to this thesis (e.g. [RG09]). We ﬁrst deﬁne a Strips planning problem.
Definition 2.1 A Strips planning problem is a tuple P = (F, I, A, G) where
F is a set of ﬂuents, I ⊆F is the initial state, A is a set of actions a with pre-
conditions, add, and delete lists Pre(a), Add(a), and Del(a) which are subsets
of F, and G ⊆F is the goal state.
We can then deﬁne a Strips planning domain as P = (F, I, A) such that we can
extend the domain with a goal G and get a strips planning problem P[G] =
(F, I, A, G). It will be made clear from context when P refers to a planning
domain and a planning problem. We can then deﬁne a plan recognition problem.
Definition 2.2 A plan recognition problem is a triple T = (P, G, O) where
P = (F, I, A) is a planning domain, G is a set of possible goals G, and O is a
sequence of observations o1, . . . , om where each oi is an action in A.
Early plan recognition eﬀorts focused on matching observed actions to prede-
ﬁned plans in a plan library (e.g. the generalised plan recognition framework in
[KA86]). Some of these approaches use handcrafted plan libraries which have
the advantage of being able to contain human, domain speciﬁc knowledge as the
designer can choose exactly what plans to include. However these plan libraries
do require a lot of time to create, and must be created for every domain. They
furthermore limit the plan recognition to plans which the designer has speciﬁed.
Other approaches have focused on automatically generating the plan libraries
as all possible plans for the given planning problem. This means dealing with a
large number of possible plans to which end optimisations have been proposed
(e.g. lazy commitment [AZK05].) In this project we investigate a Euclidean
environment where a large part of the tasks contain navigation which can be
achieved by a large number of unique paths, making an explicit plan library
prohibitively large.
As a way to circumvent the need for explicit plan libraries, Plan Recognition as
Planning (PRaP) was proposed by [RG09] which compiles the plan recognition
problem into a Strips planning problem, and uses oﬀthe shelf planners to solve
these.

2.2
Plan Recognition as Planning (PRaP)
Ramirez and Geﬀner realised that goal recognition is planning in reverse. Plan-
ning seeks a plan to achieve a goal, and goal recognition seeks a goal to explain
observed actions. Therefore it is natural that there is a link between the two.
We begin by giving an informal description of the PRaP process to provide an
intuition.
See Figure 2.2 for a follow along example goal. Consider that we are given an
initial state, a sequence of observations (green line), and a set of possible goals.
For each goal (e.g. delivery station (*)), we ﬁnd two optimal paths from the
initial state to the goal, one which satisﬁes the observations (red line, length 6),
and one which does not (blue line, length 4). The score of the goal is then the
length diﬀerence between the two paths(6 −4 = 2). We then simply pick the
goal with the lowest score as the most likely.

More formally, given a plan recognition problem T we need for each goal G ∈G
to perform two searches to ﬁnd two action sequences which reach G, π which
satisﬁes the observation sequence O, and ¯π which does not satisfy O.
Definition 2.3 An action sequence π = a1, . . . , an satisﬁes the observation
sequence O = o1, . . . , om if we have ai = oi for i = 1, . . . , m. We denote an
action sequence which does not satisfy the observation sequence O as ¯π
Note that the deﬁnition given in [RG09] is more general, allowing for missing
observations, but since our environment is fully observable we have provided
a simpler, but less general deﬁnition. In order to perform the two searches,
Ramirez and Geﬀner propose to compile the plan recognition problem T =
(P, G, O) into a Strips planning problem P[G] = (F, I, A, G) for each G ∈G and

using oﬀthe shelf planners (see [RG09] for details). The two searches produce
actions sequences. We assume that each action a ∈A has an associated non-
negative cost c(a). An action sequence π = a1, . . . , an then has cost c(π) =
Σc(ai). We assume unit cost on all actions unless stated otherwise. The most
likely goal is then chosen as:
G∗= argminG∈G(c(πG) −c(¯πG))
(2.1)
The chosen goal is thereby the one with the smallest diﬀerence between the
optimal path which satisﬁes the observation sequence, and the optimal path
which does not satisfy the observation sequence.
Ramirez and Geﬀner later extended their work to probabilistic goal recognition
producing a ranked list of probabilities for the set of goals [RG10]. For this
transition we ﬁrst need to extend the deﬁnition of a plan recognition problem
with goal priors.
Definition 2.4 A probabilistic plan recognition problem is a tuple T
=
(P, G, O, Prob) where P = (F, I, A) is a planning domain, G is a set of pos-
sible goals G, O is a sequence of observations o1, . . . , om where each oi is an
action in A, and Prob is the prior probabilities of G.
For this probabilistic approach they used Bayesian inference which has become
widespread in the literature for probabilistic goal recognition.
As usual in
Bayesian inference, they split the probability of a goal being pursued given
the observation sequence into a prior and posterior probability.
P(G | O) = αP(O | G)P(G)
(2.2)
Where α is a normalising constant, the prior probability P(G) is Prob(G) from
the probabilistic plan recognition problem, and the posterior probability P(O |
G) is calculated below. They assume a Boltzmann distribution on the posterior
probabilities, using the cost diﬀerence between satisfying and not satisfying the
observations, i.e. the same cost function as used in Equation (2.1).
P(O | G) = exp−β(c(π)−c(¯π))
(2.3)
Where exp is the exponential function. The posterior will be exactly 1 when
c(π) −c(¯π) is 0, i.e. when the observation sequence follows the optimal path
exactly.
Numerous works have proposed extensions to this framework, mainly in the
form of new cost functions (e.g. [PM16], [VKB16]). Most notably [VKB16]

to compile the plan recognition problem into a strips planning problem, and

2.2.1
Online Goal Mirroring
One of the main critiques of PRaP has been the ineﬃciency in having to compile
the observation sequence into a Strips planning problem, and having to com-
plete two full searches for every goal. Ramirez and Geﬀner’s original intention
with the compilation was to leverage advances in the research ﬁeld of eﬃcient
search, which indeed it does, however it still has plenty of potential for improve-
ment. Several of such have been proposed in online goal mirroring [VKB16].
We will argue separately for ¯π and π how we can make the planner calls free of
compilation, as well as reduce the needed searches to one per goal in an online
setting.
Originally we compiled a goal G and a planning domain P = (F, I, A) into a
planning problem P[G] = (F, I, A, G) to ﬁnd action sequences π and ¯π which
did and did not, respectively, satisfy the observation sequence O. Online goal
mirroring proposes to replace ¯π with ¯m which is any optimal action sequence
from the start I, to the goal G. ¯π and ¯m diﬀer in only a single scenario, when
all optimal paths have the observation sequence O as a subsequence.
Example 2.2 Consider the scenario depicted on Figure 2.3 where agent 0 has
a lettuce (l) and is either delivering it (*) or chopping it (/). We are given an
observation sequence O of two actions (blue line), and calculate ¯m (red line),
π (also red line) and ¯π (green line). ¯m and π are calculated straightforwardly.
For ¯π, any path going from right to left in the bottom passage would have O as a
subsequence, which is not allowed by the more general version of Deﬁnition 2.3,
hence ¯π is the shortest path taking the upper passage. Since we have ¯m = π in
both case (a) and (b), the observation sequences are both optimal. Assuming the
agents are playing optimally, the two cases should therefore reasonably be treated
equally for the posterior probability. Note that the diﬀerence in solution lengths
may mean the two cases are not treated equally for the prior probabilities. From
Deﬁnition 2.3 we see that the two cases have equal posterior probabilities exactly
when they have equal cost functions. Using ¯π we see that this is not the case
since c(πa)−c(¯πa) = 6−14 = −8 ̸= −6 = 8−12 = c(πb)−c(¯πb), and that using
¯π will therefore diﬀerentiate between the two cases. If we instead use ¯m we get
c(πa) −c( ¯ma) = 6 −6 = 0 = 8 −8 = c(πb) −c( ¯mb), satisfying that the two cases
will be treated equally.

2.2.2
Goal Recognition Design
In [KGK14] the authors propose the ﬁeld of Goal Recognition Design (GRD)
as a way to alter environments to better facilitate goal recognition. We do not
have the ability to alter the environment in this thesis. However, as argued
by [MFM+16] this school of thought is still relevant when considering multi-
agent goal recognition, as we can realise that other agents see us as a dynamic
part of the environment, and choose actions which best facilitate their goal
recognition. In other words we can replace the standard observer and actor
pattern of goal recognition by recognising that all agents are both observers
and actors. This is adopted in the single single-agent case, in the ﬁeld of active
goal recognition (introduced by [SM20]) where we still have an observer and
actor, but the observer is allowed to interact with the environment, thereby
becoming an active part of the recognition process. We are still in the keyhole
observer model in which the actor is unaﬀected by being observed, but the
observer is allowed to interact with the environment with the goal to enhance
or expedite goal recognition. We can thereby, during an observation sequence,
perform actions which aﬀect the goal recognition process.
Furthermore, the
environment we are investigating takes this one step further, the agents may
change their pursued goals at any point.
2.3
Dynamic Goal Recognition
The ﬁeld of dynamic goal recognition (introduced by [MCF+17]) is concerned
with online environments in which agents may change their pursued goals at
any point. This signiﬁcantly complicates the recognition process. In the clas-
sical goal recognition scenario, assuming that the agents are playing optimally,
observing more actions from an agent can only make the goal recognition pro-
cess more certain. Therefore we would always use all the observed actions of an
agent. However, this is not the case for dynamic goal recognition. There are
two fundamentally diﬀerent ways of handling this challenge.
The ﬁrst fundamental approach is to give more recent actions a higher weight.
This circumvents the problem of having to choose an exact cutoﬀfor which
actions aﬀect the goal recognition.
However, it does risk including a lot of
useless actions, and may put an excessively high weight on the most recent
action.
The second fundamental way is to ignore all actions up to a certain point,
thereby cut a preﬁx of the observation sequence O1...i and change the initial
==PAGE:24==
16
Theory
state accordingly Ii = (I, O1...i). This ignores all actions prior to step i, and
therefore introduces the challenge of how to choose i. Assuming an agent is
following one goal optimally and then changes to follow another goal optimally,
there exists an i for which exactly the preﬁx is irrelevant and exactly the suﬃx is
relevant. However, since we do not have access to the internal policy of the agent,
we do not know this i. One approach, which is used by BD, is for observation
sequence O1...m to set i = m every time the amount of possible goals changes.
The assumption is that when the possible goals change there is an increased
probability that the agents will choose a diﬀerent goal. This is true for both
increases and decreases in the amount of available goals, as an agent following a
goal which becomes unavailable must now choose another goal. Another way to
set i is with the sliding window approach ([MCF+17]) where we set i = m−j for
an observation sequence of length m and some constant j. Thereby we always
use the most recent j actions to estimate the goal of an agent.
In the environment which we investigate in this thesis we not only face the
challenge of agents changing their goals, but also agents changing who they are
collaborating with, and if they are collaborating at all.
2.4
Multi-Agent Plan Recognition as Planning
The ﬁeld of Multi-Agent Plan Recognition (MAPR) is a straight forward ex-
tension of the single-agent plan recognition (as formalised in [BKL10]), and by
extension, so is Multi-Agent Plan Recognition as Planning (MAPRaP). The
observation sequence O is extended to an observation matrix M where Mij is
the j’th action for agent i, and instead of assigning a single agent to a single
goal, we now assign subsets of agents to goals. One of the main challenges intro-
duced by moving to the multi-agent setting, is the exponential blow up in the
possible agent team compositions. Initial eﬀorts focused on single team scenar-
ios avoiding this exponential blowup. Subsequently the focus was on arbitrary,
but static teams which generally generated an initial hypothesis list of possible
agents to goal assignments, and then heavily pruned to reduce the amount of
needed searches (e.g. see [AD16]), partially avoiding the exponential blowup
from the possible team compositions. However, these approaches typically have
rather strict requirements, like the agents playing optimally and never changing
goals or teams. Furthermore, like single-agent plan recognition they operate in
the keyhole observer model, wherein an outside observer is attempting to recog-
nise the plans of the acting agents. In the environment considered in this thesis,
we are in the intended model, in which agents are aware that they are being
observed and let this fact inﬂuence their behaviour. This leads to an inﬁnite
recursion, as agent i’s actions depend on what agent i believes agent j will do,

which depends on what agent i believes agent j believes agent i will do, and so
forth.
2.4.1
Agent Types
In a decentralised multi-agent environment, from the perspective of an agent,
all other agents can be viewed as a non-deterministic part of the environment.
If other agents are known to be malevolent (resp. altruistic), we may plan for
the best worst-case (resp. best best-case) non-deterministic outcome. However,
if the other agents are sub-optimal (whether altruistic or malevolent), we can
either assume a random non-deterministic outcome, or try to predict the be-
haviour of other agents and optimise based on it. For this, we need theory of
mind, the ability to model the behaviour of the other agents, recognising their
hidden mental states. Two of the main methods to achieve this, as identiﬁed by
[AS18], is plan recognition and recursive reasoning.
Plan recognition as explained in previous sections, aims to ﬁnd the plan and
goal which most closely describes the observations.
Recursive reasoning as the name suggests, recursively performs perspective shifts
and reasons about the world at each of these. The amount of these perspective
shifts, the depth of reasoning, must be ﬁnite to terminate.
In [CHC04] the
authors refer to these perspective shifts as a cognitive hierarchy. They deﬁne a
level-0 cognitive hierarchy as players who act uniformly at random, and level-k
as players who assume that all other players act according to a level-(k-1). A
level-k player has a k depth of reasoning. They show that in many games an
average of a 1.5 depth of reasoning ﬁts the playing style of human players. Note
that the depth of reasoning must be a whole number, the 1.5 is an average. These
cognitive hierarchies have then been used to deﬁne composable team hierarchies
([SKWLT19]) which are capable of representing complex team structures. A
simpliﬁed version of the team structures have been used in [WWE+21] which is
further described in Section 3.1.

3.3.1
Single-Task Collaboration
Theoretically, BD is capable of multiple agents collaborating on multiple sub-
tasks concurrently. E.g. we may have an agent i which believes the subtask
allocation (i : τ1, j : τ1), and an agent j which believes the subtask alloca-
tion (i : τ2, j : τ2).
However, we will argue that this is a side eﬀect of the
sub-optimality which arises in the approximation of the expected future reward,
and therefore is unreliable. We will in the following reference some reinforcement
learning concepts from [MLG05] for completeness, but the argument is under-
standable without prior reinforcement learning experience. The goal recognition
in BD is performed from a third party perspective; it does not have access to
the internal states of the agents. Thus the only diﬀerence between the goal
recognition of diﬀerent instances of BD agents is the randomness in exploration
for BRTDP. Since BRTDP is a tabular reinforcement learning algorithm, it
keeps an explicit estimate for expected future rewards of every state action pair.
Each state action pair has a unique optimal expected future reward (according
to the Bellman equation) which can be deterministically calculated (via value
iteration). Hence, any disagreement between the diﬀerent BD agents on the ex-
pected future rewards comes from the sub-optimality of stopping BRTDP early
or setting too relaxed stopping criteria. If we assume all BRTDP instances are
given enough time to converge on the optimal expected future rewards they
would be expected to agree on the same subtask allocation. Thus the previous
example of multiple agents being assigned to multiple tasks, would not occur.
The way BD acts based on exactly the subtask allocation which is recognised as
most probable, provides an elegant link between goal recognition and the actual
behaviour of the agents. However, it signiﬁcantly limits the possible behaviours
and performance of the agent.

Handling Suboptimal Agents
BD was designed for ad-hoc collaboration and is therefore expected to collabo-
rate with other agent types in a variety of situations. Considering the extreme
of this, one can imagine a scenario where a BD agent must complete some task
while there is another, idle, agent present. To this end BD can assign up to one
agent to a non-task which is treated like any other task, but with ﬁxed action
probabilities, as explained at the end of Section 3.1.
Consider the scenario in Figure 3.3 where agent 0 is Still. When agent 1 is
played by BD (Figure 3.3b) it manages to handover the items in 12 steps, but
then fails to recognise that agent 0 is not progressing the tasks. BD encounters
a livelock, in which it performs actions continuously, but is not progressing any
of the tasks.
BD has randomness at three points; the BRTDP exploration,
choosing among best actions, and the random action chosen for agents assigned
to a non-task, all of which are controlled by a seed. Depending on the seed
used in BD, the behaviour of agent 1 will change slightly, but always be stuck
in a loop where it picks up one of the items, sometimes moves around for a
couple of steps, and then puts the item back down. BD fails to ever have the
most probable subtask allocation assign agent 0 to a non-task, and thereby fails
to recognise that agent 1 should complete the tasks on her own. One possible
explanation for this is the fact that agent 0 performing a no-op action can be
optimal for a task in some cases, thus no-op may have a higher probability on
a normal task than the non-task.

Definition 4.1 A goal g is a tuple (s, Ag, h) which is read as agents Ag
pursuing goal state s with handoﬀagent h. Where h ∈Ag if |Ag| > 1 else
h = none.
Notably this deﬁnition of a goal uses a set of agents Ag and introduces the

Definition 4.2 A handoﬀagent h for goal g = (s, Ag, h) must have a no-op
for any joint action which leads to the goal state s.
The intuition of a handoﬀagent is that we want the handoﬀagent to ﬁnish its
part of the plan as fast as possible in order to be available to help on other
goals.
We will later see that the above deﬁnition of a goal using a handoﬀ
agent will allow us to generate more diverse plans to solve goals, as well as
better estimate the length of a collaboration. The third concept is a means to
calculate collaborative solutions to multiple goals, called a collaboration.
Definition 4.3 A collaboration is a tuple (G, l, act, val) of goals G, estimated
length l, assigned value val and action to be executed act. For each g = (s, Ag, h)
and g′ = (s′, Ag′, h′) in G we have Ag = Ag′ and h ̸= h′.
We create a collaboration by ﬁnding plans to the individual goals g ∈G and
then resolving any potential conﬂicts. This allows us to ﬁnd plans for complex
behaviour in the collaborations while only performing simple planning for single
goals. We adopt this idea to the goal recognition system, where we perform
goal recognition on the simple goals, but use these to reason about whether
a collaboration is probable.
The goal recognition is using a sliding window

4.2
Step 1: Planner
The planner (Algorithm 1 Line 3) ﬁnds plans for every goal g = (s, Ag, h)
and assumes that any agent not in Ag is stationary. The only non-standard
requirements for the planner, is that it must adhere to the notion of a handoﬀ
agent which we introduced previously (Deﬁnition 4.2). We use a standard A*
search with a special condition on expanding goal nodes, to adhere to the handoﬀ
requirement, as well as an additional parameter for the frontier to promote
plans more suited for collaborations. The comparator for the frontier uses the
standard values f and g, as well as a third value actionCount which is the total
number of non no-op single-agent actions, where lower values are better. Due
to the handoﬀagent not being allowed to move in the last joint action, fewer
total actions, encourage the handoﬀagent to ﬁnish its part of the plan as early
as possible. The special condition on expanding goals node is simply enforcing
that the joint action leading to a goal state must specify a no-op for the handoﬀ
agent. I.e. upholding the handoﬀagent requirement.

4.4
Step 3: Generate Collaborations
If we simply chose to follow one of the calculated paths (from step 1), we would
allow multiple agents to be assigned to each task, but only one task per agent.
However, considering that all tasks end with a single agent doing work, and all
other agents being idle, we end up with agents assigned to tasks where they
have no more work to do. We therefore focus the system around collaborations,
which assign sets of agents to sets of tasks with the goal of reducing the idle
time of agents, and the overall execution length.
We generate all possible collaborations (Algorithm 4), with the additional con-
dition that all goals in a collaboration must be compatible. This is of course to
avoid unsolvable problems.
Definition 4.5 Goals G′ are compatible in state s for planning problem
P = (F, I, A, G) if they are non-empty and completing all g′ ∈G′ in s also
completes G or leads to a state t where some goals G′′ are compatible.
We then get the next H actions Act which the agents in the collaboration are
expected to perform (Algorithm 6), in order to resolve any possible conﬂicts.
First we must deﬁne a concept of a useful action which is intended to determine
whether an agent has an action which is actively progressing a goal.
Definition 4.6 Given an action sequence Act[1,...,m],[1,...,n] over m time steps
for n agents, which when performed in state s leads to goal state t = (s, Act) for
some goal g, and state si−1 = (s, Act[1,...,i−1]). We say that action Act[i][j] for
agent j at time i is useful if removing agent j from si−1 and actions Act[i,...,m]
leads to a state sm = (si−1, Act[i,...,m]) which is not a goal state for g.
The above deﬁnition thereby marks an action as useful if it actively contributes
towards the goal. The deﬁnition is not entirely domain independent as it relies

We can then deﬁne how to assign actions to agents in a collaboration. Recall
that a collaboration has multiple goals, all with the same agents (Deﬁnition 4.3).
Therefore, an agent may have multiple options for what goals to pursue, and
what immediate action to take. This is one of the places the handoﬀconcept
comes into play. From the perspective of an agent choosing a goal to pursue,
we choose, as a ﬁrst priority, to act towards a goal where we are the handoﬀ
agent, and we have a useful action. Note that the deﬁnition of a handoﬀagent
(Deﬁnition 4.2) does not require the handoﬀagent to have a useful action, in
fact it does not require the handoﬀagent to have any actions. As a second
priority we choose the goal with the highest probability where we have a useful
action speciﬁed. I.e. choosing any goal where we play an active role. As a last
priority we choose whatever goal speciﬁes any action for us the earliest.
Since the actions of diﬀerent agents have (potentially) been chosen based on
diﬀerent goals, they may conﬂict. In that case we attempt to replan for each
goal to resolve the conﬂict. Consider a collaboration with agents Ag, and a
subset of those agents Ag′ ⊆Ag who were assigned to goal g. We then replan
for goal g by assuming the ﬁrst H actions are ﬁxed for agents Ag\Ag′ according
to their assigned actions. This is essentially a cut down version of composable
team hierarchies (recall this is what BD use), as agents Ag′ assume agents
Ag \ Ag′ follow a level-0 policy where they in term assume all other agents to
be stationary.

contains superﬂuous agents.

4.7
Multi-Agent Planning
The main planning component of MAC aims to solve multiple goals concur-
rently. However, as we aim to solve more goals, the length of the plans increase.
In a multi-agent setting, where the number of possible joint actions grow expo-
==PAGE:46==
38
Design
nentially with the amount of agents, searching for longer plans comes at a heavy
time cost. Furthermore, attempting to search for a solution to multiple goals at
once signiﬁcantly complicates the design of a heuristic function. It is therefore
more eﬃcient to ﬁnd multiple, relatively short, plans which each solve one goal,
and then resolve any potential conﬂicts [DWC09]. In this way, the design is
quite ﬂexible when scaling up to a larger number of agents. We can for example
set a ﬁxed number of maximum agents for each goal, i.e. |Ag| ≤x in Deﬁni-
tion 4.1 for some constant x. Thereby limiting the amount of agents allowed to
cooperate at once, and circumventing the exponential blow up in the searches.
This does of course limit the amount of agents which can collaborate at any
one time. Likewise, we can limit the amount of goals in each collaboration, i.e.
|G| ≤y in Deﬁnition 4.3. Thereby limiting the amount of concurrently pursued
goals for a group of agents. Both of these limitations are fully supported by
the system due to the way step 5 selects a collaboration from a composition of
compatible collaborations. In the extreme we can set x = y = 1 and get a fast,
and full divide-and-conquer behaviour.
During the conﬂict resolution of step 3, we replan for one goal, assuming all
agents not assigned to the goal are following their assigned plans, who in term
assume that all other agents are stationary. We therefore essentially use recur-
sive reasoning during conﬂict resolution. I.e. a sparse use of recursive reasoning,
and only to a predetermined depth. This is unlike BD which uses the recursive
reasoning during every search. However, due to the handoﬀsystem we generate
more goals than BD does subtask allocations. Furthermore, in the worst case
we will have to do the conﬂict resolution for every collaboration. Thereby, in
the worst case we do strictly more recursive reasoning than BD.
BD ﬁnds the most likely subtask allocation and then simply executes a best
action for it. It uses a prior and posterior probability, where the prior is set
once, and then the posterior takes over (at least until the goals change). The
posterior ignores what goals can be solved the fastest, and simply reasons based
on what goals ﬁt the behaviour.
It therefore becomes a self-enforcing loop
where the most likely goal is most likely because it is most likely. Thus, if weird
or sub-optimal behaviour is observed, it may confuse the planning process to
enforce this, and pursue bad goals. It seems to put too much weight and trust
on recognising the behaviour of the agents.
MAC, on the other hand, was
designed to avoid this behaviour, in that MAC aims to always follow the best
plan possible. MAC ﬁnds the best plan which only conﬂicts a little with the
observed behaviour. Thereby using goal recognition as a sanity check to validate
the found plans, rather than to dictate the behaviour of the agent.

4.8
Contingency Planning
In decentralised multi-agent systems, if each agent simply calculates a plan (ac-
tion sequence) which leads to a goal state, it may be that the individual plans
of the agents conﬂict. If there exists a single optimal plan, and the agents are
assumed to be playing optimally, they would easily coordinate on it. However,
if multiple best plans exist, the agents may follow diﬀerent ones, which may lead
to a conﬂict. A common approach to handling this problem is to introduce ran-
domness among which of the best plans to choose. BD introduces randomness
in three places for which we will see that one can be disregarded, and the other
two have corresponding features in MAC.
The ﬁrst randomness from BD is in the exploration of the reinforcement learning
algorithm. However, as argued in Section 3.3.1 their reinforcement learning al-
gorithm will converge to a single optimal conﬁguration, making any randomness
it provides merely a side eﬀect of the sub-optimality of stopping the algorithm
early. Assuming that the algorithm is given enough time to converge, we can
disregard this source of randomness.
The second randomness from BD is in choosing a random best action for the
chosen subtask allocation. Similarly MAC chooses a random best action for the
chosen collaboration. However, whereas a subtask allocation has a single good-
ness measure (the solution length), collaborations have three; 1) the estimated
length of the collaboration, 2) the solution length of the goal which the plan-
ning agent has chosen to pursue, and 3) the handoﬀtime in said goal. Simply
choosing a best action for 1) would give the largest variability in plans, but may
end up hindering the goal recognition system of the other agents.
Example 4.3 Consider agent 0 following task A which takes 4 steps to com-
plete, and agent 1 following task B which takes 7 steps to complete. The length
of a collaboration of tasks A and B would be dominated by the length of task B.
Therefore agent 0 can take sub optimal actions for task A, e.g. making it take
6 steps, without aﬀecting the completion time of the collaboration. However, it
would make agent 1 think that agent 0 is not following task A, and therefore
that the collaboration is not probable.
The extra variability caused by using only goodness measure 1) was observed to
cause the sub-optimal behaviour described in the example above. MAC there-
fore only chooses among actions which are best on all three goodness measures.
The third randomness from BD is when an agent is assigned a non-task and
simply chooses a random action.
When an agent is assigned a non-task, it

presumably does not know what to do. Technically speaking, executing enough
random actions would eventually get the agent out of the situation, if a way
out exists. However, the random actions do not appear as intelligent behaviour.
Furthermore as we saw in Section 3.3.2, even when BD is playing with a Still
agent, it fails to assign either agent to a non-task, making this randomness
unreliable. MAC on the other hand, when there is no probable collaboration,
chooses among the improbable collaborations (Step 5, Section 4.6). The handoﬀ
system makes sure that for each goal the collaborations contain a diverse set of
plans. We can therefore reasonably assume that the collaborations will contain
a plan to solve the current problem, as will be discussed in the next section.
4.9
Better Eager Than Zazy
We can describe agent behaviours based on their preference for using their own
or other agents’ actions, with respectively eager and lazy agents at the extremes.
It is known that lazy agents may encounter deadlocks (all agents are waiting for
each other), even when executing optimal policies [BEMN18]. Eager agents can
prevent the deadlocks, but may in term cause live locks (all agents continuously
perform actions which do not progress the goal). BD chooses the most likely
subtask allocation, and then chooses a random best action for it. The behaviour
is neither eager nor lazy, it assumes that all agents are collaborating optimally.
However, this causes problems when playing against sub optimal agents, like
FB, or simply agents which ﬁnd diﬀerent, but still optimal plans.
Example 4.4 Consider the example on Figure 4.4 where the salad must be
merged with a plate, and we have BD as agent 0, and MAC as agent 1. The
solution found by BD (Figure 4.4b) requires agent 1 to ﬁrst pick up a plate
and then merge with the salad. However, the solution found by MAC (Figure
4.4a) requires agent 0 to ﬁrst hand over the salad, and then for agent 1 to
merge the salad with the plate. Before the handoﬀsystem, this scenario resulted
in a deadlock of continuous no-op’s. However, the handoﬀsystem is able to
distinguish between the two solutions. It realises that the solution proposed by
MAC is improbable, as it requires agent 0 to be the eager part, and instead
performs the solution found by agent 0 where agent 1 is the eager part, the
handoﬀagent.
The situation in Example 4.4 was solved due to the handoﬀsystem’s ability to
generate and distinguish between solutions with diﬀerent agents being eager.
However, this does not hold in all cases.

4.10
Goal Recognition
Our goal recognition system reasons about an agent’s goal based on the solution
length of her chosen action, as opposed to BD which utilises the solution lengths
of all the possible actions. The approach taken by BD of course requires more
information (all actions, versus only one), which is all calculated in one run of
BRTDP. We argue that in order to most accurately recognise the spectrum of
collaborative agent behaviour, we must not only recognise what goal an agent
is pursuing, but also when the agent is not pursuing a goal, i.e. an idle agent.
One approach is to represent an agent being idle as a regular goal. This faces
the challenge of how to deﬁne a solution length for an agent being idle. BD
attempted to slyly circumvent this challenge by assuming an idle agent is simply
more likely to be still that to be moving. They assign ﬁxed probabilities to the
possible actions, where the no-op action has probability 0.5 and the remaining
actions have uniformly distributed 0.5 probability. However, consider a subtask
allocation assigning agents 0 and 1 to the same goal A. They generate a plan
where agent 0 solves the goal on her own, and agent 1 just performs no-op’s.
Since agent 1 is not doing anything we would want her to be marked idle.
However, the no-op actions are a valid part of the subtask allocation, and she is
therefore not marked idle. This particular problem could be ﬁxed by changing
the ﬁxed probability of a no-op action to 1, and 0 for all other actions. However,
we would then not be able to recognise moving agents which are not pursuing a
goal. We can therefore reasonably rule out using ﬁxed probabilities for an idle
goal. Additionally, it does not make sense to have variable action probabilities
for an idle goal, and we can therefore rule out the approach of representing an
agent being idle as a regular goal.
==PAGE:51==
4.10 Goal Recognition
43
The other approach is to reason about the idle probability based on the existing
goals. If we simply check for an agent ag if some goal containing ag is being
progressed, we encounter the problem above, that continuous no-op’s are a valid
part of a plan. This leads us to reason about the idle probability by using the
relation between the solution lengths of a goal for diﬀerent sets of agent. I.e. our
notion of meaningful progress. There are thereby only three things which may
reasonably be altered; how to calculate progress, what it means for progress to
be meaningful, and how to calculate an idle probability based on the meaningful
progress.
Our notion of progress (Equation (4.2)) is simple, scaling linearly in the dif-
ference of solution lengths. Our notion of meaningful (Equation (4.3)) is like-
wise simple, a straight forward comparison of probabilities, with a parameter
to account for sub-optimality. The idle probability (Equation (4.4)) is more
complicated, using two parameters, as it is a balance between the deﬁnitions
of progress and normal probabilities.
Our notion of idle probability thereby
follows intuitively from the multi-agent goal recognition setting, and is close to
as simple as can reasonably be expected to be.

system is capable of such complex collaboration, like the one depicted on Figure

situation depicted on Figure 3.3c. This behaviour explains the perfect win rate
against the Still agent. This is in contrast to BD which is unable to recognise

the concept of a handoﬀagent, which in many cases is capable of generating a

resolving situations in which two agents both think the other agent should take
the ﬁrst move. In such a case, MAC reasons that the other agent will not be
playing the active part, and therefore engage in a plan which has itself as the
active part. One such example was shown on Figure 4.4.
The system is capable of low level coordination for which there are mainly two
categories, given that we have two agents which are pursuing a goal each. The
ﬁrst category is when there exists a single best plan for both agents to achieve
their goals the fastest, as depicted on Figure 4.3a. The system ﬁnds a plan for
each of the two goals which the agents are pursuing, and resolves the conﬂict
between them, thereby ﬁnding the single best plan for both agents. The second
category is when there exists multiple best plans for both agents to achieve
their goals the fastest. If the agents fail to coordinate on a best plan, they will
conﬂict, as depicted on Figure 4.3b. However, MAC chooses a random best
action, which has been observed to resolve this type of scenario, as depicted on
Figure 4.3c.

The following is a discussion on aspects of the system which could be interesting
to do further work on.
6.1
Reduce Free Parameters
The system currently has 9 parameters which can be set (Table A.2), where 3 are
used in the planning part of the system (F, G, and H), and the remaining 6 are
used in the goal recognition part of the system (A, B, C, D, E, I). The particular
values for the parameters were observed to work well in the environment used in
this thesis. However, the values may not work as well for other environments and
would therefore have to be tuned in. It stands to reason that fewer parameters
would make this adaption faster, and perhaps make the system more general.
In particular the deﬁnition of idle probability (Equation 4.4) currently uses 2
parameters, which should be able to be reduced to 1.
The size of the sliding window in the goal recognition system (E), and the
amount of actions considered for checking conﬂicts in the planner (H) are meant
to check immediate conditions. One can therefore reasonable assume it is pos-
sible to ﬁnd values that work well for most environments, or a way to automat-
ically set the values based on analysing the environment.

6.2
Domain Independence
One of the classical ways to improve a multi-agent system is to make it more
general, more domain independent. The heuristic used in the planner is domain
dependent, taking advantage of how agents can only hold one item at a time,
and that multiple agents are needed to pass items over counters. However, the
rest of the system only relies on some weak domain assumptions.
The planner was additionally tested using a simple Manhattan heuristic between
the two items involved in a goal. The full and open divider levels were solved
similarly to when using the original heuristic, but the partial divider levels which
have the longest sub task solutions, hit the 48 hours time limit. The planner
currently in use is not optimal, but still provides excellent results. The only
requirements we have for a planner is that any action leading to a goal state
must specify a no-op for the handoﬀagent, and it must penalise using more
single actions. It would therefore be interesting to look into more advanced
domain independent satisﬁcing planners.
One of the weak domain assumptions we make is in the deﬁnition of an action
being useful (Deﬁnition 4.6).
The deﬁnition relies on being able to remove
an agent from the environment.
It is intended to avoid recognising actions
for low level coordination as useful.
One could argue that the deﬁnition is
already domain independent, since in euclidean environments we already need
navigational actions which removes an agent from one spot and moves it to
another. Thereby already having deﬁned a notion of removing an agent. If we
do not have a euclidean environment, we do not have the problem of causing a
conﬂict on low level coordination, and therefore do not need to remove an agent.
However, more investigation into this is necessary.
Another weak domain assumption is in step 4 of the algorithm (Section 4.5)
in which we select probable collaborations. We assume that tasks are spatially
distributed in order to reason about what agents are expected to be working on
a goal. I.e. if an agent cannot solve a goal the fastest, it is due to the agent
being far away from the items related to the goal, and therefore not likely to be
working on it. We need this assumption since we are basing our reasoning around
solution lengths, and that one group progressing a goal will potentially make
all groups appear to progress said goal. It would be interesting to investigate
if we can get rid of this assumption.
For example by using a planner (like
BRTDP if it was domain independent) which calculates the solution lengths for
all possible actions, and use these in the reasoning for whether an agent was a
part of progressing a goal.

of the tunable parameters for each agent. For example the C parameter which
controls the threshold for what goals are considered possible. An agent which
had been observed to not collaborate would raise this value in any collaboration
it is in.

Conclusion
In this thesis we looked at designing a system for ad-hoc collaboration in a
heterogeneous environment. We heavily compared to the Bayesian Delegation
(BD) system [WWE+21], where we identiﬁed two main ﬂaws; the system only
allowed a set of agents to progress a single goal at a time, severely limiting the
possible collaborative behaviour, and the system had an ineﬃcient notion of
idle probability, thereby not being able to accurately recognise agents’ goals.
We designed a system dubbed (rather arbitrarily) Multi-Agent Collaboration
(MAC) which addressed these shortcomings. MAC is designed around a concept
of a collaboration which assigns sets of agents to sets of tasks by calculating
simple solutions to each task and resolving any potential conﬂicts in merging
them. We introduced the notion of a handoﬀagent as an agent doing initial
work on a task, and then handing oﬀthe remainder of the task to other agents.
This allowed us to more accurately estimate the length of collaborations, as
well as generate more diverse plans which has shown to resolve many dead lock
situations. The notion of a handoﬀagent and the collaborations have allowed
MAC to exhibit complex collaborative behaviour.
Unlike BD which performed goal recognition based on Bayesian inference on
expected future rewards of the available actions, MAC used a sliding window
approach, looking at the progression of solution lengths in the last ﬁxed amount
of steps. In BD the goal recognition was the primary driver, as it was used to
recognise the most probable agents to task assignment and then simply choose
==PAGE:64==
56
Conclusion
a best action for it. In MAC we moved the goal recognition to the back, by
ﬁrst ﬁnding the best plans, and then using goal recognition to estimate if it is
probable that the other agents will collaborate on those plans. This allowed
MAC to consistently be following best plans.
Unlike BD which estimated a probability that an agent was idle by treating
the idleness as a normal task and performing regular goal recognition on it,
we introduced a more sophisticated approach to idle recognition based on the
lack of meaningful progress on other tasks. We deﬁned the notion of progress
based on the progression of solution lengths over the sliding window, for a set
of agents on a task. We introduced the notion of progress being meaningful
by arguing that since one group of agents progressing a task, may progress
the task for other groups of agents, not all such progress is meaningful. We
then deﬁned an idle probability based on the maximum amount of meaningful
progress which occurred during the sliding window.
We showed that MAC
very eﬃciently recognises other agents as idle, as it was capable of solving all
the possible tested scenarios in the presence of an idle agent (66.7%), with a
minimal overhead. This is unlike BD which only manages to solve 6.9% of the
tested levels with an idle agent present.
We tested the system in a cooking inspired environment from [WWE+21] along
with 5 variants of BD, an idle agent Still, and a version of our system which was
limited to assigning a group of agents to a single task MAC1. In self-play, MAC
managed to solve 100% of the levels where the competing solution BD managed
to solve only 92.8%. Furthermore MAC solved these levels in an average of 27.2
time steps where BD needed 31.6 time steps. In ad-hoc play every single agent
solved more levels when matched with MAC than any other agent including
itself. Furthermore these levels were also solved using fewer time steps when
matched with MAC compared to any other agent, with the exception of the
BD variant Greedy and Still. Greedy ignores the progress of other agents and
was therefore not expected to beneﬁt from the complex collaboration oﬀered by
MAC. However, the average solution length of 36.2 for Greedy in self-play is only
increased to 36.5 when paired with MAC, showing that even when collaboration
is not beneﬁcial, MAC only adds a small overhead.
Overall the developed system, MAC showed highly satisfactory results on the
tested environment, both in calculation time and quality of solution. Further-
more, MAC showed a capacity for complex collaboration in situations where
the competing solution, BD failed.

A.1
Environment
The following is a more detailed description of the environment.
An instance of the environment speciﬁes an initial state, a goal state, and a
number of partially ordered hierarchical sub-tasks with deﬁned preconditions
and postconditions. Each sub-task has two items as precondition and one item
as the post condition, with the intuition that the two items are combined into
one. One or both of the precondition items may be movable. The environment
contains two types of unmovable objects, chopping station and delivery sta-
tion, as well as three movable base items, lettuce, tomato and plate, and their
combinations. See Figures 1.1a and 1.1b for the full list of items and recipes
respectively. All goal states specify a number of items which must be placed on
a delivery station.
When visualising an instance of the environment, we use numbers to repre-
sent agents, alphabetical characters to represent items, and non-alphanumerical
characters to represent unmovable objects. The grey squares are counters and
may contain one item. The white squares are navigational and may contain one
agent and one item which the agent is holding. We will use coloured lines with
directional arrows to represent actions, where each circle represents one action.

The agents can move in the four cardinal directions. They can interact with a
counter by standing next to it and moving to it (the agent remains in place).
If only one of the counter and agent has an item, it will be picked up or put
down. If both have items and they form a valid recipe according to Figure 1.1b,
the items will disappear, and the agent will receive the result. Note that items
can only be combined on counters, two agents interacting is an illegal operation.
Two agents cannot occupy the same cell in the same round. All actions must
be individually valid at the beginning of a step as well as when it is actually
executed during the step. If the actions of two agents conﬂict, they will both
be set to no-op’s. The environment is fully observable and deterministic. The
environment is copied exactly from [WWE+21] and contains one anomaly.
The anomaly is depicted on Figure A.1 where agent 0 puts down her salad (S) in
the same step that agent 1 combines the salad with her plate (p), which should
not be possible. The anomaly arises from the fact that the environment does not
require actions to be valid at the beginning of the step, but simply must be valid
when they are executed. However, the BD planner only allows actions which are
valid in the beginning of the step. Since the actions of both agents were valid
in the beginning of the step (as put-down actions), and they are valid during
execution (as put-down and combine actions) they are accepted. However, had
agent 1 not held a plate, the action would have been invalid. I.e. it is allowed
to put-down and combine in one step, but not put-down and pick-up. Note also
that if the order of execution had been reversed, agent 0 would have ended up
with the plated salad instead of agent 1.
The three distinct level types used in the benchmarks are shown on Figure 1.3.
For each of the three level types we consider the following three goal states; 1)
plated tomato (a), 2) plated salad (c), 3) plated tomato (a) and plated lettuce
(b).
