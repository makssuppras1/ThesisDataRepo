Summary
This thesis investigates a potential new method for reconstruction of x-ray tomographic
data. That is reconstruction of an image or model, from the x-ray measurement data.
Common methods in use today include Filtered Back Projection, which requires many
projections and produces noise in the final image, and the iterative Algebraic Recon-
struction Techniques, which is computationally intensive and less accurate. With this
in mind, the goal was to design an algorithm, using deformable methods and a priori
knowledge, that can produce accurate results from limited data sets, at similar or faster
speeds than the existing methods. Additionally the goal was to implement a prototype
of the algorithm. If this can be achieved it can potentially lead to improved accuracy
of measurement, which can lead to improved equipment, and in the case of medicinal
application potentially lower the radiation to the patients.
The algorithm was designed, based on the Deformable Simplicial Complex (DSC)
method for a movable mesh curve representation of the image. The algorithm starts
by the initialisation of a DSC curve (random or based on a priori knowledge). Next a
virtual measurement, called a forward model, is generated from the curve. The forward
model is compared with the measured data in a residual model, and the residuals are
used to deform the curve. The process is then repeated until no residuals remain, or an
equilibrium is reached.
The critical step in this algorithm is the generation of the forward model, since this is
the most computationally intensive step. The thesis therefore focussed on implementing
a prototype of this part of the algorithm. This was first done as a MATLAB prototype,
which was later ported to C++. Finally it was integrated with the DSC framework. A
working prototype was thus implemented, which can generate the forward model from
a two dimensional DSC curve.

Resumé
Denne afhandling undersøger en potentiel ny metode til rekonstruktion af røntgentomo-
grafisk data. Det vil sige, en rekonstruktion af et billede eller model ud fra data fra
røntgentomografiske målinger. Nuværende metoder inkluderer Filtered Back Projection,
som kræver mange projektioner og som kan give støj i det rekonstruerede billede, samt
iterative Algebraiske Rekonstruktions Teknikker, som er beregningsmæssigt intensive og
mindre nøjagtige. Med dette i mente var målet med projektet, at designe en algoritme,
ved brug af deformerbare metoder og a priori-viden, der kan producere nøjagtige resul-
tater fra begrænsede datasæt, ved tilsvarende eller hurtigere beregningshastigheder end
de nuværende metoder. Derudover var det også målet med projektet at implementere en
prototype af algoritmen. Hvis målet med algoritmen kan nås, kan det leder til bedre nø-
jagtighed i billederne, hvilket kan give forbedredret udstyr, og muligvis mindre stråling
for patienter ved medicinske anvendelser.
Algoritmen blev designet baseret på Deformable Simplicial Complex (DSC) metoden,
som er en implementation af en kurverepræsentation af billeder, baseret på et deformer-
bart net (Movable mesh). Algoritmen starter med at initialisere en DSC kurve (tilfældig
eller med a priori-viden). Derefter genereres en virtuel måling af kurven, kaldet en For-
ward Model. Denne Forward Model sammenlignes med de målte data, for at generere
en residual-model, som derefter bruges til at deformere kurven. Denne process gentages
indtil residual-modellen er tom eller indtil en ligevægt er nået.
Det kritiske skridt i denne algoritme er genereringen af Forward Model, da dette er
det mest beregningsmæssige intensive skridt. Denne afhandling fokuserede derfor på at
implementere en prototype af denne del af algoritmen. Dette blev først gjort som en
MATLAB prototype, som dernæst blev omskrevet til C++. Til sidst blev den integreret
med DSC biblioteket. En fungerende prototype blev produceret, som kan generere en
Forward Model fra en to-dimensionel DSC kurve.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
When observing the world around us, our eyes, as well as cameras and many other
instruments, are good are observing the surface of objects and materials.
However
the properties of materials often depend on the interior or bulk of the material. One
technique for observing the interior is X-ray tomography.
This technique works by
projecting x-ray beams through an object, and measuring the dampening of the beams
after passing through the object.
The dampening depends on the shape, size, and
properties of the material that it passes. This data can then be used to reconstruct an
image of the interior of the object.
Background
There are several computational techniques to perform the image recon-
struction. The most common techniques include Filtered Back Projection, and Algebraic
Reconstruction.
Another way of representing the images is by using a curve representation of the
objects. Today curves of objects are typically digitally represented either explicitly or
implicitly. In an explicit representation, the boundary of an object is represented by a
sequence of points, connected by line segments. An object fully inside the image will be
represented by a complete, continuous curve. For an implicit representation, each pixel
of an image is given a value. Positive values indicates that the pixel is outside the curve
and negative values indicate that the pixel is inside the curve. The further away a pixel
is from the curve the higher a value is given (or lower when the pixel is inside the curve),
and the boundary is located where the positive and negative pixels meet.
The methods for representing objects as curves, described in the paragraph above,
is combined in a technique know as Movable Mesh (MM) or Deformable Simplicial
Complex (DSC). This technique employs a triangular mesh. Each triangle (or simplex
for a more generalised description) is given a positive or negative value, depending on
whether it is inside or outside the curve, similarly to the implicit method described
above. However, the mesh can be deformed by moving any of the vertex points (the
corners of the triangles), and adjusting the edges connected to that vertex. The vertices
and edges where the positive and negative triangles meet describe the boundary of the
object, similarly to the explicit method described above.
Motivation
The objective of the project is to describe an iterative algorithm for recon-
struction of x-ray tomographic measurement data, using the DSC framework. Further-
more it is the goal of the project to implement the algorithm, especially the crucial step

image, based on the Deformable Simplicial Complex framework. The algorithm mimics
the action of an X-ray tomographic instrument. In order to calculate a reasonable result,

Before attempting to implement a reconstruction method for x-ray tomography, it is

reconstruction methods in use, namely algorithms for reconstruction with nondiffracting
sources (such as filtered back projection) and algebraic reconstruction algorithms [2]. In
more recent year, a third group is emerging, which is deformable methods using curve
representations of the images [9].

2.2.1
Filtered Back Projection
One of the most common methods for reconstruction of x-ray tomographic measure-
ments is known as filtered back projection. The method uses an algorithm derived from
the Radon transform [11]. The idea is to obtain measurements at a large amount of
angles, and run the projections back trough the image. The projections will combined
constructively in the areas of the image corresponding to the original objects measured.
In figure 2.5 this is illustrated with projections at 45° angles, where (A) shows the effect
of a single projection, and (B) the result of combining the back projections from all
angles [2], [12].
Figure 2.5: Illustration of a simple projection (A) versus back projection (B) [12].
As is evident from the example in figure 2.5, the image reconstructed from back
projections can be prone to noise and blurring. In practice this is minimised by applying
many angles (or projections) as illustrated in figure 2.6, and applying a filter (such as
a high-pass filter). The back projections and filtering together is what is known as the
filtered back projection algorithm (FPBA) [2], [11], [12].

Deformable Methods
The reconstruction discussed above, is based on traditional image reconstruction algo-
rithms, which use the line integrals of the projections to calculate the reconstruction.
Another approach use curve representation (see section 2.3 for more information) of the
unknown image, and deforms the curve iteratively, to obtain the final reconstruction.
Several approaches have been proposed, including an approach using energy minimisa-
tion internal forces of spline contours and external forces [15]. Another proposal use
level set curve representation to compute the image [9].

2.3
Curve Representation
Traditionally images have been represented by a grid/matrix of pixels, and 3D models by
a cubic grid of voxels. Another way of handling the data is to use a curve representation
of the image. The curve can be represented explicitly through a sequence of geometric
coordinates, connected by line segments. One implementation of this is Snakes (or Active
contour model). Alternatively a curve can be represented implicitly, by giving each pixel
a value that is positive outside the curve and negative inside the curve. Thus the pixels
with a value of 0 represent the curve boundary. An implementation of this is Level set.
This concepts and implementations are discussed in [16], [17], [18].
For many scientific application, such as x-ray tomography, images may be sparse
(that is they contain large amounts of empty space). Processing an image by pixel can
therefore be computationally intensive, while curve representations provide tools to more
easily handle and manipulate the data.
2.3.1
Level Set
In the level set method, an image consist of a set (grid) of values (typically corresponding
to the pixel of the image). The values outside of the curve are positive and increase
with distance from the interface, while the values inside are negative and decrease with
distance from the interface. That way, the image can be represented as a kind of contour
plot or heat map, as shown in figure 2.10 [9], [18], [19].
Figure 2.10: Level set curve representation, where the curve is implicitly computed from
the levels around [9].
The curve is given, implicitly, by the interface between the positive and negative
values. Using level set provides possibility to easily represent multiple objects in the
images (by having multiple negative regions or valleys). However, deforming the curve
requires potentially updating all the values, which can be computationally intensive [9],
[18], [19].
==PAGE:31==
2.3 Curve Representation
11
2.3.2
Snakes
The snakes method (or active contour model - ACM) is an explicit curve representation,
where the curve is given by a sequence of points or vertices. Specifically a snake is
a deformable spline, which is designed to minimise energy depending on image forces
and given constraints [20], [21], [22]. Segmentation using the energy minimisation is
illustrated in figure 2.11.
Figure 2.11: Snakes curve representation, where the curve is explicitly computed [20].
The benefit of snakes is that they can be easily deformed, by transforming the relevant
vertices, and corresponding line segments connecting them. However, it is more difficult
to handle multiple curves in an images using snakes. Especially in the case where they
are different objects (such as different materials in with different attenuation coeficients
for the x-rays).
2.3.3
Deformable Simplicial Complex
The explicit and implicit representations discussed above each have pros and cons. The
explicit representation can easily handle movement or deformations of the curve, but
if another object is in the image, a separate curve is needed. Meanwhile the implicit
representation can easily handle multiple objects or curves in the same image, but moving
or deforming the curve requires updating the whole image.
A combination of these
concepts is known as movable meshes (or deformable simplicial complex - DSC). Here
the image is represented as a mesh of simplices (triangles in a two dimensional space).
each simplex is given a positive, negative value, or 0, depending on wether it is inside
or outside the curve (similarly to the implicit representation). The boundary of the
curve can therefore be imagined as a sequence of vertices and edges from the simplices
==PAGE:32==
12
2 Theory
(similarly to the explicit representation). The simplices can then be deformed by moving
the vertices and corresponding edges [1], [23], [24]. An example of a two dimensional
DSC curve is shown in figure 2.12.
Figure 2.12: A curve representation in deformable simplicial complexes. Triangles out-
side the curve are grey, while triangles inside are blue. The interface edges are high-
lighted in dark blue [1].
The DSC method was first introduced in [1] in 2010, though [25] from 2000 and
[20] from 2001 discuss similar concepts. DSC was further elaborated in 2012 [23] and
recently in [24].
The DSC method can easily handle multiple different objects or phases, by providing
different labels for the simplices (or faces) that lie within the different phases.
An
example is the multiphase segmentation shown in figure 2.13 [24].
Figure 2.13: Multiphase segmentation of an image, using dsc [24].
A two dimensional curve represented in DSC contains collections the edges, vertices,
and faces (simplices), with associated labels for them. The edges are given as half-edges.
Each half-edge has an associated vertex, face, and opposite half-edge. The DSC library
also provides iterators, to iterate through these collections. If a simplex is outside the
curve, it is labelled 0, and if it is inside a curve, it has a positive label. Edges that
are part of an interface are labelled -1. Furthermore the DSC library also provides a
template for a velocity function, which is used to deform the curve (for example to rotate
the curve) [1], [26].

CHAPTER3
Method
Before implementation, it is necessary to consider the method used. First the problem
is analysed, and then the method is designed. In this case, that involves considering the
algorithm. This chapter describes the algorithm and the considerations that led to the
design choices.
3.1
Algorithm
When an x-ray tomographic scan is performed, the x-rays are projected through the
object and collected in a detector on the other side. The detector consists of a series
of bins that measure the intensity of the x-rays that reach them. This data is then
converted using a logarithmic transformation to provide data for the dampening of the
x-rays. The object is rotated, and a series of scans are performed, and the resulting data
is represented as a sinogram, where the x-axis is the angle of rotation, and each line
perpendicular to the x-axis is one scan of the object from one angle. The basic structure
of the algorithm is given below.
1. Initialise the curve
• Generation of the initial curve (for example a circle).
• This will probably be done manually in the beginning.
2. Generate forward model
• Construct a sinogram from the generated curve.
3. Determine residual model
• By subtracting the generated sinogram with the measured sinogram.
4. deform/adjust curve
• Based on the residual model.
5. Repeat step 2-4 until convergences is reached
==PAGE:36==
16
3 Method
The crucial step in this algorithm is step 2, where the forward model is generated.
The x-ray tomographic data can be gathered using thousands of angles, and the detectors
consist of hundreds of bins, so it is important that the implementation for generating the
forward model is efficient. This is especially important since this step must be completed
multiple times.
3.1.1
Forward Model
The main idea for generating each scan of the forward model is as follows:
• Take boundary of the curve (a series of vertices and edges/line segments).
• Calculate for each line segment seperately, the addition to each bin behind it, by
traversing the curve in one direction.
– If the object extended from the line segment to the detector, how much would
it add to the dampening recorded by the bins behind it.
– Calculate integral of the line segments for each bin.
– Partial contribution to the bins at beginning and end of the segment.
• Either add or subtract the values from the bin, depending on wether the direction
(based on the direction of traversal) of the line segment is positive or negative in
the coordinate system.
• After traversing the whole curve, output a vector/array with values for the detector
bins.
This algorithm can then be repeated for each angle of rotation for the object.
Figure 3.1: Sketch of how the algorithm traverses the curve, one line segment at the
time.

4.3
C++ With DSC Integration
The final implementation of this project integrates with the Deformable Simplicial Com-
plex (DSC) framework2 [26]. This provides many benefits (as mentioned in section 2.3.3).
The MATLAB implementation (section 4.1) and the C++ port (section 4.2) relies on
the curve to be represented as a list of points/line segments (similar to the snakes im-
plementation), given in the right order, to be able to know if the contribution of a line
segment should be added or subtracted. Using the DSC framework, however, the frame-
work provide labels for simplices that are within a curve, or 0 for simplices outside of
a curve, which makes it easy to do that. DSC also keeps track of the edges, and which
edges are interfaces. Finally the DSC framework also handles the rotation of the curve.
Some of the code from section 4.2 could be reused, but the integration required
significant rewriting. To save time, the integration was done be by extending a copy of
the demo provided with the framework. The following subsections describe the changes
and the integration itself. The source code for this implementation can be found in
appendix C.
4.3.1

When an eligible interface is found, an HMesh::Walker object (from the GEL frame-
work3) is created, to extract the related information, including opposite half-edge, adje-
cent vertices and faces, as well as labels. To calculate which face is towards the beam
(upper face) and which face is towards the detector (lower face), the x-coordinates of
the start and end of the found half-edge are compared. If the x-coordinate of the start
point is lower, the face associated with the found half-edge is the upper face, but if the
end point has the lower x-coordinate, the face associated with the opposite half-edge is
the upper face.
Next there are three scenarios. If the upper face has the label 0, the interface is
between an object and the outside, and the line segment contribution is added. If the
lower face has the labekl 0, the interface is between an object and the outside, and the
line segment contribution is subtracted. Finally if both faces have labels > 0, then the
interface is between two different objects, and the line segment contribution multiplied
by the factor associated with the lower face is added, while the line segment contribution
multiplied by the factor associated with the upper face is subtracted. Note that if no
material factors are provided, they default to 1.0 for all labels.

CHAPTER5
Discussion
The algorithm was described in chapter 3, and implemented in three variations: A
MATLAB prototype (section 4.1), a C++ port of the prototype (section 4.2), and a
C++ version integrating with the DSC framework (section 4.3). All three variation
implement the second step of the reconstruction algorithm, concerning the generation of
a forward model (a virtual scan/sinogram), as this was identified as the most critical step
of the algorithm. Though this is only the first step in the process, the implementations
work, and show the potential of the algorithm.
The MATLAB prototype and C++ port was implemented as a proof of concept, and
thus while they work they have significant limitations. These include the constraints
of the curves used, and the limited possibility for implementation of the rest of the
algorithm. As such it is clear that integration with the DSC framework is the best way
forward. There are other methods that can accomplish some of these same goals, as
mentioned in section 2.2.3, but the DSC framework provides functionality to provide
necessary information about the model, and ease the process of deforming the model.
5.1
Future Work
Even though the implementations work, there is still a lot of work left, before the algo-
rithm can be used. These include improvements to the implementation of the forward
model, implementation of the remaining algorithm, as well as converting the algorithm
to work in the three dimensional space.
Improvements to the forward model
These improvements include adjusting the
configuration of the DSC model, including velocity and accuracy of the velocity function,
and discretisation of the mesh. These factors can help provide better results. Improve-
ments to the display and output of the results can also help the algorithm perform better
and better integrate with the remaining steps.
Since the implementation in this project assumed the use of a parallel beam source,
it would also be useful to look at modifications for fan beam sources. Ideally this would
be an option, so that the algorithm can work for both types.
A big improvement to the performance would be to incorporate concurrency, and po-
tentially hardware acceleration, in the implementation. Using the information included
in the DSC model, the contribution of a line segment to each of the bins it affects can
be computed in any order. Similarly the line segments can be computed in any order,
==PAGE:62==
42
5 Discussion
and each angle of the curve can be computed in any order. Thus the algorithm is ready
for concurrency, with only minor modifications.
Implementation of remaining algorithm
Before the algorithm can be used in
practice, the remaining parts must be completed. Calculation of the residual model and
deformation of the curve may not be difficult to implement, but some considerations
are necessary to ensure that the coordinate systems, and the input and output, are
consistent. It is also important to ensure that the setup and configurations used for the
actual measurements match those of the virtual measurements.
Three dimensional implementation of algorithm
Since the world is three dimen-
sional, and not flat, providing a three dimensional version of this algorithm may also be
a priority. The 2D version of DSC method was used in this project, but a more general
version, in the three dimensional space, also exist. Therefore it is possible to implement
as well, though considerations of the geometry and similar, is also necessary.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
With the background of x-ray tomography and existing reconstruction method, this
project set out to design and implement an improved reconstruction algorithm. Using
the DSC method, the motivation for the project was to create an algorithm that can
reconstruct x-ray tomographic data, with improved accuracy and using less input data.
The idea was to use a priori knowledge, to initialise the curve, then generate a virtual
measurement from that, compare it to the actual measurement, adjust, and iterate.
Getting this to work can potentially lead to improved accuracy of measurement, which
can lead to improved equipment, and in the case of medicinal application potentially
lower the radiation to the patients.
With the in mind an algorithm was designed, as described in chapter 3. Very early
in the project, the generation of the forward model (virtual sinogram) was identified
the critical step (step 2), since this is were the majority of the computation takes place.
Therefore the rest of the projected focussed solely on implementing and testing this
step. This was done first as a prototype in MATLAB, then ported to C++, and finally
integrated with the DSC method.
All implementations worked, and the details and
results are described in chapter 4.
The implementation of the forward model, however, is just the first step for this
method of reconstruction, so a lot of work lies ahead before it can be used in practice.
The implementation and future work is discussed in chapter 5.
