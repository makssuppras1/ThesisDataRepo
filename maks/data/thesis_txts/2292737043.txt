Summary
With the recent ability to produce MEMS gyroscopes, the focus on Micro Aerial Vehicles
have increased a lot over the last few years. Many of these are remotely controlled from
the ground, and does not depend on localization, while others depend on GPS signals
for coarse localization and navigation.
This thesis is the initial investigation of a visual navigation system for drones, utiliz-
ing only a single camera as sensor. This approach can be advantageous, since it is able
to provide means of localization in GPS denied areas, such as forests, urban areas or
indoor environments. The design and implementation of such a navigation framework
have been carried out with computational and performance optimizations in mind, to
be able to compute the live data directly on the drone.
The camera depend on landmarks for motion tracking, and different techniques for
detection, tracking and subsequent movement modelling have been tested and evaluated.
This have resulted in a framework, where motion is estimated with 6 degrees of freedom,
and with an acceptable precision.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With the ability to produce MEMS gyroscopes only few years back came the possibility
to provide cheap and light-weight means of stabilizing small aerial vehicles, such as
airplanes, helicoptors and multirotors. Since then, there have been a lot of public focus,
discussion and especially development in the world of Micro Aerial Vehicles (MAV’s), or
drones as the public likes to call them.
A drone can take on many forms, depending on its purpose, mostly ranging from
high-altitude surveilance UAV’s in form of miniature airplanes, to lightweight lifting
vehicles for aerial protography, such as a multirotors. Drones are commonly operated
remotely from the ground by a pilot, but over the recent years, focus have gradually
changed to offload trivial piloting tasks to the drone itself, thus making it gradually
more autonomous.
Making completely autonomous drones has a long list of advantages over manually
piloted ones, accompanied by an even longer list of challenges, that must be tackled to
make it a reality. Less than a year ago, a graduate student at TU Delft demonstrated
the creation of his Ambulance Drone1, which is in fact a flying defibrillator, that will
navigate to its target using GPS satelites. While it is a normal to install GPS equipment
on drones for navigation, it is usually a suboptimal solution due to the precision of the
GPS system, and obsacles, such as buildings and trees, will easily render the GPS signals
useless, not to mention indoor navigation.
What if we could use a camera for navigation? This thesis will discuss the necessary
steps to build a navigation system, primarily based on the vision provided by a single
forward-facing camera, mounted on a drone.
1.1
Visual Odometry
Using a camera as the primary source for navigation is not something new, and over
the past years, research in this field have increased exponentially. A lot of different ex-

periments, papers and even complete solutions can be found online, but they commonly
require aid to be used for navigation, either in form of other sensors (e.g. barometric
pressure or laser distance) or vehicular constraints, common for ground motion vehicles.
Using sensors to estimate the position of a vehicle is called odometry, and, for ground
vehicles, often involves rotary wheel encoders to estimate the position and heading,
(x, y, Θ). Aerial vehicles doesn’t use wheels, and could instead rely on visual odometry by
using a camera as the sensor, thus estimating the position and heading as six parameters,
(x, y, z, Φ, Ψ, Θ), since there are no constraints on the movement in air.
Using sensors that provide information about the environment can simultaneously
be used to build a map of the surroundings, which is called SLAM (Simultaneously Lo-
cation And Mapping). Building a map has certain advantages, such as the possibility to
recognize locations where the vehicle have been before, and it is possible to significantly
reduce the influence of sensor drift by using this information.
One example of this is the MIR2 (Mobile Industrial Robot) robots, roaming around
mostly on hospitals at the time of this writing.
These robots depend primarily on
laser scanners to build a map of the environment where they have to work. This is
extremely useful, as the robots can first discover the environment without doing harm
to the surroundings, and later use this information to navigate and easily find alternative
routes if one is blocked.
Unfortunately, laser scanners are expensive, fragile and heavy equipment, and given
their ability to scan only a line at a certain height, not suited for drone flight at all. A
camera, on the other hand, is cheap, robust and very light, which makes it a good choise
for a sensor in the air, although it doesn’t provide a depth perception.
1.2
Problem Statement
The objective of this thesis is to develop a simple navigation framework for drones, to
allow navigation in unknown, GPS denied environments. Initially, the only sensor will
be a forward-facing camera, to estimate the position using visual tracking of landmarks
in the scene. This data can eventually be fused with an Inertial Measurement Unit
(IMU3), which is also used to keep the drone stable in air.
To limit the scope of this thesis, all parts relating to an actual flying drone has been
omitted, and a drone is instead simulated using a box, with a camera, processing unit and

CHAPTER 2
System Platform
This chapter will contain a short discussion of the platform for the drone navigation
system. First, the hardware will be discussed, namely the choise of processing unit,
IMU sensor and Camera. Following this, the camera section will additionally describe the
camera model and parameters used throughout the project, including camera calibration
and issues, that might reduce the image quality.
2.1
Hardware Selection
To make a drone truely autonomous, it should not depend on a backend to process its
data, and the entire navigation system should thus be implemented on light computing
hardware aboard the drone. Since image processing of this kind involves heavy math
and matrix operations, the newly released Raspberry Pi 2 seems to be a solid choise for
the task. Featuring a quadcore 900 MHz Cortex A7 chip, it is capable of running four
threads concurrently, and thus reducing input lag when the heavy operations are carried
out.
The weight is only 45 g, which is comparable to similar products1, and the CPU is a
bit stronger than most competitors. Another reason to choose the Raspberry Pi is the
presence of a CSI port (dedicated serial camera interface), allowing the drone to carry
the ultra lightweight (3 gram) dedicated Raspberry Pi Camera Module.
Inertial Measurement Unit
A small IMU will be installed in the drone shell as well, to aid the rotational perfor-
mance. While it can provide several useful measurements, it should initially only use
the gravitational force to reduce drift on the rotation matrix with respect to the ground.
The LSM9DS0 is a small and power efficient 6 DoF sensor (3-way Gyro, Accelerom-
eter & Compass), that connects to the Raspberry using I2C. It has been chosen due to
its price, and the fact that it comes mounted on a breakout board for easy connectivity.

2.2
Camera
When installing a camera on a drone for navigational purposes, there are a list of restric-
tions and requirements the camera should comply to. First of all, the size is of great
importance, since it must be small enough to fit in the drone shell for protection and
light enough not to influence the flight time significantly. Finally, it must deliver images
of a certain quality in terms of resolution, sharpness and speed to minimize the error
and computation time as much as possible.
In this study, the first two terms (size and weight) have not been considered, and
the camera model was selected based on what was available. The camera, a Microsoft
LifeCam Studio delivers VGA quality images (640 × 480 pixel) at 30 frames per second
(FPS) by default, and despite being a webcamera, the images look sharp and focused.
Small integratable camera modules on par or better (such as the Raspberry Pi camera
module) are easily obtainable, and most of the features available for the microsoft camera,
such as auto focus and automatic exposure control, have been disabled in Video4Linux
(the video driver framework), since full manual control of the camera is required.
The camera could be configured to deliver higher frame rate or better resolution if
desired, but since image processing is a computational heavy task, 0.3 MPixel is a great
resolution, while 30 FPS is more than enough for these relatively slow movements.
The world coordinate system is chosen to follow that of most digital images, where
x is right in the image and y is down (when looking at an image). Using the right-hand
rule2, z will thus point into the image and make depth values positive.

CHAPTER 3
Image Features
To provide the necessary means for motion estimation, this chapter will discuss the pro-
cess of extracting meaningful data from a set of images. First step is to locate landmarks
(image features) in the set of images, which will be discussed in Section 3.1. Following
this, two different means of feature tracking will be discussed, the first being Feature
Description and Brute Force Matching, discussed in Section 3.2, and the second being
Optical Flow in Section 3.3. Both Feature Detectors and Tracking will be compared and
evaluated in Section 3.4, and finally, Section 3.5 will discuss the creation of synthetic
features for testing purposes.
In computer vision, a feature can be a range of different things such as (but not
limited to) an edge or ridge, a corner or a structure or blob of pixels. Depending on the
application, some feature types are better suited than others, and Table 3.1 list a few
common detectors of each kind.

For precise movement tracking, corner detection and blob detection has proven to
be solid choices, corners being fast to process even on low-end hardware, while blob
detection is the standard feature type in the widely used SIFT and SURF algorithms.
In corner detection, features are initially nothing but pixel coordinates in the image,
where the corner conditions are met. When moving the camera, these features will move
in the image plane as well, but depending on the world points that gives rise to the
features, each feature might produce a different movement vector, depending on the
exact camera movement. In the simplest setting, this allow the use of triangulation
to estimate the 3D ‘world’ position of key features, and thus also estimate the camera
movement.

3.1
Detection
The best algorithm for detecting features greatly depend on the application, and no
general rule can be made for this. Several of the algorithms mentioned in Table 3.1 has
proved to be suitable for 6 Degrees of Freedon (DoF) indoor motion tracking, but the
execution time of these varies greatly. Because the algorithms are meant to be running
on low-performañce, onboard hardware, two interchangeable and wellperforming corner-
detectors have been selected, and will be compared for this application, namely the
FAST and GFTT Feature Detectors.
FAST
“Features from Accelerated Segment Test” (FAST) by Rosten & Drummond [RD05] was
mainly chosen because of the fast execution time.
A corner strength estimate for a
feature is calculated using the intensity level at the core pixel (IC), and compared against
a circular ring of (usually) 16 pixels (corresponding to a radius of 3 pixels). Pixels in
the ring (IX) are classified as either Brighter than, Equal to or Darker than IC.
IC + t < IX →Brighter
IC −t ≤IX ≤IC + t →Equal
IC −t > IX →Darker
If at least N contiguous pixels are either darker or brighter by a threshold t, it is
considered a FAST feature. The number of contiguous features, N, is often set to 12,
since this is equal to a 90◦corner, but anything between 9 and 16 should work, 9 being
a slightly curved line and 16 being a small dot.
A Speeded-Up version of FAST can be implemented, which will first compare the
intensity of four even-distanced pixels in the ring (e.g. pixel 1, 5, 9 and 13). At least
three of these must be of the same class for the point to be classified as a corner, given
N ≥9. The point is not of interest if there are less than 9 contiguous pixels of the same
class.
Although classified as a corner detector, FAST doesn’t produce a real corner measure,
and using the ring to find corners will sometimes yield false positives along edges too,
especially if they are slightly curved or blurred. There are several ways to reduce this
problem, e.g. by iteratively increasing t or sorting for the highest values of t, such that
each feature still passes the corner criteria. Another approach is to find and sort the

pixels that qualifies as a corner from their cornerness measure. Removing all but the
strongest contestant often reduce the number of ‘flickering’ features, i.e. features that lie
in the proximity of the threshold limit, and thus only sometimes qualifies as a feature in
the corner measure. It doubles as a method to spread features a bit more evenly across
the image, which (as we will learn in Chapter 5) helps lowering the noise ratio in an
otherwise noisy environment.
Other Detectors
Initially, a third detector type was investigated as well, which was a combination of
the Harris Corner Detector, Fast Features for Motion Estimation1 (FFME) and a bit of
home brew. It turned out that it is a tough task to come up with something better than
the bulletproof options already mentioned, and the idea was discarded.
Feature Precision
Both the FAST and GFTT features are nothing but integer pixel locations in the image.
This causes rounding errors, since a feature in the world is not limited in precision,
while the camera must sample the entire field of view using only the available resolution.
Figure 3.2 illustrates (a) a corner as it looks in the real world, and (b) the digitized
result.
When moving the camera just a bit, a feature might remain located in the same pixel
instead of moving to an adjacent pixel – this can be problematic and regarded as an
integer rounding error, adding to the noise in the system.
To reduce the effect of this problem, the position of many features can be found with
sub-pixel precision, by using the pixel gradient in a window around the location of the
suggested corner position. Table 3.3 shows the coordinates of two features in Figure 3.4

Description and Matching
Since features produce different movement vectors when the camera moves, using only
feature coordinates is not enough to track features from one image to another. One way
to deal with this problem is to create a description of each feature, just like one would
describe a person or a building.
Feature description is essentially a way to reduce the amount of (often redundant)
data, and extract the distinguisable data associated with a feature. This dimensionality
reduction can be used to match features between images, using a matching algorithm.

Description

3.5
Synthetic Features
Implementating and testing the motion estimation algorithms is not a trivial task, and
using features from a noisy camera in an unknown world makes it complicated to verify
the correctness of the implementation. To eliminate most sources of noise, feature points
can instead be generated from artificial data, based on contructed ground truth.
The artificial data consists of a cloud of fictive feature points in the world space
combined with a set of camera poses, which includes translation and rotation. For any
given camera pose, all of the features can be projected to the camera’s image plane
(given that they are visible to the camera, i.e. not behind it), which is essentially the
same as finding features in a regular image. There is no need to track the features, since
their image coordinates are all projected from the cloud of points, and each feature will
thus have the same index as its projective origin.
To find the features projected to the image plane of a camera, the rotation matrix
from the initial pose to the camera pose must first be constructed.

CHAPTER 4
Movement Model
This chapter will explain the transformation process from matched feature pairs to move-
ment with 6 degrees of freedom. Initially, it is a procedure to estimate the Fundamental
Matrix or Essential Matrix, depending on the algorithm and the feature source. This will
yield a combination of four viable solutions in terms of rotation and translation, where
only one can be valid. Validation happens by triangulating one or more feature matches,
and only the valid solution will have the feature in front of the camera in both images.
Knowing which combination is valid, the rotation and translation can be extracted, and
used in chapter 5, where the camera motion tracking happens.

eliminating some of the headaches that follows the 8-Point Algorithm.

Reflection
In addition to two possible rotation matrices, the fundamental matrix will sometimes
return the rotation as a reflection of the actual rotation, meaning that the rotations
around X and Z are inverted (±180◦). The feature triangulation might still work, but
everything else will yield false results, including the world position of the feature. The
rotation is seen as a reflection because the matrix is negative, and R = R · (−1) will fix
it.
If the Z-axis is ever rotated by 90◦or more (between consequtive images), the pro-
gram will automatically attempt to fix it, and test if the X- and Y -axes are also fixed,
and discard the result if any axes are rotated by more a threshold, e.g. 22.5◦(π/8).

4.4
Summary
Transforming matched feature pairs into movement with 6 degrees of freedom can be
done using the Fundamental or Essential Matrix, both describing the relationship be-
tween two images taken of the same scene with different viewports. There are various
algorithms for estimating these matrices, and in this project, the improved eight-point
algorithm and the five-point algorithm have been tested. Both result in two rotation
matrices and two translation vectors and the valid combination of rotation and trans-
lation is found by triangulating the features. This can be problematic if the drone has
been rotating, with little to no translation, since the triangulation parameters will be
almost identical, and their location very imprecise.

5.1
Keyframes
Even if the hardware were capable of real-time movement estimation for each video-
frame, the result would be very poor compared to skipping several frames.
This is
due to the movement estimation being dependent on the translation vector, and if the
drone has only moved a short distance, the estimate will suffer from poor accuracy, as
mentioned in Section 4.3. To make sure a certain translation has happened, the concept
of keyframes are introduced.
With the camera pointing in the flight direction, the features in the image will move
towards the edges. Those close to the optical axis will seem almost stationary, since they
origin from a point in the distance, while those getting closer to the edges will seem to
move with an exponentially increasing speed. Using the average feature movement for
keyframe detection is thus a poor choice, since the outcome highly depends on the ratio
of near-center vs. non-center features.
A mask can be applied, to ignore all near-center features, and the distance required to
find a new keyframe is more consistent. The RMS distance has proven to work well, and
if the average movement threshold is denoted t and N is the number of active features,

5.2
World Scale Factor
As mentioned in Chapter 4, the motion estimate contain the exact rotation as well as
a unitary translation vector. Being unitary means, that no matter how far (if at all)
the drone has moved, the euclidean translation will always be 1. Each new keyframe
therefore requires a scale factor, in order to estimate the actual drone translation.
While the camera sees a scaleless world, it is not very different from what the human
eye does, except the human will always make an assumption of the scale, based on visible
objects in the scene. This concept can be transferred to the camera picture as well, and
is used to determine the initial scale assumption.
There are thus two different scenarios with two corresponding procedures: A known
object is visible, and the scale can be computed using the true distances between rec-
ognizable features on the object – if no known objects are visible, the scale must be
calculated from the existing feature position estimates, i.e. previously estimated scale
factors.

QR codes turn out to be good for scale estimation, since there are three markers

Subsequent Scale Factors
When no known objects are visible, the scale computation relies on the origin of features
from previous matches, which will be discussed in more detail in Section 5.3.
The
computation is similar to the visible object, but instead of knowing the distance between
two or more points, the distance between two sets of randomly selected features are used.
Since all feature origins are estimated using triangulation, there are a much higher
level of noise involved with this procedure. This is especially true since the most reliable
features are those close to the camera edges, and because the procedure relies on data
from subsequent matches, many of the good features are no longer visible.
In the attempt to suppress the noise as much as possible, two large sets of randomly
selected features have been selected, to provide a large amount of proposals for the
scale. Some of the proposals are classified as outliers, and can be as much as an order of
magnitude higher or lower than the actual scale factor, and mean value is thus no option
here. Instead, the median has been used, since it is computationally inexpensive, and is
great for suppressing the outliers, given that the scales resembles a gaussian distribution.

6.3
Multithreading
The current implementation does not support concurrent operations, but due to the way
modules are constructed, it is trivial task to make it possible. Since the Raspberry Pi 2
features a quad-core CPU, it would be sensible to run user interaction, image aquisition
and pose estimation on separate cores, to minimize the latency when heavy operations
are carried out.
This can easily be done using network sockets, such that the image acquisition and
pose estimation parts essentially behave like servers. The acquisition server would feed
images to its client(s), while the pose estimation server can return movement information
given the feature pairs.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
During these studies, it has been shown that visual navigation is possible, using only
image features from the video stream of a monocular camera.
While there are countless ways to obtain features and track them, two feature de-
tectors and two methods of tracking features have been selected and tested. The FAST
feature detector is designed to compute features quickly, but unfortunately they also
tend to be more clustered than Good Features To Track. To track the features between
images, the ORB feature descriptor has been chosen as a candidate, and while it can
detect features even if they have been temporarily out of sight, it has a significantly
higher error rate than the optical flow method, selected as the second candidate.
To estimate the motion from matching feature pairs, the Essential Matrix has been
studied and used to extract the two rotation matrices and two unitary translation vectors.
The valid combination of these are found by feature triangulation, and only in one of
the four combinations will the features be in front of the camera in both images.
The complete system is capable of estimating the camera motion with only a small
degree of uncertainty, often less than 10%. This have been demonstrated by a series of
experiments, described in detail in Chapter 7. As could have been expected, it turns
out that lateral translation (e.g. forward movement using a down-facing camera) is a
bit more precise than pointing a camera in the direction of flight, since the features
move longer and in a more uniform way, and thus ensures a better precision of feature
locations. Depth translation still have the advantage of seeing the things in front of it,
which can later be used to avoid collisions as well.
Future Work
These are just the initial studies for autonomous drone localization, and the algorithms
and hardware must be refined before they can be used in a real setting.
The Microsoft LifeCam have been the sole cause of a lot of problems, and, in retro-
spect, a camera with documentation should have been chosen from the start.
==PAGE:64==
56
8 Conclusion
Aside from a few hardware considerations, the following is a list of the short-term as
well as long-term goals for the future development:
KeyFrame Detection If this step could estimate the axes of translation, a better
keyframe threshold could be set, to ensure keyframes are detected with optimal
timing.
Feature Triangulation The origin of new features are triangulated as soon as they
are matched among two images. This causes image noise to have a great impact
on those features lying in the far distance, since an uncertainty of 1 pixel in the
image can make a difference of several hundreds or thousand millimeters in the
distance measure. They should thus only be triangulated once they have made a
regular movement between two images, such that the triangulation parameters are
precise to a certain degree.
Feature Descriptors The system should include feature redetection, in case the ma-
jority of features have been temporarily out of sight. The software should use
descriptions of visible features as a fallback method until next keyframe, where
optical flow tracking can resume.
QR Code Information Reading the actual QR code information can provide informa-
tion about location of the code, which can be used to update the position estimate
from, and thus suppress the drift introduced.
Sensor Fusion Since a drones usually carry an IMU for stabilization, the data could
as well be used for sensor fusion with the camera.
Scene Recognition The drift can additionally be supressed if the drone is able to
recognize previously seen scenes, and thus link old features in the database to
those currently visible – This is also referred to as loop-closing.

APPENDIX B
Description of Software
Modules
The Main Module instantiates all other modules and handles the user inputs using a
menu-like navigation. This way, it is possible to control what kind of features (FAST,
GFTT or Synthetic) to use, what kind of tracking and the like.
The Debug Module is for creating formatted logs. It features only three public meth-
ods, Debug::P(module, type, format, *arguments), such that it mimics printf(...)
but with two extra arguments. Sample output:
Debug::P(CORE, WARNING, N̈o Camera Connected)̈;
→[CORE][WARN] No Camera Connected
The log is colored using VT100 terminal commands, and the colors makes it easy to
locate certain events in the log without searching for it.
The Camera Module controls everything related to the image. It is possible to adjust
focus, brightness and exposure time via the GUI, as well as image size and color scale
via the menu. Functions exist to control the image buffer and request next image.
The Synthetic Data Module is used to generate noiseless features for testing purposes.
Given a set of 3D features, the module calculates the projection of these features to the
image plane described by R and t. No tracking of features is necessary, since the features
are created from indexed 3D points, and thus the order is always the same.
The Movement Modelling Module requires an input image, and can extract a list of
features from it. Provided subsequent images, it tracks the features, and estimates the
corresponding Essential Matrix.
The Position Module verifies the rotation and translation results and estimates scale
and thus the actual movement.
The position module also keeps track of the global
position.
