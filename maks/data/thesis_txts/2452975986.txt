Abstract
The positron range of high energetic positrons limits the spatial resolution in
PET imaging. The decreased spatial resolution is especially problematic when
using a high energy radiotracer like Rubidium-82 (82Rb) for assessment of heart
disease in small animals. The aim of this master thesis is to increase the spatial
resolution of 82Rb PET images. The mathematical correction of the positron range
incorporates knowledge about the tissue and the emitted positron into the iterative
image reconstruction.
The positron range correction (PRC) is implemented as an interplay between
STIR and MATLAB. STIR is an open source software developed for PET recon-
struction. The image quality of the reconstructions is evaluated on a NEMA NU 4
2008 inspired phantom scanned with FDG and 82Rb and an in vivo 82Rb rat scan
using Siemens Inveon pre-clinical PET scanner.
The eﬀects of PRC depend on the isotope and the number of iterations. PRC
does not aﬀect the FDG reconstructions, whereas the spatial resolution improves
for both phantom and rat 82Rb reconstructions.
Resumé
Positroner med høj energi medfører dårlig spatial opløsning ved PET-rekonstruktion.
Skanning med radiotraceren 82Rb, til vurdering af hjertesygdomme hos gnavere til
præklinisk forskning og udvikling, er et eksempel på et radioaktivt sporstof med
høj energi. Formålet med denne opgave er at forbedre den spatielle opløsning for
PET-rekonstruktioner, optaget ved det radioaktive sporstof 82Rb. Dette kan op-
nås gennem en matematisk korrektion, som inkorporerer data om vævet samt den
udsendte positron i den iterative rekonstruktionsproces.
Den matematiske korrektion af positronslængde (positron range correction,
PRC) er implementeret med to programmer STIR og MATLAB. STIR er et open
source software til rekonstruktion af PET-optagelser. Den spatielle opløsning af
de rekonstruerede billeder er evalueret på et NEMA NU 4 2008 inspireret fantom
samt en 82Rb skanning af rotte med hjerte infarkt på Siemens Inveon prækliniske
PET-skanner.
Konklusionen viser, at eﬀekten af PRC afhænger af valg af isotop samt an-
tal iterationer. PRC ændrer ikke ved kvaliteten af rekonstruktionerne for FDG-
optagelserne, hvorimod billedkvaliteten forbedres signiﬁkant for rekonstruktion-
erne med 82Rb.

Introduction
1.1
Clinical motivation
Cardiovascular Disease (CVD) caused by atherosclerosis is the most frequent cause
of death in the world [1]. In Denmark approximately 17.000 deaths per year are
ascribed atherosclerosis, corresponding to one third of all deaths [2]. The coronary
arteries are blood vessels providing the myocardium with oxygenated blood. Coro-
nary Artery Disease (CAD) covers heart disease caused by reduction of blood ﬂow
in the coronary arteries due to build-up of plaque in the artery wall [3]. A plaque
in the artery wall disrupts the blood ﬂow and results in reduced blood delivery to
the myocardial cells. Ischmia occurs when the oxygen demand is higher than the
oxygen provided.
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a noninvasive method for diagnosis,
therapy follow up, drug discovery as well as clinical research in various ﬁelds of
medical science. PET enables in vivo visualization of tracer distribution in phys-
iological processor. Molecular imaging reﬂects the physiological changes whereas
modalities such as CT and MR most often reﬂect anatomy. Hybrid PET imaging
with a co-registered CT or MR image is an advantageous method of combin-
ing anatomy and physiology and is routinely used in research and medical prac-
tice. Molecular imaging is a vital part of clinical and pre-clinical cardiovascular
medicine.
Cardiac PET studies can involve two examinations:
Myocardial Perfusion
Imaging (MPI) and myocardial viability test. MPI measures the myocardial per-
fusion on a cellular level whereas the viability test measures the metabolism of
the cardiac cells. Both tests provide important information of the function and
prognosis of the cardiac disease. Diﬀerent tracers are used for MPI and viability
testing. The most often used tracer for viability test is a radioactive labeled glucose
molecule, 2-deoxy-2-(18F)ﬂuoro-D-glucose (FDG), whereas Rubidium-82 (82Rb) is
a common perfusion tracer for MPI. 82Rb has multiple advantageous properties. It

1.2
Aim of the thesis
Enhancing spatial resolution for PET imaging with high PR isotopes would open
up new possibilities for PET imaging. Consequently, positron estimation and cor-
rection has been a research area for decades.
The goal of the thesis is to implement a mathematical correction for the PR.
The motivation and approach are inspired by the results presented by Cal-González
et al.[7, 8, 9, 10]. A study performed by Cal-González el al. from 2015 [9] showed
the eﬀect of in vivo positron range correction (PRC) in PET imaging with 68Ga-
DOTATOC. PRC is a method for remodeling the tracer distribution to the sta-
tistical location of the annihilation event by taking the density of the tissue and

The mathematical correction can not be incorporated directly in the reconstruc-
tion software connected to the PET scanner, because the reconstruction software
is provided as a closed platform, which do not allow code modiﬁcations.
An-

mathematical correction suggested by Cal-González is implemented by use of the
program Software for Tomographic Image Reconstruction (STIR) [11].

• Compute blurring kernels for the mathematical correction of the PR as pro-

• Incorporate PRC within iterative image reconstruction using STIR
• Evaluate the reconstructed images on a rodent sized NEMA NU 4 2008

Theory
2.1
The cardiac system
The human heart is approximatly the size of a closed ﬁst and weights approx-
imately 250 grams for females and 300 grams males [12]. The heart is divided
into a left and right side, which is each subdivided into the ventricle and atrium,
illustrated to the left in Figure 2.1. The main function of the heart is to pump
blood throughout the body and supply oxygen and nutrition to the tissues. The
heart circulates the blood in two circuits: the pulmonary and the systemic cir-
cuit. The pulmonary circuit oxygenates the blood and expel carbon dioxide, and
the systemic circuit provides oxygenated blood to the tissues and return the de-
oxygenated blood to the heart. The oxygenated blood is ejected via aorta by a
contraction of the left ventricle. The ventricular wall of the left ventricle is thicker
than the ventricular wall of the right ventricle due to the higher pressure required
in the systemic circuit. The cardiac muscle is called the myocardium, and the
cardiac cell are called myocytes. The coronary arteries are located on the outside
of the heart, illustrated to the right in Figure 2.1. The coronary arteries are re-
sponsible of the perfusion of the myocardium and the delivery of oxygen to the
myocytes.
2.1.1
Impairment of blood ﬂow in the heart
Reduced blood ﬂow to the myocardium results in myocarrdial ischemia, which
hampers the pumping function of the heart. A sudden or serve occlusion of a
coronary artery can result in myocardial infarction, whereas a minor occlusion
might cause abnormal heart rhythms. The duration and severity of the coronary
occlusion inﬂuence functionality and recovery prognosis of the myocardium. CAD
is the most common CVD and is manifested by a progressively occlusion of the
coronary arteries caused by atherosclerosis.
The development of atherosclerosis starts with cell injury in the endothelium,
which is followed by migration of inﬂammatory cells, proliferation of smooth mus-

of myocardial ischemia might be contractile dysfunction without necrosis, and the
ultimately consequence is irreversible myocardial necrosis [14].

2.1.2
Cardiac PET
Cardiac imaging with PET enables estimation of the cardiac cells function, per-
fusions defect, the coronary blood ﬂow and ejection fraction.
Nuclear cardiac
imaging involves MPI and viability testing.
MPI reveals the amount of oxygenated blood delivered to the myocytes and
is used for diagnosis and risk assessment. MPI with 82Rb is commonly used and
the test is performed in rest and stress. The rest testing shows the blood ﬂow
in normal physiological setting, whereas the stress test is to imitate high physio-
logical activity. This is because the cardiac cells require more oxygen in activity.
The stress test is induced pharmacologic with adenosine. 82Rb PET imaging co-
registered with a CT image of the heart allows detection and evaluation of CAD
by the changes in blood perfusion between cardiac rest and stress
Viability testing with FDG is considered the gold standard for evaluation myocar-
dial viability. A viability test shows if a cardiac cell is alive, dead or in hibernation.
FDG imaging uses glucose to show which of the cardiac cells are metabolic active
[15].
When combining the information in one patient for FDG and 82Rb, a full di-
agnosis of the heart is possible. If there is a perfusion defect on the MPI, but
the viability test shows metabolic activity, the myocytes in the defect areas are
in hibernation. This means that the perfusion defect has not yet damaged the
myocytes to the extent of necrosis. Therefor the cardiac cells in this area can be
salvage with treatment. If there is a perfusion defect on the MPI and the viability
test shows no activity, the area is dead and no salvage is possible. The information
that can be derived from cardiac PET is crucial and vital for the correct diagnosis
and treatment of cardiac patients.
The spatial resolution of the PET image is degraded when using 82Rb, due to its
large PR. The diagnostic accuracy of MPI of the human heart is not aﬀected neg-
atively by the degraded resolution when using 82Rb [4]. Cardiac imaging of small
animals is on the other hand highly inﬂuenced by the decreased resolution, which
hampers precise estimation of the regional tracer distribution in the myocardium
[4].
2.2
Physics of radioactive decay
Isotopes are atoms with the same number of protons in the nucleus but with a
varying number of neutrons. Isotopes are chemically identical since the number
==PAGE:15==
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
7
of electrons and protons are equal for all isotopes even though the number of
neutrons changes. The actual mass of the isotope and the mass attributed by
protons, neutrons and electrons can diﬀer. This diﬀerence in mass is called the
mass defect, which covers the ’missing mass’ in the atom, and represents the energy
released during the formation of the atom [16]. Einstein’s equation relating energy
to mass via the speed of light states that energy and mass can be converted from
one form into the other, but never be created nor destroyed:
E = m · c2
(2.1)
The missing energy in the atom is termed the binding energy and is computed
using E = ∆m · c2, where ∆m is the mass defect, c the speed of light and E is
the energy. The binding energy, and thereby the mass defect, are generally larger
for more massive nuclides, without being more tidily bound. For that reason, the
binding energy is often considered as the binding energy per nucleon.
In general, the total mass number and the ratio of neutrons to protons de-
termine if a nuclide is radioactive or stable. Radioactive decay is a spontaneous
process in which the atom changes its number of protons and neutrons which re-
sults in a decreased inherent energy in the daughter atom [17]. The binding energy
per nucleon in the daughter atom is higher than the binding energy per nucleon
in its parents atom, indicating that the daughter atom is more stable than the
parent atom. Thus, radioactive decay is a process in which the atoms become
more stable due to increased binding energy per nucleon. Radioactivity is the rate
of atoms undergoing radioactive decay per second. This means radioactivity does
not reﬂect the type of radiation nor its energy. The number of radioactive atoms
(N) declines exponentially with time:
N(t) = N0 · e
−
t
T1/2
(2.2)
where T1/2 is a time constant representing the mean lifetime of the decaying atoms
and N0 is the number of radioactive atoms at time zero. There are four main
modes for radioactive decay: alpha, beat-minus, beta-plus and gamma. Medical
PET imaging are only concerned with the beta-plus (β+) decay, which is given by:
p →n + β+ + ν
(2.3)
where β+ is a positron, which is the antiparticle of an electron, p is a proton, n a
neutron and ν is a neutrino.
2.2.1
Annihilation
Mutual annihilation occurs when the positron meets a free electron. The energy
equivalent to an electron and positron is computed from Equation 2.1 to 511 keV

each. The energy and momentum are conserved, which means the annihilation
of the electron and positron results in two 511 keV annihilation photons emitted
with an angle of approximately 180°. The angle between the annihilation photons
might diﬀer by ±0.25°due to conservation of the momentum. The error induced
in PET imaging by this phenomena is known as non-colinearity [17].
The path the emitted positron travels before annihilation depends on its initial
energy and the electron density of the medium in which it is emitted. If the initial
energy of the emitted positron is larger than the allowed energy for annihilation
the positron will loss energy through inelastic interactions with orbit electrons in
the medium. The positron will annihilate with an electron when the positron has
decreased its energy [18]. An electron dense medium will cause closely located
interactions and a short trajectory before annihilation. Contrary, a less electron
dense medium will have larger distances between the interactions and a larger
trajectory before annihilation. The PR is the direct distance from the location of
the decay to the location of the annihilation event as shown in Figure 2.2.

The annihilation gives rise to two 511 keV photons emitted with an angle of
approximately 180°. Positron range is the distance between the radioactive
decay and the annihilation.

PET acquisition and data
PET imaging can roughly be divided into three steps: The ﬁrst step is the data
acquisition, followed by data manipulation and image reconstruction. These three
steps are the topics of the following sections. The simpliﬁed procedure for PET
imaging is illustrated in Figure 2.3. The ﬁrst two steps in the PET imaging process
involve the PET imaging system, the data acquisition, the data orientation and
corrections elaborated in section 2.3. The transition from the acquired data to the
reconstructed image via iterative reconstruction is described in section 2.4.
Figure 2.3 Procedure form the imaging system to the ﬁnal reconstructed
PET image. Inspired by [19].
2.3.1
Data acquisition
A PET scanner consists of multiple detector rings containing thousands of small
scintillation crystals arranged in detector blocks. The patient is located in the
center of the detector rings. The detectors are scintillation crystals designed for
rapid detection of 511 keV photons. The scintillation crystals consists of several el-
ements, which in short detects the emitted photons by converting them into visible
light which is converted into electrical pulses by photomultiplier tubes. Modern
scintillation crystals are often made of lutetium oxyorthosilicate (LSO) or bismuth
germanate (BGO) which are materials with high attenuation coeﬃcient at 511
keV, high light output and a short decay time [16, 18].
If two detector crystals register two photons almost simultaneously the pho-
tons are registered as a coincident event and the line between the two detectors
are called the Line of Response (LOR). This is however an ideal detection case.
Realistic PET acquisition detects scattered and attenuated photons. Five diﬀer-
ent detection events are displayed in Figure 2.4, where only the true coincidences
contain valid image information.

The scattered and random coincidences add noise to the emission data because
the photons were not emitted along the detected LOR. Detection of a scattered
coincidence occur if one or both of the two emitted photons undergo Compton
scattering. Compton scattering changes the trajectory of the photon which results
in an energy decrease. The random coincidence is a detection of two photon arising
from distinct locations emitted almost instantaneously. LORs can not be assigned
to detection of a single photon or more than two photons, and these events are
not stored in PET acquisition. The following section presents how to correct for
detection events without valid image information.
2.3.2
Data corrections
The detectors are not able to distinguish true, scatter and random coincident
events, resulting in all three detection events are stored as prompt events although
scatter and randoms do not contain valid image information. The emission data is
corrected prior to the image reconstruction through attenuation correction, scatter
correction and detector normalization.
Attenuation correction
The loss of detection of true events because of absorption and scattering is called
attenuation. The extent of photon attenuation depends on the path and the density
of the medium. The probability of both annihilation photons are emitted through
==PAGE:19==
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
11
the object is computed as the product of the individual probability of each photon
(p1 and p2) [18]:
p1 × p2 = e−µx × e−µ(D−x) = e−µD
(2.4)
this means the attenuation only depends on the total thickness D of the object
along the LOR and the attenuation coeﬃcient µ. The estimation of attenuation
and its correction is straightforward when having a co-registered transmission im-
age. An 511 keV attenuation map is created from the transmission image of the
object by measuring the attenuation factor along each LOR, and it reﬂects the
density diﬀerence throughout the body. The attenuation correction decreases the
noise and increase spatial resolution in the reconstructed PET image [18].
Scatter correction
Scatter correction can be modeled as photons scattered by Compton interactions.
The scatter distribution can be estimated from a transmission attenuation map
and an initial estimate of the activity. The amount of scatter in the acquired data
depends on several factors including the detection energy window. A lower and
upper discrimination value are used during data acquisition, which decreases the
number of detected scatter events, because a photon undergoing Compton interac-
tions before detection will have lower energy compared to a non-scattered photon.
Another possibility is the use of axial collimators, which shields the detector blocks
from axial oblique photons but results in signiﬁcantly lower count rate.
Detector normalization
The normalization data reﬂects the individual sensitivity of the detectors, which
diﬀers substantially between the detector elements. The normalization ﬁle is a
sinogram and is measured by scanning an uniform source of known activity. The
normalization correction of the raw emission data ensures uniform detector sensi-
tivity.
The last type of detection events mentioned in Figure 2.4 is the random coinci-
dence which originates from uncorrelated photons emitted almost simultaneously
and accidentally being detected as coincidence photons [20]. The detection of ran-
dom events is decreased by applying a small time window, because the number of
detected random events depends linearly on the coincidence window τ:
R = 2τS1S2
(2.5)
where S1 and S2 are count rates of singles in a pair of detectors. Detection of ran-
dom and scatter events leads to decreased image quality and should be minimized
through the design of the detector and acquisition settings.

2.3.3
Data storage
The detected emission events can be stored in two ways: list mode and histogram-
based sinogram mode [18]. The main diﬀerence between list mode and sinogram
storing is how the emission data are presented. The data stored in list-mode is
a list containing information about all the prompt events. Each item in the list
contains information linked to the detection e.g. the location of the two detectors
and the energies of the detected photons [16].
Normally, the PET scanner initially saves the coincidence events as list-mode data
and then converts it into a sinogram after the data acquisition. One advantage
of the list-mode format is it allows greater potential for data correction than the
sinogram. The sinogram is created by grouping the items in the list-mode data
into multidimensional arrays in a process called histogramming.

2.4
Image reconstruction
The imaging system can be represented by the following linear relationship:
p = H · f + n
(2.8)
where p is the known projections, f is the unknown image, H is the system model
and n is the noise present in the measurements [21]. The noise can be modeled as
Poisson or Gaussian probability functions [18]. The goal of image reconstruction
is to compute the image from the measured projections. The data values can be
==PAGE:23==
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
15
considered either as deterministic or stochastic [21].
The image solution is exact if the data are assumed deterministic, this is termed
analytic image reconstruction. Analytic reconstruction is a fast, direct mathemat-
ical solution to the inverse problem stated in Equation 2.8. On the other hand this
simpliﬁed solution give rise to images with reduced resolution and poor noise prop-
erties. An exact solution for the image does not exist in stochastic data modeling
due to the random noise. For that reason, an estimation method is introduced,
which is solved iteratively. Iterative reconstruction algorithms are ﬂexible allowing
incorporation of image formation models to correct for physical image degradation
processor e.g. attenuation and scattering [18]. The main disadvantage for iterative
reconstruction compared to analytic is the large computation time.

The following is an elaboration of some of the parameters in the parameter ﬁle.
The STIR wiki FAQ homepage contains a list of suggestions for appropriate setting
for PET reconstruction. The settings suggested concern the projector pair type
used during the reconstruction, number of subsets and regularization methods.
Matched projectors are suggested for iterative reconstructions. The default
STIR setting is not a set oﬀmatched projectors, but a combination of ray trac-
ing for forward projection and an incremental interpolating backprojector. This
backprojector is known to trigger reconstruction problems in STIR, and is therefor
avoided in this work. This work rely on a set of matched projector, instead. The
fastest matched projector is the matrix type, hence ray tracing matrix is set as the
projector type in the parameter ﬁle (see example in Appendix 7.5). The setting
number of rays in tangential direction to trace for each bin is set to 10 instead
of the default value 1. This change inﬂuences the sensitivity estimate due to the
reduction in discretisation artefacts in the sensitivity image. A value of 1 might
introduce noise and a bias, which we seek to avoid by increasing the number of
rays per bin [28].
The number of subsets is a trade-oﬀbetween reconstruction time and image
quality: A large number of subsets allow fast reconstruction by results in decreased
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 3. METHODS
23
image quality. The projectors in STIR use symmetries and it inﬂuences the num-
ber of subsets available. Hence the number of subsets must divide the number of
views into an integer number. E.g if the number of views is 160 STIR will accept
80, 40, 20, 10, 8, 4 and 2 subsets.
Concerning which regularization to use, the answer depends on the image.
Inter-iteration ﬁltering and penalised reconstruction are suggested as ways to im-
prove image quality, and mixing regularisation methods are not recommended [28].
SITR has 3 options for the prior: None, Quadratic Prior (QP) and a Median Root
Prior (MRP). The prior and the penalisation factor are added to the loglikelihood
function as:
Log-Poisson-likelihood + penalisation_factor * prior
The prior is image-dependent and the loglikelihood is proportional to the projected
data. The QP uses the squared diﬀerence between the estimated and the measured
projections. This means the prior is proportional to the square of the projection
data. The MRP is independent of the number of counts contrary to the QP. On
the other hand, the MRP depends on sensitivity and attenuation according to the
STIR Guide [29].

included in Appendix section 7.6 and section 7.7.

PRC is implemented in MATLAB via the STIR utilities forward_project and
back_project. The forward projection function projects an image into projection
space, and the backprojection function back projects the projections into the im-
age space. The combined STIR and MATLAB solution is referred to a mSTIR,
and it can reconstruct PET images with and without PRC. The implementation

The implementation of PRC requires known scanner sensitivity, initial image,

number of segments included in the sinogram, the number of subsets and the num-
ber of rays for each bin. The initial image is a volume the size of the reconstructed
image. The initial image volume does only contains ones in this implementation.

with the blurring kernels. The blurring kernels are computed using Equation 2.16
and the corresponding code is displayed in Appendix 7.3. The dimensions of the
blurring kernels are odd, which makes the center pixel of the kernel volume the
maximum value.
The size of each blurring kernel is two times the maximum
range. The blurring kernel for FDG is 9x9x9 pixels and the 82Rb blurring kernel
is 67x67x67 pixels. The density of air is very small, which means the PR for high
energy isotopes like 82Rb exceeds the scanner dimensions. The image width of
the Inveon scanner is approximately 10 cm, and a 82Rb air kernel of that size
would only model the very beginning of the annihilation distribution. A blurring
kernel for modeling the annihilations in air would be enormous and increase the
computational time. The scanned activity is not present in the air in the setup,
and with that prior knowledge the electron density of air is increased to 0.4 to
obtain modeling of the annihilations in air with smaller blurring kernels.

The image quality of the reconstructions are evaluated on a small phantom devel-

thesis. The phantom is inspired by the NEMA NU 4 2008 mouse standard image
quality phantom [30]. The phantom is made of polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA)
and consist of three diﬀerent regions, which are used for estimating the three im-
age quality parameters. A sketch of the phantom is displayed in Figure 3.4. Areas
marked in red represent areas of radioactive ﬂuid, the blue and white areas rep-
resent containers of water and air, respectively.

The region located under the phantom outlet in Figure 3.4 consists of ﬁve
ﬁllable rods of diﬀerent diameters. The diameter of the ﬁllable rods vary from 1
mm to 5 mm and each rod is 20 mm long. This region is called the rod region, and
the Recovery Coeﬃcient (RC) is computed from this region. The next regions is
the uniform region, which is one ﬁllable region with a diameter of 30 mm. The
mean uniformity and its standard deviation are computed from this region, which
are used for estimating the Percentage Standard Deviation in the uniform region
(%SDU). The last region consists of two containers surrounded by radioactive ﬂuid.
The containers are called cold since they due not contain radioactive materials.
The cold areas contain water and air, respectively. This region is called the cold
region, and the Spillover Ratio (SOR) is computed from this region.

Results
The results are based upon two PET scans of the image quality phantom using
FDG and 82Rb with a start dose of 9.15 MBq and 688.2 MBq, respectively. FDG
was injected into the phantom hours before image acquisition to ensure a more
homogeneous activity distribution. The volume of 82Rb injected into the phantom
exceeds the phantom volume and the excess is collected at the phantom outlet.
Both acquisitions were performed in 10 minutes. Furthermore, a 82Rb in vivo PET
scan of a rat was performed with an injected dose of 63.0 MBq.
The results are grouped into 3 sections. The ﬁrst section examines the inﬂu-
ence of reconstruction parameters for the iterative reconstruction. The following
section serves as a validation of the combined STIR and MATLAB reconstruction
platform mSTIR, as well as a comparison between Inveon’s and the mSTIR’s re-
constructions. The last section examines the eﬀect of PRC for both phantom and
in vivo reconstructions.
4.1

The reconstructions in this section are created using STIR’s iterative reconstruc-
tion algorithm OSMAPOSL. The images are reconstructed using a quadratic prior
with penalization factor of 0.01 and with the inclusion of 9 sinogram segments.

Discussion
5.1
Reconstruction parameters
Reconstructions are obtained using the well-documented STIR reconstruction al-
gorithm OSMAPOSL. The reconstruction parameters analyzed are the number of
subsets, iterations and segments included in the reconstruction. The motivation
for using subsets (OSEM) instead of the full data set (MLEM) was to decrease
reconstruction time. The analysis of the subsets with respect to reconstruction
time shows that the time per iteration decreases with the introduction of subsets,
as expected [16].
The number of iterations clearly inﬂuence the image quality measurements. It
is well known that the noise in the image increases with high iteration numbers
[32], which corresponds to the observation of the %SDU for both FDG and 82Rb.
The %SDU in the FDG reconstructions increases after 9 iterations, whereas the
%SDU for the 82Rb reconstruction with 10 and 20 subsets ﬂuctuates. The am-
plitude of the %SDU ﬂuctuations increases with increasing iteration number, and
the ﬂuctuations demonstrate the same periodicity in the investigated number of
iterations. A possible explanation of the ﬂuctuations might be, that each recon-
struction iteration use the previous image as initial estimate, and a perturbation of
the image estimate and a potential correction might cause the ﬂuctuation pattern.
This might be an OSMAPOSL feature for handling noisy data, since the ﬂuctuating
of %SDU are only discovered when reconstruction 82Rb with STIR’s OSMAPOSL al-
gorithm. A disadvantage of OSEM pointed out by Bailey [16] is that OSEM tend
to cycle between slightly diﬀerent image estimates which might also explain the
ﬂuctuations in %SDU for the 82Rb reconstruction with 10 and 20 subsets and not
for the MLEM 82Rb reconstruction.
An increase in number of iterations provides smaller SOR for both water and
air. All four SORs decrease exponentially with the iteration number, but the SOR
for 82Rb in water diﬀers from the other SORs by its less steep decrease. This ob-
servation can be explained by the electron density in the cold regions. A medium
==PAGE:53==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
45
with high electron density entails more interactions for the positrons and thereby
a shorter path before annihilation occurs, and this leads to a higher detection of
annihilation events from the water than in air. The estimated SOR for water and
air for FDG are almost identical, when comparing to the large diﬀerence between
the water SOR and air SOR for 82Rb. The low activity and the similarity of the
SORs measured for FDG are explained by the short PR of FDG.
The number of iterations before convergence for the RCs depends on the rod
size for FDG, but is indeﬁnable for the 82Rb rods due to the small changes in
RCs. The location of convergence for the FDG reconstruction occur earlier for
the larges rod than for the small rod. This result might indicate, that an image
with small structures would beneﬁt for a higher number of iterations. An increase
in iterations will on the other hand increase the amount of noise in the image,
indicating that the optimal number of iterations depends on the structures in the
image.
The number of segments included in the reconstruction is a trade-oﬀbetween
reconstruction time and image quality. The motivation for using fully 3D PET
instead of conventional 2D PET is to add LORs from oblique planes. The addition
of the oblique planes increases the number of counts and the scanner sensitivity,
but does simultaneously increase the reconstruction time.
A low %SDU is to
prefer in all image situations, nonetheless all results in this thesis are obtained by
an absolute number of 3 or 4 segments, due to reconstruction time concerns. The
issue with reconstruction time could be mitigated by using subsets and maybe a
more powerful computer. The inclusion of more segments in the reconstruction
would improve the image quality, and I highly recommend this modiﬁcation for
further development of the reconstruction of the Inveon data with STIR.
5.2
Comparing Inveon to mSTIR solution
The mSTIR solution is validated to STIR’s OSMAPOSL reconstruction algorithm by
comparing the image quality parameters for the two reconstruction solutions. The
values obtained for %SDU, SOR and RC are very similar for the two reconstruction
solutions. A corresponding reconstruction solution is not published in the liter-
ature, which is probably because the majority of STIR users reconstruct images
with the ready-made reconstruction solutions without the need for code modiﬁca-
tions. Published results using modiﬁed STIR solutions involve adding PRC into
the OSMAPOSL algorithm [33, 34].
The comparison of Inveon’s and mSTIR’s reconstructions reveals insight into
==PAGE:54==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
46
the strength and weaknesses of the reconstruction procedures. The Inveon recon-
structions of the FDG data are more uniform, have much smaller SORs and display
completely circular rods with higher RCs than the mSTIR reconstructions. This
means Inveon is better at reconstruction FDG data than the established mSTIR
solution.
A simple explanation for this might be, the Inveon reconstruction is
more sophisticated than the mSTIR solution. The reconstruction performed by
Inveon includes inter-iteration ﬁltering, prior and MAP, whereas the mSTIR solu-
tion only contains the very basic elements of an iterative reconstruction algorithm.
It is strongly believed that the introduction of ﬁlters in the mSTIR reconstruction
would beneﬁt the image quality. Inveons ﬁltering is highly optimized for FDG
reconstructions which might explain the superior results for FDG.
The image quality obtained from the 82Rb reconstructions are more compa-
rable between Inveon and mSTIR. Both the Inveon and mSTIR reconstruction
of the uniform region show activity outside the phantom edge. The proportion
of activity located outside the uniform region corresponds to the 2 mm thickness
phantom wall. The phantom is build of PMMA which has a density of 1.18
g
cm3
which is slightly more than the density of water but much larger than the density
of air. The annihilation events detected in PMMA is far higher than the detection
from the air due to the diﬀerence in electron density in air and PMMA.
The width of the phantom in the mSTIR reconstruction is consistent with the
width of the phantom given by the Inveon reconstruction, which indicates the
parameters for scanner setup in the STIR sinogram header-ﬁle are acceptable.
The images are scaled with the pixel scaling information provided by the image
header-ﬁles.
The dimensions of the axial plane in the Inveon and mSTIR im-
ages are slightly diﬀerent: The axial plane in the Inveon image is 128x128 pixels,
whereas the axial plane i the mSTIR image is 125x125 pixels. The axial length
of the voxels are 0.7764 mm/pixel and 0.815 mm/pixel for Inveon and mSTIR,
respectively. This results in an Inveon image with an axial width of 99.4 mm and
a mSTIR image with an axial width of 101.9 mm. The diameter of the phantom
appear identical for the two reconstructions despite the diﬀerence in axial plane
size. This means the mSTIR images are non-problematic despite the diﬀerence
of 2.5 mm in axial plane size. Neither of the results obtained by comparing the
mSTIR solution to STIR’s OSMAPOSL give rise question the scanner conﬁguration
in the STIR header-ﬁle, because the mSTIR reconstruction resembles the Inveon
reconstructions for all three phantom regions.
The proﬁles through the uniform region display a more even proﬁle for Inveons
reconstruction than for the STIR reconstructions, which might be the eﬀect of

inter-iteration ﬁltering. The normalized pixel values in the line-proﬁles for 82Rb
appear almost identical for Inveon and mSTIR, whereas the normalized pixels in
the line-proﬁles for the FDG reconstructions are slightly higher for the mSTIR
reconstruction compared to the Inveon reconstruction. Table 4.3 displays the op-
posite tendency, which indicates the reconstructed FDG image must contain a
maximum value higher than the values located in the uniform region. The data

5.3
Eﬀect of implementing positron range correc-
tion
5.3.1
Eﬀects of PRC on phantom reconstructions
The eﬀect of the PRC is highly isotope dependent: The image quality measures
for FDG are almost unchanged whereas the image quality measure for 82Rb are
signiﬁcantly improved by PRC. An explanation of the isotope dependency for PRC
can be derived from the diﬀerence in PR, and hereby the modeling of the annihi-
lation blurring kernels. The mean PR of 18F in water is 0.57 mm which is smaller
than the width of an image voxel [5]. The small PR results in modeling almost
all the annihilation probability to the central voxel of the water blurring kernel.
A possible modiﬁcation that might make PRC aﬀect the FDG reconstructions is
to decrease the voxel size by computing a larger image, because this would allow
the computation of blurring kernels with better resolution. The resolution concern
does only include the FDG blurring kernels, because the PR of 82Rb is much larger
than the voxel widths. The mean PR of 82Rb in water is 5.33 mm [5], correspond-
ing to the width of 6.5 pixels.
PRC increases the noise in the image, which is evident by an elevation in %SDU
for PRC reconstructions compared to mSTIR reconstructions without PRC. The
PRC implementation in mSTIR diverge from the PRC implementation proposed
by Cal-González et al. by using a set of matched projectors instead of unmatched
set of forward and backward projectors [8]. The use of unmatched projectors is
often applied to speed up the backprojection step [35], and the use of the matched
projectors might not cause the noise increment.
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
48
The eﬀects of only applying PRC in the forward projection were investigated by
Cal-Gonzalez et al. [8]. Their results showed that the implementation of PRC in
both the forward- and backprojection step led to smoothing of the reconstructed
images and a delay in the appearance of high resolution details, due to the slowed
convergence. But the results did not directly mention how PRC in both forward
and backprojection aﬀect the noise in the reconstructions. It is unclear how an
implementation of PRC in both the forward and backward projection step would
inﬂuence the %SDU in the reconstructed image.
The noise increment is larger for the 82Rb reconstruction than the FDG recon-
structions, and another and more likely factor that might inﬂuence the noise in the
PRC reconstructions is the size of the ﬁlter kernels. The width of the 82Rb kernels
are 7.4 times larger than the FDG kernels, which results in an enormous overlap
in tissue regions even though the 82Rb kernels are applied at their corresponding
tissue classes. The size of the blurring kernels were designed based on the expected
PR in air, which means the kernels for 82Rb are much larger than the size of the
FDG kernels.
The SORs for the FDG reconstructions are not aﬀected by PRC, while both
SORs for 82Rb with PRC are below the SORs without PRC after 10 iterations.
The kernels applied to the medium surrounding the air vary from the kernels ap-
plied to the air.
The 82Rb SOR for air is expected to decease toward zero as
the number of iterations increases, because the kernel for air will redistribute all
the activity of this area to other locations and the kernels from the surrounding
mediums will not distribute the same amount into the air zone. The 82Rb SOR
for water is on the other hand not expected to converge toward zero, because the
blurring kernel applied to water is identical with the blurring kernel applied on
material surrounding the water area.
The reconstructions without PRC display convergence within 20 and 10 iter-
ations for FDG and 82Rb, respectively. The RC for the two largest rods in the
82Rb reconstruction are highly enlarged by PRC. All the 82Rb RCs increases with
the number of iterations and a convergence point is not expected. The steady
enhancement of the RCs for 82Rb is expected to be a result of the blurring kernel,
which enhances areas of relative high activity, while the kernel decreases activity
of areas with lower activity. This means, the activity of the rods are magniﬁed for
each iteration because of their high activity compared to the background activity,
indicating that a high number of iterations would provide the best RCs. An in-
crement of iterations for better RC also entail enlargement of the noise, meaning
that the optimal number of iterations for 82Rb PRC is image dependent.

A comparison of the 82Rb PRC results to the image quality from the Inveon
reconstructions shows an increased %SDU but a mean uniformity more identical to
Inveon with PRC than without PRC. The SOR for water obtained from the Inveon
reconstructions are comparable with PRC after 20 iterations, whereas the SOR for
air is signiﬁcantly lower in Inveon’s reconstruction, which might be the eﬀect of
inter-iteration ﬁltering. The eﬀect of ﬁltering might be an interesting aspect to
explore in further research. Both SORs for PRC decreases exponentially, which
means the SOR for water will be lower than Inveon’s for iterations larger than 20.
All PRC RCs are higher than the RCs obtained from the Inveon reconstruction.
5.3.2
PRC and cardiac imaging of small animals
An in vivo image of a rat with heart infarct is reconstructed with PRC and shows
a signiﬁcant improvement in spatial resolution. This makes it possible to distin-
guish between left ventricle wall, the lumen of the ventricle and a perfusion defect
in the wall. This means that with PRC it may be possible to make a full analysis
of the heart with MPI and viability. This would beneﬁt preclinical assessment of
treatment, diagnosis and evaluation of new drugs using a rodent model.
Even though the clinical examination with 82Rb is good, the application of
PRC could improve the discrimination of perfusions defects in the left ventricle
wall. This has not been tested in the thesis, but would be of great interest to
examine.
The in vivo visualization of PRC shows an improvement in visual quality of
the image by applying PRC and Gaussian post ﬁltering. The examination of the
inﬂuence of ﬁlter standard deviation and iteration number revealed that the stan-
dard deviation of the ﬁlter must accommodate the number of iterations.
The contour of the rat appears a little smaller in the PRC corrected image
compared to Inveon’s reconstruction, which might be caused by the blurring kernels
aﬀecting the surface of the animal. The reduced size of the rat contour might be
the same phenomena seen in the 82Rb reconstruction of the uniform region of the
phantom, where the activity with PRC is redistributed to the phantom instead of
the phantom wall.
5.3.3
Improving PRC
mSTIR is estimated suﬃcient for evaluating PRC as a proof of concept for the
preclinical 82Rb scan, but several initiatives would beneﬁt the image quality of the
==PAGE:58==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
50
reconstructions obtained through the mSTIR solution, and it is strongly believed
that an improvement of the mSTIR solution would lead to better PRC results.
The inclusion of more segments would increase reconstruction time, but im-
prove image quality. Only 7 segments of the possible 53 segments are included in
the current mSTIR implementation. More segments can easily be added to the
implementation, as long as the permutation of the axis and the changed scanner
sensitivity are considered. The current mSTIR implementation does not allow sub-
sets. The introduction of subsets would decrease reconstruction time, and thereby
make the inclusion of more segments more feasible. The phantom PRC results
shown in the thesis are only tested for 20 iterations, more iterations might beneﬁt
the image quality as long as the noise is considered.
A more sophisticated PRC implementation is to apply the PRC directly to
STIR’s OSMAPOSL reconstruction algorithm. This approach has been demonstrated
by Bertolli el al. [33, 34]. This approach demands actual c++ coding of the STIR
program, whereas the mSTIR solution omits c++ coding by using STIR’s functions
without modiﬁcations. A PRC implementation in the OSMAPOSL algorithm would
allow more ﬂexibility, e.g. use of prior, subsets, inter-iteration ﬁlters and changing
the number of included segments. An implementation of PRC in OSMAPOSL is to
prefer above the mSTIR solution, because of OSMAPOSLs advanced reconstruction
possibilities.
Cal-Gonzalez et al. [9] presented two diﬀerent types of PRC: A Tissue Depen-
dent PRC (TD PRC) and a Tissue Depended-Spatial Variant PRC (TD-SV PRC).
This thesis has only implemented the TD PRC. The TD-SV PRC is know to obtain
better reconstruction results when reconstructing images with tissue boundaries
e.g. transition from tissue to air [9, 36, 34]. The TD-SV PRC method requires
computation of individual blurring kernels and a large amount of memory for stor-
age. The aim of this thesis was to investigate the possibilities of implementing
PRC into STIR, and only the TD PRC is considered for this mater thesis.

Conclusion
This thesis shows that PRC can improve the spatial resolution of PET images with
the radiotracer 82Rb. The eﬀects of PRC are evaluated on a rodent size NEMA
NU 4 2008 inspired phantom and in vivo rat scan.
The PRC proposed by Cal-González et al. is implemented as an interplay be-
tween STIR and MATLAB, called mSTIR, which provides MLEM reconstruction
of 7 sinogram segments. mSTIR without PRC is evaluated against the reconstruc-
tion from STIR, and the comparison shows insigniﬁcant diﬀerence between the
reconstruction solutions.
FDG reconstructions provided by the Siemens Inveon system are superior to
the mSTIR reconstruction, especially on RCs and SORs. The RCs and SOR for
water for the 82Rb reconstructions are contrary more comparable between the In-
veon and mSTIR reconstructions. The application of PRC to mSTIR increases the
RC for the largest rods to values higher than the values obtained from Inveon’s
reconstruction, and the SOR for water is smaller than Inveon’s after 20 PRC iter-
ations. PRC do not aﬀect the FDG reconstructions.
The in vivo 82Rb PET perfusion image of a rat with heart infarct is recon-
structed with PRC and reveals a signiﬁcant improvement of spatial resolution
compared to the Inveon reconstruction. PRC improves the spatial resolution in
vivo to a degree where the heart infarct can be localized.

7.2
Comparing reconstructions to literature
Disselhorst et al. [26], Goertzen et al. [37] and Bao et al. [31] conducted image
quality measurements of the Inveon scanner with the NEMA NU 4 2008 standard.
The reported image quality values for FDG reconstruction are displayed in Ta-
ble 7.1 along with the reconstruction method and start activity. Unfortunately
no published literature of image quality of 82Rb combining the NEMA NU 4 2008
standard and Siemens Inveon scanner are found. Disselhorst et al. [26] also mea-
sured the image quality for 68Ga and 124I, which have larger PR than FDG.
The SOR for 18F reported by Disselhorst et al. matches the values obtained
from the Inveon FDG reconstruction. The reported RC values are slightly higher
in the article than the one stated in Table 4.5, and the reported %SDU of 2 %
is noticeable smaller than the measured %SDU of 4.71% of the FDG Inveon re-
construction in Table 4.3.
The images reported in the article have dimension
256x256x159, which increases the number of pixels in the axial plane with a factor
of 4. The OSMAPOSL STIR reconstructions can reproduce the %SDU reported by
Disselhorst et al. by increasing the number of segments to the full FDG data set.
The %SDU is 2.6 % when all segments are included in the OSMAPOSL reconstruction.
The values reported by Bao et al. and Goertzen et al. are almost identical.
Their RC values are slightly smaller than the RC values obtained in this study
because the reconstruction is performed with Filtered Back Projection (FBP) and
Fourier rebinning (FORE) of the data. The reported %SDUs are slightly higher
than the 4.7 %SDU obtained by iterative reconstruction, which is also expected.
It seems worrying that image quality measurement for %SDU and RC presented
in this thesis are more similar to the analytic FBP reconstructions than the itera-
tive MAP reconstruction. The disagreement in image quality measurement include
both the Inveon, STIR and mSTIR reconstructions. This could indicate another
application of ﬁlters during the Inveon reconstructions, or a minor error in the
computation of the image quality measures or an error in the manual placement
of the VOIs.

7.6
STIR Installation - a practical guide
The setup of STIR is described on several internet platforms and guides. The STIR
WIKI provides a guide on how to install STIR on various operation systems. The
homepage for the STIR project and mailing list do also provide very useful infor-
mation and answers concerning installation and frequently asked questions.
The image reconstruction in this project is performed on an Ubuntu 18.04 lts
system and with make as the compiler. The installed version of STIR is 3.0. Prior
to the installation of STIR one need the Boost library, CMake and a compiler.
The following is a list of the mentioned tools used for the STIR setup.
• CMake: is used as a cross-platform for building STIR. All settings for STIR
is described in CMake e.g which libraries to use and other speciﬁcations
to apply (ﬂags).
CMake customizes ﬁles, called makeﬁles, based on the
operative system and versions settings chosen in CMake.
• Boost: esteemed c++ library containing eﬃcient code and mathematical
functions on which STIR depends. One do not need to install Boost, down-
load is suﬃcient for STIR. Boost is linked to STIR through CMake.
• Compiler: This project uses make as the c++ compiler.
The STIR project can be downloaded from https://github.com/UCL/STIR.
Figure 7.2 shows a sketch of the installation of STIR. The drawing is roughly
divided into to parts: creation of the makeﬁles and usage of the makeﬁles. The
makeﬁles are setup ﬁles for STIR and they are taylormade specifying the settings
clariﬁed in CMake.
The yellow box represents the CMake program and the boxes inside are the work-
ﬂow. The words listed left for the yellow box are additional libraries and utilities
which can be linked to STIR via CMake. The ECAT library is unfortunately not
supported anymore and STIR is not yet fully developed for MATLAB implemen-
tation. The only library included in CMake is Boost.
CMake generates the makeﬁles which are symbolized as the gray box between
CMake and the terminal. The makeﬁles are placed in the build directory, which
is speciﬁed in CMake. Next step is to open the build directory in the terminal
and install STIR. It is highly recommended to test the STIR utilities with the
recon_test_test which is included in the STIR download.
STIR is build from the series of Makeﬁles placed in the Release folder. The
Makeﬁles are build by typing make in the terminal. Followed by sudo make install.
The program needs a recompilation if the c++ code is modiﬁed. The recompilation
is made by running the two lines above from the Release folder in the terminal.

7.7
Running STIR reconstruction
Four diﬀerent reconstruction algorithms are implemented in STIR. Two analytic
ﬁltered back projections methods called FBP2D and FBP3DRP, and two iterative
reconstruction methods called OSSPS and OSMAPOSL, respectively.
A reconstruction is initiated by choosing a reconstruction algorithm and construct-
ing a suitable parameter ﬁle. The parameter ﬁle must match the reconstruction
algorithm (see an example of an OSMAPOSL parameter ﬁle in Code 7.4). The re-
