Abstract
This thesis explores the role of cognitive biases in concept selection within engineering design and product
development. Concept selection is a critical yet often informal stage in development processes, where engineers
often prioritize certain ideas based on gut feeling and experience rather than structured methodologies. While
systematic evaluation frameworks exist in academia, they are rarely adopted in industry, leading to higher risks
of errors in judgment and bias-prone decision-making.
A combination of literature review and exploratory interviews serves to gain both a deep and real-world
understanding of concept selection and cognitive biases in design decision-making, providing an integrated
overview that has not previously been presented. The main outcome of this research is the identification of key
biases likely to emerge at this stage of product development. However, this study goes beyond just awareness,
as these biases are further mapped to concrete design activities, and specific strategies and tools are proposed
to mitigate or avoid their effects. The culmination of the project is the development of the Bias Prompt Cards,
a practical tool designed to help engineers recognize and counteract biases in real time, fostering more objective
and innovative decision-making. The findings contribute to both academic research and industry practices by
enhancing awareness of cognitive biases and offering actionable strategies for their mitigation.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Design engineers shape the world through their choices. Every decision they make, whether defining re-
quirements, generating initial design solutions, or prioritizing features, affects elements like functionality, cost,
sustainability, and innovation. Among these decisions, concept selection is a particularly critical yet often un-
dervalued phase in the design process. It determines which ideas move forward and ultimately shape the final
product.
Despite its significance, concept selection is often rushed or handled informally, without structured eval-
uation methods. Engineers often rely on intuition, prior experience or unstructured discussions, making this
stage particularly susceptible to biased decisions. Without structured approaches, engineers may unknowingly
favor familiar ideas, overlook innovative alternatives, or reinforce existing assumptions, limiting creativity and
reducing the quality of final designs.
While academia has developed systematic methodologies to guide this phase, a significant gap exists between
these theoretical approaches and the procedures commonly observed in industry. Industry practices often rely
on experience-driven, informal decision-making. Many companies lack systematic frameworks, leading engineers
to make choices based on gut feeling rather than evidence-based methods. Given the highly analytical yet often
time-constrained nature of engineering work, rapid decision-making under uncertainty can introduce recurring
errors in judgment. However, more research is needed to fully understand how concept selection processes unfold
in real-world engineering contexts and the extent to which these errors emerge in practice.
Research on how engineers think and make decisions has identified different factors that shape engineering
judgment, including mental shortcuts and implicit preferences.
These tendencies are often called cognitive
biases, systematic ways in which the human mind deviates from rational judgment. While cognitive biases
in engineering design have been previously studied, most work focus on proving the existence and impact of
biases through protocols rather than systematically identifying and categorizing them. Only a few authors
have attempted to create an overview of relevant biases, but these efforts are often limited in scope, lacking
transparency in their methodology, and rarely exceeding a small selection of biases. Moreover, these studies
do not specifically address concept evaluation and selection, leaving a critical gap in the understanding of how
biases shape this key phase of design decision-making. Proposals of bias mitigation strategies and tools are even
more scarce.
While decision-making theory and other fields offer a wealth of knowledge on cognitive biases and mitiga-
tion strategies, there is no integrated, engineering-specific review that consolidates these insights into a practical
framework. Additionally, there is a lack of consensus on bias classification, with different authors proposing vary-
ing biases and categorization methods, further complicating the field. This fragmented landscape underscores
the need to synthesize knowledge from multiple disciplines while ensuring that insights are directly relevant to
engineers’ needs and constraints.
To improve decision-making in engineering design, a targeted and applicable approach is needed. Awareness
is essential, but it must be supported by practical tools and strategies that integrate seamlessly into engineers’
workflows, enabling them to actively recognize and counteract biases in concept selection. This research seeks
to bridge the gap between theory and practice, providing engineers with structured, effective methods to make
more objective, fair, and innovative design decisions.
1.2
Research objectives
The main purpose of the theses is to create an overview of cognitive biases in concept selection and develop
a practical approach for their recognition and mitigation. The different objectives of the thesis can be there-
fore divided into two key areas: understanding concept selection and cognitive biases present, and developing
practical tools for engineers.

==PAGE:9==
Understanding Concept Selection and Cognitive Biases in Design Decision-Making
It is necessary to establish a foundation of knowledge that identifies which biases are most relevant to concept
selection and how they emerge in engineering contexts. Before addressing biases, it is essential to understand how
concept selection happens in engineering practice, what methods, frameworks, and decision-making processes
are used, how they vary across contexts, and what challenges exist. This provides the necessary foundation to
examine how biases influence these processes and where interventions can be most effective.
• Research objective 1. Investigate concept selection in engineering design, mapping out different meth-
ods, strategies, and decision-making frameworks used in both academia and industry. Analyze how concept
selection happens in practice, examining industry perspectives on decision-making processes, the factors
influencing selections, and the challenges engineers face. This will help determine whether current selec-
tion methods are more or less prone to bias and ensure that proposed mitigation strategies align with
real-world constraints.
• Research objective 2. Critically analyze design cognition and cognitive biases, synthesizing insights
from psychology, decision-making theory, engineering design and other fields. The aim is to establish a
strong foundation that enables a structured approach to identifying and classifying relevant biases in later
steps. Additionally, explore the level of awareness among industry practitioners regarding cognitive biases
and the extent to which they apply any mitigation strategies.
Creating Practical Tools for Engineers
A deep understanding of biases and decision-making is essential, but not enough. Engineers need actionable
tools to help them recognize and counteract bias in practice. The following objectives focus on translating
research insights into applicable strategies that engineers can integrate into their workflows.
• Research objective 3. Develop a list of biases relevant to concept selection, selecting from the broader
literature those biases that are most likely to influence engineering decisions. Alongside this, provide clear
definitions, real-world examples, and specific moments in the design process where they are likely to be
present.
• Research objective 4. Identify and categorize mitigation strategies by connecting cognitive science
findings with existing engineering methods, ensuring that solutions are practical and fit within established
workflows.
• Research objective 5. Design a practical tool that engineers can use during concept selection, enabling
them to actively recognize and counteract biases in real time.

Theoretical background
2.1
Concept evaluation and selection in engineering design
2.1.1
Definition and importance of concept selection
Concept selection is the process of narrowing down a set of design alternatives to identify the most promising
concept(s) for further development. It involves analyzing concepts and evaluating them against user needs and
other criteria, comparing their strengths and weaknesses, and selecting one or more for further investigation,
testing, or refinement. Although it is a converging process, it is often iterative and may lead to additional
concept generation or modifications to existing ideas. (Ulrich et al., 2008; Van Boeijen et al., 2013).
Whether explicit or not, every design project follows some form of concept selection.
Even teams that
generate only one idea are using a selection method, choosing the first concept they think of (Ulrich et al.,
2008). While intuitive decision-making plays a role in design, it is not always sufficient, particularly for complex
or novel problems (Van Boeijen et al., 2013). To improve decision-making, many authors such as Pahl et al.
(1996) and Ulrich et al. (2008) advocate for structured methodologies, including decision matrices and weighted
scoring systems. A structured approach helps maintain objectivity and guides teams through what can be a
complex and sometimes emotionally charged decision-making process (Ulrich et al., 2008). Although decision
methods support the selection process, they do not guarantee a correct outcome. Instead, they serve as tools to
aid in making a well-considered decision. Ultimately, the responsibility lies with the decision-maker to critically
assess the outcomes in relation to the initial project goals and requirements.
Concept selection is a crucial step in product development as it significantly impacts the success of both the
product and the overall project. A well-executed selection process ensures that the best possible alternative is
pursued, reducing the likelihood of costly redesigns and failures in later stages (Nikander et al., 2013).
2.1.2
Design methods and strategies for concept selection
Design methods
Concept selection methods range from structured, analytical approaches to intuitive, experience-driven
decision-making.
Designers may employ formal techniques such as decision matrices, scoring systems, or
multi-criteria optimization, or rely on less structured methods like expert judgment and heuristics (Mirabito &
Goucher-Lambert, 2023). Systematic and analytical methods have been widely studied, offering diverse tools
for evaluating design alternatives (Nikander et al., 2013). These methods vary in complexity, from simple rating
and comparison techniques to advanced multi-criteria decision-making and optimization models. In Table 1,
different methods discussed by authors in the literature are presented. These methods are roughly organized by
increasing complexity. Some authors, such as Okudan and Tauhid (2008), have attempted to categorize these
methods into structured classifications, but such categorizations are not comprehensive. Therefore, no strict
classification is applied here, allowing for a broader view of the different approaches used in concept evaluation.

Design strategies
Concept selection strategies describe overarching approaches to how designers navigate the selection pro-
cess. While some strategies emphasize structured, sequential decision-making, others rely on flexibility and
adaptability. Several authors have explored different perspectives on how design teams structure their selection
process.
Bender and Blessing (2004) outlined four distinct strategies that designers may follow when selecting a
concept and studied the superiority of opportunistic ones.
- Hierarchically Phase-Oriented: This is the most structured strategy, where the process progresses system-
atically through predefined stages, ensuring sequential progress across all subsystems.
- Hierarchically Object-Oriented: Similar to the phase-oriented approach but focuses on completing all
aspects of one subsystem before moving to the next.
- Opportunistic and Associative: A hybrid method that balances structured approaches with flexibility, al-
lowing designers to take advantage of spontaneous ideas while maintaining some hierarchical organization.
- Muddling Through: The least structured approach, relying on a trial-and-error process without an prede-
fined plan, where decisions emerge spontaneously.
Guindon (1990) observed that designers often deviate from traditional top-down decomposition strategies,
particularly when dealing with complex and ill-structured problems. Instead, an opportunistic decomposition
approach is often used, where designers shift between different aspects of the problem as new insights emerge.
This suggests that strict hierarchical methods may not always be the most effective.
Dylla describes two strategies for generating and refining solutions during concept selection (Bender &
Blessing, 2004).
- Productive Solution Generation: Designers generate multiple independent solution variants and evaluate
them collectively before selecting the most promising one for further refinement.
- Corrective Solution Generation: A single concept is chosen early on and gradually refined in a stepwise
manner until it meets the necessary requirements.

- Stepwise Process-Oriented Strategy: Designers follow a hierarchical, sequential plan, systematically refin-
ing design operations step by step for all subsystems in parallel.
- Function-Oriented Strategy: Instead of working on all subsystems simultaneously, designers focus on
completing one function or subsystem in full before moving on to the next.
2.1.3
Disconnect between academia and industrial practices
Despite extensive research on structured design methods, their adoption in industry remains limited. Academia
promotes systematic and hierarchical approaches, yet studies indicate that these methods are often improperly
used or ignored in practice, especially when dealing with low complexity products (López-Mesa & Bylund,
2011; Nikander et al., 2013). Companies frequently lack a formal process for selecting ideas, relying instead
on informal team discussions (Onarheim & Christensen, 2012; Toh & Miller, 2016b). While these informal
methods can be effective, the limited use of systematic methods makes the concept selection susceptible to bias
and inconsistency (Nikander et al., 2013; Toh & Miller, 2016b).
Efforts have been made to better understand how practitioners approach concept evaluation and selection
in real-world settings.
López-Mesa and Bylund (2011) aimed to understand the use and impact of design
methods within a specific company, while Nikander et al. (2013) utilized verbal protocol analysis to capture
different decision strategies and evaluation criteria used by professional designers, revealing diverse approaches
from analytical assessments to quick, intuitive decision-making. Salonen and Perttula (2005) found that in-
dustrial adoption of formal concept selection methods remains low, with less than 25% of companies using
structured methodologies like Pugh’s evaluation matrix or AHP, while concept review meetings were the most
common approach. Many companies lacking formal methods reported a need for more effective tools, while
those implementing structured approaches expressed greater satisfaction with their decision-making processes.
Salonen and Perttula (2005) concluded that existing formal concept selection methods do not entirely fulfill
the needs of industry, highlighting a misalignment between structured methodologies and practical design chal-
lenges. An alternative perspective on this issue comes from research on design strategies, which distinguishes
between hierarchical and opportunistic approaches. Bender and Blessing (2004) found that real design practice
is better characterized by opportunistic procedures rather than strict, top-down hierarchical processes. De-
signers tend to follow flexible and adaptive strategies rather than adhering rigidly to predefined methodologies.
This preference for adaptability over rigid methodologies could explain the reluctance of industry practitioners
to fully embrace formalized concept selection methods.
Therefore, better alignment between academic methods and industry practices is needed to address the gap
in concept selection approaches. Future research should focus on understanding real-world decision-making to
develop structured methods that integrate with the flexible strategies designers already use.

2.2.1
Foundations of design cognition
Design cognition
Design cognition refers to the study and understanding of the mental processes and representations involved
in designing. Since its emergence in the 1960s, cognitive design research has underscored the importance of
understanding the cognitive processes, strategies, and behaviors that drive design (Hay et al., 2020). Significant

Among significant contributions to this field, (Hay et al., 2017) presented a generic classification of the
processes involved in conceptual design based on 47 protocol studies in this area. These cognitive processes can
be further categorized into 6 broader groups: long-term memory, semantic processing, visual perception, mental
imagery processing, creative output production, and executive functions. Figure 1 illustrates an overview of the
cognitive processes in each category, as well as its connection with conceptual design.

Thinking frameworks. The dual-system theory
While design cognition outlines the specific mental processes involved in design tasks, it is equally important
to consider how different modes of thinking affect overall decision-making.
This broader perspective helps
explain not only the individual tasks but also the underlying cognitive mechanisms that guide the entire design
process
The dual-system theory is a well-established model of human cognition with deep roots in cognitive psy-
chology, that was recently popularized by Daniel Kahneman in his book Thinking, Fast and Slow. It is based
on the idea that there are two different ways of reasoning or two systems. The first system is fast, intuitive and
implies effortless thinking. The second system is slow, tedious, analytical, and needs higher loads of cognition
efforts. System 1 is typically responsible for making quick decisions, whereas System 2 comes into play for tasks
that require more in-depth analysis and careful consideration. (Kahneman, 2011) System 1 is necessary for our
daily lives as it would take too much cognitive effort to analytically asses every situation encountered.
Although Kahneman’s dual-system theory is a general model of human cognition, it is highly relevant to
engineering design .
Design is typically seen as a complex activity requiring slow, deliberate thought, but

2.2.2
Heuristics and cognitive biases
Heuristics
Heuristics are mental shortcuts or frameworks that help humans navigate the complexity and uncertainty
of daily life by enabling rapid decision-making with minimal cognitive effort (Agyemang et al., 2023; Tversky &
Kahneman, 1974). Operating largely within the intuitive mode of decision-making, heuristics involve patterns
of thinking such as "rules of thumb" or "seen this many times before" approaches. In terms of Kahneman’s
dual system theory, heuristics mostly operate within System 1, the intuitive and automatic way of thinking.
Heuristics are indispensable for handling the limitations of time and mental cognitive resources, but they are
not infallible. Their reliance on simplifications can lead to flawed judgments or biased conclusions (Dimara
et al., 2020).
In the context of engineering design and product development, heuristics play an important role as design
engineers are faced with numerous decisions to be made throughout the process. The complexity of decisions
varies greatly, some fairly straightforward and others more complex and intricate, but it’s often the case that
individuals need to rely on their intuition and heuristics to make the decision-making process manageable.
Although heuristics facilitate the engineering design process, they also have the potential to introduce biases
that shape outcomes in unintended ways (Sanchez Ruelas et al., 2021). Moreover, heuristics resist modification,
requiring significant attention and energy to recognize and alter, making it particularly challenging to address
(McCollough et al., 2013). Recognizing this dynamic highlights the importance of balancing intuitive strategies
with deliberate, reflective processes to enhance decision quality in design.

Example in action. Heuristics
Our brains love shortcuts! Instead of tackling complex questions, we often rely mental shortcuts called
heuristics that simplify decision-making.
In a famous study, participants were asked:
1. How many dates have you been on recently?
2. How happy are you with your life?
When the dating question came first, people with active love lives reported feeling happier, while those
with fewer dates felt less happy. But when asked about happiness first, dating had little effect!
This is attribute substitution, where our brain swaps a hard question ("How happy am I?") for an
easier one ("How’s my dating life?"). A perfect example of heuristics at work!
Cognitive biases
Biases in general can be defined as tendencies to favor specific things and perspectives deviating from
objectivity. Cognitive biases can be defined as systematic patterns of deviation from objective reasoning that
occur when individuals perceive, remember, think, or judge under uncertainty (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974)
These biases often manifest unconsciously as gut feelings, predisposition, or tendencies, shaping judgment in
ways that are not always rational or intentional.
Cognitive biases primarily emerge from heuristics used by System 1. As discussed previously, heuristics
enable rapid decision-making by simplifying complex problems, but their application outside appropriate con-
texts can result in systematic errors in reasoning, this is what is termed cognitive biases (McCollough et al.,
2013). Biases, in this sense, represent the errors that influence heuristics, distorting judgment by introducing
subjective tendencies into what might otherwise be objective and neutral decision processes. (Agyemang et al.,
2023; Sanchez Ruelas et al., 2021)
Example in action. Biases
Our brains love compelling stories! But sometimes, they lead us astray. In a famous study, participants
were introduced to Linda, a politically active philosophy major passionate about social justice. Then,
they were asked which is more probable?:
1. Linda is a bank teller.
2. Linda is a bank teller and active in the feminist movement.
Most people chose option 2, but mathematically, that’s impossible. It’s always less likely for two things
to be true than just one.
This is the conjunction bias, where our brains assume that detailed scenarios are more probable, even
when the math says otherwise. A perfect example of how intuition can mislead us!
There are over a hundred cognitive biases, along with various other types of biases influenced by emotions,
beliefs, and societal factors (Croskerry et al., 2013a). For this discussion, the focus will be on cognitive biases,
those arising from mental shortcuts and intuitive judgments. Other types of biases, while important, will be
set aside in order to concentrate on the cognitive mechanisms that influence our judgments in more direct,
individual contexts.
2.2.3
Debiasing
Debiasing is commonly understood as the process of either preventing biases from occurring or mitigating
their negative effects, as described by Schmitt et al. (2022) and Arnott (2006). Since cognitive biases often

arise from fast, intuitive thinking, which operates at an unconscious level, addressing them poses significant
challenges (Smith & Peloghitis, 2020). And as biases influence many aspects of decision-making, the ability to
recognize and correct them is arguably one of the most essential attributes of a critical thinker (Croskerry et al.,
2013b).
Croskerry et al. (2013a) build on the work of Wilson and Brekke, who conceptualize cognitive bias as “mental
contamination” and debiasing as “mental correction,” emphasizing the need for active intervention to counteract
biased reasoning. They provide a useful framework for understanding cognitive debiasing, explaining that it is
rarely achieved through a single event; rather, it occurs in stages, progressing from a lack of awareness of bias
to the implementation of appropriate debiasing strategies (Croskerry et al., 2013a, 2013b; Smith & Peloghitis,

3.1.2
Selection of sources
The literature review employed a dual-approach methodology to build a strong theoretical foundation for
the study. The first method involved a structured and systematic search of academic databases including Google
Scholar and key journals such as Design Science, Design Studies, Journal of Mechanical Design, or Research in
Engineering Design. Relevant studies were identified using key words that aligned with the study’s objectives,
including design engineering, concept selection, concept selection methodologies, decision-making, judgment,
design cognition, bias, cognitive bias, design bias, bias taxonomy, bias mitigation, or debiasing.
While the
structured search provided a strong foundation, it also revealed several challenges inherit in the topic
First, the lack of shared terminology across disciplines complicated the search process, as the same concepts
were often described using different terms or synonyms. Second, the broad and interdisciplinary nature of the
topic resulted in an overwhelming number of papers, many of which were disconnected from the specific focus

with the project’s objectives.
To address these challenges, a second approach was adopted, using a reference-based strategy to expand
the literature review. This approach began by identifying highly relevant and foundational papers that aligned
closely with the study’s objectives. The references cited in these papers were then systematically examined to
uncover additional relevant studies. This method allowed for the discovery of influential works that might not
have surfaced in the initial structured search, bypassing the limitations posed by terminology variability and
ensuring a deeper exploration of the topic. To enhance the efficiency of this process, AI tools were utilized to
visualize connections between papers and identify additional resources. The combination of systematic database
searches and targeted reference-based exploration provided a thorough understanding of the existing knowledge
base, serving as a solid foundation for subsequent phases of the study.

Disciplinary background of the papers

3.2
Exploratory interviews with engineers in the industry
3.2.1
Intent and research alignment
A set of exploratory interviews was conducted to gain a deeper understanding of how concept evaluation and
selection is practiced in industry, the challenges professionals face, and the role of cognitive biases in decision-
making.
The study seeks to compare the findings from these interviews with existing academic research,
identifying areas where the interviews may challenge, support, or expand the current literature. Additionally,
the study aims to highlight topics that have not been thoroughly discussed in existing research, addressing gaps
and contributing to a broader understanding of industry practices.
These interviews directly support research objective 1, which seeks to map out how concept selection
occurs in practice. While previous research has already established that concept selection in industry is often
disconnected from academic methodologies, this study does not seek to reaffirm that disconnect. Instead, the
interviews were designed to spark discussion, explore practitioner perspectives, and ground the development
of practical tools for mitigating biases in decision-making, aligning with research objectives 3, 4, and 5.
Additionally, while some studies have validated the presence of biases in design engineering as a whole, very few
have specifically examined biases within concept selection, a key phase in product development where decisions
have long-term impacts. Confirming the presence of biases in this context remains essential and aligns with
research objective 2, which seeks to structure an understanding of biases in design decision-making. However,
an even more novel aspect of this study is exploring how much practitioners actually know about biases, whether
they consciously consider them in their decision-making, and if debiasing strategies are part of their process.
This study prioritizes rich, detailed insights from a small number of participants over large-scale data col-
lection. The decision to use interviews was based on several factors, but the key reason is that, since the goal is
not to quantify occurrences but to understand attitudes and perceptions, interviews allow for a more nuanced
exploration than surveys or controlled experiments could. The findings from these interviews help bridge the
gap between theoretical frameworks and industry realities.
The broad interview goals guiding the interviews are as follows:
1. Explore how the concept selection process looks like for different professionals, including methodologies
and criteria. The key focus is to understand how the process, along with personal and professional factors,
influences the emergence and intensity of various biases.
2. Assess the level of awareness among industrial practitioners regarding biases and debiasing strategies in
concept selection, and in general in design and product development.

3. Validate the presence of biases in concept selection and examine how they manifest and influence decision-

4. Explore the potential existence and application of debiasing tools by professionals, as well as their overall

3.2.2
Format and structure of the interviews
The interviews followed a semi-structured format, with a predefined set of questions serving as a reference.
However, the structure was flexible, allowing the interviewers to adapt based on the responses provided by
participants. Follow-up questions were introduced to explore insightful observations and topics as they emerged.
The interviews took place in the offices of the participating practitioners, with either one or two interviewers
present. To introduce the topic, a presentation was prepared, including slides that outlined key questions and
discussion points. Depending on the circumstances, this presentation was displayed either as a printed document
or on an electronic device. All interviews were recorded for later analysis, and participants were provided with
a data collection notice to ensure confidentiality and proper data handling.
3.2.3
Selection of interview questions & development of the interview guide
The interview questions were developed based on areas where existing knowledge is limited and identified
gaps from the first round of literature review, which explored mostly the concept selection process as well as
a small introduction to biases. The goal was to address aspects of the concept selection process that have not
been extensively explored in prior research, particularly regarding bias and decision-making strategies.
A total of 56 questions were designed, structured around five key themes:
1. Contextual questions
2. Concept selection process. Overview
3. Concept selection process. Methodologies
4. Concept selection process. Criteria
5. Biases in concept selection
Each question was developed with a specific focus area within these broad categories and was further classified
according to three analytical dimensions: factual versus opinion-eliciting, broad versus specific, and exploratory
versus closed-ended. In addition, each question was assessed in terms of its underlying hypothesis, aims, and
potential limitations. A section of the interview guide is presented in Figure 5, while the full guide can be found

The first set of questions was designed to gather contextual information about participants, including their
roles, team structures and dynamics, the types of tasks they handle, and the nature of the products they work
with. This knowledge helped frame the later discussions, ensuring that responses could be interpreted within
the correct organizational and operational context.
The concept selection process was explored in three stages. Initially, participants were asked open-ended
questions to describe how concept selection occurs within their company, without imposing predefined structures
or assumptions. This was followed by a more structured discussion focusing on formal methodologies, in which
participants were asked about specific tools, frameworks, or decision-making approaches they employ. Finally,
the last part of the discussion addressed how selection criteria are determined and applied in decision-making.
The final section of the interview aimed to explore participants’ awareness of biases in concept selection
and whether they employed any strategies to mitigate them. To avoid influencing earlier responses, this section
was deliberately introduced at the end without prior mention in the interview’s framing. However, certain

3.4.1
Intent and research alignment
This step extends the previously identified list of biases by mapping them to specific activities within concept

While some authors have proposed bias mitigation strategies in design engineering, they are often not

rather than concept evaluation. Additionally, the number of proposed strategies is limited, leaving gaps in how
engineers can systematically address biases during decision-making.
Similarly, some research has linked biases to underlying cognitive processes, which provides useful theoretical
insight but is not directly practical for design engineers. Instead of following this approach, this study connects
biases to specific and recurrent tasks within concept selection, making it easier for engineers to recognize and
address them within their workflow.

representation of bias occurrence throughout the decision-making process.
In addition to identifying where biases occur, debiasing strategies were linked to each biases and the activity

a practical tool for understanding both bias occurrence and possible interventions.

To evaluate the Bias Cards, a structured workshop was conducted with four participants to simulate real-
world concept selection scenarios. The assessment focused on three key aspects: usability, practical application,
and effectiveness. Usability was determined by how clearly the bias descriptions were written, the helpfulness
of the provided examples, and whether participants found the cards intuitive. Practical Application examined
how seamlessly the cards fit within existing engineering workflows and whether participants could naturally
integrate them into their concept selection process. Effectiveness was measured by the extent to which the
cards helped participants recognize biases, the perceived usefulness of the debiasing strategies, and their overall
impact on decision-making.
Structure
The workshop was divided into four main stages:
1. Initial questions (10 min): Participants answered a series of background questions to provide context
on their education, experience in research and industry, and prior knowledge of design methodologies and
biases.
2. Design task (30 min): Participants individually constructed a mechanism using a predefined set of
LEGO bricks. Participants were guided by a design brief, which outlined the constraints and objectives
of the task.
3. Concept selection (15 min): In pairs, participants reviewed their own designs along with other ex-
amples. They discussed and selected the best design based while integrating the Bias Cards into their
25
==PAGE:27==
evaluation process.
4. Follow-up discussion (15 min): Participants reflected on their decision-making process and the impact
of the Bias Cards. They provided feedback on the usability, clarity, and effectiveness of the cards in
identifying biases and aiding decision-making.
Materials
The key material required for the evaluation was the set of Bias Cards, which participants used and assessed
throughout the workshop. Additionally, several supporting materials were developed to facilitate the exercise:
- LEGO set. Provided as the main design medium for the task.
- Instructions. Clear guidelines to ensure participants followed a structured process.
- Initial questionnaire. Designed to collect participant background information.
- Follow-up questionnaire. Developed to gather structured feedback on the three key evaluation criteria:
usability, practical application, and effectiveness.
- Design brief. Participants were tasked with designing an elevator platform that must move in a linear
motion, controlled by either a rotating input or a tilting input. The design brief worked with this exercise
due to its simplicity while still supporting chosen for its simplicity and its ability to support diverse
design solutions and working principles. Additionally, a similar experiment is conducted in the course
Engineering design and problem solving as part of the BSc Mechanical Engineering. In the course, the
exercise has a different approach and it’s limited to the design phase. Nevertheless, it provided valuable
insights into common participant challenges and tendencies.
- Examples of different mechanisms. These examples were presented in multiple formats, including
sketches, photographs of LEGO-built models, and rendered 3D representations. This approach was in-
tended to encourage discussions about mode bias, exploring how different presentation formats might
influence decision-making and preferences.

Cognitive biases and their classification in decision-making and design
This section examines cognitive biases in engineering design as well as other disciplines, making sure key
insights from other fields are incorporated in the discussion.
It provides an overview of key contributions,

4.1.1
Cognitive biases in engineering design and product development. Design biases
Research on cognitive biases in engineering design and product development is more limited compared to
general judgment and decision-making theory. However, numerous efforts have been made to classify and analyze
how biases influence design processes. Broadly, research in this field can be categorized into two approaches:
Studies that examine multiple biases within engineering design, and research that focus on specific biases,
exploring their manifestation and impact in design, often through empirical experiments.
Among the works that examine multiple biases, Jimenez et al. (2024) provides a structured list of biases
relevant to design, organizing them into three key stages, including concept selection. Similarly, McCollough
et al. (2013) discusses essential heuristics and biases in design, providing concrete examples of their influence.
Agyemang et al. (2023) highlights that most research has focused on biases affecting idea generation, while
Liedtka (2015) presents a taxonomy of biases in three broad categories, proposing corresponding remedies and
linking them to specific design tools. Another important contribution comes from Sanchez Ruelas et al. (2021),
who examines decision-making phases in design and development, identifying biases at each stage and suggesting
mitigation techniques.
In addition to these broad studies, several researchers have conducted in-depth investigations of individual
biases. A key area of research has been design fixation, a bias that restricts creativity and leads designers to
rely too heavily on existing solutions. Youmans (2011) explores how design fixation affects team dynamics and
the use of prototypes during idea generation, while Youmans (2014) further refines the concept by categorizing
different types of fixation. Alipour et al. (2018) provides an overview of multiple studies on design fixation,
consolidating knowledge on this phenomenon.
Ownership bias, which influences designers’ attachment to their ideas, has been analyzed by several authors.
Onarheim and Christensen (2012) examines its impact on concept selection, and Zheng and Miller (2021)
expands on this research. Nikander et al. (2014) also addresses ownership bias, specifically in the context of
concept selection.
Another well-documented bias in design is the bias against creativity, along with associated factors such as
risk attitude and ambiguity aversion. Toh and Miller (2015, 2016a, 2016b) investigate how these biases shape
decision-making in concept selection.
Additional biases have been explored in the context of engineering design . Onarheim and Christensen (2012)
studies complexity bias and its implications for concept selection. Lopez et al. (2019) examines automation bias,
also known as machine bias, and its effect on decision-making in design. Mirabito and Goucher-Lambert (2021)
provides insights into confidence bias and authority bias, further expanding the understanding of cognitive
distortions in design processes.
Together, these studies highlight the growing awareness of cognitive biases in engineering design and product
development. While the field is still developing, ongoing research continues to reveal how biases shape design
decisions and how they can be mitigated through structured interventions.

Task-based taxonomies
Task-based classifications organize biases by identifying the specific cognitive or procedural phases in which
they arise. These frameworks help map biases to real-world actions, offering practical insights into their role
during decision-making.
Dimara et al. (2020) proposed a task-based taxonomy that identifies seven categories where biases commonly
manifest: estimation, decision-making, hypothesis assessment, causal attribution, recall, opinion reporting, and
other tasks. Additionally, the authors further subdivide each of these task categories into five sub-categories,
referred to as "flavors." These flavors do not represent sub-tasks but instead reflect different types of underlying
characteristics: association, baseline, inertia, outcome, and self-perspective. Dimara et al. (2020) identified and
defined 154 biases in this taxonomy; however, due to their extensive nature, these biases will not be discussed
further in this context.
Building on Dimara’s work (Dimara et al., 2020), Jimenez et al. (2024) offered an expanded taxonomy that
integrates task-based and source-based classifications. This study considering cognitive biases directly in design,
therefore the task-based portion includes only four categories, the ones thought relevant in the design process:
estimation, decision-making, hypothesis assessment, and recall. In the review, a total of 14 biases are discussed:
6 in decision-making (attraction effect, escalation of commitment, framing effect, IKEA effect, mere-exposure
effect, zero risk), 4 in recall (hindsight bias, processing difficulty effect, picture superiority effect, serial position
effect), and 2 each in estimation (availability bias, anchoring bias) and hypothesis assessment (confirmation bias,
illusory correlation effect). Additionally, the author introduced eight sources of biases, which draw from Dror’s
framework (Dror, 2020), including: data and information, reference materials, contextual information, base
rate, organizational factors, education and training, personal factors, and human and cognitive brain factors.
Pohl adopted a task-based approach, categorizing biases into three broad domains: memory, thinking, and
judgment. These can be seen as high-level tasks that offer a simplified framework for understanding how biases
impact fundamental cognitive processes. (Dimara et al., 2020)
Sanchez Ruelas et al. (2021) classified biases according to distinct phases of a decision-making model. These
phases include clarification - the "how" decision, analysis-realization, analysis-evaluation, and the "that" deci-
sion. Ruelas provided a phase-oriented framework that aligns closely with practical applications. In this frame-
work, specific biases are linked to each phase: 2 biases (optimism bias and planning fallacy, overconfidence) are
associated with clarification, 3 biases (bounded awareness, confirmation, search type) with analysis-realization, 3
biases (anchoring, inconsistency, redundancy) with analysis-evaluation, and 2 biases (framing and loss aversion,
sunk cost fallacy) with the "that" decision. A total of 10 biases are discussed.
Hogarth mapped biases onto a decision-support system schema, associating them with high-level compo-
29
==PAGE:31==
nents: acquisition, processing, output, and feedback. (Hogarth, 1987) Hogarth discussed a total of 46 biases,
distributed across the components of his decision-support system schema. For acquisition, examples include
recall, confirmation, and base rate biases, illustrating how information is selected and retained. In processing,
biases such as inconsistency, linear adjustment, and habit reveal distortions in handling and interpreting infor-
mation. Output encompasses biases like framing and desire, while feedback includes examples like hindsight and
testimony biases. This framework resembles the work of Sanchez Ruelas et al. (2021) who also aligned biases
with specific stages of decision-making, highlighting comparable attempts to integrate biases into structured
schemes.
Remus and Kottemann proposed a three-level taxonomy to categorize biases (Remus & Kottemann, 1986).
The first level distinguishes between two broad groups: biases associated with presenting data to decision-
makers and biases affecting information processing. Within the first group, biases are further categorized into
irrelevant information, data presentation biases, selective perception, and frequency effects. The second group
includes biases in information processing, which are subdivided into heuristics, misunderstanding of statistical
properties, search , conservatism, and linear. Inside each of this subcategories, the biases were included.
Similarity-Based Taxonomies
In contrast to task-based frameworks, similarity-based classifications group biases by their shared heuristics,
conceptual bases, or psychological origins. These frameworks emphasize the common cognitive mechanisms
underlying various biases.
Tversky and Kahneman (1974) developed one of the earliest similarity-based frameworks, grouping biases
according to their underlying heuristics. These categories include availability, representativeness, and anchoring
and adjustment. Their groundbreaking work demonstrated how intuitive mental shortcuts lead to systematic
errors in judgment. Examples include anchoring and adjustment biases such as conjunction, disjunction, and
overconfidence; availability biases like correlation, imaginability, recall and search; and representativeness biases
including base rate, redundancy, and sample.
Expanding on Tversky and Kahneman’s work, McCollough et al. (2013) identified six key heuristics driving
biases: framing, availability/exposure, anchoring and adjustment, representativeness/attribute substitution, af-
fect, and temporal (hyperbolic) discounting. Examples include in-group bias and negativity effect under the
affect heuristic; conservatism bias, planning fallacy, and status quo bias within the anchoring and adjustment
heuristic; attentional bias, confirmation bias, herding bias, observational selection bias, and recency bias un-
der availability or exposure heuristics; functional fixedness and observer-expectancy bias as framing heuristic
biases; base rate neglect, gambler’s fallacy, and projection (false consensus bias) within representativeness or
attribute substitution heuristics; and optimism bias and remoteness of impact bias under temporal (hyperbolic)
discounting.
Arnott (1998, 2006) developed a similarity-based taxonomy that grouped biases into six categories: memory
biases, statistical biases, confidence biases, adjustment biases, presentation biases, and situational biases. Arnott
identified a total of 37 biases across these categories. Examples include hindsight, imaginability, and recall biases
under memory biases; base rate, correlation, and conjunction biases under statistical biases; and overconfidence,
redundancy, and selectivity biases under confidence biases. Despite its similarity-based nature, this framework
also attempts to connect biases to specific moments within the decision-making process, using Hogarth’s decision-
support schema to connect the categories with each component of the schema.
Fleischmann presented another similarity-based classification, categorizing biases into eight broad domains:
action-oriented biases, decision biases, interest biases, memory biases, pattern recognition biases, perception
biases, social biases, and stability biases. (Schmitt et al., 2022). This taxonomy includes eight cognitive bias
categories and respective examples in software development. Examples of these biases include overconfidence
as an action-oriented bias, hyperbolic discounting as a decision bias, confirmation bias under interest bias,
hindsight bias as a memory bias, availability bias in pattern recognition, fixation as a perception bias, the
bandwagon effect under social biases, and anchoring and adjustment as a stability bias.
Baron categorized biases based on the normative models they violate and their underlying psychological
explanations. (Dimara et al., 2020)
30
==PAGE:32==
Larrick (2004) focused on the nature of biases, classifying them into three categories: psychophysically-based
errors (system 1), association-based errors (system 1), and strategy-based errors (system 2). This classification
emphasizes the cognitive mechanisms driving biased decision-making. 9 biases are described, including anchor-
ing, confirmation bias, hindsight bias, and subconscious priming under association-based errors; curvature of
the probability weighting function, preference reversals due to joint vs. separate evaluation, and status quo bias
within psychophysically-based errors; and lexicography effect and positive test strategies within strategy-based
errors.
Finally, Soll et al. (2015) grouped biases based on reasoning flaws, distinguishing between narrow thinking
and the interplay between system 1 and system 2 thinking.
This classification reinforces the dual-system
framework for understanding cognitive errors.
Other Taxonomies
Liedtka (2015) proposed a context-specific taxonomy tailored to engineering design.
This classification
included three categories: self-centric cognitive biases, users’ inability to articulate accurate needs and feedback,
and flaws in decision-makers’ ability to test hypotheses effectively. This approach underscores the impact of
context on the manifestation of biases.
This led to the identification of novel biases, or at least those not
commonly discussed in other taxonomies, such as the say/do gap, which highlights people’s inability to always
articulate what they actually want, as part of the second category. Another example, is the Egocentric empathy
gap inside self-centric cognitive biases, where the designer assume users needs and preferences based on their
own.
Finally, Youmans (2011) developed a taxonomy specifically for design fixation. Unlike broader taxonomies,
this framework is unique in that it focuses on a single heuristic or bias, going in-depth into its classification,
allowing a more detailed understanding of how design fixation manifest. Fixation is classified based on three di-
mensions: order, mental domains and spaces, and form or type of design fixation. The order dimension includes
first-order, second-order, third-order, and fourth-order fixation. The mental domains and spaces dimension dif-
ferentiates between conceptual-based and knowledge-based fixation. Finally, the form or type of design fixation
encompasses unconscious adherence to prior designs, conscious blocks to change, and intentional resistance to
new ideas.
4.1.3
Relevance of bias taxonomies for concept selection
Similarity-based taxonomies provide valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms behind cognitive
biases. This thematic classifications helps organize the immense amount of known biases, many of which closely
relate to one another. However, the taxonomies that focus on the heuristics behind the origin of biases are
often not exhaustive, as biases come from complex cognitive shortcuts, making it very difficult to propose an
integrated yet simple classification. Even if understanding the nature of biases is important, these classifications
are more theoretical than practical, offering limited applicability for engineers navigating concept selection.
In contrast, task-based taxonomies categorize biases according to when they are most likely to emerge. This
approach aligns more closely with the objectives of this study, as it provides a direct link between biases and
specific design activities.
Nevertheless, existing tasked-based taxonomies do not fit the needs. The tasks proposed tend to be high
level, often using cognitive science terminology that is difficult for engineers to directly relate to their day-
to-day decision-making processes. To address this gap, this study proposes a refined approach that connects
biases to connect biases to recurrent activities of the concept evaluation and selection process the engineers can
directly relate too. While cognitive functions remain the foundation of bias emergence, aligning biases with
recognizable engineering tasks ensures that engineers can easily identify when biases are most likely to arise and
apply appropriate mitigation strategies in real-world scenarios.

While different
methods take distinct approaches, certain recurring tasks appear across both structured and heuristic decision-

- Definition of objectives and criteria
- Analysis of concepts
- Comparing alternatives
- Evaluation of concepts. Scoring and ranking
- Combining and refining concepts
- Prioritizing and final selection
- Verification and validation
While previous research has classified design cognition broadly, the connection to concept selection remains
under explored. Based on the classification of cognitive processes in conceptual design by Hay et al. (2017), this
study explores how specific cognitive functions relate to concept evaluation and selection activities.
In the concept evaluation and selection stage, several cognitive processes come into play. The most clearly
identifiable ones are addressed under planning, monitoring, and selection. These include reasoning (i.e. drawing

it extends into the concept selection process as well. Furthermore, visual perception, mental imagery processing,
long-term memory retrieval, and semantic processing serve as foundational cognitive processes that support
higher-order activities in concept selection. These processes are consistently involved across all tasks, enabling
designers to retrieve information, interpret criteria, visualize alternatives, and process semantic relationships.

Summary
Understanding and categorizing cognitive biases in concept selection is essential for developing effective
mitigation strategies. While various taxonomies provide valuable frameworks, many remain too theoretical or
broad for direct application in engineering design. This section highlights the need for a task-based approach

4.2
Debiasing strategies and their classification in decision-making and design
This section examines debiasing strategies in engineering design and other fields to ensure relevant insights
are considered. It presents approaches to mitigating biases, explores the role of design methods as debiasing
tools, and analyzes existing classification frameworks to assess their applicability in concept selection.
4.2.1
Debiasing in engineering design and product development
Most of the literature on cognitive biases in engineering design has focused on proving their existence and
studying their effects on design output. However, fewer studies have explored ways to mitigate these biases and
improve decision-making within the design process.
33
==PAGE:35==
When it comes to debiasing strategies, most of the research focuses on mitigating biases on a one-to-one
basis, meaning that specific strategies are designed to counteract individual biases.
Jimenez et al. (2024)
introduces a methodology that integrates bias awareness into the design process, offering prompt questions
to help designers identify and question each bias individually. Similarly, Sanchez Ruelas et al. (2021) provides
structured strategies to address biases on a case-by-case basis, aligning mitigation techniques with specific biases
encountered at different stages of the design process. Matthiessen et al. (2022) presents the ACH (Analysis
of Competing Hypotheses) method as a way to reduce confirmation bias in failure analysis, while Youmans
(2014) focuses on countering design fixation by developing tailored interventions for different types and levels
of fixation.
Unlike these one-to-one approaches, McCollough et al. (2013) and Liedtka (2015) take a broader perspec-
tive by proposing debiasing strategies that target groups or categories of biases rather than addressing them
individually, recognizing that certain biases tend to cluster together and influence decision-making in similar
ways.
4.2.2
Design methods as debiasing techniques
Another promising approach to debiasing in engineering design is the use of design methods as debiasing tools.
Many established design methodologies inherently promote analytical and deliberate thinking, counteracting
the fast, intuitive judgments that often lead to cognitive biases. These methods encourage structured reflection,
systematic exploration of alternatives, and externalization of reasoning processes, all of which help mitigate
biases that stem from narrow thinking, overconfidence, and cognitive inertia. By fostering a more deliberate
design process, these approaches can serve as natural safeguards against biases.
Some researchers have already explored this connection. Liedtka (2015) proposed a framework linking tools
used in design thinking to different categories of biases, suggesting that structured methods can help design-
ers recognize and counteract biased thinking. However, Liedtka’s work did not address debiasing strategies in
concept selection. Similarly, Youmans (2014) explored various techniques to counteract design fixation, demon-
strating that structured methods can effectively reduce fixation and encourage more diverse idea generation.
However, Youmans’ proposals were limited to design fixation and did not extend to other biases affecting the
design process.
While these studies provide valuable insights, there remains an opportunity to further explore how broader
design methodologies can serve as cognitive debiasing tools across different stages of the design process. More-
over, since these methods are already familiar to design engineers, they may be easier to implement than external
interventions. For those skeptical of formal debiasing techniques, framing design methodologies as tools to pre-
vent flawed decision-making could help increase acceptance, demonstrating their value not just for structuring
the design process but also for enhancing judgment and decision quality.
4.2.3
Classification of debiasing techniques
While research on cognitive biases is extensive, fewer studies focus on structuring and categorizing debiasing
techniques. The existing taxonomies differ in scope, with some offering descriptive classifications and others pre-
senting practical tools to mitigate biases. It is worth noting that none of the taxonomies presented in this section
have been developed in the context of design engineering. And discarding the previously discussed debiasing
works applied to design engineering, none of this other frameworks explicitly connect debiasing strategies to
specific biases or groups of biases, they just introduce general techniques without making such links. Therefore,
remaining very theoretical.
Fischhoff (1982) provided one of the earliest classifications of debiasing strategies, categorizing them based on
their implicit allegation of culpability. The taxonomy distinguishes between faulty tasks (which include unfair or
misunderstood tasks), faulty judges (which can be either perfectible or incorrigible individuals), and mismatches
between judges and tasks (which can be addressed through restructuring or education). It’s suggested that
biases stem from errors in these areas.
However, this framework remains purely descriptive and does not
propose specific debiasing techniques.
34
==PAGE:36==
Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, proposed a high-level taxonomy that categorizes debiasing strategies,
referred to as intervention, into change-based and control-based interventions. Change-based strategies focus
on reducing individuals’ automatic associations, while control-based strategies aim to prevent these associations
from influencing behavior (Zalta et al., 2015). This classification remains largely conceptual, without specifying
concrete techniques or tools.
Soll et al. (2015) developed a structured taxonomy that distinguishes between two main approaches to
debiasing: modifying the decision-maker and modifying the environment. The first category, modifying the
decision-maker, focuses on enhancing individual cognition and decision-making processes through structured
interventions. Education plays a key role in this approach, aiming to improve awareness and understanding of
biases. Soll also highlights various cognitive strategies designed to counteract narrow thinking, which includes
techniques for generating alternatives to ensure multiple possibilities are considered, methods for dealing with
optimism to prevent overconfidence, judgment accuracy training and assessing uncertainty skills. Another key
aspect of this approach is the use of decision models such as quantitative models or checklists to improve
judgment. The second category, modifying the environment, focuses on reshaping the external conditions in
which decisions are made, rather than altering individual cognition. This involves two broad strategies: economic
levers and nudges. Economic levers, which use external incentives and accountability mechanisms to encourage
more rational decision-making, which include incentivizing wise choices, introducing accountability, providing
new information, and conveying social norms. On the other hand we have nudges, nudges are strategically
designed interventions that use psychological principles to subtly guide individuals toward better decisions
without restricting their freedom of choice (Smith & Peloghitis, 2020). Soll classifies nudges into four distinct
types: Defaults, nudges that kindly shape information (Metric transformations, Kind Representations, Smart
Disclosure), nudges to induce reflection (Planning Prompts, Forced Breaks, Active Choice, Checklists), and
nudges to induce future-focused thinking (Choose in Advance, Pre-commitment). While this taxonomy includes
specific debiasing tools, it does not explicitly connect them to particular biases.
Example in action. Nudges
Our decisions are shaped by the way choices are presented! Supermarkets use this to their advantage.
Ever notice how essentials like milk and eggs are placed at the back? That’s no accident. It encourages
shoppers to walk through the store, increasing the chances of impulse buys.
This is a strategic placement nudge, where the layout subtly influences behavior without forcing
choices. A perfect example of how design shapes decisions.
Larrick (2004), similar to Soll et al. (2015) differentiates between prescriptive strategies aimed at improving
individual decision-making and techniques used by external agents to modify the decision environment, though
his framework places a stronger emphasis on prescriptive strategies. The prescriptive strategies are divided in
modifying the cognitive strategies of individuals and expanding strategies to include techniques external to the
decision maker. The first approach enhances individual reasoning through motivational strategies like incentives
and accountability, along with cognitive techniques such as considering the opposite, training in logical rules,
and bias-awareness education. The second approach introduces technological decision aids, including group
decision-making models, linear models, multi-attribute utility analysis (MAU), decision analysis (DA), and
decision support systems (DSS), providing structured frameworks to reduce bias. On the other side, external
techniques used to modify the decision environment involve re-biasing, where one bias is used to counteract
another, and changing the decision environment, adjusting how choices and information are presented to guide
better decisions. Larrick’s taxonomy includes numerous specific tools, but they are not mapped directly to
individual biases.
In the same line of thought, the study of Smith and Peloghitis (2020) differentiates between strategies aimed
at modifying the person and those focused on modifying the environment, but it remains more conceptual
and provides fewer concrete tools for implementation. The first category includes techniques such as improv-
ing knowledge of probability and statistics, introducing decision devices, analytically reconstructing arguments
and data, identifying premises and conclusions, examining issues from multiple perspectives, and using check-
lists.. The second category incorporates interventions like nudges, forced breaks, partitioning work into smaller
segments, planning prompts, and checklists. Once again, while this taxonomy introduces concrete debiasing
techniques, it does not explicitly connect them to specific biases.
35
==PAGE:37==
Croskerry et al. (2013a) studied debiasing in medical diagnosis and proposed a comprehensive debiasing
framework that focuses on two primary areas: educational strategies and workplace interventions, with an
additional category for structured forcing functions. The educational component emphasizes long-term strategies
for improving decision-making by fostering critical thinking and bias awareness, including for example training
in reasoning theories, cognitive tutoring systems, or simulation training. The workplace component shifts the
focus to real-time debiasing techniques applied within professional environments. This includes strategies such as
structured data acquisition, affective debiasing, and group decision-making approaches. Additionally, Croskerry
introduces forcing functions, which impose structured constraints to prevent biased decisions altogether. These
include statistical and clinical prediction rules, cognitive forcing strategies, or ruling out worst-case scenarios.
Croskerry’s taxonomy provides many practical tools, but similar to other authors discussed they aren’t connected
to specific biases.
Keren’s classification, distinguishing between procedural techniques and structure-modifying techniques
(Arnott, 2006). Procedural techniques focus on decision-making processes where the user is unaware of the
internal structure of the problem, meaning the bias operates implicitly. In contrast, structure-modifying tech-
niques allow the user to actively manipulate the task’s internal structure, potentially reducing bias through
intentional adjustments. This taxonomy is descriptive and does not propose practical debiasing methods.
Beaulac and Kenyon (2018) and Kenyon and Beaulac (2014) introduced a four-level debiasing taxonomy,
structuring interventions based on their depth and effectiveness. Level 1 debiasing occurs passively through
long-term education, environmental influences, and critical thinking training, preventing biases from forming in
the first place. Level 2 debiasing addresses biases that emerge but are corrected through individual cognitive
or behavioral strategies, often aided by critical thinking training. Level 3 debiasing involves situational infras-
tructure changes that help individuals mitigate bias in real-time decision-making. Finally, Level 4 debiasing
applies when a biased judgment persists, but external situational constraints help prevent biased outcomes.
While this taxonomy provides a structured progression of debiasing interventions, it does not include specific
tools or techniques.
Rather than developing a fixed taxonomy of debiasing strategies, Schmitt et al. (2022) introduced a flexible
classification tool that allows practitioners to systematically categorize any debiasing method they consider
useful. This framework helps users understand what each strategy targets, ensuring better alignment between
debiasing techniques and specific cognitive challenges. The framework is structured around two dimensions:
content level and implementation level, as illustrated in Figure 8.

4.2.4
Summary
There have been some efforts inside engineering design to propose strategies to tackle biases, but none
that directly discuss concept selection. An interesting approach is to use existing design methods as debiasing
tools since they already encourage structured thinking and alternative exploration. Some authors have already
explored this approach to some extent, linking design methods to specific cognitive biases. Because engineers
are familiar with these methods, they may be easier to adopt. However, since many structured approaches are
36
==PAGE:38==
already underused, the same risk applies here. Clearly linking these methods to specific biases and explaining
their role in improving decision-making could help increase acceptance, but this might not be the case. These
methods should be combining with smaller, more flexible strategies to allow engineers to choose the most fitting
approach for their needs.
Despite the variety of taxonomies proposed, most remain theoretical and lack practical application. Many do
not propose concrete strategies or tools, and those that do, often fail to directly connect them to specific biases.
While there is no clear consensus on classification, a common distinction is between approaches that focus on
modifying the individual and those that seek to alter the design brief or decision environment to reduce the
appearance of bias. Given the objectives of this thesis, providing tools that support individuals in recognizing
and addressing biases, we will focus on the former.
4.3
Industry perspectives on bias & concept selection
The exploratory interviews give a closer look at how concept selection happens in practice, the challenges and
limitations professionals face. They provide nuanced insights into the perspectives and attitudes of engineers,
offering a deeper understanding of how biases are perceived and addressed in real-world decision-making.

4.3.2
Summary and implications
The interviews reinforce existing literature showing that formal concept selection methods are rarely used
in industry. Several reasons were cited, including lack of resources, limited awareness of available methods, and
a broader lack of recognition of engineering design as a structured discipline.
The two companies take different approaches: one generates multiple independent solutions before selecting
the most promising (Productive Solution Generation), while the other selects a single concept early and refines
it step by step (Corrective Solution Generation). As identified in the literature, concept evaluation and selection
40
==PAGE:42==
can take many forms, with strategies varying based on project constraints, industry requirements, and team
dynamics. Despite these differences, evaluation remains largely informal in both cases. Instead, decisions are
often driven by experience, intuition, and informal discussions.
The existence of biases in concept selection was validated, as participants described real-world examples
aligning with known biases such as confirmation bias and ownership bias. The findings confirm that practitioners
do recognize these tendencies, however, there is a general unawareness of the concept of biases.
While no
structured debiasing approaches are in place, there are implicit efforts to promote critical thinking and objective
decision-making. But most importantly, engineers recognize the relevance of bias awareness and express openness
to integrating targeted strategies into their workflows.
Providing engineers with practical tools to identify and address biases in real time can turn implicit awareness
into a more deliberate and intentional approach. However, insights from the interviews highlight the need for
these tools to integrate seamlessly into existing workflows to avoid being perceived as a burden.
4.3.3
Challenges and limitations
- Limited sample size: Insights are drawn from a small group of participants, which may not fully
represent broader industry trends.
- Reliability of participant responses: Many of the concepts discussed are complex and may not have
been explicitly addressed by participants before. As a result, interviewees may have struggled to articulate
their thoughts fully or may have relied on surface-level explanations.
- Potential biases inside the questions: It’s important to acknowledge that biases may already be
present in the answers themselves. For instance, when recalling past experiences, participants may unin-
tentionally omit key details, either because they believe them to be irrelevant or because their memories
are influenced by more recent projects, leading to potential recall bias. Another challenge is the variation
in how participants interpret situations.
- Social desirability: As with other interview-based studies, interviewees may feel pressured to provide
definitive or socially acceptable answers, especially when they are uncertain about the "correct" response.
- Researcher bias: The reliability of the researcher plays a role not only in interpreting the data but also
in guiding the interview, potentially shaping it.
These limitations have be taken into account when interpreting the findings, ensuring that conclusions are
drawn with appropriate caution and contextual awareness. For example, when preparing the interview guide a
section was created for each question regarding potential biases that could appear.

Example of procedure
It is important to constantly keep in mind what each activity actually implicates as there could be some
overlaps, especially since different concept selection strategies might use a different combination of activities.
Many of these activities are interconnected and sometimes occur implicitly, making it essential to carefully
determine where biases should be assigned. This is especially critical between analysis of concepts, comparing
alternatives, evaluation of concepts, and prioritizing and final selection. Analysis is the foundation of the other
three. Without a clear understanding of each concept, it is impossible to compare alternatives, assign scores, or
46
==PAGE:48==
make a final selection. However, while all stages depend on analysis, they serve distinct purposes. The following
questions can be asked to help decide in which activities the bias is actually emerging:
- Am I trying to understand or judge a concept alone?
→Analysis of concepts. This stage focuses
on understanding an individual concept in isolation, exploring its strengths, weaknesses, and feasibility
without comparing it to others or assigning scores.
- Am I looking at how multiple concepts compare to each other? →Comparing alternatives. This stage
directly contrasts multiple concepts to highlight differences and trade-offs but does not involve scoring or
ranking.
- Am I assigning numbers or ratings to how well each concept fits the criteria? →Evaluation of concepts.
This stage translates insights into structured judgments, assigning values to concepts based on predefined
criteria and ranking them accordingly.
- Am I making the final decision on which concept(s) to move forward with? →Prioritizing and final
selection. This stage involves choosing the best concept(s) based on prior analysis and evaluation, often
incorporating intuition, experience, or external influences beyond structured rankings. It can deviate from
previous results based on the critical thinking of the engineer.
An example on how to do this process in presented below for Action Bias, the tendency to favor action over
inaction, even when remaining still may be the better choice.
Activities where it is relevant:
- Prioritizing and Final Selection: Engineers might feel pressure to pick a concept rather than delaying for
further analysis, leading to rushed decisions. Am I choosing a concept just because I feel the need to act,
even if waiting is better?
- Combining and Refining Concepts: Engineers may push for unnecessary modifications, adding complexity
instead of maintaining a simpler, well-functioning design. Am I changing a design just for the sake of
doing something?
Activities where it is not relevant:
- Definition of Objectives and Criteria: This stage is about establishing guidelines, not making real-time
decisions where action bias plays a role.
- Analysis of Concepts: Engineers are evaluating details and understanding a concept’s feasibility, not
deciding whether to act.
- Comparing Alternatives: This step focuses on identifying differences rather than prompting engineers to
take action.
- Evaluation of Concepts (Scoring and Ranking): Since scoring is a structured process, the tendency to act
impulsively is less relevant.
- Verification and Validation: This stage is about assessing alignment with objectives, not making unnec-
essary modifications or premature decisions.
Assigning debiasing strategies to biases and activities
As discussed in Section 3.4.2, the debiasing strategies proposed in this study originate from multiple sources,
including existing literature on debiasing, insights from exploratory interviews, design engineering methodolo-
gies, and broader disciplines such as product development and critical thinking facilitation. While these tools
are often introduced, they are rarely examined in relation to specific cognitive biases, and there is a significant
gap in the literature linking these strategies to concept selection processes.
This study presents a novel, systematic approach to addressing this gap by analyzing the fundamental actions
underlying each method. Some key functions of these methods might include structuring analytical thinking,
broadening perspectives, or enhancing traceability. To effectively locate each method and strategy, it is crucial
to determine how it supports designers and, consequently, which biases it mitigates.

4.5.2
Summary and implications
This work takes a novel and practical step toward making bias mitigation more applicable in concept selec-
tion. Instead of just identifying where biases occur, it integrates debiasing strategies into existing engineering
workflows, helping move from general awareness to concrete action. The inclusion of strategies derived from
design methodologies increases their potential acceptance, as engineers are already familiar with many of these
techniques. However, caution is needed to ensure these methods are not perceived as extra steps that slow down
the process, but rather as tools that enhance decision quality.
4.5.3
Challenges and limitations
As in the previous section, a key limitation of this work is the reliance on qualitative judgment to connect
biases with specific concept selection activities and debiasing strategies. Since the available research on debiasing
in concept selection is sparse, qualitative judgment was necessary to match strategies with biases and tasks.
The goal was to create a practical framework that engineers can use intuitively, while also allowing room for
future refinement as more empirical data becomes available.
Future research will be needed to refine these mappings through empirical validation, exploring which strate-
gies are most effective in practice. The adaptability of this approach allows for expansion, integrating new
findings as engineering practices evolve.
4.6
Bias cards for design teams
The Bias Prompt Cards are the result of the research process, translating key findings into a practical tool for
engineer teams. This section presents the finalized card deck, describing its purpose, description, and intended
applications. It also includes insights from an initial evaluation to understand its usability, application and
effectiveness.
4.6.1
Purpose
The finalized Bias Card deck serves as a practical tool for teams to recognize and mitigate biases that may
unconsciously influence design decisions. By fostering awareness of cognitive biases, the cards support teams
in making more objective and well-informed decisions, improving critical thinking and design outcomes. The
cards are not a definitive solution but rather a tool to provoke thought, foster discussion, and support the design
process. The cards serve three key purposes:
- To identify cognitive biases that may impact design and development projects, helping teams recognize
potential blind spots in their decision-making processes.
- To encourage structured reflection on how biases shape judgment, team dynamics, and problem-solving,
prompting teams to critically assess their assumptions and perspectives.
- To provide actionable strategies to mitigate or reduce biases, and enhance the overall design process
They provide an accessible and practical way to integrate bias awareness into the design process, making
it easier for designers to engage with this important issue without requiring extensive training or additional
resources. Their ease of use ensures that bias awareness becomes a natural and ongoing part of design practice,
rather than something that is completely overlooked or forgotten in the fast-paced nature of design work.

==PAGE:50==
4.6.2
Description of the Bias Prompt Cards
Structure of the deck and the cards
There are a total of 33 bias cards in the deck, along with an additional card providing an overview of the deck
and its main objectives. The information card provides an introduction to the Bias Prompt Cards, outlining
their purpose and how they can be effectively used by design teams. It emphasizes that these cards help teams
recognize and mitigate cognitive biases that may unconsciously influence their design choices. Each of the main
cards in the deck corresponds to a specific bias listed in Subsection 4.4 and features four key elements on its
front side:
- Name of the bias: The specific bias being addressed (e.g., Confirmation Bias)
- Description: A brief explanation of the bias, outlining its general characteristics and how it affects
decision-making.
- Example: A concise, real-world scenario specific to engineering design, illustrating how the bias can
manifest. The aim is to create relatable experiences that help engineers recognize biases in their projects,
as some biases can be abstract and difficult to directly connect to practical situations.
- Mitigation strategy: Suggested techniques to minimize or counteract the bias. This element is particu-
larly novel, as many studies focus primarily on identifying biases without offering actionable solutions for
addressing them.
The back of each card features the title of the deck, Bias Prompt Cards, along with the university’s logo and
the name of the department that collaborated on the research. As illustrated in Figure 11, the Bias Prompt
Cards include an overview card, which provides an introduction and usage guidelines, as well as bias-specific
cards that describe individual cognitive biases along with examples and mitigation strategies.
The full collection of cards can be found in Appendix F.

Content Decisions and Exclusions
As just discussed, there are four key elements in each of the bias cards. Some things were included in the
research but were deliberately left out of the cards.
Firstly, the specific stage or task in which a bias is most likely to appear was excluded. This decision was
made to enhance the cards’ flexibility, ensuring that design teams and individuals can use them as they see fit
across different stages of the design and product development process. While the research primarily focused on
the concept selection stage of engineering design, the intention is for the cards to be applicable throughout the
50
==PAGE:52==
entire design project.
Another element that was purposefully omitted were prompt questions.
The draft card set that DTU
Skylab started working on included description, example and prompt question. However, these were found
to be somewhat redundant, as they prompt questions basically just asked if you had considered the bias.
Nevertheless, its worth noting as many of the literature papers also proposed prompt questions as debiasing
tools. In our case this were substituted with mitigation techniques that propose direct strategies to mitigate or
minimize biases.
Additionally, an important consideration was to avoid overwhelming users with excessive information. In-
cluding too many details on each card could reduce engagement and usability, making it harder for teams to
integrate the tool into their workflow effectively.
Format and visual identity
The tool was developed as a card deck, but a different format could have served the same purpose, such as a
digital tool or a checklist-style framework. The choice of using physical cards was intentional. Having a tangible
object allows users to engage with the content in a hands-on way rather than navigating abstract guidelines
or lengthy reports. One of the challenges when working with biases is that, while most people understand the
general concept and can acknowledge how biases interfere with rational thinking, biases themselves operate at
a subconscious level. This makes them particularly difficult to keep in mind and recognize in real time. The
physical cards give users something they can handle, keep in sight, and refer to when needed, making bias
awareness an active and continuous process rather than a passive or theoretical one
Additionally, the card format offers key advantages in terms of portability and flexibility. Unlike digital
tools that require access to a screen or software, cards can be used anywhere, such as in meetings, workshops,
or informal discussions, without technological barriers. Their physical nature also allows teams to keep them
visible as a constant reminder throughout a project. Furthermore, the deck is customizable, allowing teams to
just take a few cards and focus on the biases most relevant to their specific project rather than working through
an exhaustive list. This adaptability makes the tool more practical for various contexts and workflows.
The decision to use cards was also inspired by other decks developed by DTU Skylab, such as the Ability
Prompt Cards and Principle Prompt Cards, which have been proven effective in similar contexts.
In terms of design and visual identity, the Bias Prompt Cards were created to maintain consistency with
these existing card decks. This includes using the same color scheme, graphic style, and overall composition,
ensuring visual coherence and familiarity for users who may have interacted with Skylab’s previous tools.
4.6.3
Practical application
The card set is designed to be flexible and adaptable to different team needs and contexts. Given the number
of cards, it is recommended that teams do not use the entire deck simultaneously but instead select a subset of
cards at a time. There are several ways they can be applied in practice:
- As a teaching tool in classroom settings. Educators can integrate the cards into coursework or
workshops to facilitate discussions about bias in design.
- As a shared resource during workshops or discussions. The cards can be placed on tables for
participants to interact with and reference throughout the design process.
- By assigning each team member a specific card. Each person becomes responsible for monitoring
and addressing the bias described on their assigned card during design discussions.
- With a facilitator or project manager overseeing the process. A designated person can introduce
and guide the use of the cards, ensuring that bias awareness is consistently incorporated into decision-
making.
51
==PAGE:53==
- For conducting bias audits on existing projects. Teams can systematically review past or ongoing
projects to identify cognitive biases that may have influenced decisions, assess their impact, and apply
mitigation strategies to improve future outcomes.
Beyond these applications, the cards could also be used in self-reflection exercises for individual designers,
or even team-building activities focused on improving critical thinking and decision-making. The adaptability
of the cards makes them a valuable tool for fostering more conscious and fair design practices.
4.6.4
Evaluation results
A preliminary evaluation was conducted in the form of a small exercise with four participants working in
pairs. Given the small sample size, the goal was not to collect definitive data but rather to gain an initial
understanding of how users interact with the cards. This early feedback served as a foundation for refining the
cards in future iterations, ensuring they effectively support design teams in recognizing and mitigating cognitive
biases.
General impressions
Participants found the Bias Cards helpful for identifying biases, but actively mitigating them in real-time
was challenging. Bias awareness emerged retrospectively, helping participants evaluate and refine their choices
rather than shaping them in the moment.
Instead of guiding the decision-making process, the cards were
primarily used after participants had already narrowed down their options, serving more as a reflection tool
than an active aid.
Participant had access to the bias cards at the same time that they were introduced to all the new concepts
during the second stage of the exercise, but they only referred to the cards after making initial judgments about
the designs. This suggests that for the cards to be more effective in shaping decisions rather than just evaluating
them, they may need to be introduced earlier in the process, before participants commit to a particular option.
A key hypothesis was that the cards would encourage a more structured evaluation by providing mitigation
strategies, but this was not observed in practice. But as it will be discussed afterwards, the debiasing section
was mostly overlooked potentially due to time constrains. If the goal is to foster a more structured approach,
additional guidance or prompts may be needed to ensure that participants engage with mitigation strategies in
real-time rather than as a post-analysis tool.
Next, the three key aspects of evaluation, usability, application, and effectiveness, are examined to under-
stand how the Bias Cards were perceived and their impact on the decision-making process.
Usability
Participants generally found the Bias Cards clear and understandable, and they were able to discuss biases
in relation to their decisions. However, some participants felt overwhelmed by the number of biases to track
simultaneously.
• "I think there are a lot of biases that you need to keep track of while thinking of something else, that was
a lot." (Participant 1)
The examples provided on the cards were useful and helped participants recognize biases in their own
thinking
• "With the examples, it was easy to understand." (Participant 3)
• "I mostly read the example, not the description." (Participant 4)
However, some debiasing strategies were overlooked or needed further explanation.
• "I haven’t considered this mitigation strategy section." (Participant 3)
52
==PAGE:54==
• "Some of them will need some explanation, for example, ‘Itemize response’ and ‘PMI,’ I don’t think that
many people would know." (Participant 2)
Application
All participants agreed that the Bias Cards could be easily integrated into design processes, but their real-
time use during concept evaluation was difficult. Instead, some participants found them more effective as a
retrospective tool to evaluate decisions as part of an iterative process.
• "I think it’s important to choose the moment to introduce them. . . not at the beginning because you say
‘yes, yes,’ but you later don’t use them. But also not too far in the process because you are already settled
in your idea, so you don’t want to. . . you are too biased." (Participant 4)
• "It’s something you should do afterwards; you can take a decision and look at this afterwards and think,
okay, maybe I was following this bias. Kind of in an iterative process" (Participant 1)
Regarding the format, most participants favored physical cards over digital versions, finding them more
engaging and easier to reference. When asked about alternative formats like a checklist, some worried that it
could become overwhelming.
• "I like to have them physically, not digital. For sure, because I think it helps fix things and keep them
present." (Participant 4)
• "I like it with the cards. For me, it’s just nice to read these things and play around a bit." (Participant 3)
• "Maybe a checklist could be fine, but I think maybe it will be too much." (Participant 3)
Effectiveness
The Bias Cards helped participants recognize and articulate biases they might not have otherwise considered,
sparking discussions about their influence on decision-making.
• "So obviously both of us have ownership bias" (Participant 1)
• "Actually at some point I remember thinking about the fixation or focus bias, because we were only focusing
to the space as a criteria and we stopped looking at other things." (Participant 4)
However, these discussions did not always lead to changes in outcomes, as biases were acknowledged but rarely
mitigated or fully incorporated into the decision-making framework. Debiasing strategies were not fully applied,
but they liked the idea of having solutions available. Some participants attributed this to time constraints.
• "Yes it makes sense that they propose something about it. [...] It’s nice to not only know you have to
change something, but also how can you change it." (Participant 3)
• "We didn’t follow them, we would have needed more time to follow them, but if we did, I think we would
have used them." (Participant 4)
4.6.5
Summary and implications
The Bias Prompt Cards provide a practical tool for integrating bias awareness into engineering design. Their
main advantage lies in their ability to transform abstract cognitive biases into tangible, recognizable challenges
within the decision-making process. Their physical format keeps biases present throughout the process, serving
as a continuous reminder.
One of the key strengths of this tool is its adaptability. The cards can be used in various settings, from
workshops and team discussions to individual reflection and structured design reviews. This flexibility ensures
that teams can incorporate bias awareness in a way that best fits their needs, whether as a proactive strategy
during concept selection or as a retrospective tool for evaluating past decisions. Beyond raising awareness, the
53
==PAGE:55==
cards include a range of debiasing techniques, varying in complexity, so engineers can choose strategies that are
relevant and practical for their specific context.
The initial evaluation highlighted both the benefits and challenges of the tool. Participants found the cards
useful for identifying biases, and the examples helped make abstract concepts more relatable. Still, real-time
application proved more difficult, as teams tended to use the cards retrospectively rather than as an active aid
during decision-making. While participants acknowledged the value of the debiasing strategies, they were not
always fully utilized, suggesting that additional guidance or facilitation might be needed. This may be partially
attributed to the time constraints and structure of the workshop, meaning the observed behavior might not
directly translate into other real-world design contexts where teams have more flexibility in integrating the cards
into their workflow.
4.6.6
Challenges and limitations
- Potential resistance or skepticism: Not all team members may be equally open to acknowledging
cognitive biases in their decision-making. Some may resist the idea of external tools influencing their
judgment, seeing them as unnecessary or impractical. Encouraging engagement without making the cards
feel prescriptive or intrusive could be a challenge.
- Hard adoption in real-world settings: While the cards are designed to integrate seamlessly into design
workflows, their use requires intentional adoption by teams. In fast-paced environments, bias reflection
may not always be prioritized, and teams may struggle to incorporate the cards without structured
facilitation or leadership support.
- Risk of superficial engagement: Users may recognize and discuss biases without actively applying
mitigation strategies. Without structured prompts or accountability mechanisms, the cards may serve
more as a discussion tool than a behavioral change tool.
- Unproven impact on design outcomes: While there is research supporting various debiasing strate-
gies, the study of their effectiveness in real-world design contexts remains premature. There is no definitive
evidence that using the cards directly improves decision-making or design outcomes, and further research
is needed to validate their impact on reducing bias.
Limitations of the evaluation
- Small sample size: With only four participants, the results are not generalizable, and findings remain
anecdotal. A larger study would be needed to draw stronger conclusions.
- Diverse participant backgrounds: The variation in experience and expertise among participants made
it difficult to assess whether engagement with the cards was influenced by prior knowledge or training in
bias awareness and decision-making frameworks or design methods.
- Time constraints and lack of iteration: The structured nature of the exercise, with limited discussion
time and a single design iteration, restricted participants’ ability to fully engage with the Bias Cards,
particularly the mitigation strategies. Testing the cards in a multi-stage design process could provide
deeper insights into their long-term impact and effectiveness.
- Limited iterative evaluation: In real-world design, decisions have financial, functional, or user impact.
In the experiment, there were no direct consequences, which may have affected how critically participants
engaged with bias awareness.

5
Conclusions
The study provides a comprehensive synthesis of cognitive biases and debiasing theory by integrating insights
from decision-making theory, psychology, design cognition, and other relevant fields. While numerous studies
have explored biases in decision-making, there has been no structured overview that consolidates this knowledge.
By analyzing existing literature, this research identifies key trends, areas of agreement, and inconsistencies across
different taxonomies and mitigation approaches.
This research is not limited to a literature review but also incorporates firsthand experiences from industry
professionals, providing practical insights into how biases manifest in real-world engineering contexts. Interviews
with industry professionals revealed that while practitioners may not explicitly recognize biases as cognitive
phenomena, they intuitively identify biased behaviors in their decision-making processes once described. Many
engineers already employ informal mitigation strategies without formally labeling them as such.
However,
these strategies are often inconsistent, highlighting the need for a clear, practical framework to help engineers
systematically address bias.
The interviews also reinforce the disconnect between academic research and industry practices.
While
structured concept selection methods exist in theory, they are often overlooked in practice. Instead, engineers
tend to rely on opportunistic, intuition-driven, and experience-based decision-making approaches. This lack
of formalized selection frameworks makes bias mitigation even more crucial, as unstructured decision-making
increases the risk of flawed judgments
Beyond contributing to academic knowledge, this research delivers practical tools to support engineers in
making more objective and structured evaluations.
A key outcome of this study is the development of a
structured list of biases relevant to concept selection, providing engineers with clear definitions and relatable
examples. To move from awareness to action, this research connects each bias to specific concept selection
activities and proposes targeted mitigation strategies, ranging from quick perspective-taking techniques to
structured design methods already familiar to engineers. This approach ensures that solutions are practical and
adaptable, allowing teams to select strategies that best align with their resources and project needs.
As a final outcome, this research developed a set of bias cards, a hands-on tool engineers can use during
concept selection. These cards serve as structured reminders of potential biases, offering simple yet effective
debiasing strategies tailored to specific engineering tasks. Their flexible format allows for seamless integration
into different workflows, ensuring that bias awareness leads to meaningful improvements in decision-making
rather than remaining an abstract concept.

Impact and Future Work
While this research provides a structured foundation for understanding and mitigating cognitive biases in
concept selection, several areas remain open for further investigation. Expanding upon this work can lead to a
deeper understanding of how biases influence decision-making and improve the effectiveness of bias mitigation
strategies in real-world applications.
- One potential area for future research involves broadening the scope of interviews with professionals in
the field. While the current study gathered valuable insights, increasing the number of participants and
diversifying their backgrounds could provide a more comprehensive perspective on which biases are most
prevalent in concept selection and what strategies they use to address objective critical thinking, implicit
debiasing strategies.
- Another key direction is the practical application and validation of bias mitigation strategies in real-world
engineering projects. While this research explored theoretical frameworks and proposed strategies, the
limited research on debiasing in concept selection required qualitative judgment to match strategies with
biases and tasks. Implementing and testing these methods in actual decision-making scenarios would
provide empirical evidence of their effectiveness. Conducting case studies or experimental studies within
industry settings could help refine these mappings, ensuring they are both practical and adaptable as
engineering practices evolve.
- Beyond concept selection, cognitive biases likely play a significant role in other stages of product devel-
opment, such as problem definition or ideation. Expanding this framework to cover the entire product
development process could provide a more holistic approach to bias mitigation. Future studies could ex-
plore how biases manifest in other stages and whether similar or distinct strategies are required to address
them effectively.
By addressing these gaps, future research can enhance the understanding and mitigation of cognitive biases
across the entire product development process, fostering more objective decision-making and ultimately leading
to more innovative and effective engineering solutions

INTERVIEWER GUIDE 
 
CONTEXTUAL QUESTIONS 
What is your professional and educational background?  
How many years of experience? 
What is your position / current job title inside the company?  
Area of the company (what type of products do you work with)? 
Size of your company (employees)? 
How many people usually work on a design task? 
 
CONCEPT SELECTION PROCES 
What does a usual development project look like? 
Please explain where and how concept selection steps are relevant?  
Have you ever experienced problems when facing a design 
challenge that needed a different approach than you are 
usually following? (design fixation) 
Is the concept selection process a team or individual task?  
(or an individual task but team assists when in doubt/complex 
challenges? / Are the people developing concepts part of the concept 
evaluation process?) 
What do you think defines the success of a concept selection process 
In your opinion, what are the biggest challenges or issues that 
companies face when selecting concepts? 
 
CONCEPT SELECTION METHODS 
Do you follow/apply any formal concept selection methods? 
Are you familiar with any of these methodologies? Pugh Matrix, 
Analytic hierarchy process (AHP), quality function deployment (QFD), 
etc.  
Do you categorize/prioritize concepts before the final selection? 
(e.g., by categories like "pursue," "consider if feasible," or "drop"?) 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
Reasoning behind applying formal or informal selection processes 
(e.g., why choosing a specific method over others?) 
Do you trust more your own instinct, experience and 
judgment; or a result provided by a formal method? 
 
CONCEPT SELECTION CRITERIA 
How do you derive criteria for the concept selection? 
(incl. also the question of criteria beyond the user requirements) 
How many criteria do you usually consider in the process? 
(is there any standardized prioritization of criteria?) 
Are there preformulated criteria? Are there any implicit criteria that 
are not specifically listed?  
Innovation and feasibility are often key criteria considered, 
how do you handle the tension between them when 
evaluating and selecting concepts? (creativity bias) 
Is the measurability of criteria discussed in this process? 
(is the question of objectively determining results important?) 
Have you ever experienced disagreement related to criteria / 
outcome of selection processes? 
Do you think you spend more time evaluating or analyzing 
ideas from others than your own? (Ownership bias) 
 
DESIGN BIAS IN CONCEPT SELECTION 
Were you familiar with the concept of design biases and are you 
familiar with these biases? (e.g., confirmation bias, recall/availability 
bias, anchoring Bias, aversion loss bias, ownership bias, escalation of 
commitment, redundancy bias…) 
Which ones (if any) do you actively keep in mind during the concept 
selection process?  
Beyond just being aware of these biases, do you apply any tools, 
strategies, or frameworks to reduce their impact on your design 
process?  
Do you believe a direct or indirect debiasing experiment will work 
better for reducing the impact of bias in concept selection?

INSTRUCTIONS 
Welcome to the workshop! This session is designed to explore different ways of selecting the best ideas for a  
design task. Below is an overview of what you will be doing. 
Initial questions (10min) 
Before we begin, please answer a few questions about your background and experience. Your responses will 
help us better understand different perspectives. 
Designing a mechanism – Individual task (30min) 
Using the Lego set provided, you have to construct an elevator with a platform, a guiding system, and a 
mechanism. 
- 
You must use the large platform as the base of your construction. 
- 
Everyone will have the exact same set of LEGO bricks, and you cannot share or exchange pieces with 
others. 
- 
Your elevator must move in a linear motion, controlled by either a rotating input or a tilting input  
Selecting the best idea – Group task (15 min) 
In pairs, review your own design along with a series of examples of what other participants have created in 
the past. Together, discuss and decide which idea among all you think is the best solution and why. Approach 
this as a real-world design task, considering factors that typically influence the viability and implementation 
of a project. 
You will also receive a set of Bias Cards. These cards provide insights into potential influences in decision-
making that are common during design tasks. They include descriptions of different biases (Tendencies to 
favor specific things and perspectives deviating from objectivity), where they might occur in concept 
selection, and possible ways to mitigate them. Use the cards as you see fit during your discussion. 
Follow-up Questions (15min) 
After the tasks, please answer some questions about your experience selecting a design and using the card 
set. These questions aim to reflect on your decision-making process and what influenced your choices. 
 
Thank you for participating!
