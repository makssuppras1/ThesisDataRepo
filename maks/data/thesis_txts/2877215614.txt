Abstract
This thesis presents the design, modelling, and experimental validation of a mechanically
tunable Helmholtz resonator for low-frequency sound absorption. The resonator employs
a piston–slit mechanism, where the effective neck geometry and cavity volume are con-
tinuously adjusted by varying the overlap length between an inner piston and an outer
shell.
A lumped-element R–M–C model is first used to guide the geometric design and to
describe the tuning behaviour. Finite-element simulations including viscothermal losses
are then performed to account for narrow-slit effects and geometric details. Experimental
validation is carried out in a two-microphone impedance tube following ISO 10534-2 and
ASTM E2611-19 standards.
Keywords: Tunable Helmholtz resonator; Low-frequency sound absorption; Slit neck;
Lumped-element model; Viscothermal losses; Impedance tube measurements

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Helmholtz resonators (HRs) are fundamental acoustic structures consisting of a cavity
coupled to the exterior through a narrow neck, exhibiting a pronounced resonance de-
termined by their geometry. Their classical description, developed by Helmholtz and
further formalized in canonical acoustics texts such as Theoretical Acoustics by Morse
and Ingard [1], models the system as a lumped mass–spring oscillator. In this framework,
the resonant frequency depends on the cavity volume 𝑉, the neck length 𝑙, and the neck
cross-sectional area 𝑆. Figure 1.1a shows a physical prototype of a traditional Helmholtz
resonator, while Figure 1.1b presents a schematic sketch.
1.2
Previous Research
Research on Helmholtz resonators spans a wide range of theoretical, numerical, and
application-driven directions, with several scholars establishing the foundation and subse-
quent evolution of the field. Morse and Ingard established the classical lumped-parameter
description and clarified the roles of cavity compliance, neck acoustic mass, and radia-
tion impedance in governing resonance behavior [1]. Stinson showed that viscothermal
boundary-layer effects play a dominant role in narrow and non-circular ducts, fundamen-
tally altering the effective impedance of slit-type resonators [2].
Beyond theoretical studies, HRs have been widely used in practical applications through-
out the twentieth century, including musical instruments, architectural acoustics, and
industrial noise control. Typical implementations integrate HRs into duct silencers, muf-
flers, and acoustic liners, where they act as passive narrowband absorbers to suppress
tonal noise and increase transmission loss. These application-driven studies established
HRs as fundamental engineering elements long before the emergence of acoustic meta-

1.3. Motivation
1.3
Motivation
Despite the extensive research on Helmholtz resonators and their tunable variants, ex-
isting approaches still face important limitations when continuous, stable, and compact
low-frequency tuning is required in practice. Multi-neck resonators provide only dis-
crete tuning and are highly sensitive to sealing conditions and manufacturing tolerances.
Variable-volume designs based on flexible membranes or compliant walls introduce ad-
ditional structural dynamics, which may lead to nonlinear behavior, material fatigue, and
environmental sensitivity. Mechanically driven solutions, on the other hand, often rely
on idealized piston motion and simplified geometric assumptions.
In addition, most analytical and numerical models assume fixed or idealized neck geome-
tries and are rarely validated over a full tuning range. As a result, systematic deviations
between theoretical predictions, numerical simulations, and experimental measurements
are frequently observed when geometric parameters are continuously varied.
Based on these challenges, there is a clear need for a tunable Helmholtz resonator con-
cept that combines continuous geometric tuning, mechanical robustness, and a modeling
framework that remains valid across the entire tuning range and can be verified experi-
mentally. The specific objectives of this thesis are defined in the following section.
1.4
Thesis Objectives
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop, model, and experimentally validate a
continuously tunable Helmholtz resonator to adjust its acoustic resonance through con-
trolled geometric modification. To achieve this goal, the work is structured by following
objectives:
1. Design a mechanically tunable resonator architecture that enables continuous adjust-
ment of the effective cavity volume while maintaining a compact external geometry
and ensuring structural robustness during operation.
2. Establishaunifiedmulti-scalemodelingframeworkcombininganalyticalHelmholtz-
resonator theory, lumped R–M–C formulations, transfer-matrix modeling, and vis-
cothermal finite-element simulations in axisymmetric and 3D, in order to predict
the resonator’s frequency response across the entire tuning range.
3. Investigate the influence of geometric tuning on resonance characteristics, including
resonance frequency shift, absorption coefficient variation, and impedance behav-
ior.
Therefore, evaluate the limitations of the given model applied to changing
geometries.

4. Fabricate and experimentally characterize the tunable resonator using ISO 10534-

1.5
Thesis Structure
This thesis is organized into seven chapters, reflecting the complete workflow from the-
oretical formulation to experimental validation of a continuously tunable Helmholtz res-
onator.
1. Chapter 1 introduces the background and motivation for tunable acoustic resonators,
reviews previous research, formulates the research gap, and defines the thesis ob-
jectives. The overall structure of the thesis is also presented.
2. Chapter 2 establishes the theoretical foundation of the thesis. It introduces the fun-
damental principles of Helmholtz resonators, the lumped-element R–M–C model,
and thermoviscous acoustic modeling approaches. Special attention is given to the
differences between circular and slit neck geometries, providing the theoretical basis
for the numerical and experimental analyses in the subsequent chapters.
3. Chapter 3 presents the conceptual and practical design workflow. The design prin-
ciples for achieving continuous mechanical tuning are introduced, followed by pa-
rameter screening to narrow the feasible design space. Geometry selection is then
addressed. Finally, the CAD modeling process for both the resonator and the sup-
port frame is described.
4. Chapter 4 details the numerical modeling framework. An axisymmetric model is first
developed, including selection of physical formulations such as the Thermoviscous
Boundary-Layer Impedance (BLI) and Linearized Navier–Stokes (LNS) models. A
full 3D model is then introduced to account for asymmetries introduced by the
support frame. Differences between simulations with and without the frame are
discussed.
5. Chapter 5 describes the experimental methods used to validate the tunable res-
onator. The impedance tube setup is introduced, followed by sample preparation,
measurement procedures, and data-processing steps including calibration, coher-
ence filtering, and smoothing.
6. Chapter 6 presents the main findings of the work. Simulation and experimental
results are compared with analytical predictions, followed by a discussion of physical
interpretation, model limitations, and practical considerations for implementation.
7. Finally, chapter 7 concludes with a summary of key contributions and insights.
Potential extensions and future research directions, including alternative tunable
geometries and integration into acoustic metamaterials, are discussed.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
This chapter summarises the theoretical background used throughout the thesis. The
Helmholtz resonator is first described by a lumped mass - spring - damper model, from
which the main impedance and absorption expressions are obtained.
The impact of
thermoviscous boundary layers is then discussed, followed by a comparison between
circular and slit neck geometries and the end-correction models that link geometry to
lumped parameters.
2.1
Classical Helmholtz Resonator
The Helmholtz resonator is a classical low-frequency acoustic resonator consisting of a
small opening (neck) connected to a closed cavity shown in Figure1.1.
Its operating
principle can be understood by considering the air in the neck as an oscillating mass, while
the air inside the cavity stores potential energy through compression and expansion. At
a specific frequency, the interaction between these two mechanisms leads to a resonance.
The classical theory of the Helmholtz resonator is based on several key assumptions.
First, the characteristic dimensions of the cavity are assumed to be much smaller than the
acoustic wavelength, such that the sound pressure inside the cavity can be considered
spatially uniform [1].
Second, the neck is assumed to be short and narrow, allowing
the air inside the neck to move approximately as a one-dimensional plug flow. Under
these conditions, transverse velocity variations and higher-order acoustic modes can be
neglected, and the resonance is governed by the balance between the inertia of the neck
air and the compressibility of the cavity air.

2.2
Lumped-Element Model
At sufficiently low frequencies, when the acoustic wavelength is much larger than the
characteristic dimensions of the structure, the dynamic behaviour of an acoustic sys-
tem can be described by a small number of lumped parameters. In this regime, spatial
variations of the acoustic field can be neglected, and the system can be represented by
equivalent acoustic elements characterised by their impedances. These various elements
have acoustic impedances associated with them, which can, in some frequency ranges, be
represented as simple lumped elements [20].
A systematic formulation of such models is based on the electroacoustic analogy.

2.3
End Correction of the Neck
In a Helmholtz resonator, the effective length of the neck is generally larger than its
physical length.
This difference is known as the end correction and originates from
acoustic radiation at the neck opening. When the air column in the neck oscillates, a
portion of the surrounding air outside the opening is also set into motion, which increases
the effective inertance of the system. From a radiation-impedance point of view, Jacobsen
and Juhl showed that an open tube behaves as if its length were increased by a small
additional amount, referred to as the end correction [23].
In the low-frequency limit, the radiation impedance of an open circular tube consists of
a small real part and a dominant imaginary part. The imaginary part corresponds to an
added acoustic mass and can be interpreted as an additional length of air attached to the
tube [23]. For an unflanged circular opening, the end correction can be approximated as

2.5
Thermoviscous Modelling
Viscous shear and heat conduction near solid walls give rise to thermoviscous boundary
layers, which strongly affect the impedance of narrow tubes and slits. In numerical models,
these effects can either be resolved explicitly by solving the fully coupled linearised Navier–
Stokes equations, or incorporated via effective boundary conditions such as the Boundary
Layer Impedance (BLI), which accounts for both viscous and thermal boundary-layer
effects. The present section summarises the LNS approach, which serves as a reference
model for validating reduced-order descriptions, which serves as a reference model for
validating reduced-order descriptions [25].

2.5.1
Linearised Navier–Stokes (LNS) Formulation
The linearised Navier–Stokes (LNS) formulation, which is implemented in COMSOL as the
Thermoviscous Acoustics interface, explicitly resolves the viscous and thermal boundary
layers. The governing equations are the linearised continuity, momentum, and energy
equations for a quiescent background fluid. The unknown fields are the acoustic pressure
𝑝, the particle velocity u, and the acoustic temperature variation 𝑇. The pressure, density,
and temperature perturbations are related through the linearised equation of state.
In this framework, viscous shear stresses and heat conduction are included directly in
the PDE system. No-slip and isothermal (or adiabatic) wall conditions give rise to thin
viscous and thermal boundary layers whose thickness scales as

capture detailed velocity and temperature gradients in narrow slits and cavities without
additional assumptions.
The main advantage of the LNS approach is its high physical fidelity. The LNS formu-
lation remains valid independently of the ratio between the boundary-layer thickness
and the geometric dimensions, which makes it a natural reference model for assessing
reduced-order descriptions [25]. It naturally reproduces the correct frequency-dependent
attenuation and dispersion in narrow tubes and micro-acoustic devices, and, importantly,
resolves the full velocity and temperature fields within the cross-section, including the
coupled acoustic, viscous, and thermal fields. This makes it a natural reference model
for validating reduced-order descriptions such as BLI, which typically capture the correct
damping but not the detailed velocity profiles, especially in slit necks and micro-structured
resonators.
The main limitation is the computational cost. Because the mesh must resolve both the
acoustic wavelength and the thin thermoviscous boundary layers, the number of elements
grows rapidly when the frequency increases or when the geometry contains long and
narrow channels. In the present work, the LNS model is therefore used selectively: it
serves as a benchmark for the BLI model on the same axisymmetric geometry, allowing
a direct comparison of resonance frequency, peak absorption, and quality factor. Once
this comparison shows that the differences are small in the operating range of interest,
the more efficient BLI model is chosen for extensive parameter sweeps and for the 3D
simulations (see Chapter 4).

2.5.2
BLI
The boundary layer impedance (BLI) model is an approximate viscothermal acoustic
model that aims to reduce the computational cost of fully resolving viscous and thermal
effects. In the full LNS formulation, acoustic pressure, particle velocity, and tempera-
ture perturbations must be solved simultaneously, which becomes expensive for complex
geometries. To address this limitation, Kampinga introduced a class of reduced-order
models based on scale separation arguments [25].
The theoretical foundation of the BLI model originates from the work of Bossart et al., who
studied acoustic propagation in bounded thermo-viscous fluids using a hybrid analytical
and numerical approach. The key assumption is that, when the characteristic geometric
dimensions are much larger than the viscous and thermal boundary-layer thicknesses, the
acoustic field in the bulk domain can be approximated as isentropic, while viscous and
thermal dissipation are confined to thin boundary layers adjacent to rigid walls [26].
Within this framework, the bulk acoustic pressure is governed by the standard isentropic
Helmholtz equation.
The viscothermal effects are incorporated through a frequency-
dependent complex impedance or admittance boundary condition applied at the walls.
This boundary condition is derived from analytical solutions of the velocity and temper-
ature fields inside the boundary layers and represents the effect of shear stresses and heat
fluxes on the acoustic field. Kampinga showed that the BLI model can be interpreted as
a numerical extension of classical boundary impedance formulations to arbitrary geome-
tries [25].
The validity of the BLI model relies on several assumptions: the boundary layers must be
thin compared to the geometric dimensions, boundary-layer overlap must be negligible,
and the acoustic pressure can be considered uniform across the boundary-layer thickness.
When these conditions are satisfied, the BLI model provides an efficient and accurate
approximation of viscothermal losses. However, its applicability becomes limited when
local geometrical features introduce additional energy storage or when the characteristic
dimensions are comparable to the boundary-layer thicknesses [25].

3.3
Geometry Selection
Chapter 2 shows that, for the same neck area 𝑆and cavity volume 𝑉, slit necks can provide
a larger effective inertance, leading to lower resonance frequencies, and stronger but still
controllable viscous damping, resulting in moderate quality factors. The Stage A parame-
ter exploration confirms that the parameter combinations with high peak absorption and
acceptable 𝑄values fall naturally within the range achievable by slit-like apertures. For
this reason, circular necks are not considered further in the present design.
At this stage, the resonator is assumed to be mechanically tunable, with both the neck
length and the effective cavity volume adjusted by varying the overlap length between
the outer shell and an internal piston-like structure. After selecting a slit-based neck con-
figuration, the remaining question is how the slit should be implemented geometrically.
Two closely related options are a conventional rectangular slit and a circumferential wall
slit. From an acoustic point of view, a wall slit can be regarded as a rectangular slit that
is wrapped around the cavity wall. In the limit of a narrow slit width, both configu-
rations exhibit similar thermoviscous behaviour and are governed by the same physical
mechanisms.
The difference between the two concepts therefore lies mainly in their geometric imple-
mentation and mechanical feasibility. A rectangular slit typically requires a prismatic or
rectangular piston to vary the effective neck length. Such a geometry is difficult to inte-
grate with a rotational or sliding tuning mechanism and tends to occupy a larger volume
for a given effective slit length. In addition, maintaining a constant and well-defined slit
cross-section during tuning is mechanically challenging.
By contrast, a wall-slit configuration employs a circumferential slit of constant area formed
between a cylindrical outer shell and an axially movable piston. For a given slit length,
this geometry occupies less volume. In addition, the wall-slit design is well suited for a
rotational tuning mechanism based on a threaded piston. Compared to a purely transla-
tional push–pull motion without threads, rotational tuning allows the adjustment depth
to be determined accurately from the rotation angle and the thread pitch, which improves
control precision and experimental repeatability.
Based on these considerations, the wall-slit geometry is adopted in the present design.
It provides the same acoustic advantages as a rectangular slit while offering superior
compactness and compatibility with a rotational or sliding piston mechanism, which is
essential for reliable and repeatable tuning in Stage B.

Chapter 4
Simulation Methodology and Analysis
This chapter describes the simulation workflow used in this project. The aim is to iden-
tify numerical models that are both efficient and sufficiently accurate for a systematic
analysis of the acoustic response of the tunable slit-type Helmholtz resonator. The re-
sults then provide guidance for the experimental design and for the interpretation of the
measurements.
4.1
Overview of the Simulation Strategy
The overall strategy is as follows. First, a computationally efficient axisymmetric model
is established as a baseline, in order to verify the resonance frequency, peak absorption,
and impedance of the resonator under an idealized axisymmetric geometry. Although
the geometry is described by a two-dimensional meridional cross-section, the model
represents a three-dimensional problem under the assumption of axial symmetry, with the
governing equations formulated in cylindrical coordinates. On this basis, an equivalent
model based on thermoviscous boundary layer impedance (BLI) is compared with a fully
thermoviscous formulation obtained by directly solving the Linearized Navier–Stokes
(LNS) equations. This comparison is used to quantify the accuracy and computational
cost of different loss modelling approaches over the target frequency range, and to select
the most appropriate physics interface for the subsequent simulations.
It should be noted that, in the actual impedance-tube experiments, the resonator must
be mounted by a supporting frame. Due to the weight of the piston, a small downward
displacement may occur if no support is provided, which can lead to geometric mis-
alignment and increased acoustic reflection compared to the original design. This frame
divides the originally continuous slit neck into four sector-like channels, thereby breaking
axisymmetry and potentially modifying the effective mass and resistance of the neck. As a
consequence, a purely two-dimensional axisymmetric model can no longer fully represent
33
==PAGE:45==
4.2. Axisymmetric Model
the physical sample. To quantify the influence introduced by the supporting structure, a
full three-dimensional model is therefore constructed.
4.2
Axisymmetric Model
This section presents the axisymmetric model, formulated on a two-dimensional merid-
ional cross-section, used in the first stage of the simulation workflow. The axisymmetric
approach is computationally efficient and well suited for establishing an early baseline,
identifying the dominant physical mechanisms, and providing a reference for the later full
3D modeling. In this framework, two loss models are implemented: an equivalent model
based on the Thermoviscous Boundary Layer Impedance (BLI), and a full thermoviscous
model that solves the Linearized Navier–Stokes (LNS) equations. Their results are sys-
tematically compared to determine the most appropriate loss model for the subsequent
simulations.
4.2.1
Geometry and Assumptions
The axisymmetric model follows the rotationally symmetric geometry of the impedance-
tube experiment. It includes a portion of the tube, the slit neck, and the cylindrical cavity.
The model is built under the following assumptions:
• The resonator is ideal and axisymmetric, without features that break rotational
symmetry.
• All walls are rigid, and no structural–acoustic coupling is considered.
• The neck and cavity dimensions follow the nominal values of the fabricated sample;
the open-end correction is captured directly by the physics interface.
• The acoustic field is linear, with small-amplitude harmonic excitation,consistent with
the low sound pressure levels used in impedance-tube measurements.

4.3.1
Motivation and Setup
After the metal parts of the experimental sample were fabricated, it became clear that
the piston was relatively heavy. Without additional structural support, the piston could
slightly bend or tilt under its own weight. This would cause the slit cross-section to become
non-uniform around the circumference, which breaks the axisymmetric assumption used
in the two-dimensional model. To ensure stable normal-incidence conditions, a four-beam
supporting frame was added around the resonator. This frame divides the originally con-
tinuous annular slit neck into four equal sectors, thereby breaking the rotational symmetry
on which the two-dimensional axisymmetric model relies.
To quantify the influence of the supporting frame, two three-dimensional geometries
are constructed. The first is a frame-free model, obtained by directly extruding the ax-
isymmetric design into three dimensions. The second includes the four-beam frame and
reproduces the exact experimental configuration. Both models employ the same physical
interface (Pressure Acoustics, Frequency Domain with Thermoviscous Boundary Layer
Impedance), identical port conditions, and closely matched mesh settings, ensuring that
any differences observed in the acoustic results arise solely from the geometric modifica-
tion introduced by the frame.
The simulations are performed over the frequency range 50–2000 Hz and for neck lengths
𝑙1 = 20 mm, 15 mm, 10 mm, 5 mm, 2 mm.
Both 3D geometries are discretized using tetrahedral meshes with refinement in the nar-
row slit region to satisfy the resolution requirements of the BLI boundary condition. The
minimum element size, growth rate, and boundary-layer mesh parameters are kept con-
sistent across the two geometries. This modelling setup provides the foundation for the
next section, where the acoustic response of the models—with and without the supporting
frame—is systematically compared.

Chapter 5
Experimental Verification
This chapter presents the experimental validation of the tunable Helmholtz resonator
using a two-microphone impedance tube. The content is structured into four parts: the
experimental setup, the calibration procedure, the systematic measurements performed
during piston tuning, and the uncertainty analysis.
5.1
Experimental Setup
All measurements follow ISO 10534-2 [27] and ASTM E2611-19 standard [28] for normal-
incidence absorption using the two-microphone transfer-function method. Figure 5.1a
illustrates the signal chain and interconnections of the measurement system, while Fig-
ure 5.1b shows the corresponding physical layout in the laboratory.
The measurement system consists of a rigid impedance tube supporting plane-wave
propagation, a loudspeaker driven by a power amplifier at one end of the tube, two
flush-mounted microphones (CH1 and CH2) with fixed axial spacing, and the tunable
piston–slit Helmholtz resonator mounted at the tube termination. Broadband excitation
signals are generated on the control computer and transmitted to the loudspeaker via
a two-channel data-acquisition system, producing acoustic waves that propagate along
the tube towards the sample. The acoustic response inside the tube is captured by mi-
crophones CH1 and CH2 which flush-mounted in the tube wall, yielding time-domain
microphone signals 𝑥1(𝑡) and 𝑥2(𝑡).
The recorded signals are processed in the frequency domain using Welch’s method to
estimate auto- and cross-power spectral densities. Based on these spectra, the acous-
tic transfer function between the two microphone positions is calculated and used for
subsequent impedance and absorption-coefficient evaluation.

6.1
Theoretical Model Correction
During the tuning process, the outward displacement of the piston creates an additional
external opening. Initially, this opening was treated as a classical slit neck, including
the standard end-correction formulas for rectangular narrow gaps. However, comparison
with Axisymmetric BLI simulations reveals a systematic mismatch seen in Figure 6.1:
the predicted resonance frequencies are consistently lower than the simulated values,
indicating that the inertive contribution of the external slit is significantly overestimated
when modeled using classical slit theory.
Although the external opening is geometrically slit-like, with a width of approximately
6.31 mm and a circumferential length of about 314 mm, its acoustic behaviour differs
fundamentally from that of a classical narrow slit. The piston motion forms a platform-
like annular region between the resonator and the impedance tube, introducing a finite
axial thickness and creating a local cavity–duct transition.
As a result, this structure
cannot be regarded as a pure neck air column.
From a physical perspective, when the incident sound wave reaches this region, two
mechanisms occur simultaneously under the same acoustic pressure. One part of the air
is forced to oscillate through the annular slit, contributing an inertive response, while
another part undergoes local compression and expansion within the platform region,
contributing a compliant response. Consequently, the external structure exhibits a coupled

effects but influence the acoustic response of the entire neck. Under these conditions,
the LNS model can capture end effects and overall dissipation by directly resolving the
velocity field and its gradients.

an equivalent wall impedance, which limits its ability to describe axially non-uniform
dissipation. As a result, the discrepancy between the absorption predictions of the two
models increases as the slit neck becomes shorter.

6.4. Combined Comparison and Discussion: Theory – Simulation – Experiment
The three plots in Figure6.11 show the absorption coefficient as a function of frequency
obtained from the theoretical model (top), numerical simulations (middle), and experi-
mental measurements (bottom). The results are presented over the frequency range of
200–1800 Hz for 13 different piston positions.
Several common features can be observed for all three approaches. Each curve exhibits a
single, broadband absorption peak, and the peak shifts continuously and monotonically
along the frequency axis as the piston position changes. This indicates that the tuning
mechanism is mainly achieved by modifying the effective geometric parameters, such as
the effective neck length and the cavity volume, and that the overall tuning behaviour
is consistent across theory, simulation, and experiment.
It can also be observed that
the resonance frequencies corresponding to different piston positions remain within a
relatively narrow frequency interval.
The absorption curves predicted by the theoretical model are smooth and regular, with
the resonance frequency systematically increasing as 𝑙1 decreases.
The numerical simulation results show very good agreement with the theoretical predic-
tions in terms of resonance frequency and overall trends. For some piston positions, the
absorption peaks are slightly broader or exhibit small frequency shifts. This suggests that
the inclusion of viscothermal boundary-layer effects and geometric details in the simula-
tions introduces moderate corrections to the peak width and position, without altering
the general tuning behaviour.
The experimental results follow the same overall trend as the theoretical and simulation
results, but noticeable differences can be observed. For some piston positions, such as
𝑙1 = 20 mm and 𝑙1 = 18 mm, the absorption peaks are broader and the peak amplitudes
show increased scatter.
In addition, the absorption coefficients on the low-frequency
side are generally higher than those predicted by theory and simulation, and the curves
corresponding to different piston positions show stronger overlap. These effects reflect
the combined influence of experimental uncertainties, assembly tolerances, and non-ideal
boundary conditions.
Despite these differences, the experimental measurements clearly shows that the tunable
Helmholtz resonator achieves continuous and repeatable absorption tuning over the entire
piston stroke.

but shows small systematic deviations due to the simplified assumptions of the lumped

order of 0.05–0.1. This behaviour is consistent with the expected response of a Helmholtz
resonator with increasing effective neck inertance. The experimental absorption values
are generally slightly lower than those predicted by theory and simulation, reflecting
additional loss mechanisms present in the physical system.
It should be noted that the numerical simulation results shown in Fig. 6.12 are obtained
from the axisymmetric model. As discussed earlier, this model provides a good approx-
imation of the 3D geometry without supporting frame, but it does not account for the
influence of the supporting beams present in the experimental setup.
The remaining deviations between theory, simulation, and experiment can be attributed
to this modelling difference. The supporting frame segments the annular slit and modifies
both the effective inertance and the impedance balance of the neck. Depending on the
overlap length, this modification can shift the system either closer to or further away from
favourable impedance conditions at resonance.
At large overlap lengths (e.g. 𝑙1 = 20 mm), the deviation between experiment and simula-
tion becomes more pronounced. In this regime, the neck is inertance-dominated and the
influence of the supporting frame accumulates along the entire neck length, whereas the
axisymmetric simulation does not include this effect. Additional contributions arise from
the simplified assumptions of the lumped theoretical model and from small geometric and
assembly tolerances in the experiment. Together, these factors account for the observed
discrepancies while the overall tuning trends remain consistent.
Overall, the combined comparison confirms that the modelling framework successfully
captures the dominant physical mechanisms governing the tunable Helmholtz resonator.
The numerical simulations provide quantitative agreement with the experimental re-
sults, while the theoretical model offers a compact description of the underlying physical
trends.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
This thesis investigated a tunable Helmholtz resonator based on a piston–slit structure,
with the aim of achieving continuous tuning of resonance frequency and absorption
performance through geometric adjustment. The main research question was whether
this tuning mechanism is effective and whether theoretical models, numerical simulations,
and experimental results are consistent with each other.
From a theoretical perspective, a lumped-element model was developed to describe the
tunable resonator. The analysis showed that increasing the overlap length leads to a larger
effective acoustic inertance, resulting in a monotonic decrease in resonance frequency and
an increase in peak absorption near resonance.
Numerical simulations were carried out using an axisymmetric acoustic model.
The
simulation results agree well with the theoretical predictions in terms of overall tuning
trends and provide a good description of the resonance frequency and peak absorption as
functions of the overlap length. Comparisons with three-dimensional models clarified the
applicability and limitations of the axisymmetric approach when supporting structures
are not included.
Experimental validation was performed using impedance-tube measurements. The re-
sults demonstrate clear and continuous tuning of the resonance frequency within the
target frequency range, as well as a monotonic increase in peak absorption with increas-
ing overlap length.
The experimental trends are in good qualitative and quantitative
agreement with both theory and simulation, confirming the effectiveness of the proposed
tuning concept.
Overall, this work shows that the piston–slit tunable Helmholtz resonator and the as-

7.2
Future Work
The present study establishes a preliminary framework for the design, simulation, and
experimental verification of a mechanically tunable Helmholtz resonator. Several aspects,
however, warrant further investigation to bridge the gap between the idealized model and
practical implementation.
7.2.1
Improved measurement
The initial design shown in Figure5 and axisymmetric simulations predicted a tunable
resonance range of approximately 500–850 Hz as shown in Figure7.1, showing excellent
agreement with the theoretical lumped model. In the experiments, however, the tuning
process caused the piston to protrude from the cavity and form an unintended secondary
slit against the tube wall. This additional acoustic pathway deviated significantly from the
assumptions of the original model. Moreover, to ensure mechanical stability during man-
ual tuning, the prototype dimensions had to be enlarged, making the intended resonance
range incompatible with the current impedance tube setup. Future work should therefore
consider dedicated measurement environments tailored for small-scale resonators—such
as miniature anechoic chambers or high-precision narrow-tube facilities—to enable ex-
perimental conditions that faithfully reproduce the original design assumptions.
The present prototype relies entirely on manual piston adjustment, which limits tuning
accuracy, increases measurement time, and introduces repeatability issues. A motorized
linear or rotational actuator would provide automated, precise, and reproducible control
over the tuning displacement, thereby improving both reliability and efficiency of the
experiments.

7.2.2
Inspired Future Design
Figure7.2 illustrates two conceptual future design ideas inspired by the present work. The
left sketch shows a piston-like structure with a variable cross-section, where the geometry
of the piston head is modified along the axial direction. This design allows additional
control of the effective neck parameters and cavity volume and provides extra degrees of
freedom for tuning the resonance frequency and absorption performance.
The right sketch extends this concept by introducing a porous structure on the piston
head. By combining geometric tuning with additional resistive losses, this design aims to
improve impedance matching and potentially broaden the absorption bandwidth while
maintaining mechanical tunability. Both concepts represent possible directions for extend-
ing the current piston–slit resonator design toward enhanced and more flexible acoustic
performance.
