Abstract
The ﬁnal objective of this thesis is to develop a system that identify the nonlinear parameters of a loudspeaker
by tuning the parameters in a feed-forward controller directly to minimize distortions in the output.
First the theory of both linear and nonlinear loudspeakers are reviewed. The state space model of the
loudspeaker including the eddy current is derived. The state space model forms the basis of the following
nonlinear compensation and system identiﬁcation.
The theory of feedback linearisation is studied and applied to a 4th order loudspeaker system. Modelling
of the eddy current by the LR-2 network makes the system can only be input-out instead of input-state
linearizable. The stability of the internal dynamic is conﬁrmed by trying different initial values of the zero
dynamic. To perform simulations and measurements of compensation, Klippel system is used to determine
the parameters. The effect of linear and nonlinear parameter uncertainties on the compensation is studied
by simulation. It is found that Bl and Cms are the most important parameters. The measurement is based
on a pure feed-forward compensator. It is found that the parameters identiﬁed by Klippel systems are not
accurate enough to give satisfactory results on compensator. Then the parameter are tuned to minimize the
low frequency THD. THD at 20 Hz has been reduced from 20% to 2.5%.
A new scheme to identify the linear parameters are proposed. The Re and Bl(0) can be conveniently
identiﬁed using a two-input one-output memoryless system. However, the second step to identify the
electrical admittance is not succeed. Finally, the possibility to identify the parameters using compensation
is studied in the theoretical level. It is found that including the real loudspeaker in the chain of system
identiﬁcation is difﬁcult to determine the gradient of the mean square error. However, it is still possible if the
amplitude of the error is small enough.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Loudspeakers convert electrical signals to acoustic signals. This conversion is inherently nonlinear, which
produces undesirable distortions such as harmonics and intermodulations. The distortions are negligible
at low driving levels, and the loudspeaker can be modelled as a linear system using some well developed
method. However the linear model fails at higher driving levels and large amount of distortion can be
produced. Especially at low frequency where the diaphragm displacement is large, the total harmonic
distortion can be greater than 30% even at normal driving levels [Schurer et al., 1998]. Such large amount of
distortion will greatly deteriorate the listening experience.
The distortions can be reduced by improving the physical design of the loudspeaker. For example increasing
the voice-coil height gives larger linear operation range. However this increase the moving mass which leads
to less sensitivity. In fact, this kind of tactic is usually a trade-off between the sound quality and the price,
size, weight and sensitivity.
Another way to reduce the distortions is to use a nonlinear compensator, which is the topic of this thesis. The
compensator generates a pre-distorted input signal which cancels the nonlinearities in the loudspeaker while
retains the linear part. The algorithm to generate the signal is based on a nonlinear model of the loudspeaker.
For the compensator to work, it is essential to model the loudspeaker sufﬁciently and use the most accurate
parameters.
Previous studies of the compensator have been based on a 2nd or 3rd order model and good results on low
frequency harmonic distortions were obtained [Bright et al., 2002; Schurer et al., 1998]. However those
models are not capable of modelling the high frequency behaviour of a loudspeaker correctly. For this reason,
the compensator in this project is based on a 4th order model that takes the eddy current into consideration.
This model is expect to work better at high frequencies.
As for the parameters in the model, it seems that using the Klippel R&D system to identify them is a good
choice because it is easy to use and capable of identifying both linear and nonlinear parameters automatically.
However the results in previous master theses [Einarsson, 2014; Øyen, 2007] have shown that the parameters
identiﬁed by Klippel system does not ensure satisfactory results on compensation. This leads to the idea that
whether the parameters, especially the nonlinear parameters, can be identiﬁed by tuning the compensator
directly. The optimum parameters are identiﬁed if the minimum distortions are obtained in the output. It can
also be seen as an adaptive compensation system which tunes its parameters by minimizing the distortions in
the loudspeaker output. Developing such a system is the ﬁnal goal of this thesis.

CHAPTER 2
Linear Loudspeaker Model
This chapter reviews the the basis of the linear electrodynamic loudspeakers. The linear loudspeaker model
is a simpliﬁed model of a general loudspeaker, which is a non-linear system, working in the small signal
range. Thus small signal is the precondition of the discussions in this chapter.
The construction of a typical electrodynamic loudspeaker is described. Then the lumped parameter analogous
circuits are presented. Finally the transfer function model and state-space model are derived for linear
loudspeaker. The theory presented in this chapter is mainly based on [Leach, 2010].
2.1
Electrodynamic Loudspeaker
A loudspeaker convert electrical signal to acoustic waves. Although many different principles of this transduc-
tion have been investigated over the last century, the electrodynamic type remains the most commonly used
one for audio-frequency reproduction [Bright et al., 2002; Hunt, 1954]. Only electrodynamic loudspeaker
are considered in this project, thus it will be just referred as loudspeaker in the rest of this thesis.

2.2.4
Eddy current
The model in ﬁgure 2.2 predicts that the electrical impedance at high frequency where the inductance Le is
dominant, should increase linearly with frequency (6dB per octave). However the measurement shows that
the growing is much slower than the ideal inductance predicted. This is due to effect of eddy current. When
current is ﬂowing through the voice-coil, AC magnetic ﬁeld will be generated and induce eddy current in the
magnetic system like a transformer. Thus this effect decreases the voice-coil inductance and increase losses.
There are several different ways to modelled the eddy current effect. [Vanderkooy, 1989] and [Leach, 2010]
proposed two similar models that take the form:
Z(ω) = (jω)nLe
(2.5)
where n is a number between 0 and 1. The difference between Vanderkooy and Leach is just different
number of n. This model is actually a frequency dependant inductor and a frequency dependent resistor in
parallel. This model is easy in frequency domain. [King and Agerkvist, 2015] shows that it can also be used
for time domain simulation with a fractional order state-space model, though it is slower to compute.
The LR-2 (shunted inductance) model is used in this project. The voice-coil is modelled as in ﬁgure 2.6.

CHAPTER 3
Nonlinear Loudspeaker
The linear model discussed in Chapter 2 is valid only for small signals. When the input signal is large, some
of the parameters cease to be constants. In fact, they are mostly functions of the diaphragm displacement.
These displacement dependent parameters is the inherent nonlinearities in every electrodynamic loudspeakers.
Similar to all other nonlinear systems, additional spectral components, or to say distortions, are generated.
This chapter discusses the nonlinear loudspeaker behaviour and its modelling. First the mechanism of several
important nonlinearities are reviewed. Then the nonlinear state-space model is derived. Next the method of
quantifying the distortion is presented. Finally all the unmodelled dynamics in loudspeakers, including both
linear and nonlinear behaviours, are summarized.
3.1
Major Nonlinearities in Electrodynamic Loudspeakers
It is generally accepted that among all the nonlinearities in a electrodynamic loudspeaker, three of them are
the most dominant: the force factor Bl, the stiffness of suspension Kms and the voice-coil inductance Le.
They are all functions of the diaphragm displacement xd. This section reviews the causes and symptom of
these nonlinearities.
3.1.1
Force Factor
The force factor Bl equals to the integral of magnetic ﬂux density B with respect to the wire length l. Figure
3.1 shows a cross section of the electro-magnetic part of a loudspeaker. It can be seen that the Bl is almost
constant at small displacement because the number of coil windings in the air gap, where the magnetic ﬂux
is focused, remains the same. However when the voice-coil moves further away so that the number of coil
windings in the air gap reduced, the force factor will decrease. Figure 3.2a shows a measured Bl(xd) of the
speaker Peerless Model 850124. The displacement-varying force factor results nonlinear force Bl(xd)i to
the diaphragm and nonlinear equivalent mechanical damping Bl(xd)2/Re to the system. They can cause
high harmonic distortion at low frequency and high intermodulation distortion for f1 < f0 and f2 > f0.
3.1.2
Suspension
The outer and inner suspension(spider) in ﬁgure 2.1 provide restoring force for the diaphragm. The
suspension is modelled by a spring with constant stiffness in linear model. However at large displacement,
the stiffness increases as the the suspension being stretched. Thus Kms generally increases with larger
displacement. Figure 3.2b shows a measured Kms(xd) of the speaker A. The displacement-varying stiffness
generates nonlinear restoring force Kms(xd)xd which produce high harmonic distortion at low frequency. It

3.1.3
Voice-coil Inductance
The inductance of a air-core coil like the voice-coil is proportional to the magnetic permeability µ. It is
obvious from ﬁgure 3.1 that if the voice-coil moves inwards, µ increases due to the pole piece surrounding,
whereas µ will decrease if the voice-coil moves into the free air. Thus normally Le increase with negative xd
and decrease with positive xd. Figure 3.2c shows a measured Le(xd) of the speaker Peerless Model 850124.
Le also depends on the current i. This is caused by magnetic saturation in the magnetic circuit. The AC
current in the voice-coil generates AC magnetic ﬂux which is added onto the DC ﬂux generated by the
magnet. This is called ﬂux modulation and is shown in ﬁgure 3.3a. Although the H ﬁeld is linear with
the current, the resulting B ﬁeld is nonlinear due to magnetic saturation. This can be seen in ﬁgure 3.3b.

proportional to µ, Le is also current dependant. A measured example is shown in ﬁgure 3.2d.

3.5
Unmodelled Effects and Nonlinearities
The state-space model derived in the last section have included the most important linear and nonlinear
behaviours of a electrodynamic loudspeakers. There are still some effects have not being included because
either too complicated to model or have minor inﬂuence to the results. There are both linear and nonlinear
effects and are summarized in this section. It is important to understand these effects since they may
cause discrepancy between the model and measurement. They could also affect the performance of the
compensators and system identiﬁcation algorithm in the following chapters.
3.5.1
Thermal effect
The efﬁciency of electrodynamic loudspeaker is typically below 1% and most of the input power is dissipated
as heat in the voice-coil resistance. As temperature increases in large signal operation, the voice-coil
resistance will also increase. It can be modelled by:
Re = Re0 + k(T −T0)
(3.16)
where Re0 is the resistance at ambient temperature T0. k is a constant depending on the voice-coil material.
Thermal effect does not produce distortion because the resistance increases slowly. However it will affect
the performance of the nonlinear compensator.
3.5.2
Time Varying Compliance
The compliance of suspension can increases signiﬁcantly during and shortly after high level operation.
Figure 3.5 shows an example of Cms(xd) at different input level. The higher Cms at high level gives lower
resonance frequency which makes the tuning of vented box problematic. The varying compliance also affect
the performance of the compensator.
There might be two reasons for the varying compliance. First the compliance increased with the temperature.
[Pedersen and Rubak, 2007] did a measurement by placing a loudspeaker in a conditioning cabinet and
measure the compliance statically. It was found out that the compliance increases by 21% from 20◦C to
50◦C. The heating of suspension in real operation may due to the heat spreading from the voice-coil or the
mechanical loss in suspension itself [Pedersen and Agerkvist, 2007]. However it is questionable whether
the temperature increase in real operation can be as high as in the conditioning cabinet. The second reason
is probably more important: a high displacement change the geometry of the ﬁbres of the suspension and
increase the compliance [Klippel and Seidel, 2001]. [Agerkvist and Pedersen, 2009] did a measurement
using sine wave as input. Each sine wave has different frequency and the voltage has been adjusted so that
they produce the same maximum displacement. It was found out that the increasing of the compliance is
almost the same for all excitations despite that they have different frequency, input power and velocity.

3.5.4
Other Nonlinearities
Doppler Effect
Doppler effect generates nonlinear distortion in the sound pressure. It happens when the input contains both
low frequency component f1 and high frequency component f2. The large displacement at f1 will change
the the distance between the diaphragm and the receiving point. Thus the phase of the sound pressure at
receiving point is modulated. Since the f2 has smaller wavelength, it will be affected the most and cause
intermodulation distortion. Since the diaphragm velocity is measured in this project, Doppler effect will not
affect the results.
Moving mass and diaphragm area
[Olsen and Thorborg, 1995] shows that the moving mass and effective radiation area can change with the
displacement. This is caused by the structure of the single roll outer suspension which moves along the

CHAPTER 4
Nonlinear Compensation
One of the purpose of this project is to measure the nonlinear loudspeaker parameters by adjusting the
parameters in a compensator to minimize the distortion of the loudspeaker. To achieve this goal, the
compensator must be able to eliminated most of the distortion when its parameters of the loudspeaker are
correct.
In this chapter, the performance of a pure feed-forward compensator are investigated. First an introduction
to the general topic of loudspeaker control is given. Then three different algorithms of compensation are
discussed with emphasis on the feedback linearization. Next the parameters of the speakers under test is
measured by Klippel Analyser. These parameters are required for compensation. Finally simulations and
measurements of the feed-forward version of feedback linearization is presented.
4.1
Introduction to Active Loudspeaker Control
This section discusses the active control of loudspeaker in general. The term Controller here does not
necessarily mean compensator that eliminated the distortion and keep the linear behaviour. The controller
can also change the frequency response of a loudspeaker for example. There are three possible structure of a
loudspeaker controller: feedback, feed-forward and adaptive feed forward. They are discussed as follows.
4.1.1
Feedback control
A feedback or closed-loop system measures the output of the speaker and feed it back to the controller,
see ﬁgure 4.1. The main problem of feedback control on loudspeakers is that it is not easy to obtain the
feedback signal. The feedback signal is the output of the loudspeaker and it can be the displacement, velocity,
acceleration of the diaphragm or the sound pressure. Several methods are available but none of them are
both cheap and accurate. A few methods were summarized in [Larsen, 2006]:
• Placing an accelerometer on the voice-coil. This increase the cost and may not ﬁt in a micro-speaker.
• Adding a secondary magnetic circuit and secondary coil attached to the voice-coil. The induced
voltage on the secondary coil can be used to measure the velocity. However the force factor on the
secondary coil might also be nonlinear and hence the results are not reliable. It also complicates the
structure and adds additional mass. It has not been used in practical.
• Using a laser to measure the diaphragm movement. It is accurate but only suitable in laboratory.

4.2
Feedback Linearization
This section discusses the theory of feedback linearization and its implementation on the nonlinear state-
space model of loudspeakers developed in Chapter 3. Feedback linearization is a general theory for nonlinear

loudspeaker linearization in 1990’s. Although it is implied by its name that the feedback linearization is
conceived for feedback control as in ﬁgure 4.1, it can also be implemented as a feed-forward controller. The
difference is how the feedback state is obtained. Thus the discussion in this section is not conﬁned to either
two of these structures. The theory in this section is based on [Khalil and Grizzle, 1996], [Hendricks et al.,
2008] and [Schurer, 1997].

4.3
Other Compensation Algorithms
Besides the feedback linearization described in the last section, there two other notable methods for
loudspeaker compensation: mirror ﬁlter and inverting Volterra series. Mirror ﬁlter is derived by separating
the nonlinear terms in the differential equation and inverse them. Same expression as Eq. 4.78 is derived
because the same lamped parameter model is used. The difference is that the state information of xd and
ud are determined by linear transfer function. State i is calculation by a nonlinear function from the input
because i is not linearized. Another possibility is to model the nonlinear loudspeaker by Volterra series and
inverse the higher order term. This method has the limited to the maximum nonlinear order modelled.
At the beginning of this project, a method called Direct Compensation discussed in [Schurer, 1997] is studied
and also presented in the following section.
4.3.1
Direct compensation
Direct compensation is described in [Schurer, 1997]. This method is only studied at the beginning of this

4.6
Measurements
A feed-forward compensator is implemented based on the theory and the Klippel measurement discussed in
this chapter. The schematic of the measurement setup is shown in ﬁgure 4.32. The loudspeaker is operating
in free air and the velocity is measured as the output instead of the sound pressure. This has the advantage
that the inﬂuence of the acoustic load is almost eliminated, assuming the room is damped enough. Also it
is the same condition of the Klippel measurement. The velocity is measured by a Polytec Doppler laser
which convert the velocity to voltage. Both the input voltage to the loudspeaker and the diaphragm velocity
is analysed by a B&K Pulse analyser which gives the autospectrum of the signals. The gain of the ampliﬁer
has been carefully adjusted to match the calculation in the compensator.
Since a pure feed-forward compensator is used, the control u is generated as a 60 seconds long audio ﬁle
and played back in the measurement. To speed up the process of generating u, only the ﬁrst 1 second are
calculated by the compensator and the remaining 59 seconds are produced by repeating 10 periods from the
end. The sampling frequency is around 24k and tuned for each input frequency to have periodic discrete
signals.
For the TIM measurement, the high frequency f2 must be chosen to avoid the mechanical modes on the
diaphragm. This frequency is difﬁcult to estimate because it depends on the size, shape and materiel of
the diaphragm. They are just chosen arbitrarily based on the size of the diaphragm. For loudspeaker A,
f2 = 250 Hz. For loudspeaker B, f2 = 250 Hz. For loudspeaker C, f2 = 370 Hz. f1 is chosen to be 20 Hz
for all of them. An advantage of this combination is that the HD and IM is separated and lies between each
other.

CHAPTER 5
Loudspeaker Parameter Identiﬁcation
The loudspeaker parameters can be determined using system identiﬁcation. Comparing to the frequency
domain method of analysing the impedance curve, system identiﬁcation has the possibility to use a normal
driving signal such as music to determine the parameters. Hence it has the potential to be used in an adaptive
compensator for parameter tracking.
This chapter discusses system identiﬁcation and its application on loudspeakers. First the basic theory and
the LMS algorithm used in this project is presented. Next a review of previous studies on the topic of
loudspeaker parameter identiﬁcation is given. Then the identiﬁcation of linear parameter is discussed. Due
to the limitation of time, only simulations are performed. The last section discusses the method to identify
nonlinear parameters by tuning the compensator directly. The theory in this chapter is mainly based on
[Proakis and Manolakis, 2007] and [Madisetti and Williams, 1997].
5.1
Theory of system identiﬁcation
System identiﬁcation is one of the applications of adaptive ﬁlters, see ﬁgure 5.1. The unknown system and
the adaptive ﬁlter are driven by the same input signal x(n). e(n) is the error signal which is the difference
between the output of the adaptive ﬁlter y(n) and the desired signal d(n). To let the adaptive ﬁlter sufﬁciently
identify or model the unknown system, the squared error or the mean-square-error is minimized by tuning
the ﬁlter coefﬁcients using some algorithms.
The adaptive ﬁlter can be either FIR or IIR. The FIR ﬁlter is by far the most widely used [Proakis and
Manolakis, 2007]. This is because only the zeros can be adjusted in an adaptive FIR ﬁlter and hence there is
no problem of stability to be concerned 1. However for the purpose of loudspeaker parameter identiﬁcation,
there are several advantages to choose IIR ﬁlters over the FIR ﬁlters. The reasons will be discussed in
Section 5.3.2. Here the adaptive algorithm will only be discussed for the IIR ﬁlters.

5.2
Review of the previous researches
System identiﬁcation was ﬁrst applied to linear loudspeaker parameters measurement in [Knudsen et al.,
1989]. Instead of identifying the electrical admittance and displacement transfer function that are commonly
used in later researches, Knudsen identiﬁed Hix and Hixu. Hix is the transfer function from current to
displacement, corresponding to the differential equation describing the mechanical system in Eq. 2.2. Hixu
is the system that take current, displacement as input and voltage as output. Hixu corresponds to the
differential equation describing the electrical system in Eq. 2.1. Both Hix and Hixu were discretized using
bilinear transform, hence the adaptive IIR ﬁlter was used. It is easy to see that by identifying Hix, the three
mechanical parameters mixing with Bl can be found: Mms/Bl, Cms/Bl and Rms/Bl. By identifying
Hixu, the remaining parameters Bl, Le and Re can be found.
A different approach to identify the linear parameters was described in [Klippel and Seidel, 2001]. Klippel
drives the loudspeaker with a multi tone voltage signal and identify the current and displacement. Instead of
a sample-by-sample adaptation of the model, he ﬁts the model to the measured electrical impedance and
displacement transfer function in frequency domain. The impedance model is same to Eq. 2.7. The ﬁtting of
displacement transfer function separates the mechanical parameters and force factor Bl. A creeping factor is
also used in the displacement transfer function to have better ﬁtting at low frequency. This method is the
basis of the LPM module in the Klippel R&D system.
Several different schemes to identify the nonlinear parameters were discussed in [Klippel, 1999]. Klippel
referred them as parallel modelling, which is basically the same system in ﬁgure 5.1; post-inverse modelling,
which utilized the inverse system identiﬁcation; and pre-inverse modelling, which similar to the goal of this
thesis that identifying parameters using compensation. The parallel modelling is the basis of the LSI module
in the Klippel R&D system. It was further discussed in [Klippel, 2000]. Klippel use noise voltage signal as
the input and measure the current to identify the equivalent electrical lumped parameter model. The model is
just a non-linear version of the one in ﬁgure 2.8. To separate Bl and the mechanical parameters, either Bl
or Mms must be speciﬁed. Unlike the frequency domain ﬁtting in [Klippel and Seidel, 2001], a recursive
sample-by-sample adaptive algorithm is used for the nonlinear parameters.
A series of works [Bright, 2003a,b, 2007] dedicated to identifying or tracking the linear loudspeaker
parameters. [Bright, 2003a] presented a detailed and comprehensive method of discrete time loudspeaker
modelling and identiﬁcation of linear parameters. The mechanical system of the loudspeaker is modelled
by a 2nd order IIR system. The relationships between the IIR coefﬁcients and the resonance frequency f0
and damping ratio ζ are derived. The electrical part is modelled by a memoryless system by neglecting
the inductance. LMS algorithm was used to minimize the mean-squared output error in the current. Since
only the electrical admittance were identiﬁed, the information is not enough to determine the mechanical
==PAGE:87==

parameters explicitly. However, it is capable of identifying Re, Bl, f0 and ζ. Note that Bright is not
identifying the electrical circuit in ﬁgure 2.8 directly like Klippel did. The difference is that by identifying
the current, Klippel ﬁnds the Res, Lces and Cmes while Bright ﬁnds the Bl, f0 and ζ. In [Bright, 2003b], he
pointed out that if Mms can be known in advance, the mechanical parameters can be determined which is
obvious based on the relation of those parameters to f0 and ζ. In [Bright, 2007], The convergence speed is

normalized LMS algorithm. The FIR model have the disadvantage that its coefﬁcients has no direct meaning

is considered that it can be implemented online with the FIR identiﬁcation. However the disadvantage is that
only Re, f0 and ζ can be identiﬁed. Therefore it is consider better as an approach to track the change in
Cms(0) with some known parameters such as Mms, instead of an identiﬁcation of full parameters.
5.3
Linear parameters identiﬁcation
This section discusses the identiﬁcation of linear parameters which serves as a basis of the nonlinear parameter
identiﬁcation. Identifying the electrical admittance seems appealing because it contains informations of all
the parameters in the equivalent circuit in ﬁgure 2.8. Another advantage is that the current can be easily
measured, hence it can be used for parameter tracking in a adaptive compensator. To separate Bl and the
mechanical parameters, one motion signal must also be measured. The diaphragm velocity is chosen and the
reason will be explained. A two-step strategy is used and summarized as follows:
• First identify a two-input one-output, memoryless system based on the equation of the electrical circuit
in 2.1. Re and Bl can be identiﬁed.
• Second identify the transfer function of electrical admittance to ﬁnd the rest parameters.

5.4
Nonlinear parameters identiﬁcation
This section discusses the possibility to identify the parameters, especially the nonlinear parameters, by
tuning the compensator directly to minimize the distortions produced the loudspeaker. Since we have
know that the parameters determined by direct system identiﬁcations is not good enough to be used in a
compensator, this method has the advantage that the good compensation results are guaranteed. Instead of
checking the THD in frequency domain like we did in the last section in Chapter 4, here we discuss the time
domain sample-by-sample adaptive method.
The principle is shown in ﬁgure 5.7. The error signal e(n) is the difference between the loudspeaker output
y(n) and the desired signal d(n). d(n) is generated by ﬁltering the input voltage w(n) with a linear system
HLD(z) which shares the same properties (eigenvalues) with the linear dynamic in the compensator. The
parameters in the inverse dynamic is tuned by some adaptive algorithm to minimize the mean-square-error

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Developing an adaptive compensation system that tune its parameters by minimizing the distortions in
the compensated loudspeaker is the objective of this thesis. This approach enables the identiﬁcation of
parameters and at the same time to ensure the improvement of sound quality, which is the ﬁnal goal of
basically all the researched on loudspeakers.
This concerns both the nonlinear compensation and system identiﬁcation. The compensation is studied
ﬁrst to see how good the reduction in distortions can be achieved. The algorithm of feedback linearization
is applied three loudspeakers described by a 4th order system. The ﬁrst attempt based on the parameters
determined from Klippel system fails. This proves that the traditional idea of an adaptive compensator that
uses an additional model to tune the parameters might be failed. The parameter are tuned by minimizing
THD in the THD output is succeed. Although it is manually tuned, it shows the possibility of the ﬁnal
objective of this thesis.
At last, the ﬁnal objective is studied on the theoretical level. It is found that the direction of parameter tuning
is the main problem of this idea. However, it might be possible to tune the parameter simply by trial. A
system use the adaptive ﬁlter theory need to be investigated further.

The occasionally occurred sub-harmonics in the solution of ode45 is undesirable because it makes the
assessment of the compensator difﬁcult. Whether the harmonics come from the compensator or from the
ODE solver becomes a problem. To solve this, other methods including Forward Euler, Midpoint and
classical Runge-Kutta (RK4) are tried. The performance of the midpoint method is in the middle between
the other two, so it is not discussed in the following.
Forward Euler is the simplest numerical ODE solver. For a general 1st order ODE given by
˙y = f(t, y)
(A.1)
Forward Euler approximates the solution of yn+1 at tn+1 from the slope ˙y at its preceding time point tn:
yn+1 = yn + ∆t ˙y(tn) = yn + ∆tf(tn, yn)
(A.2)
Thus Forward Euler method requires the slope of y does not change too much in one step from tn to
tn+1. Forward Euler is said to be a 1st order method which means that the global error of the solution is
proportional to the step size ∆t.
Unlike the Forward Euler which only use the slop at tn, RK4 method uses several slopes between tn and
tn+1. The RK4 method has been given in Eq. 4.82 and 4.83. It can be seen that RK4 uses a weighted sum of
4 different slopes. RK4 is a 4th order method meaning that the global error is proportional to (∆t)4. Since
the slope is calculated 4 times for each time step in Eq. 4.82, the calculation time is about 4 times longer
than the Forward Euler method. But it can be expected the convergence of RK4 is much faster than Forward
Euler with decreasing step size.

A.2
FFT technique
The FFT technique for the simulation in this project is based on the following statement:
• The N points discrete Fourier transform of a signal x(n) with ﬁnite length l is equivalent to the Fourier
series of a periodic signal xp(n) multiplied by N, where xp(n) is a periodic repetition of x(n) by
every N samples.
Consider a periodic discrete signal for example the steady state solution of diaphragm velocity ud(n). Now a
l points segment of ud(n) is taken out for fft. Based on the statement above, if l equals to an integer multiple
of the period and the FFT points N equals to the signal length l, the result of FFT normalized by N will
recover the exact spectrum of the inﬁnite long periodic signal ud(n).
Figure A.4 shows an example of the spectrum of diaphragm velocity driven by 20 Hz sine wave. In the
legend, l is the signal length, N is FFT length, Tn is the period the signal. Ideally only the 20 Hz and its
harmonics can be seen and this is the case for the blue curve. As talked before, the blue curve is actually a
normalized Fourier series of the original signal. The black curve uses a non-integer periods for FFT and
strong frequency smearing can be seen. The red curve uses integer periods but with FFT ponit N >> l.
The pattern shows the spectral leakage of a window function which offers another explanation. The FFT is
performed on a rectangular windowed periodic signal. When N = l and l is integer periods, the sampling
