Abstract.
Vat photopolymerization is a type of additive manufacturing (AM) pro-
cess that produces 3D objects by selectively curing liquid resin using
light-activated polymerization. Stereolithography, the first AM process
to be patented and commercialized, exemplifies this technique. In the
context of advancing AM technology, this MSc project focuses on de-
veloping a slicing software for the Elegoo Mars DLP 4, a digital light
processing (DLP) printer, as part of an AM farm project for the Depart-
ment of Civil and Mechanical Engineering at DTU, in collaboration with
Carmo and Kerbs & Co.
The objective of this project is to enhance the efficiency and precision of
3D printing operations within the department’s AM farm, leveraging the
capabilities of vat photopolymerization. By creating customized slicing
software, the project aims to optimize the performance of the Elegoo
Mars DLP 4, facilitating its integration into the research and industrial
applications pursued by DTU and its partners, Carmo and Kerbs & Co.
This work not only contributes to the technological advancements in
additive manufacturing but also supports the strategic initiatives of the
collaborating entities in achieving higher standards of production and
innovation.

Introduction
Additive manufacturing (AM) [1], commonly referred to as 3D printing, has
dramatically changed how we handle production and prototyping. Among the
various AM techniques, vat photopolymerization is particularly noteworthy for
its ability to produce detailed, high-resolution 3D objects. This method utilizes
light-activated polymerization to selectively cure liquid resin. Stereolithography
(SLA) is a prime example of this technology and was the first AM process to be
patented and commercialized.
The continuous evolution of AM technology has opened the way for new re-
search and applications. Within this framework, the Department of Civil and
Mechanical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) has
launched an AM farm project in collaboration with Carmo and Kerbs & Co.
The project aims to explore and enhance the capabilities of vat photopolymer-
ization, specifically utilizing the Elegoo Mars DLP 4 digital light processing
(DLP) printer.
1.1
Proprietary File Formats for SLA and DLP Printers
There is not a universal format for SLA and DLP printers like FDM printers
that use GCode. Each printer company uses their own proprietary format and
most do not support other formats or even open formats like OSF [2] or UVJ

1.2
Aim
This study aims to analyse the process of converting an STL file to G-code.
The primary objective of this MSc thesis project is to develop a slicing software
tailored specifically for the Elegoo Mars 4 DLP printer, utilizing the Python
programming language. This software will streamline the conversion process,
enhancing the efficiency and precision of 3D printing operations.

1.3
Objectives
Towards achieving the goals of this MSc thesis project, the following objectives
have been defined:
– Gain Comprehensive Knowledge of 3D Printing Technology: Ac-
quire a thorough understanding of how 3D printing technology operates,
focusing on the intricacies of the process.
– Understand G-code Generation: Delve into the methods and procedures
used to generate G-code files.
– Ensure Compatibility with Elegoo Mars 4 DLP: Develop a solution
specifically oriented to produce G-code files that are compatible with the
Elegoo Mars 4 DLP printer.
– Reverse Engineer .goo Files: Fully reverse engineer and comprehend the
structure and functionality of .goo files to enable their production.
– Develop a User-Friendly GUI: Create a graphical user interface (GUI)
to provide full control and enhance the user-friendliness of the developed
software.
– Implement Unique Features: Introduce additional features not yet avail-
able in commercial slicing software, particularly the capability to control
settings for each layer individually.
– Simulation and Real-World Testing: Ensuring that the developed solu-
tion can simulate printing results accurately and function effectively in both
simulation and real-world scenarios.

Additive Manufacturing and Slicing process
2.1
Advantages of Additive Manufacturing
There are several advantages associated with additive manufacturing [4], partic-
ularly considering it allows their users to have increased design flexibility. This
is because, unlike most conventional processes of manufacturing that generally
limits the designer at the beginning by method-related restrictive boundaries.
AM enables one to make complex shapes that, by conventional processes, either
cannot be made or would be very expensive. This capability improves product
design, and thus fosters innovation.
An other very vital benefit is the ability of AM to be customised. On this
count, it allows the production of individual parts and small batches economi-
cally, which is quite significant in the health sector. This therefore means, that
prosthetics and implants that are designed for one patient are tailored and man-
ufactured accordingly to suit their needs perfectly.
Moreover, AM can also be highly resourceful in the use of materials. Since
material is added layer by layer only at locations where it is required, it reduces
waste drastically compared with conventional methods of subtractive manufac-
turing. This not only helps in terms of production cost but is also quite effective
in the protection of the environment by reducing the amount of ecological foot-
print exerted by the manufacturing process.
2.2
Challenges in Additive Manufacturing
Several challenges [5] must be overcome before additive manufacturing is more
mainstream. Of those, perhaps the most serious is that it’s literally orders of
magnitude slower than more traditional methods of production, such as injection
molding or CNC machining, especially in terms of quantity.
Another disadvantage is the limited material range available for AM, which
puts a barrier on application with parts with specific material property require-
ments, such as high strength or heat resistance. On the other hand, achieving
great quality in every production batch might be challenging because the char-
acteristics of the parts produced through this process depend on lot of variables.
Such as printer type, material, and settings, which can result in variations un-
acceptable after quality control in the tolerance-critical industries.
Moreover, integration into existing production lines involves huge invest-
ments in new equipment, staff training, and redesign of parts, which is quite
time-consuming and expensive. Despite AM is cost-effective for prototyping and
small-scale production, due to the slower speed and special requirements of ma-
terials and expertise, per-unit cost remains high for large-scale production. Tech-
nical problems, such as high precision and handling of warping and shrinkage,
further create difficulties when this is applied in applications with exact specifi-
cations and high reliability. Overcoming these barriers calls for improvements in
production speed, variety of materials, and quality control, together with work-
flow integration and cost reduction. These improvements are needed to make AM

2.3
Types of 3D Printing Technologies
There are several technologies in additive manufacturing. All of them with key
differences between processes, materials, and applications. This section intro-
duces the general overview of some of the most common AM technologies, Stere-
olithography (SLA), Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM), Selective Laser Sinter-
ing (SLS), and Digital Light Processing (DLP), with a more extensive focus in
this, since the Elegoo Mars 4 printer is a DLP printer.
2.3.1
Stereolithography (SLA)
Stereolithography, or SLA [6], was one of the initial methods of additive man-
ufacturing that was ever developed. It works by using a vat of liquid photopoly-
mer resin that an ultraviolet laser is used to systematically solidify, building up
the object in layers. The process starts with the platform submerged in resin, a
UV laser then draws the design of the object onto the surface, which becomes
solid wherever it comes into contact with the light. As each layer is complete,
the platform would shift slightly up to ready the next layer and so on, repeating
this process until an object is fully formed.
The primary materials used in SLA are photopolymer resins, whose proper-
ties are very adaptable and can be tuned to achieve various mechanical charac-
teristics. This technique is also noted for its high accuracy, which allows parts to
get smooth finishes, hence extremely applicable in areas required of fine details
such as dental moulds, intricate jewellery, and complex prototypes.
2.3.2
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) [7] is one of the most common techniques
in additive manufacturing, applied in more and more applications,both consumer
and industry wise. Essentially, an extruder head is used to print/recreate an
object by heating the thermoplastic filament to a melting point and depositing
the material in layers of an object. After each layer, the plate slowly moves down
to allow for vertical height in printing the model.
Most common materials used for FDM are Polylactic Acid (PLA), Acryloni-
trile Butadiene Styrene (ABS), and Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol (PETG).
It is highly valued for its user-friendliness, reasonable price, and, most impor-
tantly, for its versatility in fabricating functional models and prototypes. This
printing technique is quite common for making manufacturing and educational
tools, as well as for small-volume production runs.
2.3.3
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) [8] utilizes a powerful laser to bond small
particles of material into a cohesive structure. The process initiates with the

distribution of a fine powder across the build platform. The laser accurately
melts the powder following the cross-sectional outline of the design. With each
completed layer, the platform is lowered, and a new powder layer is applied on
top. This sequence is repeated until the entire object is constructed.
Materials typically used in SLS include varieties like nylon and polyamide,
with occasional use of metals. A distinct advantage of SLS is its ability to build
without support structures; the non-sintered powder surrounding the part pro-
vides necessary support during the build process. This capability makes SLS
highly suitable for producing robust prototypes and parts with intricate geome-
tries, which are essential in high-demand sectors such as aerospace and automo-
tive industries.
2.3.4
Digital Light Processing (DLP)
Digital Light Processing (DLP) [9] is similar to Stereolithography (SLA) in
its basic approach but differentiates itself through the type of light source used
to cure the resin. Instead of employing a UV laser, DLP technology utilizes a
digital light projector that casts an image of each layer simultaneously across the
entire platform. This method significantly speeds up the layer curing process,
allowing for faster production of parts compared to SLA.
DLP proves especially advantageous in fields that demand high precision
and intricate detailing, such as dental restorations, jewelry design, and complex
prototypes. The technology’s ability to quickly produce finely detailed objects
makes it invaluable in sectors where both time efficiency and detail fidelity are
paramount.
The Elegoo Mars 4 employs DLP technology, achieving high-resolution out-
puts characterized by precise details and smooth finishes. The printer uses a
variety of photopolymer resins tailored to different specifications, ranging from
standard options to specialized resins designed for dental and engineering appli-
cations.

2.4
The Slicing Process in 3D Printing
In 3D printing, slicing is the process of taking a 3D model and converting it into
an operational file that a 3D printer can work with. It is an important step that
changes a digital design to readable instructions for the printer. The result and
quality of the end product are largely dependent on the exactitude and accuracy
of this process. It is very important that this is done correctly, as even the most
advanced 3D printers cannot produce a usable printed object if the slice is not
created correctly.
Fig. 1. Slicing Flow Chart
2.4.1
.STL Files
STL files [10] are among the most crucial elements in 3D printing. Files
describe information about the surfaces of a 3D object using triangular meshes.
Stereolithography (SLA) or Standard Tessellation Language, just what it means
a format overwhelmingly supported and in use due to its simplicity and succinct
way of describing complex shapes. An .STL file can only be generated when
a 3D model has been designed in a CAD (Computer-Aided Design) program.
Once the model is designed, it is exported as an .STL file and finally ready to
be sliced.

Fig. 2. STL file analysis
2.4.2
G-Code Generation
The most important part of a slicing process is the translation of .STL files
to G-code [11]. Most of the CNC machines, including all 3D printers, utilise G-
code language while performing their tasks. So, slicing software takes the .STL
file, cuts it into layers, and develops G-code instructions for each layer. This
instruction set informs the 3D printer where to move, how fast to move, and
in which path. In other words, G-code is used to enable and drive the printer,
directing each single movement of the print head, that shall move to lay down
each layer precisely.
2.5
Considerations and Parameters in Slicing for DLP printers
Several decisions that are critical to the slicing process are key factors for the
optimization of printed quality in a DLP printer, which includes layer height,
infill patterns, and support structures. In the case of DLP, layer height is a huge
determinant of resolution versus speed of the print. Finer layers will produce a
smoother, finer finish but at the cost of time. On the other hand, thicker layers
increase the printing speed at the cost of detail resolution and layer visibility.
Infill patterns in DLP also define an object’s strength and the amount of resin
used in its manufacture. Basically, the more dense the fill, the stronger it will be,
but generally at the cost of more resin used and time needed, hence making the
process more expensive. Support structures are very necessary for DLP prints,
especially those with overhangs or other complicated geometries; if these features
are not well-supported, they may fail during the print.
These parameters should always be adjusted based on the specific capabilities
of the DLP printer, the properties of the resin used, and the detailed require-
ments for the final object. Adjustments are important in ensuring that DLP
benefits, such as its high accuracy and smooth finish, are reaped with the least
material use and print time.

3.2
How the Elegoo Mars 4 DLP Works
The Elegoo Mars 4 utilizes DLP technology, where a digital light projector flashes
each layer of the 3D model onto a vat of liquid photopolymer resin. This light
selectively cures the resin layer by layer to build up the final object. Here’s a
detailed breakdown of the printing process:
– Model Preparation: The 3D model is prepared using slicing software like
ChituBox or Lychee Slicer, which converts the model into G-code instruc-
tions.
– Resin Preparation: The resin tank is filled with photopolymer resin, en-
suring it does not exceed the maximum fill line.
– Printing Process: The build platform is submerged in the resin, and the
digital light projector flashes an image of the first layer. The light cures the
resin at the specific points, creating a solid layer. The platform then moves
slightly upwards, and the process repeats for each subsequent layer.
– Post-Processing: Once printing is complete, the model is carefully re-
moved, and any excess resin is cleaned off using isopropyl alcohol or ethyl
alcohol.

6.2.2
goo RLE encode function
Description: The function takes an image array (a NumPy array in this
context) and encodes it into a bytearray using an RLE [16] format specific to
.goo files.
Parameters: image: A numpy array representing the image to be encoded.
Returns: A bytearray containing the RLE-encoded image data.
Step-by-Step Explanation:
1. Initialization:
– LayerMagic: A constant byte value 0x55 used to identify the start of
the encoded data.
– rle: A bytearray initialized with LayerMagic.
– previous color, current color, length size, and check sum: Vari-
ables used to track the current state during encoding.
2. Flatten the Image: The image array is flattened into a 1D array (span)
for easier sequential processing.
3. Helper Function: add rep:
– This function handles adding the RLE representation of the current
streak of pixels (length size) to the rle bytearray.
– It distinguishes between different cases based on the value of current color
and length size.
Detailed Steps in add rep:
• If length size is zero, it returns immediately since there’s nothing to
encode.
• first byte index is used to remember the position in rle where the
current RLE chunk starts.
• Depending on the value of current color:
∗0xFF: Indicates a chunk of all white pixels.
∗> 0: Indicates a chunk of gray pixels with the value between
0x01 and 0xFE.
• The length size is encoded into the byte at first byte index and po-
tentially additional bytes if length size exceeds certain thresholds
(0xF, 0xFFF, 0xFFFFF, 0xFFFFFFF).
==PAGE:45==
44
4. Main Loop:
• Iterates through each pixel in the flattened image array.
• If the current pixel (span[i]) matches current color, it increments length size.
• If a different color is encountered, it calls add rep to encode the previous
streak and resets length size for the new color.
5. Final Encoding: After the loop, add rep is called one last time to ensure
the final streak is encoded.
6. Checksum Calculation: A checksum is computed over the RLE data (ex-
cluding the initial LayerMagic byte). The checksum is then appended to the
end of the rle bytearray.
7. Return value: The function returns the rle bytearray converted to bytes.
Example Workflow:
– The image is flattened to a 1D array.
– For each pixel in the flattened array, the function checks if it matches the
current streak.
– When a different color is detected or at the end of the array, the current
streak is encoded.
– This process continues until the entire image is processed.
– Finally, a checksum is added to ensure data integrity.
This RLE scheme is designed to efficiently encode pixel data by compressing
sequences of identical pixels, reducing the amount of data needed to represent
the image. The specific format used ensures that both the color values and the
lengths of sequences are compactly stored, with special handling for common
cases such as all black pixels.
6.2.3
Development of the GUI for GOOExporter
This subsection outlines the design and implementation of the Graphical
User Interface (GUI) for GOOExporter. The GUI was developed in Python
using the PySide2 library, which provides Python bindings for the Qt application
framework. This chapter analyzes the key components, design decisions, and the
overall architecture involved in the creation of the GUI.
Framework Selection
The selection of Qt (PySide2) for GUI development was driven by its flexi-
bility, ease of use, and feasibility of integration with the AMLab framework.
Architecture Overview
The GUI architecture consists of several key components, each responsible for
specific functionalities. The primary components include the GOOExporter main
window, FilePickerPanel, ScrollableForm, and AdvancedSettingsPanel. Each of
these components is designed to handle specific tasks, ranging from file selection
to displaying and editing settings.
Implementation Details
1. Main Window (GOOExporter)
==PAGE:46==
45
The main window serves as the central hub of the application, coordinating
interactions between various components and managing the application’s
state. It supports two modes: Simple and Advanced (PerLayer), allowing
users to toggle between basic and advanced settings. The main window is
responsible for initializing and arranging the primary layout, which includes
the file picker panel, scrollable forms for general settings, and an advanced
settings panel for per-layer configurations.
The code is structured to separate concerns by breaking down functional-
ity into classes, with GOOExporter managing the overall application flow.
This approach ensures that each part of the GUI is responsible for its own
functionality, making the software easier to manage and extend.
2. File Picker Panel (FilePickerPanel)
The FilePickerPanel component allows users to select files and directories
required for processing. It includes fields for specifying the file definition,
images folder, image file template, and export file path. The panel consists
of buttons for loading images and exporting data, a progress bar to indicate
operation status, and a log to display progress messages.
The FilePickerPanel compact file selection logic and progress tracking, iso-
lating these from other parts of the GUI. This design choice ensures that file
operations and user feedback mechanisms are handled consistently and can
be easily modified without affecting other components.
3. Scrollable Form (ScrollableForm)
The ScrollableForm component displays and manages user inputs for dif-
ferent settings. It dynamically generates form fields based on a YAML file
definition and supports data validation, tooltips, and default value handling.
The form updates the settings file whenever a user modifies a value.
Breaking down the form into a scrollable layout allows for a scalable interface
that can handle a large number of settings without overwhelming the user.
Each form field is created dynamically based on the configuration, ensuring
that the GUI adapts to different settings files without requiring hardcoded
changes.
4. Advanced Settings Panel (AdvancedSettingsPanel)
The AdvancedSettingsPanel component provides an interface for managing
per-layer settings. It includes a widget stack for displaying images and a
slider for navigating between layers. Users can clone settings from one layer
to multiple others using a specialized input field.
The design of the AdvancedSettingsPanel addresses the need for detailed
per-layer configuration. By using a stacked widget and a slider, the interface
allows users to navigate through layers efficiently.
5. Multithreading with QThread
To ensure a responsive UI, long-running operations such as file export are
handled in a separate thread using QThread. This prevents the main thread
from blocking and allows for real-time updates of the progress bar and log
messages.
The multithreading approach is important for maintaining a smooth user
experience, especially during intensive operations. By decoupling the main
==PAGE:47==
46
GUI thread from processing tasks, the application remains responsive and
user interactions are not hindered.
6. User Input Validation and Feedback
Custom widgets such as CustomQLineEdit and CustomQCheckBox are used
to validate user inputs and provide immediate visual feedback. These widgets
change their background color based on the validity of the input, guiding the
user to correct any errors.
Input validation and feedback are essential for ensuring data integrity. By
providing real-time visual cues, the GUI helps users quickly identify and cor-
rect errors, thereby reducing the likelihood of invalid inputs and improving
overall usability.

8
Conclusion and future work
8.1
Conclusion
The main goal of this MSc project was successfully achieved. Although the ini-
tial aim was to convert an STL file directly into a .goo file, an existing solution
for slicing STL files into images/layers for resin DLP printing within the same
AM farm project was utilized. Consequently, the project focused on converting
these images into .goo files and developing a GUI for the software. This was ac-
complished through understanding and reverse-engineering the .goo file format.
Additionally, an advanced mode feature was developed to offer per-layer settings
configuration, a capability lacking in existing solutions.
Initially, the study was focused on gcode in general, which led to some time
being wasted. This was due to the realization that the main difficulty with DLP
printers is that the gcode format is different for each printer and company, and
does not follow the common gcode commands. These formats are proprietary
and very difficult to analyze, presenting a significant problem for users of those
software solutions who do not have full control of the whole process. The com-
plexity and closed nature of these formats mean that users cannot easily adjust
the printing parameters to suit their needs.
However, the solution developed in this project offers a much easier way
to adjust the printing parameters accordingly. By focusing on converting the
images/layers into .goo files, the project bypassed the complexities associated
with proprietary .goo formats. This approach not only simplifies the process but
also empowers users by giving them more control over their printing parameters.
One of the most significant achievements of this MSc project is the develop-
ment of the per-layer settings configuration. This feature is absent in existing
software solutions and represents a crucial advancement. Allowing users to adjust
the settings for each individual layer is extremely important because it provides
them with full control over the entire printing process. This capability enables
users to fine-tune each layer, improving the quality and precision of the final
printed object. It also allows for more experimentation and iteration, leading to
better results and more efficient use of materials and time.
The development of the GUI significantly enhanced the usability of the soft-
ware compared to the console mode. The GUI provides a more intuitive and
user-friendly interface, allowing users to easily navigate through the various fea-
tures and settings without needing extensive technical knowledge. This visual
approach simplifies the process of converting images into .goo files and adjusting
per-layer settings, making the software accessible to a broader range of users.
By presenting options and configurations in a clear, organized manner, the GUI
reduces the potential for errors and streamlines the workflow, thereby improving
overall efficiency and user experience.
In conclusion, this project successfully addressed a key limitation in existing
DLP printing software by developing a solution that simplifies the adjustment
of printing parameters and introduces per-layer settings configuration. This ad-
vancement not only enhances user control but also contributes to the overall
==PAGE:65==
64
efficiency and effectiveness of the resin DLP printing process. The ability to
fine-tune each layer settings provides users with a powerful tool for optimizing
their prints, ultimately leading to higher-quality outcomes and greater flexibility
in their additive manufacturing endeavors.
8.2
Future Work
Although the .goo file can be printed properly using the Elegoo Mars 4 DLP
printer, the current solution is limited by its lack of support generation, re-
stricting it to printing very stable and often symmetrical objects. To expand
its capabilities and print more complex, balanced objects, the software needs a
feature that uses specific algorithms to automatically generate supports, with
the option for users to add supports manually. Additionally, while a function to
generate G-code from the AMLab framework was implemented, a dedicated GUI
for the software was developed to utilize the advanced per-layer settings feature.
Integrating these two software solutions is feasible since Qt (PySide2), used for
GUI development, is also utilized in the AMLab slicer. This commonality allows
for future merging or enhancements. Additionally, adding image slicing capabil-
ities to the developed software could significantly enhance its functionality and
user experience.
