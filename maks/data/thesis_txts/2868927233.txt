Abstract
There is an entry barrier for remote cybersecurity learning due to
the dependence on continuous internet access and bandwidth, poor
time flexibility of challenges and competitions, and lack of realism
in simulated environments. Current training solutions seem not to
focus on providing the best access to remote learning experiences
but on the quality of challenges, and variety. This thesis holisti-
cally addresses the cybersecurity learning problem by introducing a
carefully designed, pedagogically-supported storyline campaign with
complex challenges. The implementation was built upon StratoCy-
berLab, an existing offline cyber range.
The proposed campaign
incorporates both offensive and defensive challenges and connects
them through a cohesive narrative arc. The storyline was designed
to be enticing and attractive while requiring growing competencies.
The campaign’s educational outcomes are assessed in terms of cor-
relating skills with components of the NICE framework by NIST.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
There exists a significant entry barrier to cybersecurity training. Continu-
ous internet access is often required, as well as adherence to a sched-
ule [1]. Furthermore, the learning material often fails to sustain trainees’
interest due to insuﬀicient realism and complexity. Last but not least,
there can be significant monetary costs involved [2].
Current approaches to cybersecurity training include cyber ranges, on-
line platforms such as Hack The Box, capture the flag (CTF) competitions,
various courses and commercial trainings [3][4]. All these fail to provide a
comprehensive solution to tackle all components of the entry barrier. CTFs,
courses and trainings lack time flexibility [1]. Online platforms and most
cyber ranges require an uninterrupted internet connection [1]. Commercial
trainings additionally introduce a considerable monetary cost [2].
This thesis proposes to solve the entry barrier as a whole by creating
a campaign of complex cybersecurity challenges. The campaign is freely
accessible and can be played fully offline. The challenges are connected
through a narrative storyline. A cybersecurity skill framework is used to
evaluate the learning impact of the campaign.
An offline cyber range, StratoCyberLab [5], is used as the base platform.
Learning objectives are selected based on work roles and corresponding skills
from the NICE Workforce Framework for Cybersecurity [6] and mapped to
individual challenges.
All source code authored as part of this thesis is available in the Stra-
toCyberLab repository [5]1.
The contributions of this thesis are:
• A pedagogically-designed capture-the-flag (CTF) cybersecurity cam-
paign to teach, motivate and improve the skills of players in an en-
gaging, long-term way.
• A mapping of challenges in the cybersecurity CTF campaign to the
NICE framework [6].

CHAPTER 2
Previous Work
This chapter presents an overview of the current main cybersecurity training
options, along with their major advantages and disadvantages. Some of
them target individuals with no prior knowledge in the area, intending to
qualify themselves for an entry-level position, while others serve mostly as
amusement for practicing professionals or enthusiasts.
2.1
Cyber Ranges
A cyber range is defined by the European Cyber Security Organisation as
follows:
A cyber range is a platform for the development, delivery
and use of interactive simulation environments. A simulation
environment is a representation of an organisation’s ICT, OT,
mobile and physical systems, applications and infrastructures,
including the simulation of attacks, users and their activities
and of any other Internet, public or third-party services which
the simulated environment may depend upon. A cyber range
includes a combination of core technologies for the realisation
and use of the simulation environment and of additional com-
ponents which are, in turn, desirable or required for achieving
specific cyber range use cases.[7]
Cyber ranges can be used by cybersecurity professionals, including those
seeking to join the field, to refine their skills by gaining hands-on experience
in simulated environments. From a user perspective, cyber ranges enable
the use of industry-standard tools and a certain level of immersion in the
simulated situation, increasing its fidelity. The situations range from stan-
dalone single-player exercises to complex scenarios where one team attempts
to besiege the other team’s infrastructure.
Accordingly, cyber ranges, such as RangeForce [8], are mostly avail-
able as complex solutions for organizations, but there also exist open plat-
forms, both bundled with challenges (e.g., StratoCyberLab, described in

Most of the components required for running a cyber range can be vir-

[7].
This virtualization can take place either on the trainee’s
own hardware, referred to as conventional virtualization. Traditionally, full
virtual machines are used, typically using a Type 2 hypervisor, such as
VirtualBox. Alternatively, the cyber range can leverage an overlay con-
tainerization platform, such as Docker. Running a range locally provides
an advantage in terms of greater control and autonomy, for instance, relax-
ing the continuous internet access requirement by reducing dependency on
external services.
Simulated infrastructure can also be hosted in the cloud, which can
either be a private cloud of the organizing company or a public cloud, such
as Microsoft Azure or Amazon Web Services.
Public cloud-based solutions benefit from easy scalability and relative
lack of management overhead, while private clouds may offer an experience
more true to real network components and environments since they can also
include non-virtualized components more easily.
There is also the option of combining the benefits of both cloud-based
approaches to achieve both benefits [10]. Overall, cloud-based cyber ranges
are easier to use for the end-user since they do not require much setup and
are not bound by processing power and storage space on the user’s device.
2.1.1
StratoCyberLab
StratoCyberLab [5] (SCL) is a free and open-source offline cyber range
powered by Docker Compose and a supporting framework of scripts and
interfaces. It was developed in the Stratosphere Laboratory at the Czech
Technical University in Prague by Martin Řepa, Maria Rigaki and Veronica
Valeros, originally for the purposes of engaging online students as part of the
Introduction to Security (Bezpečnost systémů) class taught at the Faculty
of Electrical Engineering at CTU, publicly available as a MOOC1.
It consists of 4 main components:
• Dashboard: a 3-tier web application that manages the starting and
stopping of classes/challenges and persisting task solutions in the
database. The frontend interface, shown in Figure 2.1, renders class
materials, challenge lists, and descriptions, allowing students to sub-
mit flags (solutions) and generally interface with the API exposed by
the backend.

2.1.1.1
Advantages and Disadvantages
One of the primary advantages of an offline, self-hosted cyber range lies
in the fact that after installing it, no internet connection is required
to use it. This is extremely important in an educational context. Several
sources suggest that not having a reliable broadband internet connection at
home might negatively impact students’ academic performance [13][14][15].
Offline access to training material might alleviate this impact.
An offline cyber range has another strong advantage in the fact it can
be used at any time. This is mainly in contrast to CTFs, which are
commonly only available for a short period of time. An example of where
this limited availability becomes an issue for the player is when they struggle
with a challenge and are unable to successfully complete it, yet they might
want to read a write-up of it later, understand the solution, and possibly
retry it, completing it themselves.
However, since the infrastructure is
taken offline, the challenges are no longer accessible. In general, it is often
impossible to complete the challenges at one’s own pace, especially when
following them alone as a novice.
With HackTheBox, TryHackMe and similar platforms (see Section 2.2),
this issue is not as prevalent since the challenges put up there usually stay
for several months at minimum2, but it still does exist since challenges
eventually get retired [16].
It might be the case that it is diﬀicult to avoid this phenomenon (even

2.2
HackTheBox, TryHackMe and Others
Several platforms, such as Hack The Box [22] or TryHackMe [23] offer stan-
dalone challenges playable at any time. Often, there is a free tier of chal-
lenges available for everyone to play. The challenges are categorized by:
• Theme dictates what the core skill being tested is. An example is
cryptography, where contestants receive encrypted material and their
task is to uncover the cleartext. Another one is forensics, where the
input material may consist of a packet capture file documenting an
intrusion into a monitored system, and players attempt to find out
what attack technique was used.
• From the theme, it can often be inferred whether the challenge is
offensive or defensive in nature, i.e., whether the player is attack-
ing/breaking into a system or preventing/investigating an intrusion.
• Diﬀiculty is usually self-assessed by the challenge developers and
represents the skill level required in order to successfully solve the
challenge.
From an infrastructure perspective, the most common setup (employed
by both listed examples) is to offer a VPN endpoint to which the player
connects from their local machine, effectively gaining access to a virtual
network with the challenge’s machines. This approach is beneficial because
it serves to create a stronger sense of realism: the player can access services
and operate as if they were in the physical network with their own machine.
That is, of course, as long as the VPN software in combination with the
player’s connection provides a reliable service.
If the player desires more, there usually is a paid tier with more premium
challenges, such as with costlier infrastructure or, in general, challenges
that are harder to implement/maintain. Furthermore, there exist multiple
career training paths which sometimes even allow the player to receive a
well-branded certificate of completion (and thus, formally confirming their
newly gained skillset). These trainings are mostly paid [24][25].

2.3
Capture the Flag Competitions
Capture the Flag (CTFs) competitions, such as DEFCON CTF4, Patri-
otCTF5 or TUCTF6 are a type of cybersecurity challenge collections wherein
contestants, usually in teams, compete against each other.
There are two main genres of CTFs: jeopardy, where teams gain points
by successfully submitting flags (hidden strings confirming their completion
of a challenge), and attack-defense (ADCTF), where teams not only com-
pete against each other, but they actively attack and defend each others’
infrastructure [26].
ADCTFs are designed to mimic cyber warfare [26], unlike jeopardy-style
competitions where teams solve individual challenges. However, ADCTFs
also typically require a larger amount of participants and management of
infrastructure, even though there are efforts to minimize this aspect [27].
Jeopardy CTFs are similar to previously discussed platforms, such as Hack-
TheBox, offering mostly standalone challenges.
With a few notable exceptions7, these competitions tend to run for lim-
ited amounts of time, typically weekends. Teams striving for top positions
are incentivized to work throughout the night and cooperate closely with
their teammates.
In the author’s experience, if there are any services to connect to, they
are often exposed to the internet (given a public IP address), so that any
contestant can connect freely without setting up a VPN connection.
Unlike other alternatives listed in Chapter 2, these competitions often
target a different demographic group: individuals who already possess, at
least to some level, cybersecurity knowledge or experience. Most do not
give their players detailed instructions on how to proceed and thus mostly
lack the educational aspect.
One could argue that this lack is somewhat mitigated by the fact that
many player groups publish write-ups – walkthroughs on how to solve the
challenges. These naturally do not have a standardized format and thus
vary in level of detail and technical accuracy, but overall provide a means
for players to read about how others solve challenges. Write-ups are often
created throughout the gameplay but rarely get published before the com-
petition ends [28]. This is a strategic step since teams want to maintain an
edge over their rivals and not publish their know-how prematurely, thereby

2.3.1
Honeypots in CTFs
Honeypots have been included in several research and education CTFs.
Honeypots, in general, serve a dual purpose of both deceiving attackers to
reduce the number of attacks on real services and reconnaissance (gaining
an insight into how the attackers operate) [29].
In a CTF context, organizers can use them to analyze attacker behavior,
measure their effectiveness [30] and optimize the attacker-honeypot inter-
action with the goal of increasing the honeypot’s eﬀiciency [31].
2.4
Traditional Education
With the exception of cybersecurity awareness training, offensive and de-
fensive cybersecurity is usually taught in the tertiary education cycle, which
is what this section is going to focus on.
Even in cybersecurity programs, such as the Cybersecurity specializa-
tion in the Master program Open Informatics (OI) at CTU FEE8, the to-
tal share of highly security-related topics is quite low (15 percent in OI’s
case [32]).
This can be interpreted both positively and negatively: owing to this,
students get well-rounded background knowledge in several related areas
(algorithms, data structures, compilers, web applications, and databases,
etc.).
On the other hand, it also presents an obstacle in delving deeper into
interest areas, as the students are forced to keep up with their other courses
and cannot fully focus on a security topic that captures their attention.
Furthermore, tying into this is the limitation and influence of the faculty
available at the respective educational institute: while at larger universi-
ties, it is not uncommon to find entire departments dedicated to teaching
computer security, it might not be the case at smaller colleges. This may
significantly limit the perspective the student can gain.
University courses often leverage the CTF concept for introducing stu-
dents to cybersecurity [33][34].

2.5
MOOC
Massive open online courses (MOOC) have become a very popular approach
to own-pace studies [35][36]. Both commercial companies (like IBM [37])
and large universities (like the University of Maryland [38]) develop such
courses for cybersecurity. A course usually spans several weeks or months
and provides educational material in the form of lecture videos, take-home
assignments, quizzes, and sometimes peer discussion and review. Option-
ally, many MOOCs also allow the student to gain a certificate of completion.
2.6
Commercial Trainings
Numerous training providers offer comprehensive courses, some embedding
certifications, designed to equip learners with both theoretical understand-
ing and practical skills to fill cybersecurity positions.
2.6.1
Profession-oriented
While commercial training for aspiring cybersecurity professionals might
borrow elements from the aforementioned approaches or even take part in
the form of a MOOC, they distinguish themselves by being organized almost
exclusively by prominent companies providing certifications, for instance,
CompTIA Security+9.
These trainings have one shared common trait that differentiates them
from the other alternatives in this section: a significantly higher price, com-
monly reaching to the higher hundreds or even lower thousands euro [39].
This is not to imply that they are not worth the price; it rather points
out the significant barrier of entry for individuals paying out of their own
pocket. After all, these industry-recognized certifications themselves func-
tion as skill frameworks, codifying the required/expected skills between
organizations and their prospective employees.
However, they also suffer from time limitations: when one purchases the
full package of CompTIA Security+ – which includes an exam voucher, the-
oretical learning material, and a hands-on learning environment – the access
to the latter two is limited to 12 months from the package’s activation.

2.6.2
Cybersecurity Awareness
Even though it is not a tool to train expert cybersecurity practitioners, cy-
bersecurity awareness training often occurs in contexts where such prepa-
ration is not the stated goal. For example, a company with a strong digital
presence may be interested in ensuring its general workforce possesses the
knowledge on how to behave responsibly in cyberspace, avoid the most
common attacks, and follow appropriate policies.
This can either be achieved by mandatory training consisting of rudi-
mentary lessons and quizzes, which the employees may show disinterest
in and may even consider as a necessary evil [40], or by drawing positive
attention toward the training, for instance, by gamifying it.
Methods include having employees compete [41] or answer questions on
how to act in a certain situation [42]. Even further, a company can also
incentivize taking part in such competitions by rewarding the winners and
promoting the competition as a social event [43].

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
This thesis proposes to holistically address the entry barrier to cybersecu-
rity training by creating a freely accessible campaign of advanced offline
challenges that is pedagogically designed to teach, entertain and keep the
user engaged through a story. The challenges are implemented on the basis
of an existing cyber range platform, StratoCyberLab, introduced in Sec-
tion 2.1.1. Specifically, it counteracts the issues outlined in Chapter 1 as
follows:
• The continuous internet access requirement is reduced to installation
time. It is in general not necessary to have an internet con-
nection to solve the challenges. However, some challenges require
installation of additional tooling, and this will require an internet
connection.
Not all tools are pre-installed since a) providing only
the specific required tools could unintentionally lead the players to
the solution; b) pre-installing a larger variety of tools for one type of
challenge would demand more disk space.
• The challenges are available to play whenever desired, thereby solv-
ing the lack of time flexibility.
• More complex environments are simulated to help remediate the
lack of realism introduced by virtualization. Challenges often involve
multiple services and varied network topologies.
• The student takes the role of the main character in a story which all
the challenges tie into, thus increasing and maintaining interest in
completing all of them.
• Access to the challenges is free and they are open-source.
StratoCyberLab was chosen due to its open-source nature and the avail-
ability of support from the Stratosphere Laboratory at FEE CTU.
The educational aspect of the challenges is assessed using the NICE
Workforce Framework for Cybersecurity [6] is used. The NICE framework
describes itself as follows:

Gamification
Previous work on gamification unambiguously demonstrates that providing
an encompassing narrative story improves student appeal and interest in
the material [45][34]. In an academic context, this is especially important.
If there is a student on the verge of abandoning their education due to tem-
porary disinterest, they might look to secondary factors for the motivation
to stay engaged [46].
This thesis provides a motivational factor to stay engaged through the
desire to see the story unfold and the characters develop. Therefore, one of
the design goals of this thesis was to create this overarching storyline and
place the challenges in it to advance the story.

CHAPTER 6
M1r0 Campaign
This chapter describes the challenges and the steps a player can take in order
to solve each of them. The steps are proposed as an example solution but
it should be possible to approach the problem from a different perspective.
Each challenge contains at least one task, usually posing a question.
The answer to this question (the flag) advances the story.
6.1
Bearific
The goal of the first challenge is for the player to realize that an attacker is
trying to connect to the SSH port and lure the attacker into running their
tools and record their commands.
This first challenge is implemented using two containers. The first con-
tainer is the one the player accesses, The second container is an automated
“attacker” that repeatedly runs a script. This script attempts to connect
to the player container and perform several successive actions. The actions
probe whether the SSH server consistently and does not show telltale signs
of being a honeypot. The script is trying to find a SUID binary that can
be exploited further.
The player container has the Cowrie honeypot [47] pre-installed. The
player has to follow the attacker’s actions in order to find out their inten-
tions. The same solution as described in this section is also included with
the sources of the challenge as a pair of shell scripts, which, when executed,
automatically install, reconfigure, and extend a Cowrie instance to pass all
the attacker script’s checks.
6.1.1
Solution
Cowrie is pre-installed on the player container in /home/cowrie/cowrie. The
player has to start it and will begin to receive connections from the attacker.
First, the attacker tries several sets of credentials on the SSH server. If
they successfully authenticate using those, the player server is assumed to
be a honeypot. In that case, the attacker runs an echo command, containing

Copying Cowrie’s etc/userdb.example to etc/userdb.txt and removing

For all further steps, the following rule applies: when the reaction to an
attacker-performed action gives the impression of a honeypot, the attacker
will echo an informative message and disconnect.
After passing authentication, the attacker checks the machine’s host-

message and disconnect again (which is behavior for all the next
steps). To pass this check, the hostname can be very easily changed in
etc/cowrie.cfg4 by editing the hostname property in the [honeypot] block.
Next, the attacker pings an IPv4 address they expect to be inaccessi-

ping.py already contains a valid_ip method5 which can simply be modi-
fied to return False on its first line, passing this check. More robustly, a
check comparing the argument to the non-routable IP address can be im-
plemented, or even a check against all address blocks defined in RFC 5737,
which is where this specific address comes from [48].

6.1.2
Future Work
In order to improve the authenticity of the attacker’s behavior, several
improvements are proposed.
6.1.2.1
Honeypot Detection Evasion
Several previous works have discussed factors contributing to users flagging
systems as honeypots and how to avoid these detections [49][50]. Outcomes
of this research can be contributed into further checks in the attacker script,
testing the honeypot for common, identifiable traits – for instance, starting
by using the Cowrie_Detect script by Thomas Boscutti7.
6.1.2.2
Less Naive Attacker
The attacker script will retry connecting to the same machine and port after
5 seconds after each failed attempt. It is unlikely that a honeypot detector
would repeatedly contact an assumed honeypot in such a short timeframe.
Even though a part of this can obviously be subjectively adjusted for the
purposes of running in a simulated environment, the attacker could also
instead not try to connect to the same SSH server – instead of using the
same port, it might try to reach a higher port number instead, and every

6.2
Meme.jpeg
The second challenge places focus on basic steganography and cryptogra-
phy. The player starts with an image containing a message hidden using
two nested steganographic techniques. A repetition technique is applied to
ingrain this into the player’s memory [51] by chaining two similar levels of
steganography.
After extracting a character string, a cipher presents the final obstacle
in this challenge. For this purpose, the Vigenère cipher was chosen due to
its relation to the Caesar cipher, itself widely introduced very early in most
cryptography classes about ciphers due to its simplicity.
The encrypted string serves as the URL that the player should access in
the following challenge. The cipher, in its most common implementation,
only operates on alphabetic characters, which is perfect for this purpose
since the special characters :^/ separating the scheme from the hostname
(“repository”) remain unchanged and in position. This is in order to sim-
plify the solving process. The scheme is HTTP, as it is probably the best-
known four-character protocol/scheme occurring in URLs.
6.2.1
Solution
To fully immerse in the story, the player should use a web browser to dis-
play the webpage served from 172.20.0.205, as the challenge description
suggests.
To allow the host machine to access this host, a transparent
proxy script is included in the hackerlab container: /root/proxy.py. In this
scenario, this script allows using http:^/localhost:2223 in a web browser
on the host machine to access http:^/172.20.0.205:80 when run as python3
proxy.py 172.20.0.205 80.
Figure 6.1 shows what the rendered conversation looks like. The only
image on the page should then be downloaded. To allow appropriate tooling
to be installed, it should be placed in the hackerlab container, either by
downloading it there directly by copying its URL from the host browser
and using wget in the container, or by putting it in the mounted data
directory.
This image is in PNG format and is resistant to basic analysis tools
including strings, binwalk and zsteg. The player has to explore and exper-

Additional steganographic layers could be introduced, but only to a rea-

7.1
Extending SCL
Several extensions were made to StratoCyberLab in order to allow the cam-
paign to be implemented directly into the platform. These changes include
a partial refactor of both the dashboard API and frontend, extension of the
database schema and addition of new APIs.
7.1.1
Campaign Concept
In order to introduce a series of challenges linked together by a storyline, a
new entity type had to be introduced to StratoCyberLab: campaigns. A
campaign comprises campaign steps of two types: pages (static Markdown
documents) and challenges, which in turn again consist of one or more tasks.
A challenge can now thus be related to a campaign, allowing the primary
key to be composed of the combination of campaign_id and challenge_id,
potentially allowing challenges in different campaigns to have the same IDs.
To allow campaigns to unfold naturally and not prematurely disclose
key story points, the enforce_order campaign option had to be created. It
prevents players from seeing and solving challenges and tasks that they have
not unlocked yet by completing the preceding ones. When a campaign’s
tasks are listed, the completion of each task is checked, and once an unsolved
task is reached, it will be the last one to be returned to the front end.
This also affects challenges – if the last task of a preceding challenge is
not solved, the first task of the next challenge will not be visible either,
therefore stopping the entire next challenge from being displayed.
While this behavior is appropriate for the proposed storyline, it might
not always be desirable: other, less story-oriented, and perhaps more com-
petitive campaigns could foreshadow the next levels without allowing the
player to complete them. For this purpose, a new show_locked option was
introduced for the campaigns. Such campaigns do show all of their steps
and tasks, but the player cannot submit flags to solve them.

7.2
Bearific
The attacker is implemented as a simple Python script using the netmiko
Python library for high-level SSH communication [66]. It periodically tries
to connect to the defendant container’s SSH server on port 22.
When
an action is performed and evaluated to be a potential symptom of the
connected system being a honeypot, a message is printed using echo and
the connection is closed. The message consists of up to three parts:
1. First, the player should be informed about the attacker’s assumption
that the system is a honeypot; an example of this part can be “This
can’t be a real machine!”
2. Second, the unexpected situation is described, perhaps in a way with
a slight teasing inclination, such as “Who allows empty passwords?”.
3. Last, with a 50 percent probability, a mocking message like “Are you
even trying” is included, in order to motivate the player to try harder.
The attacker script then sleeps for five seconds and retries all the steps
until it either fails again or runs until the end. The steps will be described
in chronological order in this section.
First, authentication with these sets of credentials is attempted:
• root with no password,
• root:alpine – the default combination on Apple mobile devices,
• debian with no password,
• debian:debian,
• debian:1234 as an example of a too short password,
• debian:abcdef as as an example of a too weak password,
• debian:aaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaa as an example of what passwd dislikes due
to “too many similar characters”.
These combinations should not be present on any real public-facing
system in any case, since they are extremely easy to guess. A combination
==PAGE:58==
52
CHAPTER 7. IMPLEMENTATION
that is expected to work is debian:345gs5662d341, since this password is the
first one with suﬀicient entropy (62 bits).
After authenticating, the automated attacker attempts to determine
whether they are in a honeypot. The first stage is checking the hostname
using both the hostname and uname -a commands; Cowrie uses “svr04” as
the default.
The next check is to try using ping to reach an address in the TEST-
NET-3 address block as per RFC 5737, which states that network operators
“SHOULD add these address blocks to the list of non-routeable address
spaces” [48]. ICMP ping attempts to such addresses should therefore not
succeed and it is a viable choice for testing that an unreachable address will
indeed not be reached. Cowrie’s default implementation however does not
check for this and will report ICMP responses coming back from the tested
address 203.0.113.1, which the attacker interprets as a honeypot symptom.
Next, a filesystem consistency check is performed: a new directory called
test is created and a new file in it is written using echo and the > POSIX
redirection operator (echo "hello" > test/test.txt). The current direc-
tory is then recursively listed with ls -R and the test.txt file is expected
to be contained in the command’s output. If it is not, the system is consid-
ered to be a honeypot, as Cowrie’s implementation of ls does not support
the -R flag.
Now, the attacker attempts to perform privilege escalation by looking
for an executable with the setuid flag set: such executables preserve it’s
owner’s permissions when executed, no matter who executes them. A com-
mon way of searching the system for such executables is using the find
command: find / -perm -u=s -type f. The attacker first checks whether
this command is available using which find and then calls it, further lim-
iting the search results with -name *sh, as the target they’re looking for is
any shell interpreter (bash, dash, zsh). However, even though which reports
that find exists by default on Cowrie, calling it leads to a “command not
found” error.
After the attacker successfully runs find, if there are no matches for the
*sh filename, or the only match is chsh, the attacker disconnects, this time
without echoing any comments. The player must infer that the attacker
wants a shell binary to be returned so that they can proceed. Once a shell,
for instance bash, is returned, the attacker will try to pipe a script to it using
the | POSIX operator. The piped script will write a flag into /root/.flag.
It is not strictly necessary for the piped script to actually run, since the

to proceed with solving the challenge.

7.6.5.1
Varying Camera Feed
To make the camera feed more realistic, automated darkening based on
current time of day could be introduced.
To this extent, imagemagick
and https://github.com/gographics/imagick could be used. Unfortunately,

libraries written in pure Go, or at least not dependent on such large and
hard-to-obtain libraries.

7.6.5.4
Proper Isolation and Routing between Subnets
Docker’s internal option in the network block stops a default route from
being defined in the containers, but it also introduces firewall rules to drop
all traﬀic to or from other networks, which makes routing between networks
impossible even with a dedicated forwarding container connected to both
networks in question [85].
This is suboptimal since it makes the internal option unusable for the
purposes of building subnets interconnected through edge routers, and the
default behavior without this option allows any container to use its default
route to access the internet through the host. It is not clear how to prevent
this.
This challenge works around the issue by manually manipulating the
default routes on all desired containers and forcing them to route their
traﬀic through the designated routers, although there is no mechanism in
place preventing the player from simply changing the default route back to
the original one, setting the default gateway to the Docker-generated one,
allowing unrestricted access to the internet and circumventing the access
controls described in Section 7.6.1.

7.7
By the Pool
Two containers make up this challenge. First, a DNS server at 172.20.0.249,
powered by CoreDNS [86], serves to resolve the bread.forum domain name
retrieved in the last challenge to 172.20.0.250.
The second container
has this IP address and runs a hacking forum called Bread (referring to
Dread [87]), powered by phpBB – an open source bulletin board [88]. When
accessed without authenticating, no forum content is visible.
Credentials revealed in the preceding challenge match one of the user
accounts registered on this forum with the username Vigilante88. The fo-
rum uses optional two-factor authentication (2FA) and the user has a 2FA
key registered, necessitating the entry of a time-based one-time password
(TOTP) on every authentication attempt.
The 2FA implementation is
based on the phpbb_2fa extension by Paul Sohier [89], which by itself does
not have a blatant security weakness – it was therefore necessary to intro-
duce one.
To make the brute-force attack more likely to succeed in a reasonable
timeframe, both phpBB and the extension had to have their source code
modified. In the case of phpBB, the only necessary change was disabling the
form_token verification (which is a technique often employed as a measure
against cross-site request forgery: including a hidden one-time token in ev-
ery form and invalidating it upon the form’s submission) by simply making
it succeed every time. The extension added its own mechanism of the like,
8https://www.squid-cache.org/

also adding a hidden form field, this time called random. Verification of this
field’s value had to be completely removed from the extension.
Then, the core TOTP verification had to be modified, since by default,
the tokens are six digits long, which, at a conservative rate of 40 attempts
per second (cf. the author’s rates ranging between 40-60/s), would cause the
full exhaustion of the search space to take over three hours. The extension
calls another library9 to verify the OTP. The verification part of this library
was thus modified to a) truncate the OTP codes to four digits, b) accept
five codes as valid at a time10.
Last but not least, the string instructing the authenticating user to re-
trieve a TOTP from their authenticator app was extended to explicitly men-
tion the length of the password. These modifications significantly shorten
the time required to perform a successful brute-force attack.
When authenticated, the forum content becomes available: aside of

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This thesis delivers a complete campaign of advanced and realistic cyberse-
curity challenges. All of them can be played offline and at the student’s own
pace. A story connects them all together, increasing the engagement and
motivation to complete the entire campaign. Mappings to components of

8.1
Future Work
Due to time constraints, compromises had to be made in the depth of im-
plementation of most of the challenges. A multitude of suggestions on how
to them is available in the respective description sections – both in Section 6
for more conceptual improvements (such as introducing further steps the
player has to take) or in Sections 7.2 and further for additional implemen-
tation considerations (such as increasing the environments’ authenticity).
In this section, the author proposes several additional improvements to
non-challenge-related materials.
8.1.1
Evaluation
Gathering player feedback, analyzing their performance, and iterating the
challenge design is an unfulfilled learning objective of this thesis.
The test subjects should ideally come from different backgrounds and
possess varying prior knowledge in this area. Additionally, cybersecurity
students could be categorized by the grades they obtained from previously
passed cybersecurity courses.
Methods that could be used for this purpose correspond to methods
used in usability testing.
The first would be the think-aloud method, allowing the person gath-
ering the feedback to understand the mental processes behind the players’
actions by having them verbalize their thoughts and feelings at each step
they take [91], revealing the motivation behind these steps. This could allow
common pitfalls to either be purposefully eliminated or intentionally intro-
duced, so that players’ standard ways of thinking are challenged. However,
71
==PAGE:78==
72

this method can alter the players’ mental process, since it is unnatural to
comment on every task being performed.
Another selected approach is the cognitive walkthrough, where the
implementer notes down the successive tasks required to solve a challenge.
These tasks are then performed, checking for whether it is possible from a
player perspective to deduce the required next step based on information
available at the time. This can be performed without any players’ partic-
ipation, but it benefits from having these representative users give their
feedback to the proposed steps, describing how well they understood what
to do, how to do it correctly and evaluate the correctness of their action [92].
All testing must be done in accordance with ethics principles and protect
participants’ privacy by anonymizing their profiles as much as possible,
while still preserving key information about their pre-existing knowledge
profile.

8.1.2.1
UI and Sidebar Extensions
The StratoCyberLab user interface is at this point not very suitable for

To accommodate for the growing number of challenges, filtering tools
could be added to the left sidebar, for instance allowing the user to filter
displayed content by keywords, themes, completion or diﬀiculty.
8.1.2.1.1

Some of the challenges included in this campaign depend on being ac-
cessed with a web browser. For this purpose, a transparent proxy script was
added to the hackerlab, but it requires the player to run it in the shell them-
selves. A configuration option could be added to the SCL web interface,
where the target host and port could be configured in a web form, along
with toggling the proxy on and off. Furthermore, much like the SSH termi-
nal is a resizable frame in the web UI, another such frame could be added,
containing an iframe element accessing the proxied port and an address bar

implemented using a large width textfield. Alternatively, instead of intro-
ducing a new frame, the existing SSH frame could be split into switchable
tabs.
No matter how implemented, though, this solution has to be considered
from a usability perspective first – for example, switching back from the

8.1.2.2
Progress Persistence between SCL Reinstalls
Currently, all task solves are discarded when StratoCyberLab itself is shut
down using docker-compose down because the containers are destroyed. This
includes the dashboard server, which houses the SQLite database file con-
taining all the solve records. The reason for this behavior stems from the
ever-evolving nature of the database schema and the necessity to update
records. An alternative approach to simply recreating the database on every
reinstall or full relaunch is to use a Docker volume for the database,
mounted into the dashboard server container.
This, however, introduces the risk of schema incompatibilities. Of course,
that is by no means a novel issue and there exist industry-standard prac-
tices of working around the risk, perhaps the most prominent one being
database migrations: controlled sets of changes developed to modify the
structure of the objects within a relational database [93].
A simple way
of implementing migrations for a relational database is creating a direc-
tory with ordinally-named SQL scripts (such as 001-initial-structure.sql
and 002-add-synthetic-id-column.sql), incrementally editing the database
structure and potentially making necessary modifications to existing records.
The last executed migration can then be recorded in a special metadata ta-
ble and used as a version. In case a script with a higher number becomes
available during system initialization, it is executed.
8.1.2.3
Further Gamification
Achievements or progress badges are a popular feature in games. In the con-
text of SCL, an example achievement could be “Submit 10 flags”. In order
to be able to implement such functionality, though, challenge completion
progress shall first be persisted, as discussed in Section 8.1.2.2.

8.1.2.4
Challenge Data Persistence
When a challenge is stopped from the web interface of StratoCyberLab,
a docker-compose down command is executed in the challenge’s context,
which has the benefit of not leaving behind unnecessary infrastructure and
thus preserving system resources on the player’s machine. However, it also
carries the negative aspect of always discarding any data in the containers
when the player stops them, effectively disallowing the option of taking a
break from a challenge and stopping it before continuing.
One readily available solution is to use docker-compose stop rather than
down when the Stop button is clicked, but that creates a necessity for either
• another button which will indeed call the down command – for exam-
ple, it could be labeled “Discard”;
• a more full-fledged way, such as a dedicated page for managing what
challenges are downloaded, ready to start, and currently running –
much like Docker Desktop, but only including containers related to
SCL’s challenges, as well as also showing available challenges with no
currently existing containers.
The latter proposal is significantly more complex to implement, but the
author believes that it is more in line with the other goals proposed as part
of Section 8.1.
8.1.2.5
AI Assistant Integration
The AI assistant could be made more configurable, for example allowing
the user to choose other LLMs than Llama 3.1, or customizing the system
prompt. Additionally, running ollama in a Docker container causes it to
lose GPU acceleration support on Mac, which results in significantly slower
response times [94]. Unfortunately, no workaround is known at the time of
writing.
On a more challenge content-related note, the LLM integration could
also be extended to adjust the assistant’s personality to match the theme of
a challenge. This could especially help increase the authenticity of stories
by including the option for the player character to converse with other
characters from the story.

APPENDIX A
Use of Generative AI
Generative artificial intelligence, specifically large language models (LLMs),
were used in this thesis to facilitate creation of content lacking key impact
on the challenges’ implementation, underlying design or evaluation.
However, the author stringently believes that it is counterproductive to
use current generative tooling to manufacture text and resolve programming
issues. This opinion is based on the perceived importance of expanding
one’s skillset by experiencing work with no prior experience, benefitting
from gaining it. The use of such technology severely hinders this learning
process by replacing the problem resolution and critical thinking steps [95].
Therefore, LLMs were only used to create content with low overall im-
pact, especially where such material serves a distracting purpose. The ratio-
nale lies in improving the player experience by offering a more complete and
realistic-feeling environment aside from the choice that actually advances
the challenge or story completion, which was always manually created.
Furthermore, no part of the thesis text was generated.
LLMs
were employed to find grammar mistakes and inconsistencies in the text,
all resulting replacement formulations and content fixes are the author’s
own work, in accordance with the plagiarism rules as per the author’s study
program specification [96].
A.1
Used Models
Llama 3.1 [12] was attempted to be used for the content generation, but
since some of the topics are in a legally grey area (e.g., the hacker forum),
rejections started to occur and the model was discarded. Phi-3 [97] and
Gemma 2 [98] were both tested as replacements but both started with an
immediate rejection (see Appendices A.2.1 and A.2.2) and were therefore
not used.
Instead, NeuralDaredevil-8B-abliterated [99], it itself based on the same
Llama3.1:8b as previously used, was successfully tested and used for all
content generation, in the version quantized to 8 bits and available through
Ollama1. The content quality is high and there were no rejections.
