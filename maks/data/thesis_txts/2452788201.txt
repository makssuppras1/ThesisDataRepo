Summary (English)
Techniques for processing geometric data is a topic of great interest in the areas
of computer graphics, machine learning, optimisation, computer vision, and
more. One approach is through the lens of optimal transport theory, however,
traditional methods in this ﬁeld are ill-suited for high-dimensional data.
A
recently developed set of algorithms, taking inspiration from machine learning
techniques like regularisation, however, allows optimal transport theory to be
applied to practical problems that were previously intractable.
This thesis explores applications withing computer graphics and geometric data
analysis of these new algorithms, which allows computation of the optimal trans-
portation plan between two or more probability distributions. The thesis then
showcases how to apply this and related algorithms to some pracical problems
in the context of computer graphics and data analysis.
The result is a demonstration of new and existing tools for geometric data analy-
sis and computer graphics. It is shown how the optimal transport techniques can
be used as a tool for producing qualitatively interpretable analyses of geometri-
cally complex shapes, how a modiﬁcation of the existing methods can increase
stability and ﬁdelity when applied to 2d and 3d models, and it is shown how
new and existing algorithms in optimal transport theory can be used for shape
interpolation in 2d and 3d.
Overall the thesis provides a demonstration of how to apply recently developed
algorithms within optimal transport theory to a set of practical problems in
computer graphics that were intractable by traditional combinatorial methods
in optimal transport theory.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Given two probability distributions, one can imagine a mapping that transforms
one distribution into the other. The mapping that requires the least amount of
density to be moved the shortest distance, with respect to some given distance
measure, is called the optimal transportation plan or the coupling between the
distributions.
An initial idea one might have is to simply use the Euclidean distance measure
to compute the distance between the distributions, as if they were vectors. This
approach, however, does not take the geometry of the distributions into account,
and hence it is not well-suited as a distance measure to compute the coupling
between the distributions.
The Wasserstein distance, however, is designed with the exact objective of min-
imising the transportation of density between the distributions. Until recently,
however, the Wasserstein distance was very expensive to compute for large dis-
tributions, as one had to do a direct combinatorial search over the distributions.
Recent developments by J. Solomon et al. [SdGP+15][Sol15], however, has in-
troduced a handful of algorithms that can be used to compute useful approx-
imations of the Wasserstein distance, the coupling between two distributions,
and more.
The Wasserstein distance and its associated coupling have many potential ap-

1.2
Related Works
Optimal transport is a ﬁeld of active research with many diﬀerent algorithmic
approaches having been developed in recent years. A multiscale based approach
using Voronoi diagrams was presented by Mérigot in [Mér11]. A diﬀerent con-
temporary approach to optimal transport theory is presented by Lévy in [LS18].
Lévy’s method uses Laguerre diagrams on semi-discrete spaces.
The approach that eventually developed into modern transportation theory can
be traced to a french 1784 article by Monge[Mon84] who was concerned with the
most eﬃcient way to rearrange terrain for the purposes of landscaping. It was
==PAGE:13==
1.2 Related Works

reintroduced in a 1942 publication by L. V. Kantorovich[Kan42], which discusses
how to use linear programs to produce a mapping between two distributions. A
more contemporary review is found in Burkard’s 2009 book Assignment Prob-
lems [BDM09] and in [BÇ99], which discusses a range of diﬀerent combinatorial
techniques for solving the optimal transport problem. These approaches, how-
ever, are ill-suited for distributions with more than a few thousand entries, such
as large images with millions of pixels or greatly detailed 3d models.
The work of this thesis is closely related to the recent work by J. Solomon
et al.[SdGP+15][Sol15], and their development of new, eﬃcient algorithms for
solving the optimal transport problem, even for large distributions. Their new
approach is inspired by regularisation techniques from machine learning, and in
particular on techniques for estimating optimal transport distances developed
by M. Cuturi[Cut13]. These new approaches make heavy use of earlier works
by Sinkhorn and Knopp[SK67], which showed that a strictly positive square
matrix A can be turned into a doubly stochastic matrix1 D1AD2, and that
the diagonal matrices D1 and D2 can be computed using a simple iterative
algorithm. The Sinkhorn algorithm2 is central to the recently developed optimal
transport algorithms explored in this thesis.

Optimal Transport Theory on Geometric Distributions
We can adapt the theory of probability distributions to geometry by letting a
point p be expressed as a normalised Gaussian distribution around that point.
If we let the variance σ2 →0 the Gaussian becomes a delta function centered at
the point. Using a Gaussian representation has the advantage of producing non-
zero values throughout the entire space of the distribution, which is a necessity
for certain algorithms, but the delta function representation has the advantage
of being exact.
An advantage of the probabilistic approach to geometry is that several points
can be encoded into a single probability distribution as a (normalised) linear
combination, which then allows a single probability distribution to represent
complex geometric shapes.
This in turn means that methods from optimal
transport theory can be applied to geometry through their probabilistic repre-
sentations. Let M be a manifold. Elements in M expressed as a probability
distribution are then said to be elements in the space Prob(M).
One of the primary goals of optimal transport theory is to compute the op-
timal transport plan or coupling between two distributions µ0 and µ1. If we
consider a manifold M and let µ0, µ1 ∈Prob(M), then a transportation plan
is a probability distribution π ∈Prob(M × M). The transport plan π(U × V )
then describes how much mass to move from the region U ∈M to the region
V ∈M. Conservation of mass necessitates that π ∈Q(µ0, µ1) where
Y
(µ0, µ1) ≡{π ∈Prob(M × M )|π1 = µ0 ∧1π = µ1} ,
(2.3)
that is, the marginal distributions of π must reproduce the two probability
distributions with which the transportation plan is concerned, namely µ0 ∈
Prob(M) and µ1 ∈Prob(M). As a practical example, this might mean that if
µ0 and µ1 are vectors representing points on a 1d manifold, then π is a matrix
whose rows sum to µ0 and whose columns sum to µ1.
If µ0 and µ1 where
matrices, as in the case of a discretised probabilistic surface on a 2d manifold,
π would be a rank 3 tensor with the sum along the ﬁrst axis equalling µ1 and
the sum along the second axis equalling µ2. The transport plan π can thus
be thought of as a sort of conditional probability distribution consisting of the
marginal distributions µ0 and µ1.
The transportation plan that moves the least amount of mass the shortest pos-
sible distance (wrt. some distance over the manifold M) is said to be “optimal”.

two geometric probability distributions, and it provides a geometrically intuitive
distance for the space Prob(M), neither of which is a feature of the Euclidean

2.3
The Entropically Regularized 2-Wasserstein
Distance
The Wasserstein distance, outlined previously in section 2.1, can be computed
on discrete domains by solving a linear program. The underlying nature of this
approach, however, yields a time complexity that scales cubically with the size
of the domain, see [BDM09] or [BÇ99]. For this reason we consider instead the
entropically regularised or entropically smoothed Wasserstein distance, which
can be computed much more eﬃciently than the true Wasserstein distance. See
[Sol15] for a comprehensive introduction.

2.3.1
Some Perspective on W2 and W2,λ
It is important now to highlight some key diﬀerences between the smoothed
Wasserstein distance and the actual Wasserstein distance. Speciﬁcally, it is im-
portant to note that the smoothed Wasserstein distance can actually be negative
if the two distributions are very similar (or identical), and it only approximately
satisﬁes the triangle inequality. For these reasons it doesn’t qualify as a proper
distance, unlike the unregularised Wasserstein distance, which is a proper dis-
tance function, in the sense that is a positive mapping that satisﬁes the triangle
inequality.
It is also interesting to note that the numerical value of the Wasserstein distance
has an intuitive interpretation – it is the smallest total sum of “work” that would
have to be done to move each element in the distribution from one arrangement
to the other. Here “work” is a loose reference to the physical concept of work,
which is the product of the distance and force applied to move an object. So
the “work” w in the physical sense is w ∝dm where d is the distance and m is
the mass of the object. In the Wasserstein distance, the probability at a given
point is analogous to the mass of an object at that point in the physical analogy.
This is also the sense in which the discrete 1-Wasserstein distance is the "Earth
mover’s distance".
The numerical values of the smoothed Wasserstein distance is more diﬃcult to
interpret intuitively. It is the smallest diﬀerence between the “work” of moving
one distribution to another, and the entropy of the coupling pi that deﬁnes the
path of that movement, such that the entropy of the coupling is most similar to
a Gaussian distribution. It is easier to compute, as will be shown later, but it
is unfortunately not as numerically intuitive.

3.2
Parameter Sensitivity
A practical issue that kept occuring occur was that, after several iterations,
mass would "seep out" and disappear, causing the sum of the elements of the
coupling π to be less than one. The coupling elements should sum to 1 since
the coupling is a distribution (see section 2.1). The Sinkhorn algorithm turned
out to be very parameter sensitive due to the iterative Gaussian ﬁlter, which
quickly produced very small or very large values far away from the points on
M which produced the gaussians. The results were division by 0 errors. The
problem was further increased by the fact that some of the mass might be lost
if the kernel size of the ﬁlter was too small, as even the small values that are far
away from the points on M might contain mass that ought to ﬂow to a point
near the center of the kernel.

Parameterisation
In this chapter we introduce a method for parameterising simple and complex
shapes, using the entropically smoothed Wasserstein distance, in a way that can
be qualitatively understood and easily visualised.
The method works by computing the entropically smoothed Wasserstein dis-
tance between the geometric distributions of interest, and then using the mul-
tidimensional scaling (MDS) technique to create an n-dimensional parameteri-
sation. For visualisation purposes, a 2 or 3 dimensional MDS is advantageous,
but for other purposes a higher dimensional parameterisation might be advan-
tageous.
The chapter starts oﬀwith a brief review of the multidimensional scaling tech-
nique based on section 14.8 in [HTF09], before describing our Wasserstein pa-
rameterisation technique in greater detail.

4.1
Multidimensional Scaling
Multidimensional scaling (MDS) is a technique for visualising or parameterising
a set of observations xi to a k-dimensional space, based only on the distances dij
between each observation. The parameterisation is done such that the distance
between observations xi and xj in the parameterised space are kept as close to
dij as possible.[HTF09]
It diﬀers from other dimensionality reduction techniques, such as principle com-
ponent analysis, in that it is computed solely based on the distance (or similar-
ity) matrix composed of dij (or sij for variations using a similarity matrix), and
not on the data matrix composed of xi. This makes it ideal for circumstances
where the only known attribute of an entity is its distances to other entities, or
in cases where a certain distance function is of particular interest.
More technically, the MDS technique seeks to ﬁnd the coordinates zi ∈Rn for
i = 1, . . . , n, such that they minimise a stress function which is a measure of how
diﬀerent the distances in the new space are when compared to the given distances
dij. The stress function comes in several varieties catagorized as metric or non-
metric, where metric MDS methods assumes that the distances are Euclidean
and hence that there exists a conﬁguration z1, . . . , zn in some k-dimensional
space where the stress function could be 0. A commonly used stress function in
such cases is the least-squares stress function,

In order to investigate whether MDS is a viable tool for producing a param-

known 2-dimensional conﬁguration based on Wasserstein distances. A conﬁgu-

barycenter interpolation technique outlined in section 2.6 and demonstrated in

method, create intermediate shapes based on a sum of weighted Wasserstein dis-
tances. Hence one would expect that, for n ≤3, MDS ought to be able to ﬁnd a
2-dimensional parameterisation that preserves the ordinal rank and qualitative
visual appearance of the barycenters.
The exact method is as follows:
1. Produce three diﬀerent 64 × 64 × 64 geometric probability distributions.
2. Compute a number of 3-way barycenters between the models using the
iterative Bregman algorithm, see section 2.6.
3. Compute the distance matrix of all the input models D with entries dij

2.20), computed by the Convolutional Wasserstein algorithm (see section
2.5).
4. Post-process the convolutional Wasserstein distance matrix to make it a
resemble proper distance matrix suitable for the MDS technique, i.e. the
distance matrix should be symmetric and dii = 0∀i.

Wasserstein Parameterisation of Cochleae
In this section it is shown how the Wasserstein parameterisation technique out-
lined above can be used as a technique for parameterising the geometric proper-
ties of complex geometric shapes in 3d. The data chosen is that of 3d meshes of
cochleae, a peculiarly shaped part of the inner ear involved in hearing. Cochlea
are interesting in the context of Wasserstein parameterisation, as they’re both
topologically complex and can be topologically diﬀerent. The Wasserstein pa-
rameterisation method should work well even in these circumstances, and so
the cochlea ought to make a good test for the Wasserstein parameterisation
technique.

4.3.1
Preprocessing & Methodology
The dataset consists of 18 cochlea meshes in .obj format. In order to compute
the regularised 2-Wasserstein distance using the convolutional Wasserstein al-
gorithm, the meshes ﬁrst need to be preprocessed into geometric distributions.
In order to do this the distance ﬁeld of the mesh is computed, and the distance
ﬁeld is then converted to a 64 × 64 × 64 grid representation by iterating over
the mesh and querying the distance ﬁeld. For each entry inside the mesh, its
location in the discrete distribution is saved in a .txt ﬁle which was then loaded
into a 3-dimensional matlab array. The distance ﬁeld can be computed accord-
ing to techniques outlined in [ærentzen2002]. We used the PyGEL library[ea]
to compute the signed distance ﬁeld.
The voxelised representation is then smoothed to adhere to the strict positivity
requirement of the Sinkhorn algorithm, as discussed in earlier chapters. The
smoothing was done with a Gaussian kernel with σ = 1. The preprocessing for
one cochlea is shown in ﬁgure 4.3, and all the pre-processed cochleae are shown
in ﬁgure 4.4 and 4.5. After smoothing the distributions were normalised.
The models were all voxelised to a 64×64×64 grid as mentioned, but a 128×128×
128 grid was also brieﬂy investigated and, although it increases the visual ﬁdelity,
it doesn’t seem to aﬀect the Wasserstein Parameterisation of the cochleae. For
this reason a 64 × 64 × 64 grid was deemed suﬃcient for further analysis,2 and,
as will be shown in chapter 5, a much sharper visual ﬁdelity can be achieved
by using a diﬀerent preprocessing method, making a higher resolution even less
interesting.
The method for parameterising the geometric distributions of the models follows
steps 3-5 of the method described in section 4.2, which is brieﬂy summarised
here again:
1. Compute the distance matrix D such that entry (i, j) equals the smoothed
Wasserstein distance between cochlea i and j.
2. Post-process the distance matrix D to ensure the diagonal is 0 and D is
symmetric.
3. Perform non-metric MDS on the distance matrix D, using for example
MATLAB’s library method mdscale().
2A smaller grid of course has the advantage of being faster to compute on, and hence faster
to perform analysis on.

Exponential Geometric
Distributions
A continued problem with the convolutional Wasserstein algorithm, as described
in several previous sections, (see for example section 3.3.2) is its need for strictly
positive distributions and the associated the ﬁne-tuning of the Gaussian prepro-
cessing parameters in order to avoid breakdown due to overﬂow errors resulting
from repeatedly dividing large numbers by near-zero numbers. The Gaussian
preprocessing also brings a natural loss of sharpness of the image quality, which
is why one desires a lower variance. In this chapter a diﬀerent preprocessing
method for mitigating both the overﬂow issues and the loss of sharpness is de-
veloped.
In order to avoid this, a diﬀerent preprocessing method for converting a geomet-
ric shape to a probability distribution is introduced. Instead of simply using the
mesh to compute a binary voxelisation, the idea behind the new method is to
apply an exponential function to the negative signed distance ﬁeld of the mesh
at each grid point, i.e. to assign a value P(x)
P(x) = exp(−d(x)),
(5.1)
to each point x in the discrete distribution based on its signed distance d(x) to
the mesh.

5.4
Discussion
Using the clamped exponential of the signed distance ﬁeld of the mesh as the
initial grid values, rather than a smoothed voxel grid, provides a natural way
to achieve a sharper geometric distribution corresponding to the original model
by simply rendering the grid as an isosurface with isovalue 1.
It has also been shown to produce similar results when using the resulting
Wasserstein distances for multidimensional scaling, as the Gaussian prepro-
cessed models from chapter 4. The asymmetrically clamped exponential pre-
processing method actually produced an even clearer relationship to the surface
area than the corresponding analysis using Gaussian preprocessing.
Additionally the asymmetric method has been shown to be signiﬁcantly more
stable than the symmetric method. This is because a lower α will generally
allow more iterations before numerical issues can occur, and the asymmetric
method was shown to produce a smaller mass loss at lower values of α.
5.4.1
Further Work
More work remains to be done on the preprocessing of geometry into probability
distributions. One avenue would be to see how the exponential preprocessing
method compares to the Gaussian method with a wider variety of models. An-
other obvious next step would be to see how many Sinkhorn iterations each of
the preprocessing methods allow for, as a function of the parameters α and σ2.
It would also be interesting to see how the exponential preprocessing method
aﬀects Wasserstein barycenters. Would it maintain the isosurface at value 1, or
could the algorithm be modiﬁed such that it does? It would also be interesting to
investigate why the asymmetrically clamped exponential preprocessing method
produces such a strong correlation to surface area when performing Wasserstein
parameterisation. Is it coincidental, or does this preprocessing method somehow
pronounce the surface area geometry as expressed in the Wasserstein distance?

Shape Interpolation
Shape interpolation is a problem of wide interest in both real-time graphics
and rendering due its many practical applications. As a result it is an active
ﬁeld of study with many diﬀerent approaches, see e.g. [VTSSH15], [BdSP09]
or [CPSS10]. In chapter 2.6 it was shown how shapes could be interpolated
by solving the Wasserstein barycenter problem, i.e.
ﬁnding the distribution
that minimizes a weighted sum of distances to the given distributions. In this
chapter a diﬀerent approach to computing an interpolation between two distri-
butions is introduced. The new approach stochastically samples the coupling
π between the two distributions to compute a direct interpolation between the
two distributions.

6.3
Discussion
Using the Wasserstein coupling π of two particle distributions, A and B, as a
probability distribution for stochastically sampling the destination in B of each
particle in distribution A provides an alternative interpolation technique to that
of the Wasserstein barycenter method. An advantage of the stochastic sampling
method introduced in this section is that it allows a much more discrete inter-
polation, even down to interpolating voxel distributions, whereas the barycenter
method only works for smoothed distributions.
The stochastic multisampling method also works for producing shape interpola-
tions between smooth shapes, and in fact it allows more control in this case too,
since one always has control over how each particle is visualised. This is not the
case for the barycenter method, where the beginning and ending points might
not correpond entirely to the models given, but will be additionally smoothed.
The stochastic sampling method is thus a more versatile tool than the barycenter
interpolation method.
An advantage of the barycenter method is its ability to create multiway-way
interpolations, as shown in section 3.4, which is not possible using the stochastic
multisampling method introduced in this chapter.
A topic of future reasearch could be to apply a particle ﬂow technique like the
one introduced in the beginning of the chapter to the vertices of two meshes in
order to produce a more direct interpolation between the meshes, perhaps by
mapping the vertices of each individual polygon to the most likely polygon in
the other mesh based on the optimal transport plan π.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Optimal transportation has many potential applications in computer graphics
and gemetric data analysis, but until recently optimal transportation techniques
for larger data sets were simply unavailable. In this thesis a recently developed
set of techniques by Solomon et al. has been implemented and new applications
have been explored. These new algorithms include eﬃcient computation of the
entropically regularised Wasserstein distance, and its associated coupling. An
algorithm for computing the barycenters between distributions, such that the
barycenter miminizes a linear combination of Wasserstein distances to the given
distributions was also implemented.
Amongst the new techniques introduced in this thesis was a tool for analysing
geometric data. The method uses multidimensional scaling in combination with
the Wasserstein distance to produce a qualitatively interpretable analysis of geo-
metric data. The method was shown to work for a synthetic data set generated
by computing the 3-way barycenters between 3 test distributions, and it was
then shown to work for a realistic data set of cochleae, a geometrically complex
structure.
A new preprocessing technique was introduced which allows a much sharper
image quality to be retained during the discretisation preprocessing. The new
technique solved the strict-positiveness requirement of the Sinkohorn algorithm
by by letting each entry in the grid by assigned based on the exponential of
==PAGE:86==
76
Conclusion
the signed distance, as opposed to simply smoothing a voxelised version of the
model with a Gaussian kernel. A few variations on the exponential preprocess-
ing technique were explored, and overall the technique was shown to enhance
the visual quality without losing the geometric properties and relationships be-
tween the models. The technique was also shown to be a more stable technique
for computing the Wasserstein distance, compared to the standard Gaussian
preprocessing technique.
In the ﬁnal chapter of the report, a new method for interpolating shapes was
introduced. The new method works by stochastically sampling the coupling at
points in the origin distribution, and then interpolating each sample along a
geodesic towards their target in the target distribution. The result is a smooth
interpolation between two shapes. The new stochastic method was shown to
have certain advantages and disadvantes compared to the barycenter method
introduced by Solomon et al. in [SdGP+15]. The new stochastic method allows
for greater control of the visualisation, as it is based on a particle ﬂow between
the two distributions. As such, the new method can produce much sharper and
more accurate interpolations than the barycenter method. The advantage of the
barycenter method is that it naturally allows interpolation between any number
of distributions.
Further explorations might include an extension of the stochastic multisampling
method to perform n-way interpolations, as opposed to only 2-way interpola-
tions. Additional work might also reveal how the parameter sensitivity of the
Sinkhorn algorithm can be further addressed, such as by only iterating over a
limited domain in certain situations. Another direction would be to explore
how the new methods from optimal transport theory can be combined with
techniques from statistical data analysis or unsupervised machine learning to
produce new methods for geometric data analysis. Algorithms for the computa-
tion of unregularised transportation plans would be a natural (and challenging)
pursuit for future developments. One could of course also imagine diﬀerent ap-
plications of the existing regularised techniques, such as image morphing, motion
blur, and more. The recent developments in optimal transport techniques leave
a wide range of unexplored applications.
Overall this thesis showcased a series of examples of how recent developments
allow optimal transport theory to be used as a practical tool for modelling
geometric problems in computer graphics. Optimal transport theory still has
limitations, and those are worth addressing. It remains diﬃcult to solve the op-
timal transport problem without resorting to regularisation techniques, and reg-
ularised optimal transport techniques are, due to their reliance on the Sinkhorn
algorithm, very parameter sensitive. Nevertheless, optimal transport theory has
been shown to be produce a viable and robust approach to problem solving in
computer graphics and geometric data processing.
