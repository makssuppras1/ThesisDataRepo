Abstract
This Master Thesis examines the use of Novel Sulfatase-cleavable Linker for Antibody-Drug Conju-
gates, in this case for a drug targeting breast cancer. The thesis is divided into two main parts, where
the ﬁrst part covers an investigation of ADCs, aryl sulfutase cleavable linker, and the synthesis of
organosulfates. The second part investigates the synthesis of the targeted aryl sulfutase linker.
Cancer is a common disease and is often associated with death, as it is not always treatable and the
current treatment have several different side effects. The development of more selective treatments
are therefore desired. ADCs offers a more selective release of anti-cancer drugs on tumor cells, and
avoid damaging normal healthy cells, which results in fewer side effects and a better treatment. The
cytotoxic drug used in ADCs would have to be very toxic in order to kill the tumor cells, as only a
few molecules will be released in the cell. The aryl sulfatase cleavable linker contribute with higher
plasma stability and more hydrophilicity, than other cleavable linkers, which should provide a more
selective payload release and relieve aggregation of the Ab upon conjugation.
The starting material, 2-hydroxy-2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)acetic acid, was used as a core structure to syn-
thesize the targeted aryl sulfutase linker 1. The synthetis of the linker was carried out with good yields
in the ﬁrst part of the synthetic pathway. The coupling of the drug and linker was identiﬁed, as the
key intermediate step, and should be pursued with coupling a spacer to the linker before the drug is
attached.

Resumé
Denne Master Afhandling omhandler brugen af nu sulfutase kløvbar linker til Antistof-medicin kon-
jugater, i dette tilfælde for et lægemiddel mod brystkræft. Afhandlingen er opdelt i to hoveddele, hvor
første del dækker en undersøgelse af ADCs, aryl sulfat kløvbare linker og syntesen af organosulfater.
Den anden del omhandler syntesen af den primære aryl sulfutase linker.
Kræft er en almindelig sygdom og er ofte forbundet med døden, da den ikke altid kan behandles,
og de nuværende behandlingsmetoder har ﬂere forskellige bivirkninger. Udviklingen af mere selek-
tive behandlingsformer er derfor ønsket. ADCs tilbyder en mere selektiv frigivelse af kræftlægemidlet
til en kræftcelle, og undgår at skade normale raske celler, hvilket resulterer i færre sideeffekter og en
bedre behandling. Det cytotoksiske lægemiddel der bruges i ADCs skal være meget giftigt for at kunne
dræbe kræftcellen, da det kun er få molekyler der bliver frigivet i cellen. En aryl sulfutase kløvbar linker
bidrager med højere plasma stabilitet og er mere hydroﬁl, end andre kløvbare linkere, hvilket burde
give en mere selektiv kræftladning frigivelse og lindre aggregering af antistof ved konjugering.

i den første del af syntese ruten. Koblingen af lægemidlet og linkeren var identiﬁceret, som det vigtig-
ste mellemtrin, og bør udføres med kobling af afstandsstykket til linkeren før lægemidlet sættes på.

Introduction
Cancer is a group of diseases that are characterized by an uncontrolled growth of abnormal cells and
can occur in many parts of the body. The diseases occurs in many stages, depending on the size of the
cancer and how much is has spread. The cancer is most commonly labeled stage I to stage iV, with iV
being the most serious and often life-threatening. The treatment of cancer depends on both the type,
location and grade of the tumor as well as the stage of cancer.
Current treatment methods to combat cancer include, surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy.
Surgery involves removal of the tumor or the entire organ. Surgery can cure the patient, if the cancer
has not spread to other parts of the body. Surgery is usually used along with other treatments for in-
stance chemotherapy.
Furthermore, radiation therapy can be used to kill cancer cells by ionizing radiation, either externally,
or internally, to the area being treated. Radiation therapy damages DNA (the genetic material) in the
cells at the treated area, making it impossible for them to grow and divide. The aim is only to injure
and kill cancer cells; nevertheless, normal cells in the treated area get effected as well. Fortunately, the
normal cells usually recovers, whereas cancer cells cannot repair themselves. The treatment works
best in early staged cancer as more localized radiation can be used, limiting damaging the normal
healthy cells. [1]
Chemotherapy is a way to treat cancer by using drugs to kill the mutated cells. Usually chemotherapy
is cytotoxic drugs, which affect rapidly dividing cells in general. The cytotoxic drugs interfere with the
cell division (mytosis) and causes cell death from changes in the cytoskeletal architecture of cells. Due
to the effect on mitosis, the cytotoxic drugs are effective against cancer cells, which are fast dividing
cells. However, other rapidly dividing cells can also be killed, such as the cells lining the intestines,
cells in hair follicles, and myeloid cells, which leads to some of the side-effects of chemotherapy. Typ-
ical chemotherapy results in nausea, hair loss, and myelosuppression (bone marrow suppression). [2]
For most cancer drugs to have a therapeutic effect, they are used in a concentration close to their
maximum tolerated dose, which is the highest dose of a drug that does not cause unacceptable side
effects. The limiting factor of anticancer drugs could be the small available therapeutic window with
an inability to kill a sufﬁcient number of cancer cells without being toxic to the patient. To get a larger
therapeutic window of the anticancer drugs, either the potency, or the selectivity can be improved.
These properties will lower the minimum effective dose and increase the maximum tolerated dose,
respectively. Ideally, both properties would be achieved, resulting in an overall larger therapeutic in-
dex. [2]
The current cancer treatments have a low therapeutic windows, resulting in several side-effects. This
could be achieved by making them more selective towards cancer cells, compared to normal cells. In
this ﬁeld of research, antibody-drug conjugates have shown high potential to kill cancer cells without
severe side-effects. By utilizing the antibody selectivity and release of the cytotoxic drug only inside
the cancer cell, it is possible to avoid killing of normal cells.

Antibody drug conjugates
Antibody–Drug Conjugates (ADCs) are a rapidly growing class of oncology therapeutics. The ﬁrst
ADCs were approved in 2011 and 2013, which paved the way for further development in the ﬁeld.
Currently four approved ADC medicines are on the market and over 50 are in clinical trials. ADCs
consist of three parts: a monoclonal antibody (mAb), which is connected via a cleavable linker to a
cytotoxic molecule, as seen in Figure 1. Each part will affect efﬁcacy and toxicity and will be further
discussed in the following sections. [3]
Figure 1: Antibody drug conjugates (ADCs) consist of three parts: a monoclonal antibody, cytotoxic
agent (the ’drug’, which is referred to as the payload) and a structural moiety that joins the two together
called the ’linker’.
2.1
Mechanism
The overall mechanism of an ADC can be described in a few steps. First step is the binding between
the antibody and a cell-speciﬁc antigen on the target cells surface. This result in internalization of the
antibody-antigen by endocytosis, followed by cleavage of the linker in lysosomal processing to release
the cytotoxic payload (the drug). Importantly, the drug is only active after the release, where it binds
intracellularly on the target protein, resulting in its cell-killing function, as seen in ﬁgure Figure 2.
Often very toxic drugs are used, making the unconjugated drug too toxic to be administered alone in
the body. Therefore, the speciﬁcity of the antibody and a selective release in the tumor cell are of high
importance. The high speciﬁcity of ADCs, and speciﬁc delivery of cytotoxic agents to the tumor cells,
mean that in theory there is no dose-limiting toxicity of chemotherapy, as seen with non-targeted
chemotherapy, as a result of its effects on normal cells.
In some cases, the payload may be sufﬁciently membrane-permeable to diffuse out of the cell and
into nearby cells, resulting in killing of bystander tumour cells. By this ‘bystander effect’ is described
as the potency of therapeutics delivery system in killing neighboring cells independently of targeted
therapy assignment. By this ‘bystander effect’, also non-antigen expressing cancer cells in a tumor
can also be targeted. However, a high bystander activity can also result on greater side effects, as the
cytotoxic payload can also diffuse into nearby healthy cells. [3]

2.2
Monoclonal antibody
Antibodies are a proteins the body is making to attack foreign substances, as a part of the immune sys-
tem. The antibody sticks to another a speciﬁc protein on the surface of the foreign substance, called
an antigen. The antibodies circulate in the body until the speciﬁc antigen target is found. Antibodies
usually attach to a cell to ﬂag it, make it easy to detect for the immune system and target it for de-
struction. Antibodies consist of a variable and constant region, as seen in Figure 3, where the variable
region is the antigen-binding site.
Figure 3: Structure of an antibody molecule. [4, p. 129]

Nowadays, antibodies can be designed in the laboratory to target a speciﬁc antigen found on cancer
cells. Speciﬁc made antibodies are known as monoclonal antibodies (mAbs). Monoclonal antibodies
can either be used to treat cancer, as naked, or conjugated antibodies, the so-called ADCs. Naked
mAbs are antibodies without any drug attached. Usually they work by boosting the patient’s own im-
mune system against the cancer cells. [5]
The beneﬁts of using ADCs is to combine modern mAb therapies with small molecular weight cy-
totoxins. Monoclonal antibodies have high selectivity in targeting cancer cells, by recognising and
binding to cell-surface antigens. The mAbs are looking for antigens that are overexpressed in certain
cancerous indications, and therefore not targeting normal cells, which lead to limited side-effects of
the cytotoxic drugs. Monoclonal antibodies have long plasma half-lives (t1/2 = weeks), but limited cy-
totoxicity. The goal of using ADCs is to create a highly cytotoxic and selective therapeutics with long
plasma half-lives. [3]
A lot of mAbs have been evaluated clinically, but only two of them are cell-surface targeting. These
two targets are HER2 and EGFR. A current way to target the HER2 antigen, is the FDA approved IgG
mAb trastuzumab and the Ab pertuzumab. Trastuzumab is an effective treatment with overexpression
of HER2, and Pertuzumab can be used in combination with Trastuzumab, as Pertuzumab inhibits
dimerisation of HER2 and HER3 receptors. Trastuzumab mechanism is not clearly deﬁned, but it is
expected to involve extracellular mechanisms of antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC),
and intracellular mechanisms of apoptosis. Furthermore, induced chemotherapy will damage the
DNA repair. [6]
2.3
Cytotoxic agent
Cytotoxic agents in ADCs need to meet some criterias, in order to be an effective treatment against
cancer. The cytotoxic agent would need to be highly super-toxic, low immunogenic, and amenable to
modiﬁcation. The cytotoxic payload needs to be stable in the blood circulation, to avoid any conver-
sion into an unwanted drug that might cause side effects. The solubility of the cytotoxic agent is not
necessarily essential, but an important factor in ADCs.
Since antibodies are proteins, the attachment of the cytotoxic agent should be performed in aqueous
solutions with minimal organic solvents. A very hydrophobic cytotoxic agent can cause aggregation of
the antibody in storage, or even during conjugation. Cytotoxic agents with higher hydrophilicity ease
the cell membrane permeability and is an advantage for the bystander effect. However, it is prefer-
able if the cytotoxic agent get released from the ADC, as a hydrophobic metabolite, due to better blood
clearance. This can probably limit any possible side-effects, as it is reported that around 95-99% of
ADCs are metabolized before binding to the targeted tumor cell. [7]
Since the ADC only carry limited cytotoxic agents, it is important to have a highly toxic drug, in or-
der to be able to kill the tumor cells with a minimal payload delivery. Most of the antigens on tumor
cells have low expression and poor internalizing activity, which lead to a minimal delivery of the ADCs
to the targeted tumor cells. Each tumor cell approximately uptake ADCs with a rate less than 0.003-
0.08% of injected dose per gram. Therefore, if the ADCs need to have a therapeutic effect, it is very
important that the cytotoxicity of the drug is very high.

Highly cytotoxic payloads can origin from plants, animals, or microorganisms. The most important
thing to notice here, is ﬁnding a cytotoxic payload with an insigniﬁcant immunogenic potential in the
body. However, cytotoxic payloads in new generation ADCs will most likely be chemical synthesized
anti-cancer drugs. When circulating in the blood, these are shown to be less immunogenic than gly-
col/peptide cytotoxic agents. [7]
2.3.1
Cytotoxic agents used in ADCs
Different drugs have been used against cancer, among them are doxorubicin and methotrexate. Both
doxorubicin and methotrexate works by inhibiting the DNA synthesis, and cause cell death for the
tumors. Doxorubicin interacts with DNA by intercalation, which thereby inhibit the DNA helix strands
to be cut by topoisomerase II and avoid transcription. This prevents the tumors replication. [8]
Methotrexate interfere with DNA synthesis by inhibiting folic acid, which is a vitamin in the body that
is used to manufacture new cells and produce DNA. Since folic acid stimulates cancer cell growth,
inhibition of the vitamin can prevent tumor growth and cause cell death for fast dividing cells. Dox-
orubicin and methotrexate were tested in some of the ﬁrst ADCs, but in vitro studies showed less
activity and lower potency. The decrease in potency, were quite signiﬁcant for the conjugated ADC
released drug, compared to the unconjugated drugs. Although they are both good anti-cancer drugs,
they should not be considered as cytotoxic payloads in ADCs. [2]
Instead, other drugs have proven to be effective in ACDs, including auristatins, maytansinoids and
calicheamicins. Common for all of them is the unusually high cytotoxic activity, which makes them

2.5
Linker
The linker is an important part of ADCs. It not only covalent attach the monoclonal antibody to the
small cytotoxic molecule, but also have a high impact on the efﬁcacy and toxicity of the resulting ADC.
There are three important properties for a linker to possess, in order to achieve ADCs that are selective
and potent:
1. High stability in circulation.
2. High water solubility to aid bioconjugation and avoid formation of inactive ADC aggregates.
3. Allow efﬁcient release of a highly cytotoxic payload-linker metabolite. [3]
The importance of high stability in circulation is to avoid premature release of the drug during cir-
culation, which would lead to toxicity and a lower therapeutic index. Therefore, the linker needs to
have good stability for several days in the blood stream and efﬁcient release of the cytotoxic molecule
when taken up in the target cell. [2]
The linkers used in ADCs would typically be classiﬁed as ’cleavable’ and ’non-cleavable’ linkers de-
pending on the way they are cleaved inside the tumor cells cytoplasm. Cleavable linkers are designed
to have a quick release of the unmodiﬁed drug immediately after ADC internalization in the cytosol.
The release is usually triggered by protease reactions, glutathione reduction, or acidic pH environ-
ment. On the other hand non-cleavable linkers are stable late in the endosome transition and the
cytotoxic payload is released as the Ab gets metabolic degraded in the lysosomes. This results in re-
lease of the cytotoxic payload with a terminal amino acid from the degraded mAb attached. [7]

2.5.1
Non-cleavable
Non-cleavable linkers do not have a chemical trigger to cleave the linker, so the cytotoxic payload is
released as the mAb is metabolised by proteolytic cleavage. Therefore, a peptide is still attached to
the drug, which is a drastic modiﬁcation to the payload. It can change the key pharmacophore of the
payload, and the payload does not have the ’bystander effect’ as the charged peptide appendage. Of
these reasons, non-cleavable linkers are not used so often anymore. [3]

Acid cleavable linkers should balance good stability in the circulation at pH 7.4, while being cleaved

other approaches, which can afford higher tumour selectivity.

2.5.5
Enzyme-cleavable linkers
Although success with some chemically cleavable linkers, as just described, they still have some limi-
tations with premature release. This has changed focus to enzyme-cleavable linkers. Current renzyme-
cleavable linkers are usually made as dipeptide-containing linkers, cleaved by proteases.
The mechanism of enzyme-cleavable linkers starts with the transport of ADCs to the lysosome of a
cell with high concentrations of unique hydrolytic enzymes, which selectively can cleave the linker
intracellularly. For the payload to be released extracellular, the upregulated enzymes in the tumors
microenvironment should be targeted. [3]
Enzyme-cleavable linkers would be preferred over chemically cleavable linkers, due to a better stabil-
ity in human plasma and more selectively release of the payload at the targeted cells, and not during
circulation. Some of the disadvantages and limitations in using dipeptidic linkers are their instability
in rodent blood, as the Ces1C hydrolase enzyme present in rodent plasma perform a hydrolysis on
the linker and release the payload. This is a problem for the preclinical and safety studies accuracy of
the linkers.
The only enzyme-cleavable linkers explored in clinic today are protease cleavable peptides, with the
most widely used linkers contain Val-Cit or Val-Ala sequences, as they have shown high stability in
human plasma and efﬁcient payload release in the target cells. The linkers were developed to be
cleaved lysosomal cathepsin proteases, which is a cysteine protease upregulated in different cancers
tissue. However, research indicates a lack of speciﬁcity towards cathepsin B, as they are also cleaved
by other proteases. Furthermore, the dipeptidic linkers are hydrophobic, which hinder bioconjuga-
tion of lipophilic payloads. This can cause aggregation of the antibody. [10]
Therefore, it could be important to develop an enzyme-cleavable linker with better properties. This
include a linker with a better hydrophilic proﬁle as well as higher selectivity towards the cancer cell.
Ideally, the linker should be cleaved by hydrolytic enzymes present only in the lysosome of a cell, and
target enzymes that are upregulated in the tumors microenvironment. Furthermore, current link-
ers in clinical research are limited to amino functionalized drugs, whereas methods used to release
alcohol-tethered drugs are limited. This could therefore be interesting to investigate further.

Aryl Sulfatase cleavable linker
Enzyme-cleavable ADC linkers need to have high stability in circulatory conditions, but easily altered
and release the payload, when the target cell uptake the linker to the lysosome. Aryl sulfatase cleav-
able linker can provide selectively payload release in the target cell, because of the high activity inside
the lysosomes and low activity in human and rodent plasma. This is due to some of the different
sulfatases present inside the lysosome in high concentrations, which are catalysing the hydrolysis of
alkylsulfate esters to alcohols. Sulfamidases are used in the step-wise breakdown of large molecules,
such as a linker. Even though, the sulfatases in the cell are selective towards their natural substrates,
they still show activity for arylsulfatase. [10]
Previously some of the drawbacks from dipeptide linkers were mentioned, as lack of stability in ro-
dent blood and hydrophobic, which give problems in testing and aggregation of the Ab. Here studies
of aryl sulfatase linkers show high solubility and stability in both human and mouse plasma. Further-
more, high hydrophilicity should relieve the aggregation of the Ab upon conjugation. The beneﬁts
aryl sulfatase linkers could provide are illustrated in Figure 4, along with their mechanisms. In (a) the
mechanism of how dipeptide linkers are cleaved by lysosomal cathepsins or mouse plasma enzyme
Ces1C are shown and (b) display the mechanism of how arylsulfate linkers are cleaved by lysosomal
sulfatases for use in ADCs.
Figure 4: The differences in mechanisms and properties between dipeptide linkers cleaved by en-
zymes and arylsulfate linkers cleaved by lysosomal sulfatases. [10]
Some types of cancer are overexpressing sulfatases, which can provide arylsulfatase ADCs additional
selectivity towards those kind of tumor cells. The charged sulfates on the linker add hydrophilicity
and enables bioconjugation in aqueous phases. Aryl sulfatase linkers are simple to synthesize, but
during the synthesis the sulfatase would need a protecting group attached. The linkers are designed
to reveal a 4-alkoxybenzyl carbamate, which upon hydrolysis do a spontaneous 1,6-elimination of the
payload. The payload would be linked with an amine to the carbamate. [10]
In hormone regulated cancers, such as breast and prostate cancer, sulfatases are known to be over-
expressed. Therefore, it would be a good idea for an aryl sulfatase cleavable linker to target breast
cancer. A possible way to target cancer cells speciﬁcally is the receptor tyrosine-protein kinase erbB-2
or more known as HER2 (from human epidermal growth factor receptor 2). HER2 is overexpressed in
20-30% of certain breast cancers tumors. Using HER2, as a target for a payload, have proven to be an
effective treatment for breast cancer patients. [6]

Organosulfate chemistry and synthesis
In the synthesis of arylsulfate linkers different approaches have to be considered. The ideas behind
the synthetic pathway to the target linker 1 was, in comparison to the arylsulfate linkers synthesized
in the article Bargh, et al. [10], to check the possibilities for designing a new optimized synthetic pro-

as an amine. Comparing this to the targeted linker 1, it is a more ﬂexible linker, since the payload can
be released as an amine and alcohol.
The current design of the linker release the payload as an alcohol, and the amine as a urea connection,
however, it is easy to tune the properties to an amine release. Instead of the current ethylene diamine
between the linker and the drug, a simpler amine peptide would change the properties of the payload
release.
When synthesizing organo aryl sulfates, there are several things to consider. The free sulfate are very
polar, making them difﬁcult to handle and purify. Furthermore, they are only soluble in a limited
number of solvents, which complicate synthetic procedures that can be used. The free aryl sulfates
have limited stability, therefore, the use of a protecting group is desired. A protecting group would
provide better yields in the synthesis and ease the puriﬁcation steps underway. When the sulfate is
protected and uncharged, it is possible to purify by standard silica gel chromatography. A synthetic
approach for the protection of the sulfate monoester to a sulfate diesters can be seen in Scheme 8.

5.1
The design of the linker
The structure of the linker and intermediates is drawn with R groups, where R is either H or Me groups.
The different R-groups was included to see whether R=Me would improve the stability of the linker to-
wards peptidases. The synthesis of the two different variants of the targeted linker, were run parallel
during the work in the laboratory.
Some considerations in the linker design was, where to place the handle for the antibody. It could be
placed on the benzene ring or branched from the benzylic position. According to literature, aryl sul-
fatases are strongly affected by sterically hindrance and therefore, the benzylic position seemed most
favorable. [10]
The synthetic work was carried out with 7-hydroxycoumarin, as the model drug attached to the linker.
Cancer drugs are highly toxic and therefore, the chemistry was developed using a less toxic com-
pound. The linker functionalized with a model drug would also be useful for later studies of stability
as well as drug release in the presence of sulfatases. Coumarins are widely used in enzymatic studies,
since the released compound shows strong ﬂuorescence. The enzymatic studies and release of the
coumarin can then be measured in the plasma at low linker concentrations by use of ﬂuorimetry. [10]
Other considerations were the mechanism for payloa release and the length and structure of the di-
amine moiety. The ability to release alcohol-functionalized payloads was important, as it is only
possible to release amine-functionalized payloads with current linker technologies. The functional
mechanism of the target linker 1 is shown in Scheme 10. Firstly, a sulfatase cleaves the sulfate to cre-

6
Conclusion
The aim of this project was, to create an easy synthetic pathway in a few steps from the starting com-
pounds to create the target aryl sulfatase cleavable linker 1, illustrated in Scheme 1. The main struc-
ture of the linker was synthesized in a few easy steps with good yields, where the synthetic pathway
have been optimized. However, the attachment of the drug was identiﬁed, as the key intermediate
step, and provided a few struggles. Therefore, the drug and maleimide have not been attached, and
the linker have not been tested yet.
The nitrophenol activated linker was not stable during puriﬁcation and the reaction could not be
followed on LCMS. Adding a TBDMS protecting group on the phenol eased the analysis, while devel-
oping the chemistry, provided purer reactions that was easier to follow on LCMS. The coupling with
the drug and spacer lead to premature drug release, which might indicate the linker will prove to be
successful in enzyme studies. The favorable synthetic pathway to couple the drug to the linker would
be, ﬁrst to add a protected N,N-dimethylethylenediamine, as the spacer. Secondly, the nitrophenol
activated drug could be connected to the linker structure.

8.1
General Methods
Commercially available reagents from SigmaAldrich were used without further puriﬁcation and all
solvents were of HPLC quality. All reactions were monitored by thin layer chromatography (TLC)
and/or ultra-high- performance liquid chromatography mass spectrometry (UHPLC-MS). TLC was
done on Merck aluminum sheets covered with silica (C60). The plates were visualized under UV-light
or stained by dipping in the developing agent KMnO4 [1,5 g in water (150 mL) with K2CO3 (10 g) and
5% aqueous NaOH (2,5 mL)] followed by gentle heating.
Puriﬁcation
Flash column chromatography was performed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 µm) silica gel.
Preparative HPLC was carried out on a Waters Alliance HPLC system consisting of a Waters 2545 Bi-
nary Gradient Module equipped with either an xBridge BEH C18 OBD Prep Column (130 Å, 5 µm,
30 x 150 mm) or an xBridge Peptide BEH C18 OBD Prep Column (130 Å, 5 µm, 19 mm x 100 mm)
both operating at room temperature and a ﬂow rate of 20 mL/min, a Waters Photodiode Array (de-
tecting at 210-600 nm), a Waters UV Fraction Manager, and a Waters 2767 Sample Manager. 95/5%
water/acetonitrile increased to 0/100% water/acetonitrile. Run length and gradient was optimized
depending on the compound.
NMR spectrometry
1H, 13C, and 19F NMR was carried out on a Bruker Ascend with a Prodigy cryoprobe (operating at 400
MHz for proton and 100 MHz for carbon). The chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million
(ppm) and the coupling constants (J) in Hz. Spectra recorded in MeOD-d, were measured relative
to the signal for MeOD (1H δ 3.31 ppm and 13C δ 49.0 ppm). Spectra recorded in DMSO-d6, were
measured relative to the signal for DMSO (1H δ 2.50 ppm and 13C δ 39.43 ppm). Spectra recorded in
chloroform-d, were measured relative to the signal for chloroform-d (1H δ 7.26 ppm and 13C δ 77.0
ppm). [20]
IR spectrometry
IR analysis was performed on a Bruker Alpha FT-IR spectrometer.
Mass spectrometry
UHPLC-MS analysis was performed on a S2 Waters AQUITY UHPLC system equipped with a diode
array detector using a Thermo Accucore C18 column (d 2.6 µm, 2.1 50 mm; column temp: 50 °C; ﬂow:
1.0 mL/min). Tow Eluents (1, consisting of 0.1% HCO2H in H2O and 2, consisting of 0.1% HCO2H in
MeCN) were used in a linear gradient (5% 2 to 100% 2) in 2.4 min and then held for 0.1 min at 100% 2
(total run time: 2.6 min). The LC system was coupled to a SQD mass spectrometer.
Melting point
Melting points were measured using a Stuart SMP30 melting point machine.
