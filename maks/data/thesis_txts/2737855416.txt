Abstract
Synchrotron­based CT imaging offers high resolution but may be limited by the detector size,
restricting the field of view to small regions. To image larger samples, multiple separate scans
are individually reconstructed and later stitched together by aligning overlapping regions. This
method was applied to a real CT dataset of a rat kidney, the subject of this thesis. Due to
post­reconstruction stitching, the dataset exhibits significant artifacts, complicating effective in­
terpretation, segmentation, and quantification of the images. The project investigates methods
to reduce these artifacts and enhance the contrast­to­noise ratio (CNR) in selected regions of
interest (ROI). Additionally, it explores data blocking, enabling simultaneous reconstruction of
separate scans into a single image. The primary work utilized the Core Imaging Library (CIL)
open­source Python framework, along with MATLAB and the SYRMEP Tomo Project (STP)
software developed at the Elettra facility.
It was demonstrated that the CNR in chosen regions can be enhanced by reconstruction with
increased padding and the use of iterative methods with regularization parameters. Specifically,
the CNR of 2.267 in the stitched region was raised to 2.384 using FISTA with Total Variation
(TV) regularization. For the blocking method, the CNR in a chosen region within the post­
stitching reconstruction of a Shepp­Logan phantom with added noise was 0.382, while in the
phantom reconstructed using the same data but by the blocking method, it was 0.574. However,
the blocking operation’s effect on the real dataset was inconclusive. Although the data from
separate scans were successfully reconstructed into a single image, misalignment and possible
rotation in the combined data resulted in image blurring. Manual correction of the center of
rotation placements helped reduce misalignment but left the image significantly blurred, making
it unfit for CNR quantification.
It is concluded that post­stitching artifacts can be reduced by increased padding and that CNR
can be successfully enhanced by incorporating iterative methods. Simultaneous reconstruction
of blocked data using CIL is a promising alternative to stitching­based reconstruction. However,
ensuring proper alignment prior to reconstruction is crucial for this method to yield the best
results, as manual correction is not precise enough.

Resumé
Synkrotronbaseret CT­billeddannelse giver høj opløsning, men kan være begrænset af detek­
torstørrelse, hvilket begrænser synsfeltet til små områder. For at afbilde større prøver rekon­
strueres flere separate scanninger individuelt og sættes senere sammen ved at justere over­
lappende områder. Denne metode blev anvendt på et ægte CT­datasæt af en rottenyre, som
er emnet for denne afhandling. På grund af sammenføjningen efter rekonstruktionen udviser
datasættet betydelige artefakter, hvilket komplicerer effektiv fortolkning, segmentering og kvan­
tificering af billederne. Projektet undersøger metoder til at reducere disse artefakter og forbedre
kontrast/støj­forholdet (CNR) i udvalgte interesseområder (ROI). Derudover udforskes datablok­
ering, der muliggør samtidig rekonstruktion af separate scanninger til et enkelt billede. Det
primære arbejde anvendte Core Imaging Library (CIL) open source Python­framework sammen
med MATLAB og SYRMEP Tomo Project (STP)­softwaren, der er udviklet på Elettra­faciliteten.
Det blev demonstreret, at CNR i udvalgte regioner kan forbedres ved rekonstruktion med øget
padding og brug af iterative metoder med regulariseringsparametre. Specifikt blev CNR på
2,267 i den syede region hævet til 2,384 ved hjælp af FISTA med Total Variation (TV)­regularisering.
For blokeringsmetoden var CNR i et udvalgt område i rekonstruktionen efter syning af et Shepp­
Logan­fantom med tilføjet støj 0,382, mens den i fantomet, der blev rekonstrueret med de
samme data, men med blokeringsmetoden, var 0,574. Blokeringsoperationens effekt på det
virkelige datasæt var dog ikke entydig. Selvom data fra separate scanninger blev rekonstrueret
til et enkelt billede, resulterede forkert justering og mulig rotation i de kombinerede data i sløring
af billedet. Manuel korrektion af placeringen af rotationscentret hjalp med at reducere fejltilpas­
ningen, men efterlod billedet betydeligt sløret, hvilket gjorde det uegnet til CNR­kvantificering.
Det konkluderes, at post­stitching­artefakter kan reduceres ved hjælp af øget padding, og at
CNR med succes kan forbedres ved at indarbejde iterative metoder. Simultan rekonstruktion
af blokerede data ved hjælp af CIL er et lovende alternativ til stitching­baseret rekonstruktion.
Det er dog afgørende at sikre korrekt justering før rekonstruktion, hvis denne metode skal give
de bedste resultater, da manuel korrektion ikke er præcis nok.

Introduction
1.1
Overview
X­ray computed tomography (CT) uses X­ray attenuation to create detailed three­dimensional
images of object interiors, relying on computational reconstruction to solve inverse problems for
image generation. Widely applied in medical diagnostics and material science, CT enables non­
invasive examination of micro structures and internal defects. In health technology research,
micrometer­scale CT provides detailed views of tissue structures, facilitating anatomical studies
relevant to disease models and comparative medical imaging.
Imaging with a synchrotron­based CT provides a high resolution but may be limited by the size
of a detector, which restricts the field of view to small regions. If the sample is larger than the
field of view, covering it fully may require implementation of a stitching method, which involves
conducting multiple separate scans that cover the entire sample, with the individual reconstruc­
tions then stitched together by aligning overlapping regions. This is the case for a real stitching­
based CT dataset of a rat kidney, acquired by DTU Health Tech at the Elettra Synchrotron in
Trieste, Italy, that is a subject of this project. Due to post­reconstruction stitching, the dataset
exhibits significant artifacts, which can complicate an effective interpretation, segmentation, and
quantification of the images.
The objective of this project is to develop computational methods to reduce or eliminate recon­
struction artifacts in stitching­based biomedical CT. It is hypothesized that the non­uniformity in
contrast, commonly referred to as the ”cupping effect,” which is particularly pronounced in the
stitched dataset, can be mitigated with increased data padding prior to reconstruction. Addi­
tionally, implementing iterative reconstruction algorithms with regularization parameters is ex­
pected to enhance the contrast­to­noise ratio within selected regions. Ultimately, developing a
technique for the simultaneous reconstruction of data from separate scans is hypothesized to
potentially eliminate the need for stitching in the first place, if implemented correctly.
The majority of the project relies heavily on software and tools provided with the Core Imaging
Library (CIL) ­ an open source Python framework for tomographic imaging [1]. Additionally, it
employs the SYRMEP Tomo Project (STP) software [2], developed for users of the SYRMEP
beamline at the Elettra synchrotron facility and MATLAB from MathWorks [3]. The complete
code for this project is documented in an online repository to ensure reproducibility of results.
LINK Detailed instructions and links to supplementary online repositories are provided in the
Appendix Section dedicated to code, listing all utilized scripts.

2.1
Fundamentals of Computed Tomography
Computed Tomography (CT) generates images by measuring the attenuation of waves passing
through materials. This technology is crucial in medical imaging for visualising soft tissues and
smaller biological samples. While CT systems may vary in scale from large synchrotron facilities
to laboratory setups, the foundational mathematical principles remain the same.
The mathematics of CT involves operators that transform functions. For example, an operator
K takes an input function f and produces an output function g, modeling the CT scanner’s
response:
g = K[f]
(2.1)
The goal in CT imaging is to solve the inverse problem — to determine f from measured data
g using the forward model K.

2.1.1
Fundamentals of X­Rays
X­rays, a type of electromagnetic radiation, have frequencies ranging from 30 PHz to 30 EHz,
translating to wavelengths between 10 nm and 10−3 nm. They are emitted as photons from
an X­ray source, where the energy of each photon is given by E = hν, with h being Planck’s
constant. The energy of X­rays determines their penetration into materials and is crucial for
imaging applications.
X­rays are produced in a vacuum tube setup involving a heated cathode and a metal anode tar­
get, usually tungsten. When electrons emitted from the cathode hit the anode, X­rays are gen­
erated primarily through Bremsstrahlung and characteristic radiation. Bremsstrahlung results
from the deceleration of electrons near the anode nucleus, emitting X­rays with a continuous
spectrum. Characteristic radiation occurs when an electron dislodges an inner­shell electron of
the anode, causing a photon with a specific energy to be emitted as outer­shell electrons fill the
vacancy.

The intensity and energy of the X­ray beam are controlled by adjusting the current and voltage
of the X­ray source. The current affects the number of electrons hitting the anode and thus
the number of X­rays produced, influencing the image’s Signal­To­Noise Ratio (SNR). The
voltage sets the maximum kinetic energy of electrons, determining the peak energy of X­rays
and influencing the beam’s penetration and image contrast.
X­rays interact with matter primarily through the photoelectric effect, Compton scattering, and
Rayleigh scattering, which are illustrated in Fig.2.1. The photoelectric effect dominates at lower
energies where X­rays are absorbed, ejecting electrons and causing ionization. Compton scat­
tering occurs at higher energies, where X­rays scatter off electrons, reducing their energy and
changing their path. Rayleigh scattering involves the elastic scattering of X­rays, which does
not alter their energy but changes their direction.

Detection
X­rays are detected after their interaction with a scintillator, which is an element that converts
them to visible light. That light is then detected by a CCD (Charge­Coupled Device) chip. A
CCD is an analog device made up of numerous small sensors, or pixels, arranged in a large
grid. Each pixel is sensitive to light, and when a photon hits one of these sensors, it is converted
into an electric charge corresponding to the light intensity at that pixel.

2.1.2
Scanning geometries
The Lambert­Beer law models a single, infinitely thin X­ray, ignoring the nonzero extent of both
the X­ray source and detector elements, and thus provides no ”depth information” about the
object being investigated. This limitation highlights the necessity of employing different scanning
geometries, in order to gather information from multiple directions and accurately reconstruct
the object’s internal structure. Scanning geometries, such as parallel­beam, fan­beam, and
cone­beam, are used depending on the specific application, affecting the speed and detail of
image acquisition.
In the parallel­beam geometry (see Fig2.2 (left)), the X­ray source emits parallel rays that pass
through the object and are detected directly opposite by the detector array. The object is typically
rotated around a single axis to capture projection data from multiple angles. In the fan­beam
geometry (see Fig2.2 (middle)), a curved or linear array of detectors is combined with a source
emitting a divergent beam. This allows transmission values to be measured simultaneously
for all lines at the given sourced­detector position before rotating to the next position. The 2D
fan­beam geometry was generalized to the cone­beam geometry (see Fig2.2 (right)), in which a
curved or planar 2D detector allows multiple rows of data to be acquired at one source­detector
position.

2.1.7
Algebraic Iterative Reconstruction Methods
Iterative reconstruction methods in CT offer significant advantages over analytical methods like
Filtered Back Projection (FBP). While FBP requires equiangular and numerous projections,
iterative methods provide flexibility and robustness, particularly for sparse or irregular data.
These methods represent the CT reconstruction problem as a system of linear equations, Ax =
b, where A is the system matrix, b is the vector of projection data, and x symbolizes the pixel
values of the reconstructed image.
Iterative methods start from an initial estimate and progressively refine the reconstruction, con­
verging towards a solution that minimizes the difference between predicted and actual projec­
tions. They naturally incorporate regularization through their iterative process, mitigating noise
amplification via semi­convergence. The function that is to be minimized is called the objective
function.

ization techniques utilized in this project.

2.1.8
Discretization of Images and Projection Models
Discretization in computed tomography (CT) transforms a continuous function f(x1, x2), rep­
resenting an object’s linear attenuation coefficients, into a 2D array of pixels. Each pixel cor­
responds to a specific area in the object, with its value derived from f at the pixel’s center.
Projection models in CT approximate the acquisition of X­ray attenuation data from the object,
facilitated by the system matrix that connects the discrete object representation to measured
projection data. These processes are fundamental to CT, enabling the numerical analysis and
reconstruction of internal structures from projection data, essential for advancements in medical
imaging and industrial applications.
2.2
Phase Retrieval
Phase retrieval in computed tomography (CT) is a critical process for enhancing the contrast and
visibility of features within an image that are not distinguishable through traditional absorption­
based imaging techniques. This process is particularly significant in medical imaging, where
distinguishing soft tissues or other low­contrast features is crucial.
The theoretical foundation of phase retrieval in CT is grounded in the understanding that X­rays
passing through an object not only get absorbed but also experience phase shifts due to the
object’s refractive index. These phase shifts, though not directly measurable, can be inferred

of Intensity Equation (TIE) serves as a primary mathematical model for this inference. The TIE
relates the change in intensity of the X­ray beam across a series of images to the Laplacian of
the phase of the wavefront, expressed as:

2.3
Limited data and Artifacts
Artifacts are erroneous structures in the reconstructed images that do not correspond to the
actual object. There are many other sources to errors in CT apart from noise. One category of
errors arises from physical effects, such as X­ray scatter and X­ray sources emitting a spectrum
of energies instead of a single energy, leading to a so­called beam hardening artifacts.
Beam hardening artifacts occur when a polychromatic X­ray beam passes through an object,
resulting in the selective attenuation of lower energy photons. This increases the mean beam
energy (”hardens” the beam), similar to a high­pass filter effect. Those have can manifest as
streaks and in form of a cupping effect.
Streaking artifacts appear as multiple dark bands between two dense objects or along the long
axis of a single high attenuation object. Cupping artifacts cause the center of the image to
decrease in value as lower energy photons are attenuated over longer path lengths. This results
in the characteristic ”cupped­shaped” profile.
Other category of errors concerns the sample itself, and depends on its features or motion
during the data acquisition. Relatively large sample size can lead to limited data problems.
Those occur when the available X­ray data does not cover all angles or regions necessary for
a complete reconstruction of the image. Common artifacts in limited­data tomography include
truncation artifacts, which occur due to missing data outside the scanned ROI. Those can lead
to incomplete sinograms that cause blurring and distortion.
There are artifacts which arise from imperfect measurement equipment, such as dead detector
pixels or misalignments in the image. Rings or circular artifacts often result from these detector
imperfections or from reconstruction algorithms that inadequately handle the object’s rounded
nature.
There are different strategies to mitigate the challenges posed by limited data. One of them
is data padding which extrapolates the boundaries of measured data to simulate a complete
dataset. The other is to utilize algorithms designed specifically for limited data, such as iterative
reconstruction, which incorporates prior knowledge and regularization techniques. Techniques
like Tikhonov regularisation or TV minimization can stabilize the solution, reducing the impact
of noise and other inconsistencies. [6, 4]

2.6
Anatomy of a kidney
The kidneys are vital for filtering blood, maintaining body fluid balance, and excreting unneeded
substances through the urine. They’re about 10 to 12 cm long in adults and located just outside
the peritoneal cavity. Structurally, each kidney comprises of nephrons ­ the core functional units
responsible for filtering blood and managing reabsorption and secretion processes. [9]
The anatomical structure of human kidneys, share significant similarities with those of a rat.
Both human and rat kidneys possess complex structures involved in urine production making
rats valuable models for human kidney research. [19, 20]
Key kidney components include the cortex, where blood is filtered, and the medulla, composed
of renal pyramids divided by the cortex columns. Another crucial structure is the funnel­shaped
renal pelvis, located at the ureter’s upper end. Here, the fluids filtered from the blood are col­
lected before being drained through the ureter into the bladder as urine. The entire kidney is
encased in a fibrous capsule and cushioned by adipose tissue, which protects and secures the
organ in place. The kidney’s soft tissue structure comprises intricate networks of vessels and
tubules. The vessels, consisting of an inner wall lined with endothelial cells and an outer layer of
smooth muscle, encase a central lumen through which blood flows. Adjacent to these vessels
are tubules, essential for filtration and reabsorption. Blood supply to the kidneys is ensured by
the renal arteries, branching directly from the aorta. Mentioned components can be seen in the
Fig.2.8, which illustrates the human kidney as a reference. [9].

3.1.1
Preparation of rat kidneys for CT scans
Following ultrasound imaging, the rat kidney has been prepared for an intrarenal infusion proce­
dure. This preparation included the strategic placement of six ligatures for flow regulation and
to ensure stability during the infusion. To minimize the risk of blood clots during the procedure,
the rat received a heparin dose of 1000 IE/kg intravenously, using Heparin ”SAD” supplied by
Amgros from Copenhagen, Denmark.
Subsequently, a catheter has been carefully inserted into the abdominal aorta and the infusion
began with the kidney being perfused with 15 ml of preheated (40ºC) phosphate buffer. Imme­
dietaly after, the infusion solution is switched to 2% PFA, which is a common fixative used in
biological and medical sciences to preserve tissue structure and proteins by cross­linking them.
The kidney is then carefully excised, decapsulated, and fixed in 4% PFA.
After a 48­hour fixation period, the kidney underwent a dehydration process through successive
ethanol baths of 70%, 90%, and 100% concentrations. It is then infiltrated with paraffin and
finally embedded within a paraffin block. This prepared kidney is then ready to be placed in
a custom­made holder, which is specifically adapted for positioning on the scanner stage, as

3.1.2
CT scanning
The scanning protocol for rat specimens begins with positioning the holder in the scanning
room and capturing initial X­ray images to determine the scanning ranges for the z, x, and
y axes. Adjustments are made to identify boundary limits, with all findings documented for
reproducibility. The specimen holder is then rotated to ensure accuracy of these limits and
sufficient overlap between scanned areas, with details recorded for reference.
Once the setup is validated, the scanning operation is initiated. Imaging was performed using
a parallel beam setup with a sample­to­detector distance of 180 mm and a resolution of 1.6
micrometers per pixel. Post­scan, a check ensures all volumes are scanned, with missed areas
addressed through additional scans. Data is then reconstructed at three z­positions—lower,
middle, and upper sections of the kidney with a dedicated STP software. Upon completion, all
data is archived.
3.2
Data structure and geometry

5
Discussion
5.1
Comparison of CIL and STP reconstructions
The comparison of reconstructions between CIL and STP frameworks, demonstrated that com­
parable results can be achieved, at the same time validating the feasibility of continuing with
CIL for further work. Minor differences arise from variations in image conversion functions or
differences in reconstruction algorithms or the versions of ASTRA functions employed. This
divergence occurs despite using identical input phase­retrieved data, therefor it must be intro­
duced at the reconstruction stage. The decision to use phase­retrieved data as the starting point
of the first phase stemmed from the fact that CIL did not incorporate a built­in option for phase re­
trieval of images at the time of this project. Employing pre­processed and phase­retrieved data,
obtained through established scripts at the Elettra synchrotron, establishes a reliable baseline
for comparison of results between the two systems. There was no need for normalization or
transmission absorption conversion with phase­retrieved data, as these aspects are inherently
accounted for in the retrieval process.
5.2
Evaluation of CNR method on Shepp Logan phantom
CNR is favored over other metrics like the Signal­to­Noise Ratio (SNR) for several reasons.
Firstly, CNR directly relates to the visual differentiation possible between different areas of a
scan, which is more applicable to practical diagnostics than the absolute signal strength mea­
sured by SNR. Additionally, CNR can be calculated without requiring adjustments for offset
signals, making it robust against variations in imaging conditions and saturation effects that can
affect the SNR calculations.
A choice of step size between the noise standard deviation during that simulation is reflected
in ROI images, histograms and the table with measured CNR values. A relatively small step
size of increasing noise being 0.1 STD has been purposefully selected for better visualisation of
changes happening between adjacent images. With this value as a step size it can be seen that
ROI is slowly getting populated with more noise. Similarly, the two peaks can be distinguished
in attach histograms that later merge into one. If the step size was larger, then two peaks would
merge faster or already in the first iteration.
5.3
PHASE 1
From the analysis of line profiles it could be seen that, the increase in padding value, success­
fully flattens the intensity curve in a single slice, although the image brightness decreases. The
application of increased padding effectively reduces the impact of the cupping effect without
compromising the essential contrast between structural elements within the kidney. It is ex­
pected that if the contrast is indeed preserved, the CNR values will not significantly decrease
despite the overall darker appearance of the images resulting from the added padding.
The simulation of CNR measurements with the Shepp­Logan phantom yielded expected re­
sults, leading to incorporate this method instead of the initial approach for contrast preservation
measurement based on the average difference of peaks and troughs within vessels. However,
this method requires a mask, which is ideally manually segmented by an expert to accurately
delineate structures in the images into two distinct groups.
The masks used in this study were created by combining reconstructions from two extreme
cases to get rid of eventual bias towards one of the reconstruction methods. Combined mask
created this way is far from perfect and do not segment the vessels with total accuracy, as it is

challenging to achieve precise results with thresholding alone. Ideally, these masks should be
manually corrected through visual inspection, or the vessels should be marked by an expert.
However, despite the imperfect nature of that mask, it remains consistent across all measure­
ments, therefore the outcomes are comparable.
Before the CNR measurement on kidney data, the stitching code had to be modified to address
the clipping issues that arose during the stitching process of reconstruction images derived
from data padded with values greater than 512. Clipped data led to inaccurate results as all
negative pixels were set to 0. Additionally, clipping was observed during the initial execution of
PHASE 2, where algorithms like SIRT and FISTA had the non­negativity constraint enabled by
default. Attention to clipping is critical when handling data with negative values, as in case of
this dataset. Only after that was accounted for, could the CNR measurement be repeated.
It could be seen in the table from PHASE 1, that the CNR was in general less affected by padding
in the center of the slice, where the intensity changes aimed at reducing the cupping effect are
more pronounced towards the slice’s edge.
Initially, the focus was on reducing the cupping effect through increased padding, with no specific
intention for CNR enhancement ­ only preservation.
Conversely, the iterative methods were aimed at enhancing CNR, but the images with stitched
ROIs in the appendix reveal that while CNR was improved, stitching artifacts persisted.
It was only when combining FISTA TV with increased padding that both the CNR value in the
stitched region increased and the stitching artifacts were eliminated.
The reported CNR values need to be correlated with the resulting image, as numerical values
alone may not fully depict the overall scenario. Although the reported increase in CNR values
might seem minor, it should be evaluated alongside the reduction in stitching artifacts without
compromising contrast. Additionally, the enhancement of contrast adds significant value to the
outcomes.
5.4
PHASE 2
The selection of parameters during the iterative reconstruction phase was guided by objective
function plots and algorithm convergence, which are not detailed in this report. Nevertheless,
the presented tables for every algorhitm used, capture both peaks and declines of CNR values,
suggesting that the further algorithmic adjustments would unlikely yield better results.
Given the limited number of reconstruction methods and regularization parameters tested, it
is very probable that higher CNR could be achieved with additional research. The primary aim
was to demonstrate the feasibility of contrast enhancement, rather than obtainign the best metric
possible for this dataset.
5.5
PHASE 3
The initial approach for data blocking in CIL involved using a single image geometry with multiple
detectors. However, this was later adjusted to incorporate four image geometries with individ­
ual rotation centers. The method proved valid in simulation scenario, where data acquisition
from the Shepp­Logan phantom was flawless. Even after introducing noise, the data geometry
remained unaltered, which was not the case in the real dataset. The simulation however re­
sulted in a higher CNR score in the Shepp­Logan ROI when the sample was reconstructed using
data blocking rather than stitching. The ROI placement purposefully omitted the stitching arc
of overlapping slices, indicating that the lower CNR was simply due to a noisier reconstruction
and not because of the overlap with the slice arc. Both CNR values are relatively low because
the sinograms were substantially polluted with noise.

5.6. CONSIDERED IMPROVEMENTS AND FUTURE WORK
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
5.5.1
Misalignment
Unfortunately, unlike in the Shepp­Logan simulation ­ reconstructed images of different slices
from the kidney dataset are not perfectly aligned with each other, which becomes evident after
visual inspection of an image reconstructed by projection blocking. The overlap has been given
in mm while the pixel size has a precision of 0.1 um therefore using overlap information was
from beginning known to be in­precise, but was used for the initial trials. While the stitching al­
gorithm compensates for this mismatch, computing the transformation matrix which uses image
features for slice alignment relative to each other ­ the blocking method requires input data and
geometries that are already aligned. Since that is not the case, then the misalignment had to
be corrected. This was done manually and proved insufficient.
In the perfect scenario, the superimposed data would form a uniform image. Eventual misalign­
ment causes the effect of blur which is much easier to account for in a zoomed region, where
one can choose to shift individual scans relatively to each other.
It is unclear where the misalignment between adjacent slices come from. It has been tried on
two slices where the offset parameter was set to 0 in the batch file, therefore it is assumed that
the misalignment is not caused by the COR shift during reconstruction. It is also unsure if the
misalignment happens only in X and Y directions or if the slices are also slightly rotated relative
to each other.
In the final, manually corrected image of the blocked dataset, there are still arc artifacts visible
but it is suspected that blocking of all data from 16 ROI within that plane would reduce it largely
(on condition that the data was aligned perfectly).
5.6
Considered improvements and future work
As the next step in advancing this project, addressing data misalignment prior to reconstruction
using data blocking could be pivotal. This could involve firstly the investigation on the misalign­
ment nature ­ whether it is caused by detector imperfections or was the sample rotated, which
was not accounted for during this project. Secondly, implementation of misalignment correction
method. This could involve grid correction of projections, leveraging image registration tech­
niques, or incorporating frequency domain analysis. Given the limitations of manual correction,
a more sophisticated approach is essential.
Exploring the efficacy of data blocking with the full dataset of 16 slices, instead of four, could
potentially further mitigate remaining artifacts, as observed in Shepp­Logan simulations.
Lastly, while this project focused on two­dimensional data, the logical progression would involve
exploring improved techniques for handling three­dimensional data structures. However, this
poses challenges in terms of data size and computational demands.

6
Conclusion
In this master’s project, the primary objective was to mitigate the post­stitching artifacts present
in a reconstruction of a real kidney dataset while preserving crucial contrast, facilitating future
segmentation tasks. Visual assessment of the dataset identified the presence of numerous
artifacts, including the so­called ”cupping effect”, that could complicate the image analysis going
forward.
The reconstruction path of the dataset was successfully replicated, comparing outputs from
two tomographic reconstruction frameworks, SYRMEP Tomo Project (STP) and Core Imaging
Library (CIL). It was hypothesized that extending sinograms prior to reconstruction could reduce
the cupping effect within individual slices. This hypothesis was validated through flattened line
profiles with a trade­off of decreased reconstruction brightness, quantified using average peak
differences and Contrast­to­Noise Ratio (CNR). Importantly, CNR measurements demonstrated
that while brightness decreased due to padding, contrast in the image remained unaffected or
even enhanced in some cases.
Subsequently, CNR enhancement was explored using iterative methods such as CGLS, SIRT,
and FISTA with TV and Tikhonov regularizations within the CIL framework. These methods
successfully enhanced contrast in stitched region in comparison with the baseline reconstruc­
tion using Filtered Back Projection (FBP) and proved effective when combined with increased
padding, achieving optimal results where both CNR was improved and cupping effects reduced,
ensuring smooth transitions between stitched slices.
Additionally, a novel data blocking method was developed in CIL, allowing for simultaneous
reconstruction of data from separate scans. Simulation on the Shepp­Logan phantom showed
promising results, demonstrating improved CNR in images reconstructed using data blocking
compared to stitching.
Lastly, attempts to apply this data blocking technique to a real rat kidney dataset revealed align­
ment challenges, and manual corrections did not sufficiently improve image sharpness.
In conclusion, this project successfully addressed the challenge of reducing stitching artifacts
and preserving contrast in tomographic reconstructions. Future efforts could focus on refin­
ing alignment techniques and further optimizing reconstruction parameters to enhance image
quality in real­world datasets.
