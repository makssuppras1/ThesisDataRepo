1. Literature review 
 
1.1 Introduction 
 
CO2 capture and storage is an indispensable technology in the attempt to reduce CO2 
emission and mitigate climate change. This is the consensus among many credible and 
professional organizations or authorities, such as IPCC and IEA. Underground geological 
storage is considered as the major form for CO2 storage. The captured CO2 can be stored in 
depleted reservoirs or deep saline aquifers. The storage capacity, storage safety, and the long-
term fate of the stored CO2 are the main concerns for these CO2 storage options. This project 
is intended to investigate underground CO2 storage in this possible underground structures 
through reservoir simulation using ECLIPSE simulator. The models will be established for 
depleted oil and gas reservoirs and for saline aquifers. In oil reservoir scenario there would be 
3 cases, for gas reservoir 4 cases and 1 case for aquifer. This project is intended to compare also 
how the injected CO2 impacts the recovery factor in oil reservoirs. Data for the models would 
be simplified layer cake models of Valdemar Field.  
The storage mechanisms are different in the three types of reservoirs.

1.1.1 Greenhouse gasses  
 
Global warming is one of the most appealing concerns in our society. Carbon capture 
and storage (CCS) is one of the few most promising technologies to reduce CO2 concentrations 
in the atmosphere [1-2]. Countries are fighting global warming by introducing fossil-free 
strategies. Denmark for example, has already more than 30 percent of energy that comes from 
renewables. It is expected that this trend will rise and Denmark is willing to become 100% 
independent of fossil fuels by 2050 [3]. To achieve this goal the reduction of CO2 emissions 
with new technologies is necessary. One of these new technologies is Carbon Capture and 
Storage. In this chapter chemical and physical mechanisms needed for CO2 storage will be 
described and some of already existing CCS studies will be presented.  
Gases which have the ability to trap heat in the atmosphere are called greenhouse gases. 
These gases are a reason for global warming. Humans, mainly by releasing carbon dioxide, 
known as one of the major greenhouse gases, are contributing to the increase of global 
temperature. For a couple past years the worldwide emission of CO2 has risen due to the 
development of society, which comes with constantly increasing need for energy [4].

1.1.2 The status on CO2 sequestration 
To prevent an increase in global warming and the consequences associated with it, there 
are some things that could be done. One of the most promising technologies is capturing and 
sequestration of CO2 from its source points. Underground CO2 sequestration in geological 
formations is one of the techniques for CO2 storage, and some of the commercial projects are 
happening around the world [8].  Carbon and Capture (CCS) facilities currently in operation 
can capture and store permanently 40 Mt of carbon dioxide per year.

1.2 CO2 storage  
 
Process of carbon capture is the most efficient at large stationary sources such as 
industrial plants or power stations. The process of carbon capture requires separation 
from CO2 from unwanted particulates or impurities [10]. The composition of CO2 
depends on from what sources we produce it. After the process of separation, clean CO2 
can be transported for underground injection. Formations that are suitable for 
underground storage of CO2, considered in the literature can be split into few categories:  
- 
Depleted gas reservoirs 
- 
Depleted oil reservoirs 
- 
Saline aquifers 
- 
Unmineable coal seams  
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
 
Figure 7 Storage process of CCS [11] 
 
The geological formations suitable for CO2 storage are mostly found below 800m. The cause 
of it is that injected CO2 may reside under those conditions in supercritical form [2]. After 
injection CO2 will migrate upwards to the cap rock due to the buoyancy forces [1]. The 
successful CO2 storage is considered when the injected gas is stored in the reservoirs or saline 
aquifers for a considerable long period (hundreds of years). Injection of CO2 into oil and gas 
reservoirs has the advantage of being economically beneficial. Due to injection of CO2, the gas 
and oil total production can be increased. This strategy is called tertiary recovery mechanism 
and it uses already built infrastructure [10]. While this strategy is beneficial for companies it 
has some disadvantages compared to injecting CO2 into deep saline aquifers. Most of the time 
deep saline aquifers are distributed more commonly across the globe, thus they are more easily 
accessible. As far as injection of CO2 into oil and gas reservoirs, is considered to be economical, 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
injecting CO2 into saline aquifers may be costly (because of new facilities are being required 
to be build) but on the other hand, the transport cost to the saline aquifers decreases (smaller 
distances from the source points).  
1.2.1 Trapping mechanisms  
In principle, success of CCS storage is related to the long-term storage of the carbon 
dioxide. The question we have to ask is if the formation will manage to store injected CO2 
permanently in the reservoir for thousands of years. In the literature four trapping mechanisms 
have been presented [10]. Depending on the conditions in the reservoir, CO2 can be stored as 
liquid, gas or in a supercritical state. Most of the injected CO2 will be in the mobile phase, 
which means that CO2 can move freely towards the cap rock. Trapping mechanisms of CO2 
also occur as residual gas in case of formation water/brine invades the CO2 plume [13]. Carbon 
dioxide will also dissolve in the formation fluid and react with minerals. Above trapping 
scenarios are described below.  
• Hydrodynamic trapping/structural trapping  
Hydrodynamic trapping refers to carbon dioxide being trapped under cap rock, with low 
permeability. Injected CO2 as supercritical fluid or gas being lighter (less dense) than the fluid 
in the formation, due to the buoyancy forces will migrate upwards, till the point it reaches 
formation constraints. CO2 will accumulate in such hydrodynamic trapping/structural trapping 
that has vertical and lateral seals. This trapping mechanism is one of the prerequisites, for any 
storage strategies, because it prevents injected CO2 from any kind of leakage to the surface in 
the meantime for other trapping mechanisms to come into effect [12]. Such structural traps are 
most likely to be found in the oil and gas reservoirs that have stored hydrocarbons for millions 
of years [14]. Sequestration of CO2 by this trapping mechanism is dependent on the sealing of 
caprock, which makes it challenging for choosing the right formation [15].  
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
 
 
Figure 8 Structural trapping of CO2 [16] 
 
 
• Residual trapping mechanism 
This trapping mechanism happens very quickly as the porous rock may be compared to 
a rigid, tight sponge. During injection of CO2, the injected fluid first displaces formation brine 
or water in a co-current fashion, but after injection has stopped, due to difference in density of 
injection and formation fluid, CO2 tends to migrate upwards and the formation fluid flows 
downwards. CO2 displaces brine from its original position, leading to CO2 being trapped in 
pore spaces. After that, disconnected CO2 is trapped as immobile phase. This trapping 
mechanism is also called capillary trapping [14].  
 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
 
Figure 9 Residual trapping. Disconnected CO2 is left behind in porous rock as immobile 
phase. [16] 
 
• Solubility trapping 
This trapping mechanism process involves the carbon dioxide dissolving into the 
formation fluid [16]. When already migrated upwards, CO2 gets trapped under cap rock it is in 
contact with formation fluid (brine, water, hydrocarbons), and mass transfer occurs with CO2 
dissolving into the formation fluid until it reaches equilibrium state. The amount of CO2 that 
goes under the solubility process is strictly dependent on the temperature, pressure and salinity 
of the formation fluid [17]. After CO2 dissolves into brine, like for example sugar in tea, it 
increases the density of formation fluid, normally up to 1% compared to the original state [18]. 
Next on, the denser brine would flow downward due to the gravity effect. This process enhances 
even more the trapping mechanisms of CO2.  
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
 
 
 
Figure 10 Solubility trapping mechanism [16] 
 
 
• Mineral trapping 
Last but not least, the trapping mechanism is called mineral trapping. This can also be 
called the final phase of trapping. It results from the fact that dissolved CO2 in formation fluid 
undergoes a variety of geochemical reactions. For example after dissolution in water, it can 
create weak carbonic acid.  Over a long period of time this carbonic acid would react with the 
minerals inside the formation, creating solid carbonate minerals. This process may be 
beneficial, helping to chemically trap the CO2 [15,16].

1.3 Enhanced Oil Recovery with CO2 
Oil production can be defined by three phases: 
● Primary recovery 
● Secondary recovery 
● Tertiary (enhanced) recovery 
Primary recovery takes place with usage of natural reservoir pressure, combined with man made 
techniques such as pumps which bring the oil upwards to the surface. This first recovery is used 
at the beginning of the production. Downside of this technique is that only around 10% of the 
reservoir's oil or gas in place is being produced.  
The next technique that is being used is secondary recovery, which includes injecting water or 
gas to displace oil and to push it towards the production well, resulting in an increase of 
formation recovery factor up to 40%. 
Even though the formation recovery is quite significant after the first two techniques, major 
production companies have attempted few tertiary (enhanced) techniques that lead to 
production up to 60% of reservoir fluid. Tertiary techniques can be split into three major 
categories: 
● Thermal recovery – which includes injection of heat, for example injection of steam to 
lower the viscosity of reservoir fluid to enhance its ability to flow in the reservoir. 
● Gas injection – it is based on injecting different types of gases such as CO2 or nitrogen, 
which expand in the subsurface and they tend to push out the reservoir fluids towards 
the production well. It is also known to inject gases that will dissolve into the formation 
fluid to lower its viscosity which improves its flow rate.  
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
● Chemical injection – this technique involves injecting polymers to help waterflooding 
strategy or to inject surfactants, to lower the surface tension to help oil droplets to move 
inside the reservoir [19].  
 
 
 
Figure 11 CO2 flooding mechanism [19] 
 
 
 
The CO2 EOR process is a process of injecting CO2 to increase the recovery factor of the 
reservoir. So far, the only profitable usage of CO2 is using it in CO2-EOR scenarios. CO2-EOR 
has been used for almost fifty years and it is the most commonly used EOR method [20].

1.4 Review of the CCS simulation study 
Over the years there has been a significant number of CCS simulation studies, from 
which we can gain theoretical knowledge needed to conduct our simulations correctly. I 
would like to present a few conclusions and what has been done in the past in order to 
establish knowledge on CCS storage.  
Barrufet, et al. (2010) focused on determining parameters that influence storage capacity of 
CO2 in gas condensate reservoirs. It seems that the most important conclusion from their 
simulation work is that upper limitation of the injection rate, with CO2 purity, can dictate 
the feasibility of a CO2 storage project. They found out that injection rate might be an 
important concern for practical purposes, as the injection rate (after finding a suitable one) 
should remain constant. If reasonable storage efficiency is required, the chosen injection 
rate cannot be too high. It has been shown that the size of a reservoir is also a critical 
parameter. As if the reservoir volume is too small, its limited areal extent is going to prevent 
observation of the effects of injection rate [21].  
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
Li, et al. (2017) in a numerical study of the impurity effects on CO2 geological storage in 
layered formation have investigated impurity effects of CO2 storage. In conclusion they 
stated that the most suitable formations for CO2 storage are stratified. And more 
importantly, it is common that non-CO2 species which are often injected with CO2 due to 
reduction of cost of CCS projects have a great effect on the effectiveness and security of 
CO2 geological storage. N2 and H2S impurities on geological storage were investigated 
numerically. When N2 was injected with CO2, it increased its horizontal migration distance 
of the plume; and also it enhanced upwards movement of this plume, resulting in bigger 
spread of the plume of injected mixture. In fact, due to this process more of CO2 was 
dissolved in the formation brine, helping CO2 to be more securely trapped in the reservoir. 
On the other hand, when H2S was injected with CO2 the lateral plume spread compared 
with injecting pure CO2. In addition, the mass of dissolved CO2 in formation brine 
decreased. Also the study showed that injection of CO2-N2 mixture can decrease the storage 
capacity of CO2, and increase the leakage potential of the injected mixture due to larger 
expansion of the plume. On the contrary larger expansion of the plume helps in dissolution 
of CO2 in the brine. Furthermore it is worth mentioning that the partitioning phenomenon 
between N2 and CO2 could be used in the future in monitoring potential leakage points, 
since N2 flows ahead of CO2 and it would be detected much earlier than CO2, so that some 
action could be made to prevent leakage of CO2 [2]. 
Michael, et al. (2009), concluded that there remains the need for a more comprehensive 
portfolio of saline aquifer storage projects. Pilot sites usually have a great monitoring 
program, but injection rates are low compared to the commercial projects. There is still a 
need to prove that migration outside the storage site can be detected and there is a need to 
develop better ways for seismic imaging of CO2 and to test new, less costly methods.  [23]

2. Simulation model 
In order to run the simulation properly in Eclipse some sections must be specified. 
RUNSPEC section – is the first section in simulation file, where we define the title of the 
simulation, start date, dimensions of the reservoir and wells, components present, equation of 
state used for simulation ect. [22] 
GRID section – in the grid section the geometry of the simulation grid and various rock 
properties, such as, permeability, porosity, net-to-gross ratio can be defined. [22] 
PROPS section – the input data of this section contains saturation and pressure dependent 
properties of the reservoir fluids. [22]  
SOLUTION section – in this section sufficient data to define the initial saturations, 
compositions, pressure has to be given. [22] 
SUMMARY section – this section contains a specified number of variables that can be saved 
in Summary files after each timestep of the simulation. The graphics post-processor can be used 
after the simulation to display variation of graphs. [22] 
SCHEDULE section – in the schedule section, operations that would be simulated are defined. 
For example, injection rates, production specifications, BHP of specific wells, timesteps.

2.1.1 Oil reservoir 
Our first reservoir model that will be constructed and evaluated is the oil reservoir 
model. The reservoir is set to be quite simple. To calculate reservoir properties and its behavior 
METRIC units are being set in the RUNSPEC section. Grid of the reservoir is set to be 
50×30×30, specifying the number of cells in the x, y and z directions respectively. Possible 
phases inside the reservoir are OIL, GAS and WATER. Models in this thesis are simplified 
layer cake models of Valdemar Field. As the wells in place in this area are generally horizontal 
wells with sand propped fractures, 2 horizontal wells are placed on both sides of the reservoir, 
with a semi-circle fracture defined in the middle of each well using Local Grid Refinement 
(LGR). 
In the GRID section distance between wells is set to be around 300m and the thickness 
of the reservoir is set approximately to 100m. It was defined that our reservoir is placed at 2225 
m below sea level and it is a layer cake reservoir. What it mean is that the reservoir was divided 
into 30 layers with different specifications, for example porosity and permeability. Each of 30 
layers has 1500 cells. Porosities and thickness of each layer can be seen in Table 1. Permeability

2.1.5 Aquifer case 
 
By designing an aquifer model, one was supposed to evaluate storage potential in water 
reservoirs. As it was previously described in section 1.2.1, four possible trapping mechanisms 
can be active during CO2 storage in saline aquifers, among which structural trapping and 
solubility trapping have been taken into account in the current simulation study. In this model, 
no production at the beginning of the simulation would take place as usually no party is 
interested in producing just water from deep reservoirs. The only interest with underground 
water reservoirs, lies with storing CO2 in its formation. It has a high storage potential because 
CO2 would dissolve into water rather easily and availability of the suitable formation can be 
found much more common than in case of Oil and Gas reservoirs. This model is a really basic 
model that keeps the grid distribution and volume from previous simulations (Oil and Gas 
reservoirs), but contains different fluid properties, possible phases and injection strategies. 
During simulation, a specific keyword CO2STORE was used. For the CO2STORE option, 2 
phases were considered, a CO2 rich phase and H2O rich phase. In this case, CO2 phase is 
labeled as the gas phase while the H2O rich phase is labeled as the aqueous phase. The mutual 
solubilities of CO2 and H2O are calculated to match experimental data for CO2-H2O systems 
under typical CO2 storage conditions. Our conditions are set to be in the range of these 
conditions. Temperature of the reservoir would be 83˚C and reservoir pressure around 350 bars. 
To create the model water relative permeability and gas relative permeability are specified. In 
this case only one horizontal well was created ( later called WELL 1 ).  Due to keeping the same 
grid and reservoir properties (permeability and porosity) it would be easier to compare the 
results with Oil and Gas scenarios. 
Number of components in this case would be set to 2. One being CO2 and the other one water. 
Reservoir was filled 100% with water and injected fluid would be 100% CO2.

Basically, the main concern regarding the maximum BHP in the injectors is to keep it below 
the formation failure pressure, otherwise there is a chance of creating cracks and fractures in 
the cap rock and possible leakage in the reservoir. In order to determine the failure pressure, 
rock samples should be taken from the reservoir to be tested in the laboratory to measure the 
failure pressure. What we can be sure about is that the failure pressure is always greater than 
the reservoir initial pressure. So in many real cases, the injection pressure in the well is always 
kept below the reservoir initial pressure to guarantee the safety of the cap rock. For depleted 
gas and oil reservoirs, maximum BHP can be set equal to the initial pressure during injection 
because at the start of injection, the reservoir pressure has already dropped significantly from 
its initial value due to prior production from the reservoir. But in this case, for aquifer, since no 
prior production exists, BHP cannot be set equal to the initial reservoir pressure as it leads to 
==PAGE:40==

zero injection rate. This is why the failure pressure of the reservoir rock should be measured in 
the laboratory to determine the maximum injection pressure. If our case would be a “real” 
storage project, the BHP would be set to equal or preferably a little less than the failure pressure 
reported by the rock lab. However, since no such data is available for this case, the failure 
pressure was assumed to be a little higher than 400 bar; that is why the maximum BHP pressure 
was set to 400 bar.

4. Discussion and conclusions 
 
After evaluating all of the above cases, one can clearly say that depleted oil reservoirs, depleted 
gas reservoirs and aquifers are suitable options for CO2 storage. All 3 simulated reservoirs had 
the same volumetric parameters, permeabilities, porosities, but yet, different storage potential. 
Simulations in this thesis shows that it is reasonable to use CO2 for improvement of recovery 
factor for oil reservoir. With only production by depletion, one achieved a recovery factor of 
18%, and after using the EOR method with CO2 injection, recovery factor increased to 70% 
and could be even bigger. This shows how essential are enhanced oil recovery methods for 
optimizing the production. Also at the end of CO2 injection in the EOR case, some of the 
injected gas was left in the reservoir. After subtracting the total of produced gas from total 
injected gas, one could calculate that 2 × 108 𝑠𝑚3of the injected gas was stored in the reservoir. 
This is while in Oil Reservoir case 3, where there is no production after depletion period, 
1.6 × 108 𝑠𝑚3 of CO2 was stored in the reservoir. In fact, the amount of CO2 stored in case 2 
(CO2 EOR and storage) was 1.25 times higher than the amount of CO2 stored in case 3 (CO2 
storage). This is understandable, because in case 2, oil is constantly being produced during the 
injection period, contrary to case 3, which creates more space for the injected CO2 to remain in 
the reservoir. On the other hand, more CO2 can be dissolve in oil in case 3, but this does not 
contribute to CO2 storage as much as the reservoir depletion does. 
In Gas Reservoir cases, depletions of the reservoir, with injections of CO2 under different 
BHP/Rates, were simulated. In all of the gas simulation cases, 3.7 × 108 𝑠𝑚3 CO2 was stored. 
In simulations with higher injection rate, this value could be obtained quicker. It has to be 
remembered that the injection rates are strongly dependent on the amount of CO2 available on 
the surface. After comparing the amount of CO2 stored in depleted oil reservoir (case 3) to 
depleted gas reservoir, for these models, 131% more  CO2 was stored in depleted gas reservoir 
==PAGE:64==
64 
 
(1.6 × 108 𝑠𝑚3 compared to 3.7 × 108 𝑠𝑚3). ). Gas is a much more compressible fluid than 
oil, which can be one of the reasons why more CO2 could be stored in the depleted gas reservoir. 
Taking into account that production by depletion was conducted exactly the same for both 
cases, reservoir properties were also the same, one can conclude that under similar conditions, 
depleted gas reservoirs are better environment for CO2 storage than depleted oil reservoirs  
Last but not least, the aquifer reservoir model showed that it is possible to store CO2 in 
underground water formation. Aquifer model was set to be exactly the same as the other models, 
formation fluid was only changed to be water. Even though the amount of CO2 stored in the 
same pore volume is much lower, 2.5% of the amount of CO2 stored in Oil Reservoir (case 3) 
and around 1.1% of CO2 stored in Gas Reservoir, one should remember that the main advantage 
of saline aquifers is their huge , abundance and availability around the world. Also in real CO2 
storage cases, water is produced regularly to relive the increased pressure and increase the 
storage capacity.
