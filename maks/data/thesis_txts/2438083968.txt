by Patrick Strøm-Hansen (s113869) & Jens Peter Heli (s161368)
With continuously falling prices on electricity from renewable energy sources, closing
the carbon cycle through electrochemical CO2 reduction into chemicals is becoming
increasingly economically attractive. However, catalytic systems which are selective at
high current densities towards the desired reduction products need to be developed.
In this project, a scalable electrochemical CO2 reduction ﬂow reactor with data logging
and digital control of inlet gas ﬂow, current and cell voltage has been developed. Tests of
diﬀerent Ag based gas diﬀusion electrodes have given valuable insight into the potential
eﬀects of carbon and water on the CO vs H2 production ratio, suggesting that more
carbon and more water present both lead to higher H2 production, i.e. lower selectivity
towards CO.
Results show that for porous all-silver electrodes, H2 production is suppressed up to
250 mA/cm2 at 3.1 V cell voltage with humidiﬁed inlet gas, and 300 mA/cm2 at 3.2 V cell
voltage for dry inlet gas. When the inlet gas is dry the durability becomes an issue, while
humidiﬁed gas enables the cell to operate for at least 100 hours. Moreover, promising
signs for Ag nanoparticles on an all silver gas diﬀusion layer have been reported. All
tests have been conducted at 30 ◦C on-cell temperature, with an IrO2 anode electrode
and 0.1 M KHCO3 circulated on the anode side as electrolyte.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Anthropogenic climate change is one of the greatest challenges of our time, with CO2
content in the atmosphere now above 410 ppm [1]. The transition to sustainable energy
sources is underway with heavy investments in solar and wind power, but it will probably
not obviate the use of fossil fuels completely in this century [2]. Petrochemicals, shipping
and aviation are not easily electriﬁed, and the increase in population and rising standard
of living in the newly industrialised countries like China, India and Brazil will increase
the demand in these sectors [2].
In the past decades there has been an increased interest in carbon dioxide recycling,
which is the process of turning CO2 into something useful.
The literature is often
focused on closing the carbon cycle by turning CO2 into fuels, in contrast to the CO2
to chemicals approach.
One way of closing the carbon cycle is through electrochemical reduction of CO2 into
various products. This approach is backed by the dropping prices of renewable energy,
where the cost of new solar photovoltaic systems has dropped by 70% and wind by 25%
since 2010[2].
The ﬁeld of electrochemical CO2 reduction is not new. It started in the 1970’s, and
was pioneered by Yoshio Hori in the 1980’s, where his group tested diﬀerent metal elec-
trodes and grouped them by the products they primarily produced[3]. Much research
has focused on understanding the reactions and intermediates to be able to optimise the
catalysts. Most of the catalyst testing has been done in typical 3-electrode setups with
a counter, working and reference electrode submerged in electrolyte with CO2 bubbled
through. The solubility of CO2 in aqueous electrolyte is ≈30 mmol/L to 50 mmol/L

at room temperature [4] which often limits the current densities to 10 mA/cm2 to
100 mA/cm2. With the current densities reported, it is not relevant from an industrial
standpoint as the reactions in these conditions does not scale easily to higher current
densities. In order to close the carbon cycle, a close collaboration between research and
industry is essential.
In this project, the electrochemical CO2 reduction to CO has been investigated. The
Verma et al. paper from 2016 [5] conclude the economic viability of CO production
by CO2 reduction.
As the prices of renewable electrical power from wind and solar
are continuously dropping and since the value of chemicals are higher than for fuels,
the economic viability of this process is more reliant on having a high CO selectivity
at high currents than having a low overpotential. Higher selectivity at higher currents
would also mean higher production, which in turn would lead to lower capital cost per
produced CO molecule.
A new ﬁeld of methods for testing CO2 reduction in more industrially relevant conditions
has emerged over the past 5 years. There is not a deﬁned standard and many diﬀerent
setups has been proposed, with some looking to the fuel cell industry [6].
As part of this project, a scalable electrochemical ﬂow electrolyser setup has been de-
veloped. The electrolyser is based of a commercial fuel cell. This type of system may
operate at high current densities, and the technology is well developed. In this project,
diﬀerent Ag catalysts have been tested at current densities up to 500 mA/cm2, by in-
vestigating the applied cell voltage and the product selectivity, Silver was chosen as the
catalyst as it has high CO selectivity [3]. The selectivity is not as good as for gold, but
it is much cheaper and therefore more relevant for commercialisation.

The reactor is operated as an alkaline electrolyser cell. In this section, the basics of
such a cell and the CO2 to CO reaction will be discussed, before moving on to catalysis.
There will also be a brief discussion of some theoretical work done on the kinetics of this
reaction.

yser cell. The desired reaction is the following

However, the reaction takes place in two half-reactions; the cathodic

From the cathodic half reaction equation it can be seen that the cathodic reaction
consumes electrons. The anodic reaction is within literature referred to as the oxygen
evolution reaction (OER). For this reaction to take place there has to a transfer of the
OH– ions from the cathode to the anode. This is made possible with the membrane.
The membrane used in this project is an anion exchange membrane, as will be discussed
in Chapter 2. The membrane should be anionically permeable, electronically insulating,
chemically stable, mechanically strong and gas tight. The reaction is driven by a direct
current (DC) power supply.
Losses in fuel cells and electrolysers are called overpotentials, η, and can be associated
with diﬀerent parts of the system, like the anode reaction, the cathode reaction and the
ion transfer in the membrane, as well as ohmic voltage losses at cell interconnections.
When a chemical reaction takes place, there is an energetic barrier that has to be over-
come. This is called the activation energy. The goal of catalysis is to lower the activation
energy. This is done by allowing the reactants to bind to active sites on the surface of
the catalytic material, where bonds between the catalyst and reactants changes the

energetics of one or more reaction steps. This can lower the activation energy of the
rate-limiting step (RLS) and thereby change the kinetics of the reaction. The product
will then be separated from the catalyst, so that new reactants can bind to the surface.
These principles are illustrated in Figure 1.2, where the energy barrier for the uncatal-
ysed reaction is larger than for the catalysed. It also illustrates the fact that the potential
energy of the reactants and the product is unchanged by the catalysis. This is because
catalysis does not change the thermodynamics of the reaction, only the kinetics.
There is an optimum for how strong the adsorbates should be bound to the catalyst
surface. If the adsorbates are bound too strongly, they will not come oﬀthe catalytic
site.
If the bond is too weak, the catalytic eﬀect is little since the bond will have
less eﬀect on the intermolecular bonds. This is Sabatier’s principle [7], and it can be
illustrated in so-called volcano plots (see Figure 1.3).
It is therefore natural to investigate the bonding of reaction intermediates on the surface.
An eﬀective way of comparing diﬀerent catalysts for a speciﬁc reaction is to plot relevant
quantities against the binding energy of an intermediate. In a paper by the Jaramillo and

2.2.2
Membrane
The membrane used is an anion exchange membrane (AEM) from Dioxide Materials2,
called Sustainion. Its chemical structure is described in Kutz et al. [19], and is seen
in Figure 2.6. From Equation (1.2) and (1.3) in Section 1.2, the wanted reaction is
dependent on a transfer of anions, which makes the choice of an AEM suitable. The
Sustainion membrane is fragile, so it has to be treated with care. This has to be con-
sidered in the assembly process. The holes in the gaskets in which the electrodes sit
can only be slightly bigger than the area of the electrodes, to avoid overlap of gasket
and electrode, which causes uneven pressure on the membrane. If the holes are to big,
the membrane will have a lack of mechanical support, making it vulnerable to pressure
diﬀerences.
For the activation, the membrane is put in 1 M KOH for 3 hours which enables the
separation of a liner from the membrane. After discarding the liner, the membrane is
put into a fresh solution of 1 M KOH for 24 hours. The activation is then considered to
be done, and the membrane is stored in distilled water. The membrane is shipped in
dry form, and the goal of the activation is to wet it and remove the anions that have
been added before shipping and replace them with OH–.
2.2.3
Electrolyte
In a paper by Verma et al. [20], they discuss the eﬀects of electrolyte on the performance
of the Sustanion membrane. At any given potential, they get the highest partial current
density to CO in 2 M KOH electrolyte, compared to using lower concentrations of KOH,
and various concentrations of KHCO3 and KCl.
However, 0.1 M KHCO3 was used
as electrolyte in this project, for two diﬀerent reasons. First, when CO2 is dissolved in
water it creates carbonic acid, H2CO3, which is in equilibrium with bicarbonate, HCO3 –.
This will happen at the cathode side. The applied cell potential is suspected to drive
the diﬀusion of the bicarbonate ions through the membrane, to the anode side. With
KHCO3 as the electrolyte, the concentration may be changed by this diﬀusion, but the
composition will stay the same. If KOH was used as electrolyte, the composition would
have changed, making it less consistent over time. For more eﬀects on this eventual
diﬀusion, see Appendix E. Second, bicarbonate is much more used in literature, making
it appropriate for comparison [21], [6].

Gas Chromatograph
Gas chromatography is an analytical method for measuring gas concentrations. The
GC is connected to the outlet on the cathode side of the reactor, measuring the gas
composition. The gas is sampled from a sample loop, with a well-deﬁned volume. The
sample is then carried through a column by a carrier gas. The column is kept in an

oven, diﬀerent gas molecules will exit the column at diﬀerent times. When exiting the
column, the gas is measured by a detector.

of time have been used to get it up and running. Diﬀerent columns and diﬀerent carrier

let the gases out at diﬀerent times, without taking to long time. The sample loop has a
250 µL volume and is produced by VICI Valco Instruments Co. Inc.10. The detector is a
thermal conductivity detector (TCD), which sense the diﬀerence in thermal conductivity
between the sampled gas and a reference gas.

3.1.2
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) is a technique for analysing which elements
are present in a surface. It relies on a energetically well deﬁned beam of x-rays from an
aluminium anode. The x-rays are aimed at the sample where they eject photo-electrons.
The kinetic energy of the electrons is measured by a hemispherical analyser, and it can
be related to the binding energy of the electron through
Ekin = hν −EB −Φ,
(3.3)
where hν is the energy of the photon, EB is the binding energy of the emitted electron
and Φ is the work function of the detector. This gives a characteristic energy spectrum,
with ﬁngerprints for diﬀerent materials.
The XPS used in this project is a Thermo Scientiﬁc Sigma Probe, with a Theta analyser.
The x-ray line used is the Al-Kα with hν = 1486.3 eV.

trochemical surface area (ECSA) of an electrode. This is done by relating the coverage
of lead on an electrode to the amount of charge passed to that electrode. The measure-
ment is carried out by doing cyclic voltammetry (CV), with the working electrode being
a GDE sample, placed in an electrolyte containing lead ions. The measurement has been
based on the work by Herrero et al. [26], where the monolayer lead on silver adsorption
peak potential is given as E −EPb/Pb2+ = 150 mV, meaning it is 150 mV higher than
the bulk deposition potential. The structure of lead on Ag(111) is also reported in this
article, as a hexagonal closed-packed (hcp) structure.
The calculation of the ECSA is based on some assumptions. First, the Pb monolayer
is assumed to be structured in the same way at all Ag surfaces. Second, the lattice
constant of Pb is assumed to the same as in normal conditions, namely 4.95 ˚A and that
the hcp structure correspond to the (111) plane in a face-centred cubic structure. From
these assumptions the charge cost density for adsorbing lead onto a ﬂat Ag surface is
300 µC/cm2, as it is assumed it takes 2e– to adsorb the Pb2+ ion. By integrating the
lead on silver adsorption peak from the measurement, the ECSA can be found as
ECSA [cm2] =
Peak area [AV]
Scan rate [V/s] × charge cost density on ﬂat Ag [C/cm2]
(3.4)
By dividing the ECSA with the geometric surface area of the measured sample the result
is given as a roughness factor. This gives a ratio between the electrochemically available
surface area and the geometric area.

surements is shown. The counter electrode is a gold mesh cleaned in aqua regia, the
reference electrode is a mercury-mercurous sulfate electrode (MSE) and the working
electrode, the GDE, is connected to the potentiostat by a ﬂame annealed platinum wire.
The electrolyte is 0.1 M HClO4, with 1 mM Pb(ClO4)2 added in the chamber with the
working electrode. Ar gas is bubbled in the middle chamber through a glass tube with

The CV is done over an interval containing the lead on silver adsorption peak, but
restricted to not going more reducing than the bulk deposition potential.
Since the

vs MSE, which is in agreement with the measurements.
It should be noted that this method does not measure the active area available in the
reactor, as the cathode is not necessarily ﬂooded by the electrolyte, as it is in the 3-
electrode cell. However, if not the correct value, it gives an overestimation, leading to
an underestimation of the production values normalised by ECSA.

Chapter 4
Results
In this chapter the presentation of the ﬁndings obtained by testing the cathode GDEs
described in Chapter 3 in the electrochemical reactor described in Chapter 2.
The
test sequence has been designed to investigate product selectivity at diﬀerent current
densities as well as the stability of the MEAs. The test sequence has the following steps,
1. Constant voltage @ 3.5 V for 3.5 hours.
2. Constant current @ 10, 20, 50, 100, 150, 200, 250, 300, 400, 500 mA/cm2, 1.5 hours
at each step.
3. Constant current @ 200 mA/cm2 for 24 hours.
4. Constant current @ 10 mA/cm2 to 500 mA/cm2 in the same 10 steps as in 2. with
10 minutes at each.
This has been the test sequence for all the experiments, if not otherwise is stated. The
ﬁrst 3.5 hours is to activate the AEM and for the on-cell temperature to reach 30 ◦C.
This is to ensure that the rest of the measurements are comparable. The current is
kept constant in step 2 to make the production constant, making the FE calculations
more reliable. In step 3 the stability is tested by keeping a constant production rate
of 200 mA/cm2 for 24 h.
During the ﬁrst three parts of the testing sequence, a GC
measurement is taken every 90 minutes. Step 4 makes a new IV curve after the 24 h
stability test, to see if there is a change in the IV curve.
A key measure of the selectivity of the catalyst is the Faradaic eﬃciency (FE). The
FE is the ratio between the number of electrons going to the production of a certain
product and the total amount of electrons put into the system. It may also be deﬁned as
the partial current density for a certain product over the current density (see Equation

Stability
The 24 hour stability step in the testing sequence was performed for all the tests men-

something that does not happen for every test done on Sterlitech, but for some.
Due to the high selectivity towards CO and the stability, the Sterlitech GDEs were
explored further.

4.3
Eﬀect of Carbon
From the initial tests on diﬀerent GDEs described in Section 4.1, it is seen that the
systems containing carbon all have a signiﬁcant production of H2 even at low current
densities and low cell voltages, leading to the suspicion that carbon is a H2 producing
contaminant in the system. None of the cell conﬁgurations in this section were tested
for stability.
To investigate the carbon issue further, a blank Toray carbon paper was used as the
cathode, under the normal operation conditions. As expected, it only produced H2, and
the IV curve for this measurement is found in the lower panel of Figure 4.13, together
with the IV curve for the 100 nm Ag sputtered GDE. The idea is to compare the Faradaic
eﬃciency for the sputtered with the H2 production at the corresponding cell voltage for
the blank carbon paper. For instance, at 100 mA/cm2 the Faradaic eﬃciency for H2
is around 50 % and the cell voltage is slightly above 3 V. At the same cell voltage the
IV curve for the blank carbon paper is sligthly less than 50 mA/cm2, i.e.
less than
50 % of 100 mA/cm2. The blank carbon paper contained PTFE, while the one for the
sputtered did not. The PTFE could be expected to increase the overpotential for HER
as the electrode becomes more hydrophobic. Furthermore, the pure silver electrodes
from Sterlitech with pore sizes 1.2 µm and 5 µm have HER onset potential below 3 V cell
voltage, indicating that the silver on the sputtered GDE may also contribute to HER.
These factors made this test inconclusive, so further investigation was needed.
To test this further, Ag/C NPs were airbrushed onto a Sterlitech GDE with 1.2 µm
pores, to see if it changed the product selectivity. If carbon is not a hydrogen evolving
contaminant in this kind of system, then the performance should be unchanged or better
as silver NPs are supposed to be good for CO evolution [34].
The Ag/C NPs faced away from the AEM in the ﬁrst test. If they are put on the side
facing away from the AEM, they do not change the active surface area of Ag at the
interface between AEM and GDE. A worse CO to H2 selectivity would suggest that
carbon is a contaminant. A clearly reduced selectivity is seen in Figure 4.14. The same
shape of the FE as for the airbrushed GDE is observed, with the hydrogen selectivity
starting high, then decreasing, before increasing again. The ﬁnal increase happen at
3 V cell voltage, indicating that it might be the silver that starts to contribute to the
production.

ability test was performed. The gas permeability of the used cathode GDE was tested by
insulating the system with PTFE gaskets and let the GDE be the only element blocking
the gas from going from the cathode side through to the anode side. The gas ﬂow was

4.4
Long-Term Durability Test
To look more into the long-term durability, a cell with a Sterlitech GDE of pore size
0.45 µm has been operating for 105 hours at the time of writing, and is still going. As
stated earlier, the durability was drastically limited when dry CO2 was used, so for
this test the inlet gas is humidiﬁed.
The test conﬁguration started as for the other
experiments with a 3.5 hour activation at 3.5 V cell voltage, before setting a constant
current density of 250 mA/cm2. This current density was selected as it is a critical point

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this project, a scalable electrochemical CO2 reduction reactor with data logging and
digital control of inlet gas ﬂow, current and cell voltage has been developed. The sys-
tem also control the on-cell temperature, as well as being prepared for digital control of
pressure. Special care has been taken in the choice of materials, limiting the sources of
contamination. The system has been tested with diﬀerent Ag catalysts, both self-made
and commercial. To better explain the variations in performance, the catalysts have been
characterised through SEM, XPS, XRD, ICP-MS and UPD. For the self-made GDEs,
silver was deposited on to a standard carbon paper as GDL. The silver was deposited ei-
ther by airbrushing or sputtering. These GDEs were compared to commercially bought,
porous all-silver GDEs from Sterlitech.
A clear diﬀerence in H2 production is observed, when comparing the self-made carbon
containing GDEs with the all Ag ones. The self-made have more than 20 % Faradaic
eﬃciency towards HER for all tested current densities, while the Sterlitech electrodes
completely suppress HER up to high current densities, 250 mA/cm2 in the case of Ster-
litech with 0.45 µm pore size. This, together with tests done with Ag/C NPs applied
on the all silver GDEs, suggest that carbon is a H2 producing contaminant, and should
be avoided in CO2 reduction electrolysers. The results from the experiment with NPs
facing the AEM, show signs of great potential for an all silver GDL system with Ag
NPs.
Furthermore, the increase in onset potential for HER from 3 V to 3.2 V when applying
dry CO2 gas instead of humidiﬁed for the Sterlitech with 1.2 µm pores, suggest that the
presence of water favours HER more than CO evolution. Dry inlet CO2 shows even
higher current density with suppressed HER, 300 mA/cm2 for the Sterlitech with 1.2 µm
pores, but it has issues with long-term durability.

Outlook
In this chapter, ideas and suggestions for improvements to the system will be discussed.
6.1
Humidity Control
As the amount of water present in the environment of the reaction is believed to be an
important factor for the CO selectivity over H2, diﬀerent experiments are suggested that
might lead to insights into the tuning of the selectivity.
6.1.1
Hydrophobicity of Cathode Electrode
One way of limiting the amount of water present in the reaction environment is by
making the cathode electrode more hydrophobic. This could be done by applying a
PTFE solution to the electrode, e.g. like in the recent paper by Dinh et al. [35] and in
the work by Kucernak et al. from 2012 [36]. This may prove to make the environment
more favourable for CO production. However, a potential problem with adding PTFE
to the electrode is that it may block catalytic sites, as well as increasing the ohmic
resistance of the electrode. A better way could be to vary the humidiﬁcation of the inlet
CO2 gas.
6.1.2
Varying the Humidiﬁcation of Inlet CO2
In the experiments with dry inlet gas, the selectivity for CO over H2 remained close
to 99 % all the way up to 300 mA/cm2 for the Sterlitech cathode with 1.2 µm pores,
a signiﬁcant increase compared to the tests with humidiﬁed inlet gas. However, the

Pressure Control
An interesting parameter that, to the best of our knowledge, has not been investigated
for this kind of system is how changing the pressure in the electrolyser cell, changes the
selectivity. For this reason, the setup has been prepared to enable pressures from 0 −5

signal from a digital to analogue converter (DAC). The actual pressure value is read back
by the ADC mentioned in Section 2.4.2. It can all be controlled from the data acquisition
program, where a pressure setpoint can be speciﬁed in the control sequence.

6.3. CHANGES TO GC
55
Some tests have been conducted with the pressure controller, and they show that some
changes has to be made to the system for it to work properly. Because of the humidiﬁer
and liquid collection volumes (4 L in total, when no water in humidiﬁer), and the low
ﬂow rates 200 mln/min, the system has a long response time, which inﬂuences the PI
regulation of the pressure controller. It means that it is diﬃcult to maintain a stable
pressure with a continuous ﬂow out of the pressure controller.
A smaller liquid collector volume (or one could just ﬁll the one that is there with water)
should be used, and if the inlet gas is dry, a smaller humidiﬁer volume as well. They
should not be removed all together, as they function as safety features, ensuring that
the electrolyte does not ﬂow into the GC or the MFC if the membrane breaks. This
change would allow a faster regulation and response.
When increasing the pressure, the membrane might need some kind of support.
At
least sharp edges on the gaskets and GDEs should be minimised to avoid high pressure
points. For the humidiﬁer and liquid collector, special tops has been constructed where
a pressure relieve valve, and a manometer for pressure monitoring, can be installed.
As discussed in Section 4.3, it seems like most of the CO2 ﬂow just passes by the cathode
and will never react in the cell. By increasing the pressure we would expect more of the
inlet CO2 to become available to the cathode-membrane interface.
6.3
Changes to GC
The GC has been the major factor in the duration of experiments. The column used, de-
scribed in Section 2.4.4, has a long retention time for CO2. Even when operating at high
temperatures 180 ◦C, the column has a retention time of 35 minutes for CO2, and the
oven has to cool down to 30 ◦C before another measurement can start. Another column
with shorter retention time for CO2 could increase the time resolution of experiments
signiﬁcantly.
A way of decreasing retention time in the current column is to increase the ﬂow speed of
the carrier gas. This was not done in this project, as the oxygen peak overlaps with the
hydrogen at higher ﬂow speeds. However, oxygen would only be expected if there was a
leak and air got into the system, meaning that we would also see a nitrogen peak. The
nitrogen peak is in no danger of overlapping with either hydrogen or carbon monoxide.
The timing between experiments is another important factor. Towards the end of the
project ﬂuctuations in the room temperature inﬂuenced the time between GC measure-
ments, because it took longer for the GC to reach 30◦C. The timing between the IV data

Adding a Reference Electrode at the Anode
A major draw-back with the system made and used in this project, is that it has no
reference electrode. With a reference electrode it is possible to isolate losses in the system
in order to ﬁnd out where the potential for improvement is the largest. In this section, a
design for how to implement such an electrode into the already existing system, without
modifying the already existing components, is proposed.
==PAGE:69==
6.4. ADDING A REFERENCE ELECTRODE AT THE ANODE
57
Implementing a reference electrode should be done on the anode side, since the cathode
operates in the gas phase. The idea is to make a supplement to the existing electrolyser
cell, by making a thicker gasket on the anode side. At the moment, a 250 µm thick
PTFE sheet is used. Instead of this sheet, a compartment would be inserted between
two gaskets and ﬁlled with electrolyte. In this compartment a reference could then be
added. There are a couple of issues that need to be considered when building this.
(1) The AEM is very fragile and would need mechanical support for it not to break
under pressure.
(2) The gasket needs to be compressible for the system to be leak tight. The inlet
hole for the reference electrode should not be compressible, to avoid crunching of
the electrode.
(3) The distance between the membrane and the anode should be kept at a minimum
in order to reduce ionic resistance.
(4) The anode needs to be pressed tightly up against the ﬂow ﬁeld to ensure ohmic
contact.
(5) Free ﬂow of electrolyte in the compartment should be ensured.
The suggested design is illustrated in Figure 6.2 and 6.3. To solve (1), the suggestion
is to have a ﬁne grid, that has small enough pores to support the membrane, but not
to small so that it restricts the electrolyte contact with the membrane. The material
choice is essential for solving (2), and a combination of PTFE and Ti could be a good
solution, having a middle block of Ti with an inlet hole for the electrode, and PTFE
on each side of the Ti to ensure compressibility. The thickness of these elements will of
course take (3) into consideration.
The PTFE piece on the ﬂow ﬁeld side should have a perforated hole of the same size
as the hole in the Ti block, but also a small step with a slight height diﬀerence to leave
some space for the anode electrode. The height diﬀerence should be chosen to ensure
that the anode is pressed against the ﬂow ﬁeld thus solving (4). One last thing that
needs to be considered is the ﬂow, as mentioned in (5). To solve this, one may let the
ﬂow in between the anode and the membrane. If the electrolyte is to be pressed through
the anode electrode, it would cause much mechanical stress on it, and probably break
it. The idea is to let the inlet tubes go through the ﬂow ﬁeld, then through the ﬁrst
PTFE gasket, and then into a channel in the Ti block, which leads the electrolyte into
the compartment. The channels are visualised in Figure 6.3. This would put pressure
on the anode electrode up against the ﬂow ﬁeld, rather than pushing it away from it.

A.2. MEASUREMENT METHOD

A.2
Measurement Method
When designing the measurement method, multiple factors were taken into account.
(1) All the peaks should be measured with no overlap, in order to ensure correct
integration of the peaks.
(2) The measurements should have the same initial state, i.e.
no accumulation of
material in the column from one measurement to another.
(3) Each GC measurement should not take too long time.
(4) The peaks should be easily identiﬁed.
To encompass these factors, the GC-program ended up being 88 min in total. The oven
is kept at 30 ◦C for 13 min, before ramping up to 180 ◦C with a ramp rate of 30 ◦C/min.
The oven is kept at 180 ◦C for 40 min, before being ramped down to 30 ◦C where it is
being held for 30 min.
The higher the temperature in the oven, the faster the molecules exit the column. The
initial 30 ◦C, is to separate H2, O2, N2 and CO. The fast ramp to 180 ◦C, is to release
CO2 from the column, without waiting to long.
When changing the temperature in the oven, the background of the measurement changes.
This happens because the TCD measure diﬀerence in thermal conductivity between a
reference and a sampled gas. If the carrier gas changes temperature, the thermal conduc-
tivity changes too, resulting in a shift in the background. The timing of the temperature
ramp is to avoid that the change in background coincides with the CO peak.
As stated in point (2), the measurement method should ensure that the column is in the
same state before each measurement, meaning that all sampled material should leave
the column before the next measurement starts. In Figure A.3, an example of what can
happen if the column accumulate material from one measurement to another is shown.
The residue from earlier measurements can exit at unknown times, and destabilise the
background. The step where the column is kept at 180 ◦C for 40 min is to avoid this.
The column is hydrophilic, and water is suspected to be part of the residue seen in early
tests. Water does not make a peak in the measurement because of slow diﬀusion through
the column and the ”peak” is smeared out along the background.
To ensure easy detection of peaks, a gain of 16 was used, meaning that all signals were
multiplied by a factor of 16.

Appendix C
UPD on Samples with Ionomer
As mentioned in Chapter 3, the UPD measurements with XA-9 ionomer added to the
catalyst give some unpredicted results. In Figure C.1, the CV scans for diﬀerent intervals
are shown for a measurement on Sterlitech 1.2 µm with ionomer. The blue curve show
the UPD result within the limits used for all the ECSA determining measurements. For
this curve a peak at around −750 mV vs. MSE is seen as expected, but an extra peak
at around −550 mV vs. MSE is also seen. The cause of the latter peak is unknown, but
it is believed to be due to some equilibrium eﬀect. As the XA-9 ionomer have active
groups that are positively charged, it will require more energy to adsorb the positive
lead ions Pb2+, but desorption will also demand more energy when it is ﬁrst adsorbed.
This argument is backed by the ﬂat proﬁle of the adsorption peak.
Bulk deposition of lead on silver is expected to happen below −900 mV vs. MSE. At
this potential, sharp bumps in both the red and the yellow curves are seen, and they
do not start growing again until the potential is below −1.1 V vs. MSE. This cannot
be explained either, but is suspect to be caused by the bulk deposition at −900 mV vs
MSE being limited by the ionomer blocking a considerable amount of sites. For more
negative potentials, below −1.1 V vs. MSE, the potential might be strong enough to
force the lead ions through the ionomer, causing a bulk deposition.
What is not in accordance with this theory, is the large peak at around −850 mV vs.
MSE. This peak comes before the the predicted desorption peak at −750 mV vs. MSE,
suggesting that lead actually desorbs more easily with added ionomer. This may suggest
that the bulk deposition happens directly onto the ionomer, which normally would repel
the ions due to its positive active groups. The conclusion is that further investigation is

Appendix E
CO2 Diﬀusion Through the AEM
CO2 has been detected with GC measurements on the anode side, indicating that there
might be a cross-over of CO2 from the cathode to the anode. In Figure E.1, the reactions
leading to this diﬀusion are proposed. CO2 reacts with the OH– from the CO evolution
reaction, creating bicarbonate ions, HCO3 –, which is an anion that can be transported
through the AEM. The most obvious problem with this, is the loss of reactants. For each
CO, 3 CO2 molecules are required; 1 for making the CO molecule and 2 for reaction with
OH–, which is then transported through the membrane as HCO3 –. Moreover, as HCO3 –
is a bigger and heavier molecule, this could lead to an increase in the overpotential due
to ionic resistance. As discussed in Section 2.2.3, the CO2 itself will also create HCO3 –
when in contact with water.

Appendix F
Calculation of Losses
In order to ﬁnd the overpotential at the cathode, two experiments were done. First, an
IV curve was made for carbon paper in the reactor while having a 100 mL/min ﬂow of
CO2 on the inlet. The anode, membrane, electrolyte and temperature were kept as for
all experiments. A given current gives the following cell voltage;
ECell = 1.23V + ηanode + ηmembrane + ηcathode, ,
(F.1)
where 1.23 V is the thermodynamical potential for HER, and ηx is the overpotential for
component x. The overpotentials are dependent on the current density. H2 was the only
observed product from this experiment.
The idea is to use the carbon paper in a 3-electrode setup in order to ﬁnd ηcathode and
use it to isolate the membrane and anode losses in Equation (F.1). In the 3-electrode
cell, the same electrolyte as in the reactor was used, and CO2 was bubbled in the
solution through a glass frit. The next assumption is then that the overpotential in the
3-electrode cell would correspond to the one in the reactor, and that pH also is the same
in both at a given current density [37]. The setup is described in the caption of Figure
F.1. The MSE reference used can easily be calculated into RHE by
ERHE = EMSE −0.64 V −0.059 V × pH
(F.2)

electrode to the potentiostat was insulated with a glass tube and PTFE tape. However,
some of the Ti wire were still in contact with the electrolyte, so an IV curve for the
part of the Ti wire in contact with the electrolyte were made. The current at a given
potential for the Ti wire could then be subtracted from the measurement done on the
