Abstract
This thesis examines how Artificial Intelligence (AI) can help realign education with the
psychological and developmental needs of learners. Tracing the historical evolution of
education, it highlights a growing mismatch between traditional school systems—shaped
by industrial and political imperatives—and the way humans are naturally predisposed to
learn. Drawing on theories of self-determination, motivation, and narrative identity, the
work proposes a framework for fostering self-awareness and intrinsic motivation through
personalized psychological “reminders.” To implement this framework, Large Language
Models (LLMs) are used to analyze multidimensional survey data, infer individual psycho-
logical profiles, and generate reflective messages tailored to participants’ characteristics
and developmental needs. Through both conceptual and technical exploration, the research
suggests using AI not as a tool to optimize existing educational structures, but as a means
to support meaningful, self-directed learning and personal development in an uncertain
future.

Introduction
Education and technology: two pillars that shape the architecture of human life by influencing
how we think, what we think about, and the arena in which thoughts unfold. Education prepares
minds for the complexity of the world, creating the conditions for invention; technology, in
turn, continuously transforms the tools at our disposal, deeply reconfiguring how we live. These
domains are thus deeply interconnected. And yet, a growing disparity marks their evolution.
While technology advances at a staggering pace — often outpacing our ability to fully grasp
its implications — education remains anchored in inherited structures, largely unchanged over
decades, perhaps because we have underestimated its true scope and thus neglected its renewal.
This gap becomes even more concerning in the age of Artificial Intelligence (AI), where computa-
tional systems increasingly match or surpass human abilities in a wide range of tasks. Our limited
understanding of fundamental aspects of human cognition and behavior further complicates this
shift, blurring the boundary between human intelligence and technical performance. Amid this
uncertainty about the human condition itself, a fundamental question arises: what remains for
humans when they are outperformed by their own creations?
It is crucial to reflect on how this available and promising technology can be directed toward
serving education — the cornerstone of human development and societal progress. That im-
perative informs the direction and scope of this research. It begins with the premise that
any meaningful attempt to transform the educational system — and society more broadly —
must start with a deep understanding of its origins and historical development to detect its
foundational flaws. From there, by combining theoretical insights from the human sciences with
contemporary technological tools, we aim to propose fundamental reflections and practical entry
points that move toward a system more attuned to human realities and the challenges of our time.
To pursue this objective, the thesis unfolds as follows: Part I outlines the problem statement;
Part II lays out the theoretical foundations; Part III introduces the methodology and concep-
tual framework; Part IV describes the technical implementation; and Part V provides critical
reflections and considers future directions.
1
==PAGE:7==
Part I
Problem Statement
This first part aims to lay the foundation for the core issue that motivated this research. It
will examine the relationship between the biological predispositions of humans to learning and
the demands imposed by modern societies, to understand the origins of educational systems,
their historical evolution, and the consequences they have produced. By concisely tracing this
trajectory, from spontaneous learning practices of prehistoric societies to today’s institutional
models, we aim to question the relevance of the current system in light of contemporary challenges.
The reader should keep in mind that the historical analysis presented here aims to reveal
broad patterns—not to equate all cultural contexts, but to highlight the shared constraints
that have shaped the evolution of educational systems globally, particularly as they are most
commonly experienced in WEIRD societies (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and
Democratic)(Henrich et al., 2010).
1
The Origins of Learning
Formal education is commonly understood as an institutionalized, structured, and chronolog-
ically graded system that facilitates, certifies, or controls learning, typically through schools
and universities (Coombs & Ahmed, 1974, as cited in Johnson and Majewska, 2022). As
such, understanding formal education first requires a deeper look at learning itself and how
it originally emerged in early human history, around 300,000 years ago. This subsection will
explore how learning can be described during that early period, which—although chronologically
distant—still carries considerable weight in shaping our biological and cognitive heritage.
1.1
Learning Among Hunter-Gatherers
Anthropologists and cognitive psychologists have increasingly examined how children in hunter-
gatherer societies acquire knowledge, often in the absence of formal instruction (Hewlett et al.,
2016; Hewlett, 2017; Lancy et al., 2010). Although current research focuses on contemporary
foraging communities, such as the Aka or Agta, whose ways of life closely resemble those of early
human groups (Hagen et al., 2016), there is a broad consensus that learning among prehistoric
hunter-gatherer groups was also deeply embedded in daily life.
In fact, children acquired knowledge by immersing themselves in the activities of their community,
guided not by instruction, but by imitation, observation, and self-directed play. Among the
!Kung San of the Kalahari Desert in southern Africa, a nomadic hunter-gatherer society studied
extensively in the 20th century by anthropologist Marjorie Shostak, children were rarely, if ever,
explicitly taught; yet by the age of ten, they could track animals, identify medicinal plants, and
navigate their environment with remarkable precision (Shostak, 2014). Similarly, among the
Aka of Central Africa and the BaYaka of the Congo Basin — egalitarian forager-horticulturalists
2
==PAGE:8==
who maintain many aspects of their traditional lifeways today — young children are free to
explore and are consistently included in adult activities, from foraging to toolmaking, where they
learn through participation rather than correction (Hewlett et al., 2016; Lewis, 2017; Lew-Levy
et al., 2017)
One of the few remaining sea-oriented hunter-gatherer populations in Southeast Asia, the Agta
of northeastern Luzon in the Philippines, exemplify the role of storytelling as a powerful learning
tool: narratives are used not only to transmit myths but to encode essential survival strategies
and social norms, shaping behavior through moral and mnemonic structures (Hagen et al.,
2016). In the same way among the Anishinaabe, an Indigenous people of North America, oral
storytelling serves to preserve historical memory, cosmological beliefs, and ecological knowledge
— while also reinforcing community identity and intergenerational connection (Gallagher, 2013).
In more remote regions, such as with the Matsigenka of the Peruvian Amazon and the Duna of
highland Papua New Guinea, observational learning remains dominant: children acquire com-
plex skills like fire-making, weaving, or hunting through patient observation and trial-and-error
practice, long before ever receiving explicit instruction (Johnson, 2003).
As David Lancy points out in The Anthropology of Childhood, contemporary hunter-gatherer
societies offer our best window into the learning environments of early human communities
(Lancy et al., 2010). In these contexts, play, work, and ritual were deeply intertwined, all serving
a clear and practical purpose: meeting the immediate needs of the group. To date, there is
no archaeological evidence of formal schooling, yet there are numerous signs — such as tool
use, cave paintings, and fire-making — that complex knowledge was indeed transmitted. This
transmission occurred informally but effectively, through observation, imitation, storytelling,
and most importantly, through direct and active participation in communal life, in situations
rich with meaning. It is precisely this kind of concrete immersion in purposeful activity that
made learning not only spontaneous, autonomous, and deeply embedded, but also conducive to
the gradual development of mastery — as skills were refined over time through repetition and
increasing responsibility.
1.2
Learning Without Teaching
Evolutionary psychologists and cultural anthropologists increasingly argue that any serious
theory of education must be grounded in the full arc of human development (Bjorklund and
Pellegrini, 2000; Xu et al., 2024). As Peter Gray reminds us, for more than 95% of our species’
history, humans lived as hunters and gatherers in societies where structured teaching - as we know
it today - did not exist (Gray, 2011). Our cognitive, emotional, and social systems evolved under
conditions that prioritized autonomy, exploration, observation, imitation, and participation in
purposeful tribal life. And because evolution is slow, our basic neural architecture has changed
very little over the past 10,000 years—meaning we are biologically adapted to a world vastly
different from the one formal schooling tries to prepare us for nowdays (Gray, 2011). From this
evolutionary standpoint, the emergence of school as a formal institution, which will be addressed
in the following sections, is a very recent and culturally specific development, not a timeless and
universal human need.
3
==PAGE:9==
Lancy (2010), among others, underscores that teaching is largely absent in small-scale societies
because it is not necessary: children acquire complex skills and cultural knowledge effortlessly,
often without explicit instruction, through what has been termed social osmosis. The ar-
gument, then, is not that we should return to a premodern mode of existence, but that we
must critically interrogate whether modern educational systems—developed primarily during
the industrial and bureaucratic expansion of the 19th and 20th centuries—are compatible (or
could be more compatible) with the way human beings are naturally predisposed to learn. By
disregarding the learning ecology in which our species evolved, we risk designing educational
environments that conflict with, rather than support, our innate learning mechanisms, leading to
disengagement, underachievement, and the widespread pathologizing of normal developmental
variation. Revisiting the pedagogical patterns of hunter-gatherer societies offers not a romantic
ideal but a scientific reference point—a baseline against which to assess the alignment (or
misalignment) of schooling with human nature. As such, it provides a crucial lens through which
to reimagine education in a way that respects the autonomy, context, and curiosity of the learner.
2
Learning in the Age of Civilization
2.1
Shift towards Academy and Institution
While learning in hunter-gatherer societies relied on observation, imitation, and self-directed play,
the rise of civilization around 3000 BCE marked a significant turning point in how knowledge was
transmitted. As societies evolved into complex, state-level civilizations—first in Mesopotamia
and Egypt, and later independently in regions such as the Indus Valley, China, and Mesoamer-
ica—the demands of governance, trade, agriculture, and religious organization gave rise to
forms of specialized knowledge that were not directly observable in everyday life: tax systems,
religious doctrines, astronomical calculations, codified laws, and written records, for example.
To transmit these symbolic and abstract systems—especially writing, mathematics, and formal
religious instruction—explicit, structured teaching became essential (Eskelson, 2020).
According to researchers in evolutionary psychology like David Geary, these forms of knowledge
fall under what is termed biologically secondary learning: cognitively demanding, culturally
recent skills such as reading, writing, arithmetic, scientific reasoning, and technical literacy
(Geary and Berch, 2015). Unlike biologically primary abilities—like spoken language, social com-
munication, or motor coordination—which emerge naturally in children immersed in meaningful
social environments, secondary abilities do not develop spontaneously. They require formal,
repetitive, and often effortful instruction over time, typically in settings intentionally designed
for this purpose (Geary and Berch, 2015).
Formal education thus emerged as a necessary institutional response to the evolving needs of
complex societies. It was designed to transmit forms of knowledge that could not be acquired
informally, yet were essential for the functioning of bureaucracies, centralized economies, and
political-religious hierarchies. In essence, schooling arose when the knowledge required to sustain
civilization surpassed what could be learned through everyday experience alone—marking the
beginning of an educational model fundamentally distinct from the one in which our species
4
==PAGE:10==
had originally evolved (Eskelson, 2020).
2.2
Etymology and Educational Ideals
It was within this civilizational context that our modern vocabulary around learning began
to take shape. Even as educational institutions expanded, the core terms we still use today
— education, school, and teaching — retained meanings that were more philosophical and
developmental than strictly functional. From the Latin educare (used from the 1st century BCE),
to educate meant "to lead out" or "to draw forth", suggesting a process of guiding learners toward
the unfolding of their innate potential rather than filling them with content (Harper, 2024a).
The Greek skholé, root of the word school, originally meant "leisure" which later came to signify
the preferred use of free time in Athens or Rome, associated with intellectual discussion and
eventually the place where such dialogue occurred (Harper, 2024c). Teaching, from Old English
tæcan (circa 800 CE), meant "to show" or "to point out", evoking a model of discovery-based
guidance (Harper, 2024d).
These early meanings reflect a time when knowledge was seen as a noble pursuit — something
to be shared with reverence, at the service of the learner, not imposed for efficiency. As Plato
wrote in The Republic (Book III), the aim of education was not merely to prepare individuals
for labor, but to cultivate a love for what is good and beautiful (Platon, 375 BC). This view
aligns more closely with the informal and context-rich learning practices found in small-scale
societies than with the standardized classroom structures that later civilizations would formalize.
It is further echoed in the concept of the academy — from Plato’s Akademia, a garden outside
Athens in the 4th century BCE where philosophical inquiry was pursued for the love of wisdom
(philosophia), to today’s institutional "academy", often driven by competition, credentials, and
measurable outcomes. The divergence between the etymological roots of these foundational
terms and their modern institutional realities reveals the extent to which education has drifted
from its original ideals — exposing a deep tension between inner development and social utility
at the heart of contemporary schooling.
3
Education in the Industrial Age
The emergence of formal education can be therefore traced to the need to manage the growing
complexity of literate and technologically advancing societies. Since the Neolithic Revolution,
as human communities evolved into increasingly organized and hierarchical systems, education
gradually became institutionalized—shaped by the political, economic, and cultural demands of
each era.
3.1
Education Across the First Two Industrial Revolutions
In the end of the 18th century beginning of the 19th, the first Industrial Revolution accelerated
this dynamic to Western Europe, North America, and other industrializing regions (Crafts,
5
==PAGE:11==
1998). Indeed, profound transformations in nearly every domain of life—economic (capital accu-
mulation, the agricultural revolution, expanding markets), political (institutional stabilization,
colonial expansion), social and cultural (the scientific revolution, changing family and labor
structures), and technological (notably the steam engine and mechanization)—reshaped national
infrastructures and called for new forms of human capital (Bowles and Gintis, 1976a,b; Crafts,
1998).
In this context, societies required a new kind of workforce: disciplined, literate, punctual,
and easily trainable—as well as a new kind of citizen: standardized, loyal, productive, and
administratively manageable. To meet these demands, the characteristics of modern education
systems were reshaped accordingly (Carl, 2009).

The emergence of nation-states implied that education systems were developed not merely to
support industrialization but also to serve the political imperative of state-building by forging
unity among diverse populations through a shared identity, language, and moral framework.As
Andy Green has argued, mass schooling was central to the formation of national consciousness
(Green, 1990). Philosophers of the era shed light on the deeper meaning of this movement:
Herder viewed each people (Volk) as possessing a unique cultural soul expressed through language
and tradition (Godfried van Benthem van den Bergh, 2018), while Fichte saw the nation as
a moral and cultural unity, grounded in a shared language and inner spirit that shaped its
historical destiny (Fichte, 2008). On the other hand, Nietzsche warned that nationalism fosters
conformity, describing it as a “herd instinct” that suppresses individuality in favor of obedience
and sameness (Nietzsche, 2007).
While these views address nation-building from within, Martin Carnoy highlights that the global
spread of schooling occurred within the broader context of imperialism and colonialism. He
contends that modern education was shaped by capitalist and mercantilist systems, serving
not to liberate but to shift colonized populations from traditional hierarchies into new forms of
dependency under the imperial metropole (Carnoy, 1977).
Together, these perspectives suggest that the rise of nationalism—and the educational structures
built to sustain it—were not merely political instruments, but reflections of a timeless human
need: the search for identity and purpose through a sense of sovereignty and shared meaning,
with school becoming the instrument par excellence for shaping national spirit and forging
collective unity.
6
==PAGE:12==
Yet, it is important to nuance this argument by recognizing that the expansion of mass schooling
was not solely ideological and coercive. As the 19th century progressed, access to education
broadened considerably, and substantial improvements were made in teacher training and
instructional quality. The period also saw a decline in ecclesiastical influence and a growing
professionalization of the teaching profession (Thompson, 1958). These developments, though
entangled with nation-building agendas, contributed meaningfully to the democratization of
knowledge and laid key foundations for modern public education (Thompson, 1958). Moreover,
as Stephens (1998) points out, although rising literacy and a more disciplined workforce
may have supported industrial growth in certain settings, the relationship between education
and industrialization was neither consistent nor deterministic. Indeed, several countries with
literacy rates comparable to—or even exceeding—Britain’s did not undergo similar industrial
transformations.
3.2
20 th and 21st Century
During the 20th century, the World Wars and then the Cold War intensified the need for national
unity and ideological control. Educational systems absorbed the logic of bureaucratic governance
and technocratic planning, with standardized exams and certifications used to sort individuals
into predefined social and economic roles (Hartman, 2008). Mass schooling expanded rapidly
between 1950 and 1970, driven more by global norms and political pressures than by economic
need (Meyer et al., 1977). In this context, education reinforced loyalty to the state (Meyer
et al., 1977) and played a central role in social reproduction (Bourdieu and Passeron, 1990) —
promoting the cultural norms of dominant classes and legitimizing inequality under the guise
of meritocracy. Academic success depended not only on ability, but also on alignment with
values recognized as culturally legitimate — what Bourdieu called cultural capital (Bourdieu
and Passeron, 1990).
In the 21st century, these dynamics have continued under the pressure of digital capitalism and
market-driven reforms. According to Niesyto (2021), education has increasingly been shaped by
structural logics of quantification, commercialisation, and technological acceleration, prioritizing
employability over autonomy or critical engagement —a shift he refers to as the digital education
model. As Williamson (2020) shows, platforms and performance metrics now act as market
devices that reconfigure higher education around data extraction and labor market alignment.
In this context, digital education is often treated in instrumental terms and rarely implemented
in coherent or holistic ways (Niesyto, 2021). Most strikingly, Shoshana Zuboff further warns that
such transformations reflect the rise of surveillance capitalism, where datafied learning becomes
a site of behavioral prediction and control, undermining autonomy and public purpose (Zuboff,
2015). To further illustrate how these dynamics unfold in educational systems, particularly in
vocational and technological domains, see Appendix A.
While national variations exist, the patterns outlined here reflect dominant trajectories across
many literate and industrial societies. Over time, educational systems have been shaped by
a complex interplay of political, economic, and cultural forces—often prioritizing collective
organization over individual flourishing. Though the notion of "national priority" is gradually
fading in a globalized world, the standardization of knowledge and learning pathways persists.
7
==PAGE:13==
Education is still rarely seen as a space for true emancipation; instead, it continues to train
individuals to perform predetermined roles—perhaps explaining why so many today identify
more with what they do than with who they are.
4
The Consequences of the Formal Educational System
4.1
Forgotten Skills
The often-cited paraphrase— "Schools resemble prisons because they share the same logic:
surveillance, discipline, normalization"—draws on Michel Foucault’s analysis in Discipline and
Punish (1975) (Foucault, 1979), where he examined the shared disciplinary mechanisms under-
pinning modern institutions. While provocative, this framing sharply highlights the paradox
between the contemporary school system and the original meaning of education "to draw out
from within", previously discussed. In antiquity, education was understood as a process of
guiding the learner toward the realization of their potential. . . If we strayed so far from the
original intent it is because modern educational systems often favor a narrow definition of
intelligence—centered on linguistic and logical reasoning.
However, as Howard Gardner has shown, intelligence comes in many forms: musical, bodily-
kinesthetic, interpersonal, naturalistic, and more (Wikipedia contributors, 2025c). Ken Robin-
son has famously argued that schools systematically suppress creativity, curiosity, and the
arts—encouraging conformity rather than innovation (Robinson and Aronica, 2016). Similarly,
Mike Rose warns against romanticizing the intellect while trivializing the body, reminding us
that immense intelligence resides in skilled trades and manual professions (Rose, 2005). From
the lens of critical pedagogy, Paulo Freire denounced the “banking model” of education, in
which learners are treated as passive recipients rather than active agents of transformation,
each carrying lived knowledge (Freire, 1970). Together, these thinkers reveal how contemporary
schooling often fails to recognize, nurture, or celebrate the full richness and variety of human
potential.
4.2
Knowledge provided ’Just in Time’ ?
What is arguably one of the most overlooked challenges in education today is logistical: the
problem of Just-in-Time (JIT) learning. Borrowed from logistics and popularized by Toyota’s
production system, the JIT principle emphasizes delivering exactly what is needed, exactly
when it’s needed, and in precisely the right quantity (Ohno, 1988). In logistics, this principle
works because timing is everything—there’s no point in delivering car parts to a factory months
before they’re used; they’ll clutter the space, become outdated, or be forgotten (Ohno, 1988;
Waters, 2003)
In much the same way, one of education’s core challenges is also a matter of timing. Learning is
most powerful when it happens at the moment of need, relevance, and curiosity. This suggests
that education should deliver knowledge not “just in case” it might one day be useful—or simply
to pass a test or earn a credential—but because it is immediately meaningful in the context of a
8
==PAGE:14==
real project, question, or personal challenge.
The Bottega model of the Renaissance offers an early example of Just-in-Time learning, embed-
ded in collaborative workshops. Apprentices like Leonardo da Vinci or Raphael acquired skills
not through abstract drills, but by contributing to real commissions alongside their masters
(Khan Academy, n.d.). When tasked with a painting or anatomical study, Leonardo had to learn
exactly what the project demanded—be it anatomy or perspective (Educational Renaissance,
2022). Learning was timely and social, driven by concrete demands within shared projects,
which likely enhanced both its relevance and motivational impact.
Similarly, Silicon Valley’s innovation culture mirrors this model: startups cultivate knowledge
through lived experience, not formal instruction. Teams learn by risking, failing, and iterat-
ing—training through adversity in pursuit of relevance and invention, where urgency and utility
replace standardized learning pathways (Hashemi, 2019).
The shortcomings of traditional schooling are evident to anyone who has experienced a system
that frontloads abstract content disconnected from any real context—delivering knowledge years
before it becomes relevant, if ever. This approach often leads to disengagement, rapid forgetting,
and a decline in intrinsic motivation, as learners struggle to connect what they’re being taught
with anything meaningful in their lives.
4.3
Learning is not the result of Teaching
These systemic shortcomings—favoring narrow types of intelligence, delaying relevance, and
suppressing curiosity—may help explain why growing numbers of students are turning away
from traditional education altogether. In the U.S., undergraduate enrollment dropped from
18.1 million to 15.8 million over the past 13 years, while 39% of first-time, full-time bachelor’s
students do not complete their degree within eight years (Melanie Hanson, 2024). While the
causes are multifaceted, such numbers reflect a growing sense that formal education lacks mean-
ingful connection to real life. Increasingly, students are pursuing alternative pathways—seeking
learning that feels timely, aligned with their interests, and responsive to how they actually grow.
Many who leave traditional education succeed—not by abandoning learning, but by finally engag-
ing in ways aligned with their pace, interests, and needs. Programs like the Pirate Academy in
Missouri, which emphasize self-paced, competency-based learning, have demonstrated significant
improvements in academic performance and engagement compared to their peers in traditional
settings (Aurora Institute, 2020). As John Holt wrote, “Learning is not the product of teaching.
Learning is the product of the activity of learners” (Holt and Holt, 1976). When that activity is
stifled—by rigid curricula, irrelevant content, or a lack of purpose—students disengage. Outside
such structures, many rediscover their curiosity—echoed in Mark Twain’s witty remark: “I have
never let my schooling interfere with my education” (Twain, n.d.). In these cases, dropping out
isn’t a failure, but a break from an environment that failed to recognize potential.
Of course, learning often requires teaching—especially in fields like math or science—and
traditional models do work for some, particularly those drawn to abstract thinking. But the
9
==PAGE:15==
deeper question is how to reduce the number of students who leave school feeling it wasn’t real
life. John Dewey challenged this separation, writing that “education is not preparation for life;
education is life itself” (Dewey, n.d.). School should be a space of lived experience—where
creativity, purpose, and agency are cultivated—because every moment of education shapes who
we are becoming, not just what we are preparing to do.
4.4
Proof of Work vs Proof Exam
The long-standing belief that academic performance and formal credentials should take prece-
dence over practical skills, creativity, and the intrinsic joy of learning is increasingly being
challenged by the world of work.
As previously mentionned, Bowles and Gintis interpreted the logic of credentialing as a mech-
anism for reproducing capitalist labor hierarchies—socializing students into compliance with
an unequal economic structure (Bowles and Gintis, 1976a). For much of the 20th century,
institutional degrees—particularly the bachelor’s degree—functioned as the central gateway
to professional opportunity. However, this interpretation has been contested by scholars like
David Reynolds, who argued that the formal curriculum, especially in the British context,
frequently fails to develop the skills sought by employers (1984, as cited in Simply Psychology,
Perera, 2024) and this paradigm is now being even more challenged. According to recent reports
from Caroline Crist (2023), nearly half of U.S. employers are planning to eliminate bachelor’s
degree requirements in 2024. This reflects a growing shift toward skills-based hiring, where
demonstrated ability and relevant experience matter more than educational pedigree. A report
from the Boston Consulting Group (Sagar Goel et al, 2023) further reinforces this trend, showing
that companies are increasingly prioritizing proof of work—real projects, portfolios, and practical
know-how—over proof of exam. This shift suggests a profound rethinking of how talent is
identified and developed, challenging the long-standing dominance of academic credentialism
and opening the door to more diverse, experience-driven pathways into the workforce.
This evolving shift in hiring practices also echoes critiques long voiced by educational theorists.
Scholars like Biesta (2015), Illich et al. (1971), Kohn (1999) or Holt and Holt (1976) have
challenged the overemphasis on credentials, arguing that it reduces education to measurable
outputs while ignoring its ethical and creative dimensions.
4.5
Rise of AI: Redefinition of World Metrics
The growing emphasis on creativity in the workplace is also closely linked to the rapid rise of
Artificial Intelligence (AI). AI is profoundly reshaping the labor market, challenging traditional
indicators of employability and prompting a reevaluation of the skills deemed essential in today’s
professional world (IMF Blog, 2024; US. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2025; The Harvard Gazette,
2025; Tony Blair Institute for Global Change, 2024). Historically, academic training and formal
degrees have served as the primary criteria for assessing a candidate’s potential. However,
AI’s increasing ability to automate tasks—from coding to legal document drafting to customer
service—has significantly reduced the value of these traditional qualifications (Financial Times
Editorial Board, 2025).
10
==PAGE:16==
This shift particularly affects entry-level positions and jobs based on routine and predictable
tasks, whether manual or administrative. As such roles become increasingly automated, com-
panies are placing greater emphasis on both practical AI-related skills and human qualities
that AI has not yet fully replicated or modeled—such as critical thinking, creativity, emotional
intelligence, and autonomy. As a result, there is growing demand for professionals capable of
collaborating with AI to enhance productivity and drive innovation (Alavi and Westerman,
2023; McKinsey Global Institute, 2018).
The uncoming transformation calls for a profound reform of educational and training systems:
there is an urgent need to design programs that emphasize adaptability, interdisciplinary thinking,
and transferable skills, while promoting a culture of lifelong learning. By fostering environments
that support the continuous development of skills, understanding, and applied insight, society
will be better equipped to thrive in a world increasingly shaped by AI.
This need is amplified by the rapid evolution of AI capabilities. While current models already
outperform humans in many narrow tasks, the next wave — known as Artificial General In-
telligence (AGI) — is expected to bring greater autonomy, adaptability, and cross-domain
problem-solving (Tony Blair Institute for Global Change, 2024). Although such systems are still
considered to be years or even decades away, it is important to recognize that they are likely to
eventually surpass both individual and collective human intelligence, raising the prospect of
artificial superintelligence. As AI becomes more general and capable, it is likely to displace many
white-collar jobs traditionally associated with education and expertise. Further information on
the progression of AI capabilities can be found in Appendix B.
More than incremental change, it demands therefore a deep reimagining of how we educate and
prepare individuals for a future that is fluid and uncertain— so that education becomes the
fertile ground from which the possibilities of every other field can emerge and evolve.

Students in Denmark
Having traced the evolution of formal education throughout history, we set out to examine how
it manifests in a contemporary setting. This inquiry led us to Niels Brock Innovation High
School in central Copenhagen, where we designed and delivered a dedicated innovation program
within the curriculum. We worked with five classes totaling 114 students (34 girls and 80 boys,

questions were divided into two blocks administered at different times. While some students
completed both, others only answered one. This produced an average of 87 variables per student,
with minor variation across participants. As the analysis was based on percentages rather than
raw counts, these differences did not compromise reliability.

Another striking insight from the dataset
concerns how students envision their future. A large majority (67%) described aspirations

self-knowledge, purpose, or personal contribution. This reveals the enduring grip of a capitalist
narrative that equates success with material wealth and social status — a narrative that, in
turn, discourages risk-taking and narrows the space for exploring alternative, self-defined life

This trend is further reinforced by social norms, especially potent during adolescence. Students

strategy for belonging and social approval.
Together, these observations reinforce earlier critiques of an ecosystem that tends to amplify
external scripts about what success should look like — a tendency especially influential during
adolescence, when identity is still forming. While such responses may reflect age-typical priorities,
they also point to broader cultural patterns. And yet, despite these dynamics, the dataset also

Denmark’s Educational Shift
Danish education has long balanced two paradigms: the Ger-
man tradition of Bildung, centered on personal development and critical reflection (Olesen, 2010),
and the Anglo-Saxon utilitarian model, which treats education as preparation for employment.
In recent decades, the balance has shifted toward the latter. Indeed, globalization and interna-
tional benchmarks like PISA have pushed education toward standardization and performance
metrics, making the values of Bildung harder to maintain (Borne-Undervisningsministeriet, n.d.).
Education Minister Mattias Tesfaye has introduced initiatives like mandatory sex education
(Borne-Undervisningsministeriet, 2022) to broaden educational aims. Yet many of his reforms,
such as the EPX system and stricter admission criteria, continue to reflect a utilitarian logic
focused on efficiency and accountability (Borne-Undervisningsministeriet, 2024). As a result, the
system is increasingly caught between formation and function and many students, including those
at Niels Brock, seek meaning and authenticity, but face environments centered on measurable
outcomes.
As a result, the system continues to grapple with the tension between formation and function,
leaving students — including those at Niels Brock — aiming to cultivate identity and direction
amid a system centered on quantifiable goals.

Reflection and Overview
The following synthesis outlines the main points covered in this section, offering the necessary
foundation to understand the central issue explored in this thesis.
• From ancient civilizations to the industrial era, education has been deeply shaped by the
political, economic, and cultural needs of its time. Rather than emerging as a natural
response to human learning instincts, the institution of school was historically constructed
to serve specific purposes: training obedient citizens, forming administrative elites, and
producing skilled labor to sustain state power, social order, and economic growth. What we
call ’formal education’ was never designed primarily to cultivate personal growth, curiosity,
or creative exploration — but to organize knowledge in service of society’s prevailing
structures.
• As a result of this historical legacy, the educational system shaped by 19th- and 20th-
century industrial society has barely changed today. Standardization, mass instruction,
and credentialism, which were once effective tools for building industrialized, nation-state
societies, still dominate educational structures. However, these methods inherently reduce
the individual to the mass, prioritizing conformity over personal development and leading
to a widespread neglect of diverse talents and potentials. In the 21st century, this model has
15
==PAGE:21==
become increasingly outdated. There is now a profound mismatch between the structure
of education and the realities of the modern world:
– Globalization has reduced the centrality of nationalism, while education still heavily
emphasizes national narratives.
– Automation and digitalization are replacing academic and routine cognitive labor,
precisely the kinds of skills that traditional schooling prioritized.
– AI can now perform many cognitive tasks (memorization, logical reasoning, informa-
tion retrieval) far faster and more reliably than humans, and is getting exponentially
better at it.
• Learning is universal, teaching is cultural, school is recent (Lancy, 2016). Yet we often
forget how deeply our learning instincts are rooted in our evolutionary past. For more
than 95% of human history, humans learned naturally — through curiosity, necessity,
play, observation, and real-world participation. Our brains evolved for learning that is
contextual, social, and experience-based, not for sitting in classrooms absorbing abstract
content. Modern schooling, however, separates knowledge from immediate relevance,
offering information for an exam or a distant, hypothetical job rather than delivering it
"just in time." This mismatch often leads to boredom, disengagement, and even learned
helplessness. In a world where AI increasingly outperforms humans in routine cognitive
tasks, an urgent question arises: do we still need to learn what AI can perform better?
Learning remains a fundamental human drive; but formal education, as we know it, is
a recent social invention — designed for historical needs that no longer align with the
natural ways humans grow, think, and adapt.
• Importance to recognize a recurring pattern throughout human history: every major
societal transformation has involved both looking forward — through innovation — and
looking backward — by recovering lost forms of wisdom. Early hunter-gatherer societies
and Renaissance Bottegas offer powerful reminders of learning models that were organic,
purposeful, and deeply integrated into real life. Of course, this is not to idealize the past or
ignore the profound injustices that limited access to learning for many. Rather, it invites
us to draw inspiration from systems that, despite their imperfections, fostered creativity,
intrinsic motivation, and human potential in ways that much of modern education struggles
to replicate.
It is important to remind that the critical perspective adopted here does not reject formal
education per se, but seeks to critically assess how its current structures diverge from
our evolved learning predispositions and contemporary societal needs, with the aim of
identifying avenues for constructive reform.
6.1
Thesis Core Statements
These growing tensions between today’s reality and the legacy structures of education suggest
an urgent need to rethink how we conceive learning — not only within schools but across society
more broadly. The rise of AI, which threatens the long-term viability of many traditional profes-
sions, make it imperative to imagine a different kind of society, one that no longer prioritizes

the same measures of success. The question, then, is not whether formal education should have
emerged, but whether its current forms still serve us: what does it truly means to be productive?
Is it the number of hours worked, or the quality and meaning of the work itself? Likewise, the
conventional life trajectory — studying for a diploma, entering the workforce, and eventual

Part II
Theoretical Foundations
By following the evolution of the educational system, we have demonstrated the deep-rooted
influence of this structure, an influence that extends far beyond the boundaries of schooling.
Education, as it has been institutionalized, remains deeply embedded in how we perceive the
world and ourselves: it shapes our life trajectories, guides our choices, affects our sense of satis-
faction, and contributes to the construction of our self-worth. Rethinking learning, therefore, is
not merely a matter of pedagogical reform, but a broader paradigm shift—one that touches on
our relationship to knowledge, success, and identity.
We ended the previous part with two guiding statements that, while rooted in educational
critique, ultimately invite a deeper inquiry into what it means to know oneself, to grow, and to
contribute authentically to society. These statements are difficult to objectify with current scien-
tific tools, and therefore cannot be reduced to quantifiable metrics alone; they belong to a more
nuanced, experiential domain of human understanding —a view also enriched by Carl Rogers’
emphasis on the subjective dimensions of growth and self-discovery (Rogers, 1961). The aim of
this section is to explore the psychological and structural foundations that lend coherence and rel-
evance to the statemens of this work, even if their contours remain, by nature, partly interpretive.
1
Humans are not Isolated Systems
This section explores the fundamental structures that shape how humans perceive the world
and themselves. By presenting these elements as a general framework rooted in shared aspects
of the human condition, we deem to establish a common foundation from which the relevance of
our core statements can later be understood and justified.
1.1
The Fundamental Reality of Influence
The first fundamental reality of the human condition—and the foundation of this inquiry—is
that human beings are not isolated systems. From early childhood, we are shaped by our sur-
roundings, often unconsciously. As Piaget et al. (1952) emphasized, early cognitive development
is deeply tied to environmental input. Even in adulthood, what appears as personal will is
frequently the product of social imitation and prolonged exposure to external models (Bandura
and Walters, 1977; Bourdieu, 2018; Girard and Freccero, 1965).
This influence extends beyond language: interactions convey subtle signals—tone, gestures,
pheromones, heat, sound waves, and more—much of it occurring unconsciously (Watkins, 2021c).
Our environment shapes not only behavior but also self-perception through constant interplay
with social and cultural cues, as explored by Merleau-Ponty (1945) and Foucault (1977). Much
of what we learn, Rumjaun and Narod (2025) emphasizes, arises through observation and
exposure—often passively, rather than through direct instruction.
18
==PAGE:24==
1.2
Describing Human Behavior
If human beings are continuously shaped by the environments they inhabit—absorbing norms,
reacting to change, and adapting through learning—then the question is not whether we change,
but what determines the value and direction of that change. Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
offers a powerful framework for understanding humans not merely as products of social learning
or stimulus-response pairing, but as beings oriented toward growth and optimal development
(Adams et al., 2017b). Even in an interconnected world that constantly acts upon us, individuals
are not passive vessels of influence. SDT shows that learning and change are closely tied to
the pursuit of autonomy, competence, and relatedness—three basic psychological needs that
structure human motivation (Adams et al., 2017a).
When these needs support autonomous motivation—defined as intrinsic motivation and well-
internalized extrinsic motivation (Shogren et al., 2017a)—our actions reflect what SDT calls
causal agency: a process driven by self-initiated behavior, belief in one’s ability, and effective
self-regulation (Shogren et al., 2017b). In this sense, human development is not only reactive
but also intentional, shaped both by external conditions and by the internal dynamics through
which we engage with them. A more detailed account of the key constructs and mechanisms
within SDT can be found in Appendix C.
Figure 1: Self Determination Framework (Shogren et al., 2017a)
Building on the previous discussion, it appears that the human self is continuously shaped through
the ongoing dialogue between external influence and internal autonomy. The neuroscientist
Alan Watkins in his book Coherence: the science of brilliant leadership and performance offers a
structural synthesis of this dynamic through his ’Integrated Performance Model’, demonstrating
how outward results and behavior are shaped by internal processes—moving through layers
of thought, feeling, emotion, and ultimately physiology, many of which operate unconsciously
(Watkins, 2021a).

1.2.1
Two Complementary Models
SDT and Watkins pyramid combined form a cohesive, multi-layered map of human functioning.
The SDT describes why we act — it identifies the psychological needs (autonomy, competence,
relatedness) that fuel motivation and explains how the nature of that motivation (autonomous
vs. external) influences development and performance. SDT also highlights the crucial process
of internalization — the transformation of external influence into personally endorsed values and
goals — making it thus both a theory of motivation and of identity formation and self-regulation.
Watkins’ Integrated Performance Model, on the other hand, explores how the internal system —
especially its hidden or unconscious layers — supports or impairs our ability to act in alignment
with that motivation. It suggests that our nervous system, physiology, emotions, and bodily
sensations play a central role in shaping how we perceive the world, make decisions, and act.
1.2.2
The tension between Influence and Autonomy
As previously discussed, human beings are embedded in a constant web of influence—shaped by
relationships, environments, and cultural norms—yet still capable of acting as causal agents.
Through SDT and the Integrated Performance Model, we saw how such influences interact with
internal systems of motivation, emotion, and physiology to shape behavior and life direction.
SDT helps clarify what humans strive for and how goals can align with psychological needs—but
it also raises a critical question: to what extent are these goals truly self-determined?
When left unexamined, influence can easily distort this alignment, blurring the boundary between
authentic aspiration and social expectation. Individuals may follow goals that feel personal
but are, in reality, shaped by external pressures. In this subtle process, one’s sense of agency
may erode. As Erich Fromm warns in The Fear of Freedom, the very pursuit of freedom can
provoke anxiety, leading many to unconsciously seek relief through conformity—submitting
to public opinion or societal norms under the illusion of choice (Fromm, 2021). In such cases,
even personal goals can be deceptive. Sheldon’s research shows similarly that individuals often
pursue goals that appear personal but are in fact shaped by external pressures rather than
true autonomy (Sheldon and Elliot, 1998). Such goals may stem from societal expectations,
20
==PAGE:26==
internalized obligations, or the subtle drive for approval. When external influence guides our
aims without critical internalization, the results are predictable: diminished vitality, reduced
authenticity, and, as Fromm warns, a gradual erosion of individuality. The issue, then, is not
influence itself—since we are inherently social beings—but whether that influence supports
genuine self-development or quietly substitutes autonomy with conformity disguised as personal
choice.
Thus, humans evolve within a dynamic system of constant action and reaction that continuously
shapes their biological and psychological makeup, influencing how they perceive both the world
and themselves. The dual framework of SDT and Watkins gives structure to this reality: it models
how influence operates across all levels of the human system—from core motivation to embodied
response—and clarifies the conditions under which self-determination can emerge. Since influence
is inescapable, the key becomes learning to transform it into a lever for growth rather than
passively undergoing its effects. We now turn to how self-awareness enables individuals to nav-
igate influence and move toward what genuinely supports their own self-determined development.
2
The Power of Self-Awareness
The previous section outlined a foundational model of human functioning, using the frameworks
of SDT and Watkins to show how we evolve within systems of influence while striving for
self-determination. Yet while these needs and dynamics are universal, their expression is deeply
personal. Everyone seeks autonomy, competence, and connection—but autonomy for what?
Competence in which domain? Relatedness to whom? This is where the core element of our first
statement, self-awareness, becomes essential: it is the key that enables individuals to navigate
these shared structures in ways that reflect their unique path.
2.1
Know Yourself
Throughout history, many thinkers have emphasized that amid constant external influence, the
essential task of the individual is to turn inward. Long before modern psychology, Socrates
made “know yourself” the cornerstone of his philosophy, arguing that without self-knowledge,
freedom is an illusion and moral action impossible. For him, the unexamined life was not merely
incomplete—it was unworthy of being lived (Plato, 1997b). His relentless questioning exposed
how easily people mistake inherited opinions and unconscious desires for personal truth. He
also claimed that self-knowledge underpins all other knowledge: before one can understand
the world, society, or justice, one must first understand oneself (Plato, 1997a). In this light,
the risks posed by external influence and distorted motivation become even clearer. Without
active awareness of their own beliefs, emotions, and drives, individuals risk becoming strangers
to themselves—led by forces they neither recognize nor control. Thus, before any genuine
self-determination—before real change, learning, or growth—there must be the courage and
discipline to look within. This ancient insight marks the starting point for understanding both
the power and necessity of self-awareness.

Justification First Statement
“When people have a deeper understanding of themselves (self-awareness), they gain clarity on
their priorities and make better decisions that align with who they genuinely are/ want to become
— allowing them to pursue what they love and learn more effectively.”
While many remain caught in inherited beliefs and expectations, developing greater self-awareness
becomes essential for real transformation. Its absence directly affects how the two frameworks
introduced earlier interact. Take, for example, someone who has swum competitively since
childhood, driven by parental encouragement and external rewards. As an adult, they may claim
to love swimming, but they no longer feel any joy or intrinsic motivation — only a lingering
sense of obligation or identity tied to the role.
A more common example is when our beliefs don’t align with our actions—such as valuing
compassion, yet reacting with impatience under stress. This reflects a clear mismatch between
the “thinking” and “behavior” layers in Watkins’ model.
Watkins argues that true growth stems not from accumulating knowledge or skills—what he calls
horizontal development—but from vertical shifts in consciousness that reorient and deepen all
other learning. Higher consciousness enables a clearer understanding of oneself and of external
influences, helping restore coherence between deeper layers like emotion and physiology. This
expanded awareness also reshapes the SDT framework by clarifying the boundary between
intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. As a result, individuals engage in different causal actions,
ultimately reshaping the entire SDT cycle and redefining the way individuals understand and
construct their intrinsic identity.
Recent research further supports this view by showing that the ability to track and understand
one’s own emotional patterns has clear implications for social success. Barrick et al. (2024)
demonstrate that individuals with greater insight into their emotional experiences and more
typical emotion transitions are significantly better at predicting others’ emotional states. This
predictive skill, grounded in both internal awareness and external sensitivity, is linked to reduced
loneliness, deeper social bonds, and a more coherent sense of self in relationships.
Developing self-awareness is therefore not an end in itself, but a gateway: it allows individuals to

clarify what truly matters to them, which in turn lays the foundation for genuine, self-sustained
motivation. This brings us to our second core statement, focused on the power of intrinsic
motivation.
4
Justification Second Statement
“When individuals are intrinsically motivated, they naturally pursue and master what they love
without external pressure. In doing so, they are more likely to achieve excellence and create
meaningful value for society.”
1. “When individuals are intrinsically motivated, they naturally pursue and master
what they love without external pressure [...]”
As a matter of fact, when individuals engage in activities they truly love, they are much more
likely to experience what Csikszentmihalyi defined as flow: a psychological state characterized
by complete absorption in the task, the lost of sense of time, and the loss of self-consciousness
(Nakamura et al., 2002). In flow, external rewards or pressures become irrelevant because the
activity itself becomes intrinsically rewarding, Csikszentmihalyi explaining that during that
state there is no worry of failure (Csikszentmihalyi et al., 1997). The conditions that lead to flow
— such as a balance between challenge and personal skill, clear goals, and immediate feedback
— are naturally created when a person pursues something they are deeply passionate about. In
such moments, motivation is entirely self-sustained: individuals continue learning, practicing,
and pushing themselves not because they are forced to, but because the activity itself is deeply
rewarding and naturally fuels creativity (Csikszentmihalyi et al., 1997).
That said, flow is not a constant state — and its absence does not necessarily mean the activ-
ity lacks meaning or authenticity. Even deeply fulfilling pursuits include phases of difficulty,
frustration, or repetition that don’t always evoke the feeling of effortless immersion. Still,
the difference lies in the source of persistence: when the underlying motivation is intrinsic,
individuals are more likely to endure those moments and return to the activity with renewed focus.
Therefore, when someone is engaged in what they love, they require no external pressure to
persist, improve, or excel; their internal experience of flow — when it arises — continually drives
them forward. This intrinsic cycle, where love of the activity fuels attention and performance,
and where effort often feels natural or even joyful, stands in stark contrast to activities imposed
from the outside, where constant external incentives are needed to sustain engagement.
2.“[. . . ] In doing so, they are more likely to achieve excellence and create meaningful
value for society.”
When individuals engage in activities they truly love, they naturally activate the psychological
conditions necessary for sustained high achievement. Angela Duckworth’s research on grit — the
combination of passion and perseverance — demonstrates that long-term success is not simply
a matter of talent, but the result of consistent effort applied over time. Duckworth formalizes
this in her equations of achievement, showing that skill results from talent and effort (talent ×
24
==PAGE:30==
effort = skill), and that achievement results from skill and additional effort (skill × effort =
achievement) (Duckworth, 2017). Loving what one does directly fuels this equation: intrinsic
passion makes sustained effort feel natural rather than forced, reducing reliance on external
pressure and nurturing a willingness to learn and persist through challenges (Duckworth and
Eskreis-Winkler, 2013). Of course, what we love is not fixed — it can deepen over time of
completely shift. This is why awareness of one’s internal landscape remains essential: it allows
individuals to recognize when their direction still aligns with their values and when it may need
to change.
(Duckworth and Eskreis-Winkler, 2013)
As individuals deepen their skill through persistent, self-driven practice, they position themselves
to achieve excellence and create real value for society. Far from needing constant incentives or
supervision, those who are internally motivated demonstrate greater resilience, more consistent
focus and a higher likelihood of deliberate practice (Duckworth and Eskreis-Winkler, 2013;
Wehmeyer et al., 2018) — the type of hard, targeted effort that leads to outstanding performance
(Duckworth and Eskreis-Winkler, 2013). In short, when passion drives action, the pathways to
mastery and societal contribution open naturally, without requiring external coercion.

Part II - Conclusion
The aim of this second part was to provide an explanatory foundation for concepts that, while
central to our thesis, are not always easily defined or objectively measured — such as the nature
of motivation, the meaning of “loving” an activity, or what truly distinguishes intrinsic from
extrinsic desire. These notions often lie at the intersection of psychology, phenomenology, and
philosophy — and yet they shape real decisions, behaviors and life trajectories.
We began by examining the pervasive role of influence in human development, showing that
individuals are continuously shaped by their environments, often unconsciously. This led us
to explore two complementary frameworks: Self-Determination Theory, which describes the
architecture of the intentional self — the reflective part that seeks meaning, autonomy, and
growth; and Watkins’ model, which reveals the deeper layers of the embodied self — the
physiological and emotional systems that must be coherent for the intention to be sustained in
action. Together, these models offer a coherent view of human functioning under influence, that
is both structured and dynamic. In this context, self-awareness emerges as a crucial mediator: it
enables individuals to observe and regulate the impact of external forces on their inner systems,
helping them clarify what truly drives them from within. This, in turn, supports the pursuit of
authentic, self-determined goals.
Grounding these abstract concepts in a shared psychological explanation finally allowed to
justify the two statements at the heart of this thesis by showing how they take shape within
coherent and interrelated human processes.

Part III
Methodology & Framework
Part II of this work highlighted self-awareness as a fundamental lever for cultivating intrinsic
motivation, finding coherence, and ultimately reaching personal fulfillment. In this third part,
we turn to a more practical inquiry: how can individuals be supported in developing this
self-awareness?
1
Gaining Perspective
1.1
Defining Perspective
To support the development of self-awareness, we must first understand what shapes how
individuals perceive and interpret reality. At the heart of this inquiry lies the concept of
perspective. Etymologically derived from the Latin perspectiva, meaning “science of optics” or
"seeing through", the term originally referred to the art of representing depth on a flat surface
(Harper, 2024b). Over time, it has come to represent a fundamental aspect of perception —
shaped by one’s inner orientation and broader philosophical outlook (American Psychological
Association, 2023). It involves:
• The ability to mentally organize objects, ideas, or events in relation to one another —
interpreting size, depth, or significance from our position in space and time (American
Psychological Association, 2023).
• The capacity to account for one’s own perceptions, behaviors or attitudes — as well as to
imagine or grasp those of others (American Psychological Association, 2023).
• A distinct lens through which one interprets events or situations — reflecting a personal
stance or underlying worldview (American Psychological Association, 2023).
As discussed in Part II, the human mind is in constant interaction with the surrounding environ-
ment: what we perceive does not solely depend on what is outwardly present or what we might
spontaneously label as objective, it also depends on how we are connected to that environment
— in other words, on our perspective.
To better grasp this, refer to Figure 3: two persons A and B are standing side by side, facing the
same object. And yet, what they perceive is fundamentally different: they are not standing in
exactly the same spot in space and each of them sees through the lens of their own psychological
structure — that is the Watkins iceberg previously mentioned, which models the internal layers
of human experience. Therefore, those two persons see the same object but do not observe the
same reality.
Furthermore, Watkins’ integrated model is constantly being updated. Most changes occur subtly
and unconsciously—small perceptual shifts shaped by daily experience. Occasionally, however, a

meaningful interaction or disruptive event triggers a deeper reconfiguration: a shift in perspective.
These two dynamics—gradual transformation and sudden reinterpretation—coexist within us,
making it unlikely that any two people ever perceive reality in exactly the same way. Ultimately,
our perspective shapes not only how we see the world and others, but also how we understand
ourselves and our role within it. Over time, these evolving perceptions contribute to what
psychology calls narrative identity — the internalized, dynamic story we construct about who we
are (McAdams, 2018). It is not merely a record of events, but a meaningful interpretation shaped
by the perspectives we have held and the significance we’ve assigned to them throughout our lives.
1.2
The Power of a New Perspective
Common expressions like “taking a step back” or “gaining perspective” reflect our intuitive
sense that certain experiences can shift how we see ourselves and the world. Yet, as previously
noted, not all new stimuli lead to greater self-awareness. Although we’re constantly exposed to
external input, only certain interactions truly resonate — those that reorganize the emotional
and cognitive links that shape meaning.
Such shifts in perspective may arise from a conversation, a book, a chance encounter, or more
profoundly, an existential crisis. Kenneth Gergen’s theory of the relational self emphasizes
that self-understanding is shaped through social interaction. Encountering other perspectives
introduces alternative narratives and values, expanding our mental map. In this way, others
become both mirrors and catalysts — reflecting who we are while opening space for new ways
of being (Aceros, 2012). This echoes Vygotsky’s view that psychological phenomena are fun-
damentally social: thought is never entirely independent. Memory, belief, even repression, are
shaped by the cultural contexts and interactions in which they emerge (Vygotsky and Cole, 1978).
Whether relational, intellectual, or existential, such interactions often prompt us to reassess
core beliefs, uncover hidden aspects of self, and revise the stories we live by. They invite deeper
reflection and support the evolution of our narrative identity — allowing for a reorganization of

meaning that brings greater coherence to lived experience.
1.3
Building a Reminder
The central question of this section is now the following: how can we optimize the creation
of those mental connections which, linked together, form a perspective? "Optimizing" in this
work involves avoiding existential dead-ends, conserving mental energy and reducing the costs
— psychological, social, or economic — associated with poorly aligned choices.
Thus considering that certain perspectives — when encountered at the right moment can act
as profound existential turning points, we introduce the concept of the reminder: a concise,
targeted formulation designed to reactivate a personal truth that has been forgotten, overlooked,
or not yet fully integrated. Rather than revealing something entirely new, a reminder reframes
what the individual is already on the verge of recognizing — a truth in gestation. Its power
lies in timing and resonance: it must align with what the person is currently living, feeling, or
intuitively sensing. Delivered too soon or too bluntly, it risks being rejected.
The following parts of this work are dedicated to the conceptualization and construction of these
reminders. What will be presented is a first iteration — one that may not always reach the
theoretical ideal of precision and resonance, but that lays the groundwork for a more adaptive,
personalized approach to fostering meaningful inner shifts.
Thus, the focus of this thesis is to explore the feasibility of designing and delivering such
reminders by detailing the conceptual foundations that support them, along with the type of
data required to make them personally relevant and effective. Part IV will then outline the
technical implementation and present the obtained results, allowing us to assess the effectiveness
of these reminders as tools for personal alignment and transformation.
2
The Layers
2.1
Conceptualizing a Layer
To move from abstract insight to the concrete design of reminders, we must first understand how
to tailor them to the individual’s unique psychological makeup. This requires examining the
deeper architecture of subjectivity — the forces shaping how experience is processed, interpreted,
and integrated. While perspective arises from a complex interplay of emotional, cognitive, and
relational dynamics, it can be broken down into foundational structures that shape how reality
is perceived and lived.
This brings us to the concept of a layer: a structural dimension of subjectivity, offering a specific
entry point into how a person internally operates. Each layer described in the following sections
reflects a distinct way of organizing experience — how one makes sense of life, regulates emotion,
maintains coherence, responds to pain, or opens (or resists) transformation. Together, they
provide a multidimensional view of how a perspective is formed and how it might evolve.

==PAGE:34==
Each layer was designed to be conceptually clear and technically usable. To support the applied
goals of this thesis, layers are divided into subcategories that reflect distinct psychological
functions or tendencies. This structure enables systematic analysis and adaptability for technical
applications, while still aligning with the broader definition of each layer.
2.2
Meaning Making System
The first explored layer is the meaning-making system — how people construe, understand,
and give significance to life events, relationships, and the self (Wikipedia contributors, 2025d).
Psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor Viktor Frankl, a central figure in existential psychology,
argued that the search for meaning is humanity’s core motivation and key to survival (Frankl,
1985). Echoing this, Nietzsche famously observed that "he who has a why to live for can bear
almost any how" (Nietzsche ,1889, as cited in (Adhiya-Shah, 2016)). Meaning offers orientation in
the face of suffering and organizes identity and experience. Psychologist Jerome Bruner likewise
emphasized that meaning is the central concept of psychology—and that each individual’s task
is to make their own (Bruner, 2003).
Crystal Park’s Meaning-Making Model distinguishes between global meaning—our core beliefs,
values, and life goals—and situational meaning, which reflects how we interpret specific events
through that broader lens. When the two conflict, we adapt by reframing the event (assimila-
tion) or revising our worldview (accommodation), often resulting in a shift in how meaning is
experienced (Park, 2013) (detailed overview provided in Appendix E). While Park focuses on
cognitive resolution, McAdams emphasizes that meaning-making unfolds through narrative: we
shape experiences into evolving life stories that integrate global and situational meaning into
a coherent self-concept (McAdams, 2018). Though everyone develops a narrative identity, its
depth and form vary. As Maddi argued, meaning is not discovered but actively created—shaped
by each person’s capacity for symbolism, imagination, and judgment (Maddi, 1970, as cited in
(Merriam and Heuer, 1996)).
Building on narrative identity, writer Bruce Feiler offers a practical view of how meaning is
shaped in life stories. Based on hundreds of interviews, he identifies three core pillars: Agency,
Belonging, and Cause. Agency reflects the drive to act intentionally and influence one’s life;
Belonging centers on connection, acceptance, and emotional safety; and Cause expresses the
desire to serve a purpose beyond oneself (Feiler, 2020). Most people unconsciously lean on one or
two of these anchors, and this balance shapes how meaning is formed—or lost—over time. Life
transitions can disrupt or realign these elements, revealing imbalances or potential for growth.
These pillars provide a valuable lens on meaning-making and will inform our definition of the
meaning-making layer.
As previously noted, defining subcategories within this layer is essential for its technical ap-
plication in the model. Drawing on foundational research in meaning-making and narrative
psychology, we identify distinct tendencies that reflect different meaning-making styles. The
following taxonomy synthesizes these perspectives into a functional tool for recognizing an
individual’s dominant mode of meaning-making, which can then guide targeted interventions
within the system.

Achievement Focused
People who score highly in this category ground meaning primarily
in agency: fulfillment comes from setting and reaching goals, demonstrating competence, and
building a coherent self through measurable progress (Feiler, 2020). Here, meaning is tied to one’s
effectiveness, mastery, and personal success within the world. This resonates with Nietzsche’s
concept of the will to power — understood here as the individual’s drive for self-mastery and
control (Nietzsche, 1910, as cited in Merriam and Heuer, 1996) — and with Viktor Frankl’s view
that the first of the main routes to find meaning is by creating a work or deed (Viktor Frankl,
1946, as cited in Merriam and Heuer, 1996).
Connection-based
Derives meaning through belonging: significance is found in nurturing
relationships, fostering emotional closeness, and participating in shared life with others. For
these individuals, meaning arises when they feel seen, valued, and connected within families,
friendships, or communities (Feiler, 2020). For Frankl, loving something or someone was the
second main route to find meaning in life itself (Viktor Frankl, 1946, as cited in Merriam
and Heuer, 1996). Dunlop et al. (2017) demonstrated how individuals’ love life narratives —
particularly those rich in communal themes — were closely tied to markers of psychological
well-being and romantic functioning, underscoring the central role of intimate connection in
meaning-making.
Purpose-Driven
Life gains coherence when one’s actions align with core values, serve a larger
purpose, or contribute to transformation beyond the self. Individuals with this orientation are
often motivated by the sense that they can make a difference — whether through legacy, service,
or moral alignment. This form of meaning reflects a fusion of agency and cause, as described by
Feiler, where action is not only self-initiated but also directed toward something greater than
the individual (Feiler, 2020).
Existential/Spiritual
This category reflects a tendency to seek meaning through transcen-
dence—aligning with beliefs, values, or experiences that go beyond the personal or material.
As Bugental suggests, meaning may arise simply from being: presence, awareness, or spiritual
connection (Bugental, 1976, as cited in (Krug, 2009)). Frankl identified the third path to
meaning as the attitude one takes toward unavoidable suffering, famously stating: “Everything
can be taken from a man but one thing. . . to choose one’s attitude in any given set of circum-
stances” (Frankl, 2006). While similar to the purpose-driven mode, this orientation operates on
a different level—less about achieving and more about confronting existence itself: mortality,
mystery, and the sacred. Where purpose-driven meaning centers on doing, existential meaning
emerges through being—via acceptance, surrender, or unity with something larger than the self.
Individuals with this outlook are drawn to questions of purpose, morality, or transcendence,
locating meaning in what feels ultimate, eternal, or spiritually grounded.
Fragmented/ Survival-based
This category represents a disrupted or pre-integrated state
of meaning-making, often emerging in response to trauma, loss, or destabilizing events. In such
cases, individuals may struggle to maintain coherence, resulting in reactive, compartmentalized
forms of meaning that fail to integrate into a stable narrative (Park, 2013; Kring et al., 2024).
While this condition may intersect with the existential dimension—particularly in its confronta-
tion with suffering—it reflects a more fragile or unresolved stage, where global beliefs have been

shaken but not yet reconstructed.
These meaning systems reflect the different ways individuals make sense of their lives — some
more reactive and short-term, others more integrated or guided by long-term purpose. The
model is not designed to diagnose fixed identities, but rather to assess the relative prominence
of each style through self-report. It offers a structured overview of how people interpret their
experiences, adapt to challenges, and construct a sense of direction or significance. The drive
for meaning lies at the core of our psychological architecture, as emphasized by numerous
foundational thinkers. While it is likely impossible to capture this process in its full complexity
for every individual, this work aims to approximate it — however partially — by providing a
framework for understanding how meaning takes shape.
2.3
Pain Categories
While the Meaning-Making layer explores how individuals build coherence and purpose in their
lives, the Pain Categories layer focuses on what disrupts or distorts that process. This layer
maps the core forms of psychological suffering that emerge when a person’s basic human needs
are unmet.
To frame this, we return to Self-Determination Theory (SDT), previously discussed in Part
II, which identifies three core psychological needs — Autonomy, Competence, and Relatedness
— as essential to healthy functioning. But what happens when these needs are persistently
unmet? In this Pain Categories section, we propose that psychological pain can be understood
as a signal of motivational disruption — a rupture between the self and its fundamental needs.
The three categories that follow are logically derived from the internal structure of SDT itself,
outlining how each need is transformed when chronically thwarted. These forms of pain are
further contextualized through mechanisms from Causal Agency Theory, which help explain
how such experiences unfold and take root in real life.
Autonomy related pain
This pain arises when individuals feel that their choices, actions,
or direction in life are imposed from the outside rather than freely chosen. The core need for
autonomy — the sense of being the author of one’s own life — is blocked. This may happen
through social pressure, cultural expectations, or internalized obligations that override authentic
desire. Autonomy related pain is often driven by two key mechanisms: a failure to initiate
action based on personal goals (“volitional action” disruption) and the absence of advocacy or
planning that supports meaningful alternatives (“agentic action” disruption). The result is a
pervasive experience of pressure, internal conflict, and entrapment, as if one is acting out a
script written by someone else. Examples can include staying in a career path chosen by family,
remaining in a relationship out of guilt or duty, or feeling paralyzed by what others expect. In
all cases, the self feels misaligned, as if its true voice has been silenced or sidelined.
31
==PAGE:37==
Competence related pain
This pain emerges when individuals perceive themselves as
ineffective, incapable, or failing, despite effort. The basic need for competence — to feel effective
and masterful in one’s environment — is undermined. Over time, this erosion of agency can
deeply impact confidence, motivation, and resilience. The psychological mechanisms at play
often involve a breakdown in action–control beliefs (Internal beliefs about one’s capacity to
achieve goals — including control expectancy, agency, and understanding the link between
actions and outcomes) and a diminished ability to persist or adapt in response to challenges
(’agentic action’ failure). The lived experience is one of shame, inadequacy, and helplessness —
as if one is falling short in ways that feel personal and permanent.
Such experiences may arise in contexts like persistent failure in academic or professional envi-
ronments, being micromanaged or subtly undermined, or growing up in chaotic settings where
outcomes felt arbitrary and disconnected from one’s efforts.
Relatedness related pain
This form of pain is rooted in disconnection and emotional exclu-
sion. The need for relatedness — to feel seen, valued, and emotionally safe with others — is left
unmet. This can occur through neglect, rejection, or the absence of mutual recognition in one’s
social world. The pain of disconnection often builds through two interwoven processes: social
roles that are performed without authentic engagement or choice, and the slow internalization
of beliefs that one is unworthy of love or care. Over time, the person may withdraw or go
numb, even as they deeply long for connection. Phenomenologically, this pain often appears as
loneliness, emotional numbness, and a quiet yearning for contact — sometimes hidden behind
defenses like over-independence or cynicism.
This form of disconnection can take shape in emotionally neglectful relationships, unbalanced or
one-sided friendships, or social environments that prioritize image and performance over genuine
connection.
Together, these three categories are proposed as a conceptual lens for interpreting psychological
pain — aimed at identifying which unmet needs may be signaled beneath the surface of distress.
By mapping suffering back to a frustrated core need, this framework seeks to clarify the emo-
tional structure of pain and orient the path toward restoration — whether through autonomy,
competence, or relatedness.
2.4
Defense Mechanisms
This third layer addresses defense mechanisms—unconscious mental processes that distort
thoughts, feelings, or perceptions to protect against anxiety from internal or external stressors
(Wikipedia contributors, 2025a). Though often viewed negatively, they are not inherently
pathological; many serve as adaptive strategies for navigating complex inner and outer worlds.
First introduced by Sigmund Freud as responses to internal conflict, the concept was later
expanded by Anna Freud to include protection from emotional distress more broadly (Porcerelli
and Grabowski, 2023). Building on this, George Vaillant proposed a hierarchy of defenses
based on psychological maturity, showing that more adaptive mechanisms correlate with greater
32
==PAGE:38==
mental well-being. Nancy McWilliams further reframed defenses as integral to personality struc-
ture, highlighting their developmental role and variability across different levels of functioning
(McWilliams, 2011).
To define this layer, the combined contributions of Anna Freud, Vaillant, and McWilliams
provide a nuanced theoretical foundation. From this, a selection of mechanisms was identified
as both meaningful and assessable within a self-reflective framework. Focus is placed on Level
III (neurotic) and Level IV (mature) defenses from Vaillant’s hierarchy—those most relevant to
psychologically functional adults and accessible through structured self-report (see Appendix F).
Research shows that these defenses become more common with age and are linked to greater
psychological adjustment, while Levels I and II are excluded due to their clinical nature and
limited applicability (Porcerelli and Grabowski, 2023).
Secondary defenses described by McWilliams are also incorporated. While Freud viewed de-
fenses as ego functions, McWilliams and Vaillant expanded this understanding, framing them
as dynamic components of the self. This broader perspective supports the identification of
mechanisms that are both theoretically robust and practically measurable. Full definitions of
the selected defenses are provided in Appendix F.

This restructuring is based on two main considerations. First, defense mechanisms often overlap
in function and expression, making it more informative to view them as broader defensive styles
rather than isolated operations. For example, intellectualization and isolation of affect both
reduce emotional vulnerability through cognitive distancing and are better understood as part

of listing mechanisms definitively and the value of organizing them by maturity, cognitive
complexity, or function (Porcerelli and Grabowski, 2023).
Second, adapting theory for self-report data and algorithmic modeling requires more functional
groupings. Defense mechanisms were preferred over coping strategies because they represent
stable, unconscious emotional regulation patterns—more reflective of personality structure than
situational responses. Unlike coping, which is conscious and context-dependent, defenses reveal
enduring ways of managing inner conflict (Silverman and van Doorn, 2023). This trait-like
quality makes them particularly useful for identifying psychological tendencies that can in-
form personalized reminders and complement the temperament data from the 16 Personality Test.
These functional clusters help the model detect patterns of resistance and frame insights in
terms of dominant emotional regulation styles—enhancing the likelihood that messages will
resonate and bypass habitual defenses. Limitations remain: defense mechanisms rarely operate
in isolation and often appear in hybrid or context-dependent forms that resist strict classification.
Given the self-report nature of the data, the aim is not diagnostic precision, but estimating the
relative prominence of each defense type in participants’ responses.
2.5

or has the potential to develop when facing complexity, challenge, or change. The idea is to
approach the concept of strength as a central lens for navigating experience, rather than limiting
interpretation to observable skills or traits. This includes not only what is already active, but
what is aspiring, emerging, or waiting to be developed. Indeed, we aim to consider:
• What kinds of intelligence, energy, or perspective the person instinctively trusts or aligns
with
• What they reach for in moments of difficulty, growth, or transformation
• What they admire, idealize, or feel moved by in others—whether through role models,
favorite stories, or aspirational identities

2.6
Growth vs Fixet Mindset
This final layer concerns a person’s psychological readiness for transformation—how they relate
to change, challenge, and discomfort. It reflects their openness to questioning assumptions,
confronting inner tension, and adopting new perspectives. Some may need emotional safety
before engaging with new ideas; others more readily embrace dissonance and reframe their
narratives. This layer helps determine whether a reminder is likely to resonate or overwhelm.
The framework builds on Carol Dweck’s concept of fixed vs. growth mindset. Individuals with
36
==PAGE:42==
a fixed mindset see traits like intelligence or emotional capacity as static, leading them to avoid
challenges and resist change that threatens identity (Dweck, 2016). Those with a growth mindset
believe abilities develop through effort and resilience; they embrace struggle as a sign of learning,
not failure (Dweck, 2016, 2015).
This thesis refines the binary into a spectrum, ranging from resistance to deep integration. At
one end is the Defensive Fixed stance, where reflection or change feels threatening. Next is
Fragile Fixed, where growth is desired but constrained by fear or shame. Transitional stages
include Transitional and Emerging Growth, where individuals are learning to face discomfort
and believe in change. Finally, Solid Growth and Integrated Growth describe those for whom
transformation is a way of being—welcoming challenge, seeking truth, and staying adaptable.

A Multi-Dimensional Dataset
The data used in this study was collected through a comprehensive questionnaire completed
anonymously by 65 participants, ranging in age from 22 to 81 years old. They were personally
known to the authors due to the reflective and time-intensive nature of the survey, represented
a diverse demographic: 26 men and 34 women from 19 countries across Europe, North America,
Latin America, and Asia.
The survey, which took approximately 45–50 minutes to complete, was designed to explore a
wide range of life dimensions. It included the 16 Personality Test and 51 structured questions,
grouped into the following thematic categories:
• General (gender, age, core values, core priorities)
• Background Information (cultural, socio-economic history, family dynamics)
• Interests (hobbies/strengths, role models)
• Education, Jobs, and Achievement
• Obstacles and Milestones
• Relationship to Change
• Curiosity and Motivation Triggers
• Sense of Identity
The questionnaire combined multiple-choice items with open-ended prompts and reflection-based
quotes. This hybrid format was intentionally chosen to balance structure and depth: closed
questions enabled efficient data processing and cross-respondent comparison, while open-ended
answers provided richer insights into individual experience. The inclusion of quotes acted as
a projective tool to elicit more authentic reflection—often revealing implicit beliefs, internal
tensions, or aspirational values that may not emerge through direct questioning.
Such a format introduces potential biases — including social desirability, interpretation vari-
ability, or emotional accessibility depending on the respondent’s current state. Nonetheless,
the diversity of formats helped mitigate these effects by inviting multiple modes of expression,
thereby enhancing the variability and usability of the data for constructing the layers. It also
allowed for a degree of methodological exploration — making it possible to observe which types
or clusters of questions proved most effective in revealing meaningful patterns, and which formats
were more predictive or informative for future applications, including but not limited to the
generation of tailored reminders.

2
Experiments: Generation of Psychological Reminders
This section presents the experimental setup designed to transform raw psychological survey data
into reflective reminders, using a language model–based inference pipeline. The core technical
paradigm is known as automated profile inference and personalized generation using large
language models (LLMs). This approach falls under the broader category of human-centered
natural language processing (NLP), where the goal is not only to classify or predict but to
understand and mirror psychological nuance for individual benefit.
Our aim is twofold: first, to extract interpretable psychological profiles from narrative self-
report data; second, to generate a short, tailored textual reminder that reflects and reinforces a
participant’s internal strengths, values, and developmental posture.
Figure 5: Overview of our experimental pipeline. First, participant layer profiles are inferred from survey
responses via structured LLM prompting. Then, these profiles and selected answers are used to prompt the
LLM to generate a personalized reminder.
We introduce key technical terms 1 related to LLMs that are used throughout this section:
• Token: A token is the basic unit of text processed by LLMs. Tokens may correspond to
characters, subwords, words, or punctuation—depending on the tokenizer’s strategy (e.g.,
Byte-Pair Encoding or WordPiece). During inference, the input text is split into a sequence
of tokens, these are processed by the model to generate the output token-by-token .
• Large Language Model (LLM): A neural network trained on vast amounts of textual data
to model the probability distribution of sequences of tokens. The model used in this study,
LLaMA 3.1–8B Instruct by Meta (2024), has approximately 8 billion parameters and is
optimized for instruction-following via supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning
from human feedback (RLHF).
• Prompt: A structured textual input to the LLM that specifies the task, context, and
expected output format. The LLM usually generates text to continue the prompt or to
respond to it in a conversation-style with the user. A description of the prompt recipe
used for the inference of individuals’ layer profiles is described in Figure 5.
• Context Length: The maximum number of tokens an LLM can process as input during
inference. It defines the span of text (including the prompt and any prior conversation or
1We recall standard teminology as found in impactful papers and highly-cited surverys (Zhao et al., 2025;
Minaee et al., 2025; Meta, 2024).

document history) that the model can condition on when generating output. Exceeding
the context length results in truncation of earlier tokens, which are then excluded from
the model’s attention. Therefore, the context length of a model must be known and
respected in order to carefully construct prompts that include all relevant information
without omitting crucial input.
• Inference or Generation: The use of an LLM to produce a structured or semantically
meaningful output in response to a prompt. This process involves sampling text from the
model by iteratively selecting the most likely next token (word) according to the model’s
learned probability distribution over sentences.
Validity of Using LLMs for Psychological Inference.
LLMs are trained to model sta-
tistical patterns across large and diverse language corpora, enabling them to infer context,
interpret tone, and map text to semantically meaningful constructs (Zhao et al., 2025). However,
they do not possess internal psychological models, diagnostic criteria, or theoretically grounded
interpretive frameworks. They are not guided by structures such as the DSM-5, ICD-11, or
validated psychometric theory (Peters and Matz, 2024). In this sense, they do not “understand”
psychology but simulate coherence through probabilistic associations (Wikipedia contributors,
2025b).
Despite this limitation, when constrained by theory-informed prompts and grounded in partici-
pant responses, LLMs can serve as tools for automated reflective synthesis: distilling narrative
self-report data into structured hypotheses about psychological traits. In our experiments, we
construct our prompts following best-practices identified by the reasearch community (Wei
et al., 2023; Amatriain, 2024; Hewing and Leinhos, 2024) - these practices, such as including
factual data in context, or asking the model for rationales, aim to reduce LLM hallucination and
improve their reasoning. Hese inferences are not diagnostic but may offer useful, interpretable
representations for exploratory or educational contexts.
Interpretability remains a central challenge. Existing techniques—such as attention attribution,
saliency scores, and input influence tracing (e.g., SHAP, Integrated Gradients)—offer insight
into decision processes, but do not scale well to multi-thousand-token prompts and are often
opaque when applied to large decoder-only models (Zhao et al., 2023; Luo and Specia, 2024).
Moreover, the generative nature of LLMs introduces stochasticity: the same input may yield
divergent outputs depending on sampling settings.
To address these limitations, our pipeline emphasizes semantic transparency:
1. Prompts are structured and interpretable, making the model’s task framing explicit.
2. Output rationales are required for every score or label, supporting qualitative inspection.
3. Full prompt-response logs are retained for post hoc analysis and replication.
While this does not constitute formal explainability, it provides reproducibility and interpretive
access—adequate for exploratory psychological research where human oversight remains central.

2.1
Experimental Setup
We describe the choice of LLM for our experiments, as well as our overall layer and reminder
inference workflow.
Choice of Model.
The choice of LLM is critical to ensuring that the inference process yields
coherent, faithful, and psychologically relevant outputs. We describe the main factors to consider
when choosing a model for any personal application, these factors generally reflect a trade-off
between performance (which comes with greater model sizes), and ease of personal use (with
limited computational resources).
• Model scale: Larger LLMs demonstrate better reasoning skills on benchmarks, a wider
basis of knowledge, as well as better instruction-following (Meta, 2024; Zhao et al., 2025).
• Context length: Our prompts are relatively large, as they contain dozens of survey answers,
and additional guidance text - these often exceed thousand tokens. This requires the
model to be capable of attending across long contexts without degradation (Zhao et al.,
2025).
• Instruction tuning: Since our application depends on the model reliably following task
instructions (e.g., selecting a category, explaining rationale), we chose a model that
was explicitly fine-tuned for instruction-following rather than open-ended generation.
Additionally, we require a fully open-source model that is easily deployable (Minaee et al.,
2025).
Given our computational resources, we converged on the Llama 3.2 8B Instruct LLM Meta (2024),
which has 8 billion parameters, and has been instruction fine-tuned for question answering. It is
a widely used state-of-the-art open-source model at a scale on the upper end of model sizes for
portable models. It is open source and downloadable from the HuggingFace platform.
This setup offers the benefits of scale and consistency, while still allowing for psychological
nuance and expert oversight. The resulting profile dataset serves as the foundation for generating
personalized psychological reminders in the subsequent stage.
2.2
Step 1: Inference of Layer Profiles
To infer each individual’s psychological layer profiles from survey responses, we constructed an
automated pipeline using a general-purpose LLM. This setup enabled us to extract nuanced,
theory-informed inferences at scale, based on open-text responses to a broad survey. The goal
was to map each participant onto psychological constructs such as mindset, strengths, and
sources of meaning, using structured textual evidence and theory-guided prompts.
2.2.1
Method
The chosen 8B parameter model is considered as a relatively large model - these typically exhibit
more coherent, grounded reasoning and richer internal knowledge—an important asset when
interpreting psychological content. Nevertheless, we constrained the model’s interpretive space
by supplying carefully written instructions and contextual guidance to reduce hallucination and
improve faithfulness.

Performing Inference.
The inference system follows a repeatable structure: for each partici-
pant and each layer profile, a prompt is built, passed to the LLM via deterministic decoding,
and parsed into a structured profile record.
After receiving the model’s response, we extracted the first valid JSON dictionary from the
generated text and added it to a list of inferred layer profiles. Profiles that could not be parsed
correctly were skipped. Each inferred profile was associated with a unique participant index
and saved as a structured CSV file for downstream use.

The inference pipeline was run over the entire dataset of survey participants. For the Mindset
Layer, the model successfully produced valid structured outputs for over 95% of participants
(on the first try). A small fraction failed due to malformed JSON or overly verbose outputs,
which were filtered automatically.
Qualitatively, we observed that the model made plausible category assignments and provided
rationales that echoed the tone and content of the participant’s own responses. For example,
participants who described personal growth with emotional integration and intrinsic motivation

psychological features (pain, meaning, defense mechanisms, growth mindset, and strengths)

and selected answers from their raw survey responses. A general-purpose instruction-tuned
Large Language Model (LLM), specifically LLaMA 3.1–8B Instruct, was queried with these
prompts. This model was selected for its balance between interpretability, capability, and mem-

Each prompt followed a consistent structure:
• A high-level instruction describing the assistant’s role in composing a reflective, compas-

• A structured summary of the participant’s profile, including their top pain points, meaning

• Their MBTI personality type and key self-reported values, priorities, and life challenges;
• A final instruction asking the model to generate a concise but emotionally resonant
psychological reminder tailored to the student.
To see the particular prompt template, consult Appendix I.
Generation was performed in batches of 16 prompts using the Hugging Face pipeline interface,
with outputs parsed to extract the generated content alone (excluding the prompt). Prompts
and reminders were saved for transparency and reproducibility. The output reminders were
stored in a structured CSV format, indexed by student.
2.3.2
Results
The generated reminders varied in tone and content, reflecting each participant’s dominant
profile features. Participants with elevated pain scores in autonomy often received reminders
emphasizing agency and boundaries, while those high in connection-based meaning received
language encouraging social closeness and authenticity. High-scoring strengths were often ex-
plicitly referenced to reinforce self-efficacy. Overall, the model succeeded in producing insightful,
well-calibrated messages for nearly all participants without requiring manual intervention.
2.3.3
Evaluation
To evaluate the effectiveness of the generated reminders, we collected satisfaction ratings from
the same five participants as in Step 1, across several dimensions: content relevance, emotional
tone, and perceived helpfulness. Ratings were provided on a 1–10 scale. While most reminders
were well-received, a few mismatches in tone or specificity were reported. Future iterations
could improve personalization through user-adjustable tone settings and post-generation editing
options, in fact we propose such extension to our method in Appendix H. A more rigorous
validation could involve blind evaluation by external raters or comparing reminders to expert-
written equivalents.

Part V
Critical Reflections & Future Directions
1
Methodological Rationale
While numerous established psychological instruments exist for measuring specific aspects of
a person’s mind or behavior — such as personality traits, emotional intelligence, motivation,
values, or mental health symptoms — this project intentionally takes a holistic, integrative
approach that differs both technically and conceptually from those existing tools.
Standard instruments like the Big Five (John and Srivastava, 1999), VIA Character Strengths
(CLONINGER, 2005), EQ-i 2.0 (emotional intelligence) (Bar-On, 2003) or the SDT-based
surveys (Center for Self-Determination Theory, 2024) are typically designed to isolate and
quantify a single psychological dimension. They rely on scoring, fixed categories, and interpret
responses in isolation from broader emotional context or life narrative. Methodologically distinct,
this model functions as a layered inference engine powered by an LLM, building a dynamic
psychological profile by analyzing how responses interact across questions. The underlying
questionnaire takes about 40–50 minutes to complete — similar in length to the NEO PI-3
(Big Five, 240 items) — but distinguishes itself through prompt chaining, where each response
informs the next layer of interpretation, enabling deeper, cross-layer reasoning.
To illustrate the core difference between traditional assessments and this model’s interpretive
goals, consider how each approaches core values. Tools like the VIA Character Strengths Survey
rank 24 traits (e.g., Curiosity, Honesty...) based on self-reported behavior. For example, someone
who agrees with “I always look for ways to help others” might score high in “Kindness,” which
the tool interprets as a core strength. However, such assessments cannot determine whether
this behavior stems from genuine compassion, a desire for approval, or a coping mechanism
shaped by early emotional neglect. In contrast, our model treats values as narrative signals —
interpreted through links to themes like pain patterns, family dynamics, or personal priorities.
A value like “Freedom,” for instance, might be flagged as compensatory if tied to emotional
dependency. This approach goes beyond identifying what matters to uncovering why it matters,
whether it serves an adaptive or protective function, and how deeply it is integrated.
This critique extends to tools like the Strong Interest Inventory (Company, 2024b), the Reiss
Motivation Profile (Reiss, 2024), and personality typologies such as the MBTI (Company, 2024a)
and the Enneagram (Riso and Hudson, 2024), all of which classify individuals along isolated
domains. Our model, though still in a conceptual and exploratory phase, departs from static
categorization by using open-ended prompts and LLM-based reasoning to infer psychological
patterns in a more fluid and contextualized way. It aims to interpret connections across responses,
revealing internal dynamics that traditional tools may overlook.
A potential sign of success that need still to be tested would be the model’s ability to detect
traits similar to those identified by well-established psychological instruments. For example, if a
person described as “Strategic” by CliftonStrengths were also characterized by our model as
48
==PAGE:54==
valuing clarity, long-term vision, and complex problem-solving, such convergence could indicate
that the system — through flexible, narrative-based inference — is capable of capturing deep
psychological dimensions without relying on narrowly focused questionnaires. However, this
type of comparison was not conducted in the present study due to time constraints and the
proprietary cost of the CliftonStrengths instrument.
2
Technical Improvements
2.1
Extension for Reminder Generation
To enhance the personalization and emotional receptivity of the reminders, we introduce a third
stage in the pipeline: Reminder Polishing. As shown in Figure 6, this step reintroduces the
generated reminder—produced using the participant’s layer profiles and survey data—into the
LLM, this time with tailored instructions emphasizing how to deliver the message, rather than
what to say.
Figure 6: Proposed third stage: Reminder Polishing. The LLM receives the initial reminder
along with additional instructions and survey context to adapt phrasing to the participant’s
inferred communication style. Adjustments may include tone (e.g., gentle vs. directive) or
references (e.g., books, films, or figures) found in the individual’s responses, enhancing resonance
and psychological accessibility.
This stage targets the communicative framing of the reminder. Rather than altering its core
content, the model adjusts stylistic elements such as tone (e.g., soothing vs. motivational),
rhetorical form (e.g., metaphorical vs. literal), and cultural grounding (e.g., referencing personally
relevant media or figures). These shifts align the message with the participant’s inferred affective
and cognitive preferences, increasing the likelihood of emotional salience and reflective uptake.
The polishing prompt can include stylistic directives such as:
• Incorporate analogies based on the participant’s stated interests or values.
• Select first-person (“You are...”) or third-person (“She is...”) phrasing depending on desired
emotional distance.
• Adjust the level of directness or abstraction to match the participant’s receptivity and
tone preferences.
49
==PAGE:55==
While this stage has not yet undergone formal validation, early qualitative observations suggest
it improves subjective engagement. Future work may involve human-in-the-loop refinement or
user feedback studies to assess impact. When applied in coaching, educational, or therapeutic
settings, stylistic tuning may play a crucial role in making reminders feel more authentic, relevant,
and motivating.
2.2
Modeling Relational and Narrative Tensions
While the model was designed to incorporate pairwise and cross-quote relational reason-
ing—prompting it to interpret quotes in relation to each other rather than in isolation—this
dimension of analysis remains underdeveloped. Although some relational logic was embedded
into the interpretive prompts, deeper patterns could be more systematically surfaced through a
structured multi-quote comparison pipeline. Similarly, although the 16 Personality results were
cross-referenced during the construction of certain layers, they were not consistently leveraged as
a foundational lens across the full interpretive process. Anchoring each interpretive layer more
tightly to the MBTI dimension could have improved overall profile coherence. To support these
enhancements, several technical tools could be leveraged: embedding-based concept mapping
to detect latent psychological themes across quotes, few-shot prompting to boost interpretive
consistency, and longer-context models to preserve cross-response continuity. Together, these
improvements would strengthen the model’s ability to surface meaningful internal tensions and
psychological structure—advancing its capacity for nuanced, narrative-based profiling.
Finally, implementing explicit “values tension” prompts—designed to probe conflicts between
self-perceptions, compensatory narratives, or psychologically unintegrated values—could help
surface unresolved beliefs, internal contradictions, or identity fragmentation. Although these
insights are crucial for constructing effective and resonant reminders, a systematic application
of this approach systematically was constrained by prompt length limitations and the inherent
challenge of balancing interpretive depth with output clarity.
Altogether, these improvements point toward a richer, more nuanced psychological interpretation
system—one that goes beyond surface-level labeling to uncover deeper narrative and value-based
dynamics. While each approach presents its own practical challenges, they offer a roadmap for
enhancing the emotional and conceptual precision of the reminder-generation process in future
iterations.
3
Survey Enhancements and Methodological Develop-
ment
Due to the exploratory nature of this thesis, the survey questions were not originally designed
with the specific construction of psychological layers in mind. As a result, the scope and
structure of the layers were retroactively shaped on the basis of the interpretive potential of the
responses and the technical possibilities available. Thus, it would have been valuable to refine
the survey by incorporating questions drawn from brief standardized psychological measures,
which could help anchor certain layers more firmly in established psychometric theory. For
50
==PAGE:56==
instance, items inspired by SDT-based instruments (Center for Self-Determination Theory, 2024)
or the Self-Compassion Scale (Neff, 2025) could have provided stronger grounding for layers
such as pain categories or defense mechanisms. Nevertheless, as noted in section 1 of this part,
the question set was designed to leverage the potential of the technological methodology as
much as possible. While the survey was comparable in length to many standard psychological
instruments, it aimed for greater breadth—capturing multiple psychological dimensions in an
integrated format rather than isolating single traits.
Another avenue for improvement would be to define new layers and design specific questions
tailored to them, rather than relying solely on inferences drawn from open-ended responses.
A description of one such potential layer, along with broader suggestions for improving inter-
pretability through structured comparison of the various datasets collected during this thesis
can be found in Appendix H.
4
Potential Uses
As explored throughout this work, the concept of psychological reminders was initially developed
in response to issues within the educational system—issues that, in reality, have far-reaching
consequences for how individuals learn to shape their lives in society. In this sense, an im-
proved version of the reminder system could be applied across a wide range of contexts related
to personal development: from coaching and therapy, to career guidance, digital wellbeing
tools, or even transitions in adult life. However, when considering the educational system
as a primary context for implementation, the use of these reminders could play a powerful
role in normalizing personal development and self-awareness in schools. They can serve as
mentorship-at-scale—psychologically attuned messages that reflect students’ inner motivations
and values, even in environments where human support is limited or overstretched.
To be effectively integrated, psychological reminders must not be perceived as just another
academic obligation, but rather as tools that support emotional clarity and direction. At
the same time, any implementation must acknowledge the practical realities of the classroom.
Teachers already operate within tightly defined curricula and often face significant time and
resource constraints. It is crucial therefore that these tools are embedded within existing struc-
tures, not layered on top as additional workload. Some schools already incorporate elements of
personal development into collaborative activities—for example, within subjects like Innovation.
Institutions such as CPH Business have gone further, offering dedicated courses like Personal
Development and Leadership, where such reminders can be seamlessly integrated (Copenhagen
Business Academy, 2025). Successful adoption, however, depends on institutional support: staff
need training to incorporate reminders into reflective dialogue, and systems must be in place for
students to respond to and engage with the messages they receive.
When timed thoughtfully—at key moments such as the start of a new term or during periods of
transition—reminders can help foster a culture where inner development is valued alongside
academic achievement. Over time, this could ease performance pressure, nurture autonomy, and
cultivate the kind of psychological literacy often overlooked in traditional education. Ultimately,
51

such an approach may lay the groundwork for a more sustainable, human-centered system—one
that responds to foundational needs while still supporting long-term academic performance.

In recent years, growing attention and investment have been directed toward AIED (Artificial
Intelligence for Education) (EIFO, 2024; Viden.AI, 2024). While these tools are promising, they
primarily focus on optimizing learning within the framework of formal education — that is,
the acquisition of knowledge and skills defined by a standardized curriculum and established
educational objectives. AIED tools are therefore mainly designed to enhance the efficiency and
personnalization of existing teaching models, by adapting content or personalizing learning
paths to improve academic performance (Underwood and Luckin, 2011).
This thesis set out to examine the evolution of education from the earliest stages of humanity,
with the goal of establishing a foundation for identifying the root causes of today’s challenges
and revealing the internal resources humans can draw upon to grow. This is captured in two
core statements — timeless and interdisciplinary — that extend beyond the boundaries of
formal education, proposing that the pursuit of self-knowledge may have a catalytic impact,
fostering transformation at both individual and collective levels. In order to explore these
propositions in practice, a methodology based on the conception of psychological reminders was
developed. Unlike traditional psychological tools, this approach is based on a holistic reading of
the individual, enabled by advanced data science techniques. Although its scientific validity has
yet to be fully established, the initial results are promising and support the case for continued
experimentation.
A key advantage of this method lies in its modularity. Because all of its components are detailed
in this work, the approach can be readily adapted or extended. The layers of analysis can
be adjusted, definitions reformulated, or reminders tailored to specific contexts — such as
mental health, career guidance, or coaching. This flexibility invites further experimentation and
adaptation, with the potential to inform the development of educational — and, more broadly,
societal — systems that are responsive to human complexity and aligned with individuals’ deeper
needs. Through a combination of critical diagnosis and technical experimentation, this thesis has
sought to sketch a new trajectory for fostering human potential in response to emerging challenges.

Evolution of Digital Education
Evolving Paradigms of Digital Education
Recent research by (Jia and Huang, 2023)underscores how digital literacy has become essential
for vocational education, not only as a functional requirement but also as a strategic competency
in a rapidly evolving labor market. Their study reveals that vocational training institutions
must now address challenges such as unequal access to technology, gaps in teacher preparedness,
and curriculum misalignment. While current efforts include integrating digital content and cre-
ating collaborative learning environments, systemic obstacles still hinder inclusive and effective
implementation.
In parallel, (Chituc, 2021) presents a conceptual framework for ’Education 4.0’, rooted in the
digital ecosystems enabled by Industry 4.0. This framework emphasizes adaptive, personalized,
and data-driven learning environments supported by emerging technologies such as IoT, AI,
and cyber-physical systems. However, Chituc also cautions that the pedagogical and ethical
dimensions of such systems remain underdeveloped, especially regarding assessment and quality
assurance. This reflects broader tensions in digital education: between innovation and inequality,
personalization and surveillance, efficiency and educational depth.
Taken together, these perspectives underscore the urgency of interrogating the techno-economic
logics that shape contemporary education and of re-imagining digital learning not as a neutral
delivery channel but as a contested arena where values, power, and possibility are negotiated —
an endeavour that demands sustained educator up-skilling and pedagogical innovation, equitable
infrastructures that narrow digital divides, and governance mechanisms that safeguard privacy,
fairness, and the public purpose.

B
A Review of Progress in AI
This appendix provides further evidence to support the argument made in the main text: that
artificial intelligence is developing at a pace and scale that may soon place it beyond human-level
capabilities in key domains. With each new generation, AI systems are not only automating rou-
tine tasks but also beginning to outperform humans in areas once thought uniquely ours—such
as reasoning, creativity, and problem-solving.
AI and the Turing Test
First introduced by British mathematician Alan Turing in 1950, the Turing Test is a thought
experiment that asks whether machines can think. If a machine can hold a natural language
conversation such that a human judge cannot reliably distinguish it from a real person, it is said
to have ’passed’ the test. Although it does not measure consciousness or true understanding,
the Turing Test remains one of the most iconic benchmarks for evaluating machine intelligence
and conversational realism (Livingstone, 2006). More than seventy years later, this benchmark
has taken on renewed relevance in light of recent developments in large language models. In a
preregistered, five-minute text chat with 1,402 subjects, GPT-4 was judged ’human’ 54% of the
time—crossing the 50% bar Turing originally proposed and outperforming classic chatbots by
over 30 percentage points (Jones et al., 2025).
However, as Rosas (2024) argues, passing the Turing Test—while symbolically significant—should
not be mistaken for genuine intelligence or consciousness. He cautions that linguistic imitation
alone does not demonstrate understanding, and that focusing solely on conversational realism
may obscure deeper ethical, cognitive, and societal questions. From this perspective, the real
challenge lies not in whether AI can appear human, but in how society chooses to design,
regulate, and coexist with increasingly persuasive and autonomous systems (Syafuddin, 2024).
A complementary view comes from Bradford and Wollowski (1995) who argue that the Turing
Test can be formalized as a kind of interactive proof, where intelligence is demonstrated through
the ability to handle complex conversations. From this perspective, the test’s strength depends
on whether machines can simulate human-like reasoning across problems that are computa-
tionally difficult. If AI systems eventually match or surpass human performance even in these
high-complexity exchanges, the traditional Turing Test may no longer serve as a meaningful
boundary between human and machine intelligence (Bradford and Wollowski, 1995). Yet as
Watson (2023) cautions, even highly convincing performances may conceal the fact that such
systems still lack true understanding, intentionality, or consciousness—reminding us that be-
havioral imitation does not resolve the deeper metaphysical questions about what intelligence
actually is (Finnestad and Neufeld, 2022).
Toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) refers to the development of systems capable of replicating
human-level flexibility in thinking, learning, and problem-solving across a wide range of domains.
Unlike narrow AI, which is tailored to specific, predefined tasks, AGI seeks to emulate the
depth and adaptability of human cognition. With rapid increases in model scale—measured
67
==PAGE:73==
in billions of parameters—and the integration of increasingly multimodal data streams, the
field is advancing at an extraordinary pace. These developments suggest that machines with
general-purpose cognitive abilities on par with, or potentially surpassing, humans may soon
become a reality (Abdüsselam, 2023).
Contributing to this growing plausibility, (Blum and Blum, 2021) propose a formal computa-
tional architecture known as the Conscious Turing Machine (CTM)—a model inspired by Global
Workspace Theory that outlines how AGI might be structured. Rather than relying on the
monolithic design of current deep learning systems, the CTM envisions a modular, decentralized
system where specialized processors compete to place information into a central short-term
memory “stage,” which then broadcasts it throughout the system. This setup enables the
simulation of core cognitive functions such as attention, prediction, internal communication,
and behavioral self-explanation. By coordinating reasoning, adaptation, and self-monitoring
across a vast array of parallel modules, the CTM offers a scalable and interpretable alternative
to opaque neural networks. According to the authors, such a system could eventually replicate
not only intelligent behavior but also aspects of empathy, volition, and subjective awareness. As
AGI research moves beyond performance benchmarks to focus on replicating the mechanisms of
general intelligence, the CTM represents a significant theoretical step toward building adaptive,
autonomous, and potentially conscious machines.

Supplementary of Self Determination Theory (SDT)
Supplementary Overview of Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
This appendix provides additional background on SDT, offering readers a more detailed under-
standing of its foundational components in relation to human motivation, development, and
agency.
Motivation.
SDT involves understanding the foundational elements of human action, the
first of which is motivation—referring to the forces that energize and direct behavior. SDT
emphasizes both intrinsic and extrinsic aspects: intrinsic motivation refers to engaging in an
activity for the inherent satisfaction and enjoyment it brings, while extrinsic motivation involves
performing tasks due to external rewards or pressures. Early research by Deci and Ryan showed
that external incentives, like monetary rewards, can diminish intrinsic motivation, whereas
positive feedback that fosters a sense of competence can enhance it. Thus SDT evolved to focus
on the quality of motivation—whether autonomous (self-driven) or controlled (externally driven)
(Deci and Ryan, 2008; Adams et al., 2017a). Autonomous motivation includes both intrinsic
motivation and well-internalized extrinsic motivation (Shogren et al., 2017a).
Basic Psychological Needs.
According to SDT, optimal human development is shaped by
the interaction between individuals striving for growth and the social environment that either
supports or hinders the fulfillment of their core psychological needs. When these needs are met,
individuals experience greater personal growth and enhanced well-being.
The critical social environment supports are described in terms of 3 specific basic psychological
needs: competence, autonomy, and relatedness. Competence involves feeling effective and
capable in one’s actions, experiencing a sense of mastery over challenges. Autonomy refers to
the need for self-direction, where individuals feel that their actions are freely chosen and aligned
with their personal values. Relatedness is the need to feel connected and experience a sense of
belonging with others. Satisfaction of these psychological needs is central to fostering intrinsic
motivation and achieving optimal development (Adams et al., 2017a).
Causal Action.
Psychological needs initiate a causal action sequence, where interaction
with environmental supports fosters the development of self-regulatory beliefs and behaviors
for navigating life’s challenges. This sequence involves individuals actively choosing actions
in response to external opportunities or threats, shaped by internal beliefs and capabilities
(Shogren et al., 2017b). Causal Agency Theory defines self-determination through three key
characteristics: volitional action, agentic action, and action-control beliefs.
• Action-Control Beliefs: Internal beliefs about how capable individuals feel in achieving
their goals. These include control expectancy (belief in one’s ability to succeed), agency
beliefs (belief in one’s decision-making ability), and causality beliefs (understanding how
actions lead to outcomes) (Shogren et al., 2017b).
• Volitional Action: Refers to autonomy and the ability to self-initiate actions based on
personal choice. It involves decision-making, goal-setting, and problem-solving skills that
reflect control over one’s behavior (Shogren et al., 2017b).

• Agentic Action: Emphasizes self-regulation—the ability to direct and manage one’s
actions—and pathway thinking (planning to overcome obstacles). It includes skills like self-
monitoring and self-advocacy, enabling adaptation to challenges and sustained motivation
(Shogren et al., 2017b).

Meaning Making Model
The Meaning-Making Model (Park, 2013) explains how individuals respond to psychological distress by resolving
discrepancies between their understanding of a situation (appraised meaning) and their broader beliefs and
values (global meaning). When these are misaligned, people experience distress and try to reduce it.
This process can involve:
• Assimilation: adjusting the meaning of the situation.
• Accommodation: modifying one’s global beliefs or life goals.
• Effortful and unconscious coping: including reflection, reappraisal, or intrusive thoughts.
Meaning-making is especially important in situations with low control (e.g., trauma, loss), and when successful,
it results in “meaning made” — such as personal growth, identity shifts, or new worldviews.

Defence Mechanisms
George Vaillant organized defense mechanisms into four levels based on their psychological maturity, ranging
from least to most adaptive (Wikipedia contributors, 2025a):
• Level I – Pathological (psychotic denial, delusional projection)
• Level II – Immature (fantasy, projection, passive aggression, acting out)
• Level III – Neurotic (intellectualization, reaction formation, dissociation, displacement, repression)
• Level IV – Mature (humour, sublimation, suppression, altruism, anticipation))
The definitions of all the defense mechanisms presented in table 5 are the following:
• Altruism: Constructive service to others that brings pleasure and personal satisfaction (Wikipedia
contributors, 2025e).
• Anticipation: The conscious preparation for future discomfort or potentially stressful events. It involves
realistic planning and affective rehearsal to mitigate anxiety or difficulty. Rather than avoiding the
stressor, the individual imagines possible outcomes and prepares emotionally and behaviorally to face
them (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Compartmentalization: Allows contradictory values, thoughts, or behaviors to coexist by keeping
them in separate mental “boxes” that aren’t consciously reconciled. For example, someone may behave
empathetically in one context and cruelly in another without feeling conflicted (McWilliams, 2011).
• Displacement: Redirecting intense emotions (often sexual or aggressive) away from the real source to
a safer, more acceptable target to avoid direct confrontation with the original source of fear or conflict
(Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Dissociation: A temporary, drastic modification of one’s identity or awareness to avoid emotional distress.
It involves separating oneself from thoughts or feelings that would normally be part of conscious experience
(Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Humour: Expressing difficult or painful emotions through wit or self-deprecation, which makes them more
palatable and socially acceptable while still retaining their emotional content (Wikipedia contributors,
2025e).
• Identification: Unconsciously adopting the traits or values of another person in order to feel safer, more
acceptable, or to reduce inner conflict (McWilliams, 2011).
• Intellectualization: Excessively analytical or abstract thought patterns used to avoid confronting
emotional content. This creates emotional distance from distressing feelings (Wikipedia contributors,
2025e).
• Introjection: Unconsciously adopting another’s values, beliefs, or feelings—usually from authority figures
or caregivers. This can lead to self-criticism or rigid standards (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Isolation of affect: Separating emotion from the content or context to which it belongs. The individual
may discuss deeply emotional events with no visible emotion, as a way of coping (Wikipedia contributors,
2025e).
• Moralization: Reframing inner emotional tension or conflict in terms of rigid moral judgments. This
avoids dealing with complexity by splitting experiences into “right” vs “wrong” (McWilliams, 2011).
• Projection: Attributing one’s own unacceptable thoughts, feelings, or motives to someone else. For
example, a person who is angry may accuse others of being hostile (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Rationalization: Justifying questionable behaviors or feelings with seemingly logical reasons to avoid
the true, often uncomfortable explanation (McWilliams, 2011).
• Reaction formation: Transforming an unacceptable impulse into its opposite. For example, treating
someone you dislike with excessive kindness to mask hostile feelings (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Regression: Returning to earlier developmental behaviors in response to stress or threat, such as
tantrums, clinginess, or helplessness in adults (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Repression: Unconsciously blocking out disturbing desires, thoughts, or memories from conscious
awareness. The emotion may remain conscious, but the cause is buried (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Reversal: Converting a feeling into its opposite to protect self-esteem or avoid vulnerability—e.g., acting
overly independent to mask dependency (McWilliams, 2011).
77
==PAGE:83==
• Self-blame / Turning against self: Redirecting negative emotion inward by blaming or criticizing
oneself instead of confronting the external source (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Sublimation: Transforming potentially harmful or socially unacceptable emotions into constructive
behaviors. For example, channeling aggression into competitive sports (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Suppression: The conscious choice to delay dealing with a thought or emotion until one is better able to
cope. Unlike repression, the emotion is intentionally set aside (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).
• Undoing: Trying to cancel out or “erase” a distressing thought or action by engaging in the opposite
behavior. For example, being overly kind after having angry thoughts (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e).

H.1
Enhancing the Framework with an additional Layer
When considering potential improvements to the construction of the psychological layers used in this work,
one promising direction lies in exploring a deeper dimension: cognitive style. This would involve building an
additional layer aimed at mapping how individuals habitually perceive, process, and organize information—an
essential factor that shapes how meaning is constructed and how individuals approach change. However, the
dataset used for the reminder generation in this thesis lacks the necessary granularity to reliably infer such
cognitive style categories. As a result, this dimension currently serves as a conceptual foundation —a structured
basis for future development and refinement of the model.
The categories proposed for future development of this layer are mainly structured around three foundational
dimensions of cognitive processing — perception, memory, and thought — as outlined in Miller’s (1987) model
(as cited in Kozhevnikov, 2007) reported in Figure 10.
Figure 10: Cognitive Styles: An Integrated Model by A. Miller, 1987 as cited in Kozhevnikov,
2007
To provide a more complete and potentially usable cognitive style layer, a fourth dimension is added from Nosal’s
(1990) (as cited in Kozhevnikov, 2007) hierarchical framework: the metacognitive level. This additional layer
addresses behavioral regulation by focusing on executive functioning and the conscious allocation of mental
resources. Together, these four dimensions offer a structured and integrative approach to understanding how
individuals perceive, process, and respond to information in complex environments.
Thus, the obtained categories for this layer are the following:
Perception
• Pattern recognition (How the brain identifies meaningful patterns in what is seen or experienced)
– Part/Whole Relation: Does the individual focus on individual details (analytic) or the overall
picture (holistic)?
85
==PAGE:91==
∗Analytic vs. Holistic:
· Analytic thinkers tend to focus primarily on objects and their attributes. They distin-
guish focal objects or people from their contexts, ascribe causality to specific actors or
variables, explain events using formal logic, predict outcomes in a linear fashion, eschew
contradiction, and categorize objects taxonomically (Spencer-Rodgers and Peng, 2018).
· Holistic thinkers are characterized by attention to relationships among objects and their
broader contexts. They attribute causality to environmental or systemic factors, rely on
experiential knowledge, predict events in cyclic patterns, and group objects thematically
(Spencer-Rodgers and Peng, 2018).
• Attention (How mental resources are focused on specific stimuli over others)
– Selective attention: The capacity to concentrate on relevant information while filtering out
distractions.
∗Field articulation:
· Field-Dependent (FD) individuals exhibit higher reliance on the surrounding perceptual
field (Kozhevnikov, 2007).
· Field-Independent (FI) individuals are less influenced by external context and more
internally anchored in perception (Kozhevnikov, 2007).
Memory
• Representation (How information is mentally encoded and stored)
– Memory codes →Analytic-verbal vs. Visual-analog: Refers to an individual’s preferred
mode of processing information—either verbally (language-based) or through visual-analog formats

– Conceptual networks →Conceptual complexity (Concrete vs. Abstract):
∗This dimension refers to a continuum from concrete to abstract thinking, based on an individual’s

(Gregorc (1979, 1982, 1984) as cited in Kozhevnikov, 2007).

Metacognitive Level
This highest layer concerns self-regulation, decision-making, and strategic control—how individuals
allocate mental resources and adapt their cognitive behavior.
• Rigidity–Flexibility of Control (Adaptability of thought): Refers to differences in how individuals
respond to stimuli containing contradictory or ambiguous cues.
– Constricted control individuals focus on the most salient information and struggle with peripheral
distractions.
– Flexible control individuals shift focus based on context and handle conflicting or subtle cues
more adaptively.
• Internal vs. External Locus of Control (Sense of agency): Refers to how individuals habitually
explain uncontrollable events—whether attributing causes to internal factors (e.g., effort, choice) or
external ones (e.g., luck, environment).
• Reflectivity–Impulsivity: Describes a person’s tendency to process information either deliberately
and cautiously (reflectivity) or quickly and reactively (impulsivity).
• Discomfort-Averse vs. Discomfort-Tolerant (Openness to emotional and perceptual ambiguity):
– Describes an individual’s readiness to accept perceptual contradiction (e.g., in apparent movement
illusions).
– Tolerance is marked by ease in processing ambiguous or contradictory stimuli.
– Intolerance manifests as discomfort or rigidity in the face of conflicting input.
• Thinking vs. Feeling (MBTI; also relates to sequential/random processing): Refers to whether decisions
are made based on logical principles (Thinking) or personal/emotional values (Feeling).
Notably, this axis aligns with Gregorc’s sequential–random processing (Gregorc (1979, 1982, 1984) as
cited in Kozhevnikov, 2007), Kolb’s convergent–divergent styles ( Kolb (1974,1976, 1984) as cited in
Kozhevnikov, 2007), and overlaps with MBTI thinking–feeling and concrete–abstract dimensions (MBTI;
Bayne, 1995; Myers, 1976; Myers & McCaulley, 1985 as cited in Kozhevnikov, 2007).
• Judging vs.
Prospecting (MBTI): Refers to a preference for structure, closure, and decisiveness
(Judging) versus openness, flexibility, and ongoing exploration (Prospecting), especially when navigating
complex choices and ambiguous options.
Thus, although this layer partially overlaps with MBTI dimensions, constructing it with sufficient accuracy
would have required more targeted questions—specifically designed to capture participants’ cognitive habits,
reasoning styles, decision-making preferences, and attentional patterns. Nevertheless, this remains a valuable
layer for future exploration. It would be particularly interesting to investigate whether prioritizing such a
fundamental dimension—by designing the survey specifically to capture cognitive style, even at the expense of
other layers—might have led to deeper insights or more impactful results.

An additional opportunity for insight would have emerged by systematically crossing the two datasets used in this
thesis: the general adult survey used to generate personalized psychological reminders, and the responses from

aligning certain overlapping dimensions—such as motivation patterns, personal values, mindset indicators,

between adolescence and early adulthood. For instance, comparing how high school students articulate their
future priorities or express curiosity, versus how adults reflect on past obstacles or life meaning, could inform
models of value evolution, identity formation, or educational misalignment. Furthermore, embedding insights
from the student sample into the reminder-generation process might have allowed for more age-calibrated

H.3
Improvements with a new Dataset
In addition to the participant and student surveys, a third dataset was created by simulating how notable public
figures—renowned for their wisdom, resilience, or impact—might respond to the full reflective survey. This
dataset, although rich in symbolic and aspirational value, was not leveraged in the reminder-generation pipeline.
Yet, it holds considerable potential for improving both the quality and intentionality of the reminders.
One immediate application would have been to fine-tune the tone, style, and moral structure of the reminders.
Since these model figures embody high levels of self-awareness, purpose, and psychological integration, their
simulated answers could serve as archetypal templates. By analyzing the reminders that might have been
generated from those profiles, the system could:
• Learn from thematic patterns in how mature psychological traits are expressed,
• Improve the emotional resonance of reminders through stylistic alignment (e.g., “what would a Mandela-like
figure say to someone facing self-doubt?”),
• Calibrate the output toward wisdom-centered language rather than generic encouragement.
Another use would be in few-shot prompting: model figure profiles could have acted as demonstration cases,
showing the LLM how to move from complex input to high-quality reflective output. For example, presenting
a Mandela-like response and its ideal reminder could help steer the model’s interpretive space toward deeper,
value-anchored synthesis.
Finally, these profiles could have been used as a contrastive lens —for instance, comparing a participant’s answers
to a model figure’s could allow the system to suggest more personalized growth paths. If a user shows signs of
being driven by external validation, but a model figure embodies intrinsic integrity, the reminder could gently

when it’s not yet validated by others”).
Integrating this dataset in future iterations could support the creation of more narratively grounded, aspirationally
anchored, and psychologically coherent reminders, blending personal context with universal insight.

I.6
Mindset Layer Specifics
Mindset Layer: Task and Layer Description
You are a psychology assistant. Based on the individual’s reflections on change, adversity, motivation,
and identity, infer their psychological readiness for growth using a fixed–growth mindset continuum.
This layer reveals how they relate to vulnerability, discomfort, and self-evolution — whether they resist
change, cautiously explore it, or embrace it fully.
Focus on:
• How they process past challenges — resistance, detachment, or meaning-making
• Their posture toward discomfort — avoidance, tolerance, or curiosity
• What drives their growth — intrinsic desire or compensatory pressure
• Signs of cognitive or emotional flexibility
Survey evidence description.
To infer the participant’s readiness for growth and their mindset posture,
the prompt includes a curated selection of questions related to adversity, self-concept, motivation, and identity.
Each question block is accompanied by targeted guidance that helps the model interpret answers in terms of
openness, agency, and psychological integration. The blocks below represent the key interpretive anchors used:
1. MTBI Personality and Core Values (Q3, Q14–16): Used to infer the person’s psychological
posture toward growth and transformation. Growth-oriented values (e.g., Curiosity, Courage)
suggest readiness, while stability-oriented values (e.g., Security, Control) may indicate resistance.
The MBTI provides a lens into how the person cognitively frames change, control, and personal
development.
2. Obstacles and Milestones (Q44, Q46): These answers reveal how the participant narrates
past and present difficulty. Growth-aligned mindsets tend to reframe adversity through meaning-
making and action. Responses that externalize blame or lack emotional engagement may reflect
fixed or fragile stances. The needs they articulate (e.g., mentorship, skills, clarity) reveal their
perceived readiness or inhibition in navigating change.
3. Relationship to Change (Q47–51): This block directly surfaces growth mindset signals. The
model is prompted to evaluate narrative coherence (e.g., do they own their growth or express
regret?), emotional tone (e.g., empowerment vs. fear), and the type of motivation expressed
(intrinsic curiosity vs. duty or fear). These patterns allow for accurate placement along the
six-category fixed–growth continuum.
Each block of responses is introduced in the prompt with tailored guidance, which explains how to interpret the
block in relation to the mindset layer. This structure enables the model to assess not just content but also tone,
coherence, and underlying orientation toward growth and discomfort.
