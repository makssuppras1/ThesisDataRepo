Abstract
The cement industry, crucial for global construction, faces the important challenge of bal­
ancing increasing demand with environmental sustainability. Cement production is a ma­
jor contributor to CO2 emissions, which underlines the need for an absolute environmental
sustainability assessment (AESA) that assesses its impact in the context of the limits of
the Earth. Previous studies have focused on relative environmental assessments of the
cement industry. However, an AESA, considering the planetary boundaries, is lacking.
This study fills this gap by providing a detailed analysis of the cement industry’s sustain­
ability in absolute terms.
The study aims to evaluate the environmental sustainability of the cement industry at both
global and national levels using an AESA. It explores whether industry practices align with
Earth’s safe operating space, establishing a threshold for environmentally sustainable
cement production.
The study uses a cradle­to­gate perspective, focusing on the production phase. Two
rights­based allocation methods, equal per capita and grandfathering, are used to allocate
safe operating space and assess transgression levels in cement production. The study
also explores different scenarios using gross value added and price­demand approaches.
The findings reveal significant variations in sustainability between different countries. The
study demonstrates how economic statuses impact the sustainability of cement produc­
tion. It also highlights the influence of allocation principles on sustainability assessments.
The study underscores the urgent need for the cement industry to align its practices with
global environmental sustainability goals. It suggests that transitioning to more sustain­
able practices is essential to maintain the Holocene state of the Earth, especially in the
context of rapid global development and infrastructure needs.

1
Introduction
The cement industry faces a significant challenge of balancing growing global demand
with the urgent need to reduce CO2 emissions. In developing economies, the need for in­
frastructure development intensifies this demand, which requires innovative technologies
to reduce emissions [1].
Cement­based concrete is the most widely used substance after water [2]. Considering
the trends in global population growth and development, the cement industry, if it remains
unaltered, will continue to be a major source of CO2 emissions, reflecting its historical role
as a significant contributor to climate change. Cement plants cause almost 5­7% of total
anthropogenic CO2 emissions, with the production of a ton of cement emitting just under
0,6 tons of CO2 [1, 3].
In the field of environmental sustainability, there has been a growing focus on evaluating
and enhancing industrial processes. Different industries have undergone absolute envi­
ronmental sustainability assessments (AESAs) to gain a thorough understanding of their
ecological impacts. However, there is a noticeable gap in current research, as there have
been many relative assessments but no absolute assessments of cement production and
consumption. However, this study will focus primarily on cement production as it allows
for a more precise analysis. Current research is focused on the life cycle assessment
(LCA) of cement, which looks at its environmental impact from the start to the end of its
life. Two recent studies about the LCA of cement are for example Ige et al. (2021) [4]
and Rhaouti et al. (2023) [5]. LCA, while valuable for understanding the specific impact
of products and activities for certain impact categories, they often fall short of providing
a comprehensive picture of an activity’s overall sustainability. Therefore, while LCA is
a useful tool, it is not sufficient on its own to ensure that human activities remain within
Earth’s environmental limits. Due to the gap in the literature, conducting an AESA of
cement production is necessary as it will contribute to a deeper understanding of the en­
vironmental boundaries and the impact of cement, providing a clearer and more detailed
view of how sustainable cement is.
The primary source of CO2 emissions in cement production is the chemical reaction in­
volved. This process occurs when calcium carbonate, the primary constituent of limestone
used in cement, is heated. During heating, calcium carbonate releases carbon dioxide
and transforms it into calcium oxide [6].
To ensure the industry’s growth aligns with environmental sustainability goals over the next
25 years, such as the Net Zero Emissions target and the Paris Agreement, the adoption
of groundbreaking technologies and innovative approaches is crucial. Figure 1.1 shows
how cement production is expected to increase by 20% from approximately 5000 Mt in
2024 to 6000 Mt in 2050, corresponding to an overall increase in total anthropogenic CO2

Attempts are being made to reduce CO2 emissions at every stage of the cement life cycle.
This includes steps such as quarrying, producing and transporting concrete, managing
materials at the end of their life, recycling, and optimizing concrete structural designs to
reduce cement use [8]. Improving durability also plays a key role in prolonging the lifespan

tion of alternative fuels, such as municipal solid waste, into the cement manufacturing
process has become an essential component of contemporary cement production, which
has contributed significantly to waste recycling and reduction of CO2 emissions [8].
Finding alternative materials for clinkers can also reduce CO2 emissions. Coal fly ash,
for example, has been increasingly demonstrated as an alternative binder in cementitious
composites. According to Li et al. (2021) [9], recent studies suggest that concretes with
desirable properties can be obtained with 60% or higher cement replacement by fly ash.
However, the feasibility of using coal fly ash, for example, is challenged by its decreasing
availability as the world transitions away from coal­based energy.
Another approach is carbon capture storage, which reduces CO2 in the atmosphere by
capturing it and storing it in a suitable place. The existing CCS technology has the capa­
bility to capture approximately 85­95% of the CO2 emissions generated by a power plant,
although it requires more energy compared to a plant without CCS technology. After the
successful capture and storage of CO2, the CCS technology has the potential to decrease
total emissions released into the atmosphere by approximately 80­90% [10].However, this
method encounters difficulties in terms of its maturity as it requires a significant amount
of time to develop such technologies. Additionally, another issue is the requirement for a
storage solution that can be sustained over the long term.

1.1
Planetary Boundaries
Over the past 10,000 years, Earth’s environment has been exceptionally stable, this pe­
riod is known as the Holocene. Since the Industrial Revolution, Earth has entered the
Anthropocene, where human activity is the primary force affecting global environmental
changes. This could disrupt the long­standing stability of the Holocene, possibly leading
to significant global consequences. Climate tipping points are critical in this context, they
refer to conditions beyond which changes in a specific part of the climate system become
self­perpetuating [11]. These changes can have dangerous, irreversible, and abrupt im­
pacts with serious implications for humanity. Without human impact, stable conditions for
the Holocene are expected to persist for several thousand more years [12].
The concept of Planetary Boundaries (PB), introduced by Rockström et al. (2009) [13],
provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the limits within human develop­
ment to maintain the Earth system.
The PBs, also called Earth system processes, are nine in total and can be seen in Figure
1.2, if the boundaries of these processes are exceeded, it could generate unacceptable
environmental changes.
Figure 1.2: Current control variable status of the nine planetary boundaries presented by
Richardson et al. (2023) [14].

These boundaries delineate a ”safe operating space” (SOS) for various Earth system
processes, which are considered crucial to maintaining the Holocene state. However,
defining exact carrying capacities within these boundaries is challenging due to scientific
uncertainties and natural variability, which requires a precautionary approach to ensure
that these thresholds are not exceeded [13].
The green zone in Figure 1.2 shows the SOS within the boundary, while the yellow to red
zone shows the increasing risk. The purple zone represents high risk where the conditions
of the interglacial Earth system are transgressed with high confidence [14]. The dotted
line around the SOS indicates that six out of the nine PBs are exceeded.
The climate change boundary is defined by a zone where risks increase rapidly and are
interconnected, reinforcing the need for a cautious approach to setting lower PBs. Rock­
ström et al. (2009) [13] suggest a CO2 boundary of 350 parts per million (ppm), marking
the onset of this risk zone, which extends up to 450 ppm before entering a high­risk cat­
egory. This range aligns with a 1°C to 2°C increase in global mean surface temperature.
By precaution, the boundary is set at 350 ppm (≈1°C), just below the 1.5°C target of
the Paris Agreement, acknowledging significant risks and potentially irreversible changes
even with increases in temperature [14, 11].
1.2
Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment
The concept of absolute environmental sustainability is defined as the process of satis­
fying the needs of current and future generations within the biophysical constraints im­
posed by our planet’s climate and ecosystems [15]. AESA aims to holistically evaluate
the environmental impacts of cement, in this case, to ensure operations remain within
PBs. This approach is essential to ensure that the industry’s growth does not compro­
mise Earth’s ecological balance and adheres to sustainable development principles set
by global agreements like the Paris Agreement.
The SOS as presented in the PB framework serves as a useful reference for AESAs.
These reference values can be linked to the impact of human activities, which are quan­
tified using LCA or similar life cycle­based methods [16]. In AESAs, it is important to
allocate the SOS to human activities. This distribution allows the impact of each activity
to be measured against its allocated share of the SOS, determining if the activity can be
considered sustainable in an absolute sense. To make informed decisions about these al­
location principles, it is necessary to understand the distributive justice theory underlying
the principles.
The SOS must be divided and allocated to the specific entity or activity under examination,
such as countries, companies, industry sectors, individuals, or products (a process known
as downscaling) [16]. For an activity to be considered absolute sustainable, its impact
must not exceed its allocated portion of the SOS.
Building on the concept of distributing the SOS to various activities, it is critical to explore
how different sharing principles can be applied effectively to specific industries, such as

cement production. This prompts an investigation into how existing research and method­

and principles that can assist with the further development of the AESA methodology.
The 2021 work by Tulus, Pérez­Ramírez, and Guillén­Gosálbez [17] emphasizes the im­
portance of planetary metrics to assess the absolute environmental sustainability of 492
chemical products. Their study focuses on global markets, and this approach assesses
chemicals produced using mainstream or widely adopted production technologies, as op­
posed to those produced by less common or emerging methods. The analysis incor­
porates two rights­based allocation methodologies, equal per capita and grandfathering.
These methods were used as a starting point in the global scale AESA of cement in this
study.
Gebara and Laurent’s 2023 [18] study on the performance assessment of the national
SDG­7 (Sustainable Development Goal 7) aligns with the energy­intensive production of
cement. They developed four different allocation principles and performed an assessment
in 176 countries. Their developed allocation principles were used as a starting point in
the country scale ASEA of cement in this study.
The research by Owsianiak et al. in 2022 [19] on the performance of second­generation
microbial proteins as aquaculture feed in relation to PBs demonstrates how they use egal­
itarian and utilitarian allocation principles in their AESA. Although focused on aquaculture,
the principles and methodologies used in this study could be adapted to assess cement
production.
Hjalsted et al. (2020) [20] propose ethical allocation principles for sharing the SOS, at
individual, product, and sectoral levels. This approach is crucial for assessing absolute
sustainability in industries such as cement production, where environmental impacts are
significant. Their proposed framework assists in the informed and transparent selection
of allocation principles and scaling methods. It represents a progressive step toward
standardizing the distribution of SOS in absolute environmental sustainability evaluations.
1.3
Allocation Principles
Currently, a number of allocation principles are used for different purposes. These princi­
ples can roughly be categorized into two types: rights­based and duty­based. Duty­based
approaches, which include responsibility, capability, or cost­effectiveness principles, are
typically used for allocating transgressed PBs. Rights­based approaches, on the other
hand, rely on concepts of sovereignty and equality to distribute resources and environ­
mental burdens [17]. Equal per capita (EPC) and grandfathering (GF) are the two most
frequently used allocation principles in studies, and they are both based on rights.

==PAGE:14==
The EPC allocation principle, also called the egalitarian sharing principle, is often used in
country­level assessments, dividing the SOS equally according to population [21]. This
approach, which treats space as a finite resource, is also applicable at the individual level,
ensuring equal distribution among people. However, direct application of EPC principles
at the product, company, or sector level is challenging as it is difficult to justify targets
other than human beings. To allocate shares for products, companies, or sectors, an
additional principle is required, considering the value these entities offer to their ultimate
beneficiaries, individual humans.
The GF allocation principle, also called acquired rights, is another sharing principle that is
versatile and can be utilized at various scales in studies, either independently or in com­
bination with principles from other categories. This flexibility allows for tailor­made appli­
cation across different contexts and scales [21]. This allocation principle is a fundamental
aspect of the strategic approach of the Science­Based Targets initiative (SBTi) [22]. SBTi
offers a well­defined route for companies to reduce their greenhouse gas (GHG) emis­
sions, helping to mitigate severe impacts of climate change and supporting sustainable
business expansion for the future [23].
Allocating a share of the SOS to specific activities has significant consequences, as dif­
ferent sharing principles will inherently favor certain activities. Economic allocation tends
to benefit activities with high economic value, while GF or status quo methods generally
benefit established activities [21]. In contrast, newer activities, which might have a lower
environmental impact, are likely to receive a smaller share or none of the SOS.
There is, however, no standardized approach to the selection and application of shar­
ing principles. Furthermore, in­depth discussions about the ethical and normative values
that influence the choice of these principles in previous AESAs are often insufficient [16].
Given the profound impact of the chosen sharing principle on the results of AESAs, it is
important to establish a more structured and well­informed strategy for this selection.
1.4
Aim and Objectives
This study aims to provide a comprehensive analysis of the environmental impact of the
cement industry through the lens of AESA. Integrating considerations of PBs, it investi­
gates the absolute sustainability of cement production, focusing on whether the industry
remains within the SOS of Earth. The primary goal is to measure the absolute environ­
mental sustainability of industrially manufactured cement, evaluating its impact in relation
to its allocated share of Earth’s SOS, and establishing a critical threshold beyond which
cement production can be considered environmentally unsustainable.
In this study, a comparative analysis of allocation principles across a diverse set of coun­
tries is conducted. This comparison aims to demonstrate how these principles influence
the absolute sustainability of cement production in different countries and assess how far
these nations are from achieving absolute sustainability. A key aspect of this analysis is
exploring the relationship between a country’s income status and the sustainability of its

The life cycle of cement is complex, starting with the extraction of the raw material and
ending with its disposal. The accurate application of the AESA framework to each stage,
considering energy consumption, emissions, and waste, can be challenging. Therefore,
the study is limited to cement production to avoid major assumptions about the cement use
phase and the end­of­life. However, obtaining accurate and up­to­date data on cement
production, especially in less developed or less transparent countries, can also be difficult.
Ensuring consistency and comparability of data across countries with diverse reporting
standards and environmental regulations.
The methodological approach encompasses a cradle­to­gate perspective, encompassing
all upstream inputs from the technosphere and natural sources that feed into cement plant
operations within their supply chains. The cradle­to­gate approach focuses specifically on
the production phase of cement, from the extraction of raw materials to the final product
of cement when it leaves the factory gate, without considering its end­of­life phase. It
includes the extraction of raw materials, transportation to the cement plant, and manu­
facturing processes. In contrast, a cradle­to­grave approach would extend this analysis
to include the use phase of cement, considering not only the production but also the use,
potential recycling, and final disposal of cement [26]. The consumption of cement, which
involves its application in construction and other uses, often lacks precise quantitative
data and introduces considerable uncertainty to the assessment. This uncertainty comes
from the varied applications of cement in construction, the differences in regional building
practices, and the lifespan and disposal methods of cement­based structures. Therefore,
the focus on the production aspect allows for a more accurate and specific assessment of
the environmental sustainability of cement, given the current limitations in data availability
regarding its consumption.
Although the study is based on cement production, data on cement imports and exports
are used for each country, but their purpose is not to assess consumption patterns, but
rather to estimate the total demand for cement. This estimate is crucial to approximate the
Gross Value Added (GVA) of the cement industry within each country. The GVA serves
as a key metric in the application of the EPC principle. This approach ensures that the
AESA remains firmly focused on the production side of the industry, providing a targeted
and relevant assessment of its environmental sustainability.
In this study, two rights­based methods, EPC allocation and GF are used. These ap­
proaches prioritize equitable sharing and historical usage patterns, respectively. The fo­
cus is limited to the GF and EPC allocation principles, acknowledging that other allocation

Methods
Sections 2.1 and 2.2 describe the collection of data and the general methodological ap­
proach of the absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA) performed for
cement, as well as the AESA approach and the allocation principles chosen. The main
steps of the AESA calculations for two different scales, namely the global and country
scales, as well as two selected allocation principles for each scale, are described in the
following Sections 2.3 and 2.4.
This study assesses the environmental sustainability of cement on a global and country
scale for a better understanding of the global impact of cement and the impact of countries
of different circumstances. Section 2.3 incorporates the methods and allocation principles
utilized by Tulus et al. (2021) [17] for the global scale AESA of cement. The approach for
the country scale AESA in Section 2.4 is adapted from the equations applied by Gebara
et al. (2023) [18].
2.1
Data Collection
The data collection phase of the study posed a significant challenge, especially given the
need for comprehensive data covering different countries. The complexity of this effort
was further intensified by including developing and low­income countries in the study,
where data availability is often delayed due to infrastructure and institutional constraints.
The search for robust and representative data required an extensive and in­depth inves­
tigation of various databases and sources.
One of the databases used in the study is EXIOBASE, the data obtained from that database
was gross value added (GVA) associated with cement for the chosen countries and for
the world. A description of how the data were obtained is found in Section 2.1.1.

2.2
Methodological Approach
The PBs framework mentioned in Section 1.1 introduces a set of nine planetary bound­
aries shown in Figure 1.2 and they all are essential for maintaining the Holocene state.
The framework has established global boundaries that outline a safe operating space
(SOS) for humanity as a means to protect the stability of the planet. If an anthropogenic
activity exceeds the SOS, it is considered environmentally unsustainable.
To conduct an AESA on a global and a country scale, the global SOS needs to be down­
scaled. This is done with allocation principles that assign a share of the SOS to products,
sectors or services [19]. There are various sharing principles that exist, each with their
own theories of distributive justice.
The allocation principle of equal per capita (EPC), also called egalitarian, states that every
person possesses an equal moral right to access Earth’s ecological budget, also called
safe operating space. However, to apply this principle to a specific activity, such as cement
production, an additional sharing principle is required. This study uses Tulus et al. (2021)
[17], Ryberg et al. (2018) [21], and Owsianiak et al. (2022) [19] as a reference and
combines the EPC principle with a utilitarian principle based on an economic indicator,
the gross value added (GVA) of cement. This method is based on the assumption that the
economic value of an activity could act as an indicator of its impact on human well­being.
The idea is that greater economic worth implies enhanced well­being, which provides a
rationale for an increased allocation of the SOS.

trasting justice theory, the GF principle is also called non­egalitarian and is grounded in
the principle of sovereignty. The share allocated to anthropogenic activities should align
with their present impact on the global environment. However, a notable limitation of this

exports are generally overestimated [41].

Discussion
According to the results, the cement industry is not considered environmentally sustain­
able at the global level for both allocation principles. The approach of assessing on two
different scales (global and country) is important to balance global environmental goals
with local economic and development goals, ensuring that global sustainability efforts are
equitable and effective. In addition, it informs policy makers and industry leaders, guid­
ing decisions regarding investments, regulations, and technologies in the cement sector.
This comprehensive perspective not only aligns overarching environmental concerns with
practical localized solutions, but also raises public awareness about the environmental
impacts of cement production, highlighting the importance of sustainable practices and
encouraging greater accountability and participation at both local and global levels.
The study also delves into the concept of sustainability within the cement industry on a
national scale, providing a comprehensive perspective by using two allocation principles
to assess the absolute environmental sustainability. The findings from applying the EPC
allocation principle and Method 1 revealed that the cement production in all ten countries
was deemed environmentally unsustainable.
Interestingly, a different perspective emerges for the cement industry in Cambodia and
Ethiopia when the GVA metric is altered to price multiplied by demand within the same
allocation principle. These nations, classified as low and lower­middle income countries,
are deemed sustainable under this method. This discovery initiates a discussion on the
financial advantages of cement manufacturing in countries experiencing rapid economic
growth and how these advantages can be reconciled with the environmental impact.
Under the GF allocation principle, half of the countries studied, namely Cambodia, Ethiopia,
Kenya, Nigeria, and India, are considered sustainable. India is particularly noteworthy in
this analysis. Despite being the second­largest cement producer in the world, its status as
a lower­middle income country and its historically lower emissions per capita contribute
to its sustainability according to the GF principle.
It is clear that some countries have a worse performance than others according to the
results for EPC Methods 1 and 2. China has the highest overall TL, they exceed the
planetary boundary of climate change by a factor of 8.841 according to the EPC allocation
principle and Method 1 but only by a factor of 103 using Method 2. Turkey is second worst
performing according to EPC Method 1, while USA has a high TL for both methods using
the EPC principle. Denmark also possesses a high TL for both principles and exceeds
the TL of the USA using Method 2, although they have a slightly lower TL when employing
Method 1.

of fossil fuel replacement with alternative options. Figure 4.1 made by Chatterjee et al.

4.1
Influence of the Downscaling Approach
The assessment highlighted stark differences in TL between GF and EPC. GF, based on
historical emissions, tends to favor countries with a high baseline of emissions, allowing
them to maintain higher emission levels. In contrast, the EPC allocates emissions based
on the current population and the economic value of cement, leading to a more equitable
but possibly more drastic distribution, especially for industrialized nations with historically
high emissions.
EPC is independent of the environmental indicator and uses GVA to approximate the
utility to society. EPC aims to distribute thresholds uniformly while considering the eco­
nomic contribution of industrial sectors, which is measured by GVA. It aims is to find a
middle ground between ensuring fair distribution and making a positive impact on soci­
ety’s economic well­being. GF distributes resources based on past emissions, providing
greater flexibility to sectors and countries that currently have a significant impact.This
demonstrates current effects and potentially continues the current situation, instead of
encouraging efforts to reduce.
40

In order to progress towards sustainability, the cement industry needs to tackle these dis­
crepancies and work towards a fairer distribution of environmental capacity. This may en­
tail reassessing allocation methods to promote sustainable practices, investing in cleaner
technologies, and endorsing international agreements that acknowledge the diverse ca­
pabilities and responsibilities of different nations. Only by taking these factors into account
can the cement industry aspire to align with the objectives of environmental sustainability
and fair economic growth.
4.2
Uncertainties in Impact Scores for Cement
The assessment of the environmental impact of cement production is a complex task as
it often faces data gaps. This issue is especially noticeable in low­income and develop­
ing countries. Possible reasons for this could include the presence of smaller cement
industries in those countries or the delayed accessibility of data due to infrastructure and
institutional limitations.
When assessing the environmental impact of cement, there is no universally accepted
method for doing so. Certain studies rely on environmental data from different databases
[18], while others determine the unitary impact and then multiply it by the demand [17]. In
this study, both methods were tested, and the method of utilizing the impact data from the
Eora26 database was selected. The cause of this situation arose from the fact that the
calculation of the unitary impact of cement multiplied with the demand for cement caused
the global impact of cement to be almost be almost as high as the overall anthropogenic
impact measured for the entire world. That method was therefore avoided since it would
have caused the global SOS for cement, calculated with the GF allocation principle to be
roughly 70% of the SOS for humanity.
4.3
Uncertainties in Downscaling the Planetary Boundary
The selection of high income countries was straightforward since all the databases and
sources utilized in this study contained data for those specific countries. The majority of
the data was also accessible for the upper­middle income countries. However, several of
the sources initially discovered for the research did not contain data for the low and lower­
middle income countries. As a result, there was a persistent effort to explore alternative
sources and databases, while also attempting to adapt the selection of countries based
on their accessibility.
Downscaling the planetary boundaries to the country level introduces some uncertainties,
such as data quality, fast­growing economies and populations, and market dynamics re­
lated to imports and exports. The concept of PB also involves some uncertainties as it is
based on the science of complex earth systems [51]. When downscaling with the EPC
allocation principle, gaps in GVA data can lead to the reliance on less accurate metrics,
such as the price multiplied by demand, which may not capture the full economic impact
of cement production.
For instance, EXIOBASE’s database consisted of only 44 countries, comprising 28 Eu­
Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment of Cement Production and Consumption
41
==PAGE:50==
ropean Union members and 16 significant economies [52]. Therefore, EXIOBASE did
not have data available for the chosen countries in this study. As a result, an alternative
approach was incorporated, which involved estimating the GVA of cement by multiplying
the price per kilogram of cement by the demand for cement. However, even this method
encountered challenges when it came to collecting the necessary data. Inconsistent data
was observed for China, where the total GVA of China in 2022 was significantly lower, ap­
proximately 20 times, than the total GVA of China in 2021. This inconsistency may have
been influenced by various factors such as COVID­19 or difficulties in obtaining reliable
data from China.
Finding the unitary price of cement was also a challenging task, as it is difficult to locate
reliable online sources suitable for academic research. Instead, the method of using trade
unit value (TUV) was used. This made it somewhat easier to obtain the necessary data for
TUV and trade data, such as imports and exports, since these sources are government­
based and thus more trustworthy. However, after examining three sources of trade data,
it was determined that the World Integrated Trade Solution (WITS) contained all the data
required for the calculations in this study [41]. Consequently, the TUV and overall demand
in each country were obtained from this database. The initial source investigated was the
UN Comtrade database, which appeared to have inconsistent data in recent years and
exceptionally high TUV values [53]. The leading French center for research and expertise
on the world economy, called CEPII, also faced limitations in terms of the availability of
import and export data for all countries, as well as having an unexpectedly high TUV for
certain countries [54].
4.4
Implications
The TL of cement production in the countries varies significantly based on the chosen
allocation principle and the method used for GVA of cement. When taking India as an
example, the TL varies from 0,67 when using GF to 49 when using EPC Method 1 and 27
when using EPC Method 2. The large change between EPC Method 1 and 2 indicates that
there is a large difference between the estimation of the GVA using the price times demand
and the actual GVA of cement obtained from EXIOBASE. Therefore, there is an evident
need for better quality data, particularly from developing countries and during periods of
global disruptions such as the COVID­19 pandemic. The inconsistencies in Exiobase data
year­on­year, as seen with China, require a deeper investigation of reporting standards
and practices.
The choice of allocation principles and methods significantly affects the assessment, un­
derlining the importance of ethical considerations and the need for housing and infras­
tructure for growing populations. Reflecting on these points, it becomes clear that the
sustainability of cement production cannot only be assessed, but requires a holistic view
that includes ethical considerations, economic needs, and environmental impacts. The
discussion should lead to actionable steps or improvements in allocation principles that
recognize the value of cement in societal development.

5
Conclusion and way forward
This study aimed to provide a comprehensive analysis of the environmental impact of
cement production through the lens of absolute environmental sustainability assessment,
focusing on whether the product remains within the Earth’s safe operating space.
The findings of this study show a significant difference in sustainability assessments based
on the allocation principles used. The principle of equal per capita deems all countries
unsustainable when linked to the GVA associated with cement. In contrast, the Grand­
fathering principle concludes that 50% of the countries, predominantly the lower­middle
and low­income ones, are environmentally sustainable. The difference mentioned above
emphasizes how allocation methods greatly influence absolute environmental sustainabil­
ity assessments. The results also demonstrate how employing two distinct approaches
under the equal per capita allocation principle can impact the outcomes. In this case,
an estimate of the GVA of cement, calculated as the price of cement times demand, led
to mainly reduced levels of transgression and allowed two countries classified as lower­
middle and low­incomes to achieve environmental sustainability.
These findings unravel the nuanced relationship between a country’s economic status and
the sustainability of its cement industry. The research indicates that lower­middle and low­
income countries are characterized by a lower TL, suggesting that some of the countries
do not exceed the SOS, so the cement production within these regions is sustainable.
This is due to cement’s substantial contribution to their total GVA, which allocates a larger
SOS for these economies.
In contrast, upper­middle and high­income countries display a higher TL, which implies
that their cement production exceeds the SOS, meaning that it is environmentally unsus­
tainable. These countries’ broader economic spectrum dilutes cement’s impact on their
overall GVA, leading to a reduced SOS.
The study’s approach to assessing environmental impact on both global and country
scales offers a more comprehensive understanding of the cement industry’s role in cli­
mate change. This is especially relevant as countries work towards achieving the ambi­
tious objectives of the Paris Agreement.
This study represents the first instance of applying absolute environmental sustainabil­
ity assessment to cement production, marking a novel contribution to the field. This re­
search offers a distinct view on the environmental impact of cement production by examin­
ing countries with varying economic statuses and two contrasting rights­based allocation
principles.
The practical implications of this research are substantial. It provides a baseline for gov­
ernments and cement producers to evaluate and improve their sustainability practices.
Additionally, the results of the study raise important ethical concerns regarding the fair

allocation of safe operating space and the obligations of various countries in reducing
emissions. The sustainability of the cement industry cannot be assessed using a single
perspective. The study indicates that lower­income countries may be able to grow their
cement industries without exceeding their ecological limits if they continue to adopt low­
emission approaches. In contrast, higher­income countries, burdened by past emissions
and currently high production levels, must confront the task of developing and investing
in more environmentally friendly production methods.
The research recognizes its limitations, specifically in its focus on the cradle­to­gate phase
and the exclusion of the cement use phase. This constraint was necessary due to the
complexities and assumptions associated with measuring the use phase of cement. Ad­
ditionally, the study was limited to an examination of only 10 countries, which, while pro­
viding valuable insights, suggests the potential for broader applicability and generalization
in future studies. The choice to concentrate on the equal per capita and grandfathering
allocation principles, though practical, leaves room for exploring other allocation methods
in subsequent research. This limited country scope, while a constraint, was essential for
managing the study’s scale and depth but points towards the need for more extensive,
global research in the future.
