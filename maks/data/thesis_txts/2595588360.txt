1 Abstract 
Microbial organisms interact through intertwined microbial interactions. These are difficult to study in 
natural settings. Therefore, there is a need to develop simplified models, microcosms, which mimic the 
natural system and can be investigated in the laboratory. The microorganisms associated with plant roots 
are particularly interesting, as these can confer beneficial effects to the plants. This can be utilised in 
agricultural settingss. The microbial interaction between these microbes have great importance for the 
overall composition and function of the community. These microbial communities are structured in biofilms 
that form a particular type of microenvironment, which influences the interspecies interactions. Thus, 
biofilm-forming microcosms can function as a model to investigate the interspecies interactions in plant-
beneficial bacteria and thereby enhance the understanding of the plant-beneficial properties. 
 
One such microcosm has been isolated around the plant-beneficial bacteria Bacillus velezensis SQR9 (S). 
This microcosm also holds Acinetobacter baumannii XL380 (A) and Burkholderia contaminans XL73 (B). 
Previous experiments carried out by Xinli (co-supervisor of this thesis) show that B. velezensis and B. 
contaminans yield substantially increased cell numbers in this three-strain-microcosm (ABS), compared to 
mono- or dicultures. To generate hypotheses of the microbial interactions that lead to the increased 
growth, Xinli measured the gene expression through RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) of all three strains in all 
possible combinations. With this gene expression data in hand, my thesis aims to elucidate possible 
microbial interaction that could yield the observed microcosm-dependent growth differences. 
 
First, I set out to investigate the growth dynamics, which could yield the observed difference in strain 
abundance. I identified three growth parameters which could explain the cell level difference. I hypothesise 
that microbial interactions in the microcosm changed one or more of these parameters and thereby the 
growth dynamics of B. velezensis. Unfortunately, it was not possible to obtain growth curves of B. 
velezensis, which would reveal the changes in these growth parameters. Instead, I integrated a publicly 
available RNA-seq time-series data set of Bacillus subtilis with the gene expression data from the 
microcosms. With the combined data set, I conducted a principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) to reveal 
which time points of Bacillus subtilis shared the most gene expression with B. velezensis in the different 
microcosms. From PCoA analysis, I estimated the growth phase of B. velezensis at the time they had 
reached the large cell accumulation. My estimate suggested that B velezensis was transitioning from the 
exponential to the stationary growth phase, indicating that B. velezensis was relieved of some limiting 
factor which increased the carrying capacity in the three-strain microcosms.  
 
To illuminate possible metabolic interactions within the multispecies microcosm, I conducted a gene set 
analysis based on pathways from Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG). The results turned 
out to be noisy and require heavy manual curation. However, they did reveal that B. velezensis 
downregulated the production of branched-chain amino acids (BCAA) in the presence of B. contaminans. 
Contrary, B. contaminans increased the biosynthesis and decreased the degradation of BCAAs. 
Furthermore, B. velezensis upregulated the biosynthesis of the siderophore, bacillibactin, in the presence of 
both B. contaminans and A. baumannii, while B. contaminans downregulated the siderophore biosynthesis 
in the presence of B. velezensis.  Based on these results, I hypothesise that B. contaminans produce BCAAs 
==PAGE:3==
2 
 
that relieve a metabolic burden of B. velezensis and increases its carrying capacity. Meanwhile, B. velezensis 
produces siderophores that support B. contaminans. This production was not induced in the microcosm 
that only contained B. contaminans and B. velezensis. Therefore, I propose that A. baumannii induce 
siderophore production in B. velezensis and thus initiates a positive feedback loop between B. velezensis 
and B. contaminans which allows the increased cell accumulation in the three species microcosm.

2 Resumé 
Mikrobielle organismer interagerer gennem komplekse og sammenfiltrede interaktioner. Disse 
interaktioner er teknisk svære at undersøge i deres naturlige omgivelser. Derfor er der brug for at udvikle 
mikrokosmer som efterligner det naturlige system, men samtidigt kan undersøges i et laboratorium. De 
mikroorganismer som findes omkring planters rødder er særligt interessante, da de kan medføre fordele 
for planternes vækst og modstandsdygtighed mod sygdomme. Sådanne mikroorganismer kan derfor aktivt 
bruges til at øge udbyttet fra marker i landbruget. Det er derfor vigtigt at forstå de interaktioner og 
dynamikker som foregår blandt bakterierne som har positive effekter på planter. Bakterierne omkring 
planternes rødder vokser i biofilm-strukturer. Disse strukturer udformer nogle særlige mikromiljøer som 
har stor betydning for hvordan mikroorganismerne fungerer. Når man vil undersøge disse interaktioner er 
det derfor vigtig at gøre det i mikrokosmer som også danner biofilm for at efterligne den naturlige situation 
mest muligt. 
 
Et sådanne mikrokosmos er blevet isoleret med den planteforbedrende bakterie Bacillus velezensis SQR9 i 
centrum. Dette mikrokosmos indeholder også bakterierne Acinetobacter baumannii XL380 og 
Burkholderderia contaminans XL73. Medvejleder, Xinli Sun, har tidligere udført eksperimenter som viste at 
bakterierne B. contaminans og B. velezensis vokser markant bedre i dette mikrokosmos som består af tre 
arter. For at danne hypoteser for de underliggende årsager til den markant forbedrede vækst, udførte Xinli 
et RNA-sekventerings eksperiment som genererede genekspressions data fra alle mikrokosmerne. Med 
dette genekspressions data vil jeg forsøge at belyse mulige mikrobielle interaktioner som kunne resultere i 
den forbedrede vækst i mikrokosmet. 
 
Først of fremmest undersøgte jeg hvilke vækstdynamikker som kunne lede til den observerede 
vækstforskel. Jeg identificerede tre vækstparametre som hver især kunne forklare forskellene. Jeg opsatte 
hypotesen at de mikrobielle interaktioner i mikrokosmosset var årsag til at disse vækstparametre, og 
dermed bakteriernes vækstdynamik, ændredes. Det var desværre ikke muligt at producere vækstkurver for 
B. velezensis, som ellers havde kunne vise forskellene i vækstdynamikker mellem mikrokosmerne. I stedet 
kombinerede jeg et offentligt tilgængeligt tidsrække-genexpressions-dataset af Bacillus subtilis med 
genekspressions-dataen fra de forskellige mikrokosmer. Med dette kombinerede dataset, udførte jeg the 
principal koordinat analyse for at undersøge hvilke B. subtilis tidspunkter som havde mest sammenlignelig 
genexpression med B. velezensis i de forskellige mikrokosmer. På baggrund af denne analyse estimerede 
jeg at B. velezensis var ved at skifte fra den eksponentielle til den stationære vækstfase. Dette indikerede at 
B. velezensis var blevet afhjulpet en begrænsende faktor og dermed havde forøget sin vækstkapacitet i tre-
arts mikrokosmet. Jeg opsatte derfor hypotesen at B. velezensis får øget sin vækstkapacitet via 
interaktionerne mellem alle tre bakterier. 
 
For at undersøge mulige metaboliske interaktioner i mikrokosmet udførte jeg en gensæt analyse baseret på 
biosyntese-router fra Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG). Resultaterne viste sig at være 
fejlbehæftede and det krævede et stort manuelt med at kuratere. Dog indikerede resultaterne at B. 
velezensis nedregulerede sin biosyntese af aminosyrerne valin, leucin og isoleucin når B. contaminans var 
tilstede. Modsat, øgede B. contaminans sin produktion og nedregulerede nedbrydningen af de selvsamme 
==PAGE:5==

aminosyrer. Yderligere fandt jeg at B. velezensis øgede sin produktion af siderofor-molekylet bacillibactin, 
når alle tre bakterier var tilstede. I mellemtiden nedregulerede B. contaminans sin produktion af siderofor-
molekyler under tilstedeværelsen af B. velezensis. Baseret på disse resultater opsatte jeg hypotesen at B. 
contaminans producerer aminosyrene valin, leucin og isoleucin som afhjælper B. velezensis fra en 
metabolisk byrde og dermed øger dens vækstkapacitet. Samtidigt producerer B. velezensis siderofor-
molekyler som hjælper B. contaminans. Denne produktion af siderofor-molekyler er dog ikke induceret i 
mikrokosmet som kun indeholder B. contaminans og B. velezensis. Derfor foreslår jeg at A. baumannii 
kunne inducere denne sideroforproduktion i B. velezensis and dermed starte et positivt feedback loop 
mellem B. velezensis og B. contaminans som øger væksten i mikrokosmet.

3 Aim of the project 
Co-supervisor, Xinli Sun, studies the plant-beneficial effects of Bacillus velezensis SQR9 as her PhD project. 
She focusses on the microbial interactions centred around B. velezensis in the rhizosphere. To study these 
interactions, she first isolated bacteria which she predicted to positively interact with B. velezensis. Among 
these bacteria she found two strains Acinetobacter baumannii XL380 and Burkholderia contaminans XL73, 
that would form pellicle biofilms in all combinations with B. velezensis under laboratory conditions. 
Through the thesis I will refer to the microcosm with a one-letter abbreviation for each strain, e.g. the ABS 
microcosm contains A. baumannii XL380, B. contaminans XL73 and B. velezensis SQR9. Thus, all possible 
microcosm are A, B, S, AB, AS, BS and ABS. 
 
Xinli investigated the strain abundance in all possible microcosms and found that the abundance of both B. 
velezensis and B. contaminans largely increased in the ABS microcosm after 24 hours of growth. I will 
present these results in section 9.1. To unravel the microbial interactions leading to this substantial 
increase in abundance, she had the genome sequenced and conducted an RNA-seq experiment by sampling 
the biofilm pellicle after 24 hours of growth.  
 
Based on the increased abundance of both B. velezensis and B. contaminans in the ABS microcosm, I 
hypothesise that the three bacterial strains positively interact in the pellicle biofilm. In this study, I will 
elucidate the interactions through two stages. First, I will investigate the fundamental growth dynamic 
changes that could lead to the observed differences in abundance. With these results in mind, I will then 
seek to discover possible interactions that would yield the hypothesised change in growth dynamics (This 
will be elaborated in section 9.1). I will investigate this based on the gene expression data that Xinli 
collected. Thus, I hypothesise that the gene expression in the three bacteria is changed as a result of the 
interspecies interactions. 
 
In summary, the strain abundance data, genomes and RNA-seq data present the base of my thesis. With 
this data available, the overall goal was to elucidate possible microbial interactions that could yield the 
observed microcosm-dependent growth differences.

7 Introduction 
7.1 Biofilm and bacterial interactions  
In nature, bacteria rarely live alone but participate in intricate biological systems cooperating with and 
competing against each other. They organise into large microbiomes, which can consist of hundreds of 
different species, which all potentially affect each other and the surrounding environment, either directly 
or indirectly. Such natural systems are incredibly complex due to their many interactions, and are difficult 
to replicate in controlled laboratory environments. To aid this, ecologists invented the concept of 
microcosms, which are simplified ecological systems that can be controlled and investigated in the 
laboratory. These are rough simplifications of the natural microbiomes, but they can be used to provide 
evidence of the fundamental concepts of the ecological systems [1].  
 
The microbiomes around the plant roots, called rhizobiome, have received particular attention through 
many years. They harbour a range of different bacteria and fungi that have various effects on the plant. The 
presence of specific bacterial strains in the rhizobiome can be beneficial to the plant. The plant-beneficial 
bacteria are of specific interest as these can help the plants fight diseases and positively affect their growth. 
Apart from being an interesting biological phenomenon, farmers can utilise such beneficial bacteria to 
increase the health and yield of their crops. This use of bacteria is called biocontrol. In practice, the 
beneficial bacteria are spread on to a field, where they migrate to the rhizosphere. The exchange of various 
metabolites between the plant, the bacteria and the environment plays a vital role in the plant-beneficial 
effects of the rhizobiome [2]. 
 
The natural microbiomes are often organised in biofilm structures, and this is also the case for the bacteria 
in rhizobiome [2]. Biofilms are three-dimensional structures constructed from various proteins and DNA 
molecules. The structure is produced by bacteria and creates a special type of environment[3]. For 
example, nutrients are heterogeneously distributed in biofilm structures and the spatial organisation is 
particularly important for multispecies biofilms [4]. Thus, to study the microbial interactions in the 
rhizobiome, it is valuable to investigate the behaviour of the bacteria in biofilm. Inside biofilms, the bacteria 
greatly affect each other through microbial interactions. These can have various consequences for the 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
involved species and be conferred though a range of different direct and indirect mechanisms [2], [5]. A 
typical cooperative interaction is cross-feeding, where one organism produces a compound which is utilised 
by another, it is said that the compound is a public good [6]. Contrary, the organisms might also show 
competitive interactions such as production of antibiotics that inhibit the growth of competitors [7].  
 
Biofilms typically form at some interphase, e.g. solid-air, solid-liquid or liquid-air. The latter is termed a 
pellicle and forms on the surface of the medium in standing cultures. The pellicle begins as a thin layer 
across the surface and grows in thickness over time. Kobayashi (2007) showed that Bacillus subtilis forms 
intricate clusters of chaining cells in the bottom of the tubes, which resembles the structure in mature 
pellicle. From this observation they hypothesize that the structure forms in the liquid and subsequently 
floats to the surface. Furthermore, they observed a different route for pellicle formation which was flagella 
dependent [8]. This route was elucidated by Hölscher et al. (2015), who found that motility and chemotaxis 
was important for efficient pellicle formation. In particular, they found that the oxygen gradient leading to 
the surface plays a role in pellicle formation through this motility dependent route [9]. 
 
 
7.2 Bacillus velezensis SQR9 
Bacillus is a species of soil bacteria that is often assigned plant-beneficial functions. Thus, many biocontrol 
products that contain members of the Bacillus sp. are available on the market [10]. One particular isolate, 
Bacillus velezensis SQR9, has proved to be useful for several plants. B. velezensis SQR9 suppresses the 
pathogen Fusarium oxysporum on cucumber plants [11] and the pathogens Pseudomonas syringae pv. 
tomato DC3000 and Botrytis cinereal on Arabidopsis [12]. Furthermore, B. velezensis increases plant growth 
[13], [14]. Several studies of B. velezensis have investigated the beneficial effect on plants and the 
molecular mechanism hereof. These studies find an association between the ability to form biofilm and the 
plant-promoting activity of B. velezensis [15]–[17]. All these studies consider B. velezensis as a monoculture. 
However, this is far from the real ecological situation, where B. velezensis will likely incorporate in 
multispecies biofilms attached to the plant root. Thus, to get a better understanding of the mechanism 
behind the plant-beneficial effects it is required to understand the microbial interactions that B. velezensis 
SQR9 participates in.  
 
Xinli Sun (Co-supervisor of this project) created a three-member microcosm that contained the three 
bacteria Acinetobacter baumannii XL380, Burkholderia contaminans XL73 and Bacillus velezensis SQR9. In 
standing cultures, these bacteria form floating pellicles in all possible mono-, di- and tricultures. Thus, these 
microcosms can serve as model systems of B. velezensis to study its microbial interactions in multispecies 
biofilms. 
 
7.3 Bacterial growth dynamics 
Most bacterial populations follow a logistic growth curve, where the bacteria pass through different growth 
phases. Traditionally three growth phases are considered for planktonic cultures; lag phase, exponential 
phase and stationary phase [18].  
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
 
When the environment changes, the bacteria must adjust their metabolism to fit their new surroundings. In 
the laboratory, this happens when the bacteria are inoculated in a nutrient-rich medium. Adjusting the 
metabolism takes time as it requires expression of new proteins, and in the meantime, the bacteria do not 
grow at the optimal rate. This results in a period where the population only slowly increases. This period is 
called the lag phase [18]. 
 
Once the metabolism is adjusted, the population enters the exponential growth phase, where the 
population is growing at the maximum growth rate. At some point, the growth rate will start declining due 
to a low substrate concentration or accumulation of growth-inhibiting metabolites. The population starts 
transitioning to the stationary phase. This is sometimes referred to as the transition phase. Once again, the 
bacteria remodel their metabolism to compensate for the limiting factors, for example, de novo synthesis 
of amino acids or degradation of inhibiting compounds [18].  
 
In the stationary phase, population size is constant. Thus the cells divide approximately at the same rate as 
they die. The population is said to have reached its carrying capacity, i.e. the maximum concentration of 
viable cells under the given conditions. After some time, the population enters the death phase, and the 
level of viable cells begins to decline [18].  
 
Differences in the environment may strongly influence the length and the parameters of the different 
phases, such as the duration of the lag phase, maximum growth rate and the carrying capacity. These 
environmental differences can be biotic factors, e.g. available nutrient, or abiotic factors, such as 
temperature or pH. The presence or absence of other organisms can also affect the these growth 
parameters; either through direct interactions or by modulating the environment. More complex dynamics 
may occur, such as substrate shifts, but for simplicity I will mainly consider the simplified model just 
described [18]. 
 
7.4 Omics data 
To elucidate the interactions in biofilm, one needs to set up hypotheses that can be tested in the 
laboratory, e.g. through mutants or supplementation studies. In a multispecies biofilm, such as created by 
the B. velezensis microcosm, there are many possible modes of interactions. Thus, to generate reasonable 
hypotheses, it can be advantageous to use omics-based methods. These allow researchers to elucidate the 
state of one type of molecule, for instance, RNA, proteins or metabolites. Essentially, these methods create 
a snapshot of the cells with respect to the chosen molecule, e.g. the relative level of all proteins. There are 
many types of omics, but commonly used methods include transcriptomics, proteomics and metabolomics. 
These all return the number of molecules observed in a sample. The different omics methods all have their 
advantages and disadvantages based on technical and biological reasoning [19]. I will now briefly discuss 
the three classical omics proteomics, metabolomics and transcriptomics.

7.5 Proteomics 
Most often, it is the proteins that results in the biological function of interest. Thus, the protein levels in 
different biological conditions are of great interest to study. Recent advances in the mass spectrometry 
have enabled proteomics in true high throughput manner [20]. However, there are still some limitations. 
When conducting proteomics one needs to decide which fraction of the proteins to analyse, i.e. 
intracellular, membrane-bound or extracellular. Furthermore, the presence of a protein might not mean 
that it is active. Thus, the presence of the enzymes in a biosynthetic pathway is not proof that this pathway 
is active. 
 
7.6 Metabolomics  
If the biosynthetic pathways are specifically of interest, metabolomics can be an appropriate method. 
Metabolomics show which metabolites were present at the time of sampling. From the metabolites, one 
can infer which biosynthetic pathways were active. The goal is often to find metabolites that accumulate or 
new metabolites that appear as a response to some condition. Furthermore, the metabolome can be used 
to calculate the flux through biosynthetic pathways, which can reveal the activity of the pathways. Such 
calculations require a metabolic flux model of the biosynthetic network. Unfortunately, such models are 
difficult to create due to the incomplete knowledge of the biosynthetic pathways. Finally, the different 
methods to quantify the metabolites are specific to certain molecular properties, e.g. hydrophobic, 
charged, etc. Thus, with limited resources, researchers often have to prioritize which type of metabolite 
they are interested in studying.  
 
7.7 Transcriptomics 
Transcriptomics methods quantify the amount of RNA in the cell, typically mRNA. The major advantage of 
transcriptomics is that all mRNA have the same chemical properties and can, therefore, be measured 
simultaneously, as they are all found in the same cellular localisation. The major disadvantage is that the 
protein levels do not always correlate with the mRNA level. Thus, it is difficult to draw any definite 
conclusion from transcriptome data [21]. The inconclusiveness is not a large issue when the purpose is 
exploratory. Contrary, the generality is adequate when one has no hypotheses about the phenomena. In 
combination with the many years of experience of analysing the data, transcriptomics is very appropriate 
for data-driven hypothesis generation. 
 
7.8 Transcriptome analysis 
There are two methods for gathering transcriptome data: microarray and RNA sequencing (RNA-seq). In 
this thesis, I will only consider RNA-seq. When researchers conduct RNA-seq experiments, they first extract 
mRNA from a culture of cells. The mRNA is then broken into similar-sized fragments of around 50 – 300 bp 
and converted into cDNA. The cDNA is sequenced, and this results in millions of 50 – 300 bp sequences 
called reads. Computational tools can then map each read to the genome of the organisms. The gene 
expression is then inferred by counting the number of reads that are mapped to a given gene. These read 
counts are most often compiled into a matrix that contains the gene IDs as rows and the sample IDs as 
columns. Each entry in the matrix holds the number of reads mapped to the corresponding gene in the 
corresponding sample [21]. For later reference, I denote this the expression matrix 𝐸, with 𝑚 rows and 𝑛 
columns. I will use 𝑖 and 𝑗 to denote specific genes and samples, i.e. i ∈{1,2, … m} and j ∈{1,2, … n}.

7.8.1 Normalisation 
Often the purpose of transcriptome analysis is to compare gene expression levels between samples. 
Unfortunately, the total amount of mRNA that is sequenced (the library size) varies from sample to sample. 
This variation can arise due to differences in the sample preparation, such as different amounts of 
extracted RNA. Thus, without initial adjustment of the read counts, variation from the sample preparation 
will affect the results of the analysis. Additionally, more reads will map to long genes than to short genes. 
Several methods attempt to normalise the read counts and thereby remove the bias of library size and 
gene length [21].

Fragments Per Kilobase per Million mapped reads (FPKM). The two methods are analogous, where the 
Reads and Fragments refer to two different sequencing methods: single-end and paired-end sequencing, 
respectively. Both methods use the sum of mapped reads in each sample and the gene lengths to normalise 
the counts [21]. Thus, each element in the expression matrix 𝐸 can be a normalised through the following

Huber and Anders (2010) presented an alternative method for normalisation for library size in their R-

size factor normalisation assumes that the technical bias is the same for the differentially expressed genes 
(DEGs) and the non-DEGs. Furthermore, size factor normalization assumes that the DEGs are balanced, i.e. 
there are equivalent amounts of up- and downregulated genes in a sample [24]. Size factor normalisation 
does not account for the gene length and can, therefore, only be used to compare the expression level of 
the same gene between samples [23]. 
 
7.8.2 Inference of differentially expressed genes  
When the read counts are adequately normalised, we want to investigate which of the genes are 
significantly lower or higher expressed between two conditions, i.e. finding the DEGs. Immediately, this 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
seems to be a straightforward statistical problem; using student’s t-test to determine if the mean gene 
expression is significantly different between two conditions. Unfortunately, there are three major 
challenges which make the standard approach unsuitable [21]: 
1. The data cannot be assumed to follow a normal distribution 
2. Typically, the number of samples is small 
3. Multiple hypothesis testing inflates the risk of false positives 
In RNA-seq, the measurements are obtained by counting the number of reads that are mapped to a given 
gene. Count data is usually better modelled by a Poisson distribution than a normal distribution. A main 
property of the Poisson distribution is that the mean and variance are equal. RNA-seq data rarely fulfil this 
property, i.e. the sample variance is greater than the sample mean. Instead, RNA-seq data is often assumed 
to follow a negative binomial distribution, which allows the mean and variance to be estimated separately. 
This results in the second challenge: the small number of biological replicates makes it challenging to obtain 
reliable estimates of the two parameters, i.e. the mean and variance. This problem can be solved by linking 
the variance to the mean through some function [21]. Generally said

Where σ2 is the variance and μ is the mean of the negative binomial distribution. Now one can estimate 
the mean counts and calculate the variances for each gene across the two conditions. Finally, one can 
determine the probability of observing the given read counts or something more extreme (in both 
conditions) given the estimated negative binomial distribution. This produces a p-value for each gene and 
each condition comparison [21]. Several R-packages compute DEGs from RNA-seq data. In this study, I will 
use the DESeq2 R-package that utilises a general linear model with a negative binomial distribution to infer 
DEGs [23]. 
 
The multiple hypotheses testing problem occurs because RNA-seq data often holds measurements of 
thousands of genes. Consequently, the DEG analysis generates thousands of p-values, and hence increases 
the risk of false discoveries. With a significance level of 0.05, one should expect 5 % of true null hypotheses 
to by falsely rejected, i.e. approximately 5% of the true non-DEGs will falsely be determined to be DEG. 
Since the classic Bonferroni correction is often deemed too conservative for exploratory studies of genetics, 
the standard way of dealing with this issue in RNA-Seq analysis is to control the false discovery rate (FDR) 
through the Benjamini-Hochberg method. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure uses a stepwise approach to 
control the FDR rather than the comparisonwise error rate. The result is that the p-values for each gene are 
adjusted to FDR values and can thus be compared directly with a desired FDR cutoff that has an acceptable 
amount of false positives, while not being too conservative for exploratory analysis [21].

7.8.4 Clustering techniques for transcriptome data 
Once the DEGs and their LFCs are determined, the massive amount of data can be overwhelming and tricky 
to comprehend. Luckily, methods have been developed to overcome this challenge. Several ways of 
clustering samples or genes provide an overview of which behave similarly. There are two major 
approaches for providing such overview. The first relies on numerical methods that quantify how similar or 
dissimilar samples or genes are. Such methods are called clustering methods. The second approach is called 
knowledge-based clustering and utilises prior knowledge of functionality, cellular localisation, molecular 
interactions, etc. to group the genes. I will now present one method from each approach: Principal 
Coordinate Analysis (PCoA) and Gene Set Analysis (GSA) [21]. 
 
7.8.4.1 
Principal Coordinate Analysis 
PCoA is an ordination technique that reduces the number of dimensions in a data set. The goal is to identify 
the dimensions that give the best separation of the data. This allows researchers to visualise high-
dimensional data in a 2D or 3D plot. There are immediately two approaches to conduct PCoA of 
transcriptome data: either considering the similarity of samples or the similarity of genes. I will consider the 
first approach. Here, each sample represents a data point, and the number of genes determines the 
number of dimensions. PCoA finds the Principal Coordinates (PCo) that best separates each sample based 
on the expression level of each gene. Thus, samples with similar expression levels of many genes will 
cluster together. The assumption is that the cells in clustering samples are biologically similar due to their 
similar gene expression [21].

7.8.4.2 
Knowledge-based clustering 
Through many years researchers have collected biological knowledge in large biological databases, e.g. 
Uniprot.org [26], Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) [27] and Gene Ontology (GO) [28], 
[29]. It seems meaningful to incorporate the knowledge from these databases in the analysis of 
transcriptome data. GSA seeks to identify groups of genes (gene sets) that are differentially expressed 
between two conditions. These gene sets are typically created based on some biological database. For 
example, gene sets can be made based on the KEGG database to create a gene set for each biosynthetic 
pathway. Thus, a GSA based on KEGG gene sets will reveal which biosynthetic pathways are differentially 
expressed in one biological condition compared to another [21]. 
 
 
 
Generally, Applicable Gene set Enrichment (GAGE) analysis is an algorithm implemented in the R-package 
GAGE. This method utilises a two-sample t-test to test if the mean LFC of a gene set3 is different from the 
mean LFC in a randomly selected gene set of the same size. This analysis yields a mean LFC and an 
associated p-value for each gene set and is typically also subject to false discovery rate correction as 
described for the DEGs [30].

8.1 Data generation and processing before this study 
The base of this thesis is data that was generated by the co-supervisor Xinli and her group at Nanjing 
Agricultural University. There are three prior experiments which are essential for this study: microcosm

8.1.1 Microcosm growth experiment 
The experiment shows the relative abundance of the three bacteria when grown in mono-, di- or 
tricultures. The experiment was carried out in 6-well microtiter plates (VWR) with 100um Sterile Nylon 
Mesh Cell Strainers (Biologix Cat#15-1100) inserted using Tryptic Soy Broth (TSB) as growth medium. In 
addition to 100µm sterile nylon mesh cell strainer, Xinli also used a Spectra Mesh™ Woven Filter (Fisher 
Scientific, Spectrum™ 146488) inside. The mesh was manually cut into 1.5cm2 squares and autoclaved. To 
create inoculum cultures, Xinli grew overnight cultures of single strains that were grown in TSB liquid 
medium shaken at 180 RPM. The overnight cultures TSB liquid medium to remove residual compounds and 
by-products from strains specific growth medium and monoculture growth. These were mixed in equal 
amounts to a final OD600 of 1 to yield the mixed-species inoculums. Note that the inoculum of each strain in 
the tri- and dicultures were smaller than in the monocultures. The 6 well plates were inoculated with a 
single strain or mixed cultures to a final OD600 of 0.01. The 6 well plates were incubated for 24 hours at 30oC 
without shaking to allow pellicle formation at the liquid-air-interface. Samples were collected by lifting the 
mesh and the inner Spectra Mesh™ Woven Filter was transferred to a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube, which 
was frozen using liquid nitrogen and stored at -80oC. Genomic DNA was extracted from the frozen biofilm 
sampled using E.Z.N.A.® Bacterial DNA Kit (Omega Bio-tek, Inc.). The extracted genomic DNA was subjected 
to quantitative PCR (qPCR) to measure the relative abundance of strain-specific genes (Appendix 1.1).  
 
8.1.2 Genome sequencing and data processing 
B. contaminans XL73 and A. baumannii XL380 were isolated by Xinli Sun. The two isolates were sequenced 
to aid understanding of the interactions with B. velezensis SQR9. B. contaminans XL73 and A. baumannii 
XL380 cultures were grown for 20 hours with shaking at 180 RPM at 37oC in TSB medium. 40ml cell cultures 
were centrifuged and the medium was discarded. The cell pellets were frozen using liquid nitrogen and 
stored at -80oC and then transported to the company Beijing Genomics Institute (BGI, Shenzhen, China) on 
dry ice. Genomes were sequenced using a PacBio RS II platform and Illumina HiSeq 4000 platform at BGI 
and assembled using the Celera Assembler against a high quality corrected circular consensus sequence 
subreads set. Genes from the genomes were predicted and annotated using the PROKKA algorithm [31]. 
 
A genome sequence of B. velezensis SQR9 has earlier been published and is available at GenBank (Accession 
Number NZ_CP006890) [16]. This genome sequence was reannotated with the PROKKA algorithm [31].

ones in GenBank as they arise from two different annotations of the same genome.

9.1 Strain abundance in the different communities 
This study takes starting point in an observation that Xinli did when she cultivated the three bacteria

cell number in the pellicle was greatly increased in the ABS microcosm. Interestingly, The addition of A. 
baumannii to the BS culture increased the growth of both B. velezensis and B. contamins, despite of the low 
abundance of A. baumannii in the ABS (Figure 3A).

compared to the monoculture (Figure 3B). In the ABS microcosm B. velezensis yielded a 5-fold larger cell

9.2 Estimate growth phase 
To investigate the three hypotheses, I had to investigate the growth curves of B. velezensis before the 
sampling time. Therefore, I set out to estimate the growth phases at the time of sampling to try to predict 
their growth curves before the sampling.  
 
Pisithkul et al. generated an extensive time-series transcriptome data set of B. subtilis forming pellicle 
biofilm. This data set is available online, and growth curves are available in the published article [34]. I 
combined their data of B. subtilis with the data of B. velezensis in an effort to estimate the growth state of 
B. velezensis at sampling time in the different microcosms. I assumed that two related species share 
enough gene expression patterns to yield a reliable estimate. Thus, I hypothesise that the B. velezensis 
samples would have the most similar gene expression pattern to the B. subtilis samples in the same growth 
phase.

10 Hypothesis based on growth state estimation 
Assuming that B. velezensis follows logistic growth, I hypothesised about possible growth curves which 
could lead to the observed differences in cell number based on the estimated growth phases (Figure 9). A 
range of settings for the lag phase duration and maximum growth rate could yield the observed cell 
number and growth phase at 24 hours. Figure 9 indicates the extreme cases of these two parameters, i.e. 
short lag phase and low maximum growth rate and long lag phase and high maximum growth rate. In all 
possible scenarios included in my hypothesised growth courses, the growth rates were declining in all 
samples at the sampling time. This implies that all cultures of B. velezensis are approaching the carrying 
capacity. Thus, this indicates that it is unlikely that B. velezensis in the BS, AS, and S microcosms would 
reach a similar cell density as in the ABS microcosm if given more time to grow. The hypothesised growth 
courses, therefore, suggest that microbial interaction in the ABS microcosm increases the carrying capacity 
of B. velezensis relative to the BS, AS, and S microcosms. Whether interactions in the ABS microcosm also 
modulate the maximum growth rate and the lag phase duration remains unknown (Figure 9).

10.1 Gene set analysis

11 Discussion 
11.1 Growth state estimation 
11.1.1 Data foundation 
To estimate the growth rate of B. velezensis in the different microcosms, I used growth curve and the 
transcriptome data recorded of B. subtilis during biofilm formation. The two data sets have differences and 
similarities. The most obvious difference between the experiments is the involved bacterial species. 
Fortunately, B. subtilis 168 and B. velezensis SQR9 are found to be closely related, and the majority of the 
protein-coding genes were conserved between the two strains [16]. Thus, the strains should be 
comparable, especially in terms of core genes used for growth and biofilm formation. 
 
The two experiments had other major differences, such as growth medium and temperature. B. subtilis was 
grown in a defined medium with glycerol and glutamate as the sole carbon and nitrogen sources [34], while 
B. velezensis was grown in a complex medium. Furthermore, B. subtilis and B. velezensis were grown at 
30oC and 37oC, respectively. Finally, the experiments differ in the sampling procedure, sequencing method 
and the bioinformatic pipelines used to process the raw reads into gene counts.  
 
In the three PCoA, PCo1 accounts for 42.7-54.6% of the variance and PCo1 separates the two experiments. 
This is most probably because of the experimental differences described above. Pisithkul et al. conducted a 
principal component analysis of the B. subtilis time-series data that revealed an ordination similar to what I 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
observe along PCo2 [34]. This indicates that the fusion of the two data sources did not introduce any severe 
bias in the data. 
 
The B. subtilis data is available in RPKM normalised format. To get the best comparability between the data 
set, I used the FPKM normalised version of the B. velezensis data, even though this normalisation method 
seems far from optimal. Dillies et al. (2013) recommended abandoning the use of RPKM normalisation for 
inference of DEGs in RNA-seq data [64]. Furthermore, a review by Evans et al. (2018) considered 6 papers 
that compare RPKM/FPKM normalisation to other methods. RPKM/FPKM was found to perform among the 
worst methods in 4 of the 6 papers and equally to other methods in the remaining two studies [24]. This 
imposes a problem for the validity of my analysis. Unfortunately, it not possible to test the assumptions of 
RPKM/FPKM normalised data, since the unnormalized data is not available. Whether the normalisation 
methods affect the PCoA analysis is not certain. 
 
11.1.2 Principle coordinate analysis 
11.1.2.1 Growth time separation along PCo2 
The PCoA successfully separated the B. subtilis samples by increasing growth time using both the 
normalised count values and the variance-stabilised values. The growth dependency was evident in the 
position of the centroids. The p-values for the centroids turned out to be exactly equal for all three PCoA. 
This is most probably because these p-values are calculated based on a permutation test, which has a lower 
limit to the p-value, that depends on the number of permutations used. The low p-value describes that the 
position of the centroids is specific for that condition, e.g. B. velezensis in ABS or B. subtilis after 16 hours. 
The p-values do not assess the actual correlation with the PCos. The correlation between the sample and 
position along PCo2 could be calculated to support the hypothesis further that PCo2 does separate samples 
according to their growth phase. 
 
The loadings on PCo2 of both normalised counts and the variance-stabilised values show that the genes 
accounting for most of the separation along PCo2 are involved in growth-associated process’. In summary, I 
found that the expression of translation-related genes was inversely proportional to growth time. Through 
the loadings of the PCoA based on the variance-stabilised values, I found a correlation between growth 
time and the expression of genes for utilisation of a biofilm-associated fermentation product and 
biosynthesis of a cell-wall component associated non-growing B. subtilis cultures (Figure 7). 
 
Similar to the p-values for the centroid position, the p-values for each gene were calculated through a 
permutation test, which can again explain why most of them are exactly equal, due to the lower limit. 
These values arise from fitting the gene expression with the whole ordination. Thus they are not specific for 
PCo2. Future studies could investigate the correlation between gene expression and the PCo2, and such 
calculations are successfully carried out by Hansen et al. [65]. 
 
When I investigated the actual expression levels of the top genes, I found discrepancies between what I 
expected from the PCoA and the expression levels. In all cases, I observed the expected behavior of 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
expression level in one of the experiments, i.e. if a gene had a positive loading score, the expression either 
increased over time or was increased in the AS-S-cluster compared to the ABS-BS-cluster. As described in 
section Normalised counts9.2.1.1and section 9.2.1.2, the expression level trend differed between the 
strains in some cases. This could be due to the difference between the experiment that I described in 
section 11.1.1. One likely example of this is the ywmD gene which is highly expressed in the B. velezensis 
samples (mean FPKM = 19892) but weakly expressed in the B. subtilis samples (mean RPKM = 55). 
 
The TFA-based PCoA splits the B. subtilis samples in an early cluster and a late cluster. This was not as I 
expected. Pisithkul et al. found several transcription and sigma factors with time-dependent activity [34]. 
Thus, I was expecting the TFA-based PCoA to be similar to the ones based on normalised counts and 
variance-stabilised values. The unexpected ordination could arise from the non-linear behaviour of the TFA, 
which I observed for PerR and NdrR (Figure 8). 
 
My method for estimating TFA differed from the one used by Pisithkul et al. as they used a tool called the 
Inferolator [34] and I used my own implementation of the TFA estimation done in the Inferolator (section 
8.2.2.3). Though still surprising, there was no overlap between the transcription factors which Pisithkul 
highlights [34] and the transcription factors with largest loading score on PCo2 (Table 3). This suggests that 
the top 10 regulators that I find should be treated with caution. A recent study reviewed four different 
published methods to estimate TFA from transcriptome data. The four methods performed very 
inconsistently, i.e. there was little overlap between the top 100 changed TFAs between the four methods 
[66]. Thus, estimation of TFA seems to be a difficult task which has not yet been solved in a satisfactory 
way. 
 
11.1.2.2 Heteroscedasticity 
The heteroscedasticity that I observe in the combined RPKM/FPKM data set is common for RNA-seq data. 
When doing a PCoA of this data, the genes with large normalised counts has a large influence on the 
ordination of the samples. This problem is regularly solved by using a log2(counts+1) transformation. This 
transformation is known to shift the emphasis to the low expressed genes, which suffer from exaggerated 
LFC [23]. Consequently, the PCoA variance-stabilised values will be biased and have an increased emphasis 
on non-biologically relevant changes. Nevertheless, the loading score analysis revealed biologically 
reasonable top 10 genes in both PCoA based on normalised counts and variance-stabilised values. This 
indicates that both were useful to separate the samples in a growth phase-dependent manner. However, I 
would consider the PCoA of the variance-stabilised values as the best as this yields a superior time-
dependent separation of the B. subtilis samples (Figure 5B). 
   
11.1.2.3 Inference of growth state 
In the present study, I inferred the growth state of the B. velezensis samples by aligning them to the B. 
subtilis samples. This was done purely based on a visual judgement of the samples’ relative positions along 
PCo2. This judgement comes with great uncertainty and could be done in more quantitative manners 
leading to a more exact solution. The purpose of this study was only to get a rough estimate. Instead of 
developing more sophisticated bioinformatic methods, it would be far more appropriate to conduct growth 
experiments in the laboratory to reveal the growth curves of members of the microcosms. From these 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
growth curves the growth phase of each bacterium at 24h could be inferred. Such an experiment could be 
set up using qPCR similar to the one described in section 8.1.1. This was, unfortunately not possible during 
the time span of this project.  
 
I identified two clusters in the B. velezensis samples, of which the centroids were close in PCoAs of all three 
data types. I concluded that both clusters aligned with B. subtilis samples in the transition phase, thus 
approaching their carrying capacity. The order of these two clusters was also very consistent, i.e. the AS-S 
cluster aligns with the later B.subtilis samples more than the ABS-BS cluster. From this, I concluded that B. 
velezensis in AS and S consortium were closer to reaching their carrying capacity. Two observations lead to 
this conclusion: i) the alignment of the centroids, and ii) the loadings of the TFA PCoA. Both are associated 
with large uncertainty.  
 
The intersample variance differed between the microcosms. The ABS and BS samples showed the largest 
variance in all PCoA (Figure 5). Similarly, these two microcosms also had the largest variance in strain 
abundance (Figure 3B). The large variance along PCo2 in the PCoA of the variance-stabilised values suggests 
that not all the samples were in the same growth state. 
 
11.2 Hypothesis based on growth phase estimate 
Based on the growth phase estimate in section 10, I proposed a range of possible growth curves that would 
yield the observed cell number and growth phase after 24 hours. I assumed that the number of B. 
velezensis in the pellicle follows logistic growth. The growth curve of the B. subtilis samples suggests that 
the assumption of logistic growth is reasonable (Figure 5D). 
  
Both the strains abundance results (Figure 3) and the RNA-seq are specifically obtained from the pellicle. 
Thus, the growth phase estimate and proposed growth curve are of the pellicle cells and do not consider 
the cells in the liquid medium below. Lee et al. (2019) investigated the growth of B. subtilis underneath the 
pellicle and showed that these cells grow synchronized with the pellicle development [67]. Thus, it is likely 
that the estimated growth phase and growth curve also account for the planktonic growing cells. These 
cells could participate differently in the interactions of the microcosm than the pellicle forming relatives, 
but further experiments would be required to elucidate this. 
 
11.3 GAGE 
11.3.1 Differentially expressed genes 
I inferred DEGs using DESeq2. This algorithm assumes that the DEGs are balanced, i.e. has an equal amount 
of up and downregulated genes [24]. The results comply with this assumption (Table 4). The second 
assumption is that both non-DEGs and DEGs are equally affected by technical bias. It cannot be proved that 
this assumption is not violated, but the generally good performance of DESeq2 across several reviews hints 
that RNA-seq data often complies with this assumption [24].

11.3.2 Gene set analysis 
Through manual curation of pathways that were differentially between ABS and S, I found a poor 
correlation between the name of the pathway and the enzymes that were differentially expressed. For 
example, the Methane metabolism (KEGG ID ko00680) is downregulated, but the curation revealed that the 
methane metabolism map also contains parts of ribulose-P metabolism, Xylulose-P metabolism and serine 
metabolism. DEGs were found in these other pathways, while the enzymes around methane are not found 
in the annotations of the genomes (Appendix 1.14). Furthermore, the GAGE analysis found the pathway 
Carbon fixation in prokaryote (KEGG ID ko00720) significantly downregulated in the BS culture (Appendix 
1.7). I believe that these two issues occur due to the quality of the gene sets. The gene sets were created 
purely based on the eggNOG annotation, thus if eggNOG predicts a gene to participate in a given pathway, 
this pathway is included as a gene set. I applied a filter to remove pathways with less than 5 or more than 
200 genes, but this filter proved insufficient for filtering out the non-meaningful pathways. The gene sets 
should be further curated either through manual or algorithmic means to improve the analysis. 
 
KEGG builds its pathways based on K-numbers, which describe a given biological function, e.g. a specific 
catalysis of a metabolite. Several genes within an organism can be predicted to confer the same function 
and given the same K-number. Thus, when creating the gene sets based on the KEGG annotation, i.e. the K-
number, some gene sets contained several genes conferring the same reaction and the same E.C. number. I 
observed that these genes were not always regulated in the same direction nor magnitude. I did not 
manage to remove this bias. This affects the GAGE as it will affect the mean LFC and the pathway 
visualisation as only one of the LFCs can be shown on the map. I discovered this after I curated the 
pathways. Thus the pathway curation might yield different results if this bias is taken into account.  
 
11.3.3 Cross-feeding of branched-chain amino acids  
The analysis of the differentially expressed genes and pathways revealed that B. velezensis consistently 
downregulated the biosynthesis of BCAA in the presence of B. contaminans (Appendix 1.7). Only some of 
the biosynthetic enzymes required for the BCAA production was upregulated in B. contaminans, but a 
substantial upregulation of the first and last biosynthetic enzymes may still increase production rate. 
 
In the meantime, degradation of BCAA was significantly reduced in B. contaminans, suggesting that the 
BCAA would accumulate if they are neither secreted nor incorporated into proteins at a higher rate. Gierok 
et al. (2016) found that certain morphotypes of Burkholderia pseudomallei secrete BCAA intermediates (3-
methyl-2-oxopentanoate, 2-oxoisovalerate and 4-methyl-2-oxopentanoate) to the media during the 
exponential and transition phase. [68]. These metabolites constitute the last intermediate before the final 
amino acids in the biosynthesis and the first intermediate in the degradation pathway (Figure 11 and Figure 
12)6. Thus, I hypothesise that B. contaminans produce BCAA or BCAA intermediates as a public good, which 
supports the growth and increase the carrying capacity of B. velezensis.

For the cross-feeding of BCAA to have effect, the bacteria need to express membrane proteins that can 
transport the BCAAs or BCAA intermediates out of the B. contaminans and into the B. velezensis. Currently, 
6 permeases are proposed to transport BCAA through the membrane of B. subtilis [36], [69]. Thus, similar 
protein are likely to be found in B. velezensis, even though only one was found in the annotation by 
EggNOG. The downregulation of the proposed BCAA transport protein does not support the cross-feeding 
hypothesis, but I speculate that B. velezensis contains other transport proteins or that transcriptional 
downregulation has happened recently before sampling, and thus it does not reflect the protein level. If B. 
contaminans produces BCAA intermediates, other type of transporters might be required. 
 
Cross-feeding of metabolites is a well known synergistic mechanism in microbial communities. Mee et. al 
(2014) suggest that amino acid production is optimized to support synergistic growth. Especially, cross-
feeding of costly amino acids, such as isoleucine, are found to promote positive interaction [70]. BCAAs 
constitute a group of specifically important amino acids. They are the most abundant amino acids in 
proteins and they can be metabolised into branched chain fatty acids that constitute around 90% of the 
fatty acids in the membrane of B. subtilis when grown at 37 oC. These amino acids also play a key role in 
systems that monitor the physiological state of B. subtilis through induction of the transcription factor CodY 
[71]. Furthermore, cross-feeding of BCAA was suggested as a synergistic mechanism in a four member 
microcosms isolated from soil [65]. Thus, it is likely that the cross-feeding of BCAAs play a role in the 
increase of the carrying capacity in the ABS microcosm. This role could either be direct, e.g. by relieving B. 
velezensis from a metabolic burden, or indirect, e.g. by induction of a more desirable metabolic state 
through CodY and other TFs.  
 
The manual curation of the GAGE analysis results revealed that both tryptophan and histidine biosynthesis 
was decreased along with BCAA. Histidine biosynthesis is, like the BCAA biosynthesis, repressed by CodY, 
although CodY cannot full explain the repression [54]. Tryptophan is known to be important for the 
bacteria-plant interaction in B. velezensis SQR9 [13]. Thus, the role of these amino acids in the ABS 
microcosm could be subject to further investigation.  
 
11.3.4 Cross-feeding of siderophores 
Due to time constrains I did not curate the differentially expressed pathways between B. velezensis in ABS 
and BS as thoroughly (Figure 13) as for ABS compared to S (Figure 10). However, the analysis still yielded 
promising results. Especially the biosynthesis of siderophore caught my attention as iron scavenging was 
recently shown to be important for biofilm formation in B. velezensis [72].  
 
Unexpectedly, the GAGE analysis found that the expression of the siderophore biosynthesis pathway in B. 
velezensis was significantly increased between the ABS microcosm and the dicultures, but not between ABS 
and the monoculture S (Appendix 1.7 and Figure 15). This was in spite of the upregulation of all relevant 
genes in the pathway (Figure 14). Such a “false negative” could be due to the large number DEGs between 
the ABS and S. The siderophore biosynthesis gene set for B. velezensis only contain 6 genes7 and with many

The pathway map of siderophore biosynthesis showed that the biosynthesis pathway of four siderophores 
were present in B. velezensis (vibriobactin, enterobactin, mychelin and bacillibactin) (Figure 14). These four 
siderophores are all based on the same precursor, 2,3-Dihydroxy-benzoate (DHB), which is connected 
through amino acids, e.g. threonine for bacillibactin (Figure 14). Thus, their biosynthesis only differs in the 
last enzymatic step, e.g. performed by DhbF in the case of bacillibactin. The prior steps have identical KEGG 
IDs, e.g. DhbE, VibE, EntE and MxcE all share the KEGG ID K02363. I used R-package Pathview to create the 
pathway plots. Pathview [44] uses these KEGG IDs to visualize the pathway and therefore all proteins 
appear to be present. It is important to notice that the final gene of bacillibactin biosynthesis dhbF is found 
in B. velezensis, while neither entF, mxcG, or vibF were found. This aligns with experimental knowledge that 
B. velezensis is known to produce bacillibactin [72]. Furthermore, A. baumannii also produce a DHB-based 
siderophore acinetobactin [73]. The DHB preprocessing enzymes were significantly upregulated in A. 
baumannii (Figure 14). Thus it is likely to produce acinetobactin or some other DHB-based siderophore in 
the ABS microcosm. 
 
Two siderophores were proposed to be produced in B. contaminans: pyochelin and yersiniabactin. 
Pyochelin is a known siderophore from Burkholderia species [74], while I found no indication yersiniabactin 
production in the literature. Thus, I propose that the upregulation of yersiniabactin occurs from the fact 
that it shares the same precursor molecule, salicylate, and therefore the synthesis of pyochelin shares 
enzymes.  
 
The proposed acinetobactin synthesis indicate that the three bacteria can produce nonribosomal peptide 
based siderophores which are not part of the gene set produced from the KEGG annotation. Furthermore, 
B. contaminans is known to produce ornibactin [75], which biosynthesis genes are not found in the KEGG 
gene set. In fact, a study of Burkholderia cenocepcia found that ornibactin and pyochelin biosynthesis genes 
where differentially regulated under restricted iron conditions [74]. Such phenomena could question if B. 
contaminans is dependent on siderophore production from the microcosm members. Thus, further work 
should elucidate the regulation of other siderophore biosynthesis pathways to support or reject the 
siderophore cross-feeding hypothesis. 
 
The visualisations of KEGG pathways, done with the R-package Pathview, turned out to be error-prone and 
can easily be misinterpreted. Furthermore, the discussion above demonstrate that the KEGG pathway 
system fails to comprehend the full diversity and complexity of microbial metabolism. Thus, one should be 
careful when using gene sets based on KEGG data.

50 
 
Siderophores are getting much attention in the area of microbial ecology, and especially iron depletion 
seems to trigger microbial responses from bacteria. The methods of scavenging iron through siderophores 
are prone to cheaters that utilise siderophores produced by other organisms. In fact, many bacteria contain 
uptake systems for siderophores that they cannot synthesis themselves and some bacteria have even 
evolved to be dependent on siderophore produced by others [76]. Thus, it seems likely that B. contaminans 
is able to utilise the siderophores produced by B. velezensis and therefore downregulates biosynthesis of its 
own siderophores (Figure 14).   
 
My results show that B. velezensis in the BS microcosm only slightly upregulates the siderophore 
biosynthesis compared to the monoculture (Figure 15). In this microcosm both strains reached a higher cell 
number than the individual monocultures, but still much smaller than in the ABS microcosm (Appendix 1.3). 
This suggests that the siderophore production of B. velezensis is not able to support both bacteria, thus 
disabling the growth of both strains. The competition for iron can have inhibitory effects on the microbial 
growth and cause developmental arrest in biofilms [76]. Lee et al. (2020) recently presented a co-culture 
where Streptomyces coelicolor produced antibiotics as a response to iron depletion from Myxococcus 
xanthus siderophore production. Thus, I suggest that B. contaminans experience iron limitation in the BS 
culture, which induce competitive behaviour that inhibits B. velezensis.  
 
11.4 Interaction model 
My analysis indicated two microbial interactions in the ABS microcosm: B. contaminans produce BCAA and 
B. velezensis produce siderophores and both are available as a public good. In the BS microcosm, B. 
contaminans supplies BCAAs, but B. velezensis fails to support both strains with iron. This limits the growth 
of B. contaminans, thereby limiting the supply of BCAA to B. velezensis. The growth of both strains could be 
further inhibited by competitive interactions induced by the iron starvation of B. contaminans.  
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
 
A. baumannii is somehow able to induce the siderophore production of B. velezensis either directly or 
indirectly. The enhanced siderophore production is able to support both B. velezensis and B. contaminans 
and thereby disable the proposed competitive behavior of B. contaminans. This initiates a positive feedback 
loop where the two strains support the growth of each another, leading to a large increase in carrying 
capacity (Figure 16). 
 
This model is not complete. In the BS microcosm, the lack of upregulation could be a function of 
interactions with B. velezensis or that B. contaminans somehow commits to siderophore dependent 
phenotype. In monoculture B there are no such microbial interactions and the media clearly contained 
enough iron to support a much larger number of cells (Figure 3 and Appendix 1.3). Therefore, it is strange 
that the carrying capacity of B. contaminans in the monoculture should be limited by iron. Thus, the limiting 
factors of monocultures could be subject to further investigation.

12 Conclusion 
Through analysis of RNA-seq data obtained from different microcosms, I proposed a model for the 
microbial interactions in the biofilm pellicle. By comparing gene expression patterns from B. subtilis time-
series experiment with the B. velezensis in the different microcosms, I estimated that B. velezensis was 
transitioning between exponential and stationary growth in all four microcosms (S, AS, BS and ABS) at the 
24 hours point. From this estimate, I proposed possible growth curves that could lead to the observed 
abundance of B. velezensis in ABS microcosm. The hypothesised growth curves revealed that the carrying 
capacity of B. velezensis was increased in the ABS microcosm. Further analysis of the gene expression data 
yielded two possible synergistic interactions which could increase the carrying capacity of B. velezensis. My 
analysis indicates that B. contaminans and B. velezensis produce BCAAs and siderophores, respectively, as a 
public good which positively affect both strains. Furthermore, the results hint that A. baumannii modulates 
the siderophore production of B. velezensis. Finally, I speculate that the modulation from A. baumannii 
initiates a positive feedback loop between B. velezensis and B. contaminans and that this leads to a 
substantial increase in carrying capacity of B. velezensis in the ABS microcosm. 
 
13 Perspective  
Most of the hypotheses that I set up in this study are based on the growth phase estimate and on the 
assumption that B. velezensis in all microcosms approaches its carrying capacity. Thus, it would be valuable 
to validate this estimate through a growth experiment. Obtaining growth curves of all three strains in all 
combinations could also provide valuable information about the growth dynamic. This would reveal more 
information about how the larger cell number is generated, e.g. explain why and how the growth rate of B. 
velezensis is increased in the ABS microcosm. Furthermore, it would be obvious to conduct 
supplementation studies with BCAA and siderophores to test the hypothesised interactions. 
 
If the resources were available, I would conduct a new set of RNA-seq experiments to measure the gene 
expression at several time points during biofilm development. The findings from the RNA-seq time-series 
could be used to decide the most relevant next experiments. These could be metabolomics or proteomics 
experiments to narrow down the findings from the RNA-seq analysis. Such an extensive study of this 
microcosm would couple growth dynamics with gene expression data, and it would have the potential to 
uncover the dynamics of interspecies interactions that allow microbes to synergistically build a biofilm 
together.
