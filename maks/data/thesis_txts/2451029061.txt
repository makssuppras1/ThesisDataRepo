Abstract
This work is motivated by the increasing importance of renewable energy in the con-
text of climate change. The concerns around the social acceptance of these energies
have increased, and decisive variables such as land use competition and visual impact
should be considered in the context of renewable energy planning. An innovative way
of taking into account the visual impact is the use of an online crowd-sourced survey
in which people can rate photographs of the landscapes in Great Britain. Therefore,
the added value of this work is the consideration of these values as well as the land
use competition in the assessment of potential and cost of solar PV and onshore wind.
Solar PV has been separated into two different installations: ground-mounted PV
and roof-mounted PV. Indeed the available areas for these two technologies have to
be calculated differently. For rooftop PV, a top-down method that scaled up the
results obtained from a high resolution bottom-up method applied to four cities is
used. The technical potential of rooftop PV is 131 TWh for an average LCOE of
171 €/MWh. Compared to the electricity consumption of 338 TWh for the United
Kingdom, this potential is limited but this technology has the advantage to not be
impacted by either land use competition or scenicness.
Then, the correlation between the scenicness values and the planning outcome of
ground-mounted PV and onshore wind is investigated using logistic regression. It
comes out from this study that the scenicness values are not correlated with the out-
come of a ground-mounted planning application, but they impact negatively the one
of onshore wind. These conclusions indicate that ground-mounted PV potential is
not impacted by scenicness.
Finally, the technical potential of onshore wind is estimated at 817 TWh and can
be reduced up to 78% for a high restriction on scenicness. The technical potential of
ground-mounted PV is significantly higher at 6,474 TWh, but it can be reduced up to
85% by a high restriction on the land use for agriculture or bioenergy. Furthermore, if
the impact of the land use competition does on the cost of production is limited, the
costs can be increased by 29 % for a high restriction on the scenicness. However, the
onshore wind is in most of the cases the cheapest renewable energy with an average
LCOE of 79 €/MWh against 117 €/MWh for ground-mounted PV, whereas rooftop
PV is the most expensive.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Climate change is one of the biggest global challenges of the current human society.
The Paris Agreement [80] was a first step in the attempt to mitigate the consequences
of climate change. Indeed, it has set different targets in the objective of limiting the
temperature of the earth below 2◦C. This objective is only achievable with a drastic
reduction of the greenhouse gas emissions, and following the Paris Agreement, many
countries have defined objectives in term of this reduction. This is the case of the
United Kingdom whose target for 2050 is to reduce the emissions by at least 80%
compared to 1990 levels according to the UK Climate Change Act 2008 [43].
Currently, global energy production is mainly based on fossil fuels. Therefore, the
decarbonisation of global energy systems is necessary for the reduction of greenhouse
gas emissions. Different scenarios for this decarbonisation are investigated through
energy system modeling. It appears that the power sector will lead this decarbonisa-
tion (Fais et al. (2016) [5]) thanks to renewable energy, which play a crucial role as
they produce energy without any production of greenhouse gases. Thus, the estima-
tion of their potential as an input of the energy system models is a key step.
The development of renewable energy has increased significantly the past few
years, but it still needs to increase a lot more in order to meet the objectives of each
country and to lead to the decarbonisation of energy systems. In the United King-
dom, a directive [13] imposes a target of 15% of all energy consumption produced by
renewable energy in 2020 and it was up to 10.2% in 2017 [23].
However, the development of renewable energy is limited by different factors such
as social acceptance or the land use competition with the agricultural sector and
bioenergy. Social acceptance of renewable energy raises a lot of concern and discussion,
especially around the NIMBY concept (Wüstenhagen, Wolsink, and Bürer (2007)
[67]). Different factors can influence social acceptance but it appears that the visual
impact of renewable energy on natural landscapes is one that undermined it the most
[67].
Therefore, the technical potential of renewable energy can be significantly reduced
by these parameters, which should be considered to determine more accurate feasible
potentials. An innovative way of quantifying the visual impact is the use of scenicness
values that result from an online survey in which people can rate pictures of actual

1.1
Objectives
In this context, the aim of this work is to assess the technical and feasible potential as
well as the cost of onshore wind and solar PV in Great Britain by taking into account
the scenicness values and the land use competition with bioenergy and agricultural
land.
In a more detailed way, this work will answer the following questions:
• How is scenicness correlated with acceptance or rejection of wind or solar park
planning processes?
• How does the scenicness value of landscapes influence the wind and ground-
mounted PV potential?
• How does a restriction on the scenicness value affect the system costs to achieve
renewable targets?
• What are the implications of the land-use competition between wind/solar and
other uses such as agriculture and bioenergy?
The correlation between the scenicness and the planning outcome of a wind or
solar project is assessed through a statistical study.
The technical potential and
the costs of wind and solar are then calculated, and the possibility to modify them
regarding some criterion is implemented. In this way, the influence of the scenicness
and land use competition can be investigated.
1.2
Thesis structure
This thesis is divided into several chapters. The subject is broadly introduced in the
current chapter. Then, the different subjects raise in this work and what has already
been carried out about them are detailed in a literature review in Chapter 2. A first
study regarding the influence of the scenicness on the planning outcome of renewable
energy projects is presented in Chapter 3. The results of this part are used in Chapter
4 in which the methodology applied to calculate the technical potential and the cost
is introduced. Then, the main results are analysed in Chapter 5. Finally, a sensitivity
analysis, the limitations and the possible improvements of this work are presented in
Chapter 6.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
This chapter reviews the relevant literature in the context of this project. This goes
from literature about the transition towards decarbonisation of the energy systems,
to onshore wind and solar potential calculation and the influence of scenicness on
social acceptance.
2.1
Decarbonisation of the energy system
A transition towards decarbonisation of energy systems is key to reduce the green-
house gas emissions and thus to fulfil the Paris Agreement [80], which means limiting
the global mean surface temperature to rise above 2°C. Consequently, energy systems
across the world that are nowadays mainly based on fossil fuels require large-scale
changes. Therefore national energy system models as National Energy Modeling Sys-
tem [1] and TIMES (Daly et al. (2015) [30]) are used to investigate different possible
scenarios that could lead to an optimum shift towards sustainability of energy systems.
It appears that the power sector is mainly leading this transformation (Fais et
al. (2016) [5]), thanks to the development of renewable energy. However, renewable
energies are characterised by high temporal and spatial intermittency. That is why
power system modelling is so important in order to build a stable power system with
a consequent amount of renewable energy.
Thus an increasing number of studies consider renewable energy as a significant
part of the future global energy systems. ([63], [71], [7], [49], [19] and [45]). If Pur-
siheimo, Holttinen, and Koljonen (2019) [63] and Teske et al. (2018) [71] investigate
scenarios with a high share of renewable, it appears that even 100% renewable en-
ergy systems are economically feasible on a global scale as described by Heard et al.
(2017) [7]. Jacobson et al. (2017) [49] show the possibility of fulfilling global energy
demand with only renewable energy through a roadmap for 139 countries to use 100%
wind-water-solar in all energy sectors.
Child et al.
(201) [45] confirm that an economically viable transition towards
100% Renewable energy for the electricity sector of Europe by 2050 is possible and
describes in particular how the combination of flexible generation, grid interconnec-
tions, and energy storage could support this transition. Bogdanov et al. (2019) [19]
==PAGE:16==

also demonstrate that this transition is technically and economically feasible by 2050,
even though the study uses higher temporal resolution than other studies in order to
reduce uncertainties.
All these energy system models require inputs that are usually renewable energy
potential estimation. However, the development of renewable energy can face social
barriers and land-use competition that are not taken into account in all the previously
mentioned studies. Only Bogdanov et al. (2019) [19] raise some concerns about social
acceptance as it is an uncertain aspect that cannot be estimated by technico-economic
analysis.
In that sense, Price et al. (2018) [33] propose a scenario analysis of low carbon
electricity system for Great Britain in 2050 using a high spatial and high temporal
resolution model of Great Britain’s electricity system called highRES (Zeyringer et al.
(2018) [51]). The study differs from the others as it describes the scenario analysis
from an energy-land-water perspective. Indeed, through a nexus tool, restrictions on
land availability for the siting of renewable energy and on the water for the cooling
of power plants are considered. Through this focus on land use, the study takes into
account the fact that renewable energy projects can face opposition because of dif-
ferent parameters. Even though this approach accounts for some social barriers, it
is still far from reality. Indeed, the way the real world can impact the transition to
decarbonisation is really complicated and uncertain.
Hence, this work will complete this last study [33] by providing more detailed
inputs to the highRES model. The inputs needed for this model are the renewable
energy potentials in the form of cost-potential curves.
Thus, the wind and solar
potential will be assessed and the impacts that the land use competition and social
acceptance have will be taken into account in order to account for the real world
influence.

2.2 Renewable energy potential

2.2
Renewable energy potential
As explained previously, this work will focus on the inputs needed for the energy
system models, which are cost-potential curves for renewable energy. In this case,
wind and solar energies are the focus and the literature related to the estimation of
the potential of these technologies is reviewed in this part.
Regarding solar energy, two different types of energy can be distinguished as the
method to calculate their potential is different: rooftop-mounted PV and ground-
mounted PV.
2.2.1
Rooftop-mounted PV
Different methods to calculate the technical potential for rooftop PV are available.
The geographic area covered and the spatial resolution of these methods can differ
significantly.
Methods based on statistical data are usually used for large-scale assessment ([39],
[34], [62]). Schallenberg-Rodríguez (2013) [34] reviews a wide range of methodologies
to calculate the PV potential and applies them to the Canary Islands. Defaix et al.
(2012) [62] estimate the PV potential in the EU-27 also using statistical data. In
order to increase the spatial resolution, Mainzer et al. (2014) [39] propose a top-
down method to determine the technical potential for residential-roof-mounted PV
on a municipality level in Germany using statistical data combined with geographical
information systems.
However, these methodologies based on statistical data are restricted by data avail-
ability, and thus, the spatial and temporal resolutions of these methods are limited.
To address this issue, methods based on 3D model are used. For example, Roddríguez
et al. (2017) [44] uses a 3D model to calculate the technical and economic potential of
rooftop PV at an urban scale with a high resolution. 3D models can be created with
different methods, and the use of Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) is one of
them. Thus, in the same way than with 3D models, some studies use LiDAR to assess
the PV potential with a high resolution. For instance, Boz, Calvert, and Brownson
(2015) [38], and Phillips et al. (2016) [9] combine GIS and LiDAR data to quantify
the amount of suitable rooftop area for rooftop PV in the United States. Phillips
et al. (2016) [9] goes further in the analysis by developing a methodology to scale
up the results obtain with LiDAR data to areas where LiDAR data are not available.
Finally, Jacques et al. (2014) [20] also uses LiDAR data to assess roof-mounted PV ca-
pacity in the city of Leeds, which is part of the geographical area studied in this work.
However, 3D model and LiDAR data are not always available and represent sig-
nificant investment both in term of money and time.
That is why a bottom-up
methodology that uses image recognition technique is presented by Mainzer et al.

(2017) [40]. Indeed, by using image recognition on aerial images and publicly avail-
able geographical building data Mainzer et al. (2017) [40] describe a high spatial
resolution method when 3D model data is not available.
Many methods can be applied in order to calculate the rooftop PV potential and
thus the corresponding cost-potential curves. If one study has been done at a city
scale for Great Britain (Leeds), it less common at a larger spatial scale. Thus, a
top-down method based on results obtained from the bottom-up method described
in Mainzer et al. (2017) [40] will be applied in this work in order to calculate the
rooftop PV potential for Great Britain. However, rooftop PV is one of the renewable
energy which is not really affected by land use competition or social barriers contrary
to ground-mounted PV and onshore wind energy.
2.2.2
Ground-mounted PV
The selection of suitable sites for ground-mounted PV is a key step in the estimation
of the potential of this resource. Indeed, different factors such as the topography,
the soil consumption, or the land use have to be taken into account. Some studies
describe methods in order to identify these suitable areas ([28], [60], [18]). Piyatad-
sananon (2016) [60] identify the most important spatial factors for suitable sites
selection.
Mondino, Fabrizio, and Chhiabrando (2015) [28] use GIS to develop a
decision support tool based on quantitative indicators for the site selection of large
ground-mounted PV plants and apply it to the Piedmont region. Castillo, Silva, and
Lavalle (2016) [18] use GIS as well to combine solar radiation data and geographical
factors to generate a suitability map for photovoltaic power plants across the EU at
high spatial resolution.
Indeed, the combination of suitable areas with the calculation of the global irradia-
tion will result in the estimation of the ground-mounted PV potential. This method
is applied in Carrión et al. (2008) [37] which calculate the electricity production ca-
pacity of photovoltaic power plants in Andalusia.
If the studies Piyatadsananon (2016) [28], Carrión et al. (2008) [37] and Castillo,
Silva, and Lavalle (2016) [18] take into account the land use competition with agri-
cultural land, only Carrión et al.
(2008) [37] include some social barriers in the
potential estimation. Indeed, to take into account that ground-mounted PV plants
have relevant impacts on neighboring residential areas, such as pollutants emission
and landscape impact, a minimum buffer area around urban areas is applied.
The ground-mounted PV potential and the associated costs are also assess in
detail in two different thesis ([47] and [4]). Hoogwijk (2004) [47] investigates ”the
contribution of renewable sources to the present and future world and regional en-
ergy supply system.” This investigation is carried out by calculating cost-potential
curves for different types of energy on a global scale for wind, solar PV, and biomass

Onshore wind energy

2.3
Social acceptance
As it has been seen in the previous part, it seems crucial to integrate the impact of
social acceptance in the calculation of renewable energy potential.
More and more literature deal with the social acceptance of renewable energy
project and its impact on the outcome of these projects. As explained in Wüstenhagen,
Wolsink, and Bürer (2007) [67], social acceptance can be divided into three categories:
• Socio-political acceptance: acceptance by policymakers and the general public.
• Market acceptance: acceptance of new technologies by adopters such as house-
holds and businesses.
• Community acceptance: acceptance by local communities affected by the im-
plementation of technology.
Wüstenhagen, Wolsink, and Bürer (2007) [67] explain that socio-political accep-
tance is usually high.
Indeed, it has been shown through many surveys that the
general public tends to support renewable energy. However, Wüstenhagen, Wolsink,
and Bürer (2007) [67] explain as well that a problem is noticed going from global
to local as the rate of rejected renewable energy projects is high, and it cannot be
explained only by the difference in the energy resource.
Thus, some studies have investigated the influence of different parameters on the
planning outcome of renewable energy projects in the United Kingdom. Harper et
al. (2017) [46] use logistic regression to identify the key parameters for wind projects
planning outcome in the United Kingdom among geospatial, environmental, demo-
graphic, and political variables. The study highlights that the distance to urban areas
is particularly significant but with high uncertainty and that local demographic and
political parameters surprisingly appear also to be significant.
In the same idea, Roddis et al. (2018) [61] investigate the role of community accep-
tance in renewable energy project planning outcome. This statistical study includes
a considerable amount of variables and highlights that some parameters which influ-
ence onshore wind projects are different from the ones that influence solar projects.
Nevertheless, some influential factors are found to be the same for both technologies
as installed capacity, social deprivation and aesthetics variables.
Thus, many parameters can impact a renewable energy project. For solar PV,
the parameters that are highlighted the more often are the land use, the emission
of pollutants, and the visual impact ([21], [78]). However, Tsoutsos, Frantzeskaki,
and Gekas (2005) [78] explain that for a ground-mounted PV plant, the emission of
pollutant is minimal.
Regarding onshore wind energy, some other relevant parameters are the noise or the
concern about birds. Nevertheless, it has been shown and discussed in many studies

([67], [27] and [64]) that the visual impact is the most significant factor in the reject
of onshore wind energy. Indeed, it influences both the community acceptance, as the
local landscape is modified by the implementation of wind turbines, and the socio-
political acceptance as the regular view of wind turbines increase their reject by the
population (Wüstenhagen, Wolsink, and Bürer (2007) [67]).
Finally, visual impact appear to be so significant that it will be considered as rep-
resentative of the social acceptance impact on the renewable energy projects in this
work.
In Roddis et al. (2018) [61] and Harper et al. (2017) [46], the visual impact is
taken into account only through the distance to particular areas like Natural Park and
urban areas, or indicators to quantify for example the ’naturalness’ or ’remoteness’
([61]). If these parameters indeed appear as significant for renewable energy projects,
they do not really represent the way the population perceives its landscape.
Thus this work will go further by investigating the influence of visual impact on
renewable energy potential using the scenicness value of the website Scenic-Or-Not.
This website gathers the crowdsources ratings of scenicness for geotagged photographs
around Great Britain. It presents random geotagged images to the users who have
to rate them on a scale from 1 to 10. In this case, 10 stands for ’very scenic’ and
1 for ’not scenic.’ The images are sourced from Geograph and represent a 1km grid
square of Great Britain. The data available for download at this date contain over
1.5 millions ratings that have been collected for 217,00 images. This approach to
quantify scenicness is innovative as, in the past, time-consuming and costly surveys
have been the only way to gather data about scenicness and usually only on a small
scale.
The results of Scenic-Or-Not have already been used to explore the link between
scenicness and health (Seresinhe, Preis, and Moat (2015) [12]) or to investigate what
high scenicness spaces are composed of ((Seresinhe, Preis, and Moat (2017) [11]).
However, it has not yet been used in a renewable energy potential estimation.

Concluding remarks
This chapter introduced the context of this work and what has already be done in
term of renewable energy potential estimation as well as investigation about the ac-
ceptance of renewable energy.
Against this background, the present work will propose an innovative approach
in the way of assessing the potential of renewable energy. Indeed, the potential of
onshore wind and solar PV in Great Britain will be assessed under consideration of the
influence of scenicness and land use in the context of transition towards decarbonised
energy systems.

The visual impact of rooftop PV is limited as they do not impact natural land-

Logistic regression
The application decision is a binary variable, so the influence of all the variables on
the outcome of the application is investigated through logistic regression. Indeed, a
logistic regression predicts, based on one or more independent variables, the probabil-
ity that a dependent variable falls into one of two categories [41]. Thus, the statistical
relationship between the planning outcome which is the dependent variable and the
different independent variables described in the previous part can be analysed.
The assumptions of the logistic regression have to be checked for each variable. They
consist of the linearity of the logit for independent variables and the absence of mul-
ticollinearity [41]. The checks and the logistic regression are performed in R as it is
a powerful tool for statistical analysis.

The assumption that has to be checked in this case is the linearity between the logit,
which is the log(odds), of the outcome and the value of the independent variables [41].
This check can be done graphically for each independent variable by representing the
log(odds) versus the values of the variable.

that projects with many turbines but with not a too high capacity, which implies a
reduced capacity for the turbines as well is more likely to be accepted. The capacity
of a turbine is linked to its size and thus if the capacity of the turbines is reduced,
they might be less visible.

likely that an environmental impact assessment has been done before the application

This chapter describes the methodology used to estimate the potential of solar PV,
both ground-mounted and rooftop-mounted, and onshore wind. Different types of
potential can be distinguished [77]:
• Geographical potential: total area which is available for energy production con-
sidering restrictions due to land use, environmental and topographic conditions.
• Technical potential: total amount of energy that can be produced under tech-
nical constraints for a certain region and period.
• Economic potential: technical potential which is economically feasible for a
certain region and period, from an investor point of view.
• Feasible potential: the actual feasible potential that takes into account social
barriers, in this case the scenicness values, and land-use competition.
In this case, the method is used to calculate the annual technical potential of the three
technologies in Great Britain. This technical potential is then economically assessed
using the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE).
The chapter also explains how the feasible potential for onshore wind energy, which
is the only technology impacted by the scenicness, is assessed.
4.1
Ground-mounted PV potential
4.1.1
Geographical potential
The first step in the calculation of the ground-mounted PV technical potential is to
assess the available areas that can be used for solar PV electricity production. This
==PAGE:41==
4.1 Ground-mounted PV potential
29
calculation is done using a GIS software, in this case ArcGIS. Indeed, using the vec-
tor representation, the areas considered as unsuitable can be selected, gathered, and
excluded.
For technical feasibility, the areas too steep should be excluded. Various restric-
tions on the slope can be made, but it appears that the maximum slope that guar-
antees the technical feasibility of a ground-mounted PV plant is 15° ([28], [37], [18]).
Thus, all areas steeper than 15° are excluded. These areas can be gathered using the
slope calculation function of ArcGIS on the OS Terrain 50 data [59].
The implementation of ground-mounted PV installation has an impact on natural
ecosystems and biodiversity ([21], [78]). Thus all protected areas such as Ramsar
areas, Special Areas of Conservation (SAC), Special Protection Areas and the Na-
tional Parks are excluded. The National Parks data are extracted from the Office
for National Statistics [54] and the other protected areas are retrieved from the Joint
Nature Conservation Committee [14].
Ground-mounted PV can be partly feasible in urban areas but to account for the
social restrictions and the fact that ground-mounted PV need sufficient area [15], the
urban areas are excluded as well. These areas are extracted from Open Street Map
[58].
The guidance for Renewable and low carbon energy of the Department for Com-
munities
Local Government [15] explains that preference should be given to poor
agricultural land quality for the development of ground-mounted PV installation.
Thus the high agricultural lands quality are excluded.
These areas are extracted
from different websites depending on the country: for England, the data come from
the website of the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food [52], for Scotland the
source is Scotland’s soil website [72] and finally, for Wales, it is on the Welsh Gov-
ernment website [29]. Agricultural lands of England and Wales are graded one to
five with one the highest quality and five the poorest. The scale is different for the
agricultural lands of Scotland; they are graded from one to seven with one the highest
quality and seven the poorest. Using the description of each level of agricultural land
for each country, the levels are matched into a single classification (from 1 to 5) across
Great Britain.
In order to investigate the land use competition between the agriculture land that
can be used to crow crop (for food or bioenergy) and the ground-mounted PV, two
different types of restriction are considered. A low restriction in which the lands with
grade 1 and 2 are excluded and a high restriction in which lands with grade 3 are
excluded as well.
Finally, using ArcGIS, all areas described previously that have to be excluded
are selected, gathered in a common file and the function Difference Symmetric is

performed between a map of Great Britain and this file. Thus, the resulting vector
file contains all the areas that are suitable for the development of ground-mounted

intersect it with the vector files of the suitable areas produced before. Thus, this
operation gives two vector files (low and high restrictions) with all the suitable areas
and their corresponding solar irradiance value. The mean irradiance for Great Britain

4.2
Rooftop PV potential
As for ground-mounted PV, the technical potential is calculated using the GIS soft-
ware ArcGIS to combine the geographical potential and the solar irradiance data.
However, in this case, the methodology to estimate the geographical potential is dif-
ferent. Indeed, it has to be more detailed as this technology can only be installed on
roofs.
4.2.1
Geographical potential
The bottom-up method described in Mainzer et al. (2017) [40] has a high resolution.
Indeed, it uses image recognition to extract the partial roof areas available for rooftop
PV installation. A partial roof is a part of a roof that is available, which means that
all the other structures located on the roof are not taken into account, and oriented
in a certain direction. Algorithms are then used to retrieve the roof’s ridge line and
deduce the orientation of each partial roof.
However, it can not be applied to all the cities of Great Britain as it is time-consuming.
Thus, this methodology is applied to four different cities that are considered as rep-
resentative of all Great Britain and the results will be used in a top-down method.
These four cities are Birmingham, London, Leeds, and Glasgow as they are considered
to be diverse in term of building stock and represent a different part of Great Britain.
For more clarity, the different type of area used in this part are described:
• Land area: total area of specific land use category.
• Footprint area: area that the projection of a partial roof has on the floor.
• Roof area: area available on a roof.
4.2.1.1
Footprint area
The bottom-up methodology will result in the distribution of all partial roofs suitable
for rooftop PV development in each city together with their footprint area, their roof
area, their tilt, and their azimuth. Each of these partial roofs falls into a particular
land use category. Thus the results of the four cities are combined and used to define,
for different land use categories, a ratio between the land area of each category and
the total footprint area of the buildings that fall into this land-use type.
In this case, the relevant land use categories are:

maximum slope that makes a wind farm technically feasible is 20° ([77], [65], [66]). As

in ArcGIS on the OS Terrain 50 data [59].

for England, 2km for Scotland and 500 m for Wales [8]. The separation distances for
each land use are finally determined based on McKenna et al. (2014) [65] and thus,
all the areas that are within these distances are considered as unsuitable. They are
excluded by creating a buffer area around each unsuitable land use using the Buffer
function of ArcGIS. All the concerned land use and the size of each buffer zone is
detailed in Table 4.2. Due to the resolution of the CORINE data, single buildings
and small villages are not displayed. Thus, the Open Street Map [58] data are used
to complete the CORINE data for the residential areas as it has a higher resolution.

5.1
Description of the scenarios
Regarding the results described in the previous part, different scenarios are defined in
order to analyse the influence of scenicness and land use competition on the technical
potential and the cost of solar PV and onshore wind. As stated previously, rooftop
PV potential is not affected by scenicness. Moreover, it is not affected either by land
use competition with agricultural land as it can only be put on roofs.
In the scenicness study in Chapter 3, it has been concluded that the scenicness does
not influence the outcome of a ground-mounted PV project. Thus, it is assumed that
scenicness values impact only onshore wind energy. It has also been shown that this
impact on onshore wind energy is negative. Thus, it is chosen to limit the technical
potential above some scenicness values in order to account for this impact and cal-
culate the feasible potential. Three scenicness values are chosen in order to analyse
different levels of impact of this variable.
Regarding the land use competition, ground-mounted PV compete with agriculture
for food or bioenergy. Thus two different restrictions on high quality agricultural
land have been carried out: a low restriction and a high restriction. As the ground
area needed for the installation of a wind turbine is relatively small, it is assumed
that onshore wind does not prevent agricultural activity.
It is also assumed that
onshore wind and ground-mounted PV can cohabit. Indeed, this type of project are
now investigated and one has, for example, be built in Australia: the Gullen solar
farm by the Australian Renewable Energy Agency (Arena) [6]. Thus, onshore wind
is not really affected by the land use competition.

nologies, its potential is high enough to achieve the renewable energy target. However,
the corresponding LCOE is the highest at about 175 €/MWh.
The significant technical potential of ground-mounted PV implicates that the re-
newable energy target could be only achieved by ground-mounted PV. If in this case,
the LCOE will be lower than for rooftop PV, it is more expensive than onshore wind
energy. The impact of the land use competition on the cost for achieving the target
is limited as for the high restriction the LCOE only increases by 6%.
Onshore wind is the cheapest renewable energy source to achieve the renewable
energy target, and even though the acceptable potential for a scenicness of 4 is signif-
icantly reduced compared to the technical potential, it is still high enough to reach
this target. However, if the LCOE almost does not change for a restriction above a
scenicness of 6 and 8, it increases by 29% for a restriction above a scenicness value of 4.
Finally, it can be added that the energy target will be achieved by a mix of these
different technologies so the calculation of the actual LCOE will be more complicated,
but it will be influenced by the LCOE of each technology. Therefore, this analysis
gives an idea of how it will behave for different restrictions.

that this restriction impacts the LCOE. Indeed, the average value increases because
the LCOE increase faster for a high restriction.

it is a technology that is not impacted by the land use competition or the scenicness as
the other technologies, and it is nevertheless enough to achieve the renewable energy

6.2
Possible improvements
Overall, the developed methodology and the results obtained give an accurate estima-
tion of the potentials and cost of solar PV and onshore wind energy as well as a good
understanding of the impact of the scenicness and land use competition. However,
different improvements for further work can be suggested.
6.2.1
Ground-mounted PV
The assessment of the geographical potential of ground-mounted is based on the ex-
clusion of different areas. However, the technical potential of ground-mounted PV
appears as rather high. It could be adjusted with the improvement of the assessment
of the geographical potential. Indeed, more land use areas could be excluded as forest
areas or other types of areas that could create shade on the modules should also be
excluded. Buffer areas could also be applied especially around urban areas.
Moreover, this type of PV installation requires significant space on land, so it could
be considered to remove the smallest areas that offer do not offer enough space for a
relevant installation.
In term of land use competition, only two types of restriction have been applied
whereas the grades of the agricultural lands are until 5. Thus, higher restriction could
be defined by excluding as well the agricultural land of grade 4 and 5.
Furthermore, agricultural lands are used for food production but also bioenergy.
Therefore, it could be interesting in further work to assess the technical potential
of this resource and analyse the impact of the land use competition between these
two renewable energies.
Regarding technical characteristics, other types of system could be used. Indeed,
for simplification, a fixed system for ground-mounted PV has been considered. How-
ever, tracking PV system currently exists and gives the possibility to increase the
solar irradiance that arrives on the modules as they can always face the direction
from which the highest irradiance comes from.
Thus this system would increase
the technical potential but this type of system is also more expensive because of its
complexity. This implicates higher LCOE and thus different cost potential curves.
6.2.2
Rooftop PV
Regarding the assessment of rooftop PV, as it has already been explained, the method-
ology developed is not the most precise and present uncertainties. However, for a large
==PAGE:82==
70
6 Discussion
scale assessment, it is a decent start as it gives results in line with some studies carried
out at a city scale.
In order to improve it, different steps could be added. First of all, increasing the num-
ber of cities on which the bottom-up results are based. It will increase the building
stock represented by the calculated ratio and give more accurate results. However,
this will be significantly time-consuming.
Moreover, in this case, only the land use areas that are the most relevant in cities
have been taken into account. More adequate land use could also be integrated into
the calculation. However, considering the small impact that the Continuous urban
fabric have on the technical potential, it is probable that less important land use will
have even a smaller impact.
Another improvement to this methodology would be to base the top down method
on national statistics instead of land use as used in Mainzer et al. (2014) [39]. In-
deed, this would give a more precise assessment of all the suitable buildings and the
available roof area. However, it has been tried to develop a methodology of this kind
but by lack of time and data availability, the method based on land use has appeared
as more feasible. But there is no doubt that this type of method would give a more
precise assessment.
Furthermore, another consideration could be integrated into the rooftop PV as-
sessment. Only, roof-mounted PV system has been considered but it is now possible
to integrate PV system to the facades of the building which will increase significantly
the available area for PV installation. This type of facade building area estimation is
made in Held (2010) [4].
6.2.3
Onshore wind
Regarding the geographical potential assessment, the buffer areas that have been
taken into account have been defined based on other studies, but they do not es-
pecially reflect reality. Therefore, for further work, it would be interesting to try
different distances. However, this could be really time-consuming as many data have
to be processed, and many steps have to be redone in the GIS software. The automa-
tisation of this process in the GIS software would be a solution.
Moreover, some uncertainties are created due to small areas. Indeed, after the
exclusions, small and disgregated areas are created. Two different problems can then
appear. The areas too small for the installation of a wind turbine are removed whereas
it might be possible to combine them with bigger nearby areas. Moreover, some areas
are considered as suitable since they are large enough for one turbine, but they are
not close enough to be combined with other small areas. In order to improve the
methodology, an algorithm that places the turbines one by one could be developed
and implemented.

In the same idea, knowing the exact placement of turbines gives the possibility to
develop a model for the calculation of the wake effect in order to have a more precise
estimation of the possible losses. Indeed, these losses are intrinsically linked to the
positioning of the turbines to one another.
6.2.4
Further consideration
Regarding wind energy, only the onshore potential has been estimated. Indeed, it
is the one that can be impacted the more by the scenicness value. However, if the
visual impact of the offshore wind farm is limited, it can still have an impact on the
view from the shore. Therefore, using the scenicness values taken around the shore,
a similar assessment of the technical potential and the cost of offshore wind energy
would be an interesting result.
The integration into the grid has not been considered in this work.
However,
it is an essential point for the development of renewable energy. The distance of a
potential point of connection could be taken into account for the assessment of the
geographical potential as well as the cost for the grid connection in the calculation of
the LCOE.
6.3
Limitations
One of the main limitations which brings considerable uncertainty in the obtained
results is the one related to the scenicness data. Indeed, the scenicness data are based
on real votes from the population. The extracted data only present the scenicness
values that result from at least three votes, but it is still a tiny amount of votes.
Therefore for only a few votes, the scenicness value used is not necessarily represen-
tative of the actual opinion. In the data used for the calculation of the acceptable
potential of onshore wind, 75% contain more than five votes and only 8% more than
ten votes as represented in Figure 3.
Moreover, the variance of each scenicness data also creates uncertainty.
Indeed,
among all votes for one photograph, some can be completely opposed. The variance
accounts for this and thus a high variance means that the opinions vary significantly.
The maximum variance is the dataset used is 16.8. 87% of the data have a variance
lower than 5 and 60% have one lower than 3, this is represented as well in Figure 3.
The question of whether or not the scenicness values with high uncertainty should be
considered is relevant, but considering the number of votes as a criterion will remove
too many data. Nevertheless, as the dataset of the scenicness value is from 2017,
the accuracy of the results could be increased using a more recent dataset containing
more votes that is unfortunately not available yet.

Furthermore, the orientation of the photographs is not provided. Therefore there
is no way of knowing if the scenicness value considered actually represent the land-
scape in which the renewable energy installation is planned to be installed or a com-
pletely other direction. This creates as well some uncertainty but thanks to the high
resolution of the scenicness values (1 km grid square); they can still be considered as
a proper representation of the scenicness.
Finally, one consequence of the fact that the assessment has been done at a na-

in further work that would take some of the suggested improvements into account.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Different methodologies have been developed and applied in order to analyse the re-
lationship between the scenicness value, the land use competition and the potential
and cost of solar PV and onshore wind energy in Great Britain.
First of all, the correlation of the scenicness with acceptance or rejection of wind
and solar planning process has been studied using logistic regression. Indeed, this
process has been identified as the proper way to analyse the relationship between a
binary dependent variable and independent variables.
The outcome of this analysis has lead to the conclusions that onshore wind planning
outcome is influenced by many variables and especially by the scenicness value. In-
deed, it negatively impacts the outcome of a planning application as the higher the
scenicness the lower the odds of acceptance.
Regarding solar energy application, the conclusion is much different.
Indeed, the
outcome appears not to be impacted by the scenicness. This has been concluded due
to the lack of a statistically significant correlation between the two variables.
This statistical study has been done only for ground-mounted PV and onshore
wind energy as it has been considered that rooftop PV is not influenced by land use
competition or scenicness. Therefore, its potential, that has been assessed using a
top-down method based on bottom-up results for a few cities, is not reduced and is
131 TWh. This potential is not really high but it is in line with other studies that
have been done at a city scale and is high enough to achieve the renewable energy
target.
Based on the results of the outcome of a planning application, it has been con-
sidered that the ground-mounted PV potential is not influenced by the scenicness
value. On the contrary, onshore wind is impacted and therefore its technical poten-
tial, which is 817 TWh, is restricted for different levels of scenicness to estimate the
feasible potential. The impact for a high level of scenicness is only about 4% of reduc-
tion, but it increases quite fast and for a medium scenicness value of 4, the reduction
of potential is about 78%.
Through this work, it has finally be considered that onshore wind does not actu-
ally compete with solar energy, agriculture or bioenergy due to the small land area
==PAGE:86==
74
7 Conclusion
that one turbine requires. For solar energy and in this case ground-mounted PV the
land use competition with agriculture and bioenergy is really a key element in the
assessment of the feasible potential. This impact has been analysed based on the
restriction of high-quality agricultural land. The technical potential of 6,474 TWh
is reduced by 85% for the high restriction which highlights the significant impact of
land use competition for this technology.
Finally, the influence of the scenicness and the land use has not only been studied
for the technical potential but also for the costs. Indeed, it has been shown that the
land use competition and the scenicness can impact the cost required to achieve the
renewable energy target. The effect of the land use competition is only an increase of
6% for the LCOE of the ground-mounted PV. The scenicness has a much more impor-
tant impact as the LCOE increase by 29% for a restriction over a scenicness value of 4.
The conclusions that come out of this work have put some light on the significant
impact that land use competition and scenicness have on the technical potential of
solar PV and onshore wind. However, some weaknesses can be highlighted. The main
one is the uncertainty of the scenicness values. An increase in the number of online
votes would improve the results and this work provide a methodology that could be
applied again with a higher amount of data.
Moreover, further work could improve the assessment of geographical potential.
Notably, the automatisation of the process in a GIS software could be really valuable.
Other technologies and types of energy as bioenergy could also be integrated into this
analysis.
Finally, this work has assessed the technical potential of onshore wind and solar
PV but many other parameters have to be taken into account, such as the integration
to the electricity grid and the stability of the system. Indeed, the intermittency of
renewable energy creates many issues that the use of energy system modeling try to
answer.
