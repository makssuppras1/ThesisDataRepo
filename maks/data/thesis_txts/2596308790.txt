Abstract
The present study is an investigation of the wake interaction between wind turbines (fixed
Wind Turbines(WTs) and prescribed motion Floating Offshore Wind Turbines (FOWTs)).
In order to conduct this investigation, two new actuator disk models within OpenFoam
framework were developed. The first model (adaptiveActuationDisk) developed in order to
be used in wind farm simulations, whereas the second one is appropriate for the simulation
of prescribed motion FOWTs.
The adaptiveActuationDisk model was initially validated against Blind test 4 data and
then was further applied to simple wind farm cases; there, it was shown that total power
production of the farm is highly influenced by lateral and streamwise distance between the
WTs. The sixDoFAdaptiveActuationDisk was applied in single as well as pair of prescribed
motion FOWTs and revealed that prescribed motion has a significant influence on power
production, wake characteristics of a single FOWT and wake interaction between two
inline FOWTs. Finally, the cost-efficiency of the developed model was shown in a full-
scale simulation with realistic prescribed motion

1.1
Objectives
This study aims to provide more information within the field of wake interaction between
Wind Turbines (WTs) by utilizing Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). It consists of
two main branches, the first one deals with the validation of OpenFOAM built-in Actuator
Disk (AD) and eventually the application of it on fixed WTs, while the second one focuses
on how the prescribed motion of a pFOWT affects the wake development.
The most
significant part of this thesis is the extension of the capabilities of the traditional AD in
order to make it appropriate for the study of Floating Offshore Wind Turbines (FOWTs).
A summary of the objectives of this thesis is:
a Study the wake interaction of fixed Wind Turbines.
вҖ“ Replicate BT4 results and validate the OpenFOAM built-in AD model.
вҖ“ Compare various available turbulence models.
вҖ“ Develop an adaptive AD in OpenFOAM framework.
вҖ“ Investigate wind farm blockage effects.
b Study the wake interaction between pFOWTs.
вҖ“ Implement a 6 DOF AD in OpenFOAM framework.
вҖ“ Study the wake development of a pFOWT and the interaction between inline
pFOWTs.
вҖ“ Apply the 6 DOF AD on DTU 10MW WT with realistic prescribed motion.

Chapter 2
Introduction
Wind can exert a tremendous amount of force, a paradigm of winds capabilities can be
seen after the passage of a hurricane or a typhoon [1]. Human beings have managed to
exploit this force for many centuries. Even before the concept of the windmill, which is
the ancestor of modern WTs, humans used the wind in order to sail. The first known
grain mills and water pumps were installed in Persia and China, and they have dated
back AD 500-900 and AD 1200 respectively. In modern history, the first windmill in the
USA was built in 1888 by Daniel Halladay and used as a pumping mechanism [2]. When
electricity came into use, the erstwhile windmills connected to a generator in order to
produce electricity. One of the first who attempted to produce electricity from a windmill
was Poul la Cour. Before the second world war these primitive wind turbines were used
mainly to support farms, only after the oil crisis in 1973 wind turbines considered as a
potential alternative source of electrical energy production [1].
Nowadays, wind power production has expanded globally, being a multibillion industry.
At this point, wind energy market consists of more than 650 [GW] of which nearly 30 [GW]
belong to offshore market [3] . The last decade it is becoming a trend to push WTs further
offshore as it is illustrated in Figure 2.1. Offshore wind energy has access to better wind
resource, higher wind speed with lower turbulence intensity levels while at the same time
parts of the environmental impact are lower or easily mitigated (e.g. visual and noise
pollution). Another trend regarding offshore wind farms is that they are pushed further
offshore where the sea gets deeper than 50 meters[4]. However, bottom fixed foundations
(jacket, monopile, tripod etc.) cannot be installed after a certain sea depth; this is the
point that FOWTs emerge to fill the gap of wind power production in deep waters.

FOWT farms are in a primitive stage compared to onshore and fixed bottom offshore

2.1
Literature Review
The literature review is divided into two major categories in order to be consistent with
the structure of this thesis. The first category deals with the development of methods
used to predict the flow inside a wake, whereas the second one describes the numerical
simulation around the topic of FOWTs.
2.1.1
Wake modeling
Over the last three decades, the scientific community has been interested in flow prediction
of the WTвҖҷs wake region; this eventually led to the development of a variety of models
with different complexity [7].
Traditionally wake studies on wind farms were lying on analytical wake models like the
ones found in [8] and [9]. This kind of models, although they require a minimum com-
putational resource, they are highly depended on empirical coefficients and suffer from
oversimplification. As a consequence, this makes them inappropriate for wake interaction
and turbulence characterization of wakes [10].
A step higher on ladder of complexity stand the inviscid techniques of either vortex line
elements ([11][12][13]) or discrete vortices [14]. These methods can capture the 3D flow
around a WT. However, since they are based on potential flow, ignoring the viscous effects
when flow separation is present under-perform. On top of that, they come into instability
issues when vortex elements approach each other [15].

More advanced approaches include actuator methods coupled with CFD. These rotor pre-
dictive methods date back at the end of the 19th century when Froude initially introduced
the idea of the ideal rotor [16]. A continuation of this work arose when Glauert proposed
the Blade Element Momentum (BEM). Nowadays, there is a wide variety of rotor predic-
tive methods that can be coupled with CFD codes [17] . Two widely utilized methods
are the Actuator Disk Method (ADM) [18] and the Actuator Line Method (ALM) [19],
the most recent member of this family is the Actuator Surface Method (ASM) [20]. Most
famous among the three is the ADM, implemented in commercial (STAR-CCM+) and
open-source (OpenFOAM) codes with numerous applications in RANS [7] and LES [21]
framework.
Lastly, there are simulations of high fidelity full rotor CFD [22], which show great perfor-
mance. However, they suffer from a tremendous computational cost compared to rotor
model methods.
2.1.2
Floating offshore wind turbine simulations
FOWT concept is relatively recent and highly complicated from the numerical simulation
point of view, due to meshing complexities, free surface capturing challenges and the
problem known as added mass instability ([23] and [24]). As a result, the majority of the
existing investigations that focus on the wake development of a FOWT, in order to ease
the complexity usually simplify platform motion and emphasize on the aerodynamic part
[25]. Some of these cases are [4],[26] and [27].
In [28] a 2D simulation, using FVM along with BEM actuator disk, of NREL 5MW
under prescribed sinusoidal surge motion was conducted showing the influence of platform
motion on power production. Similar results were found in [26], where instead of 2D RANS
coupled with ADM, a 3D a lifting line method is used in order to simulate the wake region.
Here, all the different single DoF motions (translational and rotational) imposed to the
platform. Lastly, in [25] was developed a fully coupled 3D CFD tool that takes into account
aerodynamics, mooring line dynamics and floating platform hydrodynamics. During this
study, it was shown that the platformвҖҷs motion affects the power production of a FOWT
significantly. Although it is a highly sophisticated tool, it comes with high computational
cost compared to actuator or vortex methods.
Regarding the topic investigated in this thesis (i.e. wake interaction between FOWTвҖҷs)
the only study found in the literature that does not investigate only individual FOWTs is
[29] where NRELвҖҷs aeroelastic code FAST [30] is used to assess the wake interaction. As
a result, although the individual FOWT study has been studied from a CFD perspective,
the CFD approach of the wake interaction between FOWTs remains a virgin field.

[33].

2.3
One dimensional momentum theory
Imagine a fluid parcel passing through an ideal rotor 1 of an operating wind turbine.
Since the wind turbine extracts kinetic energy from the flow, as this particle approaches
and passed through the rotor slows down gradually from a freestream velocity V0 to a
smaller wake velocity with value u1 this means that the rotor can be described as a
permeable drag device. On the other side, pressure increases just upstream of the rotor
disk from the freestream value p0 to p+ and then follows a discontinuous drop to value
pвҲ’< p0 < p+ afterwards the pressure recovers steadily back to the freestream values.

2.4
Wind turbine wakes
The presence of a WT introduces an axial pressure gradient, this along with the geometry
of the WT generate the wake.
According to [28], wake is divided into near (2 вҲ’4D
downstream) and far (> 4D downstream) wake. Near wake region is strongly affected by
the presence and characteristics of the WT. Thus, near wake has a highly complex and
anisotropic flow structure. In conjunction with that, the far wake is influenced less by
the WT itself, but from more general WT parameters like power and thrust coefficients;
as a result, its complexity is lower. Under particular assumptions like axisymmetry, zero
axial pressure gradient and zero axial velocity curvature, it is possible to derive self-similar
solutions for the velocity and TKE profiles.
In Figure 2.3 is depicted how the inflow interacts with the WT. One can observe two shear
layers; these are produced due to the velocity difference between the inside and outside
of the wake. They are one of the main responsible mechanisms of turbulence formation
(other mechanisms are tip vortices, the turbulent boundary layer of the blades, nacelle and
tower). The higher the thrust coefficient, the lower the wake velocity and the larger the
shear that, in turn, generates more mean flow kinetic energy into turbulent kinetic energy.
The shear-induced turbulence works as an efficient mixer that helps the wake to recover
(i.e. inner velocity to recover back to ambient).
Figure 2.3: Realistic wake development sketch of WT subject to ABL inflow. Source: [38]
Offshore, where the turbulence intensity levels are usually lower than onshore, wakes tend
to be more persistent[38]. Therefore, wake modelling is a critical parameter of the design
process of offshore wind farms.

Chapter 3
Numerical Methods
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is essentially the approach of fluid mechanics from
a numerical perspective. The majority of CFD methods stems from the well known NS
equations. Thus, the goal of CFD is to provide methods for solving the NS equations
numerically. Finite Volume Method (FVM) is one of the most popular methods nowadays
implemented in numerous commercial and open-source codes dedicated to CFD. In order
to apply the FVM, the domain of interest has to be discredited into finite volumes, this
is the so-called domain discretization process, and its outcome is the computational mesh.
Afterwards, the discretization of the PDE system (i.e. NS equations) takes place. This
system through the applications of specific numerical schemes is transformed into an alge-
braic system. Finally, this algebraic system has to be resolved through a numerical solver
(e.g. TDMA, Gauss-Siedel, multigrid etc.). This was only a very brief overview about
CFD, the topic of CFD is very wide (for more information around this topic the reader
is referred to [39]). As a result, in the rest of this section, only the essential numerical
methods are presented.

In Figure 4.30 & 4.31 is illustrated the wake behaviour over a full period for sway and

Even if these motions take place in different axes, since the set up is
fully symmetric the results are the same. The first observation is that the inertia of the
flow (convection) along with the sinusoidal motion results in a curved wake region. The
strength and the expansion of the wake remain the same over the whole period; the only
change is seen on curvature.
In contrast to sway and heave, surge motion is in the direction of the flow. Although its
geometry is simple (there is no curvature), its behaviour is more complicated than sway
or heave. When turbine moves against the wind (Figure 4.32(c) & (d)) relative velocity
gets higher than freestream velocity resulting in a higher thrust thus a stronger wake. On
the contrary, when the WT moves with the wind relative velocity becomes lower than the
freestream leading to a lower thrust and therefore a weaker wake. This, of course, has also
an impact on power production as it is discussed further below.
In Figure 4.33 is illustrated the wake behaviour when pitch motion is imposed.
The
influence on the wake is minor, and this is directly correlated with the amplitude of the
sinusoidal motion and the low hub-height of the turbine (1[m]). In real motions, even
small amplitudes when combined with high hub heights can lead to more visible results.
Based on the results above (surge and heave) pitch could potentially affect both geometry
and strength of the wake since it is sort of a combined surge and heave motion.
Yaw motion does not move the WT it just redirects the normal vector of the rotor disk.
This results in a lower value of the relative velocity; therefore, it clearly affects the strength
of the wake (see Figure 4.34). Moreover, this specific case has an unrealistic combination
of frequency and amplitude, thus leads to over-amplified results. The last point that has
to be stressed is that this kind of motion can only underproduce power, in contrast, to
pitch and surge that may also overproduce.

4.3.4
Amplitude and frequency influence
In this section, the influence of the characteristics of the sinusoidal motion (amplitude and
frequency) on power production is examined. In Figure 4.36 & Figure 4.37 is shown the
power production for various frequency and amplitude setups. Both single DoF motions
show similar behaviour when amplitude or frequency gets higher the power production
rises. In all cases, the mean power production is higher compared to the fixed (baseline)
case. This outcome is in line with multiple previous studies [44], [26] and [28].
Furthermore, in pitch case (Figure 4.36) there are present fluctuations on the power sig-
nal. This issue stems from the probing mechanism of the AD. The upstream velocity is
determined based on a single probe point that moves according to the imposed motion.
When the point enters inside a new cell with different velocity, a jump on the probed
velocity occurs; hence this jump is transferred to the power production. This issue could
be resolved if the probing takes place on a volume or an area around the probe point.
Otherwise, a finer mesh has to be generated around the probe point.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and further work
5.1
Conclusion
The scope of this work was to develop a tool that would be able to perform a numerical
investigation of interacting FOWTs, and this was done by extending the capabilities of the
basic fvOption actuationDisk in OpenFOAM v19.12. Two major extensions of this AD
model took place in the course of this thesis. The first one (i.e. adaptiveActuationDisk)
enhanced dynamic Ct and Cp regulation hence made the fvOption appropriate for wind
farm wake studies. The second extension added prescribed six-DoF motion functionality
to the adaptiveActuationDisk fvOption, resulting into a moving AD model (i.e. sixDO-
FAdaptiveActuationDisk) capable of simulating pFOWT interaction cases.
The developed fvOptions were applied in various cases such as Blind Test 4 cases, wind
farm simulations individual pFOWT as well as pair of pFOWTs imposed in basic single
DoF motions. Finally, as a proof of concept, a long simulation of DTU-10 MW imposed
in realistic 3 DoF motion was carried out.
The main outcomes are drawn from the case study:
вҖў Blind Test 4 used as a base of validation for the newly developed fvOptions. The
comparison of BT4 results, both numerical and experimental showed a good agree-
ment with the current simulations.
вҖў Streamwise and lateral spacing of wind turbines affect power production and loading
of downstream WTs.
вҖў Tightly spaced wind farms in the lateral direction can have higher power production
due to speed up effects.
вҖў The platform motion of a FOWT can have a serious impact on the power production,
loading and wake characteristics.
вҖў Wake interaction between FOWTs leads to time-dependent power production and
loading of downstream FOWTs.
вҖў In realistic motions pitch shows the largest influence on power production.
5.2
Further work
This study is confined mainly inside the proof of concept boundaries of the developed adap-
tiveActuationDisk. Therefore there are multiple directions for the continuation simulation-
wise some of them are sorted below
Numerical Investigation of Wake Interactions between Floating Wind Turbines

==PAGE:71==
вҖў Investigate the influence of ABL inflow on the wake interactions between pFOWTs,
under different atmospheric stability states.
вҖў Perform large scale simulations of floating wind farms and compare the power pro-
duction with fixed ones.
вҖў Examine the compatibility between sixDOFAdaptiveActiuationDisk and LES.
In terms of development, the probing technique of sixDoFAdaptiveActuationDisk can be-
come more sophisticated. The current implementation probes the velocity based on the
value of a single cell; this can potentially lead to fluctuation of the estimated relative veloc-
ity. This issue can be eliminated if an area- or volume-averaged value used instead. This
is a minor development with high impact on the numerical behaviour and mesh resolution
requirements of this implementation. Another possible development of the tool would be
the consideration of radial effects of the rotor disk in a BEM logic.
