Abstract
Objective - The objective of this work was to characterize a yogurt fermentation
process of starter cultures produced by the company Chr. Hansen. The usability of
various mathematical approaches was evaluated. This characterization can be helpful
in the preparation and selling of the starter culture product.
Data - The data analyzed in this work comprises of two datasets containing measure-
ments for different combinations of starter cultures and pasteurization methods over
the fermentation process. One dataset contained acidic (pH) value measurements,
while the other was a metabolic dataset containing intensity measurements over the
nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectrum.
Methodology - The methods that were applied to the data were: separate time point
analysis of variance (ANOVA), mixed linear model, polynomial regression, kinetic
model, ANOVA on the principal component scores, ANOVA-simultaneous component
analysis and hierarchical heatmap clustering.
Findings - The pH analyses showed that the bacterial growth curves were slightly
different depending on both starter cultures and pasteurization temperatures. Milk
inoculated with Streptococcus thermophilus (ST) acidifies fast at the beginning of
the fermentation process compared to that inoculated with Lactobacillus delbrueckii
subsp. bulgaricus (LB). For the higher temperature (105◦C) the acidification rate of
LB surpasses ST as time evolves and finally reaches a lower pH value than ST. A mix-
ture of these cultures (ST+LB) acidifies more uniformly throughout the fermentation
and from six hours of fermentation its pH value becomes independent of milk pasteur-
ization. The NMR analysis showed that milk pasteurized at the higher temperature
contains more formic acid, and that ST produces formic acid during the fermenta-
tion. Milk inoculated with LB contains more pyruvic acid and aliphatics, but less
formic acid and combined acetone, N_acetyl_derived_AA and methionine. At
the lower temperature (99◦C) LB produces aliphatics and pyruvic acid.
Conclusion - All methods helped to characterize the fermentation process, though
some provided more valuable information than others. A statistical difference was
detected between various combinations of starter cultures and pasteurization methods
for the duration of the lag-phase, the maximum acidification decay, the pH value at
four hours of fermentation and the estimated time to reach a pH value of 4.5. The
combination of starter cultures, pasteurization methods and fermentation times had
a significant effect on both the acidic development as well as the metabolites.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years yogurt has become one of the most popular fermented dairy products
worldwide [1]. Due to the well-established health benefit of consuming yogurt with
live and active cultures the demand has increased [2]. With the growing popularity
the manufacturing process became standardized [3].
1.1
Objective
Chr. Hansen is a bioscience company in Denmark with global representation that
produces and sells among other things starter cultures - the base of yogurt [4]. The
objective of this thesis was to investigate the quality of the starter cultures. A critical
measurement of quality is how quickly and uniformly a culture acidifies milk by con-
verting lactose into lactic acid. Manufacturing requires the starter cultures to reach
a predefined acidification value (pH value) within strict time limits. Furthermore,
it is preferred to know the cultures’ participation in other biochemical conversions
impacting the quality of the final yogurt product. It can be both expensive and time-
consuming to measure every sold product to verify its traits. According to Soukolis
et al. the determination of the optimal incubation time can both reduce the manu-
facturing cost of yogurt as well as prevent deterioration of the quality of the yogurt
[5].
The University of Copenhagen carried out the experimental work on behalf of Chr.
Hansen and provided several datasets based on measurements of different combina-
tions of cultures and heat treatments over the fermentation process. In this thesis,
two datasets were used to analyze the quality of the starter cultures based on pH
value and NMR spectra (see section 4) with the goals to:
• Characterize fermentation with different starter cultures with regard to growth
dynamics (pH curves) and metabolite composition (NMR spectra)
• Asses the usability of different modeling approaches to evaluate the growth
curves and high dimensional metabolite data.

1.2
Yogurt production process
Succeeding the initial preparation of the raw milk, the milk is pasteurized which
destroys existing undesired microbes and evaporates water from the milk [6]. Different
pasteurization methods can be applied, classified by their duration and temperature.
After pasteurization, the milk is cooled and inoculated with lactic acid bacterias that
convert sugar to acid and therefore lower the pH value of the milk [7]. In traditional
yogurt fermentation, the primary starter cultures used are Streptococcus thermophilus
and Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp.bulgaricus [8]. These bacteria flourish in relatively
high temperatures which make them thermophilic. The inoculation marks the start
of the fermentation process.
The fermentation process is the most vital stage of yogurt production [3]. This process
is described in details with respect to the thesis in section 3. During this stage, the
characteristics of the yogurt, such as texture and flavor, develop and the yogurt clot
forms [3].
A key factor here is the starter culture that acts through biochemical
reactions and influences the final quality of the yogurt.
Therefore it is essential
to investigate the characteristics of different starter cultures, which is the primary
objective of this research work as previously mentioned.

CHAPTER 3
The Fermentation
Process
This chapter explains how the different pasteurization methods and bacterial strains
mentioned in section 2, influence the fermentation process. Basic knowledge of the
underlying factors in the fermentation process is essential to understand the influence
of the design variables on acidity and metabolites. Therefore, the heat treatments
will be summarized first, followed by the starter cultures with a focus on treatment
groups used in this study.
3.1
Pasteurization methods
Heat treatment of milk is carried out to ensure the safety of the product [3] and is
a critical factor for the texture formation of yogurt [5]. The heating persuades the
denaturation of the whey proteins so that they can associate with casein micelles
[9]. An increased denaturation generally reduces the fermentation time [10]. In 2007,
Lucey et al.
announced that the increased heating intensity influences extensive
denaturation, which also correlates with increased storing modulus during the acid
gelation [11].
In 1999 they showed that gelation starts earlier with severely heat-
treated milk resulting in decreased coagulation time [12]. To this date various heat
treatment approaches exist. In this study, the selected heat treatments were carried
out at 99◦C and 105◦C for 30 minutes. According to Sfakianakis et al., those methods
can both be considered between high-temperature pasteurization and sterilization [3].
High-temperature pasteurization can be described as heating at 90-95 ◦C for 5 minutes
[3]. This approach kills most vegetative microorganisms within the milk and denatures
most of the whey proteins, which increases the viscosity and firmness of the yogurt.
Sterilization can be defined as heating at 110◦C for 30 minutes [3]. It exterminates
all microorganisms, denatures whey proteins and aggregates caseins, which has a
considerable impact on the viscosity and firmness of the final yogurt product [3, 6].
==PAGE:30==
3.2 Starter cultures
8
Lactose is partly converted into formic acid (formate) during a high-temperature
heat-treatment. This treatment also stimulates the growth of the lactic acid bacteria
[13], which directly impacts the acidity. Therefore, a higher temperature approach is
expected to result in lower acidic value throughout the fermentation process.
3.2
Starter cultures
In this thesis, the applied starter cultures were lactic acid bacteria which require
a carbon source and rely on glycolysis for energy [6]. Fermentation of sugars with
lactic acid bacteria can be homolactic or heterolactic. The main difference lies in the
fermentative pathways of glucose [14]. Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus
delbrueckii subsp.
bulgaricus follow the homolactic pathway and will therefore be
considered [15]. The homolactic pathway is based on glycolysis, whose main product
is lactic acid [14].
In milk, the primary energy source is lactose, which cannot enter the glycolysis path-
way directly. Instead, it is transported into a cell with particular membrane trans-
port proteins called permease [14]; see figure 3.1. Inside the bacterial cell, lactose is
dissolved with β-galactosidase into glucose and galactose. Then glucose enters the
glycolysis as the galactose is transported out of the cell, which creates energy for more
lactose intake [14].
Figure 3.1: Illustration of the transportation of lactose into the bacterial cell by the permease
system. The figure is from Lahtinen et al. work in 2011 [14].
As briefly mentioned in the introduction, the choice of bacteria is a critical factor for
the progress of the fermentation process since it affects both the acidification rate
as well as the metabolite composition during the fermentation. Lactic acid bacterias
are, among other things, responsible for the development of the characteristics of the
yogurt. Streptococcus thermophilus can make yogurt alone and impact the flavor as
well as the texture of the yogurt [13]. Streptococcus is more tolerant to high sugar
concentrations within the milk [13]. It is also weakly proteolytic, so under aromatic
condition Streptococcus can form formic acid and CO2 from pyruvic acid [6] which is
emphasized by the following equation:

proteins within the milk to form peptides and amino acids [6]. Usually, Streptococcus
and Lactobacillus are inoculated simultaneously into the milk [13]. The mixture of cul-
tures is often introduced as protocooperation since the two bacterias grow significantly
faster when cultured together instead of being grown separately [13]. Streptococcus
capability of producing formic acid can stimulate the growth of Lactobacillus, which
again stimulates the growth of Streptococcus by breaking proteins into small peptides
and amino acids [6] see figure 3.2. Streptococcus is dominating when the acidity is
low while Lactobacillus has greater acidic tolerance [13].

3.2.1
General information on bacterial growth phases
Bacterial growth is described as the process when a cell divides into two genetically
identical cells [16]. When the bacteria is exposed to a liquid nutrient, their growth is
explained by four different phases (see figure 3.3) as follows:
1. Lag-phase: The bacteria adjusts to new circumstances and the bacterial cells
start to grow [16].
2. Log-phase: The bacteria divides at an exponential rate. The number of new
cells per time is proportional to the present population and the growth rate is
dependent on the growth conditions [16].
3. Stationary phase: When the nutrient concentration is limited, there is a bal-
ance between bacterial division and bacterial death [16].
==PAGE:32==

4. Death phase: The population dies due to unsuitable living conditions [16].

3.3
Monitoring the fermentation process
There are numerous approaches to monitor the fermentation process of yogurt. In
this project, the focus was on the acidic profile as well as the metabolites. The idea
behind pH measurements and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy will therefore
be summarized.
3.3.1
Measuring the acidic profile
The acidic value (pH value) is a measure of free acidity within a sample, which is
defined as the negative logarithm of the hydrogen ion concentration. The range for
pH values is from zero to 14, where values below 7 are considered acidic and values
above 7 are considered base. The pH value of milk ranges from 6.5 to 6.7 and the
pH value of yogurt from 4 to 4.6. Microorganisms like starter culture bacteria are
sensitive to the pH value, where a very low or high pH value will prevent microbial
growth [17]

3.3.2
Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy
The structure of molecules influences the chemical properties of substances and conse-
quently affects how they react with other units. A brief understanding of a molecular
structure has therefore become vital when producing materials such as yogurt [18].
As a result, metabolomics is becoming a critical analytical tool in nowadays nutri-
tional studies [19]. Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is often used in
metabolomics studies and can identify and quantify chemicals from complex mixtures
[20].
A magnetic field causes many atomic nuclei to rotate around their axisaxis that, due
to their electrical charge, generates a magnetic field that tends to align with the
earlier mentioned magnetic field. NMR is built on this theory and applies a strong
magnetic field to a sample to force it to have aligned and parallel magnetic moments.
The magnetic moment is not strong enough to damage the molecular structure of the
sample, but it is strong enough to get insight into the molecular feature. The magne-
tized sample is exposed to a short pulse of electromagnetic radiation (radio waves),
which forces its magnetic moments to be perpendicular to the applied field. Each nu-
cleus responds with a specific resonance frequency, which provides vital information
about the types and positions of the atoms within the molecule. The NMR signals
are then obtained by measuring the resonance frequencies in a NMR spectrometer.
The shape of a NMR signal depends on how nuclei interact with each other through
chemical bonds of the molecule[18]. The area under a NMR signal is proportional to
the number of atoms producing it and thus the molecular concentration[18].
The resonance frequency of the atoms is affected by the chemical environment. There-
fore, a frequency independent scale called chemical shift has been developed, which
allows for comparison of spectra obtained from a different instrument. The chemical
shift scale (δ) is expressed in parts per million (ppm) and can be described as follows:
Chemical shift, δ = frequency of a signal −frequency of a referance

Sample preparation
The sample preparation was carried out by KU. KU’s report on the procedure will

It can be challenging to select appropriate methods that match the criteria of datasets
derived from complicated experimental settings. In this section, possible statistical,

5.1
Taking the kinetics of fermentation into account
when modeling the acidic profile
Acidification is a crucial mechanism during yogurt fermentation [35]. In 1999 the
effects of the fermentation process conditions on the pH development were investigated
by De Brabandere and De Baerdemaker [35]. They described the pH profile based
on the modified Gompertz equation [36] for bacterial growth. Four parameters were
used to evaluate the effects of different process conditions, namely the start pH level,
the pH end level, the lag phase and the maximal rate of pH decrease with time,
which are all vital with regards to the fermentation process. In 2007 Soukoulis et
al.
successfully used the same approach to describe the pH decline development
during the fermentation process of milk to yogurt [5]. This model might, therefore,
be relevant for modeling each batch to obtain the estimated duration of the lag phase

30
and the maximal pH decreasement over time for each combination of starter culture
and heat treatment. The results could then be used to compare the effects of different
starter cultures and heat treatment combination of the fermentation parameters.
5.2
Analysis of variance
Analysis of variance (ANOVA) is a univariate approach that is amongst the most
commonly used statistical techniques for hypothesis testing and has the flexibility
to cover a wide range of experimental designs [[37], [38]]. The goal of ANOVA is
to test for differences between the means of a univariate output variable of two or
more treatment groups[38] to determine if the factors can significantly explain the
variation in the dependent variable [39]. ANOVA exploits a systematically designed
experiment and may resolve the sources of error arising from the experiment [38].
Originally, ANOVA was developed to study crop yields by separating the biological
sources of variation such as soil composition, rainfall and sunlight [40]. In this project,
ANOVA might be a straight forward alternative to obtain knowledge concerning the
effects of experimental factors mentioned in section 2 on the dependent variable at
hand. However, ANOVA assumes independent sampling [41] and is not optimal when
there is a high degree of collinearity between the independent variable since ANOVA
does not take their interaction into account [42]. Both datasets described in section 4
are longitudinal, meaning that the batches contain repeated measurements over the
fermentation time, so the assumption of independence is not met.
5.3
Multiple hypothesis testing
One way to deal with the time dependency is to perform multiple simultaneous
ANOVAs, one on each time unit. When performing multiple hypotheses at once, the
risk of rejecting a true null hypothesis increases as the number of hypotheses grows
[43].
Several methods have been proposed to overcome this problem and thereby
reduce the risk of false rejection. The straightforward and conservative approach is
likely the Bonferroni correction, which adjusts the p-value to the number of hypothe-
ses to be tested [44]. It is not uncommon to use the Bonferroni multiple comparisons
approach for repeated-measures ANOVA.
The application of multiple hypothesis testing makes it possible to perform ANOVA
on a multivariate dataset. Still, in the case of the NMR data, there are more than
9938 correlated dependent variables. The multiple testing adjusting method becomes
unsuitable with a very high number of tests [45].

5.4
Multivariate analysis of variance
Multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) is a common way to extend ANOVA to
a multidimensional setting [46]. MANOVA is generally used when dealing with a few
moderately correlated dependent variables. It is not suitable when the correlation
between the dependent variables is too high or too low [47]. MANOVA is also unsuit-
able when the number of variables exceeds the number of experiments [48], which is
the case for the NMR dataset.
5.5
Mixed linear models
A mixed linear model is another extension of ANOVA, that takes the underlying
correlation structure between subjects into account by introducing a random effect
variable [49]. This approach has been the base of statistical analysis for longitudi-
nal data in biometric settings [50] and might be relevant here since the batches are
repeatedly measured throughout the fermentation process.
5.6
Principal component analysis
Principal component analysis (PCA) is a commonly used tool in a high dimensional
setting to reduce the dimensionality of the dataset [51]. PCA captures the underlying
covariance structure of the data set by maximizing the variance of the principal
components (PCs) where the PCs are mutually orthogonal.
When data from an
experimental design contains various sources of variation, PCA might potentially
miss out on variance due to the design factors [52]. Therefore, PCA does not take
the experimental design into account [53].
5.7
Combining PCA and ANOVA
One might suggest performing a PCA dimensionality reduction before applying MANOVA,
but that is not relevant since the principal components are uncorrelated [51]. Even
though PCA is not guaranteed to account for the underlying experimental factors, it
is possible to combine PCA with ANOVA, where a PCA model is first constructed
on the entire data set. The first few obtained PCs are then analyzed with ANOVA
[54]. Performing separate ANOVAs on different PCs is usually not considered ideal
since there is a mixed contribution to the variation in the PCs [55], which might lead
to misleading results.

5.8
Supervised factor analysis
Supervised factor analysis (FA), is often used as an alternative for PCA. It rotates
the PCs to the meaningful directions such that they correlate with the factors of
the experiment [56]. Supervised methods are problematic when the data is under-
determined since there are infinitely many rotations that correlate with the factors
[52].
5.9
ANOVA-principal component analysis and
ANOVA-simultaneous component analysis
ANOVA-principal component analysis (APCA) and ANOVA-simultaneous compo-
nent analysis (ASCA) combine the power of both ANOVA and PCA. The methods
are generalizations of ANOVA to multidimensional settings. Both approaches are
based on a decomposition of the data matrix into effect matrices.
The main dif-
ference between APCA and ASCA lies in the projection of the effect matrices [42].
ASCA and APCA have been used on spectra from food products to analyze multi-
variate data from an experimental design [57]. For example, APCA was used on a
spectroscopy dataset derived from experimental design to detect biomarkers in the
amniotic fluid [52]. ASCA was introduced by analyzing proton nuclear magnetic reso-
nance spectra from urine samples of guinea prigs. There were in total of three groups
of guinea pigs, each treated with different doses of Vitamin C. Urine samples of the
pigs were taken at four different points over 12 months [58].
According to Smide comparison paper on the two approaches, ASCA turned out to
be more discriminating between factor levels than APCA, and the loadings obtained
by it were more close to the true loadings[58]. Therefore, the focus will be on ASCA
here. Combining ASCA with permutation test enables statistically testing whether
design factors have a significant effect on high dimensional response variables [42].

6.1
Analysis of variance - ANOVA
This section is inspired by a paper released in 1989 about the analysis of variance [38].
The goal of the following subsections is to explain the idea behind ANOVA. First,
assumptions behind ANOVA will be summarized. Then ANOVA tests for significant
factor effects will be described. Modifications of ANOVA to manage unbalanced data
and repeated-measurements will be considered.
Finally, the general linear model
formulation of ANOVA will be demonstrated.
6.1.1
The assumptions of ANOVA
The assumptions of ANOVA are the following:
• Random sampling [41].
• Homogenus variance across groups [41].
• Normality i.e., groups are roughly normally distributed [41].
• Additivity of components i.e. fixed factor effect has the same impacts on the
outcome and will always differ in the same way [41] and [38].

6.1.4
Adapting ANOVA test to unbalanced data
Data is considered unbalanced when there are unequal numbers of observations within
treatment groups. There are mainly three reasons that cause this imbalance; unequal
sample sizes, missing responses and missing cells [61]. When dealing with a single-
factor ANOVA the data imbalance does not cause severe problems [62]. However,
as soon as there are two factors with an interaction term, this imbalance becomes
problematic. There are several different approaches to adjust ANOVA to unbalanced
data of which all compute the SS differently.
The main three approaches can be
summarized as follows [61]:
Type I: Sequentially fits each effect in the order that it appears in the model. Then
it calculates the SS related to that effect as explained in section 6.1.3. This
approach relies heavily on the order of entry of the model and will, therefore,
not be considered here.
Type II: Assumes that the interaction term is negligible or non-existent. When testing
for the main effects, all other main effects are taken into account, unlike the
Type I test, which solely accounts for those that have previously entered the
model. For Type II ANOVA the sum of squares of a main effect is estimated
as follows:
SS(β) = SS(β|γ) = SSerror(γ) −SSerror(β, γ)
SS(γ) = SS(γ|β) = SSerror(β) −SSerror(β, γ)
(6.15)
where β, γ are described as earlier, SSerror is the residual sum of squares
obtained from the model that includes the factors within the round brackets.
As an example, SSerror(β) is the residual sum of squares of the model described
in equation 6.1, and the statistics are estimated similarly as explained
6.1.3.
==PAGE:63==
6.1 Analysis of variance - ANOVA

The car package in R offers a function called Anova where the Type of ANOVA
test can be adjusted to Type II test by setting the input parameter type=2
[63]. Type II ANOVA test is the most powerful test when there is no significant
interaction effect and will therefore be used in those casees [64]
Type III: Quantifies factor effects by adjusting it for all other factors within the model,
not only the main effects as Type II does. Type III test is thus not dependent
on the order of the model as Type I test is. This test is relevant when testing for
an interaction term as long as there are no missing cells. For Type III ANOVA,
the sum of squares of the interaction term, is described as follows:

where everything can be described as above and the Anova function in the car

here as long as the interaction term is significant and an entire cell is not missing.

6.1.7
Validating the ANOVA model
It is vital to examine the validity of the model to obtain accurate and reliable results.
Therefore, it is important to make sure that the assumptions of ANOVA described
in section 6.1.1 are met as well as the normality assumption of the random effect if
the random effect is present. Most assumptions can be evaluated with proper plots
and inspections. But according to the central limit theorem for large sample sizes,
the sampling distribution tends to be normal regardless of the shape of the data [70].
Therefore, when the sample size is greater than 30, the violation of normality should
not be too problematic [71], and in the case of more than 100 samples the assumption
can be ignored without invalidating the results [72].
6.2
Multiple hypothesis testing
When multiple hypotheses are tested simultaneously, every single hypothesis test
result is based on some statistical evidence (α-level of significance), which means that
it has an uncertainty of α of falsely rejecting a true null hypothesis (Type I error).
The probability of at least one Type I error (family-wise error rate) increases with the
number of hypotheses, and the consequences can be severe, mainly if the conclusions
must be evaluated as a whole [43]. As an example, when M independent hypotheses
are tested at once, the family-wise error rate of the collection of the test becomes
1−(1−α)M. However, when the tests are positively correlated, the family-wise error
rate is less than 1 −(1 −α)M [73].
6.2.1
Bonferroni correction
The Bonferroni correction is among the simplest approaches to multiple testing and
tries to adjust the significance level of the number of comparisons. The approach
makes each test more convincing by setting the upper limit of the family-wise error
rate to α. The Bonferroni correction approach, therefore, rejects a null hypothesis
for each test i when:
pi < α
M
(6.30)
The approach is useful when M (the number of hypotheses to be tested) is fairly
small but becomes conservative when M is too high [73].

6.2.2
Post hoc analysis - multiple comparisons of treatment
groups
The aim of an experiment is usually to investigate the central tendencies of measure-
ments across treatment groups. When there are many groups at hand, ANOVA (see
section 6.1) can be used to determines if there is a significant difference between the
means of the treatment groups. However, it does not shed light on which groups differ
from one another. A post hoc analysis test can be performed to distinguish which
groups are statistically different from one another [74]. A standard t-test can be
used if there are only two groups to compare. However, using that approach on all
possible combinations of more than two groups increases the risk of significance by
chance. The most commonly used adjustment to overcome this multiplicity problem
is the Tukey test [75], that compares the difference between each pair of means with
an adjustment for the family-wise error rate. It uses the q-distribution (studentized
range distribution) to obtain sampling distribution of the most significant difference
between a set of means from the same population [74].

6.3.1.1
Selecting the appropriate order of polynomial
It can be fascinating to select a high degree of polynomial to establish a lower mean
squared error (equation 6.1.6), but it usually results in overfitting the data. A leave-
one-out cross-validation can be used to select the appropriate order of a polynomial
curve for a small dataset.
A leave-one-out cross-validation (LOO) is a recursive procedure that can be used
to adjust hyperparameters, such as the order of polynomial regression. LOO leaves
one observation at a time from a model-fitting process and subsequently uses it to
evaluate the error of the corresponding fitted model. The generalization error (LOO
error) of the model fitting procedure is estimated as the average error obtained by
leaving all observation out precisely once. The preferred polynomial order can then
be selected as the order related to the lowest LOO-error [51].
6.4
Kinetic modeling of a pH profile
A kinetic model of a measurement profile is used to obtain desired optimal perfor-
mance. An advantage of a kinetic model is that it has the ability to account for
underlying factors. The most vital factor of milk fermentation is the starter culture
[6]. The starter cultures produce lactic acid, that lowers the pH value of the milk.
As the number of bacteria grows in the milk, the pH value decreases. It is, therefore,
a good idea to consider the impact of the growth of the starter culture on the acidi-
fication profile as mentioned in section 3 by building a model that accounts for the
bacterial growth curve. In 1990 Zwietering modified the Gompertz Sigmoidal func-
tion to describe the lag-, exponential- and stationary phases of the bacterial growth
curve [36]. That model can again be modified to describe the pH profile throughout
the fermentation process of milk to yogurt since the pH profile is dependent on the
bacterial growth [35].

across groups to construct a null distribution can be used to determine which factors
have a globally significant effect [85]. There are no restrictive assumptions apart form
the exchangeability of observations; more precisely, a measurement can fall into any
group under the null hypothesis.

be carried out where the least significant terms will be removed until all left terms are
significant. Next, post hoc analysis will be conducted and finally, the model validated.

8.4.1
The formate region
The concentration of formate increases with storage time such that old milk powder
contains more formate than a new powder. High pasteurization temperature can also
have an impact on the formate concentration at the beginning of the fermentation
process [97]. The presence of formate makes the acidification rate faster as it since
the bacterias grow more quickly. Unquestionably, Chr. Hansen ’s customers want the
bacteria growth to be particularly dependent on the culture but not on the age of the
milk powder. Therefore, the concentration of formate will be analyzed at the start
of the fermentation process (time 0) to investigate if there is a significant formate
difference between samples. By inspecting figure 4.9 in section 4.2.7 it seems that
there is more formate present when the milk is pasteurized at 105◦C as the magnitude
of the peak is of higher. For LB, the formate decreases over time while it increases
for both MX and ST, which is reasonable since ST produced formate and LB uses
formate to grow as described in section 3.2. For MX, the concentration of formate
rises at a slower rate than for St since LB is consuming formate at the same time as
ST produces it.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion and
conclusion
In this work, the main goal was to characterize the yogurt fermentation process of
different starter cultures produced by Chr. Hansen and, at the same time, evaluate
the usability of different modeling approaches.
Two datasets containing measurements for various combinations of starter cultures
and pasteurization methods from 0 to 8 hours of fermentation and after 24 hours
of fermentation were used to characterize the fermentation process of milk to yo-
gurt. One dataset contained acidic (pH) value measurements, while the other was
a metabolic dataset comprising of intensity measurements over the nuclear magnetic
resonance (NMR) spectrum. The quality of the NMR dataset was not optimal. Es-
sential steps were thus taken to improve the NMR data quality, which resulted in two
NMR datasets. One of which was based on pre-processed NMR signal intensities and
the other on a carefully estimated signal area of selected biochemical compounds.
9.1
The usability of different modeling approaches -
summary
Several mathematical and statistical approaches were applied to the datasets to
achieve useful information on the fermentation process.
The absence of measure-
ments between 8 to 24 hours of fermentation limited the performance of modeling
procedures within that time range.
For the pH-data, a kinetic model that accounts for the bacterial growth in the milk
turned out to be suitable to describe the acidic profile and to extract kinetic pa-
rameters such as the duration of the lag phase and the maximum acidification decay.
Furthermore, a polynomial approach was helpful in estimating the time of the desired
pH value.
All univariate parameters such as the acidic value, the duration of the lag-phase, the
maximum pH decay and the estimated time of a specific pH value could be compared
between level combinations of starter cultures and heat treatments over the fermenta-
==PAGE:126==
9.2 pH-data analysis - summary
104
tion time by using both mixed linear model and separate time point ANOVA analysis.
The separate timepoint analysis allowed for a more straightforward interpretation of
each fermentation time point, but did not include any knowledge across fermentation
times. The mixed linear model was more challenging to apprehend, but it made com-
parisons across fermentation times possible and therefore included more information
than the separate timepoint analysis.
For the NMR-data based on signal intensities over the entire NMR spectrum, both PC-
ANOVA and ASCA could be applied. These methods resulted in similar PC loadings.
The ASCA model was, however, more relevant as it accounts for the experimental
design, which made its score plot more appropriate to describe the discrimination
between all of the experimental factors. The ASCA modeling procedure also allowed
for multivariate statistical testing of all factor effects on the entire NMR spectrum.
In contrast, the tests of factor effects of PC-ANOVA were based on an ANOVA on
a single PC score at a time that was not guaranteed to account for the experimental
design. The hierarchical clustering approach based on signal areas gave a clear insight
into to the primary yogurt metabolites, which is the consumption of sugar-related
compounds and production of lactate (lacticacid) over the fermentation time. It also
grouped different combinations of starter cultures, heat treatments and fermentation
times having similar metabolomic characteristics
9.2 pH-data analysis - summary
The pH analyses showed no observed acidic differences depending on the starter cul-
tures, the pasteurization temperatures or their interaction at the time of inoculation.
Chr. Hansens’ starter cultures are therefore not dependent on the acidic value of
initial milk.
The acidic analysis of the first four hours of fermentation revealed that LB has a
significantly longer lag-phase than both ST and MX, and the estimated duration of
the lag-phase lasts for more than two and a half hours for both LB-99 and LB-105.
Two ANOVA approaches, one based on random effects and the other on separate
time points analysis, agreed that after four hours of fermentation, ST-105 has the
significantly lowest pH value at approximately 5.36pH, followed by MX-105 at 5.49pH.
Thus, there is a significant pasteurization temperature effect for both ST and MX at
four hours of fermentation. The effect of temperature on LB is not significant at four
hours of fermentation and both LB combinations have the significantly highest acidic
value (above 5.8pH). According to the kinetic model, LB-105 reaches the maximum
pH decay around −0.7h−1 followed by ST-105 at −0.6h−1 and mixtures of starters
(MX) end up in third place at −0.55 −0.57h−1 with no significant difference between
MX-105 and MX-99.
From five hours, the acidic value of LB becomes heavily dependent on the pasteuriza-
tion temperature. The pH value of MX becomes independent of milk pasteurization

9.3 NMR-data analysis - summary
105
from six hours of fermentation. The analysis of the estimated time of 4.5pH showed
that it takes LB-105, MX-105 and MX-99 around 6.5 hours to reach 4.5pH.
Comprehensively, starter cultures and heat treatments have significant effects on the
acidic development from one hour of fermentation, but their interaction becomes
significant from four hours. Milk pasteurized at a higher temperature (105◦C) usually
results in a lower acidic value. At the beginning of the fermentation process, milk
inoculated with ST acidifies faster than LB. Still, as time evolves, the acidic rate of
LB surpasses ST and finally reaches a lower pH value than ST. MX acidifies more
uniformly throughout the fermentation.
9.3 NMR-data analysis - summary
The NMR analyses were carried out in separate time groups:
before five hours
(timegroup1) and from five hours (timegroup2) of fermentation.
This is due to
a data distinction which can be associated to the presence of large proteins and pep-
tides at the beginning of the fermentation [29]. As formate in milk powder increases
with storage time and stimulates the growth of LB, it was decided to analyze the
formic acid (formate) concentration at the start of the fermentation to make sure
that the investigations were not dependent on the age of the milk. At the beginning
of the process, the formate concentration was generally low and dependent on the
temperature as is to be expected since high pasteurization temperature facilitates
conventions of lactose to formate.
For timegroup1, the interaction effect between starter cultures and heat treatments
alone does not have significant effect on the entire NMR spectra, but the interac-
tion between starter cultures, heat treatments and fermentation times does, which
means that the fermentation time is a vital factor concerning metabolites. The most
distinctive metabolites for the first few hours of the fermentation process is the pro-
duction of lactate and consumption of sugar-related compounds such as galactose,
lactose and glycolate. According to the analysis, the transformation of sugar-related
compounds into lactate is generally slower for LB than for the other bacteria, which
agrees to the pH analysis as the pH value has an inverse relationship to lactate con-
centration. At 4 hours, ST-105 has the highest lactate compared to sugar-related
compound proportion.
For timegroup2, all effects have significant impact on the entire spectrum, where the
full interaction between starter cultures, heat treatments and fermentation is highly
significant. Lactate has the most dominating influence on the PC1 scores obtained
by both PC-ANOVA and ASCA.
It is not surprising that lactate is the dominating biochemical when the entire spec-
trum is taken into account as it is the main product of lactic acid bacteria in the yogurt
fermentation process.
Nevertheless, there might be other interesting biochemicals
that differ between level combinations throughout the fermentation on a less extreme

scale. Therefore, the analysis was split into smaller spectral regions to detect further
metabolism differences. It turned out that there was a more significant difference in
the case of timegroup2 where the interaction effect between starter cultures, heat
treatments and times was significant for all subregions. In the case of timegroup1
approximately half of the subregions differed significantly across level combination of
combinations starter cultures, heat treatments and fermentation times.
The subregional spectral approach revealed what has previously been presented, i.e.
that milk inoculated with LB has more pyruvate and aliphatics but less formate
and combined acetone, N_acetyl_ −derived_AA and methionine compared with

Finally, data analysis based on a carefully preprocessed signal area was conducted to

concentration revealed that the interaction between starter cultures and fermentation
times is significant, and from 6 hours of fermentation ST has higher formate con-
centration than LB, and it increases over time while that for LB declines. Evidential
for the protocooperation of ST and LB, the formate concentration for MX is not
significantly different from ST or LB. Heat treatment at 105◦C results in a higher

the concentration of alanine, galactose, lactate and 3_hydroxyisobutyrate increases
over the fermentation time while glucose and lactose decrease. From five to eight
hours of fermentation, milk pasteurized at 99◦C contains more glucose and lactose
than milk pasteurized at 105◦C.
9.4
Conclusion
All applied methods helped to characterize the fermentation process. However, the
kinetic modeling of the acidic curve combined with a mixed linear model as well as
the interval-based ASCA approach, gave more explanatory information on bacterial
growth dynamics and metabolite composition throughout the fermentation process.
A statistical difference was detected between various combinations of starter cultures
and pasteurization methods for the duration of the lag-phase, the maximum acidifi-
cation decay, the pH value at four hours of fermentation and the estimated time to
reach a pH value of 4.5. The combination of starter cultures, pasteurization methods
and fermentation times have significant effect on both the acidic development as well
as the metabolites.

9.5 Practical use and implications
107
9.5
Practical use and implications
The production team of Chr. Hansen can use the lag-phase parameter, the acidic
value at 4 hours of fermentation as well as the estimated time of 4.5pH to determine
whether a production method has had beneficial impact on the activity of individual
batches or not. The number of viable cells increases as the acidic value at 4 hours
decreases and the duration of the lag-phase as well as the time to 4.5 hours shortens.
Chr.
Hansen aims to produce starter cultures with a uniform quality where the
quality can be defined by how quickly and uniformly a bacteria acidifies throughout
the fermentation process.
Therefore, it is not desirable that the duration of the
lag-phase parameter, the acidic value at 4 hours of fermentation or the estimated
time of 4.5pH varies for a bacterial strain depending on pasteurization methods and
replicates.
The observed metabolite differences could plausibly explain the acidic variation among
different kinds of pasteurization methods and replicates for the unique bacterial
strains and therefore be used to formulate an ideal test media.
According to Chr. Hansen, their testing procedures have given misleading results
that inferring that their starter cultures do not always meet up with their expected
quality. Consequently, it happens that a working product is thrown away even though
it may be in perfect order. This is expensive for the company. [98]. One factor causes
misleading test results is prolonged storage of milk, which increases the formate
within the initial milk and, in turn, causes starter cultures consisting of LB to look
as they acidify faster than they do. This project used an approach that was able to
detect that a higher pasteurization temperature (105◦C) increases the formate within
the initial milk, which should also lead to inappropriate results. This procedure could
be used before deciding on whether or not to use the product [98].
9.6
Future work
Although substantial information on the fermentation process of different starter cul-
tures has been discovered; there is always room for improvement.
Ideally, pH measurements would be made continuously throughout the fermentation
process, which would give more reliable results. Similarly, more replicates are prefer-
able for both datasets to increase the statistical reliability.
Additional measurements are both expensive and time consuming. Another possi-
bility for improvement would be to construct a mixed linear model on carefully
pre-processed biochemical signal areas of interest, which would allow for easily in-
terpretable results and significance testing. All of the selected signal areas could then
be analyzed together by applying using either MANOVA or ASCA analysis.

A.5.2
Mixed linear model validation
It is crucial to ensure that the final model is appropriate and captures that it the
systematic structure in the data. Therefore, it is vital to perform a model diagnostics
by investigating the obtained residuals as well as the random efffect of batches.
First, the normality assumption of the obtained residuals will be investigated. There-
fore, a traditional Q-Q plot of the residuals as well as a Q-Q plot for simulated
data, randomly drawn from a normal distribution with zero mean and the estimated
residual variance (σ = 0.013884), will be inspected for comparison, see figure A.10.
The Q-Q plot of the model residuals (left subplot) shows some deviation in the ex-
tremes. Still, in most cases, the points fall onto the straight line, which indicates
that the residuals can be considered normally distributed. In comparison, the Q-Q
plot for the simulated data (right subplot) is reasonably similar, which supports the
assumption of normally distributed residuals.

A.6
Polynomial approach pH-data
The appropriate orders of the polynomial curves were therefore selected separately
for each batch by using a modified version of a leave one out cross-validation where
observations at time 24 were always in the training set due to importance. If an
observation taken at time 24 is allowed to be in the test set, it dominates the model
selection procedure since it is deemed to result in a high test error because no ob-
servations are available between 8 and 24 hours of fermentation. The results of the
modified leave one out cross-validation can be seen in figure A.14. According to the
figure, a fourth-order polynomial can be considered relevant in all cases.
Figure A.14: Illustration of leave one out cross-validation for each batch in a gridded plot
where columns represent starter cultures and rows heat treatments. The test error is red,
whereas the training error is blue. The y-axis shows the logarithm (with a base of 10) of the
mean squared error and the x-axis shows the tested order of the polynomials. In all cases,
a selection of a fourth-order polynomial can be justified since both the test error and the
training error are low.
Therefore, all acidification profiles of all batches can be described with a fourth-order
polynomial as follows:
xt = θ0 + θ1t + θ2t2 + θ3t3 + θ4t4 + ϵt
(A.2)
where xt represents the pH value at time t. θ0, θ1 , θ2, θ3 and θ4 are the regression
coefficients of an order corresponding to their footprints and ϵt is the residual error.
