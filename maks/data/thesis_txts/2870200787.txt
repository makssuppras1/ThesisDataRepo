Abstract
The thesis investigates computational and experimental optimization of stirred
tank reactors (STR) for pharmaceutical manufacturing, addressing critical indus-
try challenges of reducing mixing times while controlling shear stress to protect
sensitive biological materials. Using a combined experimental and computational
fluid dynamics (CFD) approach, this thesis systematically explores the impact of
impeller geometry (pitch angle, diameter and height) and operational conditions
(clearance and impeller speed) on mixing efficiency and shear stress. Due to the
computational expense of CFD simulations, a surrogate response surface model
(RSM) was developed, demonstrating strong predictive capabilities.
Multi objective optimization (Pareto front), using the NSGA-II algorithm, identified
Pareto optimal configurations, revealing that single-loop flow patterns generally
offered the best balance between mixing time and shear stress. The resulting
Pareto front offers practical guidance by clearly illustrating trade-offs between mix-
ing time and shear stress, enabling easy selection of optimal STR configurations
for pharmaceutical production. Significant differences between simulated and ex-
perimental mixing times were observed, possibly due to measurement limitations
or the turbulence model’s inability to fully capture flow dynamics. Despite these
discrepancies, both approaches identified impeller height, diameter, and rotational
speed as key factors influencing mixing efficiency and shear stress.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1
Introduction
STRs are equipped with a rotating impeller used to mix fluids or suspended solids.
This mechanical agitation aims to distribute substances evenly, making STRs cen-
tral to many industrial processes. In pharmaceutical applications, a STR typically
contains liquid media and active ingredients, which must be kept well mixed to
ensure product uniformity. At the same time, the impeller’s motion creates shear
stresses that can damage sensitive materials if not carefully controlled.
A STR’s performance depends on multiple factors, and many different types of
STRs exist in the industry: Disposable tanks, steel tanks, large or small tanks, and
tanks with varying width-to-height ratios. Impellers also vary significantly: Some
are small and rotate quickly, driven by magnetic fields and generating turbulent
flow, while others are large paddles rotating slowly driven by a steel shaft. To
assist with the selection of the right STR configuration, the flow patterns within
tanks can be analysed using CFD, which uses numerical methods to simulate fluid
flows. However, CFD modelling can be costly, further increasing the expenses
involved in choosing a suitable reactor. Furthermore, certain processes require
specialized, non-standard STRs, and detailed design parameters often remain
proprietary, limiting openly available guidance.
Although the basic principle of using impellers to stir fluids is straightforward,
complexity emerges from the interaction of many factors influencing reactor per-
formance. Additionally, proprietary constraints within pharmaceutical companies
mean that comprehensive data and detailed guidelines for optimal STR configu-
rations are often limited. This thesis addresses these challenges by developing
surrogate models based on CFD simulations and experimental validation, aiming
to simplify and reduce the cost associated with selecting appropriate STR config-
urations for pharmaceutical applications.

Chapter 2. Fundamental Background
2
Fundamental Background
Mixing processes have been studied through physical experiments [1, 2, 3, 4]
and computational investigations [5, 6]. These studies provide insights into mix-
ing performance, flow characteristics, and parameter effects. Research on STRs
has examined aspects such as tank geometries and designs [2], impeller con-
figurations [3, 4], flow patterns [6], and computational optimization approaches
[5].
The following sections focus on fundamental models and key parameters relevant
to the mixing process. The aim is to provide information that supports the analysis
in this thesis, rather than an extensive review of all available literature.
2.1
Confidentiality concerns in the pharmaceutical
industry
Finding detailed scientific articles about STRs, including their dimensioning and
technical specifications in the pharmaceutical industry, can be challenging be-
cause this kind of information is often confidential. Pharmaceutical companies
often treat details about their manufacturing processes and equipment as propri-
etary to safeguard their competitive edge and protect intellectual property. Conse-
quently, much of the technical and specific data on equipment (like STRs) remains
undisclosed in public scientific literature, leading to a scarcity of openly available
detailed studies and articles on the subject.
Despite the limited availability of detailed technical specifications on STRs in pub-
lic literature, a review of publicly accessible sources has uncovered several rel-
evant articles. These studies explore recent advancements in STRs, including
impeller design, dimensioning, and positioning. The following sections highlight
articles that provide valuable insights and could serve as a foundation for further
exploration in the thesis.
2.2
Relevant tank geometries and designs
Doran et al.
[2] authored the book Bioprocess Engineering Principles, which
explores chemical engineering, and has a chapter about mixing equipment and
its various applications. They first handle tanks, which are typically cylindrically
shaped, where the base has rounded edges to prevent fluid currents to penetrate
and avoid formation of stagnant regions. This will result in non-homogenous con-
centration of the media. To ensure easy cleaning, the tank is often made from
stainless steel, so rust does not contaminate the media.
Some tanks are designed for single-use applications, eliminating the need for
cleaning. These disposable systems often incorporate plastic bags equipped with
magnetically driven impellers, which operate without a central axle. For large-
scale, high-purity manufacturing, stainless steel tanks remain the preferred choice

In cylindrical tanks, baffles minimize the amount of vortexing and swirling, which

2.3
Impeller design
Impeller designs can be categorized in many different ways, when reading chem-
ical handbooks and scientific articles from around the world. In this thesis, the
categorization of impellers will follow the classification system used by Gogate et
al. [3], which distinguishes impellers into two types: Turbine and paddle impellers,
each suited for different flow regimes.
Besides classifying impellers into the different regimes laminar and turbulent flow,
others classify impellers according to the flow direction: Radial or axial [4]. This
means that the impellers can either pump the media out towards the wall of the
tank, or parallel to the axle that drives the impeller.
Turbine impellers are widely used to achieve strong mixing and high shear in low
to medium viscosity fluids. In an axial configuration, turbine impellers, such as
pitched blade turbines, produce a combination of axial and radial flows that ensure
efficient flow and moderate shear. When a radial configuration is used, the flow
is primarily directed radially, which generates higher shear and turbulence [3, 4].

2.5
Flow categories in STRs
The classification of flow patterns in STRs is essential for understanding and op-
timizing mixing performance. Zhu et al. [6] conducted a CFD study examining
the transition between double- and single-loop flow patterns in a fully baffled tank
stirred by a Rushton turbine. Their findings highlight how impeller design and
placement significantly influence the development of these flow patterns.
The study demonstrates that the formation of either a single-loop or double-loop
flow pattern is primarily dictated by the impeller’s clearance from the tank bottom.
When the impeller discharge stream initially contacts the tank wall, a double-loop
flow pattern is established, characterized by two circulating loops—one above
and one below the impeller. Conversely, when the discharge stream reaches the
tank bottom first, a single-loop flow pattern emerges, where the entire bulk fluid
circulates in one dominant loop. This classification is illustrated in Figure 2.4.

that pharmaceutical companies prioritize both optimal mixing times and controlled
shear conditions, further investigations integrating shear stress analysis with flow
pattern transitions would provide valuable insights into optimizing STR perfor-
mance in bioprocessing applications.

Chapter 3. Statement of Intent
3
Statement of Intent
This thesis investigates the optimization of flow and mixing processes in STRs, fo-
cusing on pharmaceutical applications. The pharmaceutical industry often faces
challenges with batch mixing, including non-homogeneity and protein denatura-
tion, both of which can lead to inconsistent product quality and economic losses.
Ensuring uniform mixing while minimizing shear stress is therefore essential to
preserve the integrity of sensitive biological products.
To address these issues, this work aims to identify STR configurations that reduce
mixing time and shear stresses, providing effective mixing without compromising
product. Various impeller geometries and operating conditions are explored to de-
termine their impact on overall mixing efficiency. Since computational simulations
can be time-consuming and costly, a surrogate model is proposed to enable easy
evaluation of multiple design parameters. Using data collected across systemati-
cally varied operational conditions, the surrogate model ensures a more efficient
identification of optimal STR setups.

Chapter 4. General Approach
4
General Approach
This chapter provides a framework describing how the thesis is structured and
introduces the design of both experimental and numerical approaches. By the
end of this chapter, the reader will understand the modus operandi for the entire
thesis.
To determine how the configuration and positioning of the impeller design influ-
ence mixing time and shear stress, it is essential to select appropriate factors and
number levels. The number of levels is very important to determine, as this al-
lows for better detection of non-linearity, and ensures the model will fit with good
precision, depending on the resolution that is within the timeframe.
To build the model, data has to be collected by running simulations using CFD soft-
ware. These simulations must be validated through physical experiments, as ac-
curately simulating the physics involved is challenging due to the extensive range
of influencing factors. The data must first be processed to eliminate noise from
the measurements, allowing for the extraction of key metrics such as shear stress
and mixing time. Once this is complete, the simulations can then be validated.
Additionally, the flow patterns will be qualitatively categorized.
After identifying the mixing time and shear stress, the subsequent step involves
selecting an appropriate surrogate model. A list of potential models will be eval-
uated based on how well the models fits the data and the average magnitude
of their prediction errors, among other relevant metrics. Lastly, there will be re-
flected on the previous chapters, to see what could have been done differently
and possibly provide a better fitting model.

Design of Experiments
5.1
Introduction to DOE
DOE is a systematic approach for planning and conducting experiments to obtain
valid insights about an underlying system. Originating from the work of Fisher [7]
and further refined by Montgomery [8], DOE involves both the selection of factor
level combinations to be tested and the analysis of the resulting data.
For example, consider a simple experiment that investigates the effect of daily
water amount and hours of sunlight on plant growth. In this experiment, there are
two factors, each tested at two levels: Daily water amount is provided at either 100
mL per day or 200 mL per day, and hours of sunlight are provided at either 4 hours
per day or 8 hours per day. Combining these two factors and their levels results in
four unique experimental conditions: (100 mL, 4 hours), (100 mL, 8 hours), (200
mL, 4 hours), and (200 mL, 8 hours).
Figure 5.1: A two factor, two level design space with configurations at the corners.
Figure 5.1 illustrates the traditional representation of the design space. In this
diagram, the two continuous factors are plotted along the horizontal and vertical
axes, and the four experimental conditions are shown as points at the corners of
the rectangular space. This visualization clearly depicts the discrete combinations
of factor levels used in the experiment.
DOEs must be carefully designed to ensure meaningful and reliable results. Even
a simple DOE, like the one shown in Figure 5.1, requires thoughtful planning to

balancing three key elements:
• The desired information: Information the experiment is designed to extract
• The experiment/response characteristics: Practical constraints and how

• The analytic method: The statistical or modelling approach used to analyse

Ideally, these three considerations align to create an optimal DOE. However, in
real experiments, trade-offs are often necessary due to limitations in resources,
measurement feasibility, or analytical complexity. This relationship is illustrated in
Figure 5.2.

In the plant example, the goal is to understand how different levels of water and
sunlight affect growth. Designing an effective experiment requires considering the
three key elements: The desired information, which refers to how plant growth is
influenced by water and sunlight; the experiment/response characteristics, includ-
ing practical factors such as measurement accuracy and maintaining consistent
watering and light exposure; and the analytic method, which in this case is a sim-
ple DOE with two factors at two levels, resulting in four conditions. Ideally, these
elements work together to ensure meaningful results. In practice, adjustments
may be needed, such as accounting for water evaporation when setting watering
levels or modifying how growth is measured to match the analysis method.
When selecting a DOE model, it should be chosen based on a balanced evaluation
of the number of factors, available resources, and the required level of detail in
the analysis. One of the most fundamental approaches is a full factorial design
Modelling Flow and Mixing in STRs
13
==PAGE:22==
Chapter 5. Design of Experiments
[10], where all possible combinations of factor levels are tested. If a DOE has
k factors, each tested at n levels, a full factorial design requires nk experiments.
This guarantees that all main effects and interactions can be analysed. However,
as the number of factors increases, the number of required experiments grows
exponentially, making full factorial designs expensive for complex systems.
To address the exponential growth in the number of experiments required for full
factorial designs, fractional factorial designs offer a more efficient alternative [10].
Instead of testing all possible factor level combinations, only a selected subset
is used while still capturing the most important effects. This method is particu-
larly useful when the number of factors increases, making full factorial designs
expensive.
A fractional factorial design is defined as a fraction of the full factorial:
Nfractional = Nfull
2p
(5.1)
Where Nfull is the total number of runs in a full factorial design, and p represents the
fractioning level, which determines how many fewer experiments are conducted
compared to the full design. The term 2p represents the reduction factor, meaning
that a half fraction (p = 1) uses half of the runs, a quarter fraction (p = 2) uses one
quarter of the runs, and so on.
Reducing the number of runs introduces aliasing, where some effects become
indistinguishable. The resolution of a fractional factorial design describes the de-
gree of confounding and follows these general principles:
• Resolution III: Main effects are distinguishable, but two factor interactions
may be aliased with each other.
• Resolution IV: Main effects are distinguishable, and two factor interactions
are separated from each other, but higher-order interactions are aliased.
• Resolution V: Main effects and two factor interactions are distinguishable,
but three factor interactions are aliased.
The resolution of a design depends on the number of factors k and the level of the
fraction p. As p is increased, so will the aliasing, making it harder to distinguish
between main effects and interactions. Lower resolution designs often result in
main effects being aliased with interactions, while higher resolution designs allow
for clearer separation of effects, reducing uncertainty in the analysis.
For systems where factorial designs require an impractical number of runs, space
filling designs offer an alternative approach by ensuring that experimental points
are well distributed across the design space [11]. Unlike factorial methods, which
focus on specific factor level combinations, space filling designs aim to cover the
entire factor space to capture complex relationships and nonlinear effects.
These designs are particularly useful when the response is highly nonlinear and
difficult to approximate with a limited number of points, and when factors are con-
tinuous, where increasing factorial levels leads to excessive experimental runs.

While fractional factorial and space filling designs improve efficiency compared to
full factorial designs, they do not necessarily maximize the statistical information
gained per experiment. Optimal experimental designs aim to select the most in-
formative experimental points while minimizing the number of runs, ensuring that
the collected data provides the best possible parameter estimates [12]. Com-
pared to non-optimal designs, they achieve the same level of precision with fewer
experimental runs, thereby reducing the overall cost of experimentation.
Optimal designs are particularly useful when the number of possible experimen-
tal conditions is too large to test completely, or when the goal is to estimate a
structured model efficiently rather than broadly exploring the factor space. By
strategically selecting experimental points, these designs minimize redundancy
while maximizing information gain.

Factor selection
To apply one of the DOE models described in the previous section, a set of STR
design parameters must be selected as factors. The design of the impeller is
essential to the system, as it directly affects shear stress and mixing time [2].
Because of its significance, this thesis focuses on impeller related parameters to
better understand their impact on mixing performance.
Additionally, practical constraints influenced the choice of factors. Only one tank
configuration was available at DTU, meaning parameters related to the tank itself,
such as diameter and shape, could not be varied. However, impeller related pa-
rameters were easily adjustable, as all impellers would be 3D printed using fused
filament fabrication (FFF). This flexibility made impeller modifications both feasible
and practical for experimentation.

• Impeller pitch angle
• Clearance
• Impeller diameter
• Impeller height
• Impeller speed

5.3
Sampling Strategy
A two level design would allow for the estimation of linear effects and two-factor
interactions, but it would fail to detect nonlinear relationships that may exist in
the system. Hörmann et al. [5] used RSM [10] to model mixing homogeneity and
shear stress in pharmaceutical mixing processes and found that a quadratic model
provided a good fit. This suggests that capturing curvature effects is essential for
accurately modelling the system’s behaviour. Therefore, at least three levels per
factor are necessary to account for potential nonlinearity.
However, incorporating three levels per factor significantly increases the number
of required experiments. A full factorial design with five factors at three levels
Modelling Flow and Mixing in STRs
17
==PAGE:26==
Chapter 5. Design of Experiments
results in 243 experiments (35 = 243), making it computationally infeasible. As
later observed, a single CFD simulation takes approximately 24 hours to complete.
While three simulations could be run in parallel, completing all 243 experiments
would still require approximately 81 days of continuous computation. Given these
constraints, alternative approaches were considered.
A fractional factorial design could reduce the number of required runs by selecting
a subset of the full factorial design. While this approach is effective for screen-
ing significant factors, it assumes that higher-order interactions are negligible. A
three level fractional factorial design does allow for some detection of curvature
by introducing an intermediate level for each factor. However, it does not system-
atically estimate quadratic effects, as these terms can still be aliased with other
interactions.
Alternatively, space filling designs aim to distribute points evenly across the entire
design space, rather than focusing on predefined levels. These designs are par-
ticularly useful for highly nonlinear responses or when there is no prior knowledge
of the system’s behaviour. However, as mentioned earlier, Hörmann et al. [5]
used RSM to model pharmaceutical mixing processes and found that a quadratic
model provided a good fit. Since this suggests that the system follows a known
structure, a space filling approach is not optimal in this case. Instead, an optimal
design can be chosen to focus experimental runs on the most informative points,
maximizing efficiency while maintaining model accuracy.
Knowing this, the number of required simulations can be significantly reduced,
making the experiment feasible within the timeframe of this thesis while still en-
suring accurate parameter estimation.

CFD Analysis
CFD simulations are valuable for understanding and optimizing pharmaceutical
mixing processes. Due to resource limitations, only a limited number of exper-
imental conditions can be practically tested. CFD allows a broader exploration
of STR configurations, providing deeper insights into fluid dynamics than can be
achieved through experiments alone. The primary advantages of CFD simulations
include:
• Increased number of probes: Unlike the experimental setup, where the
probe’s non-waterproof wiring restricted its submersion depth and left certain
areas such as the bottom corners untested, CFD allows for multiple virtual
probes. In this simulation, 12 virtual probes are placed, with one in each
corner and two in the middle of the tank’s lower and upper regions. The
placement of these probes is illustrated in Figure 7.1.
Figure 7.1: Location of the 12 virtual probes within the CFD simulation.
• Shear Stress: CFD simulations enable the calculation of shear stress within
the STR, allowing evaluation of the risk of protein denaturation, an aspect
that is difficult to measure experimentally yet highly significant for the phar-
maceutical industry.
• Flow pattern visualization: Flow patterns can be difficult to observe ex-
perimentally. CFD, however, provides opportunities to use detailed vector
plots, streamlines, and velocity fields. This makes it possible to analyse how
different impeller configurations may affect mixing performance.

7.1
Overview of the Simulation Approach
The following steps were taken to develop the CFD simulation in Star-CCM+ [15].
• Geometry definition: The computational domain consists of two separate
computer-aided design (CAD) models: A rectangular tank and an impeller-
shaft assembly responsible for fluid motion.
• Regions and boundary conditions: The simulation domain is divided into
two regions: A rotating region for the combined impeller-shaft and a station-
ary region for the tank. A defined contact interface between these regions
facilitates the transfer of motion. Boundary conditions are applied to the
tank, including a mass flow inlet and a pressure outlet, ensuring accurate
representation of tracer introduction.
• Meshing strategy: A structured hexahedral mesh was used to achieve ac-
curate flow resolution and computational efficiency. Prism layers were in-
cluded near walls to capture boundary layer effects, and mesh refinement
was applied around the impeller to improve local flow accuracy. Addition-
ally, an adaptive mesh was created to dynamically refine regions with high
gradients, optimizing resolution where needed.
• Physics models and solver settings: A multiphase Volume of Fluid (VOF)
model was used to capture liquid-gas interactions, as the top part of the tank
contains air. The flow was modelled using the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-
Stokes (RANS) equations with the Realizable k-ε turbulence model and a
two-layer all-y+ wall treatment for accurate near-wall resolution. The sim-
ulation employed an implicit unsteady solver with an adaptive time-step to
ensure numerical stability while efficiently resolving transient flow behaviour.
• Automation and parameterization: All five factors described in Table 5.1
were parameterized to systematically explore the design space as part of
the DOE study. Custom field functions were implemented to define dynamic
behaviours, including impeller acceleration, shear stress calculation, and
tracer addition, ensuring automated control over key process variables.
7.2
Geometry and Domain Definition
7.2.1
CAD model and geometry import
The computational domain is constructed using two CAD models, both created
within Star-CCM+:
• The tank model represents the stationary rectangular tank that serves as
the STR.
• The impeller-shaft model combines the impeller and shaft to induce flow
within the tank. This model features an adjustable parameter interface for
controlled variations in impeller pitch angle, clearance, diameter, and height.
Prior to importing these CAD models into Star-CCM+, preprocessing steps were

7.2.2
Domain setup and simplifications
The computational domain is divided into two distinct regions: A stationary tank
region and a rotating impeller region. These regions are connected via a contact
interface, allowing momentum transfer between them. The impeller rotation is
simulated using the Sliding Mesh method, where the impeller region physically
rotates at each time step, while the tank region remains stationary. This approach
explicitly captures transient rotational phenomena, providing accurate predictions
of mixing time and shear stress distributions.

The computational domain was discretized using a structured hexahedral mesh.
Prism layers were applied near walls to accurately resolve boundary layers and
improve the prediction of near-wall velocity gradients and shear stresses. Volu-
metric mesh refinement was used around the impeller to capture regions of high
shear stress and turbulence. Additionally, adaptive mesh refinement dynamically
adjusted cell resolution based on local flow gradients, optimizing computational
efficiency and accuracy in critical regions.

Physical models and conservation equations
The fluid flow in the STR is described by the fundamental conservation laws of
mass and momentum, expressed through the Navier-Stokes equations under the
assumptions of an incompressible, Newtonian fluid. These equations include:

Continuity equation (mass conservation)
For incompressible flow, the conservation of mass is expressed as:
∇· u = 0
(7.1)
where u is the velocity vector.
7.4.2
Momentum conservation (Navier-Stokes equations)
The motion of the fluid is described by the incompressible Navier-Stokes equa-

∂u
∂t + (u · ∇)u = −1

(7.2)
where ρ is the density, p is the pressure, ν is the kinematic viscosity, and F repre-
sents external forces, which in this case is the motion induced by the impeller.
7.4.3
Turbulence Modeling
Turbulence is modelled using the RANS approach with the Realizable k-ε tur-
bulence model, which offers improved accuracy for swirling and rotating flows,

both of which provide higher accuracy in resolving turbulence. However, their sig-
nificantly higher computational cost makes them less practical for the timeframe of
this thesis. To balance accuracy and efficiency, the RANS approach was chosen.
7.4.4
Multiphase Flow (VOF Model)
A VOF model is employed to track the interface between water and air. The VOF
method assigns a volume fraction (α) to each phase, ensuring that:
αwater + αair = 1
(7.3)
This approach accurately captures the free surface dynamics by solving a single
set of momentum equations while distinguishing between phases based on their
respective volume fractions.
7.4.5
Tracer Transport (Convection-Diffusion Model)
The introduced tracer mixture (H2O-NaCl) is governed by the advection-diffusion
equation, which accounts for both convective transport by the flow and molecular
diffusion:
∂C
∂t + u · ∇C = D∇2C
(7.4)
where C represents the local concentration of the tracer, and D is the molecular
diffusion coefficient.
This equation describes how the concentration of the tracer evolves within the fluid
inside the STR. The left side of the equation accounts for the change in tracer con-
centration over time, influenced by both the passage of time and the movement of
the tracer with the fluid flow. The right side of the equation details how the tracer
diffuses, moving from areas of higher concentration to areas of lower concentra-
tion, a process that occurs regardless of fluid flow. The balance between these
two sides allows for the prediction of the distribution of tracer concentration at any
point within the STR.

7.4.7
Assumptions and Approximations
Certain physical phenomena are difficult or impractical to model in the CFD sim-
ulations, requiring assumptions and approximations to simplify the analysis.
• Incompressible flow: The fluid is treated as incompressible since density
changes due to pressure variations are negligible in the mixing process.
• Rigid impeller and shaft: The impeller and shaft are assumed to be perfectly
rigid, meaning no deformation or vibration effects are considered.
• Impeller surface texture: The rough texture from FFF 3D printing is approx-

7.5
Boundary and Initial Conditions
Before running the CFD simulation, appropriate boundary and initial conditions
must be defined to accurately represent the STR. Boundary conditions establish
how the fluid interacts with its surroundings, while initial conditions specify the
starting state of the system.
7.5.1
Boundary Conditions
A mass flow inlet is used for tracer addition, while a pressure outlet allows air
to exit, maintaining mass balance. No-slip walls are applied to the impeller, tank
bottom, and side walls, ensuring zero velocity at these surfaces. The top boundary
is set as a slip wall, allowing free tangential motion of the air without introducing
shear forces.
7.5.2
Initial Conditions
At the start of the simulation, the tank is partially filled with water to a height of 350
mm, while the remaining volume is occupied by air, forming a two-phase system.
This is modelled using the VOF, as described earlier.
The tracer mixture (H2O-NaCl) is introduced at a mass flow rate of 0.135 kg
s for
a duration of 3 seconds. This equates to introducing 400mL of water with 5g of
salt into the STR. The fluid is initially at rest, with the velocity field set to zero
throughout the domain and an initial pressure of 0.0Pa relative to the reference
pressure.
The impeller starts from rest and undergoes gradual acceleration according to a
predefined field function, ensuring a smooth transition into rotational motion. The
acceleration function describing this behaviour is detailed in the Appendix H.

enables the use of larger time steps compared to explicit methods while preserv-

cell per time step.

Data Processing
This chapter explains how the CFD simulation data is processed to extract the re-
sponse variables, including mixing time, shear stress, and flow patterns. The raw
data from Star-CCM+ contains numerical noise, which are reduced using filtering
and post-processing techniques.
8.1
Noise in mixing time measurements
Mixing time is determined based on concentration measurements obtained from
probe data. However, noise in these measurements primarily originates from nu-
merical fluctuations in the CFD solution.
Discretization errors, solver settings, and turbulence modelling (e.g., the k-ϵ tur-
bulence model) create fluctuations in concentration predictions. Additionally, the
VOF-model introduces numerical diffusion near phase interfaces, affecting con-
centration gradients.
To mitigate noise in mixing time calculations, the following strategies are imple-
mented:
• Mesh refinement: A finer mesh near high-gradient regions improves con-
centration resolution and reduces numerical diffusion.
• Adaptive time step control: The time step is dynamically adjusted based
on the CFL number to ensure numerical stability while minimizing artificial
dissipation.
• Volume-averaged probe considerations: Since concentration is measured
using volume-averaged probes, the choice of probe size affects data ac-
curacy. Larger probes smooth fluctuations but may obscure local concen-
tration variations, while smaller probes retain more detail but still introduce
some averaging effects.
• Moving average filtering: A moving average is applied to the concentration
data to smooth out high-frequency fluctuations. Figure 8.1 illustrates this ef-
fect by comparing raw data with the smoothed data using a moving average
window. The corresponding Python code is provided in Appendix C.

8.2
Noise in shear stress measurements
Shear stress calculations are sensitive to both numerical noise and transient startup
effects. Discretization errors and turbulence modelling lead to small scale fluctu-
ations in shear stress predictions. Additionally, when the impeller is ramped up
to operating speed, the flow field undergoes transient oscillations before reaching
steady state conditions.
To ensure that shear stress values reflect steady state conditions, the following
processing methods are applied:
• Steady-State selection: Only the last 50% of the simulation data is used
for shear stress calculations to ensure transient effects have dissipated.
• Outlier removal: Outliers in the last 50% of the data are identified and
removed using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method, where values outside
[Q1 −1.5 × IQR, Q3 + 1.5 × IQR] are excluded.
• Time averaging: A time averaging approach is used over the steady state
data to reduce high frequency numerical noise.

Surrogate Model
9.1
Overview
A surrogate model is a computational approximation used to predict system be-
haviour based on a set of precomputed simulations or physical experiments. In-
stead of running costly simulations for every parameter combination, it provides a
mathematical representation of input-output relationships.
Some models rely on structured sampling methods [10, 16, 17], while others learn
patterns from general datasets without a predefined sampling strategy [18, 19, 20,
21, 22].
9.2
Model Selection
RSM was selected as the primary modelling approach based on findings from Hör-
mann et al. [5], as discussed in chapter 5. They demonstrated that a quadratic
model provided a good fit for mixing homogeneity and shear stress for a single
impeller STR in pharmaceutical mixing processes. This indicated that captur-
ing curvature effects is essential for accurately modelling the system’s behaviour.
Consequently, the experimental design included at least three levels per factor to
account for potential nonlinearity.
While RSM serves as the foundation of the predictive framework, it is still valuable
to explore alternative surrogate models to assess if they offer improved accuracy.
To investigate this, a model screening analysis was conducted in JMP [13], eval-
uating various machine learning and regression-based models. The goal was not
necessarily to replace RSM, but to compare its performance against other mod-
elling techniques and determine whether certain methods might enhance predic-
tion quality or better capture complex interactions.
The models considered are listed below:
• Machine learning models
– Gradient-boosted trees [18]
– Bootstrap forest [19]
– K-nearest neighbors [20]
– Boosted neural network [21]
– Support vector machines [22]
• Regression-based models
– Linear regression (Fit Least Squares) [16]
– Stepwise regression (Fit Stepwise, backward selection) [17]
– Generalized regression (Lasso) [23]

9.3.1
Optimization of the surrogate model
The model handles multiple responses, including shear stress and mixing time,
and supports various optimization methods such as constrained optimization, the
desirability function, and Pareto front optimization.
The constrained method, as the name implies, requires setting constraints on
the responses. This means the model can only be optimized if, for example, the
shear stress is restricted to a specific threshold. However, this thesis explores
how processes can be optimized for a wider range of applications with varying
thresholds.
The desirability function, on the other hand, requires the user to assign weights to
different responses. In this model, the weight is defined within the interval [0, 1],
where 0 represents a completely undesirable response and 1 indicates an ideal
response. Since this weighting is subjective and may vary between applications,
this option was not chosen either.
Choosing the Pareto front does not require weighting or prioritizing objectives, as
it presents all Pareto optimal outcomes from the model. It also provides a clear
visual representation, allowing for an overview of the trade-offs between differ-
ent configurations. One potential drawback is that the large number of possible
solutions can be overwhelming, making it difficult to select a single optimal STR
configuration. However, the goal here is not to choose a single configuration, but
to identify a set of desirable configurations. This broader perspective is partic-
ularly useful when optimizing mixing processes for different applications, where
shear stress thresholds and mixing time constraints can vary significantly.
The Non-Dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) was used to generate
the Pareto front, because it efficiently finds a diverse set of optimal solutions,
while balancing exploration and convergence. It is widely used in multi-objective
optimization due to its ability to handle complex, nonlinear relationships between
counteracting objectives [27].
NSGA-II mimics natural selection to identify trade-offs between conflicting objec-
tives. It starts with an initial population, and evolves solutions over multiple gen-
erations. At each step, solutions are ranked using Pareto dominance, where a
solution is better, if it improves one objective without worsening the other. To
maintain diversity, crowding distance ensures an even spread along the Pareto
front. Over time, the population converges to a well distributed set of optimal
solutions. The Python code for NSGA-II optimization and visualizing the Pareto
front progression, simulation comparison, and knee point detection is provided in
Appendix G.

disproportionately large sacrifices in the other. This makes it a strong candidate
for selection when there is no clear preference between the objectives. Deb and
Gupta [28] introduced a curvature based approach to identify the knee point, as it
typically exhibits the highest change in slope along the Pareto front.

Chapter 11. Conclusion
11
Conclusion
The thesis investigated the optimization of flow and mixing processes in STRs for
pharmaceutical applications, specifically targeting reduced mixing times and mini-
mized shear stress. Through CFD simulations validated by physical experiments,
this thesis explored the influence of five impeller related parameters: Pitch angle,
clearance, diameter, height, and rotational speed.
A comparison of the simulated and experimental mixing times revealed signifi-
cant differences. The simulations were generally longer than the experiments.
The probe’s slow sampling rate may have caused shorter measured times, while
the RANS-based CFD approach could introduce numerical diffusion that extends
predicted mixing times. More advanced turbulence models (e.g., LES or DES) or
improved measurement methods might narrow this gap. Despite these discrep-
ancies, both data sources indicated that impeller height, diameter, and rotational
speed strongly influence mixing efficiency and shear stress distribution.
A surrogate model, based on the Response Surface Method, demonstrated its
effectiveness by capturing complex, nonlinear relationships between design pa-
rameters and the responses. The surrogate modelling facilitated a more efficient
exploration of the parameter compared to direct CFD simulations alone.
By creating a Pareto front, obtained from multi-objective optimization using the
Non-Dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II), the trade-off between mix-
ing time and shear stress was highlighted. Optimal configurations favoured single-
loop flow patterns, suggesting this flow category as advantageous for minimizing
both objectives simultaneously.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates a structured approach to optimizing STR
configurations, emphasizing the importance of balancing mixing efficiency with
shear sensitivity, essential for pharmaceutical manufacturing processes.

Chapter 12. Further Work
12
Further Work
To continue developing and refining the model, the following three improvements
are recommended:
1. Better experimental setup: Increase the number of probes in the experi-
mental setup and use high precision probes with better sampling rates, de-
signed for continuous measurement, to ensure more reliable data and re-
duce uncertainties when validating mixing times.
2. More advanced turbulence modelling: Consider using more computation-
ally demanding approaches like LES or DES. While these methods require
significantly more computational resources and time, they can better capture
the complex behaviour of turbulence.
3. Flexible surrogate modelling: Avoid imposing a predefined structure by
using space-filling experimental designs for broader coverage of the param-
eter space. With a larger, more representative dataset, train and validate
a neural network model, which can learn complex, nonlinear relationships
without the constraints of a traditional RSM.
By addressing these three areas, future studies can significantly improve the pre-
dictive accuracy of their surrogate model and strengthen confidence in their vali-
dation experiments.
