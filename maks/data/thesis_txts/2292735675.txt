RESUME 
Efterspørgelsen på udvikling af et termisk kalkulations værktøj fra DTU byggeri 
og anlæg har ført til udviklingen af TCD-Plus (Thermal Calculation Design). 
Efterspørgelsen på programmet er både grundet udannelsesmæssige årsager, 
samt at solindfaldet progressiv bliver et mere og mere vigtig design parameter 
for bygninger. Programmets udgangspunkt var værktøjet TCD, som er et simpelt 
Excel baseret værktøj med få input. Det var et ønske, fra udviklers side, at det nye 
program skal gøre brug af solar algoritme i stedet for Danvaks solkurve, blev 
TCD-Plus programmeret forfra. Yderlige var der et ønske om at programmet 
skulle have flere muligheder for input, som for eksempel: transmission tab, 
forskellige ventilations muligheder, solafskærmning, samt at kunne gøre brug af 
termisk masses etc.  
Programmet er primært baseret på En13790, En13986, DS418 samt ”best 
practice” inden for beregning af solvarmetilskud. Solvarmetilskuddet er beregnet 
på baggrund af: sol algoritmer, direkte og diffuse solstråling baseret på et 
anisotropisk himmelegne, reflekteret solstråling baseret på en uniform himmel, 
vinkelafhængigheds faktorer for sol transmittansen, samt time beregninger as 
skygge faktorer. 
TCD-Plus blev sammenlignet på daglig og årlig basis, mod det professionelle 
bygnings performance værktøj BSim. Generelt blev det fundet at TCD-Plus har 
gode estimering af det termiske indeklima på daglig basis, mens større afvigelser 
kan forekomme på de årlige simuleringer. Afvigelserne skyldes hovedsagligt: 
- 
Forskel på transmission tab 
- 
Forskel i tids step af beregningerne  
- 
Reaktionstid for systemerne 
- 
Forskel på den termiske masse 
- 
Ingen brug af hysterese til vurdering af over temperaturer 
TCD-plus er vurderet som et godt værktøj i den indledende design fase af 
bygninger, til at vurdere det termiske indeklima, og hvordan systemerne vil 
reagere.

ABSTRACT 
A desire for developing the Thermal Calculation Design (TCD) program in order 
to estimate the solar gain in buildings is sought by the department of civil 
engineering at DTU. The request for a more flexible tool with several input 
parameters and solar gain calculation based on solar algorithms, led to the 
redesign of the TCD program. The demand is primarily due for educational 
purpose and the fact that solar gain progressively represents a more important 
design parameter in buildings.   
As the desired parameters for the program is far from the old TCD tool, the new 
program was programmed from scratch. The new program is called TCD-Plus 
and is able to determine the indoor thermal climate and related energy 
consumption based on a vast number of inputs such as; transmission loss from 
building envelope, venting, ventilation, night ventilation, cooling system, heating 
system, thermal mass and up to four different types of windows with shading. 
The Program is primarily based on EN13790, EN13986, DS418, solar algorithms, 
angle correction factor for the solar gain etc. The solar algorithms used are based 
on an anisotropic sky for direct and diffuse radiation, while the direct radiation is 
based on an isotropic sky. 
TCD-Plus was investigated against the professional program BSim, on a yearly 
and hourly basis. It was in general found that TCD-Plus were accurate when 
looking at the thermal condition and the systems tendencies at a daily basis, 
while the yearly estimation shows a bit larger deviation on parameters such as: 
- 
transmission loss 
- 
venting and ventilation loss 
- 
heating and cooling demand 
The main reasons for the deviation were found to be: 
- 
differences in definition of transmission loss 
- 
time steps and reaction time 
- 
difference in definition of thermal mass 
- 
no use of hysteresis 
TCD-Plus is a good tool for when investigating daily case of the indoor thermal 
environment and how the systems performs, in preliminary design faces.

CHAPTER 1 
INTRODUCTION 
The main focus of this thesis is to develop the TCD (Thermal Calculation Design) 
program, made in 2009 by (H. Ellingsgaard and A. Kastbjerg, 2009). The original 
TCD Program was developed in order to estimate the solar gain in buildings, as 
this parameter progressively represents a more important design parameter in 
building design.  Earlier the estimation of thermal design was done by hand 
calculations or advanced simulation tools. With the availability of TCD a fast 
calculation was provided in excel, based on sun curves from Danvak as more fast 
estimation methods was requested.  
The request for a more flexible model with input parameters and solar gain 
calculation based on solar algorithms, has led to the redesign of the TCD.  
SCOPE 
The scope is to rethink the design of the TCD program, as the program neglects a 
lot of parameters such as thermal mass, different systems and solar radiation 
based on location and weather data. The TCD-Plus program will therefore 
especially focus on solar algorithms to calculate the solar geometry and radiation 
on the external surface, as well as the energy transmitted through the glassed 
areas of the room. 
Additionally the solar energy radiated into the room will be combined with the 
European (EN13790, 2008). The program is based on the hourly calculation of 
both the solar radiation and the thermal indoor environment. 
In addition to the program excel sheets for calculation of the thermal 
transmission and the thermal mass is developed, as these input parameters 
should be known to make use of the program. The Excel sheet, which is omitted 
the report and are found on the DVD placed at the back cover. 
At last it was desired to be able to find a Reference Design Day for different 
building designs. The purpose of the Reference Design Day is to identify a certain 
day for the applicable design, where the design obtains the requirements for 
excessive temperatures above 26°C and 27°C. This is important as student often 
tends to look at the yearly estimation, thus not knowing how the system is 
functioning on nearly critical situations. The Design Reference Day can be found 
be and extra excel sheet. The excel use to find the design reference day is omitted 
the report and are found on the DVD placed at the back cover.

BACKGROUND 
The background for the TCD-Plus is primarily for educational purpose. Under the 
education on DTU several program is used by the students, such as Be10, EIS-VE, 
IDA-ICE, BSim, TCD, WinDesign and IDbuild. Common for all the programs is that 
the programs either are very simple with a good description of calculations 
methods or very detailed programs with pure description of how the program do 
the estimation. 
The TCD-Plus program is developed as an advanced excel sheet, making it 
possible for the students to track the calculations, custom design of the program 
or developing the needed input and output. To give the students an idea of which 
algorithms and standards that can be used for building simulations, the program 
is build up such that the top rows of the sheet shows the originate of the 
formulas. The built up makes it easy for students to gain knowledge about 
building performance simulations.

STANDARDS USED 
As part of the solar gain estimation the sun position is determined by algorithms 
found in (Beckman, J.A. Duffie, W.A., 20013). The algorithm clarifies the solar 
geometry. The solar radiation is determined by formulas in (Munier, 2004)and 
(Beckman, J.A. Duffie, W.A., 20013).  Three different diffuse radiation models are 
tested as well as different beam radiation methods in accordance to ratio of beam 
factors and incidence angle for the solar ray. The diffuse radiation is assumed to 
be isotropic, as this tends to be the case even for the advance simulation tools. 
Shading factors is determined by the hourly method described in (EN13790, 
2008) appendices G. The recommended hourly approach is used in almost every 
literature found. Three active solar shading is modelled by a shading coefficient 
factor and a max threshold for the allowed solar gain. The method is not found in 
the literature but is based on simple excel formulas and geometrical 
relationships. 
Calculation of thermal mass is done in accordance to (EN13786, 2008). The 
thermal mass estimate cannot be entered in TCD-Plus but are chosen on a scale 
from very light to very heavy mass in accordance to EN13790. In the program it 
will however be possible to override the simple input by using the calculation 
sheets if needed. 
The heat loss for buildings is determined by (DS418, 2002)The transmission 
losses and thermal bridges can be entered for opaque building elements, 
windows and joints. 
The thermal indoor environment and related energy demand is estimated by 
(EN13790, 2008). The calculation methods used are the hourly calculation 
described in the standard. 
Evaluation of the indoor environment and energy demand is done in accordance 
to (EN15251, 2007) and (BR10). The air quality is based on estimation in 
(EN15251, 2007). The hours with excessive temperatures above 26°C and 27°C 
should be evaluated by (BR10). No environmental factors are used to estimate 
the energy consumption, meaning that the energy demand calculated is system 
energy. 
The VBA and macros are used to minor program size and stop the spread sheet 
for being active. It is believed that the smaller file size makes the program easier 
to share and the none-active sheet faster to use. The knowledge of macro 
programming is obtained by Youtube tutorials during the master thesis.

BUILDING SIMULATIONS PROGRAMS 
Numerous of simulations programs from the different engineering fields, as well 
as individual consultants programs or excel sheet is currently found on the 
market. These programs all have their pros and cons, from the advanced dynamic 
simulation programs such as EISVE, BSIM and IDA ICE to the more simple 
programs and excel sheets such as Be10, IDbuild and WINdesign.

investigation yield that all participating firms asked used Be10, while the more 
advanced building performance programs BSim and IES-VE, was used of 
approximately 50% of the time. The investigation showed that a large shared 
used hand calculation or other method like own designed excels sheets.

DYNAMIC VS. STEADY STATE CALCULATIONS 
The investigation made by (Schiønning, Dethlefsen, 2013) differentiates between 
simple steady state calculations and advanced dynamic simulation. The 
methodologies are capable of investigating parameters to predict indoor 
environment and energy consumption. Both methodologies is strongly 
depending on assumptions made of building components, building services 
systems, user behaviour and strategies associated with the operation of the 
building.  The assumptions often led to deviation between the predicted and 
actual energy consumption.  
Dynamic calculations tools are in general more precise, since it has the ability 
to interrogate parameter at peak times and during peak loads on room level. The 
methods are good for more unique, complex or large building design and 
installation. Use of the dynamic building performance tools, are often time 
consuming due to the extent of data input and complexity.  The methodology is 
often used in advanced programs like IESVE, IDA-ICE, BSim and similar 
programs. 
The steady state methodology uses steady periods, calculations under similar 
condition over time, to predict overheating, heating demand or cooling demand 
for a simplified model. The methods are fast and easy to use and are capable to 
predict more small or simple building designs. The steady state methodology is 
used for programs like TCD, Be10, Windesign and other simple excel programs. 
The use of the simplified programs is often developed, when new methods for 
predicting either energy consumption or indoor environment are needed. Thus 
are those simple programs often the driver for the more advanced programs 
development, such as TCD-Plus.

THE PREVIOUSLY TCD PROGRAM 
The previously Thermal Calculation Design (TCD) Program was developed in 
order to estimate the solar gain in buildings, as this parameter progressively 
represents a more important design parameter in building design.  Before the 
TCD program the estimation of thermal design was done by hand calculations or 
advanced simulation tools (H. Ellingsgaard and A. Kastberg, 2009). TCD 
calculates the solar gain and daily mean temperature in Danish buildings based 
on a clear sky. The calculation is based on the Indeklimahåndbog (SBi196, 2000) 
and sun curves from the Danvak book (Hansen, 2006). The TCD is an excel-based 
program consisting of 7 sheets. The first sheet is for the input, while the output is 
divided into 6 sheets. The model is simplified and contains different parameters 
such as; building design, orientation, solar shading, infiltration, mechanical 
ventilation and internal loads. The program is freely available and can be 
acquired by contacting DTU byg. 
Method for calculation 
The average temperature in a room depends on the outdoor temperature, 
internal heat gains, solar gain, transmission loss, infiltration loss, ventilation as 
well as the ventilation supply temperature and heat transmission to other rooms. 
TCD utilizes a simple calculation method to determine the daily mean 
temperature, and assumes that the heat gain during the day is equal to the heat 
loss during night. Thus the program assumes, that a steady state condition is 
obtained, instead of calculating with the heat capacity of the room. Normally the 
steady state condition is obtained after 5-6 days under similar conditions 
(SBi196, 2000). The rough steady state assumption limits the input for systems 
and creates poor input data for the estimation of the thermal indoor 
environment. An example is the internal heat gains and ventilation system, 
where the input solely relies on number of hours running and not the specific 
time on the day. Additionally no difference between weekdays and weekends can 
be made, resulting in the same system setup, when the building is occupied and 
not in use. 
Weather data 
The TCD program utilizes different weather data to estimate the daily outdoor 
air temperature.  Based on the Danish DRY (Design Reference Year) file for a 
reference year, the TRY (Test Reference Year) file for a test reference year and by 
values entered into the sheet by the user, three different daily outdoor air 
temperatures is calculated. Firstly to the average daily air temperature one can 
add an offset depending on the variation in the average temperature for the 
given month.  The solar radiation is based on the Danvak sun curves, hence can 
weather data from files not be used. The curves are based on a clear sky, creating 
a worst case with a free sky and high solar gain based on steady state conditions

IES-VE 
Integrated Environmental Solution – Virtual Environment is an advanced 
building performance program, used for investigation of the thermal indoor 
environment and energy demand. The program consists of different integrated 
building applications. The program is valid for estimating heating and cooling 
demands in accordance to (ASHRAE, 2007)and (EN13790, 2008), which complies 
with the energy performance measures in United Kingdom.  
EISVE uses a 3D interface where projects can be drawn into the user interface or 
imported through the open source format gvXML (green building XML). The 
format allows for import of some of the most used modelling programs such as 
Archicad, Autodesk Revit, Google sketch up etc. The program therefore uses a 
three dimensional geometry which creates zones and building elements that can 
be defined. User behaviour, ventilation operation etc. can be added into a 
calendar, creating more accurate calculation. 
The main application in EISVE is the ApacheSim, a simulation engine, which 
estimates dynamic thermal performance through mathematical modelling of heat 
transfer processes. The application can be linked together with other application 
to calculated on HVAC, Natural ventilation, Daylight and artificial.   
BSIM 
BSim is one of the common used building performance tools in Denmark. The 
program is developed by the Danish building institute, SBi, in the middle of the 
1980’s. BSim uses a 3D model, including building of the construction elements. 
The program is capable to predict a lot of building performance parameters such 
as; thermal indoor environment, energy consumption, daylight, moisture and 
energy transport in spaces, calculation of natural ventilation and electrical 
production yield from photovoltaic. 
The program takes into account, the thermal mass by means of the construction 
materials, and the distribution of the solar gain into the different building 
elements. The indoor temperature is based on the surface temperatures, and the 
air temperature giving a possibility for a fixed indoor operative temperature. 
BSim allows for analysing of exterior radiation on a surface as well as the solar 
gain transmitted into the room through the glassed areas.

WEATHER DATA 
The weather data is an important input parameter for calculation of the 
magnitude of the solar radiation.  The input needed is described in the weather 
data sheet in the program and are: the exterior temperature, diffuse-, direct- and 
global radiation. These data is available in DRY files and TRY files from SBi. 
However the global radiation is not available when making an inquiry at DMI. It 
is possible to convert the diffuse and direct radiation from DMI, in order to find 
the global radiation. This is done by (I. Hansen and S. Becerbasic, 2014) 
The tool developed is called FWT-creator and uses a macro script, to write the 
weather file. The creator uses hourly data for a year, where the measured data 
can be entered into the sheet. Values missing are found by interpolation in 
between the measured data. The creator ignores leap years, thus is the total

SOLAR GAIN 
Prediction of the thermal indoor environment in a room is effected by the solar 
gain as well as internal heat gains, heat transmission and systems available to 
control the thermal environment. The solar gain often is one of the heat gains 
that creates peaks in the indoor thermal environment, and is therefore important 
for calculation of thermal environment as well as energy consumption for 
removing unwanted over temperatures. The (DANVAK, 2006) mentions two 
different methods to calculate the solar gain through a window.  
The first method is based on solar radiation through a vertical reference window 
in Denmark.  The sun curves illustrated in figure 5 shows the direct solar gain 
and indirect solar gain as a function of time and orientation in clear weather.  The 
transmittance can be determined by the ratio between the design and reference 
window. Furthermore is the solar radiation as a function of the orientation 
determined by interpolating between the different sun-curves, described in the 
(DANVAK, 2006). 
The second method is based on sun radiation from the Danish reference years 
(DRY) and the suns movement on the sky. This calculation is more complex and 
takes the average values for the hourly sun radiations, direct, indirect and 
reflected into consideration.

INTRODUCTION TO SOLAR RADIATION AND SOLAR GAIN 
Prediction of thermal indoor environment in buildings requires a lot of 
knowledge’s about the sun’s behaviour and the surface.  
The position of the sun is expressed as a function of the geographical location as 
the solar azimuth angle (γ), solar altitude (α) while the geometrical relation to 
the surface also depends on the orientation and slope of the surface and is 
expressed by the incidence angle. 
When estimating the solar radiation, not only the suns position on the celestial 
sphere plays a role, but also the magnitude of the radiation. The magnitude from 
the sun is measured and described by the local weather data. 
The solar gain can be described by the direct, diffuse and reflected solar 
radiation.  The intensity can vary a lot depending on the time, orientation of the 
sun position on the sky and the weather conditions. 
 
𝐼𝑠𝑢𝑛= 𝐼𝑑𝑖𝑟+ 𝐼𝑑𝑖𝑓+ 𝐼𝑟𝑒𝑓 
(3) 
 
The direct solar radiation is described by the sun beams that directly hit the 
window pane. The direct radiation penetrates the atmosphere and varies in 
intensity based on the distance it has to travel through the atmosphere and the 
cloud cover. The radiation is scattered in the earth atmosphere due to particles 
and water droplets results in diffuse radiation. The diffuse radiation arrives with 
a lower magnitude, than the direct solar radiation. The last radiation comes from 
the reflected solar radiation, from the surroundings and ground. (ASHREA, 2009) 
The solar gain into the zone additionally depends on how much of the radiation 
is penetrating the transparent building elements such as window panes and the 
sun blocked by shading, the solar transmittance of the window pane, dirt on the 
window pane and the angular correction of the direct radiation etc. All of the 
factors will be further described in the following section.

EARTH IN RELATION TO THE SUN 
The solar radiation travels directly from the sun to the earth atmosphere, where 
some of the radiation gets scattered.  
As an outcome, the radiation hitting the surface gets divided into diffuse 
radiation from the celestial sphere and direct radiation from the sun. As the 
diffuse radiation is scattered it reaches the earth’s surface from all directions, 
while the direct beam radiation is strictly directional. When dealing with tilted 
surfaces, the surfaces is not only hit by the direct and diffuse radiation, but also 
the reflected radiation by the surroundings and objects nearby. The surface may 
have different geographical location, nearby surroundings, inclination and other 
factors that all affect the receiving of solar energy. 
The location of the surface is related to the geometrical relationship between the 
sun and the surface, the earth’s movement and the spherical geometry. The Earth 
spins around its own axis, with a full revolution taking 24 hours, perpendicular 
to the equatorial plane. As the sun takes 24 hours to spin one full revolution, the 
earth spins (360°/24h=15°/1h) 15° every hour. The division of the earth into 
15° longitudes is commonly used for dividing the earth into time zones thus 
measuring time. The spinning speed of 15°/h corresponding to 1° every 4min.

relation to the sun. The hour angle, ω, describes the angular displacement sun in 
relation to the local meridian caused by the rotation of the earth. The hour angle

afterwards and spins with the apparent speed of 15°/h. The hourly angle can be

SCATTERED RADIATION 
Determination of the scattered radiation or diffuse radiation consists of several 
components and can be a complex task to calculate. 
- 
The isotropic diffuse radiation is the scattered radiation that arrives from 
all direction of the hemisphere. The isotropic diffuse radiation is 
considered uniform. 
- 
Circumsolar diffuse radiation is the scattered radiation that is close to the 
direction of the beam direction. It is assumed concentrated around the 
sun as a cone with the vortex at earth’s surface. 
- 
The horizontal brightening is concentrated along the horizon and can 
particularly be observed during clear sky.

CORRECTION FACTOR FOR DIFFUSE AND REFLECTED RADIATION 
The preceding correction factor only applies for the beam radiant component. As 
the radiation consist of direct, diffuse and ground radiation, a correction factor 
for the diffuse and reflected needs to be determined. 
The factor for scattered diffuse and reflected ground radiation can be a difficult 
matter to estimate as the radiation arrives to the surface from all angles, and thus 
is the angular distribution of those radiation components general unknown.  
The diffuse radiation composes of the circum solar, horizon and the isotropic 
diffuse radiation from the hemisphere. The correction of the circum solar 
radiation can be estimated based on the angular profile assuming the same angle 
as the direct beam radiation. The diffuse radiation from the horizon has in 
general a small contribution to the total solar radiation, and can be evaluated 
with the isotropic diffuse sky radiation. 
The window is often oriented with a view factor to both the sky and ground. In 
1980 Brandemuehl and Beckman developed a method that integrated the beam 
transmission over the appropriate incidence angle. Hence resulting in diffuse and 
ground radiation can be expressed by a single effective incidence angle for both 
radiation components.   
Two simple methods covers a wide range of glazing types where the effective 
incidence angle for the diffuse, fi,dif, and reflected, fi,ref, radiation can be estimated 
based on the surface slope, β, as described by (Beckman, J.A. Duffie, W.A., 
20013)by:

THERMAL CALCULATION BY DESIGN 
Thermal indoor environment in a room depends on thermal loads and outdoor 
environment. The simplified methodologies utilize simple equations and 
fundamental theory to establish the thermal zones heat balance. The models 
includes heat exchange between room air, internal surfaces heat loss from 
building envelope and systems, as well as the thermal capacity and effect from 
thermal loads such as human, equipment and solar radiation. (DANVAK, 2006) 
 
FIGURE 17 - THERMAL ZONE WHIT LOADS AND LOSSES 
The thermal loads as occupant, equipment and lighting are depending on the 
user behaviour in the building and can therefore be unpredictable, just like 
change in the weather condition and can have an outcome on the predicted 
indoor environment. The heat gain can be divided into radiation and convection, 
where convective heat transfer primarily affects the room air, and radiant heat 
transfer affect the building components. The convective heat transfer applies 
higher temperature fluctuations due to direct transfer to the room air, 
meanwhile the radiant heat transfer heat up the building component before 
transferred into the room. 
As the human senses detects both the air temperature and radiant temperature, 
the thermal indoor environment should be evaluated based on a ratio between 
the temperatures. The ratio is described as 30% of radiated temperature and 
70% air temperature in accordance to (EN15251, 2007) or (EN13790, 2008) 
The operative temperature should be estimated for the most critical room in the 
building. The Danish building regulations requires that the number of hours with 
==PAGE:79==
 
Page ê67 
temperatures above 26°C should be less than 100 hours and for 27°C less than 
25 hours. 
It can be complicated to identify the most critical room as the loads from solar 
radiation, occupants and equipment often deviates depending on the room. SBi 
recommends to select the room with the highest solar gain, even though the tool 
provided by SBi do not have this option. Other litterature recommends to use the 
room with the highest thermal load during the daytime based on a steady state 
condition V (V. Homics and M. Kirkedal, 2013). Hence it is recommended that 
both the room with the highest solar gain and internal load are investigated 
when using TCD-Plus.  
THERMAL MODELS 
The purpose of hourly calculation in a critical room is to identify possible 
overheating in the thermal zone. Previously comparison of three simple thermal 
modelling methods; Bo Adamson, (EN13790, 2008) simplified method and SBi’s

investigation shows that (EN13790, 2008)has a rather sophisticated resistance-
capacitance scheme, while SBi uses the most simplified scheme for calculation of 
the thermal indoor environment. This project will only use the simplified hourly 
calculation method from (EN13790, 2008) as the best results was obtain with 
this methodology. (V. Homics and M. Kirkedal, 2013)

HEATING AND COOLING BY AXILLARY ENERGY 
The BSim setup for heating takes the heat loss for installation into consideration 
among other parameters. The heat loss for installation is in TCD-Plus assumed to 
be 0, as the installations are placed inside the building envelope, thus is the 
parameter for the heating system set to a fixed value of .  Both in BSim and TCD-
Plus is the heating set point is based on the operative temperature, assumed to 
be entered identically. BSim has extra input for the dimensioning of the system 
based on the exterior temperature and the desired indoor temperature. To 
remove this parameter for the heating system, in Bsim the design temperature is 
set at 40°C and the Te min to 41°C. This means that the system is able to deliver 
full effect if the outdoor temperature is below 40°C. The same matter is valid for 
the cooling system where the design temperature is set to 1°C and the Te min at 
0°C. This ensures that the cooling system is able to deliver full effect until the 
outdoor temperature reaches 1°C, if needed.

VENTILATION 
The parameter, ventilation part to air, equals the heat gain from the fans. This 
parameter is not implemented why in the TCD-plus program, is the factor set to 
0.  
The ventilation rate in accordance to the indoor air quality, (EN15251, 2007), is 
performed by estimating the air change rate. The minimum air change rate in the 
ventilation is therefore entered at the estimated rate, as it is assumed that the

BUILDING ENVELOPE 
BSim calculate the heat loss through the building envelope by the 3D geometry 
and U-values entered.  TCD-Plus do not have 3D-model but calculates the heat 
gain in accordance to DS418. Thus an external Excel sheet were developed, in 
order to calculate the areas, and differences in internal and external 
measurements. The BSim as thermal bridging as the areas are measured in 
accordance to DS418 equals gross areas. Thus has the two programs the same 
conditions for the thermal bridges occurring for example in building corners. 
Additionally transmissions between rooms are simulated based on a thermal 
environment zone, defined in BSim. TCD-Plus do not take the surrounding 
compartment into consideration, thus is the internal wall created as adiabatic 
walls. 
The windows are as standard defined to be recessed into the wall. Thus are the 
recessed windows as standard set at 0.1m. As a simplification of the model no 
framing is constructed and linier loss for windows is disregarded. The U-value 
for the centre point of the glazing is as standard set to 0.8 while the solar energy 
transmittance as standard is entered to be 0.43. 
ACCURACY OF TIME STEPS IN SIMULATION 
BSim allows the user to vary the number of time steps in the simulation in order 
to find a balance between the simulation time and accuracy. An investigation of 
such is performed by (V. Homics and M. Kirkedal, 2013)as a function of thermal 
mass capacity, room height, solar shading etc. The accuracy was tested on 
numerous of simulations with different number of steps. When the simulation 
converged with an acceptable error margin the number of time steps was 
marked in the different cases. The investigation yielded the time steps that where 
sufficient were in the range from 8 to 32 steps per hour. The report concludes 
that the optimal number for simulation should be set at 32 steps. The large 
number of steps is however not comparable to the TCD-plus program as the 
program is based on (EN13790, 2008) hourly calculation algorithm. The number 
of time steps will therefore create some difference in between the simplified 
TCD-Plus simulation and BSim. Thus is the number of steps in BSim set at the 
lowest possible number in order to compare the accuracy of the TCD-Plus 
program, which in BSim is 2 steps per hour.

SUMMARY OF THERMAL MASS 
The investigation of the thermal mass when adding many systems shows that a 
deviation occurs, primarily due to the difference in the transmission loss. A small 
devotion of how the thermal mass absorbs and emits the energy into the room 
was revealed. The deviation is primarily believed to be due to the simplification 
of the room in TCD-Plus using a lump method for the thermal mass. The lump 
method simplifies the thermal mass to a lump. This will create a difference as 
BSim uses the ratio between the surfaces to calculate the mean radiant 
temperature while TCD-Plus uses the mean temperature of the lumped mass. 
The investigation additionally revealed that the light model in wood had higher 
temperatures, which is believed to be due to less storage in the thermal mass. As 
the thermal mass has less possibility for storage in the light model, the air 
temperature is the main dominating factor in the thermal indoor climate. The 
light office often tends to fluctuate more in temperatures due to less thermal 
mass, and slower rate of emitting the heat back into the room, which would even 
out the operative temperature.  
The overall investigation showed promising tendencies when investigating the 
systems and thermal mass together.

program can take a while to open. The process bar in the lower left corner shows 
how far in the process the program is.

The thermal calculation is done in several steps as shown on the figure. Every

pre-calculation to final operative temperature used for evaluating the indoor

FIGURE 51- THE THERMAL CALCULATION SYSTEM 
When a row reaches the 9th step the capacity in the thermal mass is used to 
calculate the hour in the year in next row. The calculation is done for every hour 
of the year giving a sequence from t=1 to t=8760. The program cannot handle 
leap years as the formulas is limited by 8760 cells. 
PRE- ASSUMPTION OF THERMAL SYSTEM 
The calculation sheet for the thermal indoor environment uses the EN13790 
standard for the calculation. The first sequence is based on the set point for the 
heating set point. The reason for using the set point for heating in the first 
calculation sequence, is because it is assumed that the heating system has been 
able to heat up the room in the days before the calculations starts. Hence has the 
system often a start temperature of 20 °C to 21 °C depending on the heating set 
point chosen. 
TIME AND GAINS 
The first six columns defines the time in year, month, day, hour, day number of 
the year and workday or weekend, while the seventh column is the outdoor 
temperature from the weather file used. 
VENTING AND VENTILATION OF EXCESSIVE TEMPERATURES 
Two type of pre-calculation is made, one based on the needed air flow for the 
indoor air quality and one for the max venting + ventilation rate for removal of 
excessive temperatures. The needed air flow from the occupant schedule creates 
the basis for the ventilation loss in the pre calculation, while the second pre-
calculation is the systems max capacity. The two pre-calculations are used to 
determine the use for mechanical ventilation and venting systems in step 5. 
==PAGE:156==
 
 Page ê144 
The 1st step in the calculation uses the eighth and ninth columns and describes 
the heat gains for the room. In step 1 the heat is distributed in accordance to 
section EN13790 sub section Heat flows from internal and solar loads.The heat 
gains are distributed into the air, surface and thermal mass. 
The 2nd step is the pre-calculation of the ventilation by CO2 and building 
emission. The air and operative temperatures are found in accordance to section 
EN13790 sub section, Air and operative temperature, for a given Heating and 
cooling capacity. The air and operative temperature found in step 2 is based on 
EN15251 demand for internal air change described by an air change rate for 
number of people and building emission when occupied. The estimation do not 
have a doublet air change rate in the hour before the room is occupied as 
suggested in the standard. 
The 3rd step uses the max possible air change rate for both venting and 
mechanical ventilation if the systems are modeled. The step is called pre-
calculation by max ventilation loss as it describes the thermal environment with 
maximum air change is use. The air and operative temperatures are found in 
accordance to section EN13790 sub section, Air and operative temperature for a 
given Heating and cooling capacity, as in the case of pre-calculation by C02 and 
building emission. 
The 4th step defines the actual needed venting rate for the different systems by 
interpolation between the operative temperatures and ventilation losses found 
in step 2 and 3. As the interpolation creates a risk for the system performing, at 
higher air change rates than possible, a limitation for the different types of 
ventilation is created through the minimum function in the excel sheet. As part of 
the different ventilation strategies that can be utilized the active systems is 
chosen based on a set point for the system entered into the input sheet. The 
program allows the use of the HRU as well as bypassing the HRU if needed, 
adding higher ventilation rates by the mechanical ventilation or venting all 
controlled by set point. The formula for choosing the system in excel is: 
=IF(occupancy>=0.001, 
IF(Top, previous time <=setpointhru, Hve,CO2, 
IF(AND(Top, previous time <setpointhru, Top, previous time >= setpointventing), Hve,CO2, 
IF(AND(Top, previous time < setpointventing, Top, previous time >=setpointventilaion), Hve,mech, 
Hve,cs))), Hve, infiltration) 
 
 
After determining the ventilation strategy the air and operative temperatures are 
found through the same method as in step 3 and 4.

HEATING AND COOLING SYSTEM

ventilation the heating or cooling system is defined from step 6 to 8.  
The 6th Step describes the desired temperature interval that the systems should 
obtain, in other words the set point for the heating and cooling system. It is at 
this current step assumed that the systems can fulfill to stay inside the 
temperature range.  
The 7th step is a pre-calculation of heating and cooling in accordance to chapter

described in chapter 5, Thermal calculation by design, under the sub section, air 
and operative temperature for a given heating and cooling capacity. The new 
operative temperature is used to find the heating or cooling effect needed to 
obtain the desired temperature range. A max effect is added if entered into the 
input sheet ensuring that the system has a limit.  
The 8th step utilizes the needed ventilation system found in step 4 to 5 and the 
needed heating and cooling defined in step 7 to 8 to determine the final surface 
temperature and air temperature in the room

CONCLUSION 
The purpose of the thesis was to develop a tool for assessing the thermal indoor 
environment and related energy consumption. The tool, TCD-Plus, was based on 
hourly estimation of the solar gain and indoor thermal environment. The tool 
was tested against the Professional building performance tool BSim, were input 
parameters were simplified in order to compare the tools. 
The investigation showed some deviation on the indoor thermal climate and 
related energy consumption. The irradiation on the exterior surface showed a 
very minor deviation on a yearly basis. The solar irradiation deviation was found 
very acceptable. The solar gain into the zone was determined by the solar 
irradiation, shading and correction of the angle of which the sun hit the glazing. 
The investigation showed that a small error could occur in the shading if for 
example the overhang was moved too far away from the window. The solar gain 
investigation additionally showed that hourly calculated shading and angle 
depend factor added a bit to the deviation in the solar gain. The overall 
calculation of the solar gain and solar irradiation was found very satisfying. 
When looking at the test for systems and real cases, the investigation showed a 
deviation on the yearly energy consumption for heating and cooling. The 
deviation is primarily due to the difference in the transmission loss in the two 
programs.  
Furthermore it was found that the estimation of hours with excessive 
temperatures deviated. The main reason is believed to be due to a hysteresis of 
zero in the program, which means that the threshold of exactly 26°C determines 
whether an hour with excessive temperatures is estimated or not 
It was found that the ventilation and venting acts very identically under the same 
conditions. However does the same condition not always occur due to difference 
in the transmission loss and thermal mass. An example showed, when insulating 
the building more effective the temperature stayed more constant during the 
night. Another example showed that the accumulated heat in the thermal mass 
got the operative temperature to increased when the systems was switched off.  
When examining the different days, a very close relation between the maximum 
temperatures in the occupied hours occurred. The investigation showed that 
almost the same design reference days were found and that the maximum 
temperatures in most case deviated with less that ±0.5°C. When investigating the 
systems and temperatures of different design reference days, a very close 
relation was shown in most cases.  
 
 
==PAGE:165==
 
Page ê153 
The TCD-Plus tool is general found good when looking at the thermal condition 
and the systems tendencies at a daily basis, while the yearly estimation shows a 
bit larger deviation on parameters such as: 
- 
transmission loss 
- 
venting and ventilation loss 
- 
heating and cooling demand 
To sum the main reasons for the deviation were found to be: 
- 
difference in definition of transmission loss 
- 
time steps and reaction time 
- 
difference in definition of thermal mass 
- 
none use of hysteresis 
To sum up: 
The deviation on the daily basis did not show a large deviation, while the 
yearly deviation was found relative large compared to the expected.

glasses and the type of coating. It is suggested, that this method should be further 
investigated and implemented into the program 
Another improvement of the program is the possibility to add a heating and 
cooling coil to the ventilation system. Small errors are currently occurring in the 
program, when estimating the energy demand and in the assumption about the 
sensing of the thermal indoor environment.  
An example; if the heat recovery unit does not work efficiently enough, due to 
low outdoor temperatures, the inlet air might create draught. A heating coil 
==PAGE:166==
 
 Page ê154 
heating up the air to a minimum, will remove the risk for the draught 
substantially, as a warmer air will lower the risk of creating draught in the room.  
An example of an error in the heating demand for the ventilation system is when 
the air needs to be heated, but the room might not have the same needs. TCD-
plus currently allows for a ventilation system to have a very low inlet 
temperature creating draught as previously described. If the room do not have 
any heating at this time step, due to being above 20°C, the air will cool down the 
room. Hence will the actual heating demand for raising the air temperature in 
such a situation, not add up to the energy demand. This will lead to a small 
deviation in the hours, where no heating in the room is needed, but the inlet air 
needs to be heated avoiding draught. Furthermore the horizon shading factors is 
not designed. The factors could either by designed in accordance to EN13790 
with a simplified angle, or by defining the horizon through an angular profile, like 
in the simple building performance tool IDbuild. 
Furthermore adding a hysteresis into the program will give the possibility to 
adjust the small deviation of excessive temperatures, as it was found that some 
values came very close or just a bit over the threshold. 
Finally an investigation of the importance of the critical angles for overhangs, 
side fins and horizon, could be investigated to ensure, that the hourly shading 
factors are accurate.
