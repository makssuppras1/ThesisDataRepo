Abstract
The current rise in occupational stress and shift towards a hybrid work environment calls
for new ways of managing stress. Biofeedback constitutes a promising tool which measures
physiological activity (e.g. changes in heart rate variability associated with stress), and enables
the user to regulate this activity, conditioning a healthier response to stress. Current research
on biofeedback for stress management is conﬂicted as to how such systems should be designed.
The regulation can be done passively by increasing users’ awareness of their stress, or actively by
inﬂuencing their physiology via relaxation/breathing exercises. Furthermore, some researchers
suggest delivering the feedback continuously via an ambient media while working, and others
discontinuously in sessions. In an experiment (N=33), this thesis found that an active regulation
and discontinuous feedback consisting of a HRV-waveform and respiration guide was most
eﬀective in increasing heart rate variability. The inﬂuence on the users’ awareness of stress along
with their motivation and ability to regulate it was investigated, but showed no statistically
signiﬁcant diﬀerence. Lastly, a biofeedback system integrated in headphones is conceptualised.
This represents a promising possibility to create a versatile and easily accustomable biofeedback
system that can facilitate self-management of stress.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1
Relevance and background
Stress on the rise. In fact, stress is now such a widespread problem, that WHO has deemed it
”the health epidemic of the 21st century” [24]. The 2021 Work Trend Index by Microsoft [12]
reveals that while self-assessed productivity has remained the same or higher across the global
workforce, 54% now feel overworked and 39% exhausted. Weekly time spent in meetings has
more than doubled compared to 2020, and after-hours work-related Teams chats are up 42%.
Not only are people working more and more, [28] the recent COVID-pandemic has disrupted the
work structure as we know it and introduced a sudden shift towards more remote and ﬂexible
work. And the hybrid structure of mixed remote- and oﬃce work is here to stay. A 2020 survey
of 9000 managers and employees across Europe showed that 88% of business leaders expect
ﬂexible ways of working to continue [28].
The hybrid work structure makes it diﬃcult for both companies and employees to manage
stress. The employees’ freedom to manage their own time brings many beneﬁts, but also comes
at the risk of loosing control over the work-life balance.
This risk is further instigated by
the managers’ and occupational health representative’s increasingly diﬃcult task of mitigating
stress across a decentralised workforce.
A possible solution to this lies in self-management systems - empowering the individual em-
ployee to manage their own stress, by giving them the right tools.
Monitoring health data is an increasingly popular trend, where users can get insights and

Through various sensor-technologies, people can monitor their own stress response and learn

While the biofeedback practice has previously been associated with clinics and heavy lab-

2
Aim
The biofeedback technology is moving out of the clinics, and a new paradigm is emerging as
therapist-mediated biofeedback sessions are being replaced by computer-mediated self-use. [65].
This paradigm-shift calls for an exploration of the challenges involved in this new context and
how biofeedback can be designed to ﬁt it.
Most studies have assumed a successful perception of the feedback to automatically mean reg-
ulation and behaviour change. While this might be valid for controlled studies and clinical use
driven by oversight and extrinsic motivation, it doesn’t apply to the use of biofeedback outside
the typical experimental setup, where adherence relies on other behavioural- and motivational
components. The thesis will therefore seek to understand these components and forces through
behavioural design theory, in order to aid biofeedback systems in changing behaviour outside
of clinics and controlled setups.
3
Research question and objectives
The thesis revolves around a central issue - how biofeedback systems should be designed to best
facilitate positive behavioural change. The research question and objectives outlined below,
describes what is to be investigated to better understand the issue, and how.
RQ: What inﬂuence does diﬀerent representations of biofeedback have on driving
behavioural change?

Chapter 2
Theoretical background
1
Biofeedback
The thesis builds on the Association for Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback’s deﬁnition
of biofeedback systems.
Biofeedback is a process that enables an individual to learn how to change physiolog-
ical activity for the purposes of improving health and performance. Precise instru-
ments measure physiological activity such as brainwaves, heart function, breathing,
muscle activity, and skin temperature. These instruments rapidly and accurately
“feed back” information to the user. The presentation of this information — often
in conjunction with changes in thinking, emotions, and behavior — supports desired
physiological changes. Over time, these changes can endure without continued use
of an instrument [1]
The foundation of biofeedback is a psychophysiological principle, particularly formulated by
Green, Green and Walters [20], proposing that changes in mental state can lead to physiological
changes and vice versa. This means, that by gaining an understanding and changing your
perception of stress, you can inﬂuence the body’s physiological response to it. In the same
way, by inﬂuencing physiological parameters, such as breathing rate and heart function, one’s
psychological experience of stress can be altered [34].
The general approach to biofeedback found in research is described in ﬁg. 2.1, highlighting the
connection between the ”technical side” of the biofeedback system, and the ”human system”.

CHAPTER 2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
Figure 2.1: The general practice of biofeedback (inspired by Osman et al. [3])
All biofeedback systems rely on collecting biometric data. A variety of sensors allow for mea-
suring diﬀerent physiological or neurological activities, which are processed in the biofeedback
device. Most often, a single physiological activity is monitored, [65] but more can be combined
to form more elaborate, multimodal, and precise insights. The aﬀordance of this device exists
within the boundaries of a use case, dictating the context that the feedback is delivered to the
user (i.e. through the medium of a smartphone at work). This allows the user to perceive
his/her live bio-signals and performance within the area of interest. Some biofeedback systems
give explicit instructions on how to regulate the physiological activity, such as breathing ex-
ercises or cognitive training, while others rely on the user’s own perception and awareness to
motivate autonomous regulation [3]
Biofeedback has, historically, been used for a wide range of purposes, although most commonly
within a clinical domain, such as treatment of mental health disorders like Anxiety, ADD, and
PTSD [51] [34].
Even though biofeedback is a fairly old technique developed in the late 1950 [52], it has received
a lot of attention in recent years, due to advances in sensor technologies and processing power.
More and more applications are becoming feasible outside the clinical domain as the technical
possibilities are moving from complex stationary setups to mobile inexpensive solutions, that
can be used in an everyday setting [3]. This means, that biofeedback use cases like focus-
and stress management are becoming increasingly relevant as it becomes technologically and

1.1
Biofeedback for stress management
One’s physiological response to stress is largely automatic, as it is controlled by the hypotha-
lamus realising hormones, the amygdala regulating emotional behaviour, and the autonomic
nervous system (ANS) controlling the non-voluntary activity of organs - e.g. heart function
[11].
Stress responses controlled by these automatic processes can be measured in various ways -
some of the most prevalent ones are described below.
1.1.1
Galvanic skin response
One of the non-voluntary responses that the ANS initiates when subjected to stress or cognitive
load, is sweating [53].
While this is not always noticeable to the individual, galvanic skin
response (GSR) sensors can pick up the subtle changes in skin conductivity. As the combination
of moisture and salt (sweat) starts to form, the skin’s conductance increase. This type of sensor
technology is also sometimes known as eletrodermal activity (EDA).
This technique is, however, not very suited to give feedback on immediate changes, at it may
take several minutes or hours for the change in physiology to be detectable in the data [7].
1.1.2
Electroencephalogram (EEG)
EEG sensors are used to measure brain activity. Via electrodes connected to the scalp, electrical
activity can be measured across diﬀerent frequency bands. For example, frequencies of 22-30
Hz (high-beta) in the left prefrontal cortex is associated with agitation, restlessness, stress, and
anxiety [21]. An increased activity within this spectrum can therefore be used as a measure of
the brain’s stress response.
EEG technology is, however, a very complex procedure that requires heavy computing power
and precise instruments. While some commercial products are available for portable self-use
(e.g. the Muse headband [42] or Emotiv Mobile Brainwear [56]), the technology is still very
sensitive to diﬀerent environments and movement. Furthermore, to get more extensive and
reliable insights, a net of electrodes covering larger areas of the scalp is needed. It therefore

risks becoming a very invasive solution and is ultimately not very suited for an everyday use
case.
1.1.3
Respiration
Most often, when a person is subjected to stress, their breathing pace starts to increase auto-
matically [54]. This change in frequency (and depth) can be measured by sensors - usually a
stretch sensor around the abdomen or thorax.
However, as breathing is very inﬂuenced by other activities that the user might do, (eating,
talking, etc.) this measure of stress is not very reliable in adverse environments outside the
laboratory where these factors can’t be controlled.
It is therefore not very suited for self-
administration of biofeedback.
1.1.4
Heart rate variability
The most common way for biofeedback systems to measure stress is with heart rate variability
(HRV) [65] [29]. Through an expanding body of literature, this has proven to be one of the
most reliable indicators of stress [31]. This section will go more into depth with the use and
analysis of HRV, as it makes up the main measure of stress response in this thesis.
Heart rate variability is essentially the variance in how quickly the heart beats. In stressful
situations, the inter-beat intervals (the time between heartbeats) are mostly regular.
The
general heart rate can still rise and fall, but the time between each heartbeat is consistent (as
seen in ﬁg. 2.4 (A). As the mind and body relaxes, the time between each beat starts to vary
more, as your heart rate slowly increases and decreases. This forms the larger, softer waves
illustrated in ﬁg. 2.4 (B).

2.1
Frameworks for understanding behaviour change in biofeedback
The end goal of practising biofeedback is to improve health an performance by becoming better
at regulating physiology and changing behaviour [1]. To describe how diﬀerent biofeedback
systems promote this change and regulation, a framework is needed. This section will discuss
and compare two frameworks to determine which is more suitable for the mapping of behaviour
change with biofeedback systems.
The ﬁrst framework is developed by Hamari et al., and explains the behaviour change through
motivational aﬀordances [22] - the function/element of a technology that motivates a certain
outcome.
Figure 2.5: Hamari’s framework for persuasive technologies [22]
According to Hamari’s framework, the motivational aﬀordance drives a psychological outcome,
which in turn becomes a behavioural outcome. This conceptual framing plays very well together
with biofeedback systems and resembles the general biofeedback framework’s connection be-
tween the technical system and the human system, as described in one of the very ﬁrst ﬁgures,
ﬁg. 2.1.

has a psychological outcome / inﬂuence on the user’s perception. This change in perception in
turn becomes a regulation of physiology - the behavioural outcome of the biofeedback system.
Hamari’s framework is developed for persuasive technologies. This link between persuasive
technology and biofeedback devices, is something that is generally sought for in literature [65][3]
in order to create eﬀective ubiquitous biofeedback systems. While Hamari’s framework does a
good job in explaining the relationship between the motivational outcomes on an abstract level,
the taxonomy of motivational aﬀordances only present very general categories such as ”Visual
or audio feedback”, ”Social support, comparisons and sharing”, ”Progress”, and ”Persuasive

To be more speciﬁc on what behaviour change mechanisms are incorporated in biofeedback

analyse the diﬀerent ways that biofeedback systems are designed to change behaviour.
While Michie’s taxonomy generalises biofeedback under a single behaviour change technique
(2.6 Biofeedback), this thesis proposes a more nuanced understanding based on Michie’s own
framework, which will be discussed further in chapter 3: Model and hypotheses. Not only can
the biofeedback itself promote behaviour change in diﬀerent ways, the system and use case
that is associated with the biofeedback practice can incorporate additional behaviour change
techniques, such as prompts/cues [3] or goal-setting [5].

2.2
Fogg’s behaviour model for persuasive design
In Yu’s review of biofeedback for stress management, one of the promising future research
directions (and current challenges) highlighted, is the integration of persuasive technologies in
biofeedback[65]. The challenge is closely linked to biofeedback’s paradigm-shift from therapist-
guided training-sessions to computer-mediated self-use. In this new use-case the eﬀect of the
system relies on the user’s own capability and drive.
To better understand how a persuasive element can be introduced in biofeedback, Fogg’s be-
haviour model is used to describe some of the basic principles of initiating a desired behaviour
[19]. Fogg highlights three elements which are crucial for any behaviour to happen; motivation,
ability and a trigger.
Motivation and ability exist in a trade-oﬀ- the more ability a user has to perform an action
(the easier it is) the less motivation is needed to do it. In the same way, the less ability the
user has, the more motivation is needed.
Besides the two aspects of motivation and ability, a trigger must be present to initiate the
behaviour. The more ability and motivation the user has, the more likely the trigger is to
prompt the desired behaviour.
Figure 2.7: Fogg’s behaviour model for persuasive design [19].
Fogg’s behaviour model for persuasive design is used as a general framework in the thesis to
understand the behavioural output of biofeedback systems. This structure will be elaborated
in chapter 3.

• Novelty: How familiar the intended behaviour is.
• Scope: How extensive the behaviour is.
• Frequency: How often the activity should be done.

Again, it comprises of three sub-features:
• Environmental opportunity: How much the solution can change the physical context.
• Social opportunity: The involvement and inﬂuence of social actors, e.g. colleagues and

Technical system
This section uncovers key issues in the technical side of the research framework (ﬁg. 3.1). This

2.1
The use case
The beneﬁts of biofeedback for stress management have become clear through its clinical use
[29] [65], and the advances in technology now allows for portable and less intrusive sensor
equipment. This means that biofeedback is becoming available for a wider range of people, to
be used as a part of everyday stress management. Al Osman formalised this concept in what he
calls ”Ubiquitous biofeedback” (or U-biofeedback), where continuous measurements facilitate
the user’s own long term management of physiological processes [3].
Although Osman formalises the concept and even provides guidelines on the design of such
systems, the approach is largely technical and centred around the features of the system, such
as it’s ability to log and store data over longer periods of time [3]. What is arguably missing,
is a link to the use-context of the biofeedback device.
The controlled setting of the clinical domain is now replaced by the typical busy workday ﬁlled
with stressors, social norms among colleagues and an external expectation of productivity. This
means, that for the biofeedback system to successfully integrate into such an environment, the
use case has to be considered in the early design of the system.

The use case is highly dependent on the media that the biofeedback is delivered through. Most
systems work through computer screens/interfaces [65][2], some through smartphones [27][44].

2.1.2
The behavioural constraints
To better understand the context of a possible biofeedback use case, a small workshop was
conducted to gain insights from relevant stakeholders in the industry. Contacts from GN Store
Nord (Jabra) Jesper Glad (Director of IPR Development & Innovation) and Bettina Lundstrøm
(Senior Experience Product Manager) was invited to discuss how their current products are
being used, and how biofeedback for stress management would ﬁt into this environment. The
following section summarises insights that have emerged from these discussions.
Technical
While problems with stress are found across all types of occupations, this thesis mainly focuses
on oﬃce work, where most of the tasks take place in front of a computer in the form of
information work or meetings. According to Microsoft’s annual report on work trends, weekly
time spent in online meetings has more than doubled [12]. In this perspective, the increasing use
and availability of headphones and virtual collaboration equipment presents new possibilities
of collecting and utilising data.
Headphones are being integrated into oﬃce work in a variety of ways. Not only are they used
for online calls, but also listening to music and simply signalling to co-workers around you,
that you are focused and not to be disturbed. Some newer headphones can even assist this
focused state through noise-cancelling technology [39]. Through increasingly aﬀordable sensor
technologies, it is also possible to collect biometric data via headphones such as movement and
heart rate. This technical possibility is what makes biofeedback feasible with a combined head-
set / stress management tool. Such a tool, along with the use of a smartphone or computer, is
what sets the technical constraints of the use case.
Environment
The work environment deﬁnes the boundaries of the use case, as it constraints the biofeedback
practice to exist within a certain work structure. This structure has, however, become much
more ﬂexible in recent years, as hybrid work (mixing remote online work with the oﬃce/desk
job) has become very widespread and increasingly sought after by both employees and leaders
[12]. The increased need for ﬂexibility and online presence plays very well together with the

integration of a biofeedback system in a head-set. The portability of such a device also helps
loosen the environmental constraints, as most biofeedback practices for stress management
involves some form of relaxation, which is easiest to achieve in quiet surroundings. Furthermore
it can be practised both when working from home or in the oﬃce.
Social
The acquisition of a head-set with stress management capabilities comes with certain social
implications. If not framed properly, the device risks the stigma of becoming an ”aid” to treat
stress problems.
From a commercial perspective, a biofeedback head-set for stress management is most likely
either bought as a consumer-product or oﬀered to employees among other possible head-sets.
To reach a large consumer base and make the product economically and socially feasible, it is
important that the product is framed as tool for everyone to manage their stress better, and
not to ”treat” stress. In other words, it should be an enabling tool, rather than a treatment of
a disability.
This framing also helps to make the biofeedback practice itself more socially acceptable among
co-workers. As the practice is novel, the display and feedback should be shown in a way, where
the users easily can familiarise themselves with the data and manage it in a personal way. Like
most physiological data, stress data is a sensitive thing, which should be reﬂected in the mode
of display. Some researchers have experimented with ambient and public displays of stress
levels [64] [35] [66], which some participants found discomforting to have shown openly around
others. Discretion is therefore an important aspect to the display of feedback.
2.1.3
The change demand
Understanding what the products demands from the user is essential, in order to make it as
easy as possible to integrate it into a busy workday.
Scope
Based on the behavioural constraints, diﬀerent use-cases emerge. A head-set ﬁtted with sensors
allows for the collection of data over longer periods of time, meaning that the system could
potentially be able to provide more extensive insights into stress patterns and prompt the
user with timely feedback (e.g. the most eﬀective times to take breaks). The common use of
a head-set is, however, very situational - meaning that the insight would only be in speciﬁc
times, when the head-set was in use for online meetings, etc.. It would therefore not collect a
complete picture of the user’s stress levels throughout the day, unless it was worn at all times

(which is not likely).
A periodic use is therefore assumed, in which the user wears the head-set to help them regulate
their stress levels over a certain amount of time.
As the coming sections will detail, biofeedback can be practised in very diﬀerent ways. The
scope of this practice therefore varies greatly - from simply being aware of one’s stress levels
while working [64] [35] [16] to actively inﬂuencing physiological aspect, e.g. increasing heart
rate variability through guided respiration and relaxation [36] [61] [68]).
Frequency
Biofeedback for stress management poses an inherent issue - the more stressed you are, the less
time you feel like you have to practice it.
Lehrer et al. suggest in their manual for HRV and respiratory resonance training [31] a frequency
of two times a day for 20 minutes. Other studies found that a weekly practice of 45 minutes
was also enough to induce a sustained eﬀect on subjective and physiological stress [41].
While the most eﬀective frequency is still unclear [65], it is generally assumed that - like most
training - the more frequent practice, the better one becomes at regulating stress.
This also implies that the best time to practice regulating stress is at the workplace before or
after stressful tasks or meetings, conditioning the ANS to respond to stress in a healthy way.
Novelty
Another beneﬁcial aspect of integrating a biofeedback system with a head-set, is that the user is
familiar with it’s use. It doesn’t require extra electrodes or components that can discourage the
user, making the adaptation much easier. Biofeedback systems does, however, often involve an
introduction to a new type of physiological data and practice, which might take some getting-
used-to. Nevertheless, self-monitoring is an increasingly popular trend made possible through
smart-watches’ and smartphones’ capabilities of tracking steps, diﬀerent health parameters,
etc. [47], so more and more people are becoming used to tracking and interacting with health
data.

When breaking the representation of biofeedback data down, one of the most fundamental

the diﬃculty would decrease, and when it was rapid the game would become harder. Again,
the risk of a negative feedback loop is present, as the users might become more stressed or
frustrated the more diﬃcult the game becomes, or simply loose interest if the game became too
easy.
Timing
The sensory input also has an element of timing - when and how the input is being delivered.
The two main categories of timing examined in this thesis is a continuous or discontinuous
delivery. Continuous feedback is presented to the user over longer periods of time, while dis-
continuous is administered once in a while.
The discussion on the continuity of the feedback springs from the possibility of moving the
tech out of the lab. Traditionally, most practices were discontinuous, as patients would have to
come in in sessions to practice biofeedback with the help of professionals. The general belief is,
that a continuous use case outside the clinic allows for more exposure to the biofeedback than

2.2.2
Behaviour change mechanisms
Another very inﬂuential component in the representaion of biofeedback data, is the behaviour
change mechanism. This describes how the change demand is encouraged and the practice is
motivated.
Generally two diﬀerent practices are found in biofeedback depending on the type of regulation
that the system aﬀords. The most basic is a passive regulation designed to make the user more
aware of their physiology. This helps the user develop introceptive skills and enable them to
better regulate physiological activity/behaviour on their own [34].
Biofeedback systems with an active regulation involves a practice where the user accustoms
their physiology to a state by actively inﬂuencing it. As an example, Munafo et al. created
a biofeedback system for stress management which included active regulation sessions, where
the subjects would inﬂuence their heart rate variability through a respiratory sinus arrhythmia
(RSA) practice. Live respiration and heart rate data was displayed in a tachogram (as seen in
ﬁg. 3.9) during a weekly session of 45 minutes, where the subjects would try to match their
respiration with the variation in their heart rate, thus increasing the variability.
Figure 3.9: Munafo’s tachogram of heart rate (on top) and respiration (below) [41]
This weekly practice of 45 minutes was enough to induce a sustained eﬀect, as the subjects
reported lower anxiety levels, improvement in health-related quality of life and a reduced heart
rate at rest [41].
An example of a biofeedback system with passive regulation is the StressTree by Yu et al. [64]
- a visual projection of your stress levels in the form of a tree blooming when relaxed and

Metaphorical components
Although not a focal point of this thesis, the metaphorical component is an important element to
the delivery of biofeedback and must be understood when designing and discussing biofeedback
systems as a whole.
As presented throughout the report, a huge range of representations have been made with
very diﬀerent visualisations and forms. As the user’s perception and interpretation of the data
is the foundation of an eﬀective biofeedback system, the analogies and metaphors that the
representations convey, play an important role in the facilitation of the feedback.
An example of a BF system with an explicit metaphorical component is found in the previous
example - the Stress Tree by Yu et al. [64] (shown in ﬁg. 3.10), where the stress levels are
depicted as the health of a tree.
Although not always explicitly noticeable by the user, the metaohorical component inﬂuence
the user’s experience and interpretation of the biofeedback. For example, the connection to the
life of a tree brings meaning to the data, as the user begins to associate their stress levels with
properties relevant to the health of a tree.
All visualisations contain some form of metaphorical component, although not always as ex-

The ﬁrst characteristic is based on Ball and Christensen’s framework describing the analogical

two analogies’ elements and narrative are similar. If there is a structural diﬀerence, the analogy
can become weaker. While some structural similarities can be found within mental health /
stress and nature / plant life such as the need for maintenance/nourishment to uphold balance
and good health, the two domains vary on a lot of other elements.
Surface similarity describes the related descriptive properties like the shape, size and colour

The human system
This section will cover how the human system is inﬂuenced by the technical system - in other
words, what behavioural output the biofeedback results in.
3.1
Behavioural output
This thesis suggests an approach to measuring biofeedback’s eﬀect that is slightly diﬀerent from
most literature. Commonly, the eﬀect of the biofeedback practice is measured physiologically,
and often times also psychologically via self-report instruments assessing stress/anxiety [29].
What is arguably missing, is a behavioural element examining how the subjects perceive- and
is motivated by the feedback [65]. Since most studies are done in a controlled fashion with
facilitators explaining the data and ensuring that the participants are following the procedure,
intrinsic motivation isn’t required for the subjects to adhere to the practice. However, when
moving towards a computer-mediated self-use of biofeedback systems, it is critical that the
users perceive, and ﬁnd value in the feedback themselves, and that they are motivated and able
to act on it. These components of the behavioural output will be investigated in the following
sections, along with the actual physiological and psychological regulation achieved through the
biofeedback practice.
3.1.1
Perception
The perception and understanding of the biofeedback is a fundamental requirement for the
system to have any eﬀect. This perception can be inﬂuenced in a lot of diﬀerent ways and with
diﬀerent purpose.
Aﬀecting user perception can be a goal in itself. Green, Green and Walters demonstrated that
changes in people’s mental-emotional state can aﬀect their physiological activity and vice versa
[20]. This means that changes in perceived stress can actually lead to changes in physiological
stress, as later demonstrated by Oldehinkel et al. [43]. By communicating the biofeedback in a
way that celebrates progression or makes the task of decreasing it seem less overwhelming, the
feedback can help change the user’s perception of their own stress [34]
Another fundamental objective is to communicate data of a high quality - easily interpretable
information that is useful to the subject. This can be done in numerous ways, and many diﬀerent
methods have been tested in literature. Yu et al. found in their review of biofeedback systems
for stress management, that there was a general need for more consistency, and suggested
43
==PAGE:47==
CHAPTER 3. MODEL AND HYPOTHESES
introducing more HCI principles in the communication of data [65]. As an example, Miller’s
law [38] tells us that we can only keep about 7 things in our working memory and proposes
to keep the number of elements in an HCI to a minimum, as too many can generate confusion
and a loss of focus. Osman et al. elaborates this in their article on ubiquitous biofeedback:
”In our opinion, one of the focal goals of biofeedback is to relay as much informa-
tion as possible to individuals regarding their psychophysical status and thus better
acquainting them with their bodies. Nonetheless, users should not be overwhelmed
with huge amounts of data that they cannot process or understand. A careful balance
must be established to relay just the right amount of information.” [3]
This emphasises how the quantity of information can compromise the quality. It is therefore
important that the right balance is not found.
3.1.2
Motivation and ability
Fogg’s behaviour model for persuasive design suggests that behaviour is the product of three
factors: Motivation, ability, and triggers [19]. This understanding is essential when designing
biofeedback systems for self-use, as the success of the biofeedback relies on the user’s own
initiative and adherence to the practice.
Firstly, the user must have the ability to perform the desired practice. While the actual training
involved in biofeedback for stress management is often rather simple (relaxation/respiration
exercises), the change demand can be diﬃcult.
The user has to integrate it into a work-
schedule, which is, for many, already packed. This is a central issue to this thesis, as the ability
to practice it regularly can be eased through proper timing of the feedback and by ﬁnding the
most convenient type of regulation.
Motivation is closely related to ability, as the two can trade oﬀ[19]. If the action is diﬃcult to do
(the user’s ability is low), the motivation has to be high for user to take action. Similarly, if the
behaviour is easy, motivation doesn’t have to be as high for the action to be performed. In the
case of biofeedback for everyday stress management, the practice very much relies on intrinsic
motivation, as there are no apparent extrinsic factors (e.g. rewards or punishment). Even
though the motivation has to come from within, there are lots of ways that biofeedback can
promote this intrinsic motivation. Some researches have tried incorporating gaming elements
in biofeedback systems [44] [2] [5], while others include points and achievements [41] [26], or
insights into progression [3].
44
==PAGE:48==

Triggers are also an important component in designing eﬀective biofeedback systems. Without
a trigger, the target behaviour isn’t initiated, even if both motivation and ability are high.
Triggers can take many forms, as they can both come from within, like a growling stomach
reminding you to eat, or from external prompts like notiﬁcations, sounds, or cues in your
surroundings.
Timing is a key element of a trigger - ﬁnding the right moment where both motivation and
ability is high enough to initiate the behaviour [19].
The general idea behind continuous
biofeedback systems, is to increase the number of times that the user is triggered and made
aware of their stress level by incorporating the feedback as a salient part of the environment
[64].
3.1.3
Regulation
The core behavioural output of biofeedback is regulation. In this context, the term, regulation,
encompasses inﬂuencing one’s physiological or psychological state.
As previously discussed
through Green, Green and Walter’s body-mind loop [20], the two dimensions of body and mind
aﬀect one another, as a change in mind-set can induce physiological changes and vice versa.
For stress management, users most often focus in a single self-regulation goal based on the
physiological data, such as achieving a sine-wave-like IBI wave and thereby a high heart rate
variability [65]. The immediate feedback allows the user to better control these physiological
parameters - for example through resonant breathing [31].
Biofeedback systems commonly support users in developing introceptive skills - an increased
awareness of psychological of physiological activity [34]. Strengthening these skills helps the
users regulate and trigger behavioural change by noticing more cues that you are stressed. For
example, Peck et al. found that headaches could be relieved by becoming better at noticing
tension in shoulders and relaxing through biofeedback [45].
Biofeedback systems for heart rate variability training works by conditioning the autonomic
nervous system (ANS). When a higher HRV is achieved through the practice, the ANS becomes
better at reaching this state on its own, lessening the severity of the body’s stress response in
general. Consequently, the behavioural output sometimes contain an unconscious element that
doesn’t manifest itself in the form of a deliberate behavioural change.

H1: Biofeedback users are more aware of their stress levels during continuous
biofeedback, compared to discontinuous
A fundamental element to regulating your stress, is understanding the data and being aware

of your stress. With a continuous stream of real-time information on stress levels, the user is
able to gain a better understanding of their physiological / psychological state [34]. This is at
least the assumption in a range of studies exploring continuous biofeedback systems [64] [35]
[16]. However, none of these studies actually measure the level of awareness / attention that
the user’s put on their stress levels.
While continuous biofeedback exposes the user to more information over time, it also risks
becoming an unnoticeable part of the background, as it relies on the user’s peripheral attention.
Because the human brain is only able to focus on one thing at a time, the user has to shift
their attention back and forth, which might not be likely when placed in front of other more
important tasks. A discontinuous use case might therefore be more eﬀective, as the users full
attention is deliberately shifted towards the feedback for a while.
H2: Biofeedback users are more aware of their stress levels during active regu-
lation, compared to passive
One of the central elements to biofeedback, is the feedback loop that occurs, when the user is
able to see their physiology change in real time. This perception of changes in physiological
states, such as stress responses, fosters changes in the mental-state, like becoming more relaxed,
which then again inﬂuences the physiological state. Inﬂuence on this mind-body loop [20] is
made possible by the awareness and information that the user gets through the biofeedback[3].
This hypothesis investigates whether active regulation enhances this loop of change in mentality,
physiology and awareness by focusing on the type of regulation’s inﬂuence on the latter. By
actively inﬂuencing one’s physiological state and enabling the user to see the immediate eﬀect,
it is hypothesised that active regulation promotes higher levels of awareness than passive.
H3: Less eﬀort is put into continuous biofeedback than discontinuous
The amount of eﬀort put into a task gives a variety of insights into the user’s state. As previously
mentioned, eﬀort can be an expression of motivation, as people are simply more likely to do
their best at something they are motivated by [59]. It’s also an expression of importance, as one
tends to work on whichever task seems more relevant/important [13]. Lastly, it is inﬂuenced
by ability - if a task is very diﬃcult, more eﬀort is needed to perform it [19].
This hypothesis explores the relationship between motivation and ability. The basic assump-
tion is, that regulation demands attention on one’s physiology. Continuous feedback relies on
50
==PAGE:54==

peripheral attention, while discontinuous requires that the user focus fully on the regulation.
Because others tasks demand attention from the user during a continuous use case, less mental
capacity is left to focus on regulating stress.
Familiarity also plays a role, as the act of regulating stress is novel to most people. This makes
it less intuitive and presents a barrier to start regulating, as it is easier to revert to known tasks
and forgot to put eﬀort into regulating stress.
It is therefore hypothesised, that the users put less eﬀort into relaxing during continuous biofeed-
back, as they are more motivated by the immediate task at hand, rather than to regulating
their stress.
H4: More eﬀort is put into active regulation compared to passive
Similarly to H2, this hypothesis investigates whether the loop of increased awareness through
feedback, leads to a change in mentality and thereby physiology.
For this loop to work, not only does the user have to stay aware of their stress, they also have
to put an eﬀort into actually relaxing. This hypothesis seeks to investigate, whether active
regulation guidance increases the subjects ability and motivation to relax, compared to passive
regulation
During active regulation practices, the user has their full attention towards controlling their
physiological parameters. With this increased focus on relaxing, it is hypothesised, that the
subjects will try harder to decrease their stress levels when they are given clear instructions on
how, and the eﬀect is made visible. During passive regulation, it might be easier to forget to
relax, as the user has to stay aware of their stress levels while working on other tasks.
H5: Continuous biofeedback results in lower perceived competence than discon-
tinuous
Generally, it is more motivating to do something that you are good at, than something you feel
incapable of [59]. Perceived competence is therefore a good way to measure intrinsic motivation,
as the important factor is how good the participants felt they were doing – not how well they
actually performed.
Another insight that perceived competence provides, is how diﬃcult the task was. It might
therefore reveal if a particular type of biofeedback practice is easier than another. Here, the

assumption is, that having to keep track on continuous feedback during other tasks is more
diﬃcult than doing one thing at a time. This would make the participants feel less competent
during continuous biofeedback, compared to a discontinuous administration.
H6: Perceived competence is highest during active regulation, compared to pas-
sive
This hypothesis examines how good the subjects felt they were at actively regulating stress
responses through respiration guidance, compared to a more self-reliant passive regulation
practice.
On the one hand, perceived competence naturally increases when explicit instructions are given,
and the individual is able to see an immediate positive eﬀect through the biofeedback. On the
other hand, the lack of progress also becomes very visible through active regulating. Because
of this, perceived competence and motivation can drop if the active regulation fails to have an
eﬀect, even though the user is following the practice.
In general, it assumed that active regulation is easier to practice than passive, as more feedback
and instructions are given.
The higher quantity of information should enable the users to
practice better and become more motivated to decrease stress. It is therefore hypothesised that
active regulation will have the users feel more competent than passive regulation.
H7: Continuous biofeedback is more mentally demanding than discontinuous
The user’s ability to regulate stress is a vital component. The key to integrating biofeedback
practice into a busy work routine, is therefore making the regulation practice as easy as possible.
As only one type of continuity is typically tested in most studies, [65] it is diﬃcult to say whether
a discontinuous or continuous biofeedback practise is easier. This hypothesis examines the
mental demand / diﬃculty of the two diﬀerent continuities that biofeedback can be practised
in.
The general assumption is that focusing on one thing at a time is easiest, and that the subjects
therefore will ﬁnd continuous biofeedback more demanding than discontinuous feedback.

This hypothesis tests whether active regulation is easier to practice than passive. Whether
clear instructions and control of regulation - such as guiding respiration - is more mentally
demanding than relaxing without actively inﬂuencing your physiology (passive regulation).
H9: Continuous biofeedback results in a lower HRV, compared to discontinuous
Maclean et al. found in their experiment with a continuous biofeedback system during stressful
scenarios (a driving simulator) that the subjects became more stressed by the feedback [35].
Continuous feedback requires, that the user stays attentive of the feedback and shifts their
attention back and forth between the task at hand and relaxing. Because the stressful task was
more demanding than the feedback / regulation of stress levels, more energy was put into the
stressful task, and not enough into relaxing.
A positive feedback loop might therefore have occurred. The more stressed the subjects could
see themselves become, the more urgency was put on the task of regulating. However, if the
subject is fully occupied with another task, this regulation would demand too much attention,
causing further stress / frustration from not being able to do anything about the increasing
stress levels.
This hypothesis investigates, whether continuous feedback can induce more stress, if practised
in a stressful scenario.
H10: Active regulation results in a higher HRV than passive
One of the key objectives of biofeedback systems (formulated by Al Osman [3]), is to relay as
much information to the user on a physiological parameter as possible (without the subject
being overwhelmed by the amount of information).
As active regulation provides the best
premise to do this, it is hypothesised that this works best in increasing the subjects’ heart rate
variability.
Still, Yu’s experiment with a passive biofeedback system (a tree representing the user’s stress
levels) [64], found that the subjects were able to relax better with the feedback present. This
stands in contrast to Maclean’s experiment where the subjects became more stressed from the
passive feedback [35]. One of the major diﬀerences in the two experiments was the setting.
Maclean’s subjects were exposed to a stressful environment (driving), while Yu’s participants
were practising the regulation in a quiet environment. This shows the importance of the setting

Chapter 4
Empirical study
This chapter covers an empirical study on how biofeedback’s timing and type of regulation
inﬂuence the user. An experiment of a within-subject design have been conducted with 33 sub-
jects who are exposed to various stress- and relaxation conditions. The participants’ subjective
inputs and bio-metric data make up the foundation for the analysis, as the changes induced by
the diﬀerent conditions are examined within each subject.
1
Experimental design
In order to examine the subjects’ ability to regulate stress under various conditions, each subject
will be exposed to alternating stressful tasks and relaxation sessions. The experimental design
is inspired by Osman et al. who tested the eﬀect of a ubiquitous biofeedback system in a similar
way [3] (seen in ﬁg. 4.1).
Figure 4.1: Al Osman’s sequence of experiment sessions [3]
The control- and stress-session comprised of a series of simple math problems that the subject
had to solve within a time-limit. In the control session, the subjects had 7 seconds for each
problem, and HRV data was collected with an ECG sensor for a baseline measurement. During
the stress session, the time-limit was lowered to 2 seconds, making the task much more stressful.

After the stress session, the subjects would practice resting with the biofeedback system, making
it possible to see how eﬀective system was in decreasing stress.
After each session, a break was given, where the subjects were asked to rate their emotions - how
stressed, nervous, happy, angry, and bored they felt. This allowed for the immediate subjective
inputs on the participants experience, which could be compared with the physiological data.
The experiment conducted here follows a similar structure, but contains a few more sessions,
as each session represents a diﬀerent condition to be tested.

Biofeedback is practised in both relaxing and stressful contexts. When investigating the inde-
pendent variable of timing and continuity, the most relevant context becomes a busy or stressful
workday. In this context, biofeedback systems have been developed to increase awareness of
your stress levels continuously throughout the day while working [64] [16] [35] and discontinu-

[44]. The stress tasks are therefore used to investigate what inﬂuence a change in continuity
(continuous vs. discontinuous) has on the behavioural output. Stress 1 investigates the contin-

Stress 2 encompass the discontinuous use case, as it consists of a mid-break, where the subject

ﬁg. 4.3 places stress 2 in the intersection between active and passive regulation, as the dis-

To investigate active vs passive regulation, two sessions are composed in the experiment -
relaxation 1 where the subject will relax and check their HRV from time to time (passive),
and relaxation 2, where the subject will relax with guided respiration and visible HRV (active).
Both of these sessions are based on discontinuous feedback, as a continuous use case, where
the subject practices biofeedback in a relaxed setting for a long period of time, is considered
unlikely/superﬂuous and generally not found in literature.
The feedback was given through the open source programme, OpenHRV [8], which presents IBI
intervals, a HRV score, and a variable respiration guide (commonly set to 0.1 Hz / 6 breaths a
minute, as proposed by Lehrer [31]).

Although there are tons of ways to visualise biofeedback, this type of HRV waveform seen on
top in ﬁg. 4.4 is the most commonly used visualisation for stress management, and the one
proposed by Kennedy & Parker to standardise the ﬁeld [29].
The stress sessions comprised of randomly generated math problems (multiplication, addition,
or subtraction) with a semi-controlled diﬃculty. For example, the two factors in multiplication
problems were generated to always have a sum between 11 and 17. A few other adaptations
from Osman’s version [3] were also made after some initial testing with a few subjects. Instead
of typing in the answer, three options were presented in a multiple choice format, as controlling
a mouse is easier for more people than having to type fast. Three seconds were given for each
math problem instead of two, but each time an answer was clicked, a now problem would

As with any new task, there’s a learning curve. Therefore, the subjects were guided through a
demo-session which would familiarise them with the test-format. Furthermore, the order of the
stress- and relaxation sessions were randomised to mitigate the eﬀect of the learning-curve (the

All subjects went through a small onboarding process, where the procedure was explained and

4.2.1
Awareness and understanding / Situational Awareness Rating Technique
The Situational Awareness Rating Technique (also known as SART) is a seven point rating
scale to measure situational awareness. It contains three overall dimesions: 1) demands on
attentional resoursces, 2) supply of attentional resources, and 3) understanding of the situation
[55]. The latter of the three dimensions have been used in the experiment to quantify the
type of awareness that is interesting to the hypotheses. The sub-scale contains three questions,
where the subjects are asked to rate (from 1 = low, to 7 = high) the quantity of information
62
==PAGE:66==

on their stress, the quality of information and how familiar they are with reading it.
The ﬁrst two questions on the quantity and quality of the biofeedback information that the
participants received is asked after each session in the experiment.
Since the biofeedback
interface was new to all participants, the third question on their familiarity with this type of
information was used in the pre-questionnaire to determine their general level of experience in
tracking data on their health and behaviour.
4.2.2
Eﬀort, perceived competence, and pressure / Intrinsic Motivation Inventory
Eﬀort, perceived competence, and pressure are all variables to determine how motivated the
subject is to regulate their stress during the biofeedback. To measure this, the Intrinsic Mo-
tivation Inventory (IMI) [13] was used. This consists of seven Likert scales on the following
parameters: Interest/enjoyment, perceived competence, eﬀort/importance, pressure/tension,
Perceived choice, value/usefulness and relatedness.
The sub-scales all contain 5-6 statements, which are answered on a 7 point scale ranging
from 1 = ”Not at all true”, to 7 = ”Very true”. Having 5-6 diﬀerent statements for each
variable mitigates possible ambiguities/misunderstandings, increasing the overall reliability of
the answers. Furthermore, all statements across the scales are presented in a randomised order
to prevent bias.
Eﬀort/importance and perceived competence was chosen among the IMI sub-scales to reﬂect
motivation, as they were deemed most relevant in a biofeedback use-case. The level of eﬀort
highlights how driven the subject was in regulating their stress, and perceived competence gives
an indication of how much control and ability to regulate the subjects felt they possessed.
Motivation is especially diﬃcult to measure in this case, as the subjects have two tasks at hand
during the stress session - solving the math problems and keeping their stress low. To avoid
ambiguities, each question was carefully rephrased to focus on stress regulation. For example,
the IMI question on eﬀort / importance was rephrased from it’s generic form: ”I put a lot of
eﬀort into this.” to ”I put a lot of eﬀort into lowering my stress levels.”.
The IMIs sub-scale for pressure / tension was also used - partly to investigate motivation, but
also to examine the mental task load, speciﬁcally, along with the NASA-TLX described in the
next section.

4.2.3
Mental task load / NASA Task load index
The mental task load is examined to determine the participants ability to regulate stress during
each of the sessions in the experiment. NASA created a task load index (NASA-TLX) which
has been validated through countless experiments [23]. The index contains 6 parameters, which
is rated through sliders from low (0) to high (100): Mental demand, physical demand, temporal
demand, eﬀort, performance, and frustration level.
As this experiment had no physical demand, this parameter was excluded from the scale. While
some of the other parameters, such as eﬀort, performance and frustration level were similar to
some of the questions asked in the Likert scale, the repetition makes for a more robust analysis
afterwards, and a higher validity in terms of keeping the NASA-TLX format.

the biofeedback. Therefore, the heart rate variability was be measured throughout the whole
experiment via the programme OpenHRV [8]. The mean variability would then be calculated

Overview of analytic methods
The hypotheses are all investigated in paired tests comparing the outcome of one condition to
another (e.g. passive vs. active regulation). The exact test depends on whether the sample is
normally distributed or not. If it has a normal distribution, a parametric test, such as a paired
samples t-test, is conducted. If it is not, a non-parametric test like the Mann-Whitney U-test
or the Wilcoxon Signed Rank test is carried out. In this case, where the comparison is made
between two dependant samples from the same set of people, a Wilcoxon Signed Rank test is
deemed most correct, as a Mann-Whitney U would require independant samples.
To determine whether the data sets are normally distributed, a Shapiro-Wilk test is performed
for each data set. This test is most suitable (compared to the common Kolmogorow Smirnov
test) due to the fairly low sample size. If the p-value is greater than 0.05, the data is considered
normally distributed.
Furthermore, to ensure internal consistency and reliability across the Likert scales, Cronbach’s
alpha is calculated for each scale in all of the four sessions.

H1: Biofeedback users are more aware of their stress levels during continuous

The analysis of awareness is mainly based on two questions from the Situational Awareness

two stress sessions and relaxation sessions.

information quantity and -quality scores during the two sessions
Although the summed score was slightly lower during the continuous stress session (M=7.55,

signiﬁcant diﬀerence (t(32)=-3.15, p=0.377).
Therefore, it can’t be concluded that the higher exposure of information achieved during con-
tinuous biofeedback, results in a higher awareness, compared to discontinuous feedback.

Chapter 6
Discussion
Firstly, this section will review the implications of the main ﬁndings from the analysis. There-
after, these ﬁndings will be put into perspective, when their implementation in a real-world
context is discussed. Next, the limitations of the study will be covered, and the possible future
work reﬂected upon.
1
Discussion of main ﬁndings
This project has picked up on some of the research directions that previous reviews of biofeed-
back for stress management have suggested [65] [34]. Among these, there seem to be a consensus
that a new biofeedback paradigm is emerging, where the users are no longer reliant on a ther-
apist to guide the practice. Instead, convenient and ubiquitous self-management systems are
the key in integrating biofeedback into an every-day scenario [65] [3]. To do this, key issues in
the way that biofeedback systems are designed must ﬁrst be investigated.
The main questions that the analysis has sought to uncover, regard the eﬀectiveness of active-
versus passive regulation, and continuous versus discontinuous biofeedback. These questions
will be reviewed separately, where after they will be related and the implementation of the
most promising combination discussed.
Continuous- versus discontinuous feedback
Historically, the most common way to practice biofeedback is through discontinuous sessions,
where the users take a break from work and focus on stress regulation training [52]. However,
some researches have begun to explore continuous feedback systems where the users are contin-
76
==PAGE:80==
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
uously exposed to their stress levels while working on other tasks [64] [16] [35] [2] [6]. The idea is
to make it easier to integrate the routine into a busy workday, make the user more aware of their
stress throughout the day, and continuously trigger relaxation when the feedback is noticed [34]
[65]. The eﬀect of such a system does, however, rest on the assumption that the user’s are actu-
ally made more aware of their stress from the continuous feedback. The experiment conducted
for this thesis showed that the higher exposure of information during continuous biofeedback
did not result in a signiﬁcantly higher awareness, compared to discontinuous feedback (H1).
The reason why the higher exposure to feedback doesn’t result in higher awareness might be
that it is more diﬃcult to shift one’s attention back and forth between the feedback/relaxation
and the task at hand. Although the results were not statistically signiﬁcant, this is somewhat
supported by the slightly higher mental demand found during continuous feedback (H7).
While it was not possible to conclude on a statistically signiﬁcant level whether continuous or
discontinuous feedback induced the most awareness (H1), motivation, (H3 & H5) or which was
more mentally demanding, (H7) the results from the analysis of HRV (H9) showed that contin-
uous feedback was signiﬁcantly less eﬀective at increasing HRV than discontinuous feedback.
The relaxation practice involved in the discontinuous biofeedback more than doubled the heart
rate variability in many cases. In this experiment, the inﬂuence of the peak in HRV is very
large as the whole session only lasted 4 minutes. However, when the stress session after the
mid-break were isolated, a signiﬁcant increase in HRV was still found.
This study therefore points to discontinuous biofeedback as the best approach to increase heart
rate variability.
Active versus passive regulation
Passive biofeedback systems are becoming a more and more common. Tracking capabilities
in smartphones, smartwatches, and other health gadgets make way for a trend in monitoring
one’s own health and behaviour, such as sleep patterns, step counts, etc. [47].
Making users more aware of these parameters provides them with the ability to better under-
stand their behaviour and do something about it. Stress is a clear-cut candidate for this type
of monitoring, as it can go unnoticed and become quite severe without the person even noticing
[14]. Developing introceptive skills and becoming more aware of one’s own stress response helps
prevent this type of stress creep [34].
Passive biofeedback forces the user to think about what they, themselves, can do to mitigate
77
==PAGE:81==
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
their own stress. Active regulation provides guidance on how to do so, but doesn’t guarantee
insights into the bigger picture, as it is practised momentarily and only involves immediate
real-time feedback. The experiment presented in this thesis found that active regulation did
convey a higher quantity of information than passive-, but not necessarily of higher quality
(H2).
The higher quantity of information speaks to the eﬃciency of active regulation in reducing
immediate stress.
Furthermore, the study also found that active regulation does induce a
higher HRV on a statistically signiﬁcant level (H10).
However, the beneﬁcial eﬀects of an
active regulation might only be a treatment of a symptom, rather than the cause. Without the
users getting an insight into their own behavioural patterns (e.g. when and why stress occurs),
it becomes easy to fall back into old habits and diﬃcult to prevent stress from reoccurring.
Therefore, passive feedback is important for the user to identify the underlying causes and
break unhealthy patterns by increasing awareness of stress throughout the day. On the other
hand, one could argue that active regulation also has an important preventative eﬀect, as it
builds resilience to stress by conditioning one’s ANS/physiology through active training [30].
In this thesis, active and passive regulation were tested up against each other to provide insights
into their diﬀerent behavioural outputs. However, one does not exclude the other. A single
biofeedback system could very well aﬀord both active regulation (e.g. through respiratory sinus
arrhythmia training to increase HRV) while still keeping the user’s informed of their day-to-day
stress and larger behavioural patterns through passive biofeedback.
How such a system would be implemented in a real-world context will be discussed in the
following section.

Implementation
2.1
practical implications
As the previous section has reviewed, discontinuous biofeedback is more eﬀective in regulating
stress than continuous biofeedback. Furthermore, active regulation worked best in increasing
HRV, although a passive approach based on feedback about stress/behavioural patterns brings
a beneﬁcial element to managing and preventing stress in general.
When discussing the implementation of such a biofeedback system, the behavioural constraints
are critical. From a technical side, it could be viable to integrate the system into an exist-
78
==PAGE:82==
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
ing technological architecture. Smartphones, for example, makes for a versatile and portable
medium, which most people already carry. The only draw-back is the multi-functionality of
the device, which introduces e-mails, notiﬁcations and other possible disruptions to the active
regulation, which requires focus on the practice itself. However, this could possibly be solved
with a feature disabling notiﬁcations during the regulation practice itself.
To capture the physiological data, this thesis has proposed integrating the sensor technology
into headphones, as these are portable and minimally intrusive (as encouraged by Osman [3]).
The technical feasibility of incorporating PPG sensors and measuring HRV have already been
validated [46], and the robustness of the placement enables a fairly steady and reliable stream
of data.
It also plays very well together with a discontinuous use case, as headphones are often not worn
for long stretches of time. Furthermore, it accommodates the novelty of the change demand as
most people are more comfortable putting on a set of headphones rather than strapping on an
unfamiliar device to capture data.
The discontinuous use case comes with social constraints, as the active regulation practice
requires a social distance to coworkers or other people around you.
Physically, it can be
practised in both a room alone or among people, but socially, the user has to turn their
attention inwards and focus on regulating. Headphones help create a personal sphere, isolating
the user from the social environment. Not only does it signal to people around you, that you are
occupied, one could also imagine using noise-cancelling eﬀects or music to make the experience
more immersive and increase focus on the biofeedback [39].
While headphones allow for an eﬀective discontinuous use case, they favour active regulation
over passive. Passive regulation is most eﬀective when the user wears the sensors for longer
stretches of time. This enables a more extensive collection of data on one’s stress patterns,
and allows for timely notiﬁcations to about their stress levels. If the headphones are only worn
once in a while for the active regulation sessions, meetings, or similar, only a narrow view into
the overall stress pattern can be seen. Ideally, the headphones would be worn all day to collect
as much data as possible. This might, however, be uncomfortable for the user or result in an
unwanted dissociation with the social environment, as wearing headphones in an oﬃce space
tends to signal unavailability.
This inability to gather data over longer periods of time might be mitigated if the headphones
are of a true-wireless / earbud kind, as the user wouldn’t have to wear both sides, but the
feasibility of such a solution would have to be tested among users.

Theoretical & research implications
In the process of reviewing the current literature and unravelling the key design issues, a research
framework was created. This framework, consisting of a use case, representation of data, and
behavioural output, could bring some much needed structure to the ﬁeld of biofeedback, which

matrix [10], the representation of data incorporates Michie’s taxonomy of behaviour change

3
Limitations
The experiment examined biofeedback practices in two extreme scenarios - either when fully
relaxed, or during a very stressful task. While the extremities made ground for the most clear
results in an experimental setting, tasks as stressful as the one practised in the experiment are
rarely found in an every-day work-setting. It would therefore have been interesting to include a
moderately stressful task as well, to see if the tendencies found by looking at the two extremes
were representative of this as well.
Another limitation to the study is the time-frame of each session. Continuous biofeedback
can be practised over a full day’s work, but the session conducted here only lasted 3 minutes.
The short time-frame was a consequence of the within-subjects test design, as all participants
were exposed to each of the conditions that were tested. With a between-subject design, each
participant could have been exposed to only one condition, and would therefore have been able
practice for longer time. This would, however, have required a lot more participants to reach
a decent sample size for each condition. Another huge advantage to the within-subjects design
is that it is less likely that an actual diﬀerence between the conditions will stay undetected
due to random noise between the groups. Since the inﬂuences of the conditions are compared
within each subject, the diﬀerences among the participants won’t aﬀect the relative increase or
decrease in HRV, motivation, etc..
Nevertheless, the short timeframe brings uncertainties to the conclusions of the experiment.
While H9 conﬁrmed that discontinuous biofeedback increased HRV more than continuous, it
is not certain how long the eﬀect would last. This has, however, been investigated by other
studies, which found biofeedback to have long-lasting eﬀects that are sustained after the practice
is ended [33] [41] [48].
Likert scales were used in the breaks after each session to investigate the subjects’ subjective
state. With each scale consisting of 5-6 questions, this amounted to a lot of positions to take
for each participant. On top of this, the same questions were asked four times in total - once
after each break.

Future work
This study has examined the combinations of the two independent variables that are most
prevalent in literature. When investigating active vs. passive regulation, the continuity was kept
constant for both sessions to isolate the inﬂuence of the regulation type. Both the active- and
passive regulation session were therefore discontinuous, as this is the most common approach.
It would, however, have been very interesting to also include a comparison of a continuous
passive- and active regulation session. While these type of systems are not common, Moraveji
has explored an active continuous regulation system, which guides the users’ respiration through
peripheral feedback [40]. This active peripheral regulation would allow the user to practice
continuously while working on other tasks, conditioning their physiology to maximise HRV.
The eﬃciency of such a system would therefore be interesting to look further into in a future
study.
Although the experiment only focused on the continuity of the feedback and the type of regula-
tion, the research framework contains several other inﬂuential components that would be worth
exploring further. In this study, the visual display of HRV was limited to a color-graded scale
with a score and a tachogram of the live IBI. While visual displays have been shown to be the
most eﬀective [68] and widely used sensory modality [65], the diﬀerent forms/visualisations of

the feedback is yet to be tested. The inﬂuence of the metaphorical component is also currently
unclear, even though it varies greatly among the systems from trees [64] to simple bar charts
[41], to an oasis with animals [27].
Another element that would be interesting to examine if the time had allowed for a broader
scope, is the inﬂuence of the user’s stress level on their motivation to practice. One could
imagine, that a user who have - or have had - problems with stress, would be more intrinsi-
cally motivated to practice regulation and gain insights into their stress response. While this
experiment did collect data on the subjects’ immediate perceived stress levels within the past
month with the PSS-4 scale [63], it was only used as a screening to possibly exclude people with
severe stress. This data could also have been used as a factor in the analysis to investigate the
diﬀerence in motivation among stressed, and non-stressed people. This would provide further
insight into the research of Kennedy & Parker, who found that biofeedback was most eﬀective
among people who had stressful occupations, or were dealing with stress [29].
The thesis has also discussed the practical implications of incorporating a biofeedback system
into a headset. If such an idea was to be realised, more extensive research would have to be
done among potential users, to determine if this is a viable option. Since biofeedback is an
unfamiliar concept to most people, the ﬁrst barrier to it’s adoptation would be communicating
it’s functionality and beneﬁts. One approach would be to create mock-ups and pretotypes of
the user interface to use as boundary objects when discussing what type of insights and beneﬁts
the biofeedback system could provide with potential users.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The aim of the thesis was to understand the diﬀerent components and boundary conditions that
make up the biofeedback practice, identify key issues within biofeedback for stress management,
and investigate their inﬂuence on the user’s behavioural output.
The review of literature revealed a huge variety of ways to design such biofeedback self-
management systems without much structure or motivation behind each design.
Some re-
searches suggest that continuous feedback systems with ambient displays or sound, might be
an eﬀective way to regulate stress [64] [16] [35] [2] [6]. This would enable the users to practice
biofeedback while working and build introceptive skills and awareness to better manage stress
throughout the day [34].
This practice opposes to the conventional discontinuous way of practising biofeedback, where
the users practice regulation their stress response once or twice a day in a ”session” format [52]
[30]. This way, the users are able to put their full attention on actively regulating the stress
response, rather than having to shift between the task at hand and the feedback.
It was therefore hypothesised that discontinuous biofeedback and active regulation would be
more eﬀective in increasing heart rate variability (HRV) than continuous feedback and passive
regulation, respectively.
Besides these, hypotheses were created to investigate the type of regulation and continuity’s
inﬂuence on the user’s awareness of their stress level along with their motivation and ability to
regulate it.
While no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was found on the last-mentioned hypotheses, an experiment
84
==PAGE:88==
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
(N=33) showed that discontinuous feedback and active regulation was more eﬀective in in-
creasing HRV than continuous and passive regulation. The experiment exposed the subjects
to alternating stress- and relaxation sessions, where the diﬀerent types of biofeedback was
administered. The biofeedback was based on respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA), where the
participants would try to maximise their HRV through a respiration guide.
The implementation of such a system was then discussed, and a concept integrating PPG sensor
technology into headphones was proposed. This would allow users to track HRV, monitor their
stress levels, and facilitate an RSA biofeedback practice. The headphones, paired with a phone
delivering the feedback, would make for a portable and versatile solution to manage one’s own
stress.
Self-management systems like this are becoming increasingly relevant. Stress is on the rise [24]
and the work environment has been disrupted by the current COVID-19 pandemic. Not only
are people working more [12], the hybrid structure mixing on-site and remote work challenges
the work-life balance [28].
All in all, this thesis has found biofeedback to be a promising and powerful tool for the individual
to manage- and build resilience towards stress in a modern work environment.

It was ﬁrst assumed that the scoping literature review would be a central part to the thesis,
as the inconsistencies in the literature called for a more thorough analysis. Data points from
relevant articles on biofeedback for stress management were therefore collected and structured,
as seen in appendix A. This would provide insights on key components from the research
framework. Emerging similarities would be classiﬁed in an inductive process forming guidelines
to summarise patterns in the design of - and motivation behind - biofeedback systems.
In this process it became clear, that there were many more architectural diﬀerences in the design
of the biofeedback systems. Without having the fundamental elements of the practice in place,
it therefore seemed superﬂuous to investigate the inﬂuence of metaphors in the visualisations,
which was initially the main objective. To do that, the metaphors would have to be compared
across biofeedback systems designed on very diﬀerent premises.
Although fairly late into the project, it was decided to pivot and change the scope of the project.
Rather than focusing on the metaphorical component and visualisation, the type of regulation
and continuity of the feedback became focal points of the thesis. These elements were much
more deﬁning for the overall biofeedback practice, and therefore important cornerstones to build
an understanding of how biofeedback could be integrated into everyday stress management.
The new scope ﬁt nicely into the research framework by moving the emphasis from the metaphor-
ical component to the sensory input and behaviour change mechanism in the representation of
data. The information gathered in the literature study (appendix A) therefore still provided
valuable insights.

Project management
The management of this project has been driven by two very diﬀerent methodologies. On the
one hand, Jabra’s use case implied a user-driven design approach that would explore what value
could be created for their customers.
On the other hand, the project very much been guided by the classic research funnel, as
the exploratory research of current literature amounted to a research framework that could
synthesise the knowledge found in other studies/literature. Based on this, hypotheses were
formed and a formal experiment was designed to test the inﬂuence of key independent variables.
The management of the project has been mostly driven by scientiﬁc research. However, inputs
from users and relevant stakeholders were gathered throughout the project to provide context
to the insights found in research. For example, Jesper Glad (Director of IPR Development
