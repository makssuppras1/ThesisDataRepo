Abstract 
The aim of this project was to produce high yields of hydrogen from mixture of methanol and water 
emitting low-levels of the byproducts such as carbon monoxide and also by using low temperature 
steam reformer cells.   
Fuel cells require fuel to produce hydrogen, there are several methods of producing hydrogen from 
a fossil fuel resource, but this project require a steam reforming cell method, by using methanol 
and water as the fuel, the theoretical amount of hydrogen yield produce should be about 75% and 
the emitted amount of carbon dioxide yield should be about 25% at the same time a very low 
amount of carbon monoxide should be emitted 
Methanol stem reformer cell was filled with MDK-20 catalyst of Cu/ZnO in the catalytic reformer. 
The mixture of water/methanol was at a ratio 1.5/1,0. The reformer was tested under atmospheric 
pressure for temperature at 240°C, 260°C and 280°C. Then the reformer was also tested over 
pressure of 0,5 bar -1 bar at a temperature of 260°C and 280°C. The amount of product gases, 
under these conditions, was measured with the help of the HP 6890 GC, by using two different 
columns and two different carrier gas. Molecular sieve 5Å column with nitrogen as carrier gas to 
detect hydrogen gas and Molecular sieve 5Å column with helium as a carrier gas to detect carbon 
monoxide gas while BoraBund U column with hilum as carrier gas to detect carbon dioxide. 
From the data’s collected, it was concluded that a low amount of emission of CO2 and CO was 
emitted and a high conversion of hydrogen yield was achieved at a temperature 280°C and at 
atmospheric pressure.

1. Introduction 
Clean sources of energy are one of the world’s biggest concerns. Problems including environmental 
pollution are major source for seeking into green sources of energy. Hydrogen, the ultimate energy 
carrier, represents a solution of one of the world’s biggest problems(1). 
To maintain technological development and life quality wealth, the world nowadays is searching for 
a sustainable energy system that can assure increased supply and increased energy security, and at 
the same time putting considerations on the emissions that is been emitted in the environment. 
Hydrogen and fuel cells are strategic technologies that ca be used to great these aims. 
This has led to different Researchers have come together to work on finding a way that will involve 
methanol steam reforming for PEM fuel cells. The European Commission also funded two projects 
to develop onboard fuel processors for fuel cell vehicles as part of the JOULE II project. (2).  
Hydrogen is considered one of the key starting materials that can be used on its own or with other 
elements in a wide range of applications for many years, it can be used in chemical reaction to 
produce energy, substituting combustion reactions with chemical reaction has a huge impact on 
both environment and economy(3). Hydrogen can be produced from a number of sources, such as 
water, hydrocarbon fuels, biomass, hydrogen sulfide, boron hydrides, and chemical elements with 
hydrogen. Hydrogen can be produced by different technologies; hydrocarbons reforming, 
electrolysis and thermolysis. However, hydrocarbons reforming is the dominant technology.  
Hydrocarbons reforming, as the first candidate for green energy sources, have gained a global 
attention. Reforming plants around the world produce about 5x1011N m3/ year(4).  
Efforts have been made to produce hydrogen by processes involving solar energy, wind energy, 
nuclear energy and biofuels. However, fossil fuels remain the major choice. The technology of 
producing hydrogen from fossil fuels is known as Steam reforming technology.  
The methanol steam reforming is a process used in chemical engineering, especially in the area of 
fuel cell technology, which utilizes methanol-water mixture as a hydrogen carrier to produce pure 
hydrogen gas and carbon dioxide.  
Methanol, dimethyl ether and methane are an example of hydrocarbons applied for steam 
reforming technology. At normal environmental conditions and under atmospheric pressure, 
methanol is a liquid. Using methanol as the chemical carrier for hydrogen generation is depending 
on its ready availability and chemical- physical properties such as high energy density, easy storage 
and transportation (5, 6).  
Reforming reaction is dependent on several factors, however the catalyst has a major role in the 
reformer cell. The reforming reaction takes place inside the catalytic reformer cell, therefore the 
quality and the concentration of the catalyst play the major role of conversion of methanol to 
hydrogen (7) .Catalysts such as Cu/ZnO are the ones used in this project.

1.1. The aim of thesis  
Temperature, pressure and flow rate are critical parameters that have a significant impact on 
conversion yield of methanol steam reforming. However, when hydrogen is extracted by steam 
reforming, carbon dioxide (CO2) and carbon monoxide (CO) are produced.  
Carbon monoxide (CO) adversely affects the chemical reactions in fuel cell, therefore optimization 
conversion yield is achieved by varying the process temperature, pressure and flow rate. The 
optimum parameters are obtained on the basis of trials and error experiments, thus, several 
experiments are done at temperatures of 240⁰C, 260⁰C and 280⁰C, pump setting of 30, 60, 80, 100, 
120 and 140 and different pressure. 
The aim of this work is to produce high amount of hydrogen (75%), low amount of CO2 (25%) and low 
amount of CO from steam mixture of methanol and water by using a methanol fuel cell reformer with 
Zn/CuO catalyst at temperatures of range 240⁰C - 280⁰C and different pressure.

2. Theory  
2.1. Hydrogen 
Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe, nearly nine out of ten atoms in the 
universe are hydrogen atoms. It is considered to be the third most abundant element on the earth, 
after oxygen and silicon, it’s presence is about 15.4 % of all the atoms found on the Earth(8).  
Hydrogen gas is similar to natural gas in that it is lighter than air, so it rises and disperses quickly. 
Due to the electronegativity1 of hydrogen is 2.20, it can react chemically with most other elements 
(high and low electronegativity)(9). 
It has one of the highest energy density values per mass; 1 kg of hydrogen contains 132.5 MJ, which 
is around 2.5 times more energy than is contained in 1 kg of natural gas(10). In addition, it has a 
highly flammable with contact with oxygen and burn in air. It is considered as a clean energy carrier 
similar to electricity. However, it can be used to replace non-renewable fossil fuels, thus can resolve 
the availability, environmental, and health concerns of the latter (11). Naturally hydrogen is found 
in different compounds like water, biomass and hydrocarbon, where it is bonded with other 
elements such as carbon and oxygen. All these production routes will be discussed in the paragraph 
hydrogen production in section 2.3.  
Hydrogen is considered an important material in chemical synthesis and has various applications in 
chemical and petroleum industries. However, it is used in ammonia production, fertilizers, 
petroleum processing and methanol. Nowadays, its major application is as an energy carrier 
because it creates almost no pollution (12).   
The energy content from mole of hydrogen in fuel cell is given as enthalpy (heat), which would 
either have a lower heating value (LHV) of 242 KJmol-1 or a higher heating value (HHV) of 286 kJ 
mol-1. This enthalpy is indication of combustion for one mole of hydrogen at constant pressure and 
temperature(13).  
Nowadays world have quite a substantial production of hydrogen, where more than 50 million tons 
produced globally each year(14). The total production of hydrogen in Denmark is around 300 
million normal cubic meters per year (MNm3/y)(15). 
2.2. Fuel cell  
The principle of fuel cells was discovered by Swiss scientist Christian Friedrich in 1838, while they 
were developed by Welsh scientist Sir William Grove a few years later. Fuel cells are an energy 
conversion device that generates electrical power by a chemical reaction where hydrogen and oxygen 
are converted to water producing electricity without involving combustion cycles (16), in accordance 
with equation (1).   
 
2H2 + O2 → 2H2O + energy        (1)

(difference high).

Hydrogen atoms enter a fuel cell at the anode that contains a catalyst where an oxidation reaction 
ionizes the molecule, in accordance with equation (2).  
 
H2 → 2H+ + 2e-                            (2) 
 
Hydrogen proton moves toward the electrode through the electrolyte while electrons are drawn 
from the anode to the cathode through an external circuit, producing direct current electricity, as 
shown in figure 1. At the cathode, hydrogen ions, electrons, and oxygen react to form water in 
accordance with equation (3). 
 
½ O2 + 2H+ +2e- → H2O               (3)

Fuel cells are classified by their operating temperature and their type of electrolyte into five 
categories(17): 
Alkaline fuel cells (AFC) use a solution of potassium hydroxide in water as their electrolyte. Variety of 
non-precious metals as a catalyst at the anode and cathode.  Operation temperature is between 
150°C and 250°C. The presence of the hydroxyl ions travelling across the electrolyte allow a circuit 
to be made and electrical energy can be extracted. Alkaline fuel cells can reach power generating 
efficiency of 70%, although their production costs have long rendered them out of range for mass 
production (18). 
Phosphoric Acid fuel cells (PAFC) uses a pure phosphoric acid as electrolyte. The acid is contained in 
a Teflon-bonded silicon carbide matrix and porous carbon electrodes containing platinum catalyst.  
The operation temperature are between (150-200°C) and can reach 50% power generation 
efficiency(19). 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Solid Oxide fuel cells (SOFC) uses a hard ceramic electrolyte that allows operating temperatures to 
run as high as 1000ºC. Operating efficiency could reach 60%(20). 
Molten Carbonate fuel cells (MCFCs) uses electrolyte consists of alkali (Li, Na & K) carbonates 
retained in a ceramic matrix of LiHO2. They operate at temperature between 650 ºC and this 
temperature permits the use of non-platinum group catalysts, and show high power generation 
efficiency(21).  
Direct Methanol fuel cells (DMFCs) are considered a newer sub type of the proton exchange 
membrane cells, where they both use a polymer membrane as the electrolyte. The anode catalyst 
extracts hydrogen, which can be fed to the fuel cells directly or generated by methanol reforming 
or another hydrocarbon. They operate at a temperature between 90–100°C and operating 
efficiencies around 30-40%. Higher efficiencies can be achieved at higher temperatures(22). 
Polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells (PEMFC)  
PEMFCs also called proton exchange membrane fuel cells that use a thin polymer membrane 
instead of using a liquid electrolyte. Which means these fuel cells use a solid polymer membrane as 
an electrolyte and porous carbon electrodes coated with a platinum catalyst. This solid electrolyte 
allows a compact construction of the fuel cell, in addition, more straight forward operation under 
pressure can be achieved, which can improve the performance considerably. It has a low operating 
temperature relatively high power density (26), which is thought to be the best type of fuel cell as 
the vehicular power source to eventually replace the gasoline and diesel internal combustion 
engines. They are being considered for automobile applications because they typically have an 
operating temperature in range of 60-80°C and a rapid start up time. This advantages makes them 
ideal for use in transport, home power generators, and other small applications(23). They need high 
purity hydrogen, oxygen from the air and water to operate and do not require corrosive fluids(24).  
 
2.3. Hydrogen production  
Since hydrogen is found in nature only as part of other compounds, it must first be converted to 
elemental hydrogen (H2) through the use of energy, before hydrogen itself becomes available as a 
fuel. This implies that hydrogen is an energy carrier, not an energy source(25). 
Hydrogen is able to store and deliver energy in a usable form, but first it must be produced from 
compounds that contain it. There are a variety of primary energy sources and feed stocks with 
related technologies for hydrogen production; where the feedstock primarily dictates the choice of 
production method. Primary energy production presently means hydrogen production from fossil 
fuels via natural gas reforming as well as the partial oxidation of heavy fuel oil (or diesel) and coal. 
Along with these further processes are in the research and development phases(26). 
Hydrogen can be produced by three major technologies: electrochemical, and biological and 
thermal, figure 2 gives an overall idea about different feedstocks available for hydrogen extraction.

2.3.1. Electrochemical technology  
There are two types of electrochemical processes to produce hydrogen; Water electrolysis process 
and photo-electrolysis process(27).  
Water electrolysis is a method of decomposing water molecule into hydrogen and oxygen by means 
of electrical energy. One mole of water produces one mole of hydrogen gas and a half mole of 
oxygen gas at room temperature, 25°C, and 1 bar in accordance with equation (4)(28).  
e- + H2O→ ½ O2 + H2        (4) 
Photo-electrolysis uses special semiconductors and energy from sunlight to directly split the water 
molecule into hydrogen and oxygen(29). 
2.3.2. Biological technology 
Biological methods are of two types: Photo-biological of producing hydrogen by using solar energy 
and microorganisms such as cyanobacteria and green algae2. When plants produce oxygen during 
photosynthesis, these microorganisms consume water to split into hydrogen ions (H+) and 
electrons (e-). Then by using special enzymes called hydrogenases, hydrogen ions combined with 
electrons to produce hydrogen H2 gas (See equation 5,  6)(30). 
Photosynthesis: 2H2O 
4H+ +4e- +O2  (5) 
4H+ + 4e- → 2H2  
 
(6) 
Hydrogen can be produce also by anaerobic bacteria that grows on carbohydrate-rich substrates in 
the absence of light, such as biomass(31). 
These bacteria break down the biomass, which is high in carbohydrates. Ongoing extensive research 
is trying to understand, control and find these specific bacteria, which can ferment organic 
materials to hydrogen(32).  
2.3.3. Thermal technology 
With thermal methods being the most common technology, where each technology has its own 
subcategories and requires a specific feedstock. In this technology hydrogen is released from the 
feedstock by gas reforming and heating. This technology includes processes such as 
thermochemical water splitting, gasification, pyrolysis and reforming (33).  
Thermochemical water splitting. Water under very high reaction temperature (1000°C) is converted 
to hydrogen and oxygen through several endothermic and exothermic chemical reactions.

Gasification method is a controlled partial oxidation of carbon containing compounds (as coal, 
petroleum coke, biomass or waste) into synthesis gas(34).  
Pyrolysis process involves the conversion of biomass into hydrogen using a moderately high 
temperature gas reforming method. Fast pyrolysis is a process in which organic materials are 
rapidly heated to 450 - 600°C in the absence of air. Under these conditions, organic vapors, 
pyrolysis gases and charcoal are produced. The vapors are condensed to bio-oil(35).  
Reforming process is an important process for the conversion of hydrocarbon based fuels to a 
gaseous mixture that contains hydrogen. In principle, there are three types of reforming processes 
to obtain hydrogen by different methods such as steam reforming (SR), partial oxidation (POX), and 
auto thermal reforming (ATR) (36). 
2.3.3.1. Steam reforming 
Hydrogen can be extracted by steam reforming of feedstocks, it is considered as the most common 
conventional method for producing hydrogen in the chemical process industry and with wide 
progress on fuel cells (FC) technology(36). The feedstock used in the thermal steam reforming from 
hydrocarbon can be gas fuel such as methane, neutral gas and naphtha or from liquid fuels, such as 
methanol, dimethyl ether or ethanol by several reaction processes(37). 
2.3. 3. 2. Partial oxidation 
Partial oxidation is rarely used for converting alcohols to hydrogen; however, it is used for higher 
hydrocarbons such as methane. In this process, the hydrocarbon or another feedstock reacts with 
oxygen to attain hydrogen yield and carbon dioxide (see equation 7). This method includes a 
catalytic process whereby the fuel is partially combusted in an exothermic reaction (38). However, 
this method produces less hydrogen for the same amount of fuel than steam reforming(39). 
CH3OH + ½ O2 → CO2 + 2H2 + Heat    
(7)  
2.3.3.3. Auto Thermal Reforming   
Auto thermal reforming is a combination of both the steam reforming and the partial oxidation, in 
which the feedstock is mixed together with the oxidant (air fed or oxygen fed) and steam over a 
catalyst in a fixed bed reactor.  First, partial combustion reactions take place in the combustion 
chamber, and then followed by steam reforming reaction, and shift conversion to equilibrium over 
the catalyst bed. Combination of reactions in steam methane reforming and methane partial 
oxidation reforming is shown in equations 8 and 9(40, 41).  
CH4 + H2O → 3H2 + CO                        (8) 
CH4 + ½ O2 → 2H2 + CO

2.4. Hydrogen storage 
The chemical and physical properties of hydrogen affect its transport and its storage. Due to its low 
volumetric energy density, it is difficult to store in comparison to other fuels, such as petroleum. 
Thus it requires high pressure to be compressed and stored. Furthermore, due to its small size, 
weight and low viscosity, it requires a complex storage material (42).  
Storage methods of hydrogen can be developed by three primary technologies: Compressed 
gaseous hydrogen, liquid hydrogen and solid method storage. The compressed gaseous hydrogen is 
stored in lightweight tanks made of steel, designed to tolerate such high pressures to around (350-
700 bar), due to lack of space aboard a vehicle. The tank wall material has a crucial role regarding 
safety due to the high pressure(43).

2.5. Methanol Steam Reforming (MSR) 
Methanol Steam Reforming is the potential and common method of hydrogen producing, which is 
consider as one of the several alternative and traditional liquid fuels method, since this method can 
provide a high amount of hydrogen (46).  
It is also possible to use methanol for industrial hydrogen production instead of hydrocarbon. 
Steam reforming is the most studied and used in industries since it has the highest hydrogen yield, 
hydrogen can be produced from liquid energy carriers such as methanol(47).  
 
2.5.1. Methanol 
Methanol (CH3OH, MeOH) is a clear and colorless liquid that is also known as methyl alcohol or 
wood alcohol, since it was first prepared from wood sources by the dry distillation of wood 
feedstock(48). 
Methanol is a promising high energy density hydrogen carrier, compared to natural gas or other 
hydrocarbons. Which is considering as a more efficient energy source to produce hydrogen used in 
fuel cells or by direct conversion in direct methanol fuel cells (DMFCs) (49). 
However, methanol has the highest priority due to many reasons; methanol has a high H/C ratio 
(4:1), equal to that of methane. At normal environmental conditions and under atmospheric 
pressure, methanol is a liquid, which is not the case with methane liquefied petroleum gas. 
Methanol has a low boiling point (65 °C) which allows for facile vaporization, can be converted to 
hydrogen at relatively low temperatures, ca. 240–280 °C than most other fuels because it contains 
no carbon-carbon bonds that must be broken, compared to the methane reforming, which its 
reforming reaction normally occurs at 800–1000 °C (50). 
Today, methanol, is not a basic energy source, it acts as an energy carrier and can be made from 
many resources and by many process routes such as natural gas, coal, other fossil fuels as well as 
renewable resources.  However, it is primarily formed from natural gas. Through steam reforming 
of methane, a mixture of CO2, CO, and H” is formed in accordance with equation 10 (51). 
 
2CH4 + 3H2O → CO + CO2 + 7H2  
 (10) 
 
Products of this reaction, the syngas, is then converted to methanol at 200-300°Cover catalysts, in 
accordance with equation 11. 
 
CO2 + 3H2 → CH3OH + H2O   
(11) 
 
Methanol is a liquid fuel that can be more easily stored and transported than hydrogen. When 
comparing, the energy density per unit volume of methanol and hydrogen under standard 
conditions. Methanol has a higher volume energy density (1.8 x 104 kj.dm-3) than hydrogen  
(13kj.dm-3)(52).   
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
Methanol is can be an ideal replacement for gasoline, as it is cheaper to produce and it has lower 
level of danger then gasoline, as it has a lower chance of flammability, since it has a much higher 
flash point. Additionally, it is easier to handle and safe to store transport and handle(53).  
 
2.5.2. Methanol stems reforming processing 
Methanol steam reforming (MSR) is a particularly attractive and promising process used to generate 
hydrogen. It can be occurred through several process alternatives: decomposition, steam reforming, 
partial oxidation and auto thermal (54). In this project the methanol steam reforming process which 
is an ideal method to produce high amount of hydrogen with a little amount of CO by combination 
of methanol with water in vapor in the presence of a heterogeneously Cu/ZnO catalyst. as shown in 
equation 12 (55). 
 
CH3OH + H2O → CO2+ 3H2                  (12) 
 
This reaction is an endothermic process which means it requires heat, through reaction 
temperatures between 200 and 300°C. it takes place with an increase of moles number and has 
ability to produce a high percent of hydrogen up to 75% with 25% carbon dioxide as the major by-
product, and it also produces low amount of by-gasses CO as shown in equation 13(56,57).  
 
 
 CO2 + H2 → CO + H2O 
 (13) 
The formation of CO has negative effects on the fuel cell performance; whereas carbon monoxide 
acts as to be very poisonous for Pt (or other) on the electrode in the fuel cell. CO reduces the 
activity of the platinum catalyst by blocking active catalyst sites. Thus CO poisoning reduces the 
voltage output of a fuel cell by limiting the number of catalyst sites available for the hydrogen 
oxidation reaction. Therefore the decomposition process of methanol is found to be unsuitable for 
fuel cell applications(46, 55).

2.6. Gas chromatography 
Gas chromatography is an analytical technique that is separate analytes (sample) in a mixture of 
different component. It is consisting of column inlet (injector), mobile phase (carrier gas), (column), 
oven, detector and data analysis (see figure 5)(58). 
 
Figure 5: Schematic over all view for typical gas chromatograph.(59) 
It is important that the sample is in gas phase, when it enters the column inlet (injector) to be 
analyzed. A micro-syringe is used to inject the sample through a rubber septum into a heated small 
chamber at the head of the column. The temperature of this chamber may be a higher than the 
boiling point of the least volatile component in the sample mixture. The component of sample 
transported into the column by the carrier gas(60).  
The carrier gas is comprised of an inert gas such as helium, argon, or nitrogen, which flows through 
the injector and pushes the gaseous components of the gaseous sample onto the GC column 
without reacting with the sample or the stationary phase.  
The column is kept inside a temperature controlled oven, which means the oven is used to 
accurately control the column temperature(61). However, the inside walls of the columns are 
coated with a thin ﬁlm of polymeric material called the stationary phase.  
In the columns occurs separation of the components in the sample, where the components are 
distributed differently between the stationary phase and mobile phase.  This distribution is 
depending on difference of their physical and chemical properties. The mixture compounds of 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
sample enter the column and collects in the front of the column.  When column heated in the oven 
to interested temperature, each compound begins to break their attraction with the stationary 
phase and moving through the column to the end and enters the detector at different time 
(retention time). Retention time can be defined as the time it takes for a compound pass through 
the column from it is injected to it is detected(62).  
The components retain strongly with stationary phase, will spend less time in the mobile phase and 
thereby pass slowly through the column. While the weakly retained components, will spend more 
time in the mobile phase and thereby move quickly through the column. Types of column, carry 
gas, compound structure and molecular weight can be affect the separation of compound in the 
column.  The lower molecular weight compounds will move earlier than higher molecular weights 
compounds due to they have lower boiling points and will separate faster than those with higher 
boiling points, enters the detector and this result the ﬁrst peak in the chromatogram, followed the 
next fastest compound will separate(63, 64). There are two general types of columns that are used 
in gas chromatography;  
 Packed columns are 1.5 - 10 m in length and have an internal diameter of 2 - 4 mm and filled 
with a stationary phase component that made of stainless steel or glass and contains a 
packing of solid support material.   
 
 
 The capillary columns have a very small internal diameter, on the order of a fine layer 
usually less than 10 µm of particles, and lengths between 25-60 meters. They are made of 
fused silica with a polyimide outer coating, where capillary columns also known as open 
tubular that have a very small internal diameter. The capillary columns can be two types; 
wall-coated open tubular (WCOT) or support-coated open tubular (SCOT).  
 
The WCOT columns are the most popular type of open tubular column, which consists of a capillary 
tube with its interior surface coated in a tiny layer of stationary phase. 
In the SCOT columns the inner column walls are coated with a thin layer of support material. Some 
of the columns are filled with many parallel micro-pores (PLOT columns), in which this column is 
made from inorganic materials e.g. aluminum oxides, silica gel or molecular sieve(65). 
 
Molecular sieve 5Å column is a PLOT capillary column in which it is made of a zeolite, molecular 
sieve consisting of aluminum silicon oxide with a tunnel-like pore structure. The size of the pores is 
defined by the geometrical structure of the zeolite.  
Product gases of steam reformer can be analyzed by gas chromatography(66). In this project HP 
6890 GC and 6890 GC software performed H2, CO and CO2 determination. Two different columns 
used: 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
 Molecular sieve 5A and 13X packing’s differ in pore size and composition, causing 
differences in retention and selectivity for many gases. This column is used with nitrogen 
carrier gas to separate hydrogen gas (H2) and hilum carrier gas to separate carbon monoxide 
(CO).  
 The other column is BoraBund U, which is used with helium as a carry gas to separate out of 
the larger molecular weight compound as well as carbon dioxide and methanol.  
After the components in the sample exit the column, they will flow past the detector and will be 
detectors one at a time. There are several different types of detectors common to gas 
chromatography instruments such as Flame ionization detection (FID), thermal conductive detector 
(TCD), Catalytic combustion detector (CCD) and Electron capture detector (ECD). 
The general classes of compounds are being analyzed by determine the choice of detector and the 
sensitivity required. 
In this project the gas chromatographs are used with thermal conductive detector (TCD), which is 
the most common detector in the Gas Chromatography because of it simple, universal responding 
to all analyzes and it is less sensitive than other detectors. Its work based on the measurement of 
thermal conductivity, which depends upon the composition of the gas.  
The detector measures differences in thermal conductivity between the sample and the pure 
carrier gas. The carry gas most has a very different thermal conductivity between the column 
effluent flow (sample components in carry gas) and the reference flow of carry gas alone, produces 
a voltage signal proportional to this difference. Hydrogen and hilum are good thermal gases, where 
hilum is the most common carry gas given the highest sensitivity that is used with the thermal 
conductivity detector(67).   
A detector cell contains a heated filament with an applied current. As a pure carrier gas passes over 
the filament through the cell, removing heat at a constant rate and then the filament is cooled. 
When an analyst enters the detector, the conductivity of the gas steam decreases. This will heat up 
the filament and with increasing electrical resistance and the voltage across the filament will 
change, the detector measures the change in voltage(68).

5.3. Temperature optimization of reformer operation at atmospheric pressure. 
5.3. 1. Determination of sample products H2, CO, CO2 and MeOH  
In order to ascertain the conditions for the optimal production of energy, the performance of the 
reformer cell was tested at three different temperatures 280 °C, 260 °C and 240 °C, at atmospheric 
pressure. At each operating temperature, different pump settings (30, 60, 80, 100, 120, and 140) 
were tested. All the gases collected at the output of the reformer cell were subsequently analyzed 
by gas chromatography. Triplicate measurements were carried out as to obtain possible accurate 
results.   
 
5.3.2.1. Determination of hydrogen yield at different temperature  
For each specific process conditions set (pump setting and temperature), a 100 µl sample of 
product gas was analyzed by gas chromatography.   
The chromatograms showed a negative peak at retention time 0,35 min, corresponding to 
hydrogen. Peak areas for the triplicate measurements were noted and an average area value was 
calculated for the three different temperatures (see Appendix 3.3-3.5).

and this implies to all the gases. In this process there were also some uncertainties in the GC, this 
can lead to bad reading in the makeup flow, of the detector. The flow in the program changes every

6. Conclusion 
The purpose of this project was to introduce the production of hydrogen yield at a level of 75%, 
25% CO2 and a lesser amount of CO. 
The overall view of this experiment indicates that the process of using this reformer catalytic cell 
was successful. From the data’s collected, it was concluded that a very low amount of production of 
CO was emitted and a high conversion of hydrogen yield was achieved at a rate of 75%. However, 
the CO2 yield could be achieved at a rate lower than 25%. 
A high amount of hydrogen can be achieved at 280°C and 260°C, the lowest CO% emitted can be 
achieved at temperature 260°C with all pump settings, except at pump setting 30. The lowest CO% 
at 280°C can be achieved at all pump settings, except at pump setting 30 and 60. The lowest 
amount CO2% can be reached at 280°C, furthermore, the lowest amount of not converted 
methanol can be achieved at 280°C. 
Therefore, the temperature 280°C was best to use the as operating temperature with a flow rate 
operating from 80-100 at atmospheric pressure.
