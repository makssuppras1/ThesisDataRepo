Abstract
Production of heterologous proteins in Escherichia coli is of great importance for both
academia and the biotechnological industry. The most used system for protein expression
in E. coli is the pET expression system which already counts 220,000 published studies and
900 licenses sold. However, this system was developed in the late 1980s, when methods
in molecular biology and the knowledge about protein production were far less advanced
than they are today. It is known that the system has several suboptimal features, such as
toxicity from produced proteins and excessive transcriptional readthrough from the gene
of interest.
In that sense, the field might benefit greatly from a new standardized platform for recom-
binant protein production that implements the advances that synthetic biology has devel-
oped in the last 30 years to create a system that is more stable, robust, and efficient. One
vision of this new system is a more modular approach using Bacterial Artificial Chromo-
somes (BACs) to carry both the protein production cassettes, but also auxiliary modules
that support the production of heterologous proteins in various E. coli strains.
This thesis focusses on building one category of those auxiliary modules: stress sensors.
Aiming at providing to the users of this new system an easy way to identify potential prob-
lems. For that, four biosensors sourced from literature were adapted to the context of bac-
terial artificial chromosomes through TIR optimizationand characterized for their ability
to detect stresses associated with production of heterologous proteins.
Biosensors based on theJ23100 promoter andhtpG1promoter wasused to measuremetabolic
burden, the rne promoter was used to measure RNA degradation stress, and the IbpA pro-
moter was used to measure misfolding of proteins. The rne and htpG1 biosensors were
prone to mutations, and therefore hard to characterize, the J23100 biosensor showed an
increase in burden as a response to continual burden from plasmid maintenance but a
decrease in burden in response to protein expression. The IbpA folding sensor showed a
distinguishable difference between known misfolding and well-folding proteins, and was
used to show that production of two other proteins, YidC and Bat LPMO, could potentially
be improved by optimizing the folding of these proteins.
In time, these biosensors can be used to rapidly identify the sources of error in the pro-
duction of heterologous proteins. This information can be used to guide strategies of mit-
igation, which can ultimately lead to increased protein production in E. coli, which will
benefit all who uses heterologous protein production, both in academia and in industry.

Dansk resume
Produktion af heterologe proteiner i Eschericia coli er yderst vigtig både for forskning og for
den bioteknologiske industri. Det mest brugte proteinproduktionssystem i E. coli er pET
systemet der er blevet citeret over 220,000 gange og har solgt 900 licenser. Systemet blev
dog udviklet i slutningen af 80’erne hvor molekylærbiologiske metoder og den generelle
viden om proteinproduktion var mindre avanceret end den er i dag. pET systemet har flere
kendte suboptimale egenskaber, såsom toksicitet ved høj proteinproduktion og manglende
evne til at stoppe transskriptionen i området udenfor den designerede produktionskassette.
I den forstand kan feltet have stor gavn af en ny standardiseret platform for rekombinant
proteinproduktion der implementerer de fremskridt som syntetisk biologi har udviklet i
de sidste 30 år for at skabe et system der er mere stabilt, robust og effektivt. En vision
for dette nye system er en mere modulær tilgang ved hjælp af bakterielle kunstige kromo-
somer (Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes (BACs)) til at bære både proteinproduktionskas-
setterne, men også hjælpemoduler, der understøtter produktionen af heterologe proteiner
i forskellige E. coli-stammer.
Denne afhandling fokuserer på at bygge én kategori af disse hjælpemoduler: stresssen-
sorer. Stresssensorer der kan give brugerne af det nye proteinproduktionssystem mulighe-
den for at identificere potentielle problemer. Til dette formål er fire promotere hentet fra
den videnskabelige litteratur og de er blevet tilpasset konteksten af en BAC gennem TIR-
optimering. Efterfølgende blev de karakteriseret efter deres evne til at måle stress associeret
med produktion af heterologe proteiner i E. coli.
Biosensorer baseret på J23100-promotoren og htpG1-promotoren blev brugt til at måle
metabolisk belastning, rne-promotoren blev brugt til at måle RNA-nedbrydningsstress, og
IbpA-promotoren blev brugt til atmåle fejlfoldning afproteiner. rne-og htpG1-biosensorerne
var tilbøjelige til at mutere, og var derfor svære at karakterisere, J23100-biosensoren viste
en stigning i belastningen som en reaktion på kontinuerlig plasmidvedligeholdelse, men
et fald i belastningen som reaktion på proteinekspression. IbpA-foldningssensoren viste
en skelnelig forskel mellem kendte fejlfoldende og foldende proteiner, og blev brugt til at
vise at produktionen af to andre proteiner, YidC og Bat LPMO, potentielt kunne forbedres
ved at optimere foldningen af disse proteiner.
I fremtiden kan disse biosensorere blive brugt til hurtigt at identificere kilder til suboptimal
proteinproduktion. Denne information kan derefter bruges til at foreslå strategier til at
afbøde de målte stresser og derved lede til en øget proteinproduktion i E. coli, hvilket er til
gavn for alle som bruger heterologe proteiner, både i forskning og i industrien.

Introduction
1.1
Heterologous Protein Production in Biotechnology
Biotechnology can broadly be defined as the use of biological systems to advance human
goals [1]. This can take many forms, such as using microorganisms to ferment foods, to
using synthetic proteins to treat diseases. Common for all biotechnology is that there is a
feedback cycle between using biology to improve life for humans and learning more about
biology to increase the potential of the biotechnological solutions.
The origins of biotechnology can be traced back to human prehistory with the use of mi-
croorganisms in the fermentation of food products. This includes yeasts such as Saccha-
romyces cerevisiae for brewing of beer and baking of bread [2], and lactic acid bacteria to
produce cheeses and yoghurts [3]. Over time, as our understanding of these processes de-
veloped, humans became better at running the processes, but their understanding of why
was still lacking.
The collective human understanding of biological processes took a leap forward with the
discovery of microorganisms. Thefirstmicroorganisms wereseen by AnthonievanLeeuwen-
hoek in 1673 through his home-made microscope [4], but it was first later in the 19th
century that pioneers such as Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch began to link these microor-
ganisms to macroscopic phenomena, such as disease and spoilage of foods [5]–[7]. This
new knowledge of how biological systems worked allowed for the creation of new tech-
nologies, such as pasteurization, and the isolation of pure cultures for production of food
stuffs.
The following century marked the beginning of industrial biotechnology as it is known to-
day with the direct application of microorganisms in industrial processes on a large scale.
One example of this is the use of Clostridium acetobutylicum for the production of acetone,
butanol, and ethanol from starch, which was invented by Chaim Weizmann in 1916 [8].
During the 20th century, scientist continued to learn about how microorganisms func-
tion, and why they could be used as biocatalysts. With the discovery of enzymes, and the
improvements in their purification, many more products could now be made using bio-
catalysts on a large scale [9].
Beginning with the DNA revolution in the 1970’s, it became possible to express protein
recombinantly, which means to produce a protein in another host, most commonly Es-
cherichia coli. The first example of this was the production of insulin in 1978 [10], [11],
which was a massive improvement over the previous situation where insulin had to be ex-

In our modern world, production of recombinant proteins has transitioned from being

insulin in 1978, many other therapeutics have also been produced in microbial hosts, such
as human growth hormone (hGH), blood factors, interferons, interleukins, and more [22].
The current research to improve and widen the scope of produced proteins has many in-
teresting facets. Directed evolution is being used to alter and improve the specificity of
enzymes [23] and protein therapeutics [24], [25], while de novo protein engineering is
developing proteins that look nothing like anything ever seen in nature [26], [27]. Con-
currently, new classes of enzymes are being discovered and characterized, like the lytic
polysaccharide monooxygenases (LPMO), which were only given a name in 2010 [28].
Common for all these proteins and techniques is the requirement for protein expression.
Without that, no further research can be conducted, and no final product can be formed.
It is therefore essential that an efficient, stable, and robust method for the expression of
proteins exist.
1.2
Established protein production platforms
Production of recombinant proteins is based on two main pillars: the cloning of the coding
sequence of the protein of interest into an appropriate expression vector, and the transfor-
mation into an appropriate host, which will be the cell factory for the protein production.
In the following two subsections, these two pillars will be further examined, starting with
the choice of an appropriate host.
1.2.1
Choice of host
The choice of an appropriate host is an essential decision that must be taken during the
design phase of any project involving recombinant protein production. In modern litera-
ture, these hosts are often called “cell factories” to emphasize their role as biocatalysts that
convert raw materials to the products that humans desire. Many different microorganisms

Among the eukaryotes, various excellent cell factories exist. Common for them is that
they are more often used to express human therapeutics if these need specific glycosyla-
tion patterns to be effective, or to avoid being immunogenic [31]. One of the most used
eukaryotic cell factories is the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, also known as baker’s yeast.
This is a simple eukaryote, and it serves as the model eukaryote, which means that a lot is
known about its physiology and molecular biology. This organism is heavily used in re-
search and industry, where its biggest production target is insulin [32]. One disadvantage
of using S. cerevisiae for production of therapeutics, is that it lacks the glycosylation pattern
found in humans [32]. This can cause problem for certain proteins but can be solved by
moving to another host.
Also in the kingdom of fungi, the filamentous fungi are also a big group of important cell
factories. Here, organisms such as Aspergillus niger, Aspergillus oryzae, and Penicillium spp,
are among the most used [33]. As opposed to the other eukaryotes, filamentous fungi are

Moving on to the prokaryotes, many more common cell factories emerge. Among the
Gram-positive bacteria, the bacterium Bacillus subtilis is a common organism used in in-
dustrial processes [40]. It shares many of its advantages and disadvantages with the fila-
mentous fungi. It is mostly used to produce industrial enzymes [32], as it is excellent as
secreting them to the culture medium. The genetic tools are not as well developed as for
other model organisms such as S. cerevisiae or E. coli, but that is beginning to change [41]–
[44].
Within the Gram-negative bacteria, we find organisms such as Pseudomonas putida and
Vibrio natriegens, which are very promising cell factories. However, they are currently not
in active use for industrial protein production, as their tools are a bit more underdeveloped
compared to the standard Gram-negative E. coli. Among their advantages, we find a lot of
interesting features. P. putida is very resistant to many chemicals, which means that it is
well suited for bioremediation purposes [29], [45], [46]. This also means that it can be
grown on substrates that are toxic for other organism, which is a big advantage in modern
biotechnology. Currently, the toolset available for P. putida is not yet as extensive as the
one for yeasts or E. coli, but many new tools have recently been developed [47], [48], which
could introduce P. putida as a central player in the production of heterologous proteins.
The main advantage for V. natriegens is its very fast growth rate. It can divide every 10
minutes when grown in optimal conditions, which is twice as fast as E. coli [49]. Further-
more, many of the tools that have been developed for use in E. coli are easily transferrable
to V. natriegens, which suggests that it can become a very promising cell factory in the
coming years [50]–[55].
Lastly, we have the model organism E. coli. This is probably the most studied bacterium
on Earth [32]. The main advantages of using E. coli is its fast growth rate, but also that so
much is known about it, and so many tools have been developed and repeatedly applied
for industrial purposes [32]. Many different strains of E. coli exist, but for the purpose of

1.2.2
Choice of expression system
At the expression vector side, the pET (plasmid for expression by T7 RNA polymerase)
expression system is by far the most widely used in both academia and for research in
industry for protein expression in E. coli [58]. The system has been cited more than 220,000
times in research [58], and approximately 900 liscences to the system have been sold to
various companies, earning Brookhaven National Laboratory about $55 million [59].
An overview of the system can be seen in Figure 1 below. The system was developed by
William F. Studier and colleagues in the late 1980’s and early 1990’s and is heavily based
around parts taken from the T7 bacteriophage. The most important parts of the T7 expres-
sion system are the T7 RNAP and the T7 promoter. The T7 RNAP is an orthogonal RNA
polymerase that strongly recognizes the T7 promoter, which does not occur in the native
E. coli genome [57]. Furthermore, the T7 RNAP is approximately 5 times faster than the
native E. coli RNAP, which further adds to its useful qualities [56]. In most implementa-
tions of the T7 expression system, the T7 RNAP is produced from the DE3 cassette when
the strains are induced with IPTG [57]. This activates transcription from the T7 promoter,
which is placed on the pET plasmid to produce high amounts of recombinant protein in
E. coli [60], [61]. The pET system is designed in such a way that many different pET vec-
tors exist, but they are all very easy to clone your Gene of Interest (GOI) into [60]. This
is typically done by using restriction enzymes to open the Multi-Cloning Site (MCS) that
have been engineered into the pET vector, followed by a ligation and transformation into
E. coli. However, it should be noted that many other assembly techniques, such as Gibson
[62] and USER [63] also can be used to insert GOIs into the pET vectors.
The T7 transcription system is so powerful that it is common to see growth defects and
toxicity from the system. Several strategies have been developed to handle these issues.
First, it has been shown that adding glucose to the growth medium lowers the level of
transcription from the T7 promoter [56]. This is caused by carbon catabolite repression

1.3
Improvements on the current protein production plat-
forms
While the pET system is an excellent system for protein production in E. coli, the meth-
ods used to construct it has since been superseded by more accurate and powerful tools.
The system’s components were first described in the 1970’s and 80’s, and have remained
relatively unchanged since the 90’s [58]. For example, the T7 promoter has been shown
to be truncated compared to the consensus sequence in the pET28a vector, which is one
of the most used pET vectors [58]. This truncation was introduced early on because it
lined up with the restriction enzymes available at the time. Today, assembly of DNA can
be performed using PCR-based methods without being limited by the recognition sites of
restriction enzymes. By restoring the full T7 promoter, a three-fold increase in protein
production yield was immediately seen [58]. Furthermore, the Translation Initiation Re-
gion (TIR) of the plasmid has also been left unoptimized. Following a simple screening
experiment, the production of proteins could be increase by up to 47-fold [58].
The transcriptional terminator from the T7 phage, which has been used in many pET plas-
mids, only has a Termination Effeciency (TE) of 80% [73]. This is relatively low, and many
systems with better TE already exists. As a comparison, Chen et al. created a library of ter-
minators where 158 had a higher TE than the T7 terminator [74]. However, this modest
TE makes sense evolutionary for the T7 phage, as the downstream genes need to be tran-
scribed too [75], but for biotechnological purposes, this introduces problems. For example,
readthrough of a pET plasmid can impact the copy number of the plasmid by transcribing
through the Origin of Replications (Ori). Mairhofer et al. found that this could increase the
copy number of pET30a from approximately 40 to 160 within six hours of induction with
IPTG [73]. In a follow-up study, Mairhofer et al. tested different terminator designs and
arrived at a terminator with 99% efficiency [76]. The use of this improved design increased

1.4
Expanding the toolkit with next generation protein
production systems
The development of molecular biology and synthetic biology tools have now reached a
state where it is fitting to reconsider and redesign the standardized tools from the past.
As mentioned above, even though they work well, new tools and principles allow for the
construction of robust systems with fewer arbitrary restrictions. The next generation of
protein production platforms should continue to be easy to use and function robustly, but
they should also include more features that are optimized and enhanced to the best of
our ability. This will provide a stronger platform from which proteins are expressed, and
thereby provide better conditions for protein production both in academic research and
industrial process. In Figure 2 and the sub-chapters below, different aspects that could be
present in this new protein production platform are described. This includes an improved
production strain, Bacterial Artificial Chromosome (BAC)(s), which can be used to pro-
vide different modularized functionalities, such as a protein production module, and other
auxiliary helper modules.

1.4.2
Modularization of features: BACs
Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes (BACs) are single-copy plasmids that behave like extra
chromosomes. These plasmids can be used to transport very large fragments in E. coli. Ex-
amples of their use has been in sequencing of the human genome [92], and in the recoding
of the E. coli genome [93].
As mentioned above, these plasmids are maintained in a very-low copy number, maybe
even one copy per cell [94]. This feature is provided by the RepE protein, whose func-
tion is twofold: First, it initiates replication of the plasmid at ori2, and second, it represses
its own transcription, thereby maintaining a very low copy number [95]. Furthermore,
these plasmids also encode the machinery for partitioning during cell division [96], as op-

posed to the random distribution of high copy-number plasmids [97], [98]. The SopA and
SopB proteins bind to the sopC sequence during cell division to partition the BAC into
the two daughter cells, thus ensuring robust transfer of DNA between generations, with-
out stochastic loss of plasmids [99]. It is this feature of partitioning that is the reason why
these plasmids will be called BACs in this thesis.
One possible application of BACs in protein production is to act as a replacement for
genome engineering. Since BACs and the genome both are present in one copy per cell,
it is reasonable to assume that little to no adjustment is needed when cassettes are moved
between them [100]. Furthermore, BACs allow for DNA manipulation both in vivo and in
vitro which greatly increases the ease with which they can be engineered. Lastly, it is also
possible to transfer BACs between different E. coli strains, and maybe even between some
other Gram-negative bacteria in the future [101], which greatly expands the possibilities
for cloning the BAC in a cloning strain, and then transferring it to an optimized protein
production strain.
Since BACs can carry large DNA fragments, it is also possible to carry many different fea-
tures on the same BAC. This is illustrated in Figure 2b with blue squares indicating sites
for integration of functional modules. For example, it could be possible to carry both the
T7 RNA polymerase, the T7 promoter and GOI, and the T7 lysozyme on the same BAC
to minimize the need for multiple plasmids and resistance markers. This could also result
in an expression system that is less toxic due to the lower burden associated with plasmid
maintenance, since the BACs are present in very few numbers per cell.
1.4.3
Standardization of cloning and genome integration: SEGA
Standardization of cloning is highly important for protein production systems that strive
to be as easy and effective to use as possible [41], [61], [84]. One clear example of this is
the Standard Genome Architecture (SEGA) [89]. This is a standardized protein production
platform that works on the level of genome engineering in E. coli. Briefly, the system works
by having a standardized landing pad, referred like that as it serves as a destination for the
introduction of the gene of interest, integrated in the genome of E. coli. The landing pad is
shown in Figure 2c. This landing pad can then be used as target for recombination using
lambda proteins [102], which are easily expressed in this strain.
The SEGA landing pad has several features that improves its functionalities above being just
a locus for easy integration. First, the landing pad contains a GFP cassette that is replaced
by the gene of interest upon recombination. This allows for easy green/white screening
if genes other than gfp are integrated [89]. Second, the SEGA landing pad contains the

1.4.4
Post-translational modifications: DisCoTune
Many proteins of eukaryotic origin do not produce well in microbial hosts [105]. In eu-
karyotes, the disulfides are formed in the Endoplasmatic Reticulum (ER), but this organelle
does not exist in bacteria. Instead, bacteria like E. coli usually form disulfide bonds in the
periplasm, away from the reductive environment of the cytoplasm [106], [107]. Therefore,
additional engineering is needed if one wants to produce a protein that contains disulfide
bonds in the cytoplasm of E. coli.
Various approaches have been used to solve this problem of disulfide formation in the cy-
toplasm of E. coli. One option is to modify the cytoplasmic environment in E. coli to make
it less reductive [108]. This was done in the Origami™B(DE3) strain from Novagen [109]
and the SHuffle strain from NEB [110], [111]. Another approach is demonstrated by the
CyDisCo system [112], which expresses a yeast mitochondria thiol oxidase (Erv1p), and a
human protein disulphide isomerase (hPDI) to create and isomerize disulfide bonds, re-
spectively. When this system was originally developed, it was carried either on the pET
vector, or on the pLysS vector. Of these two variants, the pLysS version was the most
popular, as it already functioned as an auxiliary helper plasmid in the protein production
system.
An updated version of this system was introduced in 2021 with DisCoTune [90], which
combines the expression of disulfide bond factors from CyDisCo with the tunable expres-
sion of the T7 lysozyme from the pLEMO plasmid [113]. This system is shown in Figure
2d. The DisCoTune system allows for better control of helper functions, and thereby better
expression of heterologous proteins in E. coli. Currently, the DisCoTune system is being
transferred to a BAC and the expression level of its constituent proteins are being optimized
to account for the change in genetic context. So far, this has shown promising results from
the production of proteinase K, the reference disulfide bond-containing protein [Magnus
Haahr, personal communication].

1.4.5
Using biosensors to detect problems in protein production
The use of various biosensors can be hugely beneficial in many biotechnological contexts.
This can be seen in metabolic engineering studies where the detection of an intermediate
or the end products allows for easy optimization of the pathway [114]–[117]. Other types
of biosensors work by highlighting things that are not working, and thereby alerting the
scientists that something needs to be fixed [118].
In a recent study by Tan et al., the different types of stresses associated with production of
heterologous proteins were investigated using transcriptomics [119]. They authors found
that the differentially expressed genes grouped together into several different types of stress
responses such as metal homeostasis, respiration, protein folding, amino acid biosynthesis,
nucleotide synthesis, and more [119]. These categories can serve as effective markers for
important stresses involved in the production of heterologous proteins, as indicated in
Figure 2e. In the following subsections, four different biosensors are described that have
been used to monitor different kinds of stresses associated with production of heterologous
proteins in E. coli.
The capacity monitor J23100
The J23100 promoter is a strong Anderson promoter from the Anderson library [120].
Promoters from this library have been used in numerous applications since their initial
addition to the Registry of Standardized Parts by the 2006 Berkley International Geneti-
cally Engineered Machine (iGEM) team. Their uses include: reference promoters to quan-
tify expression [121], [122], weak constitutive expression of dCas9 enzymes for Clustered
Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) Interference (CRISPRi) [123],
and for high constitutive expression for protein production [89], among others.
The J23100 promoter was used by Ceroni et al. in their paper from 2015 as a capacity
monitor to measure the metabolic burden of heterologous expressed proteins [124]. This
capacity monitor consists of the J23100 promoter driving expression of a green fluorescent
protein (GFP), inserted into the lambda locus in an E. coli DH10B and a MG1655 strain,
both of which are K12 derivatives. Their reasoning behind this design was that “because this
cassette lacks regulation, changes in GFP expression due to global expression changes would
reflect changes in resource availability” [124]. The capacity monitor is shown in Figure 3,
where the functioning of the resource competition is demonstrated. The principle of the
capacity monitor aligns with recent research on retroactivity in microbial systems, which
has shown that all cellular systems are connected by their use of shared resources, be it
nucleotides, amino acids, or available ribosomes and RNA polymerases [125]–[128].

Since its publication in 2015, the capacity monitor has been used in a number of other
projects [122], [129], [130], most notably being the 2019 iGEM project from Austin Uni-
versity, who used the capacity monitor to measure the burden associated with 330 different
parts from the iGEM registry [131].
The burden sensor htpG1
In a follow-up study, the promoter of the HtpG chaperone in E. coli was found to be re-

described above [124], which allowed them to quantify the metabolic burden in vivo. Con-
currently, they used RNA-seq to identify other native promoters that were up-regulated
during the heterologous expression to search for more biosensors for metabolic burden.
Importantly, they took the samples shortly after induction (15 and 60 mins), to identify
promoters that have a rapid response to metabolic burden.

The folding sensor IbpA
Protein misfolding and aggregation is a big problem when expressing heterologous pro-
teins [133]. The misfolding can be caused by different factors, many of which are poorly
understood at a rational level, which complicates the design of constructs to avoid misfold-
ing. It is therefore practical to use large libraries of variants and screen for useful proteins

oping a dual sensor system in E. coli [133]. An overview of this biosensor can be seen in
Figure 6. They coupled the expression of a Red Fluorescent Protein (RFP), more specif-
ically mCherry, to the successful translation of the protein of interest through a hairpin
that sequesters the Ribosome Binding Site (RBS) of the mCherry. This means that only if
the protein of interest has no nonsense mutations and is therefore successfully translated,

INTRODUCTION
1.5
Project aim
Standardized systems for the expression of heterologous proteins have been of great impor-
tance to biotechnological research and industry. Of special note is the pET system which
relies on the T7 expression platform, which, since its introduction in the 1980’s has been
widely used by the scientific community.
Over the last years, new tools and methodologies have been developed to facilitate easier
design and assembly of DNA constructs and expression systems such as the pET system.
Furthermore, the pET system has been scrutinized and different parts of the system have
been found to be suboptimal. In the Genome Engineering group of Morten Nørholm, there
is now a push towards making a new and better standard for the expression of heterologous
proteins. This new system is being based on the SEGA platform for DNA integration and
protein expression, and it will use bacterial artificial chromosomes (BACs) to easily move
genetic elements around between strains and organisms. Additionally, the use of BACs
allows for the application of auxiliary modules that can provide important functions to the
protein expression system that previously were provided by additional plasmids.
In this thesis, modern synthetic biology tools and methodologies will be used to develop,
test, and characterize a set of biosensors for the detection of stresses associated with het-
erologous protein production in E. coli Evo21(DE3). The biosensors will be sourced from
literature, and subsequently optimized to function in the genetic context of the single-copy
BACs. This will be done by optimizing the TIR, to adapt the biosensors to the new genetic
context, possibly by increasing the translation strength to compensate for the lower copy
number. The criteria for the TIR optimization is not solely expression strength, but the
ability to sense the stresses associated with protein production in the cell and provide an
easily distinguishable output in the form of a fluorescent signal.
Characterization of these biosensors will allow them to be used as auxiliary modules in the
new protein production platform where they can be used in conjunction with the SEGA
protein production module, and other auxiliary modules, to diagnose the different stresses
associated with the production of the heterologous protein, and thereby provide an oppor-
tunity to identify stresses, implement solution to alleviate them, and verify the improve-
ments rapidly in vivo.

2.2.6
USER cloning
Following PCR using the USER primers, 1 µL DpnI enzyme was added per 50 µL PCR
product. This was incubated at 37 ◦C for 30 minutes before heat inactivation at 80 ◦C
for 5 minutes. The PCR products were subsequently purified using a NucleoSpin® Gel
and PCR Clean-up kit (Macherey-Nagel), following the manufacturer’s instructions as de-
scribed above. The purified products were then mixed in a PCR tube using approximately
100 ng per fragment, and keeping a 1:3 molar ratio between the backbone and the inserts.
To this, 1 µL CutSmart buffer (NEB), 1 µL USER enzyme (NEB), and water to 20 µL was
added. This was then incubated at a gradient, starting at the melting temperature of the
overhangs, and decreasing one degree per five minutes. Following a decrease of 5 ◦C (25
minutes), the USER products were incubated at 10 ◦C for 20 minutes before transforma-
tion.
2.2.7
Construction of biosensor BACs and TIR optimization
The BAC biosensors were cloned using USER cloning. The constructed BACs consist of
three parts: the biosensor promoter, the mCherry gene, and the BAC backbone. The
IbpA, htpG1, and rne promoters were amplified from the genome of E. coli MG1655 using
primers IbpA-USER fw/rev, htpG1-USER fw/rev, and rne-USER fw/rev respectively. The
J23100 promoter was added by overhang PCR in the BAC backbone fragment using the
BAC-bb-J23100-USER rev primer. The BAC backbone was amplified using primers BAC-
bb-USER fw/rev, for the IbpA, htpG1, and rne sensors, and using primers BAC-bb-USER
fw and BAC-bb-J23100-USER rev for the J23100 biosensor.
The Translation Initiation Region (TIR) randomization was incorporated into the mCherry
fragment using primers mCherry-TIR- USER fw and mCherry-USER rev, as described
in [141], [142]. Briefly summarized, TIR optimization by randomization was performed
by randomizing the six nucleotides upstream of the start codons and by mutating the six
nucleotides downstream of the start codon to synonymous amino acids.

3.1
Common design considerations for all biosensors
All biosensors constructed during this project share a set of design features and consider-
ations. These, and their differences will be presented below.
Common for all biosensors is the backbone of the Bacterial Artificial Chromosome (BAC).
We are using the pCap3CC2M1 plasmid as the basis. This is a modified version of the
pCC2M1 BAC that has an additional set of terminators from the SEVA collection [84], and
the synthetic terminator L3S2P21 [74], inserted around the MCS [Andreas B. Bertelsen,
Personal communication]. These terminators were added to provide transcriptional insu-
lation from the rest of the BAC, and from the inserted cassette to the rest of the BAC. This
BAC also has a chloramphenicol resistance gene, the repE and sopABC genes needed for
DNA replication and segregation, and an inducible origin, oriV, which requires a special
strain to activate.
For the fluorescent output signal, mCherry was chosen. This fluorescent protein was cho-
sen for multiple reasons. First, since these biosensors need to be used together with the
SEGA system, they cannot use GFP, as that system has a green/white screening system as
a central functionality. This means that another fluorescent protein needs to be used, even
though the original biosensors using the J23100 [124], rne [88], and IbpA [133] promoters
use GFP as their output signal. Second, mCherry was chosen since it has a bright color
that can be visible to the naked eye when highly expressed. This can help with identifying
valuable TIR variants with high translation efficiency. Lastly, the spectrum of mCherry
does not significantly overlap with GFP or Yellow Fluorescent Protein (YFP), which allows
the use of these fluorescent proteins simultaneously.
All biosensors are assembled using USER cloning [63]. This was chosen as it allows for easy
cloning the three fragments required: the BAC backbone, the promoter, and the mCherry
gene. Furthermore, it also allows for the incorporation of a TIR library during cloning
[141], [142], which simplifies the process. For the J23100 biosensors, the synthetic pro-
moter was added as a primer overhang, which simplified the USER cloning, as only two
fragments were needed.
Lastly, a design decision was made specifically for the rne biosensor that deviates from
the original authors’ design [88]. The original biosensor uses the full rne promoter and
the first 181 amino acids of the rne gene in the biosensor. We decided to remove these
additional amino acids from the biosensor to simplify both the resulting proteins, but also

the genetic parts that are involved in the biosensing. If successful, this promoter can serve
as a simplified genetic part that can easily be incorporated into other synthetic biology
contexts.
3.2
The capacity monitor J23100
3.2.1
Initial construction and characterization of a J23100 TIR li-
brary
The J23100 biosensor has previously been used as a capacity monitor to detect a reduction
of resources in a cell by having a constitutive expression of a fluorescent protein without

Next, based on results from Tan et al. [119], we decided to try and express Maltose Binding
Protein (MBP) from a pET plasmid. This protein was chosen as it has been shown to
constitute up to 50% of the protein in the cytoplasm of E. coli without having a large impact
on many stress responses, such as the folding stress response associated with the heat shock
proteins [119]. It should be noted that the plasmid expressing MBP has two differences to
the previous plasmids. First, it uses an ampicillin resistance marker instead of a kanamycin
marker. Second, it uses a rhamnose promoter to express MBP instead of the T7 promoter
used previously. This means that the gene is transcribed by the native E. coli polymerase
and not the T7 RNAP. This means that competiteon for RNAP availability is also being
included in the overall burden measurement. These two differences are not expected to
have a big impact on the results, but it is always important to be cognizant of the differences
in genetic background.
The results as seen in Figure 13 present the already observed behavior, with relative flu-
orescence increasing slightly for the induced strains over the uninduced strains carrying
pMBP. However, this difference is very small and not significant. Interestingly, the rel-
ative fluorescence for the D11 biosensor variant is lower when it is co-transformed with
the pMBP plasmid, regardless of induction. One possible explanation for this is that the
metabolic burden of maintaining the pMBP plasmid is sufficiently high to decrease the
fluorescence output of the J23100 promoter placed on the BAC.
Altogether, this could suggest that the maintenance of the pMBP plasmid constitutes a
slight metabolic burden, and then either the MBP is not expressed when induced with
rhamnose or the J23100 biosensors on the BACs are insensitive to the expression of MBP.

3.3.1
Verification of folding sensor functionality in Evo21(DE3)
The IbpA folding stress sensor has been shown to respond to the production of misfolding
proteins [133]. In this project, we are using the Evo21(DE3) strain, which is different from
the Rosetta™2(DE3) pLysS strain used in the original study. We were therefore first inter-
ested in verifying that the IbpA sensor still works in this new strain context. Evo21(DE3)
was therefore transformed with the biosensor plasmid (pSEVA631-IbpA-GFP-ASV), and
the two human proteins (pET22b-BRCA-BRCT-trunc and pET22b-PARP1-BRCT-trunc).
Of these, the BRCA is known to misfold, thereby inducing a response from the IbpA pro-
moter after induction. Upon induction, the proteins will also express an mCherry, which
indicates serves as an indicator of successful translation of the whole protein. In this ex-
periment, the PARP expressing strains generally have a higher red fluorescence than the
BRCA strains. This is to be expected as the original authors also found a slight difference
in the mCherry expression levels between the two constructs [133].
The results from this experiment can be seen in Figure 17. We can see that both the
BRCA and the PARP expressing strains have a higher red fluorescence when induced,
which means that the protein is successfully expressed, and that the mCherry can be trans-
lated. The translation sensor response activates slower in the BRCA strains compared to
the PARP strains, which could indicate that the difference in fluorescence levels could be
caused by difference in translation efficiency. Next, we see that only the expression of the
BRCA protein induces the production of green fluorescence from the IbpA biosensor. Here
it is interesting to note that the response from the IbpA promoter and the GFP becomes
noticeable very quickly after induction with IPTG. This is a great feature of the biosensors,
since it allows for rapid diagnostics of protein misfolding. With this experiment, we have
now verified that the exact system developed by Zutz et al. also works in the Evo21(DE3)
strain background, also without a pLysSRARE plasmid, which enables us to move forward
with the construction and characterization of the IbpA biosensor on a BAC.

DISCUSSION
4
Discussion
In this project, four different biosensors have been adapted to the genetic context of a bac-
terial artificial chromosome. The results and progress of the four different biosensors is
discussed below, in the order in which their results were presented. At the end, future
perspectives and opportunities of this system and methodology are discussed.
4.1
The J23100 capacity monitor
The J23100 biosensor was used as a capacity monitor by Ceroni et al. to measure the
metabolic burden associated with the expression of heterologous proteins. They integrated
the system on the genome of two K-12 derived E. coli strains and produced very interest-
ing results that showed the burden could affectively be measured quantitatively through a
decrease in the production rate of GFP.
In this project, the capacity monitor was adapted to function in this new protein production
system. The main differences between the original system from Ceroni et al. and this
version are: the change of GFP to RFP, move from chromosomal integratino to BAC, and
change in strain from DH10B to Evo21(DE3). Of these, the difference in strain could be
the greatest, as BL21 derived strains generally behave differently than K-12 derived strains
in several areas, including stress response [145].
The results gotten from the use of the J23100 biosensor on the BACs have showed multiple
things. Two of the three initial TIR variants (E3 and F5) had identical TIRs and very similar
expression levels. However, when the new B2 variant was identified, it showed a higher
fluorescence compared to both D11 and E3, but the DNA sequence of the TIR was identical
to the E3 variant. This suggests that something else is affecting the expression strength of
the biosensor. One possibility is that the copy-number control of the plasmids has been
mutated, which could increase the basal expression of mCherry. However, no difference
in sequence between the variants have been found yet.
Going forward, it could be interesting toscreen the wholeTIR library through Fluorescence-
Activated Cell Sorting (FACS), possibly even FACS-sort [146], [147], to gain a better un-
derstanding of the TIR-to-expression relationship. This could also widen the available
search space from the 94 variants that could fit in a microtiter plate, to how many were
able to being transformed into E. coli.

DISCUSSION
4.1.1
Effectiveness of the BAC-based J23100 biosensor as a ca-
pacity monitor
As mentioned above, the expected results from the capacity monitor were that the pro-
duction of fluorescent protein would decrease when heterologous proteins were expressed.
However, for almost all experiments, upon induction, we instead saw an increase in fluo-
rescence, whether it was measured as relative fluorescence or capacity, as was done for the
Lux and Luc operons.
Generally, we see a decrease in growth rate upon induction with the chemical inducers.
This suggests that resources are being directed away from growth towards production of
the heterologous proteins. This is the classical way of measuring metabolic burden, and
clearly shows the cells being burdend by the expression. The question then becomes why
this does not translate into a decrease in fluorescence from the capacity monitor.
Interestingly, we have indicationsthatthe capacity monitoris affected bycontinual metabolic
burden of plasmid maintenance. This is most clearly demonstrated in the induction of the
Luc and Lux operons from pSB1K3 in M9 media. Here, the uninduced states of the strains
with the sensors and the stressor plasmids co-transformed have a lower growth rate, lower
capacity, and lower relative fluorescence. All of this indicates that the three identified ca-
pacity monitors variants might work as intended in this scenario.
One possible explanation for this could be that the induction of heterologous protein ex-
pression is consuming so many of the cells’ translation resources that they respond by pro-
ducing more ribosomes to maintain a level of free ribosomes that can translate the proteins
essential for survival and growth [148]. This increased level of ribosomes could then be ap-
plied to translate the mCherry mRNA produced from the J23100 promoter, which would
lead to increased fluorescence.
The involvement of the translation system through the synthesis of new ribosomes aligns
with the fact that we see similar results irrespective of which RNA polymerase is used. If
competition between different native promoters were the major cause of this behavior, we
would have seen different results whether the orthogonal T7 RNA polymerase was used,
or if the native RNA polymerase was used, as in the MBP and Luc/Lux experiments.
4.1.2
Possible further experiment for the J23100 capacity monitor
A possible way to investigate the behavior in further detail is to include measurements of
transcription alone, instead of transcription and translations coupled together. This could
be done by using fluorescent aptamer such as broccoli [149], or through qPCR. By doing
Page 54 of 77
==PAGE:68==
4
DISCUSSION
this, we can examine whether the transcription from the J23100 is upregulated or not when
the heterologous proteins are expressed.
The state of ribosome stress could also be assayed by measuring the ppGpp levels in the cells
with and without induction. This could provide valuable information on the internal state
of the cell, and which adaptations the cells are making to accomodate the new production
of hetrologous proteins [148].
Another approach is to examine the difference between the system presented in this thesis,
and the system described by Ceroni et al.. Differences such as the fluorescent protein,
TIR, and strain background have already been mentioned, and these could be investigated
individually. For example, the original capacity monitors in the DH10B strain could be
tested with both a GFP and a RFP to verify the effects these have on the functioning of the
capacity monitor. Subsequently, the system could be moved to the genome of Evo21(DE3),
or to a BAC and transferred from DH10B to Evo21(DE3). By taking these smaller steps,
the continual function of the monitor could be verified, and the impacts of the different
factors could be better understood.
Overall, many options exist for verifying the results from Ceroni et al. in a stepwise manner
until the desired system is produced. However, it is not possible to know which of these
steps will work as intended and which will not before it is tried.
4.2
The IbpA folding stress sensor
The folding stress sensor IbpA was originally developed by Zutz et al. to detect misfold-
ing proteins in E. coli. The system was based around a plasmid with the pBBR1 origin of
replication since this version showed a clearer separation between induced and uninduced
strains.
In this project, production of a misfolding protein was used to identify three TIR variants
of the IbpA folding sensor that showed the highest dynamic range when the protein was
expressed or not. These three variants show no conservation of nucleotides in the random-
ized TIR, which is promising for a screening of diversity such as this. However, only 48
different colonies were tested in this project, and it is therefore possible that an untested
variant would be even better than the ones identified. To solve this, more variants could
be screened in the same manner, or using cell sorting in a FACS. However, since we are
concerned about the dynamic range of the cells, the sorting would need to be performed
twice with an induced and uninduced culture. Then, the identification of specific variants
would become more difficult, even if analyzed with powerful tools such as FACS-seq [146],
[147].

Lastly, the two new proteins, Bat LPMO and YidC-GFP were tested using variant 38 of
the IbpA folding sensor. In this experiment, a large response to both proteins were seen
as a result of induction with IPTG. However, the Bat LPMO uses a PelB secretion tag to
be transported to the periplasm of E. coli, so it is unclear why this protein provides such
a high fluorescence from the folding sensor. It could be that the protein is not secreted to
the periplasm and therefore activates the folding response, but this is currently unclear. It
could be investigated by analyzing the proteins from both the cytoplasm and periplasm to
determine the amounts of LPMO that is secreted.
The expression of the YidC-GFP protein also showed a clear response from the folding
sensor. One explanation for this could be that strong overexpression of the YidC protein
from the T7 expression systems is so toxic that multiple stress systems are activated at
once. Alternatively, it could be that the folding sensor responds to the production of YidC
because it misfolds when overexpressed. This aligns well with the fact that it is a membrane
protein, and if these cannot be inserted into the membrane, the proteins tend to aggregate
due to the large number of hydrophobic amino acids that usually interacts with the inside
of the membrane [150], [151].
4.2.1
Further experiments for the IbpA folding sensor
Going forward, the folding sensor could be used to screen for a wider set of heterologous
proteins. One place to start would be to use the set of heterologous proteins tested by Tan
et al. [119] to compare the results of the in vivo folding sensor, to their transcriptomics
data. Of special interest would be the proteins which highly activated the RpoH folding
response, as the IbpA promoter is an RpoH responsive promoter. Furthermore, doing the
experiments with defined calibrants [152] and/or using flow cytometry could improve the
resolution and comparability of the results across larger sets of experiments.
An interesting difference between the original system described by Zutz et al. and the
BAC based IbpA biosensor described in this thesis is that the GFP used on the original
biosensor is an unstable variant called GFP-ASV. This means that the fluorescent protein
generally stays at a lower concentration, and that the measurement of direct fluorescence
is a more accurate measurement of protein production as opposed to protein production
and accumulation [153]. This could be one reason why the BAC version of the biosensor
sees a lower dynamic range than the original biosensor, since the uninduced version will
accumulate mCherry over time. This could be fixed by introducing a degradation tag to
mCherry, like the ssrA tag [154].
There is also an ongoing project that is testing the ability of the IbpA folding sensor to

sensor is being used in conjunction with folding factors provided by CyDisCo [112] or

This application is a good demonstration of the promise of the new protein production

4.3
The RNase E biosensor rne
The rne biosensor was developed to measure the activity of RNase E in vivo, which indi-
cated to be an important factor in mRNA stability for heterologous protein production.
The hope was that the biosensor could be used to identify cases where the limiting factor
in the production of proteins were the mRNA stability, and then a solution could be ap-
plied. This could include various options, such as helper plasmids like pLysS-Max [88], or
the transfer to other production strain.
The progress of constructing andcharacterizationof therne biosensorin this project started
with the identification of interesting TIR variants co-transformed with the YidC-GFP plas-
mid. However, since the rne BAC had a low transformation efficiency, it was not possible
to screen a large library of variants. This means that the diversity of the library has been
severely limited, and the risk that the optimal variant is not part of the screened library is
high.
Many of the nine screened variants had a large uncertainty in their relative fluorescence.
This could indicate that the tested rne biosensors were not isogenic, but rather contained
a mixture of different variants, be it TIR variants or others.
It was therefore chosen that one TIR variant with a small spread in individual measure-
ments and one variant with a large spread should be used for further characterization. In-
terestingly, when these were analyzed in more replicates, the spread in the measurements
of variant 1 increased while the spread in variant 6 stayed relatively consistent. This could
suggest that the original characterization that indicated that variant 1 was more homogenic
was the real outlier.
Unfortunately, it was not possible to isolate and sequence the TIRs of the two variants.
When sequenced, the rne promoter and the mCherry gene were missing and had been
replaced by random parts from elsewhere. This replacement was BLASTed and found to
be present in various cloning vectors, but not in the pCap3CC2M1 BAC that has been
employed throughout this project. It is unclear where this sequence comes from, and it is

also unclear where in the process this was introduced. With more time, this experiment
should be repeated and extra care should be included to verify the DNA sequence of the
library at multiple steps to avoid this sitation repeating.
Going forward, it would be interesting to compare the shortend design presented in this
project to the original design from Heyde et al. Here, the first 181 amino acids of RNase
E were also included, which could have a large effect on the behavior of the sensor. As
mentioned in section 3.1, this sequence was omitted to simplify the design and to split up

the results.
4.4
The metabolic burden sensor htpG1
The htpG1 biosensor was developed by Ceroni et al. to rapidly measure the metabolic
burden imposed on a system by the production of proteins. The original authors then
used this to repress transcription of the heterologous protein through CRISPRi, where the
gRNA was expressed from the htpG1 promoter. In this project, the goal was to test this
promoter by using it to express a fluorescent protein.
The initial construction process looked promising with a successful sequencing of the TIR
library. The noise in the chromatograms indicated a large variety in the randomized TIR,
which was what we intended. However, it was not possible to isolate and identify an indi-
vidual TIR variant from this library. This behavior is unexpected, as it is distinct from the
others. In the rne biosensor, the promoter and mCherry genes were missing, but in this
case, no sequencing results was produced. One possibility for this case is that the BAC with
the biosensor was lost at some point during the cultivation, or that the strains somehow
acquired resistance to chloramphenicol.
In any case, it could be very interesting to repeat this experiment to identify TIR variants
of this biosensor on a BAC. In parallel, it could also be worthwhile to investigate the orig-
inal constructs by Ceroni et al., as they were also tested in a BL21(DE3) strain, thereby
minimizing the difference in systems, as discussed above.

4.5
Future perspectives
Common for all these biosensors is their purpose as tools that can be used in a larger
system. This has largely defined their design, with the use of BACs, and the use of mCherry
as the fluorescent protein. Going forward, these biosensors should be tested together with
the other tools that are going to be used in this protein production platform. This includes
the DisCoTune system, and the SEGA system, either genome integrated or moved to a
BAC. From this, the merits of this new system can be more directly compared with the
existing T7 expression system.
As an example, a BAC with the IbpA folding sensor and the SEGA landing pad could
be used to effeciently screen for proteins that misfold. A library of heterologous proteins
could easily be integrated into the SEGA landing pad in a strain expressing the lambda
recombination proteins. During expression from the already integrated T7 promoter, the
folding of the proteins could be monitored from the fluorescence levels observed from the
folding sensor. Based on these results, misfolding proteins could easily be transferred to
an alternative strain that overexpresses chaperones to facilitate protein folding, still under
supervision of the IbpA folding sensor.
The use of more than one biosensor at a time could be interesting for rapid screening of
heterologous protein production. However, since all biosensors currently use the same
fluorescent protein, it would be impossible to distinguish them. One option to solve this
problem would be to use different, orthogonal fluorescent proteins. This would require
that the TIRs be re-optimized for each combination of sensor and fluorescent protein. A
solution to this could be to include protein tags at the N-terminal of the protein, such as
a His-tag, to have a standardized, optimized TIR. This has been done before in the LyGo
system, and works to great effect [41].

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
In this project, four biosensors that respond to various stresses associated with heterolo-
gous protein production have been identified from literature, and incorporated as biosen-
sors in the context of a bacterial artificial chromosome. All biosensors were TIR optimized
to adapt them to the new context of the bacterial artificial chromosome. These four biosen-
sors were: the J23100 promoter, which was used as a capacity monitor to measure the
available resources in the cell, the IbpA heat shock promoter, which was used to measure
misfolding of proteins, the RNase E promoter rne that was used to measure RNase E ac-
tivity, and the htpG1 heat shock promoter, which was used to measure metabolic burden.
Of these fourbiosensors, thehtpG1 metabolicburden biosensorand therne biosensor were
harder to sequence verify on the level of individual TIR variants. Further experimentation
is therefore required to identify the specific variants of these biosensors. For the J23100
capacity monitor, three promising TIR variants were isolated and sequenced. It was found
that the burden associated with plasmid maintenance could be measured as a decrease in
growth rate and fluorescence from the strains, but production of heterologous proteins
resulted in an increase in relative fluorescence for most tested proteins. This behavior was
unexpected and runs counter to the precedent set in the original literature. This suggests
that parts of this system is still not fully understood, and that more research is required.
Lastly, variant 38 of the IbpA folding biosensor was identified as the best of the screened
variants for the purpose of measuring the misfolding of heterologous expressed proteins.
This biosensor was used on two proteins that previously have not been known to misfold,
but found that they triggered the misfolding response. This is very promising as it could
enable further optimization of the protein expression system to increase the efficiency of
protein production through the elimination of these stresses.
In the future, these biosensors can be integrated to the new protein expression platform to
serve as tools for the identification of stresses during protein production. This will allow
researchers easy access to pre-optimized diagnostic tools, thereby lowering the barrier of
entry, and increasing the breadth of possible research.
