Abstract
This thesis presents the theory, design, and development of an organic sensor surface that uses magne-
tometers todetectshearandnormalstresseswithoutdeadzones. Thesensorsurfaceisbeingdeveloped
to aid the development of offloading devices for treating patients with diabetic foot ulcers. The sensor
surface is constructed by suspending magnets in silicone above tri-axial magnetometers which measure
the relative displacements of the suspended magnets. The surface is made up of multiple individual
points which use the elastic properties of silicone to act like a continuous surface. In this thesis, a sen-
sor point and flat surface have been constructed and tested with a custom-built testbed that has been
developed in parallel with the sensor. These tests have then been used to design the final organic sur-
face. Further work is required to manufacture the final organic sensor surface and calibrate it to measure
forces.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Pressure ulcers are localized injuries that occur when prolonged pressure and shear are applied to hu-
man tissue (Haesler, 2019). The appliedpressure reduces blood flow, depriving the surrounding tissueof
oxygen, leading to tissue necrosis (Lyder, 2003). Although anyone can develop pressure ulcers, diabetics
are at an increased risk because of the neuropathy and circulatory problems caused by diabetes.
Since 1980, the number of people with diabetes has risen from 108 million to 422 million in 2014 (World
Health Organization, 2016). Within this population, around 19% - 34% will be diagnosed with diabetic
foot ulcers (DFUs) in their lifetime (Armstrong et al., 2017). Diabetics who have developed DFUs have a
mortality rate that is 2.5 times higher than diabetics without a prior DFU (Walsh et al., 2016). More than
half of DFUs get infected, and around 20% of DFU infections lead to some level of amputation (Armstrong
et al., 2017). Treating diabetic foot ulcers alone has cost the world upward of 900 billion dollars (World
Union of Wound Healing Societies (WUWHS), 2016).
Foot ulcer treatment and prevention is commonly done by applying pressure offloading devices to the
patient’s foot. Pressure offloading devices redistribute pressure over a larger area, reducing localized
pressure and allowing blood flow. Typically, these devices consist of thick padding and must be worn
untiltheulcerishealed. The"goldstandard"ofpressurereliefdevicesisconsideredtobenon-removable
knee-highoffloadingdevices,accordingtotheInternationalWorkingGroupontheDiabeticFoot(IWGDF)
(Bus et al., 2024).
Unfortunately, less than 2% of specialists use the "gold standard" of offloading devices (Wu et al., 2008).
One of the most common reasons for low usage is patient intolerance (Samuelson et al., 2020). While
there are other options for offloading devices, they are not as effective because patients either take
them off, or they have low quality of evidence. Because of low patient compliance, Bispebjerg Hospi-
tal’s wound department contacted DTU to start a research program to develop less cumbersome foot-
offloading devices that perform well.
Currently, the quality of evidence for pressure relief devices is determined by medical trials, which are
very ressource and time-consuming. Furthermore, the results are difficult to utilize when improving the
design of the relief devices. There are some tools that allow for testing of relief devices, before going
through medical trials, but they are designed for flat surfaces. These tools for foot-offloading devices
have to be folded flat, which adds noise and uncertainty to the collected data. To properly test foot-
offloading devices, a testing platform is needed that measures the forces on a foot-like surface.
In previous projects, we’ve created a tool that quantitatively compares foot-offloading devices by mea-
suring the normal forces on the surface of an electronic foot. We built and calibrated a 3D-printed foot
with force-sensing resistors (FSRs) covering the plantar malleol. Our tool allows designers of offloading
devices to measuretheiroffloadingcapabilitiesandcomparethemwitheachother. Thetoolcanbeused
during different stages of the design process to compare and choose better designs.
In this thesis, we continue our prior work by designing a new sensing surface that improves upon the
limitations of our current tool by measuring both normal and shear forces with a significant reduction in
dead zones.

1.1
Initial Problem Statement
During our prior work, we found that even a simple 3D surface can detect pressures that are not de-
tectable on flat sensing surfaces. However, our simple prototype has large dead zone areas and does
not measure shear forces, which is one of the most important factors in pressure ulcer development. We
expect that with a 3D sensing surface that also measure shear forces, we’ll gain even more knowledge in
understanding the efficacy of different pressure relief devices.
Our initial problem statement is therefore:
" How might we design an organic surface that can detect shear and normal stresses, helping pres-
sure relief device designers develop better products for patients with diabetic foot ulcers? "
Although the sensing surface we intend to design should be able to take any shape, not just the foot, we
specifically work towards helping patients with diabetic foot ulcers because of its challenges. Diabetic
foot ulcers require very low amounts of shear and normal forces and the surface of the foot is quite com-
plex. If we can create a concept that solves the challenges of the diabetic foot, we should be able to use
it for other parts of the body as well without having to completely redesign the technology.

2.1.1
Patient
Eric is a 64-year-old male with type 2 diabetes who lives on his own. A couple of days ago, Eric spilled
some hot tea on his foot. He didn’t feel much pain, but he treated it like any other injury with antiseptic
cream and bandages. Today, while getting ready for his grandson’s birthday, he removed the bandage,
expecting the wound to have healed. To Eric’s surprise, instead of healing, it had gotten worse. Eric had
noticed a bad smell for the past few days but didn’t realize it came from his foot. He thought it was a dead
mouse somewhere outside. At the birthday party, Eric tried to hide the smell from his family because he
felt embarrassed. His daughter caught onto the smell and convinced Eric to go see a doctor.
At the doctor’s office, Eric finds out that the wound is not an ordinary wound but a pressure ulcer, which
developed from the damage caused by the hot tea. The doctor explains to Eric that pressure ulcers occur
when an excessive amount of pressure is applied to the tissue. The pressure restricts the blood flow in
the applied area, causing the tissue to suffocate and break down. Since Eric thought it was a regular
wound, he has been wearing his regular footwear, not realizing it restricted his blood flow. The doctor
goes on to explain that since Eric is a diabetic, his blood flow is further restricted, which decreases the
amountofpressureneededforhimtodevelopulcers. Furthermore, hisdiabetesalsodecreaseshissense
of touch and pain, which is why he couldn’t feel the pressure from his shoes.
To help Eric heal his foot ulcer, the doctor prescribed a non-removable total contact cast (TCC) that redis-
tributes the pressure around his foot so the wound gets the necessary blood flow for healing. This cast
is the "gold standard" of pressure relief devices based on the guidelines from the International Working
Group on the Diabetic Foot (IWGDF) (Bus et al., 2024).
Eric lives in a two-story house with his bedroom upstairs. When wearing the offloading device, Eric strug-
gles to walk up and down the stairs. He needs to wear a cast shoe to avoid slipping on the hardwood
floors, but he often walks around without it because it is too cumbersome to wear. Eric also has trouble
sleeping with the cast on. When he moves, his boot keeps getting stuck in the sheets, and he can only lie
flat on his back without laying awkwardly. He manages to fall asleep but wakes up during the night to
go to the bathroom. On the way to the bathroom, Eric almost falls because of the slippery floor.

After a few weeks, Eric goes back to the doctor and insists on having the cast removed. His doctor pre-

device is very similar to the TCC, but Eric only needs to wear it while walking and can take it off when

Eric has worn the RCW for a few weeks but feels very unstable while wearing it. In a span of four days, Eric

2.1.2
Designing relief devices
When designing offloading devices, also known as pressure relief or relief devices, you must ensure they
work as advertised. If you design a pressure-relieving mattress, you can ensure that it distributes pres-
sureevenlybyplacingapressure-sensingmatbetweenthemattressandthetestsubject. Thisallowsyou
to see how well it works and what needs improvement. This is also the case when developing specialty
insoles and other generally flat relief devices.
However, available sensors can’t be used as easily to measure the efficacy of pressure relief boots. Cur-
rent pressure and shear sensing surfaces only work on flat or cylindrical shapes. When testing pressure
relief boots, designers must unfold the boot into a flat sheet and push it onto a sensor mat. Deforming
the relief boot when taking measurements can cause a lot of information about its efficacy to be lost.
More quantitative methods have been explored, such as Finite Element Analysis (Shaulian et al., 2021).
This approach shows a lot of promise, but without real-world measurements, it is difficult to verify the
efficacy of devices designed in this way.
The relief devices are also used in medical trials for a more quantitative test. However, these trials take a
long time, and their data are difficult to generalize since every patient is unique. Generally, when testing
these devices on patients, it is difficult to get precise data since they suffer from neuropathy and can’t
feel the devices working. There are a lot of other factors that affect foot ulcer healing, making it difficult
to figure out how much of an effect the offloading device has had. Furthermore, the data acquired from
medical trials can be challenging to use for improving the relief device since they aren’t specific.
The uncertainties that arise from the lack of testing force designers to add significant safety factors to
theirdesigns. Theyaddextrapaddingtoensurethedevicesrelievepressureasexpected,asillustratedon
figure 2.1 . This becomes a problem when trying to make smaller relief devices. Without measurements,
designers must guess their way to a final design and hope the medical trials succeed.

2.2
Pressure Ulcer Development
Pressure ulcers, also known as bed sores or pressure sores, are localized injuries that are mainly caused
by prolonged pressure being applied to human tissue (Haesler, 2019). The pressure deforms the under-
lying tissue and reduces blood flow in the applied area. When blood flow is restricted, the surrounding
tissue is deprived of oxygen, leading to tissue necrosis (Lyder, 2003). Pressure ulcers can develop any-
where on the body, but they mainly develop near bony prominences since there is less tissue to redis-
tribute the pressure. An illustration of pressure applied at a bony protrusion can be seen on figure 2.3
.
Figure 2.3: Tissue Distortion Pressure Baharestani et al., 2010
As the name implies, pressure is the main extrinsic factor in pressure ulcer development. However, shear
is often mentioned as another high-risk factor (Baharestani et al., 2010). Shear, in combination with
pressure, is thought to increase the deformation of tissue, resulting in further reduction of blood flow as
shown in figure 2.4 .

time shows that high pressures can cause pressure ulcers faster than low pressures. It can be seen in

2.3.3
Problems with the Current Prototype
As stated earlier in the report, we’ve identified some problems with our current prototype that we’d like
to resolve.
The first problem is the sizeable dead zone area on the sensor surface, as seen on figure 2.10 . The
current size and arrangement of FSR sensors result in 40% of the surface being dead zones, leaving only
60% of the surface being active. This results in a lot of the forces not being picked up by the sensor. We’ve
tried to mitigate it by conforming the silicone so the forces get directed toward the active sensing areas,
but that only marginally improves the problem. In an ideal world, we would like to have no dead zones,
meaning that our surface can detect forces anywhere on the surface.
Deadzones
Figure 2.10: Illustration showing the dead zone area of our current prototype based on FSR

The second problem is the low resolution. When comparing the foot sensor to the sensor mat, it is very
clear that there are benefits to having a higher resolution. The sensor mat captures much more detail
than our prototype and it is even possible to see the shape of the relief device as seen on figure 2.9
. Achieving a higher resolution is not as simple as scaling down the sensors and adding more. With
FSR sensors, the outer lip limits the benefits of smaller sizes since the lip width takes up most of the
area saved. There is also an issue in manufacturing and reading the sensors since there would be many
more connections to manage and read from. This would require us to use more detailed manufacturing
methods and expensive microprocessors.
The third problem is the lack of shear measurements. Our research on pressure ulcer development
shows that shear force is a significant risk factor (Baharestani et al., 2010). Few studies currently mea-
sure shear forces, and the majority of them do so using Finite Element Analysis. There are currently no
sensors that can measure shear and pressure on an organic surface. Based on the information we got
from developing an organic surface that measures normal forces, we imagine there is more to learn if we
can also measure shear forces.
2.4
Measureable metrics
Before defining our final problem statement and creating a target specification, we need to figure out
what measurable metrics have been found to cause pressure ulcers. During our previous projects, we
found that it is very difficult to get an exact number. We’ve learned from research that it is highly variable
across the body and differs from person to person (Haesler, 2019). However, we’ve found some sources
that suggest lower limits for normal and shear pressure and will use those values as a starting point.
12
==PAGE:22==
2.4.1
Normal Pressure
Since the needed pressures for developing pressure ulcers vary widely, there isn’t a generic quantitative
number we can use for our target specification. However, from our research, we found papers stating
that for durations longer than 2h, a pressure of 9kPa consistently resulted in tissue damage (Linder-Ganz
et al., 2006). We, therefore, aim to be able to detect 9kPa of normal pressure with our concept.
2.4.2
Shear Pressure
Shear pressure always acts in conjunction with normal pressure. Therefore, the value of shear pressure
alone is not definable. However, a study showed that shear pressures around 9.8kPa lowered the re-
quired pressure to stop blood flow by half. This means that if 9.8kPa shear force is applied to tissue, the
pressure required to stop blood flow is half of what is needed without shear forces (Bennett et al., 1979).
9.8kPa is, therefore, what we aim to be able to measure with our final concept.
2.4.3
Deadzones
Dead zones are difficult to define and measure unless they are binary. In our previous prototype, that
was the case since applying forces in between the sensors resulted in no readings. For this project, we
aim to eliminate dead zones from the surface, but we will not necessarily be able to detect the full forces
acting on the surface. That means that we want to detect if forces are applied in between sensors, but
we don’t expect to be able to measure these forces accurately.

Final Problem Statement

sub-points to our problem statement and devise a target specification.
" How might we design an organic surface that can detect shear and normal stresses, helping pres-
sure relief device designers develop better products for patients with diabetic foot ulcers? "
1. How can we measure shear and normal stresses on a surface while reaching a satisfactory balance
between dead zones and resolution?
2. How can we test if our sensing surface accurately measures the forces applied to it?
3. How can we create a general surface for diabetic foot ulcers while also being able to utilize it for

Chapter 3
Approach and Methods
Our project continues our previous work, in which we explored the project more widely and developed
a working proof of concept. In our prior work, we went through an entire design process, so this project
serves as the next iteration. Throughout designing and testing the first iteration of our concept, we found
areas of improvement that needed to be addressed.
This project is very technical to meet the demands of our improvement areas. We are trying to build
something that hasn’t been built before without knowing how or if we can achieve it. A flexible and
iterative structure is therefore needed to allow for continuous learning and evaluation.
The iterative aspect of design can be found in a lot of larger models and can also be seen as a tool. There
will naturally be iterations in any design project, but structuring a project around them can be challeng-
ing. Iterative loops can quickly become complex if you have multiple variables you are trying to under-
stand or refine. The learnings gained from each iteration can change the direction of the project which
makes it difficult to plan with traditional project structures. However, technical challenges need struc-
ture to be achieved since they heavily rely on data to measure their performance. We, therefore, need
to strike a balance between solving technical problems and allowing our findings to change our project
plan.
3.1
Design Approach
We were inspired by the Pahl and Beitz approach to design, which balances the complexities of technical
projects while allowing for flexibility. Using their model as a guide, we defined a systematic yet flexible
process by incorporating aspects of other design processes as well. Furthermore, we incorporate their
suggestions when designing mechatronic problems especially when regarding dividing the responsibil-
ity of each discipline.
3.1.1
Pahl and Beitz
The general design process is based on Pahl and Beitz’ model of the design process as seen on figure 3.1
. This process consists of four phases that guide designers from understanding the problem to a finalized
design.
This overall design approach elegantly breaks down the project into steps of increasing complexity. The
model is intentionally built around flexibility and serves only as a guideline. It emphasizes the iterative
approach, but the sequence of the steps is not considered rigid (Pahl et al., 2007). The model should be
used in accordance with the team’s design experience to create a suitable process for the project.
16
==PAGE:26==
Figure 3.1: Pahl and Beitz’ general approach to design. Pahl et al., 2007
The four phases of Pahl and Beitz’s process are useful divisions of the design process, each serving an
important purpose.
The first phase, also called Planning and Task Clarification, defines the concept’s specifications in the
form of a requirements list (Pahl et al., 2007). This is the base for the following phases and is expected to
be continuously updated throughout the project. This phase has been covered extensively in our prior
projects. For the explorative nature of our project the detail level of our requirements list has been very
broad to allow us to shape it with the knowledge gained throughout the project.
The second phase is called Conceptual Design. This phase defines the principle solution by abstract-
ing the problem. Since we don’t know how the solution will work, it is valuable to work with abstrac-
tions of the problem without getting into specific details. This allows designers to ideate and prototype
muchfasterwithoutbuildinganything. Generally, thisphaseconsistsofestablishingfunctionstructures,
searching for working principles, and then combining the principles into a working structure.
The third phase Embodiment Design is the largest phase of the process and is extensively covered by
17
==PAGE:27==
(Pahl et al., 2007). This phase uses the conceptual design and constructs it into a final ’layout’. A layout
is defined as the arrangement and relative motions of the components (Pahl et al., 2007). In this phase,
designers will iterate and refine the layout through prototypes and testing. The complexity of this phase
makes it difficult to define a strict plan to follow. Instead, a general approach is suggested that divides
the phase into the main working steps.
The final phase is the Detailed Design phase, which defines the final design. In this phase, all of the ar-
rangements, forms, dimensions, and manufacturing details are defined to prepare the product for mass
production.
Pahl and Beitz explain that it is not always possible to clearly divide each part of the process (Pahl et al.,
2007). Sometimes, it is necessary to take a step forward before being able to complete the previous.
They also explain that backtracking is inevitable since you will find unexpected results that influence
previous parts of the process.
These reflections from Pahl and Beitz are a large reason why we drew inspiration from their process.
By acknowledging the messiness of the design process, we allow ourselves to plan accordingly, thereby
designing a better product than if we follow a linear process.
3.1.2
Division of work
Since our project is a mechatronics project, we drew some more inspiration from Pahl and Beitz. To de-
velop mechatronic products, an interdisciplinary team with skills in mechanical, electrical, and software
engineering is required. Because of the complexity of mechatronic design, a systematic and holistic ap-
proach is needed (Pahl et al., 2007). It is not possible to draw clear boundaries between each discipline
since all of them have to work together. The boundaries have to be fluid, which increases the need for
flexibility. Experts from each discipline take responsibility for their tasks, requiring continuous coordina-
tion and adjustments to the plan. The workload, complexity, and time requirements of each discipline
can vary throughout the project, further complicating the process.
In this project, we divided the work based on the team’s competencies. The author of this paper was
responsible for the electronic and software aspects, and the partner was responsible for the mechan-
ical aspects. Although we’ve divided responsibility, we’ve kept the boundaries fluid to allow for more
interdisciplinary work.

The stage gate model is widely used and is based on the idea of breaking down a project into smaller
stages. Each stage functions as a decision point where the team decides whether or not to continue with
the project (J. Edgett, 2022).

The agile development model is a fairly new approach popularized by the tech industry. It focuses on
creating larger products by iteratively creating minimally viable products that increase in functionality
(Boeijen et al., 2014). A nice way of illustrating the differences between Agile and Stage Gate can be seen
on figure 3.2 .

The general approach of the agile method is very good since it forces the team to work on the most
important parts of the product and test them throughout. However, for physical products it can be very
difficult to utilize the agile process. The reason agile works so well for software is that there is almost

that allow for modular code to be written which makes it a lot easier to collaborate on projects. The
process from concept to embodiment is almost instant in software since specifying the requirements
and architecture is the hardest part.
3.1.5
Double Diamond
Double diamond is both a design model and a general design philosophy. The double diamond is a
simple visualization of the fundamental design methodology in its entirety (The Design Council, n.d.).
While models like agile mostly focuses on developing the product, the double diamond illustrates the
importance of both understanding and solving the problem. This is what sets designers apart from other
engineers since we are just as interested in why we solve the problem as how we solve it. A trap that a lot
of engineers can fall into is creating very complex and impressive technical systems but forget to make
it usable to the people they were trying to help. It is generally very difficult to balance deep technical
work with a more abstract purpose, which is why so many models exist. In other models, the task of
figuring out the problem and defining it is usually separated from the task of creating the solution. This
can lead to a disconnect between the solution and the problem. The double diamond suggests that the
same team does both, greatly reducing this divide.
3.1.6
Discussion on using methods and processes
All design methods and models try to help manage a project and guide teams to create the best possible
solution. They all have different approaches that might work in some cases and not in others. As design-
ers, we know that the work is much more messy and complex than the models suggest, and the team
rarely sticks closely to the initial plan of the project. It is, therefore, better to understand what situations
each model and process is trying to guide you through and use them for that. For example, the stage gate
model is not the most suitable model to use when exploring the problem since you don’t know enough
about the problem to define the gates.

==PAGE:29==
The most important part of design processes and methods is the shared understanding they create. A
teamcannotfunctioniftheyareworkingindifferentdirectionsandneedasharedstructuretocooperate.
Design methods provide this structure and explains it in a way that the whole team can understand. A
study at DTU found that this is the value generated by these methods (Jensen & Andreasen, 2010). They
foundthatdesignersrarelysticktotheirmethodcompletely, butbystartingwithasharedunderstanding
of what they need to do, they bend the methods to fit their needs and facilitate teamwork.
This is not a critique of methods or processes. There is no doubt about the value these methods have
generated and the projects they’ve helped. The paper reframes the understanding of how design meth-
ods work, which allows us to utilize them better. With this perspective, we can be more flexible in the
planning of our project and tailor a process that fits the project and the team.

General Methods

Desk Research
Method 3.4.1
Desk research is the search for existing knowledge about a subject or topic. The source of knowl-
edge can be anything from books to videos. It is largely used to get an overview of a topic before
going into more in-depth research methods. (Valgeirsdottir et al., 2024)
Litterature Review
Method 3.4.2
A literature review is the process of distilling information from literature and showing how it has
affected the paper or project. To perform a good literature review, a systematic approach is re-
quired to organize the information. Although literature reviews may be freestanding, they are
often one component of a larger paper. (Martin & Hanington, 2012)
Data-Centric Design
Method 3.4.3
Data-Centric design consists of utilizing quantitative data to gain design-relevant insights. This
data can give the designer a more detailed understanding of the project and help guide the de-
sign process. The largest limitation of this approach is the technical knowledge required to col-
lect and analyze quantitative data. (Boeijen et al., 2014)

==PAGE:32==
Clustering
Method 3.4.4
Clustering is an organizing method where knowledge gets put into groups based on their similar-
ity. The most popular clustering method is the KJ method, where the team clusters knowledge
in silence before naming them. (Martin & Hanington, 2012).
Prototyping
Method 3.4.5
Prototyping is the process of manifesting an idea to answer questions about it (Hansen, 2021).
The manifestation can take many shapes but is usually physical, digital, or a process. Prototypes
can test ideas on various levels, from low-fidelity cardboard mockups to high-fidelity electronic
devices. To use prototypes effectively, designers need to ask the right questions and build the
right model to answer them (Hansen, 2021).
Rapid Prototyping
Method 3.4.6
Rapid prototyping is a group of methods used to fabricate models or prototypes quickly using
computer-aided design (CAD) tools. Usually, these prototypes are created with additive man-
ufacturing methods, but other manufacturing methods can also be used if they are fast. Com-
mon manufacturing methods are FDM 3D printing, SLA 3D printing, and laser cutting (Wikipedia,
2024c).
Electrical Circuit Design
Method 3.4.7
ElectricalCircuitDesignconsistsoffindingcomponentsthatmeettherequirementsandconnect-
ing them together to achieve the desired effect. There are often many different ways of designing
a circuit with many components that do the same thing. To design electrical circuits effectively,
one must read and understand the datasheets for each component to properly connect and use
them.
Programming
Method 3.4.8
Programming is the process of instructing a computer to do a task. In the modern age of com-
puters, programming is an essential tool that can be utilized in almost any process. Although
programming is very time-consuming, modern programming languages such as Python are very
easy touseandcanworkdirectlywithalmostanydigitaltool. Sincemostpeoplehavecomputers
nowadays, more and more people use programming to solve problems. Thanks to the internet,
these solutions are shared and built upon to make programming tasks even simpler. Program-
ming is especially useful for tasks that are tedious to do by hand, such as data analysis, repeated
tasks, and controlling different software or hardware.

Serial Communication
Method 3.4.9
Serial communication is a common method of communication between a microcontroller and a
computer. The process consists of sending data one bit at a time over one or more wires, which
is called a BUS. Connectors and protocols define how different devices can communicate over
serial. The most commonly used protocols for low-cost electronics are SPI and I2C (Wikipedia,
2024f).
Experiments
Method 3.4.10
Experiments are used to determine cause and effect. They are a structured form of testing in
which one variable of the design is changed at a time to see its effects. This method allows the
team to gain very specific knowledge about a design or process. Experiments need to be de-
signed as unbiased as possible to reduce the influence of external variables. Experiments can
take a long time to perform and usually require close control over the testing procedure with
multiples of the same design to be accurate. (Martin & Hanington, 2012)
Silicone Casting
Method 3.4.11
Silicone casting is used to create flexible silicone parts of molds. Castable silicone usually comes
as two parts that need to be mixed to cure. Once mixed, an optional step of degassing the sili-
cone can be performed in a vacuum pot to suck out the air introduced while mixing, leading to a
more clear and smooth result. After degassing, the silicone is poured into a mold that has been
prepared with mold release. The filled mold can then be left to cure or optionally be put into
a pressure pot to pressurize the silicone which will shrink the remaining bubbles. The biggest
challenge of silicone casting is the mold and its design. Molds need to be well designed so that
the silicone can fill them up without trapping bubbles or leaking out. Silicone’s flexibility allows
the finished part to be removed from the mold more easily, and the silicone part can have un-
derhangs that typically require much more complicated mold designs for non-flexible materials.
Silicone can also be used to create molds; however, because of its flexibility, silicone molds don’t
keep accurate dimensions. (formlabs, 2024b)
Iterative Design
Method 3.4.12
Iterative design is a method for improving an existing design by testing it, analyzing the results,
and creating a new version. It is used both consciously and subconsciously in many fields and
for different purposes. Since it is used in many different fields, it goes by many names with their
own twists that make it suitable for their specific purpose. Examples of these are "The Lean Start-
up" (Boeijen et al., 2014), "IBM Loop" (IBM, n.d.), and the "Agile" process (Boeijen et al., 2014),
to name a few. It is a great tool for refining a design but should not be used in divergent phases
of the design process. This way of designing can lead the team towards suboptimal solutions if
they haven’t done the necessary work to explore the solution space beforehand.

Robust design is the practice of designing products so that their performance is insensitive to
different sources of variation. There are six sources of variation: material stock, manufacturing

can be used to decrease performance sensitivity in each of these variation sources (Eifler, 2023).
Fused Deposition Modeling - 3D Printing
Method 3.4.16
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) is an additive manufacturing method of 3D printing where
molten plastic is fed through a heated nozzle and deposited in a stack of 2D layers. This stack of
2D layers end up as a 3D object once all of the layers are deposited. This manufacturing method
is highly versatile and cheap and is therefore used a lot in prototyping method 3.4.5 . By stacking
layers on top of each other, FDM printing allows for unique geometry to be produced at a much
cheaper rate than traditional subtractive manufacturing methods. The main limitations of FDM
3D printing are concerning surface quality, strength, and mass production. (Carolo, 2024)
Stereolithography - 3D Printing
Method 3.4.17
Stereolithography (SLA) is a method of additive manufacturing that uses a vat of UV curing resin
on top of a UV screen. The UV screen exposes 2D images onto a rising buildplate one layer
at a time. Based on the resolution of the UV screen and the buildplate mechanism SLA print-
ing can produce geometry with almost perfect surface quality. Compared to FDM 3D printing
method 3.4.16 , SLA printing is mechanically simple. However, the complexities of SLA printing
lies in the resin. UV curing resin is very toxic and needs to be handled with care. Furthermore,
SLA prints need mandatory post-processing steps to fully cure the resin and make them safe to
use. SLA printing is therefore not as widely used as FDM printing and should only be used when
precision and surface quality is important. (formlabs, 2024a)

sensors we used had a fairly large deadzone border, which limits how much of the deadzone can be

because of the deadzone border.
For this project, we want to decrease the deadzones as much as possible, so we want to find a sensor
that doesn’t have a deadzone border, but rather sensing range that goes beyond the footprint of the
sensor. Since we don’t know if such a sensor exists we used abstraction method 4.1.1 to describe our
ideal sensor.

within the range of the nob. This lack of stepwise limit means that the value is continuous. On figure 5.2

5.1.4.4
Sensor Reading and Data Collection
Regarding sensors, there is always a tradeoff between quality and speed. Sensor readings can be very
fast, but then they aren’t precise. To overcome this, microcontrollers are needed to average the readings
from the sensors before saving them. The more readings there are in the average, the more precise the
measurement. We, therefore, needed to program averaging into the sensor readings. Although a very
simple task, this now adds a bit of latency to our sensor measurements since it needs to take multiple
readings and average them.
For the averages to be precise, the magnet and sensor should be static during all of the measurements.
If they moved while measuring, the average would be off and skew the results. Therefore, we had to
implement a communication protocol that would allow the 3D printer to stop and wait for readings from
the sensor before moving to the next point. This was done through serial communication by running a
bash script.
When the 3D printer arrives at the specified point, it activates the bash script. The bash script then sends
a request to the sensor asking for its readings. The sensor receives this request, takes measurements,
and returns the average. The bash script then saves the readings to a CSV together with the position of
the magnet. Once the bash script has finished, the 3D printer resumes and moves the magnet to the next
point. This cycle continues until all the points have been measured.

5.1.6.4
Calibration of magnet position
To relate this sensor to our mechanical understanding, we have to model the relationship between the
magnetreadoutandmagnetposition. Currently,thesensorcannotdetermineamagnet’slocation. Itcan
sensethemagnet’smagneticfluxbutnotitsposition. Themagneticfieldofamagnetishighlydependent
on its shape and size. We, therefore, need to calibrate the sensor with a chosen magnet to convert the
magnetic flux readings into the magnet’s position. The spherical nature of the sensor readouts shows
that the sensor response is not linearly dependent on the position of the magnet. The closer the magnet
is to the sensor, the more extreme the readouts get when moving the magnet the same amount. To
figure out the relationship between position and sensor readout we need to calculate the regression of
this relationship.
Regression is very straightforward with Python for a single axis. A couple of lines of code does the trick.
However, it becomes a lot more difficult when trying to regress three axes. We tried to get ChatGPT to
help us, and it gave us a very promising script. Running the code resulted in a very tight regression of
the data. However, when testing the regression model, we quickly found out that it was overfitted to our
data and not usable. We could measure a magnet envelope that has many more points and then use
the dataset as a lookup table. The sensor would then check which point it is closest to in the table and
choose it as its magnet point. This would however, go against our requirement of having a continuous
sensor and the reason we chose this sensor in the first place. By using a lookup table we would make the
sensor readings discrete.
While researching solutions to our problem in literature, we found a paper that also uses the MLX90393
to measure shear and force. According to this paper from (Tomo et al., 2016), they had great results by
using a quadratic regression to calibrate their sensors. They didn’t calibrate it based on magnet position
but rather based on the applied forces once the magnet was inside a suspension material. We, therefore,
decided to return to the calibration problem when having our point prototype. Furthermore, the paper
achieved a very high accuracy with this idea, thereby showing that the idea works and can be calibrated.
We can, therefore, black-box some of the calibration and focus on creating the 3D sensor surface.

Working Principle - Suspension Material
Now that we’ve chosen the components for our point displacement principle, we will choose a suspen-
sion material. Based on our suspended point being a magnet, mechanical understanding and research,
the suspension material has the following requirements:
• Be able to embed magnets into it
• Vary its spring coefficient
• Be attachable to 3D prints without sliding
• Behave isotropically
5.2.1
Suitable Suspension Materials
In our original desk research in the conceptual phase, the two suspension materials found were silicone
andsprings. Sincewewantoursensortobeassmallandsimpleaspossible, wedon’twanttousesprings
as they increase the part count and mechanical complexity. Therefore, we expanded our research to find
suitable suspension materials based on our requirements and landed on the following three.
5.2.1.1
Silicone
The first suspension material we investigated was silicone, which was used in most examples of sus-
pended point sensors we found. It is inherently isotropic, can be found with varying hardnesses, and
magnets can be embedded into it through a two-step molding process inspired by (Chathuranga et al.,
2016) and (Tomo et al., 2016).
Our largest concern with silicone is attaching it to 3D prints. Silicone is known for not sticking to anything
other than silicone, so using an adhesive is out of the question. In our previous project, we attached the
silicone by wrapping it around the 3D print. This approach attached it to the 3D print, but the silicone
could stretch and slide around. We need the embedded magnets to always return to their original point,
and sliding silicone does not allow that.
Another approach could be to create cavities in the 3D print that the silicone can flow into. These cavi-
ties can be shaped so that the silicone doesn’t pull out once it has hardened. For this to work properly,
we’d need the holes to be uniform so we don’t create a bunch of sliding areas but maintain a uniform
attachment. This approach has been explored in a paper by (Rossing et al., 2020), who achieved silicone
attachment to 3D prints by printing grid structures that the silicone could flow into. This sparked the
idea of using the infill of 3D prints since they are very fine structures that the printer can easily print. By
removing the top layers of a print, we can expose this structure and allow the silicone to flow into it.

5.2.1.2
Flexible 3D Prints
Another suspension material that we investigated was flexible 3D prints. This approach could give us
a lot of control over the properties of our suspension material since we can alter the structure of the
print. Inspired by (Carbon 3D, n.d.) we could generate lattice structures with varying hardnesses and
properties. This could allow us to better simulate real skin since the hardness and spring coefficient of
skin varies across the body.
However, this approach has some challenges. The first one is manufacturing. To achieve the precision
needed with flexible 3D prints, we need to use resin-based 3D printers. These are expensive and very
toxic to work with. Furthermore, resin printers print upside down, and the print doesn’t harden until the
postprocessing stage. This approach would require us to pause the prints at certain intervals, manually
add magnets while the print is still dripping with toxic resin and hope the magnets don’t fall out once we
resume the print. Based on the surface shape we want to print and its print orientation, we could end
up doing so many times per print, increasing the risk of print failure. Finally, the lattice structures would
also leave a bunch of holes in the final product, which might be a problem when it comes to cleaning.
These holes cannot be printed over since the added material would affect the mechanical properties.
5.2.1.3
Compliant Mechanisms
Compliant mechanisms are mechanisms that utilize the inherent flexibility of the material to perform
mechanical functions. Unlike traditional mechanisms, compliant mechanisms usually consist of a sin-
glepartandhavebeenshapedinawaythatitsflexibilitydoesnotsurpasstheelasticlimitofthematerial.
The main benefits of compliant mechanisms are their improved durability and scale ranges. Since there
are no moving parts that contact or slide on each other, there is almost no wear and tear on compli-
ant mechanisms. Furthermore, they can be utilized in both microscopic and giant applications where
assembly can be a challenge for more traditional mechanisms (BYU, n.d.).
Compliant mechanisms are very suitable for 3D printing and could allow us to gain a lot of control over
how the magnets move by designing high-sensitivity zones. For example, we could print internal struc-
tures that change the Poisson ratio and use mechanical leverage to move the magnets further than if the
material was isotropic. However, compliant mechanisms are very difficult to design and not feasible for
us to design and manufacture in this project.
5.2.2
Choice of suspension material
We chose silicone as our suspension material based on our research, prior experience, and manufactur-
ing facilities. Flexible 3D prints sound very interesting and could be explored in the future, but for now,
it makes the most sense to use silicone.
We’ve chosen Platinum Cure silicone because it has the most suitable properties for our use case. Plat-
inum cure silicone has a long shelf life, is FDA-certified, and has a low viscosity, allowing it to flow into
small crevices (Lin, 2024a).

Magnetic Inteferance
We also explored the bottom magnet concept further. Since the magnets are pointed toward each other
with opposing fields, we tested to see if they interfere with each other. During our original multi magnet
testing we didn’t point opposing fields together to see if they’d react so we were curious to find out. The
test consisted of placing the bottom magnet under the sensor and saving the sensor readings. We then
introduced the top magnet at different positions and recorded the total sensor output for these points.
We then removed the bottom magnet and repositioned the top magnet at the same spots as before and
measured the sensor readings. If the magnets influence each other we’d expect the total sensor readings
to differ from the bottom and top sensor readings summarized. After summarizing the data we found
that there is no significant differences between the readings being done separately or together, further
verifying the model of vector addition. The data and calculations can be found in Appendix J. Magnetic
fields are, of course, much more complicated than we explored during this project, but for our use case
and precision, this level of detail seems to work.

6.9.2
Calibration of force in multiple axies
Calibrating the final testing unit with the silicone suspension material proved to be a larger challenge
than anticipated. When trying to measure strain in an object, we know from the math that the area it is
applied over is a driving parameter. However, when applying forces to a surface, the area changes based
on what you are applying. For the calibration of our testing unit, we have multiple options for defining
the area.
The first option is a point. If we use a sphere or a point as our probe, we can apply different normal and
shear forces directly over the magnet and use that data to calibrate it. However, when trying this, there
wasn’t enough friction to apply shear forces.
64
==PAGE:74==
The second option would be to create a probe that is the full size of the sensor unit, as seen in the paper
by (Tomo et al., 2016). Doing so would ensure that all of our testing unit calibrations were similar and
that there was enough friction to apply shear forces. However, when applying forces to different areas,
the calibration wouldn’t be accurate since you can displace the magnet with less force if applied over a
smaller area.
Another approach would be to redefine what a calibrated sensor is. Instead of telling us what forces are
applied on the surface, it would tell us the strain in the tensor of the magnet. We could calibrate that by
applying forces over a known area directly on top of the magnet. When forces are applied to the sensor
that isn’t within the calibrated parameters, the sensor simply tells us the equivalent forces it would feel
if the forces were within the calibrated parameters.
We could also calibrate the magnet position and calculate the strain based on how much the magnet
moves. Thiswillrequireustorevisitthemagnetpositioncalibration,whichseemstobeaclearersolution
to the problem. It still requires us to show the forces as tensors instead of surface forces, but maybe both
are possible with this approach.

For our project scope, the uncalibrated visualizations already give shear and force information. We’ve

Flat Surface Prototype Testing
Since calibration has been moved out of scope for this project, we did not develop and perform tests
with our testbed on this surface. However, testing it by hand shows how responsive and sensitive it is.
There are no dead zones, even in the middle of the sensor between all of the magnets.
When testing the surface we noticed how normal forces are not detected precisely in between the mag-
nets. The forces get dispersed and show up as shear forces instead. This confused us a lot with the sensor
point and we thought it was because the magnet overrotated. However with the added context of the
rest of the sensor points, we can see that it in fact makes sense and is actually more accurate than we
thought. Soft tissue expands outwards from the point of force, and that is exactly what we see here. As
stated in the paper by Baharestani et al, when normal forces are applied it results in shear forces inside
the tissue. These are the forces we are measuring. While we don’t necessarily know if the forces applied
are from a single point of force between the sensors, or multiple smaller forces at each sensor going per-
fectly outwards from a single point, we can probably figure it out with more points at a slightly higher
density.

7.3.1
Tensor to Surface Forces
At the beginning of the project we wanted to measure surface forces by triangulating them based on
the tensor measured by the suspended point. The thinking being that if we have multiple tensors in a
material we should be able to triangulate where the forces come from. That might still be the case, but
after using the surface, it seems unnecessary.
As defined at the beginning of the project, stress is based on the area of the surface to which force is
applied. The area will be very difficult to figure out with our sensors since we only measure tensors.
We’d need a much higher resolution to detect the area of applied forces. However, measuring tensors
still gives a lot of information about how well the pressure relief devices work. Showing the direction and
amplitude of forces on a surface gives the necessary context to figure out where the forces are applied on
the surface. That means that without us having to triangulate the forces with software, it can be inferred
simply by looking at the visualization once you have a surface. The added points on the surface give the
necessary context for the brain to infer the surface pressures.
Furthermore, thisapproachismuchclosertothemechanicalunderstandingwehaveofmeasuringstress
through strain. Instead of adding more levels of abstraction, we decided to follow the mechanical model
more directly. After all, the performance of the mechanical model is what determines the quality of the
sensor, so for us to triangulate the surface forces in the future, we’d need it to follow the mechanical
model anyway.
73
==PAGE:83==
We, therefore, decided that the sensor surface should report the data as tensors instead of triangulating
the forces on its surface.
7.3.2
Challenges
Even though we learned a lot from the prototype, there are still some areas of improvement. The main
issues stem from the problems we encountered when manufacturing it. The silicone molding problems
have resulted in a wonky surface which has caused the embedded magnets to be misaligned. These
misalignments are very clear in the sensor data but can be minimized with software. By software taring
the sensor data, the misalignment disappears and is not a big problem. Furthermore, it is something
we should expect moving forward. Manufacturing will cause alignment problems and our design needs
to be robust enough to deal with it. Although software taring helps, the magnet misalignment causes
the sensor to be less sensitive in the direction of the misalignment. Since the sensor is most sensitive
the closer the magnet is to it, if the magnet starts off further away from the sensor, the same movement
results in a smaller sensor reading. We don’t see this as a big issue since we expect the difference in
readings to be calibrated out of the system in future work.
Another problem with this prototype is the placement of the bottom magnets for each sensor. Somehow
they’ve been placed too far away from the sensor in this new design, which causes the Z axis to get
oversaturated when applying enough force on the magnets. This is, however, not a large problem since
we already know how to fix it with a proper bottom magnet distance.

8.1
Variables
The added complexities of creating an organic surface are mainly the bendability and sensor placement
inconsistencies. To create an organic surface made out of multiple points we’d want the points to be as
small as possible. The size of the points is what defines the bendiness of a surface. As seen on figure 8.1
smaller sensors can follow the curvature of surfaces much better than larger ones.
Figure 8.1: Illustration showing the relationship between the curvature of a surface and the
sensor size by comparing large sensors (left) with small sensors (right). Notice the
higher amount of sensors on the surface with small sensors.
Another problem occurs when placing sensors on a grid. Since there is curvature the sensors cannot be
placed in a perfect grid. The curvature changes the distance between sensors so there has to be some
wiggle room allowed. Luckily, we know that with our current sensors, the flat distance between them
is close enough for there not to be deadzones. However, we don’t know if the curvature of the silicone
changes this behavior and absorbs the forces even though the distance between the sensors is the same.
We need to test different curvatures with preferably smaller sensors until we find the relationship be-
tween sensor distance, curvature, and deadzones.

deeper understanding of the sensor and digital communication.
To understand the sensor we read its datasheet, which was provided by the manufacturer, Melexis. This
datasheet explains everything about the sensor, including how to wire it up to a circuit. The MLX chip
can be connected to a microcontroller using the I2C protocol or SPI protocol which are forms of serial
communication method 3.4.9 . Each protocol has its pros and cons, but we decided to use I2C because
it uses fewer connections. The I2C protocol uses 4 wires, two for data and two for power. This makes
it very simple to connect to a microcontroller. Furthermore, the I2C protocol allows multiple devices to
be connected to the same two data wires. This is possible since each I2C device has its own address. As

four different addresses. This is done by pulling them up or down, which changes the last two bits of the
I2C address. By doing that, we can connect four MLX sensors to the same two I2C data wires. To connect
even more sensors, Melexis offers four variants of the MLX sensor, each with different I2C addresses in
the first two bits. If we get four of each variant, we can connect 16 sensors to the same I2C wires.
To connect more sensors to a microcontroller, we’ll need to use additional components. Since I2C is a
verycommonprotocol, therearemanydeviceswiththesameaddress. Sometimes, twodifferentdevices
with the same address need to be used, and they cannot modify their address. To solve this problem,
there are multiple options. One option is to use an address translator (Ceresoli & Valkeinen, n.d.). This
deviceconnectstoacomponentandallowsustochangeitsI2Caddress. Itworksasatranslatorbetween
the component and the microcontroller, converting the communication between the devices. Another
option is to use an I2C multiplexer, which can rapidly switch between multiple I2C busses (Wikipedia,

When choosing the MLX sensor as our sensor of choice we bought 5 breakout boards based on a design

larger surface of the maleol, we would need to order more sensors to fully populate it. With the increase
in sensors, we’d need multiple sensor variants to connect it to our microcontroller. Furthermore, we
have some issues with the design of the Sparkfun PCBs so we decided to design and build our own PCBs
to overcome them.
The current MLX sensor PCBs from Sparkfun are much larger than necessary. They are designed to be
used with breadboards and jumper wires, which use standardized components large enough to be tin-
kered with by hand. Since we want to populate a surface with these sensors, we would like the PCB to
be as small as possible. A smaller size allows for a higher resolution and more curved surfaces.
To properly secure a sensor we need to have 3 attachment points. The two holes in the SparkFun design
are fine for screws, but since we don’t want anything to protrude above the sensor chip, we cannot use
screws. Screws are also often ferrous which can interfere with the magnetic sensor readings. In the
previous milestones, we used the edges of the PCB to avoid screws, but they are not tightly toleranced.
Although PCB manufacturing is a very precise process, the precision stops at the edges of the boards.
Small PCBs are made on a big sheet and snapped or cut out of it. For most PCBs, the edges don’t have
to be precise, and they, therefore, have very loose tolerances. Using the PCB edges does not, therefore,
result in precise positioning. Furthermore, the MLX chip itself is not centered within the PCB, making it
more difficult to use in assemblies since it cannot be rotated.
Wiring the Sparkfun PCBs is done with standard jumper cables. These cables are great for prototyping
but are very easy to accidentally disconnect. Building a system with multiple sensors, each needing 4
wires, quicklybecomesdifficult. Duringourbachelorproject, wehadtoovercomethisissuebydesigning
our own clips to hold the connections in place, which had its own problems. A proper connector would
be a much better solution.

PCB Design
Method 8.3.1
Printed Circuit Board design, also known as PCB design, can be a very complicated process re-
quiring deep knowledge in electrical engineering and manufacturing. However, modern tools
allow people with basic electronics skills to create simple PCBs.
Designing PCBs with modern tools splits the process into 2 steps. Designing the schematic and
designing the layout (Peterson, 2020).
An electronics schematic is a diagram of all the components and the way they are wired together.
This is an example of abstraction method 4.1.1 widely used in electrical engineering. It is an
abstract overview of all the components without being bound to a physical layout. Schematics
make it much easier to design and understand complex electrical systems and ensure that all
components and connections are accounted for.
The next step is to design the physical layout of the schematic. In this step each component
gets defined into a specific formfactor and placed on a board. Once each component has been
placed, wires are routed to connect the components as defined by the schematic. Modern tools
have algorithms that can automate this process, but in many cases manual routing is preferred
or even necessary.
To design our PCBs, we utilized PCB Design method 8.3.1 . We started by designing the schematic,
choosing our components, and designing the layout.

Surface Mount Devices, also called SMD for short, are soldered onto the surface of a PCB and
are only held onto the PCB with solder as seen on figure 8.8 . These components allow us to
create much smaller electronics since the components themselves are very small. In modern
electronics almost all components are SMDs. However, since these are attached to the surface of
PCBs, they are much more difficult to hand solder. To solder SMDs, a soldering paste is needed,
which is a paste consisting of small beads of solder suspended in flux. This paste is spread onto
the contacts on the PCB in a thin layer by using a similar process as silkscreen printing (Sierra
Circuits, n.d.). The SMD component is then placed on top of the solder paste in the correct ori-
entation either by hand or with a pick-and-place machine (Wikipedia, 2024d). Then the PCB gets
heated until the solderpaste melts, and solders the SMD onto the PCB. Once the solder paste
is melted, the surface tension of the solder helps position the SMDs correctly and accounts for
small misalignments during placement. The heating process either happens by using a reflow
oven (PCBWay, n.d.-a) or by using a heat gun (EEVblog, 2013). However, the process of heat-
ing solderpaste is very complex and some solders require very specific heating profiles (Compu
Phase, n.d.). These profiles cannot be obtained by using a heat gun and are generally not rec-
ommended outside of hobby use. Furthermore using a heatgun sometimes results in the SMDs
flying off the PCB since they aren’t attached to the PCB before being soldered.

The second challenge was to figure out how to solder components on two sides of the same board, with-
out them falling off. Since the entire board goes through a soldering oven, both sides of the board will
experience the necessary heat for the solder to melt. By having components on both sides of the board,
there is a risk of the bottom components falling off during the process. To solve this issue we found out
that we can use two different soldering pastes, one with a high melting point and one with a low melting
point. By soldering the first side with the high melting point solder paste, the solder won’t melt when
soldering the second side with the low melting point solder paste.
We acquired two solder pastes and assembled the first couple of sensors to test our process which can be
seen in Appendix L3. The first step was to apply solder paste to the contact points on the PCBs. To do so
we ordereda stencil withour PCBs thathas holes cutout for thispurpose. By aligningthe stencil withour
PCB, we could spread the solder paste through the holes to apply the solder paste. Once the solder paste
was applied to one side of the PCB we placed the components by hand. Most of our components can be
placed in any orientation but the MLX90393 has to be placed correctly. To account for this we marked a
corner of the PCB and aligned the mark on the MLX90393 with the PCB mark to make sure it has been
placed correctly. After the components are placed, they are brought into the solder oven and baked. The
solder oven is programmed based on the solder profile of the solder paste. Once the components have
been soldered, the steps repeat on the other side of the PCB with the low-temperature solder paste.
During our first assembly of the PCBs, we noticed how well the SMDs seemed to stick to the PCB while
melting. We did some more research on SMD soldering and found that with small enough components,
thesurfacetensionofthesoldershouldbeenoughtokeeptheminplaceevenwhenturnedupsidedown.
We tested this theory on a small batch of PCBs by using the same solder paste on both sides. None of
the components fell off, so we decided to simplify the assembly and use the same solder paste on both
sides.

8.4.1
3D Modeling of silicone attachment
Another problem with resin printing is the lack of infill patterns. Resin prints are usually solid or have
holes to drain any trapped resin. Since we relied on the infill patterns of FDM slicers, we didn’t have to
model the complex geometry. Slicing software for resin printing does not have infill patterns, so we have
to model them ourselves as part of the 3D model.
FDM slicers create the infill pattern by defining the path of the extruder as a line. These lines are then
shaped and offset for each layer based on the pattern type. They utilize the extrusion width of the printer
to make the pattern solid and are, therefore, not geometry but simply g-code. Therefore, we cannot
utilize the same approach as FDM slicers to generate the pattern.

Organic surfaces are generally difficult to work with in traditional CAD programs. Especially when the
surface is defined as polygons. The surface of the malleol is based on a 3D scan of a foot which is made of
polygons. It is possible to use surface modeling to approximate the polygonal surface in a CAD program.
But outside of Rhino + Grasshopper (McNeel & Associates, n.d.), CAD programs don’t have the proper
tools to deal with surfaces. Since we don’t have access or experience with Rhino + Grasshopper, we
decided to use polygonal 3D modeling instead with 3DS Max (Autodesk, n.d.).
Initially, we developed a manual approach to generate these patterns by extruding vertices of a mesh
outwards from a surface and using a wireframe modifier to give them thickness. Although it worked, it
required a lot of manual tweaking to get right and was not easy to modify. The process had to be done
on each surface manually, which would take a lot of time.
To improve the pattern generation, we completely changed our approach. Instead of using 3DS Max, we
used Blender(Foundation, n.d.). Blender is an open-source polygonal 3D modeling tool that has soared
in popularity recently. Recently, Blender introduced a feature called "Geometry Nodes," which is very
similar to Grasshopper for Rhino. Geometry nodes allow users to modify 3D models with node-based op-
erations (Blender, n.d.). Geometry nodes are a non-destructive and parametric way of modeling, which
is perfect for our needs. Using geometry nodes, we developed a setup that generates a grid pattern
based on any surface, as seen on figure 8.12 . Since it is parametric, we can even define the number of
layers, thickness, and length. This approach proved much better than anticipated and geometry nodes

8.4.1.1
Silicone Poisoning
With the silicone attachment geometry figured out, we performed some silicone tests. The original pur-
poseofthesetestswastotestthestrengthofthegridandseeifitperfmedaswellaswiththeinfillpattern
from the FDM slicer. However, during this test, we found that the silicone did not cure.
This phenomenon is called Silicone Cure Inhibition (Lin, 2024b) and happens because the silicone reacts
with the resin (Lin, 2023). The type of silicone we use is called Platinum Cure Silicone which we chose
based on its mechanical benefits as discussed in section 5.2.2 . Unfortunately, Platinum Cure Silicone
is very sensitive and can be poisoned by different chemicals, one of them being 3D-printed UV-curing
resin. It reacts with the 3D-printed resin because resin prints don’t fully cure after printing. The curing
process of resin takes a long time. After printing, they are still soft and need to be post-processed to be
touched. However, this post-processing step only hardens the resin for general use but does not fully
cure it. The resin off-gasses for up to a couple of months after printing, and these chemicals react with
the silicone prohibiting it from curing (Lin, 2023).
88
==PAGE:98==
To solve this problem, we tried different approaches. Initially, we found a PhD student in the lab who
had prior experience with platinum cure silicone and resin 3D printing. She used a special resin that
shouldn’t poison the silicone. When we used it, however, the silicone still didn’t cure properly. We then
coated the 3D print with polyurethane to prevent it from contacting the silicone. However, given the
complex structureofthegridpattern, weweren’tabletofullycoverit, andthesiliconewasstillpoisoned.
Afterresearchingonline, wefoundexamplesofpeoplecuringtheresinprintslongertofullycureit. Based
on our research, we set up an experiment method 3.4.10 to find the necessary post-curing parameters.
The two main factors in post-curing are UV and heat. So far, we only cured the prints with UV since that
is enough for general use. According to some sources, too much UV curing can cause over-curing of
the print, making it brittle, which we want to prevent (Zguris, n.d.). Not all sources agree, and formlabs,
whichisalargemanufacturerofresin,performedtestsshowingthatover-curingstrengthenssomeresins
without making them brittle. When it comes to heat, we found similar answers to our problem. Our
research found that baking resin prints at 60-80 degrees for 4 or more hours drastically decreases cure
inhibition. This was not guaranteed, however, since resins are very different, and these tests were not
performed on a large scale. We, therefore, had to find out for ourselves.
We printed five identical test prints for the experiment. All of them would go through a post-cure of 10
minutes in a UV curing station at 60 degrees. Three of the prints were then baked for 1-3 hours, respec-
tively, to test how long they needed to bake. One print was set aside as a control, and another went back
into the posturing station for multiple intervals to find the brittle limit. After they had been processed,
we poured silicone on them, as seen in figure 8.13 . We found that the print that had been baked for 3
hours resulted in the best curing silicone. We also found that even though the brittle test ended up being
postured in UV and 60-degree heat for well over 12 hours, it did not cure.

9.2
Final concept design
Inthisproject, weuseMLX90393tri-axialhalleffectsensorstocreateanorganicsurfacethatcanmeasure
shear and normal pressure. However, we are aware of some potential problems with this approach as
well as alternative methods to reach a similar result.
A problem with our approach stems from the use of magnets. Magnets are inherently magnetic and like
to stick to ferrous objects. This can be a problem if the sensor is to be used for testing metal devices.
Although our research didn’t come upon metal relief devices, we don’t know if designers use metal de-
vices while prototyping their devices or if other magnetic fields from machinery might influence the sen-
sor readings. During our testing, we did not experience problems with nearby magnets, but this is an
important factor to keep in mind.
If a problem like this occurs, there are some ways of addressing it without going away from a magnet-
basedapproach. ByutilizingmagnetswithaHalbacharray(Wikipedia,2024e),themagneticfieldscanbe
directed to one side of the magnet, which reduces external interference. Similar ideas might be utilized
to reduce the magnetic fields that leave the sensor surface. These solutions might be expensive and
more difficult to manufacture so other options are also possible.
As described in section 5.1.2 , we found multiple ways of achieving our conceptual design. Throughout
the project, we’ve tried to keep all of the surface-related theory as abstract as possible to allow for other
working principles than magnets to be used. We decided to use magnets because it would result in less
work for us since there are components readily available, but if this concept proves itself to be useful, it
might be worth it to develop other approaches and reap their benefits.
The contribution our project brings is the theoretical approach of designing deadzone-free organic sur-
faces that can measure shear and normal pressure. Our specific way of embodying the concept is not
necessarily the best-performing product, but our abstract and conceptual designs propose a method
of measuring shear and normal pressure on a surface without dead zones that is tied very closely to its
mechanical principles.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis project was to create an organic surface that can measure shear and normal
pressures. Pressure relief device designers can then utilize this surface to make high-quality products
with higher patient compliance.
Our proposed method for measuring shear and normal pressure on an organic surface consists of multi-
ple magnets suspended in silicone above tri-axial hall effect sensors to measure their displacement. By
using the elastic properties of silicone with the displacement measurements of the magnetic sensors,
we have a physical model for measuring shear and normal strain, which is the key to measuring stresses
and pressures. Although this project focuses on creating a plantar maleol for testing offloading products
for diabetic foot ulcers, the proposed method can be used to turn any surface into a normal and shear
pressure-sensing surface.
Through a structured design process, we broke down the problem into steps of increasing complexity
while still allowing us to explore and learn along the way. Throughout the project, we had to learn new
processes, develop custom testing equipment, and design and manufacture custom electronic compo-
nents.
By staying close to the mechanical understanding of shear and normal pressures, we used abstract defi-
nitionsofourdesiredfunctionalitytoachieveadeeperunderstandingofourconcept. Theseabstractions
allowedustodesignasensingsurfacewithoutdeadzonesandwithoutgettingstuckinthedetailsofsen-
sor technologies. Based on this abstract design, we synthesized and found sensors capable of meeting
our requirements, which are not typically used for the purpose.
To create the final surface, we went through stages of increasing complexity by starting with a sensor
point and extrapolating it into surfaces. Each stage was tested with a testing tool developed in parallel
to the sensor surface to ensure that our sensor surface accurately detected the forces applied to it.
Finally, we developed a digital prototype based on open-source parametric modeling. This prototype
turns any surface into a shear and normal pressure sensor surface. It takes any surface as an input and
creates all the models necessary for manufacturing the sensor surface. By utilizing our custom PCB de-
sign and off-the-shelf electronic components, anyone can create a sensing surface.
