M. Sc Thesis
Abstract
In line with the United Nations sustainable development goals, many countries are tran-
sitioning to renewable energies, here included off-shore wind energy. This has resulted in
many shallow water sites being filled in, meaning wind farms are now expanding into deeper
waters. As such, it has become more important than ever to understand the systems that
govern cathodic protection of the windmills’ monopile. One of these systems is the calcare-
ous layer that develops on the monopile as it acts as the cathode in the cathodic protection.
The beneficial effects of the layer could potentially help boost the range of the cathodic
protection by netting large savings for the manufacturer and simplifying installation of the
monopile.
This project tries to understand the development of the calcareous layer on the monopile
with the intent of observing if the water flow around a monopile might create zones where
the calcareous layer develops more easily. The effect is tested for a constant flow and a
variable flow comparative to tidal flow. Two experiments are performed: at first a single
piece monopile is used as test specimen and then a monopile which has been subdivided
according to flow theory around a cylinder.
First experiment comprises two test specimens; each a steel (ST37) cylinder of dimensions
Ø30 mm, height 30 mm. These are placed onto a plastic pedestal with a titanium net sur-
rounding the base, acting as the anode. Each specimen is placed in a flume and cathodically
polarized to potentials of -900 mVAg/AgCl for the initial 48 hours and -1000 mVAg/AgCl for the
remaining period. One specimen is placed in a variable flow flume, another in a constant flow
flume. The second experiment is similar to the first. However, the height of the specimen
is now 105 mm and the cylinder is subdivided into 4 pieces. The electrical properties of
the specimen and of the synthetic seawater is monitored throughout the experiment. Post-
processing of the specimens is carried out with optical and SE microscopes, EDS analysis.
The setup attempts to mirror the conditions for a monopile in the North Sea as well as
stagnant flow conditions.
The results show that a lower flow velocity results in greater calcareous deposits and overall
a lower current density to maintain potentials. It shows that the current density develops
similarly across a cylinder with minor differences based upon whether one considers the wake
zone or the front of the cylinder. The calcareous deposits are shown to be comprised of pri-
marily CaCO3 with minor deposits of Mg(OH)2.

Introduction
As coal power plants and nuclear reactors are shut down across Europe in the fight against
climate change, new and green power plants are needed for the ever growing demand of elec-
tricity. Denmark, Belgium, Germany, and the Netherlands signed The Esbjerg Declaration
([4]) in May 2022, promising to double the capacity of offshore wind to at least 150 GW
by 2050 to replace energy produced by fossil fuels, Russian oil, coal and gas. This offshore
renewable energy system connects the North Sea partners with multiple connected offshore
projects and hubs, including wind turbine parks. As more of these structures are built, the
farther out from the coast you have to go, where different types of monopiles can be chosen
based on water depth, soil conditions, water flow and more. Below figure showcases the use
of monopile, jacket and floating substructures.
Figure 1.1: Monopile, jacket and floating substructures used for offshore wind turbines. The
figure is courtesy of Incorporation of Multi-Member Substructure [...]. [5]
In rough estimates, monopiles are applicable to depths of 30m, whereafter tripod and jacket
structures would be more suitable (down to 60m depth).
This growing demand for offshore wind structures requires research into optimization as
harsh conditions exists on the open sea. Wind, waves and high salinity are all risks towards
the service life and a real challenge to the corrosion protection systems. The submerged
parts are arguably the most exposed part of the wind structures as periodic and regular
maintenance is expensive and difficult. This is where impressed current cathodic protection
(ICCP) is used, as the objective is to prevent corrosion by delivering sufficient current to
these exposed parts so that they meet the potential criteria.

Problem Statement
2
Problem Statement
2.1
Background and motivation
This project investigates monopiles for offshore wind parks in the North Sea. In recent years
it has become relevant to protect the monopiles of offshore windmills with use of Impressed
Current Cathodic protection (ICCP). The system has several advantages over the old Gal-
vanic Anode Cathodic Protection (GACP). GACP functions by attaching the monopiles to
sacrificial anodes made from zinc. The zinc then corrodes instead of the monopile. This
causes damage to the local marine environment as it releases high concentrations of zinc
into the local ecosystem. ICCP has none of these environmental impacts. However, im-
pressed current has the risk of overprotection if the current being impressed is too high. As
monopiles are getting longer and longer, it is necessary to increase the impressed current
in order to ensure sufficient coverage of the monopile section furthest away from the ICCP
installation. Research suggests that a calcareous layer that develops on the monopile, has
beneficial attributes to avoiding overprotection. However, the way the layer develops around
the monopile is still poorly understood, and the industry today assumes it to be develop-
ing uniformly. It is therefore especially interesting to find out if the calcareous deposit is
generated non-uniformly around the monopile. By knowing this, it could open up to the
opportunity of multi-zone model with different current densities to make up for any slow
developing zones.
2.2
Project content
The master thesis ’Cathodic protection of offshore wind structures. Measurements of the
development of cathodic protection and characterization of electrical and chemical properties
of the calcareous layer on round structures with impressed current’ by Oliver Falck (s173746)
and Mikkel Frederiksen (s165156) researches a steel cylinder and a MMO titanium net as
respectively cathode and anode. The main experiment is done in a closed dual flume system
with synthetic seawater, water cooling and a water pump to replicate the environment of a
monopile standing in North Sea. By using a potentiostat to maintain the electrode potential
at -900 and later -1000 mVAg/AgCl/seawater on the steel specimen, theory says that the steel
specimen is protected from corroding. Furthermore, a calcareous layer should be forming
on the steel surface. This is supported by the M. Sc thesis done by Jesper Schelde Lund
(s173739) in spring 2022, which is a predecessor to this project.
After several weeks of
running the experiment, it is possible to analyse the test specimen using a Scanning Electron
Microscope (SEM) apparatus. This way, it is possible to further develop the understanding
of the effects of different electrode potentials and flow regimes surrounding the cylinder. If
the calcareous layer shows differences as a result of vortexes, then the plans of a multi-zone
model becomes relevant. The steel cylinder would thus be split into four parts of 60-60-60-180
degrees which would cover the zones of stagnation, separation and wake for a flow velocity of
0.3 m/s in the flumes. Furthermore, the synthetic seawater will have to be periodically tested
to ensure appropriate levels of pH, salinity, oxygen, and temperature, so that it replicates
the North Sea as well as possible.

2.3
Simplifications
Water temperature
The North Sea has a temperature of approximately 10◦C. The water cooling used for the
project was capable of lowering temperatures, but only down to 15◦C.
Test specimen
The test specimen is a scaled down section of a monopile structure. However, in the North
Sea, given the flow velocity and average diameter of monopiles, it can be calculated that the
flow regime around monopiles exist in the trans critical regime (see section 3.2.7). Meanwhile,
due to the small diameter of the test specimen (Ø30 mm), the flow regime surrounding the
test specimen exist in the subcritical regime, as can be seen by the calculation below and
referring the number to fig. 3.11:
Re = D · U

Where:
• ν = 10−6 m2/s viscosity.
• D = 30 mm diameter of test specimen.
• U = 0.3 m/s flow velocity.
A significant difference being that the test specimen experiences laminar flow around the
cylinder, while the actual monopile sees turbulent flow (see fig. 3.11).
Another obvious difference is that, in real life applications, the monopiles are often painted
as well as cathodically protected.
Water flow
While the water pump is capable of producing sufficient flow in the flumes to mirror the 0.8
m/s found in the North Sea, the heat produced by the pump starts adversely affecting the
water temperature. The cooling system thus becomes insufficient, and a water flow of 0.3
m/s is chosen based on previous experience (ref. [2]).
Closed water system
The system is filled with about 100 liters of synthetic seawater. The system is sealed, meaning
no water should be lost due to evaporation. The risk of a closed system is that the seawater
composition might change significantly over time.
Given the size of the test specimen,
it was assumed that the seawater had sufficient minerals to develop the calcareous layer,
without significant variation of composition in salinity, bicarbonate, magnesium, chlorine,
and calcium content.

Theory
3.1
Corrosion characteristics
The combination of an anodic and cathodic reaction is an electrochemical process called wet
corrosion. Fig. 3.1 displays this process in an aqueous solution. In this example, a metal is
dissolved and transferred to the solution as ions in the anodic reaction, while reduction of
oxygen to hydroxide happens in the cathodic reaction.

3.2.5
Langlier saturation index
The Langlier Saturation Index is a way of approximating the corrosive tendency of a solution.
The saturation pH (pHs) describes that CaCO3 can be deposited in water when pH > pHs
at a given temperature and salt content. At lower pH values, the water will not possess the
right characteristics and any existing CaCO3 precipitate will be dissolved. To determine
whether the pH value is above the saturation pH or not, one can calculate Langlier index by
L = pH −pHs
If L is a positive value, then the water is scale-forming. Conversely if the value is negative,
then the water is not scale-forming and often corrosive [12].

3.3
Cathodic protection
To protect a structure against corrosion, cathodic protection can be used in either of Galvanic
Anode Cathodic Protection (GACP) or Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP). The
requirement for these methods is to provide sufficient current density to all buried or sub-
merged components of an offshore structure to prevent corrosion. At the same time, it is
important not to over-protect by impressing excessive current which can possibly lead to
reduction in the fatigue limit.
As shown in fig. 3.12, it is important for the design of cathodic protection to stay within the
limits of -800 and -1100 mVAg/AgCl. Reaching a minimum of -800 mVAg/AgCl ensures corrosion
protection for carbon and low-alloy steel, whilst surpassing -1100 mVAg/AgCl increases the
chance of hydrogen formation, which could lead to paint adhesion issues or HSC (Hydrogen

3.3.1
Galvanic Anode Cathodic Protection method
GACP uses the advantages of the electrochemical series by sacrificing the less noble metal in
favor of the more noble one. The GACP method is often used for offshore structures, ships
or other structures exposed to water. The typical materials used as galvanic anodes are zinc
or some sort of aluminum alloy. This is done since they are more electronegative than steel,
and it is often steel structures that are being protected.
Anode attachment is made through a steel core to the structure, and as long as this con-
nection is maintained, then the structure should be protected. This is thus one of the main
advantages of GACP, since it is not reliant on any external energy to drive the reaction. The
lowest free corrosion potential of zinc and aluminum is -1050 mVAg/AgCl/seawater, which also
means that there is no danger over overprotection according to fig. 3.12. Because debris is
continuously released in the electrolyte, a replacement of the anode will at some point be
needed, which is a disadvantage of the method. Fig. 3.13(a) shows the main processes of
the CAGP method.

3.4
Polarization
Polarization for a metal is the change of the electrode potential away from its natural equilib-
rium state. Methods to do this include polarization resistance, Tafel extrapolation, galvanic
current, but relevant to this thesis is the impressed current from ICCP system. During
polarization of a metal structure, the potential can be brought down to a state of immunity
in which corrosion of the material is no longer possible. At this state, a reduction of oxygen
happens at the surface, which in the long term can lead to calcareous precipitation. Should
the potential be brought past the state of immunity to a state of overprotection, then there
is a possibility for hydrogen formation which can weaken or damage the structure. An il-
lustration of the cathodic protection curve for steel with water as the electrolyte is seen in

• Simulate a monopile exposed to tidal flow in the North Sea.
• Observe a distinct zoning of the buildup of the calcareous layer across the cross section
of the cylindrical steel specimen cathode.
• Examine the chemical and electrical properties of the distinct zones.

5.2.6
Potentiostats
The potential of the test specimens are being maintained by two potentiostats, one for each
specimen. The potentiostats are of the model PGP201 Voltalab and are lent to the project
by DTU Mechanical Engineering. The potentiostats are operated through a computer via
the VoltaMaster 1 software. Among other features, the VoltaMaster 1 program is capable
of running a Chronoamperometry sequence. This sequence is capable of maintaining the
potential of a working electrode against a reference electrode by regulating and outputting a
electrical current over time. The reference electrode used in this experiment is a zinc cylinder
(see appendix B.2).
Figure 5.10: Front of PGP 201 potentiostats and coax wiring leading into experimental
system.
Use of virtual machine for VoltaMaster 1
In an attempt to optimize the setup, a virtual machine was installed on a PC to run Volta-
Master 1 through. This way, multiple PGP201 potentiostats could be connected simulta-
neously. Because of accompanying delay when using such software, the machines thought
a disconnection had happened and thus stopped any sequence. The idea of using a virtual
machine was thus discarded.

5.2.8
Three-way valve
The setup consist of two flumes. One has constant flow, the other simulates tidal currents

5.2.9
Synthetic seawater
The synthetic seawater is made in accordance with ASTM D1141-52 (appendix B.3). The
standard ensures a chemical composition to that of seawater, without the biological material,
such as algae, being present. The biological material is not introduced, as it would have an
unpredictable effect on the system that could not be replicated.
5.2.10
Oxygen valve
To ensure that oxygen can be transferred into the otherwise closed system, the system has
an attached inlet placed at the beginning of flume 1. Once opened, the inlet can transfer
oxygen from a nearby oxygen tank. To prevent a dangerous buildup of pressure, flume 2
has an attached overflow pipe that the water can expand into as needed. Pressure is then
normalized through slowly opening the pressure release valves.

Optical microscopy
Using a Nikon SM25 stereo microscope, the calcareous layer on the surface of the specimen

5.4.2
SEM/EDS analysis
The calcareous layer is inspected using a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) used in
conjunction with a Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS). SEM is used to look at
the microstructure of the calcareous layer, while EDS is used to figure out what materials
the layer is comprised of. The process is done from a top view and from a cross-section view.
Top view can show how deposits change depending on the zone around the cylinder (see
section 3.2.7). Meanwhile, cross-sectional view can show the buildup of the calcareous layer.
5.5
Experimental process
Preparing test specimen
1. Produce 2 test specimens. Varies with experiment, see section 5.2.3.
Prior to experiment
2. Clean flumes and ensure that there is no water from prior experiments anywhere in
the system. This includes the extensive pipes inside the water cooling tank.
3. Fill the water cooling tank with regular faucet water until it is filled about 20 cm below
tank top.
4. Start cooling of the water tank (faucets on the wall) and start the yellow circulation
underneath the water tank.
5. Start air-conditioning in the room.
6. Fill 90 liters of synthetic seawater into the flumes. Be aware of air pockets inside the
pipes.
7. Turn the pump up to a frequency of 3-7 Hz to create a flow in the system. Balance
the seawater pH to 8.2, using 0.1 M NaOH.
8. Calibrate the zinc reference electrode with a calomel electrode by putting both in
the seawater at the approximate location of the zinc electrode and the test specimen
respectively.
Page 40 of 68
==PAGE:49==
M. Sc Thesis
5
Experiment
9. Mount zinc reference electrode on the plexiglass outlet.
10. Place test specimen in the divot in the center of the flume.
11. Put titanium wires through their outlets on the plexiglass lid. This is done in conjunc-
tion with lowering the lid onto the flume.
12. Start datalogger with relevant monitoring settings.
13. Start potentiostats with relevant chronoamperometry setting.
14. Turn on the 3-way valve, using the Labview software.
15. Seal plexiglass lid with screws. Make sure to do this gently to avoid damage to flume.
16. Slowly increase frequency of the water pump until desired water flow is achieved.
17. Turn on webcams and set them to record test specimens through the glass window.
During experiment
18. Every 2 to 4 days, take water sample to ensure water quality is maintained.
Post experiment
19. Dry test specimens by first submerging briefly in deionized water, then cold air blow
dry.
20. Keep in desiccator with silica gel while not in use.
21. Disassemble specimen. Be careful to avoid damage to calcareous layer.
22. Check zinc references against calomel electrode.
23. Cut off smaller specimens for cross-sectional analysis.
24. Analyze calcareous layer with post analysis devices (see section 5.4)

means that it yields the average velocity of a, perhaps, very complex flow field.
7.1.2
Flow cycle
The flow cycle for constant- and variable flow flume can be seen in fig. 5.15, with the mean
values for the cycle seen in table 7. The variable flow cycle has an extended period of maxi-
mum flow (seen in hours 0-∼3), followed by a small increase in flow (hours 3-4), before then
rapidly decreasing in value until it passes below the 0.05 m/s minimum threshold for the
propeller (hours 4-7.5). There is then an extended period where no movement is registered
by the propeller (hours 7.5-∼18). A sudden increase in velocities is then seen with the pat-
tern mirroring the initial hours (hours ∼18-24).
It is important to note that there might still be a water flow in hours 7.5-∼18, it is merely
below what’s measurable by the propeller. Meanwhile, the constant flow seems pretty consis-
tent but has an increase in flow when the variable flow flume is closed. There is no apparent
explanation for the peaks seen in hours 3-4.
Theoretically the flow should’ve developed as a sinusoidal curve (depicted in red on fig. 5.15).
Instead of this gradual change in velocity, it appears more as an on/off function. These mea-
surements indicate that the specimens experience too much peak flow and, though this is
conjecture, extended periods of zero flow. This is problematic and might explain the rather
violent jumps in current densities.
The mean velocity of the variable flow flume is significantly lower than for constant flow (see
table 7). This is important as it sets up the conclusion that the flow velocity does effect

7.2
Current densities
This section covers the current densities seen in section 6.2.1 for the single zone cylinder and
section 6.3.1 for multi zone cylinder.
To explain the cyclical developments in the current density graphs, seen from hour 216 on-
ward, the following two possible hypothesis are put forward:
Hypothesis 1 - Experiment 1
1. A period of high water flow damages the calcareous layer by removing small areas or
flakes.
2. Parts of the bare steel surface is now exposed and reactive.
3. The exposed surface requires larger electrical current to keep cathodically protected.
4. This leads to peaks in the current density while there is minimal water flow in variable
flow flume.
Hypothesis 2 - Experiment 2
1. A period with high water flow leads to high oxygen diffusion.
2. This results in increased current consumption, but the hydroxide needed for calcareous
precipitation is washed away by the water flow.
3. In periods of close to no water flow, the oxygen diffusion is low but the hydroxide is
more likely to stay in the vicinity.
4. The hydroxide forms a local alkaline environment which is advantageous for calcareous
precipitation, which then develops during low water flow.
5. This leads to peaks in the current density while there is maximum water flow in variable
flow flume.
In regards to experiment 1, it is evident that the current densities for variable flow specimens
trend downwards faster than the constant flow specimens. This, alongside the clearly more
visible calcareous layer and a lower mean velocity supports the conclusion that a lower
flow velocity is beneficial to calcareous development, which in turn is beneficial for required

According to hydrodynamic theory, one would expect that the largest current densities are
required at the peak of the water flow, but instead, the current densities peak when there
is little to no flow in the case of experiment 1, which is where hypothesis 1 could be an
explanation.
This idea of requiring larger current densities to account for damaged calcareous layers could
also lend to explain the upward trending current densities in the days following a shift in
potential. The theory that the calcareous layer can be damaged is based on the state of the

8
Conclusion
Two experiments have been performed in this project. The experiments involved applying
Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP) to two steel cylinders per experiment. The
cylinders, acting as surrogate monopiles, were placed in two distinct water flows: A constant
flow, and a variable flow. In both experiments, the steel cylinders were cathodically polarized
at electrode potentials of -900 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater for the initial 48 hours, and then at -1000
mVAg/AgCl/Seawater . The two experiments were distinguishable by their test specimens:
• Experiment 1: The test specimens were Ø30, height: 30, thickness: 2.5 mm steel
(ST37) cylinders.
• Experiment 2: The test specimens were Ø30, height: 105, thickness: 2.5 mm steel
(ST37) cylinders which had been subdivided into zones which according to hydrody-
namic theory should experience distinct water flows.
The current density was logged and the calcareous deposits forming on the steel surface was
examined with the following conclusions:
• Lower mean flow velocity and stagnant water yields larger amounts of calcareous de-
posits and a faster decline in required current density.
• Current density develops similarly across flow regions on cylindrical surfaces. Only
minuscule differences occur between stagnation-, separaration-, and the wake zone.
• The chemical composition of the calcareous layers consists of a small amount of Mg(OH)2
and a larger portion of CaCO3 developing into cauliflower-like structures, when de-
posited in abundance on the surface.
• The overall current density on the multi zone test specimen for variable water flow
stabilized at a value of approximately 121 mA/m2 whilst the constant flow was 212
mA/m2. The experiment was stopped after 350 hours.

Future Works
9
Future Works
Depolarization
It could be beneficial to include instant-off measurements throughout the testing period to
map the resistance of the calcareous layer in situ.
Because of time limitations and fear
of receiving more noise, only visual inspections with the use of cameras have been made
during this project. It could be interesting to investigate the electrochemical properties and
development of the calcareous layer and the differences between variable and constant flow.
Artificial turbulent flow
The flume size and pump head restricts the flow to subcritical regime where laminar flow is
most prominent. To closer imitate true conditions in the North sea, a thin steel wire (e.g.
fishing wire) can be strung between the bottom of the flume and the lid to create turbulent
flow. The effects of turbulent flow versus laminar flow could thus be mapped.
Optimization of steel segments
The steel segments of the multi-zone test specimen have been designed according to basic
hydrodynamics and an assumption of 0.5 m/s water flow in this project. It could be in-
teresting to elaborate on the design when implementing artificial turbulent flow and more
accurately measure the water velocity profile during its entire cycle.
pH experiment near cathode
An experiment using phenolphthalein could be carried out to support the theory of calcareous
deposits having advantageous growth conditions on the wake side. In theory, discolorations
could be seen a little in the stagnation and separation zones whilst being more prominent
in the wake. This experiment would require a smaller separate setup with a pump as to not
compromise the artificial sea water.
Improvements to experimental setup
Experiences with unexpected pressure buildups and electrostatic noise could call for a rebuild
of the setup. One important point would be to place a water tank such that the water table
would lie above the top of the flumes. This would solve problems with air bubbles getting
stuck under the lids and would provide a good spot to apply compressed air into the system
without the risk of overpressure. A second point would be to move the pump as far away
from the flumes and test specimens as possible. This could provide the best method to
reduce the noise since it would be a longer and more indirect distance to the test specimens.

Improving flow cycles
With present flow cycles, one should be wary of making direct comparisons between results
from variable- and constant flow specimens. At this point in time there are two different
effects caused by the water flow, which could influence the results. It can either be due to a
lower mean velocity or because the cyclical flow creates periods of zero flow. The significance
of these effects are currently unknown. But could be explored further by fixing the valve in
a position which ensured a slower flow in the variable flume and then running an experiment
with essentially two constant flow flumes. If one of these flumes had a flow velocity same
as the mean velocity listed in table 7 and the calcareous layer didn’t match the ones seen
in this project. Then one could conclude that it is the cyclical flow which causes the rapid
buildup of calcareous deposits on the variable flow flume.
Pre-developed calcareous layer before insertion in flume
As experienced during this project, only a very thin calcareous layer could be developed in
the 16 days of testing. I could be interesting to start out by having no water flow, thus pre-
developing the calcareous layer and to investigate the effects it has towards ohmic resistance
and current density demands.

mounted with tracks on the flume. The crane’s speed could be adjusted with a % gauge to

was logged. This data allows to establish a relation between RPM and m/s, as seen in

C.1.1
Experiment - Reducing noise in measurements
Introduction:
This project is the 4th generation to use the experimental setup. Previous projects used
three 10x10 cm plates protected on both sides of the plate, as test specimens in each flume.
This yield a total surface area of 600 cm2. Meanwhile, this project has a surface area of 28
cm2 per flume. This reduction in surface area means that the potentiostats need to send less
current to have the same kind of current densitites. This lowering of the currents resulted in
the noise to current ratio growing to unacceptable levels, making the measurements either
completely useless by yielding clearly impossible currents or yielding readings with large
spread. This experiment sought to remedy this noise pollution By ensuring Electromagnetic
compatibility between the electronic devices in the experimental setup. This is experiment
primarily set out to fix the variable flow flume. As for whatever reason, the constant flow
flume wasn’t affected as badly. Three components were located as potential sources of noise:
1. The water pump. section 5.2.7
2. Power strips drawing power from multiple ungrounded sockets.
3. Non-insulated circuitry in electrical panel.
Furthermore, a separate series of experiments were performed to see if moving the zinc
reference upstream would improve the spread.
Theory:
Electromagnetic compatibility is the a devices ability to operate without interfering with
other nearby systems. Relevant theory can be seen covered in section 3.
Experiment:
The experiments were performed at slower water flows due to the water flumes being kept
open for the sake of having easy access to test specimen and zinc reference. Each of the
noise contributors were handled by their own installations:
1. Faraday cage surrounding the pump.
The water pump was covered in a steel chassis with an opening, pointing away from the
potentiostats, being left for ventilation. The Faraday cage was connected to the potentiostats
own ground socket. The improvement can be seen in fig. C1.
2. 5-pole 400 V brancher socket.
The large power demand of the experiment necessitated the installation of a 400 V brancher
socket splitting into power strips for computers, potentiostats and a high power output for
water pump and frequency converter. The improvement can be seen in fig. C1.
3. Insulated coax cables and Faraday box around circuit board

current densities were effectively zero and occasionally negative. Introducing the Faraday
cage around the water pump saw a huge improvement in the readings. It can be argued that
the Faraday cage was the only essential addition to the experiment, as Common grounding
has had an, at best, marginal improvement on the readings.
The readings seen in fig. C2 seem to suggest that the placement of the zinc reference is
paramount. Placing the zinc reference downstream and closer to the water pump in the
variable flume, sees a larger spread of the readings compared to placing the zinc reference
upstream and before the test specimen. Despite being the location with the less accurate
readings, it was decided that the zinc should be placed in the downstream location. This was
in part due to the concern that placing the zinc reference upstream from the test specimen
would create turbulent flow that would then change the water flow around the test specimen.
The other consideration was that placing the zinc reference upstream in the variable flow
flume, would mean that the constant flow- and variable flow flume no longer had the same
setup.

C.1.2
Experiment - Flow velocity in general and over tidal cycle
In principle, if the constant flow flume sees a flow of 0.5 m/s. Then the variable flow flume
should cycle between 0.0 and 0.5 m/s flow, with the flow developing as a sinusoidal curve
over a 24 hour period.
This tidal flow would be accomplished by moving the 3-way valve between its’ 45o and 90o
position for maximum flow and zero flow respectively. (see appendix D.2).
It was decided during the predecessor experiments that the tidal cycle should be 24 hours
instead of 12. However, the flow characteristic of the 3-way valve states that at most, only
75% of the flow from constant flow flume, gets transferred into variable flume, which stood
in contrast to the specifications which previous projects had listed. It was unclear where
flow calibrations for the frequency converter had been performed.
This prompted the following experiments to investigate the flow characteristics of the flumes
in the general and the tidal variation in particular.
First attempt using pressure gauges:
Using a pressure gauge and Pitot-Static pipe it’s possible to calculate the flow velocity by
measuring the difference between the static + dynamic pressure and a separate static pres-
sure measurement. This is accomplished by rewriting Bernoulli’s equation.
This line of measuring was abandoned when it was discovered that, in changing the vari-
able flow, the static pressure inside the flumes changed drastically, leading to useless readings.
