Abstract
This project investigates the effectiveness of protocol-specific tarpits as a defensive strategy against
botnet activity in the Internet of Things ecosystem. Four tarpits were designed and deployed to
emulate vulnerable services commonly used by IoT devices, which are: Telnet, UPnP, MQTT, and
CoAP. Each tarpit was carefully designed to exploit behaviors of its respective protocol, with the
goal of trapping clients for as long as possible and collecting behavioral data. Over the course
of multiple weeks, data was collected and analyzed using metrics such as connection durations
and IP reputation with Greynoise. The Telnet tarpit received the most traffic and was effective at
wasting botnet resources, especially from Mirai-like activity. The MQTT tarpit showed evidence of
growing botnet interest. In contrast, the UPnP and CoAP tarpits received mostly benign scanning
traffic, with little indication of active exploitation by botnets.

1
Introduction
The persistent threat of malware and botnets on the Internet continues to pose significant risks to
global cybersecurity. These botnet owners systematically take over innocent peoples’ devices, un-
knowingly to the owners, and use these devices for illegal activities, such as DDoS attacks.
These devices that are taken over by the botnet owners are mostly IoT devices, which has lim-
ited protection against these attacks, due to their limited processing power[1]. The reason for the
limited processing power is because of many factors, such as cost efficiency, power constraints,
and their physical size.
To combat botnet owners, traditional approaches, such as law enforcement takedowns, face limi-
tations due to being labor-intensive, and in some cases, the botnet owners can simply restart their
botnet after such a takedown[2]. Therefore, new approaches are needed to combat the evolving
malware spread, which is where tarpits come into play.
The primary idea for tarpits, is to have clients connected for as long as possible, by exploiting
protocol-level behaviors that cause delays or blocking operations. The tarpits developed in this
project attempts to appear like legitimate IoT devices, which accepts being scanned and connec-
tion attempts, but tries to make everyone that connects to it become stuck for as long as possible.
By doing so, tarpits can trap malicious bots, and thereby prevent malware from spreading to legit-
imate vulnerable devices.
This thesis explores the potential of tarpits as a defense mechanism to slow and disrupt botnets,
without requiring a physical removal of botnet infrastructure or direct intervention by device own-
ers. It will also evaluate the success of these tarpits based on key metrics, including the duration
of engagement with malicious entities and scalability.
The expected outcome is a robust, scalable tarpit solution that integrates seamlessly with existing
cybersecurity measures, providing a proactive and autonomous defense against botnets. This work
aims to offer a novel contribution to the field of cybersecurity by demonstrating the practical value
of tarpits in slowing and disrupting botnets

Related works
2.1
Foundation of Tarpits
Tarpits in the context of cyber security have existed for a long time. The first tarpit ever developed
was the LabRea tarpit, developed by Tom Liston [3][4]. Liston developed the tarpit to combat the
spread of the CodeRed worm. The way it works, is by monitoring the network for consecutive
ARP requests, which triggered the tarpit, which then would create an ARP reply, with a fake MAC
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
11
==PAGE:13==
address[5]. Then, when a TCP SYN packet is destined for the fake MAC address, the tarpit replies
with a TCP SYN/ACK package which tarpits the client from there. The way the tarpit works is
dependent on how the tarpit is configured, but the default behavior is by responding with a small
TCP window (10 bytes), and responding with TCP RST packets to TCP SYN/ACK and PING
packets to avoid spoofing. The client would be tarpitted, until it was manually stopped, or when
the client timed out, due to the very small TCP window.
2.2
Kernel-Level Tarpits
A similar tarpit is that provided by the Netfilter TARPIT from the xtables-addons package[6],
which also utilizes a small TCP window to tarpit attackers, and operates directly at the kernel level
within the Linux networking stack. Unlike LaBrea, this tarpit doesn’t run on a subnet, but instead
operates at the TCP level and can be configured to listen on specific IP addresses and ports, con-
figured by an administrator.
The way this tarpit works, is by setting the TCP window size to zero in the initial TCP hand-
shake. After this response, the tarpit intentionally ignores all subsequent packets from the client.
Since no RST or FIN packet is sent and the TCP connection is never formally closed, the client
continues to probe the connection by sending window update or retransmission packets, wasting
its time.
Also, this tarpit can be configured in different modes, such as the honeypot mode, where it re-
sponds with a normal TCP window size to make attackers send their malicious data, which can
then be captured for analysis. It can also be configured with the reset mode, where the module
simply replies with a TCP RST to all connection attempts, to immediately terminate the connec-
tion.
2.3
Automated Tarpit generation
Since the LaBrea tarpit was developed by first analyzing and understanding the network behavior
of the CodeRed worm, which can be very time consuming, a tarpit was developed that skips this
step by automatically finding tarpit vulnerabilities. This was achieved by the MALPITY tarpit
[3], developed by Walla S. et al. This tarpit doesn’t rely on any manual analysis, since it only
takes a malware sample as input, and from that identifies tarpit vulnerabilities and generates tarpit
code specifically for the input sample. It doesn’t need access to the human-readable source code
to work, making it possible to completely skip the reverse-engineering part of analyzing malware
binaries. MALPITY uses both a slowing and sticky tarpit, which respectively traps a client (or a
thread of the client) for a finite amount of time, and the other traps for an indefinite amount of time.
Walla S. et al. list four tarpit patterns, which are:
• Receiving data tarpits utilize the recv and recvfom functions, since they are blocking oper-
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
12
==PAGE:14==
ations that are waiting for data. Another tarpit vulnerability is that receiving data is blocking
if the kernel buffer is non-empty, but the caller requests to wait until a certain number of
bytes have been received, using the MSG_WAITALL flag[7].
• Sending data tarpits only works in TCP connections, since UDP connections don’t usually
block. The tarpit vulnerability in this case, is to fill up the senders kernel buffer by never
acknowledging the sent data. However, at some point the OS will give up, and the connection
will be aborted.
• Peek function tarpits utilize functions that can peek at whether data can be read from or
written to, such as select[8]. These functions can have a timeout set, which blocks the
function for as long as the timeout value allows, or until one of the sockets becomes available.
The tarpit vulnerability in this case, is to have the functions wait until the timeout occurs, or
of no timeoutis set, then the function will wait indefinitely.
• Connection establishment is a simple tarpit vulnerability, which utilizes the fact that estab-
lishing a connection, such as a TCP connection, blocks until the connection is successful, or
the connection times out.
Walla S. et al. used these vulnerabilities to identify vulnerable execution paths and using dynamic
analysis to identify them. From this, they created MALPITY, which they tested on six known
malware families (Bashlite, SalityP2P, Pynamer, Pushdo, WannaCry, and Tofsee), and found at
least one tarpit vulnerability in each of them. They confirmed these tarpit vulnerabilities, by also
manually reverse-engineering these samples, and found the same tarpit vulnerabilities.
The authors also note the limitations of MALPITY, such as the use of dynamic analysis, since
it only looks at execution paths that are executed, and that some paths are missed due to this.
However, MALPITY is effective in revealing socket-level tarpit vulnerabilities, and use this in-
formation to create specific tarpits.
2.4
Application-Specific Tarpits
2.4.1
Telnet and Transport-Layer Tarpits
An other way to implement and use tarpits is to trick bots in a botnet to connect to a tarpit, by
sending a crafted TCP SYN/ACK to bots, which convinces bots that they tried to connect to it.
The reason this works, is because some botnets, like the Mirai botnet, have no book-keeping of
all the scanning they do, due to it being ineffective for mass-scanning. The reason these botnets
utilize this feature, is to avoid the overhead by opening a socket through the OS. Instead, they man-
ually craft TCP SYN packets and send them to random IP addresses, and wait for incoming TCP
SYN/ACK packages. The way they craft their TCP packets includes a fingerprint. This fingerprint
refers the fact, that the Mirai bots have a constant source port and embed the destination IP address
into the TCP initial sequence number.

SYN/ACK packets to Mirai-infected devices, based on the mirai fingerprint. They designed five
tarpits in total, where three of them were tarpits that utilized the TCP protocol (the transport layer)
and two that utilized the telnet protocol (application layer). They deployed these tarpits on the ports
23, 1023, and 2323, which are all used by Telnet. The five tarpits used the following techniques:
• Only complete the handshake: To limit the evaluation of incoming requests to spare pro-

• Send TCP keepalives: This prevents the OS from timing out the connection, but a timeout
will happen at the malware level
• Reply with random data: Keep sending random data indefinitely, but never send special
characters, such as newlines, null bytes or EOF. This will keep the client stuck indefinitely,
if no timeout is set.
• Display a fake login prompt using telnet, accept every login attempt and present a fake shell.
• Emulate a fake telnet shell, but delay all responses by 5 seconds.
The authors explain, that all of the aforementioned tarpit strategies are all equally effective.
The authors did note the ethical aspect of impairing innocent users, since it is possible that innocent
users’ devices perform malicious activities by the command of botnet owners. They created an ex-
perimental setup to test the impairing caused by a tarpit, and found that no noticeable impairment
is found.
They also note how effective the tarpits are at trapping Mirai-infected bots. Since the tarpits keep
sending out TCP SYN packets to Mirai bots, the tarpits fill up the brute-forcing queue for bots,
which has a size of 128, each representing a thread that concurrently brute forces victims creden-
tials. They found that a single tarpit can occupy 126.2 threads on average, based on their experi-
ment.
A challenge the authors faced was IP-churning, which is when Internet Service Providers dy-
namically reassign public IP addresses to devices. This causes an infected device to appear on
the Internet under a different IP address, while the infection itself persists. To detect when an IP
address had churned, the authors exploited the fact that Mirai-based malware generates fixed ran-
dom values, which is used to set the TCP source port and window size, that remain constant for
the duration of the infection[9]. By observing traffic at their network telescope, they could link IP
addresses to previously observed IP addresses, meaning an IP-churn had happened, if it was within
the same Autonomous System. This passive detection method allowed the authors to accurately
track infections across IP changes without active probing, and to quantify the effect of churn on
their tarpitting effectiveness.

SMTP Tarpitting for Spam Control
Another application of tarpitting has been done by the ISP company Eircom in 2003, which has
now rebranded as Eir, where they applied a tarpitting strategy to their SMTP protocol to fight off

minutes per mail, as to not violate RFC 2821[11]. This allows false positives to not be completely
impeded, and a high-delay can trigger the IT team to look into it, and manually make a decision.

be tarpitted, but every single one afterwards gets tarpitted, with the amount increasing every time.
In order to not tarpit every single client, they decrement the r_max in a way to not let the offenders
off the hook too easily, which can be configured per IP address.
They found great results from implementing this strategy, where they reduced their spam incident
traffic down to only 2-3 times their normal traffic. Interestingly, a malicious actor quickly noticed
this tarpitting strategy, and began to lower their send-rate to go under the radar and steadily in-
creased it to try to reverse-engineer how the tarpitting strategy works.

A tarpit developed for the SSH protocol is the Endlessh tarpit, developed by Chris Wellons[14]. In

actually not breaking any rules, related to RFC 4253.

An unusual take on the idea of tarpits comes from Virtanen T. et al[17], where they focus on con-

3
Architecture of botnets
A botnet is a collection of devices that are controlled by a single user. These devices, which are
the ”bots” of the botnet, are infected with malware without the knowledge of the owner, and is
used for different types of attacks, such as a DDoS attack. These botnets can be designed with
either a centralized architecture[18][19], decentralized architecture[20], or a hybrid between the
two[21]. They can also implement different strategies for communicating with the owner of the
botnet, which is explained in-depth in section 8 located in the Appendix.
In a centralized setup, all bots connect to a single C2 server for instructions, making coordination
simple but creating a single point of failure. Decentralized or P2P models distribute control among
bots, improving resilience. Fast-fluxing hides infrastructure by rapidly rotating DNS records, while
blockchain-based botnets use platforms like Bitcoin to embed commands in transactions.
While botnets targeting traditional computing systems have existed for decades, the first known
IoT-specific botnet was in 2008 called Hydra[1]. Since then, the development of botnets took off,
with the Mirai variant becoming the most popular one. By exploiting default credentials in poorly
secured IoT devices, Mirai generated one of the largest DDoS attacks in history, peaking at over 1.1
Tbps, targeted at the french service provider OVH[18] and also using DDoS to affect major inter-
net services like Dyn, which took down websites like Netflix, Reddit, Twitter etc. for several hours.
Since Mirai’s source code was publicly released[22], a flood of similar botnets has appeared, each
with different improvements and functionality. Some of these include the same scanning mecha-
nism as Mirai, and therefore also inherits its fingerprint as mentioned in section 2.4.1, while others
don’t.

However, some botnets are actually not malicious, but instead operates as a ”gray hat” system,
where they don’t launch any attacks, and instead focus on infecting other devices, and makes it
more difficult for other botnets to infect said devices.
An example of this is the Hajime botnet, which has not been observed making any attacks. In-

An interesting property of the IoT botnet malware, is that it is almost always non-persistent, mean-

Takedown efforts against botnets has previously involved sinkholing, where law enforcement takes
control over the C2 infrastructure. This can be done if the botnet relies on hardcoded domain names
or IP addresses for its C2 servers. By taking over these domains, authorities can prevent bots from
receiving commands, stopping the botnet.
3.1
Centralized botnet
A centralized botnet is defined by its architecture in which all bots communicate with one or more
central C2 servers. The C2 server is responsible for distributing commands to bots and collecting
data from them. This design makes coordination straightforward but introduces a single point of
failure, since if a C2 server is taken down, the botnet is disrupted.
A well-known example of a centralized botnet is the Mirai botnet. The Mirai botnet, and its many
variants, follow a specific multi-stage infection process, which consists of four parts: The bot it-
self, a C2 server, a reporter, and a loader[18][19]. The bot is actively trying to infect other devices,
while also waiting for commands from the C2 server. The C2 server is responsible for distributing
commands to all of the infected devices. The reporter is responsible for receiving credentials for
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation

==PAGE:20==
a vulnerable device, and informing the loader of said credentials and address. The loader then
connects to the vulnerable device, and makes it download a malware to become infected.
The way Mirai-variant botnets work, is through a multi-stage process:
1. Scanning: Bots search the internet for vulnerable devices, looking for open, vulnerable
ports.
2. Attacking: After finding a vulnerable device, the bot begins attacking the device. This can
be done with bruteforcing credentials or exploiting known vulnerabilities. If it succeeds, it
goes on to the next step. If not, the process starts over.
3. Reporting: After having found a pair of valid credentials, the bot reports its findings to the
report server.
4. Dispatching. After the report server retrieves information about a vulnerable device, it uses
a loader program to infect the the vulnerable device.
5. Loader: The loader program connects to the vulnerable device using the reported credentials
and instructs it to download and execute the malware. The loader ensures the right binary is
selected for the device’s processor (e.g., ARM, MIPS). Once the malware runs, the device
becomes part of the botnet.
6. Attack command: The owner of the botnet can send an attack command for an attack, such
as a DDoS attack, to a target address. This is sent to the C2 server, which relays the command
to all the infected devices.

3.2
P2P botnet
A P2P botnet avoids the single point of failure, that a centralized botnet has. It does this by dis-
tributing C2 functionality across all infected devices, so that each bot can communicate with its
peers. Instead of relying on a single C2 server, the botnet forms a network, where commands,
updates, and data are shared between nodes. This decentralized structure allows the botnet to con-
tinue operating even if parts of the network are taken down.
An example of a P2P botnet is the Hajime botnet[20]. The way it works is by using a DHT to
track what clients are hosting specific files, which is very similar to how BitTorrent uses the DHT
aswell.

The smart thing the Hajime botnet does, is that it reuses the public BitTorrent DHT, which is used

the botmaster does not have to directly contact individual bots or operate a central command server.

between two IDs is calculated by applying a bitwise XOR operation to the two IDs. The node
whose ID has the smallest XOR distance to a key is responsible for storing the value associated
with that key. This allows the Hajime botnet to find peers hosting a particular payload, as the
lookup process quickly converges on the node closest to the target key.

3.3
Hybrid
Some botnets can also be a hybrid between a centralized and decentralized architecture. In these
cases, the botnet can use a decentralized P2P network for distributing commands and updates be-
tween bots, while using elements of centralized control for issuing commands or collecting data.
An example of a hybrid architecture is with the use of a proxy layer, that act as an intermediary
node between the C2 server and the rest of the bots, which are called harvester-bots. The proxy-
bots job is to inform the harvester bots of commands issued by the botmaster, and also collect data
from the harvester bots, so the botmaster can retrieve them.
The harvester bots are responsible for harvesting stolen data, which is done through monitoring
activity on an infected machine. A bot can be both a harvester-bot and a proxy-bot at the same time.
That way, C2 infrastructure remains hidden behind the bots, similar to the fast-flux architecture.
An example of a botnet that used the proxy-layer architecture is the GameOver Zeus botnet. This
botnet was a P2P variant of the earlier centralized Zeus banking trojan. Certain bots within the
network were designated as proxy bots via digitally signed announcements from the botmasters.
Among the proxy bots there was also dedicated HTTP servers, that were not bots, but acted as an
extra layer of communication between the proxy bots and the root of the C2 communication.
The GameOver Zeus network was also divided into virtual sub-botnets, each controlled indepen-
dently by a different botmaster. Every bot was hardcoded with a sub-botnet identifier, ensuring
that even within the whole network, data flows remained separated between the sub-botnets. This
made the botnet more resilient to takedown efforts, since taking down one sub-botnet did not affect
the others.
The way the GameOver Zeus botnet used P2P communication was by having each bot maintain a
list of peer bots, which it used to exchange information. Each bot started with a hardcoded boot-
strap list of known peers and continuously updated its peer list based on successful interactions. If
a bot lost connection to all of its peers, the botnet also implemented a fallback method that used
DGA, to contact alternative sources and rejoin the network.
GameOver Zeus was taken down by law enforcements with Operation Tovar, where the FBI, in
coordination with Europol, successfully disrupted the botnet’s infrastructure in May 2014[26]. The
operation involved sinkholing the botnet, by reverse-engineering the DGA fallback function, and
taking control of several C2 servers.

4.1
Telnet
The Telnet protocol was created as part of the ARPANET in the late 1960’s, due to the need to
allow a user at a terminal to control and use a process in a remote host, as if they were on a local
user of that remote host[31]. Telnet, short for ”teletype network,” was one of the earliest protocols
for remote login and command execution. It operates over TCP, typically using port 23, and facil-
itates bidirectional, text-based communication between devices[32].
Over time, Telnet’s lack of encryption became a a significant security risk, since transmitted cre-
dentials were in plaintext, and therefore it’s usage declined over time, in the favor of more secure
protocols, such as SSH[33]. However, with the rise of IoT devices, telnet has seen a comeback in
its use, due to it being a lightweight and simple protocol.
There are many RFC’s related to the telnet protocol, but most of them are building on top of the
original, by including extra functionality. The original RFC regarding to the Telnet protocol, is
RFC 854.
4.1.1
RFC854
RFC 854[32], titled Telnet Protocol Specification, was the original internet standard for the telnet
protocol, that formally describes the Telnet protocol. It defines Telnet as a general, bidirectional
byte-oriented communication protocol that is primarily intended for terminal-to-host communica-
tion. The specification is built on three ideas, which are:
1. Concept of Network Virtual Terminal
When a Telnet connection is established, a Network Virtual Terminal (NVT) is created. The
NVT serves as an abstraction layer, providing a standard interface that both the client and
server map their local terminal conventions onto. This allows heterogeneous systems with
different terminal types and control codes to communicate seamlessly, as they each translate
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
24
==PAGE:26==
their own system-specific instructions to and from the standardized NVT format.
2. The principle of negotiated options
It is possible to use a more elaborate set of conventions when establishing a telnet connection,
which can be done with negotiated options. Both parties can initiate a request, which will
change the telnet connection. This negotiation is conducted using a special set of Telnet
command codes (e.g. DO, DON'T, WILL, WON'T). If it is accepted by the other party, it takes
effect immediately. If it is rejected, the NVT faces no change. A party may always refuse
a request to enable, and must never refuse a request to disable some option. If the server
and client requests the same option at the same time, each host will see the other request as
a positive acknowledgment of its own request, meaning that the option will immediately be
accepted.
3. A symmetric view of terminals and processes
Telnet assumes that both parties have equal control over option negotiations and commu-
nication modes. This symmetry requires certain protocol rules to avoid non-terminating
acknowledgment loops and inconsistencies:
(a) A party may not send a request, just to announce what mode it is in.
(b) If a party receives a request to enter some mode it is already in, the request should not
be acknowledged. However, if a request is denied by a party, a response must be given.
(c) If an option request that will have an effect on the interpretation of the data being sent
from the first party to the second, the command must be inserted exactly when the
change should occur. Due to the nature of network communication, some time will
elapse between the transmission of a request and the receipt of an acknowledgment,
which may be rejected, which is why it is a good idea to buffer outgoing data, until it
learns if the request is accepted or rejected.
NVT
The NVT itself consists of two logical components: a keyboard, which generates outgoing data,
and a printer, which displays incoming data. All communication over Telnet is interpreted through
this abstraction. Characters typed locally are converted into a standardized form — specifically,
seven-bit US-ASCII encoded in an eight-bit field — and transmitted across the network. Incoming
data is treated as if printed on a terminal display, using NVT conventions for control sequences
such as CR LF for newlines.
Any system-specific handling of input modes, timing, or character translation is expected to occur
at the local implementation level. The NVT layer is intentionally kept simple and uniform, so both
ends of a Telnet session can map their native terminal behaviors onto this shared virtual model.
This standardization is critical for interoperability between heterogeneous systems, where termi-

• WILL: Indicates the sender is willing to enable an option.
• WON’T: Indicates the sender refuses or will disable an option.
• DO: Requests that the other party enable an option.
• DON’T: Requests that the other party disable an option.
Negotiations can be initiated by either side using DO or WILL, while DON’T and WON’T are used as
negative responses to such requests. Therefore, if a host is requesting the other party to enable an
option with DO X, and at the same time the other party sends that it is willing to enable option X
with WILL X, then each side interprets the other’s message as an affirmative response to its own.
In this way, the simultaneous exchange of DO X and WILL X is treated as mutual agreement to
enable the option, without requiring further negotiation.
Telnet also allows for a more detailed kind of negotiation called subnegotiation, which is used
when an option needs more than a simple on/off agreement. If the basic DO and WILL commands
aren’t enough to cover all the necessary settings, subnegotiation lets both sides exchange more
specific information. This is done using the special markers SB (Subnegotiation Begin) and SE
(Subnegotiation End), which wrap around the extra details being sent. However, this kind of de-
tailed exchange should only happen after both sides have agreed to support the option, so that
neither one tries to interpret something it doesn’t understand. This helps keep communication
clear and prevents errors.
Importantly, a Telnet implementation does not need to support or even recognize all possible op-
tions. According to the protocol, any unknown or unsupported option should simply be rejected
using a WON’T or DON’T, which always returns the session to a safe state, which is the default Net-
work Virtual Terminal (NVT) mode. This design ensures backward compatibility and robust error
handling in mixed or minimal implementations.

UPnP
The UPnP protocol was developed to provide seamless and automatic connectivity between de-
vices on a network, with a focus on ease of use and vendor interoperability. The first release of
the protocol was in June 2000, with the publication of the Universal Plug and Play Device Ar-
chitecture 1.0 by the UPnP Forum[34]. Originally initiated by Microsoft, the protocol was later
developed and maintained by the UPnP Forum, an industry formed in October 1999 to promote
and standardize UPnP technologies. In 2016, UPnP Forum assigned their assets to the Open Con-
nectivity Foundation, which continues to develop the protocol[35].
The UPnP protocol aims to simplify the process of connecting and configuring devices on a local
network without requiring user intervention. It does this by using automatic discovery of devices
and advertisement of services to make smart devices, such as routers, smart-TVs and printers com-

municate seamlessly. UPnP allows devices to join or leave the network dynamically, announce
their capabilities, and interact with other devices without user intervention or centralized manage-
ment.
The UPnP protocol operates with different protocols, such as IP, TCP, UDP, HTTP, and XML.
It uses IP for basic networking and addressing, while TCP and UDP serve as transport protocols
depending on the service. UDP for discovery via SSDP and TCP for control and eventing mes-
sages. HTTP is employed for retrieving device and service descriptions, and XML is used to format
these descriptions and control messages.
The UPnP Device Architecture 2.0[36] is the most recent specification detailing how UPnP op-
erates. While documents, such as RFC 6970[37], provide implementation guidance for discovery
mechanisms, they do not represent the full protocol definition. This chapter therefore only refers
to the UPnP Device Architecture 2.0 specification, which covers the complete communication life-
cycle within UPnP.
The UPnP protocol is media-independent, requires no device-specific drivers, and is not tied to any
particular operating system or programming language. These qualities have enabled its adoption
across a wide range of devices, such as IoT devices. This makes UPnP relevant for the development
of tarpits aimed at trapping malicious activity.
4.2.0.1
UPnP Device Architecture 2.0
A UPnP network consists of two kinds of devices. A
control point and a service device. There can exist multiple control points and service devices in a
UPnP network. The service devices are responsible for sending data for a specific topic, or multi-
ple topics, to the control points, which then forwards the data to users, based on their subscription
to one or multiple topics.
The first step in the UPnP protocol is the addressing of devices. When a device joins the network,
it must obtain an IP address to enable communication. UPnP relies on standard IP mechanisms for
addressing, primarily using DHCP to request an address from a DHCP server on the local network.
If no DHCP server is available, the device falls back to Auto IP to assign itself an address. Auto
IP defines how a device or control point intelligently chooses an address from a set of reserved
addresses.
The second step is discovery. When a service device joins the network after it has obtained an
IP address, it uses the SSDP protocol, which is UPnP’s discovery protocol, to announce its pres-
ence to control points. A service device sends out advertisement messages containing essential
information such as its device type, unique ID, and a URL pointing to its full description. This full
description is an XML document containing its available actions, input/output parameters, state
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation

==PAGE:30==
variables, and associated URLs for control, eventing, and presentation. Similarly, when a control
point joins the network, it can also search for devices using SSDP. It does this by sending out a
multicast request on the network, to which matching service devices respond with their own de-
scription URLs.
The third step is description. After a control point has discovered a service device, it retrieves
a detailed XML-based description from the URL provided in the discovery message, based on the
LOCATION header. This description includes the service device’s model and manufacturer informa-
tion, the list of embedded devices and available services, and various URLs for further interaction,
such as control, eventing, and presentation. Each service service description lists the available
actions the service supports, their arguments, and the related state variables, including data types
and whether they support eventing.
The fourth step is control. With the service device and its services described, the control point
can now interact with those services by sending action requests. These requests are formatted in
XML and sent using SOAP over HTTP to the service’s control URL. The service device then pro-
cesses the action, performs the requested task, and sends a response. Any changes to the internal
state of the service as a result of the action are reflected in the relevant state variables.
The fifth step is eventing. To keep control points updated about changes in a service device’s
current state, UPnP supports an eventing mechanism. A control point can subscribe to a service
device to receive event notifications whenever one or more of its state variables change. Sub-
scriptions are initiated with a SUBSCRIBE request sent to the service device’s event URL, which
is specified in the service device description XML. This request includes a CALLBACK URL,
which specifies the address where the service device should send event notifications, along with
a requested timeout. The CALLBACK URL typically points to an HTTP listener on the control
point that is ready to receive asynchronous NOTIFY messages. The service device replies with
a subscription ID and confirms the timeout. If the control point does not resubscribe within the
timeout period, the service device will stop sending updates to that control point.
Once subscribed, the service device sends HTTP NOTIFY requests to the provided CALLBACK
URL whenever an evented state variable changes. These notifications contain information listing
the updated variable names and values. An initial event message is also sent immediately after the
subscription, providing the current values of all evented variables so the control point can initialize
the service device’s state.
The final step is presentation. If a service device provides a presentation URL in its description,
a control point can load this page in a browser. Depending on the device’s capabilities and the
design of the page, the user may be able to control the service device or view its status through a
GUI.

MQTT
MQTT was created in 1999 by Andy Stanford-Clark, from IBM, and Arlen Nipper, then from Ar-
com Control Systems, to solve a specific industrial challenge, which was to make real-time com-
munication possible between oil pipeline sensors and control systems over unreliable, high-cost
satellite links[38][39]. The protocol had to be extremely lightweight, reliable, and bandwidth-
efficient.
Initially used in industrial contexts, MQTT gradually gained traction in other applications. IBM
released it to the public in 2010 with version 3.1, and in 2013, it was submitted to OASIS for stan-
dardization, which led to the release of MQTT version 3.1.1 in 2014, and later MQTT version 5.0
in 2019.

MQTT is a lightweight, publish-subscribe network protocol designed for efficient communica-
tion between devices, particularly over networks with low bandwidth, high latency, or unreliable
connections. It follows a client-broker architecture, where clients (publishers or subscribers) com-
municate through a central broker that manages message distribution.
Today, MQTT is used to facilitate communication between IoT devices, gateways, and cloud plat-
forms. For example, sensors can publish temperature or humidity data to a broker, while appli-
cations subscribe to those topics to react in real-time. This flexibility and efficiency have made
MQTT a popular choice for IoT messaging. It is maintained by OASIS and supported by a large
open-source community, with implementations like Eclipse Paho and brokers like HiveMQ.
Since there are currently three versions of the MQTT protocol specifications, we will focus on
the core elements common to all of them.
4.3.1
MQTT Specifications
An MQTT network consists of two components: the clients and the broker. The clients can be
a publisher, a subscriber, or both. Publishers are responsible for sending messages to a specific
topic or set of topics. Subscribers express interest in receiving messages related to particular topics.
Both publishers and subscribers act as clients, which communicate through the broker. The broker
is the central component in the MQTT architecture, managing all message distribution, session
management, and delivery assurances.
MQTT uses TCP for its transport layer to ensure reliable delivery of messages between clients
and brokers. Starting from version 3.1.1, MQTT also supports optional use of TLS, which pro-
vides encryption and ensures confidentiality, integrity, and authentication of the communication
channel. This is especially important in IoT applications where sensitive data may be transmitted,
or where devices operate in untrusted network environments. With MQTT version 3.1.1 and later
versions, MQTT brokers can also be configured to support other forms of authentication mecha-
nisms, such as LDAP or OAuth.
The first step in establishing communication in MQTT is the connection phase. A client begins
by initiating a connection to the broker using a CONNECT packet. This packet includes essential
details such as the MQTT version, keep-alive interval, optional credentials (username and pass-
word), and optional flags. Upon receiving the CONNECT packet, the broker must respond with a
CONNACK packet indicating whether the connection was accepted or rejected and why. It is not
allowed to send multiple CONNECT packets after already being accepted.
Once connected, the client can use the rest of the control packets, such as the SUBSCRIBE packet.
When a client sends a SUBSCRIBE packet to the broker, it contains a list of topics and the desired

==PAGE:33==
Quality of Service (QoS) level for each topic. The broker processes the request and must respond
with a SUBACK packet, indicating the granted QoS levels. Subscriptions can include exact topic
names or wildcard characters. The single-level wildcard (+) matches one level of a topic hier-
archy, whereas the multi-level wildcard (#) matches all remaining levels. For example if a client
subscribes to +/tennis/#, this can match with both home/tennis/score and stadium/tennis
topics.
To send a message, a client uses the PUBLISH packet. This packet includes a topic name, a
payload, the desired QoS level, and control flags, such as the DUP flag, which is responsible for
informing if the PUBLISH packet is a retransmission or not. The broker then receives the PUB-
LISH packet and forwards it to all clients subscribed to the relevant topic. The behavior of message
delivery depends on the QoS level chosen. At QoS 0, the message is delivered at most once, with
no acknowledgment. At QoS 1, the broker must acknowledge receipt with a PUBACK packet,
ensuring at least one delivery. At QoS 2, the protocol engages in a four-step handshake, where
both the broker and client must acknowledge receipt. This is done by sending the packets in the
following order: PUBLISH (client to broker), PUBREC (broker to client), PUBREL (client to
broker), and PUBCOMP (broker to client). This guarantees that the message is delivered exactly
once, with no duplicates.
When a client no longer wishes to receive messages from a topic, it sends an UNSUBSCRIBE
packet to the broker. The broker must respond with an UNSUBACK packet, confirming the re-
moval of the subscription.
If a client has established a connection with a broker but does not need to send any control packets
within the keep-alive interval from the CONNECT request, it must send a PINGREQ packet to
confirm that it is still connected. This is a minimal, fixed-length packet with no variable header
or payload. Upon receiving a PINGREQ, the broker responds with a PINGRESP packet, which
also contains only a fixed header. This exchange resets the keep-alive timer and signals that the
connection is still valid. If the keep-alive interval exceeds 1.5 times its set duration without the
broker receiving any packets from the client, the broker is required to consider the connection lost.
The message structure in MQTT is composed of a fixed header, an optional variable header, and
an optional payload. The fixed header is present in all MQTT control packets and indicates the
packet type and flags such as QoS level, DUP, and retain. It also includes a Remaining Length
field specifying the size of the variable header and payload combined.
The Remaining Length field is encoded as a variable-length integer. This encoding allows the
length to be represented compactly: the field uses one to four bytes, where each byte contributes 7
bits to the length value. The most significant bit of each byte is used as a continuation flag. If the
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
32
==PAGE:34==
MSB is set to 1, it indicates that another byte follows, and if it is set to 0, it means that this byte is
the last. This scheme allows MQTT packets to efficiently represent a range of message sizes from
0 to 27·4 −1 bytes.
The variable header contains different information, based on the packet type. For example, in
a CONNECT packet, it includes the protocol name and version, whereas in a PUBLISH packet, it
contains the topic name and packet identifier. The payload, which is optional, contains the actual
data of interest, which can be sensor readings.
An important feature of MQTT is the retain flag, which allows the broker to store the last re-
tained message for a topic. When a new client subscribes to that topic, it immediately receives
the most recent retained message, ensuring it has up-to-date information without waiting for the
next publication. Another important feature is the Will flag, which allows a client to specify a
last message, that the broker will publish on its behalf if the client disconnects unexpectedly. This
helps alert other clients or systems that the connection was lost, supporting better handling for un-
expected disconnects in IoT environments.
The final step in the MQTT lifecycle is the DISCONNECT phase, where a client sends a DIS-
CONNECT packet to the broker to gracefully terminate the session. This informs the broker that
the client is shutting down voluntarily, and no Will message should be sent.

4.4
CoAP
CoAP is a lightweight protocol designed for use in resource-constrained environments, such as IoT
devices. It follows a client-server model similar to HTTP and uses methods like GET, POST, PUT,
and DELETE to interact with resources. CoAP is RESTful, meaning it follows the same principles
of addressing and manipulating resources through standard methods, just like HTTP. The differ-
ence between CoAP and HTTP is the fact that CoAP is designed to be far more lightweight. This
can be seen in CoAP’s compact header, and the fact that it uses UDP instead of TCP.
The first version of CoAP was officially standardized in 2014 with the publication of RFC 7252[40],
developed by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) in the Constrained RESTful Environ-
ments (CoRE) working group. The goal of CoAP was to enable resource-constrained devices to
communicate efficiently with the Internet using a lightweight protocol. CoAP runs over UDP and
uses compact binary headers to support RESTful CRUD (Create, Retrieve, Update, Delete) opera-
tions for resources from constrained devices. However, CoAP’s development started much earlier.
A foundational paper detailing the protocol’s core design and use cases was published well before
the formal RFC was finalized and is regarded as the first step in the development of the CoAP
protocol[41].
In 2015, RFC 7641 was published, which introduced the Observe extension to CoAP, allowing
clients to ”observe” a resource and automatically receive updated whenever its state changes. This
functionality is very similar to the publish-subscribe systems, like MQTT, while preserving the
RESTful model. The Observe feature was created, due to constrained IoT devices needing real-
time values, where constant polling would be too resource demanding for the device.
To tackle one of CoAP’s limitations, RFC 7959, published in 2016, was created to address an-
other key limitation of constrained networks, which was message size. CoAP’s use of UDP meant
messages were often constrained by packet size, making it difficult to transmit large payloads ef-
ficiently. RFC 7959 defined block-wise transfers, a mechanism that allows CoAP messages to be
split into blocks and be transmitted sequentially. This allowed CoAP to support larger resource
representations, while maintaining reliability and simplicity[41]. Importantly, block-wise transfer
was designed to work independently of transport-layer fragmentation, giving applications explicit
control over how data is chunked and reassembled.
In 2018, RFC 8323 was published, which made is possible for CoAP to run with TCP, aswell
as TSL and WebSockets, instead of UDP, which was originally used. This allowed CoAP to be
used in other kinds of network environments, particularly where UDP was not an option. Running
CoAP over TCP or WebSockets also made it easier to interact with web infrastructure, making
it easier to integrate constrained devices with cloud services, browsers, and existing HTTP-based
systems.

Since all of the specifications are built on top of RFC 7252, except for RFC 8323, which have
differences in the message layer, we will write about RFC 7252. However, we will also address
how CoAP sends blocked responses, in cases where large amounts of data has to be transferred.
4.4.1
RFC 7252
A CoAP network consists of clients that send requests and servers that host resources and respond.
An endpoint may act as both client and server depending on the situation. CoAP uses a URI-based
addressing scheme similar to HTTP, with resource identifiers like coap://sensor.local/temperature.
Unlike HTTP, however, CoAP is designed for asynchronous, multicast-capable, low-bandwidth
networks and includes mechanisms for reliability over UDP.
CoAP uses four different types of messages, which are:
• CON (Confirmable): Requires an acknowledgment from the receiver.
• NON (Non-confirmable): Does not require an acknowledgment from the receiver.
• ACK (Acknowledgment): Sent in response to a CON message.
• RST (Reset): Sent when a message is received, but could not be processed.
Each message includes a Message ID and a Token. Message ID is used to detect retransmissions of
messages, while the token is used to match a request with a response. The reason for the seperation
between the Message ID and the token is because they serve different roles at different protocol
layers. The Message ID operates at the transport layer to ensure message reliability and detect
duplicates, while the Token functions at the application layer to maintain correlation between re-
quests and responses.
Each CoAP message may also include one or more options, which provide additional informa-
tion that influences how the message is processed by the recipient. The options part is encoded
in a compact, delta-encoded format to minimize overhead. Delta-encoding means that instead of
encoding the full numeric option number for each option, the message stores only the difference
between the current option number and the previous one. This approach reduces the amount of data
sent, as smaller numbers can be represented more efficiently. The first option’s delta is relative to
zero. Since CoAP options must appear in order of increasing option number, this approach ensures
that these deltas are always positive.
Because CoAP uses UDP, it includes its own reliability functionality. When a client sends a CON
message, it expects an ACK from the recipient. If the ACK is not received within a timeout period,
known as ACK_TIMEOUT, the message is retransmitted. The default value for ACK_TIMEOUT
is 2 seconds, although this can be adjusted. To avoid synchronization effects that could cause net-
work congestion, CoAP introduces ACK_RANDOM_FACTOR, a multiplier applied to ACK_TIMEOUT
that randomly varies the actual timeout duration within a defined range, with a default value of 1.5.

fined limit, specified as MAX_RETRANSMIT, with a default value of 4 attempts.
Responses can be sent in piggybacked form, where the ACK and payload are in the same packet,
or separately, where first the ACK is sent, and the payload sent in another packet.
CoAP also uses a response code system similar to HTTP, but with the values encoded as an 8-
bit field. The code format follows a class.detail structure, where class are the first 3 upper bits
(values 0-7), and the class the lower 5 bits (values 0-31). The detail value is used in combination
with the class value, to give more context to the class value. The class value 2 are for successful
handling of a request, such as 2.01 Created, and class value 4 are for clients errors, such as 4.00
Bad Request, and class value 5 are for server errors, such as 5.00 Internal Server Error.
CoAP also supports multicast, allowing a single request to be sent to multiple servers simulta-
neously using a multicast address. This address can either be an IPv4 address or an IPv6 address.
Each server that receives the request may decide whether to respond or not. To prevent over-
loading the sender with responses, servers can implement randomized response delays, to avoid
congesting the sender. Multicast is especially useful for resource discovery, such as querying
.well-known/core to identify CoAP-enabled devices within a local network segment.

CoAP also addresses the security of messages, by supporting the use of DTLS, which is TLS
over UDP instead of TCP. DTLS provides confidentiality, integrity, and authentication of CoAP
messages.
CoAP also defines different security modes, to protect devices using the CoAP protocol. The four
different security modes are:
• NoSec: This security mode offers no security. This mode can be used in trusted environ-

• PreSharedKey (PSK): Devices share a pre-established secret key for mutual authentication
and encryption. This mode is suitable for constrained devices as it avoids the overhead of

• RawPublicKey (RPK): Devices use raw public keys (without certificates) for authentica-

• Certificate mode: Devices authenticate each other using X.509 certificates, providing ro-

secure communication even across intermediaries such as proxies.
4.4.2
RFC 7959
A limitation with RFC 7252 is when a large amount of data has to be sent. Since CoAP was de-
signed for constrained environments, it operates over UDP with small message sizes. RFC 7959
introduces blockwise transfer, a mechanism that allows large payloads to be broken into small
blocks for transmission over CoAP.
Each blockwise transfer uses CoAP options Block1 (for requests) and Block2 (for responses) to
indicate blockwise operations. These options include:
• Block number: The numbre which specifies the position of the block in the sequence of
blocks. Can be up to 20 bits long.
• The more bit: Specifies if this is the last block of the block sequence.
• Block size exponent: Determines the size of the block.
When a blockwise transfer occurs, the requesting party is responsible for requesting the next block,
until all the blocks has been received.
4.5
Vulnerabilities
There are a number of vulnerabilities for every protocol, which can and will be exploited by ma-
licious actors. These vulnerabilities stem from factors such as a lack of authentication, lac of
encryption, and design decisions that prioritize ease of use over security. The focus is on how
these flaws can be abused by attackers, particularly in the context of IoT devices, where limited
resources often mean security measures are minimal or absent.
4.5.1
Telnet
One of the most fundamental shortcomings of Telnet is its transmission of all data, including user-
names and passwords, in plaintext. There is no form of encryption. This exposes Telnet sessions
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
37
==PAGE:39==
to eavesdropping and interception using network sniffing tools, like Wireshark. Khandre et al.
demonstrated how Telnet credentials could easily be captured using Wireshark, showcasing the
protocol’s vulnerability to eavesdropping attacks[42]. The researchers concluded that any user
monitoring a shared network could potentially retrieve sensitive login information without the
need for any kind of special privilege.
Also, the default credentials for Telnet are weak, which can easily be bruteforced. This is shown
by Klein J. et al., who explain that Telnet is often left turned on by default in IoT devices, partly
because it was carried over from older WSN designs, and partly because it’s a cheaper option that
avoids the extra cost and complexity of using secure alternatives like SSH[43]. These devices often
use default login credentials, or none at all, making them easy targets for automated brute-force
attacks.
Klen J. et al. created an experimental setup, where they configured a simulated IoT environment
with an exposed Telnet service to observe real-world attack behavior. Over a five-day period,
attackers conducted both passive and active intrusions, ranging from data harvesting to disabling
network hardware. The experiment demonstrated that Telnet-connected devices are scanned for by
malicious actors, and once compromised, can be used as an entrance point to attack other wireless
systems on the same network. For example, attackers accessed a connected printer and a wireless
router, making them inoperable [43].
4.5.2
UPnP
In a UPnP network, any device can advertise a service to the network, while there is no kind of
authentication involved. This makes it possible for a device to impersonate as another device. For
example, if a legitimate service device joins the network and advertises its services to the mul-
ticast address 239.255.255.250:1900, then a malicious service device can retrieve the device
description document from the legitimate service, and use that as it’s own, tricking other control
points to think there are two identical service devices on the network. The malicious device can
then broadcast any kind of forged information it wants, to other devices listening on the network.
This lack of authentication is thoroughly explored by Kayas G. et al.[44], who demonstrate how at-
tackers can exploit this weakness to manipulate device discovery and interaction processes, which
we describe below.
Not only can service devices be malicious, but control points can also be malicious, since there
is also no form of authentication of commands received by service devices. A malicious control
point can send repeated or malicious requests to a service device in order to disrupt its normal
functioning. For instance, a smartphone acting as a control point could flood a security camera
with requests for its MonitorOccupancy service, or flood it with discovery messages, keeping it
busy and preventing it from responding to the actual control point when needed. This effectively
disables the security feature through a form of denial-of-service, and will also quickly drain the
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
38
==PAGE:40==
battery of the service device. It can also change the state of a service device, by sending malicious
commands to service devices. In more critical environments, such as healthcare, this type of im-
personation could have severe consequences. For example, a malicious control point could send
dangerous commands like increaseHeartRate to an IoT-connected pacemaker, posing a serious
threat to patient safety.
Another attack vector stems from the lack of authentication in the discovery phase of UPnP. A
malicious control point can capture legitimate discovery messages broadcast by another control
point and later replay them to the network. For example, when a legitimate control point sends out
a discovery request searching for devices, all service devices respond with their service descrip-
tions. A malicious device or control point on the same network can eavesdrop on this message and
store it for later use. At any time, it can replay the discovery request, to make the service devices
send their descriptions to the malicious control point.
Since UPnP discovery requests are sent over UDP, an attacker can spoof the source IP address
of the discovery message. In doing so, the attacker pretends that the request is coming from a
victim’s device, rather than their own. Because UDP is connectionless and lacks handshake veri-
fication, service devices cannot verify the true origin of the message. Therefore, a malicious con-
trol point can send a spoofed discovery request to the standard multicast UPnP discovery address
239.255.255.250:1900, such that all service devices on the network that match the search crite-
ria respond by sending their service descriptions to the victim’s IP address. As a result, the victim
is flooded with unsolicited network traffic, turning the unknowing UPnP devices into actors for a
DDoS attack. Russow C. discovered that this amplification factor can be up to a factor of 30.8[45].
Another vulnerability in UPnP comes from its event subscription mechanism. When a control
point wants to receive updates about changes in a service’s state, it sends a subscription request to
the service device, specifying a CALLBACK URL where the events should be delivered. How-
ever, just like with discovery requests, there is no form authentication or verification involved in
this process. The service device blindly accepts any subscription request and starts publishing
event notifications to the provided CALLBACK address. This allows a malicious control point
to send forged subscription requests with spoofed CALLBACK URLs. For instance, the attacker
could specify the IP address of an unsuspecting third-party system as the callback target. The
service device, unaware that the callback URL is fake, begins sending event notifications to this
victim, potentially flooding them with unexpected traffic. This vulnerability has been dubbed Call-
Stranger, with CVE-2020-12695.
Also, an attacker can send a large volume of these forged subscription requests in a short pe-

4.5.3
MQTT
One of the most significant security concerns in MQTT systems is its vulnerability to DoS attacks,
to target the availability of the protocol. Because the broker acts as the central hub for all com-
munications between publishers and subscribers, its availability is critical. This is why flooding
attacks, where the broker is overwhelmed with illegitimate traffic, can be an effective attack, since
it can overload the broker, making it become unresponsive or even crash it. Attacks like this has
been explored by Emrah A. et al. [47], which outlines the different vulnerabilities the MQTT pro-
tocol is facing with version 5, as well with previous versions, of the specifications.
In addition to general flooding, attackers can create a lot of traffic by creating, subscribing to,
and deleting topics. Doing this very quickly can consume a lot of resources from the broker, since
it needs to keep track of all the publishers and subscribers. A variation of this, is to simply publish
an enormous amount of content, which takes up bandwidth and connection slots, which will deny
services to legitimate clients.
Another vulnerability in the MQTT protocol is the possibility for a Man-in-the-Middle attack.
This occurs when MQTT communications are left unencrypted, which is when the MQTT potocol
uses TCP instead of TLS. This allows an attacker on the same network to intercept, modify, and
forward MQTT packets without detection[48].
Wong H. et al. demonstrated such an attack using a WiFi Pineapple to impersonate a legitimate
wireless access point[48]. The WiFi Pineapple, a device designed for penetration testing, was used
to de-authenticate and spoof the legitimate router, tricking nearby IoT devices into connecting to
it, instead of the legitimate one.

They also created a custom parser for the MQTT traffic, to be able to read the data, and then
inject the data with incorrect information. They also circumvented the anomaly detection, by us-
ing a BERT-based model to generate malicious payloads that were realistic. BERT (Bidirectional
Encoder Representations from Transformers) is a machine learning model originally developed for
natural language processing, capable of understanding the meaning of text in context. In this case,
it was adapted to craft MQTT messages that appeared legitimate.
The MQTT protocol is also vulnerable to an attack, called SlowITe, which is a slow DoS attack[49].
This attack exploits the fact, that MQTT can only handle a certain amount of connected clients at
once, and is only applicable, since it runs with TCP. The way the attack works is by opening a
large number of connections to the MQTT broker and sending a CONNECT packet to establish
each connection. Once the broker responds with a CONNACK packet, the client stops communi-
cation. This consumes one of the broker’s limited connection slots without requiring any further
interaction.
By default, MQTT brokers maintain inactive connections for a duration defined by the Keep-Alive
parameter times 1.5[50]. Since the specification allows the keep-alive value to be 16-bits, the max-
imum keep-alive value is 65535 seconds, which is equal to 18 hours 12 minutes and 15 seconds.
This means a single connection can occupy a connection for 27 hours, 5 minutes, and 2.5 seconds
(65535 seconds ·1.5). This means that a single connection can occupy a broker slot for a long time,
without sending any additional packets after the initial CONNECT message. When this behavior
is repeated across many connections, up to the broker’s maximum connection limit, the attacker
can completely exhaust the broker’s capacity, denying service to legitimate clients.
One of SlowITe’s advantages is it’s low bandwidth usage. Each connection uses only an initial
CONNECT packet, which is around 30 bytes (excluding username and password), and the at-
tacker does not need to maintain any active data transmission. This makes it difficult to detect
this kind of attack using traditional DoS detection techniques, which often focus on high-volume
traffic. Also, MQTT brokers usually do not enforce lower or upper bounds on Keep-Alive values,
since they just focus on legitimate connections instead.
However, version 5 of the MQTT specification counters this problem, by letting the MQTT bro-
ker have the final say in the Keep Alive value of a client. Instead of just using the client’s value,
the broker can specify a Keep Alive value in the CONNACK packet, which the client must use.
However, MQTT brokers using version 3.1.1 and earlier are still susceptible to this attack.
Finally, MQTT is also vulnerable to brute-force attempts to figure out client credentials, if the
MQTT broker uses TCP with credentials as its form of authentication. However, authentication

4.5.4
CoAP
For unicast communication, CoAP supports security through DTLS, which provides mutual au-
thentication, integrity, and confidentiality. While DTLS enhances unicast security, it comes with a
prize, which is a performance hit, since there is a noticeable communication overhead, which can
overwhelm constrained devices. Also, DTLS does not encrypt CoAP headers, only the payload,
which exposes metadata.
More importantly, DTLS cannot be used for multicast communication, which is a core feature
in the CoAP protocol. The DTLS handshake is strictly pairwise, making it imposssible to im-
plement in the context of multicast, since it would require each client to perform multiple DTLS
handshakes before a multicast operation, which is way too much computational overhead for con-
strained devices.
Because DTLS cannot be used to secure multicast messages, many CoAP deployments fall back
to using no security (NoSec mode). Even when DTLS is used for unicast, it does not eliminate all
risks. Several vulnerabilities and attack vectors exists, both at the protocol level and due to CoAP’s
operational assumptions in constrained environments.
Just like using multicast with UDP in UPnP, the same problems applies to CoAP, which includes
the spoofing of messages and its use in amplification attacks. However, there are also some addi-
tional vulnerabilities, only specific to the CoAP protocol. This has been discussed thoroughly by
J. P. Mattsson et al.[51], which is used as the source for the following findings.
An attack they found is the blocking attack, which is a DoS threat, where an on-path attacker
prevents the delivery of CoAP messages. An on-path attacker is an attacker who is able to block
or delay messages between two endpoints, since it is positioned within the communication path,
allowing it to intercept, modify, suppress, or replay messages without being detected by the end-
points. In actuator-based scenarios, this can cause uncertainty of the state of a device. An example
of this, is that a user may not know if a smart lock command was executed. Even when using
DTLS, TLS, or OSCORE, attackers can observe metadata such as message sizes and endpoints to
selectively block important messages. To avoid this, use confirmable messages.
Another attack is the request delay attack, which is also exploitable by an on-path attacker. This
attack allows an on-path attacker to delay the delivery of a valid CoAP request so that it is executed
at a much later time than the client intended. For example, an unlock command to a door might
be intercepted and held back by the attacker. After the door is relocked, the attacker can release
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
42
==PAGE:44==
the delayed unlock command, causing unauthorized access. However, it is possible to avoid this
by implementing the logic as specified in RFC 9175[52], which include timestamps or challenge-
response exchanges that allow the server to verify that a request is recent.
Another kind of attack is the The Response Delay and Mismatch Attack, which is also exploitable
by an on-path attacker. In this attack, an on-path attacker delays a valid response and later injects
it as a response to a different request with the same CoAP token. This causes the client to associate
an outdated response with a new request, which can lead to dangerous misinterpretations. For in-
stance, a response confirming a lock action might be mistaken for confirmation of a different, later
command.
This vulnerability comes from lacking correlation between requests and responses, and can oc-
cur even when using DTLS or TLS. That is because DTLS or TLS do not link a response to its
request and instead rely on the token values, which can be reused or poorly randomized in some
implementations. To avoid this, use the logic specified in RFC 9175[52], which improves token
generation to mitigate this kind of attack.
An other kind of attack is the Request Fragment Rearrangement Attack, which utilizes the block-
transfer protocol specified in RFC 7959[53]. In this attack, an on-path attacker intercepts and stores
individual blocks of a CoAP request that has been split into multiple parts. The attacker then reuses
one or more of these blocks in a different request at a later time. Because CoAP servers usually
reassemble the full message from the received blocks before processing it, the attacker can trick
the server into combining blocks from two different operations. This can result in the server to
assemble and execute a request body that was never intended by the legitimate client. However,
the success of such an attack is very low, due to many practical problems. First, the attacker must
identify and intercept blocks from two different operations that are structurally compatible, and
secondly, the timing and sequence of injecting the withheld block must align precisely with the
new request.
To avoid this attack, developers should use the Request-Tag option introduced in RFC 9175[52].
This option allows clients to label each block-wise transfer with a unique identifier, avoiding any
potential mismatched blocks.

Designing Novel Tarpits
We developed four tarpits in this project, one for Telnet, UPnP, MQTT, and CoAP. The reason
behind the choice of these protocols are explained at section 4. The goal was for us to trap bots
for as long as possible, by exploiting mechanisms from each protocol, to trick the bots into staying
indefinitely. It should be noted, that the tarpits does not make any outbound connections, they
simply just try to tarpit every incoming connection, and therefore it assumes all connections are
malicious. The reason for this is that the tarpits doesn’t actually do anything but tarpit. This means

==PAGE:45==
there are no actual full implementation of each protocol to support all the mechanisms of each
protocol, just the ones that were necessary to tarpit.
5.1
Telnet
The tarpit idea for the telnet protocol was to send an endless amount of option negotiation options as
soon as a client connects, and never listen to what the replies are. According to RFC 854[32], there
is no limit in how many option negotation commands that can be exchanged between the server and
client. There is also not a rule specifying when option negotiation can take place, meaning it can be
initiated at any point during the connection, without breaking protocol standards. By doing this, the
clients would be forced to reply to each option negotiation, before being able to actually send their
intended payload. Furthermore, some bots may not support this kind of endless option negotiation,
and may instead become stuck, since they don’t know how to handle the endless option negotiation.
According to RFC 854[32], there are rules to avoid non-terminating acknowledgment loops. In
order to prevent such loops from happening, the following are rules that must be complied with:
1. Parties may only request a change in option status; i.e., a party may not send out
a request merely to announce what mode it is in.
2. If a party receives what appears to be a request to enter some mode it is already
in, the request should not be acknowledged.
3. Whenever one party sends an option command to a second party, whether as a
request or an acknowledgment, and use of the option will have any effect on
the processing of the data being sent from the first party to the second, then the
command must be inserted in the data stream at the point where it is desired that
it take effect.
— RFC 854
Additionally, the specifications also state that a party shouldn’t keep sending the same option ne-
gotiation request if it keeps getting denied. However, this is not a requirement, and is therefore not
a violation of the protocol to do so.
The telnet tarpit therefore implements six different option negotiations, which are:
• WILL Echo
• DO Suppress Go Ahead
• DON’T Status
• WILL Negotiate About Window Size
• DO Terminal Type
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
44
==PAGE:46==
• WON’T New Environment Option
There is no particular reason why we chose most these option negotiation options, since the idea
behind this design is to overwhelm clients with an unending option negotiation process. However,
we included the Supress Go Ahead and Negotiate About Window Size options, since those are han-
dled specifically by the Mirai and BASHLITE botnets. By including these, the tarpit ensures that
Mirai and BASHLITE bots proceed further into the negotiation phase rather than immediately re-
jecting the options. we also included a WON’T and DON’T as options, since these are usually sent
in response to a DO and WILL request, which may confuse bots since they made no such request.
Upon connecting to the tarpit, the client will receive a random option negotiation at a config-
urable interval. This can cause the same option negotiation to be sent multiple times, even if the
client denied the first one, and also it may break rule 1 of the non-terminating acknowledgment
loops rules, since the tarpit may send a request for a status that has already been accepted. Also, it
may send DON’T and WON’T responses to a client, when there was no initial request for such a
change, which are meant only as responses.
All this leads to undefined behavior (implementation specific), since the specifications don’t spec-
ify what should happen in any of these cases. This will confuse bots, since this is not what is
”supposed” to happen during a connection.
5.2
UPnP
The way this tarpit works is by exploiting the fact that according the RFC 854, the RFC for
HTTP/1.1, a chunked HTTP response is sent, but it never ends.
This comes into play during the discovery phase of UPnP, which uses the SSDP protocol. Ac-
cording to the specifications (UPnP Device Architecture 2.0), when a control point wants to search
for service devices on a network, it has to do so by sending an M-SEARCH request to either the
multicast address 239.255.255.250 or using unicast by specifying the IP address of the service
device using port 1900. Since our implementation can’t use the multicast address, because that
would require all malicious activity to already be in the same local network, it makes use of the
unicast address.
In our implementation, the tarpit listens for these M-SEARCH requests on port 1900, and when
it receives such a request, it replies as if it were a valid UPnP device, sending a properly format-
ted HTTP/1.1 response, with the LOCATION header pointing to a fake device description URL
hosted by the tarpit’s HTTP server. This HTTP server, when receiving a GET request for the fake
device description, will send an unlimited amount of fake data, emulating a real UPnP device’s
description XML. This is possible, due to the fact that HTTP/1.1 is the recommended protocol for
all transport of data for both control points and service devices. This can be seen below:

5.3
MQTT
The MQTT uses a publish-subscribe model, which makes it very different from Telnet and UPnP.
The way the tarpit was initially designed, was for the newest specification, which is the version 5.
When a client connects using this version, the tarpit will send a CONACK containing a Receive
Maximum value of 1. This is also called the send quota. This means, that the tarpit tells the client
that it can, at most, handle one QoS 1 and QoS 2 publication concurrently.
This is supported by the following extract from the version 5 specification:
Ëach time the Client or Server sends a PUBLISH packet at QoS 0, it decrements the
send quota. If the send quota reaches zero, the Client or Server MUST NOT send any
more PUBLISH packets with QoS
0. It MAY continue to send PUBLISH packets
with QoS 0, or it MAY choose to suspend sending these as well.”
— MQTT Version 5.0
Also, it is allowed for the server to send a PUBLISH message to a client, even if they are not
subscribed to any topics. This can be seen in the following extract from the version 5 specification:
”Under normal circumstances Clients receive messages in response to Subscriptions
they have created. A Client could also receive messages that do not match any of its
explicit Subscriptions. This can happen if the Server automatically assigned a sub-
scription to the Client. The Client MUST acknowledge any Publish packet it receives
according to the applicable QoS rules regardless of whether it elects to process the
Application Message that it contains”
— MQTT Version 5.0
By combining these two rules, each connected client will, upon connecting using version 5, Re-
ceive a CONACK setting the Receive Maximum value to 1, immediately followed by receiving a
QoS 2 PUBLISH message, which they will respond with by sending a PUBREC, and then wait for
to corresponding PUBREL from the tarpit. However, this PUBREL message will never be sent to
the clients, making them wait indefinitely. Also, since the Receive Maximum value was set to 1,
the clients are not allowed to send any other PUBLISH message using QoS 1 or QoS 2.
However, there are some design flaws with this design. First, it requires the clients to connect
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
46
==PAGE:48==
using version 5, since the Receive Maximum value was first introduced in this version, and sec-
ond, the clients may time out, since there will be no traffic from the server to the clients.
To solve the first flaw, the tarpit had to support multiple versions, such as version 3.1 and ver-
sion 3.1.1, which doesn’t include the Receive Maximum value, which can be set in the CONACK
response. However, there are still tarpit potential using these versions, since it was still allowed to
send unsolicited PUBLISH messages of any QoS to clients. However, the client was allowed to
make other PUBLISH messages while waiting for the PUBREL response from the tarpit.
To solve the second flaw, the most obvious solution would be for the tarpit to send a ping re-
quest to the client. However, it is only allowed for clients to ping a server, not the other way
around. This holds for all versions of the MQTT specification. Therefore, to check if a client was
still connected to the tarpit, the tarpit would send the PUBREL message to the client. This is done
just before the keep-alive interval, declared by the client in their CONNECT request, is about to
expire. If the client responded with a PUBCOMP, they were still connected, and a new PUBLISH
message with QoS 2 would immediately be sent to the client, starting the tarpitting all over again.
According to all versions of the MQTT specification, the server is permitted to keep the connection
open for up to 1.5 times the keep-alive value without receiving any data from the client. It is the
responsibility of the client to restart this interval, by sending PINGREQ’s to the server. However,
this may not be done by bots in a botnet.
It should also be noted, that any kind of request that requires an acknowledgment response from
the tarpit, such as a SUBSCRIBE request, would not be sent by the tarpit, making the client also
have to wait for an other acknowledgment, in addition to the PUBREL response.
5.4
CoAP
The CoAP tarpit is similar to the UPnP tarpit, where the same idea is to send an unending amount
of data to the client, over several messages. The way this tarpit works, is when receiving a GET
request, it will then initiate a block-wise transfer, in accordance to RFC 7959[53]. It would start
by sending block number 1, all the way up to the maximum block size, which is 220. If the client
is still listening for new blocks, the block numbers would restart. RFC 7959 does not specify what
happens if more blocks are necessary, but if a client has been connected for 220 · blockinterval
seconds, then the tarpit has done its job.
Each block should be confirmed as received by the client before the next one would be sent. If
the client didn’t acknowledge receipt within due time, the last block would be resent. If too many
block has been resent without acknowledgment, the tarpit will assume the client disconnected, and
cancel the connection.

client, until the client stops responding for a specified amonut of times, or until they make a GET
request.
Another idea is to respond to confirmable messages as late as possible, while still being com-

5.6
Countermeasures
Since tarpits fall under the cyber deception umbrella term, it is important that they appear legiti-
mate and avoid detection as tarpits, as being identified as such would diminish their effectiveness.
A tool to detect a tarpit has already been developed, and is called Degreaser[54], which was cre-
ated by Alt. et al. This tool identifies potential tarpits by analyzing network anomalies, focusing
on the TCP window size and the presence (or absence) of TCP options.
To detect abnormal behavior, Degreaser uses a TCP window size threshold of 20 bytes. This
choice was based on captured traffic data showing that approximately 9̃9.7% of TCP connections
use a window size larger than 512 bytes. In contrast, tarpits, such as LaBrea and the Netfilter
TARPIT, use smaller default window sizes, which is 5 and 10 bytes respectively. Although the
Linux Netfilter TARPIT plugin allows its window size to be configured, it is set to 10 bytes by
default.
Another tarpit indicator is the absence of TCP options. Both LaBrea and the Netfilter TARPIT
omit options like timestamps and selective acknowledgments, which are almost always present in
legitimate TCP connections. This lack of options stands out in traffic analysis.
However, Degreaser was designed specifically around the behaviors of LaBrea and the Netfilter
TARPIT. As a result, when scanning the internet, many tarpits fell into a generic ”other” category.
These tarpits were not identified as either LaBrea or the Linux Netfilter TARPIT, but were still
classified as tarpits, due to their small window sizes and lack of TCP options. This means the tool
might misclassify legitimate servers by mistake and miss better disguised tarpits.
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
49
==PAGE:51==
Therefore, in my implementation of my tarpits, we set the TCP receive window to 512, to avoid
being classified as a tarpit, since Degreaser[54] used a small TCP receive window as a classified
for detecting tarpits. The server also did not use a ”stateless” implementation, but used the OS to
handle all traffic, which also caused Degreaser into not classifying it as a tarpit. This is due to how
the OS handles TCP options, since modern operating systems include standard TCP options such
as window scaling, timestamps, and selective acknowledgments in their responses.
6
Implementation
All of the tarpits were implemented in C to achieve high performance with minimal overhead. All
of the TCP-based servers were created in the same way, with the only difference being the ports
used for each specific protocol implementation. A single server creation function was used, han-
dling socket initialization, binding, and listening. This function also configured the server with an
extremely high backlog to handle a surge of incoming requests.
The implementation used the OS to handle all traffic, meaning each connection would take up
a file descriptor. Therefore, to set the number of concurrent clients the server can handle, the file
descriptor limit must also be set. This was also done in all implementations of a tarpit using a TCP
server.
6.1
Base client
Since some implementation of the tarpit requires different information stored from the client, there
is a unique struct for such implementations, such as the struct coapClient for storing specific
data for the coap tarpit. However, some information that has to be stored in the clients are the same
in all implementations, which is why there is a struct baseClient. This struct baseClient
contains information, such as the time connected, and the ip address of the client. However, since
there does not exist classes, and much less super-classes, in C, this inheritance is done with struct
embedding.
Struct embedding is when a struct includes another struct as its first member, allowing the em-
bedded struct to act as a ”base” type. In this way, the embedded struct’s fields take up the first part
of the memory in the enclosing struct, which enables a form of polymorphism. Because of this, a
pointer to the embedded base struct can be safely cast to or from a pointer to the enclosing struct.
6.2
Queue
Both the Telnet and UPnP tarpit use a queue structure to handle when a client should be sent a
message. The queue works under the assumption that all clients are treated the same, meaning that
each client must wait the same amount of time before receiving a message. This is to avoid a client
being at the end of the queue, but is scheduled to be the next client to receive a message. Because
of this, the queue is ordered in a simple way, where the head of the queue is always the next client
to receive a message, and the tail is the last one in line.
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
50
==PAGE:52==
The queue is implemented by having each client point to the next client in queue, and having a
struct queue simply storing the first and last client in the queue (head and tail). That way, the
struct queue will always point to the first and last client in the queue, ensuring a constant lookup
and appending time of O(1).
When a new client is added to the queue, the last client in the queue will point to the newly-added
client, and the struct queue will set the newly-added client as the tail of the queue. Conversely,
when a client is removed from the queue, the head of the queue will become the one the removed
client was pointing to, and the struct queue will set that client as the new head of the queue.
6.3
Telnet
The telnet tarpit uses a single TCP server to handle all traffic. It uses a poll file descriptor to handle
all traffic, where the poll only uses a single pollfd struct to listen for all incoming traffic.
When a client connects to the tarpit, which is registered by the poll, it is assigned a file descrip-
tor. The file descriptor is not added to the poll, since the tarpit does not care about the readiness
for sending or receiving data to/from the client, Instead, poll is used only to monitor the server’s
listening socket for new incoming connections. This means that the tarpit does not track the state
of the client’s NVT. The clients file descriptor is used only to send data to, which is set to non-
blocking mode using O_NONBLOCK. This is important, so a single client can’t block the entire queue.
A struct telnetAndUpnpClient is malloced to store data about the client, such as its file de-
scriptor, which of course embed the baseClient struct as the first field, and is then appended to
the queue.
The program then iterates the queue, where it goes through each client one by one, and send-
ing each of them a random option negotiation option, if the client is scheduled for it. After sending
the negotiation option, the clients are then re-added to the queue, but this time at the end of the
queue.
If sending the negotiation option to the client would be blocking, the client is then immediately
added to the end of the queue, without being sent a negotiation option. Also, if sending the negoti-
ation option to the client would fail, then it is assumed the client has disconnected, and the amount
of time it was connected would be recorded, as well as freeing the malloc’ed data structure and
closing the connection, freeing the file descriptor.
If the head of the queue is not scheduled for a negotiation option yet, that would also mean the
rest of the clients in the queue are not scheduled either, and the iteration of the clients in the queue
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
51
==PAGE:53==
will stop. The time before the client in the head of the queue is scheduled to receive the next ne-
gotiation option is used as the timeout value in poll, which is the maximum amount of time the
program will wait before checking the queue again. This allows the server to efficiently sleep until
it actually needs to send something, instead of constantly looping. If a client connects during this
timeout time, poll will wake up early and handle it accordingly. Otherwise, once the timeout is
reached, the server will wake up, check the queue again, and repeat the process.
This approach keeps the CPU usage low while still maintaining accurate timing for client interac-
tion. The interval between the negotiation options can be configured, as well as the port number
and the maximum number of connected clients.

The way BASHLITE handles option negotiation is rather straightforward. It does not save any

The reason for this is that this is the hardcoded limit each BASHLITE connection is allowed to
stay open. This means the tarpit resulted in the best-case scenario for the BASHLITE botnet.

with the opposite response to reject almost all requests. However, Mirai makes an exception for

WILL and follows up with a hardcoded subnegotiation sequence that sets a fake terminal window
size. This subnegotiation informs the other client that its NVT has 80 columns and 24 rows, which
are typical default dimensions for a standard terminal. By doing this, Mirai becomes more com-
patible with devices that require window size information.

UPnP
Since the tarpit design for UPnP included both an HTTP server, and a server running on port 1900

The SSDP server creates a UDP server, which handles all of the incoming SSDP related traffic,

that would assume the bots are already in the network.
Then, when receiving an M-SEARCH request, it replies back with a legitimate-looking response,

The HTTP server was setup, such that it uses TCP, and was responsible for handling all HTTP-

data. However, the client was not blacklisted, and would be able to try again.
If the HTTP server received a GET request to /hue-device.xml, then the tarpitting would begin.
The struct telnetAndUpnpClient data structure for the client would be initialized, where the
client will first receive a legitimate-looking service description, describing a Phillips Hue device,
which contains a field called ServiceList, which is the list of services the device can offer. Then,
the client would receive a service, called SwitchPower, over and over again. This can be done
by using the Chunked Transfer Coding[13] with the HTTP protocol. Additionally, this does not
violate the XML structure, since there is no limit in the size of the ServiceList field.
This tarpit makes use of the exact same implementation for sending data to clients as the Tel-

poll as the Telnet tarpit.
The ports used in this tarpit can be configured, as well as the delay between sending each SwitchPower

6.5
MQTT
The MQTT tarpit was the most complex tarpit to implement. The reason for this is due to the nature
of the MQTT protocol, which is a publish-subscribe model. Because of this, clients has to estab-
lish a connection, perform a handshake, and then either subscribe to topics or publish messages.
Designing a tarpit for this protocol required implementing enough of the broker’s specifications
without actually implementing a full broker.
The tarpit uses TCP for all of the connections. Also, since each client has to receive specialized
responses based on requests, it is not possible to use the same queuing technique used in the Telnet
and UPnP tarpit, which simply sent the same data over and over again. Because of this, we used
epoll to handle all of the clients, which allows the server to monitor a large number of connec-
tions simultaneously without the overhead of creating separate threads or processes for each client.
epoll makese it possible for the server to react only when there is new activity on a client’s socket,
such as incoming data or readiness to send a response. With epoll, the server can maintain thou-
sands of connections without using a lot of CPU resources, as it avoids the need to repeatedly
scan all the file descriptors, which is done by poll or select. We implemented epoll as level-
triggered, meaning that the server is notified as long as a file descriptor remains in a ready state,
compared to edge-triggered, where the server is only notified once when a file descriptor transi-
tions from not ready to ready.
Level-triggered mode is simpler to implement because the server continues to receive notifications
until all available data is read, therefore avoiding missing any events. However, it may result in
more notifications if many file descriptors remain ready for a long time. However, edge-triggered
mode is more efficient since it requires less notifications, but requires all data to be processed every
Leveraging Tarpits to Mitigate Botnet Propagation through IoT Protocol Simulation
54
==PAGE:56==
time, in order to not miss an event. However, due to the low popularity of the MQTT protocol, as
seen in [29], edge-triggered mode offers no advantage, due to the low amount of traffic, and also
the tarpit design is to make the clients wait for acknowledgments, further lowering the amount of
traffic to handle at once. Because of this, a simpler and safer implementation is preferred, by using
level-triggered mode.
A big challenge during the implementation was supporting multiple MQTT packets sent by the
same client in quick succession. Since TCP is a stream-oriented protocol, data can arrive in chunks,
where a packet may arrive fragmented over multiple reads, or a client may send multiple MQTT
packets in in the same read. To handle this, the server has a fixed-size receive buffer for each client.
Each time data is received, it is appended to the client’s buffer, which consists of 1024 bytes which
size is configurable. If the client fills the buffer, without having sent a complete packet, the client is
disconnected. The server then parses as many complete MQTT packets which are available in the
buffer. It calculates the total length of each packet based on the fixed header and the variable-length
Remaining Length field. If the received amount of bytes is less than that of the calculated amount
of bytes, the tarpit considers the packet as incomplete and waits for more data to arrive before
attempting to process it. Parsed packets are processed according to their type, such as CONNECT,
and SUBSCRIBE, and any leftover bytes, which belongs to incomplete packets, are shifted to the
beginning of the buffer for the next read. If there is a mismatch between the expected amount of

Another consequence of the MQTT protocol being a publish-subscribe model, is that it is nec-

When a client disconnects, its entry in the uthash hash table is removed, the data structured freed,
and the connection closed.

6.6
CoAP
The CoAP tarpit is similar to the MQTT tarpit, but uses a request-response model instead of a
publish-subscribe model, and is also based on UDP instead of TCP. In this tarpit, the same idea for
a queue is used as with the Telnet and UPnP implementation, but since each client has to receive re-
sponses at different intervals, due to the delay with ACK_TIMEOUT and MAX_RETRANSMITS,
the same queue cannot be re-used in this case. Therefore, a priority-queue has to be used instead,
to make sure each client receives their response at the right time, as to not have a client being at
the end of the queue scheduled to be the next client to receive a response.
However, by using a priority-queue instead, the time complexity increases from a constant time
to a logarithmic time, when getting the next client to receive a response. The priority-queue was
implemented by using a min-heap, based on when each client should receive their response.
This tarpit also uses poll the exact same way as with the Telnet and UPnP implementations,
where the only file descriptor in the poll is the tarpit itself, and it sets all the connected clients to
non-blocking.

To simplify deployment of all the tarpits, Prometheus, and Grafana, Docker was used to container-
ize all components of the project. Each tarpit runs in its own dedicated Docker container, alongside
a container for the Prometheus exporter, a container for the Prometheus server, and a container for
Grafana. By having each program run in its own container, the deployment to different envi-

together, such that the programs behave consistently, regardless of the operating system.
Each tarpit is built from a common Docker image that compiles the tarpits first using gcc and

A challenge tarpits in general are faced with is IP-chruning, which is when Internet Service Providers
dynamically reassign public IP addresses for devices. This causes an infected device to appear un-
der a different IP address, while the infection itself persists. However, it is possible to link churned
IP addresses together, using the method described by Griffioen H. et al. in [9]. The way it works
is by observing the TCP port and window size used by Mirai-infected devices, which is constant
throughout the lifetime of the bot. These values can be used to identify a specific bot, and when
activity from a bot disappears and shortly afterwards reappears from a new IP address, but with the
same TCP port and window size, it suggests that the device has undergone IP churn rather. How-
ever, this is only possible for Mirai-derived botnets that use the same scanning technique used by
the Mirai botnet[22] as their building block for their own botnet. This kind of IP-churning is not
taken into account for the Telnet tarpit, which skews with the true number of connections the tarpit
received.
An improvement to the tarpits is to use raw sockets, meaning all packets could be crafted and
controlled manually without relying on the operating system’s TCP/IP stack. This would make
each tarpit able to handle more traffic, not limited to available file descriptors, and make them

8
Conclusion
This project explored the practical value of using tarpits as a defence mechanism against botnets
targeting IoT devices. Through the design and deployment of tarpits simulating IoT protocols,
which were Telnet, UPnP, MQTT, and CoAP, the project demonstrated how these systems can trap
malicious bots, and also sheds light onto the amount of malicious activity targeting these protocols.
The results showed that the tarpits attracted a mix of benign and malicious traffic, with the Tel-
net tarpit receiving the highest amount of traffic, mostly malicious, underlining Telnet’s ongoing
popularity as a target for botnets. The other tarpits traffic was mostly considered benign, but there
were also a significant amount of malicious and suspicious activity targeting these protocols.
The solution was engineered for high performance and scalability using the C programming lan-
guage, to avoid unnecessary resource overhead. To support monitoring and analysis, a custom
Prometheus exporter was developed, providing metrics on client interactions, which were visual-
ized with a Grafana dashboard. Docker was then used to containerize all components, simplifying
deployment, and ensuring consistency across all kinds of environments.
The limitations of the developed tarpits is the fact that they rely on the OS network stack to handle
connections. This limits scalability, as the number of concurrent connections is capped by system
resource limits. Stateless implementations using raw sockets would scale better under high traffic.
This limitation was seen during the DoS attack on the Telnet tarpit.
Future work could explore implementing stateless versions of the tarpits using raw sockets to
increase scalability, or integrating tarpits with intrusion detection systems. Also expanding the
number of protocols, such as IPP, would give a broader coverage of common attack surfaces.
Overall, this work contributes to the field of cybersecurity by demonstrating how tarpits can be
used as a low-cost measure to mitigate botnet threats, particularly against vulnerable IoT devices.
Tarpits offer an alternative to traditional botnet takedowns, as they can disrupt botnets enough,
to the point of making botnets unsustainable, without the challenges associated with traditional
botnet takedowns. Future work could focus on improving the scalability of the tarpits, such as
incorporating raw sockets or by integrating the tarpits with other cybersecurity measures.

Appendix
Botnet Communication and C2 Mechanisms
The way that botnets communicate with their C2 infrastructure can be very different. While the
architecture of a botnet defines how control is structured, the communication mechanisms define
how commands, updates, and data are transmitted between the botmaster and the infected devices.
These mechanisms often aim to evade detection and resist takedown efforts.
Fast-flux
This kind of botnet tries to avoid detection by having a domain, which IP address is switched out
to the address of an infected bot very quickly, in order to avoid static IP-based blacklisting[64].
Each bot in the botnet can serve as a proxy, temporarily resolving the domain and relaying requests
to a server known as the mothership. By rapidly rotating which infected machines is used in the
domain’s DNS A records, the attackers ensure that the domain remains available, even if individual
bots are discovered and blocked. The criteria for a bot to be selected as the one for the DNS A
record depends on the bot’s availability and latency. This technique is visualized in figure 34.
The mothership, which hosts the actual malicious content or acts as a centralized server for for
the botnet, communicates with the rotating pool of proxy bots that forward traffic on its behalf.
This use of proxies conceals the mothership’s location and adds a security layer against takedown
efforts.
To further protect the mothership, attackers often place it on servers operated by so-called bul-
letproof hosting providers in countries that do not cooperate with cybercrime investigations. In
some cases, the mothership’s IP address may also change periodically, or it may only be brought
online temporarily to issue commands or collect stolen data.
There are two kinds of fast-flux, namely single-flux and double-flux. Single-flux only changes
the DNS A records as previously described, while double-flux also changes the DNS NS records,
which is responsible for storing the authoritative name server.
A well-known example of a fast-flux botnet was the Storm Worm. The way it worked was by
using single-flux with a P2P infrastructure, which also used the Kademilie DHT architecture, like
the Hajime botnet[65][66]. The Storm botnet used spam, usually with e-mail, to infect new de-
vices, by utilizing real breaking news to lure victims to click a malicious link. These malicious
links were domains that the botnet owner controlled, which would use the bots to redirect traffic
to the mothership, hosting the actual malware. After doing so, the device would get infected by
the malware, and become one of the bots itself.
The way the Storm botnet used the DHT architecture, was by using the public OVERNET net-

work, a Kademlia-based DHT, which was used by the eDonkey2000 file-sharing system. So just
like the Hajime botnet, the Storm botnet also ”infiltrated” another public DHT. However, in 2007,
the botnet transitioned to a private DHT implementation known as Stormnet. Stormnet was struc-
turally identical to OVERNET but with all communications XOR-encrypted using a fixed 40-byte
key.
The way the Storm botmaster distributed commands to the bots, without being detected, was very
similar to a ”treasure hunt”, where the bots have to find the instructions from the botnet owner,
hidden at a specific place. Each day, bots would compute a set of 32 keys using the current date
and a random value from 0 to 31. The botmaster, knowing the same KDF, could precompute these
keys in advance and use them as predictable rendezvous points. The botmaster would then publish
instructions, which could be deciphered to an ip-address and port number, at the key destination
in the DHT. This destination is a bot in the DHT network. The bots in the DHT will then connect
to the bot that is hosting the current days key, to obtain the ip-address and port-number, which is
another node in the DHT network, called the control node. This is where the bots will actually
receive the command that is issued by the botmaster.
When the bots connect to the control node, the control node initiates a simple authentication pro-
cess by sending a 4-byte challenge. The bots uses a hardcoded XOR key to compute the response
and verify its identity. If authenticated, the control node delivers a compressed command payload,
typically containing spam templates, email target lists, or DDoS instructions.
Today, the Storm network is no longer active, partly due to the program StormFucker, developed
by Wicherski G. et al., which disrupts the P2P Storm botnet[67]. The researchers of StormFucker
reverse-engineered how the peer discovery and communication protocols work, allowing them to
impersonate a Storm bot, and distributing false routing information into the network, while also
disinfecting bots. However, this has some ethical issues, since this will also affect users devices
without consent, just like malware.
However, the use of fast-flux techniques are still very relevant in 2025, as FBI has issued a state-
ment describing its use in C2 infrastructure, especially in relation to botnets[68].
Public platforms
Instead of using a private server for issuing commands, some botnets have instead opted for a more
simpler approach, by utilizing Twitter as their choice of operations for their C2 server.
One example of this is the Flashback botnet. Flashback initially used traditional control servers
to manage its network of infected machines, but later versions implemented a fallback mechanism
that relied on Twitter. If the bot could not reach the primary servers, it would dynamically gen-
erate a hashtag based on the current date and then search Twitter for tweets containing that tag.

DGA
Some botnets also use DGA as their C2 architecture. The way it works is that each bot periodically
runs a DGA to produce a set of domain names. The bot attempts to resolve each domain name,
and upon finding one that leads to a responsive and correctly behaving server, it connects to it and
now has a connection to the C2 server.

An example of a DGA-based botnet was the botnet Torpig. In Torpigs setup, bots generate a deter-
ministic sequence of domain names, based on inputs like the current date. The bot cycles through
them until it receives a valid C2 response. The DGA in Torpig is fully deterministic, meaning that
once the seed is known, all bots will generate the same list of domains in the same order.
This has the extremely big vulnerability, that it is not guaranteed that the botnet master is the
owner of the generated domain, which is exactly what happened for the Torpig botnet[71]. The
researchers managed to hijack the botnet for 10 full days, completely locking out the botnet owner,
until the bots changed domain again.
However, this technique is still used today. The Vo1d botnet uses a DGA-based C2 architecture
which is highly resilient, since it uses a mix of hardcoded and DGA-generated domains[72]. It
uses RSA encryption to prevent hijacking, even if the DGA domains gets ”stolen”.
IRC
Some botnets have used the IRC protocol, which was initially developed for chat applications, as
their choice for C2 communication. IRC is made to allow clients to communicate with a server,
which is responsible for delivering the messages to other clients and servers. The messages can
either be sent to a group of people, using a channel, or a single person. The channels can be
password-protected. IRC also allows transfer of files and not purely text messages.
The IRC protocol was used in the Phatbot botnet, one of the earlier examples of IRC-based mal-
ware. Phatbot connected infected machines to an IRC server, where they would idle in a designated
channel awaiting commands from a botmaster[73].
The use of IRC botnets have heavily declined and is now seen as an outdated botnet architec-
ture, and there doesn’t exist a modern version of a botnet that uses IRC in its architecture[74]. The
reason for this is that this kind of traffic was easily detectable for an IDS and was then blocked by
a firewall.
HTTP
The HTTP protocol has been used by botnets as a C2 communication channel due to its ability to
blend into normal web traffic. Since HTTP traffic is almost always allowed through firewalls and
proxies, HTTP-based C2 channels can disguise botnet activity as legitimate activity.
However, HTTP is a request-response protocol, so it is not well-suited for group communica-
tion. There is an increased overhead, since every bot must actively request commands from the C2
server, leading to higher latency, especially when compared to protocols like IRC, which allow for
real-time broadcast communication for multiple clients.
Also, since HTTP was designed for a centralized architecture, if it is used in a P2P architecture, it

An example of an HTTP-based botnet is the Sality botnet[75][76]. Sality began as a centralized
network where bots retrieved commands from hardcoded HTTP URLs. This had the disadvan-
tage of having a single point of failure, and therefore later versions of Sality adopted a hybrid P2P
architecture on top of HTTP. In this design, each bot functioned as both an HTTP client and server.
Peer discovery in Sality was done with embedded bootstrap peer lists which was in each infected
binary. Communication within the botnet occurred over both UDP and TCP, with HTTP used as
the application-layer transport. Communication within the botnet occurred over both UDP and
TCP, with HTTP used as the application-layer transport. To avoid flooding the network with the
same pack, each pack had its own ID which allowed the bots to discard duplicate packs. It also
used peer scoring to prioritize reliable nodes and cryptographic signatures to filter out invalid data,
ensuring that only new and verified information was used.
Due to this design, the Sality botnet is resilient enough to still be operational to this day.
SMB
Another protocol used by botnets as a way to hide their C2 activity, is the SMB protocol. Like
HTTP, SMB is a request-response protocol, used for file and printer sharing in home and enterprise
networks. A client must first authenticate, either with credentials or anonymously, before it can
access resources.

SMB also supports named pipes, which enable IPC across networked systems. This feature can be
abused with malicious communication and command execution across compromised hosts. This
can happen with passive infection, where a machine shares a writeable file, a bot can inject mali-
cious code into that file, and when opened on another PC, that system also becomes infected[75].
An example of a SMB botnet is the Duqo 2.0 botnet. The way it works is by installing malware
on a gateway, that sits between the public network and the internal network. When the gateway
received a specially crafted trigger, such as a password embedded in incoming network traffic, it
would store the IP address of the sender and silently activate a redirection rule.
The way it works is when it receives a special password, the gateway will save the ip-address
of where the password came from, and secretly forward all traffic from port 443 (HTTPS) to port
445 (SMB) of that ip address[77]. This secret redirection enabled external attackers to communi-
cate with SMB services inside the internal network.
Once this redirection is active, the attacker can interact with SMB services inside the network.
This allows them to use SMB named pipes for IPC, send and receive commands, or move laterally
across machines.
Blockchain
Since the rise of the blockchain technology the past years, it has also found its way into botnets.
Several studies have explored the use of blockchain technology for C2 communication. While
they differ in how they embed and distribute instructions, they all share the property of using the
blockchain’s decentralized nature.
ZombieCoin[78] is a conceptual botnet that uses the Bitcoin blockchain as its blockchain. In this
design, bots monitor the blockchain for transactions sent to specially crafted Bitcoin addresses,
where each address symbolizes a single command. The botmaster creates these addresses where a
command is XORed with a shared secret and then hashed into a valid address format. By sending
a zero-value transaction to such an address, the botmaster signals the presence of a new command.
Bots that observe a transaction to one of these special addresses can extract the command by re-
versing the hash and XOR operations using their shared secret.
Because commands are hidden in the destination address, the transactions are indistinguishable
from normal ones, making the method stealthy and low-cost. However, if a bot is reverse-engineered
and the shared secret exposed, attackers could forge or recover commands. The system also lacks
encryption or authentication, leaving it vulnerable if the secret is compromised.
ChainChannels[79] is also a conceptual botnet, that takes a more secret approach. Instead of plac-
ing commands in visible blockchain fields, it hides them inside the cryptographic signatures used

Unblockable Chains[80] is also a conceptual botnet, that takes the concept even further by us-

A real-world example of a botnet that uses a blockchain as part of it’s C2 infrastructure, is the
Glupteba botnet[81]. The way it works, is that the botnet utilizes the OP_Return field, that is
available in Bitcoin transactions, where one can put arbitrary text.
When Glupteba infects a machine, it uses hard-coded C2 domain names. However, if those do-
mains become unreachable, it uses the Bitcoin blockchain as a fallback mechanism. The malware
monitors transactions from a set of specific Bitcoin addresses, controlled by its operators. These
transactions include data embedded in the OP_RETURN field. The embedded data is encrypted with
AES, and once decrypted by the bots, reveals an updated C2 domain.
