Abstract
2023 was the warmest year on record and the future holds still increasing temperatures accord-
ing to the UN’s Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change. Climate change and increasing
temperatures can affect the energy demand and comfort of our buildings. Current Danish build-
ing practice focuses on minimizing the heating demand, and Danish housings therefore might
be poorly prepared to accommodate potentially increasing cooling demands in a future warmer
climate. National surveys show that people living in multi-storey buildings more frequently ex-
perience problems with the indoor environment, including overheating, than residents of other
housing types. Furthermore, the projected housing demand growth for apartments is signifi-
cantly more rapid than for other housing types in Denmark.
This master’s thesis applied climate projected weather data to building performance analyses
of Danish multi-storey apartment buildings with the aim of determining the projected cooling
demand in future climate scenarios. Representative building models were constructed with
detailed information of each of the 10 common typologies defined in the TABULA database.
The models were subject to dynamic energy simulations in IDA ICE which determined the con-
sequential cooling demands and the development of these towards mid- and end-of-century
following different projected weather files. Furthermore, a list of 20 different parameters influ-
ential to the cooling demand was compiled. For each parameter, a range was defined based
on common occurrence of parameter values. The cooling demand influence of each parameter
was investigated with both current and projected weather data.
Examinations in this study found that the largest influence on the cooling demand occurred
among parameters related to the windows, shading and ventilation. Analyses uncovered signif-
icant differences among projected weather files which ultimately led to fundamentally different
projections of the cooling demand. The assessed weather files agreed on increasing tempera-
tures, although of different magnitude, but projected opposite developments of the solar irradi-
ation. Scenarios with increasing irradiation led to drastically increasing cooling demands, while
scenarios with decreasing irradiation led to slightly decreasing cooling demands. Thus, the
findings of this master’s thesis highlighted that the assessment of the future risk of increasing
cooling demands and overheating had a decisive dependency on the choice of projection.

Introduction
Historical observations show, that in the last 150 years, the average outdoor temperature in
Denmark has increased by 1.5 °C.[25], and the future holds still increasing global tempera-
tures according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). Their 6th and latest
assessment report (AR6) released in the spring 2023 presents 5 emission scenarios and the
consequential change in global surface temperatures, as presented in Figure 1.1 [16].
Figure 1.1: Changes in global surface temperature for IPCC’s AR6 emission scenarios [16]
While the different emission scenarios present great differences in the resulting temperature
change towards the end of the century, they do not vary in the conclusion that the future climate
will be warmer than that of recent times [16]. The most optimistic of the 5 scenarios, and the only
scenario which meets the Paris Agreement’s goal of keeping the global temperature increase
below 1.5 °C above preindustrial levels, SSP1-1.9, predicts a temperature change around 1.4
°C towards 2100, while the least optimistic scenario, SSP5-8.5, predicts a temperature change
around 4.4 °C towards 2100.[35]
Climate change and increasing temperatures impose the relevancy of assessing the potential
impacts that may be caused on the energy demand and comfort in our buildings and perhaps
review current practices. In that relation, the Danish Minister for Social Affairs and Housing,
Pernille Rosenkratz-Theil, was asked the question in The Danish Parliament on August 31st
2023: ”Does the Minister agree that new buildings in Denmark are built with too great a risk
of overheating and the need for cooling?” The Minister replied, that she does not agree based
on the reasoning that ”the accuracy of the climate projections over the many-year lifespan of
buildings is subject to too great uncertainties to demand that they be used for dimensioning”
[15].

Current Danish practice for assessments of indoor thermal comfort in buildings makes use of
historic weather data measured from 2001-2010 assembled in a one-year weather file repre-
senting a typical year in that period [32][38].
Danish buildings might be poorly prepared for a warmer climate, as the current Danish building
practice focuses on minimizing the heating demand. High levels of insulation and large window
sections, which provide daylight as well as solar heat gains, contribute to minimizing the heating
usage during winter. This can however, have the opposite effect in summertime, and thereby
impose a risk of overheating [2].
Furthermore, a recent study from the University of Oxford concludes that Denmark among other
Northern European countries are placed in the top 10 of countries that face the biggest relative
change in cooling degree days (CDD) in case of a global temperature rise at 2.0°C instead of
1.5°C [26].
Already under current climate conditions Danish residents experience issues with overheating.
A nationwide survey, by Videncentret Bolius and Realdania, on life quality and satisfaction in
Denmark 2023, shows that people living in multi-storey buildings more frequently experience
problems with the indoor environment than residents of other housing types. Overheating is no
exception to this.
Figure 1.2: Percentage of residents experiencing issues with ”heat / too high temperatures”
[36]
Regardless of housing type, as seen in Figure 1.2, the percentage of people dissatisfied due to
overheating is greatest for newly built buildings. While residents in buildings built after 2000 in
general experience less issues with the indoor environment compared to residents in buildings
built before 2000, this is not the case when solely looking at the issue of overheating. For
new multi-storey buildings too high temperatures are placed among the most frequent indoor
environment issues.
Furthermore, multi-storey apartment buildings is the housing type in Denmark, according to
national projections, with the significantly most rapid growth in housing demand over the next

20 years [18].
This study will apply climate projected weather data to building performance analyses of Danish
multi-storey apartment buildings, with the aim of determining the projected cooling demand in
future climate scenarios.
1.1
Objectives
The purpose of this study is to investigate the risk of increasing cooling demands and overheat-
ing in Danish multi-storey apartment buildings in future climate change scenarios. To support
and accommodate this purpose the following objectives are defined:
1. Identify the change in cooling demand from current to future climate scenarios in multi-
storey apartment buildings in Denmark.
(a) What is the magnitude of cooling demand changes, when applying climate projected
weather files?
(b) Which building typologies face the most critical changes?
2. Analyze the influence of relevant building parameters on cooling demand in Danish multi-
storey apartment buildings.
(a) What are the impacts of relevant parameter variations on cooling demand?
(b) Which measures can be implemented in the building design to mitigate potential risks
of increasing cooling demand and overheating?
3. Investigate and compare current weather data with future climate projections.
(a) What is the variability in emission scenarios and consequential climate projection?
(b) How do climate projected weather files compare to each other, to measured data,
and to the currently used DRY_2001-2010 weather file?

2
Climate change - past, present and future
”Human activities, principally through emissions of greenhouse gases, have unequivocally
caused global warming, with global surface temperature reaching 1.1°C above 1850–1900 in
2011– 2020” [24]. The United Nations’ (UN) body for assessing the science related to climate
change, IPCC, identify ”unsustainable energy use, land use and land-use change, lifestyles
and patterns of consumption and production” among the causes of increased emissions. These
emissions lead to changes in the behavior of the earth, not only in terms of rising temperatures,
but also increasing sea levels, changes in ecosystems, and more extreme weather patterns
[24].
To combat the crisis and reduce the dangerous risks related to climate change the Paris Agree-
ment was founded in 2015, and adopted by 196 parties at COP21 in Paris, with the overarching
goal to hold ”the increase in the global average temperature well below 2°C above pre-industrial
levels” and pursue efforts “to limit the temperature increase to 1.5°C above pre-industrial lev-
els.” On the basis of assessments from the IPCC, the need to limit global warming to 1.5°C by
2100 has been stressed by world leaders [35].
2.1
IPCC emission scenarios
The IPCC have since the release of their 1st assessment report (AR1) in 1990 been develop-
ing emission scenarios predicting the future climate. In 2023, the 6th and latest assessment
report (AR6) was released, presenting 5 different emission scenarios representative of differ-
ent courses for the global society, so-called ”Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP)” [21].
The resulting global surface temperature changes show great variance between emission sce-
narios, however, only 1 of the 5 scenarios will limit the temperature increase to 1.5°C above
pre-industrial levels in 2100, see Figure 1.1. Thus, it seems plausible, that the global average
temperature in 2100 could exceed the 1.5°C-goal.
The IPCC emission scenarios will be further elaborated in Section 6.
2.2
The future of cooling
According to recent research, the prospect of limiting the global mean temperature rise to 1.5°C
is ”increasingly out of reach” [26]. The study identifies impacts on global and regional cooling
demand, should the global warming move from a 1.5°C increase to a 2°C increase. The study
found, that African countries will experience the highest absolute increases in cooling demand.
Meanwhile, Denmark finds themselves in the top 10 of countries with the highest relative in-
creases in cooling demand along with other countries traditionally unprepared for heat, such as
Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and Norway [26].

Furthermore, cooling is the fastest growing use of energy in buildings, according to the In-
ternational Energy Agency (IEA) [20]. The IEA claims that ”without action to address energy
efficiency, energy demand for space cooling will more than triple by 2050 – consuming as much
electricity as all of China and India today” [20].
Thus, the need for efforts in limiting the future cooling demand of buildings seems obvious.
3
Regulations and standards
New buildings in Denmark are legally required to follow the Danish Building Regulations 2018,
BR18. Since early building laws to the first version of the Danish Building Regulations in 1961,
and up until the current BR18, regulations and standard documentation for the thermal comfort
in the indoor environment and energy consumption for residential buildings have developed and
grown in extent.
3.1
Historic requirements to thermal transmittance
A closer look at the development of requirements for the thermal transmittance of different build-
ing components since BR61 sheds a light on a focus in Danish building practice of keeping
buildings warm. The demands to meet for the thermal performance of building components
have become higher throughout the last 60 years to minimize heat losses and lower both cost
and energy consumption in regards of heating.
Figure 3.1: Historical minimum requirements for thermal transmittance in The Danish Building
Regulations (NB: Left axis applies for exterior walls, floor slabs, and roofs. Right axis applies
for windows.) [32]
The Danish Building Regulations have become more strict regarding minimum requirements for
the thermal transmittance of building components the last 60 years. From Figure 3.1 a general
tendency for the minimum requirement of U-values appears, and they have had a decreas-
ing development since 1961. Exterior walls have faced a significant difference from BR72 to

throughout the Danish Building Regulations.
Furthermore, the requirements for windows have faced significant changes from 1961 to 2018.
BR10 replaced the minimum U-value requirement for windows with a requirement for the energy

to the respective building code.

period is, however, not evaluated, and the possibility of overheating is not considered by the
calculation of Eref.
3.2
Energy consumption
The BR18 prescribes that buildings must be designed to avoid unnecessary energy consump-
tion regarding heating, cooling, hot water and ventilation [32]. In Danish residential buildings
mechanical cooling is uncommon, and thus natural ventilation is the preferred solution to fulfill
any needs of cooling [6].
All new buildings in Denmark have to comply with the requirements for the energy performance
framework described in BR18 §250-298. The energy performance framework for residential
buildings is the calculated energy demand of the building, and includes the total primary energy
used for heating, cooling, ventilation and hot water. The total energy demand for residential
buildings must not exceed 30.0 kWh/m2 + 1000 kWh / A per year. Low-energy buildings are
required to comply with a of maximum 27.0 kWh/m2 per year [32].

Weather data and climate projections
In accordance with BR18 §386, documentation of thermal comfort must be based on either sim-
ulations of critical rooms or simplified calculations cf. SBi 213 (applies for residential buildings)
with use of weather file DRY 2013, for calendar year 2010 [32]. The DRY 2013 weather file is
based on measurements from the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) in the period of 2001-
2010, with assembled data from 12 typical months in the period. Thus, the data set represents a
typical year’s climate in the period from 2001-2010 in Denmark. The data set cannot be used for
simulations, that wish to account for variability in the general meteorological conditions [38][27].
In other words, buildings built today to last the next 50-100 years are - in terms of thermal
comfort - built to endure a climate representing a period ranging more than 20 years back from
construction date. Should the weather in the mid- and late 21st century deviate from that of
the early 21st century, this could cause the buildings to be insufficiently equipped to ensure
reasonable thermal comfort.
As per July 1 2023 BR18 has introduced the guideline that the effect of climate change on indoor
thermal comfort can be assessed based on simulations with use of climate projected weather
files TMY 2050s and TMY 2090s. These contain projections of the climate for the 2050’s and
2090’s following the IPCC’s RCP8.5 emission scenario [32].
6.1
Prediction of future climate
The future climate is both largely unknown and uncertain. The United Nations’ IPCC have since
the release of their 1st assessment report (AR1) in 1990 until the 6th and latest report (AR6) in
2023 developed and released emission scenarios predicting the future climate. The scenarios
are based on different emission patterns of greenhouse gases which vary due to different pro-
jections of global population, technology development, policy making etc. An introduction to the
emission scenarios developed in the 3 latest assessment reports is presented in the following:
6.1.1
IPCC's 4th Assessment Report, AR4 - 2007
AR4 from 2007 presents 6 different emission scenarios distributed on 4 different scenario fam-
ilies (A1, A2, B1, and B2). A1 is divided in 3 different scenarios (A1FI, A1T, and A1B). The A2
scenario represents a pathway of a high population growth with slow development in economic
and technological sectors. Overall, the scenarios are referred to as the SRES scenarios [22].
6.1.2
IPCC's 5th Assessment Report, AR5 - 2014
The RCP8.5 is one of 4 emission scenarios from the IPCC’s 5th assessment report (AR5) re-
leased in 2014. The 4 emission scenarios, RCP2.6, RCP4.5, RCP6.0, and RCP8.5 represent
different projections of radiative forcing based on different Representative Concentration Path-
ways (RCPs). In example, RCP8.5 is corresponding to a radiative forcing of 8.5 W/m2 in 2100,
and represents a ”business-as-usual”-scenario with increasing emissions [23].

The energy performance of each typology, was under the application of different weather files,
assessed with the use of IDA ICE 5.0 Beta. IDA ICE is a dynamic multi-zone simulation ap-
plication for the study of thermal indoor climate and energy consumption [13]. This allows for
detailed energy assessments as well as the possibility of investigating overheating.
The purpose of the typology study was to assess cooling demands of each typology and to iden-
tify changes in the cooling demand in different future climate scenarios reflected by the different
projected weather files. Furthermore, changes in the total energy balance were assessed and
analysed to investigate the contributors to the heating and cooling demand respectively.
For each typology a building model was constructed in IDA ICE, each with its own set of per-
formative properties.
The modelled geometry consisted of 32 zones on four levels and intended to represent common
zones of Danish multi-storey apartment buildings. Each zone was 5 m x 5 m with a room height
of 2.5 m. The zones were equally distributed in all orientations. A 3D illustration of the basic
geometry is presented in Figure 8.1:

The basic geometry of the assessed zones was identical across all typologies, as TABULA
do not provide information on neither typical geometry data for average buildings nor actual
geometry data for example buildings. Furthermore, the basis of the model aimed to be broadly
representing different orientations and levels.
Zone parts without adjacent zones or walls are in IDA ICE by default treated as adiabatic zones
[13], therefore adjacent zones (the grey ones) were modelled to simulate realistic heat trans-
fers between the primary zones (the blue ones). Please note, that the adjacent zones are not
included in the results of this study.
One window was placed in each exterior wall of the zones to represent a window-to-wall ratio
related to the typology and period. The applied window percentages are window-to-wall ratios,
as presented in Figure 7.3, which caused corner zones to have double the window area as zones
with only one exterior wall. This was chosen to allow for variations in the represented window
areas within zones. The distribution of window area on every facade in the four orientations
was omitted in this study, as these are assumed to represent only example buildings, and not
a general distribution for a certain typology. Thus, the total window-to-wall ratio was applied
identically for all facade orientations.

8.1
Internal gains
Internal gains from occupants, equipment and lighting were combined 5 W/m2 in accordance
with SBi 213 [30]. Furthermore, internal gains in residential buildings are assumed to be always
in use (or present), cf. SBi 213 [30].
8.2
Infiltration rate
Additional air change rates from infiltration was defined from data provided by TABULA. For
typologies constructed up until 1978 the infiltration was 3 h−1 at 50 Pa and 1 h−1 at 50 Pa for
typologies from 1979 and later [11].
8.3
Heating and cooling setpoints
Heating and cooling setpoints for the models followed the instructions described in SBi 213,
in which the heating set point is defined at 20°C and the cooling set point at 25°C [30]. The
models were equipped with ideal cooling and heating units, which are units used to condition
zones without detailing the systems. It means that there is no limiting capacity of the units. In
addition, the heating and cooling demands could easily be determined from the energy usage
of ideal heating and cooling units.

==PAGE:40==
8.4
Window opening control
In accordance with SBi 213, window openings were modelled with a temperature controller
with a set point at 23 °C [30]. It means that windows opened when the operative temperature
exceeded 23 °C and closed again when the temperature went below 23 °C. Windows were
modelled with an effective window opening area of 60%, corresponding to a typical side-hung
window cf. SBi 213 [30].
8.5
Ventilation
In addition to the natural ventilation from the window openings and infiltration, models were
constructed with a CAV ventilation system to accommodate minimum ventilation rates for res-
idential buildings. A constant air change rate of 0.4 h−1 corresponding to 0.28 L/(s ·m2), cf.
values from TABULA, was supplied to each zone [11].
8.6
Wind profile
From Section 4.3, projections indicate increasing population growth in the largest cities of Den-
mark. Therefore, and since the focus is on multi-storey apartment buildings, the models for the
typology study were constructed with a wind profile (ASHRAE 1993) representing an area of
City Center.
8.7
Simulation period and weather conditions
The simulations were carried out for an entire year in Denmark under the application of the
weather file DRY_2001-2010 and the projected weather files A2_2020, A2_2050, A2_2080,
TMY_2050s and TMY_2090s.
9
Parameter study
A study of selected parameters’ influence on the cooling demand was conducted with the the
purpose determining the magnitude of influence and identify measures to implement in the
building design. The parameter study was assessed through simulations.
Furthermore, the influence of the parameters was investigated with the application of the
weather file DRY_2001-2010 as well as the projected weather files A2_2080 and TMY_2090s
representing end-of-century climate scenarios. The end-of-century weather files were chosen,
as they represent the most extreme climate change for each of the A2 and TMY scenarios and
would therefore highlight the most critical parameter influences.
In addition, the findings from the parameter study were used to make a selection of realistic
measures to minimize the cooling demand for a multi-storey apartment building. The measures
were applied to the 1973-1978 typology to serve as an example of the application of the findings.

the implementation of external blinds had little impact with TMY_2090s, and this measure may
therefore not be a reasonable implementation, should TMY_2090s be the preferred climate
projection.

PART V
Discussion
==PAGE:69==
13
Discussion
13.1
Weather data and cooling demand
Future climate scenarios are many in number. The scenarios represented by the weather files
included in this study, must in this context, be considered few. The weather files used for
future cooling demand assessments in this study represent projections following the A2 and
RCP8.5 scenarios from the 4th and 5th Assessment Report from IPCC. Thus, these climate
projections origin from 2007 and 2014. The A2 scenario is but one of 6 emission scenarios
from AR4, while RCP8.5 is but one of 4 emission scenarios from AR5. Furthermore, the IPCC’s
6th and latest Assessment Report released in 2023 includes 5 SSP scenarios, of which none
were represented in the assortment of available projected weather files included in this study.
Considering the different results following simulations with A2 (A2-projected) and TMY (RCP8.5-
projected) weather files, it must be considered a possibility, or perhaps rather be expected,
that application of weather files representing other emission scenarios would lead to yet again
different results.
Analyses of the weather data showed significant discrepancies among the projected weather
files. Both A2 and TMY weather files agreed on rising temperatures, although of different mag-
nitudes. In terms of solar irradiation however, the development was opposite as A2 weather files
had increasing irradiation while TMY weather files had decreasing irradiation. The difference
in weather projections ultimately led to fundamentally different predictions of the future cooling
demand.
Thus, the results showed great dependency on the choice of projected weather file, and the
validity of the weather files is therefore of great importance. In this study, the validity of neither
any weather data basis nor projection method was evaluated. Merely, the data content and the
results of applying the data were analyzed.
Data analyses found that the GHI in neither of the projected weather files for the end-of-century
period fell within the RCP8.5-projected national range from DMI. GHI was however, not a param-
eter considered by simulations in IDA ICE. Projections of DNI and DIF, irradiance parameters
considered by IDA ICE, were not available from DMI Climate Atlas to allow for comparisons.
Future works could advantageously further explore the contents of the weather files included in
this study as well as other projected weather files if possible. Future works are suggested to, if
means be, seek to conclude whether current available projected weather files are aligned with
national climate projections.
Analyses show a lack of consensus among the projected weather files, and thereby empha-
size the uncertainties of the future climate conditions, which might invoke a reluctance towards
applying projected weather data. However, it must be considered, that the continued use of

DRY_2001-2010 implies the indirect assumption that the climate conditions to come are alike
the climate conditions that have been, which likewise is not without uncertainty. The uncertain-
ties of predicting future climate change must thus be considered in relation to the uncertainties
of predicting no change at all.
The weather files representing mid- and end-of-century do not represent identical time periods.
In example, end-of-century A2 weather file, A2_2080, represents a period from 2071-2100,
while TMY_2090s represents a period from 2090-2099. The differences in the projected cooling
demands for mid- and end-of-century among A2 and TMY weather files is not considered to be
attributable to these differences in represented time periods. The results do however show
significant differences from mid- to end-of-century for each of the projections, A2 and TMY. The
time horizon is thus important to consider depending on the intended purpose of analyses with
projected weather data.
Analyses in this study focused on the influence of climate change on energy demand and ther-
mal comfort. However, the emission scenarios from IPCC project substantial changes in the
atmosphere’s CO2 concentration. In example, the RCP8.5 emission scenario projects an atmo-
spheric CO2 concentration of 1000-1370 ppm in 2100, in contrast to the current level around 430
ppm [23]. Therefore, future works could analyse the influence of increasing CO2 concentration
on atmospheric indoor environment, which is especially relevant for commercial buildings.
13.2
Application of results
This study investigated the energy performance of multi-storey apartment buildings related to
the need for cooling and risk of overheating in future scenarios. Nevertheless, the overall ten-
dencies of the different scenarios identified in this study are considered to be indicative for other
building types.
The most influential building characteristics related to the cooling demand are not considered
distinctive for multi-storey apartment buildings, why similar overall tendencies from applying the
projected weather files to detached housings would be expected.
Commercial buildings are typically distinguished from residential buildings on certain parame-
ters proven influential for the cooling demand, such as the window area. Therefore, the magni-
tude of change would presumably differ, although similar overall tendencies would be expected.
Future works could however, seek to test these expectations through analyses of other resi-
dential housing types and commercial buildings.
13.3
Building typologies
This study made use of the building typologies defined in TABULA, which contain building char-
acteristics and data for Danish multi-storey apartment buildings. Only a few other Danish build-
ing typology databases exist (e.g. Bygningsguiden [10]) but only TABULA has separate char-
acteristics for multi-storey apartment buildings.
The TABULA project ran from 2007 to 2013, and the latest typology in the Danish database is
58

2011-. Since the completion of the TABULA project, two new versions of The Danish Building
Regulations have been published. This means that information in typology 2011- might not be
representative for buildings constructed in years from 2011 to 2024, although assumed so in
this study.
Furthermore, TABULA provides information about Average buildings and Example buildings,
where the latter were used for the modelling in this study. The comparison of U-values showed
little to no difference across the typologies in the Average buildings, the Example buildings
provided larger variations. Thus, results in this study represent the variation of the Example
buildings, and the result variation would presumably be less, had Average buildings data been
applied.
13.4
Modelling
Analyses of the typologies’ energy performance included modelled zones equally distributed on
all orientations. These results thereby estimated the cooling demand on a building level, rather
than for critical zones, but did highlight differences among the typologies. The cooling demand
change per area in critical zones must be assumed to exceed that of the total building.
Modelling for the parameter study, on the other hand, consisted of a single south facing zone.
This zone was modelled with properties, as far as possible, matching the weighted average
properties of the different typologies. Thus, the projected cooling demands of a typical critical
zone is best read from results in the parameter study.

PART VI
Conclusion
==PAGE:73==
14
Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to investigate the risk of increasing cooling demands and over-
heating in Danish multi-storey apartment buildings in climate change scenarios.
To support and accommodate this purpose dynamic energy simulations of Danish multi-storey
apartment buildings were performed with application of both current and climate projected
weather files. Results showed fundamentally different developments of the projected cooling
demands following A2 weather files versus TMY weather files. Following A2 weather files, the
cooling demand was projected to be approximately three times larger in mid-century than with
DRY_2001-2010 and more than five times larger in end-of-century. On the contrary, results
with TMY weather files projected a slight decrease in the future cooling demand.
Representative building models were constructed with detailed information of each of the 10
common typologies defined in the TABULA database. Results showed that a large window area
was characteristic of the typologies with the highest cooling demand, while a low window-to-wall
ratio or a low g-value was characteristic of the typologies with the lowest cooling demand.
In addition, examinations of parameters identified that the window area, mechanical ventilation
rate and the window g-value had the highest influence on the cooling demand. Categorizing the
parameters, the largest influence on the cooling demand occurred among parameters related
to the windows, shading and ventilation.
Investigations of the overheating in a critical zone showed a drastic increase in overheating
hours following the A2 weather file for end-of-century and a slight decrease following TMY com-
pared to baseline results with DRY_2001-2010.
Analyses of the projected weather data showed that A2 and TMY share similarities and con-
currence in projections of temperature with increases of 3.2°C - 4.0°C in yearly mean towards
the end of the century compared to DRY_2001-2010. The assessed weather files project op-
posite developments of the solar irradiation, as the A2 weather files project an increase, while
the TMY weather files project a decrease due to a larger amount of clouds. Neither A2 nor TMY
projections of GHI for end-of-century fall within the national projection range from DMI, with A2
overshooting this and TMY undershooting it.
In conclusion, this study found that the differences in climate projections ultimately led to funda-
mentally different predictions of the future cooling demand. Following A2 the risk of increasing
cooling demands was high, while the risk following TMY was low. Thus, the findings in this
study highlighted that the assessment of the future risk of increasing cooling demands and
overheating had a decisive dependency on the choice of projection.
