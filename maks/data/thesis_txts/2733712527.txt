Abstract
This thesis explores advanced techniques in video object segmentation (VOS), focus­
ing on the integration of state­of­the­art models to enhance segmentation accuracy and
efficiency in video sequences. The primary objective is to merge the strengths of the
Segment Anything Model (SAM) [1] with point tracking techniques, specifically the Seg­
ment Anything Meets Point Tracking (SAM­PT) model [2], and traditional mask­based
VOS methods. This approach aims to combine the interactive click­based capabilities
of SAM with the temporal continuity of mask­based methods, addressing the disparity in
performance between SAM­PT and other leading VOS techniques.
The study begins with a comprehensive review of the current state of VOS, examining the
historical development, advancements in algorithms, and challenges. It then describes
the novel methodology of integrating the SAM model with point tracking and memory
features from traditional VOS methods. Experiments are conducted to reproduce results
from existing models like MIVOS and SAM­PT, followed by exploratory tests with modified
versions of these models.
Despite the promising initial training phase, the final models exhibit sub­optimal perfor­
mance. However, the study presents hope on potential areas for improvement. Future
research could explore the integration of clicks with ground truth masks during training,
the use of a mix of accurate and sub­optimal masks, and a possible revision of the code
to rectify any underlying issues.

Introduction
The realm of video object segmentation (VOS) is continually evolving. It aims to achieve
more accuracy and efficiency in identifying and tracking objects through segmentation
within a video sequence. This thesis explores a novel approach in this domain, leveraging
the strengths of advanced models to enhance segmentation capabilities.
The focus is on the integration of a state­of­the­art segmentation model, SAM (Segment
Anything Model) [1], which is known for its exceptional performance in segmentation
tasks, with techniques specifically built for video applications. SAM is distinguished by its
training on an extensive dataset and excels in segmentation due to its rich representation
capabilities. It has set benchmarks in identifying and isolating objects within still images.
However, it becomes much more complicated when transitioning from static images to
video sequences. This is where SAM­PT (Segment anything meets Point Tracking) [2]
becomes relevant. It extends the capabilities of SAM to apply it to video sequences by
incorporating point tracking models such as PIPS (Persistent independent particles) [3].
The point trackers aim to propagate clicks (points) from a frame to a subsequent one. This
process allows SAM to be prompted with these propagated clicks making SAM consistent
and accurate in tracking objects across frames.
Despite significant advancements in the field, there remains a notable disparity between
SAM­PT and other video object segmentation (VOS) techniques. Leading models in VOS,
such as MIVOS (Modular interactive video object segmentation) [4] inspired from STM
(Video object segmentation using space­time memory networks)[5], mostly rely on uti­
lizing masks from prior predictions to offer information that would improve segmentation
in subsequent video frames. In simpler words, MIVOS is propagating masks while also
holding a memory of the predicted masks. This effective technique doesn’t encapsulate
the capabilities offered by interactive click­based methods. However, when it comes to
performance metrics, SAM­PT falls behind these more traditional VOS models. In fact,
in this work’s experiments, using an oracle model for SAM­PT where the point tracker is
always accurate, still yields worse performances than models like MIVOS. A hypothesis
for this gap is that SAM­PT lacks mask­based information.
This thesis proposes the following approach to address this issue: the merge of traditional
mask­based VOS propagation methods with the advanced capabilities of SAM and point
trackers. In particular, to achieve this proposal, SAM is prompted with features extracted
from the memory of a VOS module. SAM’s prompt encoder is modified to accept features
from the memory, then the prompt encoder and the mask decoder are fine­tuned to effec­
tively integrate and process these features. This integration allows SAM to leverage both
spatial and temporal information, enhancing its ability to track objects accurately in video
sequences. This approach aims to reach strong performances that could out­perform the
ones of SAM­PT.
This work is then divided in four main sections. The first one focuses on related work
to get a general overview of how different state­of­the­art models function, especially the
ones that will be used in this work. The following section describes the method and the
approach used in this study. The next section presents all the experiments and results
that have been sought and obtained throughout this thesis. Finally, the last section is the
general conclusion to close this work and open to future work.

VOS: Video Object Segmentation
Video object segmentation (VOS) aims to produce high quality segmentation of a target
object instance across an input video sequence. Nowadays, it is very common to see the
use of memory in the VOS task. This memeory generally contains previously interacted
frames and/or predicted masks. There are two main type of video object segmentation.
These can be categorized by the type of user input:
Semi­supervised methods require pixel­wise annotation of the first frame of a given query
video.
Interactive VOS methods take user interactions as input in the form of scribles, clicks or
more. Then the user can iteratively refine the results until satisfaction.
Throughout time, these methods have been explored extensively, offering better perform­
ing models by the years. The point of the related work section is to show how video object
segmentation has significantly evolved. It starts with the first use of attention mechanisms
[6] in VOS models. Then involving the use of memory and how to optimize its space. Then
continues with the use of point trackers to support state­of­the art segmentation tools. The
next parts of this section are about studying a selected set of scientific papers relevant to
the thesis’ work.

2.2
STM: Space­Time Memory Networks
STM extends the capabilities of traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by in­
corporating space time memory networks, making them particularly suitable for analyzing
video data.
The paper titled ”Video Object Segmentation using Space­Time Memory Networks” intro­
duces a groundbreaking approach in the field of video object segmentation. The main
concept of the task is to have the ground truth mask of the target object given for the
first frame. The goal is then to estimate the object mask for all other subsequent frames.
The paper shows how to leverage memory networks and learn to read relevant informa­
tion from all available resources. Extending this concept, STM’s proposed deep neural
network (DNN) leverages memory networks for spatio­temporal attention across all video
frames, enhancing pixel classification.
2.2.1
Memory networks
Before diving into the architecture of STM, it is important to understand what memory
networks do in general, since it is the innovative addition to the model.
Memory networks refer to the neural networks that have an external memory that can
be accessed when needed. The idea came from natural language processing (NLP)
research where memorable information is kept separate and embeded as keys (input)
and values (output) feature vectors. Keys are used to address relevant memories whose
corresponding values are returned. In this context, frames with object masks are put in
the memory and a frame that is to be segmented will act as a query. The memory will
then be dynamically updated with the newly predicted masks.
The goal is to have pixel­wise predictions given a set of annotated frame(s) as memory.
Thus each pixel in the query frame needs to access information in the memory frames at
different space­time locations. Conceptually, the memory reading can be considered as
a spatio­temporal attention algorithm because it is computing when­and­where to attend
for each query pixel to decide whether the pixel is on the object or not.
Figure 2.1: Overview of STM. The network has two encoders, one for memory and an­
other for the query frame. It also includes a space­time memory read block and a decoder.
The memory encoder, EncM, processes an RGB frame and an object mask. This object
mask appears as a probability map, using softmax output for estimated object masks.
The query encoder, EncQ, uses the query image as input.

2.2.2
Architecture of STM
At its core, the STM model utilizes a unique combination of memory networks and con­
volutional neural networks (CNNs). The model consists of several key components:
As can be seen in Figure 2.1, the network consists of two encoders each for the memory
and the query frame, a space­time memory read block, and a decoder. The memory en­
coder (EncM) takes an RGB frame and the object mask. The object mask is represented
as a probability map (the softmax output is used for estimated object masks). The query
encoder (EncQ) takes the query image as input.
The query encoder processes the query frame into two feature maps: key and value,
using parallel convolutional layers. These layers reduce the feature size and produce
2D key and value maps. The memory encoder, similar in structure, takes an RGB frame
and an object mask as inputs. The mask is a single­channel probability map. Inputs are
combined and processed to produce 3D key and value maps for each memory frame.
Both encoders use ResNet50 [7] as the backbone, with the memory encoder adapted for
four­channel input. Network weights are initialized from ImageNet, except for new filters
which are randomly initialized.
In the framework, the video frames are processed sequentially starting from the second
frame, using the ground truth annotation from the first frame. As previously mentionned,
all past frames get to be considered as the memory frames and the current frames as the
query frames.
First, both memory and query frames are processed into key and value maps using en­
coders. The key map is for addressing, it computes similarities between the query and
memory frames to determine where to retrieve relevant memory values. The key map is
designed to match visual features. The value map contains detailed information for mask
estimation, like object and boundary details. The query frame’s value map focuses on
detailed appearance for accurate mask decoding, while the memory frame’s value map
includes visual semantics and foreground/background information. These keys and val­
ues are then processed in a space­time memory read block. Here, every pixel in the query
and memory frames is compared across the video’s space­time, using matching scores
to address the memory frame’s value map. The decoder then uses this information to
reconstruct the mask for the query frame.
2.2.3
Performance and Impact
The STM model has demonstrated state­of­the­art performance in benchmark tests, out­
performing existing methods in both speed and accuracy. Its ability to efficiently process
video frames and maintain high segmentation accuracy under various challenges has
made it a pivotal model in the field. However, it has limitations, including its dependence
on the quality of features extracted and potential challenges in scenarios with extremely
complex object movements or environments.

2.3
STCN: Spatial­Temporal Correspondance Networks
As the field of computer vision and video analysis continues to evolve, Spatial­Temporal
Convolutional Networks (STCN) emerge as a pivotal development. This section is dedi­
cated to the review of the STCN paper ”Rethinking Space­Time Networks with Improved
Memory Coverage for Efficient Video Object Segmentation” [8].
STCN is a straightforward method for tracking objects in videos. It connects frames di­
rectly without redoing the mask features for each object. The method is easier, more
efficient, and better than STM [5]. It looks closely at the connection between images, sug­
gesting L2 similarity over dot product for improved memory use. This approach ensures
all memory nodes are used, not just a few. Combining these ideas creates a powerful,
simple technique. It outperforms other methods without extra complexity and is relatively
fast.
2.3.1
Architecture of STCN
In this method, when given a video and its first­frame label, each frame is handled one after
the other, while storing a collection of features. For every new frame, a unique feature is
identified. This feature is then matched with similar features in the stored collection. The
matching process is like the STM technique, using key affinities to find and pull out the
related features from the memory.
Figure 2.2: Network comparison between STM (a) and STCN (b). a: This shows the ba­
sic setup of widely­used Space­Time Memory (STM) networks. It doesn’t include small dif­
ferences. Each object is encoded on its own. Affinities are unique to each object. b: The
Space­Time Correspondence Networks (STCN) presented here are different. Siamese
key encoders compute affinity straight from RGB images. This makes it stronger and
more efficient. Importantly, the query key can be stored and used again as a memory
key. This is not possible in STM, as it does not depend on the mask.
Figure 2.2 shows that STCN, unlike the traditional Space­Time Memory (STM) networks,
employs Siamese key encoders. These encoders directly handle color (RGB) images,
enhancing STCN’s strength and efficiency. A significant difference from STM is in the
encoding process: STCN uses a ResNet50 [7] for its Key Encoder and a ResNet18 for
its Value Encoder. This approach allows STCN to generate key features and connec­
tions independently of the object’s mask, requiring only a single computation per frame,
applicable to both memory and query frames.

2.4
MIVOS: Modular Interactive Video Object Segmentation
MIVOS is a video object segmentation model that uniquely decouples interaction­to­mask
and mask propagation. This decoupling is shown to be simple, effective and generaliz­
able. The paper also proposes a novel lightweight top­k filtering scheme for the attention­
based memory read during propagation. Lastly a novel difference­aware fusion module
to capture the user’s intent.
2.4.1
Architecture and Core Components
MIVOS is composed of three main elements, illustrated in Figure 2.3. MiNET is MIVOS’
network. Each component plays a vital role in the segmentation process:
Figure 2.3: Overview of MiNet. In each interaction round, the user selects a frame and
adjusts the object mask until satisfied. This uses the Scribble­to­Mask (S2M) module,
which works in real time. The improved mask is then spread both ways through the video.
The system combines masks from previous and current rounds using a special fusion
module. This module focuses on changes made by the user during interaction. It uses
these changes to understand the user’s goal. In the first round, all masks start as zeros.
Interaction­to­Mask: This module is responsible for converting user interactions into an
object mask. It allows users to employ various interactive image segmentation methods,
such as scribbles or clicks, to produce an initial mask for the object of interest. There is
also an instant feedback loop that breaks once the user is satisfied with the generated
mask.
Temporal Propagation: Once an object mask is created, the propagation module tracks
the object and produces corresponding masks in subsequent frames. This process uses
an attention­based memory read operation, drawing inspiration from existing methods like
STM (Space­Time Memory) [5], as seen in section Section 2.2. MIVOS introduces a novel
top­filtering strategy in reading the space­time memory, enhancing the precision of object
tracking over time.

Difference­Aware Fusion: This innovative module is designed to effectively fuse the
masks before and after each user interaction. It takes into account the user’s intent and
aligns the target frames using the space­time memory. This approach ensures that user
corrections are reflected in the final output.
2.4.2
Memory read with Top­k Filtering
The memory read function is integral to how MIVOS tracks objects across video frames. It
leverages past frame information to maintain consistency in object segmentation. Drawing
inspiration from existing methods, MIVOS employs an attention mechanism as in [5] [8].
This ensures that the most relevant features from past frames are used to predict object
masks in current and future frames. Traditional memory read operations consider all past
frames equally, which can lead to less accurate segmentation. MIVOS introduces Top­
k filtering to address this issue. This filtering strategy focuses on the most relevant k
features from past frames.
Figure 2.4: Space time memory reader implementation
As can be seen in Figure 2.4, MIVOS utilizes two encoder networks ­ a memory encoder
and a query encoder derived from ResNet50 [7]. The encoders generate key feature
maps, which are then used to calculate affinities between different frames. The Top­k
strategy filters these affinities to focus on the most relevant information.
By focusing on the most relevant features, Top­k filtering enables more precise object
tracking, especially in scenes with varying object appearances or movements. This se­
lective approach can potentially improve the computational efficiency of the memory read
process, as it avoids processing irrelevant information.

Modular Interactive Video Object Segmentation (MIVOS) introduces an innovative ap­

2.5
XMEM: Long­Term Video Object Segmentation with an
Atkinson­Shiffrin Memory Model
XMem [9] represents a groundbreaking approach in the domain of video object segmen­
tation, particularly for long­duration videos. Inspired by the Atkinson­Shiffrin [10] mem­
ory model, XMem incorporates a unique memory­based mechanism for improved object
tracking and segmentation over extended video sequences.
2.5.1
Core Components
The core architecture of XMem is inspired by the human memory system, incorporating
features that mimic the way humans process and retain information over time.
Figure 2.5: Overview of XMem. The memory reading process takes important details
from three types of memory. It uses these details to make a mask. Every time, the sensory
memory gets new information. The working memory updates less often, only every r­th
time. When the working memory is full, it moves to the long­term memory in a smaller
form. The long­term memory forgets old details after a while.
XMem’s implementation of the memory model has three main components. Memory read­
ing is performed for each query frame from the long term memory, working memory and
sensory memory respectively. The readout features are used to generate a segmentation
mask.
As can be seen in Figure 2.5, the sensory memory is updated every frame. Every r­
th frame, features are inserted into the working memory. When the working memory
reaches a pre­defined maximum of Tmax frames, the features from the working memory

of many thousands of frames, the long­term memory gets full, obsolete features are then

XMem offers a novel and effective approach to long­term video object segmentation. It

2.6
Point trackers
Point trackers are designed for precision and efficiency in tracking points across video
frames. Their main challenges are complex scenarios, including occlusions, rapid move­
ments, and varying environmental conditions. This section contains a condensed overview
of three point trackers: PIPS [3], TAP­vid [11] and TAPIR [12]
2.6.1
PIPS: Persistent Independent Particles
”Particle Video Revisited: Tracking Through Occlusions Using Point Trajectories” revisits
the concept of particle video in the context of tracking pixels across video frames. The pa­
per’s unique contribution is the development of Persistent Independent Particles (PIPS).
It is a method that is designed to track pixels through occlusions using point trajectories.
This approach stands out by tracking each pixel with a trajectory over multiple frames that
aims to handle occlusions more effectively. It combines the strengths of both optical flow
and feature tracking methods.
2.6.2
TAP­Vid: A Benchmark for Tracking Any Point in a Video
The TAP­Vid paper [11] introduces a unique approach to motion tracking in video analy­
sis. It presents the concept of ”Tracking Any Point (TAP),” which focuses on estimating
long­term motion of points on physical surfaces in videos. A distinctive aspect of this work
is the TAP­Vid benchmark that combines real­world videos with human annotations and
synthetic videos with perfect ground­truth tracks. This benchmark is important in evaluat­
ing the performance of point tracking models. The paper also proposes TAP­Net, a novel
point tracking model designed specifically for this task. TAP­Net is notable for its ability
to outperform existing methods on the TAP­Vid benchmark, showcasing its effectiveness
in complex, real­world scenarios.
2.6.3
TAPIR: Tracking Any Point with per­frame Initialization and temporal
Refinement
The TAPIR [12] paper introduces a novel model for tracking points on physical surfaces
in video sequences. TAPIR’s uniqueness is in its two­stage approach: a matching stage
for identifying a candidate point match in each frame, followed by a refinement stage
that updates both the trajectory and query features. This method leads to significant
improvements in tracking performance, especially on the TAP­Vid benchmark. TAPIR
shows its efficiency in processing long and high­resolution video sequences. Additionally,
the paper showcases a diffusion model concept for generating trajectories from static
images.

2.7
SAM: Segment Anything Model
SAM stands for Segment Anything Model. It is a promptable model that is pre­trained
on a broad dataset that enables powerful generalization. Its main task is to generate
accurate object segmentation in images using prompts.These prompts can be of many
types: points, boxes, text and masks (2.6).
Figure 2.6: Promptable segmentation task [1]
This model’s primary innovation lies in its ability to adapt zero­shot to new image distribu­
tions, a competence crucial for handling the diverse nature of video object segmentation.
2.7.1
Architecture of SAM
The architecture of SAM (2.7) consists of three main components: an image encoder, a
prompt encoder, and a mask decoder. Each of these components plays a pivotal role in
the model’s functionality.
Figure 2.7: Overview of the Segment Anything Model (SAM): This model features a ro­
bust image encoder that generates an image embedding. This embedding can be quickly
accessed using various input prompts. The mask decoder then enables the production
of object masks. In cases where prompts are unclear and could refer to multiple objects,
SAM is capable of providing several appropriate masks along with their respective confi­
dence scores. [1]
At the heart of SAM is its image encoder. This component is responsible for converting
raw image data into a format that the model can process. The image encoder in SAM
is based on a minimally adapted Vision Transformer (ViT) [6] pre­trained with a Masked
Autoencoder (MAE)[13]. This adaptation allows the encoder to process high­resolution
inputs effectively. The image encoder runs once per image and can be applied before
prompting the model.

The prompt encoder in SAM is a unique feature that sets it apart from traditional seg­
mentation models. It can process various prompts, including sparse prompts like points,
boxes and text, and dense prompts like masks. Points and boxes are represented by
positional encodings and learned embeddings. For free­form text prompts, it uses an off­
the­shelf text encoder from CLIP [14], a text­image alignment model. This flexibility allows
SAM to interpret a wide range of inputs, making it highly versatile.
The mask decoder is the final step in the SAM pipeline. Its primary function is to generate
the segmentation masks based on the combined inputs from the image and prompt en­
coders. The decoder uses a modified Transformer decoder block [15], employing prompt
self­attention and cross­attention mechanisms. This structure enables it to efficiently map
the image embedding and prompt embeddings to generate accurate segmentation masks.
2.7.2
Mechanics of SAM
The mechanics of SAM involve a series of steps that enable it to segment objects in
images with high accuracy and adaptability. These steps include:
Figure 2.8: Each column shows 3 valid masks generated by SAM from a single ambiguous
point prompt (green circle). [1]
Promptable Segmentation Task: A key element of SAM’s mechanics is its ability to per­
form a promptable segmentation task. This task involves returning a valid segmentation
mask given any prompt, similar to how a language model generates coherent responses
to prompts. This feature allows SAM to adapt to various segmentation scenarios, includ­
ing ambiguous or complex images. This task then leads to a natural pre­training algorithm
and a general method for zero­shot transfer.
Pre­Training: SAM undergoes a thorough pre­training process that simulates a sequence
of prompts (points, boxes, masks) for each training sample. The model’s mask predictions
are then compared against the ground truth. The aim is to always predict a valid mask for
any prompt including ambiguous ones.

2.8
SAM­PT: Segment Anything Meets Point Tracking
Building upon the foundations set by the Segment Anything Model (SAM), the novel ap­
proach introduced in the paper ”Segment Anything Meets Point Tracking” (SAM­PT) [2]
represents a progress in video object segmentation technology. This section delves into
the innovative aspects of SAM­PT, highlighting its capabilities, applications, and impact
on the field of computer vision.
The core innovation of SAM­PT lies in its integration of point tracking techniques with the
robust image segmentation capabilities of SAM. This combination enables SAM­PT to
effectively handle the dynamic and complex nature of video content.
2.8.1
Mechanisms of SAM­PT
SAM­PT’s segmentation process is a multi­step procedure that begins with the selection
of query points and ends with the generation of accurate per­frame segmentation masks.
Figure 2.9: Overview of SAM­PT. SAM­PT’s goal is to make image segmentation models
work well with videos. It has four main steps: Query Points Selection, Point Tracking,
Segmentation and Point Tracking Reinitialization.
Interactive video object segmentation with click­based label propagation
13
==PAGE:22==
Figure 2.9 presents the four main steps in SAM­PT’s algorithm. Below are the steps
explained:
Query Points Selection: The process of Query Points Selection starts by identifying
specific points in the initial frame of a video. These points either represent the target
object (positive points) or the background and unrelated objects (negative points). The
user has the option to manually input these query points, or they can be automatically
generated from a ground truth mask. In semi­supervised video object segmentation, for
instance, the ground truth mask of the first frame is used to extract these points.
Various point sampling methods exist to select these points. The techniques can be visu­
alized in Figure 2.10. In the paper, the four sampling methods are:
Random Sampling: A simple approach where the query points are selected at random
from the ground truth mask.
K­Medoids Sampling: A technique that ensures a uniform distribution in the object’s
area. In other words, it takes the cluster centers of K­Medoids clustering [16] as query
points.
Shi­Tomasi Sampling: Simply samples the Shi­Tomasi corner points when considering
the mask as the query image [17].
Mixed sampling: A hybrid method that is a combination of the other three sampling
methods.
Figure 2.10: Positive point sampling. For an image with a ground truth or predicted
segmentation mask, positive points are picked from inside the ground truth mask. This
can be done using different methods: Random, K­Medoids [16], Shi­Tomasi [17], or Mixed.
Random Sampling and K­Medoids only need the segmentation mask, not the image itself.
For negative points, Mixed Sampling is always used on the background mask of the target
object.

The sampling method and number of sampled points have a significant influence on the
accuracy of SAM­PT. These are parameters that have been explored in the paper. Re­
sults show that sampling points with K­medoids offer the best results.
Point Tracking: Following the initial selection, these points are tracked across all of the
next frames. In this paper they use PIPS [3] (explored in Section 2.6.1) to propagate the
points. The point tracker also provides point trajectories and occlusion scores. If points
are deemed to be occluded, they would not count as foreground points in the next step.
Segmentation: As previously mentionned and as can be seen in Figure 2.11, the trajec­
tories from point tracking are then used to prompt SAM, which generates segmentation
masks for each frame. The occluded points are left out, not considered as positive points
when prompting SAM.
Figure 2.11: SAM­PT step 3. Interacting with SAM in SAM­PT happens in two main steps.
Initially, SAM receives only positive points to locate the object for the first time. Then, in the
next step, it gets both positive and negative points, along with the earlier mask prediction.
This helps SAM refine the mask further. The negative points are crucial. They take away
parts from the background and nearby objects. This is especially useful when the point
tracker wrongly places positive points outside the target object. This second step is done
over and over to improve the segmentation mask.
This step leverages SAM’s ability to produce high­quality masks, crucial for maintaining
consistency in video object segmentation.
Point Tracking Reinitialization: Optionally, the process includes a reinitialization step,
where query points are re­selected based on the latest segmentation masks. This step
ensures that the segmentation remains accurate throughout the video, especially in longer
or more complex sequences.

performance and impact
SAM­PT stands out in video object segmentation by uniquely combining sparse point
tracking with SAM, diverging from traditional methods reliant on dense object mask prop­
agation as the ones seen in Sections 2.4, 2.3 and 2.2. Traditional techniques typically
employ feature matching with mask memory, frame­by­frame matching, optical flow [18],
and recent visual prompting methods. SAM­PT, however, uses local structure context for
zero­shot generalization. This allows for diverse object tracking in various environments.
Its sparse point approach provides compact object representation and is naturally compat­

video segmentation data in training, a performance gap remains against those utilizing
such data.

to effectively integrate and process these features.
The mask decoder is also taken from SAM, it s a lightweight module that maps image
and prompt embeddings to output masks. It is also fine­tuned during training.
3.2
Training
When training the model, two distinct data sets are defined. A training set that is either
the training set of the DAVIS 2017 [19] videos or the training set of the You­Tube VOS
[20] data set. A validation set that is taken from the DAVIS 2017 [19] dataset. both of
these data sets are extensively reviewed in Section 4.1.
In the training phase, the process is designed to ensure diverse conditions. From the
entire collection of objects in the training dataset, a random frame is selected for each
object. A corresponding previous mask is then randomly chosen from within a span of
five indices preceding the index of the chosen frame. This method of selection introduces
variability, which is crucial for robust training. These two key elements, a query RGB
18
Interactive video object segmentation with click­based label propagation
==PAGE:27==
image and its associated previous mask , are then utilized to condition the SAM to obtain
a predicted mask.
The training is evaluated on the binary­cross entropy (BCE) loss [21] which is explained
in Section 4.2. The loss is performed on the predicted mask and the ground truth mask.
It is then back­propagated to update the weights in SAM’s model. The only concerned
weights to this update are the ones in the ”mask down­scaling” and the mask decoder.
The ”mask down­scaling” is a convolutional neural network of a single channel that takes
in a mask, in this case the corresponding previous mask to the query frame. All the other
weights are frozen during the update.
After each training batch is a validation loop. Here the data is loaded in the exact same
manner, getting a random query frame and a corresponding mask for each object of the
set. At the end of the validation loop, the average intersection over union (IoU) between
the predicted mask and the ground truth of the batch is computed (the IoU is explained in
Section 4.2). When all batches of an epoch are completed, the average IoU of the whole
epoch on the validation set is reported. The epoch having the highest IoU will have its
model weights saved as the best model of that training.

|Prediction ∪Ground Truth|

Ground truth: This is the simplest sampling method. It is just taking the ground truth
mask of the object as starting mask.
Human clicks: The process of obtaining a mask with human clicks involves a user in­
teractively refining the mask by selecting points on an object. Initially, the user chooses
the center of the object as a foreground click. After the mask is generated, the user as­
sesses it to find the largest incorrect area, which could be either a false positive area or
false negative area. The center of this largest area is then identified. If it’s a false positive
area, the user places a background click at its center; if it’s a false negative, a foreground
click is placed instead. This iterative process of placing clicks is repeated until the desired
number of clicks is reached, progressively improving the quality of the mask.
Bounding box: This method is similar to the humans clicks. The difference is that hu­
man clicks are only applied to the foreground clicks. This means that if there shall be n
foreground clicks, then using the human clicks method, n foreground clicks will be sam­
pled. When it comes to finding the position of the background clicks, two bounding boxes
are computed. The first bounding box is the best fix bounding box, in other words, it is
the smallest possible rectangle where all the generated foreground points can fit in. The
second bounding box is the box with the same center as the best fix box and has each
of its sides 20% larger than the best fit box. Next, the background clicks are randomly
sampled in this area.
Fully random: The fully random clicks are based on the ground truth mask and the
structure of the bounding boxes. In fact, the foreground clicks are randomly sampled
from the set of points of the ground truth mask. The background clicks are sampled in the
exact same way as in the bounding box method, meaning that it randomly samples clicks
in the area between the two generated boxes.
k­medoid Finally, as seen in Section 2.8.1, k­medoid is a technique that ensures a uniform
distribution in the object’s area. In other words, it takes the cluster centers of k­medoid
clustering [16] as query points to find the foreground clicks. Here again, the background
clicks are obtained in the same process as the previous two methods, randomly sampling
clicks in the area between the two generated boxes.

4.2.4
Point tracker
In this work figures the evaluation of the performances of point trackers. To do so, two
metrics are chose:
Point accuracy is a very simple metric. The idea is to simply find the percentage of
correctly labeled points (foreground or background). The point accuracy is then defined
as:
Number of correctly labeled points
Total number of points
Inverted normalized point error distance is more complex metric. The idea is to find
for the mislabeled points, how far they are from the ground truth mask while keeping the
value between zero and one. The idea is to start computing for each frame, the shortest
distance between each mislabeled point to the ground truth mask using a breadth­first
search [22] (BFS). Next, compute the sum of these error distances. Once that is complete,
the normalization is operated by simply dividing the sum of errors for each frame by the
largest error obtain in a frame. Now all errors for each frame is between one and zero.
However, if there are no errors the value is zero. In order to make it consistent with the
point accuracy where the largest value is the best accuracy, the final step is to subtract
one to error value. The general formula for a given frame can be seen below:
Inverse normalized error distance = |
∑error distance
Max error distance across all frames −1|
Now the best possible value, meaning that all points are correctly labeled is one. The
worse possible value would be is a zero, when the largest distances of mislabeled points
to the ground truth are found. It is also important to notice that if a point is mislabeled, it
is guaranteed that at least one frame would score a zero for the inverse normalized error
distance since the computation is relative to the worse frame of a given video sequence.
The frame with a value zero for this metric is simply the worse frame of the given video
sequence on that metric. Finally, the average inverse normalized error distance can be
found by averaging all the inverse normalized error distance of all frames of a given video
sequence.

labeled as foreground or background points. Next it will disregard the incorrectly labeled
points to only withhold true information. Thus, only the correctly labeled points will prompt

Conclusion
In conclusion, while the final models presented in this study demonstrated underwhelm­
ing performance, it’s important to recognize the potential for improvement in future work.
The training phase, though promising, did not translate into satisfactory final results. It
lead to mediocre results. However, this outcome should not overshadow the numerous
unexplored ideas and propositions that emerged during the course of this work.
One immediate area for enhancement involves the training methodology. A more effective
approach might include incorporating clicks alongside the ground truth masks during train­
ing. This adjustment could align the training conditions more closely with those of the final
testing and potentially improving model performance. Additionally, integrating predicted
masks back into the training process could be beneficial. Since test inputs increasingly
deviate from the ground truth masks, using a blend of accurate and sub­optimal masks in
training could better prepare the model for real­world scenarios. Specifically, maintaining
two sets of masks, the ground truth and predicted masks, and randomly selecting from
these during training might offer a more robust learning. Furthermore, there could also be
a code revision to ensure that there aren’t any bugs that could alter the desired process.
These propositions for future research and development provide hope for the initial hy­
pothesis of this study. By addressing these areas, there is a possibility of enhancing
model performance and achieving strong results.
