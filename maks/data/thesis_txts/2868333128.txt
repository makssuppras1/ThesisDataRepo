Abstract
This thesis investigates structure identification for automatic Dantzig­Wolfe decomposi­
tion, and presents different approaches to extend its applicability to generic Mixed­Integer
Programs (MIPs) without relying on manual inputs or domain­specific insights. Four dis­
tinct methods were implemented: two hypergraph partitioning (HP)­based approaches
using two different problem representations; a community­based decomposition (CD)
method employing a bipartite graph representation; and a two­stage Identical Subprob­
lem Detector (ISD) algorithm leveraging color­preserving automorphisms and subgraph
isomorphism. These methods were integrated into a unified workflow for structure identi­
fication, decomposition solving, and result aggregation.
The effectiveness of these methods was demonstrated through computational experi­
ments on MIPLIB and VRPTW instance sets. On the MIPLIB instances, the HP­based
approaches achieved competitive or superior root node dual bounds compared to exist­
ing results in the literature. The CD approach successfully identified well­balanced, com­
petitive decompositions, validating its utility in fully automated scenarios. On VRPTW
instances, the ISD method consistently identified the identical subproblems. Beside the
computational experiments a detailed discussion was also provided comparing and con­
trasting the four approaches.
By providing a detailed overview of the above methods, this work highlights the potential
and challenges of automated structure identification in advancing DWR methodologies
for generic MIPs.

1
Introduction
Optimizing our use of resources such as energy, water, and raw materials is crucial for
sustainability. However, these systems are large in size and very complex. Therefore, ad­
vanced optimization strategies suitable for large­scale optimization are required to solve
problems defined within these systems.
One such strategy is Dantzig–Wolfe decomposition that relies on the specification of so­
called blocks. Traditionally, these blocks are provided by a modeler for a specific problem.
This limits the use of Dantzig–Wolfe decomposition to problems, where there is an evident
decomposable structure, and where the modeler has domain specific knowledge.
The development of algorithms capable of automatically detecting decomposable struc­
tures within optimization problems aims to broaden the applicability of Dantzig–Wolfe de­
composition.
Overall, by focusing on structure identification for automatic Dantzig–Wolfe decomposi­
tion, my thesis seeks to contribute to the development of automatic decomposition al­
gorithms capable of solving complex optimization problems pertinent to climate change
mitigation and sustainable development.
1.1
Thesis overview
This thesis is organized as follows: chapter 1 introduces the reader to the foundations of
the concepts and methods used. Thereafter, the relevant literature is presented in chap­
ter 2, with focus on the current methods for decomposition detection and decomposition
ranking. In chapter 3 the used methodology of structure identification and decomposition
solving is summarized. The main results are presented in chapter 4, and discussed in
chapter 5, which includes a comparison with results from the literature and a comparison
of the used methods. Finally, in chapter 6 conclusions are drawn, and potential areas of
future research is identified.

1.2.1
Mixed integer linear programs
Mixed integer programming is a class of optimization problems where some of the decision
variables are restricted to take integer values, while others are allowed to be continuous.
Generally, these problems are NP­hard, and even small instances can require extensive
computational effort to be solved to optimality.
A general mixed integer linear program can be written as:

s.t. Ax ≤b

coefficient matrix, b is the vector of constraint bounds, and c is the vector of coefficients
of the linear objective function.
Throughout this thesis the MIP abbreviation is used for mixed integer linear program.

1.3
Dantzig­Wolfe reformulation
Dantzig­Wolfe reformulation (DWR) is a technique that can be used to improve the relax­
ation bound for MIP problems. It was originally proposed in 1960 [4] as a reformulation
for linear programs, but it has later been applied to MIPs as well. In the following I sketch
how DWR is applied to a MIP, and then show this relates to the different matrix structures
discussed in section 1.2.2.

DWR with multiple subproblems
In the more general setting when the number of blocks is more than one (K > 1) and the
coefficient matrix has RBBD (1.2) structure, the constraints corresponding to the block­
diagonal component D can be convexified per block. The resulting reformulation is DWR
with multiple subproblems.

DWR with identical subproblems
A special case of DWR with multiple subproblems is when:
• The coefficient matrix A has a RBBD 1.2 structure, where the submatrices in the
block­diagonal component diag(D) = [D1, . . . , DK] are identical (Dk1 = Dk2,
∀k1, k2 ∈
{1, . . . , K}).
• The constraint bounds b = [b1, . . . , bK] are matching for each constraint block (bk1 =
bk2,
∀k1, k2 ∈{1, . . . , K}), and the constraints’ relation types (equality, inequality)
are also the same.
• The coefficients of the linear objective function c = [c1, . . . , cK] are identical for each
variable block (ck1 = ck2,
∀k1, k2 ∈{1, . . . , K}). Additionally, the variables also
have the same bounds and restrictions (e.g. integer valued) in each block.
If all three of these conditions are met, then all the subproblems are identical, and it is
enough to solve one of them instead of all. For this reason, this type of DWR is called
DWR with identical subproblems.
DWR with linking variables
DWR with linking variables is an extension of DWR with multiple subproblems, where
some of the variables are assigned to multiple blocks. These variables link subproblems
together and are therefore called linking variables. This type of reformulation requires a
DBBD (1.4) structure in the coefficient matrix of the original MIP.
For additional details about DWR with multiple subproblems and DWR with linking vari­
ables, the reader is referred to [5] and [6].
In summary, one can identify possible DWRs by detecting block­diagonal matrix
structures (e.g. RBBD 1.2 or DBBD 1.4) in a general MIP’s coefficient matrix.
Column Generation
In practice solving the DWR of a larger MIP directly is computationally infeasible, since
one would need to generate all the extreme points and rays corresponding to the convex­
ified constraints as separate variables (columns). To avoid this a technique called column
generation (CG) is used. Instead of solving the DWR’s relaxation (Dantzig­Wolfe relax­
ation) directly, CG iteratively adds the necessary columns to the problem, guided by their
potential to improve the solution. The process alternates between solving a restricted
master problem (RMP), which includes only a subset of the columns, and a set of pricing
subproblems, which identify new columns with negative reduced cost to be added to the
RMP. This iterative refinement ensures computational efficiency by focusing on the most
relevant parts of the solution space.
Furthermore, to solve a given MIP to integer optimality the Dantzig­Wolfe relaxation can
be used within a branch­and­bound framework in place of the LP relaxation. In this case
the nodes of the branch­and­bound tree are solved with CG, and the overall framework
is called branch­and­price. This approach leverages the tighter bounds provided by the
Dantzig­Wolfe relaxation to improve the computational efficiency of solving the MIP prob­
lem. [7]
Note that in the literature it is common practice to measure the quality of a decomposition
with the root node dual bound, which is the dual bound provided by solving the Dantzig­
Wolfe relaxation of the problem. [8][9]

A hypergraph is the generalization of a traditional graph where edges, called hyperedges
or nets, connect any number of vertices. Formally, a hypergraph is defined as H = (V, E),
where V is a set of vertices and E is a set of hyperedges, with each hyperedge being a
subset of V :

The k­way hypergraph partitioning problem is the generalization of the graph partitioning
problem: partition the vertex set into k disjoint blocks of bounded size (at most 1+ϵ times
the average block size), while minimizing an objective function defined on the nets. Here
ϵ is the imbalance parameter that can be used to limit the imbalance between partitions.

1.4.3
Graph isomorphism and related topics
This section briefly introduces the concepts of graph isomorphism, graph automorphism,
coloring vertices and edges, and color­preserving automorphisms. These concepts are
helpful for studying the symmetries and structural properties of graphs, and they can also
be used to identify identical subproblems (see section 3.2.3 for details).
Graph isomorphism
Two graphs G = (VG, EG) and H = (VH, EH) are said to be isomorphic if there exists a
bijection φ : VG →VH such that any two vertices u, v ∈VG are adjacent in G if and only if
φ(u) and φ(v) are adjacent in H. Formally, φ satisfies:
∀u, v ∈VG, (u, v) ∈EG ⇐⇒(φ(u), φ(v)) ∈EH
Graph automorphism
A graph automorphism is an isomorphism from a graph G to itself. It is a permutation of
the vertices that preserves the adjacency relation. Formally, an automorphism of a graph
G = (V, E) is a bijection φ : V →V such that:
∀u, v ∈V, (u, v) ∈E ⇐⇒(φ(u), φ(v)) ∈E
Colored graphs
A colored graph’s vertices (and edges) have colors (labels) assigned to them. The colors
can be assigned arbitrarily and be used for a number of different applications including
identifying color­preserving automorphism of the graph.

2
Literature review
The traditional approach of using DWR for MIPs involved tailoring the implementation
to specific problems in the majority of cases. To generalize this method Gamrath et al.
[11] developed a framework, named Generic Column Generation (GCG), to solve MIPs
with user­specified decompositions. GCG could automatically apply the specified DW
decomposition to the problem and solve it with a branch­price­and­cut approach. In their
computational study, the authors solved capacitated p­median, bin packing, and resource
allocation problems with GCG to demonstrate the framework’s versatility and efficiency.
Based on these results they concluded that the developed general method is both feasible
and promising. They also highlighted that the integration of existing MIP solvers in the
framework enables reusing the already existing advanced MIP solving methods, which
make the computations more efficient. However, they admitted that problem­specific en­
hancements can further improve performance, and that for this reason the generic frame­
work may lag behind specialized solvers. Regarding possible future work they pointed
out that investigating the performance of the framework on a broader class of problems is
necessary, and that automating the detection of decomposable structures in MIPs could
potentially reduce the required user input and make the system fully generic.
2.1
Graph­based automatic decomposition of MIPs
The question of how to automatically find feasible decompositions of a MIP has no final
answer ever since. In this section I collected the graph­based approaches that have been
tried.
2.1.1
Hypergraph­based methods
Bergner et al. [12] introduced a nearly automatic process to detect DBBD (1.4) structures
in the coefficient matrix of MIPs using hypergraph partitioning. To detect these struc­
tures they supplied the Row­Column­Net hypergraph representation of the problem to
hMETIS [13] and experimented with different number of blocks K ∈{2, 3, 4, 5}. They
also reported the use of net weights of 1 and 2 for continuous and discrete variables,
respectively; and two different settings for the constraints weights: a 5 mild, and a 105 ex­
treme case to penalize the presence of linking constraints in the resulting decomposition.
Since their goal was to provide a proof­of­concept it is understandable that they have not
provided further details about why these weigths were chosen. Overall, their approach
favors selecting continuous variables as linking variables over discrete variables, and de­
pending on the penalty setting, it mildly or extremely favors linking variables over linking
constraints. In the latter case the ”discovered” matrix structure is forced to be a CBBD
(1.3) matrix. Nevertheless, their results showed that the obtained DWR often provides
stronger dual bounds compared to state­of­the­art solvers of the time. For example for
dynamic 0­1 knapsack problems, the reformulation significantly closed integrality gaps
compared to traditional solvers. Whereas for the instances from MIPLIB 2003 [14] the
obtained dual bounds were sometimes stronger and sometimes weaker. So, overall the
authors successfully provided a proof­of­concept for using the Row­Column­Net hyper­
graph representation of MIP problems for (almost) automatic detection of decompositions
for DWR.
Two years later Wang et al. [15] focused on identifying RBBD (1.2) structures in the MIPs’
constraint matrices. They also used hypergraph partitioning to obtain the decompositions,
but with a different Row­Net representation of the problem. One of their main contributions
Structure Identification for Automatic Dantzig­Wolfe Decomposition
9
==PAGE:18==
was, that they compared two hypergraph partitioning tools: PaToH [16] and hMETIS [13].
They found that hMETIS produced more balanced partitions and the number of blocks was
typically equal to the requested block number. Additionally, when the number of blocks
was the same in the resulting decompositions, the root node’s dual bound was stronger for
the one produced by hMETIS. Discovering RBBD structures meant that they only allowed
linking constraints in the resulting decomposition, and in their conclusions they noted that
for some instances a more general framework allowing for DBBD structures may be more
advantageous.
In their next paper Bergner et al. [8] provided a proof­of­concept for automating DWR.
This time, they used both RBBD (1.2) and DBBD (1.4) as target structures and their cor­
responding hypergraph representations: Row­Net and Row­Column­Net. For the con­
ducted computational experiments they were using instances from MIPLIB 2003 [14] and
MIPLIB 2010 [17] and tested block number K ∈{2, 3, . . . , 10}. They demonstrated that
automatically reformulated models can yield strong dual bounds and, in some cases, out­
perform state­of­the­art MIP solvers. However, they also highlighted that performance
variability, a common issue in MIP solving, is significant in the context of DWR as well.
Small changes in the decomposition can lead to dramatically different computational out­
comes.
More recently Clausen et al. [18] investigated how linking cuts improve the root node’s
dual bound of DWR for generic MIPs. They tested selected instances from MIPLIB 2017
[19] by generating decompositions with hMETIS [13] using the Row­Column­Net hyper­
graph representations. In contrary to previous papers they tested higher number of blocks
besides lower ones (K ∈{2, 5, 10, 20, 50}). They found that decompositions using smaller
number of blocks do not always yield the best root node dual bound. Additionally, they
concluded that the effectiveness of applying linking cuts is highly instance dependent, as
the technique improved the obtained dual bound in 20 out of 62 applicable cases.
2.1.2
Community­based methods
A significant drawback of the prior hypergraph partitioning approaches is that the number
of blocks needs to be predefined. To mitigate this issue Khaniyev et al. [9] introduced
a new approach based on community detection for identifying RBBD (1.2) structures in
MIP constraint matrices. Their approach uses the bipartite graph representation of the
coefficient matrix and tries to balance the quality of the resulting block­diagonal and bor­
der components. To achieve this they developed an iterative algorithm that consists of
consecutive remove and merge phases. This can be interpreted as performing Large
Neighborhood Search (LNS) [20] for finding the best decomposition. The remove phase
identifies ”out­of­community” row nodes by maximizing the modularity (1.7) of the block­
diagonal component, and moves them to the border component. While, the merge phase
greedily readds row(s) to the block­diagonal component until there are only two compo­
nents. This way, in each iteration alternative RBBD (1.2) structures are generated, and
the search stops if an iteration results in an already seen structure. At the termination of
the search a list of all alternative structures are returned, and the one with the highest
”goodness” score (discussed in section 2.2) is selected.
The authors benchmarked their approach on the same instances from MIPLIB 2003 [14]
and 2010 [17] as [8]. Based on the results they concluded that the proposed algorithm out­
performs existing methods in detecting RBBD (1.2) structures as it requires less compu­
tational time, while providing competitive or better bounds compared to prior techniques.
The use of the bipartite graph representation and community detection was also proposed
for solving large­scale, non­convex optimization problems by Allman et al. [21]. Their
10

==PAGE:19==
algorithm, called DeCODe (Detection of Communities for Optimization Decomposition),
relies on the Louvain algorithm [22] for subproblem detection. The Louvain algorithm, a
modularity­based community detection algorithm well­known in the field of network sci­
ence [10], optimizes modularity with a bottom­up approach, starting with each node in its
own community and iteratively merging nodes to maximize modularity. It is also compu­
tationally efficient and therefore scalable for large graphs [22].
Indeed, when tested on a biorefinery/microgrid design problem, which was formulated as a
mixed­integer nonlinear programming (MINLP) problem with 87,778 variables and 70,853
constraints, DeCODe provided a possible decomposition in 371 seconds. The proposed
algorithm was also tested on two much smaller MINLP problems. For both problems,
the authors compared the community­based decomposition solution’s performance with
attempting to solve the problem monolithically with BARON [23], a solver designed for
global optimization MINLP problems. In the first case the decomposition detected by
DeCODe enabled faster convergence to feasible solutions with better objective values.
In the second case, using the decomposition also resulted in faster convergence, and this
time an optimal solution was found in 24 minutes, that BARON was unable to find within
an hour.
2.2
Decomposition ranking
Besides the task of generating feasible decompositions the most important question is:
what characterizes a good decomposition?
2.2.1
Indicators and scores
One possible answer is goodness scores that measure the quality of generated decompo­
sitions. Wang et al. defined five features that might indicate the quality of a decomposition
from which two were used in their combined goodness metric:
1. Ratio of linking non­zero elements (α)
Defined as the ratio of the number of non­zero elements (NZE) in the linking con­
straints and the total number of nonzero elements in the coefficient matrix. Lower
values of α indicate fewer linking constraints, improving block independence and
computational efficiency.
2. Ratio of integer linking non­zero elements (γ)
Defined as the ratio of the number of NZEs corresponding to integer variables in the
linking constraints and the total number of NZEs corresponding to integer variables
in the coefficient matrix. With this definition lower γ values suggest that integer
variables are largely confined to individual blocks, enhancing decomposition quality.
With these features their proposed combined goodness metric was:
Π = 1 −min(α, γ)
(2.1)
where a higher Π value indicates better decomposition quality by minimizing coupling and
confining integer elements to blocks. At the same time, the authors also noted that there
is much more work to be done in determining how to judge decomposition quality, and
pointed out that one should also take the computation time into account as a component
of goodness rather than simply considering the root bound.
Nevertheless, the root node’s dual bound remained the primary proxy measure used by
many following papers including [8][9] and [24].

Bergner et al. [8] conducted a comprehensive overview of the input measures and their
observed impacts on the root node dual bound and computation time of the decompo­
sition. They categorized the input measures into direct indicators that can be explicitly
controlled by changing the parameters of the hypergraph partitioning problem, and indi­
rect indicators that can not be minimized or maximized with their approach. The analyzed
direct indicators included:
• Number of blocks (K) They found that increasing K reduced the computation time
by creating smaller, more manageable subproblems. However, they also noted that
fewer blocks typically yield tighter root node dual bounds.
• Ratio of linking variables Defined as the ratio of the linking variables and the total
number of variables. In their analysis higher ratio increased computation time and
negatively impacted the obtained bound.
• Ratio of linking constraints Defined as the ratio of linking constraints and the total
number of constraints. A higher ratio potentially leading to a weaker bound as fewer
constraints are being convexified.
• Relative border area The relative border area measures the ratio of border compo­
nent’s area (both linking variables and linking constraints) and the total matrix area.
They found that this indicator weakly correlated (negatively) with stronger root node
dual bounds. However, very small border areas may increase computation time due
to the increasing complexity of subproblems.
Among the two indirect indicators investigated only the average subproblem density showed
observable trends with respect to the provided root node dual bound:
• Average subproblem density The average subproblem density is calculated by
dividing the number of NZEs with both dimensions of the block matrix Dk corre­
sponding to the subproblem k. They observed that as the average subproblem
density increased, the obtained bound got weaker, which was again probably due
to the increase in computation time because of higher complexity of the denser sub­
problems.
As a final conclusion the authors suggested using the relative border area as the default
goodness measure for decompositions because of its ease to compute and (weak) nega­
tive correlation with stronger root node dual bounds. Here I can also note that the relative
border area and the average subproblem density measures are still reported in the current
version of GCG [25], which was and is developed by the authors.
The iterative algorithm developed by Khaniyev et al. [9] used modularity (1.7) as the
quality measure of the block­diagonal component D, and an exponential decay function
of the relative border area (b): P(x) = e−λb with a tunable λ parameter as the quality
measure of the border component.
At this point I need to highlight that this list of investigated goodness measures is not
complete. In fact I only introduced a handful of the ones that were tried, but overall it can
be said that no goodness measures was proved to accurately predict the root node dual
bound or computation time of the root node on its own. And this is exactly the motivation
of the papers that investigated the possibility of using machine learning.

2.2.2
Machine learning for ranking
One potential use case for machine learning (ML) proposed by Kruber et al. [24] is to
decide whether applying DWR will be beneficial for a given MIP instance. Additionally,
the authors defined a second task as well for the case when DWR is potentially beneficial,
namely which decomposition to choose if multiple options were detected. Their approach
used more than 100 statistical features derived from both the original problem structure
and the characteristics of the decomposition. To create their dataset they selected 400 in­
stances, detected possible decompositions with GCG [25], and then solved each instance
with both SCIP [26] and GCG with a 2 hour time limit.
In their computational experiment they used hold­out cross­validation, where 60% of the
instances and their corresponding results were allocated to the training set and the re­
maining 40% to the test set. They reported the use three different classifiers, namely k­
nearest neighbors (KNN), support vector machines (SVM) with the stationary RBF kernel,
and random forests (RF). Further refinements, such as hyperparameter tuning, are not
detailed explicitly, suggesting that their evaluation focused on increasing practical solver
performance rather than exhaustive model optimization. As a result, the best performing
classifier achieved a 66% precision for the first classification task, and an accuracy of 65%
for the second task.
An alternative approach, a ranking pipeline was proposed by Basso et al. [27] with the
following steps: decomposition generation, filtering, scoring and ranking, and decompo­
sition solving. In this pipeline a classification model is used to filter out decompositions
that are not Pareto­optimal in terms of root node dual bound and computation time. And
thereafter, the remaining decompositions are scored and ranked based on two regres­
sion models predicting the dual bound and the computation time respectively. The need
for the filtering step was mainly due to the fact that they decided to randomly generate
decompositions (described in their previous paper [28]), despite citing the papers utilizing
hypergraph partitioning approaches. Consequently, they concluded that root node dual
bound prediction is out of reach, but time prediction can be performed with ”very good
accuracy”. Note that for the prediction of the time score (that is within the [0, 1] interval)
they reported an RMSE of 0.213.
In their later publication Basso et al. [29] presented a fully data­driven DW decomposition
framework. The main modification compared to the prior pipeline was that the filtering
and the ranking steps were replaced by a local search guided by pre­trained regression
models.
Weiner et al. [30] also employed a ML approach to rank decompositions generated for
MIPs. They built a dataset of over 40,000 decompositions with RBBD (1.2) structure
with greedy­random and multi­objective genetic algorithms. They used various regres­
sion models to predict decomposition quality based on an equal weight sum of the min­
max normalized optimality gap and the solve time scores. However, their ”approximating
leave­one­out cross­validation” approach meant that they trained the regressors on all de­
compositions from the same problem type excluding the test instance. In the conclusions
they claimed ”state­of­the­art predictive performance in ranking decomposition quality,
particularly for instances with structural similarity to the training data” based on the 14
instances tested.

3.2.1
Hypergraph partitioning
One option for efficiently generating partitions of a hypergraph is to use heuristic partition­
ing algorithms to solve the k­way hypergraph partitioning problem.
As a reminder, the task is to partition the vertex set if a hypergraph into k disjoint blocks of
bounded size, while minimizing an objective function defined on the nets. The imbalance
between partitions is limited by the imbalance parameter (ϵ).
The objective function used in this thesis is called cut­net, which minimizes the number of
nets that are cut. From another perspective it minimizes the number of nets that connect
to more than one partition. Note that this objective directly minimizes the number of linking
constraints, or both the number of linking constraints and linking variables depending on
which hypergraph representation of the instance is used.
Additionally, the imbalance parameter (ϵ) allows direct control over the block size imbal­
ance, so the size of any block will only be 1 + ϵ times the average block size at most.
Partitioning the Row­Net representation
By using the Row­Net hypergraph representation (section 1.5.2) of an instance one can
detect RBBD (1.2) structures in the coefficient matrix, which lead to DWR with multiple
subproblems and without linking variables.

Community detection algorithms
The three community detection algorithms tested in this thesis all seek the partition (non­
overlapping communities) with the largest modularity.
• The Optimized Greedy Algorithm [44] is a computationally optimized version of
the Greedy Algorithm [45], which was the first modularity maximization algorithm for
community detection. [10]
• The Louvain Algorithm [22] is a modularity optimization algorithm with better scala­
bility compared to the Optimized Greedy Algorithm, so it is better suited for detecting
communities in very large graphs. [10]
• The Leiden Algorithm [46] is an improvement over the Louvain Algorithm as it en­
sures that communities are well­connected and provides stronger guarantees about
the quality of partitions.
Since only the igraph graph library in Pyhton had an implementation of the Leiden algo­
rithm at the time of writing I chose to use this library for all three community detection
algorithms.

2. The constraint bounds b = [b1, . . . , bK] must match for each constraint block (bk1 =
bk2,
∀k1, k2 ∈{1, . . . , K}), and the constraints’ relation types (equality, inequality)
must also be the same.
3. The coefficients of the linear objective function c = [c1, . . . , cK] must be identical for
each variable block (ck1 = ck2,
∀k1, k2 ∈{1, . . . , K}). Additionally, the variables
must have the same bounds and restrictions (e.g. integer valued) in each block.
Based on these requirements, the following equivalence classes can be defined:
1. Non­zero elements are in the same equivalence class if they have the same value.
2. Constraints are in the same equivalence class if their corresponding non­zero el­
ements and the constraint bounds match, and the type of relation they describe is
also the same.
3. Variables are in the same equivalence class if their corresponding non­zero ele­
ments and objective function coefficients match, and their bounds and restrictions
are the same.
One can notice that if the MIP is represented as a colored graph so that the colors corre­
spond to unique equivalence classes, then the graph’s color­preserving automorphisms
are mappings that map the problem’s identical subproblems to one another. So, by iden­
tifying color­preserving automorphisms of a colored graph representation one can identify
identical subproblems within a MIP.
The colored graph representation can be one of the following:
22
Structure Identification for Automatic Dantzig­Wolfe Decomposition
==PAGE:31==
• colored bipartite graph representation: The nodes of the colored bipartite graph
representation are the variables and the constraints of the MIP. The node colors are
defined based on unique variable and constraint equivalence classes. The edges
correspond to the NZEs of the coefficient matrix connecting the variable nodes to the
constraint nodes. The edge colors are defined based on unique non­zero values.
• colored tripartite graph representation: The nodes of the colored tripartite graph
representation also include the NZEs. The node colors are defined based on the
unique equivalence classes. The edges connect variable nodes or constraint nodes
to their corresponding NZE nodes and have no colors assigned. The graph is tri­
partite as variable and constraint nodes only connect to NZE nodes and vice versa.
An advantage of a colored graph representation for identical subproblem detection is that
the variable and constraint equivalence classes can be simplified. This is due to the fact
that the variable and constraint equivalence classes do not need to take into account the
corresponding non­zero elements as these connections are already encoded in the graph
representation.
The colored bipartite graph representation has the obvious advantage of being smaller
in size compared to the colored tripartite graph representation. However, not all graph
isomorphism algorithms support the use of edge colors.

on fig. 3.11. For this problem there are two equivalence classes of variables, which are

yellow and orange. The constraints with the same color have equal right hand side val­

classes for the two unique NZE values in the problem’s coefficient matrix. These are

For the desired application, namely ISP detection for DWR, the igraph’s interface of VF2

VF2 support edge colors, while the ones that use bliss do not. This allows for using the
smaller, colored bipartite graph representation of the problem. So, for this and several
other reasons described in appendix A, the functions using the VF2 algorithm were used
for implementing the ISP detector algorithm.
The implemented algorithm
The major challenge when trying to use color­preserving automorphisms for ISP detection
is that there is a corresponding color­preserving automorphism for each permutation of
the identical subproblems. This means that the number of automorphisms grows by the
factorial of the number of ISPs. Therefore, finding all automorphisms is computationally
infeasible for instances with more than a few identical subproblems.
For this reason, the support of callbacks after finding an automorphism is a necessary
feature, which allows for stopping the search early if all the ISPs have been identified.
When detecting ISPs for DWR a sufficient stop condition is that there remain only (identity
mapped) nodes that correspond to constraints if the bipartite graph’s nodes relating to the
identified ISPs are removed.
One more trick that can be used to mitigate the chance of finding the same ISPs (isomorph
subgraphs) over and over again is to remove the nodes corresponding to the already
identified ISPs. Then, instead of finding more color­preserving automorphisms of the
original graph, one can search for subgraphs in the remaining graph that are isomorphic
to the ones already identified, and their nodes and edges have the same colors.

The two­stage algorithm’s operation is as follows:
Initialization
The colored bipartite graph representation is constructed, where the colors correspond to
the equivalence classes.
Stage I: Finding Color­Preserving Automorphisms
A search is performed to find a non­trivial color­preserving automorphism of the bipartite
graph.
• If such automorphism is found, the corresponding isomorphic subgraphs are identi­
fied and the algorithm goes on to the next stage.
• If no automorphism is found, the process terminates with no ISPs detected.
Stage II: Iteratively Identifying Isomorphic Subgraphs
The graph is split, so that nodes of the already identified isomorphic subgraphs are re­
moved. Then, a subisomorphism search is conducted on the remaining part of the bi­
partite graph. If isomorphic subgraphs with matching colors are discovered, then the
corresponding nodes are removed and the search continues. When the subisomorphism
search does not find additional isomorphic subgraphs with matching colors then the search
is terminated and the remaining graph is analyzed.
• If there are only constraint nodes in the remaining graph, then the algorithm found
a possible reformulation that can be used for DWR with identical subproblems. In
this case the found isomorphic subgraphs are translated to variable and constraint
blocks.
• If there are still variable graphs in the remaining graph then the algorithm found
identical subproblems, but the allocation of the remaining nodes is necessary to get
a DWR for the original problem.
An example run of the two­stage ISP detection algorithm (fig. 3.12) on the toy problem
(1.8) is visualized on fig. 3.13. In the first stage, the algorithm finds a color­preserving
automorphism of the colored bipartite graph representation. The mapping of nodes are
shown with two­way arrows on fig. 3.13a. The two isomorph subgraphs found (high­
lighted with thicker borders) are then removed for the second stage (fig. 3.13b). In this
case, it is trivial that there are no further subgraphs within the remaining graph (left) that
are isomorphic with the already removed subgraphs (right). In fact, the remaining graph
consists of a single constraint node only, which leads to the termination of the algorithm
with a successful detection of two identical subproblems. The linking constraint in the re­
sulting reformulation is the constraint node that stayed in the remaining until termination,
while the two subproblems are given by the two isomorph subgraphs identified.

Decomposition solvers
The following decomposition solvers that support Dantzig­Wolfe reformulation (DWR)
were considered during the development process:
• AutoDec [51] is a generic tool for automatic DWR of MIPs developed at DTU and
implemented in Julia. It supports linking variables, dual stabilization, and the addi­
tion of cuts, but is not publicly available.
• Coluna.jl [52] is an openly available framework written in Julia to implement a de­
composition approach to optimize block­structured MIPs. It supports DWR with cut
generation and dual stabilization, but it lacks the capability of handling linking vari­
ables in DWR.
• Generic Column Generation (GCG) [25] is an openly available generic decomposi­
tion solver for MIPs written in C. It supports DWR with linking variables, dual sta­
bilization, and cut generation. It has built­in structure detectors, and also supports
solving externally defined decompositions. However, it does not have an interface
in Julia.

Discussion
I divided the discussion into two sections. Section 5.1 compares the obtained root node
bounds with results from the literature for instances in the MIPLIB set. Then, section 5.2
focuses on comparison of the different structure identification methods presented in this
thesis based on the results of both instance sets.
5.1
Root node result comparison with the literature
For an easier overview of the root node results (table 4.6), table 5.1 shows how many
times a given identification method provided the best decomposition in terms of root node
dual bound for each analysis group and in total.
Note that DWRauto and DWRbest are the HP based approaches from [8]. The ”auto”
version automatically selects the decomposition based on the relative border area, while
”best” is simply a report of best decomposition (in terms of root node gap closed). KEEauto
is the community based approach from [9] that does not require predifining the number of
blocks, however it can only detect RBBD (1.2) structures.
The general trend is that the HP based approaches provide the best root node dual bound
more often compared to the community detection approaches. Additionally, RNroot and
RCNroot, the HP based identification methods from my thesis, performed equally well
for instances in group A and B, while outperforming other methods in these groups. In
group C the best counts are distributed more evenly across the different methods with
no apparent winner. Note that the higher best count of DWRbest is due to the fact that it
reports the best results for two different HP based approaches.

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, I explored the topic of structure identification for automatic DWR, and con­
tributed to the development of methods that can extend its applicability to generic MIPs
without relying on manual, domain­specific insights.
Specifically, I implemented two hypergraph partitioning (HP)­based structure identification
methods in Julia, which utilize the Row­Net and Row­Column­Net hypergraph represen­
tations for the detection of block­diagonal structures, that can be exploited by DWR. Ad­
ditionally, a community­based decomposition (CD) method was implemented in Python,
relying on community detection algorithms to identify possible decompositions automati­
cally using the bipartite graph (BG) representation. Furthermore, a novel two­stage Iden­
tical Subproblem Detector (ISD) algorithm was developed and implemented in Python,
employing color­preserving automorphisms as well as subgraph isomorphism to identify
identical subproblems based on the colored bipartite representation.
These four methods were integrated into a cohesive structure identification, decomposi­
tion solving, and result aggregation workflow to enable systematic handling of the large
number of identified decompositions and corresponding solver results.
Regarding performance evaluation of the proposed methods, I conducted computational
experiments on two sets of instances. For the MIPLIB set, I found that the decomposi­
tions identified by my approaches provided stronger or competitive root node dual bounds
compared to existing methods in the literature. Furthermore, my results showed that
the two HP­based approaches perform similarly on general instances of the MIPLIB set.
However, the method using the Row­Column­Net representation showed specific advan­
tages in cases where a DBBD structure was beneficial. This set of results also provided
a proof­of­concept for the community­based structure identification approach, as it suc­
cessfully identified competitive decompositions for many instances fully automatically, and
produced well­balanced blocks in general.
For the VRPTW set, I observed that the ISD approach reliably identified the identical sub­
problems in the case of all instances. Meanwhile, the community­based method yielded
similar results but introduced a few additional linking constraints.
It is also worth highlighting some of the limitations of the presented structure identifica­
tion approaches. The two HP­based methods require predefining the number of blocks,
which limits their use in a fully automated setting. In contrast, the community detection
based approach automatically discovers the number of blocks, however it fails to identify
a feasible decomposition in some cases.
Future work might allow for the mitigation of the above limitations, for example by combin­
ing HP­based approaches with the automatic block number detection of the community
based approach within a single method.
Alternatively, a more general machine­learning approach could be developed for the au­
tomatic selection of decompositions based on a large enough dataset.

A
Implementation overview
An overview of the final implementation of the whole pipeline is shown on fig. A.1.
My original plan was to only use Julia for the implementation of the whole structure iden­
tification and decomposition solving pipeline.
This plan turned out to be infeasible for multiple reasons:
• Graphs.jl [60] the most mature graph package in Julia has limited support for com­
munity detection, and it only provides an experimental interface to the VF2 algorithm
[49].
• The AutoDEC tool developed at DTU is not openly available and a significant part
of its features lack documentation, so for the reproducibility of results I decided to
use GCG.
The next main question was the selection of a suitable graph package.
One option was NetworkX [61] that offers a great selection of community detection algo­
rithms including the fast­greedy algorithm [44] and the Louvain algorithm [22]. Addition­
ally, it also provides an implementation of an improved version of the VF2 algorithm [49],
namely VF2++ [62]. However, the function that calls the improved algorithm does not
support callbacks and edge colors. So, for this reason I chose the igraph [47] in which the
isomorphic_vf2() function provided support for both edge colors and the use of a callback
function.
Since solving all the generated decomposition with GCG would have been impossible on
my desktop computer within reasonable time I used DTU’s HPC for this purpose. How­
ever, the cluster’s operating system is Alma Linux 9.4 [63], so GCG had to be compiled
from source via CMake [64].
