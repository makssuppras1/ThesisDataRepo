Abstract
The youthful C-RAN concept oﬀers many opportunities for mobile operators. Large
savings and easy deployment are some of the main beneﬁts of a C-RAN architecture.
However, there are some challenges when deploying a C-RAN fronthaul network. In-
creased capacity and strict latency require dedicated ﬁber connections between the
centralized BBU pool and the RHHs. However, an entire network of dedicated ﬁber
connections is a large investment for any operator. Therefore other transport alter-
natives are being researched.
A packet-based Ethernet transport network would be more economical to de-
ploy and many operators already own a packet-based infrastructure. Despite this,
a standard packet-based network cannot meet the latency requirement of a fronthaul
network. Research of enabling Ethernet transport networks to deliver time-sensitive
streams has begun by the IEEE 802.1 Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN) Task Group.
TSN oﬀers a wide range of network operations for fronthaul networks. Frame
preemption is one of the most prominent features, and will most likely be included in
a packet-based fronthaul solution. The standards governed by the TSN task group
are at a very early standardization stage. And therefore, research in ﬁnding the best
usage of TSN features is needed to drive the standardization forward.
The project focuses on frame preemption as a network feature and a hardware
implementation of the feature have been completed to illustrate the beneﬁts and
challenges. The implementation is capable of showcasing the beneﬁts of frame pre-
emption. Positive results of jitter and latency reduction have been found when using
frame preemption between two link partners. However, it is still unclear whether the
results are suﬃcient to justify using frame preemption in all deployment scenarios of
a packet-based C-RAN fronthaul. Further studies of network-wide support of frame
preemption will have to be conducted to determine the exact deployment scenarios
where frame preemption is mandatory to fulﬁll the fronthaul requirements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, mobile data traﬃc have continued to grow rapidly. This continuous
growth is caused by the increase in mobile subscriptions and the increased average
data volume per subscription. In 2015 there were 7.3 billion mobile subscriptions
worldwide, this is expected to be 9.1 billion in 2021 [18]. The average monthly data
traﬃc per smartphone in 2015 where 2 GB/month worldwide. According to Ericsson,
this number is expected to be 18 GB/month in 2021. In total, an 11-fold increase
in smartphone data traﬃc from 2015 to 2021 is expected. Cisco predicts an 8-fold
growth in mobile data traﬃc from 2015 to 2020 [12]. For a mobile operator, these
numbers are alarming, and it is clear that extreme measures have to be taken in order
to keep up with the data demand.
Mobile operators are seeking new methods to increase coverage and capacity of the
networks. In recent years, the development of mobile networks has boosted due to
the capacity demand. From the second generation Global System for Mobile commu-
nication (GSM) to fourth generation Long Term Evolution (LTE), the networks have
become more sophisticated and eﬃcient. Even a ﬁfth generation of mobile networks
is expected to be rolled out by 2020 [5]. The rapid development of mobile networks
has to continue in order to keep up with the future data demand.
Deploying and maintaining a mobile network is very costly for operators.
Total
Cost of Ownership (TCO) for mobile networks is divided into CAPital EXpenditure
(CAPEX) and OPerational EXpenditure (OPEX). CAPEX covers the cost of deploy-
ing a network, and can be anything from network planning to hardware components.
OPEX refers to the cost of maintaining a mobile network, like upgrades and electric-
ity. The Radio Access Network (RAN) in a mobile network is the most costly part,
China Mobile indicates that 72% of their expense are used on power for the RAN
[6]. Reducing the power consumption of a cell site will not only reduce operators’
expenses but will also make mobile networks more environment-friendly.
The concept of Cloud-RAN (C-RAN) aims to reduce the power consumption of the
RAN by reusing resources. This is done by a centralization of baseband processing and
thereby utilizing statistical multiplexing of resources. C-RAN will surely introduce
new opportunities for mobile operators to reduce power consumption, but new chal-
lenges will surely arise when deploying a new network architecture. ZTE estimates a
power saving of 67-80% with C-RAN compared to traditional RAN architecture [4].

C-RAN introduces a new RAN architecture which is called the fronthaul and oﬀers a
more scalable, eﬃcient, and robust RAN.
Nokia Siemens Networks suggests that C-RAN will oﬀer 2-5% in CAPEX and 10-20%
in OPEX savings, but these calculations exclude the cost of deploying ﬁber connec-
tions to base stations [11]. A C-RAN network will increase the need for capacity
and synchronization in the RAN, which will require ﬁber connections to all base sta-
tions. Some operators already have dedicated ﬁber connections to base stations, but
deploying or leasing a ﬁber infrastructure is costly and could create an unsustainable
business case.
The capacity demand and timing requirements of a fronthaul network cannot be
supported by other transport technologies than a dedicated ﬁber. However, an ex-
tensive research of enabling a packet-based Ethernet fronthaul is being carried out.
A functional split of processing and resources will allow an adaptive implementation
of C-RAN and will relax the strict requirements for a fronthaul [16]. This could re-
move the need for a dedicated ﬁber connection in some deployment scenarios, but
will require more processing to be performed at the base stations. An ongoing stan-
dardization of Radio over Ethernet (RoE) is being done by IEEE 1903.4 Task Force,
which will enable In-phase Quadrature (I/Q) data transmissions across an Ethernet
based networks. [1]
In order to truly enable an Ethernet fronthaul, more work has to be done. Standard
Ethernet cannot support fronthaul requirements as it is inherently unsynchronized
and non-deterministic with best-eﬀort data delivery. To accommodate for this, the
IEEE 802.1 Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN) Task Group is working on a set of
standards to allow time-synchronized low latency streaming over Ethernet [2]. TSN
capabilities in an Ethernet-based fronthaul could fulﬁll the strict requirements of
bandwidth, latency, jitter and synchronization in the fronthaul. TSNTG is working
on a standard called 802.1CM - Time-Sensitive Networking for Fronthaul, which de-
ﬁnes TSN features, protocol, and more, that are necessary to support time sensitive
fronthaul streams over an Ethernet based network [8].
This thesis will describe the diﬀerent enablers for a packet-based Ethernet fronthaul.
Hardware implementation of a TSN feature will be conducted. This hardware system
will demonstrate the beneﬁts and challenges of deploying TSN in a fronthaul network.

CHAPTER 2
Mobile Network
Architecture
Mobile networks have developed tremendously in the last decades. In 1991, the sec-
ond generation of mobile network Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM)
was rolled out worldwide. It became the ﬁrst globally used technology for mobile
wireless communication. GSM was initially designed for transmitting voice and call
information, back then data services were not considered an application for mobile
networks. The voice was carried over a 13 kbps connection, and the network was built
on a circuit-switched architecture. Later some enhancements as GPRS and EDGE
made the transmission of data services possible, with low data rates up to 384 kbps.
GSM revolutionized the telecommunication industry, by enabling users from all over
the world to dial up each other, no matter their position. Fixed line phones existed
as did other technologies for wireless communication. But GSM made long distance
communication possible on the ﬂy.
In 2001, the third generation of mobile networks was deployed, which used, and
still uses, Wideband Code Division Multiple Access (WCDMA). Universal Mobile
Telecommunication System (UMTS) also known as just 3G, was built to deliver us-
able data rates for multiple users at the same time. Even though later versions of
GSM supported data services, UMTS was developed to increase the data rates dra-
matically. Users could achieve data rates up to 2 Mbps, which increased the user
experience to an acceptable level. Further enhancements to the 3G mobile networks
were introduced over the years to increase the performance of the network services.
In this time period, the smartphone became popular, which opened a new market and
a higher data capacity demand. As of release 11 from Third Generation Partnership
Project (3GPP), which was released in 2013, theoretical data rates of up to 336 Mbps
is possible.
In 2008 the newest mobile network technology Long Term Evolution (LTE) was stan-
dardized in Release 8 from 3GPP. Today 4G networks have more subscriber than
any other network in North America and is expected to have the largest share of
subscribers worldwide in 2021 [18]. LTE can today commercially support downlink
speeds of 300 Mbps, and 3GPP Release 12 deﬁnes a UE category to support 3.9 Gbps
downlink data rates [21]. LTE is based on ﬂat network architecture and is the ﬁrst

2.1
LTE Network Architecture
The architecture of an LTE network can be divided into two parts. One, the Radio
Access Network (RAN) also known as the Evolved UMTS Terrestrial Radio Access
Network (E-UTRAN), which is responsible for the physical radio communication with
User Equipment (UE). Two, the core network, also known as the Evolved Packet Core
(EPC), which handles the transport of data and signaling. Figure 2.1 illustrates the
network architecture of an LTE network.
Figure 2.1: The architecture of a LTE mobile network
The network consists of a number of functional elements, which all have diﬀerent re-
sponsibilities. The network delivers connectivity to multiple UEs in an eﬃcient and
secure manner. The UEs are connected to eNodeBs (eNB), which are the various base
stations deployed to deliver large scale radio coverage. Multiple eNBs are connected
to the EPC via a Serving GateWay (S-GW), which is connected to a PDN GateWay
(P-GW). For signaling purposes, a Mobility Management Entity (MME) and a Home
Subscriber Server (HSS) is located in the core network [22]. All these entities will be

2.1.1
eNodeB (eNB)
An eNB is responsible for the direct wireless connection to the UEs, which is carried
over an LTE-Uu interface. The access technology used to carry the wireless inter-
face from the eNB to the UE uses Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access
(OFDMA) in the DownLink (DL) direction and uses Single Carrier Frequency Divi-
sion Multiple Access (SC-FDMA) in the UpLink (UL) direction. Compared to base
stations in legacy networks such as GSM and UMTS, the eNB in an LTE network is
responsible for more control features. These features were traditionally performed by
a centralized control entity, like a Radio Network Controller (RNC) in UMTS. An
eNB is responsible for radio resource management, user data scheduling, air interface
control signaling, and much more.
Each eNB is connected to the EPC, and can optionally be connected to neighboring
eNBs. Connections from the eNB to the EPC uses an S1 interface, which is split up
into two individual interfaces. An eNB is connected to an MME over an S1-MME
interface, which carries control plane information. An S1-U interface connects the
eNB to an S-GW and carries the user plane traﬃc. These two interfaces are manda-
tory for an eNB to operate. An optional interface called X2 can interconnect two
neighboring eNBs. The X2 interface can carry both control plane information and
user plane traﬃc between two eNBs to perform network functions such as handovers,
load management, and Coordinated MultiPoint (CoMP).
The S1 interface connecting the eNB to the core network has to be transported over
a physical interface. Various physical interfaces can be used for this transport, but
normally ﬁber, copper or microwave radio is used. There are no restrictions on the
physical interface, although it has to support the capacity demand of the eNB. The
choice of physical connection depends on the capacity of the eNB.

because the BBU has to be installed indoor. However, the distance between the BBU
and RRH does not have to be limited, because the ﬁber connection does not have a
high signal power loss.
2.1.2
Serving GateWay (S-GW)
The Serving GateWay (S-GW) is the entity between multiple eNBs and a P-GW. The
S-GW acts a router for the user plane traﬃc in the DL directions, routing data to
the intended eNB. In the opposite UL direction, the data is forwarded from any eNB
to a common P-GW. The S-GW is an aggregation point for user data between the
RAN and the core network.
The S-GW can perform diﬀerent operations such as inter-eNB handover and inter-
technology mobility. In the case of handover, the S-GW can act as an anchor point
for the data connection. If no X2 interface exists between the two eNBs involved in
==PAGE:18==
2.1 LTE Network Architecture
7
the handover, an indirect tunnel can be setup over the S-GW, and thereby allows a
smooth connectivity transition from one base station to another.
It can support inter-3GPP mobility by relaying 2G/3G data traﬃc to the P-GW.
Inter-3GPP mobility enables users to move in between diﬀerent mobile networks,
without losing connectivity. This is beneﬁcial in networks with a mixture of diﬀerent
mobile technologies.
2.1.3
Packet Data Network GateWay (P-GW)
The Packet Data Network GateWay (P-GW) is the entity that connects LTE net-
works to external PDN networks, either owned by the same or another operator. As
the S-GW acted as an anchor for inter-3GPP mobility, the P-GW can act as an an-
chor for non-3GPP. Non-3GPP networks such as Wi-Fi and WiMAX could be used
to oﬄoad data capacity from the LTE network, with users still connected to the same
P-GW.
2.1.4
Mobility Management Entity (MME)
Unlike the S-GW and the P-GW, the Mobility Management Entity (MME) does not
carry any user plane traﬃc. The MME is the main control entity for the whole LTE
network, and it is responsible for paging and authentication of UEs. The MME is
connected to eNBs over the S1-MME interface and is administrating signaling to UEs
during handovers and other network operations.
Together with the HSS the MME keeps track of each UEs location and activity. The
information stored in the MME is used for high-level network functions such as load
balancing, high availability and cell discovery assistance.
2.1.5
Home Subscriber Server (HSS)
The Home Subscriber Server stores all user proﬁle and subscriptions information in
a centralized database. User proﬁle information is used to grant the appropriate ser-
vices and Quality of Service (QoS) the subscriber is inclined to. The service activity
of each UE is monitored and used to charge subscribers depending on their data plan.
Authentication and authorization of each UE are performed by the HSS to only allow
access to legitimate connection requests and reject invalid or malicious requests. Also
ciphering of the user data on the radio interface is performed, to ensure protection
from eavesdropping and alteration of sensitive personal data.

2.1.6
Physical Layer and Channels
The physical radio interface in LTE is the interface connecting all users to the network.
The interface is transmitted in diﬀerent frequency bands dependent on geographical
availability and operator conﬁgurations. The bandwidth of the carrier signal is ﬂexi-
ble with widths from 1.4 to 20 MHz for each carrier.
DL carriers operate with the multiple access scheme OFDMA, which can be modu-
lated with QPSK, 16 QAM 64 QAM, and 256 QAM and use up to 8x8 MIMO. The
modulation of the carrier depends on the radio conditions and the capabilities of the
UE. The highest DL data rate is achievable with UE category 15 and is around 3.9
Gbps with 5 combined 20 MHz carriers modulated with 256 QAM and using 8x8
MIMO. This requires very good radio conditions and a handset capable of receiving
that amount of data.
UL carriers use the SC-FDMA multiple access scheme and can be modulated with
up to 64 QAM. The highest data rate for UL is 1.5 Gbps [21].
A carrier is divided into a number of sub-frames, which is the smallest data frame that
can be assigned to a user. Sub-frames consists of 12 sub-carriers in the frequency do-
main and 14 symbols in the time domain. A single symbol of a sub-carrier is called a
Resource Element (RE), which dependent on the modulation can be converted into a
number of bits (e.g. 6 bits with 64 QAM modulation). Not all REs contains user data,
some are used for diﬀerent control channels. The number of available sub-frames on
an LTE carrier is dependent on the assigned bandwidth of the carrier. A 20 MHz
carrier contains 100 sub-frames per 1 ms, which can be individually scheduled for
diﬀerent users. An illustration of a single sub-frame is shown in ﬁgure 2.3.

2.2
Cloud Radio Access Network (C-RAN)
Cloud Radio Access Network (C-RAN) is an emerging concept of centralized base-
band processing of multiple eNBs. Traditionally, the baseband processing has been
performed at the eNB by the BBU hardware component. Centralizing the baseband
processing of multiple eNBs will enable statistical multiplexing of resource utilization
across the network. In this section, the beneﬁts and challenges of implementing a
C-RAN architecture for a mobile network will be presented and discussed.
One of the biggest concerns for mobile network operators is the power consumption
of their network. High power consumption is a huge problem because it sets an en-
vironmental footprint, and is a large part of the operational expenses. The power
consumption of all telecommunication networks was 4.6% of the total global power
consumption in 2012, which is a high increase from 2007 where the power consumption
of the networks was 3.9% of the total global power consumption [26]. The increase
in power consumption is caused by the growth in data usage and increased cover-
age worldwide. With the expected future boost in mobile data demands, the power
consumption is also expected to increase. Actions to minimize the increase in power
consumption is needed.
When looking at mobile networks the power consumption mainly originates from the
RAN. Around 80% of the total energy consumption of mobile networks is used by the
base stations [17]. Furthermore, the power consumption is only faintly related to the
traﬃc load of the base stations. This means that base stations with low traﬃc load
still consumes about the same amount of power as base stations with high traﬃc load.
The reason is that some data processing is not traﬃc dependent, and the antenna has
to broadcast radio waves constantly.
The intensity of the traﬃc load depends on the time of day and location of the base
station. The load varies throughout the day with low traﬃc load during the night
and high traﬃc load during the daytime. Diﬀerent urban areas in large cities have
diﬀerent patterns of traﬃc load throughout the day. In residential areas the traﬃc
load is highest in the evening when people are at home streaming movies and brows-
ing the Internet.
A well-planned mobile network should be able to support the maximum traﬃc load
in all areas. However, the consequence is that much of the base stations capacity is
unused for long periods of the day, and power is wasted in those periods.

network by centralizing and sharing network resources. Figure 2.4 illustrates an ex-

To support this with distributed BBU processing, each base station needs its own

When sharing resources between the two base stations only a single BBU entity with

Diﬀerent mobile network operators and vendors claim, that C-RAN will introduce
large power savings.
ZTE, which is one of the largest mobile equipment vendors,
estimates that C-RAN will deliver 67-80% power saving, compared to a traditional
RAN architecture [4]. Research from China Mobile, which is China’s largest mobile
operator, shows a power saving of around 71% [7]. In addition to the power savings,
savings in cooling, location rental, and maintenance will also be achieved.
implementing C-RAN in a mobile network will change the network architecture of
the RAN and the equipment needed in base stations.
The BBU will be removed
from multiple base stations and replaced by a centralized BBU pool. The BBU pool
will be directly connected to each base station where the baseband I/Q data will be
transported.
To transmit the I/Q streams a transport protocol called Common Public Radio In-
terface (CPRI) is used, which is a digitized and serial radio interface. The capacity
requirements for the connection between the BBU pool and base stations is higher
than the capacity of a traditional connection to a base station. The data transported
between the BBU pool and RHH is not just simple user data, but the digital wave-
form produced by the BBU. To support the capacity of a CPRI stream, a dedicated
ﬁber connection is required.

2.3
Packet-Based Fronthaul
An alternative transport technology for C-RAN, that is under consideration is Eth-
ernet. Packet-based Ethernet networks are desirable because of their cost of scale
and the widespread deployment of existing networks. However, the requirements for
a fronthaul network cannot be fulﬁlled by a standard Ethernet solution.
Ethernet is a packet-based transport technology, which is prone to congestions from
bursts of traﬃc. Ethernet provides no guarantee of delay, jitter or even delivery of
packets, which is why the fronthaul requirement will be diﬃcult to accommodate.
Extensive research and development of extensions to the Ethernet protocol is being
carried out by diﬀerent working groups, to provide the needed functionalities to sup-
port the fronthaul requirements. Numerous enablers are needed for a true Ethernet
fronthaul solution, some of which will be presented in this section. These enablers
all aim to overcome the challenges of using Ethernet as a transport technology for
C-RAN.
China mobile is one of the driving forces behind researching the Next Generation
Fronthaul Interface (NGFI) [15], that is designed to be more ﬂexible in terms of data

rates and functionalities. This interface is a packet-based approach, set to replace
the existing fronthaul interfaces. A more reﬁned and scalable split of functionalities
between the RRH and the BBU is deﬁned to allow diﬀerent implementations of the
fronthaul link.
The main concept of the functional split is to allow more signal processing to be done
at the base station. This will oﬄoad some of the processing from the BBU pool and
can potentially reduce the required link bandwidth. Five diﬀerent splits have been
proposed to allow a ﬂexible interface between the BBU and RRH.
The functional split simply means where the diﬀerent processes will be performed,
and where the protocol layer stacks will terminate. The ﬁve diﬀerent splits are de-
signed to fulﬁll the needs of diﬀerent implementation and architectures.

ments for both the BBU and fronthaul network. With this split, there is a relaxed
timing requirement for the fronthaul delay of 100 ms, as all time-critical processes are
performed at the RRH alone. The required bandwidth for the fronthaul interface will
be around 180 Mbps, which is quite low. With this split, there will only be limited
processing required at the BBU pool, and the relaxed timing requirements can be
met with various transport technologies.
The calculations of bandwidths requirement for all splits are based on the assump-
tion, of an LTE network with 20MHz carriers, base stations with 2 terminals and 8
antennas, and a maximum of 100 users.
Split 2 is a separation between the MAC and PHY layer, which allows a more cen-
tralized processing at the BBU of high-level MAC processing. This split introduces
a stricter timing requirement, as the Hybrid Automatic Repeat Request (HARQ) is
performed at the BBU. This leaves 1 ms for fronthaul transmission delays. The re-
quired bandwidth for split 2 is also higher, with a peak bandwidth requirement of
225 Mbps.
Split 3 enables a varying bandwidth between the RRH and BBU as no cell level pro-
cessing is performed at the BBU. The total processing is divided into 2 parts, user
data processing, and cell level processing. Cell level processing is independent of the
traﬃc load of the cell, which means that there will be a constant need for processing,
even when the air interface is unused. When placing all the cell level processing at
the RRH, the capacity requirement of the fronthaul network only depends on the user
data. The maximum bandwidth required in the fronthaul with split 3 is 464.6 Mbps.
Split 4 requires more bandwidth in the fronthaul interface, as some of the symbol-level
processing is placed at the BBU. RB allocation information and antenna conﬁgura-
tion are some of the extra data exchanged between the RRH and BBU. This results
in a bandwidth requirement of 2.689 Gbps.
Split 5 is the most transport heavy split, where all the processing is placed at the
BBU, and the transport interface is carrying all the I/Q samples. This requires a
constant data rate of 9.839 Gbps. The bandwidth is independent of the user data
load on the cell. This split resembles the original proposal of a C-RAN, where all the
processing is centralized.

frames and request a retransmission of the corrupted data. LTE requires a retrans-
mission of data only 4 ms after the original transmission. However, when the average
retransmission time exceeds 3 ms, the cell throughput is aﬀected signiﬁcantly. The

Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN) [2] is a common description of the work being
carried out by the TSN Task Group (TSN-TG), which is a part of the IEEE 802.1
working group. The IEEE 802.1 working group is a standard committee that focuses
on Bridging and Architecture for data link layers, i.e. layer 2 of the OSI model. The
working group is divided into four diﬀerent task groups, each with their own area
of expertise. The four groups are Security, Data Center Bridging, OmniRAN, and
Time-Sensitive Networking with the latter being the focus of this chapter.
TSN-TG was formerly known as Audio Video Bridging Task Group (AVB-TG), which
addressed the professional audio and video market. AVB-TG was formed in 2005 and
aimed to allow low-latency streaming of audio and video in an 802.1 network. The
ability to transport synchronized low-latency data over Ethernet was recognized as
an important feature in other networks as well and the scope of the working group
was broadened.
AVB-TG worked on some of the standards that the TSN-TG has taken over and are
working on today, but the focus was only audio and video applications. As these
streaming capabilities became interesting for other areas such as automotive, indus-
trial and transport networks, the scope and name of the task group were changed.
In 2012 the name was changed to Time-Sensitive Networking Task Group and the
scope was broadened to include all general low-latency streaming applications in a
802.1 network.
The main objective of TSN-TG is still to deliver high-speed low-
latency streaming services, and many of the concepts and much of the work done by
AVB-TG were transferred to TSN-TG. Timing and synchronizations, stream reser-
vation, and forwarding and queuing are still among the primary research areas of
TSN-TG, but more amendments and extensions have been added to the project. The
most signiﬁcant of these TSN projects will be discussed in this chapter.

3.1
Time-Sensitive Networking for Fronthaul
As the work of TSN-TG reaches maturity, and the standards become realizable, more
and more industries can gain value from implementing TSN features. Not only the
automotive and industrial areas are applicable for TSN. As of recent years, the ability
to carry time-sensitive data streams over a large scale bridged network has become a
focus for a number of network operators. The cost beneﬁts of a packetized network
and the ability to support time-sensitive application in a standardized manner attract
numerous new industries.
One of the biggest new markets where the use of TSN would enable new functional-
ities is mobile networks. The introduction of C-RAN in a mobile network requires
TSN features to deliver the necessary QoS. This change requires time-sensitive stream
to be transported over long distances in what is referred to as the fronthaul network
(see section 2.2).
TSN-TG has begun to deﬁne proﬁles, conﬁgurations, defaults and procedures for the
use of TSN in a mobile fronthaul network. The purpose is to describe the use of TSN
features, in order to fulﬁll the requirements of the fronthaul streams. Conformance

3.2
Timing and Synchronization
Time synchronization is a key feature in fulﬁlling timing requirements in a packet
based network, like an Ethernet network. Each network element has its own internal
clock, which runs independently of other clocks in the network. Each network ele-
ment will have their own local time-of-day, which might be close to each other, but
not the same. Clock Wander and misconﬁgurations could be the reason why clock
diﬀer throughout the network. No two clock runs at precisely the same rate, as many
outside and inside forces aﬀect the clock rate, like temperature and quality of the
crystal oscillator.
To perform high-precision mapping and de-mapping of data across a network, the
internal clock of all involved network elements have to be synchronized. User data
like audio or radio data can be mapped with an associated presentation time, which
the receiver will use to de-map the data at the precise time it is needed. This requires
the sender and receiver to have the same time-of-day, or else the receiver will present
the data too early or too late. This is one example of why timing and synchronization
can be important in a packet based network.
TSN features like Cyclic Queuing and Forwarding requires network-wide synchroniza-
tions. The feature requires bridges along the path, to schedule the transmission of
frames ahead of time, meaning that certain frames have a designated time slot to be
forwarded from each bridge. All bridges along the path have to be synchronized in
order to set up this scheduling ahead of time. If not, a frame might miss the prede-
termined time slot at the next bridge if it is sent too late.
To synchronize two or more network elements in a packet based network, a protocol
called IEEE 1588 PTP [3] is used, which deﬁnes procedures to synchronize a network
with sub-millisecond accuracy. A PTP network can consist of PTP and non-PTP
devices. The PTP devices are categorized into ﬁve types of devices, ordinary clock,
boundary clock, end-to-end transparent clock, peer-to-peer transparent clock, and
management node. Ordinary clocks are PTP devices with only one PTP enabled
port, which sends and receives PTP-messages.
Boundary clocks are devices with
multiple PTP ports which can connect more ordinary clocks to form a large network.
Transparent clocks forward all packets as a normal switch, but perform measurements
and corrections of reside time on PTP messages.
The ordinary clocks are either assigned to be a slave or a master, representing who
determines the time-of-day to be used in the whole network. The master or grand-
master is elected by a Best Master Clock (BMC) algorithm, which compares the clock
accuracy of each ordinary clock and chooses the most accurate to be the grandmaster.
All slaves synchronize to the grandmasters time-of-day to ensure that the PTP devices
are synchronized with each other. The synchronization is performed by sending small
time-stamped packets throughout the network. All PTP-devices respond to these

3.3
Frame Preemption
Frame Preemption is one of the core elements of the work done in TSN-TG. It enables
a lower jitter and latency for high priority Ethernet frames. Frame preemption, or
just preemption as it will be referred to, solves a well-known problem in packet based
transmission, head of line delay. When a frame is sent from a device, it ﬁrst enters an
output buﬀer, and when all preceding frames have been sent and the link is available
the frame can be sent. The time from the frame ﬁrst enters the output buﬀer to is
it sent out on the link is the head of line latency imposed by other frames in front of
the frame.
Normally some frames have higher priority than others, and two or more output
buﬀers are used. If a frame with high priority i.e. an express frame is to be sent,
it is forwarded to an express buﬀer. The express buﬀer is emptied before all other
buﬀers are. When an express frame enters the buﬀer two types of latency can delay
the frame. The ﬁrst one is if other express frames are present in the buﬀer, they have
to be sent ﬁrst. The second one is, even if the express buﬀer is empty, an ongoing
transmission of the lower priority frame will prevent the express frame from being sent
immediately. Preemption will minimize the delay imposed by ongoing transmission
of lower priority frames.
Frame preemption is described in IEEE 802.1Qbu [14], which speciﬁes procedures and
protocol extensions. IEEE 802.1Qbu is referrers to the IEEE 802.3br [25] Interspers-
ing Express Traﬃc (IET) standard, which is standardized by the IEEE 802.3 working
group. The IET standard deﬁnes the procedures of preempting frames in a packet
based network.
With preemption, an express frame can suspend the ongoing transmission of a lower
priority frame i.e. a preemptable frame, and resume it after the express frame has
been sent. Figure 3.6 illustrates the concept of preemption when an express frame
request transmission.

Preemption might seem to only make a small impact of the express frames delay in
some special cases. The added Inter-Frame Gap (IFG) and CRC bytes will even lower
the link eﬃciency. The preemptable frame will experience an increase in latency. The
operation conﬂicts with some of the core concepts of packet-based networking. Still,
this is a ﬁeld of interest for researchers. When transmitting highly time-sensitive data
even the smallest jitter can interfere with the quality of the data stream. A guarantee
of maximum jitter can be given when using preemption, which is very valuable for
vendors and operators. Even with all the downsides, preemption solves some of the
limitations of time-sensitive packet based networking.
A preemptable frame can be preempted any number of times by more express frames,
and more express frames can be sent before resuming transmission of the preemptable
frame. Each time a preemptable frame is preempted an mCRC ﬁeld and IFG bytes
are added after the fragment. Each non-ﬁnal fragment of a preempted contains an
extra mCRC ﬁeld, which is an FCS of the fragment with the ﬁrst two bytes inverted.
Frame preemption will minimize the delay and jitter of express frames, but it will

delay of express frames with preemption, depends on the minimum fragment size.
Each fragment of a preemptable frame has to be at least 64 bytes, which is the min-
imum packet size in Ethernet. The minimum fragment size can be conﬁgured to be
min_frag_size = 64bytes+(n∗64bytes), n = {0, ..3} and the maximum delay experi-

preempted. 12 bytes of IFG will be sent before the express frame can be sent. If the
minimum fragment size is set to 64 bytes, the maximum delay that an express frame
can experience is 135 byte-times. The minimum delay that can be experienced is 18
byte-times, 4 mCRC bytes and 12 IFG bytes.

Enhancements for Scheduled Traﬃc
Enhancements for scheduled traﬃc uses a concept of opening and closing transmis-
sion for diﬀerent types of traﬃc. One simple way to guarantee bandwidth to one type
of traﬃc would be to allow only that particular type of traﬃc to be sent for a time
period. This method will certainly guarantee bandwidth to any type of traﬃc, and
that is the core concept used in enhancements for scheduled traﬃc.
To guarantee high priority time-sensitive traﬃc a speciﬁc bandwidth, all other types
of traﬃc can be halted for a time period. A high priority Constant Bit Rate (CBR)
data stream like CPRI, requires a predetermined bandwidth to operate. If this band-
width could be allocated to the high priority stream, it would always be able to
operate, even in high congestion networks. Figure 3.10 shows an example of how this
could be realized with three diﬀerent types of traﬃc.

The high priority stream is allocated a certain percentage of the bandwidth each time

The scheduling principle of Enhancements for Scheduled Traﬃc, managed object and
extensions to existing protocols are described in IEEE 802.1Qbv [13]. The time-aware
procedures assume network synchronization provided by IEEE 802.1AS [9]. To dif-
ferentiate between types of traﬃc, the Virtual Local Area Network (VLAN) Priority
Code Point (PCP) tag can be used. The PCP is a 3-bit ﬁeld in the VLAN tag, that
is used to deﬁne 8 traﬃc classes, which refers to the IEEE 802.1p priority levels.
Enhancements for scheduled traﬃc, is an enhancement of transmission selection for
any time-aware end-station or switch. It implements a time-aware shaper for diﬀerent
traﬃc classes, where all classes have their own transmission indicator. This transmis-
sion indicator, which is also referred to as a gate, can be in one of two states, open
or closed. If the gate is open the traﬃc class can be scheduled, otherwise it cannot.
Transmission of a single frame cannot be scheduled if the gate is set to close during
the transmission. This can be diﬃcult to provision but diﬀerent methods can be used.
One way to satisfy this is to set up a guard period before gate transition from open
to closed. No frames can be sent right before the gate is about to close. This will
lower the eﬀective bandwidth, proportional with the number of state transitions.
Another way to be sure to never send a frame during a gate transition, is to know
the length of each frame. The length of the frame is compared to the remaining time
before the gate transition, and thereby avoiding an active frame during gate state
transition.

3.5
Frame Replication and Elimination for Reliability
(FRER)
Frame Replication and Elimination for Reliability (FRER) is an enhancement to in-
crease the reliability of a TSN stream across a network. Time-sensitive streams cannot
wait for retransmission of lost frames, because of the added delay. This is why a reli-
able connection between endpoints is crucial for the performance of any TSN network.
If a network experiences a high Bit Error Rate (BER), FRER can be used to lower the
BER. For example, CPRI requires a BER of 10−12 to operate, which is relatively low
==PAGE:48==
3.5 Frame Replication and Elimination for Reliability (FRER)
37
compared to an Ethernet network. If you would transport a CPRI stream over an Eth-
ernet network, you would have to lower the BER and make the network more reliable.
The general idea of FRER is to duplicate each frame of a TSN stream and trans-
port it over a diﬀerent paths in the network. This requires two main features to be
added to the network, being able to setup multiple paths in the between two end
nodes and distinguishing between duplicate frames at the destination node. The ﬁrst
requirement of setting up multiple paths is subject of other standards such as IEEE
802.1Q or IEEE 802.1Qcc [20]. Distinguishing between duplicate frames and the be-
havior of talkers, listeners and bridges is described in the IEEE 802.1CB standard [10].
Sequence numbers are used to identify duplicate frames in a stream. The same se-
quence number is inserted in each duplicate of a frame, which is then transported
over diﬀerent paths. The sequence number is then used to identify duplicates and
allows the receiver to discard one of them. The ﬁrst arriving duplicate of a frame will
be acknowledged and forwarded, the last arriving duplicate will be discarded. If just
one of the duplicates is lost, the system will not be eﬀected by it, which is the main
beneﬁt of FRER. If one of the two stream completely fails, there will essentially be no
recovery time as the working stream will be active during recovery of the failed stream.
Figure 3.13: Illustration of FRER from a single talker to a single listener
Figure 3.13 shows how a stream can be duplicated and transported over diﬀerent
paths in the network, and received by the talker as one stream. The two streams are
referred to as member streams, and the member streams make up a single compound
stream. The data content of each member stream is identical except for the stream
identiﬁcation. The ﬁgure only show a point-to-point connection, but the stream could
also be point-to-multipoint. More listeners could subscribe to the stream, and the
individual member streams would be disjoint point-to-multipoint connections.

into one compound stream, by eliminating duplicates. Individual recovery is identi-

frames from the lower layers and forward them upwards after recovery. Stream re-
covery is generally deﬁned as a part of the sequencing function, but is closely related
to, and similar to the individual recovery function.

frame level. This can be done in various ways, and have yet to be clearly deﬁned.

3.6
Stream Reservation Protocol (SRP)
The Streams Reservation Protocol (SRP) is a protocol used to reserve resources in a
network to support a certain data stream. If resources are reserved on a network wide
scale, the stream will always be able to meet its external requirements. The protocol
is a layer-2 protocol that can reserve bandwidth along a path with a latency guarantee.
SRP utilizes three signaling protocols, Multiple MAC Registration Protocol (MMRP),
Multiple VLAN Registration Protocol (MVRP), and Multiple Stream Reservation
Protocol (MSRP), for reservation of resources across a network.
.
MMRP is a
layer-2 protocol which registers multicast MAC addresses to ports in switches and
end-stations. The registrations of certain ports conﬁne the traﬃc to the required ar-
eas of a network. MVRP is a protocol to automatically conﬁgure VLAN information
on switches along a desired path. If a switch port is added to a VLAN only the one
port needs to be conﬁgured, all other VLAN trunks in the network are automatically
setup on devices by MVRP.
MSRP is used to propagate declarations between Talkers and Listeners, with the
main purpose of issuing a success or fail status for a stream reservation at setup. If
a stream cannot be supported by any single link on the path, the stream will be
declared as failed. This is a vital feature in order to guarantee a QoS for any or all
streams, because non-conforming streams can be excluded. A single non-conforming
data steam could potentially cause other streams to violate their requirements.
Setting up a stream between a listener and a talker requires two main announcements,
the supply and demand for a stream, and transmission ready. The supply for a stream
is advertised by the talker using an oﬀering declaration. A listener can demand a
stream by using a asking declaration. If a listener is asking for a stream and a talker
is oﬀering the same stream, the a ready declaration is sent from the listener to the
==PAGE:51==
3.6 Stream Reservation Protocol (SRP)
40
talker. Both is asking and oﬀering declaration contains characteristics of the stream,
such as max latency or bandwidth. If any of the connecting links or switches cannot
support the stream a failure declaration will be sent to talkers, listeners, and involved
switches. This ensures that no network device unnecessarily allocates and reserves
resources for a stream that the network cannot support.
The standard IEEE 802.1Qat speciﬁes protocols and procedures for SRP. IEEE
802.1Qcc, which is a standard under the TSN-TG, speciﬁes enhancements to the
MSRP procedure, and a software interface between time-sensitive applications and
network components. The newer version of the MSRP protocol, called MSRPv1, have
enhanced stream attributes that are propagated throughout the network, to enable a
better conﬁguration of stream reservations. MSRPv1 also supports the new software
interface, called User/Network Interface (UNI). The UNI interface is a simple inter-
face that allows talkers and listeners, which are the Users, to conﬁgure TSN functions
in bridges, which are the Network, without a detailed knowledge of the network.
Conﬁgurations of TSN features like Frame Preemption and Enhancements for Sched-
uled Traﬃc are detailed in the IEEE 802.1Qcc standard. Conﬁguration of switches
in a TSN network is done with the UNI interface, and will allow a tight conﬁguration
of time-sensitive streams across a network. The modeling of conﬁguration informa-
tion can be done with various modeling languages such as MIB and YANG, which
is used to create textual abstract objects.
This allows any management protocol
that can carry textual information, such as SNMP and NETCONF, to be used. The
UNI interface is simply an interface to carry various protocols for TSN conﬁgurations.

3.7
Cyclic Queuing and Forwarding
Cyclic Queuing and Forwarding (CQF) enables a true deterministic delay, even
through a large network. The traﬃc shaping method used distributes the delay for
multiple streams and minimizes jitter for any time-sensitive stream. This is done by
blocking frames from incoming streams for a short period of time, to allow a determin-
istic forwarding. In principle this is done by implementing two time divided queues
in each switch. The queues alternate between receiving and sending opposite of each
other. In odd time periods queue 1 receives incoming frames from multiple streams
and queue 2 sends the frames in the queue. In even time periods queue 1 sends the
frames received in the previous time period, and queue 2 receives incoming frames.
This continues in a cyclic manner.
The reason for using CQF in a large network is the ability to easily predict the exact
delay of any stream, as the delay only is eﬀected by the number of hops, if conﬁgured
correctly. The duration of the time period is a critical factor to consider in CQF. If
chosen to be too short some frames can miss the time slot, and if chosen too long,
==PAGE:54==
3.7 Cyclic Queuing and Forwarding
43
the end-to-end delay will increase. Determining the duration of a time period, also
called T, is done by calculating the maximum transmission time between any two
devices in the network, including queuing delay and transmission delay. This will
ensure that all frames from one time period is received before they are sent. The
operation also requires all switches to align the time periods, to enable network wide
delay guarantee. The can be done by time synchronizing the network with 1588 PTP.
The standard describing CQF, IEEE 802.1Qch, is at a very early stage. Currently
it refers to IEEE 802.1Qci Per-Stream Filtering and Policing (PSFP) and IEEE
802.1Qbv Enhancements for Scheduled Traﬃc as the functionalities needed to sup-
port CQF. PSFP can be used to direct streams to one of the two egress queues on
a time basis, alternating each time period T. In section 3.8 the operation of PSFP
is described, and a simple conﬁguration of the stream gate instance identiﬁer would
alternate between assigning two diﬀerent Internal Priority Speciﬁcations (IPSs) each
time period. An illustration of a switch implementation of IEEE 802.1Qch is shown

Per-Stream Filtering and Policing
The main purpose of Per-Stream Filtering and Policing (PSFP) is to perform QoS
functions on individual TSN streams at frame level. Basic QoS functions can be used
on a stream, to ensure conformance of the speciﬁed behavior. A single non-conforming
stream in a TSN network can potentially compromise all or any time-sensitive stream.
TSN oﬀers options to conﬁgure and transmit conforming streams based on a wide set
of parameters, where the talker has to conform to the characteristics of the stream.
Scheduling with IEEE 802.1Qbv will assign streams a certain percentage of the band-
width. However, non-conforming streams can occur in a TSN network for a number
of reasons, faulty hardware, faulty software or malicious attacks.

Timing and Synchronization ensures a more robust and precise distribution of the
ToD to the whole network, but requires a careful construction and conﬁguration of
the network.
Frame Preemption minimizes the head of queue delay for express traﬃc, and thereby
reduces the total delay and jitter for time-sensitive streams.
Enhancements for Scheduled Traﬃc will allow a network wide scheduling of streams

of the switches along the path of any ﬂow, as any misconﬁguration could violate the
requirements of the stream.
FRER oﬀers a lower drop rate of time critical frames by replicating them and sending
them over diﬀerent paths, and thereby increasing the chance of reception.
SRP can be used to manage a TSN network by enabling diﬀerent management pro-
tocols to conﬁgure individual TSN feature. The ability to use diﬀerent management
topologies enables a tailored solution for many applications.
CQF establishes a deterministic scheduling for any or all types of traﬃc by switches
synchronously sending and receiving in timeslots. This makes the latency easy to
calculate, as it only depends on the number of hops.
PSFP allows a switch to perform a number of operations on a stream basis, by ﬁlter-
ing incoming traﬃc.
All these features all play a role in implementing a TSN network for any usage, and

of Inter-Frame Gap (IFG), preamble and Start of Frame Delimiter (SFD), are the
central features of an MAC layer. These functions and their design are described in
section 5.3.
The system is build on a 10 Gigabit Media Independent Interface (XGMII) interface,
which is a 64-bit parallel data interface located between the TSN MAC and the phys-
ical layer. The interface is described in the standard IEEE 802.3 clause 46. The
interface exists in two variations, a 32-bit interface at 312.5 MHz and a 64- bit inter-
face at 156.25 MHz. The 64-bit interface has been chosen for this implementation,
because of the relatively low achievable clock speeds on an FPGA chip. However, the
64-bit interface requires a larger data path.
Figure 5.2: XGMII interface and control messages
Figure 5.2 illustrates the structure of the interface, with 8 control bits and 8 data
bytes each clock cycle. The Start byte is always aligned to the ﬁrst lane and is fol-
lowed by the preamble. The position of the SFD is right before the ﬁrst data byte.

The main purpose of the MMS component is to perform Frame Preemption. Priority

sub-components and their purpose are described in detail this section.

The HOLD/RELEASE control signal can be used by the client layer, to ”HOLD” all

The minimum fragment size is a parameter that determines the minimum size of any
fragment created by frame preemption. This value can be set to 64, 128, 192 or 256

changed at runtime. With further development of Enhancements for Scheduled Traf-

Transmit Processing
Transmit processing is the sub-component controlling the merging and transmission
of express and preemptable frames. In this component, the essential mechanism of

the preemptable_buﬀer.
If an express frame is received by the MMS, it cannot be transmitted right away. A
preemptable frame might need to be preempted, which takes a short period of time.
In this period of time, the express frame needs to be buﬀered, which is done in the
express_buﬀer. While an express frame is being transmitted, the preemptable_buﬀer
will store incoming preemptable data.
Both buﬀers can be relatively small because they only need to contain the small por-

layer.

5.2.3
Validate
Validation is an optional feature that ensures the link partner also supports frame
preemption. This is controlled by a frame based handshake procedure that both link
partners participate in. At startup, a special frame, designed to only be recognizable
by other devices supporting Frame Preemption, is transmitted. If the other device
recognizes the frame, it replies with a similar frame to indicate that Frame Preemp-
tion is supported. Frame Preemption can be enabled as soon as the special frame is
received, either as a reply or request.
The special frame request contains an SMD-V (0x07) as the SFD, and the response
frame contains an SMD-R (0x19). The frame contains all zeros, and an mCRC value
at the end. This frame would under normal operation be ignored or discarded as the
FCS is incorrect.
Protocols for exchanging capabilities with a link partner already exist, such as Link
Layer Discovery Protocol (LLDP). However, these protocols cannot be used to ex-
change Frame Preemption capabilities, as some network devices or switches are trans-
parent to the link layer protocols. This means that it is not always the true link
partner that replies, but it can also be a link partner several hops away that replies.
Frame Preemption requires a response from the direct link partner, to establish frame
preemption. Or else, all preemptable traﬃc could be discarded.

5.2.4
Receive Processing
Receive Processing is the sub-component that reassemble preempted frames, received
on the external XGMII interface. Like the Express Filter sub-component, the Receive
Processing sub-component is connected to the receiver part of the XGMII interface.
Unlike the Express Filter, Receive Processing only receives preemptable frames. The
frames are forwarded to the pMAC for further processing. When a preemptable frame
is being received, there is no way of knowing how many fragments, if any, the frame
is divided into. The whole frame or all fragments have to be received before it can be
forwarded to pMAC. Otherwise, the absence of one fragment could be disruptive for
the entire operation.
If fragments are forwarded to the MAC layer when they are received, they would not
contain an FCS for integrity validation. This is why the entire frame needs to be
resembled, to ensure the FCS can determine the integrity of the entire frame. The
incoming frames are reassembled in the buﬀer of the Receive Processing.
To be sure that subsequent fragments are fragments of the same frame, the SMD con-
tains sequencing information. A frame contains an SMD, which is also used in all its
fragments. This SMD can take one of four values, depending on the sequence of the
==PAGE:76==
5.3 Media Access Control
65
frame. Furthermore, the non-initial fragments also include another SMD value telling
the sequence of the fragments of a single frame. The four values of the two types of
SMDs are repeated in sequence, for the receiver to identify the order of frames and
fragments. Thereby, the loss of a single frame or fragment can be detected by the re-
ceiver. If four four consecutive fragments or frames are dropped, it cannot be detected.
Figure 5.8: Design of Receive Processing sub-component
The size of the buﬀer depends on the maximum frame size, as only one frame needs
to be buﬀered at a time. Jumbo frames are not supported by the implementation,
therefore a buﬀer size of 256 (28) slots is suﬃcient to contain a 1500 byte frame. The
Fragments of a frame are reassembled in the buﬀer to create whole frames to forward.
This is done by identifying the SMDs to detect missing fragments, and by checking
the mCRC ﬁeld of the non-ﬁnal fragments.
5.3
Media Access Control
The Media Access Control (MAC) layer in the network stack, is a layer with well-
known features and capabilities. The design for this implementation is an MAC with
limited features, but still satisfactory for the intended operation. The procedures are
split into two directions of data, which are independent of each other. The receiver
part performs the CRC-check of the FCS ﬁeld, to ensure integrity. The transmit part
performs DIC procedures, to maintain an average IFG of 12 bytes.
==PAGE:77==
5.3 Media Access Control
66
Figure 5.9: Design of MAC components
The MAC is structured after a cut-through design. Normally an ingress and egress
buﬀer is placed in the MAC layer, but these have removed to keep the delay low. With
a cut-through design, the upper layers have to be cut-through aware because frames
will be forwarded before their FCS is checked. The MAC can only signal errors at
the end of the frame, and at that point, the frame could be used or forwarded further
on. In a switch, this will cause ﬂawed or damaged frames to be forwarded further in
the network. In end-stations, the integrity will have to be checked before the frame
can be used, or else malicious or invalid frames could cause malfunctions. This can
still be done with some modiﬁcations to the higher layers.
This project has been designed interface with another simultaneously developed project.
A RoE de/mapper have been developed alongside the TSN MAC, to build a part of
a mobile end-station transceiver. The RoE de/mapper and the TSN MAC have been
closely designed to communicate with each other. Because the MAC layer is designed
as cut-through, the RoE de-mapper buﬀers an entire frame before de-mapping it.
This allows the error indication to arrive before the frame is de-mapped for further
use.
Because of the structure of the XGMII interface, the IFG have to be designed in a
special way. If two frames are sent directly afterward each other, the number of idle
frames cannot always be 12 bytes, which is mandatory. This is because a frame can
==PAGE:78==
5.3 Media Access Control
67
Terminate at any lane, but the Start has to be aligned to lane 0. This is managed by
the Deﬁcit Idle Count (DIC) feature in the transmit part of the MAC.
The buﬀer is used in the transmitter to compensate for the extra IFG that is inserted
between frames. If a constant stream of frames from the upper layer comes back-to-
back, the small IFG would cause a small buildup of unused capacity of the MAC.
Additionally, it is always wise to implement egress buﬀers, as transmission from dif-
ferent internal sources can cause congestion.
5.3.1
Receiver
The main objective of the MAC receiver is to perform a CRC-check. This is done with
a standard CRC-32 with the polynomials algebraic representation of 0x04C11DB7.
Figure 5.10 show a CRC-8 design, with the same structure as the CRC-32 design
used in the implementation.
The example in ﬁgure 5.10 uses a polynomials with
algebraic representation 0x25, and only have 8-bit shift register. The CRC-32 design
uses the 0x04C11DB7 polynomial and has a 32-bit shift register.
Figure 5.10: 8-bit shift register CRC implementation
The incoming Ethernet data is feed bit-by-bit into the shift register and forwarded
without alterations. When all bits have been feed into the shit register, from the ﬁrst
bit of the destination address to the last bit of the FCS ﬁeld, the residue of the shift
register for a valid frame is 0xC704DD7B. If the residue is not equal to 0xC704DD7B,
then the frame has encountered an error and has to be discarded.
5.3.2
Transmitter
The main feature implemented in the transmitter is the DIC, which maintains an
average IFG of 12 bytes. 12 bytes of IFG on Ethernet links is mostly a legacy fea-
ture, which allows a port to switch from transmitting to receiving in the gap between
frames. However, the IFG is still a feature that is supported on many devices, even
==PAGE:79==
5.3 Media Access Control
68
though bidirectional interfaces are used.
The need for DIC lies in the structure of the XGMII interface. XGMII sends 8 bytes
of data each clock cycle, and the Start control word has to be aligned to lane 0 for
each frame. This means that the IFG cannot be constant all the time because the
Terminate control word can be sent in any lane.
Figure 5.11: Example of an end of frame with Terminate a position 5
Figure 5.11 show an example of an IFG that is not 12 bytes because the Terminate
control word is in the ﬁfth position. There are 11 idle bytes between the end of the
ﬁrst frame and the beginning of the next frame (Terminate included). If this struc-
ture of the frames continues, the average IFG would be 11 bytes and not 12 bytes.
This could be compensated for, with an extra clock cycle of 8 Idle bytes inserted after
every eighth frame. This would keep the average IFG at 12 bytes. Now imagine that
the Terminate control word arrives at a random position every time, then the IFG
accounting becomes diﬃcult to administrate. For this purpose the DIC algorithm is
developed.
A DIC value keeps track of the balance of Idle byte that needs to be sent. If an excess
of Idle bytes is sent, a whole clock cycle of Idle bytes are removed, and likewise, if
too few Idle bytes are sent, an extra clock cycle of Idle bytes is inserted. The initial
DIC value is 4, and if a single excess Idle byte is sent the value decreases with 1, to a
DIC value of 3. The DIC values can vary between 0 and 7, and indicates how many
excess Idle bytes are sent. Dependent on the position of the Terminate byte and the
DIC value, the number of clock cycles containing only Idle bytes, can be determined.

The frame generators transmit frames with an exponentially distributed interval, and

data stream, that can be used to test the TSN MAC properly.

Simulation Test 3: Maximum Transmission Delay
The last feature tested is the maximum delay an express frame can experience. This
is one of the requirements of the TSN MAC, 123 byte-times (98.4 ns) should be
the maximum delay imposed by preemptable frames. The delay experienced by the
express frame can be observed in the transmit_processing sub-component. If a 123
byte long preemptable frame is transmitted right before an express frame, the express
frame has to wait for the entire preemptable frame to be transmitted. This is the
worst case delay an express frame should suﬀer.
The Ethernet frame generators are carefully conﬁgured to transmit a 123 bytes long
preemptable frame and an express frame at the right time, to produce the worst case
delay for an express frame. The scenario will be examined in a waveform of the rele-
==PAGE:92==
6.3 Simulation
81
vant signals.
In theory, the worst case delay is experienced with a 123 bytes long preemptable
frame immediately before an express frame. Because the granularity of an XGMII
interface is 8 bytes per clock cycle, this is not true for a 10 Gbps Ethernet link. The
length of the preemptable frame can be between 120 and 127 bytes long and will then
produce the same delay of 128 byte-times (102.4 ns). If the preemptable frame was
128 bytes long, it would be preempted by the express frame, and the delay would only
be around 64 byte-times (51.2 ns)

Figure 6.10 shows the waveform of the relevant signals during max delay scenario

At 134.4 ns the preemptable frame arrives from the pMAC and is then partially stored
in a buﬀer before being transmitted. At 185.6 ns the express frame is received, and
right before it can be transmitted the preemptable frame starts transmission at 217.6
ns. The timing and size of the preemptable frame will ensure, that transmission of
the express is only possible after a complete transmission of the preemptable frame.
At 320 ns the express frame is ﬁnally transmitted, 134.4 ns after it was received.
This simulation shows a higher delay than expected, but there are diﬀerent reasons
for this. First, the normal delay though the transmit_processing sub-component is
not subtracted from the result. Second, the IFG after the preemptable frame is also
included in the result. If both these delays are subtracted, the expected delay of 102.4
ns is achieved. This is higher that the theoretical maximum delay of 98.4 ns that are
used in IEEE 802.1Qbu, but this implementation allows a slightly higher delay.

6.3.2
Results
Further testing of the TSN MAC has been performed, to illustrate the impact of im-
plementing Frame Preemption on a link layer basis. The same simulations setup have
been used as shown in ﬁgure 6.7. All tests have been performed with and without
frame preemption, to show the diﬀerence between a normal priority queuing imple-
mentation and a frame preemption implementation.
The delay of a single frame is measured by a timestamp inserted at the start of the
frame at generation. The Ethernet frame generator replaces the ﬁrst eight data bytes
with a timestamp of the current system clock. When the analyzer receives a frame,
the timestamp is compared with the ongoing system clock, and the delay of the frame
==PAGE:94==
6.3 Simulation
83
can be measured.
The jitter or Frame Delay Variation (FDV) is calculated by the time diﬀerence be-
tween the maximum and minimum delay of frames. It is assumed that the network
setup is static, and the delay will not drift over time, which is also the case for this
setup.
Four test scenarios have been tested to show the implications of frame preemption on
delay and jitter. The Ethernet Frames Generators have been conﬁgured to produce
frames corresponding to a certain percentage of the link load. All individual test
was run for 500 µs, which allows the bias due to initial conditions to be removed,
but no statistic or probability theory has been applied to estimate this simulation
duration. These simulations follow no guidelines for modeling and simulating, and
do not include conﬁdence intervals or any advanced probability calculations, but are
only performed to give an estimate of the performance. The setup could be used to
produce results with higher precision, but this is not in the scope of this project.
Test with both an equal distribution of traﬃc load and with changing distributions
of traﬃc have been performed. All frames generated have a uniformly distributed
random length, and the interval between frames is exponentially distributed. To sim-
ulate a higher link load, the random intervals between frames are shortened, this is
done by changing the probability of transmission of a frame. In general, a higher
probability of transmission means a higher link load.

6.4
Summary
This chapter gives a detailed overview of the speciﬁc implementation of the TSN
MAC component. Top-level signals and generics have been provided, to show the
how the component is integrated into a system. The MicroBlaze software core used
for conﬁguration and management along with its interface to the TSN MAC are ex-
plained. The soft core enables a dynamic and quick conﬁguration at runtime, which
is preferable for extensive hardware testing.
A number of tests have been conducted. Low-level behavioral testing of individual
sub-components has been performed to verify the expected behavior. High-level sim-
ulations of diﬀerent traﬃc patterns have been performed to verify the capabilities of
the TSN MAC. And last simulations of frame preemption have been conducted, to
illustrate the impact on a link partner basis.
The simulations all showed positive results, and a better understanding of the impact
of frame preemption have been given. The requirements from chapter 4 have been
compared with the implemented features. Most of the requirements haven been ful-
ﬁlled, and only a few have been postponed for later implementation.

7.2
Integrated System Testing
An integrated system testing of an entire LTE setup has been performed with the TSN
MAC serving as the MAC layer. The integrated system includes other functional hard-
ware implementations intended for deployment in a mobile fronthaul network. Layer
1 oﬄoading of LTE signals, according to spit 4 in the NGFI, have been implemented
to reduce the traﬃc intensity between an RRH and a BBU. A Radio over Ethernet
(RoE) mapper and de-mapper have been implemented to encapsulate the LTE signal
in Ethernet frames.
Layer 1 oﬄoading and RoE mappers have been implemented by others, with the in-
tention to build a fully functional LTE transport system for use in a fronthaul network.
Test Setup
A static LTE frame has been constructed in MatLab and is stored in a ROM in the
FPGA. The LTE signal is then read from the ROM and passed through a BBU gen-
erator block, that performs signal processing to mimic the signal generated in a BBU.
The BBU LTE signal is then mapped into Ethernet frames in a RoE mapper. These
Ethernet frames are then forwarded to the TSN MAC and treated as express frames.
The Ethernet Frame Generator is included to resemble preemptable traﬃc, and to
test the ability to run an LTE signal over a busy connection.
Frames are sent over the ﬁber connection and received by another instance of the
TSN MAC. The frames are de-mapped by a RoE receiver. The inverse function of
the signal processing is performed to regenerate the original LTE signal. All this is
evaluated by outputting the LTE signal to an LTE spectrum analyzer. A Digital to
Analog Converter (DAC) evaluation board is used to produce an analog signal to the
spectrum analyzer.

7.3
Summary
This chapter serves as a recap of the testing performed in physical hardware.
An isolated test has been performed, with the intention to reproduce the results gen-
erated in simulations. The results show an almost similar behavior of the TSN MAC
in hardware as in simulations. There are however some small diﬀerences, that can be
caused by errors in the implementation.
An integrated system test of the TSN MAC in a larger system has also been conducted.
The purpose of this test is to verify the performance of an entire LTE fronthaul trans-
port link. An LTE signal is generated, encapsulated, transmitted, received and then
evaluated on a spectrum analyzer. This test shows satisfactory performance with
no background traﬃc on the link. However, the introduction of background traﬃc
causes the entire system to fail.
The errors of the TSN MAC that are identiﬁed in the hardware test, indicate the
current state of the TSN MAC implementation. The implementation is at its ﬁnal
stages, but still require some debugging before it is complete.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
An implementation of frame preemption has been completed to illustrate the bene-
ﬁts and challenges of the network feature. This implementation of frame preemption
serves as a reference for a discussion on the usage of the TSN in mobile networks.
Results from simulations of the implementation are provided to illustrate the impact
frame preemption between two end stations.
The simulations clearly show a beneﬁt of using frame preemption, compared to tradi-
tional scheduling methods. Jitter and latency of express traﬃc can be reduced when
using frame preemption on a transport link. The jitter can be reduced to around the
twice length of the minimum fragment size. The maximum jitter experienced with a
minimum fragments size of 64 bytes is around 100 ns on a 10 Gbps connection. The
jitter experienced in a traditional setup is equal to the maximum size of the back-
ground traﬃc, which is 1200 ns with a maximum frame size of 1500 bytes.
There are many components of jitter in a network, and the jitter depends on several
parameters such as frame size, buﬀer size, the number of hops, and traﬃc intensity.
For simplicity, this discussion will not include buﬀer sizes and traﬃc intensity, but
only the number of hops and frame size.
A simple equation of jitter experienced by an express stream using frame preemption
can be described as:
jitterframepreemption = number_of_hops × 100ns.
The same calculation without frame preemption would be:
jitternormal = number_of_hops × 1200ns.
The jitter requirement initially proposed in the IEEE 802.1CM standard was 5 µs or
10% of the transmission time, see table 3.2. If the equations for jitter are used, the
maximum number of hops for a stream with and without frame preemption can be
determined. For a stream without frame preemption, the maximum number of hops
is 4 (4.8 µs jitter), and for streams with frame preemption the maximum number of
hops is 50 (5 µs jitter). These calculations assume no other source of jitter in the
==PAGE:110==
8 Discussion
99
network and a total link latency of ≤50 µs.
If other sources of jitter are taken into account, the maximum number of hops will be
reduced. This limits deployments without frame preemption to only a few hops per
stream. Whereas, deployments with frame preemption will be able to have a higher
number of hops between the BBU and RRH. This also means that frame preemption
is not always required in a fronthaul network.
When looking at the latency requirement, frame preemption also helps to reduce the
latency of a stream. The latency can be split into diﬀerent components when trans-
porting over a network. Propagation time, internal switching and jitter are the three
large components of latency in a network. Frame preemption only eﬀects the latency
introduced by jitter and not the propagation and switching latency. Frame preemp-
tion has a small eﬀect on the total latency of a stream because it reduces the jitter.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The recent interest in C-RAN opens up for a large number of opportunities for mobile
operators. Large savings, easy network deployment, and a more ﬂexible equipment
installations are some of the main beneﬁts of switching to a C-RAN architecture.
However, there are some challenges when deploying a C-RAN network. Increased
transport capacity and strict latency requirements have to be supported in the fron-
thaul transport network.
Today, a C-RAN deployment would require a dedicated ﬁber connection to support
the extra capacity and fulﬁll the latency requirement. A dedicated ﬁber connection is
expensive to roll out for each base stations in a mobile network. An entire network of
dedicated ﬁber connections to every base station is a large investment for an operator.
Therefore other alternatives are being researched.
In comparison to a dedicated ﬁber network, a packet-based Ethernet network would
be more economical to deploy on a large scale. Many operators already own a packet-
based infrastructure, that is used to connect the base station in a traditional LTE
network architecture. Despite this, a standard packet-based network cannot meet the
timing and latency requirement of a fronthaul network.
IEEE 802.1CM is a standard describing the use of Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN)
in a mobile fronthaul network. TSN is one of the enablers for a packet-based fronthaul
network, which allows time-synchronized low-latency streaming in a packet-based net-
work. The main objective of TSN is to enable packet-based networks to deliver low-
latency streaming services, and many industries are now requiring this feature.
TSN oﬀers a wide range of network operations to suit multiple implementations and
deployments. For fronthaul networks, frame preemption is one of the more prominent
features, and it is the most likely feature to be included in a packet-based fronthaul
solution. All the standards governed by the TSN task group are at a very early stan-
dardization stage. And therefore, research in ﬁnding the best usage and development
of TSN features is needed to drive the standardization forward.
An implementation of frame preemption has been completed to illustrate the ben-
eﬁts and challenges of the TSN feature. This implementation of frame preemption
is meant to serve as a reference for a full implementation. Some control features of
==PAGE:112==
9.1 Future Work
101
the frame preemption implementation are excluded because of a limited development
time frame. Still, the implementation is capable of showcasing the beneﬁts of frame
preemption.
Positive results of jitter and latency reduction have been found when using frame pre-
emption. However, it is still unclear whether the results are suﬃcient to justify using
frame preemption in all deployment scenarios of a packet-based fronthaul. Further
studies of network-wide support of frame preemption will have to be conducted to
determine the exact deployment scenarios where frame preemption is mandatory to
fulﬁll the fronthaul requirements.
9.1
Future Work
The implementation of frame preemption is only partially completed. Some of the con-
trol features are missing, and hardware tests indicate implementation errors, which
causes an extensive frame drop rate. All this have to be added and ﬁxed before the
development is considered complete. The current state of the implementation serves
as a scarce but functional base for further development. The implementation allows
for easy development because the diﬀerent control features have been built into the
design.
With a fully compliant frame preemption implementation, more detailed case studies
can be performed on the various conﬁgurations and deployments. Tests performed
on a network-wide deployment of frame preemption would illustrate the beneﬁts of
diﬀerent deployments. This would be required before deﬁnitively deciding to include
frame preemption in diﬀerent fronthaul network architectures .
The development of TSN standards is a huge subject of interest for both network
equipment vendors and mobile operators. As the development matures, the diﬀerent
beneﬁts will become more apparent and the adoption of TSN features will most likely
aid in the deployment of C-RAN. Other industries already welcome TSN as a future
solution for transporting time-sensitive data, and there will surely be a lot of inter-
esting progress to follow in the future.
