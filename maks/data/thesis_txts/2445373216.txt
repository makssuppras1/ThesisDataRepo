Abstract
In this thesis an initial screening of the industrial coﬀee waste from a new process invented by GEA
A/S have been carried out, with the purpose of using the coﬀee residue as a building material.
Subsequent an investigation of the coﬀee residue used as a small pore forming agent in ﬁred clay
bricks was done.
It was known that the coﬀee residue hardened under drying, and thereby gained strength,
which made it a possibility to be used as a building material. The water content of the material
was found to 84 - 86 % which makes it diﬃcult to form under drying due to shrinking, which
causes the dried material to crack. There was not found any working method to obtain a desired
form of the material during the drying.
A characterization of the coﬀee residue showed that grain size of the material is between 10 -
100 µm, and the pH value was slightly acidic with 4.9. A digestion with ICP showed that the
concentration of cadmium, copper, nickel and zinc exceeds the limits stated by the Danish Envi-
ronmental Protection Agency, so a puriﬁcation of the coﬀee residue is necessary before used as a
building material.
Diﬀerent methods were used in an attempt to lower the water content of the coﬀee residue,
among centrifugation, vacuum suction, oven and microwave. It was found that it is possible to
lower the water content by drying the material at a low temperature or by using the microwave,
the material should be stirred frequently or small pieces will harden. Furthermore, the material
was dried, crushed and remixed with water, but the results showed that when drying the second
time, the strength of the material were not the same. There was not found a durable way of using
the coﬀee residue as a building material on its own, and under these circumstances an investigation
of the use of the material as a pore form agent was done.
For mixing the clay and coﬀee residue, diﬀerent combinations with both dried and wet materials
were done in form of small brick pellets, from this it was found that the dried/crushed coﬀee
residue would make lumps under mixing leading to a brick pellet which was easy to break. The
best results was if the wet material was dried in the microwave to a water content of 66 %, which
resulted in a material with no lumps. In order to ﬁnd a ﬁtting mix between brick clay and coﬀee
residue small pellets were made with diﬀerent mix designs.
In parallel with the forming and attempt to produce a proper brick, a test trying to determine
if it would aﬀect the results if the same sample set was used in more experiments. The samples
used in this test were dried in 3 days at 80 ℃and ﬁred 3 days at 1020 ℃, which resulted in cracks

and the quality of the samples were not good. From the test it was found that there could be an
eﬀect of the results if the samples were used for more experiments.
Based on the results of the pellets, 4 x 3 small brick samples of each 300 g were produced
by mixing brick clay with 0 %, 5 %, 7.5 % and 10 % of coﬀee residue. The brick samples were
formed under a pressure of 4000 N with a rate of 8000 N/min. The best result from the ﬁring
was obtained if the brick samples were dried 24 hours at 40℃, 24 hours at 105℃, 16.5 hours at
200℃and burnt 6 hours at 1000℃. Only three samples were made with each mix design, so all
experiments were done on the same sets of samples.
The physical properties, determined with experiments done on the brick samples, were: shrink-
age, thermal conductivity, water absorption, minute suction, porosity, density and compressive
strength.
The ﬁred clay bricks, with small pores from coﬀee residue, showed that it was possible to
increase the porosity of the bricks, together with a decreasing of the density, which resulted in a
decreased thermal conductivity when the amount of coﬀee residue increased, causing the bricks to
be better insulating. The thermal resisting of the coﬀee residue brick did not show a huge impact
on the total U-value for an exterior wall. The water absorption showed an increase in the total
absorbed water for the bricks with coﬀee residue. A decrease in the compressive strength was seen
when adding the coﬀee residue.
It can be concluded that it is possible to use the coﬀee residue as a small pore forming agent in
ﬁred clay bricks and get satisfying physical properties. Since all the experiments were conducted
on the same sample sets, the results could be improved by a more precise investigation using new
samples for each experiment.

Resume
I denne afhandling er der foretaget en indledende undersøgelse af restsproduktet fra en ny pro-
duktionsmetode, af instant kaﬀe, opfundet af GEA A / S, med det formål at anvende det som et
byggemateriale. Efterfølgende blev der foretaget en undersøgelse af om kaﬀeresten kan anvendes
til at lave små luft porer i brændte mursten.
Det var kendt, at kaﬀeresten blev hårdt under tørring og derved opnået styrke, hvilket kunne gøre
det muligt at anvende det som et byggemateriale. Vandindholdet i materialet blev fundet til 84
– 86 %, hvilket medførte, at det var udfordrende at formgive under tørringen, da det krymper og
det tørrede materiale derved revnede. Der blev ikke fundet nogen metode hvorpå en ønsket form
af materialet kunne opnås under tørringen. En karakterisering af kaﬀeresten viste, at kornstør-
relsen af materialet er mellem 10 og 100 µm, og pH-værdien lidt sur med 4,9. En oplukning med
ICP viste, at koncentrationen af cadmium, kobber, nikkel og zink overstiger de grænser, der er
angivet af Miljøstyrelsen, så en rensning af materialet er nødvendig, før det kan anvendes som et
bygningsmateriale.
Forskellige metoder blev anvendt i et forsøg på at sænke vandindhold i kaﬀeresten, blandt andet
centrifugering, vakuumsugning, ovn og mikrobølgeovn. Det blev fundet, at det er muligt at sænke
vandindholdet ved at tørre materialet ved en lav temperatur eller ved at bruge mikrobølgeovnen,
materialet skal omrøres ofte, eller opstår der små klumper. Desuden blev materialet tørret, knust
og blandet med vand, men resultaterne viste, at ved tørring anden gang var materialets styrke
ikke det samme. Der blev ikke fundet en holdbar måde, hvorpå kaﬀen alene kunne benyttes som
et byggemateriale, og under disse omstændigheder blev der foretaget en undersøgelse af om ma-
terialet kunne benyttes som et porerformende tilslag.
Ved blandingen af ler og kaﬀe blev forskellige kombinationer med både tørret og våde materialer
afprøvet i form af små murstens piller, hvoraf det blev fundet, at det tørrede / knuste kaﬀe ville
medføre klumper under blanding, og derved førte til en mursten pille, der var let at knække. De
bedste resultaterne blev opnået, hvis det våde kaﬀe blev tørret i mikrobølgeovnen til et vandind-
hold på 66 %, hvilket resulterede i en blanding uden klumper. For at ﬁnde et passende blandings
forhold mellem ler og kaﬀe blev små piller fremstillet med forskellige mængder.
Parallelt med formningen og forsøget på at fremstille en ordentlig mursten, blev der lavet et forsøg
hvor formålet var at undersøge om, resultaterne ville blive påvirket, hvis det samme prøve sæt
blev brugt i ﬂere eksperimenter. De prøver, der blev brugt i denne test, blev tørret i 3 dage ved
80 ℃og brændt i 3 dage ved 1020 ℃, hvilket medførte revner og kvaliteten af prøverne var ikke
gode. Fra testen blev det konkluderet, at der kunne være en eﬀekt, hvis prøverne var brugt til
mere end et eksperiment.

med følgende kaﬀe indhold 0 %, 5 %, 7.5 % og 10 % kaﬀerest. Prøverne blev formet under et tryk
på 4000 N med en hastighed på 8000 N / min. Det bedste resultat fra brændingen blev opnået,
hvis murstens-prøverne blev tørret i 24 timer ved 40 ℃, 24 timer ved 105 ℃, 16,5 timer ved 200

undersøgt, af de følgende forsøg: krympning, termisk ledningsevne, vandabsorption, minutters
sugning, porøsitet, Densitet og trykstyrke.
De brændte mursten, med små porer fra kaﬀeresten, viste, at det er muligt at øge porøsitet i
mursten, og sænke densiteten, hvilket resulterede i et fald af varmeledningsevne, når mængden af
kaﬀerest steg, hvilket resulterede i, at mursten blev mere isolerende. Den termiske modstand af
kaﬀeresten viste ikke en enorm eﬀekt på den samlede U-værdi for en udvendig væg. Vandabsorp-
tionen, viste en stigning i det samlede absorberede vand i de murstenen med kaﬀerest. Der blev
observeret et fald af trykstyrken, når kaﬀerest var tilsat prøverne.
Det kan konkluderes, at det er muligt at anvende kaﬀeresten som et porerformende tilslag i brændte
mursten, og få tilfredsstillende fysiske egenskaber. Da alle forsøgene blev udført på de samme
prøvesæt, kunne resultaterne muligvis forbedres ved en mere præcis undersøgelse ved anvendelse
af nye prøve sæt for hvert forsøg.

Introduction
Coﬀee in one of the most popular drinks in the world, with the average coﬀee drinker consuming
three cups of coﬀee per day, coﬀee is also one of the most widely used commodity in the world
[12]. In Denmark every ﬁfth cup of coﬀee that is made is instant coﬀee. More people are using
instant coﬀee because they think it tastes good, is easy and without waste [20].
The coﬀee is brewed in diﬀerent ways, each leaves waste from the coﬀee beans. Home coﬀee
brewing utilizes approximately 15-25 % of the ground coﬀee beans, whereas industrial coﬀee pro-
duction utilizes up to 50 %, and with a new process from GEA A/S up to 70 % of the coﬀee beans
are used. Regardless which brewing method used, there is a waste product. At home the small
amount of coﬀee waste goes into the trash or is being composted, whereas the coﬀee residue from
the production of the 776,000 t instant coﬀee (2015), leaves a much larger amount of byproduct [15].
In this project, the properties of the industrial coﬀee waste from the new process from GEA
A/S has been investigated in a screening. A Development scientist at GEA A/S had observed that
the coﬀee residue would harden under drying, and had in that connection contacted ZeroWaste at
DTU, with the idea to use coﬀee residue as a building material or additive in an existing material.
The Coﬀee residue looks very diﬀerent from the ground coﬀee known from the home brewing, the
residue is more similar to frosting used for cakes, see Figure 1.0.1
Figure 1.0.1: Coﬀee residue as received from GEA A/S.
By recycling waste into building materials the environment is protected. The environment is
in a large focus in the building industry, and the requirements have been tightened in order to save
energy and reduced the materials which are harmful for the environment or save on the materials
which not are renewable or storage resource, such as clay.
Bricks has been used as a building material for a very long time, in the beginning the clay

bricks were hand molded and sun dried. The use of ﬁred clay bricks can be dated back to around
5000 BC, and through up the building history the bricks have continuously been used for building
due to their physical and engineering properties [19]. The properties which makes and made bricks
a good building material is the ﬁre resistance, durability, strength and the acceptable thermal re-
sistances, which will keep the building cool during summer and warm in the winter [19].
The beneﬁts of the brick makes it an interesting building material to improve. In this project,

thereby be used as a small pore forming agent. By producing these bricks the weight and the use of
clay can be reduced, and along with that, the emission of CO2 during transport. Furthermore, the
thermal conductivity, lowered by the small pores, gives higher insulation properties and thereby
the thickness of the exterior walls can be reduced.

Screening and Idea
Development Process
This is an initial screening done of the waste product from a new procedure in the manufacturing
of instant coﬀee by GEA A/S. The experimental work and result discussion have been done in
cooperation with the two other master students Emilie Oline Buch Larsen and Rikke Klavstrup
Wittendorﬀ.
Before this project ZeroWaste Byg was contacted by Development Scientist, Anders Holmen
Pedersen, from GEA A/S. because of an interesting observation was done during a routine dry-
ing of the coﬀee residue (CR). After the drying of the CR, for approximately 16 hours at 80 %,
the material hardened and could then withstand being hit with a hammer. Furthermore nothing
happened if the hardened material was exposed to water added with enzymes, 60℃in 14-16 hours.
Based on these informations the initial idea was to investigate if the pure hardened CR could be
used as a building material.

2.2
Idea Development Process
The screening process was performed by multiple loops as in The Scientiﬁc Method [7], illustrated
in Figure 2.2.1.
Figure 2.2.1: Illustration of the experimental selection using the Scientiﬁc Method.
As the illustration on Figure 2.2.1 shows, the scientiﬁc method starts with a question to an
observation, then a background research, which leads to a hypothesis. The hypothesis is then
tested by doing an experiment followed by an assessment. In case of the procedure is not working,
the setup is checked and modiﬁed, when the procedure is working the data is analyzed. If the
hypothesis was not supported, a new hypothesis and prediction based on the information learned
are conducted [7].
The screening process consisted of an initial idea, three loops and a ﬁnal idea leading to
the subject of the ﬁnal work.
An illustration of the idea development process is presented in
Figure 2.2.2.
The Initial Idea is the initial idea of using the raw undried material as a building material, the
hypothesis is then investigated and a conclusion is made. From experience done through Initial
Idea, a new hypothesis is presented in Idea Loop 1, and new experiments are set up in order to
fulﬁll it. If the procedure is not working the experiments are modiﬁed, and a conclusion is made
at the end. After three loops, Final Idea is presented, this is the idea ending up being the subject
for the ﬁnal project. All the loops are explained in full later on in Section 2.2.1 to 2.2.5

The density of the hardened CR was calculated to 1323 kg/m3, which is approximately 18

Modiﬁed Experiment: Decreasing the Density
Stated earlier the density of the pure hardened CR was too high to meet the requirements for
lightweight aggregates. To produce other lightweight aggregates, such as leca nuts, which is made
by clay burned at high temperatures in a rotating kiln, an expansion of the clay happens during
the process, which makes air pores in the leca nuts [1]. It was not a possibility to use this method
to lower the density in the CR, since the loss of ignition at 950 ℃, Section 5.1.3, showed that 99.5
% of the CR is burned away at these temperatures. And no other materials could be added which
would incinerate before the CR, another light material could be added.
In the attempt to decrease the density of the CR samples, styrofoam balls was added. By having
a density of only 50 kg/m3 [10], the styrofoam balls could work in the same way as the air pores
in laca nuts. On Figure 2.2.4a of the pellets, one pellet added styrofoam balls can be seen in the
middle, it was diﬃcult to add the styrofoam balls to the CR due to a slippery consistency of the
CR. After drying the samples added styrofoam balls, it was experienced that the styrofoam balls
were not binding with the CR at all and the two materials was not connected, the sample cracked
more than before adding the styrofoam balls.
Conclusion: Cracking = Not Possible
The experiments regarding the density showed that the density was too high to use the hardened
material as a lightweight aggregate. In an attempt to lower the density of the aggregates, styrofoam
balls were added to the material, but this just resulted in more cracks, because the two materials
did not bind together. The hypothesis was therefore not supported and a new one was needed.

Theory
The insulation requirements for the building industry are been increased and the demands to the
materials as well. A new construction for a normal family home have been increased from 2010 to
2015, and will in the new requirements for 2020 be increased further [27].
The requirements in the Danish Building Regulations of 2015 (BR15) for the thermal insulation
for a external wall is 0.30 W/m2K with a dimensional transmission loss of 4 W/m2.
To comply with these requirements the insulation layer have to be increased, which leads to an
increase of the total external wall, and a decrease in the living area. An alternative to avoid
loosing living area, is to have building materials with better thermal properties. A way of making
the material better is to lower the thermal conductivity of the material.
The thermal conductivity can be used to ﬁnd the thermal resistance, R, the higher the thermal
resistance, the smaller the heat ﬂow for a given temperature, which means the better the material
insulates. The thermal resistance is found by dividing the thickness of the material, d, with it
thermal conductivity λ. This means that a lower thermal conductivity is equal to a decrease in
the heat ﬂow rate, which means less heat lost or gained [8].
3.1
The Composition of Clay Bricks
The brick should be a mixture consisting mostly of pure clay and sand, so that when mixed with
water it becomes easy to mold and dried without cracking, shrinking or warping. A good brick
consists of the following [5]:
• Silica: (50 to 60 %) The presence of the silica prevents the raw brick from cracking, shrinking
and warping. When adding the silica in a proper amount it produces hardness, resistance
to heat and durability, whereas have too much of it will destroy the cohesion and make the
brick brittle.
• Alumina: (20 to 30 %) The alumina is a ﬁnely grained mineral compound which adds the
plasticity, allowing the clay to being shaped. When ﬁring the brick, it looses the plasticity,
and causes the fusion of its components and thereby making it homogeneous, harder and
stronger. Having too much alumina with insuﬃcient quantity of sand, will make the brick
too hard and cause it to crack, shrink and warp dying drying.
• Lime: (2 to 5 %) The lime should be added in a very ﬁne powdered state, if too large particles
are present it causes ﬂaking of the brick. The present of the lime reduces shrinkage, and

==PAGE:38==

acts as a ﬂux, enabling the silica particles to melt and thus binding the particles together.
In cases with lumps of unhydrated lime unsightly defects on the brick called ’lime pops’ may
occur.
• Oxide of iron: (5 to 6 % ≯7 %) Acts as a ﬂux like the lime helping the particles to bind,
giving hardness and strength to the brick. The content of iron oxides have an eﬀect of the
colour of the brick, ranging from light yellow to red. If the iron oxide is present excess 5- 7
%, the red iron oxide will be brown or dark blue in colour which is not good.
• Magnesia: (<1 %) If the magnesia is present in a small quantity it imparts yellow tinge to
the brick and decreases the shrinkage, whereas too much will cause the brick to decay.

The perforated brick is deﬁned as a clay brick manufactured with verticals holes, in order to reduce
weight, improve insulating properties and reduce the water absorption the wall [25].
A perforated brick has a minimum coring or void area of 25 %, and classiﬁcation of the brick
are done from several parameters e.g. type of stone, water absorption, minute suction, density,
strength and void area [25].

Similar Studies
In order to save raw materials, increase the insulation properties or to recycle waste, there have
been done similar studies with the focus of using additives as pore forming agents in ﬁred clay brick.
"The Use of Large Pore Forming Agent in Fired Clay Bricks" [31]
This study is conducted in parallel with this study, where the coﬀee residue was dried in coﬀee

aggregates with the diameters of 10 mm and 15 mm, and mixed with raw clay brick from Wiener-
berger. Brick samples containing 0 %, 2.5 % and 5 % of Coﬀee residue aggregates in each size
were produced an the thermal conductivity, porosity, density, water absorption, minute suction
and compressive strength were investigated. The results showed that the porosity of the bricks
increased, and the density was decreased when adding more coﬀee residue. This was reﬂected in
the decreasing thermal conductivity of the bricks, but also in the decreasing compressive strength.
"Eﬀect of organic residues addition on the technological properties of clay bricks" [14]
This study was conducted in Turkey in 2008, and is investigating the use of several organic residues
in clay brick. The pore forming additives used were: Sawdust, tobacco residues and grass. Raw
brick clay was used to produce brick samples of mixtures containing 0 %, 2.5 %, 5 % and 10 %
additives. The eﬀects on shaping, plasticity, density, and mechanical properties were investigated.
The results from the study showed that the additives were found to be eﬀective as pore forming
agents in ﬁred clay bricks, and still maintaining acceptable mechanical properties. Furthermore,
it was found that an increase of the additives in the mixtures would require a higher water content
to ensure the right plasticity. From this study it was concluded that sawdust, tobacco residues,
and grass can be used in an environmentally safe way as organic pore forming agents bricks.
"Thermal Conductivity Of Fired Clay Bricks Incorporated With Cigarette Butts" [28]
The study was conducted in Malaysia in 2013, and is about diﬀerent amounts of cigarette butts
incorporated in ﬁred clay brick, with the percentages of 0 %, 2.5 % and 5 %. The thermal con-
ductivity of the bricks with the cigarette butts were investigated using the Hot Guarded Plate
Method. From the study it was found that the thermal conductivity could be lowered, at the same
time as the porosity is increased, thus the density reduced to become lightweight brick.
"Utilization of Opium Seeds as an Organic Pore Forming Agent in Heavy Clay Products" [4]
The objective of this study was to investigate the use of opium seeds of pore forming agents in
masonry clay product. Increasing the seed addition made the need for water in the mix go down,
which was assumed to be because of the high amount of oil and presence of other organic sub-
stances in the seed. Up to 10 % additives the properties changes, but further addition did not
change the properties so much and cause bloating and black coring.
Other informations regarding ﬁred clay bricks:
In connection with the investigation of the thermal conductivity and the density it was found that
an technical information sheet from ibstock, is pointing out that the density of the masonry outer
leaf have a very little impact on the overall U-value of a cavity wall [22].
They calculated the thermal conductivity from the density and hold two diﬀerent bricks up against
each other, the low density brick, 1400 kg/m3, had a thermal conductivity of 0.60 W/mK, and
the high density brick, 2000 kg/m3, have 0.96 W/mK. By making the U-value calculation on a
wall construction it is shown that the two bricks, with the same wall construction, have the same
total U-value.

The minute suction is the absorption of water after the ﬁrst minute of the experiment, in

was about 5 mm above the messing rods. The samples were placed with the suction area, As,

the sample was in contact with the water, the sample will absorb the water though the area, so
water was added to keep the water level at 5 mm. The samples were weighed in diﬀerent time
intervals: 1 - 2 - 4 - 8 - 16 - 32 - 60 - 120 - 240 minutes from start, and the weight noted, mt.

Conclusion
From the initial screening of the coﬀee residue (CR) it can be concluded that the CR had a high
water content of 84 - 86 %, which made it challenging to work with. The digestion with ICP puts
the CR in category 3, according to the Danish Environmental Protection Agency, because of the
concentration of cadmium, cobber, nickel and zinc exceed the limits for category 1 and 2. This
means that the CR can not be directly used in building materials, before it have been puriﬁed.
The grain size of the wet coﬀee residue was found to be 10 - 100 µm, and the pH value was slightly
acidic with 4.9.
When drying the material in an oven, at 80 to 105 ℃, it gets hard, but during the drying the
material cracks and the dried material is not in one piece. There were not found a solution to
keep a certain shape when drying the CR.
The CR hardened at 80 - 105 ℃got very hard, the speciﬁc compressive strength was not found,
but the material was crushed manually with a mortar. Furthermore it was found that the harden
material got soft after being in water, so the use of the material alone should be in dry placed.
Diﬀerent methods were tested in order to ﬁnd a way to control the water content, neither cen-
trifugation or vacuum have a huge eﬀect, it was possible to lower the water content by drying the
material at a low temperature in a long time. The method used in this project was to heated up
in the microwave under frequently stirring, which was a lot faster but still time consuming. In
this project the CR was dried to having a water content of 66 % by using this method, a lot of at-
tention is need under the drying and stirring to avoid the material to harden and make small lumps.
In the investigation of the possibility of using the CR as a small pore forming agent in ﬁred
clay bricks diﬀerent brick pellets were produced. To make small brick pellets both the undried
material, and dried and crushed residue was mixed with clay, which had been dried and crushed.
To control the water content in the pellets water was added to the powder mix in the desired
amount. From this it was observed that the residue made small lumps and the ﬁred brick pellets
could easily be broken.
Subsequently the CR was dried in the microwave to a water content of 66 %, and there after mixed
with the wet untreated clay from Wienerberger A/S. Diﬀerent mix designs with CR was made,
and the pellets with a residue content of 5 %, 7.5 % and 10 % were chosen to be used in a further
investigation.
Because of the limited amount of material and time, it was investigated if a sample set of
3 samples could be used in more than one experiment. This was done by exposing the sets to
the same experiments but in diﬀerent order, and in the end ﬁnd the compressive strength of the
59
==PAGE:76==

samples.The results showed that the samples used in prior experiments had a lower compressive
strength than the sample which had not been used before. So from this it could indicate that there
is an eﬀect of the compressive strength when the samples have been used on prior experiments.
For the ﬁred clay bricks made with 5 %, 7.5 % and 10 % CR, it was found that when the
amount of coﬀee residue was increased the wt-% and vol-% loss of the brick after ﬁring would
increase. Correlating with an increase in the porosity, together with a decrease of the density.
The thermal conductivity would be lowered when the amount of CR increased, causing the bricks
to be better insulating. The thermal resisting of the CR brick did not show a huge impact on the
total U-value for an exterior wall. The water absorption showed a evenly distribution of water up
though the brick under the experiment, and an increase in the total absorbed water for the bricks
with CR. The results from the compressive strength showed that the addition of CR made the
causes the values to decrease.
It can be concluded that it is possible to use the coﬀee residue as a small pore forming agent
in ﬁred clay brick and get satisfying physical properties. Since all the experiments were conducted
on the same sample sets, the results could be improved by a more precise investigation using new
samples for each experiment.

Further Research
After the screening of the CR new ideas of how the material could be treated or used came in mind
when working with the clay brick. furthermore, the work done in the ﬁnal part lead to interesting
observation which could be the bases of further research.
Investigations for the use of the material
• It might be possible to form the CR in a desired form by adding glue to the dried material,
the CR could be dried and crushed or partly dried as when mixed with the clay.
• Another interesting forming method could be to dry the CR under pressure. This should be
tried for the partly dried CR with some form of piston under a low temperature drying. In
the bottom of the form a water drain will be needed to transport the water out.
• To lower the water content of the material it could be investigated if the dried and crushed
material could be mixed with the UCR. Do the CR still have the ability to be hard after
being mixed with dried CR?
• In this screening the CR was only dried up to 105℃, would a high temperature in a shorter
time give the same result?
• An investigation of the actual strength of the hardened CR to ﬁnd the optimal drying
temperature. This could be done by testing the compression strength with the same method
as leca nuts.
Coﬀee residue in ﬁred clay bricks:
To get the optimal results for the used of CR as a pore forming agent, investigations with a lager
amount of samples could be done.
• The compressive strength of the CR bricks should be tested with the same method used for
other brick, to give a better idea of the strength.
• The similar study referred and compared to, was about larger pores in ﬁred clay bricks. It
could be interesting to make the aggregates in other shapes, and try to use diﬀerent patterns
for the placing of the pores.
• The coﬀee aggregate formed in a rolling container and dried under movement, could this
form round CR aggregates?

Conductivity in soil 
 
 
A 
Principle 
 
A soil liquid containing ions and dissolved compounds are of great interest, as 
this can provide a picture of a soil liquid total concentration (ionic strength), the 
ion activity ratio in the liquid and give an impression of equilibrium conditions 
between solid particles and dissolved compounds.  
 
The conductivity of the soil solution is an indication of how good an electrical 
current which can be passed through the given fluid and depends on the number 
of conductive particles present in the liquid. 
 
 
 
B 
Special equipment 
 
For measurement a conductivity meter 
 
 
 
C 
Procedure 
 
Weigh 10.00 g dry, crushed soil on a technical balance into a 50ml plastic bottle. 
 
Add 25.00 ml distilled water with a volumetric pipette. Place the plastic bottles on 
a shaking table for 30 min. 
 
Take the samples off and let it stand for 10 min for the liquid to separate from 
the soil. 
 
Rinse the conductivity electrode with distilled water. Place electrode in the liquid. 
When the display on the conductivity shows “STAB” the result is read as mS/cm.

The conductivity used without further calculation as mS/cm.

Porosity and density (LBM standard) 
 
 
A 
Princip 
 
The porosity of a material tells us how porous the material is, i.e., how fragile it 
is. The higher the porosity is, the greater ability the material has to take up 
water. It also means a good ability to absorb water. The density of the material's 
mass per unit volume. The mass of a porous material can be a dry mass or a 
mass of water in the open pores, that is, by finding the density can be calculated 
mass at different conditions. 
 
 
 
B 
Special equipment 
 
For measurement use the vacuum pump and a technical balance with the 
possibility to weight under water. 
 
 
 
C 
Procedure

Weighed the sample on a technical balance and note the weight (m105).

vacuum pump and pumped it down for min. 3 hours.

hose and the under pressure in the desiccator. Close the tap as soon as the 
water level is 3 cm above the sample. It must be close for 1 hour. 
 
After 1 hour open the tap and let the sample be under water overnight at 
atmospheric pressure.  
 
First weight the saturated sample under water on a technical balance. The water 
in box must have room temperature. The weighted is noted as (msw) 
 
The sample is dabbed with a cloth before weighing over water. Note the 
weight as (mssd)

Capillary suction 
 
 
A       Principle 
 
If a dry porous material is brought into contact with water, the moistened 
material as a result of capillary suction. By capillary suction, there is a 
transport of water from the surface into the material. This transfer is made 
through narrow channels in the material, which is also called capillaries. 
 
The figure shows the ideal conditions in which the recorded amount of water 
per. area (Q) as a function of t ½ shows a straight dotted line, until the 
amount of water corresponding to Qkap is aspirated. After recording no more 
water and the slope of the curve will be zero. 
 
In reality a curve look as shown by the solid line, since the pores of refilling 
quickly. Likewise, for some materials may be rising slightly, after the water 
level has reached the top of the sample. This is due to the filling of the 
smallest pores will take longer. Therefore, the water transports slow if the 
pores of the material is small, and the reverse speed if the pores are greater.
