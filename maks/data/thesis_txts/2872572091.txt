Abstract
The transition to Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) is essential for decarboniz-
ing Denmark’s heating sector, but its implementation in existing networks faces complex
challenges. This thesis investigates the political, economic, social, technological, envi-
ronmental and legal factors influencing LTDH deployment, using the PESTEL framework
and qualitative analysis of four semi-structured stakeholder interviews. Respondents in-
cluded representatives from municipal utilities, academia, planning bodies, and technical
campus services. The results reveal persistent stakeholder misalignment, regulatory in-
ertia, and economic disincentives for retrofitting. Building owners often bear high upfront
costs while utilities benefit from long-term efficiency gains, creating a structural mismatch
in incentives. User autonomy and behavioral norms further complicate return tempera-
ture optimization, undermining system performance. On the technological side, digital
tools offer significant potential, but their their effectiveness is hindered by fragmented
data systems and a lack of integration into operations. Legal frameworks, particularly the
Danish Heat Supply Act, impose rigid temperature requirements and limit utilities’ author-
ity, thus creating additional obstacles to innovation. Despite these barriers, successful
pilot projects demonstrate that centralized control, early stakeholder engagement, and
performance-based incentives can enable effective LTDH rollouts. The study concludes
that overcoming LTDH barriers requires integrated strategies: revising regulatory frame-
works, aligning financial incentives and investing in both physical and digital infrastructure.
Strategic recommendations are offered to support Denmark’s shift toward a more efficient
and equitable district heating future.

Introduction and Literature Review
1.1
Background
District heating has played a central role in Denmark’s energy system for decades.[1]
Traditionally, these systems have operated at high temperatures, often above 70°C, to
ensure heat delivery at long distances and through older building networks. However,
high supply and return temperatures can result in significant energy losses and limit the
integration of renewable energy sources. [2], [3]
Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) is being promoted as a solution that reduces
heat loss, supports the use of renewable energy, and increases overall system efficiency.
[4], [5] LTDH systems operate below 55°C, which makes them more compatible with heat
pumps, solar thermal systems and surplus heat from industrial or digital sources.[6], [7]
Despite these benefits, transitioning to LTDH in existing urban networks is not simple.
Most buildings were designed for higher temperatures, and many technical, economic,
and legal barriers remain.[8], [9] Retrofitting old systems, aligning incentives, and ensuring
efficient user behavior all require coordinated effort and careful planning. [10]
This thesis explores how LTDH can be implemented in existing Danish networks, focusing
on real-world challenges and strategies to overcome them.
1.2
Problem Statement
Many existing district heating networks in Denmark were designed for high-temperature
systems.[1], [8] As the country aims to reduce carbon emissions and improve energy
efficiency, Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) appears to be a promising path for-
ward.[2], [3] However, implementing LTDH in older networks is not straightforward.
Technical, economic, and regulatory challenges make the transition complex. [9], [10] [9,
10] Buildings often require upgrades such as improved insulation, installation of larger
heat exchangers or even complete retrofitting of internal heating systems.[4], [6] High
return temperatures reduce efficiency and limit the integration of renewable heat sources
like solar thermal or industrial surplus heat.[7] At the same time, current Danish regulations
and tenant-controlled heating systems make it hard to control and optimize temperature
settings at the building level.
Another key issue is that the financial benefits of LTDH are not equally distributed. District
heating companies may see quick efficiency gains, but building owners and users often
face high upfront costs with long payback periods. [9], [10] This makes coordination be-
tween stakeholders essential, especially in mixed-ownership buildings and public-private
partnerships. To make LTDH viable in existing systems, we need a better understanding
of these barriers and how to overcome them in practice.

1.3
Research Objectives
This thesis aims to investigate how Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) can be suc-
cessfully implemented in existing Danish district heating networks. It focuses on under-
standing real-world technical, regulatory, and economic barriers, and on developing ac-
tionable strategies based on expert insights and practical cases.
The main objectives are:
1. Assess the technical and economic feasibility of implementing LTDH in existing net-
works, including building compatibility, return temperature optimization, and cost-
benefit considerations.
2. Analyze regulatory and policy barriers, especially those affecting tenant heating au-
tonomy, disconnection rights, and institutional coordination.
3. Gather stakeholder insights through expert interviews to understand practical im-
plementation challenges, decision-making dynamics, and user behavior.
4. Develop actionable recommendations for municipalities, district heating companies,
and policymakers to support LTDH deployment in retrofitting contexts.

Literature Review
This chapter provides a comprehensive review of the existing literature relevant to the im-
plementation of Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) in Denmark. It aims to establish
a foundational understanding of the historical development, technical considerations, eco-
nomic models, regulatory frameworks, behavioral aspects, and digitalization trends that
shape the feasibility and adoption of LTDH in existing networks.
This review is structured to first introduce the historical trajectory of district heating and
define LTDH conceptually. It then examines the technical, economic, regulatory, and be-
havioral barriers that influence LTDH deployment. The chapter concludes by identifying
key knowledge gaps in the literature and positioning the present thesis to contribute to
these unresolved areas. The PESTEL framework (see Section 2.9.3) was used to organ-
ise the literature thematically.
2.1
Evolution of District Heating Systems
2.1.1
Generations of District Heating (1G–4G)
District heating (DH) has evolved significantly over the past century, with each generation
reflecting advances in efficiency, safety, and system integration. The First Generation
(1G) systems used steam produced from coal-fired boilers. These networks operated at
high pressures and temperatures and were typically limited to small, local grids. Due to
poor insulation and high heat losses, 1G systems had limited efficiency and safety [2]. The
Second Generation (2G) introduced hot water systems with temperatures above 100◦C,
which was usually transported through concrete ducts. These were common in post-
war Europe and allowed for larger-scale district heating with somewhat better control.
The Third Generation (3G) adopted pre-insulated, directly buried pipes, reducing both
installation costs and heat losses. Operating temperatures were reduced to 70–90◦C in
supply and 40–55◦C in return, enabling the integration of biomass and other renewables.
The Fourth Generation (4G) systems were designed to meet modern energy transition
goals.
They operate at lower temperatures (typically 50–70◦C) and are optimized to
integrate variable and decentralized renewable energy sources such as solar thermal,
geothermal and industrial surplus heat. These systems also aim for bidirectional flows,
smart grid compatibility and sector coupling with electricity and transport [2]. An emerg-
ing Fifth Generation (5G) concept is also currently being explored. It involves ultra-low
temperature and ambient heat loops (e.g., 10–30◦C) and is often combined with decen-
tralized heat pumps at the building level. While it looks promising, 5G is still at a pilot
stage and not yet widely deployed [11].

2.1.2
Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) and its relevance for
carbon neutrality and the energy transition
Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) refers to systems operating below 55◦C in sup-
ply and around 25–30◦C in return temperatures. It is considered a subset of 4G DH,
focusing specifically on reducing distribution temperatures to minimize heat loss and im-
prove compatibility with low-exergy heat sources [4]. Compared to earlier systems, LTDH
enables higher energy efficiency and lower infrastructure costs over time. However, it also
places stricter requirements on building insulation, radiator size, and return temperature
control. These systems often require changes in both network design and user interaction
[4]. LTDH plays a key role in achieving national and EU-wide climate targets. Denmark
aims to be fossil-free in its heating sector by 2030, and the EU’s “Fit for 55” package
requires member states to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030.
Transitioning to LTDH supports these goals by allowing integration of renewable and
waste heat, as LTDH systems can efficiently use low-temperature heat from solar col-
lectors, industrial processes, data centers, and wastewater treatment. It also improves
energy efficiency by lowering distribution temperatures, which reduces heat loss, pump
energy demand, and return temperatures—boosting network-wide efficiency. LTDH fur-
ther supports sector coupling and electrification by allowing excess renewable electricity
to be converted into heat using large-scale or building-level heat pumps. Lastly, it im-
proves building synergy, as new buildings require less heating energy, making LTDH
increasingly compatible with modern standards, while retrofitted buildings can gradually
transition through energy efficiency measures. The successful implementation of LTDH
depends not only on technical performance but also on coordinated efforts across sectors
and its practical application under real-world conditions.
2.2
Technical Requirements and Constraints
2.2.1
Temperature Regimes in District Heating Systems
District heating (DH) systems operate under various temperature regimes, which have
evolved to enhance energy efficiency and integrate renewable energy sources. Tradi-
tional systems often functioned at high temperatures, such as 90/55◦C (supply/return),
suitable for older buildings with high heat demands. However, modern systems are shift-
ing towards lower temperature regimes, like 70/40◦C, to reduce thermal losses and ac-
commodate low-temperature heat sources. [12], [2]
Lowering supply temperatures necessitates careful consideration of domestic hot water
(DHW) requirements. For example, to prevent Legionella bacteria growth, DHW sys-
tems typically require temperatures above 60◦C. Solutions include localized temperature
boosting or integrating supplementary heating systems within buildings [12].
2.2.2
Role of Heat Exchangers, Pipe Systems and Radiators
Heat exchangers are essential in DH systems, facilitating the transfer of thermal energy
from the network to individual building heating systems without mixing fluids. Plate heat

insulation of these exchangers is crucial to minimize energy losses. [13] The distribu-
tion network consists of insulated pipes that transport hot water from the central plant

mitigate this issue. [12], [2]
2.2.3
Barriers: Hydraulic Balancing, System Inertia and One-Pipe
Systems
In practice, several technical barriers complicate the transition to low-temperature district
heating, particularly in older networks. One such challenge is hydraulic balancing, which
ensures uniform heat distribution across all connected buildings. Without proper balance,
some buildings may be underheated while others are overheated, reducing overall system
efficiency and comfort. The use of balancing valves and differential pressure controllers
can help maintain consistent heat distribution across the network. Another critical fac-
tor is system inertia. Buildings with high thermal mass respond slowly to temperature
changes, thus complicating demand forecasting and real-time control. Addressing this is-
sue requires advanced control strategies, often based on predictive algorithms, to ensure
efficient operation under varying conditions. [16] Finally, one-pipe systems still found in
many older buildings pose a significant retrofit challenge. In these configurations, water
passes sequentially through radiators, making temperature regulation inefficient and less
compatible with modern DH setups. Retrofitting to two-pipe systems or integrating bypass
solutions can improve performance and enable better integration with LTDH standards.
[12], [2]
2.3
Building-Level Retrofitting Challenges
2.3.1
Typical Retrofit Needs, Costs and Disruptions
Danish buildings, particularly those constructed before the 1980s, possibly require signif-
icant retrofitting to accommodate low-temperature district heating (LTDH) systems. Com-
mon retrofit needs include upgrading insulation, replacing windows, and modifying heating
systems to ensure compatibility with lower supply temperatures. For instance, a study on
a typical old Danish multi-family building demonstrated that energy retrofitting measures,
such as improved insulation and window replacements, could reduce energy consumption
to 30.3 kWh/m² per year, aligning with modern energy standards. [17]
Retrofitting existing buildings involves considerable costs and potential disruptions. The
investment required for deep retrofits can be substantial, with payback periods varying
depending on the extent of the upgrades and the level of energy savings achieved. Fur-
thermore, the retrofitting process can disrupt occupants due to construction activities and
temporary loss of heating services. A comprehensive technical analysis of retrofitting a

2.4
Behavioral and Social Aspects
The transition to low-temperature district heating (LTDH) systems necessitates a balance
between individual autonomy and centralized system coordination. While centralized con-
trol can optimize energy efficiency and integrate renewable sources, it may clash with
users’ desire for control over their heating environments. However, studies indicate that
even though collective heating systems may limit individual autonomy, they offer advan-
tages in efficiency, affordability, and environmental sustainability. [20]
User behavior significantly impacts the overall performance of district heating systems.
In Denmark, for example, research has shown that occupants’ interactions with heating
systems, such as radiator settings and window opening habits, influence operating tem-
peratures and energy consumption.[21] Educating users on optimal heating practices is
essential to maximize the benefits of LTDH.
However, even well-designed systems may fall short without effective communication be-
tween stakeholders. A study in Sweden identified poor information exchange between
district heating operators and end-users as a major barrier to system optimization.[20]
Cultural and institutional factors also influence user engagement. For instance, Danish
consumers exhibit high trust in district heating due to transparent pricing and strong reg-
ulatory frameworks. [22]
By comparing the Danish and Swedish contexts, we can see how governance models
shape public attitudes and behaviors. Both Denmark and Sweden have extensive district
heating networks, but their approaches differ. Denmark emphasizes centralized planning
and regulation, leading to high consumer trust and widespread adoption of LTDH.[22] On
the other hand, Sweden, with a more deregulated market, faces challenges in informa-
tion sharing and customer engagement.[21] These contrasting experiences offer valuable
insights for designing socially responsive LTDH implementation strategies in various con-
texts.

2.5
Governance and Regulatory Context
2.5.1
Legal Limits on Utility Control
The governance of district heating (DH) in Denmark is governed by a strong regulatory
framework designed to ensure transparency, efficiency, and public accountability. This
is called the Danish Heat Supply Act (Varmeforsyningsloven), which requires DH compa-
nies to operate on a not-for-profit basis. This ensures that consumer prices reflect actual
production and distribution costs without profit margins. This regulation aims to protect
consumers and promote transparency in the DH sector. [23]
Municipalities play a central role in the planning and implementation of DH systems. They
are tasked with developing heat supply plans based on socio-economic analyses to deter-
mine the most sustainable and cost-effective heating solutions for their regions. Through
this planning authority, municipalities can zone areas for DH, approve new projects, and
align local initiatives with national energy goals. [22], [23]
While municipalities often own or co-own DH companies, their ability to dictate specific
technical solutions or fuel choices is constrained by national regulations. They cannot
mandate the adoption of particular technologies beyond the scope allowed by national
policy frameworks. [22]
Beyond municipal actors, housing associations are key stakeholders in DH implementa-
tion, especially in urban areas with high-density residential buildings. Their cooperation
is essential for connecting buildings to DH networks and implementing building-level up-
grades necessary for low-temperature DH compatibility. Active engagement with housing
associations not only facilitates technical integration but also helps address tenant needs.
This leads to broader public acceptance and smoother implementation. [24]
2.5.2
Heat Supply Act and Disconnection Rights
Under the Heat Supply Act, areas designated for DH can impose connection obligations
on buildings, requiring them to connect to the DH network. However, since 2019, manda-
tory connection in new areas is no longer permitted, granting consumers the right to dis-
connect from DH systems if they choose alternative heating solutions. [22]
This shift aims to strengthen consumer choice and promote competition among heating
providers. Nevertheless, it introduces challenges for DH companies, as consumer discon-
nections can impact the financial viability of DH networks, particularly in areas with lower
population densities or where significant investments have been made in infrastructure.
[23]
2.5.3
Fragmented Ownership and Decision-Making
The Danish DH sector includes many different ownership types, including municipal own-
ership, consumer cooperatives, and private entities. This fragmentation can complicate
decision-making processes, especially when coordinating infrastructure investments, tar-
iff structures, and service standards across different ownership models. [25]

2.6
Economic and Business Models
The transition to Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) systems presents both eco-
nomic and environmental benefits. By reducing supply temperatures, LTDH minimizes
thermal losses and improves heat generation efficiency, especially when integrating re-
newable energy sources. A recent study shows that such temperature reductions can
lower the levelized cost of heat (LCOH) by up to 20%, mainly due to improved efficiency
in renewable energy use. [26]
However, LTDH systems also impose additional investment requirements, especially on
the consumer side. Lower supply temperatures may necessitate building upgrades, such
as improved insulation or low-temperature radiators, in order to maintain indoor comfort.
These added costs show the importance of comprehensive cost-benefit analyses in the
early stages of LTDH implementation.[26]
To improve system performance while managing consumer behavior, many Danish util-
ities have adopted innovative tariff structures. Motivation tariffs, where consumers are
financially rewarded for maintaining lower return temperatures, have shown promising re-
sults in aligning individual behavior with system-wide efficiency goals in Denmark. For
example, in Viborg, the introduction of such a tariff led to measurable efficiency gains
and an estimated net benefit of €400,000 annually.[27] A similar approach was taken by
the Kalundborg utility, which offers discounts to users who maintain return temperatures
below 45°C, while applying penalties for higher values. These incentives not only re-
duce return temperatures but also enhance the overall efficiency and performance of the
heating network. [28]
Despite the potential for long-term savings, several financial barriers slow down the adop-
tion of LTDH systems. For consumers, upfront costs for retrofitting, such as upgrading
heating infrastructure or insulation, can be a significant issue. These challenges are espe-
cially acute in multi-family buildings where fragmented ownership complicates investment
coordination.[26] At the same time, district heating suppliers face high-capital infrastruc-
ture investments which are often characterized by long payback periods. This financial
profile can discourage private investors who require clearer returns on investment, thus
emphasizing the need for risk-mitigation strategies and supportive financial mechanisms.
[29]
To address these financial challenges, Denmark and other countries have introduced a
variety of public subsidies and alternative business models to support LTDH deployment.
Public subsidies, including grants and low-interest loans, help offset building retrofit and

capital and distributing financial risks. All these mechanisms enable broader financing for
LTDH by leveraging public funds to de-risk sustainable infrastructure investments. [30]
2.7
Digitalization and Optimization Potential
Digitalization is changing the district heating (DH) landscape by enabling smarter, more
efficient and responsive systems. The integration of smart meters, sensors, and Appli-
cation Programming Interfaces (APIs) is transforming district heating (DH) systems by
enabling real-time data collection and advanced analytics. In Denmark, the widespread
deployment of smart heat meters has provided utilities with detailed data on consumption
patterns, facilitating more accurate billing, fault detection and overall system optimization.
Projects like D4Heat are leveraging smart meter data to automatically detect faults in DH
installations, aiming to reduce energy losses and improve operational efficiency. By an-
alyzing data from heat meters and fault reports, the project seeks to identify and resolve
issues without the need for manual inspections. [31]
The availability of high-resolution data from smart meters enables real-time control and
forecast-based operation of DH systems. By utilizing predictive algorithms and machine
learning techniques, utilities can anticipate demand fluctuations and adjust supply accord-
ingly, enhancing system responsiveness and efficiency. For example, the use of smart
meters as feedback mechanisms allows the estimation of supply temperatures in distri-
bution networks, enabling dynamic adjustments to maintain optimal performance. [32]
A leading example of this approach is the Aarhus Smart Metering Initiative. In this in-
stance, the city of Aarhus has implemented one of the world’s largest smart heat metering
networks, replacing over half of its 56,000 meters with smart devices. This large-scale
deployment has led to significant reductions in water losses and improved leak detec-
tion capabilities. The increased amount of data has also enhanced customer service
and enabled more precise system management. [33] Despite the benefits of digitaliza-
tion, several barriers constrain the full realization of its potential in DH systems. Data
privacy regulations, particularly the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), impose
strict conditions on the collection and use of personal data. In Denmark, frequent data
collection from heat meters is permissible without explicit customer consent if used for
public interest tasks or legitimate interests, such as improving energy efficiency. How-

2.8
Energy Equity and Fairness
As Denmark transitions toward low-temperature district heating (LTDH), questions of eq-
uity and fairness become increasingly important.
While municipalities and utilities fi-
nance the expansion and modernization of district heating networks, the responsibility
for building-level upgrades, such as radiator replacements and improved insulation, usu-
ally falls on individual consumers. This cost allocation can disproportionately affect low-
income households, increasing the risk of energy poverty unless it is adequately mitigated
through supportive policies and financial assistance programs.[22]
The timing of adoption also raises equity concerns. Early adopters of LTDH systems often
benefit from government incentives, subsidies, and lower energy prices due to economies
of scale. In contrast, late movers may face higher connection fees and fewer financial in-
centives, leading to perceptions of unfairness and resistance to adoption. This dynamic
can create social tensions and prevent the overall progress of the energy transition. With-
out policy interventions to ensure fair cost distribution, these dynamics can lead to social
tensions and slow the pace of the energy transition.[37]
Transparent and fair pricing mechanisms are critical for maintaining consumer trust and
promoting behavioral accountability in energy consumption. In Denmark, district heating
tariffs are regulated to reflect actual costs, ensuring that consumers are charged fairly. Ad-
ditionally, some utilities implement incentive-based pricing models that reward consumers
for energy-efficient behaviors, such as maintaining lower return temperatures. These ap-
proaches encourage responsible energy use and contribute to the overall efficiency of the
heating system.[22]
Finally, disparities in infrastructure quality can lead to “comfort gaps” among consumers.
Buildings with outdated or poorly maintained heating systems may struggle to achieve ad-
equate indoor temperatures, especially when connected to LTDH networks operating at
lower supply temperatures. Vulnerable populations living in such buildings are at greater
risk of inadequate heating. Addressing these “comfort gaps” requires targeted invest-
ments in building retrofits and infrastructure upgrades, supported by comprehensive pol-
icy measures and accessible financial assistance programs. [37]

2.9
Research Gaps and Contribution
2.9.1
Gaps in Integration Across Technical Social, and Institutional
Themes
The current literature on Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) tends to focus on in-
dividual dimensions, such as technical optimization, economic feasibility, or policy frame-
works, in isolation. While each of these areas is essential, there is a clear gap in research
that integrates technical performance with social behavior and institutional governance.
For example, studies on return temperature optimization rarely consider how tenant be-
havior or regulatory constraints impact control possibilities.[2] Furthermore, LTDH has
been widely studied in new developments, however there is limited research focused on
upgrading existing district heating networks.
This independent perspective limits the real-world applicability of many proposed solu-
tions. LTDH systems operate within complex environments where technological efficiency
depends on human behavior, ownership structures, and legal frameworks. A more holistic
understanding is needed to design solutions that work in practice, not just in theory.
2.9.2
Lack of Stakeholder-Centered Qualitative Insights
Many existing studies are based on simulations, quantitative models, or pilot projects in
new developments. However, the retrofitting of existing networks requires a deep under-
standing of stakeholder dynamics. Issues such as decision-making in housing associ-
ations, tenant autonomy, and the role of municipalities are often underexplored. Build-
ing owners, tenants, and utilities rarely have aligned interests, which makes investments
harder to justify. Few studies have gathered direct input from the actors involved in LTDH
transitions in mature, mixed-ownership urban areas.
Without understanding the perceptions, constraints, and motivations of those implement-
ing or affected by LTDH, proposed strategies could fail because they don’t match real-
world needs. While some papers briefly mention user-side dynamics, such as behavioral
influence on return temperatures,[21] they often treat users as passive system compo-
nents rather than active decision-makers. This lack of qualitative, stakeholder-driven re-
search results in blind spots during implementation, especially in retrofit projects where
social dynamics have a major influence.

structured interviews with stakeholders in the Danish heating sector, including utilities,
municipal representatives, building managers, and policy experts, the research aims to
generate grounded insights into the real-world challenges and opportunities of LTDH de-
ployment in existing networks.
The focus on Denmark provides a rich context for exploring these issues. Denmark is
a global leader in district heating innovation but still faces obstacles in retrofitting older

Methodology
3.1
Research Design
This thesis applies a qualitative case study design to investigate the implementation of
Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) in existing networks in Denmark. The aim is to
gain a detailed, context-sensitive understanding of both the technical and non-technical
factors influencing LTDH deployment. This includes technical feasibility, economic impli-
cations, end-user behavior, governance barriers and institutional dynamics.
The research centers on four embedded cases selected for their relevance and insight
into real-world LTDH efforts. These cases represent a diverse mix of technical expertise,
regulatory context, and operational experience. The rationale for this selection is to ensure
a multi-actor, multi-level perspective that can point to both systemic challenges and local
adaptations. A case-based approach is suitable for studying LTDH, as it allows close
examination of how infrastructure, policy, and organizational practices interact in specific
settings [40].

Results
This chapter presents the empirical findings of the thesis, based on four semi-structured
interviews with stakeholders involved in the implementation of Low-Temperature District
Heating (LTDH) in Denmark. The interviewees represent municipal utilities, academia,
planning, and campus technical services. The findings are organized using the PESTEL
framework, which captures the Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental,
and Legal dimensions of the transition to LTDH.
4.1
Political factors
Stakeholder Misalignment and Institutional Inertia
The successful LTDH implementation requires coordination among utilities, municipalities,
housing owners and tenants. However, the interviews revealed persistent misalignment,
conflicting priorities, delays, and lack of shared responsibility.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) explained that utilities are often motivated by long-term
energy efficiency goals, but their efforts are constrained by housing owners’ short-term
financial concerns. Building owners frequently hesitate to invest in retrofitting unless there
are direct, visible benefits such as improved comfort or reduced maintenance costs. Re-
turn temperature optimization or grid efficiency, which are key priorities for utilities, are
rarely compelling enough for housing owners to initiate upgrades.
Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) confirmed that the legal and regulatory framework limits
utility influence over private buildings. While municipalities are expected to support LTDH
rollout, their role is often passive or fragmented. In some cases, municipal housing units
are more cooperative, but coordination across sectors remains inconsistent.
Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) noted that even within public entities, departmental silos can
lead to contradictory decisions. For example, energy departments may support LTDH ob-
jectives, while building maintenance teams delay retrofits due to budgetary or operational
constraints.
Another recurring theme in the interviews was the lack of clarity about who is responsible
for implementing specific retrofit actions. In the Lyngby case (Appendix A.8), the transition
planning involved multiple actors, including technical advisors, municipal representatives,
housing administrators and contractors. However, decision-making was delayed because
each party expected the other to take the initiative.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) also pointed out that energy renovations often fall into a grey
area between operational budgets (handled by housing maintenance teams) and capital
investments (which require board-level or municipal approval). Projects often stall despite
their technical and economic feasibility, particularly when no actor assumes leadership. In

pilot cases, sustaining momentum is difficult due to bureaucratic delays and limited feed-
back mechanisms. Further insight was provided by Interviewee C (Appendix A.7), who
mentioned that utilities frequently lack insight into the internal structure or decision-making
process of the buildings they serve. This limits their ability to plan strategic interventions
or coordinate retrofits across multiple housing units.
Interviewees consistently mentioned the absence of a strong sense of urgency to imple-
ment LTDH-compatible retrofits. While national and municipal climate goals are in place,
interviewees viewed them as insufficiently actionable at the building level. Interviewee
A (Appendix A.5) stated that many building owners adopt a “wait-and-see” attitude, de-
laying action until regulations tighten or subsidies improve. Even when pilot projects are
launched, the momentum is hard to sustain. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) described
how initial enthusiasm in some LTDH projects faded due to long approval timelines, lim-
ited feedback mechanisms and the lack of immediate results. As a result, efforts often
are limited to “business-as-usual” maintenance routines rather than systemic transitions.
This inertia is reflected in the literature as well. Mortensen and Shaker [43] point out that
traditional asset management in district heating is reactive and based on fixed life ex-
pectancies, rather than dynamic system performance. Without institutional mechanisms
to act on data insights, technical innovations are not going to be implemented.
Planning Flexibility and Long-Term Readiness
The transition to LTDH is a multi-decade effort that requires strategic planning and institu-
tional flexibility. Stakeholders described several planning bottlenecks, such as short-term
budgeting, siloed decision-making and the absence of forward-looking frameworks.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) emphasized that district heating systems built today must be
designed with future operating conditions in mind. Pipes, substations and radiators which
are installed under conventional assumptions may become obsolete within a decade if
LTDH becomes the standard. However, short-term cost pressures often drive decisions
that do not account for this evolving context. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) echoed this
concern. In many public housing renovations, components are replaced “like-for-like”
rather than upgraded for future temperature regimes. This practice may save money in the
short term, but it locks buildings into high-temperature dependence, creating retrofit needs
again later. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) added that long-term readiness also includes
digital infrastructure. Buildings and networks without data connectivity, sensor integration,
or remote control capacity will struggle to participate in smart LTDH operations. Planning
for digital compatibility must therefore be part of every major upgrade.
A common challenge across cases was the rigidity of funding cycles and planning pro-
cesses. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) noted that utilities and municipalities often work on
annual or biannual budget cycles, which do not align with the long-term nature of heating
transitions. Opportunities for co-investment or strategic upgrades are therefore frequently
missed because funding is not synchronized across actors.

4.2
Economic factors
Economic Barriers, Incentives and Cost Allocation
Economic considerations are very important to the implementation of Low-Temperature
District Heating (LTDH), particularly in existing networks.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) explained that the economic value of LTDH is often dis-
tributed unevenly. Utilities benefit from lower operational costs and reduced heat losses,
while building owners face most of the upfront expenses for retrofits, radiator replace-
ments and pipe upgrades. This imbalance discourages housing owners from taking initia-
tive, even if the long-term benefits are shared across the system. Interviewee A (Appendix
A.5) added to this statement, by noting that from a building owner’s perspective, the re-
turn on investment (ROI) is often too slow to justify the capital outlay. LTDH benefits, such
as lower peak demand or improved system flexibility, are typically not reflected in tenant
billing structures or short-term maintenance savings. Without direct economic incentives,
building managers focus on immediate cost control rather than strategic upgrades.
Additionally, Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) pointed out that even when retrofits are tech-
nically feasible, the decision process is highly sensitive to annual budgets. If there is no
external funding or subsidy, LTDH projects are frequently postponed or reduced in scope.
In many cases, energy savings are not significant enough to compete with other pressing
renovation priorities.
Across all interviews, unwillingness to commit to high initial investments came up as a
dominant theme. The Lyngby case (Appendix A.8) illustrated how even a research in-
stitution with sustainability goals struggled to justify large-scale retrofitting due to rigid
budget cycles and competing capital projects. This is also the reality in many public and
private housing sectors, where investment planning is fragmented and long-term bene-
fits are discounted. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) mentioned that even pilot projects with
partial funding face resistance when the remaining costs must be covered by the housing
owner. Some buildings explore low-cost retrofits or behavioral interventions, but these
are rarely sufficient to bring return temperatures down to LTDH targets. Interviewee A
(Appendix A.5) observed that fear of the risk also plays a role. Owners are afraid of cost
overruns, unforeseen technical complications or tenant pushback during construction. As

a result, they often choose to delay action rather than become involved in complex and
expensive projects with uncertain payback periods.
Another important factor is the economic model for district heating, which typically charges
users based on energy consumption, not on return temperature performance. Interviewee
B (Appendix A.6) explained that even when users maintain high return temperatures, they
are neither penalized, nor are they rewarded for improving them. This lack of feedback re-

adopted. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted that without a broader policy framework or
regulation requiring performance-based pricing, these efforts remain isolated.
The academic literature supports this view. Mortensen and Shaker [43] argue that while
data-driven maintenance models can identify inefficient buildings, implementation is often
blocked by funding gaps or cost disputes between utilities and owners. Without aligned
economic signals, even advanced analytics cannot lead to real change.
4.3
Social factors
Behavioral Constraints, Autonomy and User Practices
User behavior is a key factor in LTDH performance. Across the interviews, stakehold-
ers discussed common behavioral patterns that conflict with LTDH goals, which are often
rooted in cultural norms and lack of system awareness. All of those increase return tem-
peratures and reduce efficiency.
Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) highlighted that many users still hold outdated beliefs about
heating use, such as keeping radiators on full and opening windows to regulate temper-
ature, rather than adjusting thermostats. These habits are very common in buildings with
poor insulation or uneven heat distribution, where users feel compelled to overcompen-
sate. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) pointed out that some residents manipulate radiator
valves to force more heat into their units. In older one-pipe systems, for example, block-
ing thermostatic valves in one room can redirect flow and pressure elsewhere, causing
unbalanced heating across the building. This leads to higher overall return temperatures
and worsens comfort for others on the same circuit. The Lyngby case (Appendix A.8)
confirmed this challenge. Interviewees described how residents in poorly heated end-of-
line rooms often used electric heaters or tampered with valves to boost comfort, which
further destabilized the system. These behaviors are not only inefficient but also increase
electricity use and safety risks.
An important challenge in the Denmark is the high degree of autonomy granted to individ-
ual tenants regarding indoor heating. Unlike in countries such as Sweden or Germany,
where heating systems may be centrally managed or regulated, Danish tenants have the
legal right to control their own radiator settings. While this autonomy is positive personal
comfort and democratic housing values, limits the ability of utilities or housing managers

to optimize system performance. Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) explained that this decen-
tralized control creates a fragmented operating environment. Even when buildings are
retrofitted for LTDH, the variability in user behavior can prevent the expected energy sav-

when financial paybacks depend on system-wide efficiency gains.
Stakeholders agreed that a lack of awareness among users contributes significantly to be-

Equity, Fairness, and Justice in Heating Transitions
The transition to Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) raises significant questions
about equity and fairness in the distribution of costs and benefits. Utilities gain efficiency,
but building owners and tenants absorb most retrofit-related disruptions and costs. In-
terviewee B (Appendix A.6) noted that while utilities gain system-wide efficiency, it is the
building owners who must fund radiator upgrades, heat exchanger replacements and pipe
retrofitting. Tenants, in turn, may face temporary disruptions or rent increases during ren-
ovation periods, even though they often have little influence over the decision to retrofit.
Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) emphasized that in some cases, tenants living in older or
poorly insulated buildings are asked to accept lower comfort levels to support LTDH tar-
gets. This is particularly problematic when residents already face energy poverty or limited
heating control. Without protection or compensation, such practices can deepen existing
social inequalities.

for early LTDH experiments tend to be better maintained or more institutionally connected,

4.4
Technological factors
Return Temperature Optimization
One of the most persistent technical challenges in LTDH implementation is achieving
low return temperatures. High return temperatures reduce system efficiency and limit
integration of renewable energy sources. Interview data revealed that both user behavior
and legacy system design are critical contributing factors to this issue.
With regards to end-user behavior, as discussed in Section 4.3, all stakeholders identi-
fied it as a key factor affecting return temperatures. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) noted
that “user misuse” is common, with tenants often blocking thermostatic radiator valves
or keeping windows open while heating is running. These behaviors lead to high return
flows, especially in older buildings where manual control is widespread.
On the other hand, Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) pointed out that many Danish residen-
tial buildings still operate on outdated heating designs, including oversized radiators and
insufficient balancing valves. These systems, originally configured for 70–80°C supply
temperatures, perform poorly under LTDH conditions, forcing return temperatures above
desired thresholds.
Even with building-level renovations, achieving low return temperatures remains difficult.
The Lyngby case (Appendix A.8) underlined a mismatch between theoretical optimization
and real-world conditions. For instance, while the system was hydraulically balanced on
paper, fluctuating user behaviors during peak hours consistently pushed return tempera-
tures higher. Moreover, the limited insulation of vertical pipes and hydraulic bottlenecks
in multi-floor buildings aggravated the issue. Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) also described

==PAGE:30==
physical constraints in retrofitting, such as low ceiling heights that restrict pipe reconfig-
uration and prevent the installation of larger heat exchangers needed for efficient LTDH
performance.
From an operational perspective, Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted that utilities often
have limited access to real-time return temperature data at the substation or apartment
level. Without granular monitoring, it is difficult to identify problem zones or react quickly
to temperature spikes. This data gap creates a reactive rather than proactive approach
to return temperature management.
To address these issues, participants advocated a combination of monitoring, technical
upgrades, and user engagement to address return temperature challenges. Interviewee
A (Appendix A.5) stressed the need for digital monitoring tools that can flag buildings with
irregular return patterns. Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) added that room-by-room bal-
ancing and automatic flow-limiting devices could help standardize system performance.
Finally, Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted the value of tenant education and visual feed-
back systems (see Section 4.3. Academic literature supports the shift toward data-driven
monitoring and performance optimization. Mortensen and Shaker [43] demonstrated how
machine learning models, like the Neural Weibull Proportional Hazard (NWPH) model,
can predict failure likelihood based on operating conditions such as anomalous return
temperatures. These approaches allow more targeted strategies for maintenance and
retrofitting. Additionally, Festila and Gahrn-Andersen [44] observed that the adoption of
remote sensing and GIS tools is transforming how utilities detect system inefficiencies.
Technical and Retrofit Challenges in Buildings and Networks
Retrofitting existing networks for LTDH is technically complex due to legacy infrastruc-
ture. Interviews pointed to several practical barriers, including one-pipe radiator systems,
undersized emitters, ceiling height limits and missing technical documentation.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) emphasized that many buildings in Denmark still use one-
pipe radiator systems, particularly in older multi-apartment blocks. These systems are in-
compatible with LTDH, as they rely on sequential heat transfer between radiators, leading
to uneven heating and insufficient performance at low supply temperatures. Converting
such systems to two-pipe configurations requires invasive and expensive renovations that
many housing owners are reluctant to invest in.
Radiator sizing is another critical issue. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) noted that under-
sized radiators, common in apartments originally designed for high-temperature supply,
fail to deliver adequate comfort when operating at LTDH levels. As a result, tenants may
increase flow rates or tamper with system settings, which leads to higher return temper-
atures and operational inefficiencies. Additionally, Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) pointed
out spatial constraints in retrofitting, especially in buildings with low ceiling heights or
complex pipe layouts. In such cases, replacing or rerouting pipes to accommodate larger
heat exchangers, pre-insulated piping or decentralized substations become technically

==PAGE:31==
challenging. These constraints often force utilities to opt for suboptimal or partial retrofit
solutions.
From a network perspective, the Lyngby case (Appendix A.8) highlighted the presence
of hydraulic bottlenecks in certain branches, especially those with mixed building types
and uneven insulation levels. Older buildings which are connected to the same branch
as newer, better-insulated ones can disrupt pressure balance and lead to temperature
drops, particularly during peak demand. Inadequate or deteriorated pipe insulation was
also identified as a recurring issue. Heat loss from poorly insulated pipes, particularly in
underground segments, increases the energy demand on the supply side and reduces
the effective temperature at the point of delivery. According to Interviewee B (Appendix
A.6), this becomes especially problematic when trying to push LTDH to the outer edges
of a network that was not designed for low-temperature operation.
Another issue that came up during the interviews was potential infrastructure incompatibil-
ities. Legacy buildings often include outdated technical installations that are not compati-
ble with LTDH. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) mentioned that domestic hot water systems
without thermostatic mixing valves may struggle to provide safe water temperatures un-
der LTDH. Upgrading these systems requires access to apartments and tenant approval,
which is not always feasible in large or privately owned housing complexes. Further-
more, in some buildings, centralized heat exchangers or control cabinets are located in
hard-to-reach technical rooms, making it difficult to install new sensors, valves, or mon-
itoring equipment. As Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) explained, utilities sometimes find
themselves ”retrofitting blindly” when they cannot obtain accurate schematics or access
to interior installations. Finally, Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) raised concerns about the
lack of standardized building documentation. In many older buildings, records of previ-
ous renovations or internal pipework are either missing or unreliable. This uncertainty
complicates planning and increases both the cost and risk of retrofitting projects.
Digitalization, Smart Systems and Data Gaps
Digital technologies, such as smart meters, remote sensors and APIs, offer major op-
portunities for monitoring and optimizing LTDH systems. Stakeholders reported growing
interest in integrating these tools into daily operations.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) emphasized that smart meters and sensors can dramatically
improve the visibility of temperature profiles, flow rates and energy consumption across
buildings and user zones. These tools allow utilities to detect irregularities, diagnose in-
efficiencies, and adapt supply strategies more responsively. The Lyngby case (Appendix
A.8) demonstrated how APIs and building management systems are being integrated to
centralize control and monitoring. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted that the system in-
cludes automated alerts for return temperature spikes and pressure drops, which allows
technicians to respond more quickly than with traditional inspection routines. Interviewee
A (Appendix A.5) described how remote sensors and flow limiters can be used to cre-
ate feedback loops for individual apartments. These devices support optimization based

on temperature, meaning that they allow gradual reduction of supply temperatures while
maintaining comfort.
Another very commonly cited challenge was the fragmentation of data systems and the
limited access to usable insights. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted that although many
utilities collect granular data from smart meters, the data is often stored in separate plat-
forms that are not easily integrated into operational workflows. This results in information
not being fully exploited and opportunities for optimization being lost.
This agrees with the analysis by Festila and Gahrn-Andersen [44], who noted that even
when data is available, it is often not utilized in daily maintenance routines. Without suffi-
cient training and integration, digital tools become passive dashboards rather than active
decision aids. In this context, the stakeholders called for more investment in interopera-
ble platforms, user-friendly interfaces, and workforce training to fully realize the benefits
of smart systems.
Utility Capabilities, Workforce Skills and Knowledge Transfer
The successful implementation of Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) systems de-
pends heavily on the operational capabilities of district heating utilities. Interviews re-
vealed two key trends: an aging workforce with undocumented field knowledge and a
shift toward digital tools that require new skills.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) noted that many district heating utilities in Denmark are
facing an aging workforce, especially in technical and maintenance roles. These work-
ers possess decades of hands-on experience, often acquired through field practice rather
than formal training. While this experience is invaluable, it is rarely documented in acces-
sible formats. As senior staff approach retirement, there is a risk that critical operational
knowledge will be lost. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) emphasized that utilities often rely
on informal routines and personal judgment to respond to network events. This expe-
rience (knowing which valve to check first, or how a certain building typically behaves
under cold conditions) is not recorded in standard operating procedures. In LTDH sys-
tems, where operational tolerances are narrower and response time is critical, this reliance
on undocumented knowledge becomes a bottleneck. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) con-
firmed that new hires are often not trained in traditional diagnostic methods or physical
troubleshooting. Instead, they are introduced directly to software tools and dashboards.
Without proper mentoring or documentation, this can lead to a skills gap when there is a
need for troubleshooting in the field.
At the same time, the increasing role of digital tools (e.g. smart meters, thermal maps,
sensor data) has created demand for a new type of worker in utilities: one who can an-
alyze data, operate interfaces, and interpret digital diagnostics. Interviewee A (Appendix
A.5) pointed out that this shift is changing the recruitment profiles and internal training pro-
grams. However, Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) warned that this digital upskilling some-
times comes at the cost of physical system understanding. In the Lyngby case (Appendix

A.8), several technicians expressed concern that younger colleagues lacked familiarity
with basic components like expansion tanks, flow limiters or manual balancing techniques.
When systems fail or sensors give conflicting signals, these workers may struggle to re-

This is also documented by Festila and Gahrn-Andersen [44], who describe how digital-

training paths. Without proper training and documentation, these roles become difficult to
fill and manage effectively.
Some utilities are addressing this gap. Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted that some
utilities have started pairing experienced field staff with new digital technicians in mixed
teams. This improves knowledge transfer and ensures that decisions made in digital con-
trol rooms reflect physical system realities. Furthermore, Interviewee A (Appendix A.5)
emphasized the importance of formalizing internal learning. Creating documentation, op-
erational playbooks, and digital training modules based on real-life cases can help bridge
the generational knowledge gap. By doing so, utilities can retain system-level insight even
when personnel changes occur.
Despite the challenges, stakeholders also identified opportunities for capability building.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) described how data analysts, software integrators, and pre-
dictive maintenance coordinators are emerging as new professional roles within utilities.
These roles are essential to make sense of large volumes of sensor data and translate it
into actionable strategies.
4.5
Environmental factors
Although environmental concerns were not a primary focus for most stakeholders, many
of the findings have clear implications for sustainability. Improvements in return tempera-
ture performance, system efficiency and building retrofits all help reduce energy use and
related emissions.
High return temperatures limit the use of low-carbon heat sources like heat pumps, geother-
mal energy and surplus industrial heat. By optimizing return temperatures through both
technical measures and user behavior, networks can operate at lower supply tempera-
tures, enabling better integration of renewables and reducing the carbon footprint of heat
supply. Similarly, building-level inefficiencies, such as poor insulation, hydraulic imbal-
ance, and outdated radiators, result in heat loss and higher energy demand. Addressing
these issues through targeted building upgrades, supports not only operational goals but
also environmental objectives by minimizing heat waste. Finally, digitalization also con-
tributes by allowing faster detection of inefficiencies through smart meters and predictive
tools. However, in order to enable the full environmental potential of digital tools, it is
necessary to use the existing data properly.

4.6
Legal factors
Regulatory Fragmentation and Control Limitations
The transition to Low-Temperature District Heating (LTDH) is formed not only by techni-
cal and behavioral factors but also by regulatory frameworks. Interviews and case data
revealed that the regulatory framework in Denmark presents structural barriers to LTDH
deployment. Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) explained that under the Danish Heat Supply
Act, utilities are legally obligated to provide a minimum supply temperature to end-users.
While this regulation ensures heating reliability and user rights, it also prevents utilities
from lowering supply temperatures to LTDH levels, even when technically feasible and
desired for efficiency. In practice, this creates a ceiling on how low LTDH supply can go,
regardless of building readiness or digital control systems.
Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) noted that this legal structure reflects a fundamental tension:
utilities are held accountable for system performance but have limited authority to control
how buildings or tenants interact with the system. For example, they cannot mandate
return temperature thresholds or enforce behavior changes that would reduce thermal
losses. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) added that even pilot LTDH projects sometimes
face legal complications when deviating from standard temperature norms. Although ex-
ceptions can be negotiated, the process is often slow and bureaucratic, thus discouraging
wider experimentation or scaling. Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) added that GDPR regu-
lations further complicate data use. While utilities are allowed to collect operational data,
accessing room-level or apartment-level information requires user consent and legal clar-
ity. These constraints limit the ability to fine-tune LTDH performance at the individual level,
especially in rental housing.
Moreover, there is a lack of performance enforcement. Although utilities collect increas-
ing amounts of data on return temperatures, flow rates and energy use, there is no formal
mechanism for enforcing performance improvements based on this data. As Intervie-
wee C (Appendix A.7) explained, utilities may reward buildings that perform well or share
comparative data with housing associations, but they cannot penalize underperforming
buildings or tenants.
Interviewee B (Appendix A.6) pointed out that without enforceable standards or incentives,
many housing owners choose not to act. The retrofits are postponed and the system up-
grades are seen as voluntary rather than necessary. This regulatory attitude impedes
progress, especially in mixed-ownership buildings or large rental complexes where align-
ment is difficult. The lack of enforcement also affects digital systems. For instance, the
Lyngby case (Appendix A.8) revealed that although sensors and digital platforms were in
place, no formal requirements existed for acting according to data insights. As a result,
the available data was not always fully exploited.
Another issue that was brought up by the stakeholders was how the gap between legal de-
sign and operational reality creates daily friction. Interviewee A (Appendix A.5) described

==PAGE:35==
how utilities are expected to maintain optimal performance but cannot access the tools or
authority they need to influence building-level decisions. This results in inefficiencies and
limits innovation.
Interviewee C (Appendix A.7) emphasized that legal rigidity can hold back the optimisa-
tion of the system. For example, the inability to dynamically adjust supply temperatures
or introduce load-dependent pricing mechanisms, compromises the flexibility required for
LTDH. While progressive municipalities may experiment with alternative governance mod-
els, these are the exception rather than the norm.
These insights match the findings Mortensen and Shaker [43], who argue that predictive
analytics are only effective if they are integrated into legally supported asset manage-
ment strategies. Without regulatory support, even accurate performance data has limited
impact.

5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the results of this research and connects them with existing liter-
ature on low-temperature district heating (LTDH), emphasizing the thesis’s original con-
tributions. While previous studies have extensively discussed the potential of LTDH tech-
nologies, this thesis points out the systemic, behavioral and institutional challenges that
affect their implementation in practice. Organizing the findings using the PESTEL frame-
work clarified the diversity of challenges and highlighted the domains where stakeholder
priorities and institutional responsibilities meet.
The interviews conducted across Danish district heating stakeholders confirmed that achiev-
ing low return temperatures, which is essential for LTDH efficiency and renewable integra-
tion, is still a major technical challenge. While the literature often attributes this to outdated
infrastructure [46], [47], our findings emphasize the role of user behavior, such as tam-
pering with valves or misusing thermostats. Tenant autonomy, which is highly prevalent
in the Danish housing system, limits centralized control and complicates behavioral inter-
ventions. Though previous research has proposed consumer feedback systems and bet-
ter control technologies [48], this study highlights how behavioral inertia and institutional
norms often neutralize their impact. This aligns with Jordan’s argument that technical
fixes must be embedded in broader social systems [49].
A critical barrier is the fragmentation of responsibilities and the persistence of outdated
regulations, as discussed by Werner and Lund [3], [50]. The Danish Heat Supply Act,
which mandates minimum supply temperatures, constrains utility efforts to optimize op-
erations. Utilities are legally responsible for system performance but lack authority over
building-level components or user practices. This creates a policy misalignment where
system-level efficiency is required, but utility companies lack the authority to enforce it.
The interviews revealed how this disconnect creates a climate of hesitation, particularly
when utilities are held accountable for outcomes they cannot control. Similar concerns
have been raised by Geels in the context of governance lock-ins [51].
The interviews also confirmed that retrofitting remains an important obstacle, particularly
in older building stock. One-pipe radiator systems, spatial constraints and missing doc-
umentation make retrofits both costly and unpredictable. This corroborates earlier tech-
nical literature [47], [52], while our research further emphasize that these obstacles are
compounded by organizational ambiguity. When ownership structures are fragmented,
as it happens in multi-family housing or co-ops, there is often no clear decision-maker to
initiate upgrades. Budgeting practices, annual planning cycles and siloed responsibilities
further hinder action. These findings support Gram’s argument that retrofit feasibility must
be understood as a sociotechnical issue, not just an engineering one [53].
While previous studies have highlighted the theoretical potential of digitalisation for opti-
Implementing Low Temperature District Heating in Existing Danish Networks
30
==PAGE:37==
mizing district heating systems [43], [54], there are operational challenges when imple-
menting such technologies in practice. Tools like smart meters and fault detection systems
offer valuable insights, but their adoption is undermined by gaps in workforce skills and
legacy system compatibility. The interviews confirmed that digital roles in utilities now re-
quire a hybrid skill set; part technician, part analyst. However, many utilities lack the inter-
nal training programs or hiring strategies to bridge this gap. Festila and Gahrn-Andersen
describe this as a mismatch between digital ambition and organizational capacity [44], a
diagnosis which is supported by the thesis’s research.
One of the main takeaways from the study is that the challenges in implementing LTDH are
not separate or isolated—they are closely linked. Legal restrictions, user habits, technical
difficulties, and economic barriers all influence each other. For instance, when tenants
have full control over radiator settings (protected by current laws), it leads to behaviors
that raise return temperatures. This, in turn, makes utilities less willing to invest in digital
monitoring tools, since they lack the authority to act on the data. As one utility represen-
tative (Appendix A.5) put it, “We can measure everything, but we can’t do anything about
it unless the building owner cooperates.” This creates a situation that Mortensen and
Shaker call “optimization lock-in” [43], where no single stakeholder has enough power or
incentive to make meaningful changes to the system.
The findings also highlight important issues around fairness and inclusion. Investments in
LTDH, such as pilot projects or infrastructure upgrades, are often directed toward areas
with strong institutions or wealthier stakeholders. Meanwhile, older buildings or neigh-
borhoods with fewer resources risk being excluded, creating potential “cold spots” in the
heating transition. These areas face a double disadvantage: outdated systems and lim-
ited influence in decision-making processes. This supports arguments in the energy jus-
tice literature, which emphasize the need for fair access and active participation, both of
which were often missing in the case studies.
Nevertheless, the study identified several encouraging examples. Performance-based
pricing models, regular feedback reports, and centralized radiator control (especially in
Sweden and Germany) have shown measurable improvements in system efficiency. In-
terviewee A (Appendix A.5) pointed out that even modest financial incentives in Danish
utilities have helped reduce return temperatures in some districts. However, for these
solutions to work broadly, they must be adapted to local regulations, user behavior and
institutional capacity.Without such adaptation, even proven interventions may underper-
form in the Danish context.
In sum, the thesis confirms many known barriers to LTDH but provides a more meticulous
and grounded account of how these barriers interact. It also contributes new insights into
the organizational and human dimensions of the transition, an area which is still under-
developed in the literature. Successful implementation of LTDH will depend not only on
technical innovation but also on coordinated governance, inclusive planning and institu-
tional reform.

Conclusions and Recommendations
6.1
Summary of Key Findings
This study explored the complex technical, regulatory, behavioral, and digital challenges
associated with implementing low-temperature district heating (LTDH) in existing urban
environments. This study draws from interviews with key stakeholders and is grounded in
Danish case studies. It provides a holistic view of the barriers and opportunities in LTDH
transitions. This final chapter summarizes the main findings, translates them into strate-
gic recommendations and highlights the study’s contributions, the study’s contributions,
limitations, and the paths for future research.
The findings confirm that LTDH cannot succeed through isolated technical upgrades alone.
While supply-side technologies and retrofits are essential, their effectiveness, however,
is constrained by fragmented governance, outdated regulation, and limited user engage-
ment. One of the central insights is that these barriers are often interconnected. For
example, high return temperatures, a recurring technical problem, are shaped not only by
building infrastructure but also by tenant behavior, regulatory autonomy, and the absence
of shared responsibility among stakeholders. This reflects what Mortensen and Shaker
call “optimization lock-in,” a condition in which no single actor has enough leverage to
change the system without broader institutional support [43].
The interviews underscored the need for coordinated planning and better alignment be-
tween utilities, municipalities, and housing associations. Across the cases, it became
clear that even where technical solutions are available, such as flow limiters or predictive
maintenance algorithms, they often remain underused due to unclear responsibilities, rigid
budget cycles, and low digital capacity. In Lyngby, for example, return temperatures were
influenced as much by mixed building typologies and missing documentation as by the
actual infrastructure.
The research also identified growing equity concerns. Retrofit programs and infrastructure
upgrades are frequently concentrated in areas with stronger institutional capacity, leaving
under-resourced neighborhoods at risk of exclusion. This risks creating “cold spots” in
the energy transition. Ensuring that vulnerable buildings and tenant groups are included
in the transition will require deliberate efforts in funding, communication and governance.
Despite these challenges, promising strategies were also highlighted. In Sweden and
Germany, centralized radiator control systems and monthly feedback reports have im-
proved return temperature performance. Danish utilities have piloted incentive schemes
that show positive results, even with small behavioral nudges. These examples demon-
strate that progress is possible when digital tools are integrated with policy incentives and
stakeholder collaboration.

6.2
Strategic Recommendations
To support future LTDH implementation, several strategic actions are proposed. Utilities
should invest in internal training programs and digital upskilling to ensure they can operate
increasingly data-driven systems. Real-time monitoring tools and predictive models, such
as the NWPH model [43], should be adopted to anticipate failures and optimize return
temperatures. Utilities also need to act as facilitators and help buildings navigate funding
applications and coordinate retrofit planning.
Policymakers, on the other hand, must address regulatory misalignments. The Danish
Heat Supply Act, for example, currently mandates a minimum supply temperature, which
restricts utilities from implementing lower-temperature operations even when technically
feasible.
A more dynamic regulatory framework would enable customised standards
based on building readiness. Additionally, national and EU-level policies should promote
performance-based rebates and subsidies for buildings that reduce return temperatures
or complete retrofits.
Building owners and operators play a crucial role in enabling change within individual
properties. Collaboration with utilities is essential, not just for technical alignment, but
for resource-sharing and coordinated planning. Monitoring tools at the building-level, can
offer real-time data on internal heat distribution and detect anomalies early. Operators
should also take advantage of available funding opportunities and aim to involve tenants
in heating practices, using dashboards or monthly feedback mechanisms to encourage
energy-efficient behavior.
Technology providers can contribute by designing user-friendly, interoperable systems
that integrate with existing platforms such as SCADA and building management systems.
These tools must be co-developed with end users to ensure operational fit. Attention
should also be paid to GDPR and data-sharing compliance to ensure trust and trans-
parency in digital heating systems [44].
6.3
Thesis Contributions, Limitations and Future Directions
From an academic perspective, the study offers several contributions. It emphasizes
the importance of viewing LTDH as a sociotechnical system, where success depends
on the interaction of infrastructure, institutions, and users. On a practical level, it draws
on real-world stakeholder experiences in Denmark to provide a grounded roadmap for
future transitions. The use of semi-structured interviews across technical, municipal and
utility domains shows how qualitative approaches can illuminate systemic barriers that
are otherwise difficult to quantify.
Of course, the study is not without limitations. The interview sample, while diverse, was
relatively small and focused primarily on professionals within the LTDH ecosystem. Direct
input from tenants or vulnerable populations was limited. Moreover, the Danish focus
means that while findings are insightful, it may not be fully applicable to generalise them
to other countries without contextual adaptation. Future research could broaden the scope
Implementing Low Temperature District Heating in Existing Danish Networks
33
==PAGE:40==
by incorporating comparative case studies in Sweden, Germany or the Netherlands, or
by conducting quantitative analysis of behavioral patterns using smart meter data.
Future longitudinal studies could track the long-term outcomes of pilot programs or dig-
ital tools, providing insights into what works, what scales and what hinders. Deeper ex-
ploration of retrofit solutions for one-pipe systems, a known technical bottleneck, would
further enrich the field. This would also be achieved by more targeted research into gov-
ernance models that bridge utility, policy and user domains.
In conclusion, this study confirms that the transition to LTDH is not just an engineering
challenge, but a governance and behavioral one. Success depends on the ability to align
fragmented systems, engage users and develop institutions that are flexible, inclusive,
and data-literate. Although the transition remains complex, the strategies and insights
presented here provide a practical starting point for advancing the heating transition; one
that is smarter, fairer, and more resilient.

Insights and Explanations
Building Efficiency as a Prereq-
uisite for LTDH
Honoré emphasized that building-level efficiency is the corner-

Copenhagen are inefficient due to outdated substations, un-

but progress is slow due to limited consumer engagement and
financial constraints.
Economic Barriers Across the
System
Both consumer and utility sides face economic hurdles. On the
consumer side, retrofit costs are high and often deprioritized.
On the supply side, the deployment of large-scale heat pumps
is constrained by space, cost, and infrastructure needs. In-
centive programs are under development, including tariffs and
financial partnerships.
Regulatory Constraints and Lim-
ited Utility Authority
Current laws prevent HOFOR from intervening directly in
building-level systems. Although consultancy services are of-
fered, ownership and responsibility remain with building own-
ers, limiting systemic optimization.
Need for Digitalization
Approximately 75% of substations are not online.
Honoré
stressed that digital connectivity is crucial for real-time control,
predictive maintenance, and future innovations. Compared to
consumer tech (e.g., Wi-Fi-enabled heat pumps), DH infras-
tructure is lagging.
Infrastructure and Urban Con-
straints
Copenhagen’s complex, aging infrastructure limits the feasi-
bility of standard LTDH definitions. While a 75°C supply tem-
perature may not qualify as LTDH elsewhere, it represents a
significant shift locally. Pilot zones (e.g., Nordhavn) are being
used to experiment with retrofit strategies and supply temper-
ature adjustments.
Thermal Storage as a Flexibility
Tool
With the phase-out of waste and biomass CHP plants, ther-
mal storage becomes essential to balance renewable variabil-
ity. However, urban space limits where such infrastructure can
be installed.
Strategic and Policy Recommen-
dations
Honoré advocates for increased digital infrastructure, ex-
panded utility roles in building-level optimization, and financial
instruments to support retrofits. He also underlined the need
for a phased withdrawal of CHP capacity in parallel with renew-
able scaling.
Balancing Innovation with Re-
silience
A key concern was maintaining energy security during the tran-
sition. Without backup CHP capacity, system vulnerabilities
could emerge. Strategic flexibility must be embedded into plan-
ning.

Key Theme
Insights and Explanations
Systemic Misalignment of Incen-
tives
Utilities benefit most from improved return temperatures, but ten-
ants and housing associations lack clear financial motivation to
change behavior. Incentives are often too modest and benefits
delayed, making it hard to communicate the value of optimization
to end-users.
Behavioral and Cultural Barriers
Tenants have full autonomy over heating in Denmark, unlike Swe-
den. Limited understanding of system impacts leads to radiator
misuse and inefficiencies. Cultural norms emphasizing individual
control clash with the need for coordinated energy behavior.
Technical Constraints in Existing
Infrastructure
Older buildings suffer from outdated heat exchangers, unbal-
anced radiators, and insufficient electrical capacity. These tech-
nical issues reduce the feasibility of LTDH, though some retrofits
could be relatively simple if prioritized.
Institutional and Legal Bound-
aries
Utilities are legally restricted from intervening in building-level sys-
tems, even when problems are known. This separation limits the
ability to implement data-driven optimization and slows response
times due to bureaucratic requirements.
Untapped Potential of Digitaliza-
tion and Flexibility
Smart control systems and predictive optimization offer efficiency
gains, but their success is limited by fragmented infrastructure
and user unpredictability. Forecast-based heating reduction dur-
ing morning peaks can save up to 8%.
Strategic Blind Spots in District
Heating Business Models
Utilities are structurally unprepared to buy surplus heat, despite
technical feasibility.
Traditional business models centered on
heat sales limit the integration of decentralized sources like server
rooms or supermarkets.
Social Learning and Demonstra-
