Abstract
This study investigates the e↵ect of the listening room on sound externalization of
headphone-based virtual sounds. Moreover, this intends to determine if the decrease
in the perception of externalization when changing the listening environment is due to
the visual cues provided by the room, or caused by incongruent room acoustics. For
this reason a listening experiment was conducted in three rooms with di↵erent acoustic
features and dimensions. 18 normal-hearing participants were presented with speech
stimuli delivered over headphones and provided with either auditory or visual knowledge
of the room. Sound externalization was then rated using a subjective scale in terms
of three attributes: Auditory distance perception, angular orientation and sound source
compactness. Seven azimuthal sound source positions were tested and a further con-
dition without loudspeakers was assessed. The results revealed signiﬁcant di↵erences
in auditory distance perception (ADP) scores when the listening environment and the
recording room di↵er. Moreover, ADP ratings showed that the auditory impression of
the room becomes more critical for sound externalization the more reverberant the lis-
tening environment is compared to the reference room. Although this is not the only
sensory modality that inﬂuences the externalization percept. In fact, congruent visual
input showed improvements in the resolution of front back confusions as well as en-
hancement in ADP ratings when delivering virtual stimuli via headphones. This proves
that the degree of externalization relies not only on the auditory stimulus but also on
visual cues provided by the listening environment.
Keywords: Sound externalization, virtual sounds, visual cues, incongruent
room acoustics.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Human beings are often exposed to di↵erent sound sources during daily activities. Each
of these sound stimuli present particular features that make them distinguishable from
one another in space and distance. The brain retrieves the information presented in
the sound to keep track of what is going on in the immediate surroundings, as a pro-
tective measure against possible risks. For instance, one can easily localize a vehicle
approaching on a street under low traﬃc density conditions, even when this is beyond
the sight or hidden around a corner. This would not be as e↵ective without the natural
phenomenon of sound externalization. For a normal-hearing listener this is a matter of
everyday experience, and refers to the ability to perceive sound sources as originating
outside the head. When listening to normal recordings over headphones, however, the
same listener typically experiences a perceptual internalization of the sound. This causes
the auditory image to appear inside the head or very close to it, resulting in a rather
unnatural listening condition.
It is still possible to generate the illusion of a sound source at a particular location
away from the listener’s head, even when sounds are delivered through headphones. To
recreate these realistic acoustic scenes with externalized sound sources, one can use the
1
==PAGE:22==

Virtual Auditory Space Technique (VAST). This consists in recording BRIRs to be con-
volved with anechoic sound samples. The BRIRs describe both the acoustical features
of the room they were recorded in and the inﬂuence of the listener’s head on the sound
ﬁeld. Anechoic recordings, refer to sounds captured under free ﬁeld conditions, meaning
that there is no contribution of energy in the signal due to acoustic reﬂections. Thus, to
make the technique work the sound ﬁeld perceived at the listener’s ear drums must be
identical to the one generated by the real sound source in free ﬁeld. As a result, when
the BRIRs are recorded for each listener individually, the simulation can be surprisingly
authentic.
Nevertheless, it has been described several times that the simulation performs best
when the stimuli are played back in the same room where the BRIRs are recorded (Møller
et al. [1999]). This is, however, far less convincing, or the externalization percept even
breaks down completely in other rooms where the acoustic information of the playback
signal and the information about the room are incongruent. This infers that sound alone
is not always suﬃcient for recreating the perception of sound events by means of illusory
auditory images. Instead, the bottom-up e↵ect resulting from the auditory processing of
the acoustic stimulus might be accompanied by a presumed top-down e↵ect. This could
be supported by the fact that the perceptual system usually combines multi-sensory
information to yield a single percept according to the sources of information (Blauert
[2005]). The phenomenon describe above will be investigated, by attempting to separate
the role of the processing of the physical attributes of the signal from that of experience
driven top-down processing.
1.2
Background and literature review
It is well known that the human brain utilizes multi-sensory processing for spatial orien-
tation and navigation through the external world. Information gathered across di↵erent
senses intervenes in the detection of objects and events. However, the localization and
discrimination of objects is a task that mainly concerns the auditory and visual modalities
(Bulkin and Groh [2006]). In natural environments, sound stimuli provide suﬃcient cues
to spatial location, enabling a perceptual judgment regarding the exact position of an
object in terms of direction and distance. Moreover, binaural information can be used
to detect a target source in the presence of competing sound events. As opposed to
monaural listening, the processing of binaural cues requires comparisons of the incoming
sound signals from both ears. These occur in the higher stages of the auditory system

due to the interconnections between the neural paths from the two ears (Plack [2013]).
1.2.1
Directional sound localization
The fact that humans are provided with two ears emphasizes the idea that each ear is
exposed to a slightly di↵erent sound ﬁeld. Pioneer work on this topic, from early to mid
20th century, revealed that the Interaural Level Di↵erences (ILDs) and Interaural Time
Di↵erences (ITDs) between both ears determine, in most cases, the direction of sounds
in the horizontal plane (Feddersen et al. [1957], Rayleigh [1909]). Similarly, Mills [1958]
showed that the resolution of the angular orientation of a sound source is frequency
dependent, 1400 Hz being the upper limit where ITDs dominate and above which the
ILDs become more determinant. This was observed by studying the minimum threshold
angles for the detectability of the spatial independence between two sources producing
successive tone pulses. In turn, the discrimination thresholds were found to be 0.5 dB
and 10 µs for ITDs and ILDs, respectively. Ever since, a vast number of studies have
addressed the factors that inﬂuence the performance on lateral angle judgments and the
role of binaural hearing in complex listening situations (Hawley et al. [2004], Macpherson
and Middlebrooks [2002], McFadden and Pasanen [1976]).
Much research has also been devoted to sound localization in the median plane,
demonstrating the key role of the pinna for elevation perception. Ro✏er and Butler
[1968] and Gardner and Gardner [1973] explained the phenomenon as an anatomic ﬁl-
tering e↵ect caused by the ridges and valleys of the auricle, which presents a marked
dependency on the direction of sound incidence. Thus, it has been argued that the
accuracy for the localization process is closely related to the high-frequency content of
the signal, particularly above 7 kHz. This explains the reason why sound events can be
localized in the vertical plane, even with limited presence of di↵erential binaural cues.
Slattery and Middlebrooks [1994], supporting these ﬁndings, claimed that monaural pro-
cessing is suﬃcient for localization in both the azimuth and median plane, considering
that three out of ﬁve monaural patients showed little or no lateral displacement dur-
ing localization tasks of noise bursts presented at di↵erent positions. Nowadays, it has
been demonstrated that spectral shape cues not only depend on the pinna but also on
reﬂections caused by the head and torso (Vorl¨ander [2007]). The usual way to quantify
the resulting spatial ﬁltering are the so-called Head-Related Transfer Function (HRTFs),
which are distinctive across individuals and unique for any particular position in the space.

1.2.2
Auditory distance perception
Even though most of the academic attention has been centered on directional aspects of
the sound localization, in recent years several studies have focused on estimating sound
source distance. Zahorik et al. [2005] presented an outstanding literature review on the
ﬁeld, pointing out the main factors that a↵ect ADP. According to this, the main acoustic
cue responsible for distance perception is the intensity, which decays by an inverse-square
law as the separation between source and receiver positions is increased. The two main
additional cues reported are direct-to reverberant energy ratio and spectrum. The ﬁrst
obeys to an inverse relation that decays with increasing the distance to the source.
The second, on the other hand, determines the distance percept depending on the high-
frequency components of the stimulus, and the ﬁltering due to acoustic reﬂections of the
room. Zahorik et al. [2005] also concluded that listeners tend to underestimate distance
for close sound sources, and overestimate faraway source positions. Considering this, a
compressive power function model was proposed to account for the auditory distance
perception results in the literature (Kim et al. [2001], Nielsen [1991], Zahorik [2002]).
However, two recent studies are in disagreement with this assumption, since the reported
data did not present the characteristic compressive behavior Calcagno et al. [2012], Cu-
bick et al. [2014]. In contrast, at close distances the source position was underestimated
rather than overestimated. Both experiments were done in a room with the presence of
visual markers, unlike Zahorik’s experiment, in which distance estimates were carried out
in a booth. This, which is of particular interest for the current investigation, infers that
visual stimuli inﬂuences auditory distance perception, being more precise when sounds
are accompanied by visual cues.
1.2.3
Sound externalization
There is not a clear deﬁnition of the externalization percept yet, however, it is known to
present a strong subjective component. For the purpose of this investigation, correctly
externalized sounds are regarded as those with a well deﬁned perception of distance, di-
rection and compactness. This is based on the fact that real world sources are perceived
with a clear spatial deﬁnition in terms of azimuthal direction, elevation and distance. The
ﬁltering e↵ects caused by room acoustics and listener’s anatomy produce the individual
HRTFs that contain the distance cues along with binaural information. Considering this,
Hartmann and Wittenberg [1996] attempted to determine the features of these signals
present at the ears which are responsible for the perception of externalization. By emulat-
ing the spatial information produced by real sound signals, listeners could not distinguish
4
==PAGE:25==
Chapter 1. Introduction
between the real source and the virtual auditory image delivered via headphones. With
further processing of the stimuli, it was then possible to introduce interaural di↵erences
in level, time and phase. This enabled to study the externalization with respect to a
baseline synthesis, performing discrimination tasks and subjective ratings. It was found
that Interaural Phase Di↵erences (IPDs) only a↵ect externalization at low frequencies,
particularly below 1 kHz. ILDs, on the contrary, are equally important at all frequencies.
Likewise, an interesting ﬁnding consists in the need of preserving the signal spectrum
at each ear, being the interaural spectral level di↵erences insuﬃcient for the correct
externalization of sound events. Despite the fact that all experiments were done in an
anechoic environment (unfamiliar condition), and the limited number of participants (4),
this experiment served as a ﬁrst insight into the factors a↵ecting externalization.
Similarly, Kulkarni and Colburn [1998] investigated externalization perception by means
of paired-comparison experiments of sounds presented from loudspeakers and over head-
phones. It was found that smoothing drastically the spectral features of the signals
results in changes in the perceived elevation of the sound events. However, even in the
most extreme condition listeners reported externalization of the virtual sounds. Hence,
it was concluded that sound externalization and localization do not rely on spectral de-
tail, although it might be possible that the discrimination performance was biased by the
presence of the loudspeaker and the anechoic environment in which the experiments were
conducted. An additional publication in the ﬁeld was made by Kim and Choi [2005], who
compared di↵erent setups for reproduction of virtual sound images to determine perfor-
mance in terms of distance perception in the horizontal plane. The results proved the
importance of individualized head-related impulse response recordings and headphone
equalization on externalization. The ﬁrst parameter correlates with consistent results in
distance perception ratings, whereas the second directly inﬂuences the degree of exter-
nalization.
In recent studies, some e↵orts have also been made to identify the perception of
externalization in hearing-impaired listeners. Ohl et al. [2010], for instance, combined
internalized and externalized versions of the same stimulus to study the sensitivity of test
subjects to externalization. This enabled to determine how much of the external image
was needed to perceive a change from the complete internalization perception and vice
versa. Results across subjects showed less consistency in the case of hearing-impared
listeners, who were in general less sensitive to changes in externalization compared to
normal-hearing listeners. In a similar manner, Boyd et al. [2012] showed that, unlike
normal hearing participants, hearing impared listeners displayed a compressed percep-

tion of externalization, which could be attributed to hearing loss at high frequencies.
Furthermore, the absence of high-frequency pinna cues decreased the perception of ex-
ternalization in normal-hearing listeners. The latter was studied by convolution of speech
stimuli with BRIRs recorded with microphones placed behind the listener’s ear.
During the last years, several researches have published studies addressing diverse as-
pects of sound externalization. Such is the case of Catic et al. [2013] who studied the
e↵ect of ILDs ﬂuctuations caused by room-related and head-related binaural informa-
tion. To do so, dummy-head recordings were captured when speech was played back
at di↵erent source distances. The inﬂuence on externalization was then assessed in a
listening experiment in which participants were presented with virtual sounds generated
with previous recordings of individual BRIRs. The stimuli were modiﬁed in a way that
natural ILDs ﬂuctuations were limited by compression. The results showed a decreased
of the perceived externalization of the virtual sound sources delivered over headphones.
This was consistent with the fact that decreasing the distance to the source results in
smaller ILDs ﬂuctuations. Another recent outstanding investigation presented by Brimi-
join et al. [2013] demonstrated the importance of head movements for the externalization
of sounds. Listening experiments were conducted using virtual acoustics to compare real
signals stemming from loudspeakers with those presented over headphones. It was found
that self motion improves externalization when both virtual and real stimuli are ﬁxed rel-
ative to the head.
1.2.4
Visual e↵ects on sound perception
As it was stated before, auditory perception depends on non-acoustic factors as well.
Diverse physiological studies have shown that sound localization relies on both visual and
auditory cues to provide a combined perception according to this multimodal information
(Stein et al. [1989]). Likewise, King [2009] states that cross-modal associations between
auditory and visual senses are established based on experience, and suggest that vision,
being more accurate for localization tasks, is used to calibrate the perception of the
auditory space. Head movements have also been suggested by Goossens and Van Opstal
[1999] to be especially important for accurate spatial resolution of peripheral and rear
acoustic stimuli, as well as localization in the darkness. In consequence, the auditory
processing of spatial information should be a↵ected by the direction of gaze (Razavi et al.
[2007], Zwiers et al. [2004]). An additional investigation by Gardner [1968] revealed the
e↵ect of visual cues on the reported sound source position. In the experiment, listeners
were seated in front of ﬁve loudspeakers positioned in line at equal intervals of increasing
6
==PAGE:27==
Chapter 1. Introduction
distance. Only the nearest loudspeaker unit was visible, whereas the speech stimuli were
only played back from the most distant unit. The participants reported, without excep-
tion, that the signal came from the nearest loudspeaker. This phenomenon, in which
listeners tend to associate the nearest rational position with the apparent source distance
has been coined as ’proximity-image-e↵ect’. However, when Zahorik [2001] attempted
to replicate this e↵ect on distance perception, no capture e↵ects were seen in a semi-
reverberant environment, although the presence of visual targets improved accuracy and
lowered variability in the responses. The latter outcome is consistent with the ﬁndings of
Calcagno et al. [2012], who studied apparent distance perception including and excluding
visual cues. From the results, it became evident that visual cues biased the apparent
distance from the source. Moreover, the listeners who experienced the room prior to the
listening task presented an improved performance during the experiment.
Some early studies claimed that, despite the fact that auditory cues alone provide suf-
ﬁcient information to spatial recognition of objects, this is much more accurate when the
target is visible for the listener (Shelton and Searle [1980], Stein et al. [1989]). Hence,
detection of sound events can be enhanced by multiple inputs across senses. However,
it requires congruent information from the di↵erent modalities to provide a reliable rep-
resentation of the external world. This was illustrated by Sumby and Pollack [1954] who
studied the e↵ect of visual cues on oral speech intelligibility. When listeners could see
the speaker’s facial and lip movements, the speech intelligibility improved irrespective
of the speech-to-noise ratio. The opposite e↵ect was also investigated by McGurk and
MacDonald [1976], proving that speech perception is a↵ected by incongruent visual in-
put as well. During their experiment normal adults were presented with acoustic stimuli
of the syllable (ba), while seeing the lip movements corresponding to the syllable (ga).
However, listeners reported hearing a third syllable (da) as long as the visual input re-
mained present.
Several studies have addressed the study of conﬂicts arising between auditory and
visual modalities, specially, the visual capture e↵ect, also referred to as the ventriloquist
e↵ect. This occurs when an acoustic stimulus is accompanied by a spatially shifted visual
input. In such conditions, the auditory perception turns out to be displaced towards the
position of the visual event (Recanzone [1998]). This was further analyzed by Alais and
Burr [2004], concluding that the capturing e↵ect depends on the reliability of the visual
information. When it is clear enough, vision captures the auditory stimulus. However, by
blurring the visual stimuli it is possible to decrease the capturing to a point where it does
not bias the auditory modality anymore. Moreover, it was found that the phenomenon
7
==PAGE:28==

holds in reverse with auditory stimulus capturing vision when the visual target is severely
blurred. Besides, bimodal processing proved to be more accurate for localization than
each modality separately.
Nowadays, the literature concerning the e↵ect of visual stimuli on sound externaliza-
tion is limited. However, in recent years it has been given some attention. For instance,
Udesen et al. [2014] examined sound externalization using headphone-based virtual sound
in three environments with di↵erent visual stimuli. The experiment was performed in
two rooms, one model of a living room that represents a dry environment and a hall,
which accounts for a more reverberant condition. The experiment consisted of four con-
ditions: stimulus delivered from the loudspeaker in the standard room, virtual acoustics
in the standard room, and virtual acoustics in the hall with and without the loudspeaker
visually present. The results showed a decreased of the externalization ratings for all
conditions with respect to the reference. This was attributed to the change of the visual
impression of the rooms, since it was assumed that only visual stimuli were varied across
conditions. Nevertheless, this is unlikely to have occurred for two reasons. The ﬁrst is
that the acoustic information of the auditory scenes was incongruent with respect to the
acoustics of the hall, since the recordings were only made in the standard room. The
second is due to the fact that test subjects might be inﬂuenced by the room acoustics of
the environment, specially when entering the room and between stimuli presentations.
In order to study this e↵ect the experiment should provide a way to separate the perfor-
mance as a result of auditory impression and visual impression of the rooms.
Furthermore, similar research by Werner and Siegel [2011], attributes the decrease in
the externalization percept when changing the listening environments to room acoustics.
In this investigation two customization methods were implemented, one with individu-
alized BRIRs recordings and a second with dummy head recordings. The test was con-
ducted in two rooms that served as both recording and playback environments, one being
considerably more reverberant than the other. It was shown that the stimuli convolved
with the spatial information of the dry room were signiﬁcantly less externalized in the
reverberant environment. This was seen as a proof of the e↵ect of room acoustics on
binaural auralization, and by extension to the externalization of sound images through
the virtual auditory space technique.

1.3
Goals of the project
This thesis was inspired by previous work carried out by Cubick et al. [2014] on ADP, in
which the e↵ect of visual cues was considered as a possible explanation for the deviation
of the results with respect to previous data reported in literature.
Moreover, since
the last two investigations on sound externalization aforementioned presented di↵erent
conclusions regarding the reasons for the decrease in the externalization percept: Visual
impression of the room (Udesen et al. [2014]) and mismatch in room acoustics (Werner
and Siegel [2011]), these served as a basis for deﬁning the two main research questions:
• Is there an e↵ect of the listening room on sound externalization of headphone-
based virtual sounds?
• Is the e↵ect due to visual cues provided by the listening environment or is it caused
by incongruent room acoustics?
Considering this, the aim of this project was to test whether a clear inﬂuence of the
playback room on the externalization percept can be shown. For this purpose listening
tests are performed with normal-hearing participants in rooms with di↵erent acoustic and
visual characteristics. The goal is then to determine the key factors that a↵ect sound ex-
ternalization in incongruent listening situations: To which extent do visual stimuli alone
inﬂuence sound externalization perception? Is the decrease in the externalization rat-
ings caused by the mismatch between the acoustics of the listening and recording rooms?.
The ﬁrsts attempts to answer these questions included several pilot tests on sound
externalization carried out in di↵erent environments: Sport hall, lecture hall, anechoic
room, reverberant chamber, lecture room, audiology booth and outdoors. From these
experiments, it was clear the dependency of the background noise and the room acous-
tics on the degree of externalization of the virtual stimuli. The e↵ect of the visual cues
on the externalization percept, however, was less evident. This was only clear when
comparing the results with and without the visual presence of loudspeakers or markers.
Nevertheless, the experiments could be biased since these were conducted by few people
who had prior knowledge of the environments. For this reason, it resulted crucial to
include naive listeners with di↵erent levels of awareness during the test in order to deter-
mine exactly what causes the detriment in externalization performance. This would allow
for minimizing the multi-sensory interaction between the auditory and visual modalities,
and conclude based on comparisons of externalization scores.

This chapter presents an overview of the most relevant concepts involved in the gener-

some basic room acoustic concepts are presented as a summary of the interactions that

2.1
The concept of auralization
Auralization is the technique used to synthesized sounds from numerical measured or sim-
ulated data (Vorl¨ander [2007]). The resulting signals will therefore include the features
of the sound generation mechanism, the sound transmission path and sound reproduc-
tion device. When the transfer functions of the elements in this transmission chain are
known, then the signal to be delivered can be transformed by convolution.
This task is specially relevant if one considers that the quality of the stimuli presented
during the listening test a↵ects directly the test subject’s performance. For this reason,
the direction of sound incidence, the perceived distance of the event, the source char-
acteristics and the room impression must be preserved. To do so, the signal path for
both the recording and the reproduction channel should be ﬁrst identiﬁed to be later
described in terms of the transfer function from the source to the receiver. This can be

2.2
Impulse response measurement technique
Many parameters can be derived from the IR of a time-invariant system and its frequency
equivalent TF. The IR represents the output in time of a system when being excited by
an inﬁnitely narrow impulse. The output of such system can be obtained by convolving
a signal with the IR of the system.
y(t) = x(t) ⇤h(t)
(2.1)
Where y(t) accounts for the system’s output, x(t) represents the input and h(t) the
system’s IR. This task is simpliﬁed considering that convolution in the time domain is
equivalent to multiplication in the frequency domain. For this purpose, one can use the
Fourier transform, which yields:
Y (W) = X(W) · H(W)
(2.2)
Being Y (W) and X(W) the Fourier transforms of the output an input signals, and
H(W) the complex transfer function of the system, also known as the spectrum. This
principle is depicted in Figure 2.1. Thus, the TF is determined by performing a complex
division between the system’s output spectrum and the spectrum of the input signal.
Then, by applying an inverse Fourier Transform one can retrieve the system’s IR.

contains more energy at low frequencies. For this reason, one can use a non-white signal
such as a logarithmic sine sweep, which counterbalances this e↵ect by emphasizing the
low frequency content during the excitation.

the resulting spectrum, and ﬁnally the time domain version is obtained by implementing
an inverse Fourier transform. This can be then convolved with the system’s response to

Method
This chapter deals with the methods implemented in order to determine to what extent
sound externalization is a↵ected by the listening room and the available auditory and
visual cues. First the experiment design is presented, followed by the experiment rating,
subjects, and a detail description of the experiment procedure. Finally, further technical

3.1
Experiment design
The aim of the project is to answer the main research questions formulated in 1.3. The
ﬁrst is concerned with the e↵ect of the listening room on sound externalization of virtual
stimuli. The second considers the inﬂuence of visual cues versus the e↵ect of room
acoustics on sound externalization. For this purpose, a psychoacoustic test has been
designed for normal-hearing listeners with no prior experience in listening tests. The
challenge is therefore to implement an intuitive listening experiment that enables the
assessment of the externalization percept. This, in turn, should be carried out in a way
such that interactions between sensory modalities can be separated to analyze the e↵ects
independently from one another.

Experiment ratings
The perceived sound externalization is an attribute with an important subjective com-
ponent. For this reason, one can expect misleading results when attempting to estimate
this parameter using an objective scale.
Considering this, sound externalization was
assessed by implementing a subjective scale based on Hartmann’s deﬁnition for external-
ized sounds (Hartmann and Wittenberg [1996]). According to him, real sound sources
are externalized, localized, and compact. This leads to three well deﬁned attributes for
sound externalization performance:
• Auditory distance perception
• Angular orientation perception
• Source compactness perception
These three parameters were rated independently to simplify the experiment task and
to avoid interactions between the attributes during the ratings. For instance, the direc-
tional aspect of sound perception is known to be inﬂuenced by visual cues. However,
the e↵ects on auditory distance perception and source compactness are not totally clear
yet.
The ratings where done by means of a listening experiment implemented in MATLAB
R2015A. The subjects were then able to rate the di↵erent parameters by pushing buttons
using a tactile interface (Ipad). The attribute of angular orientation was assessed to-
gether with sound source compactness in the ﬁrst part of the experiment. This was done
by interleaving the ratings of the two attributes for a same sound stimulus. This means
that a virtual sound presented at a particular direction was repeated for the immediately
following compactness rating. Once all the signals were given a score in terms of this
two parameters, the auditory distance was rated in a separate experimental block.

3.2.3
Auditory distance perception rating
After completing the aforementioned tasks, the third externalization rating consisted in
assessing subjectively the auditory distance perception of the stimuli. The question to be
answered was: Which direction do you perceive the sound coming from? The rating was
done based on a subjective scale inspired by Catic’s (Catic et al. [2013]) and Hartmann’s
(Hartmann and Wittenberg [1996]) experiments. For this purpose, concentric zones with
increasing distances were presented, and the subjects were required to select one out of
six options that best matched the perception. The rating scale ranged from zero to ﬁve,
’0’ being a sound perceived entirely inside the head, ’4’ a sound coming exactly from the
loudspeaker position, and ’5’ a stimulus perceived at a distance beyond the loudspeaker
position. ’2, ’3’, and ’4’ were intermediate intervals that accounted for a perception at
the ears, at a position closer to the loudspeaker than the subject, and at some place
close to the loudspeaker, respectively. The rating interface used in this procedure can be
seen in Figure 3.3. For this particular attribute the subjects were instructed to use the
reference distance to the loudspeaker provided by the setup in order to emit judgments
in the other directions.

3.3
Subjects
Eighteen listeners (twelve male and six female; age, 20-29 years old) participated in this
study. All of them were naive listeners, who did not have previous experience in listening
experiments and did not know the experimental rooms either. The test subjects were
all master or exchange students recruited from the Technical University of Denmark
community, who participated voluntarily and were paid on an hourly basis. All partici-
pants reported normal or corrected-to-normal vision, and three of them wore corrective
lenses. Likewise, all participants presented normal hearing with the criterion of less than
20 dBHL for any frequency in the pure tone audiogram. This was tested prior to the
experiment performing a manual pure tone audiometry in an audiometric booth at DTU.
The procedure for obtaining the listener’s audiogram consisted in measuring the pure-
tone air conduction thresholds by means of the ascending method (see ISO8253-1 [1989],
for review). First, the tested tone was presented at a level of 40 dBHL, and after receiving
a conﬁrmation of the perception via a pushed bottom, the level was decreased in steps
of 15-20 dB until the stimulus was not longer audible. Then, the level was increased by 5
dB steps until a positive feedback was obtained, in which case it was decreased by 10 dB.
In total three ascending series with positive responses were necessary for determining the
hearing threshold at a particular frequency. The results of these procedure are presented
individually for each test subject in Appendix A.1. Once the hearing thresholds were
obtained, the subjects proceeded to perform the psychoacoustic test. All experiments
were approved by the Science-Ethics Committee for the Capital Region of Denmark
(reference H-3-2013-004).

3.5
Test stimuli
Previous studies on sound localization and externalization have used noise, music or
speech stimuli, among others. However, considering that the experiment participants do
not have any previous experience in listening tests, the most familiar signal is desirable.
For this reason, speech signals were selected on the basis that listeners have shown
problems to localize individual unfamiliar sounds, especially for distance (Coleman [1962],
Hebrank and Wright [1974]). A total of 24 di↵erent male speech sentences in Danish
were used as the stimuli for the listening experiment. The speech material was taken
from the Danish version of the hearing in noise test (HINT) (Nielsen and Dau [2011]).
These were divided into three groups of eight di↵erent signals to be presented in the three
test rooms. In each environment, seven signals were convolved with the individualized
BRIRs, and were presented in a random order. The remaining signal was used as the
reference diotic stimulus for in-head localization. An additional signal was also delivered
directly from the loudspeaker position 3 o’clock in the IECM room only. This served
as a reference for the optimal externalization rating at a position with clear binaural
information.

3.6
Experimental procedure
Before the listening test, each participant was given information about the study and
instructions of the test procedure in written form.
The ﬁrst part of the experiment
consisted in recording the individual BRIRs as described in Chapter 2. For this reason,
test subjects wore earplugs and blindfolds before being guided into the recording IECM
room. The earplugs help to avoid getting acoustic cues while walking through the room,
and from the sound sweeps played back during the recordings. The blindfolds helped
removing any visual interaction with the room and with the loudspeakers. The listeners
were also instructed to avoid speaking and to keep as quiet as possible while staying
in the room. This was done to avoid any prior knowledge of the environment before
the ﬁrst experimental condition was conducted. The participants were then seated on
a listening chair looking straight ahead in the middle of the recording setup shown in
Figure 3.4. A headrest was provided to help the listeners keep the head still. Once in
the listening position, onmidirectional DPA 4060 lapel microphones were inserted into
the opening of the listener’s ear canals, and positioned on top of the earplugs. This
task was simpliﬁed by placing a wire hook, attached to one end to the microphones,
around the listener’s pinna. This was then ﬁxed in position using the blindfold’s elastic
straps. Blocking the ear canal not only helped avoiding auditory cues, but also this is
known to increase the repeatability of the results by removing the e↵ects of the ear canal
resonance and eardrum impedance (Blauert [2005]).
Seven azimuthal locations were recorded by placing loudspeakers Dynaudio BM6P
at the positions 2, 3, 6, 7, 9, 11 and 12 o’clock. The criteria for the selection was
to account for front-back positions (12 and 6) within cone of confusion positions (2,
7 and 11) and outside cone of confusion positions (3 and 9), as shown in ﬁgure 3.4.
These were located at eye level (1.2 m above the ﬂoor) at a distance of 1.5 m from
the listener. 5-s Logarithmic sine sweeps were played back from each position at a time,
and the BRIRs were recorded averaging 6 sweeps repetitions per position. The level
at the listener position was 65 dBA, and was measured with a B&K 2250 sound level
meter. The playback and recordings were made through a M-audio Fast Track Ultra
sound card. This was suitable for the listening experiments since it does not require
external power, and provided a more ﬂexible setup for the displacement between rooms.
Once all seven source positions were captured, the last part of the recordings consisted
in compensating for the e↵ect of the headphones, as mentioned in Section 2.4. To do
so, Sennheiser HD 800 circumaural headphones were put on the listener with the mi-
crophones in the same placement. Additional sine sweeps were played back through the

4.1
Statistical considerations
In order to determine whether the data was normally distributed or not, a Kolmogorov
–Smirnov test implemented in MATLAB R2015A was performed. This can be used to
prove or deny the null hypothesis that a random sample population belongs to a stan-
dard normal distribution at a given signiﬁcance level (see Massey Jr [1951] for review).
The test showed that in general for all the externalization ratings (p<0.05) cannot be
assumed a normal distribution. Hence, non-parametric statistics were considered. In
general, there were not outstanding outliers in the distribution of the data, and thus all
scores were considered for the analysis.
A Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Bonferroni corrections was used to determine quanti-
tatively if the di↵erence between the tested conditions were statistically signiﬁcant. This
was implemented in MATLAB R2015A, and was appropriate since it analyzed paired
data on the basis of di↵erences in the medians, rather than the means. This test has
the advantage of not being severely a↵ected by outliers or heavily-tailed populations
Wilcoxon et al. [1970], although it requires the assumption of symmetric distributions.

4.4.4
General discussion
For the distance and compactness ratings, none of the participants consistently presented
lower or higher externalization scores throughout the experimental conditions. This was
seen in the small number of outliers shown as separate marks in the distributions of
ADP and source compactness ratings, which infers that the technique implemented for
the auralization of sound scenes worked properly across subjects. The argument is also
supported by the fact that the reference diotic signal, as well as the stimulus delivered
directly from the loudspeaker were given ratings according to a perceptual internaliza-
tion and externalization of the sound, respectively. Nevertheless, the inter-subject and
individual variability were large for some particular source positions irrespective of the
test condition. This was expected since it is known that certain positions, such as front
and back, tend to be more problematic due to the ambiguity of binaural cues (Plack
[2013]). The e↵ect is especially critical when the head movements are restricted during
the test condition (Brimijoin et al. [2013]). As for the angular orientation perception, the
listeners reported reasonably consistent values that deviate to some extent depending on
the position.
Sound externalization performance in terms of ADP ratings proved to deteriorate
across rooms. This result is consistent with recent studies, which have shown that the
best externalization performance of acoustic scenes delivered over headphones is achieved
when the listening and recording rooms are the same (Udesen et al. [2014], Werner and
Siegel [2011]). The least suitable environment for sound externalization of virtual stimuli
was found to be room RevS, which has a T60 of about 2.8 s. In this room, the speech
signals were not only given lower externalization scores, but also the perception of ex-
ternalization seems to present a compression. This was observed by the di↵erence in the
ratings obtained for the diotic reference signal, which was not completely internalized
in this environment compared to the rooms IECM and DryB. Although it should be
considered that the virtual stimuli were recorded in a less reverberant environment of
about 0.4 s at mid frequencies. This is in agreement with a previous investigation that
found that it was not possible to externalized correctly binaural tests signals recorded
in a dry room in a wet environment (Werner and Siegel [2011]). From these results,
the ﬁrst research question presented in 1.3 is answered aﬃrmatively, considering that
signiﬁcant di↵erences were seen on externalization ratings across the three environments.
The results obtained comparing conditions AL and VL for ADP ratings suggest that
congruent multimodal information enhances the perception of externalization. This in
turn, implies that externalization performance not only relies on the processing of the
49
==PAGE:70==
Chapter 4. Results and discussion
auditory stimuli, but also depends on top-down e↵ects. When the listening room is the
same used for the recordings, restricting either auditory or visual information about the
room results in a slight decrease of the externalization percept. However, the e↵ect can
only be seen in the most challenging positions (front and back). This indicates that re-
moving a priori knowledge of the room is not as problematic for externalization as having
incongruous listening environments. On the other hand, listener performed best when
peripheral virtual stimuli, particularly at the positions of 3 and 9 o’clock, were delivered.
This suggests that subjects are more sensitive to externalized sounds that present clear
binaural di↵erences.
From ADP results in room RevS, it was seen a signiﬁcant increase of the external-
ization ratings for condition VL, compared to VAL and AL. This infers that the acoustic
incongruence of the room is responsible for poor externalization performance in rever-
berant environments. As soon as the source of incongruence is avoided, the perception
of externalization improves signiﬁcantly, which proves that the acoustic impression of
the room causes the e↵ect. By contrast in room DryB, it was not possible to see fur-
ther improvements of the ratings by restricting either modality at a time. This could
mean that both sensory modalities (visual and auditory) were equally important for the
perception of externalization. For this case, the di↵erence in the reverberation time with
respect to the recording room was of 0.4 s, which is not as high compared to room RevS.
This suggests that the visual cues become more important in anechoic environments by
the decrease in the auditory information available from the room, which agrees with the
proximity image e↵ect found by Gardner (Gardner [1968]). The phenomenon could not
be replicated in further studies carried out in reverberant rooms due to the presence of
additional auditory cues. In this sense, the visual presence of the loudspeakers in the
anechoic room should be strong enough to produce a signiﬁcant di↵erence in the ratings
when removing them from the setup. However, the di↵erence was not as signiﬁcant as in
the other rooms. Perhaps, no further visual e↵ects were seen due to the large variability
found in the ratings of room DryB compared to the other two room. This could be
caused by the unfamiliarity of the listening environment for naive listeners, who reported
have never been to a room with such acoustic conditions. Considering this, one can
argue that both visual cues and room acoustics play an important role in the perception
of externalized sound scenes.
Unlike Udense’s experiment Udesen et al. [2014], it was possible to ﬁnd a signiﬁcant
e↵ect on sound externalization due to the presence of the loudspeakers in the room.
The e↵ect was more prominent in the recording room in comparison to the other en-
50
==PAGE:71==
Chapter 4. Results and discussion
vironments.
This in part explains the reason why the aforementioned study did not
ﬁnd a signiﬁcant e↵ect, since, in that investigation the loudspeakers were not removed
from the recording room. However, one cannot ignored that the loudspeaker setup used
in the two investigations di↵ered from each other.
While Udense’s experiment used
three loudspeakers, in the present investigation there were four loudspeakers placed in
an asymmetrical setup (see Figure 3.4).
As for the angular orientation perception, it was found to be independent of the room.
The ratings were mostly based on the localization of the virtual stimuli. However, the
results indicate that the presence of the loudspeakers in the room biased the ratings
given for positions 2, 6 and 12. When the loudspeakers were placed at the stimulus
position, an improvement was seen in the answers, which helped to resolve front back
confusions. This is supported by similar investigations that also found more accurate
results in auditory localization in the presence of vision Calcagno et al. [2012]. By con-
trast, when the loudspeaker was at a di↵erent position than the auditory scene, a spatial
shift in the ratings was seen towards the direction of the loudspeaker. This e↵ect that
has been previously mentioned in the literature Lewald [2002], Radeau and Bertelson
[1974], decreased when the loudspeakers were removed, and under low light conditions,
VA and AL, respectively.
Regarding the source compactness perception, it was expected a change in the percep-
tion across rooms. This is because the parameter is known to correlate strongly with LF
and the perceived spaciousness of the listening environment Pralong and Carlile [1996].
Despite this, no signiﬁcant di↵erences in the results were found when changing the lis-
tening room. In fact, these were dependent on the position of the virtual speech signal
delivered via headphones. Some positions were found to be more compact than others,
however, all the scores were focus on a small range of the rating scale. Additionally,
this results also suggest that listeners were able to separate the sound stimuli from the
environment that was being slightly excited by the external noise source.
Considering the nature of the task and the diﬃculty to rate the attribute ASW with-
out special training Merimaa and Hess [2004], it was surprising to see how consistent
were the ratings given by subjects. This indicates that the rating interface was intuitive
enough and that the auditory scenes were realistic. However, the downside is that the
steps in the scale used may not have been suﬃciently small to account for a signiﬁcant
change in the perception.

Summary and conclusions
Previous studies have reported a detriment in sound externalization of headphone virtual
stimuli when the listening environment and the recording room di↵er. Even though it is
not clear yet what causes this decrease in the externalization performance, recent stud-
ies have concluded upon di↵erent approaches. Some researchers have argued that room
acoustics a↵ects adversely binaural auralization, whereas others attributed the e↵ect to
the visual impression of the room and top-down processing.
Considering this, the present study aimed at determining whether a clear inﬂuence of
the playback room on the degree of externalization can be proven. Moreover, the e↵ects
caused by the visual and auditory sensory modalities were separated in order to examine
which was responsible for the reduced externalization performance. For this purpose,
listening experiments were conducted by 18 naive listeners in three rooms with di↵er-
ent acoustic features and dimensions. The experiments were conducted under di↵erent
conditions based on the level of awareness of the visual and auditory impression of the
room. Several conditions were performed with di↵erent levels of awareness, including a
further analysis of the e↵ect of visual targets on the externalization. Seven azimuthal
source positions were assessed in terms of three independent externalization attributes:
Auditory distance perception, angular orientation perception and sound source compact-
ness. From the results obtained the following conclusions are derived.
52
==PAGE:73==
Chapter 5. Summary and conclusions
There is a clear e↵ect of the room on the externalization percept, which is seen as
signiﬁcant di↵erences in the ratings given across the three rooms for the attribute of
auditory distance perception. However, the parameters angular orientation perception
and source compactness showed no dependency from the listening environment. Instead,
these can be described as source position-dependent attributes. Since the ratings ob-
tained for these parameters were mostly based on the direction of the virtual stimuli. In
this sense, positions with clear interaural di↵erences such as lateral positions presented
improved externalization performance compared with those that usually present ambi-
guities in spatial resolution (directly in front or behind)(Plack [2013]). Hence, the latter
were located with less accuracy and were given less compact ratings.
The auditory impression of the room becomes more critical for the externalization
percept the more reverberant the listening environment is compared to the reference
room. This can be interpreted as a mismatch in the auditory modality that produces a
detriment in the degree of externalization. Thus, when the incongruence is suﬃciently
high this overcomes the visual modality. Although it was shown that virtual sound scenes
generated from BRIRs recorded in dry rooms can still be externalized correctly in rever-
berant environments, provided that the incongruence is removed. Furthermore, results
from rooms IECM and DryB showed that this is not the only sensory modality that
inﬂuences externalization performance. In fact, for less reverberant conditions visual in-
put produces changes in the externalization performance comparable to those produced
by the auditory stimuli.
In a similar manner, the presence of loudspeakers in the room not only improved ADP
ratings but also biased the directional aspect of sound externalization. The results sug-
gest that congruent visual input help resolving front back confusions when delivering
virtual stimuli over headphones. On the other hand, conﬂicting visual information leads
to mislocalized sound images as a consequence of a visual capturing e↵ect.
In addition, the compactness rating scale implemented in this investigation provided
a feasible approach to test this attribute of sound externalization that has received little
attention. However, care should be taken when selecting the scale, since it might not be
suﬃciently well-deﬁned to see a signiﬁcant e↵ect on the ratings given by naive listeners.
For this purpose, trained listeners might yield more promising results.
Further research should focus on hearing-impaired listeners, determining to what ex-
53
==PAGE:74==
Chapter 5. Summary and conclusions
tent the visual cues inﬂuence the externalization perception, and if incongruous envi-
ronments could decrease the externalization percept even more. Likewise, further work
should also include di↵erent source positions in the median plane and distances that were
not considered in the current investigation. Finally, more research is needed to deter-
mine the importance of the context of the visual stimulus. This could include listening
experiments with di↵erent visual target stimuli such as signs and markers.
