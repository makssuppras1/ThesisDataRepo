Abstract  
Enzymes are an important part of everyday life. They help to facilitate chemical reactions in 
our bodies as well as in other organisms that are pivotal in sustaining life, without undergoing 
any permanent change. This project aims to understand the effect of different conditions on 
enzyme quality during production. In order to do that, we first try to find the best conditions to 
produce the enzyme, in this case Choline Oxidase. This involves using different strains of E.coli 
as a host organism, choosing different induction times to start enzyme production. Then 
followed by testing different temperatures to grow the cells after induction. All these parameters 
help us to find a condition which will provide us with high product concentrations as well as 
higher enzyme activities, which is not always correlated to each other meaning it is possible to 
get high amount of enzyme with very low activities as well as enzyme produced at low 
concentrations may exhibit high activities.  
The enzyme is among a few flavin-dependent enzymes that oxidize unpolarized alcohols and 
share a highly conserved catalytic site. Choline oxidase catalyses the four-electron, two-step, 
flavin mediated oxidation of choline to glycine betaine.  
Two different version of the enzyme was used, wild type and a mutant called CO6. Techniques 
used to find the best way of producing and testing the stability and activity of choline oxidase 
was SDS-Gel electrophoresis, Affinity chromatography (Ni-NTA), size exclusion 
chromatography, enzyme concentration assay, kinetics experiments involving colorimetric 
assays, NanoDSF and CD spec. to analyse the structure of the enzyme.  
It was found that E. coli BL21 SHuffle T7 cells seems to produce enzyme that are slightly more 
stable and active than E. coli BL21 DE3 Star cells does. This project also showed that there is 
a big difference between the wild type and the mutant of choline oxidase. The mutant produces 
higher amounts of hydrogen peroxide, has a higher melting temperature, and remains active 
longer under different storage conditions. The CD spectrum of the wild type and mutant is also 
very different although the number of the mutations are a few. While the wild type has a clear 
alpha helix structure, the mutant looks very different, but the exact structure is not possible to 
determine at this point.

1. Aim of the thesis 
 
Enzymes are an important part of everyday life. They help to facilitate chemical reactions in 
our bodies as well as in other organisms that are pivotal in sustaining life, without undergoing 
any permanent change. In industry enzymes are used as biocatalysts for many reasons. These 
reasons include their catalytic activity and stability which has caused a rapid increase in the use 
of them in bioprocesses in the past few decades1. When comparing to chemical catalysts 
enzymes is a more economical and environmentally friendly way for industry. Microbial 
enzymes have a wide range of applications in food, pharmaceutical and biotechnological 
industries1. Some examples are Glucose oxidase used in the bakery industry to improve bread 
quality and Tannases used in the pharmaceutical industry in the production of antibacterial 
drugs among many more enzymes used in many other industries1.  Because all the possible 
applications of enzymes it is also interesting and important to do research on new enzymes that 
could possibly be used for medications to help people, or maybe to produce healthier and better 
food for the future.  
This project aims to understand the effect of different conditions on enzyme quality during 
production. In order to do that, we first try to find the best conditions to produce the enzyme, 
in this case Choline Oxidase and its variant. This involves using different strains of E. coli as a 
host organism, choosing different induction times to start enzyme production. Then followed 
by testing different temperatures to grow the cells after induction. All these parameters help us 
to find a condition which will provide us with high product concentrations as well as higher 
enzyme activities, which is not always correlated to each other meaning it is possible to get 
high amount of enzyme with very low activities as well as low amount of enzyme production 
with very high activities. Therefore, we tried to find conditions with highest concentration of 
enzyme with relatively high activity under the conditions which the cells also grow in a fast 
manner. Eventually, enzyme stability under different impurities is also tested in order to 
understand its effect on enzyme activity and stability.  
To develop the method of producing choline oxidase it was grown under many different 
conditions and then determined which gave the most active enzyme. Concentration is of course 
important, but the activity is more important as it says something about how well the enzyme 
catalyses the reaction. Testing the stability and activity was done by doing several different 
experiments. The hydrogen peroxide production from the reaction Choline oxidase does with 
hexanol was measured to understand how much substrate was used. Stability was tested by

2. Introduction 
 
2.1 Enzymes as Biocatalysts  
 
Biocatalysts can be defined as the use of natural substances such as enzymes from biological 
source or whole cells to speed up chemical reactions2 . Compared to chemical catalysis, 
biocatalysis offers several benefits. Reactions are normally carried out in milder temperature 
ranges which gives a lower amount of energy needed for the reactions. Enzymes are typically 
stable under industrial conditions and can be used for a long time without the need of replacing 
them 3. Another positive point is that biocatalytic reactions can be carried out in an aqueous 
environment, this reduces the cost and the use of solvents, using water instead of organic 
solvents is also a lot safer for the people working with it. Enzymes are typically stable in the 
ranges of moderate temperatures and moderate pH values and will be extremely active under 
optimal conditions 3.  
Biocatalytic processes can be classified into three groups depending on the format of the 
biocatalyst used for the reaction. First group is resting whole-cell biocatalysis, second is 
isolated-enzyme biocatalysis, and the last one is immobilized-enzyme biocatalysis. The format 
of the biocatalyst is important when developing a suitable process and structure and the 
flowsheet 4.  
Resting whole-cell biocatalysis is when the growth and conversion of the substrate can be 
separated while still operating the process in whole-cell format. This gives several advantages 
when considering the downstream processing, and also provides extra freedom because of the 
option to change the cell concentration after fermentation 4. The productivity of the reaction 
can be increased when compared to aerobic fermentation and that the growth of the biocatalyst 
and use of the biocatalyst can operate optimally. With this method the cells could be recycled 
at the end which would reduce cost to the final product. However, this is rarely justified in 
practice 4.  
Isolated enzyme biocatalysis describe the reactions where the enzyme is used outside the cell 
in which it was produced. In this case the enzyme is either secreted during the fermentation or 
the cells need to be disrupted and the cell debris removed.  One condition that must be filled to 
be able to use these enzymes is that they can easily be separated from the product rich stream. 
==PAGE:10==
 
5
In many cases the cost of the enzyme is so high that it makes enzyme recycling necessary to 
provide cost efficient process. 4 this can most easily be achieved by using enzyme 
immobilization.  
Immobilized enzyme catalysis is when the enzyme is immobilized, usually on a spherical solid 
or more frequently withing a porous support, to facilitate removal from the product stream by 
filtration, which enables subsequent recycle 4.  
In this study we mostly focus on isolated enzymes, enzymes within cell lysates and utilize biocatalysis 
reaction to investigate their quality as a biocatalyst. 
 
2.2 Choline oxidase  
 
The first description of choline oxidase is from 1977 by Ikuta et al. 5 who purified the enzyme 
from Arthrobacter globiformis, which is a bacteria found in soil. This article by Ikuta et al. is 
also the first to describe initial characterization of the enzyme. Even though the enzyme was 
first characterized over 40 years ago, there has been few mechanistic or structural studies of 
choline oxidase.  
Choline oxidase is an enzyme grouped in the GMC oxidoreductase enzyme superfamily with 
the number BRENDA:E.C 1.1.3.176. The tree of the classification of Choline oxidase is as 
follows: The main group is oxidoreductases, the next is that it is acting on the CH-OH group of 
donors and lastly oxygen as acceptor. The enzyme is among a few flavin-dependent enzymes 
that oxidize unpolarized alcohols and share a highly conserved catalytic site. The oxidation of 
alcohols to carbonyl compounds is a very important chemical reaction that is central to several 
biochemical pathways. 7 One example is the polyol pathway in glucose metabolism8. Another 
example is the D-xylose catabolism, in which microorganisms use xylan as a carbon source for 
growth 9.  
Choline oxidase catalyses the four-electron, two-step, flavin mediated oxidation of choline to 
glycine betaine. Glycine betaine is one of the few compatible solutes used by cells to counteract 
osmotic pressure 10. The oxidation happens in two steps. First choline is oxidized to betaine 
aldehyde and the molecular oxygen is reduced to hydrogen peroxide. In the second oxidation 
step, betaine aldehyde is hydrated and gives gem-diol-choline. This is oxidized to glycine

Choline oxidase can however catalyse other reactions with alcohols and do alcohol oxidation, 
this was proven by Heath et al. 12, they showed that there is a lot of activity towards hexan-1-
ol, especially the mutant called CO6. In this project hexan-1-ol is used as the substrate in all 
reactions, this was chosen because of the findings from Heath et al.12 
Besides choline, the enzyme can also oxidize betaine aldehyde as a substrate. The enzymology 
of choline oxidase with betaine aldehyde has been investigated to a much lesser extent. Based 
on the observation that the actual substrate of the second oxidation reaction is hydrated 
aldehyde, which is similar to choline with an extra hydroxyl 13. 
Choline oxidase has three histidine residues and no lysines or arginines in the active site. It has 
an alternate mode of O2 activation, where O2 is catalysed by the nonionizable, positively 
charged trimethylamoniom group of the product generated in the oxidation of choline catalysed 
by betaine aldehyde 11.  
Choline oxidase in solution exists as a concentration-independent homodimer with a molecular 
weight of 120 kDa. The dimer interface includes two sets of six identical inter-subunit contacts 
between residues with opposite charge at the outer edges of the interface. A collection of 
nonpolar residues acting as a gate that allows substrate access and product egress to and from 
the active site of the enzyme is found in the central portion of the dimer interface 10. Choline 
oxidase’s active site cavity is shielded from the surrounding solvent, and the isoalloxazine of 
FAD is not apparent from the protein’s molecular surface. The active site cavity of choline 
oxidase lies beside the re-face of the FAD and is isolated from the solvent. Re-face refers to the 
stereochemistry of FAD, a face refers to the side of the chemical and Re is like the rules of R 
and S  when determining the stereochemistry of the stereocenter14. Additionally, a section of 
the cavity located far from the flavin is partly encircled by hydrophobic residues10. A three-

There has been developed mutants of the wild type of choline oxidase to give better stability 
and better activity. CO6 is a mutant that has shown promising results in research done by Heath. 
et al.12 This mutant shows a higher stability and higher activity when compared to the wild type. 
This project uses this to do further research on this mutant when comparing it to the wild type. 
The mutant CO6 has a better stability and activity in all the different test that were run. This 
mutation is remote from the active site, something that has previously been shown to increase 
the activity of enzymes by lowering the free energy of the catalytic conformation of the

the mutant has a higher specific activity than the wild type. Wild type has 0.034 µmol/min/mg

2.3 Choline Oxidase in organisms 
 
Choline oxidase can be a very useful enzyme in many ways, and it could be used for both 
improvement of crops for important plants, as well as the pharmaceutical applications as betaine 
which is a very important molecule for the human body. Choline oxidase has been proven to 
help plants with salt stress and therefore could be a huge help for crops in areas with drought 
and floods. At the same time the enzyme can be used in medical applications. The human 
version of choline oxidase has some medical relevance in male fertility, homocystinuria, 
metabolic syndromes and breast cancer. Despite this interest in the human and prokaryotic 
organisms, the biochemical characterization of choline oxidase has been very slow due to high 
instability of the enzyme once it is removed from the inner mitochondrial membrane18.  
2.4 Applications of Choline Oxidase 
 
The physiological role of choline oxidase is to produce glycine betaine which is one among 
several biocompatible solutes used by cells to compensate for high osmotic stress10. There are 
several biotechnological applications for choline oxidase. One example is that it has been used 
for genetically engineered water and osmotic stress resistant crops that lack efficient glycine 
betaine biosynthetic pathways however carry economic importance 10. The codA gene from 
Arthrobacter globiformis is responsible for production of choline oxidase, which can also be 
called a salt tolerance gene 19. By looking at transgenic plants, that has been engineered with 
the codA coding for bacterial choline oxidase it has been shown some resistance to various 
types of stress on the cells. One example is from Hayashi et al. showed that some plants had 
both choline oxidase and betaine when having the codA gene compared to the wild type where 
just betaine was detected 20. Another example is from Goel et al. showed that tomato plants 
with the codA gene grew better under salt stress than wild type plants 21. That suggests that 
presence of choline oxidase and as a result biosynthesis of glycine betaine is critical for attaining 
the enhancement of salt tolerance of plants 19. Similarly in humans, Glycine betaine is an 
important osmoprotectant in human kidney, while it also serves as a methyl donor in the 
biosynthesis of methionine13. 
Another example of biotechnological application for choline oxidase is immunoassays. 
Immunoassays are an important tool in clinical diagnostic because of the intrinsic advantages 
such as good specificity, high sensitivity, and miniaturization. Choline oxidase has been used 
as a better option when using horseradish peroxidase as a catalytic label to generate a large 
==PAGE:14==
 
9
number of electrochemically active products for the quantitative determination of an analyte  22. 
Glycine betaine is also important for a number of human pathogens since it takes part in 
controlling intracellular water content by adjusting levels of intracellular solutes. For example 
E.coli, Staphylococcus spp., Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Enterococcus faecalis, Klebsiella 
pneumoniae accumulate glycine betaine to compensate for high osmotic strength encountered 
at human infection sites 23.  
2.5 Characterizing biocatalysts performance 
 
Thermodynamic stability is a required component for industrial applications with regards to the 
folding and unfolding process enzyme 24. The biocatalysts need to be stable to be active and 
kinetic stability must therefore be achieved to a satisfactory level. Kinetic stability brings 
resistance to degradation and maintain or increase reaction efficiency of the enzyme. In nature 
enzymes evolve kinetically and thermodynamically to adapt to their environments. There are 
several types of mutations that occur within an enzyme over time according to the theory of 
neutral drift. There is advantageous mutations that drifted to maintain functionality, neutral 
mutations that has no effect on the functionality and negative mutations that drifted to alter the 
function of the enzyme 24. The neutral drift is not directly linked to thermal stabilization but 
both neutrally drifted and thermostabilised templates have a higher tolerance for mutations and 
are able to a higher number of changes towards new properties 24. 
The thermodynamic stability of a biocatalyst is indicated by the melting temperature, Tm, of the 
protein or the Gibbs free energy of unfolding, ΔG. The Tm is when 50% of the enzyme is 
unfolded.  The kinetic stability of the biocatalysts is related to a given temperature and salt 
concentration, reported as half-life t1/2 or as an intrinsic deactivation rate kd. This needs to be 
carefully distinguished from observed rate constants. For enzymes with a first-order rate of 
deactivation, the observed deactivation constant, kd,obs is linked to the observable half-life. 24 
Thermodynamic instability typically registers conformational change of a protein from native 
state N to an unfolded state U resembling a random coil, N ↔ U. This shows it as reversible 
but not all protein unfolding events are reversible. The unfolding can be measured because 
during the unfolding hydrophobic residues are exposed to the solvent. This allows the use of 
fluorescence to record increase in emission upon unfolding. Circular dichroism (CD) is also 
used measure unfolding. CD uses circularly polarized light to measure changes in ellipticity 
upon the disappearance of secondary structure elements e.i. α-helices and β-sheets 24. Other 
==PAGE:15==
 
10
methods that have been used is differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and differential 
scanning fluorimetry (DSF). 24.  
Another powerful tool that can give a lot of insight to the stability of an enzyme is using 
nanoDSF. This can tell us something about thermal unfolding, for example the melting 
temperature. By using the ratio 350nm/330nm it is easier to see the changes in the unfolding of 
the enzyme. A higher ratio means a higher stability of the enzyme25.  DSF measures the 
unfolding of protein by seeing the changes in fluorescence as the function of temperature. While 
conventional DSF uses a hydrophobic fluorescent dye that binds to the proteins as they unfold, 
nano DSF measures the changes in intrinsic protein fluorescence as the proteins unfold26.   
Circular dichroism  
Circular dichroism uses plane polarised light. It can be viewed as it is made of two circularly 
polarised components of equal magnitude. One of them will be rotating clockwise (right handed 
R) and the other as counter clockwise (left handed L)27. These two components and their 
differential absorption is what is used in circular dichroism. The L and R pass through the 
sample being examined and will be absorbed. A signal will be observed when a chromophore 
is chiral (optically active) for one of the following three reasons. It is intrinsically chiral because 
of its structure, this could be because of a C atom with 4 different substituents or disulphide 
bonds which is chiral because of the dihedral angels of the C-S-S-C chain of atoms27. Another 
way a signal will be observed is if the chromophore is chiral because it is covalently linked to 
a chiral centre in the molecule or the last reason if it is placed in an asymmetric environment 
by virtue of the 3-dimentional structure adopted by the molecule27.  
While thermodynamic stability measures the unfolding of the enzymes, kinetic stability 
measures the length of time the protein is active before it undergoes irreversible denaturation. 
The most common measure of kinetic stability is the half-life of denaturation, in other words 
the time it takes for the activity of the protein to be reduced by half. Even though unfolding and 
deactivation can sometimes be related they clearly refer to different processes and are quantified 
differently.28

2.6 Enzyme activity  
 
Enzyme activity can be defined using Units. A standard unit definition of enzyme activity is 
given below:  
1 unit (U) is the amount of enzyme that catalyses the reaction of 1 µmol of substrate per 
minute29. (Definition A) 
However, in research and development 1 µmol of substrate might sometimes be quite a lot of 
material, therefore other definitions might be preferred to avoid expressing quantities in 
fractions of units. The following non-standard is commonly used29:  
1 unit (U) is the amount of enzyme that catalyses the reaction of 1 nmole of substrate per 
minute29.(Definition B) 
==PAGE:17==
 
12
Specific enzyme activity refers to the number of enzyme units per ml divided by the 
concentration of protein in mg/ml. this measurement is important to have more knowledge 
about enzyme purity. Values of different enzyme batches of a pure enzyme should have the 
same values within normal experimental error29.  
2.7 Measuring product formation  
 
Enzyme activity can often be measured by measuring the rate of product formation, or by-
product formation. By starting the reaction, the enzyme catalyses the substrate and 
measurements of the product and by-products can be done. Colorimetric assays and gas 
chromatography are two good methods for this.  
2.8 Using colorimetric assays 
 
Knowing all the by-products in a reaction that a biocatalyst starts can help measure the activity 
of the biocatalyst. If one by-product is hydrogen peroxide for example, several different kits 
can be used to measure the amount of H2O2 in samples. One kit used in this project is called 
PierceTM Quantitative Peroxide Assay Kit from Thermo FisherTM. This kit measures the amount 
of hydrogen peroxide in biological samples. It does this by using iron and xylenol orange. 
Hydroperoxides convert Fe2+ to Fe3+ at acidic pH. The Fe3+ complex together with xylenol 
orange, in sulfuric acid, yield a product with a purple colour and maximum absorbance at 
560nm30.  
A way to calculate the activity is to find the amount of µmoles that is produced per minute. A 
reaction is started, and the amount of hydrogen peroxide is measured. And by knowing the 
amount produced in 30 minutes the rate of µmoles/min hydrogen peroxide can be calculated.

2.9 Downstream processing of Enzyme production  
 
Escherichia coli is widely used as an expression host for protein production. This is because of 
many advantageous factors presented by these bacteria such as its rapid growth, high yield of 
the product, cost-effectiveness, easy scale-up processes as well as easier genetic manipulations 
for high level recombinant proteins synthesis rates. It has relatively easy protein production 
system, and there is a large amount of E. coli expression vectors and strains that makes it 
attractive for industrial applications 31. E. coli was the first expression host that successfully 
was used to produce a biopharmaceutical, which resulted in approval of human insulin in 1982. 
Currently around 30% of approved therapeutic proteins are being produced with E.coli as a host 
31.  
When sufficient quantities of an enzyme of interest cannot be achieved by the native host 
organism, heterologous expression of a recombinant product is often the preferred strategy32. 
In industrial manufacturing they often adopt E. coli as a heterologous host for protein 
expression to facilitate rapid production. A common challenge when using this expression 
method is the generating of inclusion bodies due to protein misfolding. Inclusion bodies are 
aggregated masses of misfolded or partially folded peptide chains that can result from a variety 
==PAGE:19==
 
14
of factors32. Some of these factors could be when the rate of protein synthesis in vivo surpasses 
the capabilities of the cell and limited post-translational machinery. This can often be the case 
when overexpressing a protein product in a recombinant expression system. the misfolded state 
of the enzyme can be inhibitory for the biocatalytic capability. Because of the aggregates that 
form from the inclusion bodies, solubility is a property that is viewed as highly valuable in

2.11 
Purification  
 
Downstream processing describes the series of operation required to take biological materials 
such as cells, or plant tissue and from the drive a pure and homogenous protein product 46. The 
key to successful and efficient purification is to select the appropriate techniques that maximize 
==PAGE:22==
 
17
yield and purity with the minimum number of steps47. There are several methods that are used 
for protein purification. Some examples of these methods are filtration, centrifugation, 
ultrafiltration, diafiltration, precipitation and chromatography. Often in purification there is a 
mix of the different methods used47. For industry it is important that these methods are as easy, 
cheap, and effective as possible. Choosing the right conditions for downstream process is very 
important. The downstream process can be a costly process and the gap between the upstream 
and downstream has become a problem for the biotechnology industry. There has been a larger 
progress towards producing more protein per unit culture than it has been to improve the rates 
these products can be purified48. Therefore, reducing the number of separation steps and 
maximizing the yield at each step is important for an economical process. There is a growing 
need to develop new methods and rapid purification protocols and this is driving research in the 
protein purification market, which is growing rapidly 48.  
Bioprocesses are also more sustainable than chemical processes, this is an important thought 
when considering enzyme production and using them as catalysts instead of chemical catalysts.  
Some things to consider when designing the downstream process is the upstream process. For 
example, having an organism producing the product extracellularly rather than intracellular 
gives one less step in the downstream process. When product is produced extracellular there is 
no need to break the cells open, but there can however be other reasons for using an organism 
that produces the product intracellular. E. coli is an example of this because it is simple to get 
E. coli to produce the target protein, it is worth the extra steps in the downstream process. 
Sometimes it might also not be possible to produce the protein in question without using 
organisms that produces it intracellular.  
2.12 
Affinity Chromatography 
 
Affinity chromatography is one of the most powerful and diverse chromatographic methods for 
purification. It is based on highly specific biological interactions between two molecules. These 
interactions are usually reversible and is used during purification by placing one of the 
interacting molecules on a slid matrix. This creates a stationary phase while the target molecule 
becomes the mobile phase.   
A very simple schematic explanation of how affinity chromatography works is shown in figure 
6 below:  
==PAGE:23==
 
18
 
Figure 5 simple schematic explanation of affinity chromatography. Made with biorender.com 
 
The way affinity chromatography works is that the solution contain the enzyme is poured into 
the resin and enzyme will bind to the resin. The resin is then washed, and all non-specific 
compounds is washed away and only the enzyme is left. The enzyme is then eluted where a 
solution releases the enzyme from the resin and the enzyme is collected. This elution solution 
binds to the resin stronger than the enzyme and the enzyme is therefore released. Very often the 
enzyme needs to have the salt removed after the affinity purification. This to remove salt that 
might be left in the buffer, but it could also remove detergents, nucleotides and lipids49.  
His/Ni-NTA system is used for affinity purification or recombinant proteins and antigenic 
peptides. It is based on the high affinity of six consecutive histidine residues to immobilized 
nickel ions (6xHis). This gives a highly selective interaction that allows purification of tagged 
proteins or protein complexes 50. The tight association between the tag and the resin allows 
contaminants to be easily washed away, while at the same time the bound proteins can be gently 
eluted by competition with imidazole 50. Since the affinity tag for this type of purification is 
only six consecutive histidine residues means that the small size only gives a minimal addition 
of extra amino acids. They are also uncharged at pH 8.0 which rarely affects the secretion or 
the folding of the protein to which it is attached 50.  
2.13 
Size exclusion Chromatography/ Gel filtration 
 
Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) has two phases. One mobile phase that contains the 
product and one stationary phase that is a porous gel filtration polymer. Because the stationary 
==PAGE:24==
 
19
phase is porous larger particles will elute or move faster while smaller particles will elute or 
move slower. This is because the bigger particles will not meet as many pores as the smaller 
ones will. The smaller ones will therefore need more time to be able to move withing the pores. 
The stationary phase can be packed with different types of gels, some examples are crosslinked 
dextrans, agarose, polyacrylamide, or allydlextran51.  
Size exclusion chromatography is based on size, specifically on the stokes radii. This is 
synonymous with the hydrodynamic radius of the isolate in question. This radius is defined as 
the apparent size of the solvated/hydrated particle. The shape of the particles must also be taken 
into consideration as well as molecular size and weight of the particles. If the shape is not 
spherical it might elute at a different stage than a molecule with equal size but different shape.  
2.14 
Gel electrophoresis 
 
One dimensional gel electrophoresis of proteins can provide information about the molecular 
size, amount, and purity of the protein samples. In polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE), 
the proteins will migrate following an electrical field through pores in the gel. The pore size 
will decrease with increased amount of acrylamide concentrations. The combination of protein 
charge, gel pore size, and shape determines the migration rate of the protein52. When the 
electrical charge is removed the proteins will stay in the place they were when the charge was 
removed. these will show up as bands on the gels.  
Glycine-SDS-page is a commonly used SDS electrophoretic technique for separating proteins 
in the mass range 1-100 kDa. To make the bands visible they need to be stained. There are 
different staining techniques that can be used to visualize the protein bands on the gels. 
Coomassie can be used when there is a minimum of 0,2 µg of protein in the band. If the 
concentration of the protein is much lower silver staining can be used53.

3.2 Chemically competent cell preparation 
 
Chemically competent cells were made so that transformations of the cells could be done. One 
colony from an agar plate with the desired bacteria was added to 5mL LB broth in a falcon tube 
and was grown overnight at 220 RPM at 37°C. 1 mL of this overnight culture was added to 100 
mL of LB broth in an autoclaved flask. The culture was incubated at 37 °C at 220 rpm until it 
==PAGE:26==

RPM for 10 min, then resuspended in 15 mL 80 mM MgCl2 +20 mM CaCl2. Centrifuges one 
more time and resuspended again. 1mL of 85 mM CaCl2 + 15% glycerol was added to the tubes. 
100 µL of the competent cell solution was added to each Eppendorf tube and stored at -80 oC. 
3.3 Bacterial Transformation  
 
Bacterial transformation was done to insert the desired plasmid/genes into the bacteria. 2µL of 
plasmid was added to each Eppendorf tube of competent cells. The cells were left on ice for 
20min. they were then heated for 60 seconds at 42 °C and then put on ice again for 2 min. 800 
µL of LB media was added to each tube. The cells were incubated for 1 hour and 30 min at 37 
°C and then transferred to agar played with the corresponding antibiotic. The plates were left in 
the incubator over night at 37 °C. In this project the pET28a vectors were used. They are 5369 
bp long and resistant to kanamycin.  The expression sequence and a map of the plasmid can be 
found in appendix 1 and 2.

4.8 Effect of growth temperature  
 
Temperature is an important element for both cellular growth as well as the correct folding of 
the protein. Therefore, before producing big batches of enzymes it is important to investigate 
the right temperature for each particular enzyme. This is why both BL-21 DE3* and Shuffle 
cells were grown under different temperatures and the reactions were set with the cell lysates 
from each condition. The product formation was followed by taking samples at certain time 
intervals. 
The temperature effect was seen differently for both E. coli strains as well as the enzymes. 
Lower temperatures were favoured for both strains and enzymes. The effect of lower 
temperature however seems to be dependent of the induction time. In the meantime, enzymes 
behave differently for the same temperature and induction time in different strains. For example, 
for BL-21 DE3* cells, when induced at OD600 0,8, both enzymes showed high activities at 16 
C, yet when induced at OD600 2,5 better enzymes that were produced at 20 C showed better 
performance. In the case of enzyme produced in Shuffle cells, it was the opposite; for induction 
at OD600 0,8, enzymes favoured 20 C, and showed higher activities at 16 C when induced at 
OD600 2,5. (Figure 19, Table 14 )

production, and this process becomes more costly by addition of each step. Purification in 
particular lengthens the process and increase the cost. Therefore, it is important to understand 
to what degree enzymes needs to be purified, and does it have any positive or negative effect 
on their performance. In order to understand the effect of impurities on the enzyme, we mixed 
purified enzyme 1mg/ml with different concentrations of the cell free extract from empty BL-

performed biocatalysis reaction.

5. Discussion  
 
Downstream process optimisation for enzyme production is an important stage for generating 
efficient biocatalysts. Usually, most common mistake is made by directly expressing enzymes 
under commonly used conditions while neglecting the fact that each enzyme has different 
structure and can behave quite differently under similar conditions. This, when not taken into 
consideration, results with misguiding negative results meaning we may assume that the 
enzyme is not working meanwhile it is the expression conditions that are not right for the 
enzyme to work efficiently.54 Therefore, before working with any enzyme, there needs to be a 
downstream process optimisation to be able to achieve highest, or detectable activity during 
selection and for biocatalysis reactions. Table 27 shows an overview of how to higher amount 
of product by using enzymes in biotechnology industry.

In the case of commercially available enzymes that are usually used for biocatalysis, the 
companies typically provide only Quality Control (QC) information with minimal data such as 
gel electrophoresis results, buffer information, and maybe the enzyme sequence. However, 
there are no further information regarding the enzyme solubility, aggregation amount, 
homogeneity, or activity.55 This results in problems such as data reproducibility, or performance 
differences between the same enzyme from different batches.

First part of the project is to decide the best condition to produce the most stable and active 
enzyme. This includes a lot of different tests, but the most important part is to decide what is 
most important to achieve. Is it to produce the most enzyme, produce it as fast as possible 
(doubling time) is it the most active one, or is it the most stable. To have a high concentration 
and a lower doubling time might seem good at first, but when thinking about what to use the 
enzyme for later it might not be as good as originally thought.  
For some of the growth curves there is a large error rate. It is possible to say that the results are 
reliable but at the same time when working with living organisms it is always difficult to get 
the same results every time. There can be small variations that can cause big differences in the 
results. It is difficult to say exactly what causes the difference in the results, but it could be that 
there was some soap left in the flasks that were used. The media it was grown in can come from 
a different batch, even though it was all made in the same way all the time there can still be 
==PAGE:74==
 
69
small variations that is impossible to predict. Also just using a different colony of the bacteria 
that might be growing a little bit different than the colony used earlier.  
The difference in growth however is important to consider because each batch can be slightly 
different, and therefore give different results when doing further testing. In industry this is 
especially important since the same product is expected to be delivered each time and therefor 
the quality controls to be sure of these differences is crucial.  
The SDS gels shows the first indication of the concentration the different conditions produce. 
They also show that all the conditions actually produce enzyme. That is a good start and so far 
does not eliminate any conditions. The highest concentration came from BL21 DE3 CO6 grown 
at 20°C after induction. Initially this might tell us that this could be the best way to produce the 
enzyme because it gives us a lot of the enzyme, however that might not be the case. An 
important question in this research is what do we actually want. Do we want to have the highest 
concentration of enzyme, the “easiest” way to produce it, the fastest growing cells or the most 
stable and active enzyme. When saying easiest it is referred to that for example many incubators 
do not have cooling and therefore it can be easier to keep something above room temperature 
compared to below room temperature.  
When looking at costs for example we can think that the highest concentration is the best, and 
some enzymes and applications it might be, depending a lot on what it is used for. Often though, 
a high activity and high stability is important. Since enzymes is biocatalysts very often the 
desired use of them is the reaction they catalyse, therefore the activity is important.  
The stability is also important, just thinking about transporting the enzyme to the company that 
uses it, it needs to still be active when it arrives. Therefore, storage conditions are important. 
Having a mutant that gives a more stable enzyme can be a good solution for this. It is much 
easier to transport something that doesn’t need cooling or needs to be stored at 4°C rather than 
at -80°C. It looks like this is the case for the CO6 mutant. When measuring different storage 
condition, the mutant always has a higher activity than the wild type, and the wild type loses its 
activity much faster.  
The different strains of E. coli seems to have made a difference. The BL21 DE3 grows faster 
than the Shuffle cells. But the shuffle cells seem to produce a more stable enzyme. This could 
be explained, because shuffle cells are specifically designed to work with the disulphide bridges 
of the enzymes. This is promising when thinking about future production of new and existing 
enzymes and other targets. Improving all aspects of the production process important so that 
==PAGE:75==
 
70
the whole process can be as effective as possible and choosing the right production host is very 
important.  
The small reactions set up before purification to test the conversion rate of hexanol from the 
different conditions was to confirm which condition was the one that created the most active 
enzymes. This showed that for the mutant CO6, induced at OD 2.5 and grown at 16 °C gave an 
almost 100% conversion rate for both Shuffle and DE3. It was also looked at the wild type at 
the same conditions of induced at OD 2.5 and grown at 16 °C. The condition that produced the 
highest conversion rate for BL21 DE3 Wild type and mutant and SHuffle wild type and mutant 
was chosen, and it was decided to continue with these four conditions. For all four induction 
OD was 2.5 and temperature for growing was 16 °C. The reason to continue with 4 and not only 
one is the get a better knowledge of the difference between the mutant and the wild type, but 
also that differences in the expression host can give differences in the activity of the enzyme. 
By continuing with these 4 conditions, it is also possible to see if there is a difference in the 
production host and which one could possibly be better at producing this enzyme. If it is 
possible to prove that one host is better than another in combination with induction OD and 
temperature it might be possible to use this production strategy for other enzymes. Every 
enzyme is different but there is probably something that can bet taken from this process and 
used for other enzymes.  
The low temperature of 16°C slows down the growth, which in turn gives the cell more time to 
divide. This could possibly be a reason for why the low temperature creates a more active 
enzyme. The more time it has to divide, the more time it has for the enzyme to fold correctly. 
This is also the reason for why the cells are grown at 37°C before induction, because before 
induction the goal is just to get a larger population of cells and they are still not producing the 
product yet. However, when the enzyme is produced a lower temperature can help with correct 
folding, this might not be the case for every enzyme but for choline oxidase it does create a 
more active enzyme. It would be interesting to see if this is the case for other enzymes.  
One important question is that we do not actually know if the low temperature when producing 
the enzyme creates a more stable enzyme, form the results we can see a more active enzyme 
compared to the other temperatures, but we do not know the stability for sure. This is something 
that would be interesting to test, but a lot more time would be needed to do more research on 
this. One study56 suggests that mutations made at enzymes close to the active site play a role in 
the activity, while mutations made further away might give the enzyme higher stability. It can 
look like for stability there is no specific place for the mutation that fits all enzymes and that 
==PAGE:76==
 
71
the alterations that happens from the mutation might happen close or far away from the mutation 
itself. This indicates that mutations for stability has to be tested for each enzyme and the desired 
outcome of the mutations56. Another study suggests that the optimal temperature is dependent 
on each enzyme and that there might be other enzymes that has 16°C as the best temperature 
for protein expression44. Can the way this enzyme is produced might be good for other enzymes 
as well. It might. Every type of enzyme is unique and might require different conditions to be 
as stable and active as possible.  
The difference in colour of the wild type and the mutant after purification is an interesting 
observation. It shows that there is difference between them. The reason for the colour difference 
could be different oxidized states of the cofactor. FAD is a cofactor for choline oxidase. FAD 
when it is fully oxides is yellow while FADH which is half reduced can be a redder colour. This 
observation can indicate that the mutant has more FAD present while for the wild type could 
have more FADH and this causes the difference in colour observed.  
Activity of the enzyme is a very important, and measuring it tells us a lot about the overall 
quality of the enzyme. Since choline oxidase produces hydrogen peroxide, is this one way to 
measure the activity of the enzyme. From these measurements we can see that the mutant 
enzyme produces a lot more hydrogen peroxide and therefore has a higher activity.  
The higher activity in the mutant confirms what Heath et al.12 found.  The melting temperature 
is also consistent with their results. They are also talking about that to make the CO6 mutation, 
mutations remote from the active site has been shown to increase the activity of enzymes by 
lowering the free energy of the catalytic conformation, as well as increasing thermostability and 
solvent tolerance.  
Purity of the enzyme is important for the activity of the enzyme 
Looking at the results when removing the values from the cell free extract it can look like that 
after 22h there is hydrogen peroxide production which could indicate that less purity might 
delay the reaction slightly. Also, less production of hydrogen peroxide but in shuffle cells it 
does seem like it is delayed some. But what is mostly interesting with these results is that they 
show that there are some other reactions going on in the cell free extract. There are some other 
enzymes or something that is possibly using the substrate. The GC data also confirms this that 
the cell free extract does react with some of the substrate and therefore the enzyme does not 
have much substrate to react with. The result from the GC shows that the cell uses some of the 
substrate without producing the product. The cells are using hexanol but not producing hexanal.

This can explain why there is little hydrogen peroxide production in the reactions containing 
cell free extract. It shows that there is importance in the purity and that a less pure product can 
have different outcome from reactions than what a pure enzyme product has.  
It does however seem like the Shuffle cell enzymes produces some hydrogen peroxide after 
22h. of course it is difficult to say if this is from the enzyme or the cell free extract but since it 
only happens in shuffle cells. Also, when tested just cell free extract and not with enzyme there 
seems to be more hydrogen peroxide in the Shuffle cells than the DE3. This does indicate that 
it might just be the cells producing it and not the choline oxidase. It is difficult to give an exact 
answer, but it might seem like the cell free extract takes all the substrate or in other way stops 
the reaction from happening and that the shuffle cells somehow produced hydrogen peroxide 
on their own.  
An interesting observation with the kit used to measure the hydrogen peroxide concentration 
was that it went from brown to green. Normally it is supposed to turn purple when hydrogen 
peroxide is present. As far as I can see there is no information about this and why it turns purple. 
in the kit hydroperoxides converts Fe2+ to Fe3+ in an acidic pH and the Fe3+ complexes with 
xylenol orange that yields a purple product. Perhaps there could be some oxidation in the cells 
that somehow turns it green instead. This would be interesting to look more into in the future30.  
Thermostability  
The melting temperature is very different for the wild type and the mutant but there is no huge 
difference between the Shuffle cells and the DE3. But when comparing to Heath et al. the 
melting temperature we observed is much higher than the melting temperature they observed. 
Increase of almost 15 °C for each enzyme. This is very interesting and the explanation for this 
is difficult to say. It is most likely something with the growth conditions and the purification 
that have somehow improved the thermostability of the enzymes. It might be the concentration 
they were tested at, a higher concentration might take longer to reach the melting temperature 
but that should only change it a little and not as much as it has here.  
The ratio 350nm/330nm that the nanotemper nano DSF gives shows a better difference between 
the two strains of e.coli. since the melting temperature just gave a clear difference between the 
mutant and the wild type but not the strains it is very helpful to look at this ratio. There the 
shuffle cells gets a higher ratio which indicates higher stability. So based on just the nanoDSF 
results it can look like Shuffle cells creates more thermostable enzymes and that the mutant 
CO6 is a lot more stable than the wild type.

After all the tests it can look like SHuffle is a better production host than DE3 is. This is 
interesting results as there is a big chance it will also be better for other enzymes. Of course, 
enzymes are different from each other but finding enzymes similar to choline oxidase might 
benefit from using SHuffle cells as well.  
So why is all this research important? The uses of enzymes are endless and finding new 
enzymes or new uses for enzymes can improve many different industries and areas. It can be to 
improve the growth of plants, or perhaps cure diseases in humans. Choline oxidase catalyses a 
reaction with choline that produces glycine betaine. The concentration of glycine betaine is 
considered important for some metabolic syndrome, lipid disorders, diabetes, vascular diseases 
and the development of embryos57. Perhaps with more research being done on choline oxidase 
it can eventually lead to medications that can help with the diseases mentioned earlier. To reach 
this of course a lot of studies has to be done, but just starting with knowing more about the 
enzyme and the potentials it can have, gives some hope for a possible use in the future.

7. Future research  
 
Future research could involve more stability and activity testing.  
One interesting thing to use would be the bubble column to check the enzyme stability at air-
liquid interface. This can give more important knowledge about the stability of the enzyme. 
This can be done by setting up a bubble column and have bubbles of air in it. Make sure the 
bubbles are the same size and see how the enzyme binds to the surface of the column. This 
would give a more information about the stability of the enzyme during industrial applications, 
which is important when thinking of upscaling the production of the enzyme. Some studies 
indicate that the presence of gas-liquid interface induces enzyme deactivation by protein 
unfolding and then aggregates and create precipitation58. By testing this more it can help large 
scale production in the future. Often there is a problem with the supply of oxygen to bio 
oxidation reactions and looking into that can find new ways to ensure sufficient oxygen transfer 
from gas to liquid phase58.  
More analysis with CD spectra to get the structure of the mutant. Because the More research 
about the shape of the enzyme, and how it affects the activity and stability. If there was more 
time in this project, it would be very interesting to be able to find more about the structure and 
how it potentially can affect the stability and activity of the enzyme. Based on the results that 
it looks very different than the wild type, indicates that the structure has something to do with 
the activity and stability, so doing more research on that would be good. This would be done 
with trying to test the structure at different concentrations. In this project 1mg/ml was used but 
perhaps 0,2 mg/ml or somewhere in between could give a more clear answer of the structure. 
Could perhaps test enzymes with known structure and compare the results with the unknown 
structure of CO6.  
Another thing that could be good to look more into is if the process or producing choline oxidase 
can be used to produce other enzymes. Can be interesting to see if SHuffle cells are better for 
producing enzymes than the BL21 DE3 Star cells or if it just the case for choline oxidase. This 
involves finding enzymes that are similar to Choline oxidase, mostly similar in structure but 
perhaps similar in the reactions they catalyst. But what would be most interesting is similar in 
structure, perhaps the low temperature let it fold correctly and therefore gives a better stability 
and activity. Also looking more into the E. coli strains and see if more enzymes that are similar 
to choline oxidase would also have better outcome if they were produced in Shuffle cells.
