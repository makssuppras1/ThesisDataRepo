Abstract 
The construction industry has widely adopted the concept of Reverse Logistic Supply Chains (RLSC) for 
demolition waste and recognizes the potential for utilizing such materials in new construction projects. 
Nevertheless, implementing effective RLSCs for demolition waste poses challenges due to the inherent 
complexity in the construction sector.  Specifically, the post-end-of-use phase, involving the manage-
ment and recovered materials from demolition waste, lacks maturity and presents difficulties. Demol-
ishers serve as gatekeepers of this reclaimed material, determining whether materials can be directed 
to new construction projects and secondary market operations with product manufacturers and other 
stakeholders.  
Through an exploratory case study this thesis aimed to investigate the collaboration between demolish-
ers and product manufacturers to increase the circulation of reclaimed materials from demolition pro-
jects. A theoretical framework was employed to provide structural guidance, focusing on the case study 
of Næste, a company specializing in building sheds from reclaimed wood. The research utilized a theo-
retical framework comprising two parts: descriptive and prescriptive. This framework incorporated 
concepts such as RLSC demolition waste, reversed directed value proposition, and platform thinking.  
The investigation involved semi-structured interviews with stakeholders from across the entire con-
struction industry. Also, secondary data sources from the case company and economic data pertaining 
to selective demolition from demolisher was gathered. A demolisher provided valuable insights and real 
cases of conducting selective demolition and managing reclaimed materials. 
Through the identification of inhibitors affecting material, information and financial flow of reclaimed 
materials, the research revealed the potential for collaboration between demolishers and product man-
ufacturers to enhance the circulation of reclaimed material. By adopting platform thinking and estab-
lishing a defined interface, repeatable capabilities that add value to demolishers can be developed, in-
creasing the likelihood of collaboration. However, it is important to note that the effectiveness of the 
interface requires further validation, and additional research is needed to understand demolishers' per-
spectives on value and the capabilities that facilitate collaboration with product manufacturers.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Background for Topic   
In recent decades, the issue of climate change has become one of the most critical challenges confronting 
humanity. With its far-reaching consequences on the environment, ecosystems, and human societies, 
climate change has emerged as a defining issue of our time. The escalating global temperatures, rising 
sea levels, extreme weather events, and shifts in ecosystems all bear witness to the magnitude of this 
phenomenon and its potential to reshape our planet's future (IPCC 2023). 
Global greenhouse gas emissions have continued to increase, with unequal historical and 
ongoing contributions arising from unsustainable energy use, land use and land-use 
change, lifestyles and patterns of consumption and production across regions, between 
and within countries, and among individuals (IPCC 2023 p. 4) 
While climate change remains a pressing concern, it is crucial to recognize that other socio-economic 
factors also play pivotal roles in shaping our world's future. One such factor is the relentless growth of 
the global population, which has far-reaching implications for our planet's resources and sustainability. 
The surge in population growth has inevitably fueled rapid urbanization, leading to the expansion and 
densification of cities worldwide. By 2050, approximately 68% of the global population is expected to 
reside in urban areas, up from 55% in 2018 (UNEP 2019). This escalating trend places immense pres-
sure on urban infrastructure, including housing, transportation, and utilities. As urbanization intensifies 
the need for adequate housing, becomes a critical challenge to address. Rapid urbanization and popula-
tion growth require the construction of new dwellings and retrofitting and renovation of existing struc-
tures. The construction industry, which plays a crucial role in meeting this demand, carries its own set 
of environmental implications. 
The construction sector stands as one of the largest consumers of resources globally, accounting for 
approximately 50% of the resources extracted today (European Commission 2020). This alarming sta-
tistic highlights the urgency to reduce overconsumption and implement innovative strategies to miti-
gate environmental impacts. In addition to its resource-intensive nature, the construction industry also 
generates a substantial amount of waste. In the European Union alone, the construction sector contrib-
utes to 30% of the total waste generated (ibid). This waste stream poses significant challenges for sus-
tainable waste management and necessitates a paradigm shift towards circularity. Furthermore, the in-
dustry's stagnant productivity rate, growing at a mere 1% per year over the past two decades, calls for 
transformative measures to enhance efficiency, reduce costs, and minimize environmental impacts 
(McKinsey 2017). 
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
 
To address these challenges, the concept of the circular economy has gained traction as a valuable alter-
native for redesigning value- and supply chains and business models in the construction industry. The 
circular economy envisions an economic system that reduces, reuses, recycles, and recovers materials, 
thereby replacing the traditional "end-of-life" concept. By adopting circular economy principles, the 
construction industry can achieve resource efficiency, reduce waste generation, and minimize environ-
mental footprints. Various regions and governmental bodies, including the European Union and Den-
mark, have recognized the potential of circularity and have begun developing policies to promote its 
adoption. These policies target areas such as product durability, resource reuse, waste avoidance, and 
incentivize collaboration and innovation among different sectors and stakeholders. 
Although circular practices have gained recognition, their implementation in the construction industry 
is still at an emerging stage. Ding et al. (2023) highlight a challenge regarding the limited range of path-
ways assessed reflecting the current maturity level of the construction industry's transition to a circular 
economy. Additionally, there is a lack of emphasis within the industry on the post-end-of-use phase, 
referring to the ability to recover the demolition materials and their value post-demolition (Tennakoon 
et al. 2022). Selective demolition has gained traction to allow the post-end-of-use phase to be possible 
to be in place and it has been emphasized as a means to allow more materials to be circulated. However, 
the ability to conduct selective demolition is still a term under development and demolishers face great 
difficulties in the practice of it (Værdibyg 2020).   
In Denmark, academics and companies try to address this as several practice-driven research projects 
aim to bring knowledge and solutions towards the transition of the construction industry. One example 
of a project is Circular Construction Platforms (CircOp). CircOp is a practice-driven research project tar-
geting a circular transformation within the construction industry stating that there is a wish to “transi-
tion from short-term linear projects based on a fragmented supply and value chain to lasting circular plat-
forms based on reversed, coordinated and integrated supply and value chains – build to scale” (Thuesen 
2021 p.18). These platforms serve as innovative frameworks that facilitate the integration of circular 
solutions and materials, addressing key challenges and promoting sustainable development. CircOp is 
founded on the premise that platforms can enable various benefits and opportunities for the construc-
tion industry's circular transition. Thuesen et al. (2022) explain that “platforms provide a promising en-
abler to tackle various challenges of adopting fully circular construction practices and supporting the in-
dustry’s productivity” (p. 2).  
CircOp brings together an extensive network of industry participants called Circular Chain Operators 
(CCO) and academic experts. This multidisciplinary collaboration includes notable companies namely 
GXN which is an architectural firm, Enemærke & Petersen which is a contractor, RGS Nordic which 
serves as a waste handler, but in the case of CircOp as a material supplier, and lastly Næste which serves

as a product manufacturer within secondary buildings. The CCOs are partnered alongside academic pro-
fessionals at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). CircOp aims to equip industry stakeholders 
with practical tools and resources for implementing circular principles enabled by platform thinking in 
the construction industry. The collaboration with the CCOs plays a pivotal role in validating and refining 
the theoretical concepts developed within the project.  
 
1.2 Case Description 
Næste has as a CCO joined CircOp with the purpose of contributing to its framework with its knowledge 
and perspectives, but also to fulfill its ambition, which is to  
Grow a sustainable business and value chain by using demolished materials for secondary 
buildings as sheds. Create new architectural typologies. Exporting the model via local 
partners. (Thuesen 2021 p. 19) 
In this thesis, the term "reclaimed material" is used to describe what Næste refers to as demolishing 
materials. Reclaimed materials pertain to the reuse and recycling of materials obtained from selective 
demolition and into new construction. More specifically, reclaimed wood refers to the wood utilized by 
Næste for the manufacturing of their sheds.   
Næste is a Danish pioneering shed manufacturer, within the secondary construction industry. They de-
sign and produce sheds using reclaimed material and have a dedicated team of 11 employees, with the 
ambition of reshaping the construction industry. At the heart of Næste's philosophy is the embrace of 
the circular economy, where the focus lies on secondary construction and reusing of especially re-
claimed wood but also other reclaimed materials to extend their lifespan. By leveraging a production 
with 95% reclaimed materials. Næste aims to reduce waste and minimize the consumption of virgin 
resources, fostering a deeper sense of responsibility towards the Earth's limited resources (Næste 
2023). 
Founded in 2019 by Niels Jakubiak Andersen, an architect with a passion for sustainable building prac-
tices, Næste is revolutionizing the way sheds are produced, highlighting their potential in terms of social, 
functional, and aesthetic aspects. Niels mentions 
One could say that what we really emphasize is the ability to create a strategic advantage 
in being the best at transforming waste materials into incredibly cool designed products 
for outdoor environments (X1, R1, 18:30 p. 7) 
Since its establishment, Næste has experienced significant growth and they estimate that the m" shed 
production from 2022 to 2023 will show an increase of 204%, together with an increase from 2023 to

shed per month from external suppliers, without having our own storage or office space.” (X1, R1, 25:52 p.

ceived in the market. While sheds have traditionally served as storage units, recent legislation mandat-

One of the things that Næste has faced is the perception that sustainability comes at a higher price. While

key1 solution, Næste minimizes the hassle for customers, handling aspects such as building approvals

Næste sees themselves as a product manufacturer, as mentioned by Næste “Our special abilities lie in 
being able to design the products and produce them efficiently” (X1, R1, p. 10 33:41). Reasoning for doing

construction industry, because of the ease of doing it through secondary buildings compared to primary

reclaimed wood but also as a business. Næste has big goals of reaching a production of one shed a day 
as well as scale their set-up into the rest of Denmark and the Nordic countries.  
To reach these goals Næste faces two difficult tasks as a product manufacturer working with reclaimed 
wood, which is supply variance and supply of high-quality wood. Næste is introduced with inherent

Næste has developed a physical framework called the mounting table, which enables the creation of 
standardized sub-assemblies for their sheds, which accommodate the varying dimensions of the wood. 
Second challenge is ensuring the supply of high-quality reclaimed wood, where Næste explains that ”our 
next challenge will be to ensure that we have enough materials in the flow that we can actually scale into 
the market.” (X1, R1, p. 8 22:29). To overcome the challenge of securing a sustainable and abundant

1.3 Research Questions 
This thesis aims at researching the potential for collaboration between demolishers and product manu-
facturers, utilizing reclaimed materials, to enhance the circulation of reclaimed material in Denmark. 
This thesis conducts a case study of Næste, utilizing a theoretical framework that encompasses concepts 
such as Reverse Logistics and Supply Chain, reversed directed value proposition, and platform thinking. 
The purpose of this framework is to provide structural guidance for the study. The theoretical frame-
work will be explained in detail in section 3. The case study has included stakeholders from across the 
entire construction landscape to obtain a holistic understanding of the flow of reclaimed wood. Despite 
Næste has existing collaboration with the demolisher, the depth of this collaboration between the two 
parties has not been thoroughly investigated. Accordingly, the following research questions have been 
identified:  
How can the collaboration between demolishers and product manufacturers be developed to ena-
ble a higher degree of circulation of reclaimed materials from demolition projects? 
In addition, to the foundational question, two sub-research questions are presented:  
1. What are the current inhibitors affecting the flow of reclaimed material from demolishers to prod-
uct manufacturers? 
2. How can value proposition support collaboration between demolishers and product manufactur-
ers? 
3. How can platform thinking sustain the collaboration between demolishers and product manufac-
turers?

2. Literature review 
2.1 Circular Economy 
This section begins by providing an explanation of the overall concept of the circular economy, further-
more, by showcasing political initiatives that support a transition towards a circular economy. It subse-
quently narrows down its focus to examine the circular economy specifically within the construction 
industry as the thesis aims to explore how reclaimed materials from the construction industry can be 
effectively utilized and extended in their lifespan within the context of the circular economy.  
The circular economy is a concept that has gained increasing attention in recent years as a response to 
the challenges posed by the linear economic model, which relies on the unsustainable use of natural 
resources and the generation of waste (Ellen MacArthur Foundation 2012). The circular economy can 
be defined as  
[…] a regenerative system in which resource input and waste, emission, and energy leak-
age are minimized by slowing, closing, and narrowing material and energy loops. This can 
be achieved through long-lasting design, maintenance, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, re-
furbishing, and recycling. (Geissdoerfer et al. 2017, p. 759).  
The idea of a circular economy is to create a regenerative system in which resources are kept in use for 
as long as possible, waste is minimized, and the environmental impact is reduced. The creation of a re-
storative system is also of big importance, together with the regenerative system, where the intention 
is to have endless use, reuse, and repair. One of the main goals of the circular economy is to decouple 
economic growth from resource use and environmental impact. This means that the circular economy 
aims to create economic growth while minimizing resource consumption and environmental degrada-
tion (Ellen MacArthur Foundation 2012).  
The concept of circular economy has some of its roots in the book “The Entropy Law and the Economic 
Process” by Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen which was published in 1971 (Ghisellini et al. 2016). The book 
argues that the laws of thermodynamics made it impossible to have perpetual growth in a finite system. 
It proposed that the economy should be based on the idea of entropy. Entropy implies that through the 
production and use of minerals and materials, there will always, even when recycling, be a depreciation 
by for example deterioration, accident, loss, and so forth, thus making recycling and reuse not complete 
(Daly 1977). Hereby, the circular economy cannot be seen as a mean to fight entropy completely but 
prolong the process. Other notable ideas that have laid the foundation of circular economy are the emer-
gence of General Systems Theory, regenerative design, performance economy, biomimicry, and blue 
economy (Ghisellini et al. 2016). Some other notable ideas that will be explained further are industrial

8 
 
ecology and cradle-to-cradle which are ideas that emphasize the need to design products and processes 
with the goal of eliminating waste and pollution and creating closed-loop systems.  
2.1.1 
Industrial Ecology 
Industrial ecology emerged as an opposing way of thinking to the current neo-classical economy, the 
linear economy, where industrial systems are being studied separately from the environment (Ghisellini 
et al. 2016). Erkman (1997) explains that instead of studying them separately they should be viewed as 
a whole.   
The idea is first to understand how the industrial system works, how it is regulated, and 
its interaction with the biosphere; then, on the basis of what we know about ecosystems, 
to determine how it could be restructured to make it compatible with the way natural 
ecosystems function (Erkman 1997, p. 1). 
 Industrial ecology aims to create sustainable industrial systems that integrate environmental, eco-
nomic, and social factors and seeks to transition from open to closed-loop systems and hereby minimize 
waste in industrial processes (Ellen MacArthur Foundation 2012a; Erkman 1997; Ghisellini et al. 2016). 
2.1.2 
Cradle-to-Cradle 
The cradle-to-cradle emerged as a concept in 2002 and is created by architect William McDonough and 
chemist Michael Braungart in their book “Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things” which 
promotes the creation of products and systems regenerative and circular in nature (Diaz-Granados 
2021). It is based on the idea that materials should be reused and recycled, creating a closed-loop system 
in which waste is eliminated. This is in contrast to the traditional "cradle-to-grave" model of production 
and consumption or to the likes of the linear economy, which involves taking resources, using them to 
create products, and then disposing of them as waste or recycled at the end of their useful life. In this 
sense, recycling is more seen as a way of downcycling because products by design are made for recycling 
and hereby value (Braungart et al. 2007; Diaz-Granados 2021). Braungart et al (2007) express that cra-
dle-to-cradle design can be seen as a model and a practical expression to incorporate the eco-efficient 
philosophy that seeks synergies between economic, environmental, and social business aims. This is in 
contrast to the eco-efficient philosophy that underlies today’s linear approach. Eco-efficiency aims to 
maximize economic value as efficiently as possible by reducing unintended negative consequences 
within the processes of consumption together with zero adverse ecological impact. This in itself has 
similarities with what industrial ecology tries to fight because there is a here a decoupling between the 
industrial systems and the ecosystem. Eco-effectiveness is, therefore, the counterpart to this by trying 
to reunite these two systems by offering a new perspective to product and systems design through 
==PAGE:18==
 
9 
 
cradle-to-cradle design (Braungart et al. 2007; Diaz-Granados 2021; Ellen MacArthur Foundation 
2012a; Erkman 1997). 
2.1.3 
The Circular System Diagram  
Today the Ellen MacArthur Foundation is seen as one of the forefront organizations to accelerate the 
transition to a circular economy. Through continuous work on the development and promotion of a cir-
cular economy with industries, governments, and academia they try to move from ambition to action 
(Ghisellini et al. 2016). The circular system diagram, also known as the butterfly model, has been for-

2.1.5 
Circular Economy in the Construction Industry 
When looking at the transition to a circular economy within the construction industry, Ding et al. (2023) 
describe that “the CE [circular economy] goals are still far from reach for the construction industry”. Fur-
thermore, Thuesen et al. (2022) explain how different initiatives have been initiated but state that “de-
spite their potential to address the negative impacts, current practices and the application of circular strat-
egies in the [construction] industry remain limited.” (p. 2). The maturity level of the transition to a circu-
lar economy in the construction industry is evidenced by the limited range of pathways assessed. Within 
the construction industry, there is a lack of focus on the post-end-of-use phase, Ding et al. (2023) explain 
“the phases in the post-end-of-use phases are not clearly defined” (p. 3).  The post-end-of-use phase in-
volves how to treat the construction waste that occurs from demolition and hereby be able to recover 
the materials and their value (Tennakoon et al. 2022).  The waste hierarchy has been developed to pro-
vide a framework for managing this waste in a sustainable and environmentally responsible manner, it 
is visible in Figure 3. It is a tool that explains how materials need to be handled within the post-end-of-
use phase in order to recover the highest value, or by preventing waste to be generated (CIRCLE HOUSE 
lab 2020). Construction waste materials should be prioritized for use as high up as possible in the waste 
hierarchy due to several compelling reasons. First and foremost, this approach enables the conservation 
of valuable resources. By reusing and recycling construction waste materials, the need for extracting 
and processing new raw materials is reduced, leading to the preservation of natural resources and mit-
igation of environmental impacts associated with their extraction. Additionally, utilizing construction 
waste materials higher up in the waste hierarchy contributes to waste reduction efforts. By diverting 
these materials from landfills, the amount of waste sent for disposal is minimized. This not only reduces 
the strain on landfill capacity but also helps decrease the environmental and financial costs associated 
with waste management and disposal (CIRCLE HOUSE lab 2020; European Commission 2020; Ghisellini 
et al. 2016).

The end-of-use phase of a building is the phase where demolition happens (Ding, Wang, and Chan 2023;

2.2 Supply Chain Management 
Reverse Logistics Supply Chain (RLSC) is a means to enable a circular economy. “One of the most ambi-
tious goals of CE is “closing the loop” in material and resource flows throughout product life cycles” (Ding 
et al 2023, p. 1).  A way of closing the loop is through RLSC, which strongly correlates with the Butterfly 
Model, presented earlier (Ellen MacArthur Foundation 2012). Furthermore, RLSC is a pillar within Sup-
ply chain management (SCM). Hence the following section seeks to outline the SCM and the connection 
to RLSC and more specifically RLSC for demolition waste to reintegrate reclaimed material.  
Studies in the supply chain started to gain traction in the 1980s and many researchers and industry 
peers have contributed to the definition (Belvedere & Grando 2017; Trang Rausch-Phan & Siegfried 
2022). Studies on supply chain management emerged as several companies realized that by collaborat-
ing and creating relationships with other companies there could be realized profits and effectiveness 
for the benefit of all companies in the supply chain. Furthermore, by applying focus to the supply chain 
companies have better prerequisites for the right decision-making (Trang Rausch-Phan & Siegfried 
2022). According to the Council of Supply Chain Management Professionals (2023), the management of 
the supply chain is explained as:  
Supply Chain Management encompasses the planning and management of all activities 
involved in sourcing and procurement, conversion, and all logistics management activi-
ties. Importantly, it also includes coordination and collaboration with channel partners, 
which can be suppliers, intermediaries, third-party service providers, and customers. In 
essence, supply chain management integrates supply and demand management within 
and across companies. (p. 1)  
SCM has evolved from a traditional siloed approach to a holistic and integrated framework, where firms 
collaborate with their partners to achieve common goals (Belvedere & Grando 2017). SCM is a critical 
component of organizational success, and measuring its performance is essential to identify areas for 
improvement and maintain a competitive advantage throughout the supply chain. The performance is 
critical as it reflects the value in the perspective of customers, and in the end, determines if the customer 
finds the product either equal to competitor’s products or better  (Belvedere & Grando 2017). Slack et 
al. (2007) present five performance objectives that reflect the end customers’ requirements and there-
fore should be maintained at a high level. These five objectives concern quality, speed/time, dependa-
bility, flexibility, and cost. The quality objective focuses on the ability to meet customer requirements 
and expectations. Speed refers to two perspectives, on one hand, it refers to how a supply chain complies 
with promised delivery dates, secondly, it refers to how fast a product/service moves through the sup-
ply chain. Dependability indicates if on-time in-full is fulfilled, which refers to timely and promised 
==PAGE:25==
 
16 
 
completion, this is important both between the supply chain to the end customer and also within the 
supply chain itself. The flexibility objective relates to the ability to quickly and effectively respond to 
changing customer demands and market conditions. The last performance objective is the cost which 
refers to the ability to control and reduce costs associated with supply chain operations (Belvedere & 
Grando 2017; Slack et al. 2007). Measuring and managing these five objectives of performance in a sup-
ply chain is essential to ensure the overall success of the supply chain and its actors. 
Supply chains and value chains are two interconnected concepts within the field of business manage-
ment. While supply chains primarily focus on the physical flow of goods, information, and finances from 
suppliers to customers, value chains take a broader perspective, encompassing all activities involved in 
creating value for customers (Belvedere & Grando 2017; Kumar & P. V. 2016). Supply chains emphasize 
the efficient management of logistics, procurement, production, and distribution, aiming to optimize op-
erational efficiency and cost-effectiveness. In contrast, value chains encompass the entire range of ac-
tivities, including marketing, sales, research and development, and customer service, which collectively 
contribute to the value offered to customers. Despite these distinctions, supply chains and value chains 
intersect closely, as the effectiveness of supply chain operations directly impacts the overall value de-
livered to customers. By managing supplier relationships, enhancing operational efficiency, and ensur-
ing reliable and timely delivery, organizations can strengthen their supply chains and, in turn, improve 
the value generated for customers. 
While recognizing the distinctions between supply chains and value chains, it is important to clarify that 
the focus for further chapters will be on supply chains. The thesis specifically aims to address the chal-
lenge of securing high-quality materials from construction projects, ensuring sufficient quantities, and 
establishing robust supplier relationships. The central objective is to capture and reclaim value from 
demolition waste rather than dispose of it. While value chain activities are indeed important, the em-
phasis will be on the supply chain aspects directly related to material sourcing, logistics, and supplier 
management. This targeted approach allows for a thorough examination of the processes involved in 
securing the necessary materials while optimizing resource utilization and minimizing waste generation 
within the broader context of the supply chain. 
2.2.1 
Reverse Logistics Supply Chains 
RLSC is a part of SCM and refers to the management of the reverse flow of goods, services, and infor-
mation from the point of consumption to the point of origin. This includes product returns, recycling, 
refurbishment, and remanufacturing activities. The objective of reverse logistics is to optimize the value 
of products or materials at the end of their lifecycle and reduce waste. Successful implementation of 
reverse logistics requires coordination and collaboration among various stakeholders in the supply 
==PAGE:26==
 
17 
 
chain, including customers, retailers, manufacturers, and service providers. Reverse logistics has be-
come increasingly important for companies seeking to improve sustainability and reduce environmen-
tal impact. Effective management of reverse logistics requires an integrated approach that considers 
both forward and reverse logistics (Mallick et al. 2023; Rogers & Tibben-Lembke 1998; Trang Rausch-
Phan & Siegfried 2022). Forward and reverse logistics are depicted in Figure 4. 
 
 
Figure 4: Forward and reverse supply chains. Adapted from (c3controls 2023) 
The concept of an RLSC has been developed over several decades and has emerged as an important 
strategy for companies seeking to reduce waste, increase resource efficiency, and improve sustainability 
(Carter & Ellram 1998; Tennakoon et al. 2022). Rogers & Tibben-Lembke (1998) defines RLSC as a “pro-
cess of moving goods from their typical final destination for the purpose of capturing value, or proper dis-
posal.” (p. 2) Moreover, RLSC is concerned with reintegrating materials into the circular supply chain. 
The development of reverse logistics can be traced back to the 1970s when companies became aware 
of the importance of managing product returns and end-of-life products in a more efficient and sustain-
able manner. The early focus of reverse logistics was primarily on product returns management, but it 
has since evolved to encompass a wide range of activities, including remanufacturing, refurbishment, 
and recycling (Rogers & Tibben-Lembke 1998). The development of reversed logistics supply chains has 
been facilitated by advances in technology, transportation, and communication (Rogers & Tibben-
Lembke, 1999). In addition, regulatory and social pressures have encouraged companies to adopt more 
sustainable practices and seek out new opportunities for waste reduction and resource recovery (Mal-
lick et al. 2023). 
2.2.2 
Reverse Logistics Supply Chains for Demolition Waste 
The emergence of a new kind of RLSC for demolition waste has evolved within the construction industry 
as the focus has shifted toward the recovery and reuse of materials (Hosseini et al. 2015). The emer-
gence of RLSC for demolition waste reflects the growing recognition of the environmental impact of 
construction and demolition activities and the need for more sustainable and socially responsible prac-
tices in this field.

This requires effective management of the flow of materials in reverse, from the point of waste genera-
tion to the point of reuse or recycling. The development of an RLSC for demolition waste is an important 
step towards more sustainable and socially responsible practices in the construction and demolition 
industry (Tennakoon et al. 2022, 2023). 
Although there is growing recognition for RLSC for demolition waste Tennakoon et al. (2022) explains

extreme specialization in construction”, there is simply a need for understanding between the different 
engineering disciplines involved in a construction project and this problem exists especially at the build-
ings end-of-use and post-end-of-use, which is where RLSC for demolition waste will be present (Ding et 
al. 2023). 
 
2.3 Platform Thinking 
Platform thinking can serve as a strategy to enhance the potential of RLSC (Reverse Logistics and Supply 
Chain) for demolition waste, as it is adaptable to various stages of development. Additionally, it offers a 
sustainable framework for RLSC by incorporating extendable features that facilitate the transition to-
ward a circular economy. Consequently, this section offers an overview of platform thinking and shifts 
the attention toward its application in the construction industry. Platform thinking has evolved over 
time, with its origins rooted in the development of traditional marketplaces and physical platforms. 
These physical platforms allowed for the exchange of goods and services as users within the platform 
had good conditions for matching and hereby creating value for every customer (Parker et al. 2016). In 
recent years, platform thinking has expanded beyond the physical marketplaces to encompass a wide 
range of industries, including healthcare, transportation, and education amongst many other industries. 
This shift has been driven by advances in especially information technology, which have made it easier 
to create and manage platforms that can connect large numbers of users, Thomas et al. (2014) explains 
that platforms will become a “fact of life” and mentions that “any product can become a platform” 
(Alstyne et al. 2016; Parker et al. 2016). One of the things that is efficient about platforms is their ability 
==PAGE:28==
 
19 
 
to eliminate gatekeepers. Parker et al. (2016) describe the gatekeepers as those who “manage the flow 
of value from the producer to the consumer”. The operational mechanisms of these gatekeepers differ 
across various industries, but what remains consistent is that bypassing the gatekeepers and aligning 
with market trends offers the potential for greater value creation. By doing so, consumers gain increased 
freedom to make choices based on their own needs. 
Platform thinking entails various platform streams where Mosca et al. (2020) and Thomas et al. (2014) 
describe the four streams of platforms as (1) Platform organizations, (2) product platforms, (3) platform 
ecosystems, and (4) market intermediaries.  
(1) A platform organization is when an organization is structured to deal with rapidly changing markets, 
by focusing assets and capabilities towards new endeavors. This approach allows for greater flexibility, 
scalability, and reduced development costs. (2) Product platforms, on the other hand, are platforms that 
offer a standardized set of features and functionalities that can be easily customized and adapted to 
different use cases. Meanwhile, (3) platform ecosystems refer to the network of users, developers, and 
partners linked to or are part of a platform and contribute to its growth and success. Finally, (4) market 
intermediaries are entities that facilitate transactions between buyers and sellers on a platform, such as 
payment processors or logistics providers. The different types of platforms can be defined within inter-
nal and external platforms, with internal platforms having an emphasis on economies of scale, whereas 
external platforms focus on market leadership (Mosca et al. 2020; Thomas et al. 2014).  
2.3.1 
Platforms in the Construction Industry  
The literature surrounding platforms within the construction industry is still limited when looking into 
their utility and applications although there are implications showing that platform thinking can solve 
some of the challenges that are evident within the construction industry in transitioning to circularity 
(Thuesen et al. 2022).  Platform thinking has found its relevance in electronics, automotive, and general 
manufacturing which are industries that have evolved digitally throughout the years, this separates the 
construction industry from the manufacturing industry, as explained by Kudsk et al. (2013) “the princi-
ples and work methods in the construction industry have stood still for more than a hundred years” which 
implies a lack of progress. This lack of progress is based on the notion that the construction industry is 
“… highly complex, fragmented and project-based” and that “… the complexity of generating data is an 
obstacle” meaning that the maturity level of the construction industry is low in regard to digitalization 
and the design of components which is not bespoke and unique (Mosca et al. 2020; Thuesen et al. 2022). 
But where there is a lack there are possibilities for changes, as existing academic literature states that a 
digital and platform-based transformation will be present within the construction industry  (Bartlett et 
al. 2020). Especially literature regarding product platforms within construction is starting to emerge, as

3.1. Reverse Logistics Supply Chain – A Conceptual Framework 
RLSC is applied as it can be utilized for companies to improve sustainability and reduce environmental 
impact. At the same time, it can act as a tool to achieve more circular processes. RLSC aims to reintegrate 
reclaimed materials into the forward supply chain through a series of activities or proper disposal meth-
ods, aligning with the principles of the circular economy and its core objective of closing the loop, it can 
therefore be seen as a proven field enhance circularity and hereby circular economy (Ding et al., 2023; 
Mallick et al., 2023; Wijewickrama et al., 2021). Osobajo et al. (2022) express that “the lack of interest in 
the supply chain as revealed by this systematic review serves as a pointer for scholars to explore this area 
in more depth in understanding how the construction industry can embed circular economy across its sup-
ply chains” (p. 10).  This provides a potential research avenue and justification for exploring the imple-
mentation of RLSC practices as it has proved to be an efficient strategy within the manufacturing indus-
try by creating possibilities for competitiveness and environmentally sound results (Hosseini et al. 
2015).  
The application of RLSC in the construction industry is challenged by complexity and uncertainty. This 
coincides with Thuesen et al. (2022) stating that “the needed transformation comes with added complex-
ity, uncertainty, and a requirement to innovate, historically challenging the industry” (p. 2). Hence, it is 
recognized that RLSC holds the potential for facilitating the transition to a circular economy, despite the 
industry's entrenched habits and existing standards. In light of these challenges, this thesis acknowl-
edges the obstacles faced by industry. However, by employing the conceptual framework developed by 
Tennakoon et al. (2022), these hurdles can be effectively addressed. The assessment conducted through 
this thesis will contribute to the existing body of research on transitioning the construction industry 
toward a circular economy.

Together these three steps provide answers to enhance RLSC integration and hereby fill the knowledge

Tennakoon et al. (2022) argue that “The ultimate goal of an integrated supply chain is to enhance the 
efficiency and effectiveness of material, information, and financial flows within and across organizations 
to provide the highest possible value to customers in the shortest possible time and at the lowest possible 
cost” (p. 3). Hence, addressing material, information, and financial flow and creating synergies across 
these is the goal to enable efficiency and effectiveness across an RLSC for demolition waste. This argu-
ment is backed by Belvedere & Grando (2017) who argue that increasing collaboration with both up-
stream and downstream stakeholders necessitates extending responsibility and coordination across the 
supply chain. Thus, we will be analyzing the material, information, and financial flow.

process of identifying the inhibitors and measures to apply to our thesis (see section 3.4). The identifi-
cation of inhibitors is conducted through analysis of the three flows and interviews with stakeholders

3.2 Value Proposition – Reversed Directed 
The value proposition plays a pivotal role in business development and is the central element of a busi-

creates, delivers, and captures value” (p. 14). This is aligned with Teece’s (2010) definition of a business

172). While there are numerous definitions of a business model, a shared characteristic among them is 
the emphasis on the concept of value as a pivotal component within a business model (Westerveld et al. 
2023; Zott and Amit 2007). Throughout the thesis, we will lean towards the Value Proposition frame-
work, which is derived from the 9-block business model by Osterwalder and peers (Osterwalder et al. 
2009, 2014), see Figure 6. Having acknowledged the commonly used context in which the value is com-
prehended, we then proceed to implement the theory within this context, where value within a business 
model will be referred to as the value proposition.

Value proposition can be defined as creating and delivering unique value to customers, surpassing com-
petitors, by addressing their needs and preferences effectively (Westerveld et al. 2023). Moreover, a 
value proposition is the distinctive combination of products, services, features, and benefits offered by 
a company to customers. It represents the perceived value and reasons why customers choose one prod-
uct or service over others. The aim is to differentiate firms from competitors by making a promise of 
==PAGE:35==
 
26 
 
value. Value proposition follows a customer-centric approach, involving market research to understand 
customer needs and tailoring the value proposition accordingly. Key steps include market analysis, cus-
tomer segmentation, defining customer characteristics, and developing a clear and concise value prop-
osition that highlights unique advantages (Osterwalder et al., 2014). To create an effective value propo-
sition, companies can utilize frameworks such as the Value Proposition Canvas developed by Osterwal-
der et al. (2014), see Figure 7. The Value Proposition Canvas helps companies analyze and articulate 
their value proposition. By facilitating the identification of value drivers and the alignment of the value 
proposition with customer needs. Furthermore, the value proposition should highlight unique selling 
points, competitive advantages, and customer benefits. 
It consists of two main components: the customer profile and the value map. The customer profile rep-
resents the needs and preferences of the target customers. It includes the jobs, which are the tasks or 
problems that customers are trying to accomplish or solve. These jobs represent the functional needs 
that customers have. Additionally, the customer profile includes the pains that customers experience 
while trying to complete their jobs. These pains highlight the challenges, frustrations, or obstacles that 
customers face. Lastly, the customer profile considers the gains that customers seek to achieve. Gains 
are the positive outcomes, benefits, or desires that customers expect or hope for, encompassing emo-
tional, social, or economic rewards (Osterwalder et al. 2014). On the other hand, the value map focuses 
on the value that the company provides to customers. It includes the products and services offered by 
the company to address the customer's jobs, pains, and gains. These offerings represent what the com-
pany provides to create value for customers. In addition, the value map identifies the pain relievers, 
which are the specific aspects of the products or services that alleviate or eliminate the customer's pains. 
Pain relievers provide solutions or features that directly address the challenges or frustrations custom-
ers face. Similarly, the value map also includes the gain creators, which are the specific aspects of the 
products or services that generate or enhance the customer's gains. Gain creators provide features or 
benefits that fulfill the customer's desires or expectations, leading to positive outcomes (ibid).

3.3 Platform Thinking 
The inherent nature of platform thinking makes it a good approach to navigating uncertain environ-
ments. As highlighted in the work of Ansar and Flybjerg (2022), platforms are founded on the under-
standing that "the world is a dynamic, complex adaptive system" (p. 7). This perspective proves valuable 
when pursuing prescriptive approaches within the realm of RLSC for demolition waste, given the high 
complexity and uncertainty inherent in the construction industry (Thuesen et al. 2022). Over time, sev-
eral perspectives on the platform have been developed.  
As mentioned in the literature review section Platform Thinking, platforms can be expressed within four 
streams (1) platform organizations, (2) product platforms, (3) market intermediaries, and (4) platform 
ecosystems, which all entail valuable insights on how to engage with platforms in several ways. This 
thesis will primarily lean toward product platforms. This decision is motivated by a strong emphasis on 
product platforms within Næste. However, the focus will not be on the physical product of a product 
platform, but on collaboration with and across product platforms.  
 A product platform is by Robertson and Ulrich (1998) defined as “the collection of assets that are shared 
by a set of products” (p. 20) and refers to the following four assets: components, processes, knowledge, 
and people and relationship. This is aligned with Mosca et al. (2020) definition of “core assets” (p. 5) for 
a platform. The same four assets are defined as “common repeatable elements” in the platform rulebook 
by the Construction Innovation Hub (CIH) (2022, p. 18). CIH et al. (2022) and Mosca et al. (2020) have 
further expanded on what common features across platform streams contain, by adding three other 
features besides the inclusion of assets: A complementary set of peripherals, a stable interface, and a set 
of rules/standards governing how components can be integrated. A complementary set of peripherals pro-
poses distinctive offerings in the market through the utilization of a complementary set of peripheral 
components that demonstrate high variety. The incorporation of interchangeable peripheral compo-
nents introduces diversity, leading to unique and differentiated products or services (CIH 2022; Mosca 
et al. 2020). The presence of a stable interface serves as a crucial link between the stable core and vari-
able peripherals within the platform. This interface enables innovation to occur in both the core and 
peripherals, facilitating continuous improvements and adaptations across the platform (ibid). Lastly, a 
set of rules/standards governing how components can be integrated refers to how governing the platform 
is important in making sure that standardization is reached (ibid).  
According to CIH et al. (2022), a product platform is defined as (p. 11):  
• 
The kit of parts associated with production processes, knowledge, people, and relationships required 
to deliver all or part of construction projects using a platform approach. 
• 
A product platform provides a stable core that is configured and combined with complementary 
components (via defined interfaces) to suit a particular project. 
==PAGE:38==
 
29 
 
• 
A product platform also includes the processes tools and equipment required for assembly.  
Considering the scope of this thesis on the collaboration between demolishers and product manufactur-
ers to enhance the circulation of reclaimed material, the establishment of a defined standard interface 
is a critical feature of a product platform. CIH et. al (2022) outline that as a rule “Product platforms shall 
have defined interfaces which can be made available to the designers and suppliers of peripheral or com-
plementary products.” (p. 18). However, Interface is within this context understood as conceptual to-
wards suppliers, which means an abstract representation of the interaction and relationship between a 
product platform and its suppliers. The conceptual interface outlines the high-level principles, guide-
lines, and expectations that govern the collaboration and engagement between the platform and its sup-
pliers. It is emphasized the importance of ensuring clear interfaces to facilitate collaboration and inte-
gration with external participants. Moreover, Mosca et al. (2020) highlights that “a key challenge for 
owners of platforms with external interfaces is to ensure that external participants […] meet the platform’s 
participation, performance and interface requirements” (p. 10). This emphasizes the need to manage and 
enforce interface requirements for external participants to ensure effective collaboration within the 
platform. The establishment of a standard interface plays a vital role in fostering collaboration, inter-
changeability, and seamless integration within and between product platforms. This significance is par-
ticularly relevant to the focus of this thesis, which examines the collaboration between demolishers and 
product manufacturers to enhance the circulation of reclaimed material. 
Another crucial element to highlight in product platforms is repeatability. The purpose of a product 
platform is to enable repeatability, as highlighted by Ansar and Flybjerg (2022) who argue “Repeatabil-
ity allows platforms to scale in terms of unit volume—that is, supply more of the same thing” (p. 6). Re-
peatability holds great value in the collaboration between demolishers and product manufacturers, as 
it ensures the establishment of replicable flows. Additionally, repeatability serves as a catalyst for plat-
form scalability (Ansar & Flybjerg 2022). CIH et al. (2022) further emphasizes the significance of com-
mon repeatable elements, which encompass components, processes, knowledge, people, and relation-
ships. 
For this thesis, the concept of a product platform will be utilized to delve into the underlying elements 
of demolishers and product manufacturers’ platforms. The goal is to establish a level of understanding 
that enables the definition of a standard interface, ultimately applicable across the construction indus-
try, that allows for repeatability, and thus scalability of the Circular Economy.

4.1 Research Philosophy –Pragmatism 
Research philosophy refers to the beliefs and assumptions about how researchers are concerned with 
the development of knowledge in a particular field of study and the relationship between the researcher 
and the research participants. According to Saunders et al. (2016) research philosophy provides the 
overall lens through which the researcher approaches the research question, data collection, and data 
analysis.  
The chosen research philosophy for this thesis is pragmatism. Pragmatism is a research philosophy that 
emphasizes practical solutions and outcomes. Pragmatism recognizes the importance of context in re-
search and therefore emphasizes the need to take the social, cultural, and historical factors that may 
influence research outcomes into account. It focuses on the practical application of knowledge and the 
outcomes. Additionally, it acknowledges that there are multiple ways to view reality and that knowledge 
is constantly evolving. “For a pragmatist, research starts with a problem, and aims to contribute practical 
solutions that inform future practice.” (Saunders et al., 2016, p. 143). For this thesis, the research started 
with the problem of gaining access to high-quality reclaimed material. More specifically, gaining high-
quality reclaimed wood for Næste to enables the ambition of progressing from building a shed a month 
to a shed a day.

4.2 Approach to Theory Development – Abductive  
Given the utilization of pragmatism in this thesis, it is essential to recognize that pragmatists 
acknowledge the absence of absolute truth and the inadequacy of a single perspective to capture the 
complete picture (Kelemen & Rumens, 2008; Saunders et al., 2016). Consequently, the most plausible 
understanding of truth is dynamically derived as information, observations, and data emerge. To facili-
tate the dynamic development of information, observations, and data throughout this thesis, an abduc-
tive research approach is adopted. The abductive approach combines deductive reasoning (moving from 
theory to data) and inductive reasoning (moving from data to theory), allowing for a reciprocal inter-
change between theory and data (Saunders et al. 2012). By employing an abductive approach, it be-
comes possible to modify the theoretical framework and analytical considerations within the context of 
a relatively new and unexplored research area, as this approach provides flexibility in line with the find-
ings discovered during the research process (Saunders et al. 2016). 
By employing abduction, this research project can adapt the theoretical framework and analytical con-
siderations to the specific context. The abductive approach enables flexibility in response to the findings 
(Saunders et al. 2016). According to Bryman (2016) when using abductive reasoning a crucial step for 
us as researchers of social science is to “…come to a social scientific account of the social world” (p.394) 
based on our research participants' perspectives and still “…not lose touch with the world as it is seen by 
those whose voices provided the data” (p. 394). Thus, we acknowledge as a large part of our data is based 
on qualitative data from the interviews, we focus on finding the balance of contribution to both practice 
and research generalization to the social world.

4.6 Time Horizon – Cross-Sectional 
A cross-sectional time horizon is a research design that focuses on a specific point in time. The cross-

sectional. A cross-sectional time horizon is commonly used in survey research. However, case study re-
search also utilizes this design by collecting data from participants through interviews at a single point 
in time. The advantages of a cross-sectional time horizon include efficiency and convenience, as data 
collection occurs at a one-time point, resulting in a quick turnaround for data analysis. However, it has 
limitations. It fails to capture changes or developments over time since data is collected only once. Fur-
thermore, it does not account for temporal relationships or establish causality as it lacks repeated meas-
urements or tracking of changes (Saunders et al. 2016).

4.7.2 Primary Data – Participant Observation 
Another primary data approach used is participant observation which is a qualitative research method-
ology that involves the systematic involvement of the researcher in a social setting to gather firsthand 
and in-depth insights. By actively engaging with the participants, the researcher can access rich and 
nuanced information that might not be easily captured through other research methods. This immersive 
approach allows for the collection of primary data by directly observing the behaviors, interactions, and 
social processes within the natural context, providing valuable insights research topic (Saunders et al. 
2016).  There are different types or variations of the participant observation approach, which vary in 
the level of involvement and participation of the researcher. Saunders et al. (2016) explain that the com-
monly recognized types of participant observation include complete participant, complete observer, ob-
server-as-participant, and participant-as-observer. What has been used within this thesis is the com-
plete observer approach. Utilizing this approach involves observing the social setting without actively 
participating in the activities being studied. We hereby maintain a more distant and observational role, 
not directly engaging with the participants (ibid) 
Interviews X2 and X3 with Søndergaard were conducted at two distinct construction sites, namely Dam-
husengens School and Helmershus, both situated in the Copenhagen area. Prior to the interviews, the 
building (de)constructor from Søndergaard, provided tours of both construction sites. These tours were 
conducted with the aim to develop an understanding of a construction site, with a more direct focus on 
renovation and selective demolition. During the tours, observations were noted, and photos were taken, 
with the purpose of including them within the thesis, in order to showcase the observations.

4.8 Research Reliability and Validity  
In the following we outline the importance of ensuring data quality in research, particularly in terms of 
addressing biases, ensuring reliability, and establishing validity. 
Interviewer bias – This may be introduced through tone and non-verbal behavior, potentially influencing 
how respondents answer questions based on the interviewer's own beliefs and interpretations (Saun-
ders et al.2016). To mitigate this, open-ended questions were prepared, attention was given to body 
language and tone, and efforts were made to maintain engagement. Another concern regards establish-
ing trust and credibility with the interviewer (ibid). This was addressed by honestly communicating the 
research project's purpose and our intention with the interview and allowing for participation anonym-
ity. 
Participation bias - We have chosen the semi-structured interview as our primary method; we chose to 
investigate the world through the eyes of someone else. Thus, we attempted to apply active listening 
and not intimidating questions. Some participants may be willing to participate but be reluctant to pro-
vide information on certain topics. (Saunders et al. 2016). In addition, there is a tendency for partici-
pants to present themselves in a better or more negative manner (ibid). This was addressed by signaling 
an understanding of the construction industry and an understanding of negative aspects and critiques.  
Location of the interviews- The participants of the interviews had the choice of location for the inter-
views. This was presented to make them most comfortable as possible. Some of the meetings were con-
ducted via Teams or phone calls. This approach allowed for increased participant accessibility, accom-
modating their busy schedules. However, it is important to acknowledge that conducting interviews 
online or via phone calls may lack the personal touch of in-person interactions. 
Validity – Is enhanced through careful design and implementation, addressing biases, and ensuring that 
the collected data accurately reflects the research objective. Through the use of triangulation, a level of 
validity has been applied to the research (Saunders et al. 2016). Triangulation is possible to achieve due 
to the use of mixed methods research through this thesis, where both quantitative and qualitative data 
have been utilized. Due to the uncertainty in the quantitative data, it has been emphasized to especially 
focus on multiple sources of qualitative data. Furthermore, member validation has been utilized 
throughout the study, as interviewees have been able to receive the transcript interviews in order to 
comment, correct, or validate it, which some of the participants have taken to use. Alongside, structured 
models and figures worked out in collaboration with participants have been reviewed for validation.  
Reliability – This concerns the level of consistency and dependability of the data collected, ensuring that 
similar results would be obtained if the interview were conducted again under similar conditions (Saun-
ders et al. 2016; Yin 2003). Yin (2003) argues that “The general way of approaching the reliability 
==PAGE:52==
 
43 
 
problem is to make as many steps operational as possible” (p. 38). This is aimed to be achieved through 
the rigorous structure of our theoretical framework. Furthermore, a number of interview guides have 
been developed that fits the different stakeholder’s position in a construction project, this will allow for 
higher replicability if interviews were to be conducted again. In regard to the reliability of the observa-
tions that have been made at Helmerhus and Damhusengens School, it can be questioned if replicability 
of observations can be made, as construction projects are progressing and therefore is it not possible to 
observe the exact same as what has been observed in the time period of the thesis. Additionally, con-
struction sites vary, and they cannot be compared exactly one to one, which highlights some of the issues 
in creating the overall reliability of research on construction projects. 
 
4.9 Delimitations of Research 
Our thesis is aimed at investigating enhancing the circulation of reclaimed wood through collaboration 
between the demolisher and Næste. It is acknowledged that another sourcing channel for Næste regards 
wood from industry waste providers e.g., Kebony, Keflico, and Stark. However, we chose to solely focus 
on reclaimed wood from demolishers. This choice was made early in the thesis project to adhere to 
Næste’s ambition within CircOp to grow a sustainable business using demolition materials for second-
ary buildings as sheds. 
Furthermore, we focus on the construction of secondary construction, as Næste sheds fall within this 
category.  Hence, Næste is not limited by the same regulatory landscape as for primary construction and 
is able to use reclaimed materials in their sheds without accompanying the same warranties and guar-
antees found in primary buildings. 
Our focus centers on the Danish construction sector. Through our interview participants, we aim at cre-
ating a representation of the majority of stakeholders in the construction industry. Our investigation 
consisted of 12 interviews with 9 participants who are specifically involved in sustainable construction 
practices. As a result, our thesis predominantly represents the segment of the construction industry that 
demonstrates a certain level of maturity in sustainable construction. 
Construction and demolition projects vary greatly and there are many different demolition sites and 
approaches. We have chosen to investigate two different demolition sites, which were conducted by the 
same demolisher. Thus, our study provides a snapshot of the approaches to conducting demolition. In 
addition, another demolisher was interviewed to provide insights. Both demolishers, which were in-
cluded are considered quite advanced in their approach to conduct sustainable and selective demolition.

5. Analysis  
The following sections will constitute the analysis of our yindings. The analysis follows the structure of 
the theoretical framework. We start by conducting (5.1) a flow analysis of the material, information, and 
financial flow followed by (5.2) an analysis identifying key inhibitors affecting flow. Thus, constituting 
the descriptive part of the theoretical framework. Hereafter (5.3) a characterization of demolishers is

5.1 Flow Analysis 
5.1.1 
Contextual Factors for Flow Analysis 
To efficiently conduct the analysis of the material, information, and financial flow, we start by outlining 
contextual factors for Næste. There are five main contextual factors, which have been highlighted. These 
are (1) Material use, (2) Regulations regarding the construction of sheds as secondary construction, (3) 
Reprocessing, (4) Upstream factors: Næste’s placement in the RLSC for reclaimed wood, and (5) down-
stream factors: Næste’s customers 
(1) Material use - As mentioned, Næste specializes in the production of wooden sheds. Hence, the mate-
rial of focus is reclaimed wood from demolished buildings. When examining the RLSC for demolition 
waste, the emphasis is on the flow of wooden materials, in the following referred to as RLSC for re-
claimed wood. RLSC for other materials may vary in terms of stages, stakeholders, and overall charac-
teristics. This becomes evident as handling reclaimed wood requires other stakeholders than handling, 
e.g., reclaimed bricks (X2, X4). Miljøstyrelsen (2018) estimates that approximately 230,000 tons of wood 
from demolished buildings can be reused or recycled annually. Næste's mission is driven by recognizing 
the potential for more sustainable utilization of reclaimed wood, as emphasized by the significant an-
nual quantity of available wood. 
(2) Regulation - Since Næste is manufacturing sheds that are categorized as secondary construction they 
need to follow the guidelines set by Bygnings Reglementet (Bygningsreglementet 2023),. however, they 
are not limited by the same regulatory landscape as for primary construction and are hereby free to use 
reclaimed materials in their sheds, without the same warranties and guarantees as for primary build-
ings.  
(3) Reprocessing - Following this thesis’ theoretical framework, the three key stages outlined within the 
RLSC for reclaimed wood are dismantling, reprocessing, and secondary market operations. Tennakoon et 
al. (2022) portray the reprocessing stakeholders solely as companies involved in waste processing. 
==PAGE:54==
 
45 
 
However, this generalization does not apply within the context of Næste. Næste had previously de-
pended on a collaboration with RGS Nordic to handle the reprocessing of reclaimed wood. However, this 
collaboration has seized to exist. Næste express “You have heard about our attempts to scale a collabo-
ration brand through [RGS] Nordic, and how it ultimately didn't work out. They essentially withdrew from 
it” (X1, R1, p. 11 36:35). Thus, Næste is in a position, where there is no dedicated stakeholder responsible 
for conducting reprocessing of reclaimed material. Also, there is no third-party stakeholder in the mar-
ket, who is conducting reprocessing of wood. As a result, every time Næste intends to buy reclaimed 
wood for a demolition project, the reprocessing process is a topic for discussion. (X1) 
(4) Upstream factors - Næste is positioned at the end of the RLSC for reclaimed wood as a stakeholder 
within the secondary market operations. Hence, they are also positioned at a significant distance from 
the decision makers i.e., building owners and turnkey contractors, and the decision-making processes 
i.e., tender formulation and regulatory policies. Due to their position, their ability to influence the pro-
cess of circulating reclaimed wood is to a large extent through their closest stakeholders, such as the 
demolishers, who are also positioned at a significant distance from decision-makers and decision-mak-
ing processes (X1, X2).  
(5) Customers - To enhance a higher degree of circularity of reclaimed wood for Næste, it is essential 
that their customers are purchasing sheds made from reclaimed wood. Inputs from the customers, 
therefore, have a big importance in how the sheds need to fulfill their needs and bring value, in order to 
make reclaimed wood flow. A representative of one of Næste’s customers argued that the willingness to 
collaborate is due to Næste’s ability to innovate and make sheds from reclaimed wood with high quality 
(X10).

This section primarily focuses on the inhibitors that impact the material flow but is not exclusively lim-

1. Construction site context determines the ability for selective demolition – Accessibility for ma-
chines and dedicated space to place materials on the construction site is critical to conduct selective 
demolition. However, the physical construction site determines the accessibility i.e., “[…] They [Demol-
isher] were not allowed to drive their machines on public roads. They couldn't close off the road during the 
demolition period” (X6, R7, p. 78 03:49). Hence, specific circumstances make it difficult to conduct selec-
tive demolition, which is often related to demolition sites in the city (X1, X2, X12).  
2. Flexibility between stakeholders is needed to increase the ability to circulate reclaimed materials –
Typically the construction project, will always be a driving force, and time schedules need to be adjusted 
to cater to this (X9). Thus, there is a challenge amongst contractors, demolishers, and product manufac-
turers to be flexible to find a solution to ensure that reclaimed material is circulated, while also ensuring 
the timely progress of the construction project. Furthermore, there is a high degree of uncertainty when 
conducting selective demolition of the quantity and quality which can be retrieved from the buildings 
(X1, X2). Between demolishers and Product manufacturers, this concerns flexibility in the quality of ma-
terial agreed upon "[…] high standards need to be set. But at the same time, there should also be flexibility 
[…] It's almost utopian to expect a piece of wood without notches, marks, or anything else, without foreign 
objects” (X9, R3, p. 116 33:42).  
3. Lack of transparency in sourcing channels - Potential reclaimed material for secondary market 
operations is being missed, due to the lack of transparency between demolishers and product manufac-
turers/material handlers to receive the reclaimed material in the market. Demolishers find it difficult to 
find recipients and outlets for reclaimed material amongst product manufacturers. “There just really 
aren’t any recipients for it [reclaimed material]” (X5, R6 p. 65 28:59). Hence, demolishers tend to drive 
demolition material to residue disposal sites,” Well, we have recipients we typically drive it to, such as 
RGS, Norreco, and Danifo for example” (X5, R6 p. 65 28:59). But product manufacturers find it difficult to 
locate quality reclaimed material (X1, X4).  
4. Multiple handlings of reclaimed materials on dynamic demolition site increases the price of re-
claimed materials – It is acknowledged that this inhibitor also affects financial flow. However, as the 
physical material handling is causing this, the inhibitor is categorized under material flow. Construction 
sites exhibit a dynamic and ever-changing nature, evidenced by our initial visit to DS in February (X2), 
and further emphasized by the drone imaging of the daily transformations observed in May (X9) at the 
demolition site. Due to the dynamic nature of a construction site reclaimed materials were moved sev-
eral times, increasing the handling costs (X9). Another perspective comes from Tscherning stating:

This section primarily concentrates on the inhibitors that impact financial flow but is not exclusively 
limited to these. 
10. Varying conceptions of responsibility for processes for reclaimed materials – One area of uncer-
tainty lies in determining who should be responsible for transportation costs of reclaimed materials 
from the construction site to product manufacturers. Søndergaard perceives that “where it really hurts 
and could hurt in the future, is in the transportation expenses” (X9, R3, p. 116 36:10). Current considera-
tions of how to address this cost tend to lean more towards enablement of circulation of material, rather 
than standardized processes and agreements “Shouldn't we share them [transportation cost]? (…) So 
there is something in these transportation costs that also have a moral aspect, perhaps, or a collaborative 
aspect” (X9, R3, 36:10). Furthermore, there is a notable misalignment of who should be responsible for 
the reprocessing of materials (X4). Repeatably mentioned in interviews is that the quality of the material 
is a primary factor, which determines the purchase price for Næste according to the demand specifica-
tion document they sent to demolishers (X9, X12, Appendix 5.14). As mentioned in inhibitors 1 and 4, 
the context of the construction has a large say in the demolisher’s ability to conduct cleaning on the 
construction site. Still, there is a lack of guidelines on who is responsible for this process, demolisher, 
product manufacturer, or third party in different construction contexts. Søndergaard believes “the opti-
mal solution for demolition would actually be to take it down, separate it, and send it, in a container un-
cleaned” (X9, R3, p. 113 17:17). Whereas, during an interview with Tscherning (X5) questioned about 
the responsibility for the cleaning process of reclaimed wood, they (R6) replied “We have to take care of 
that part [cleaning]. So, there is a finished product ready for sale.” (X5, R6, p. 71 45:41). On the receiving 
side on the reclaimed material, Næste as a product manufacturer, believes that “It seems more appropri-
ate that it [cleaning process] lies with the demolisher, and that is definitely my own view, rather than with 
a waste management company” (X1, R2, p. 13 45:54).

11. Limited ability to mitigate risk amongst each other in construction projects – In the construction 
industry, there is a lack of transparency when it comes to the economic risks associated with selective 
demolition. Demolishers and contractors face difficulties in being transparent of financial risk, due to 
fear of losing a tender. As a result, there is a limited ability to effectively address and mitigate risk 
amongst stakeholders in construction projects. This lack of risk mitigation can lead to various negative 
consequences, such as cost overruns, delays, and disputes (X7)  
12. Economic barriers for demolishers to enter selective demolition operations - Selective demolition 
in a construction project presents significant economic challenges for demolishers. These challenges lie 
within the costs associated with carefully deconstructing and segregating materials which adds an ad-
ditional layer of labor and time-intensive processes compared to conventional demolition methods (X2, 
X5). This is expressed by Tscherning as “[...] it is often challenging to achieve an economic balance in 
relation to the potential value of the salvaged materials. This is because significant labor hours are invested 
in the selective demolition process, and labor costs can be high” (X5, R6, p. 58 05:21). This includes the 
need for skilled workers to identify, extract, and preserve reusable materials, which can increase labor 
expenses, where Tscherning adds “[...] there is also the aspect that if you only do something occasionally, 
you don't become proficient at it [...] it requires that it's something you do almost every day” (X5, R6, p. 60 
09:32). The market demand and value for reclaimed material may vary, making it uncertain to predict 
the return on investment for the reclaimed material. Additionally, the lack of standardized pricing mech-
anisms and market transparency for reclaimed materials can further complicate the economic viability 
of selective demolition (X4). The potential risks of delays, increased project duration, and higher overall 
project costs also deter some demolishers from adopting selective demolition practices (X2, X5). There-
fore, the economic factors surrounding pricing, market demand, labor costs, and potential financial risks 
pose significant barriers for demolishers to effectively engage in selective demolition within construc-
tion projects.

5.3 Characterization of Demolishers as Suppliers  
The following section provides a current state characterization of demolishers as suppliers of reclaimed 
material. To enable the reversed directed value proposition, a clear characterization of demolishers as 
suppliers of reclaimed material are necessary. Selective demolition necessitates demolishers to acquire 
new competencies in material circulation (X2, X9, X12). Thus, the following sections outline the devel-
oping characteristics of demolishers during selective demolition and apply this understanding to the 
reversed directed value proposition to establish future collaborative avenues, between Næste and de-
molishers as suppliers of reclaimed wood.  
Gatekeeping is a pivotal component in managing the reverse ylow, as it involves making decisions about 
which products should be directed toward the reverse logistics processes. Belvedere et al. (2017) em-
phasize the signiyicance of gatekeeping and highlight its crucial role in effectively managing the reverse 
ylow. In the context of the circulation of reclaimed material, demolishers serve as a gatekeeper for two 
key reasons. Firstly, the potential utilization of materials within a building, regardless of quantity, de-
pends on the demolisher's ability to conduct selective demolition effectively and retrieve usable mate-
rials for new construction (X2, X5). Secondly, once the materials are retrieved, demolishers must guide 
them to appropriate destinations where they can be utilized (X2, X5). Therefore, demolishers possess 
the capability to direct the ylow of materials. The maturity level of demolishers' selective demolition 
skills to some degree determines the availability and quantity of materials in the market. Through the 
examination of ylows and interviews with various stakeholders in the construction industry, we have 
identified two methods through which demolishers facilitate the flow of reclaimed materials down-
stream: by directly supplying to product manufacturers and by acting as suppliers to physical or digital 
marketplaces. We acknowledge that in both cases the demolisher continues also to supply material and 
waste to waste processors and residue disposal.  
A critical factor to include in the current characterization of demolishers as suppliers is, that they can 
supply reclaimed wood and/or other reclaimed material, without the ability to grant insurance. This is 
mentioned by Tscherning in the following statement “We do not want to be the ones providing insurance 
and guarantees. We cannot envision ourselves doing that. We do not have the setup to handle it at all, and 
==PAGE:71==
 
62 
 
there is a risk associated with it.” (X5, R6, p. 70 42:37). This is furthermore emphasized by Søndergaards 
“So it cannot be veriried if the wood comes from the same tree and has the same requirements or charac-
teristics. Therefore, getting the wood tested for its strength capabilities is something that must be included 
in the project [at secondary markets operations]” (X9, R3, p. 112 15:40).  
5.3.1 Demolishers as Direct Suppliers to Product Manufacturers  
In the case of Søndergaard providing reclaimed wood to Næste (and additional reclaimed material to 
other product manufacturers) from DS and HE, Søndergaard is characterized as a direct supplier to the 
product manufacturer. This setup is characterized by close communication between the demolisher and 
the product manufacturer. There is an initial contact from the demolisher “Currently, the process works 
such that the demolishers call us. When they have some wood, they give us a call They say they have some-
thing they want me to come and look at. So, I go out and inspect it, and then we discuss the possibilities.” 
(X1, R1, p. 11 36:35). Moreover, there is close communication throughout the process until the material 
is retrieved “Both of us are well aware that if we don't communicate regularly and frequently, it [the col-
laboration] will fall apart.” (X9, R3, p. 117 43:22). Moreover, close communication is needed for the flex-
ibility to allow changes in transaction agreement, if reclaimed material is not available in quantity or 
quality as originally agreed (X2, X9). Another phase of managing reverse flows regarding disposition, 
concerns sending the reclaimed material to specific destinations (Belvedere & Grando 2017). When De-
molishers are direct suppliers to product manufacturers, there is a need for the establishment of multi-
ple disposition channels to distribute reclaimed material. In the case of Søndergaard, they establish 38 
different sourcing channels (Appendix 5.13).  Management of numerous disposition channels for re-
claimed material demands additional time and effort in terms of establishing partnerships, ongoing co-
ordination, and resource allocation to conduct effective monitoring to ensure successful transactions 
(X2, X5). 
5.3.2 Demolishers as Suppliers to Marketplaces  
Another characterization involves demolishers supplying reclaimed material through a marketplace. 
This can be exemplified by the case of DS, where Søndergaard sells additional material via the digital 
platform, Klaravik (Appendix 5.13).  Klaravik is an online auction site that facilitates the sale of materi-
als, tools, and machinery on behalf of companies, without storing the materials themselves. However, it 
is notable that goods sold on Klaravik are mainly sold to private customers (Klaravik 2023). The fact 
that Søndergaard supplies both to a marketplace and directly to product manufacturers illustrates that 
within the same construction project, a demolisher can serve as both types of suppliers. (X2). By selling 
through an online platform the disposition becomes simpler, due to the online platform handling estab-
lishment and coordination disposition channels to distribute reclaimed material (Belvedere & Grando 
==PAGE:72==
 
63 
 
2017). With the inclusion of an additional actor, the online platform, in the RLSC for demolition waste, 
the demolisher's profit from the reclaimed material may be reduced, as it needs to be shared with the 
platform (X11, X12).

to marketplaces. With conventional demolition, the reclaimed material is often of too poor quality to be 
used in new construction (X1, X2, X5).

Pains – The primary pain which has been highlighted several times by demolisher “is always the econ-
omy. The economy, the economy, the economy” (X9, R3, p. 114 23:19). This statement aligns with the 
challenge of finding economic balance as expressed by Tscherning “It is often difficult finding economic 
balance” (X5, R6, p. 58 05:21). In most cases, the current operational expenses for conducting selective 
demolition are higher than demolition due to the need for extra time and resources “When you have to 
recover materials that can be used by Næste, they [reclaimed material] must maintain their size, quality, 
and strength. And that simply takes more time. It requires more manual labor. So, if you have a very tight 
schedule, there's often simply not enough time to do it.” (X5, R6, p. 58 04:31). Another pain concern is the 
alignment of expectations between demolishers and their primary customers i.e., building owners, and 
contractors, and adhering to their expectations and their time schedule, which often contradicts the 
need for additional resources and time to recover reclaimed material (X5, X9). A pain regarding the lack 
of ability to document the quantity of material being reused – “This issue of registering reuse. […] It's a 
bit challenging to track quantities when registering. We would like to measure how much goes towards 
reuse. It's a problem.” (X12, R6, p.140 09:47). This represents an increasing demand among building 
owners and contractors. “I have some customers [Building owners and contractors], which say they would 
like to have documentation of what goes to reuse” (X5, R6, p. 65 28:59).  
Gains – A potential gain concerns the ability to gain documentation of the material which has been re-
used in new construction (X6, X9, X12). Due to difficulties of quantity counting on the construction site 
potential gain concerns “[…] receiving some form of backward message. We have received this quantity.” 
(X12, R6, p. 141 11:02). To make it easy and accessible to deliver reclaimed wood with little notice would 
also be a gain, and with a dedicated person of contact enabling Næste to receive it. This is expressed by 
Tscherning in the following statement “Who is the contact person when we need deliver something for 
them? It's important that we can say we'll come tomorrow with X. They [Næste] should try to arrange it 
[delivery] quickly” (X12, R6, p.139 06:28). Another gain is the ability to earn goodwill and increase their 
sustainability profile through the reuse of materials instead of moving towards waste processors or res-
idue disposal. Another gain concerns the ability to introduce an additional revenue stream through sales 
of reclaimed material. Søndergaards believes that there is a potential gain “to demonstrate to others that 
it is possible and to qualify some economics in this [Selective demolition]. [Our Production Director] has 
always had a gut feeling that this is not a loss if you isolate it with economics.” (X9, R3, p. 113 20:08). 
Furthermore, this positions demolishers, who act as suppliers, to be frontrunners in the market of re-
circulating material.

Based on the gain creators and pain relievers outlined above Næste is currently compatible in many

as mentioned in 5.3 Characterization of Demolishers as Suppliers, demolishers do not have the set-up 
to grant insurance on the purchased reclaimed material (X9, X12). The fact that Næste is able to pur-
chase the reclaimed wood, without insurance and guarantee from demolishers emphasizes the yit be-
tween demolishers as suppliers and product manufacturers within secondary construction/buildings. 
This is due to the freedom that is found within the guidelines for secondary construction/buildings 
(Bygningsreglementet 2023).

However, Næste is currently not a fit for demolishers who solely are categorized as suppliers to market-

Nevertheless, Næste should not shy from approaching these demolishers, if they can showcase and con-
vince them of a reversed directed value proposition, which surpasses the value proposition from mar-
ketplaces. Næste is currently not providing data of the quantity of reclaimed material, which has been 
reused. Therefore, having documented quantity information for reuse can serve as a significant motiva-

would also be an option for Næste to approach the demolisher categorized as suppliers to the digital 
marketplaces in collaboration. Another area that was outlined by Tscherning as valuable would be to 
have a firmly established person of contact towards Næste, thus creating the ability for the demolishers 
to drive reclaimed material towards Næste with short notice (X12)

6.1 Theoretical Contributions – Discussion of Theoretical Framework 
Figure 19 illustrates how the different sections of our theoretical framework have enabled us to answer 
our sub-research questions and main research question. 
 
Figure 19: Theoretical framework with research and sub-research questions. Adapted from (Tennakoon et al. 2022) 
The descriptive part of the theoretical framework suggests that through analysis of the material, infor-
mation, and financial flow of the RLSC for demolition waste it is possible to identify inhibitors affecting 
the flow of circulating reclaimed materials from demolishers to product manufacturers.  
The theoretical framework includes the context of flow for reclaimed material. The contextual factors 
are critical to consider when enabling proper mapping of flows, as there is a great deal of variation to 
consider. Moreover, the framework focuses on the key stages namely dismantling, reprocessing, and sec-
ondary market operations, and within these key stages the main inhibitors affecting flow are located. 
Through our flow analysis, it becomes clear that there are several additional stages affecting the flow of 
reclaimed material toward product manufacturers. This regards the Building’s end-of-life stage, where 
time and resources are currently not being prioritized for selective demolition. This affects the Resource 
Mapping stage, as time is not prioritized to do proper resource mapping, which is a crucial step in 
==PAGE:91==
 
82 
 
conducting selective demolition. Thus, inhibitors, which are affecting the flow of reclaimed material 
from the demolisher to product manufacturers need to look across stages for the entire construction 
project, and not solely focus on the three key stages where the demolisher and product manufacturers 
are involved. Hence, demolishers and product manufactures are largely depended on the collaboration 
between stakeholders across the construction industry, to enable the circulation of reclaimed material.  
As mentioned, RLSC is a means to achieve a more circular process and aims to reintegrate reclaimed 
materials into the forward supply chain through a series of activities or proper disposal methods, align-
ing with the principles of the circular economy and its core objective of closing the loop (Ding et al., 
2023; Mallick et al., 2023; Wijewickrama et al., 2021). However, as collaboration across the entire in-
dustry is needed, there is a need for a focus on responsibility allocation. In which stages and with which 
actors should the responsibility lie for the circulation of reclaimed material? As the theoretical frame-
work is focused on demolishers and product manufacturers, crucial stakeholders such as the building 
owners and turnkey contractors are being overlooked. Moreover, the theoretical framework suggests 
that reprocessing is a present stage. However, through our mapping of the flows, it became clear that 
this stage is a fluctuating stage between the demolisher and Næste. Thus, it can be questioned if the 
descriptive part of the theoretical framework is too preconceived in having key stages outlined, to begin 
with.  
The prescriptive aspect of the theoretical framework suggests that outlining the reversed directed value 
proposition supports product manufacturers with valuable insights into the common repeatable plat-
form elements that need to be addressed in order to accommodate the challenges of demolishers as 
suppliers. By addressing the challenges, it is possible to enhance collaboration between demolishers and 
product manufacturers. The reversed directed value proposition provided valuable insights regarding 
the demolishers’ characterization as suppliers and their current position as gatekeepers of reclaimed 
material. This emphasizes the increased need for focus on demolishers in the construction industry to 
enable the circulation of reclaimed material.  
Moreover, the reversed directed value proposition allowed for a more focused view of the inhibitors 
which are currently the biggest challenges for demolishers, by mapping their pains and gains. By map-
ping the pains and gains, while simultaneously mapping the pain creators and pain relievers of Næste, 
as a product manufacturer it became possible to outline the current level of compatibility between de-
molisher and Næste. Thus, utilizing the reversed directed value proposition a new perspective of actions 
that product manufacturers can enable to increase the circulation of reclaimed material is provided.  
It can be questioned if demolishers are the right stakeholder to focus on. As it was mentioned, stake-
holders such as building owners and contractors, play a vital role in enabling the circulation of reclaimed 
==PAGE:92==
 
83 
 
material. Thus, using the reversed directed value proposition towards these stakeholders may have out-
lined other common repeatable elements which Næste as a product manufacturer needs to develop or 
continue to develop capabilities for. 
Additionally, it can be questioned if the reversed directed value proposition is the optimal use for sup-
porting collaboration between demolisher and product manufacturers. This can be questioned due to 
the very specific perspective of Næste as the product manufacturer, and Tscherning and Søndergaard 
as demolishers. Thus, there is a likelihood that other suppliers and product manufacturers within the 
construction industry do not have the same view of respectively pains/gains and pain creators/gain 
creators.  
The prescriptive aspect of the theoretical framework also proposes the adoption of platform thinking, 
which involves identifying the common repeatable elements that the product manufacturer’s platform 
should develop or further enhance. This aims to establish a defined interface between the product man-
ufacturer’s platform and the demolisher’s platform, ensuring sustainable collaboration that fosters the 
enhancement of circulation of reclaimed materials. This interface can be applied across various demoli-
tion projects, with the aim of repeatability for the circulation of reclaimed material. A way for a platform 
to understand which capabilities they should develop and further enhance is through the identification 
of the common repeatable elements from the key inhibitors and the reversed directed value proposition. 
This understanding allows them to create a defined interface towards a demolisher, increasing the de-
molishers’ perceived value and thus enhancing the likelihood of engagement with the platform. Platform 
thinking approach can be utilized to establish the repeatability of the economic baseline for selective 
demolition. Product manufacturers can address specific processes within selective demolition by ad-
justing purchase prices including transportation costs, and partially insourcing the reprocessing pro-
cess if needed.  
The platform thinking approach aims to establish a repeatable, and thus standardized, interface be-
tween demolishers and product manufacturers, ensuring the efficient circulation of reclaimed materials 
in various demolition projects. This is achieved by identifying the common repeatable elements which 
are viewed as highly valued by all demolishers. E.g., in every demolition project, the documentation of 
the quantity of materials sent for reuse is considered valuable. However, there is a competitive aspect 
that needs to be considered. As the reclaimed material has proven to be difficult to source, product man-
ufacturers must ensure that demolishers choose to send the materials their way. This can be accom-
plished by creating a company-specific interface towards demolishers that is perceived as valuable to 
them, and not an industry interface. This approach may seem contradictory to the goal of ensuring a 
standardized interface across the industry.

This thesis offers a framework to future product manufacturers and other stakeholders involved in sec-

valuable guidelines for especially other product manufacturers or stakeholders within the secondary

The current circulation of reclaimed materials is small-scale, the present uptake is mainly based on

live up to the regulatory demands towards the materials and currently no other stakeholder in the mar-

materials is sent to waste processing. Furthermore, it is anticipated that a new regulation in Denmark 
concerning selective demolition will be in place in the coming year, which will emphasize standardized 
plans and guidelines for selective demolition, which possibly will make even more materials available 
for circulation (CIRCLE HOUSE lab 2020). This leads to the notion that materials are available, but to 
make it flow towards primary buildings, there is a need for regulatory support and a proper RLSC for 
demolition waste. This thesis does address the issue regarding a proper RLSC for demolition waste, as 
it can provide a guideline for stakeholders within the secondary market operations to actively promote

6.3 Limitation  
Data foundation – Our findings are mainly reliant on the participation from the 12 interviews. Moreover, 
Søndergaard provided tours of the construction site and additional economical insights. Thus, there is a 
risk of bias towards their perspective. Despite attempts to ensure the validity and reliability of data, we 
acknowledge that this may have influenced our data foundation. If we had more time, we would have 
incorporated more insights from more field research of other demolishers, which would have increased 
the data foundation.  
Generalization – In our research, we carry out a single case study involving Næste, a product manufac-
turer that acquires reclaimed wood for secondary construction purposes. Since Næste is a novel com-
pany, it can be challenging to generalize our findings to a wider range of cases. Hence, it is crucial to 
enhance our study by including multiple case studies to achieve statistically significant results. However, 
in order to do that, the scope of our investigation may need to be broadened beyond reclaimed wood 
and instead focus on reclaimed materials in general, specifically within the realm of secondary construc-
tion. 
==PAGE:98==
 
89 
 
Validation of interface – The interface aims at changing the collaboration between demolishers and 
product manufacturers to enable a higher degree of reclaimed material from demolition projects. The 
interface has not been validated in collaboration with both Næste and the demolisher together. This 
limits us in evaluating their ability to develop this interface. If more time were available, we would have 
conducted a workshop with demolishers, e.g., a representative from Tscherning and Søndergaard, and 
Næste and have them in collaboration validate the platform and agree on the communication channels 
which could enable the interface.  
 
6.4 Further Research  
We have found that there are several avenues for future research. First, the thesis presents several in-
hibitors. However, the focus has remained on the inhibitors which affect demolishers and Næste as a 
product manufacturer. Hence there is potential to further investigate the remaining inhibitors. We spe-
cifically highlight inhibitor 13 Current selection of demolition tenders disregards time and money spent on 
material recovery, due to the low margin construction industry, as this has been shown to be affecting 
flow through the RLSC. Second, the economic analysis presented in this thesis offers fresh perspectives 
on selective demolition. However, due to the inherent uncertainty in the data, it is recommended to 
pursue further research in order to enhance the accuracy of the economic analysis for conducting selec-
tive demolition. This entails conducting time-based experiments and tests across various demolition 
sites, involving different types of materials. By doing so, valuable insights can be gained regarding which 
materials hold the potential for economic viability when compared to conventional demolition practices. 
Third, an additional examination is needed to delve into the reprocessing aspect. Our findings have re-
vealed a lack of clarity regarding the stakeholder responsible for conducting material cleaning and de-
fining the optimal cleaning process. Thus, it is imperative to conduct additional investigations to deter-
mine the suitable entity accountable for reprocessing, which could potentially be the demolisher, Næste, 
or a third party. Another, further research is to assess the potential of scaling the platform interface in 
order to establish a standardized industry interface between demolishers and product manufacturers. 
This investigation will involve analyzing the feasibility of ensuring consistent and replicable processes 
across various demolition projects. Furthermore, from a Næste perspective, the focus will be on evalu-
ating the interface's adaptability to expand to other regions in Denmark and enter the Nordic countries, 
allowing Næste to get closer to achieving their goal of constructing a shed a day. Final avenue for further 
research is the utilization of digital platforms. Currently, the platforms are in the early phase of adoption 
and show low maturity, as prices are high due to low volume and the assortment is minimal. But the 
area is trending and as platforms develop a deep insight into their functionalities and possibilities would

7. Conclusion 
This research aimed to provide an understanding of the extent to which collaboration between demol-
ishers and product manufacturers can be developed to enable a higher degree of circulation of reclaimed 
materials from demolition projects. The research is based on a theoretical framework to provide struc-
tural guidance for our case study.   
This thesis has been centered around the case company Næste. The case study has included stakehold-
ers from across the entire construction landscape to obtain a holistic understanding of the flow of re-
claimed wood. To allow for a sufficiently deep exploration of the collaborative nature between Næste 
and demolishers, valuable insights into real cases of conducting selective demolition and handling of 
reclaimed material have been provided by the demolisher, Søndergaard. These aspects have enabled 
the possibility of investigation of the current dynamics between demolishers and product manufactur-
ers, while also delivering opportunities for further development of collaboration to enhance the circu-
lation of reclaimed material. To enable a rigorous approach to explore the case of Næste, a theoretical 
framework has been developed with two guiding parts: Descriptive and prescriptive which encompass 
the three concepts of RLSC for demolition waste, reversed directed value proposition, and platform 
thinking, as further described in section 3.4. Overall, our theoretical framework in connection to the case 
study of Næste proved instrumental in answering the following research question:  
How can the collaboration between demolishers and product manufacturers be developed to enable a 
higher degree of circulation of reclaimed materials from demolition projects? 
The research question was further explicated through three sub-research questions. The first sub-re-
search question was answered in relation to the descriptive part of the theoretical framework. Nineteen 
inhibitors were identified in the construction landscape, affecting the flow of reclaimed material. Ten of 
these inhibitors could be addressed by the case company Næste to enhance collaboration with demol-
ishers. Specifically, inhibitors regarding economic barriers for demolishers conducting selective demo-
lition were examined, highlighting the economic viability when time was included in the tenders. The 
second sub-research question and the third sub-research question were answered in relation to the 
prescriptive part of the theoretical framework. The prescriptive part of the theoretical framework ex-
amined the relation between reversed directed value proposition, and platform thinking. The findings 
revealed a strong synergy between the concepts, as a reversed directed value proposition facilitated a 
more profound comprehension of the capabilities that a platform should develop or enhance to facilitate 
collaborative efforts. 
In summary, the identified inhibitors have highlighted the potential for fostering collaborations between 
demolishers and product manufacturers to improve the flow of reclaimed materials. Moreover, utilizing 
==PAGE:101==
 
92 
 
platform thinking to establish a clear interface, through developing repeatable capabilities which are of 
value to demolishers, can increase the chances of collaboration. However, the interface has not yet been 
proven to work, but the potential has been outlined. The potential of scaling the platform interface to 
establish a standardized industry interface between demolishers and product manufacturers depends 
on additional coherence of demolishers’ perspective of value, in terms of capabilities that support col-
laboration between them and product manufacturers.
