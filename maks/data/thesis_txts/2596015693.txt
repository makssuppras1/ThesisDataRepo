Abstract
Existing technology oﬀers a broad spectrum of opportunities to be explored within the structural en-
gineering ﬁeld. By combining emerging digital workﬂows with technical expertise, engineers have the
chance to enhance the design process by implementing structural optimization techniques at diﬀerent de-
sign and construction stages, seeking both minimal material usage as well as constructability of modern
construction works.
This thesis aims to investigate the potential of implementing structural optimization techniques into the
design process through parametric design, advanced optimization tools and machine learning algorithms.
The ﬁrst technique involves a combination of Genetic Algorithms (GA’s), Finite Element Analysis (FEA)
and structural parameterization. The technique at issue will ﬁrst solve a Single Optimization Problem
(SOP) for a stadium composed of inclined supports, an outer compression ring, and an inner tension ring
that will be suspended by a set of post-tensioned cables. Subsequently, a Multi-objective Optimization
Problem (MOP) will be solved for a train station deﬁned by a hanging structural model.
Lastly, a quadrangular free-form space structure will be optimized towards buildability by clustering
its connections using the so-called "K-Means" algorithm. The output of this process will be a set of
modularized connections that will simplify the manufacturing process as well as the ﬁnal assembly of the
roof structure.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The architecture, engineering and construction (AEC) industry is facing unprecedented challenges, mainly
related to digital transformation [5]. The digital tools available at the moment allow designers to go fur-
ther than ever. This is due to the fact that digital processes automate old manual work, and the resulting
data ﬂow connects client’s needs with high-value outcomes.
Parametric Design is playing an important role in the AEC development. This modeling process has
the ability to change the geometric shape of a model as soon as the dimension value is modiﬁed. The
process is implemented by ﬂexible scripts that deﬁne the dimensions, shape and properties of the model
[6]. These features allow designers to broaden the design space as well as to explore non-conventional
solutions, leading to more creative designs as well as a signiﬁcantly reduction of manual work.
Figure 1.0.1: Changing nature of structural engineering profession [1]
With regards to the structural engineering profession, these emerging digital workﬂows have change the
way engineers participate in projects. In fact, the engineers involvement in projects appeared at a later
stage of the design process (Figure 1.0.1), missing the chance to impact the design with structural con-
siderations at an earlier design stage, where everything is more ﬂexible.
Design decisions made at early design stage can highly impact the ﬁnal outcome of the project. The
presence of structural engineers at this stage is key, as it can lead to make smart decisions on the
conceptual design by providing fast feedback and guidance when the design is still ﬂexible. This gives the
structural engineers an opportunity to include structural optimization in the conceptual design stage, by
optimizing parameters that deﬁne the structural system.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Inﬂuence of Structural Strategy
Structural engineers face multiple challenges such as ensuring structural safety and minimizing material
usage. Speciﬁcations made by structural engineers impact the ﬁnal cost of the projects as well as the
resulting carbon footprint (10% of mankind’s greenhouse emissions [7]) due to the fabrication of steel and
cement (the active ingredient in concrete).
With sustainability as an increasingly important topic within the AEC industry, the need to implement
‘sustainable’ design practices must be addressed. This can be achieved by using computational design
strategies, with the main objective of reducing emissions from construction related activities by optimiz-
ing towards best structural performance leading to minimum material usage, as well as implementing
new techniques to minimize both fabrication and installation time of construction elements [8].
1.1.1
Structural Performance
Years and years of structural engineering practice have shown to us that structural form directly deter-
mines the distribution of internal forces.
As a simple example, the Figures 1.1.1, 1.1.2 and 1.1.3 illustrate three diﬀerent ways of spanning a certain
distance with the same cross sectional area and material properties. The higher the curvature results on
higher resisting forces due to a shift in the distribution of the internal forces. Thereby, if a certain force
has to be withstood, the most adequate topology would be the arc with the highest curvature (Figure
1.1.3), as it would require the minimum amount of material.

1.1.2
Buildability
Modern construction works usually require to be ‘Computationally designed’ and are usually tagged as
expensive and non-rational. However, the perceived complexity of modern architecture often obscures
the potential of digital workﬂows [8].
Despite this common perception, digital workﬂows can highly boost the design process when used in
an useful and productive way. For instance, by exchanging data (i.e., speciﬁcations and materials data)
between designers and manufactures [9]. In fact, by using these modern tools, new collaborative oppor-
tunities arise, creating unexpected synergies between all stakeholders involved in the AEC industry [10].

INTRODUCTION
As it was mentioned before, computational design can also oﬀer savings in material resources (Section
1.1.1) and time in the AEC industry. Moreover, time can be subdivided in design time and construction
time [8].
In this context, designer’s time can be easily saved by automating processes in order to perform both
calculations and models in a more agile way. However, time on site is more expensive [8], and therefore
savings in time during construction works are of highly importance. One way structural engineers can
contribute to achieve this, is by optimizing the buildability of the project.
Modern architecture works with complex geometries often present a large amount of diﬀerent connec-
tions (Figure 1.1.4) which can substantially increase both the construction and manufacturing time and
consequently the total cost of the building. However, their buildability can be optimized by modularizing
the connections in order to minimize their fabrication and assembly time [8].
Figure 1.1.4: Morpheus Hotel designed by Zaha Hadid
Consequently, buildability optimization can be brought into play by combining geometric modularization
with data exchange between designers and manufactures.

CHAPTER 2
State of the Art in Structural Optimization
Structural Optimization techniques have achieved a signiﬁcant progress in recent years. Advances in
computer technology allow designers to reduce the computational time, and consequently methods such
as Evolutionary Computation (EC) or Machine Learning algorithms can be implemented in the design
process. These modern techniques enhance the design process by an eﬀective solution space search [6].
Even though structural design often involves heavy calculations, the initial projection of the design
requires a combination of creativity and expertise. Consequently, the challenges that a structural engineer
might encounter can vary from projecting an adequate structural system to solve the problem, to member
sizing and detailing.
2.1
Structural Optimization Problems
Structural engineering challenges can be categorized as a continuum or discrete optimization problems
[11].
Figure 2.1.1 illustrates a simple continuum optimization problem of a cross-section, where (a)
represents the simplest optimization problem, where a predeﬁned topology and parameterized shape
remain constant only varying the dimensions of the ﬂanges and web of the element (member sizing
optimization); (b) illustrates a Shape Optimization (SO) problem where the aim is to ﬁnd the optimal
contour of an element with a ﬁxed topology; and ﬁnally (c) represents a Topology Optimization (TO)
problem, where new boundaries can be created throughout the optimization process [12]. In addition, the
same deﬁnitions can be extrapolated to a discrete optimization problem by combining diﬀerent elements
together as it is illustrated in Figure 2.1.2.

STATE OF THE ART IN STRUCTURAL OPTIMIZATION
2.1.1
Member Sizing Optimization
This optimization technique is the oldest and simplest of the three methods listed beforehand. Being
the problem constrained by the shape and topology of the structural system, the aim is to ﬁnd the
optimal the minimum mass associated to the initial geometry. Extensive literature related to solving this
optimization problem can be found in [13].
2.1.2
Shape Optimization
As it was stated before, SO is a technique which aims to ﬁnd the shape of the structural system from a
ﬁxed topology. In continuum optimization problems where adjacent elements are not perceptibly diﬀerent
from each other, the latter is deﬁned by the oriented boundary curves in a 2D structure or boundary
surfaces in 3D structures [3].
On the other hand, discrete SO problems are solved by variations in the geometry through changes in
locations of the nodes connecting diﬀerent structural elements [14].
2.1.3
Topology Optimization
TO has been an area of intensive research for the last forty years [11]. In the continuum optimization
problem, the method described in [15] is signiﬁcantly relevant as an Evolutionary Structural Optimization
(ESO) method was proposed by following the concept of removing lightly stressed elements. Moreover,
the insertion of Genetic Algorithms (GA’s) to the problem was done in [16], by determining the optimal
layout of a plate structure, being the latter constrained by its developed stresses when subjected to
loading and a maximum displacement. Regarding the discrete TO problem, recent research review can
be found in [3], whereas new investigations regarding the application of Evolutionary Computation (EC)
to the problem are presented in [17].
2.2
Technological Environment
In this context, Evolutionary Computation (EC) has played a signiﬁcant role in solving Structural Opti-
mization problems [11, 6] as this technique can be applied to any of the optimization problems described
beforehand. However, new techniques involving Machine Learning algorithms are emerging, such as a
technique to optimize free form space structures [18] to overcome long computation periods, and a clus-
tering tool developed by SMART Solutions Network at Buro Happold Engineering to optimize free form
space structures towards buildability.
The application of these techniques is only possible by the use of novel Computer Aided Design (CAD)
tools. In fact, these tools have changed the role of the computer from being a design assistant to being
a design collaborator [19].
This upgrade has only been possible by combining associative geometry, fast structural performance
evaluation and advanced optimization tools [19, 20].

CHAPTER 3
Computational-based Structural Optimization
Current structural optimization techniques usually involve iterative simulations and evaluation runs (i.e.
GA’s) [18]. Conversely, the emergence of Machine Learning techniques in the design process oﬀers a more
direct problem-solving approach.
As it was stated in Chapter 2, optimization problems can vary from member sizing, to shape optimization
and topology optimization. Furthermore, in order to overcome these problems, a computational-based
approach is required.
Due to the complexity of the AEC industry and its well-known constrains such as client needs, site limits,
national codes and collaboration between disciplines, the optimization problem must be ﬂexible enough
to fulﬁll all these requirements. Consequently, the methods presented hereafter will only be applied to
shape and/or member sizing optimization techniques.
3.1
Evolutionary Computation
Evolutionary Computation (EC) is a search technique which uses computational models of processes of
evolution and selection [11]. The concepts of this method follows the Darwinian principles of natural se-
lection [21], which are encoded in the algorithms in order to solve problems in many ﬁelds of engineering
and science.
The Evolutionary Algorithm (EA) (Figure 3.1.1) consists of a population of individuals that describe
the model. Moreover, the characteristics of each individual are deﬁned by genotypes. In order to create
further generations of individuals, the algorithm uses operators such as mutation or crossover by adjusting
the genotypes of each individual. Once a new generation is created, a ﬁtness function is used to reward
those individuals whose phenotypic behaviour corresponds with the best performance in the problem
domain, allowing them to survive to the next generation [22].

3.2
K-Means clustering
Unlike other machine learning methods, K-Means clustering is an unsupervised learning technique, where
considering a data-set composed of a data matrix X the main goal is to ﬁnd structure in the data such
as similarities, outlier detection, density estimation and associate mining. On the other hand, supervised
learning techniques require a set of target values y as an output [4].
This particular unsupervised learning problem aims on identifying similarities in the input data to then
create groups (clusters) of data points in a multidimensional space.
There are many ways of clustering a data-set, from the simplest clustering technique (distance-based
cluster types) to more complicated methods such as density based methods or conceptual clusters [27].
The K-Means algorithm operates with distance-based cluster types, speciﬁcally with center-based clusters
[4], which are deﬁned by the distance between observations and the cluster centers. Nevertheless, distance-
based clustering techniques can be performed in three diﬀerent ways [27], as it is illustrated in Figure
3.2.1.
Figure 3.2.1: Distance-based clustering techniques [4]
• Well-Separated: An individual observation is closer to all observations in its cluster than any
other observation in another cluster.
• Center-Based: Each observation is closer to the center of its cluster than any other cluster centers.
• Contiguity-based: Observations are closer to at least one observation in its cluster than to any
other observation in diﬀerent clusters.
The clustering is accomplished in the following way: being X a data set with N observations x1, . . . , xN
and K a predeﬁned number of clusters in which the data-set will be split into, k parameters µk that
represent the centers of each k-cluster are randomly introduced, to then assign each observation to the
closest cluster (normally based on the Euclidean distance ∥x1 −µk∥). The sum-of-squares between each
point to the cluster is then computed, and the cluster centers µk updated until the algorithm converges
on a solution by minimizing the sum-of-squares error at each step.

CHAPTER 5
K-Means clustering applied to modular connection design
In this chapter, a new design approach is proposed to rationalize structural connections in quadrangular
free form space structures by altering their shape, seeking modular connections to simplify the manufac-
turing process. In order to do so, the so-called ’K-Means’ algorithm will be implemented. The clustering
process is conducted in the following way:
Firstly, the angles of the adjoining bars are measured creating a NxM matrix, being N the number of
connections and M the number of measured angles (M=12).
Next step is to normalize all measurements in order to operate all angles (even the empty ones) on the
same scale. Once the matrix is normalized, the node clustering analysis can be performed, grouping the
connections by their similarity and subsequently building a clustered matrix produced by the average
angles of each cluster.
Finally, new vectors representing the extremes of the bars can be obtained from the clustered matrix,
deﬁning a new set of connections. These connections can then be projected by placing them onto their
corresponding position on the initial surface.
The process is developed within a parametric environment in Grasshopper. Furthermore, the data is
treated in a Grasshopper Python component (Figure 5.0.1) fed with both geometric information and the
number of clusters to be derived from the process. This Python component outputs a collection of vectors
and their corresponding anchor points where they need to be projected from. In addition, the cluster

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In this chapter, the developed workﬂows and its conditioning factors to be implemented in the design
process will be discussed. Moreover, the importance of taking into account all stakeholders involved in a
construction project will also be argued.
The adoption of the structural optimization techniques at issue present evident beneﬁts to the design
process. As a matter of fact, the resulting optimized solutions from the Evolutionary Optimization (EO)
process clearly verify the success of the latter as these individual solutions present an outstanding struc-
tural performance in comparison with their corresponding competing individuals within the same design
space. However, the computational cost of the process is likely to be signiﬁcantly high if the problem at
issue is of certain complexity. Therefore, a mastery of the theory behind the Genetic Algorithms (GA’s)
as well as a proper understanding of the structural engineering profession and its corresponding constrains
is required in order to ﬁnd solutions within an adequate design space. Additionally, the engineer in charge
of carrying out the optimization must have ﬂuent programming skills.
Moreover, the Single Optimization Problem (SOP) approach presents a direct way of ﬁnding an optimal
solution, while as the Multi-objective Optimization Problem (MOP) can result on multiple optimal so-
lutions that would need to be analyzed individually. However, the ﬁnal solution for the MOP was found
in a straight forward way as the all solutions where FitnessV alues1 ̸= 0 (number of collisions) were
dismissed, simplifying the magnitude of the solution space to a one dimensional problem.
Regarding the buildability optimization process applied to quadrangular free-form space structures, the
resulting modular connections would deﬁnitely reduce or prevent errors, delays, and cost overruns on-
site. Nonetheless, the structural analysis of the ﬁnal model would certainly become more complex as
the resulting connections would cause an irregular distribution of forces in the structural system. Con-
versely, most existing Finite Element Analysis (FEA) softwares can handle the complexity of the analysis.
The need of collaboration between diﬀerent disciplines and stakeholders involved in the architecture, en-
gineering and construction (AEC) industry is unquestionable, and therefore both optimization techniques
should be constrained by client needs, site limits, national codes and demands from other disciplines. This
is only possible by an eﬃcient exchange of data between all stakeholders and achievable by implementing
Building Information Modelling (BIM) workﬂows.
Additionally, as the resulting collaboration constrains on the models might vary throughout the design
process along with the Level of Detail (LoD) of the project, the structural optimization should provide
guidance to other disciplines in order to adjust these parameters in a smart way.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis aims to investigate the use of current technology applied to the structural engineering ﬁeld.
The developed digital workﬂows can achieve optimized structures towards both structural performance
and buildability. First technique is conducted by combining associative geometry, fast structural perfor-
mance evaluation and Genetic Algorithms (GA’s) (Chapter 4), and subsequently, buildability is optimized
by computing the so-called ’K-Means’ clustering algorithm to connections in a quadrangular free-form
space structure (Chapter 5).
The Evolutionary Computation techniques conducted in this thesis involve both shape and member siz-
ing optimization, leading to a performance-based evaluation for the Single Optimization Problem (SOP)
and an evaluation based on performance and number of collisions for the Multi-objective Optimization
Problem (MOP). Moreover, the ﬁnal solutions from both methods were found to be highly competent
within their corresponding solution space. Consequently, the author states that both techniques could
be easily implemented at an early design stage where the project requirements are still uncertain and the
design ﬂexible as well as at a more advanced design stage, by adjusting the parameters and speciﬁcations
that deﬁne the structural system at issue.
Additionally, the clustering technique, identiﬁed as a shape optimization problem, was applied to a roof
structure featured by its double curvature and composed of a quadrangular grid in order to modularize
its connections, and appeared to be highly eﬀective in terms of performance. As a matter of fact, the
algorithm was capable of ﬁnding similarities in both angles from adjoining bars and connection types.
Hence, the adoption of this technique would certainly reduce time on-site when assembling the connections
as well as their corresponding manufacturing cost, especially if the process is combined with a smart data
exchange with both the manufacturer and the client, in order to ﬁnd out the adequate number of k-
clustered connections to be modularized.
