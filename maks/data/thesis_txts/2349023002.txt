1. INTRODUCTION	  
	  
Organizations	  that	  are	  involved	  in	  the	  technological	  market	  are	  often	  faced	  with	  
rapid	  and	  unorthodox	  changes.	  These	  changes	  are	  brought	  forth	  by	  the	  growing	  
demand	   for	   innovation	   and	   diversification,	   opening	   up	   opportunities	   for	  
emerging	  companies	  to	  enter	  and	  take	  over	  the	  market.	  In	  such	  an	  unstable	  and	  
harsh	  environment,	  it	  is	  important	  for	  firms	  to	  keep	  iterating	  their	  strategies	  and	  
constantly	   scan	   the	   external	   forces	   at	   play.	   Companies	   that	   manage	   to	   stay	  
proactive	  and	  not	  reactive	  when	  faced	  with	  changes	  will	  be	  able	  to	  strengthen	  
their	  capabilities	  and	  maintain	  a	  high	  level	  of	  performance,	  enabling	  them	  to	  gain	  
an	  advantage	  over	  their	  competitors	  and	  gain	  better	  market	  intelligence.	  	  
	  
Before	  companies	  are	  able	  to	  define	  and	  implement	  strategies	  they	  will	  have	  to	  
undergo	   a	   strategic	   planning	   process,	   and	   an	   important	   role	   of	   management	  
within	   this	   process	   is	   to	   provide	   accurate	   market	   intelligence.	   An	   array	   of	  
analytical	   methods	   can	   be	   used	   to	   help	   firms	   better	   define	   their	   external	  
environments	  and	  internal	  capabilities,	  these	  methods	  have	  been	  pioneered	  by	  
the	  likes	  of	  Michael	  Porter,	  Hamel	  and	  Prahalad,	  with	  their	  inputs	  on	  competitive	  
strategies	  and	  core	  competencies.	  	  
	  
Charles	  Darwin	  said,	  “It	  is	  not	  the	  strongest	  of	  the	  species	  that	  survive,	  nor	  the	  
most	  intelligent,	  but	  the	  one	  most	  responsive	  to	  change”,	  therefore	  it	  is	  essential	  
for	   firms	   to	   understand	   that	   their	   awareness	   and	   analysis	   of	   the	   internal	   and	  
external	  environment	  should	  not	  only	  be	  done	  when	  warnings	  of	  change	  become	  
deafening.

1.1. 	  Research	  Questions	  	  
	  
The	  following	  questions	  are	  the	  fundamental	  core	  of	  this	  research	  project.	  These	  
questions	  will	  guide	  the	  project	  and	  assist	  in	  the	  construction	  of	  arguments.	  	  
	  
o What	   Impacts	   do	   the	   external	   and	   internal	   environment	   have	   on	   a	  
company’s	  strategy?	  
	  
o How	  can	  companies	  in	  the	  Middle	  East	  and	  Gulf	  devise	  useful	  strategies	  to	  
ensure	  future	  competitive	  advantage?	  
	  
	  
1.2. 	  Thesis	  Focus	  	  
	  
This	   project	   revolves	   around	   identifying	   new	   strategies	   that	   could	   be	  
implemented	  
to	  
allow	  
medium-­‐sized	  
enterprises	  
reach	  
new	  
value	  
propositions.	  The	   first	   part	   of	   this	   paper	   draws	   on	   theory	   and	   literature	  
review,	  describing	   strategic	   management	   and	   key	   analytical	   methods.	   This	   will	  
give	   us	   a	   clearer	   understanding	   on	   how	   to	   analyze	   a	   company’s	   strengths,	  
weaknesses,	  threats	  and	  opportunities.	  
	  
Putting	  all	  this	  into	  perspective,	  the	  author	  has	  partnered	  with	  Software	  Design	  
Consulting	  Group	  (SDCG),	  a	  company	  founded	  in	  1985.	  Having	  had	  over	  the	  past	  
years	  dominant	  leadership	  in	  the	  Middle	  East	  and	  Gulf	  region,	  SDCG	  is	  now	  facing	  
the	  challenges	  of	  acclimating	  to	  the	  market	  changes	  and	  new	  realities.	  The	  main	  
objective	  of	  this	  is	  to	  discover	  previously	  unnoticed	  external	  or	  internal	  factors	  
that	  could	  facilitate	  future	  sustained	  competitive	  advantage.

1.3. 	  Thesis	  Structure	  
	  
As	   mentioned,	   this	   thesis	   is	   divided	   into	   two	   parts,	   first	   is	   the	   theoretical	  
framework,	  where	  the	  author	  will	  present	  acknowledged	  theories	  that	  relate	  to	  
the	  research	  problem.	  These	  theories	  are	  comprised	  of	  analytical	  methods	  that

the	  
internal	  
and	  
external	  
environment	  
and	  
the	  
market’s	  
competitiveness.	  These	  methods	  will	  be	  summarized	  into	  a	  SWOT	  analysis	  table	  
that	  will	  serve	  as	  the	  basis	  of	  the	  argumentation	  for	  the	  empirical	  section.	  	  
	  
The	  empirical	  section	  consists	  of	  three	  chapters.	  First	  are	  the	  research	  methods,	  
where	  the	  author	  will	  discuss	  how	  he	  collected	  data	  via	  interviews	  and	  surveys	  
and	  how	  they	  were	  processed,	  the	  author	  will	  also	  evaluate	  the	  reliability	  of	  the	  
findings.	  The	  next	  chapter,	  titled	  research	  findings,	  will	  allow	  the	  author	  to	  put	  
into	  perspective	  the	  findings	  from	  the	  case	  company	  with	  the	  research	  gathered	  
from	  the	  literature	  review.	  This	  will	  lead	  to	  the	  section’s	  last	  chapter,	  Discussion,	  
where	   the	   author	   will	   give	   strategic	   recommendations	   for	   the	   case	   company.	  
Lastly	  is	  the	  conclusion;	  this	  will	  allow	  the	  author	  to	  reflect	  back	  on	  the	  research	  
questions	  and	  assess	  whether	  or	  not	  he	  was	  able	  to	  answer	  them	  accurately.

2. THEORY	  
	  
2.1. 	  Strategic	  Management	  	  
2.1.1. Strategic	  Planning	  	  	  
	  
With	   the	   increase	   uncertainty	   and	   dynamism	   of	   technological	   environments,	  
software	  companies	  find	  themselves	  in	  highly	  complex	  and	  disordered	  situations	  
that	   require	   numerous	   capabilities	   in	   order	   for	   them	   to	   keep	   up	   with	   the	  
competition.	   To	   achieve	   a	   high	   level	   of	   performance,	   firms	   have	   to	   pay	   more	  
attention	  to	  the	  strategic	  management,	  which	  requires	  them	  to	  analyze	  integrated	  
decisions	  and	  their	  effects	  on	  responding	  productively	  to	  emanate	  competition.	   
	  
In	  this	  theoretical	  framework,	  the	  author	  will	  only	  be	  focusing	  on	  the	  strategic	  
analysis,	  which	  comprises	  of	  evaluating	  external	  forces	  and	  internal	  capabilities.	  
“Environmental	   scanning	   is	   the	   monitoring,	   evaluating,	   and	   disseminating	   of	  
information	  from	  the	  external	  and	  internal	  environment	  to	  key	  people	  within	  the	  
corporation	  or	  organization”	  (Kazmi,	  2008).	  
2.1.2. Strategic	  Thinking	  
	  
Designing	  an	  effective	  strategy	  in	  a	  competitive	  environment	  requires	  a	  great	  of	  
planning,	   but	   also	   strategic	   thinking,	   “Strategic	   planning	   isn’t	   thinking.	   One	   is	  
analysis,	   and	   the	   other	   is	   synthesis”	   (Henry	   Mintzberg,	   1994,	   p107).	   Strategic	  
thinking	   differs	   considerably	   from	  planning;	  it	  involves	  intuition	  and	  creativity	  
and	   allows	   you	   to	   build	   a	   vague	   vision	   of	   direction	   that	   can	   be	   shaped	   once	  
opportunities	  arise.	  Unfortunately,	  a	  lot	  of	  companies	  nowadays	  take	  precedence	  
in	  planning	  and	  do	  not	  allocate	  enough	  resources	  towards	  that	  concept,	  by	  doing	  
so,	  they	  tend	  to	  refer	  to	  the	  same	  strategies	  and	  expect	  to	  obtain	  different	  results.	  	  	  
	  
==PAGE:9==
	  
9	  
“Strategic	  thinking	  relates	  to	  the	  processes	  of	  analyzing	  a	  firm’s	  current	  strategic	  
environment,	  defining	  a	  perception	  of	  the	  firm’s	  future,	  and	  devising	  new	  ideas,	  
which	  enables	  the	  company	  to	  out-­‐think	  its	  competitors”	  (Juan	  M.	  Benito,	  Juan	  A.	  
Sanchis-­‐Llopis,	  2013).	  When	  software	  firms	  today	  are	  faced	  with	  a	  challenge	  that	  
requires	  them	  to	  change,	  it	  usually	  means	  that	  a	  new	  software	  or	  hardware	  has	  
entered	   the	   market	   and	   there	   is	   simply	   no	   way	   to	   get	   around	   it,	   except	   adapt.	  
That	  is	  where	  the	  problem	  lies,	  companies	  use	  these	  occasions	  to	  define	  a	  viable	  
strategy	  that	  answers	  their	  problem,	  and	  once	  they	  succeeded,	  they	  keep	  relying	  
on	   it	   when	   in	   need.	   The	   market	   is	   so	   competitive	   nowadays	   that	   any	   small	  
mistake	  or	  occasion	  missed	  and	  you	  can	  be	  certain	  that	  another	  competitor	  will	  
rise	  up	  to	  the	  occasion	  and	  provide	  where	  you	  failed.	  That	  is	  why	  firms	  have	  to	  
stay	   proactive	   buy	   continuously	   scanning	   the	   environment	   and	   reconfiguring	  
internal	  and	  external	  competencies	  to	  address	  these	  rapid	  changes.	  “Strategy	  is	  
the	   direction	   and	   scope	   of	   an	   organization	   over	   the	   long-­‐term:	   which	   achieves	  
advantage	   for	   the	   organization	   through	   its	   configuration	   of	   resources	   within	   a	  
challenging	  environment,	  to	  meet	  the	  needs	  of	  markets”	  (Johnson	  G.,	  Scholes	  K.,	  
and	  Whittington	  R.,	  2008)	  
	  
A	   first	   step	   towards	   an	   efficient	   strategic	   thinking	   is	   to	   extensively	   involve	  
managers	   and	   other	   workers	   in	   some	   of	   the	   company’s	   strategic	   decisions.	   A	  
great	   deal	   of	   companies	   in	   Europe	   has	   built	   a	   complex	   matrix	   around	   this	  
concept,	   thus	   diminishing	   significantly	   top-­‐down	   management.	   But	   before	  
looking	  inwards,	  we	  must	  first	  understand	  our	  environment	  and	  how	  it	  operates.	  	  
	  
2.2. 	  External	  Environment	  	  
	  
External	   environments	   constitutes	   of	   conditions,	   events	   and	   factors	   that	   exist	  
around	   an	   organization.	   These	   entities	   should	   not	   be	   taken	   lightly	   as	   they	  
influence	  greatly	  the	  actions	  and	  decisions	  a	  company	  makes.	  By	  analyzing	  these	  
external	   forces,	   we	   will	   be	   able	   to	   determine	   opportunities	   and	   risks	   that	   lay	  
ahead	   of	   us,	   thus	   rendering	   a	   firm	   capable	   of	   manipulating	   certain	   elements	  
through	   marketing,	   while	   other	   forces	   will	   necessitate	   that	   firm	   to	   undergo	  
==PAGE:10==
	  
10	  
change.	  That	  is	  why	  it	  is	  important	  to	  keep	  a	  close	  eye	  at	  all	  times,	  this	  can	  be	  
done	  using	  an	  array	  of	  analytical	  methods,	  called	  foresight	  exercises.	  Foresight	  
will	   give	   firms	   a	   better	   understanding	   on	   the	   impact	   of	   their	   actions	   in	   future	  
events.	   This	   assessment	   of	   a	   firm’s	   strategic	   planning	   is	   very	   helpful	   to	   the	  
improvement	   of	   its	   technology,	   as	   they	   could	   find	   weaknesses	   that	   would	  
otherwise	  go	  unnoticed.	  	  
	  
It	   is	   also	   important	   to	   keep	   in	   mind	   that	   environments	   operate	   differently	   in	  
every	  country,	  According	  to	  Barry	  M.	  Richman	  and	  Melvyn	  Copen,	  “Environment	  
factors	  of	  constraints	  are	  largely	  if	  not	  totally	  external	  and	  beyond	  the	  control	  of	  
individual	   industrial	   enterprises	   and	   their	   arrangements.	   These	   are	   essentially	  
the	  ‘givers’	  within	  which	  firms	  and	  their	  managements	  must	  operate	  in	  a	  specific	  
country	  and	  they	  vary,	  often	  greatly	  from	  country	  to	  country.”	  
	  
2.2.1. PEST	  Framework	  
	  
PEST	  is	  an	  acronym	  for	  “political,	  economic,	  social	  and	  technological”	  and	  is	  an	  
effective	   analysis	   tool	   when	   it	   comes	   to	   understanding	   the	   economical	   and	  
technological	   environment	   that	   surround	   an	   organization.	   When	   analyzing	   a	  
situation,	  there	  are	  a	  great	  of	  variables	  to	  take	  into	  consideration,	  which	  is	  why	  
strategists	   have	   made	   it	   simpler	   by	   dividing	   it	   into	   macro	   and	   micro	  
environments.	  	  
	  
The	   microenvironment	   of	   an	   organization	   refers	   to	   factors	   that	   influence	   the	  
performance	  and	  decision	  making	  of	  a	  firm;	  these	  elements	  are	  represented	  by	  
competitors,	  employees	  and	  the	  community	  where	  a	  company	  operates.	  Macro-­‐
environments	   however,	   consist	   of	   forces	   that	   are	   beyond	   an	   organization’s	  
control	   and	   have	   a	   powerful	   influence	   on	   their	   operations.	   Harvey	   Carruthers	  
(2009,	  p.	  31)	  suggest	  that	  “by	  applying	  PEST	  analysis,	  it	  is	  hoped	  that	  businesses	  
and	  individuals	  can	  forecast	  from	  which	  direction	  and	  with	  how	  much	  effect	  the	  
environment	  will	  impact	  on	  them”.	  As	  change	  occurs	  great	  opportunities	  arise	  as	  
well	  as	  significant	  threats,	  but	  by	  using	  the	  right	  tools	  we	  will	  be	  able	  to	  have	  a	  
==PAGE:11==
	  
11	  
better	  understanding	  of	  the	  ‘big	  picture’,	  and	  take	  control	  of	  occasions	  that	  they	  
present.	  	  
	  
One	  of	  the	  most	  important	  aspects	  of	  this	  tool	  is	  brainstorming	  the	  changes	  that	  
are	   currently	   surrounding	   your	   company,	   and	   formulate	   a	   proper	   response	  
accordingly.	   Brainstorming	   can	   seem	   relatively	   easy,	   but	   when	   there	   are	   this	  
many	   factors	   to	   take	   into	   consideration,	   it	   can	   be	   quite	   overwhelming.	  
Nevertheless,	  PEST	  is	  a	  useful	  guideline	  when	  in	  doubt	  of	  what	  factors	  to	  consider	  
and	  to	  what	  degree	  will	  it	  affect	  the	  organization.	  As	  already	  mentioned,	  there	  are	  
four	  influences	  that	  form	  the	  basis	  of	  this	  tool,	  each	  one	  of	  these	  factors	  can	  have	  
a	   significant	   impact	   on	   the	   working	   environment,	   but	   what	   is	   important	   is	   to	  
understand	   the	   key	   drivers	   of	   structural	   change	   and	   how	   a	   firm	   can	   relate	   to	  
them.	  	  
	  
Political	  factors	  play	  an	  essential	  role	  in	  an	  organization’s	  operation,	  for	  example,	  
we	   have	   witnessed	   legislations	   being	   put	   in	   place	   in	   the	   European	   Union	   that	  
allow	  organizations	  to	  openly	  move	  goods	  and	  services	  in	  27	  different	  countries	  
with	  few	  restrictions	  (New	  Legislative	  Framework,	  2008).	  And	  on	  the	  other	  hand,	  
we	  can	  see	  organizations	  struggling	  in	  the	  Middle	  East	  and	  some	  parts	  of	  Asia,	  all	  
because	  of	  the	  level	  of	  corruption	  and	  political	  stability	  of	  neighboring	  countries,	  
rendering	  operations	  arduous.	  When	  analyzing	  economic	  factors,	  an	  organization	  
should	   always	   be	   looking	   out	   for	   the	   economy’s	   inflation	   rate,	   because	   it	   has	  
consequences	  on	  the	  pricing	  of	  a	  product.	  Technological	  factors	  have	  become	  one	  
the	   most	   important	   elements	   for	   organizations	   today.	   Due	   to	   the	   disruptive	  
nature	  of	  the	  market,	  firms	  that	  fail	  to	  keep	  up	  with	  the	  modernized	  world	  leave	  
an	  opening	  for	  emerging	  companies	  to	  enter	  their	  market	  and	  take	  their	  market	  
shares.	  We	  saw	  this	  happening	  to	  market	  leaders	  Nokia	  and	  Blackberry	  who	  were	  
too	   slow	   to	   implement	   smartphone	   technology	   and	   got	   replaced	   by	   Apple	   and	  
Samsung.	   Organizations,	   who	   want	   to	   protect	   their	   market	   shares	   today,	   are	  
resulting	  in	  the	  use	  of	  patents	  and	  licenses	  to	  preserve	  their	  intellectual	  property.

2.2.2. Five	  Forces	  	  
	  
Using	  PEST	  tools	  alone	  will	  only	  give	  you	  half	  the	  picture,	  as	  it	  is	  crucial	  to	  have	  a	  
good	   understanding	   of	   your	   competitors,	   which	   is	   why	   Michael	   Porter’s	   five	  
forces	  are	  often	  combined	  with	  PEST.	  It	  is	  a	  useful	  tool	  in	  figuring	  out	  a	  firm’s	  
current	  competitive	  position	  and	  the	  strength	  of	  an	  envisioned	  position	  that	  the	  
organization	   would	   like	   to	   attain;	   Porter’s	   five	   forces	   helped	   simplified	   micro-­‐
economics	   and	   defined	   the	   forces	   that	   determine	   an	   industry’s	   profitability.	  	  
These	  two	  tools	  go	  hand	  in	  hand	  because	  one	  of	  the	  most	  important	  criteria	  when	  
utilizing	   the	   five	   forces	   is	   the	   organization’s	   ability	   to	   accurately	   define	   its	  
market.	  Porter’s	  tools	  are	  regarded	  as	  a	  more	  practical	  approach	  because	  it	  also	  
looks	  at	  the	  forces	  that	  your	  competitors	  might	  exert	  on	  a	  specific	  market	  and	  the	  
repercussions	  it	  could	  have	  on	  the	  long	  run.	  	  
	  
When	  using	  both	  tools	  it	  is	  important	  not	  to	  define	  your	  market	  too	  specifically,	  
because	  you	  might	  miss	  out	  on	  other	  market	  demands	  and	  emerging	  competitors.	  
A	  popular	  example	  of	  this	  is	  Coca-­‐Cola	  when	  being	  the	  first	  in	  the	  market;	  it	  was	  
able	   to	   define	   it	   as	   a	   ‘soft	   drinks’	   market	   before	   any	   of	   its	   competitors.	   This	  
definition	  was	  too	  narrow	  and	  did	  not	  meet	  all	  of	  the	  customer’s	  needs,	  which	  in	  
fact,	   prevented	   Coca-­‐	   Cola	   to	   prepare	   for	   the	   impact	   of	   new	   markets	   such	   as	  
coffee	  (ex:	  Starbucks)	  and	  energy	  drinks	  (ex:	  Red	  Bull),	  thus	  not	  able	  to	  have	  its

biggest	  threats	  to	  organizations	  come	  from	  new	  entrants,	  emerging	  technologies,	  
bargaining	  power	  of	  suppliers	  and	  buyers	  and	  finally	  threat	  of	  substitution.	  These

on	  that	  company’s	  sustainability.

2.2.3. Market	  Fragmentation	  	  
	  
New	  markets	  are	  born	  on	  a	  regular	  basis,	  and	  they	  have	  a	  substantial	  impact	  on	  
the	   profit	   an	   industry	   makes	   at	   that	   time.	   From	   the	   moment	   a	   new	   market	  
emerges	  till	  it	  becomes	  fragmented,	  it	  undergoes	  variations	  as	  organizations	  shift	  
their	   strategies	   to	   better	   exploit	   it.	   These	   new	   markets	   are	   born	   from	   new	  
concepts	  or	  technologies	  at	  an	  accelerated	  rate	  today,	  and	  the	  public	  demand	  for	  
these	  new	  services	  are	  exponentially	  growing,	  making	  it	  a	  very	  appealing	  sector	  
for	  other	  businesses.	  	  Edward	  Nugent	  said	  “If	  more	  and	  more	  businesses	  access	  
the	   knowledge	   necessary	   for	   entry	   then	   supply	   will	   eventually	   exceed	   demand	  
with	  the	  degree	  of	  competition	  greatly	  intensifying.”	  
	  
These	   new	   segment	   upset	   otherwise	   homogenous	   markets	   as	   they	   have	   their	  
own	   distinct	   needs,	   prerequisites	   and	   inclinations.	   They	   diminish	   the	  
effectiveness	  of	  mass	  marketing	  methods	  and	  remove	  any	  brand	  loyalty,	  which	  is	  
why	  firms	  must	  focus	  their	  attention	  on	  specialization	  or	  customization.	  To	  avoid	  
entering	   price	   wars	   with	   their	   competitor	   Kazem	   Chaharbaghi	   suggests,	   “an	  
organization	  should	  constantly	  be	  developing	  the	  knowledge	  base	  of	  its	  offerings	  
at	   a	   rate	   greater	   than	   that	   of	   its	   competitors,	   thereby	   focusing	   on	   value-­‐added	  
features	  and	  services	  on	  which	  new	  markets	  depend”.	  By	  doing	  so,	  organizations	  
have	  to	  constantly	  renew	  their	  strategies	  in	  order	  to	  be	  able	  to	  provide	  creative	  
and	  innovative	  solutions.	  
	  
A	  good	  example	  of	  a	  fragmented	  market	  is	  the	  industry	  of	  hardware	  components	  
for	  computers.	  Today,	  the	  market	  for	  cheap	  computer	  hardware	  is	  very	  large,	  and	  
we	  can	  see	  a	  lot	  of	  new	  entrants	  all	  across	  the	  globe,	  each	  trying	  to	  reduce	  the	  
cost	  as	  much	  as	  possible,	  reducing	  the	  profit	  margin	  to	  barely	  anything.	  Even	  if	  
market	  leaders	  were	  to	  merge	  to	  achieve	  monopolization,	  they	  would	  be	  forced	  
to	  retain	  the	  same	  low	  prices	  since	  other	  businesses	  could	  undercut	  them	  at	  any	  
point.

2.3. 	  Internal	  Capabilities	  	  
	  
For	   an	   organization	   to	   put	   in	   place	   a	   successful	   and	   efficient	   strategy,	   it	   must	  
formulate	   a	   realistic	   assessment	   of	   its	   internal	   resources	   and	   capabilities.	   This	  
internal	   analysis	   will	   allow	   a	   firm	   to	   identify	   is	   strengths	   and	   weaknesses	   and	  
build	   on	   them	   when	   devising	   a	   strategy.	   By	   effectively	   making	   use	   of	   an	  
organization’s	  internal	  capabilities,	  resources	  and	  core	  competencies,	  it	  will	  be	  
able	  to	  better	  utilize	  the	  market’s	  opportunities	  and	  threats	  and	  gain	  advantage	  
over	  their	  competitors.	  	  
	  
All	  organizations,	  whatever	  the	  field	  they	  work	  in,	  will	  have	  to	  be	  engaged	  in	  the	  
functions	  of	  finance,	  marketing,	  production	  and	  human	  resource	  development.	  An	  
effective	   strategy	   will	   demand	   careful	   planning	   and	   execution	   of	   these	   various	  
functions	   and	   the	   joint	   performance	   of	   these	   elements	   will	   have	   direct	  
repercussions	   on	   the	   firm’s	   performance	   in	   terms	   of	   better	   product	   design,	  
product	  quality,	  and	  customer	  service.	  	  
	  
2.3.1. Boston	  Matrix	  	  
	  
As	  stated	  earlier,	  a	  company’s	  ability	  to	  change	  can	  only	  be	  carried	  out	  effectively	  
if	  it	  has	  a	  clear	  understanding	  of	  its	  core	  capabilities.	  One	  of	  the	  most	  effective	  
tools	  to	  do	  so	  is	  the	  Boston	  matrix,	  a	  technique	  that	  was	  first	  coined	  by	  Bruce	  
Henderson	  in	  1970.	  	  
	  
The	  Boston	  matrix	  puts	  in	  perspective	  two	  variables,	  market	  share	  and	  market

classify	  their	  products	  into	  one	  of	  the	  shown	  categories	  based	  on	  their	  market	  
shares	  and	  growth	  rate.	  By	  doing	  so,	  organizations	  will	  be	  able	  to	  decide	  how	  to	  
allocate	   resources	   to	   different	   services	   they	   provide,	   depending	   on	   their	  
product’s	  performance	  and	  position	  in	  the	  market.

have	  monopolized	  the	  market.	  This	  is	  not	  always	  the	  case	  as	  some	  products	  may	  
be	  market	  leaders	  but	  is	  not	  necessarily	  the	  most	  profitable.	  On	  the	  other	  hand,	  
market	  growth	  is	  the	  percentage	  of	  growth	  compared	  to	  the	  previous	  year;	  it	  is	  a	  
measure	  of	  how	  attractive	  a	  particular	  market	  is,	  thus	  opening	  opportunities	  for

2.3.2. Value	  Chain	  Analysis	  
	  
The	   aim	   of	   combining	   the	   Boston	   matrix	   with	   Porter’s	   value	   chain	   is	   so	   that	  
organization	   can	   optimize	   their	   products	   so	   that	   they	   end	   up	   having	   a	   greater	  
value	  than	  the	  original	  cost	  of	  creating	  the	  product.	  By	  adding	  more	  value	  to	  a	  
product	  or	  service	  it	  becomes	  more	  profitable,	  and	  by	  giving	  more	  value	  to	  the	  
end	  customers	  it	  will	  be	  able	  to	  build	  a	  bigger	  competitive	  advantage.	  By	  making	  
efficient	   use	   of	   this	   tool,	   companies	   can	   examine	   all	   of	   their	   activities,	   and	  
understand	   how	   well	   they	   perform	   together.	   “The	   concept	   of	   the	   value	   chain	  
captures	   the	   notion	   that	   a	   firm	   engages	   in	   a	   number	   of	   activities	   to	   transform	  
inputs	  into	  outputs,	  and	  through	  this	  process	  adds	  value	  at	  each	  stage”	  (Porter,	  
1985)	  	  
	  
The	   activities	   that	   Michael	   Porter	   was	   referring	   to	   are	   categorized	   into	   two	  
subclasses	   in	   order	   to	   better	   capture	   value	   and	   to	   understand	   the	   key	  
components	   that	   enable	   the	   success	   and	   profitability	   of	   a	   firm.	   These	   distinct	  
activities	   are	   classified	   as	   primary	   activities,	   which	   directly	   add	   value	   by	  
transforming	   inputs	   into	   outputs,	   and	   support	   activities,	   that	   help	   add	   value	  
indirectly.	  The	  two	  activities	  are	  depicted	  in	  the	  graph	  below.

2.3.3. Competitive	  Advantage	  
	  
A	   company	   that	   has	   a	   competitive	   advantage	   in	   a	   particular	   market	   will	   most	  
likely	  be	  more	  profitable	  that	  its	  competitors	  simply	  because	  it	  was	  able	  to	  add	  
more	   value	   to	   its	   products,	   thus	   capturing	   more	   market	   shares.	   A	   firm’s	  
profitability	  derives	  from	  the	  difference	  between	  its	  total	  revenues	  and	  the	  cost	  
of	   the	   product’s	   production	   and	   implementation.	   “We	   quickly	   realize	   that	   firm	  
profitability	  depends,	  simply	  put,	  on	  three	  factors:	  (1)	  perceived	  value	  created	  for	  
customers;	   (2)	   the	   price	   of	   the	   product	   or	   service;	   and	   (3)	   the	   total	   costs	   of	  
producing	   the	   product	   or	   service”	   (Frank	   Rothaermel,	   2008).	   Based	   on	   this	  
concept	   a	   firm	   can	   attain	   a	   competitive	   advantage	   by	   creating	   more	   economic	  
value.	  Even	  if	  a	  company	  manages	  to	  create	  a	  better	  product	  with	  lower	  costs,	  it	  
doesn’t	  necessarily	  mean	  it	  will	  gain	  an	  advantage	  over	  its	  competitors.	  If	  we	  look	  
==PAGE:20==
	  
20	  
at	  laptops	  in	  today’s	  market,	  we	  can	  clearly	  see	  that	  Apple	  has	  managed	  to	  create	  
more	  value	  to	  their	  customers,	  that	  is	  why	  they	  can	  charge	  a	  higher	  price	  than	  say	  
Lenovo	   or	   Dell,	   which	   by	   analyzing	   the	   components	   and	   hardware	   they	  
implement	   offer	   a	   better	   overall	   performance.	   This	   means	   that	   one	   of	   the	  
important	   aspects	   of	   competitive	   advantage	   derives	   from	   the	   perceived	   value	  
created	  for	  customers,	  this	  benefits	  the	  buyer	  and	  seller	  because	  buyers	  will	  often	  
value	  their	  purchased	  goods	  at	  a	  higher	  price	  than	  what	  they	  actually	  paid	  for	  and	  
sellers	  will	  be	  able	  to	  sell	  their	  products	  at	  a	  higher	  cost.	  	  
	  
Following	   this	   train	   of	   thought	   we	   can	   deduce	   that	   companies	   can	   generate	  
advantage	  in	  multiple	  ways,	  either	  by	  cost	  advantage	  or	  differentiation.	  Michael	  
Porter’s	   definition	   of	   differentiation	   is	   “the	   capability	   to	   provide	   customers	  
superior	  and	  special	  value	  in	  the	  form	  of	  product’s	  special	  features	  and	  quality	  or	  
in	  the	  form	  of	  aftersales	  customer	  service”.	  Getting	  back	  to	  our	  example	  of	  Apple,	  
we	  can	  clearly	  see	  that	  they	  have	  perfected	  their	  strategies,	  as	  they	  are	  able	  to	  
gain	   superior	   profits	   by	   providing	   a	   product	   that	   doesn’t	   focus	   on	   the	  
functionalities	  but	  rather	  the	  ergonomics,	  which	  is	  what	  their	  target	  audience	  is

3. RESEARCH	  METHODS	  
	  
3.1. 	  Qualitative	  Research	  	  
	  
A	  qualitative	  research	  method	  has	  been	  chosen	  for	  this	  thesis,	  because	  compared	  
to	  a	  quantitative	  method,	  it	  allows	  the	  author	  to	  explore	  more	  in	  depth	  a	  set	  of	  
predetermined	   problems.	   In	   order	   to	   find	   answers	   to	   these	   questions,	   it	   is	  
important	   to	   first	   understand	   the	   phenomena	   by	   analyzing	   a	   set	   of	   predefined	  
methods	  that	  will	  serve	  as	  guidelines	  when	  collecting	  data.	  	  
	  
The	  author	  chose	  to	  pursue	  a	  qualitative	  research	  because	  it	  can	  be	  an	  effective	  
method	   in	   identifying	   unapparent	   elements	   that	   could	   provide	   a	   deeper	  
understanding	  of	  the	  research	  problem.	  Since	  a	  qualitative	  research	  favors	  people	  
as	  instruments	  of	  information,	  the	  author	  chose	  to	  conduct	  interviews	  with	  seven	  
high	  placed	  mangers	  in	  order	  to	  reveal	  new	  data	  on	  their	  personal	  perspectives	  
and	   experiences.	   The	   data	   collected	   from	   the	   case	   study	   will	   help	   the	   author	  
reveal	  new	  findings	  that	  were	  previously	  unknown.	  
	  
3.2. 	  Interviews	  as	  Data	  Collection	  Methods	  	  
	  
When	  using	  interviews	  as	  one	  of	  the	  qualitative	  research	  methods,	  it	  is	  important	  
to	   carefully	   choose	   the	   respondents,	   as	   they	   need	   to	   have	   considerable	  
knowledge	  and	  exposure	  on	  the	  subject	  of	  study.	  That	  is	  why	  the	  interviews	  in	  
question	  were	  planned	  and	  managed	  in	  advance	  with	  the	  help	  of	  the	  company’s	  
CEO.	  There	  are	  different	  variations	  of	  interviews	  that	  can	  be	  used	  in	  a	  qualitative	  
research	  such	  as	  In-­‐depth	  interviews,	  semi-­‐structured	  or	  structured.	  Structured	  
interview	   follow	   a	   specific	   set	   of	   questions	   that	   were	   drafted	   in	   advance	   and	  
allow	   very	   little	   room	   for	   variation	   in	   the	   interviewee’s	   response.	   This	   type	   of	  
interview	  is	  normally	  used	  when	  the	  researcher	  has	  deep	  understanding	  of	  the	  
subject	   discussed	   and	   thus	   the	   use	   of	   close-­‐ended	   questions	   would	   be	   more	  
==PAGE:23==
	  
23	  
appropriate.	  On	  the	  other	  hand,	  semi-­‐structured	  interviews	  are	  carried	  out	  with	  a	  
relatively	  open	  framework	  that	  allow	  for	  an	  attentive	  two-­‐way	  discussion.	  These	  
interview	  generally	  start	  with	  broad	  questions,	  that	  are	  drafted	  in	  advance,	  and	  
moves	   on	   to	   more	   detailed	   ones	   that	   are	   created	   along	   the	   way	   based	   on	   the	  
interviewee’s	  answers.	   
By	  working	  and	  observing	  the	  case	  company	  for	  four	  months,	  the	  author	  was	  able	  
to	   gather	   information	   concerning	   the	   firm’s	   operations	   and	   structural	   matrix.	  
Being	  that	  there	  were	  still	  strategies	  and	  operations	  that	  were	  undisclosed,	  the	  
author	  chose	  to	  conduct	  interviews	  with	  the	  employees,	  allowing	  him	  to	  extract	  a	  
maximum	   of	   information.	   The	   interviews	   themselves	   were	   very	   flexible	   and	  
allowed	   the	   author	   to	   ask	   additional	   questions	   whenever	   peculiar	   topics	  
emerged.	  	  
Because	   the	   aim	   of	   this	   research	   was	   to	   gather	   and	   examine	   data	   on	  
predetermined	  themes,	  which	  are	  organizational	  strengths,	  opportunities,	  threats	  
and	   weaknesses,	   the	   order	   in	   which	   the	   questions	   were	   asked	   do	   not	   play	   a	  
significant	   role.	   That	   is	   why	   semi-­‐structured	   interviews	   were	   selected	   as	   the	  
appropriate	   research	   method,	   allowing	   the	   respondents	   to	   freely	   express	   their	  
minds	   and	   opening	   up	   opportunities	   for	   more	   sensible	   topics	   to	   be	   addressed.	  
Semi-­‐structured	  interviews	  can	  greatly	  benefit	  the	  interviewer	  on	  acquiring	  new	  
knowledge	  and	  since	  he	  doesn’t	  know	  the	  answers	  in	  advance	  it	  will	  allow	  him	  to	  
discover	  new	  topics	  and	  arguments	  concerning	  the	  subject.	  	  
	  
The	  author	  held	  individual	  and	  separate	  interviews	  with	  seven	  employees,	  and	  
their	   duration	   was	   approximately	   40	   minutes.	   The	   interviews	   were	   conducted	  
during	   the	   author’s	   last	   week	   at	   the	   case	   company	   in	   Lebanon,	   and	   all	   the

opinion	   on	   the	   current	   situation,	   which	   will	   allow	   him	   to	   explore	   unforeseen	  
subjects	  by	  asking	  more	  specific	  questions.	  The	  interview	  questionnaire	  can	  be	  
found	  in	  Appendix	  A.	  The	  questions	  that	  were	  drafted	  were	  not	  always	  asked	  to	  
all	  the	  interviewees,	  but	  rather	  served	  as	  guideline.	  Some	  topics	  were	  addressed

3.4. 	  Self-­‐Assessment	  	  
	  
Although	  the	  author	  chose	  to	  pursue	  a	  qualitative	  research	  for	  the	  thesis,	  it	  can	  be	  
useful	   to	   incorporate	   a	   quantitative	   analysis	   by	   using	   structured	   analytical	  
methods	  such	  questionnaires	  or	  surveys.	  Quantitative	  research	  can	  be	  described	  
as	  an	  inflexible	  process	  due	  to	  the	  nature	  of	  the	  closed-­‐ended	  questions,	  where	  all	  
the	  participants	  are	  asked	  the	  same	  questions;	  “The	  advantage	  of	  this	  inflexibility	  
is	   that	   it	   allows	   for	   meaningful	   comparison	   of	   responses	   across	   participants”	  
(Natasha	  Mack,	  A	  data	  collector’s	  field	  guide,	  p.	  3).	  	  
	  
As	  mentioned	  earlier,	  during	  his	  stay	  in	  Dubai,	  the	  author	  asked	  four	  managers	  
from	  different	  departments	  to	  answer	  a	  simple	  survey	  that	  will	  allow	  him	  to	  get	  a	  
better	   understanding	   of	   the	   company’s	   current	   strategic	   practices.	   BCG	   global	  
strategy	  experts	  Martin	  Reeves,	  Knut	  Haanæs,	  and	  Janmejaya	  Sinha	  published	  a	  
book	  “Your	  strategy	  needs	  a	  strategy”	  that	  “offers	  a	  practical	  guide	  to	  help	  you

survey	  in	  question	  was	  used	  by	  the	  author	  and	  can	  be	  found	  in	  Appendix	  B.	  	  
	  
3.5. 	  Credibility	  Of	  Findings	  	  
	  
As	   mentioned	   earlier,	   using	   interviews	   as	   a	   research	   method	   is	   a	   very	   flexible	  
method	  of	  generating	  data.	  By	  doing	  so	  a	  lot	  of	  factors	  come	  into	  play	  that	  could	  
disrupt	  the	  reliability	  and	  validity	  of	  the	  research	  and	  this	  is	  because	  the	  findings	  
and	   data	   collected	   come	   from	   individuals	   and	   their	   behavior	   can	   affect	   the	  
results.	  Elements	  like	  moods,	  misinterpreted	  questions,	  tension,	  stress,	  belief,	  etc.	  
can	  have	  notable	  repercussions,	  which	  is	  why	  when	  examining	  the	  validity	  of	  the	  
interviews	  these	  factors	  need	  to	  be	  taken	  into	  account.

When	  assessing	  the	  credibility	  of	  a	  research	  it	  is	  important	  not	  to	  confuse	  validity	  
and	   reliability.	   Reliability	   represents	   how	   consistent	   the	   findings	   would	   if	   the	  
research	   would	   be	   repeated	   under	   similar	   conditions	   and	   validity	   is	   used	   to	  
determine	  if	  the	  data	  collected	  matches	  the	  findings	  that	  were	  intended	  to	  be	  to	  
gathered.	   “Although	   you	   can	   never	   prove	   reliability	   or	   validity	   conclusively,	  
results	  will	  be	  more	  accurate	  if	  the	  measures	  in	  a	  study	  are	  as	  reliable	  and	  valid	  
as	   possible”	   (At	   Work,	   Issue	   84,	   Spring	   2016:	   Institute	   for	   Work	   &	   Health,	  
Toronto).	  
	  
3.5.1. Ensuring	  Validity	  	  
	  
When	  ensuring	  the	  validity	  of	  a	  research,	  the	  author	  needs	  to	  consider	  the	  face	  
validity	  and	  content	  validity	  of	  the	  drafted	  questionnaire.	  Face	  validity	  measures	  
if	  the	  questionnaire	  is	  constructed	  well	  to	  enough	  to	  achieve	  the	  desired	  results	  
and	  content	  validity	  measures	  if	  the	  questionnaire	  actually	  produced	  the	  desired	  
results.	  	  	  	  
	  
Before	   conducting	   the	   interviews,	   the	   author	   sent	   the	   respondents	   an	   email	  
containing	   four	   general	   questions	   that	   address	   the	   company’s	   strength	  
opportunities,	  threats	  and	  weaknesses,	  as	  seen	  in	  Appendix	  D.	  	  The	  author	  chose	  
to	  keep	  the	  questionnaire	  brief	  and	  to	  the	  point,	  so	  that	  he	  may	  follow	  up	  with	  
additional	  questions	  depending	  on	  the	  interviewee’s	  answer	  on	  these	  topics.	  	  The	  
questionnaire	  was	  kept	  short	  because	  the	  respondents	  did	  not	  have	  enough	  time	  
to	  partake	  in	  long	  interviews	  where	  they	  could	  discuss	  the	  mentioned	  elements	  in	  
detail,	   so	   the	   author	   only	   pursued	   further	   questions	   when	   he	   felt	   that	   more	  
information	  could	  be	  extracted.	  By	  selecting	  respondents	  that	  work	  in	  different	  
departments,	   the	   author	   was	   able	   to	   generate	   data	   on	   different	   aspects	   and	  
operations	   of	   the	   case	   company.	   This	   helps	   improve	   the	   research	   validity,	  
because	  even	  though	  some	  aspects	  were	  developed	  more	  than	  others,	  the	  overall	  
aim	  of	  the	  interviews	  was	  achieved.

3.5.2. Ensuring	  Reliability	  	  
	  
One	   aspect	   of	   reliability	   is	   where	   the	   researcher	   produces	   the	   same	  
questionnaire,	   under	   the	   same	   circumstances	   to	   see	   if	   he	   will	   obtain	   similar	  
results,	  this	  is	  called	  test-­‐retest	  reliability.	  Since	  the	  author	  is	  not	  able	  to	  perform	  
this	  test,	  he	  will	  look	  at	  the	  internal	  consistency	  of	  the	  questions	  that	  were	  asked.	  
By	   doing	   so,	   he	   will	   be	   able	   to	   find	   out	   if	   asking	   similar	   questions	   to	   different	  
people	  will	  produce	  similar	  findings.	  	  
	  
High	  reliability	  can	  easily	  be	  achieved	  when	  conducting	  a	  quantitative	  research,	  
but	   can	   be	   quite	   difficult	   for	   a	   qualitative	   research	   as	   the	   author	   presents	  
questions	  in	  a	  different	  way	  for	  each	  respondent,	  who	  in	  return	  answer	  them	  in	  
their	   own	   personal	   view.	   This	   can	   lead	   to	   unpredictable	   and	   inconsistent	  
answers,	   reducing	   the	   research’s	   reliability.	   As	   mentioned	   earlier,	   the	  
respondents	   work	   in	   different	   departments,	   so	   the	   author	   was	   bound	   to	   find	  
dissimilarities	   in	   their	   answers	   concerning	   the	   external	   environment,	   because	  
their	  scope	  of	  work	  focuses	  on	  different	  aspects	  of	  the	  business	  environment.	  On	  
the	  other	  hand,	  the	  author	  found	  that	  after	  interviewing	  six	  employees,	  they	  all	  
had	  similarities	  in	  their	  answers	  concerning	  the	  internal	  capabilities	  of	  the	  case	  
company,	  and	  so	  he	  deemed	  that	  reliability	  was	  reached,	  as	  interviewing	  more	  
employees	  would	  only	  bring	  about	  similar	  answers.	  In	  order	  to	  achieve	  reliability,	  
“The	   analysis	   should	   continue	   until	   theoretical	   saturation	   is	   achieved,	   that	   is,	  
when	  no	  new	  themes	  or	  issues	  arise	  regarding	  a	  category	  of	  data	  and	  when	  the	  
categories	   are	   well	   established	   and	   validated”	   (Simon	   M.K,	   2011,	   Validity	   and	  
reliability	  in	  qualitative	  studies).
