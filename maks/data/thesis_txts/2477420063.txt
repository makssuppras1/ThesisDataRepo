2 Abstract 
Snakebite envenoming is a devastating Neglected Tropical Disease that is estimated to kill up to 
125,000 people each year. Currently, and for the past 120 years, the only specific treatment for 
snakebite envenoming is animal-derived antivenoms, which have saved countless lives and 
continues to do so. However, the treatment does have some major drawbacks which include 
dangerous adverse reactions and a composition of up to 90% antibodies with no therapeutic effect 
against snake toxins. Considering these drawbacks, it is obvious that there is room for innovation 
and improvement in the area of snakebite therapeutics. 
The research carried out in this thesis is part of a larger research project aiming at developing 
antivenoms, against various venomous animals, based on human, recombinant antibodies. Human 
recombinant antivenom is a promising new alternative within snakebite therapeutics that offers the 
possibility of no adverse reactions due to the human origin of the antibodies. Furthermore it offers 
the possibility of ensuring that all constituent antibodies have been proven to recognize and 
neutralize snake venom toxins. In this thesis, phage display technology was employed to discover 
human scFvs able to neutralize myotoxin II from the notorious B. asper. These scFvs were discovered 
using a novel phage display selection pH-mediated elution methodology, which should result in 
scFvs binding myotoxin II at pH 7.4 and dissociating from myotoxin II at pH < 6. Once converted to 
the IgG format, such antibodies should have the ability to undergo FcRn-mediated recycling in the 
lysosomal degradation pathway. The IgG recycling could result in lower doses of antivenom needed 
in cases of snakebite envenoming.  
The scFv-displaying phages discovered with the pH-dependent phage display selection had their 
scFv insert subcloned into an expression vector which was then transformed into BL21(DE3) 
competent E. coli cells. Then, two different ELISAs were employed to eliminate scFvs with binding 
abilities under a certain threshold and scFvs with unspecific binding. The remaining scFvs were 
sequenced, and scFvs with duplicate sequences in all six CDR regions were deleted. To further test 
the binding abilities, two of the unique scFvs were selected for expression and purification. A cell 
viability assay, in which human keratinocytes were subjected to myotoxin II, was used to test if the 
scFvs were able to neutralize the toxic effects of myotoxin II in vitro. The best neutralizing scFv of 
the two, was able to neutralize almost all toxic abilities of myotoxin II at the lowest tested scFv 
concentration (1:1 toxin:scFv molar ratio). Thereby, the work presented in this thesis shows the 
successful discovery of human recombinant scFvs able to neutralize myotoxin II. In the future, the 
most promising of the discovered scFvs should be converted to Fab format to use in SPR affinity 
assays and fully human IgGs to be tested in vivo. 
The pH-dependency of the scFvs was in this thesis briefly tested through a modified ELISA, however 
no conclusions could be made on these results. In the future, the converted Fab formats will also be 
tested for pH-dependency using SPR. The results from the SPR will show if the novel phage display 
selection pH-mediated elution methodology actually selects for pH-dependent scFvs.

Slangebids forgiftning er en forfærdelig negligeret tropesygdom som er estimeret til at dræbe op 
mod 125.000 mennesker hvert år. Den eneste specifikke behandling mod denne forgiftning er 
modgift af animalsk oprindelse og har været det de sidste 120 år hvori den har reddet utallige liv. 
Dog er modgift af animalsk oprindelse kendt for at have nogle store ulemper, som inkluderer farlige 
bivirkninger og, at den kan bestå af op mod 90% antistoffer, som ikke har nogen terapeutisk effekt 
mod slangegifts toksiner. Hvis disse ulemper tages til eftertragtning, er det åbenlyst at der er plads 
til innovation og forbedring indenfor terapeutiske produkter mod slangebids forgiftning. 
Forskningen som er udrettet i dette speciale, er en del af et større forskningsprojekt, hvis mål er at 
udvikle modgifte, som er baseret på humane antistoffer, mod forskellige giftige dyr. Denne form for 
modgift er et lovende nyt alternativ indenfor slangebids terapi, som tilbyder nye muligheder der 
inkluderer: i) et potentiale for ingen farlige bivirkninger, da antistofferne er af human oprindelse, ii) 
en forsikring om at all de indeholdende antistoffer har beviste neutraliserende effekter på 
slangegifts toksiner. I dette speciale anvendtes fag display teknologi til at opdage humane scFver 
som er i stand til at neutralisere myotoxin II fra den berygtede B. asper. Disse humane scFver blev 
opdaget ved brug af en ny fag display selektion pH-medieret eluerings metode, som burde resultere 
i scFver som binder myotoxin II ved pH 7,4 og dissocierer fra myotoxin II ved pH < 6. Efter 
konvertering til IgG format burde disse antistoffer besidde evnen til at gennemgå FcRn-medieret 
genanvendelse via den lysosomale degraderings pathway. Denne igG genanvendelse kunne 
resultere i lavere nødvendige modgiftsmængder i tilfælde af slangebids forgiftning. 
De scFv-visende fager, der blev opdaget via den fag display selektion pH-medierede metode fik 
deres scFv gen subklonet ind i en ekspressions vektor, som så blev transformeret ind i BL21(DE3) 
kompetente E. coli celler. Derefter blev to forskellige ELISAer udført for at eliminere scFver med 
bindings evner under en bestemt grænseværdi og scFver med uspecifik binding. scFverne blev 
derefter sekventeret og scFv sekvenser der var duplikater i alle seks CDR-regioner blev slettet. For 
at facilitere yderligere tests blev to scFver valgt til at blive udtrykt og oprenset. Et celle overlevelses 
assay, hvori humane keratinocytter blev udsat for myotoxin II, blev brugt til at teste scFverne for 
evnen til at neutralisere de toksiske effekter forårsaget af myotoxin II in vitro. Den mest effektivt 
neutraliserende af de to scFver, var i stand til at neutralisere alle de toksiske effekter af myotoxin II 
ved den lavest testede scFv koncentration (1:1 scFv:toxin molar ratio). Derved har arbejdet udført i 
forbindelse med dette speciale succesfuldt opdaget humane scFver i stand til at neutralisere 
myotoxin II. I fremtiden skal de mest lovende opdagede scFver konverteres til Fab format til brug i 
SPR affinitets assays og til fuldt humant IgG format til at teste in vivo. 
Hvorvidt scFverne var pH-afhængige blev testet igennem et modificeret ELISA, dog kunne der ikke 
konkluderes noget på baggrund af dette. Efter konvertering af Fab formater vil disse også blive brugt 
til test af pH-afhængighed. På baggrund af disse resultater vil der konkluderes på hvorvidt den nye 
fag display selektion pH medierede eluerings metode faktisk selekterer for pH-afhængige scFver.

5 Introduction 
5.1 Epidemiology of snakebite 
Snakebite envenoming is a devasting Neglected Tropical Disease affecting hundreds of thousands 
of people each year1. Snakebite envenomings often have lethal outcomes and even in cases of 
survival, there is a high risk of long-term debilitating sequalae. This includes amputation, 
disfigurement, blindness, photosensitivity, mental disorders, and more2–5. The disease 
disproportionally affects people in poor and rural communities in tropical or subtropical regions of 
the world6. Snakebite envenoming spirals these poor people further into poverty through high 
treatment costs, enforced borrowing and loss of income7. 
When trying to track down an estimation on the annual amount of snakebite envenomings and 
deaths, it is observed that there is some variation between different studies. One study estimates 
over 2.5 million snakebite envenomings and over 125,000 deaths each year8 (Figure 1), whereas 
another study estimates total envenomings as being minimum 400,000, but possibly up to 1.8 
million with minimum 20,000 and possibly up to 94,000 deaths each year9. These numbers are, 
however, believed to be grossly underestimated due to a lack of robust record keeping in the most 
affected areas, and the complication of many victims not presenting to hospital due to logistical or 
cultural reasons10,11.

5.2 Treatment of snakebite envenoming 
5.2.1 Traditional antivenom 
The specific treatment for snakebite envenomings is animal-derived antivenoms consisting of 
antibodies of immunoglobulin G (IgG), F(ab’)2, or Fab formats, which are administered 
intravenously12,13. This has been the case for over 120 years, since the first antivenom came into 
clinical use in 189514. Snake antivenom is produced by the immunization of larger mammals (often 
horses or sheep15) with venom obtained from one or several species of snakes (Figure 2), resulting 
in either monovalent or polyvalent antivenom, respectively. Once immunized, blood is drawn from 
the animal and the antibody-containing plasma is separated from the erythrocytes, which are then 
delivered back to the animal. Lastly, the antibodies are purified from the plasma, concentrated, and 
formulated16,17.  
Animal-derived antivenoms are, to this date, the only specific treatment for snakebite envenomings 
and they have saved countless lives18. However, the treatment is often associated with major 
drawbacks such as early or late adverse reactions which can be severe and in rare cases fatal19,20. 
The adverse reactions of antivenoms can occur due to the heterologous nature of the antivenom21, 
or due to impurities in the antivenom if it is of questionable quality22. Another complication with 
traditional antivenom is, that it can be composed of a majority of antibodies with no therapeutic 
effect against snake toxins, but instead antibodies that are specific for other antigens the immunized 
animal has encountered during its life23. Antibodies with no therapeutic effect against snake toxins 
can amount to as much as 90% of the antivenom18,24,25. 
 
Figure 2. Traditional antivenom development. A) Venom is obtained from one or more venomous snakes. B) The venom is then used 
to immunize a large production animal (often a horse or sheep). C) After immunization is completed, blood is collected from the

5.2.2 Human recombinant antivenom  
Considering the drawbacks of the traditional antivenom it is obvious that there is room for 
innovation and improvement. Indeed several innovative snakebite therapeutics are currently being 
researched, including: small molecule inhibitors, oligonucleotides, various antibody formats, and 
novel immunization and production methods26,27. Another novel snakebite therapeutic currently 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
being researched is human recombinant antivenom, which is the focus of this thesis (Figure 3). The 
main benefit of this novel treatment is that it does not have the drawbacks of the traditional 
antivenom. First, the human recombinant antivenom is based on human recombinant antibodies, 
hereby being more compatible with the human immune system compared to animal-derived 
antivenoms28. Second, when developing a human recombinant antivenom, the specificity of the 
components can be specifically selected by the developer. This means that the human recombinant 
antivenom can be composed mainly of antibodies with neutralizing abilities to the most medically 
relevant toxins, resulting in almost 100% of the content being therapeutically relevant29. Lastly, the 
antibodies can be selected to have specific properties such as pH-dependency for increased half-life 
and neutralization effect, or cross-specificity to multiple toxins (called cross-reactivity). This novel 
treatment has already been estimated as being cost-effective compared to traditional antivenom30. 
In addition, in 2018 an experimental recombinant antivenom containing antibodies raised against 
dendrotoxins from the black mamba, Dendroaspis polylepis, was demonstrated to neutralize these 
toxins in vivo31, thereby constituting the first published example of a human recombinant 
antivenom.

5.3 Bothrops asper 
B. asper is a notorious snake that inhabits Central America and northern South America32. In these 
regions, it is responsible for 50% - 80% of the total snakebite envenomings, and 60% - 90% of the 
snakebite envenoming related deaths33. B. asper presents a high average venom yield of 130.5 mg 
and can yield as much as 1000 mg according to one study, but an older study describes the average 
yield as 458 mg with a maximum venom yield of 1530 mg34.  The venom of B. asper contains a wide 
range of proteins (toxins) belonging to at least 8 different families: disintegrins, L-amino acid 
oxidases, serine proteases, cysteine-rich secretory proteins, DC fragments, C-type lectin-like 
proteins, snake venom metalloproteases (SVMPs), and phospholipases A2 (PLA2s)32. Most of the B. 
asper venom is composed of SVMPs and PLA2s (Figure 4), which are also the main culprits regarding 
the clinical manifestations that presents in a B. asper envenoming. The SVMPs are responsible for 
severe coagulopathy and bleeding in patients, which are the most lethal effects of the venom. The 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
PLA2s are mostly related to myotoxic activities, which involve dermonecrosis and myonecrosis and 
are thereby responsible for the majority of the debilitating sequelae (Figure 4)34.

5.4 Myotoxin II 
The focus in this thesis is to discover human single-chain variable fragments (scFvs) able to 
neutralize the 15.229 kDa Lys-49 myotoxic PLA2 from B. asper, called myotoxin II, using phage 
display selection36. The myotoxic PLA2s from B. asper are divided in two subcategories: the Lys-49 
PLA2 myotoxins and the Asp-49 PLA2 myotoxins. Asp-49 PLA2 myotoxins can be further divided into 
an acidic and a basic variant, whereas the Lys-49 PLA2 myotoxins are all basic37. The Asp-49 PLA2 
myotoxins are catalytically active and catalyzes the hydrolysis of the ester bond in the sn-2 position 
of glycerophospholipids whereas the Lys-49 PLA2 myotoxins are devoid (almost) of catalytic 
activity36,38. However, despite their low catalytic activity, the basic PLA2s have acquired the highest 
toxicity compared to the acidic isoform39. The exact mechanism of action for the non-catalytic Lys-49 
PLA2 myotoxins is poorly characterized40. However, it appears to be the ability of a “toxic-site” to 
alter the membrane permeability that is the critical event that leads to downstream degenerative 
processes which rapidly brings the cell to a point of irreversible damage, culminating in death via 
necrosis40. The basic Asp-49 PLA2 myotoxins and Lys-49 PLA2 myotoxins have been shown to have a 
synergistic effect where minute amounts of basic Asp-49 PLA2 are able to enhance the myotoxic 
effects of the Lys-49 PLA2. This effect was shown not to be present when testing if minute amounts 
of Lys-49 PLA2 would potentiate the myotoxic effects of the basic Asp-49 PLA2. Therefore Lys-49 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
PLA2s are obvious targets for neutralization as a decrease in these will lower the synergistic effects 
more than a decrease in a basic Asp-49 PLA2 (unless all of these could be neutralized)41.  
5.5 Phage display technology 
The following is a brief overview of phage display selection based on Ledsgaard et al, 2018,28 and 
Laustsen et al.,2017,42. This overview is biased towards the specific library and methods that were 
used in this thesis. For a thorough unbiased explanation on how phage libraries are made and can 
be used, see the before mentioned articles.  
Phage display selection is a high throughput method used to discover antibodies specific to different 
antigens. In this technique bacteriophages have been genetically engineered to express a myriad of 
different human scFvs on their coat protein. This is referred to as a phage library. A commonly used 
phage for these libraries is the M13 bacteriophage. The M13 phage uses the coat protein, G3P, to 
infect an E. coli cell. Upon successful infection, the phage utilizes E. coli proteins to replicate its 
genome and express its genes. This results in the assembly of new phages which exits the cell in a 
non-lytic manner, ready to repeat this cycle. 
M13 phage libraries can be constructed in two ways, either as a phage library, or as a phagemid 
library. In the phage library, the scFv genes are linked directly to the gene encoding the G3P coat 
protein. Since the M13 phage displays 3-5 copies of this protein, it results in polyvalent display of 
the scFv. To avoid polyvalent display, a phagemid library can be constructed instead. The library 
used in this thesis uses phagemids, which are vectors encoding phage genes that cannot replicate 
on their own. A phagemid vector typically contain three key elements: (i) an antibiotic marker for 
selection and propagation of the vector, (ii) the gene encoding a scFv-G3P fusion protein, and (iii) a 
M13 origin of replication. The phagemid library is dependent on coinfection with helper phages that 
carries all other phage genes, a different antibiotic marker, and an ineffective origin of replication. 
When E. coli cells are co-infected with phagemids and helper phages, mainly the phagemids are 
replicated due to the ineffective origin of replication in the helper phage, but proteins from both 
phagemids and helper phages are expressed. This results in the G3P-scFv fusion protein competing 
with the cognate G3P protein from the helper phages for incorporation into the new generation of 
phages. This typically results in monovalent or no-display of the G3P-scFv fusion protein.  
In this thesis, the IONTAS phage library λ was used. This library contains phages that display 1.6∙1010 
different human scFv sequences. Using this library for high throughput antibody discovery is a 
five-step process (Figure 5). (A) First an antigen of interest is immobilized on a surface, using a 
biotin-streptavidin capture system, and then the phage library is presented to it. (B) This allows the 
phages displaying scFvs with specificity to the antigen to bind to it. (C) Then the mixture is washed, 
which removes the phages displaying scFvs unable to bind the antigen. (D) After washing, the bound 
phages are eluted using either trypsin or a pH change. (E) Subsequently, the eluted phages are used 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
to infect E. coli cells and with the assistance of added helper phages they can here be amplified. This 
five-step process is often repeated 2 more times to accumulate phages displaying high affinity scFvs.   
 
Figure 5. Phage display selection. A) The phage library is presented to the immobilized antigen of interest. B) Phages displaying scFvs 
that are able to bind the antigens bind to these. C) The phages with scFvs not able to bind the antigen are washed away. D) the bound 
phages are eluted from the antigens. E) Phages are used to infect TG1 E. coli cells and are herein amplified, facilitated by helper 
phages. The amplified phages are then used to repeat the binding step. Usually three “rounds” of phage display selection are applied. 
The figure is created by Christoffer Vinther Sørensen. 
5.6 Subcloning  
While the pIONTAS1 phagemid vector is highly useful for phage display selections, it is not suitable 
for protein expression. Therefore, the phage display selected scFv genes were subcloned into a 
dedicated scFv expression vector called pSANG10-3F (Figure 6). The vector pSANG10-3F carries a 
high level expression T7lac promoter that drives the scFv expression into the periplasmic space via 
a pelB leader sequence, that is also present in the vector43. The vector furthermore contains: a 
6-histidine tag; a tri-flag tag; a Lac I gene, a kanamycin resistance gene, and E. coli and M13 phage 
origins of replication. The gene expression is governed by the T7 promoter which is only recognized 
by the T7 polymerase. This polymerase is not present in wild-type E. coli. However, it is present in 
the genetically engineered strain BL21(DE3). The BL21(DE3) strain carries a chromosomal copy of 
the T7 polymerase, which is governed by the lacUV5 promoter. When induced, which can be 
accomplished by using a simple autoinduction medium, the T7 polymerase results in expression of 
genes driven from the T7 promoter.

5.7 Antibodies with pH-dependent binding 
The difference between conventional antibodies and pH-dependent antibodies is that while both 
readily binds their target antigens at physiological pH (7.4), the pH-dependent antibody-antigen 
complex dissociates upon exposure to acidic pH (<6.0). Hence, when a pH-dependent 
antibody-antigen complex is internalized into the early endosome it dissociates44 (Figure 7). This 
allows the now antigen-free antibody to be recycled back to the cell surface by the neonatal Fc 
receptor (FcRn), while the dissociated antigen is trafficked to the lysosome and degraded45. This 
leaves the recycled pH-dependent antibody ready to bind another antigen and repeat the cycle, 
thus prolonging the plasma half-life and increasing the number of antigens that can be cleared by 
each individual antibody46–48.  
5.7.1 Antibodies with pH-dependent binding in snakebite antivenom 
Antivenoms composed of antibodies with the pH-dependent binding ability, could theoretically 
reduce the cost of treatment for victims of snakebite envenomings. The explanation behind this 
theory is, that with the increased number of antigens cleared by each antibody46, the amount of 
antivenom needed per snakebite envenoming should be reduced. This increased antigen clearance 
introduces no immediate increased antibody development cost, hence the overall price per 
snakebite envenoming should be reduced. This is especially relevant for snakes that injects larger 
volumes of venom such as the B. asper.

6 Project aim 
The research presented in this thesis is part of a much larger research project aiming to develop 
human recombinant antivenom against different venomous animals to accommodate the 
drawbacks of traditional antivenom. The objective of this thesis is to discover pH-dependent human 
scFvs against myotoxin II from B. asper. These scFvs should, with the pH-dependent function, be 
able to bind their target antigen at neutral pH and dissociate from the antigen when presented to 
an acidic environment, thereby making them recyclable.  
The plan is to discover these scFvs using a novel phage display selection pH-mediated elution 
methodology and validating the phage display results using a plate test. Upon successful validation 
of the phage display output, the scFv genes should be subcloned into an expression vector and 
transformed into genetically engineered E. coli cells. Several scFvs are then to be tested through a 
monoclonal ELISA, and all binders above a preset threshold will be picked for continuation. These 
are then carried on to a new ELISA which will test the specificity of the scFvs and also test the 
pH-dependency of the scFvs. The next step will then be to sequence the scFv inserts in these 
monoclonal cultures and evaluate the results to remove scFvs that are identical in all six 
Complementarity Determining Regions (CDR) regions. The remaining monoclonal cultures should 
undergo an Expression Normalized Capture (ENC) ELISA to quantify which monoclonal cultures 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
produce the best binding scFvs. After this, the monoclonal cultures producing the best binding scFvs 
should be used for scFv expression and purification. These purified scFvs are then to be tested in a 
pull-down assay and a novel cell viability assay using immortalized human keratinocytes. See Figure 
8 for a graphical overview of the planned steps in this project. 
It is important to mention that the research carried out in this thesis also includes a side project not 
presented in the thesis but enclosed as a scientific manuscript. 
 
Figure 8. Project plan overview. The plan is first to biotinylate myotoxin II (1), then the biotinylated myotoxin II is used in a novel 
phage display selection pH-mediated elution methodology (2), to discover phages displaying pH-dependent scFvs able to bind to 
myotoxin II. The results from the phage display selection is validated through a plate test (3) as an initial assessment of specificity. 
Then the scFv genes are subcloned (4) from the pIONTAS1 vector into the pSANG10-3F expression vector, which is then transformed 
into BL21(DE3) competent E. coli cells. The scFvs produced by the BL21(DE3) competent E. coli cells are then tested through different 
ELISAs for pH-dependency (5a) and for binding abilities and binding specificity (5b). The cells passing the set parameters for the ELISA 
continues to sequencing (6) and sequences that are duplicates in all six CDR regions are deleted. The scFvs are then tested in an ENC 
ELISA (7), to check for binding abilities independent of expression level, and the most promising scFvs are expressed (8) and purified 
(9). Lastly these are tested in a pull-down assay, to test if the purified scFvs can pull down myotoxin II from whole B. asper venom 
(10a) and in a cell viability assay to test if the scFvs are able to neutralize the toxic effects of myotoxin II in vitro.

7.2 Methods 
7.2.1 Biotinylation of myotoxin II 
Myotoxin II was biotinylated by mixing it with PEG4-conjugated biotin (EZ-LinkTM NHS-PEG4-Biotin, 
Thermo Scientific, #A39259) according to manufacturer’s protocol in a 1:1.5 molar ratio 
(toxin:biotin)  and this was incubated for 30 minutes at room temperature. For removing hydrolyzed 
Biotin, the biotin/myotoxin II mixture was added to Vivacon 500 Sartorius columns (Sartorius, 
85034-538-74) and centrifuged at 14,000 × g for 30 minutes, eluents were discarded. 400 µL PBS 
was added to the columns, and they were again centrifuged at 14,000 x g for 30 minutes and eluents 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
were discarded (this step was repeated twice). Biotinylated toxins were recovered by inverting the 
columns and 100 µL of PBS was added. The columns were centrifuged at 14,000 x g for 15 minutes 
(this step was repeated with 50 µL PBS). The concentration of the eluent from the last step, 
containing the purified biotinylated myotoxin II, was measured using a NanoDrop 
spectrophotometer and stored at -20 °C. 
7.2.2 Phage display selection 
Day 1: A preculture was prepared by inoculating 5 mL of 2xYT medium in a 50 mL Falcon tube with 
a colony from a minimal media plate previously streaked with TG1 E. coli cells. This was incubated 
overnight (O/N) at 30 °C with 280 rpm shaking. For each selection one Maxisorp vial (Nunc Maxisorp, 
Nunc Cat #444202) was coated with 250 µL of 10 µg/mL streptavidin (Thermo Scientific, #21125) in 
PBS, covered with parafilm and stored at 4 °C O/N. 
Day 2: The streptavidin solution was removed by pipetting, and the vials were washed thrice with 
PBS to remove unbound streptavidin. Then the vials were coated with 250 µL of 10 µg/mL 
biotinylated antigen in PBS supplemented with 3% (w/v) dried milk (PanReac AppliChem, A0830) 
(hereon referred to as 3% MPBS) and incubated at room temperature for one hour. For each 
selection 25 µL of Dynabeads (Dynabeads, M280 Streptavidin, Thermo Fisher #11205D) was cleared 
from its solution using a magnetic rack and was washed twice with 50 µL of 3% MPBS. The 3% MPBS 
was removed, and blocking was carried out by adding 100 µL of 3% MPBS followed by mixing with 
end-over-end rotation for one hour. Meanwhile, for each selection 50 µL of IONTAS phage library λ 
(Donated by IONTAS) was mixed with 150 µL 3% MPBS and blocked by one-hour incubation with 
end-over-end rotation. Simultaneously, the antigen solution was pipetted out of the O/N coated 
vials, and the vials were washed once with PBS supplemented with 0.2% tween (hereon referred to 
as PBS-T) and thrice with PBS, then the vials were blocked by filling them with 3% MPBS and 
incubated for one hour at room temperature. After one hour of blocking, the Dynabeads, the 3% 
MPBS was removed using a magnetic rack, and the Dynabeads were resuspended in 50 µL 3% MPBS. 
For each 200 µL blocked phage library, 50 µL of resuspended blocked Dynabeads was added and the 
mixture was incubated for one hour at room temperature with end-over-end rotation to deselect 
streptavidin binding phages. Just before this incubation time was finished, the 3% MPBS was 
removed from the vials and they were washed thrice with PBS. Then the deselected phages were 
separated from the Dynabeads using a magnetic rack, and 250 µL of the Dynabeads was added to 
each vial and incubated for one hour with end-over-end rotation. The phage solution was removed 
from the vials with a pipette, and the vials were washed six times with PBS-T followed by six times 
washing with PBS, then 250 µL of phage elution buffer containing 100 µL/mL of trypsin was added 
and incubated for 15 minutes at room temperature. These steps were timed with simultaneous 
growing of TG1 E. coli cells. This growing was carried out by inoculating the pre-culture from Day 1 
in preheated 2xTY media in an Erlenmeyer flask to a final OD600 of 0.05 and was incubated at 37 °C 
with 280 rpm shaking. This culture was then timed to grow to an OD600 of 0.5 (exponential growth 
phase) at the same time the phage solution had incubated in the vials for 15 minutes. For each

antigen the phage solution was pipetted up and down five times and transferred to 5 mL of TG1 
E.  coli culture in exponential growth phase in a 50 mL Falcon tube. This culture was incubated one 
hour at 37 °C with 150 rpm shaking. During this incubation time, one 2xTY-GA and one 2xTY-GK plate 
was plated with 100 µL of the non-infected TG1 E. coli culture as control and incubated. Then upon 
finishing incubation time 20 µL and 100 µL of each TG1 E. coli culture was plated on 2xTY-GA plates 
(to quantify the colony forming units or CFU). The culture was then centrifuged at 4,400 x g for 5 
minutes and most of the supernatant discarded except for approximately 100 µL that was used to 
resuspend the pellet, which was then plated on a 2xTY-GA plate (named output plate). All plates 
were then incubated O/N at 30 °C and 37 °C for 2xTY-GA and 2xTY-GK plates, respectively. 
Day 3: The cells in each O/N output plate were resuspended using 2 mL of freezing medium and 
added to cryogenic vials, then they were mixed for 10 minutes using end-over-end rotation before 
the OD600 was measured.  Before storing the cells at -80 °C in cryogenic vials, they were used to 
inoculate 5 mL of 2xYT-AG medium in Falcon tubes to an OD600 of 0.05. The Falcon tubes were

and the 250 µL blocked phage solution was added to the respective vial and incubated for one hour

7.2.4 Subcloning 
PCR amplification of scFv DNA insert  
The TG1 E. coli stock from the 3rd round, containing the pH-eluted phages, was thawed and a dilution 
of 1:20 was performed in nuclease free water (Invitrogen, 10977-035). Next, 2 µL of the diluted 
bacteria was transferred to a PCR tube and used in a PCR mix. The PCR mix contained: 42 µL nuclease 
free water, 50 µL Platinum SuperFi Green PCR MM 2x (Invitrogen, #12359), 3 µL of 10 µM 
M13LeadSeq primer (sequence: 5´-AAATTATTATTCGCAATTCCTTTGGTTGTTCCT-3’), 3 µL of 10 µM 
NotMycSeq primer (sequence: 5´-GGCCCCATTCAGATCCTCTTCTGAGATGAG-3’). The PCR reaction 
was carried out using the following program: 98 °C for 30 seconds; 25 cycles of the following, 98 °C 
for 10 seconds, 58 °C for 10 seconds, 72 °C for 15 seconds; 72 °C for 3 minutes, 4 °C indefinitely. To 
check if the PCR fragments presented the expected size of approximately 800 bp, a sample was 
mixed with 6x gel loading dye (BioLabs, B7024S) and loaded onto a 1% agarose gel containing 10 µL 
SYBR Safe DNA gel stain (Thermo Fisher Scientific, S33102) per 100 mL of gel. Generuler 1 kb Plus 
DNA ladder (Thermo Scientific™, SM1331) was used and the gel was run in TAE buffer at 100 V for 
20 minutes. The scFv PCR products were purified using a GeneJET PCR purification kit (Thermo 
Scientific, #K0701) according to the manufacturer’s protocol, except that the elution was carried 
out using 30 µL 50-60 °C nuclease free water. The DNA concentration of the purified scFv PCR 
product was measured using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer.

a GeneJET PCR purification kit according to the manufacturers protocol, except that the elution was 
carried out using 50 µL 50-60 °C nuclease free water. The DNA concentration of the purified scFv

7.2.9 Purification of scFvs  
For purification of scFvs, HisTrap FF 1 mL columns (GE Life Sciences, 17531901) were used. These 
were loaded with the collected supernatant from the expression of scFvs (one column per scFv 
culture). Prior to loading the supernatant, the columns were equilibrated by running 10 mL 
deaerated wash buffer through them using Gilson MiniPuls 3 peristaltic pump system, with a speed 
of 2 mL/min. After equilibrating, the loading of the columns was carried out using the same pump 
with the same settings. Simultaneously, the ÄKTA prime plus system was prepared by adding 
deaerated wash buffer (buffer A) and deaerated elution buffer (buffer B) to the system. Equilibration 
of the ÄKTA prime plus system was carried out using the system-wash application. Following this a 
manual run with 1 ml/min, buffer B = 0% and buffer A = 100%, was carried out for 5 minutes. Then, 
a HisTrap FF 1 mL column, loaded with the scFvs, was added to the ÄKTA prime plus system. When 
the absorbance was constant, the system was autozeroed. The fractionation was initiated by 
changing the fraction size to 1 mL and scFvs were eluted by changing the buffer B concentration to 
10%, and then gradually increasing. After the elution was completed, the fractions were collected 
based on chromatography results.

8 Results and Discussion 
8.1 Phage display selection 
Phage display selection was carried out using the IONTAS λ phage library to discover scFvs that bind 
to biotinylated myotoxin II, which has been immobilized using a streptavidin-biotin capture system. 
Before initiating the phage display selections, the phage library was deselected against streptavidin 
and biotin using streptavidin-coated Dynabeads and 3% MPBS respectively. When initializing phage 
display selection, the phage library was first panned against the immobilized myotoxin II, then the 
unbound phages were washed away. Thereafter, the bound phages were eluted using trypsin or a 
pH change and used to infect TG1 E. coli cells in which they were amplified. Three rounds of phage 
display selection were conducted according to these procedures, with the purpose of amplifying the 
high affinity binders in the selection output from round to round. In the first two rounds trypsin was 
used for elution, however, in the last round the phages were eluted by a pH change to pH 6.0 instead 
of using trypsin. 
The phage display selections resulted in a small number of CFUs in round one (Figure 9), a much 
larger amount of CFUs in round two, and then fewer again following the third round of selections. 
Whilst, findings from the first two rounds matched results from previous phage display experiments, 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
round three usually results in a higher number of CFUs than round two, which was not the case in 
this experiment. However, this discrepancy from the expected results can be explained by the 
pH-elution in the third round. This change in methodology should inevitably lower the expected 
number of CFUs as the third-round phage binders are exposed to an extra selection criterion, which 
is that they are able to dissociate from myotoxin II at pH 6. 
Subconclusion 
The phage display selection was concluded to be successful as round one and two followed 
expectations. The pH-elution used in round three was a novel methodology change which added a 
new selection criterion on the third-round phage binders, and it was therefore expected that the 
number of CFU would be lower which is what was observed. Therefore, this was also concluded to 
be successful.

Subconclusion

B. asper venom were inconclusive. The reason for this can be observed in lane 15 in Figure 18

of different sizes and that myotoxin II presented a band other than myotoxin II. An explanation was 
not found for the first, however, for the latter the most plausible explanation is a contamination in

was used together with the CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability assay. This assay measures the 
amount of ATP, which is an indicator of metabolically active cells.  
First the toxicity of myotoxin II to the cell line was confirmed, and an IC50 value of 23.66 µg/mL was 
experimentally determined by Prof. Manuela Pucca. The cell viability experiment was then carried 
out by testing the neutralization of two IC50s of myotoxin II by three different concentrations of 
scFvs. Unfortunately, the two IC50s of myotoxin II failed to kill a satisfying amount of the cells. This 
was tested twice (data not shown). However, after increasing the myotoxin II amount to three IC50s, 
the amount of cell death increased to an accepted 81%, and scFv neutralization could be evaluated 
(Figure 19). From the figure it can be observed that both B12 and E2 scFvs are able to neutralize the 
toxic effects of myotoxin II, although with differing effectiveness. The B12 scFvs increased the cell 
viability to 64% at a molar ratio of 1:1 (toxin:scFv), and at a molar ratio of 1:3, the cell viability 
increased to 90% which was the same value as for the control (1:3 scFv concentration without toxin). 
The E2 scFvs increased the cell viability to 95% at a molar ratio of 1:1 (toxin:scFv), which was 
approximately the same value as the control (1:3 scFv concentration but without toxin). These 
results thereby suggest that the E2 scFvs are the most effective at neutralizing myotoxin II.

Figure 19. Cell viability assay. Numbers e.g. 1:1 refers to the toxin:scFv molar ratio. Experiments were performed in triplicates and

Since this was a novel experiment, it is yet unknown if the in vitro neutralizing effects translates into 
in vivo neutralizing effects, and this will have to be subsequently tested. 
Subconclusion 
The results from the cell viability assay allow us to conclude that both scFvs were able to neutralize 
the toxic effects of myotoxin II on immortalized human keratinocytes, with the E2 scFvs being the 
best. However, it is still unknown how this novel in vitro assay translates to in vivo models, and this 
should be subsequently tested to verify that the method correctly depicts the neutralizing abilities 
of scFvs on myotoxin II.  
9 Future perspectives 
Before the project moves further in the antibody discovery pipeline, it is recommended to test the 
123 unique scFvs using the now functional ENC ELISA. Based on the ENC ELISA, the most promising 
scFvs displaying the strongest binding capacity, with normalized expression, should be chosen as 
leads for further investigation. After this, the next step will be to prepare the most promising scFvs 
for SPR affinity assays and in vivo testing, which will require conversion from the scFv format to the 
formats Fab and fully human IgGs, respectively.  
In the future, to validate or disprove the ability of the novel phage display selection pH-mediated 
elution methodology to select for pH-dependent scFvs, the converted Fab fragments should also be 
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
used to test pH-dependency using SPR. Upon successful validation of pH-dependency in either the 
scFvs from this thesis, or other scFvs from our group, they could be utilized to setup an ELISA 
pH-dependency screening. This would save valuable time and resources by allowing the 
pH-dependency to be validated earlier in the antibody discovery pipeline. 
10 Innovative and commercial potential 
The scFvs discovered in this thesis are, to the best of our knowledge, the first discovered human 
recombinant scFvs able to neutralize myotoxin II from B. asper. The technique and the idea behind 
the discovery of these scFvs will be repeated for other toxins from B. asper venom, but also for other 
venomous snakes and animals. To prevent external commercial exploitation of our findings we are 
currently weighing our options for the protection of our intellectual property. However, as the here 
described antibody development techniques are only novel in the context of antivenom, it is unlikely 
that the process will be patentable. Nonetheless, the sequences of discovered and validated scFvs, 
are likely patentable, though the economic benefit of such endeavor is questionable at this stage. 
Currently, the discovered scFv sequences only target one toxin, yet many more are needed for an 
antivenom against whole B. asper venom. Considering the cost of patent application and the largely 
low profit market of antivenoms, patenting might not be the optimal approach at this stage. 
However, certain measures might be required to retain the freedom to keep operating in this field. 
An alternative and free approach to patenting would be to keep the sequences of interest a secret. 
Thereby, if someone else was to try to replicate the work presented in this thesis, or from the 
research group, they would have to establish their own antibody discovery platform; however, this 
would be a laborious and expensive process. Whilst the above-mentioned options are the two most 
obvious avenues towards the protection of intellectual property presented here, the determination 
of the “optimal approach” will require further assessment. 
11 Concluding remarks 
The work presented in this thesis has impacted the research of Tropical Pharmacology Lab in 
different ways. First, by completing all the steps currently set up in the antibody discovery pipeline 
(except for ENC ELISA) the work has been part of both validating and troubleshooting this discovery 
process. Second, the work in the thesis has introduced and tested several novel techniques and 
protocol changes such as a novel phage display selection pH-mediated elution methodology, test of 
pH-dependency through ELISA, and a cell viability assay using human keratinocytes. For a graphic 
overview of all steps carried out in this thesis see Figure 20. 
==PAGE:43==
42 
 
 
Figure 20. Project overview of successful and unsuccessful experiments. The figure is identical to Figure 8 except the additions 
regarding experimental success or failure. Notice that step 7 is skipped due to the non-functional ENC ELISA at the time.  
The figure is created by Christoffer Vinther Sørensen, inspired by work done by Jonas Jürgensen. 
The work commenced with biotinylation of myotoxin II, which was then used in phage display 
selection to discover scFvs binding to myotoxin II. The selection successfully discovered phages 
displaying scFvs specifically binding myotoxin II. The scFv insert in these phages were then subcloned 
into the pSANG10-3F scFv expression vector and transformed into BL21(DE3) competent E. coli cells. 
Next, the binding abilities of the scFvs were tested against myotoxin II in a monoclonal ELISA, and 
all scFv-producing monoclonal cultures (175) with a signal above 0.2 (measured as absorbance at 
492 nm) were carried on to another ELISA. In the second ELISA, an extensive specificity test was 
performed by testing if the scFvs recognized either 3% MPBS, streptavidin, neutravidin, or a PLA2 
from N. mossambica. The results from this ELISA showed, that all 175 scFv-producing monoclonal 
cultures were producing scFvs that specifically recognized myotoxin II and therefore, they were all 
included for further investigation. Additionally, a novel ELISA, to test pH-dependency of the cultures, 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
was performed. The pH-dependent ELISA did not show pH-dependency in the scFvs. However, these 
results should not be regarded as accurate indications of pH-dependency as the used experiment 
was novel, and thereby lacks validation as a functional indicator of pH-dependence. In the future, 
the scFvs will be converted to Fab format and tested for pH-dependency using SPR, which is a 
validated method to test pH-dependency of Fabs or IgGs. 
The scFv inserts in the 175 scFv-producing monoclonal cultures were PCR amplified and submitted 
for sequencing at Eurofins and Macrogen. The resulting sequences were then translated using 
EMBOSS and analyzed using Jalview to check for scFv sequences that were duplicates in all six CDR 
regions. Any discovered duplicates were deleted which left 123 unique scFv sequences.  
Next, two scFv-producing monoclonal cultures with unique scFv sequences were picked for 
expression and purification. The decision on which two scFvs to express would optimally be based 
on the results from an ENC ELISA, which can measure binding abilities quantitatively by normalizing 
the expression levels of the scFv-producing monoclonal cultures. However, at the time, and until 
very recently, this protocol was non-functional and was therefore skipped. This left the decision, of 
which scFvs to express, to be based on the monoclonal ELISA results. Thereby, the two unique scFvs 
B12 and E2, which presented the highest signals, were picked for expression and purification. During 
the process of expressing the scFvs, difficulties were encountered which resulted in abandoning half 
of the steps in the current scFv expression protocol. However, despite abandoning half of the 
protocol, the expression was still successful with protein yields of 7.7 mg and 4.5 mg for E2 and B12, 
respectively. The successful expression could indicate that the abandoned steps in the current 
expression protocol are redundant, and these should be tested for redundancy. 
The neutralizing abilities of the expressed and purified scFvs were then tested in a novel cell viability 
assay, in which human keratinocytes were subjected to myotoxin II. The B12 and the E2 scFv were 
able to neutralize the toxic effects of myotoxin II at a toxin:scFv molar ratio of 1:3 and 1:1, 
respectively. These results demonstrate, that both scFvs neutralize the toxic effects of myotoxin II, 
however, the E2 scFvs possess the most potent neutralizing abilities. 
The next step in the antibody discovery pipeline for this project is, to convert the most promising 
scFvs to Fab and IgG formats for affinity assay testing, pH-dependency testing, and in vivo testing. 
Prior to this, it is recommended to test the 123 unique scFvs in the now functional ENC ELISA to 
check if the expressed scFvs present as the strongest binders in this assay as well. 
The work presented in this thesis, demonstrate a successful discovery of human scFvs able to 
neutralize the toxic effects of myotoxin II in vitro. This is the first small step in the making of a human 
recombinant antivenom against the venom of B. asper, which in the future could save thousands of 
lives and limbs in Central and South America.
