Abstract
This thesis explores the use of Laser Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy LIBS to
detect trace elements in wine, emphasizing copper and iron analysis. The research
focused on exploring sample preparation and an analytical technique designed for
wine quality assessment.
Wine samples went through reﬁned ashing methods, examining both electric and
microwave furnace approaches.
The ashing process of 30 mL wine samples in
an electric furnace operating at 525°C yielded the most optimal ash quality with
negligible char residues.
The proposed method underwent application across
white, red, and appassimento wines to assess matrix eﬀects alongside analytical
performance.
Copper 219.2 nm emission line served as the basis for LIBS analysis. While, for
iron the 273-278 nm wavelength range was selected since it provided the best
reproducibility.
The process of method validation incorporated a spike recovery
experiments across a single parameter, with examination of 0-10 mg
L concentration
ranges alongside ICP-OES reference measurements as a cross-validation method. In
addition, the developed method underwent statistical analysis to assess its linearity
as well as its precision and accuracy.
The analytical performance results showcased exceptional quality as correlation
coeﬃcients R² scored 0.93 on average, demonstrating a uniform score for both
copper and iron. In addition, the method accuracy was veriﬁed through 95-105%
spike recovery rates with a cross-validation with ICP-OES, demonstrating a 99-105%
agreement.
The investigation conﬁrms that LIBS emerges as a feasible substitute for traditional
approaches in detecting trace elements in wine. However time is still a constraint
that has to be dealt with, since the sample preparation is signiﬁcantly more
time-consuming as compared to ICP-OES.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For thousands of years, wine has been a part of human life, not merely as a drink but
as a symbol in social and religious rites. Winemaking is so rooted in some cultures
that, in Georgia for example, it is recognized by United Nations Educational,
Scientiﬁc and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) as an intangible cultural heritage,
citing how, wine plays a vital role in everyday life and in the celebration of secular
and religious events [1].
Wine holds signiﬁcance of great culture, and at the same time remains one of the
world’s greatest industries. Currently, it has a global production and consumption
on an extensive scale, and recent estimates place annual world wine production at
around 230 – 240 million hectoliters per year [2]. In economic terms, the global
wine market was estimated to be at $385 billion in 2023, highlighting the presence
of wine in society [3].
In terms of composition, wine is rather simple when looking at it on the bigger
picture, as water and ethanol make up around 97% of the total mass of wine,
leaving just about 3% of the total mass for smaller yet relevant compounds as can be
seen in Figure 1.1. This 3% is composed by glycerol, diverse phenolic compounds,
polysaccharides, other alcohols and organic acids as well as minerals and volatile
compounds [4].
Soil minerals, vineyard geology, climate and weather, as well as the cultivation
methods, all can aﬀect how the grapes absorb nutrients and elements. Thus, wines
from diﬀerent areas or regions often show measurable diﬀerences in their elemental
and chemical constituents. This concept is observable since an area of particular
grape cultivation will exhibit distinct features of the composition such as wine’s trace
elements. The concentration of some macro and trace elements can give evidence of
the origin of a wine. For that reason, a high ratio of certain constituents or unusual
ones can raise suspicion if the wine is said to come from a region which does not

1.1
Wine quality and Authenticity Concerns
Modern wine production encounters multiple obstacles in maintaining quality
control and verifying authenticity, since wine stands as one of the most frequently
adulterated food products.
The increased production of counterfeit high value
wines through the addition of unauthorized sugars and/or by falsely declaring
geographic origin, remains unregulated. These deceptive practices cause damage to
consumers who might never receive their purchased goods, while genuine producers
are faced with unjust market competition. The need to protect wine culture brought
authenticity veriﬁcation into focus as a critical pursuit within the wine industry [6].
Furthermore,
rigorous restrictions upon potentially dangerous elements and
contaminants conform to the standards set by the International Organization of
Vine and Wine (OIV). The presence of cadmium, lead, arsenic, and various other
elements at toxicity levels within wine which exceed deﬁned maximum allowable
concentrations causes signiﬁcant concern [6].
In addition, certain trace elements impact wine stability and sensory quality
even when present at non-hazardous levels. The intricate challenge of sustaining
wine quality emerges from producers needing to protect against fraudulent
practices, while meeting chemical safety regulations and maintaining wine’s desired

1.2
Analytical Importance of Elemental Analysis
in Wine
The wine sector depends on analytical measures due to its complex nature.
Enologists together with chemists must conduct analyses of wine composition to
track fermentation progress while detecting faults and contaminants alongside fraud
and verifying label accuracy. Through comparison with established data records the
elemental ﬁngerprint of wine reveals its regional origin, or if a potential blending
from diﬀerent areas was used. The detection of counterfeit and misrepresented wines
becomes possible while simultaneously strengthening wine quality control through
the measurement of metal concentrations, which may disrupt ﬂavor or stability [7].
To complement these challenges, newer rapid-sensing methods are being explored.
The technique known as LIBS represents a promising method that has started to
attract interest for wine analysis. A key beneﬁt of LIBS technology is its ability
to examine samples with little preparatory work while providing immediate results,
which makes it desirable for on-site testing applications. Scientists have implemented
LIBS technology to distinguish wines by regional origin through trace element
analysis, showcasing its eﬀectiveness for authenticity studies [7].
1.3
Aim of the Thesis
This thesis explores LIBS capabilities on elemental analysis in wine, for establishing
a dependable technique for trace element detection.
The accurate identiﬁcation
of elemental proﬁles in wine functions as a critical component for quality control
and veriﬁcation of geographical origin, while also detecting potential contaminants
harmful to human health.
To achieve this, the execution of the following steps is required:
• Optimization of sample preparation.
• Elemental calibration and validation using certiﬁed reference materials by the
use of ICP-OES and testing on 3 diﬀerent type of wines.
• Make use of statistical methods to analyze and interpret LIBS data for wine
sample diﬀerentiation.

2.1
Signiﬁcance of trace element detection in
wine
Wine originates as an agricultural product, and therefore includes numerous macro
elements together with trace components and toxic substances coming from the
soil. This characteristics result from intricate interactions between soil composition,
vineyard location, grape variety, wine-making processes and storage conditions. The
simultaneous occurrence of these factors causes changes in both its fundamental
structure and sensory attributes [8].
2.1.1
Wine Quality and Organoleptic Characteristics
Trace elements such as iron (Fe), copper (Cu), zinc (Zn) and manganese (Mn)
together with macro elements including potassium (K), magnesium (Mg) and
calcium (Ca), naturally exist in wine, where they inﬂuence its chemical structure
and sensory attributes.
From keeping its antioxidant properties to maintaining
stability during fermentation processes or delivering enhanced nutritional value. The
study of these elements enables detection of both natural and human inﬂuences in
wine production, which helps in improving an understanding of terroir along with
vineyard management techniques [9].
Wineries gain the ability to ﬁne-tune production methods and create diﬀerent
product lines by studying element concentrations which showcase vineyard terroir
uniqueness [8].

==PAGE:16==
2.1.2
Wine Classiﬁcation and Authenticity
Reliable methods for fraud prevention have emerged through the classiﬁcation of
wine by its origin grape variety and production techniques. Key elements such as
strontium (Sr), barium (Ba), nickel (Ni), and Cu have been identiﬁed to assist in
wine classiﬁcation by geographical origin [9]. While wines bearing the Protected
Designation of Origin (PDO) label rely on these elements as distinctive identiﬁers
which represent the speciﬁc regional origins [10].
2.2
Elemental determination through Advanced
Analytical Techniques
An extensive array of analytical techniques are available to detect and quantify trace
elements in food matrices, each displays diﬀerent beneﬁts regarding sensitivity and
speciﬁcity while serving diﬀerent applications. ICP-OES stand as the main methods
for detecting trace elements in food, as it delivers precise results while enabling a
fast analysis [11].
In addition, ICP-OES stands as a well established technique that delivers exceptional
sensitivity and accuracy while it simultaneously enables multi element detection.
This analytical method demonstrates exceptional power for analyzing intricate food
matrices, as the detection thresholds range from µg
L to ng
L during delivery [11].
However, the technique requires advanced professional skills, together with complex
equipment and a lengthy sample preparation process, which increases operational
costs. Despite encountering some challenges ICP-OES remains to be the preferred
method for detailed analyses across regulatory-dependent industries, including food
and beverage sectors [9].
In contrast to other methods,
LIBS emerges as a rapid and cost-eﬀective
technique for trace element detection. Having said that, it demonstrates reduced
sensitivity relative to ICP-OES, yet advancements in calibration methods alongside
chemometrics have signiﬁcantly improved its accuracy and reliability.
The
advantages of this system makes it a good selection for real time quality control
and ﬁeld testing situations where quick operation and simple procedures are critical
[12].

2.3
Wine Composition and Quality Factors
As mentioned in the introduction, water and ethanol make up around 97% of the
total mass of wine, leaving just about 3% of the total mass for smaller yet relevant
compounds which make the diﬀerence between each of the available wines [4]. Even
that this represents a very little percentage of its total mass, it determines the ﬂavor,
aroma and mouth-feel of the wine.
The pH of the wine is around 4 and it is largely determined to its acid content,
mainly tartaric and malic acids, even that citric and acetic acid play a role on its
acidity too. Poly-phenolic compounds like tannins and ﬂavonoids are some of the
key molecules that contribute to the ﬂavor of the wine, tannins for instance are the
molecules responsible for the astringency of red wine. These compounds are mainly
found on the skin, seeds and stems of the grapes used for wine production, and are
related to bitterness or dry sensations. In the case of red wines, its color is given by
anthocyanins, while for white wines, ﬂavonols are thought to be behind its yellowish
color [13].
2.3.1
Macroelements in wine
Macroelements are known as those which concentrations are on the range of mg
L .
However, concentrations can vary depending on many factors like grape variety, soil
composition and winemaking techniques.
The most abundant macro element found in wine is K, which can usually be found
in concentrations ranging from 500 to 2000 mg
L . K plays a key role in regulating
pH, potassium salts, like potassium bitartrate (KC4H5O6), are naturally formed
during fermentation and can precipitate. If they do, tartrate crystals are formed.
Concentrations of potassium are often higher in red wines, providing a smoother
mouthfeel and a lower acidity feeling [8].
Ca is the second most abundant macroelement found in concentrations of 30-150 mg
L
in wine, and it is mostly related on the stabilization of wine, more precisely on the
prevention of the formation of unwanted precipitates during aging. Ca can interact
as calcium carbonate (CaCO3) with tartaric acid to form calcium tartrate as seen in
Equation 2.1, which can cause inconveniences if present in large quantities. However,
calcium is also important, given that it can enhance ﬂavor stability [14].

2.3.2
Trace elements in wine
Trace elements are those which are found in much lower concentrations, in the range
of µg
L . However, trace elements can still have a strong impact on the overall quality
and properties of wine.
The trace element typically found in the highest concentration is Fe. Fe exists in
wine as a stabilizing agent for helping with color retention. Additionally, it serves
as a catalyst for oxidation reactions which result in the degradation of anthocyanins
(the pigments responsible for the characteristic red colour of some wines). If Fe is
found in excess, it can cause both an unwanted brownish color to appear and the
formation of a haze following the reaction of Fe3+ with phosphates or proteins found
on the wine to then form insoluble ferric phosphate or ferric protein complexes [8].
Cu serves as an essential trace element because it eﬀectively eliminates hydrogen
sulﬁde, which is responsible for causing undesirable aromas in wine. Nonetheless, the
presence of excess Cu causes turbidity and can contribute to a metallic bitter taste.
Elemental Cu participates in oxidative reactions which enhance wine maturation
potential [8].
Another relevant trace element is Zn, which functions as a critical cofactor for yeast
enzymes that occur in fermentation processes. Zn exists only in reduced quantities
yet it aﬀects fermentation performance and contributes to long-term wine stability.
Notwithstanding, elevated Zn levels suppress yeast function resulting in inferior wine
quality [15].

Mn participates in numerous enzymatic processes throughout fermentation stages,
particularly in red wine production. In addition, it plays a critical role in controlling
redox reactions which aﬀect both the color stability and sensory characteristics of
wine. Mn also shares a similar characteristic with Cu and Fe, since when it is found
in excessive amounts, it can catalyze oxidation [8].
2.3.3
Toxic elements in wine
Toxic elements are deﬁned as those which pose severe health problems to humans,
such as lead (Pb), arsenic (As), cadmium (Cd), and mercury (Hg). These elements
might be found in trace amounts in wine, and since even at low concentrations
can be very noxious, its monitoring is essential. These elements can come from
environmental contamination, agricultural practices, or wine-making equipment,
and their presence is strictly regulated due to potential long-term health eﬀects
[16].
Pb contamination might happen through environmental contamination, water, or
wine-making equipment, particularly from the corrosion happening from old metallic
wine equipment [17].
As contamination may arise from pesticides or natural soil deposits, as it is usually
found as a result from vast pesticide and herbicide spraying on the vineyards as
well as due to the wine-making conditions. While Cd is usually introduced in wine
through excess fertilizers found in soil. Lastly, Hg contamination while more unlikely,
can still occur through environmental pollution [17].
These toxic elements, even in small amounts, can accumulate over time in the human
body and cause chronic and fatal consequences, that is why its monitoring and
regulation is essential. While the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) or the
OIV are not a regulatory bodies, they play a key role on independent scientiﬁc advice
to set strict limits for these contaminants to protect public health [18]. For instance,
Regulation (EU) 2023/915, sets the European Union (EU) maximum levels for lead
in food. Maximum concentrations of this toxic element are established for wine and
apply to similar food categories [19].

Chapter 3
Laser-Induced
Breakdown
Spectroscopy
(LIBS) technology
LIBS stands as an analytichal method employed to ascertain the elemental
composition of various substances. Usually, a Q-switched Nd:YAG laser delivers
high energy pulses focused on the sample surface creating micro plasma which
excites the sample’s elements as can be seen in Figure 3.1. Upon returning to their
ground state, excited atoms and ions emit photons at element-speciﬁc wavelengths.
A spectrometer collects this photons which enables rapid elemental identiﬁcation
and quantiﬁcation for the sample.
Throughout this chapter these concepts will
undergo detailed exploration to achieve a more thorough understanding.
Figure 3.1: LIBS instrument setup, the laser is focused through a lens which then
creates a plasma.
Photon emissions are gathered by a mirror, transferring the
photons into a spectrometer which is then processed and shown as spectra into
the PC.

3.1
Ablation
The initial step in LIBS is the interaction of the laser with its target material. This
process is known as laser ablation and refers to the vaporization and ionization of
the material, leaving a crater on solid samples and from which at the same time a
plasma will originate as presented in Figure 3.2. Plasma is composed of both neutral
and ionized elements responsible for emitting photons across a range of wavelengths
[20].

crater and a plasma medium.

3.5
Sample preparation for liquids on LIBS
When it comes to liquids, LIBS can be challenged with a variety of diﬃculties.
Some of the most relevant are plasma instability, poor reproducibility and splashing.
These factors have led to the discovery of innovative methods of sample preparation
to improve the stability of the plasma formation and the precision and accuracy of
the measured sample [25].
Some of the LIBS sample preparation techniques for liquid samples can be grouped
based on the physical form of the sample at the time of laser ablation, stabilizing
the laser–sample interaction, and whether preconcentration or conversion to another
phase is needed [25].
3.5.1
Direct liquid phase analysis
The methods described in this section can deal directly with the liquid form,
involving interaction of the laser with a moving or stabilized liquid sample.
One of the most common methods of this type of analysis is to apply the laser pulse
directly onto the surface of the analyte or to the bulk liquid itself. While simple
and requiring minimal equipment, it is usually problematic due to splashing of the
liquid. More advanced versions include the use of droplets or liquid jets, which are
continuously feed into the focal point of the laser. This helps in reducing splashing as
well as avoiding cavitation, though more complex it ensures a better reproducibility
[21].
3.5.2
Liquid to solid conversion
In contrast to direct liquid phase approaches, liquid to solid techniques seek to
transform the liquid into a solid prior to the ablation of the sample.
One well known technique is surface drying, where a certain volume of liquid is
placed on glass or quartz and is then dried under controlled conditions. After a thin
solid layer is formed, it can later be ablated like if it would be a solid, making it
possible to make accurate repeated measurements. A more advanced version of this
16

technique is pelletization, where a binder like cellulose or silica is mixed with the
dried residue to create a pellet using a mechanical press. By pelletizing the sample,
better homogeneity and compatibility with calibration models can be achieved. This
method works especially well for ﬁeld-deployable LIBS systems where robustness is
crucial, as well as for the detection of trace metals in biological or environmental
ﬂuids [26].
Another method is freezing, in which liquid samples solidify into ice.
Although
this technique eﬀectively reduces splash, it needs a carefully controlled cryogenic
temperatures and might not be appropriate for analytes that are sensitive to heat

3.5.3
Aerosol-Based techniques
The third category includes aerosol-based methods,
in which the liquid is

immediately while in suspension.
Typically, ultrasonic nebulizers are used to
accomplish this. Having improved homogeneity, greater laser-sample coupling, and
frequently increased sensitivity are all part for beneﬁting LIBS analysis of aerosols,
particularly for trace-level studies. For example elements like arsenic and antimony,
can be detected more eﬀectively when the aerosolization process is combined with a
hydride production, a process in which some of the elements create volatile hydrides
that are carried into the laser interaction zone [26].
3.5.4
Preconcentration and Microextraction Methods
Finally,
the fourth category comprises preconcentration and microextraction
methods, which aim to isolate and enrich the target analytes from the bulk matrix
prior to analysis. Dispersive Liquid–Liquid Micro Extraction (DLLME) and Single
Drop Micro Extraction (SDME) are the most well known and involve partitioning
the analytes into a small volume of extraction solvent. This is then subsequently
dried or clamped on a substrate for ablation [26].
In similar terms, Solid Phase Micro Extraction (SPME) and Thin Film Micro
Extraction (TFME) utilize sorbent materials, such as polymer membranes or
graphene oxide, to selectively adsorb the analytes of choice from the given sample.
These methods not only concentrate the analyte but also create a simpler matrix,
improving the detection limits.
This ability to integrate with compact systems
makes them a promising option for portable or on-site LIBS setups [26].

Emission Spectroscopy (ICP-OES)
ICP-OES stands as a powerful analytical method frequently employed to ascertain
elemental concentrations within intricate food matrices including wine. Through
complex plasma excitation at high temperatures combined with optical emission
detection, ICP-OES enables simultaneous multi-element analysis while maintaining
excellent sensitivity, accuracy, and reliability.
4.1
Principle
In ICP-OES a liquid sample enters the plasma via a nebulization system which
transforms it into a ﬁne aerosol. Argon gas is used as a carrier stream that transports
the aerosol to the plasma torch where the sample undergoes excitation, atomization,
vaporization, and desolvation. Plasma temperatures reach extreme levels between
6000 and 10000 K to provide the necessary energy for exciting sample ions and
atoms. The energetic species transition back to ground states by emitting photons
at element-speciﬁc wavelengths.
After that, a spectrometer collects the emitted
photons which then pass through a diﬀraction grating before reaching a detector
where the intensity of each wavelength is recorded. The measured intensity directly
represents the element concentration within the sample [27].
4.2
Beneﬁts
ICP-OES demonstrates exceptional performance in detecting multiple elements at
once while maintaining brief analysis durations. This technology identiﬁes macro
and trace elements with exceptional reproducibility which makes it particularly
valuable for food analysis as well as environmental water quality assessments
19
==PAGE:30==
alongside metallurgical research and clinical toxicology [28].
Within the wine industry sector, ICP-OES technology ﬁnds application to quantify
elemental concentrations including potassium K, magnesium Mg, calcium Ca,
sodium Na, iron Fe along with trace toxic elements such as lead Pb and cadmium
Cd.
The assessment of these measurements plays a vital role in maintaining
quality standards while ensuring safety regulations are met and verifying product
authenticity [29].
This method demonstrates substantial resilience against matrix variability when
suitable sample preparation techniques are employed.
Usually, the preparation
procedure involves using ultrapure nitric acid at 2-5% concentration to eﬀectively
diminish matrix interferences while maintaining plasma stability. As well as the
application of dilution ratios such as 1:10 and 1:20 [30].
4.3
Limitations and Considerations
ICP-OES demonstrates strong analytical capabilities yet still faces certain
operational constraints.
This method requires extensive laboratory setups that
include cooling systems and argon gas supplies while demanding substantial
power consumption.
Moreover, the process of sample preparation frequently
demands intricate acid digestion techniques which prove to be lengthy while
simultaneously presenting potential dangers including contamination and the loss of
volatile elements. The method demands expert technicians to adjust measurement
conditions while interpreting spectral data with precision [28].
A signiﬁcant limitation of ICP-OES emerges from its dependency on liquid sample
analysis. Before analysis solid materials need to be converted into a liquid phase
through digestion processes which restricts the technique use for quick on-site
measurements. The detection limits of ICP-OES, which typically reach the ppb
range [28].
4.4
Comparison with LIBS
ICP-OES demonstrates superior sensitivity and quantiﬁcation precision over LIBS,
particularly for trace element detection. The established protocols combined with
exceptional reproducibility render it a dependable option for standard laboratory
examinations. Despite its inherent complexity, LIBS presents unique beneﬁts that
eﬀectively mitigate several ICP-OES limitations [29].

4.3.
LIMITATIONS AND CONSIDERATIONS

The LIBS technique allows swift elemental analysis without extensive sample
preparation which makes it ideal for examining solid and heterogeneous materials.
The method operates without reagents and can be used across diverse environments
such as ﬁeld locations and industrial sites because of advancements in compact
portable LIBS systems. The inherent design of LIBS enables it to deliver operational
versatility and straightforward usage which ICP-OES fails to achieve because of its
requirement for advanced infrastructure and restricted laboratory environments [31].
As for now, ICP-OES remains the benchmark technique for precise elemental
analysis, yet LIBS is gaining recognition as a supplementary method. The true worth
of this technology emerges in scenarios where rapid processing, budget eﬃciency, and
sample adaptability take precedence over detection sensitivity. The integration of
these techniques allows for expanded elemental analysis applications across research,
environmental, and industrial sectors [31].

6.1
Optimization of sample preparation
Initial experimental trials were performed to identify the ideal wine volume necessary
for eﬀective ash recovery while reducing carbon residue formation. An evaluation
was conducted on three diﬀerent volumes of appassimento wine: 20 mL, 30 mL, and
40 mL. This choice was made in order to use as little of wine as possible as well as
to obtain the necessary ash content for analysis, knowing that ash content in wines
usually ranges from 1.2 to 3 g/L [38].
In addition to the 3 diﬀerent sample volumes, two diﬀerent furnaces were employed
in order to asses if the process could be done faster by the use of the microwave
furnace or by the use of the more traditional electric furnace.
As a result, 20 mL volume produced an insuﬃcient ash quantity in both furnace types
in order to produce triplicates, which failed to support a consistent pellet formation
as it is described in Table 6.1. Since LIBS analysis demands approximately a 1.5
mL of sample volume or 50-100 mg in order to produce a 14 mm diameter pellet,
meaning that around 150-300 mg of ash would be required to produce triplicates for
each sample.
In contrast, the 40 mL volume led to an overproduction of carbon residue, specially
for the microwave furnace as it can be seen in Figure 6.1, probably due to incomplete
combustion of organic material during the ashing process.
Through experimentation the 30 mL volume emerged as the optimal choice, as it

produced suﬃcient ash to perform triplicates from each sample while keeping carbon
content within acceptable limits. The chosen volume parameters correspond with
prior research directives which stress reducing carbonaceous deposits to achieve clear
LIBS signals [39].

terms as it can be visually inspected in Figure 6.2, samples ashed using the electrical

Chapter 7
Summary and conclusions
This thesis explored the application of LIBS for the determination of Cu and
Fe in wine, evaluating its performance against the established ICP-OES method.
Through systematic optimization of sample preparation, analytical validation, and
comparative assessments, this study provides a foundation for LIBS as a viable
alternative for trace metal analysis in complex matrices like wine.
7.1
Conclusions
The experimental investigation achieved several important outcomes. The process of
sample preparation optimization revealed that electric furnace ashing at 525°C with
30 mL wine samples produced superior ash quality compared to microwave ashing,
minimizing carbon residue while ensuring suﬃcient material for pellet formation.
Notably, appassimento wine exhibited the lowest carbon interference, whereas white
wine the most.
The calibration curves for LIBS analysis of Cu at 219.2 nm and Fe at 273–276 nm
in the three wine types exhibited exceptional linearity, with R² averaging values
greater than 0.93 and RMSEs of 0.2–0.5
g
kg.
Upon conversion to liquid-equivalent concentrations the method demonstrated a
reasonable accuracy with an RMSE varying from 0.5–1.3
mg
L , yet matrix eﬀects
caused variability especially for Fe in red wine.
Spike recovery results through
ICP-OES scoring about 95–105% recoveries, established method reliability across
the tested concentrations for Cu 0–5 mg
L and Fe: 0-10 mg
L .
LIBS faced limitations on detection limits at mg
L levels as compared to ICP-OES
µg/L sensitivity, and it suﬀered from a lengthy sample preparation (drying, ashing,
pelletizing) meaning that the often-claimed speed advantage of LIBS was not
realized. By comparison, ICP-OES in this context only required dilution, making it
51
==PAGE:62==
in fact faster in terms of total analysis time.
7.2
Practical Implications
The existing approach demonstrates potential eﬀectiveness as a preliminary model,
yet developing durable and adaptable models demands much larger datasets.
Usually, over 100 samples for each wine type would be required in order to address
matrix variability and improve its predictive reliability.
Although the present work did not attempt to diﬀerentiate wine origin or
classiﬁcation, LIBS combined with non-linear multivariate analysis (e.g., Neural
Networks) could enable such tasks by learning complex patterns in elemental
ﬁngerprints, particularly for large or high-dimensional spectral datasets [42].
7.3
Limitations and Future Work
The sample preparation process remains simpliﬁed, yet still depends on ashing
techniques which require specialized high-temperature equipment like muﬄe
furnaces and involve tedious time-consuming processing and cooling durations.
These requirements introduce some restrictions that aﬀect resource access and time
eﬃciency while increasing the complexity and cost of the analytical processes.
Future research should explore direct liquid-phase LIBS, like aerosol analysis to
reduce preparation time and energy consumption. As well as in-situ sample methods
which would preserve liquid sample integrity while reducing analyte degradation or
contamination risks.
