ABSTRACT 
 
This master’s thesis develops a predation index time series for the Pandalus borealis stock in the 
Skagerrak, based on the stomach samples, and assesses whether scaling of the assumed natural 
mortality with this time-varying index will improve the performance of the current shrimp stock 
assessment model. This was done with laboratory analyses, where 2780 stomach samples were 
examined from twenty-two predator species, measuring five prey categories. Then predator biomass 
indices were calculated using fish catch data from a yearly shrimp survey conducted by the Marine 
institute in Norway. Based on differences in Pandalus borealis biomass, and thus prey availability, of the 
survey stations, six scenarios have been investigated to evaluate if low shrimp biomass affects index 
performance. The first index which was calculated is the PFO, providing the percent frequency 
occurrence of shrimps in the stomach content of each species. The predation pressure index (PPI) was 
estimated from 1988 to 2018 according to the weighted sum of the predator biomass, with data 
obtained from previous surveys, and the PFO index. The resulting PPI index time series was used as a 
basis for a time-varying natural mortality of Pandalus borealis. Thus, time-varying M was used in the 
assessment model, replacing a constant M (0.75), in order to estimate if the model fit was improved. It 
was concluded that the inclusion of time-varying natural mortality rates based on the developed PPI 
indices did not improve the model fit. 
Keywords: Stock synthesis model, Pandalus borealis, natural mortality, Skagerrak, predation pressure 
index, stomach content analyses

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
Fish populations have been under growing pressure due to overfishing, human population growth, 
global climate change, pollution and habitat degradation and the use of other marine resources (FAO, 
2016). Marine resources and their sustainable use, require more effective monitoring and a more 
integrated management of ecosystems, not just the continued exploitation of fish stocks (McHugh, 
1959; Mercer, 1982; Tyler, Gabriel, & Overholtz, 1982; Fogarty, 2014; Richards & Jacobson, 2016). There 
are better approaches for the management of fishery resources owing to the fact that they have been 
invented advanced observation systems, there is better ecological understanding and modelling 
capacities. (Murawski, 2007). However, parameterizing complicated multi-species and ecosystem 
models can be challenging for the operator and manageable approaches are required (Murawski, 2007; 
Fogarty, 2014; Richards & Jacobson, 2016). 
The assessment and fisheries management of invertebrate stocks present some additional sustainability 
challenges (Hjort & Ruud, 1938; Caddy, 2004; Cadrin et al., 2004; Link & Idoine, 2009). These difficulties 
incoporate a combination of short-lived stocks, questionable and uncertain ecological and 
environmental impacts and laborious acquisition of fisheries data due to the fact that they frequently 
are constrained in scope, extent, and the quantity of detail (Haynes & Wigley, 1969; Bergstrom, 2000; 
Cadrin, Boutillier, & Idoine; Link & Idoine, 2009). Invertebrates often play a crucial fish-forage role in the 
food chain and their stock dynamics therefore directly affect the fish stocks (Link & Idoine, 2009; Caddy, 
2004; Parsons, 2005, Link & Idoine, 2009). It is therefore important to examine the food web role of 
target invertebrates, and more specifically the changes in natural mortality, when assessing and 
managing these stocks. Knowing the size of the pressure on the inveterbrates from potential predators 
provides us more information about the dynamics of stock (Link & Idoine, 2009).  
The northern shrimp Pandalus borealis has a wide distribution in North Atlantic and is a target of 
significant fisheries (Shumwa et al., 1985; Bergström, 2000; Richards & Jacobson, 2016). More 
specifically, in the North Sea, the Pandalus borealis population has been divided into 3 different stocks 
(ICES, 1990). The first concerns the Skagerrak-Norwegian Deep stock, which is defined by the ICES Divs. 
IIIa and IVa east. The second stock concerns the Fladen Ground stock in ICES Div. IVa west and the last 
Farn Deep stock in ICES Div. IVb west (Ulmestrand et al., 2015). However, the Pandalus borealis stock 
which is distributed in Skagerrak-Norwegian Deep area is estimated as one since 1990 because it 
comprises one biological unit (Knutsen, et al., 2015). Countries that surround the North Sea, such as

Norway, Denmark, Sweden and the United Kingdom, have exploited these stocks for food  (Søvik & 
Thangstad, 2013; Garcia, 2007).  
The exploitation of the Pandalus borealis stock in Norwegian Deep and Skagerrak began in the 19th 
century, although a Total Allowable Catch (TAC) was not implemented before 1992 using the outcome of 
a cohort-based assessment until 2002. However, in 2003 there was a new proposal for TAC estimation 
using data from CPUEs from commercial catches and scientific surveys. The previous TAC estimation 
method was rejected because this approach did not account for natural mortality, which contributes 
twice as much removal from the Pandalus stock as the fishery. In 2013, a new length-based assessment 
model was proposed. However, the model fit to observed data was not good and outputs were likely 
overestimating fishing mortality rates, and at a benchmark in 2016 it was decided to implement another 
length-based model, which was implemented in Stock Synthesis (SS3) (ICES, 2016).  
An important factor influencing the stocks of Pandalus borealis is the natural mortality due to the 
predation (Garcia, 2007) because this species is often a key ingredient for their predators food chain 
(Richards & Jacobson, 2016). In previous surveys that have been carried out, it was estimated that the 
predominant predators of northern shrimp are gadoids like Cod and Haddock (Parsons, 2005; Richards & 
Jacobson, 2016; Jónsdóttir, 2017). In our study area, an analysis conducted by Jørgensen, et al., 2014, 
showed that main predator species for Pandalus borealis stock are Cod (Gadus morhua), Roundnose 
Grenadier (Coryphaenoides rupestris), Velvet belly (Etmopterus spinax) and Blue whiting (Micromesistius 
poutassou). 
The standard SS3 model outputs refer to parameters such as Fishing mortality (F), Spawning Stock 
Biomass (SSB) and Recruitment (R) at age zero and these are based on an assumption of a constant 
natural mortality (0.75) (ICES, 2016) for all years and age groups. At the benchmark in 2016 it was, 
however, considered that there are improvement potentials of the SS3 assessment model through 
further investigations of predation pressure with the and the introduction of time-varying natural 
mortality (M). 
 
2. AIM OF STUDY 
 
In recent years the shrimp stock Pandalus borealis has declined in size with significant consequences for 
its various ecosystem services (Søvik & Thangstad, 2016). The aim of this project is the investigation of

the predation pressure on Pandalus borealis shrimp with the objective to better understand stock 
dynamics and improve the current stock assessment model (Stock synthesis) in Norwegian Deep-
Skagerrak. The current model that it is used to estimate the stock of northern shrimp is a length-based 
model. For the yearly assessments and advice, the model takes into consideration year-to-year 
alternations in recruitment and how these changes affect catch options in the short term. The natural 
mortality (M) of shrimp is modelled as a constant of 0.75 over time (Bergenius, et. al., 2016). The main 
objective of this project is to estimate a predation index time series, based on the stomach samples, and 
evaluate whether scaling the natural mortality with this time-varying index will improve the 
performance of the shrimp stock assessment model (SS3). 
To achieve this objective potential predator stomachs were sampled during the 2018 North Sea NOR 
shrimp NDSK cruise. In total, 2780 stomachs were sampled and subsequently analysed in the laboratory. 
These 2018 stomach data were used to estimate the Percent frequency of Occurrence (PFO) of prey 
items in the potential shrimp predators. This PFO index was then combined with a biomass time series 
of the same species from the yearly survey (1988-2018), to produce an annual Predation Pressure (PPI) 
index by adapting the methodology developed in Richards & Jacobson, 2016. This PPI index was then 
used as a basis for introducing different scenarios of time-varying natural mortality  in the assessment 
model, which were then compared with respect to model fit and outputs (Fishing mortality, Spawning 
stock biomass and Recruitment).

The survey for the Pandalus borealis stock is named “North Sea NOR shrimp NDSK” and it has been 
organized from the Norwegian Institute of Marine Research (IMR) since 1984 (Søvik & Thangstad, 2016). 
The survey is carried out with the aim of assessing the distribution, biomass, amount, recruitment, 
length distribution, and demographic composition of the Pandalus borealis stock, as well as the 
measurement of hydrographic conditions in the distribution area of this shrimp stock (Søvik & 
Thangstad, 2016).The study area consists of the eastern part of the Norwegian Trench and the northern 
part of the Skagerrak (ICES Division Iva and IIIa respectively) (Map 1). Norwegian Trench, is a deep 
trench, and it is located along the coast of Norway with maximum depth of 250 m. In addition, this area 
is characterized by a stratification of salinity, due to the outflow of the water mass from the Baltic Sea 
and from the local rivers. The northern area of the Skagerrak along the Swedish coast is characterized by 
a Deep Trench where it is connected to Norwegian Trench with a maximum depth of about 700 meters. 
(Sejrup, et al., 2008; Rodhe, Tett, & Wulff, 2004; Svansson, 1975). In this area, the stock of Pandalus 
borealis, prefers a wide depth range, but frequently make an appearance at 80-500 depth meters. The 
survey covers a broad area of ICES divisions and an amplitude of depth from 108 to 550 m.  In particular, 
the survey has a fixed station design, determining four depth zones (100-200 m, 200-300 m, 300-500 m, 
and> 500 m), presuming the randomly temporal variation of the stock (ICES, 2016). Overall, the survey 
contains 126 stations. However, in the first 13 stations, it has not been integrated any sampling 
procedure owing to the assessment the condition of the equipment (Søvik & Thangstad, 2016).

3.1. 
 Sampling methods for predators 
 
The sampling of stomachs of predators was carried out in all stratums, although excluding some stations 
when the weather conditions were rough. As it was mentioned previously, there were 126 stations 
fished but only 112 of them where valid. Depending on the predator species and the catches in each 
station, 3 to 4 stomach samples per species and station were taken. Information about the biological 
characteristics of predators, in order to estimate the correlation of the length size between predators 
and Pandalus borealis, was also registered. These data are related to the species, weight, length and sex 
of predators (Spiny dogfish, Rabbit fish, Velvet belly, Roundnose grenadier, Anglerfish, Blackmouthed 
catshark and Thorny skate). The samples were placed in plastic bags labeled with information of the 
predator species and the station from which they were collected. Samples were kept in a freezer at -50 
°C on the vessel. In total, 2780 numbers of stomachs were sampled for all predators. The duration of the 
==PAGE:11==

trawl haul was approximately half an hour in each station (differs in each station depending on the 
depth). Also, sampling for shrimps was carried out for each station/haul (approximately 300 individuals), 
and then sorted according to sex, and the Carapax length was measured. 
 
3.2. 
Laboratory analysis of stomach content 
 
After the sampling of stomachs, the stomach content was analyzed in the laboratory. According to 
Hyslop, 1980, the method used to analyze the stomach content was the frequency occurrence method. 
According to this, it is possible to record data by analyzing the content of the stomachs, containing one 
or more individuals for each food category. In this survey, five food categories have been defined and 
the contents of each predator stomach was analyzed/registered accordingly (Table 3).  A laboratory 
protocol for the analysis of the stomach content was developed (Appendix Β). For each prey, weight, 
length and the stage of digestion was recorded (Appendix C). Thus, with the completion of stomach 
analysis, the percentage of occurrence of the different categories could be estimated across all 
stomachs.

The PFO method which has been implemented for the stomach analyses indicates the identity of 
organisms which the predators have fed on. It is also possible to assess the interactions between species 
and seasonal variations of a prey resource using this approach (Chipps & Garvey, 2007). Furthermore, 
the frequency occurrence method is a powerful tool for the interpretation of diet composition (Baker et 
al., 2014). However, using this method, it does not provide any information about the relative 
importance of the prey to the overall diet of the predator. In addition, this method has given evidence 
==PAGE:12==
Eleni Theofania Skorda 
DTU Aqua 
Master’s Thesis 
11 
 
that it ignores the relative amounts of prey as it provides incomplete information when different food 
categories are regularly consumed but in different abundance (Hyslop, 1980; Lima-Junior & Goitein, 
2001; Chipps & Garvey, 2007; Braga et al., 2012). Frost (1977) found that the frequency occurrence 
method is appropriate when there are few food categories. 
The method of frequency occurrence has a number of practical advantages. It is a very simplistic method 
with easy identification of prey categories, and the equipment necessary for the analysis is very limited. 
However, one of its disadvantages is that there is little indication of the percentage biomass or volume 
of each food category which is presented in each stomach. Nonetheless, this method gives a qualitative 
picture of the food spectrum. This method was used in order to calculate three indices. These indices 
are (i) the average percent frequency of occurrence (PFO) of Pandalus in predator stomachs, (ii) the 
predation pressure index (PPI) and (iii) the Time varying natural mortality, which will be further 
described below. 
During the analysis, it was observed that some of the stomach samples were damaged or the stomachs 
of the predators were in their mouths. In the latter case, this happens because when the trawl is hauled 
from the bottom, there is a high-pressure differential with the reduction of the depth, and this causes 
vomiting in the fish. Thus, the remaining of content of the stomachs is removed from the analysis due to 
vomiting and is recorded as damaged.

Data cleaning

programming language R was deployed to construct the three indices described above. 
Before calculating the indices, the data were quality ensured by the processes depicted in scheme 1

stage, a preprocessing is necessary, because it is almost impossible to read and process erroneously

3.4. 
Estimation of Biomass of Pandalus borealis 
 
The biomass (kg per nautical mile) of Pandalus borealis was estimated for all sampling stations of the 
survey. This information was used to exclude stations that did not meet two different minimum 
thresholds of shrimp biomass (90th percentile or 1 kg per nautical mile). These thresholds were 
compared against a baseline scenario including all stations, to take into consideration variation in prey 
abundance in the production of the indices. For the estimation of shrimp biomass, the weight of 
Pandalus borealis in kilos at each trawl was divided with the distance trawled (in nautical miles). It was 
observed that in some stations the values of biomass were almost zero or very low (Figure 1). In 
Appendix C there are the corresponding figures which illustrates the number of stations where included 
in each scenario.

were defined as listed in Table 4. The first and second scenarios include all sampling stations and they 
are referred to as “baseline”. These scenarios were defined in order to detect differences when stations 
are excluded with low biomass of shrimp. The third and fourth scenarios are referred to as “90th 
Percentile”. In these scenarios, a threshold was set which excludes all stations where the biomass values 
are less than 0.03 Kg per trawled nautical mile (corresponding to 90th percentile of the observed

3.6. 
Application to assessment model 
 
The estimation of the stock of Pandalus borealis was made using Stock Synthesis model (SS3). This 
model is a length-based model with a quarterly time-step. The SS3 model is characterized by a specific 
pattern of length based selection for the fishery, which remains steady throughout the year. The model 
includes nine age categories (0 to 8+), but age data is reported as total length values assuming a von 
Bertalanffy growth function (ICES, 2016).

However, there are several studies such as Berenboim, et. al., 1991, Richards & Jacobson, 2016, that 
provide somewhat divergent outcomes as to what value should be assumed as constant M. In 2016, at 
the benchmark on Pandalus borealis in Skagerrak and Norwegian Deep Sea (WKPAND), it was decided to 
accept the value of 0.75 until it proved otherwise (ICES 2016).

4.1. 
Predation Pressure index 
 
The PFO index was calculated for 22 predator species under each of the six different scenarios which are 
mentioned in Table 4. In scenario 1, 15 of 22 predators have PFΟ values of more than 10% (Appendix A, 
Table 1). In scenario 2, the PFO values are the same as in scenario 1, but Blue whiting was excluded. In 
scenario 3, 14 predators have PFO values more than 10%. Comparing scenarios 1 and 3, it is observed

predators are identical to those from scenario 3, apart from Blue whiting. For scenario 5, it can be seen

5. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 
 
Replacing a constant M model assumption with a time-varying M assumption, based on six differently 
estimated time series of predation pressure, did not improve the model fit. However, in a similar 
analysis conducted by Richards & Jacobson, 2016, the outcome was the opposite. 
Table 5 shows that all scenario likelihood values are higher than the baseline likelihood of the model 
(130.5). In order for the time-varying Ms to improve the model, their likelihood values should be lower 
than the baseline. There is a significant difference between the scenarios containing Blue whiting 
predator (scenarios 1, 3, 5) and those that do not contain it (scenarios 2, 4, 5). The scenarios that do not 
contain Blue whiting are observed to have lower likelihood values of the model, whereas the opposite 
occurs with scenarios involving this predator. The exclusion of this predator occurred because, from 
1988 to 2003, the catches of it were very high but only at certain stations and not throughout the survey 
area. Thus, including this predator species in the analyses, it would not be representative. The same 
==PAGE:25==
Eleni Theofania Skorda 
DTU Aqua 
Master’s Thesis 
24 
 
occurs with low biomass values of Pandalus borealis. In particular, setting a threshold which excludes 
stations containing biomass of Pandalus borealis less than 1 kg per nautical mile, have a better model fit. 
An important factor that may bias the outputs of the model is that there was no time series data for the 
PFO index. Only stomach data from 2018 were analyzed and the resulting PFOs were then extrapolated 
to the previous years of the biomass time series.  
The estimation of the PFO index demonstrated that the predators who contained a higher percentage of 
Pandalus borealis in their stomachs are Cod, Saithe, Anglerfish, Roundnose grenadier and Blue Whiting 
almost in all scenarios. Corresponding results were also found by (Richards & Jacobson, 2016; Parsons, 
2005; Jónsdóttir, 2017; Jørgensen, et al., 2014; Berenboim, et al., 2000). However, the estimation of PFO 
was only made for 2018 in contrast to Richards & Jacobson, 2016; Berenboim, et al., 2000 who assessed 
the PFO index for a time series for many years. Perhaps this single-year limitation of this analysis is a 
key-factor in explaining the fact that no scenario has improved the model. Another factor that might 
explain why no scenario improves the baseline model fit, is that maybe the number of stomach samples 
taken in each trawl, for each predator, was not enough. 
The PPI index strongly mirrors the biomass values in all scenarios but not in those that do not include 
the Blue Whiting predator species, as in (Richards & Jacobson, 2016). In particular, from 1988 to 2004, 
the biomass values of Blue whiting and are slightly higher. It is important to note that the estimation of 
biomass for this time series was based on NDSK cruise data because we wanted to overlap the values of 
the PPI index and the biomass. However, due to the calculation of the PFO index only for the single 2018 
year, PPI values are perhaps not representative for the entire time series. 
Time-varying natural mortality has been assessed over time by various methods for different stock 
assessment models (Johnson, et al., 2015; Fournier, 1983; Shepherd & Pope, 2002; Thorson, 2011). 
Different approaches indicate that the estimation of time-varying natural mortality should not be 
assessed if it is in reality stationary (Punt, et al., 2013) because it will give an error in the assessment of 
the Spawning Stock Biomass (Thorson, 2011). In this study, M was calculated according to equation 3. 
Baseline natural mortality was set to 0.75 y-1 as recommended in the benchmark workshop on Pandalus 
borealis in Skagerrak and Norwegian Deep Sea (WKPAND) in 2016. However, all the methods which have 
been used in different studies, have some bias in the estimations of fishing mortality (Linton & Bence, 
2011). Time-varying natural mortality values as they are represented in Appendix A, Table 3, did not 
improve the assessment model in this study. Apart from the reasons mentioned above, it may be due to 
==PAGE:26==
Eleni Theofania Skorda 
DTU Aqua 
Master’s Thesis 
25 
 
the fact that a misleading value has been defined as baseline natural mortality. Jørgensen, et al., 2014 
suggest that the value of M (0.75) is very low and should be adjusted upwards. 
In addition, several studies (Worm & Myers, 2003; Wieland & Siegstad, 2012; Apollonio, et al., 1986) 
suggest that environmental parameters such as temperature affect Top-Down and Bottom-up cascades, 
influencing the natural mortality for predators and preys respectively. In the first case, Worm & Myers, 
2003 discovered that the mortality of Pandalus borealis is significantly affected by the abundance of 
Cod. Temperature influences the growth and recruitment of Cod, having a positive correlation. 
However, Wieland & Siegstad (2012) and Apollonio, et al. (1986) discovered that an increase in 
temperature had a negative effect on Pandalus borealis in Gulf of Maine and west Greenland waters 
respectively, as it prefers more cold environments (1-6 oC). In corresponding results of Worm & Myers, 
2003, they seem to agree that the increasing temperature has a negative correlation with Pandalus 
borealis. 
In figures 6, 7 and 8 which illustrate the outputs of the model (Spawning stock biomass, Recruitment and 
Fishing mortality) Scenario 6 can be seen to best fit the baseline of the model compared to the other 
scenarios. This may be due to the fact that scenario 6 includes only stations with more than 1 kg of 
Pandalus borealis per trawled nautical mile making it more representative of true PFO values. The 
difference with the corresponding scenario 5 is that it does not contain the predator species Blue 
whiting. This species, due to the high values of biomass during the period 1988-2003, also automatically 
increases the values of the indicator PPI and M. 
 
6. SUGGESTIONS AND IMPROVMENTS 
 
An obvious approach to identify PPI estimations that could improve the current model fit (with 0.75 
constant M assumption) would be to obtain time series data for all previous years of the model time 
series involving samples of stomach content for the PFO index. Moreover, it might be worthwhile to  
include only the four predominant predators of Pandalus borealis as it is suggested by (Jørgensen, et al., 
2014) or to exclude those containing less than 1-5% PFO values (Richards & Jacobson, 2016). This will 
potentially result in a more representative PPI index time series and time-varying M. It would also be 
useful to have a better understanding of the constant M value which is introduced in the SS3 model in 
order to obtain optimal results. A better approach would be to first establish an optimal baseline natural 
==PAGE:27==
Eleni Theofania Skorda 
DTU Aqua 
Master’s Thesis 
26 
 
mortality for equation 3. This could be done by comparing model likelihood values for a range of M-
values, and might result in a value greater than 0.75, as suggested by Jørgensen, et al., 2014. 
Environmental factors affect the natural mortality of the Pandalus borealis and its potential predators. 
For example, as it is mentioned from Wieland & Siegstad, 2012, temperature greatly influences the egg 
mortality of shrimps and recruitment success. An implication to that would be to evaluate potential 
correlations between shrimp abundance and temperature (CTD profiles) based on real-time data and 
not from meta-analysis. Finally, it would be important to have knowledge of Top-down levels as they 
affect shrimp abundances. (Fuentes-Yaco, et. al., 2007; Lilly, et. al., 2000; Worm & Myers, 2003).

APPENDIX B 
 
Protocol on laboratory analyses of the contents of sampled stomach 
Thawing of the stomachs: 
Initially, a reasonable number of stomachs should be thawed in each session of analyses. Particular 
attention should be paid to the time at which the samples are in a defrosted condition as prolonged 
time in a defrosting state can deteriorate the sample. If the sample is thawing in cold water, it reduces 
the thawing time and maintains the quality of the sample in a better condition for later analysis. 
Recovery of the contents: 
Open the stomach with a longitudinal cut using scissors and then pour stomach contents into a sieve. 
Flush out possible remains from the stomach with water from a spray bottle. 
Processing of individual prey items: 
Separate the individual prey items carefully with tweezers (if necessary with a squirt of water from a 
spray bottle), place in on wet paper towel, weigh it, and measure the appropriate lengths. 
NB1: Process small prey items first. They easily dry out because of the large surface to mass relationship, 
and so bias the mass determination. 
NB2: Always dab the prey items with wet paper towel on the top to get rid of excess water. Be aware of 
possible water pockets inside the exoskeleton of crustaceans. Remove this water. 
NB3: The different prey-specific lengths-standard length [Cylinder representation of the different prey 
types] and partial lengths. 
NB4: Be careful when stretching out the prey in order not to underestimate the length. This may 
seriously bias especially the back-calculated time from catch of predator to the actual ingestion of the 
prey. 
Handling of the last remains in the sieve: 
1. Optimize the use of water from a spray bottle to put together the remaining materials in the sieve. 
2. Remove water from the material by application of paper towel from the underside of the sieve. 
3. Use tweezers to lift the material from the sieve.

Digestion stages of fish prey 
1. Shiny body surface – probably with scales. Clear eyes. 
2. Intact body, which however may be discolored (brownish especially in the margins) 
3. Body cavity opened. Parts of the head region digested or deformed 
4. (a) Nothing or only something of the body cavity left; (b) tail triangle muscle mass left; (c) 
backbone with a little muscle mass; (d) only backbone left.
