Abstract
This study investigates the complex interactions between blade flexibility, ice accretion, and
wind turbine performance in cold climates. A novel computational framework integrating Com-
putational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), dynamic analysis, and ice accretion modeling is developed
to consider multi physics.
The framework is applied to DTU 10 MW reference wind tur-
bine model, comparing four configurations: fixed/adjustable rotational speed and rigid/flexible
blades. Results indicate that blade flexibility and rotational speed control significantly influence
ice formation patterns and aerodynamic performance. The study highlights the importance of
coupled fluid-structure interaction analysis in predicting wind turbine behavior under icing
conditions, with implications for design optimization and operation in cold regions.

Introduction
2.1
Introduction
Wind energy has emerged as a cornerstone in the global shift towards sustainable power sources,
with projections suggesting it could fulfill over one-third of worldwide electricity demands by
2050. This ambitious target necessitates a more than tenfold increase in global installed wind
power capacity from 2019 levels, reaching 6,000 GW (IRENA (2019)). As wind turbine tech-
nology advances, exemplified by designs like the DTU 10MW Reference Wind Turbine with
blade lengths exceeding 100 meters (Bak et al. (2013)), new challenges arise, particularly in
cold climate regions.
Ice accretion on turbine blades presents a significant obstacle to the performance, safety, and
longevity of wind energy systems. This issue is particularly acute in areas with high wind
resources but harsh winter conditions, such as Northern and Central Europe, Northern America,
and parts of Asia (Laakso et al. (2010)).
The impact of icing can be severe, with reports
indicating annual production losses of up to 20% for 15 MW turbines (Chuang et al. (2023)),
and in extreme cases, leading to prolonged shutdowns.
Traditional icing experiments, conducted through wind tunnel tests or field studies, face con-
siderable limitations. Wind tunnel tests using scaled models struggle with parameter similarity
issues (Anderson, David N. and Tsao, Jen-Ching (2003)), leading to discrepancies between
small-scale and full-scale results. Additionally, the size constraints of icing wind tunnels, diﬀi-
culties in controlling environmental parameters, and high costs (Lehtomäki et al. (2016); Shu
et al. (2018)) hinder comprehensive experimental studies. These challenges have propelled the
development of numerical icing simulations as a more versatile and cost-effective alternative.
The evolution of numerical icing simulations traces back to the early 1980s, initially focused on
aviation safety. Institutions such as National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA),
Oﬀice National d’Etudes et de Recherches Aérospatiales (ONERA), Defence Research Agency
(DRA), and Centro Italiano Ricerche Aerospaziali (CIRA) pioneered two-dimensional ice ac-
cretion solvers, which were subsequently adapted for wind turbine applications, for example, an
assessment for icing impacts on wind turbine power output by Jasinski et al. (1998) and M. C.
Homola et al. (2010). Early models employed panel methods or Euler equations with boundary
layer theory for aerodynamics, Lagrangian approaches for droplet tracking, and the Messinger
model (Messinger (1953)) for phase change prediction. While these methods provided valuable
insights, they were limited by their inability to account for rotational motion, wake effects, and
accurate droplet trajectory prediction near blade tips.
The 1990s marked a significant advancement with the development of three-dimensional icing
simulations. These models typically utilize Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equa-
tions for aerodynamic calculations and Eulerian approaches for droplet field analysis. To model
water runback, researchers have utilized either thin water film theory or integral methods that
consider both span and chordwise directions. Notable three-dimensional icing simulation codes
include FENSAP-ICE (Reid et al. (2013)) and ICECREMO (Verdin, Charpin, and Thompson
(2009)). Reid et al. (2013) have been applied on the NREL Phase VI wind turbine to assess
blade performance under icing scenarios.
However, challenges persist in adapting aircraft-
oriented models to rotating wind turbines and accurately modeling the complex interactions
between aerodynamics, droplet physics, and thermodynamics in rotating systems.
Recent studies have made strides in addressing these challenges.
Fu and Farzaneh (2010)
employed FLUENT with Moving Reference Frame (MRF) to predict ice accretion on wind
2
==PAGE:8==
turbine blades. Hu et al. (2018) developed a three-dimensional icing simulation for rime ice
conditions, incorporating MRF in the aerodynamic module. Wang and Zhu (2018) expanded on
this work by including a thermodynamic model and considering centrifugal forces in water film
behavior. Despite these advancements, limitations remain in accounting for rotational effects
across all simulation aspects and in modeling complex flow scenarios.
To address these ongoing challenges, Son and Kim (2020) developed the WISE (Wind tur-
bine Icing Simulation code with performance Evaluation) method, focusing on extending icing
simulation capabilities from fixed-wing aircraft to rotating wind turbines. Nevertheless, most
existing three-dimensional icing simulations treat aerodynamics and structural dynamics as
separate domains rather than integrated systems.
This thesis introduces a novel computational framework that integrates Computational Fluid
Dynamics (CFD), dynamic analysis, and ice accretion modeling. This approach enables the
simultaneous consideration of blade flexibility, time-varying ice shapes, and their mutual influ-
ences on aerodynamic performance. By implementing a two-way coupling between fluid and
structural solvers and incorporating an advanced ice accretion model, this framework provides
a more comprehensive analysis of wind turbine performance under icing conditions. Applied to
a reference DTU 10 MW wind turbine model (Bak et al. (2013)), this method reveals critical
insights that may be overlooked by decoupled approaches, potentially advancing our under-
standing and mitigation strategies for wind turbine icing.
The results indicate that the interaction between blade deformation and ice accretion can lead to
non-intuitive ice shapes and load distributions. It is observed that blade flexibility can alter the
local flow field in ways that significantly affect the ice accretion process, leading to asymmetric
ice formations that would not be predicted by rigid blade models. Furthermore, the study
shows that neglecting this coupling can result in significant errors in accurately predicting
ice accretion.
The framework simulations reveal that the dynamic interaction between ice
growth and blade deformation can lead to different load distributions, potentially affecting
power production. These findings have important implications for wind turbine design, and
operation in cold climates.
This work contributes to the growing body of knowledge on wind turbine performance in
extreme conditions and underscores the necessity of integrated, multi-physics approaches in
wind energy research. By providing a more accurate representation of the complex physics
involved in wind turbine icing, the presented framework enables better prediction of power
losses, structural loads, and potential failure modes. This, in turn, can inform the development
of more effective anti-icing and de-icing technologies, as well as optimization of turbine designs
for cold climate operation.
As the wind energy industry continues to expand into harsher
environments, driven by the need for clean energy and the availability of strong wind resources,
such comprehensive modeling techniques will be crucial for ensuring the reliability and eﬀiciency
of next-generation wind turbines.
In conclusion, this study lays the foundation for future research in this critical area, implying the
way for more resilient and productive wind energy systems in challenging climatic conditions.
The insights gained from this coupled fluid-structure interaction analysis not only advance
the understanding of wind turbine dynamics under icing conditions but also suggest valuable
framework for improving the design and operation of wind turbines in cold climates. Future
work may focus on extending this framework to include additional physical phenomena, such as
thermal effects and torsion of the blades, further enhancing the ability to model and mitigate
the impacts of icing on wind energy systems.

2.2
Objective and research question
2.2.1
Research Objectives
The primary objective of this research is to understand the necessity of a computational frame-
work that integrates Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), dynamic analysis, and ice accre-
tion modeling to simulate the complex interactions between blade flexibility, ice formation, and
control of large-scale wind turbines in cold climates. The objectives are listed below.
1. To develop and understand a novel computational framework that integrates Computa-
tional Fluid Dynamics (CFD), dynamic analysis, and ice accretion modeling for wind
turbine blades.
2. To investigate the coupled effects of blade flexibility, rotor control and ice accretion on
wind turbine performance in cold climate conditions.
3. Quantify the differences in predictions between coupled and decoupled analysis approaches.
4. Investigate the impact of blade flexibility and rotor control on ice accretion patterns and
resulting aerodynamic effects.
5. Evaluate the potential losses on power production.
2.2.2
Research Questions
This study aims to address the following research questions:
1. How does the integration of blade flexibility into ice accretion models affect the predicted
ice shapes and load distributions on wind turbine blades?
2. What are the key differences in ice accretion patterns and aerodynamic performance
when comparing coupled fluid-structure interaction analysis to traditional decoupled ap-
proaches?
3. How does blade flexibility influence the local flow field and subsequent ice formation
processes on large-scale wind turbine blades?
4. What are the potential errors in ice accretion prediction and performance assessment
when neglecting the coupling between blade flexibility and ice formation?
5. What are the implications of this comprehensive modeling technique for the design and
optimization of next-generation wind turbines intended for operation in harsh environ-
mental conditions?
By addressing these questions, this thesis aims to advance the understanding of wind turbine
performance under icing conditions and contribute to the development of more resilient and
eﬀicient wind energy systems for cold climate regions.

Literature review
Wind energy has become a crucial component of the global renewable energy mix, but the
operation of wind turbines in cold climates presents unique challenges. This literature review
examines the current state of knowledge regarding wind turbine icing, its impacts, prediction
methods, and modeling approaches. The review aims to provide a comprehensive overview of
the field, highlighting recent advancements and identifying areas for future research.
3.1
Impact of Icing on Wind Turbines
The impact of ice accretion on wind turbine performance has become a critical area of study as
wind energy installations expand into cold climate regions. Recent research has highlighted the
significant influence of blade icing on power output, operational characteristics, and longevity
of wind turbines, underscoring the importance of accurate icing accretion modeling.
3.1.1
Power Loss and Operational Impacts
A comprehensive study by Chuang et al. (2022) on a 15 MW wind turbine designed by NREL
revealed the substantial impact of icing on annual power production. Using a novel CFD-WTIC-
ILM multi-program coupling analysis method, they provided a holistic approach to assessing
icing impacts. Key findings include:
1. Annual Power Loss: Blade icing led to annual power losses of at least 22%, translating
to 7,810.87 MWh of lost energy production annually.
2. Altered Power Curve: Icing caused a significant shift in the turbine’s power curve,
delaying the rated wind speed from 10.59 m/s to 13 m/s.
3. Power Reduction at Different Wind Speeds:
• At rated wind speed (10.59 m/s), hourly power generation decreased by 5.564 MW,
a 38% reduction.
• In the wind speed optimization stage (Region 2 of the power curve), power loss due
to icing reached 37.48%.
5
==PAGE:11==
4. Operational Time Impact: Icing affected turbine operation for 3506.7 hours annually,
a significant portion of the year.
These results align with earlier work:
• M. Homola et al. (2011) investigated icing effects on an NREL 5 MW wind turbine using
blade element momentum theory. They concluded that ice accretion can reduce output
power by up to 27% in the wind speed optimization stage.
• Etemaddar, Hansen, and Moan (2014) reported that ice accumulation can push the max-
imum power loss to 35% under certain conditions.
The consistency in significant power losses across different turbine sizes highlights the pervasive
nature of the icing problem in wind energy generation. The variation in reported power losses
across studies underscores the complexity of the icing phenomenon and the need for more
accurate prediction models.
3.1.2
Aerodynamic Effects
The aerodynamic penalties caused by ice accretion are a primary driver of performance degra-
dation. Ice accretion on wind turbine blades significantly alters their aerodynamic properties,
leading to substantial power losses. Recent studies have provided detailed insights into these
effects.
Chuang et al. (2022) conducted a detailed analysis of the aerodynamic changes caused by icing,
focusing on the FFA-W3-211 airfoil at 95% of the blade tip. Their key findings include:
1. Lift and Drag Coeﬀicients: At 0◦angle of attack:
• The lift coeﬀicient decreases by 24.32%.
• The drag coeﬀicient increases by 165.47%.
2. Maximum Coeﬀicients:
• The maximum lift coeﬀicient is reduced by 41.19%.
• The maximum drag coeﬀicient increases by 4.67%.
3. Stall Angle: Icing causes the stall angle to advance from 14.8◦to 8◦, significantly altering
the blade’s aerodynamic performance envelope.
These changes in aerodynamic properties explain the substantial power losses observed, as they
directly affect the blade’s ability to eﬀiciently convert wind energy into rotational motion.
The findings of Chuang et al. (2022) are consistent with earlier research by Gao et al. (2021),
who conducted field studies on utility-scale wind turbines. Their work provided real-world
validation of the significant aerodynamic impacts of ice accretion, demonstrating how even
small amounts of ice can drastically alter blade performance.
The dramatic changes in lift and drag coeﬀicients, coupled with the advancement of the stall
angle, underscore the severe impact of icing on wind turbine performance. These aerodynamic
penalties are a key factor in the power losses observed in icing conditions, highlighting the
critical need for effective ice prevention and mitigation strategies in cold climate wind farms.

3.1.3
Icing Mechanisms and Patterns
Understanding the mechanisms and patterns of ice formation is crucial for developing accurate
icing accretion models. Recent studies have provided valuable insights into this complex process.
Kraj and Bibeau (2010) characterized different phases of icing on wind turbine blades, identi-
fying distinct ice formation patterns depending on atmospheric conditions. Their work high-
lighted the complexity of the icing process and the need for models that can account for various
icing scenarios.
Han, Palacios, and Schmitz (2012a) conducted scaled ice accretion experiments on rotating
wind turbine blades, providing valuable insights into how blade rotation affects ice formation.
Their findings suggested that current static icing models may not fully capture the dynamics
of ice accretion on operational wind turbines, emphasizing the need for more sophisticated
modeling approaches.
Recent studies, such as Chuang et al. (2022), have provided detailed observations of icing
patterns:
1. Icing Location: Ice accumulation is most pronounced at the leading edge of the airfoil,
with slight icing also occurring at the trailing edge.
2. Spanwise Distribution: Icing is most severe at the blade tip and decreases gradually
towards the root. This is attributed to the higher relative velocity between the blade and
air at the tip.
3. Droplet Behavior: Analysis of droplet movement and velocity distribution shows how
the airflow and blade geometry influence where ice forms on the blade surface.
These observations contribute to a better understanding of the icing process, which is crucial
for developing accurate predictive models. The complexity of ice formation patterns, influenced
by factors such as atmospheric conditions, blade rotation, and spanwise location, underscores
the need for sophisticated, multi-factor icing accretion models.
3.1.4
Computational Modeling Approaches
As computational capabilities have advanced, researchers have developed increasingly sophis-
ticated models for predicting ice accretion and its impacts on wind turbine performance.
Yirtici et al. (2019) presented a comprehensive tool for predicting icing-induced power losses,
integrating ice accretion modeling with aerodynamic performance calculations.
Their work
demonstrated the potential for integrated computational approaches to provide more accurate
assessments of icing impacts.
Building on this trend, Chuang et al. (2022) introduced a novel multi-program coupling analysis
method called CFD-WTIC-ILM:
1. CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics): Used to calculate the aerodynamic perfor-
mance of each airfoil section of the blade after icing.
2. WTIC (Wind Turbine Integrated Calculation): Performs integrated calculations
and analysis of blade motion and dynamic response of the wind turbine before and after
icing
3. ILM (Ice Loss Method): Calculates power loss based on simulated SCADA data.
This integrated approach allows for a comprehensive analysis of icing effects, from ice formation
to overall turbine performance impacts.
7
==PAGE:13==
The complexity of ice formation patterns, influenced by factors such as atmospheric conditions,
blade rotation, and spanwise location, underscores the need for sophisticated, multi-factor icing
accretion models. These advanced computational approaches contribute to a better understand-
ing of the icing process, which is crucial for developing accurate predictive models.
As computational power continues to increase, there is potential for even more detailed and
integrated models that can account for the full range of factors influencing ice accretion and
its impacts on wind turbine performance. Such models will be essential for optimizing wind
turbine design and operation in cold climate regions.
Building on this, Jin et al. (2020) compared 2D and 3D numerical approaches for studying
ice accretion on wind turbine blades. Their findings highlighted the importance of considering
three-dimensional effects in icing models, particularly for large-scale turbines where spanwise
variations in ice accretion can significantly impact overall performance.
3.1.5
Economic and Operational Implications
The economic impact of icing on wind turbines extends beyond direct power losses, affecting
operational strategies and maintenance costs.
Rissanen et al. (2016) conducted a comprehensive analysis of wind power in cold climates,
estimating that icing-related issues could affect up to 20% of global wind power capacity. This
highlights the widespread economic significance of icing in the wind energy sector.
Davis (2014) investigated the potential for using real-time icing forecasts to optimize wind
turbine operations in icing conditions. His research highlighted the practical applications of
accurate icing models in improving wind farm productivity and reducing maintenance costs
associated with icing events.
Based on the findings of Chuang et al. (2022) and other studies, the economic and operational
implications of icing include:
1. Annual Energy Loss: The 22% annual power loss translates to 7,810.87 MWh of lost
energy production, which would have substantial economic impact.
2. Operational Strategy Implications: The shift in the power curve and reduced eﬀi-
ciency at lower wind speeds suggest a need for adapted operational strategies in icing
conditions.
3. Maintenance Considerations: Although not directly addressed in the study, the sig-
nificant ice accumulation implies increased wear on turbine components, potentially lead-
ing to higher maintenance costs.
These economic and operational implications underscore the critical need for accurate icing
prediction models. Such models are essential for:
• Informing economic assessments of wind farm based on power losses in cold climates
• Developing effective operational strategies to minimize power losses
• Optimizing maintenance schedules to reduce costs and turbine downtime
Future research should focus on integrating economic modeling with icing prediction to provide
comprehensive cost-benefit analyses for wind farms in cold climates. This could include assess-
ing the economic viability of various anti-icing technologies and operational strategies under
different icing scenarios.

3.2
Icing prediction
This section mainly focuses on introducing the information about icing on airfoil and the
developed computational methods to predict the icing accretion accurately.
In-cloud icing is a critical challenge for wind turbines operating in cold climates. This phe-
nomenon occurs when supercooled water droplets suspended in the atmosphere collide with
wind turbine blades and rapidly freeze upon contact under sub-zero temperature conditions.
The nature and severity of ice formation are heavily influenced by meteorological variables,
primarily temperature, humidity, and the Median Volume Droplet diameter (MVD).
3.2.1
Types of Ice Accretion
Ice accretion on wind turbine blades can be broadly categorized into two main types:
1. Glaze Ice: Glaze ice typically forms at relatively high temperatures, ranging from just
below 0◦C to approximately -5◦C. It is associated with high Liquid Water Content (LWC)
in the atmosphere, generally exceeding 0.2 g/m3. Glaze ice is characterized by its trans-
parent, smooth appearance and strong adhesion to the blade surface.
2. Rime Ice: Rime ice, in contrast, forms under lower temperature conditions, typically
below -10◦C to -15◦C, and in environments with lower humidity (LWC below 0.2 g/m3).
Rime ice has a more opaque, brittle structure due to the rapid freezing of droplets upon
impact. It is important to note that in most real-world icing conditions, wind turbines
encounter a mixture of these ice types.

Understanding these differences is crucial for accurate modeling and the development of effective

As aforementioned in section 3.1, the accretion of ice on wind turbine blades has multifaceted
effects on turbine performance and integrity, highlighting the factors below:
• Surface Roughness and Blade Deformation: Both rime and glaze ice significantly
alter the blade surface roughness within seconds of formation. As ice accumulation con-
tinues, it can substantially deform the blade shape over periods ranging from several
minutes to hours. This deformation is particularly problematic in the case of glaze ice,
where the formation of ice horns can lead to flow separation behind these protrusions.
• Aerodynamic Degradation: The changes in blade geometry due to icing result in se-
vere aerodynamic penalties. The formation of ice horns, especially in glaze ice conditions,
9
==PAGE:15==
can cause flow separation, leading to a significant increase in drag and a decrease in lift.
Generally, the aerodynamic penalty associated with glaze ice is more severe than that of
rime ice due to its tendency to form more prominent protrusions.
• Power Generation Losses: The aerodynamic penalties caused by ice accretion trans-
late directly into decreased power generation. In severe cases, icing can lead to complete
loss of production. Recent studies have shown that power losses due to icing can range
from 20% to 50% annually in severe cold climate conditions (Chuang et al. (2022); Davis
(2014)).
• Structural Implications: Ice accumulation introduces unexpected load changes stem-
ming from modified aerodynamic forces and irregular mass distributions along the blade.
These changes can potentially induce structural fatigue damage, shortening the opera-
tional lifespan of the turbine components (Frohboese and Anders (2007)). The irregular
mass distribution can also lead to imbalances in the rotor, causing vibrations that further
stress the turbine structure.
The following section introduces the computational approaches that have been developed to
address these challenges, emphasizing the importance of developing methods to accurately
predict ice accretion.
3.2.2
Experimental Limitations in Icing Research
The evolution of methodologies for predicting ice accretion on aerodynamic surfaces, initially
introduced at the outset of this thesis, warrants further elaboration. The progression of these
techniques can be traced through distinct developmental stages.
In the nascent phase of icing research, investigations primarily focused on aircraft and airfoils,
relying heavily on physical experimentation. While icing studies can be conducted via special-
ized icing wind tunnels or field tests, both approaches present inherent limitations that merit
consideration:
• Icing Wind Tunnel Tests: These are typically performed using small-scale models.
However, the similarity parameters for scaling have not been fully established, leading to
discrepancies between the ice shapes, thrust coeﬀicients, and power coeﬀicients obtained
from scale models compared to full-scale turbines (Han, Palacios, and Schmitz (2012a);
Shu et al. (2017)).
• Full-Scale Testing: The sheer size of modern wind turbines precludes full-scale testing
in existing icing wind tunnels. Furthermore, field tests, while valuable, present diﬀiculties
in controlling meteorological parameters and accurately measuring ice accretion shapes
(Lehtomäki et al. (2016); Shu et al. (2018)).
Moreover, the variability of atmospheric conditions in cold climates makes it challenging to
replicate real-world icing scenarios in controlled environments. Factors such as temperature
fluctuations, varying wind speeds, and changing humidity levels all contribute to the complexity
of icing events.
Accurately measuring ice accretion shapes and thicknesses on operational wind turbines poses
significant technical challenges. Remote sensing techniques are still in development, and direct
measurements often require turbine shutdown, which is not always feasible or economically
viable.
10
==PAGE:16==
Both wind tunnel testing and field experiments for icing studies are notably expensive, limiting
the breadth and depth of experimental research that can be conducted. This economic con-
straint has driven the need for more cost-effective research methods, particularly in the realm
of numerical simulations.
3.2.3
Evolution of Numerical Icing Simulations
Given the limitations and challenges associated with experimental studies, numerical icing
simulations have emerged as a crucial tool in wind turbine icing research. These computational
approaches offer several advantages:
• Ability to study a wide range of icing conditions and turbine designs
• Cost-effectiveness compared to full-scale experimental studies
• Capacity to provide detailed insights into the icing process and its effects on turbine
performance
• Flexibility to investigate hypothetical scenarios and extreme conditions that may be dif-
ficult or impossible to replicate experimentally
The development and refinement of these numerical tools represent a critical area of ongoing
research in the field of wind energy, particularly as the industry continues to expand into
cold climate regions. The following sections will delve into the history and current state of
numerical icing simulations, highlighting their evolution and the challenges that remain in
accurately modeling the complex phenomenon of wind turbine blade icing.
Early Developments in icing simulation
The field of numerical icing simulations has its
roots in aviation safety research dating back to the early 1980s. This period marked a significant
shift from purely experimental approaches to computational methods, driven by the need for
more eﬀicient and cost-effective means of studying ice accretion on aircraft.
Initially, two-dimensional ice accretion solvers were developed by prominent aerospace institu-
tions:
• National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)
• Oﬀice National d’Etudes et de Recherches Aérospatiales (ONERA)
• Defence Research Agency (DRA)
• Centro Italiano Ricerche Aerospaziali (CIRA)
These institutions pioneered the development of computational models that could predict ice
formation on airfoils under various atmospheric conditions. The work of Wright, Gent, and
Guffond (1997) was particularly influential, laying the groundwork for subsequent advancements
in icing simulation technology.
As the wind energy sector expanded in the late 20th and early 21st centuries, researchers
recognized the potential to adapt aircraft icing methodologies for wind turbine applications.
This transition was driven by the growing awareness of icing as a significant challenge for wind
turbines operating in cold climates. A notable development in this transition was the TURBICE
tool, created by VTT Technical Research Centre. TURBICE, as described by Makkonen et
al. (2001), was specifically designed for wind turbine icing simulations, marking one of the first
dedicated computational tools for this purpose.by applying the Messinger model (Messinger
(1953)).
11
==PAGE:17==
The initial approaches to icing simulation were characterized by several key features:
• Aerodynamic Modeling: Early models primarily used panel methods or Euler equa-
tions combined with boundary layer theory. These methods provided a reasonable ap-
proximation of the flow field around clean airfoils but had limitations in capturing the
complex flow patterns around iced surfaces.
• Water Droplet Tracking: Lagrangian approaches were predominantly used for tracking
water droplets. In this method, individual droplets or parcels of droplets are followed as
they move through the air and impact the surface. While computationally intensive, this
approach offered detailed insights into droplet behavior.
• Phase Change Modeling: The Messinger model, introduced by Messinger (1953), was
widely adopted for predicting phase changes of impinging water. This model provided a
thermodynamic framework for determining whether impinging water freezes or runs back
along the surface.
These early models, while groundbreaking, had significant limitations in their ability to capture
the full complexity of ice accretion on wind turbine blades, particularly the three-dimensional
and rotational aspects of the problem.
Advanced Two-Dimensional Icing Simulations
As the field progressed, early efforts in
wind turbine icing simulation focused on two-dimensional models. These models represented
a significant step forward in understanding ice accretion on wind turbine blades, although
they were still limited in their ability to capture the full complexity of the problem. Notable
contributions in this area include:
• M. C. Homola et al. (2010) utilized TURBICE, employing panel methods with boundary
layer theory. Their work was significant in adapting icing simulation techniques specifi-
cally for wind turbine applications, considering the unique operational conditions of wind
turbines.
• Han, Palacios, and Schmitz (2012a) introduced the use of FLUENT, a commercial compu-
tational fluid dynamics (CFD) package, for icing simulations. Their approach was based
on Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations, which offered a more sophisti-
cated treatment of the flow field compared to panel methods. This allowed for improved
predictions of lift and drag coeﬀicients for iced airfoils.
However, these 2D approaches had limitations:
• Rotational Effects: Due to their reliance on blade element theory (BET), these models
were unable to account for the complex rotational motion of wind turbine blades and
the associated wake effects. This limitation was particularly significant for large, modern
wind turbines where rotational effects play a crucial role in both aerodynamics and ice
accretion patterns.
• Tip Region Inaccuracies: The Lagrangian approaches commonly used for droplet
tracking in these models showed inaccuracies in predicting droplet trajectories near blade
tips. This region is critical in wind turbine performance and is particularly susceptible
to ice accretion due to high relative velocities.
• Thermodynamic Limitations: The inviscid assumptions often used in these models
limited their ability to accurately predict heat transfer rates. This limitation was partic-
ularly problematic for predicting glaze ice formation, where heat transfer plays a crucial
role in determining the freezing fraction of impinging water.
12
==PAGE:18==
• Power Generation Penalties: The simplified aerodynamic models used in these 2D
approaches had limited ability to accurately predict the power generation penalties asso-
ciated with ice accretion, particularly those resulting from induced flow separation caused
by ice formations.
Advancement to Three-Dimensional Simulations
The 1990s marked a significant leap
forward in icing simulation capabilities with the emergence of three-dimensional models. This
advancement was driven by increases in computational power and a growing understanding of
the limitations of 2D approaches. The key developments in this era included:
• RANS-based Aerodynamics: The widespread adoption of Reynolds-Averaged Navier-
Stokes (RANS) equations for aerodynamic modeling represented a major improvement.
RANS models provided a more accurate representation of the turbulent flow around
wind turbine blades, crucial for predicting both the local flow field and the resulting ice
accretion patterns.
• Eulerian Droplet Tracking:
A shift from Lagrangian to Eulerian approaches for
droplet field calculations marked a significant advancement.
Eulerian methods treat
the droplet phase as a continuum, allowing for more eﬀicient and often more accurate
predictions of droplet impingement patterns, particularly in complex 3D geometries.
• Advanced Water Film Modeling: The introduction of thin water film theory (Beau-
gendre, Morency, and Habashi (2005)) and integral methods (Zhu et al. (2012)) for mod-
eling water runback provided a more sophisticated treatment of the liquid water behavior
on blade surfaces. These models allowed for better prediction of glaze ice formation,
where the movement of unfrozen water plays a crucial role in determining ice shapes.
• Comprehensive 3D Icing Codes: The development of comprehensive 3D icing codes
like FENSAP-ICE (Reid et al. (2013)) and ICECREMO (Verdin, Charpin, and Thomp-
son (2009)) marked a new era in icing simulation.
These codes integrated advanced
aerodynamics, droplet impingement, and thermodynamic models into cohesive packages
capable of simulating the full icing process on complex 3D geometries.
These advancements allowed for a much more realistic simulation of ice accretion on wind
turbine blades, capturing 3D effects, rotational impacts, and complex ice shapes that were
beyond the capabilities of earlier 2D models.
This evolution reflects the increasing complexity of wind turbine designs and the growing de-
mand for accurate performance predictions under various icing conditions. The transition from
2D to 3D models, in particular, has been driven by the recognition that spanwise variations in
ice accretion can significantly impact overall turbine performance.
3.2.4
Recent Developments in 3D Icing Simulation
In recent years, several notable studies have further advanced the field of 3D icing simulation
for wind turbines:
• Reid et al. (2013) applied FENSAP-ICE to the NREL Phase VI turbine, a well-documented
experimental turbine often used as a benchmark in wind energy research. Their work was
significant in incorporating detailed thermodynamic considerations into the icing simu-
lation, allowing for more accurate predictions of ice shapes under various atmospheric
conditions.
13
==PAGE:19==
• Fu and Farzaneh (2010) utilized FLUENT with the Moving Reference Frame (MRF)
approach for ice shape prediction on NREL Phase VI blades. The MRF method allowed
for a more computationally eﬀicient way of accounting for blade rotation, a critical factor
in wind turbine icing that was often overlooked in earlier models.
• Hu et al. (2018) developed a specialized 3D simulation code for rime ice conditions,
applying the MRF method in FLUENT to account for rotational effects. Their work was
particularly notable for its focus on the unique challenges posed by rime ice formation on
wind turbine blades.
• Wang and Zhu (2018) made significant strides by employing both MRF and advanced
thermodynamic modeling to study both rime and glaze ice conditions. Their work repre-

range of icing conditions encountered by wind turbines.
These recent studies have significantly advanced our understanding of ice accretion on wind
turbine blades and have paved the way for more accurate and comprehensive icing simulations.
3.2.5
Development of WISE
Building on these advancements, one of the latest icing code which is WISE aims to further
extend icing simulation capabilities, with a particular focus on the transition from fixed-wing
aircraft to rotating wind turbines. Key aspects of this research include:
• Advanced Aerodynamic Modeling: The application of the Moving Reference Frame
(MRF) method in conjunction with RANS equations provides a sophisticated approach to
modeling the complex aerodynamics around rotating wind turbine blades. This method
allows for eﬀicient simulation of rotational effects without the need for time-dependent
calculations.
• Eulerian Droplet Tracking: The use of Eulerian approaches for droplet field calcula-
tions offers improved accuracy and eﬀiciency in predicting droplet impingement patterns,
particularly important for the complex geometry and flow fields associated with wind
turbine blades.
• Advanced Water Film Modeling: Implementation of a thin water film model provides
a more realistic simulation of runback water behavior, crucial for accurate prediction of
glaze ice formation and resulting ice shapes.
• Improved Shear Force Calculation: The derivation of air shear forces directly from
RANS equations, rather than relying on boundary layer theory, offers a more accurate
representation of the forces driving water film movement on the blade surface.
The developed code has been validated against existing numerical simulations, with the NREL
Phase VI turbine serving as a baseline for comparisons. This choice was necessitated by the
scarcity of public domain experimental data on 3D wind turbine ice accretion, highlighting an
ongoing challenge in the field.

3.3.1
Overview and Structure of WISE
To investigate ice accretion on wind turbine blades, this study employs the structure of Wind
turbine Icing Simulation code with performance Evaluation (WISE). WISE is an extension of
the Ice Shape Evaluation and Performance Analysis Code (ISEPAC), originally developed for
fixed-wing aircraft icing analysis (Son and Yee (2017)). The structure of WISE, as illustrated
in Figure 2, comprises four main modules:
1. Aerodynamic module
2. Droplet field module
3. Thermodynamic module
4. Ice growth module
The simulation process in WISE follows a sequential approach:
1. The aerodynamic module calculates converged velocity vectors.
2. These vectors are used to compute droplet trajectories in the droplet field module.
3. The thermodynamic module then performs thermal analyses.
4. This process is iterated for a specified number of steps.
WISE is developed on the OpenFoam v1712 platform, offering several advantages:
• Seamless data transfer between modules
• Extensive parallel computing capabilities via OpenMPI
• Open-source architecture allowing user modifications Greenshields (2023)
The choice of OpenFoam as the development platform enhances the accessibility and adaptabil-
ity of WISE, making it a valuable tool for both academic research and industry applications.
WISE addresses several limitations of previous models:
1. Integrated Approach: Unlike many earlier codes that treated aerodynamics, droplet
transport, and thermodynamics as separate modules, WISE provides a more tightly inte-
grated framework. This integration allows for better capture of the complex interactions
between these processes.
2. Computational Eﬀiciency: Through the use of adaptive mesh refinement and par-
allelization techniques, WISE achieves improved computational eﬀiciency compared to
many existing 3D icing codes.
This eﬀiciency makes it more suitable for parametric
studies and optimization tasks.
3. Flexibility: The modular structure of WISE allows for easy incorporation of new physical
models or numerical methods as they become available. This ensures that the code can
evolve with the state of the art in icing research.

between impinging water and ice formation. The energy conservation equation (Equation 13)
encompasses various heat transfer processes, including the kinetic energy of impinging droplets,
latent heat released during freezing, and convective heat transfer with the surrounding air.

approach that leverages physical constraints and conservation laws.

The treatment of ice density in the model is an important consideration, particularly given the

rime ice scenarios (Buser and Aufdermaur (1973)).
In the current implementation of WISE, a constant ice density of 920 kg/m3 is applied. This
simplification is justified for the moderate icing events that are the focus of this study, as
supported by the findings of Aliaga Rivera (2008). Their research indicated that ice density
does not vary dramatically under most conditions encountered in wind turbine icing.

This comprehensive approach to ice growth modeling, combined with the sophisticated thermo-

on wind turbine blades under a variety of operational and environmental conditions.
3.3.3
Model Assumptions and Limitations
The current version of WISE operates under several key assumptions, which are important to
consider when interpreting simulation results:
• Rigid blade structure: The model assumes that the blades are rigid, neglecting structural
vibrations and gravitational bending. This simplification reduces computational com-
plexity but may limit accuracy in scenarios where blade flexibility significantly affects ice
accretion patterns.
• Quasi-steady state conditions: WISE assumes quasi-steady state conditions, justified by
the disparity between icing phenomenon timescales (minutes to hours) and geometric
deformation timescales (seconds) (Ruff and Berkowitz (1990)). This assumption allows
for more eﬀicient computation but may not capture rapid transient effects in highly
dynamic icing conditions.
• Neglect of certain physical phenomena: The current model does not account for phenom-
ena such as ice shedding or the impact of blade pitch changes during operation. These
limitations should be considered when applying the model to real-world scenarios.
While these assumptions and limitations are necessary for computational tractability, they may
affect the accuracy of results under certain conditions. For example:
1. Rigid Blade Structure: The assumption of a rigid blade structure may lead to overes-
timation of ice accretion, particularly near the blade tips where flexibility can alter local
flow conditions.
2. Quasi-Steady State Assumption: This assumption may not capture transient effects
during rapid changes in atmospheric conditions or turbine operational parameters.
3. Neglecting Ice Shedding: Ignoring ice shedding could result in overestimation of ice
loads and performance degradation over long simulation periods.
Ongoing research aims to address these limitations through improved physical models and
computational techniques.

Summary of WISE
WISE, an extension of ISEPAC, is a comprehensive tool for simulating ice accretion on wind
turbine blades. Its structure comprises four main modules: aerodynamic, droplet field, ther-
modynamic, and ice growth. Key features and advancements include:

Aerodynamic solver
The novel framework proposed in this thesis incorporates an investigation under shear wind
conditions. This approach addresses limitations in the aeroelastic method coupled with the
WISE model, which does not account for blade torsional deflection. To compensate and induce
more significant impacts on the airfoils, wind shear was introduced into the model.
Wind shear creates spatial variations in the flow field relative to blade position, resulting in
oscillating inflow conditions for the wind turbine. This complex scenario requires a more sophis-
ticated solver than the original WISE model. Consequently, a density-based solver, previously
implemented in ICS-FOAM (Oliani, Casari, and Carnevale (2023)), has been adapted for this
study.
Built upon the ICS-FOAM library, which provides a framework for implicit coupled simulations
in OpenFOAM, this solver is well-suited for handling complex flow conditions introduced by
wind shear. Key features include:
• Compressible Flow Handling: Accurately models compressible flow phenomena, crucial
for capturing wind shear effects on blade aerodynamics.
• Shock-Capturing Capabilities: Resolves shock waves and discontinuities, accurately rep-
resenting transonic flow regimes at blade tips under certain wind conditions.
• Implicit Time Integration: Allows for larger time steps, improving computational eﬀi-
ciency while maintaining stability in rapidly changing flow conditions.
• Coupled Equation System: Employs a block-coupled matrix structure for simultaneous
solution of mass, momentum, and energy equations, capturing intricate interactions be-
tween flow variables in sheared wind conditions.

• Advanced Turbulence Modeling: Maintains compatibility with OpenFOAM’s built-in tur-
bulence models, allowing accurate representation of turbulent flows in wind shear condi-

This density-based solver represents a significant advancement over the original WISE model
for wind turbine icing simulation. By accurately capturing oscillating inflow, it enables a more
comprehensive analysis of ice accretion patterns and their impact on blade performance under
realistic atmospheric conditions.
The solver’s ability to handle compressible flows and shock phenomena provides a robust foun-

This capability is particularly important as wind turbine designs evolve, with
larger rotor diameters and more flexible blade structures becoming increasingly common.

Structural Modeling for Wind Turbine Blades

3.5.1

The one-dimensional momentum theory, dating back to the early days of ship propeller design,
provides a fundamental framework for understanding wind turbine aerodynamics. This theory
(Hansen (2015)) considers the flow as one-dimensional and ideal, with changes occurring only
in the flow direction.

1. The rotor is modeled as an actuator disc, creating a discontinuity in pressure.
2. The flow is steady, incompressible, and frictionless.
3. There is no rotation of the wake.

Limitations of One-Dimensional Theory:
While the one-dimensional momentum theory
provides valuable insights, it has several limitations:
1. It does not account for the effects of wake rotation, which can be significant, especially
for high-speed rotors.
2. It assumes uniform flow across the rotor, neglecting radial variations that occur in real
wind turbines.
3. It does not consider the finite number of blades or their geometry, which affect the flow
field and turbine performance.
4. The theory breaks down at high axial induction factors (a > 0.5), where the assumptions
of steady, incompressible flow are no longer valid.
These limitations led to the development of more sophisticated models, including the Blade
Element Momentum (BEM) theory, which addresses many of these shortcomings by combining
momentum theory with blade element analysis.
Extension to Blade Element Momentum Theory
The Blade Element Momentum (BEM)
theory, developed by Glauert (1963), extends the one-dimensional momentum theory by incor-
porating the aerodynamic characteristics of the blade airfoils. This approach divides the blade
into multiple elements and applies both momentum theory and blade element theory to each
section.
3.5.2
Blade Element Momentum (BEM) Theory
The Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory is a fundamental approach in wind turbine aero-
dynamics, extending the one-dimensional momentum theory by incorporating the aerodynamic
characteristics of blade airfoils. This method provides a computationally eﬀicient means to
calculate aerodynamic loads along the blade span, from which the overall dynamics of the wind
turbine can be determined.

BEM theory divides the blade into discrete elements and iteratively calculates the induced
velocities to determine the aerodynamic forces acting on each element. The key aspects of
BEM implementation include:
• Incoming wind velocity: Wind shear causes the inflow velocity to oscillate based on
the blade’s position. This oscillation is crucial in reflecting real-world conditions where
wind speed varies with height, resulting in differential loading along the blade length.
• Rotational speed: As the investigation focuses on a location near the blade tip, ro-
tational speed significantly influences the velocity angle. The tip region experiences the
highest tangential velocities, making it crucial for power generation and susceptible to
icing effects.
• Relative velocity: The velocity triangle varies over time based on the velocity com-
ponents, affecting the relative velocity. It is primarily influenced by incoming velocity
and rotational speed. Additionally, wake effects and blade deflection velocity can be im-
portant factors that alter the relative velocity, especially in icing conditions where blade
aerodynamics may be compromised.
• Angle of attack (AoA): Determined by the relative velocity on the airfoil sections, the
AoA is crucial for optimizing power production from the wind turbine. Ice accretion can
significantly alter the effective AoA, potentially leading to reduced eﬀiciency or even stall
conditions.

Challenges in Applying BEM to Iced Blades
While BEM theory provides a robust
framework for analyzing wind turbine performance, its application to iced blades presents
several challenges:
1. Modified Airfoil Shapes: Ice accretion alters the effective shape of airfoil sections,
changing their lift and drag characteristics. This requires updating the Cl and Cd data
used in the BEM calculations.
2. Increased Mass and Inertia: The additional mass of ice affects the dynamic behavior
of the blades, potentially leading to imbalances and increased loads. This may require
modifications to the structural model coupled with BEM.
3. Changed Stiffness Properties: Ice formation can modify the stiffness distribution
along the blade, affecting its vibrational characteristics and potentially altering the as-
sumed mode shapes used in aeroelastic analysis.
28
==PAGE:34==
4. Non-uniform Ice Distribution: Ice typically accumulates non-uniformly along the
blade span and chord, creating complex load distributions that may violate the BEM
assumption of independent elements.
To address these challenges, advanced modeling techniques are required that can integrate icing
effects into the BEM framework. This often involves:
• Coupling BEM with ice accretion models to update airfoil data dynamically.
• Incorporating structural dynamic analysis tools to account for changed mass and stiffness
properties.
• Implementing correction factors or modified BEM algorithms to account for the break-
down of certain assumptions under icing conditions.
• Utilizing CFD simulations to inform BEM models about complex flow behaviors around
iced airfoils.

In this study, WISE is extended to investigate the icing accretion under oscillating inflow,

• Collection eﬀiciency computation
• Thermodynamic analysis for phase change
• Ice growth and shape prediction

4.3.4
Solver and model selection for CFD
The introduction of oscillating inflow conditions for the simulations necessitated modifications
to the solvers in each module to enhance prediction accuracy. The following list outlines the
modifications, contrasting them with the solvers used in WISE:
• Aerodynamic module: Density-based solver (dbnsFoam solver)
• Turbulence model: k −ω SST
• Droplet impingement module: Eulerian
• Temporal discretization (for transient simulations): Dual time stepping
• Droplet flux for spacial scheme: First-order upwind
The density-based solver is typically employed for high-speed flows (Mach number > 0.3) and
compressible fluids. The dbnsFoam solver has been developed with enhancements to resolve
shock waves, providing improved calculations for the aerodynamic module compared to WISE.
Moreover, the k−ω SST turbulence model is more suitable for high Reynolds number cases than
the Spalart-Allmaras model. It also offers advantages in interpreting external flow problems
around complex geometries, in this case, generated by icing.

4.4
Aeroelastic Analysis
To account for blade flexibility and its impact on ice accretion, aeroelastic analysis is incor-
porated into the methodology. This approach combines aerodynamic load calculations with
structural dynamics to provide a comprehensive picture of blade behavior under icing condi-
tions.
4.4.1
Table Look-up Update Logic for Aerodynamic Coeﬀicients
The aerodynamic coeﬀicient tables used in the BEM calculations are updated based on the
CFD results for the iced airfoil. This update process ensures that the BEM model accurately
reflects the changes in airfoil performance due to ice accretion. The update process involves
the following steps:
• Calculate the mean values of coeﬀicients for the clean airfoil from CFD simulations.
• Run the BEM model to find the coeﬀicient values at the same inflow conditions as the
CFD simulations.
• Obtain the coeﬀicients of the iced airfoil from CFD simulations.
• Update the BEM table based on the ratio between the values of the clean airfoil and the
iced airfoil from CFD results.
This methodology allows for a dynamic update of the aerodynamic coeﬀicients used in the
BEM calculations, accounting for the effects of ice accretion on the airfoil’s performance. By
incorporating CFD results, the approach captures complex flow phenomena that may not be
adequately represented in standard airfoil data tables.
4.4.2
Dynamic Analysis Model
The dynamic analysis model is based on the equation of motion to obtain the values for blade
deflection. The loads on the wind turbine are calculated using the Blade Element Momentum
(BEM) theory, while the calculation for deflection and dynamics is achieved using the Newmark
method. This comprehensive model accounts for:
• Aerodynamic loads on the wind turbine obtained through BEM
• Mass distribution of wind turbine components
• Stiffness characteristics of wind turbine components
36
==PAGE:42==
• Damping properties of wind turbine components
The structural model utilizes the material properties and layup details of the DTU 10MW
Reference Wind Turbine (RWT) blade (Bak et al. (2013)). This approach allows for a realistic
representation of the blade’s structural behavior under various loading conditions, including
those induced by icing.
Once the dynamics of the wind turbine are solved, both flapwise and edgewise bending can be
determined. The solution methodology, including the implementation of the Newmark method
and the handling of time-varying loads, is elaborated in section 3.5.3.
While the current model provides valuable insights into blade dynamics, further refinement is
necessary to fully capture the effects of icing on the structural behavior. Future work should
focus on incorporating the time-varying nature of ice accretion and its impact on mass distri-
bution, stiffness characteristics, and damping properties of the blade.

may be an artifact of these modeling limitations rather than a true representation of physical
behavior. Therefore, these results should be interpreted with caution.
Future work should focus on improving mesh refinement techniques to more accurately capture
the complex geometry changes caused by ice accretion. Additionally, experimental validation
of these computational results would greatly enhance our understanding of the drag behavior
under icing conditions.

thrust behavior under icing conditions. It suggests that strategies effective in maintaining lift
performance may also be beneficial for thrust maintenance. This relationship could be partic-
ularly useful in developing control strategies for icing conditions, where maintaining adequate
thrust is crucial for turbine stability and performance.

Conclusion
This study investigated the complex interactions between blade flexibility, ice accretion, and
wind turbine performance in cold climates through the development and application of a novel
computational framework. By integrating Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), dynamic
67
==PAGE:73==
analysis, and ice accretion modeling, this research has provided valuable insights into the be-
havior of wind turbines under icing conditions. The key findings and conclusions are as follows:
1. Icing Shape Predictions: The coupled framework provided detailed predictions of ice
shapes under various conditions, showing how blade flexibility and operational parameters
influence ice distribution. This information is crucial for developing effective de-icing and
anti-icing strategies.
2. Importance of Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI): The study demonstrated that
blade flexibility significantly influences ice accretion patterns and aerodynamic perfor-
mance. Flexible blades (C3 and C4) consistently showed lower drag coeﬀicients and more
gradual changes in lift coeﬀicients compared to rigid blades (C1 and C2) under icing con-
ditions. This highlights the critical importance of considering FSI in wind turbine design
for cold climates.
3. Impact of Rotational Speed Control: Adjustable rotational speed strategies (C2 and
C4) showed advantages and disadvantages in lift and power coeﬀicients after initial icing
events depending on blade flexibility. This suggests that adaptive control strategies needs
to be chosen by considering blade feature.
4. Complex Interplay of Factors: The study revealed counterintuitive results, such as
rigid blades (C1) maintaining higher power coeﬀicients despite flexible blades (C3 and C4)
having better lift to drag ratios. This underscores the complex, non-linear interactions
between blade flexibility, rotational speed, and ice accretion.
Addressing the initial research questions, this study has:
• Demonstrated that integrating blade flexibility into ice accretion models significantly
affects predicted ice shapes and load distributions.
• Identified key differences between coupled FSI analysis and traditional decoupled ap-
proaches, particularly in predicting aerodynamic performance under icing conditions.
• Shown how blade flexibility influences local flow fields and subsequent ice formation pro-
cesses, leading to different ice accretion patterns compared to rigid blade models.
• Highlighted potential errors in neglecting the coupling between blade flexibility and ice
formation, especially in predicting power and thrust coeﬀicients.
• Provided insights into the design and optimization of wind turbines for cold climates,
emphasizing the need for integrated, multi-physics approaches.
