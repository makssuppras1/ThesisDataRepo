Abstract 
 
Monopiles are the most commonly applied support structure for foundations 
in offshore wind turbines. The many advantages of the monopiles have 
sparked the development and use of supersize monopiles applicable on deep 
waters. Due to the method of fabrication of monopiles which consists of 
joining rolled can sections together by welding, residual stresses are 
introduced in the structure. These can be in the magnitude of the yield 
strength affecting the fatigue strength. 
This thesis investigates the residual stresses present in butt welds of different 
sizes as a diminished investigation of how an increased wall thickness for 
supersize monopiles impact the residual stress state. Two plates have been 
investigated, one with a thickness of 20 mm and one with a thickness of 10 
mm. 
The investigation is performed by FEM modelling of the two welded plates 
on the basis of information about the welding procedure. The welding has 
been simulated by sequentially coupled thermo-mechanical analysis with the 
Abaqus Welding Interface. 
Neutron diffraction measurements of the 20 mm plate were used to 
investigate the validity of the model. Subsequently the numerical results for 
the 20 mm plate and the 10 mm plate were evaluated against each other. 
The numerical investigations as well as neutron diffraction measurements 
found that the longitudinal residual stresses exceeded the yield stress for 
both plates. Comparisons of the numerical acquired residual stresses showed 
the largest residual stresses to be present in the larger weld.

Introduction 
 
1.1 Offshore substructures - Monopiles 
The first offshore wind farm in the world Vindeby wind farm was installed in Denmark in 1991. The harvesting 
of energy from offshore wind has grown into a massive industry since then. This industry growth accompanies 
an interest in design and optimization of offshore wind structures. This chapter gives an introduction to a 
specific part of the offshore wind structures, namely the support and foundational structures. 
A number of different types of support structures for wind turbines exist. These include jacket structures, 
tripod structures, gravity base structures, floating structures and of course the monopile. Different forms of 
support structures are shown in Figure 1. The gravity base and monopile foundations are typically used at 
shallow to medium water depths whereas the jacket and tripod foundation are applicable to deep waters. 
 
Figure 1: Offshore wind turbine foundations (O2). 
The most commonly used structure is the monopile as it is the easiest structure to install in shallow and 
medium water depths. The installation method is typically driving the monopile into the seabed by hydraulic 
hammering. A transition piece is attached to the monopile and the tower with the wind turbine is attached 
on top. The monopile itself is made up from cylindrical steel tube elements welded together to obtain the 
desired monopile length. 
Due to the advantages of easier installation and construction by the use of monopile foundations new wind 
farm projects have started to incorporate supersize monopiles as a foundation method for deep waters 
replacing the traditional use of jacket foundations for such purposes. The gains are expected to come as a 
consequence of cost reduction in installation and manufacturing. The new supersize monopiles are giant 
structures with diameters of as much as 11 meters. The development in the diameter of monopiles is 
reflected by the production capability of manufacturers such as Sif Group who have continuously expanded 
the diameter of their monopiles from 7.8 meters in 2014 to 11 meters today. The pictures in Figure 2 shows 
the scale of supersize monopiles with a diameter of 7.5 meters. The figure also illustrates how the monopile 
is constructed from the steel tube elements welded together circumferentially. 
1 
 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
Figure 2: Steel tube element and Supersize monopile taking form (O3). 
1.1.1 Design factors 
Monopile structures are exposed to rough environments and even more so when they are placed at deep 
waters far from the coast. The monopile will experience lateral and cyclic load as well as bending from 
current, wind and waves. The large cyclic loads are especially problematic due to the production method of 
the monopile. The welding of monopiles induces large tensile residual stresses which is a concern in the 
fatigue assessment of the structure. In the general guidelines the fatigue design of structures containing 
residual stresses should apply residual stresses of yield strength magnitude (Eurocode 3). However if it is 
possible to proof that the residual stresses are of lower magnitude, reduction of the design stresses is 
possible. 
1.2 Line of work/Scope of work 
The first part of this thesis will contain the theoretical background of the focus in the thesis. The importance 
of fatigue life and the factors that affect it will be outlined as well as a specific look on welds and how welds 
are specifically important from a fatigue perspective. Welding in general will be discussed but with an 
emphasis on the welding techniques that were used for the experimentally investigated welds namely 
Submerged Arc Welding (SAW). The welding technique will be explained as well as the thermo-mechanical 
implications it has on the final product and how temperature fields and heat propagation contributes to 
residual stresses emerging. There will be a theoretical clarification of residual stresses. This will go through 
the nature of residual stresses, how they come to be but also how they can be measured and investigated. 
The thesis will touch on some different methods just to give an idea of the vast variety of residual stress 
measurement methods. A chapter is dedicated to the specific measurement technique neutron diffraction as 
that is the experimental method applied for this thesis. It will discuss the theoretical background, advantage 
and disadvantages and the practical workings of a diffractometer. 
The second part will cover the experimental neutron diffraction measurements. This includes a description 
of the experimental setup of the HZB E3 diffractometer and the relevant technical details. Furthermore it 
presents and treats the findings of the measurements. 
 
The third part of the thesis contains Finite Element Method calculations. The investigation consists of 2 
compared SAW welded plates of different thicknesses. It will go through setting up the models using ABAQUS

Welding 
 
Welding is the process in which components are joined together by metallurgical bonding of two otherwise 
separate pieces. This chapter will present the welding type investigated in this thesis. Furthermore a 
presentation of weld terminology and the propagation of heat from welding will be given. 
2.1 Welding methods 
 
Welding is a very large field and thus a wide variety of different welding types exists. Each welding type has 
its own strengths and weaknesses and the type used is heavily dependent on the circumstances and 
application. The different types of welding can generally be sorted under three categories (Sindo kou, 2003): 
• 
Gas welding 
• 
Arc welding 
• 
High energy beam-welding 
This thesis will primarily be concerned with arc welding or more specifically submerged arc welding as this 
is the welding method applied in the investigations. 
2.1.1 Submerged arc welding 
Submerged arc welding is an arc welding process. Arc welding heats up metal and joins them by establishing 
an arc between the welding electrode and the base metal. The arc is an ionic gas that conducts electricity. 
For submerged arc welding the arc is submerged underneath a granular flux, see Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3: Submerged arc welding (Sindo Kou, 2003). 
4 
 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
As seen from Figure 3 the electrode holder is connected to the power source at one pole and the base metal 
(work piece) is connected to the other thereby creating the electric arc providing heat to melt the metals. 
The power source can be both AC and DC and sometimes both are used in tandem. The wire electrode for 
submerged arc welding is consumable and is a part of the weld. A granular flux is supplied from a hopper to 
protect the molten metal against atmospheric gasses such as nitrogen, oxygen and hydrogen. Some flux is 
also consumed and the remaining non-consumed flux can be reused. Because the flux is consumed the 
composition of it will contribute to the composition of the weld metal thus the weld material is composed as 
a combination of the base metal, electrode (filler) and flux.  
2.1.2 Polarity 
When welding with a DC power source the characteristics of the weld differ depending on the electrode 
polarity. The polarity can be either Direct-Current Electrode Negative (DCEN) or Direct-Current Electrode 
Positive (DCEP). The DCEP polarity provides deepest penetration and the best bead shape and resistance to 
porosity (Houldcroft, 1989). DCEP is often preferred however DCEN can be utilized when low penetration is 
required. The flux/wire consumption ratio differs for the two so the choice of polarity also influences the 
composition of the weld metal. 
 
2.2 Welding procedure specification 
 
A welding procedure specification (WPS) is a means of qualification of welding procedure. The WPS is 
established by manufacturers to specify a welding procedure guideline to be followed by the welder. The 
WPS is supposed to ensure that the welding process meets the requirements from the existing standards and 
codes (e.g. DS/EN 1011 etc.). 
To qualify the welding procedure the welded joints are tested to make sure they meet the requirements. The 
welding conditions for a test that meets the requirements are recorded on a procedure qualification record 
(PQR). 
2.3 Welding anatomy 
 
Figure 4 shows the characteristics of a weld. The surface of the weld is termed the face. The reinforcement 
is the part of the weld that reaches beyond the work piece surface. The toe is the intersection between 
weld surface and work piece surface.  
5 
 
 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
Figure 4: Weldment overview (Welders universe, 2016). 
The weld can be divided into 3 regions divided by the impact that the thermal load has on these. These are 
the fusion zone, surrounded by the heat-affected zone (HAZ) which is again surrounded by the unaffected 
base material. The last two are not shown in the figure. The fusion zone consists of the zones that reach 
melting temperature i.e. the molten weld pool (filler, flux and base metal). The heat affected zone consists 
of the zones that are not heated enough to melt but enough to cause microstructural and material property 
changes.

transformation strains arise in the fusion- and heat-affected zone.  This section gives an introduction to the 
heat input and heat propagation of welding.

Residual stresses 
 
Residual stresses are internal stresses present without the application of external forces. These stresses are 
in equilibrium with themselves i.e. compressive stresses cancel out tensile stresses. Eurocode 3 defines 
residual stress as: “A permanent state of stress in a structure that is in static equilibrium and is independent 
of any applied action”.  
This chapter elaborates on the general causes of residual stresses as well as the influence of welding. 
Furthermore it presents methods of manipulating residual stress and methods on how to measure these 
stresses. 
Residual stresses can emerge in several ways and it is very difficult to avoid some stresses appearing during 
manufacturing and manipulation of materials. Typically the internal stresses emerge from thermal Non-
uniform plastic deformation any type of surface modification or thermal manipulation. The residual stresses 
can be divided in to three types of stresses: Type 1, type 2 and type 3. Also referred to as first order (σI), 
second order(σII) and third order stresses(σIII). The division classifies the stresses as a scale of size. First order 
stresses are stresses on a macroscopic level in the range from 1mm and up. Second order stresses are stresses 
on a microscopic level equilibrated between grains or crystallites in the material. Finally 3rd order stresses 
are on atomic scale. 
 
3.1 Causes of residual stresses 
 
Residual stresses emerge from any process or treatment that causes inhomogeneously distributed elastic or 
plastic deformations thereby causing strain incompatibility within the component (Niko-Lari, 1987). Some of 
these processes include: Rolling, machining, heat treating, quenching and welding, that is almost any 
manufacturing process of steel and steel structures generates residual stresses in a component.  Furthermore 
residual stress can arise due to loading of part of a structure into the plastic range causing permanent 
deformations.  
 
3.2 Residual stresses due to welding 
 
In welds, residual stresses arise due to thermally induced stresses. As a consequence of localized heating 
during welding the temperature distribution in the weld and work piece is non-uniform. The non-uniform 
temperature distribution results in shrinkage differences between regions close to the heat source and 
regions farther from the heat source. These shrinkage differences are responsible for distortion and residual 
9 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
stresses in the work piece. An example of the temperature and stress distribution during welding is shown in 
Figure 6. 
 
Figure 6: Temperature and stress distribution during welding (AWS, 2001). 
Residual stresses due to shrinkage are caused by the contraction of the weld upon solidification of the weld 
pool and cooling to ambient temperature combined with the restraint towards contraction by the adjacent 
material thus generating incompatible strains. As mentioned the contraction also causes undesirable 
distortion of the work piece in the form of shrinkage or angular distortion (AWS, 2001), see Figure 7. 
 
Figure 7: Distortion due to welding (AWS, 2001). 
 
In addition to shrinkage residual stresses, welding also causes transformation residual stresses. These arise 
due to volume changes associated with phase transformation (Niko-Lari, 1987). This may happen if highly 
heated regions (HAZ) go through a transition which less heated regions do not.  
The shrinkage upon cooling and solidification happens in all directions, therefore residual stresses will be 
present as longitudinal, transverse and normal stresses. Figure 8 shows a butt weld and how the weld will 
want to contract upon solidification. 
10 
 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
Figure 8: Shrinkage stresses in longitudinal and transverse direction (AWS, 2001). 
As explained earlier the restraints against these contractions are cause of the residual stresses. The typical 
stress distribution in the longitudinal direction will have the form of a “Gaussian-like” spline with high tensile 
residual stresses in and around the weld and compressive residual stresses in the more distant regions. For 
the transverse stresses, tensile stresses are present in the middle of the plate and compressive stresses near 
the ends of the weld, See Figure 9. The longitudinal stresses are usually of great magnitude compared to the 
transverse stresses hence why longitudinal residual stresses are of special interest regarding fatigue 
assessment. 
 
Figure 9: Typical stress distributions in a butt weld (AWS, 2001). 
The presence of external restraints during welding will result in so-called reaction stresses. These stresses 
are uniform and are added to the residual stress thus affecting the magnitude but not the distributions of 
these. Normal residual stresses are generally insignificant for weldments of thickness less than 25 mm 
(AWS, 2001). 
 
3.3 Influencing factors on residual stresses 
 
Generally the residual stresses are based on and governed by the temperature field during welding. The 
temperature field is discussed in 2.4. This paragraph gives an overview of influencing factors. These factors 
are: Material parameters, geometry and welding process (Niko-Lari, 1987). Regarding material parameters, 
an increase of the thermal expansion coefficient and Young’s modulus are independently associated with an 
increase in the magnitude of tensile residual stresses in the center of the weld. When considering the 
geometric effect on the residual stresses, factors such as the length of the weld and the thickness of the 
11 
 
 
==PAGE:23==
 
component have an influence. A longer weld is accompanied by an increase in tensile residual stress in the 
longitudinal direction in the center of the weld. However for weld lengths beyond 457 mm the longitudinal 
stress does not increase further (AWS, 2001). The residual stresses can vary with the thickness i.e. the 
thickness has an influence on the distribution of residual stresses through the thickness (see Figure 10). This 
variation is less significant for plates thinner than 25 mm. Furthermore intuitively the thickness of a plate 
should influence the residual stress as a thicker plate will result in a bigger joint and thus larger shrinkage 
stresses. This is investigated through numerical models in chapter 8. 
 
Figure 10: Variation of residual stresses for a butt weld in a 25 mm plate (AWS, 2001). 
 
3.4 Reducing residual stresses 
 
A reduction of residual stress in a component can be achieved in a variety of ways. Some of these are 
presented in this paragraph. Actions to reduce residual stresses can be taken prior to, during or post welding 
(Radaj, 1992). Prior to welding design decisions can  be made to generate as small residual stresses as 
possible e.g. by considering some of the parameters discussed in 3.3. During welding preheating and cooling 
can reduce the thermal gradients during welding, thereby reducing residual stresses. Furthermore residual 
stresses can be reduced by different post-treatment methods, some of which are described below. 
3.4.3 Post-treatment 
Annealing as a post-heat treatment method involves heating the specimen to its recrystallization 
temperature to reduce the elastic modulus and yield limit whereby creeping processes are enhanced and 
stress relaxation is achieved and retained after cooling as well. The heating and cooling is done slowly to 
avoid major temperature differences within the specimen (Radaj, 1992).  
Stress relieving by vibration is another possibility. Here the component is vibrated on an oscillation table. The 
component should reach its yield limit at least if high residual stresses are present. The residual stresses are 
in this way relieved due to plastic deformation. This method can be damaging if the number of load cycles 
from the vibrations is too large. 
12 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
Furthermore hammering in both cold and hot state in the direction of the weld can reduce the longitudinal 
shrinkage and thereby also reduce the residual stress. Another related method is shot peening. Shot 
peening is performed by “shooting” the surface with small particles, this creates overlapping indentations 
in the surface inducing compressive residual stresses. 
 
3.5 Measurement of residual stresses 
The ability to determine residual stresses is an important tool for gathering knowledge about the behavior 
and nature of these. Overall there are two general approaches for residual stress measurement: Destructive 
methods and non-destructive methods. Under each of these, several different methods exist. This paragraph 
elaborates on some of these. 
3.5.1 Destructive methods 
As the name suggest the destructive methods are all methods that damage the measured object, most often 
making it unavailable for use after measurement. The destructive methods are also referred to as Relaxation 
methods because it is based on removing/cutting material from the object in question, thereby relieving the 
residual stresses deforming the material into its relaxed state. The methods do not really measure the 
stresses but rather the strains, wherefrom the original residual stresses can be determined by use of elastic 
theory. Among the destructive methods are: Splitting method, sectioning method, layer removal method, 
hole drilling method. A brief conceptual description of the hole drilling method and the contour method is 
given below as these methods were used by other projects within the same cluster of investigations as this 
thesis. 
The hole drilling method is probably the most used method of measurement (Gary S. Schajer, 2013). When 
measuring residual stresses using the hole drilling method, a hole is drilled in the specimen and the resulting 
deformations are measured with strain gauges wherefrom the stresses can be determined. Figure 11 
illustrates the hole drilling method. 
 
Figure 11: Hole drilling method (Gary S. Schajer, 2013). 
The hole drilling method exists in a variety of forms however they are all based on the same principle. One of 
the reasons the method is so popular is that it is only “semi-destructive” as only a small hole is drilled and 
the damage can therefore be insignificant. 
13 
 
 
==PAGE:25==
 
The advantages of destructive measuring are that it is relatively easy to do and that little equipment is 
needed. There are however some limitations to the method. The obvious disadvantage is that the method is 
destructive and therefore the specimen can be unusable after measurement at least for most of the methods. 
Another downside is that the part of the material that is cut out contains stresses and since these are not 
measured it has to be taken into account mathematically when evaluating the measurement. 
The contour method is a method where a specimen is cut into two pieces where upon the resulting 
deformation due to residual stress redistribution can be measured as a consequence of relaxation. The 
deformations can then be implemented in a FEM model by predefined boundary conditions and the residual 
stresses can be extracted from the model. This is achieved by forcing the deformed surface in the model back 
to un-deformed state and determining the resulting stresses. By superposition of the deformed and un-
deformed state the original stress distribution can be determined. 
3.5.2 Non-Destructive methods 
 In contrast to Destructive-methods there is also a variety of Non-destructive methods. These include 
Diffraction methods, magnetic measurement, ultrasonic measurement, thermoelastic measurement and 
photoelastic measurement. Diffraction is a method in which beams of radiation are diffracted by the 
measured specimen and from the angle where the diffracted beam shows peak intensity it is possible to 
determine the internal strains. The specific diffraction method Neutron diffraction is thoroughly treated in 
Chapter 4. Since the emphasis in this project is on neutron diffraction the above mentioned methods will not 
be described but information about these can be found in e.g. (Gary S. Schajer, 2014).

Neutron Diffraction 
 
As mentioned in chapter 3, neutron diffraction is a non-destructive residual stress measurement method 
applicable on crystalline materials. By measuring changes in the crystal lattice spacing the lattices act as a 
“natural strain gage”.  The change in lattice spacing can then be transferred into residual stresses in the 
material by means of elasticity. 
The method is a successor of x-ray diffraction, which was first developed in the early 20th century with notable 
contribution from William Lawrence Bragg, who formulated Braggs law on which diffraction measurement 
relies. The neutron diffraction method itself dates back to the 1940s where the first neutron diffraction 
experiments were performed by Ernest O. Wollan and Clifford Schull (Hutchings et al, 2005). However the 
first papers reporting on experimental setups for residual strain measurement titled were publish in the early 
1980ies among others: “Measurement of internal stress within bulk materials using neutron diffraction” 
(Hutchings et al, 2005). 
There are two approaches to neutron diffraction measurement. Using a monochromatic instrument or using 
a Time of Flight instrument. This chapter will mainly be concerned with the monochromatic approach as this 
is the type of instrument used in the actual measurements. 
To measure the strains using neutron diffraction a beam of neutron radiation is directed against the 
specimen. The specimen will scatter and diffract the neutron beam and the intensity will be different in 
different directions. The peak intensity of the diffracted beam is the key to calculating the lattice spacing, 
hence the strain. This is because it is possible through Bragg’s equation to estimate the lattice spacing from 
the peak intensity diffraction angle: 
𝜆𝜆= 2 ∙𝑑𝑑∙sin (𝜃𝜃) 
Where d is the lattice spacing, θ is the angle between the incident neutron beam and the specimen or 2 θ is 
the angle between incident and diffracted beam and λ is the wavelength.  
To explain how Bragg’s equation is related to the lattice plane spacing a dissection of the formula is 
convenient. For 2 parallel lattice planes arrayed the diffracted beams will have traveled different distances 
equal to 2 ∙𝑑𝑑∙sin (𝜃𝜃) (see Figure 12) and will therefore only be in phase if the distance is equal to an integer 
times the wavelength of the beam cf. Braggs equation. When in phase the amplitudes will be summed and 
peak intensity can be observed.

4.2 Neutron sources and moderation 
The sources of neutrons are either fission or spallation. Fission is the cleavage of nuclei into protons and 
neutrons well-known from nuclear power where the large release of energy from the process is utilized. 
Typically the neutrons are “produced” by fission of U235. Today nuclear reactors are built specifically for the 
“production” of high-flux neutron beams (Hutchings et al, 2005). 
Spallation is an accelerator based method where high-energy protons are set to strike a heavy metal and 
neutrons are thereby emitted. This method is often used for Time of flight diffraction measurements which 
is not used in this project. 
Whether the source of neutrons is fission or spallation a moderation of the neutrons is necessary to ensure 
the right energy and velocity of the neutrons so that they can be used for scattering. Neutron-moderation is 
done by inelastic scattering (Loss of kinetic energy as a result of scattering) in a medium that slows down the 
neutrons. Typical neutron-moderators are: H2O, D2O and carbon/graphite. The velocity/energy of the 
neutrons can be manipulated both up and down in reactors with built in cold sources for example H2O and 
hot sources for example heated carbon (Anderson et al, 2009).

4.3 Diffractometer 
 
From the source the neutrons are transferred to the actual diffractometer. A schematic drawing of the exact 
diffractometer used for this project can be seen on Figure 15. 
 
Figure 15: Schematic drawing of E3 diffractometer (Boin and Wimpory, 2016). 
From the reactor the neutrons are transferred to the monochromator. The monochromator is most often a 
single crystal that the neutrons are diffracted from to obtain a given wavelength the crystal used in the E3 
diffractometer is Silicon but Germanium is also sometimes used. Next the neutrons beam travels through a 
monitor counter. The monitor counter monitors the neutron flux into the sample. The primary slit is used to 
adjust the size of the gage volume, the volume created by the overlap of the incident and the diffracted 
beam. The detector registers the diffracted beam.

Fatigue 
 
Steel and especially welded structures can fracture due to fatigue without ever reaching the ultimate stress 
level of the material. This happens due to cyclic loads. Fatigue is relevant for this report because monopiles 
are exposed to wave and wind loads (cyclic loads) and because residual stresses have an impact on the life 
expectancy of structures exposed to cyclic loads. Fatigue failure occurs in three steps: crack initiation, crack 
propagation and eventually failure/fracture.  
In welded structures crack initiation will most often happen at welds due to metallurgical discontinuities that 
make the weld more vulnerable to cracking if small imperfections do not already exist in the weld. Also welds 
will experience stress concentrations so-called notch stresses at the toes of the weld therefore cracks might 
initiate and grow faster at these points. As a consequence the focus in this chapter is primarily on fatigue 
strength of welded details. 
 
5.1 Fatigue strength 
 
The fatigue strength or the fatigue life of a welded structure is defined as the number of cycles N of a given 
stress range ∆𝜎𝜎 the structure can be exposed to until failure. The fluctuation of stress i.e. the stress range

5.2 Stress concentrations 
 
Stress concentrations are inevitable to a high degree in welded joints. They are highly dependent on the 
shape and geometry of the considered weld. An example of the stress distribution and stress concentration 
at a weld toe is shown in Figure 17.  
 
Figure 17: Stress concentration at weld toe (Maddox, 1991). 
As seen on the figure a stress concentration arises due to the abrupt changes in geometry at the toe. The 
figure shows a rather simple and smooth transition at the weld toe however discontinuities such as 
undercutting and intrusion at the toe might further complicate the geometry increasing the stress 
concentration. The most severe stress concentrations arise from transverse loading although welding 
induced surface irregularities do also cause stress concentrations when the load is parallel to the weld. 
  
5.3 Effects of residual stresses on fatigue 
 
Intuitively an entirely tensile stress range causes more damage than an alternating stress range as cracks 
generally tend to propagate in the presence of tensile stresses. Additionally an increase of the mean stress 
in a stress cycle is often associated with a decrease of fatigue life. However for as-welded joints which may 
contain residual stresses in the magnitude of the yield stress the mean stress is of very little importance. The 
effects of the residual stresses overshadow the mean stress of the external loading since the external load is 
merely added to the internal stresses by superposition (see Figure 18). Thus the mean stress of the external 
loading becomes redundant as the stress range will be fluctuating around the magnitude of the residual

==PAGE:33==
 
stresses. As a consequence an entirely compressive stress range is also damaging to a welded structure 
containing tensile residual stresses. 
 
Figure 18: Superposition of the stress range (MacDonald, 2011). 
In the case of tensile residual stresses in a weld of yield strength magnitude the stress range will reach into 
the plastic regime. 
 
5.4 Fatigue design 
 
When designing steel details to for example Eurocode 3 the design relies upon fatigue test result data 
obtained from constant amplitude loading which are compared to S-N curves. Stress-Number of cycles to 
failure curves (S-N curves) are logarithmic curves that illustrate the stress range as a function of number of 
cycles (fatigue life) obtained from test results. Since these are derived from experimental results the S-N 
curves used for design are typically two times the standard deviation lower than the mean results. This 
ensures a 97.7 % survival limit (Maddox, 1991). A diverse range of different S-N curves exist and should be 
chosen to fit the particular joint, stress assessment and environment. 
Two approaches are generally used when designing to Eurocode: The nominal stress method and the Hot 
spot stress method. The difference between them lies in the stress assessment of the detail. Both are based 
on details categorized in the code so that the details that have equivalent fatigue strengths are contained in 
the same category. 
The Nominal stress method applies a nominal stress range derived for example by means of classical Euler-
Bernoulli beam theory. Whereas the hot spot stress method applies the geometric stress range and is often 
calculated numerically. The stress calculation method should be used with its corresponding S-N curve i.e. 
Nominal stress S-N curve or hot spot stress S-N curve. If the welded joint is located in an area of stress 
concentrations due to structure geometry and not the weld geometry, the stress concentration factor should 
be applied to the nominal stress. 
To approximate to reality to a further extend it can be necessary to account for variable amplitude loading. 
This is done from a hypothesis of linear damage accumulation. In practice it is based on the point that relative 
fatigue damages for each stress range is cumulative. It is calculated using the Palmgren-Milner’s rule

Finite Element Analysis 
 
This chapter presents the conducted FEM analysis in terms of the assumptions and input to the model as well 
as the findings. The investigation is conducted by performing a sequential-coupled thermo-mechanical 
analysis using a plugin tool for the commercial FEM-software ABAQUS. As the name implies sequential 
coupled thermo-mechanical analysis is a numerical modelling approach in which the thermal equations are 
solved first and the mechanical problem is solved subsequently on the basis of the thermal solution. 
The cases that have been studied numerically in this thesis are based on welding experiments that have been 
carried out by others as a part of their projects. The following weld types have been investigated: 
• 
SAW welded plate (10 mm thickness) 
• 
SAW welded plate (20 mm thickness) 
The 10 mm plate has been welded as a part of a master thesis (Jessen, 2016) and the 20 mm plate has been 
welded as a part of a special course (Andersen & Hassan, 2016). Further information about the plates can be 
found in chapter 6. 
 
8.1 AWI 
 
Welding simulation for this project was performed using the plugin tool AWI (ABAQUS Welding Interface). 
The plugin was made freely available through the ABAQUS center. The plugin has been used for 3D weld 
models in this thesis but also supports 2D modelling. The model tree in AWI has the following features: 
• 
Welds 
• 
Pass controls 
• 
Passes 
• 
Job controls 
• 
Jobs 
Under welds is where the weld(s) is defined. A weld can then be separated into different beads which each 
consist of a number of chunks. The division of beads into chunks makes it possible to deposit the weld in 
discretized portions simulating a continuous deposit instead of instantaneous.  
36 
 
 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
Figur 35: Illustration of a chunk of a weld bead being deposited. 
Under pass controls is where the applied torch step incrementation is defined as well as where the heat 
transfer properties are controlled. Under passes it is defined which pass and in which order they are put. 
Under job controls the names of the heat and stress job are defined and the bead removal method is 
assigned. AWI can use two different methods when running the stress model: Pass-By-Pass Bead removal or 
Model-Level Bead removal.  
With the pass-by-pass method all beads are active in the stress analysis until their corresponding torch step, 
and then they are removed and reinserted strain-free. The fact that all the beads are active from initiation 
will of course affect the resulting stresses but on the other hand it reduces the risk of distorting the elements 
as they will follow the deformations as they develop. 
With the Model level method all beads are removed in the beginning of the stress analysis and then inserted 
in their corresponding torch step. This method does not affect the resulting stresses, but may cause distortion 
of the elements in the model. AWI recommends not using Model level method for other than first order 
elements. 
The weld torch is simulated by specifying a prescribed torch heat up temperature which is applied to the 
boundary between the weld bead and the adjacent/base material.

8.2.1.5 Calibration of heat input 
In AWI the target torch heat up temperature is constant. It is assumed that the welding speed is constant as 
well. This means that the only way to adjust the heat input is by modifying the chunk length of the beads. 
Since AWI heats up the interface between beads and base metal a smaller chunk length means a smaller area 
is heated but the total heating time governed by the welding speed is the same, however the more chunks 
the less torch time is applied to each chunk.  
The calibration of the heat input was performed by evaluating the temperature output from ABAQUS against 
the temperatures measured with thermocouples during the welding-experiments. For all models the 
temperature was obtained by using the view cut function in ABAQUS and thereby evaluating the 
temperatures at half the depth corresponding to the placement of the thermocouples. The models were all 
meshed according to 8.2.1.4. The chunk length of the 10 mm plate was based on findings in (Madsen & 
Pedersen, 2015) and (Jessen, 2016) where an appropriate chunk length of 12 mm was found.

of the thermal stresses is presented to give an estimation of the extend of the heat affected zone of the

Conclusion and future work 
 
This thesis has sought to contribute to the field of investigation and understanding regarding residual stresses 
in welded monopiles. Its emphasis has been on the implications of an increase in weld size as structures are 
magnified, which is a very real issue in offshore steel construction and specifically with regard to the demand 
for supersize monopiles that are/have increased in size in recent years. The investigation included residual 
stress measurements by means of neutron diffraction and numerical modelling of induced residual stresses 
by submerged arc welding. The residual stresses have been investigated in SAW butt welds of two different 
steel plate thicknesses of steel grade s355. The plate thicknesses examined were 10 mm and 20 mm.  
The experimental examination was performed only on the 20 mm plate due to limited time at the 
diffractometer used. The stresses from the measurement were obtained by the use of reference comb 
samples cut by wire-EDM from plates welded in a manner similar to that of the investigated plate. The results 
displayed longitudinal tensile stresses considerably above the expected yield strength in the weld center for 
all 3 depths examined. Furthermore the longitudinal stresses shown to be the largest by far. Both transverse 
and normal stresses were considerably below yield strength. 
On the basis of information about the welding procedure of the two plates, FEM models were developed for 
computational welding simulation. A sequentially coupled thermo-mechanical analysis was performed on 
both models using the Abaqus plugin tool, Abaqus Welding Interface. The heat source was applied as a 
prescribed torch temperature applied at the interface between weld metal and the base plate. The 
application prescribed temperature demanded a division of the weld into discretized chunks as to simulate 
the transient movement of the heat source. The discretization was approximated through heat calibration of 
the model by evaluating the temperatures obtained against the experimental measured temperatures. A 
reasonable calibration was obtained for most passes, however a few deviated considerably from the 
experimentally measured values. 
The analysis of the mechanical results was initialized by comparing the obtained residual stresses of the 20 
mm model to the residual stresses obtained through neutron diffraction in order to investigate the validity 
of the model. The model seemed to exhibit lower residual stresses than those acquired through neutron 
diffraction in the top and bottom of the weld. The stresses in the middle of the weld however fit very well 
with the experimental values. This could indicate that the model fits better to reality than the impression at 
first glance. As the calculated residual stresses from neutron diffraction are all calculated longitudinally from 
a reference comb cut from the middle of the plate, it is uncertain how accurate the calculations of stresses 
in top and bottom are. 
After comparing experimental stresses with the 20 mm model. A comparison of the mechanical results from 
the 20 mm plate model and the 10 mm plate model was performed. The deformation due to shrinkage was 
observed for both plates as bending along the weld. The 20 mm plate showed clear upward angular distortion 
by the centerline of the plate whereas the 10 mm plate due to boundary conditions showed downward 
translation of elements farthest from the weld.  
67 
 
 
==PAGE:79==
 
The longitudinal and transverse stresses were compared for the two models to evaluate the differences in 
terms of magnitude and distribution in the welds. Assessing the stresses in the top, middle and bottom of 
the welds showed that the large weld displayed the largest longitudinal tensile residual stresses at the top 
and at the middle but was exceeded by the smaller weld at the bottom. The overall largest residual stress 
was determined to be present in the larger weld. The same was true for the transverse residual stresses. The 
models showed the same tendencies distribution wise with the main difference being the magnitudes of 
stresses. 
The results show that the increase of thickness and thereby increase of weld size causes larger residual 
stresses and that these are very likely to be in the magnitude of the yield strength. The results are found on 
a minor scale but the problems highlighted by the results are very likely to be extrapolated into the large wall 
thicknesses of supersize monopiles. 
As of future work within the framework of this thesis it would be interesting to have neutron diffraction 
measurements of the 10 mm plate and more reference values for the 20 mm plate to be able to further 
investigate the validity of the models. Furthermore it would be interesting to investigate development of 
residual stresses caused by a heat source with a higher heat density such as Laser-hybrid welding and 
examine the effects of increased thickness.
