Abstract
Recovery of waste heat and utilization of renewable heat sources for electric-
ity production are important methods for reducing the use of fossil fuels. A
promising technology to achieve this is the organic Rankine cycle (ORC). In
order to evaluate the potential and economic feasibility of organic Rankine
cycles, modelling of the main components is essential.
This thesis presents the development and validation of mean-line model of a
radial inﬂow turbine. The purpose of the model is integration with thermo-
dynamic cycle tools for optimization and feasibility studies. Model features
include capability of using either ideal gas assumptions or real ﬂuid prop-
erties (’Coolprop’ and ’refprop’ ﬂuid libraries) and possibility of super sonic
nozzle design.
As the efﬁciency prediction in mean-line models rely solely on empirical loss
models, the thesis focuses on comparing the loss models that have been ap-
plied in other studies. Models for estimating different types of losses in radial
turbines are numerous, but well-established procedures for estimating the to-
tal loss are practically non-existing.
Several loss prediction procedures were identiﬁed, and attempts to validate
the model with and without these procedures have been made. Validation
was conducted with: 1) A well-described experimental test of a turbine using
air as medium. 2) A series of air turbine (experimental and numerical) tests
with limited test data. 3) A series of turbine tests (experimental and numeri-
cal) using organic ﬂuids as medium and with limited test data.
It was investigated whether convincing procedures could be developed by
combining the loss models in new ways. Furthermore, the most promising
of the loss prediction procedures were used for optimization of a test case to
evaluate the impact of the loss models on the optimized design.
The mean-line model was veriﬁed by comparison with another mean-line
code achieving matching geometry and ﬂow within 5%. Likewise, the valida-
tion of the code with an experimental air turbine test achieved results within
11%.

Abstract
A comparison of the loss prediction procedures based on the accumulated
insights showed that three of the investigated approaches performed better
than the rest. The three approaches demonstrated different strengths and
weaknesses, but they all seemed useful in an optimization context. However,
the optimized turbine geometries were considerably different (up to 20-25%
on certain parameters). It is evident that more work can and should be done
to increase the accuracy of the loss prediction procedures.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The relevance and potential of the organic Rankine cycle (ORC) in the energy
situation today deﬁnes the background for this thesis. Within this frame-
work, the methods for design and performance prediction of radial turbines
are of interest. This connection is described in the ﬁrst section of this chapter.
To provide a more comprehensive description of the context, the basics topics
of ORC technology are described in the second section.
1.1
Motivation and purpose
1.1.1
Potential of ORC technology
The organic Rankine cycle is a power producing cycle like the Rankine cycle,
which is well known from conventional power plants. ORC’s use organic ﬂu-
ids as working media, such as alkanes, refrigerants, and other organic com-
pounds. Since the properties of organic ﬂuids are very different from steam
properties, ORC’s have potential to utilize heat sources, which would be in-
feasible to exploit with steam cycles. More speciﬁcally, they have proven
to be superior to steam cycles in low-temperature (< 150◦C) and medium-
temperature (150 −400◦C) ranges and low power ranges (1 kW - 1 MW). The
following three areas of application are in particular suitable for ORC tech-
nology.
1. Utilisation of natural heat sources, such as solar and geothermal heat,
since they typically are contained in these temperature ranges.
2. Heat recovery of low- and medium-temperature waste heat from exist-
ing and future industrial processes and vehicle and ship engines.
3. Design of future decentralized units in the low power range.

1.1.2
Relevance of radial turbine modelling
In order to evaluate the potential of ORC technology, accurate models must
be developed to predict performance, size and cost. The expander is typically
the most expensive component and different studies show that it is a major
contributor to the destruction of exergi (or potential of mechanical work) [45],
[36]. Therefore, a lot of attention should be paid to modelling of the expander
design and performance. The optimal choice of expander type depends a lot
on the capacity range and application. This thesis focuses on radial inﬂow
turbines, which scales well for low-medium power ranges. Other expander
types include screw and scroll expanders and axial turbines, where each type
scales best to a certain power range, see ﬁgure 1.2. Since the speed of sound
of organic ﬂuids are very low compared to speed of sound in air, supersonic
conditions occur more often in ORC turbines. Therefore, attention must be
paid on super sonic design of nozzles and the associated shock losses.
1.1.3
Incentives for 1D mean-line modelling and evaluation of loss
prediction procedures
What is known as "mean-line one-dimensional modelling" can be used to es-
timate both efﬁciency and overall sizes with a small computational effort. It
is therefore a suitable tool to combine with ORC simulation and optimisation

programs. A mean-line 1D model uses a mix of physical principles such as
energy and mass balances, thermodynamic relations and geometry to predict
the turbine geometry and empirical correlations is used to predict the internal
losses occurring in the turbine. The performance prediction is highly reliable

very different for gas turbines and ORC turbines, it is questionable whether
the same empirical loss models can be used for these two types of ﬂuids. This
makes it relevant to evaluate the loss models used in radial gas turbine de-
sign and, if necessary, to modify them to obtain speciﬁc models for organic
ﬂuid low temperature applications.
1.1.4
Overall goals
The purpose of this thesis can be summarized to four overall goals:
• To develop a mean-line model for radial inﬂow turbines.
• To validate the model with the best possible available data.
• To identify, implement and compare different loss prediction proce-
dures.
• Based on this, to make suggestions for selection of a loss prediction pro-
cedure and possibly for reﬁnements for application with organic ﬂuids.

1.2.1
Cycle conﬁguration
The simplest possible Organic Rankine cycle design is shown on Figure 1.3. A
quick description of the cycle: The high pressure working ﬂuid in liquid state
enters the evaporator (4), where it is heated by the driving heat source, and
exits as a saturated or superheated gas. From there (1) it expands through a
turbine to (2) to produce mechanical power, which can be used in a generator
to produce electricity. The ﬂuid is then condensed to (3) in the condenser.
The cooling stream can be a ﬂow at ambient temperature consisting of air or
water, or it can have a purpose such as district heat production. At last, the
feed pump increases the pressure of the ﬂuid to return to the initial state (4).
Another common conﬁguration is seen on Figure 1.4, which has a recuperator
added. The recuperator preheats the stream prior to entering the evaporator
while cooling the exiting stream from the turbine. This is only possible if the
turbine exit state is in the superheated region, which in general depends on
the selected ﬂuid. It has the effect of directly increasing the cycle efﬁciency
by reducing the required heating in the evaporator.
Re-heaters are not common in ORC plant layouts, and a drum for liquid-gas
seperation is not needed. This is linked to following differences in property
characteristics between organic ﬂuids compared and water.
• Gas-liquid density difference. Since the density difference between liq-

and pentane a ’dry’ ﬂuid. Wet ﬂuids often require a reheating step to
avoid vapor in the turbine, because it causes damage and reduces the
lifetime expectancy.
The topic of ﬂuid characteristics and how it affects performance and design
of components will be revisited again in this chapter. To summarize, the most
common cycle designs used in commercial units today are simple and due to
the different ﬂuid properties, some of the complications of steam cycles are
avoided.
1.2.2
Working ﬂuid selection
A important feature of the ORC technology is the wide range of possible
working ﬂuids, which makes it possible to customize the thermodynamic
cycle to the application. Many studies have concerned the selection of work-
ing ﬂuids, for example [50], which aims to identify and compare the 8 most
applied working ﬂuids for ORC applications in the medium and low tem-
perature ranges. The purpose of other studies have been more speciﬁc and
concerned the ﬂuid selection for various applications ([26], [49], [36]) For ex-
ample, Quoilin et al suggest using n-pentane for a waste heat application

Thermodynamic criteria.
The thermodynamic performance of working ﬂuids must be investigated by
a complete cycle analysis, since the achievable efﬁciency of a working ﬂuid
depends on all its thermodynamic properties. However, some good indica-
tors are:
• Boiling point. The heating and boiling of the ﬂuid should take place at a
temperature close to the temperature of the heat source. For subcritical
cycles this means that the critical point must be above or in the range
of the source temperature. For transcritial cycles, the curve of he su-
perheating should be as ﬂat as possible (and therefore, not too far from
the critical temperature). However, a security margin should be kept to
avoid problems of ﬂash evaporation / condensing.
• Positive sloped or near-vertical gas saturation curve.

• High speciﬁc enthalpy drop. A large enthalpy drop between the high and

• High gas-phase density. As discussed in section 1.2.1, a small liquid-gas

• Low freezing point. The freezing point must be lower than the ambient
temperature (at all times of the year), since freezing can not just prevent
operation, but also cause damage to components.
• Low viscosity. High viscosity gives rise to high frictional losses.
• Good heat transfer properties. This will reduce the needed areas of the
heat exchangers.
Chemical criteria.
• High temperature stability. At high temperatures, organic ﬂuids decom-
pose, which puts a strict limit to the usable range for each ﬂuid.
• Non toxic and non ﬂammable. Depending on the required safety level
and the nearby activities, it is important that the ﬂuid does not cause
any threat to people and machinery.
• Non corrosive and non etching. These factors are important to consider
when evaluating the expected lifetime of the plant, but it can also put
limits to material selection of sealings and bearings.
Environmental criteria.
• Low Ozone Depleting Potential (ODP). Earlier, many of the refrigerants
used damaged the atmosphere quite badly by depleting the ozone layer.
This was stopped during the 80’s by the Montreal Protocol [44], which
prevented a list of substances from being used for this application. Most
of the refrigerants used today have an ODP of 0.
• Low Greenhouse Warming Potential (GWP). The greenhouse warming po-
tential describes the effect of global warming that a gas demonstrates
when released to the atmosphere. It is deﬁned relative to CO2, which
has a GWP of 1. Values for the most common working ﬂuids range
from 0 to over 1000.

1.3. Applications of ORC
1.3
Applications of ORC
Low temperature heat is available both as natural occurrence and as primary,
secondary and waste product of different processes. Every location with a
low temperature heat source can in theory utilize an ORC for power produc-
tion. This section describes the most common applications focusing on the
basic concepts, the potentials, the approximate temperature ranges and the
competing technologies.
1.3.1
Biomass plants
As a consequence of political and environmental goals about phasing out fos-
sil fuels, a shift towards utilizing biomass in power plants instead of coal has
been observed many places. The term biomass describes all organic plant
material and straw, ﬁrewood, wood chips, wood waste, and wood pellets are
the most common types. In Denmark, the total consumption of biomass has
increased from 24.9 PJ in 2009 to 40.94 PJ in 2014 according to the Danish
electricity distributor ("Energinet") [11]. Small scale plants are quite common
and achieve electrical efﬁciencies around 10-20% [46]. In the biomass plant,
the heat is most commonly transferred from the combustion product gas to
the evaporator through a heat transfer loop containing a thermal oil, to avoid
exposure of the organic ﬂuid to high and possibly ﬂuctuating temperatures.
Biomass CHP plants must ideally be located near to both biomass supplier
(forests, crop ﬁelds, etc.) and heat customers (e.g. district heat customers
or industrial processes), which often limits the size of the plant. This is es-
pecially important in rural or remote areas. As discussed in section 1.2.1,
the component requirement for an ORC is smaller than for a steam cycle.
ORC plants are therefore more cost-effective in the low power ranges. In
Ref. [46], some other general advantages compared to the steam cycle are
mentioned. This includes less or none maintenance requirements and better
expander off-design performance. Another competitor to ORC technology
in biomass plants is gasiﬁcation. Gasiﬁcation transforms the solid biomass
into gas which can be either stored and work as long term energy storage
or burned directly in a gas turbine or combustion engine. Most gasiﬁca-
tion plants are still prototypes, and does therefore have high investment and
maintenance costs. It is therefore not a market competitor yet, but it might be
in the future.
Waste heat recovery of industrial processes
Recently, Bühler et al. mapped the low and medium temperature heat sources
in Denmark [10], showing that approximately 266 PJ waste heat per year is
available. The largest potential for heat recovery was seen in the industry and
transport sectors. They concluded that the heat sources can be divided into

the component ratio. While ORC is more well-proven, some studies show
that Kalina Cycles has the potential of achieving lower levelized cost of en-
ergy. The results of such comparisons depend on the application and as-
sumptions, an example of this is the two studies [38] and [18].
Waste heat recovery in the transport sector
In the transport sector, the produced power can supplement the main engine
and thereby reduce fuel consumption, it can be used for equipment or stored
in a battery. One example from the transport sector is recovering heat from
a vehicle internal combustion engine using an ORC cycle. As discussed in
[1] this gives many challenges including physical space and control, since
both heat supply and power demand varies during operation. Another area
in the transport sector, which show a big potential, is the maritime industry
(ships and platforms). Singh et al. [32] presents the main waste heat sources
for large maritime vessels as waste incineration, engine exhaust gas, scav-
enge air, and engine cooling water. Of these, the waste incineration heat has
the highest temperatures, but exhaust gases has the largest energy potential,
while still having reasonable high temperatures. Approximately 50 % of the
fuel energy is lost to the atmosphere, and half of this is released through the
exhaust gases. The exhaust gases will normally have a temperature between
300 and 500◦C depending on the type of engine. Cooling will be restricted to
a temperature limit around 165◦C [9] where acid corrosion and soot build-up
occurs. However, this limit could be removed in the future by using clean
fuel alternatives. Singh et al. [32] perform a comparative literature review of
waste heat recovery technologies for maritime applications including turbo

1.3.2
Geothermal heat
Another heat resource is the energy content of the core of the earth, which
is large, but not so easy accessible. The temperature gradient in the earth’s
crust is approximately 2-3 degrees per meter, but varies from location to loca-
tion. Typical reachable temperatures for the bore holes are in the range 100 −
300◦C. [16] The conventional methods for producing power from geother-
mal heat use two types of steam cycles: dry steam and ﬂash steam. Dry
steam transports steam from the underground wells up to the surface, where
it is expanded through a turbine and either released to the atmosphere or
condensed. In ﬂash steam plants, the hot water ﬂows up to the surface due
to its high pressure, and as the pressure decreases, some water ﬂash evapo-
rates. At the top, the steam is separated from the water and used to power
a turbine, while the water is pumped back into the reservoir. These meth-
ods use the water directly, where methods using organic Rankine cycles (and
Kalina cycles) pumps up the water to vaporize the working ﬂuid in a heat
exchanger. After that, the water is pumped back into the reservoir. The use
of ORC makes it possible to use heat reservoirs of lower temperatures, and
therefore of smaller depth. Since the power consumption of the pump is seen
to consume up to 50 % of the gross power output, the bore depth has a very
large impact on efﬁciency. As for biomass plants, ORC is primarily used for
low capacity units, while the larger units tend to use steam cycles.
1.3.3
Solar heat
The potentially largest energy resource on earth is the direct irradiation from
the sun. Electricity production from solar energy is possible as direct conver-
sion in photovoltaic cells (PV) and as heat production in collectors combined
with a thermal power machine. PV is superior to thermal systems in lev-
elized cost of electricity, but the thermal systems has other advantages such
as possibility for combined heat production and day to day energy storage.
Often, the solar receivers in thermal solar systems are mirrors concentrating
at a point or line shaped collector. This is known as concentrated solar power
(CSP) and work with a heat transfer ﬂuid (typically molten salt) exchanging
heat to the working ﬂuid. The power cycle is most commonly a steam cycle,
but ORCs and Kalina cycles show potential to decrease the investment costs
for low power and low temperature cases, such as discussed for biomass and
geothermal heat sources. Only few CSP ORC plants exist worldwide [1].

Radial Turbine Design Theory
In this section the theory behind the developed mean-line model is described.
First, the role of mean-line modelling in radial turbine design is presented.
Next, some of the most basic concepts of radial turbines are described. Then,
a literature survey presents some of the mean-line approaches and codes
developed by other authors. At last, the different types of losses in radial
turbines and methods for estimating them are introduced. The approaches
found in literature and the loss models will provide the basis for the devel-
opment of the mean-line code described in the next chapter.
2.1
Turbine design procedure
Development of turbo machinery from performance speciﬁcation to test of
a prototype, requires many aspects to be considered in order to achieve a
successful design. A design process typically includes (but is not restricted
to) following steps:
• Meanline (Quasi-2D) model for design point and off-design operation
• 3D geometry generation and ﬂow calculation
• Computational ﬂuid dynamic analysis
• Finite element stress and vibration analysis
• Prototyping
• Laboratory test
It is a highly iterative procedure, where the early steps are revisited if the re-
sult of an analysis is dissatisfying. Often, the pre-knowledge of the designer
and data from existing turbines support each step. This thesis focuses on the
initial step, the mean-line model, which provides the basis for the rest of the

procedure. A well considered and detailed mean-line model that estimates
the performance with precision will decrease the amount of work needed in

Mean-line modelling can be simpliﬁed using similitude theory to scale an
existing turbine design to another size. This is a common method in ﬂuid dy-
namics and a new design can quickly be obtained. However, when designing
for another operating point or pressure ratio, a higher level of analysis sophis-

In this case a full mean-line analysis is conducted. This involves calculating
the states and ﬂow parameters at key locations through the entire turbine.
Therefore, a set of stations or locations for the calculations must be deﬁned.
At each station, the mean value of the ﬂow is calculated, so it is assumed that
this is representative of the average ﬂow conditions1. Physical equations of
mass, momentum, and energy balance are applied to relate the states and ve-
locities of the stations.

2.2.6
Degree of Reaction
Another parameter that is also used for both radial and axial turbines is the
stage reaction (or degree of reaction) SR. It is deﬁned as the ratio of static
enthalpy change in the rotor divided by the static enthalpy drop in the entire
stage:
SR = h4 −h6
h1 −h6
(2.7)
Degree of reaction is another way of characterizing turbines. It describes
where the energy conversion from internal energy (high pressure) to kinetic
energy (high velocity) takes places. If SR = 0, the entire pressure to velocity
conversion (or enthalpy drop) takes place in the nozzle, and the turbine is
called an impulse turbine. In turbines with higher degree of reaction, a part
of the energy conversion takes place between the rotor blades. In that case,
the blades must be designed to facilitate the accompanying volume change.
2.3
Mean-line analysis in literature
Various mean-line analyses of radial inﬂow turbines can be found in the lit-
erature and many different approaches have been used. Most approaches
starts with a given initial state and assumes a set of non-dimensional param-
eters and ﬂow angles. Then, the states and overall geometry are calculated.
Some reported programs start at the rotor, some starts at the stator. At last,
a set of loss correlations is used to estimate the efﬁciency based on the cal-
culated geometry and states. The efﬁciency, which must be guessed initially
is then updated and the entire calculation is repeated until convergence. The
largest differences lies within the choice of input parameters and loss models.
To make a comparison of the input parameters more clear, the input variables
are sorted in three categories:
• Fixed input.
Fixed variables, that must be met for the turbine to meet its purpose
such as inlet conditions and mass ﬂow rate and in some cases work
output and pressure ratio and rotational speed.
• Primary input.
The governing non-dimensional parameters, geometric ratios and an-
gles which are varied to achieved different solutions. They provide the
starting point of the calculations together with the ﬁxed input and have
a big impact on the solution.
• Secondary input.
Input of lesser signiﬁcant effect, which can be neglected in a simpliﬁed
analyses. Most of them are assumed values to take secondary effect
into account in order to make the model more precise. Examples are

2.4.2
Types of losses and loss correlations
The types of turbine losses can as be divided into three main categories: stator
losses, rotor losses and exit energy losses.

• Stator losses can be divided into separate components such as inci-
dence, friction, secondary ﬂow, and vaneless space. However, since
the stator loss is almost one order of magnitude smaller than the rotor
loss, it is often estimated using an assembled loss coefﬁcient (e.g. [22],
[14] [28]).
• Rotor losses are commonly divided into at least following components:

loss. The passage loss can be divided into subcategories such as: fric-

assembled loss [22]. Different procedures are described here.
• The exit kinetic energy loss is simply the loss due to remaining energy

Incidence loss
As the ﬂow reaches the end of the vane less space it encounters the fast turn-
ing rotor blades at a relative ﬂow angle β4. The incidence angle is deﬁned
as the difference between the blade angle and the relative ﬂow angle. If it is
assumed that the blades are radial at inlet (which is the case in this thesis),
the relative ﬂow angle equals the incidence angle. While one would expect
that zero incidence angle would be optimal, this is not true. The explanation
is that the pressure gradient across the blade passage forces the ﬂuid towards
the pressure surface, i.e. opposite the direction of rotation. If the relative inlet
ﬂow angle is far away from optimal, there might even be separation and a
recirculation zone. The ﬂow ﬁeld was illustrated by Woolley and Hatton, see
ﬁgure 2.8.

Chapter 3
Development of Meanline
Model
This section introduces the developed radial turbine mean-line model. The
environment is Matlab by MathWorks. Two different codes were developed,
an "Analysis model" for veriﬁcation, validation, etc. and a "Design model"
for optimization. The two codes uses the same mathematical "core", which
consists of a rotor description, a stator description and loss estimations. The
structure of the "core" is seen on Figure 3.1.
In this chapter the overall assumptions and limitations of the model are pre-
sented. This is followed by descriptions of the Analysis model and the Design
model. This includes options, input variables, output and code structure. At
last, the mathematical description of the "core" calculations are found. This
includes the rotor and stator models and the feasibility constraints used in
the optimization model. A short description of the implementation of the
thermodynamic libraries "Coolprop" and "refprop" is also added.
3.1
Model assumptions
The purpose of the mean-line model is to give an estimate of the turbine ge-
ometry and performance. Instead of describing exact blade contours and an-
gles, it focuses on computing the mean ﬂow. It thus assumes that the blades,
hub and shroud can be designed to facilitate the computed angles and veloc-
ities. Some of the other model assumptions are:
• Adiabatic process is assumed for all components.
• Gravitational forces are neglected.
• The inlet ﬂow is assumed to be steady and containing purely the de-
sired input media.
37
==PAGE:52==
3.2. Analysis Model
Figure 3.1: Overall computational ﬂow of the mean-line model.
• The inlet ﬂow is assumed to be radial.
• Deviations between ﬂow angles and blade angles are neglected, but this
could eventually be replaced by other assumptions.
• The rotor blades are radial at the inlet section and all the blades extend
through the whole turbine (no splitter blades)
3.2
Analysis Model
The goal of the analysis model is to make the calculation as customizable as
possible in order to allow veriﬁcation with other codes, validation with real
turbines tests, comparison of loss model procedures, estimation of design
parameters from a given design, etc. Therefore, a lot of calculation options
are available, and these will be described in the ﬁrst section. Next, the input
variables are described. Then, the obtained output and plots are described.
Finally, the code structure and computational ﬂow are presented.
3.2.1

The calculation options serves to make the analysis model as versatile as pos-
sible. Some options concern which kind of calculation should be performed,
others which assumptions should be made, and again others which plots
should be generated. Some of the inputs were found to be ’case speciﬁc’ dur-
ing the validation. Therefore these were deﬁned within each case description
in separate scripts rather than in the main ﬁle. The available options are:

3.2.4
Code structure
Figure 3.2 displays the full overview of the Analysis Model including scripts
and computational ﬂow. A quick walk through is given here.
First, as already mentioned, options and input parameters are provided in the
main script, in the case speciﬁc input script and in "Initialize.m". After load-
ing the input ﬁle, "Initialize.m" prepares the calculation by providing initial
guesses and calculating the input state.
Then the main loop is entered beginning by saving the guessed values (for
comparison with the next set of guess values). Here, the "core" calculation of
rotor and stator takes place. Additional three variables are guessed and later
updated for the next iteration (see Section 3.4). The routine for these calcu-
lations calls functions from the chosen ﬂuid library "CoolProp" or "refprop"
(see section 3.4.3). Also, separate functions were written/obtained for certain
parts of the calculations or for certain phenomena. These are located in the
"Other" folder and described in Section 3.4.
Next, the "Lossmodel_v2" script calls all the different loss model scripts (one
for each loss model type) and combines the loss models according to the loss
prediction procedures described in 2.4.3. Having the break down of losses
for each procedure, the chosen procedure is selected, the losses are summed
and the resulting efﬁciency is calculated according to Eqn. 2.10 (if the ’ﬁxed
efﬁciency’ option is not selected).
If it is desired, the design variables are now updated according to the proce-
dure described in Appendix B. This is followed by calculation of the relative
errors and update of the guess values for the next iteration. Next step is eval-
uating the efﬁciency. In case the efﬁciency somehow exceeds unity (100%),

zero. Else, the convergence criteria are evaluated leading to next iteration or

3.3
Design Model
The purpose of the Design model (or Optimization model) is to identify the
design, which achieves the best possible efﬁciency according to the loss mod-
els. In addition, the design is constrained by certain feasibility limits. In con-
trast to the Analysis Model, the only options that must be selected is which
optimization function to use. The input parameters are now reduced to:
• Parameters for the optimization tools
• Cycle parameters (media, temperature, massﬂow, and pressures)
• Name of loss combination
The rest of the input variables from the Analysis model are either replaced by
default assumptions or subject for the optimization. The output variables are
simply the optimization variables and the efﬁciency. The ﬁrst three sections
describe the available optimization function, default model assumptions and
the optimization variables. The same core of equations from the Analysis
model has been used, but a few modiﬁcations in the code structure had to be
made. This is described in the following section. The selection of reasonable
feasibility constraints is described in the last section.
3.3.1
Matlab optimization function options
As the ﬁrst thing in the Design Model, the optimization tool must be selected.
The possibilities are brieﬂy described here:
• "Particle Swarm". An algorithm initially evaluating a population n of
particle locations distributed in the search space. Each particle location
is then assigned a velocity based on its own velocity and the best loca-
tion of its neighbours. The velocity gives the particle a new location for
the next iteration.
• "Generic Algorithm". Does also initially evaluate a population of n par-
ticle locations. The particles are the randomly selected to produce chil-
dren for the next generation. Through "mutation rules" the population
evolves towards an optimal solution.

• "Pattern Search". Pattern search evaluates one initially given point and
the function values close to the point. If it ﬁnds a better function values
it moves to that point and repeats.
As the strength of "Pattern Search" is to ﬁnd a local optimum close to a given
point, it was used in combination with either "Particle Swarm" or "Generic
Algorithm". As it does not require the whole search space, it is much more
suitable for such reﬁnements of an initial point. The different methods were
both applied and compared to get as close to the true optimum as possible.
Input variables for the tools are population size (n), maximum number of
iterations, and tolerance.
3.3.2
Default design assumptions
In some situations the entire set of input values contained in Table 3.1 might
not be known. This could be during validation, where the available infor-
mation is less detailed or situations where some degree of simpliﬁcation is
beneﬁcial. Some of the default assumptions provided here will be used later
for both validation and optimization. The assumptions are based on guide-
lines from literature and supported by real turbine data.
Geometric ratios
• Rotor exit hub to shroud ratio ν. The hub ratio should be large enough
to prevent that the hub blockage factor becomes too high. A value of
0.4 is adopted from [40]. Observed turbine test values ranges from 0.2
to 0.55.
• Stator exit to rotor inlet radius ratio Xr1. A ﬁxed value is not used, but
the Watanabe relationship (Eq. 3.76) is applied as suggested in various
sources (e.g. [22], [40], [43]). This produces values within the range of
typical turbine test values, which is Xr1 = 1.04 −1.10.
• Stator inlet to exit radius ratio Xr2. In practice this value should be large
enough to obtain the necessary ﬂow turning with a smooth proﬁle. A
value of 1.25 is assumed in [14] and adopted here. Common turbine
test values ranges from 1.15 to 1.25.
Number of blade and vanes
• The number of rotor blades is estimated by Eqn. 3.54, if not provided
in the data source. The common values range is 10 to 16.
• The number of stator vanes is estimated by assuming a stator solidity
of 1.35, which is adopted from [14] and [40]. Observed number of stator
vanes range from 12 to 27.

be based on blade/vane height. Normally, the leading edge is thicker than
the trailing edge to obtain an aerodynamic design. In [4] it is assumed that
the leading edge thickness of both stator vanes and rotor blades is twice the
trailing edge thickness. Combined with the suggestion in [40], following ex-

centrifugal forces might affect the blade length and shape. Therefore, the
margin should be higher for high temperature turbines than low temperature

If nothing else is speciﬁed, zero inlet angle, zero exit

"Aungier" loss models) reveals that the main trade-off is between fric-
tion and blade loading losses. However, variation of ψ greatly impacts
the inlet ﬂow angle, so the ﬂow feature constraints might cause this
optimal point to be located in the infeasible region.
• Flow coefﬁcienct φ
This was also an obvious choice, and like the load coefﬁcient this pa-

add to the exit energy loss, but low values will be restricted by the r6s

limit or by the natural frequency constraint. At high values, it might be
restricted by the minimum b4 value.
• Meridional velocity ratio ξ
This variable mainly affects the inlet ﬂow conditions and especially the
inlet blade height. A low value will result in large blade area and cause
high friction and curvature losses, whereas high values will increase
the velocity and many associated losses. It affects the incidence loss as
well. Like φ some of the relevant restrictions are the rotor minimum
inlet blade height and the natural frequency limit.
• Number of rotor blades Zr
A low number of blades will cause high blade load and deviation losses
while increasing the number will increase friction and clearance losses.
It is a good example of loss trade-off.
• Axial length to rotor inlet radius ratio Xz
The axial length is a trade-off between friction and curvature losses.
A really short turbine will cause big curvature losses while it reduces
friction and vica versa. If a low weight design is desired, it might be
reasonable to compromise this variable.

incidence angle and is restricted by the ﬂow feature constraints on α4
and β4. However, this also makes it a "tool" for allowing a wider range

on accompanying gearbox (if any) and generator. Topics like lubrica-

in the model, so it did not make sense to optimize this variable. For all input

3.3.4
Design code structure
The overall structure of the optimization code is seen on Figure 3.3. The com-
putational ﬂow is straight forward. First, the options and input values are
given, then the objective function is deﬁned, at last the optimization is per-
formed. The objective function is similar to the analysis model, with follow-
ing modiﬁcations:
• The form is modiﬁed from script to a function with the following three
inputs: name of input script, name of loss combination, vector with the
optimization variables.
• Instead of calculating all loss models, another version of the "S14_Loss-
Models.m" script is added. This does only calculate the losses accord-
ing to the given loss prediction procedure. Only three loss prediction
procedures are implemented for this purpose so far ("Baines","Aungier"
and "Glassman", see Section 2.4.3 for details), but more could easily be
added. This modiﬁcation was made to increase computational speed.
• A feasibility script is added after the post processing script. It evaluates
if the calculated design is feasible or not. If the design is not feasible,
the efﬁciency is set to zero.
• No plots are generated. The feasibility criteria are described in the fol-
lowing section.

Veriﬁcation
Veriﬁcation is a conﬁrmation that the model is implemented without errors,
so that the equations are right. It is also ensuring that the results are not far
from the results of similar code. Veriﬁcation is conducted as a comparison
with other mean-line models to document that the same or similar results are
obtained.
The initial calculation steps of the model was developed using the procedure
in the book "Radial and Axial Turbomachines" [22] by Baines et al. At a very
early design stage, the core equations of the rotor model (no stator model,
no loss modeling) were veriﬁed using the example cases A-D on page 280.
As all equations and input parameters were explicitly provided, the output
matched perfectly at this stage, so no discussion of this result is necessary.
The model was veriﬁed using three sources where extensive input and output
data were available. First, Ventura et al. [40] with air. Next, Fiaschi et al. [28]
with r134a and r245fa. Third, Glassman [14] with argon.
4.0.4
Comparison with Ventura et al. [40]
The procedure of the code developed by Ventura et al. is very similar to the
procedure of present code. In addition to veriﬁcation of the rotor and sta-
tor equations, some of loss models could be assessed too. Many of the loss
models used in this paper are also used in other prediction procedures. Fur-
thermore, both the ideal gas procedure and the real ﬂuid procedure can be
tested. The obtained difference between ideal gas and real ﬂuid was negligi-
ble and is not discussed any further.
The model input parameters can be directly used in present model, but two
adjustments has to be made within the procedure. First, the effect of blockage
factors is not mentioned and it is therefore assumed that they are neglected.
Second, the length of the turbine is calculated assuming the shroud radius to
be a quarter circle (instead of the hub radius).

Chapter 5
Validation
The goal of previous chapter was to verify the code by comparing outputs
to similar codes. Validation is the next step, which conﬁrms that the model
is able to predict the behaviour of a real (experimental) system. To rephrase,
it corresponds to testing that the assumptions (that lie behind the equations)
are suitable. Ideally, validation relies on experimental data. However, experi-
mental data can be difﬁcult to obtain. An alternative to a physical experiment
is a numerical experiment (or a simulation) conducted using CFD, which is
much easier to produce. Like a mean-line model, CFD does also rely on equa-
tions and assumptions rather than measurements. However, the equations
are of a more fundamental character and the assumptions are fewer.
This section takes four steps to achieve the goal of a full validation:
1. Validated with air using a well-described experimental turbine test case
(speciﬁc validation).
2. Validation with air over a range of designs using a list of experimental
and numerical (CFD) turbine test results (general validation).
3. Validation with organic ﬂuids over a range of designs using a list of
experimental and numerical turbine test results (general validation).
Afterwards, the loss models from the loss prediction procedures are com-
bined in every possible, but reasonable, way in order to investigate if bet-
ter prediction procedures can be found using the existing loss models. This
is conducted using the same test cases as the general validations. It is per-
formed both for ORC cases and for air cases.

5.1.1
Geometrical data and ﬂow
When assuming a constant efﬁciency for the rotor and stator calculations,
the geometry and ﬂow data in Table 5.1 is obtained. Several parameters are
not within 5% of the experimental data. These are r6s (9.84%), r6h (9.84%),

and BF6 (-5.62). Especially, the discrepancies of rotor exit radius and inlet
velocity (Mach numbers and angle) are signiﬁcant.
Many uncertainties and sources of error arises when comparing a 1D mean-
line model to experimental data:
• Non-meridional ﬂow distribution. The mean-line model does only cap-
ture average ﬂow conditions, so variations in the cross section of the
ﬂow channel (axial and tangential at rotor inlet, radial and tangential at
rotor exit) are not presented.
• 3D ﬂow effects. Secondary ﬂow effects (e.g. relative eddies in rotor inlet
section) are not computed. It is likely that velocities way higher and
with other angles than predicted by the 1D mean-line model occurs.
• Unsteadiness. The predictions of the mean-line model are not only av-
eraged in space, but also in time. During an experiment, pressures and
velocities will ﬂuctuate due to the relative motion between rotor and
stator blades/vanes.

• Radius of evaluation. At rotor exit, the radius of evaluation in the mean-

• Mean velocity triangles. The Mach number and ﬂow angle values from

averaged, there will be a discrepancy if the mean ﬂow line is not rep-
resentative of the averaged ﬂow. Figure 17b in the article [17] shows
the ﬂow angle variation along the radius at exit and is a good example
that the angles might vary signiﬁcantly. Extracting a mass-average ﬂow
angle from this ﬁgure was a possibility, but since no such ﬁgures were
provided for Mach numbers, this would have lead to non-consistent
velocity triangles. Therefore, the mean ﬂow line data was used instead.
• Method of calculating input parameters φ and ξ. These two parame-

also subject to the above mentioned source of error. Speciﬁcation of

at these stations (4 and 6). For example, increasing φ would decrease

dure described later in Section B). This would have resulted in smaller

Effect of ηts on geometry and ﬂow
This section will brieﬂy discuss how the loss models affects the geometry and
ﬂow characteristics. A prediction of ηts, which is far from the reported value
will lead to a different geometry which again will affect the efﬁciency. As
a result, the geometries and ﬂow characteristics which Figure 5.1 and Table
5.2 are based on, are not identical. Table 5.3 highlights the deviation of some
parameters. The tables clearly illustrates the importance of the efﬁciency es-
timation in a rotor design process. If for example the estimated efﬁciency is
20% (percentage points) too low ("Whitﬁeld-1"), the output blade height of
the model can be 40% too large.
Extra attention can be paid on the nozzle efﬁciency. Some loss prediction
procedures give a good estimation of the total-to-static efﬁciency, but a poor
estimate on the nozzle efﬁciency, or entirely neglects it . An example of this
is "Ventura-2". As a result of ignoring the nozzle efﬁciency, the pressure at
the rotor inlet is higher than measured. This will have an impact on the es-
timation of the rotor inlet blade height. "Ventura-" loss prediction procedure
does show a deviation on the rotor inlet pressure, but the inlet blade height
is predicted accurately. It can be concluded that the effect of nozzle efﬁciency
on the rotor design is small in this case. However, this might have a larger
impact in cases with higher temperatures and pressures.

strongest procedures are highlighted.

For air, the 5400 test case evaluations resulted in 3860 combinations with real,
positive rms values. A large part of the evaluations did not converge, most
likely due to over-prediction of the losses resulting in too low efﬁciencies.
The ten lowest rms values and the corresponding loss model combinations
are displayed in Table 5.8. The loss model combinations are represented here
as numbers refering to Table 2.2. The lowest possible rms value is 1.75 %,
which is 0.7 ??? %-points lower than the lowest rms value achieved with the
loss prediction procedures in Section 5.2. For all of the top ten combinations
the proﬁle, passage, and trailing edge losses are not applied. Instead, fric-
tion and blade load losses are used. Furthermore, the incidence loss model
"NASAx2"(3) and the blade load loss model "Aungier"(3) are used in most of
the combinations. Two friction models ((3)"Fiaschi" and (4)"Aungier") and
two clearance models ((2)"Jansen" and (5)"Aungier" stand out as the only
ones of their type, while all three nozzle models and all three windage mod-
els are present. This indicates that the nozzle and windage loss models have
a less signiﬁcant impact on the rms values than the other models. The com-
bination with lowest rms value is:
• (2)"Glassman" nozzle loss model, Eqn. 2.19.
• (3)"NASAx2" incidence loss model, Eqn. 2.36.
• (3)"Fiaschi" rotor friction loss model, Eqn. 2.46.
• (3)"Aungier" blade load loss model, Eqn. 2.52.
• (2)"Jansen" clearance model, Eqn. 2.62.
• (3)"Daily & Nece" windage model from Glassman [14], Eqn. 2.75.
This combination is reasonable in the sense that all the loss types are repre-
sented. A seperate proﬁle loss model is not needed, since the friction model
accounts for turbomachinery curvature. The combination is not similar to
any of the loss prediction procedures of Section 5.2, but a close look on the
3rd best combination shows that it is similar to the "Aungier" loss prediction
procedure. The friction, blade loading and clearance losses uses the same
models whereas the nozzle and incidence loss models are substituted by the
"Glassman" nozzle loss Eqn. 2.19) and the "NASAx2" (Eqn. 2.36 incidence
loss models. The "Daily & Nece" windage loss model (Eqn. ??) is added.
In a similar way, the 4th lowest rms value is a modiﬁcation of the "Fiaschi"
loss prediction procedure. Nozzle loss, friction loss and windage loss are
estimated in the same way, but the incidence, blade load and clearance are
substituted. Furthermore, the proﬁle loss is removed. The remaining 7 of the
10 combinations can be considered as variations of the 1st and 3rd with mi-
nor changes. (Number 2, 6 and 10 are variations of number 1. Number 5, 7,
8, and 9 are variations of number 3).

The different number of blades stresses the fact that friction loss, blade load
loss and incidence losses are weighted differently in the three prediction pro-
cedures. In ’Baines’ a very low number of blades is estimated, so the blade
load loss might be underestimated. "Glassman" suggests a high number of
blades, which might be because no incidence loss model is included. In the
"Aungier" loss prediction procedure the optimum is found somewhere in be-
tween, but still a relatively low number of blades.
The length of the turbine is clearly not reﬂected well enough in the "Baines"
loss prediction procedure, since a very low value is found optimal. This is an
indication that the proﬁle loss is underestimated. In "Aungier" and "Glass-

proﬁle losses are found.
The rotational speeds are seen to follow the load coefﬁcients (lower rotational
speeds allow higher load coefﬁcients). This is due to the constraint on the rel-
ative rotor inlet ﬂow angle β4. In Table 6.3 the details of the rotor design are
presented. Here, it can be seen that β4 takes the same value (lower boundary)
for all three solutions. It is interesting that this value seems to be a direct con-
straint for the efﬁciency. The effect of changing or removing this constraint
should be investigated. It is likely that removing the constraint would cause
solutions with larger incidence losses but higher efﬁciencies.

Chapter 7
Discussion
12 different loss prediction procedures for radial turbines have been analyzed
by the use of several different approaches.
First, the literature study indicated that some loss models were more well-
established than others. Next, some loss prediction procedures could be ver-
iﬁed by using other mean-line codes. All of the procedures were then com-
pared during a general validation with an air turbine test, where also the
break down of losses according to each model were compared. Then, the
performance of a wide range of experimental and CFD turbine tests were es-
timated and rms values were calculated for air turbines and ORC turbines
for each loss prediction procedure. It was evident that some of the loss pre-
diction procedures seriously over predicted the losses while others obtained
more reasonable values. Then it was investigated if better combinations of
the loss models existed resulting in guidelines for seemingly appropriate and
non-appropriate loss models. Finally, three of the most promising loss esti-
mation procedures were used for optimization which allowed comparison of
the predicted optimal design in addition to predicted efﬁciency.
No loss prediction procedure was superior through the entire analysis. In
other words, every loss model did show both strengths and weaknesses (some
mostly weaknesses).
This chapter discusses the main points from each part of the analysis sep-
arately and focuses on comparing the loss models qualitatively. In the last
section a full comparison of the loss prediction procedures is presented high-
lighting the strengths and weaknesses from all parts of the analysis.
7.1
Outcome of literature review
The outcome of the literature review is more than just the mathematical for-
mulations of the loss models that were presented in section 2.4. After inspec-
tion of several loss prediction procedures applied by different authors it be-
came apparent that a common practice is to select seemingly well-established
105
==PAGE:120==
7.2. Outcome of veriﬁcation
loss models from different sources and combine them without further consid-
eration. This is not necessarily a bad approach (especially if the loss models
are chosen carefully), but it was shown through validation with air and orc
test turbines that very different efﬁciency estimates are obtained when com-
paring the prediction procedures. Even though each loss model in a loss pre-
diction procedure has been tested and validated, it does not guarantee that
the mix of the models performs well. Furthermore, there is no guarantee that
the loss prediction procedure is suitable for turbines in other size- and power
ranges. In other words, the validity of the combination of loss models is not
necessarily ensured by the validity of the individual loss models. The loss
prediction procedures can be assessed prior to any calculations using follow-
ing two criteria. 1) Has it been veriﬁed / supported by experimental/CFD
investigation? 2) Is it missing any loss contributions or are two models ac-
counting for the same loss type? Evaluating the loss prediction procedures
(see discussion in Section 2.3) with regard to these criteria leads to the follow-
ing highlights:
• The loss prediction procedures by "Aungier" and "Baines" has been tested
against experimental air turbine tests demonstrating good results.
• "Suhrmann" and "Nithesh" loss prediction procedures have been veri-
ﬁed by CFD models.
• The loss prediction procedures "Withﬁeld-1", "Whitﬁeld-2", "Glassman",
"Fiaschi", and "Jung" remain (to authors knowledge) unsupported by
test data.
• Windage loss is not accounted for in "Withﬁeld-1", "Whitﬁeld-2", "Aungier",
and "Suhrmann". However, this is in all cases a minor loss.
• Incidence loss models are not used in "Whitﬁled-1" and "Glassman".
• Nozzle loss is not included in "Ventura-1", "Ventura-2", "Jung", "Nit-
hesh".
• "Nithesh" does not include clearance loss.
The loss prediction "MIX 1" was constructed during the validation and is
therefore by no means supported by any literature.
7.2
Outcome of veriﬁcation
The developed mean-line model was veriﬁed by comparing against three
other mean-line models from literature applying same loss prediction pro-
cedures. A few difﬁculties due to data availability lead to some discrepan-
cies, but all of them could be explained by logical reasons. Another difﬁculty

was the difference of input variables. These could for most part be estimated
through output values, but some uncertainty remain. The veriﬁcation did
lead to insights about one particular loss model applied by several authors.
The blade load model by Rodgers found in [40] (Eqn. 2.50) could not clearly

that the implementations of the other loss models seemed acceptable.
7.3
Outcome of speciﬁc validation
A validation of the geometry and ﬂow conditions predicted by the mean-line
model was achieved using a well described air turbine test case. Deviations
up to 11% were observed on some parameters. It was discussed how the
calculation of mean-line model input parameters and the quality of available
velocity data could explain discrepancies of this magnitude. In addition, the
general simpliﬁcations of mean-line modelling and other sources of uncer-
tainty were described.
A breakdown of the losses in 11 loss prediction procedures (most adopted
from literature) was presented and compared to the losses measured during
test. This revealed that while some of the prediction procedures estimated
the efﬁciency well, none of them had a break-down of losses that matched
the measurements. Therefore, a 12th loss prediction procedure ("MIX 1") was
constructed by choosing the loss models that were closest to the measured
loss components. Following conclusions about the efﬁciency estimation and
breakdown of losses could be made:
• The nozzle can potentially account for a big part of the losses and should
therefore not be neglected.
• The rotor and stator losses were predicted well by the "Aungier" loss
prediction procedure, but due to underestimation of the other losses
the efﬁciency estimation was too high.
• The break down of losses predicted by procedures "Whitﬁeld-2", "Baines"
and "Glassman" were generally closer to the measured losses than the
rest of the models (except "Aungier" and "MIX 1").
• Loss prediction procedures "Whitﬁeld-1", "Ventura-1", "Fiaschi", "Suhrmann",
and "Nithesh" over predicted the losses more than 15%-points.

7.4
Outcome of general validation (air)
The general validation with air used a range of air turbine tests to validate the
efﬁciency prediction of the mean-line model. 8 different turbine test data set
were used, 2 being CFD results, 6 being experimental. The mass ﬂow rates
ranged from 0.33 kg/s to 2.96 kg/s and the pressure ratios from 2.7 to 5.8. It
was occasionally necessary to supplement the data sets with reasonable as-
sumptions about e.g. turbine axial length, blade thickness and clearance gap
width. Due to these extra assumptions the root-mean-square (rms) differ-
ences in total to static efﬁciency were generally higher than the deviations
from the speciﬁc validation. To summarize the rms values:
• The loss prediction procedures "Whitﬁeld-1", "Ventura-1", "Fiaschi", and
"Suhrmann" gave rms values above 15%-points.
• "Nithesh" loss prediction procedure had rms value between 10 and 15
%-points.
• "Baines" had rms value between 5 and 10 %-points.
• "Whitﬁeld-2", "Aungier", "Glassman", "Ventura-2", "Jung" and "MIX 1"
achieved rms values between 2 and 5 %-points.
• "Aungier" showed the best predictions with a rms value of 2.4%.
• No loss prediction procedure lead to rms values below 2 %-points.
Furthermore, the loss prediction procedures "Whitﬁeld-1", "Whitﬁeld-2", "Fi-
aschi", "Suhrmann", and "Nithesh" encountered convergence problems when
applied to some of the validation cases.

7.6
Outcome of "best loss combination" search
It has previously been emphasized that combining loss models from different
sources is a questionable practice. However, an analysis doing exactly this
was conducted anyway, with the aim to compare the possible outcomes to
the implemented loss prediction procedures and look for possible improve-
ments. The search space created by the loss models that are included in this
work were evaluated. The objective was to achieve the lowest possible rms
values when validating against the air tests and the ORC tests, respectively.
When analyzing the 10 lowest rms values of the air and ORC tests, following
insights were obtained:
• Signiﬁcant decreases in rms value could be obtained using alternative
loss model combinations for both air and ORC.

==PAGE:124==
7.7. Outcome of design optimization
• The "optimal" loss combinations for air and ORC test cases were not at
all similar. A few conclusions can be drawn from this point. First, that
no superior combination of loss model exists by combining the given
loss models. Second, that the different media causes different loss mod-
els to be effective.
• The separation of rotor passage loss into friction and blade load loss
(and possible proﬁle loss) makes lower rms values achievable than us-
ing assembled rotor losses.
• The NASA incidence loss model (Eqn. 2.35), which seemed to be the
most well-established in literature, was not supported very well by this
analysis. Models predicting higher incidence related losses were gener-
ally giving better results.
• Speciﬁcally for the air turbine tests, an "improved" version of the "Aungier"
loss model combination was identiﬁed, where three minor loss models
are substituted with others.
• Similarly for the ORC tests, an "improved" version of the "Glassman"
loss prediction procedure was obtained. Based on this, two suggestions
for modiﬁcations were made.
To summarize, this section did not as such lead to new insights about the
loss prediction procedures. Rather a few indications about which loss mod-
els to prefer were identiﬁed, and a few suggestions for alternative loss model
combinations (or loss prediction procedures) were discovered. (If these new
loss prediction procedures were very similar to each other or to the suggested
model "MIX 1", a further veriﬁcation and validation would have been very in-
teresting. However, as no superior loss prediction procedure was not found,
it was decided to focus on further evaluation of the loss prediction procedures
from literature.)
7.7
Outcome of design optimization
The optimization case clearly illustrated that even though different loss pre-
diction procedures might estimate similar efﬁciencies, the optimized design
can be very different. Three loss prediction procedures ("Baines", "Aungier",
and "Glassman") were compared, and none of them predicted very similar
designs. It is an important evaluation aspect to consider if the loss prediction
is capable of optimizing a certain set of design variables. Following insights
were achieved:
• The "Baines" loss prediction procedure was able to optimize 4 of the 6
optimization variables. The number of rotor blades and the axial length
were not reﬂected enough in the loss models, so the optimal values
occured at the boundary.
Page 110
==PAGE:125==
CHAPTER 7. Discussion
• The "Aungier" loss prediction procedure was able to optimize 6 of the 6
variables. The procedure estimated a very large stator losses compared
to the two other methods.
• The "Glassman" loss prediction procedure was also able to optimize
6 of 6 optimization variables. It suggested a blade number that was
higher compared to the other two. Probably due to lack of incidence
loss model.
A lot of additional work could be done to investigate the optimization possi-
bilities of the model, in particular by adding shock wave losses to the mod-
els and remove the subsonic constraint. Other extensions of the investigation
could include extending the feasibility constraints and optimizing some other
loss prediction procedures (e.g. the procedures suggested in Section 5.4). The
analysis could also be strengthened by additional optimizations with more
iterations and larger populations to reveal eventual better designs.

Conclusion
A model for the preliminary 1-D meanline design of radial inﬂow turbines
was developed. It included calculations of the stator and rotor blade radii,
blade heights, number of vanes, number of blades, ﬂow angles and axial tur-
bine length. Moreover, a calculation of the stator throat for supersonic ﬂows
was added. To estimate the efﬁciency, various empirically developed loss
models from literature were included. Two different codes were produced:
1. An Analysis code with the purpose of calculating the efﬁciency of a given
design and set of operating conditions. Moreover, it was capable of
comparing the efﬁciency prediction and break-down of losses using dif-
ferent combinations of loss models or loss prediction procedures.
2. An Optimization code with the purpose of calculating the optimal efﬁ-
ciency and design using a given loss prediction procedure and operat-
ing conditions. Several constraints of different types were applied to
ensure a feasible design solution.
The analysis code were used for veriﬁcation and validation of the model. The
veriﬁcation was conducted using other similar codes using both air and or-
ganic ﬂuids. Relative errors of maximum 5% on ﬂow and geometry parame-
ters were obtained when verifying with the most similar mean-line code from
literature. Validation of the model was achieved using a speciﬁc test case of
an air turbine. Results within 11 % of published geometry and ﬂow values
were obtained.
A comprehensive comparison of loss prediction procedures from literature
was conducted. 13 different procedures were identiﬁed (12 from books and
articles and 1 from validation of the code) and applied in the analysis code
to estimate the efﬁciency of 8 different air turbine test cases and 13 different
ORC turbine test cases. Based on this and other insights about the loss predic-
tion procedures obtained through the work, following three loss prediction
procedures were highlighted:

==PAGE:128==
• The procedure based on Ref. [22] ("Baines") is well established in lit-
erature having been validated with lots of test cases of air turbines. In
this work, it generally performed well, but especially the result of the
general validation with organic ﬂuids was good compared to the other
models. The weaknesses was the break-down of losses that did not
match the measured values in the validation case and a low rms value
was obtained in the general validation with air. When using this proce-
dure with the optimization code, it was found that the number of rotor
blades and the axial turbine length were not well represented in the loss
models and therefore could not be optimized.
• The procedure from Ref. [4] ("Aungier") did not appear as well-established,
but has priorly been validated with air turbine test cases. This predic-
tion procedure had the overall best results and presented the break-
down of losses better than any other model. The weaknesses of this
procedure was the results that were obtained during the general vali-
dation with ORC turbines. When used with the optimization code, the
estimation of losses reﬂected all the design variables, so the applica-
tion in design optimization seemed promising. However, it produced
very larger nozzle losses compared to the other models. On basis of the
general validation with air, an improved version of this procedure was
identiﬁed. The modiﬁcations were: 1) Replacing the nozzle loss model
with the "Glassman" nozzle loss model (Eqn. 2.19). 2) Replacing the in-
cidence loss model with the "Nasax2" incidence loss model (Eqn. 2.36).
3) Adding a model for the windage loss, e.g. the "Glassman" windage
loss model (Eqn. 2.75).
• The procedure used in the turbine design code from Ref. [14] is not
as recent as the two other procedures. A validation of the code was not
found and no incident loss model was included. However, this loss pre-
diction procedure achieved good results in the speciﬁc and general val-
idations with air. The drawbacks were the general validation with ORC
turbine tests and that it was experienced one time that the model did
not converge using this loss prediction procedure. The general valida-
tion with air revealed a possible improvement for this procedure. This
is to replace the nozzle loss with the "Aungier" loss model (Eqn. 2.22)
and to add an incidence loss model (Eqn. 2.34). As with the "Aungier"
loss prediction procedure, it was possible to optimize all investigated
optimization variables when using this procedure with the optimiza-
tion code.
The three highlighted loss prediction procedures did both show strengths
and weaknesses, and it is difﬁcult to recommend one in front of the other.
However, the "Aungier" loss prediction procedure did produce slightly bet-
ter results than the other two during the validation. Further investigation of
the suggestions for improvement for the "Aungier" and "Glassman" proce-

8.1. Proposals for further work
8.1
Proposals for further work
Many options exist for the next step of the developed radial turbine model.
This includes both model improvements, additional model features and ap-
plications. The following is a non-exhaustive list of possible topics.
• Additional validation of the model using detailed data from experimen-
tal organic ﬂuid turbine test. This would strengthen the model and pos-
sibly give new insights about loss prediction.
• Additional validation of the loss estimation procedures using experi-
mental ORC turbine data and identiﬁcation of a preferred loss predic-
tion procedure. The screening and ranking of the available loss predic-
tion procedures in this study was thorough, but no deﬁnite conclusion
about which loss prediction procedure to use was made. Experimen-
tal ORC turbine data with more details than what was available to the
author could possibly provide new insights.
• Validation of shock loss models. Very little information was found in
the literature about shock losses in radial turbines. Validation of the
loss model used in the code or development of new estimation methods
for this type of loss would be of good value.
• Deviation angles. Models for estimating deviation between blade and
ﬂow angles at rotor exit where not applied. This would increase the
precision of the model.
• Development of off-design model. An off-design model would predict
the real turbine performance over varying operating conditions. For
some applications the operating conditions change signiﬁcantly (due
to whether, available heat, power requirement etc.), which will turbine
performance. A such model would not be very different, but devia-
tion models and a change in input parameters would be required. The
loss models would not require any adoption, but a larger incidence loss
would be experienced.
• Extend model to make multi-stage calculations possible. The additional
stages could be axial or radial.
• Repeat optimization with super sonic nozzle design strategy and shock
losses. Optimize using other loss combinations and with increased pre-
cision (higher populations and more iterations).
• Application of the model to test cases by integration with a thermo-
dynamic cycle calculation tool. This would provide valuable results
about turbine performance and size and make a comparison to other
expander types possible.

Appendix C
Reduction of computational
effort
It is described how the computational effort was reduced when searching for
improved loss prediction procedures. The number of required model evalua-
tions, or the search space is the product of the number of loss models for each
loss type. For example, with three nozzle loss models, four incidence models,
three passage loss models and one of all other models the search space would
be three-dimensional and it would require 3 · 4 · 3 = 36 model evaluations to
test all combinations. With the amount of described loss models, the search
space is huge and would require signiﬁcant computational effort. Therefore
a few restrictions are made:
• Reduction 1. The blade load models following Rodgers (Eqns. 2.51 and
2.52) have so far greatly over-predicted the loss. Therefore, these are
not included.
• Reduction 2. The windage loss is a minor contribution, and the three
different models were shown to predict only slightly different values.
To reduce the search space by a factor of 3, only one of the models will
be applied (2.75. This is not expected to have any signiﬁcant impact
on the optimal combination of the other models. Selection of the most
appropriate windage loss model is outside the scope of this analysis.
• Reduction 3. To keep basic reason within the loss model combinations,
assembled passage loss and trailing loss models should not be com-
bined with rotor friction, curvature and blade load models. This is be-
cause they intend to describe the same phenomena. The search space
will be divided into combinations using an assembled passage loss and
combinations based on friction and blade load models.
