Abstract 
 
As concerns about the environment are growing, new efforts are needed to achieve more 
sustainable processes. The highest constituent greenhouse gas is the carbon dioxide, at 82% and is 
growing with time. These trends make it evident that there is a need for methods to reduce these 
greenhouse gases emissions. While there are two methods of reducing carbon dioxide emissions, 
carbon capture and storage (CCS) and carbon capture and utilisation (CCU), CCU is considered 
promising as it makes further use of the carbon dioxide as a solvent, raw material, or reagent to 
produce valuable products [1]. Using such utilisation processes, the emissions can be reduced as 
they are being utilised and profit can be obtained, or the cost of operation for the carbon dioxide 
treatment can be returned. 
In order to systematically reduce such emissions, carbon capture and utilisation is considered in 
this work rather than carbon capture and storage. To achieve this a methodology is developed to 
design sustainable carbon dioxide utilisation processes. First, the information on the possible 
utilisation alternatives is collected, including the economic potential of the process and the carbon 
dioxide emissions. The carbon dioxide emissions can be classified as direct and indirect emissions 
in a chemical process. The net carbon dioxide is determined as the direct and indirect carbon 
dioxide emissions minus the carbon dioxide utilised. Processes that present zero or negative net 
carbon dioxide emission are desired in order to reduce the carbon dioxide emissions. Using this 
estimated preliminary evaluation, the top processes with the most negative carbon dioxide 
emission, are investigated by rigorous detailed simulation to further evaluate the net carbon 
dioxide emissions. The top products are succinic acid, direct dimethyl carbonate, propylene 
carbonate and methanol. Once the base case design is established and evaluated, targeted 
improvements are made by exploiting opportunities, for example, optimization, heat integration 
and improved design decisions so that more sustainable and lower net carbon dioxide emission 
alternatives are obtained. Then, the net CO2 emissions are calculated from the rigorous 
simulations.  
==PAGE:14==
Sustainable carbon dioxide capture and conversion process design 
14 
 
Not all the studied processes could be designed for zero or negative net carbon dioxide emission. 
Propylene carbonate production is found to have a negative net carbon dioxide -0.40 kg of carbon 
dioxide per kg of propylene carbonate. On the other hand, for succinic acid production, even after 
targeted improvements, the net carbon dioxide remained positive at 1.20 kg of CO2 emitted per kg 
of succinic acid. 
Then, a carbon dioxide based network is built with the rigorous simulations carried out and other 
results available from previous work. A proposed network for carbon reduction that showed 
rigorous negative net carbon dioxide has been considered and further analysed by economics. The 
DMC produced by following this network that includes the production of propylene carbonate and 
methanol showed a rigorous net CO2 of -0.45 kg of CO2 per kg of DMC and presented economical 
favourable results. A payback time of 4 years, IRR of 34.5 and a net present value of 110.6M$. 
The proposed methodology is able to find a problem solution for the carbon dioxide emissions. 
This CCU solution has both negative net CO2 emissions and is economically interesting. Thus, is a 
feasible technology to be applied in the industries because is a economic feasible and sustainable 
solution.  
This work shows that for meaningful net carbon dioxide reduction, the carbon dioxide utilisation 
processes need to be selected very carefully to obtain the best results. However, the opportunity 
to potentially reduce the net carbon dioxide emissions for the production of some bulk chemicals 
with carbon dioxide as feedstock exists.

1.1 Carbon dioxide problem 
As concerns about the environment are growing, new efforts are needed to achieve more 
sustainable processes. One such environmental concern is global warming. Global warming is 
mostly caused by the greenhouse effect, which is the increase in concentration of greenhouse 
gases. As can be observed in Figure 1 the main constituent of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions is 
carbon dioxide (CO2) which is also increasing faster over time. This indicates that something has to 
be done in order to reduce the GHGs and especially the CO2 emissions.

1.2 Why is carbon dioxide a problem 
Carbon dioxide is produced as a by-product in a large variety of industries, such as in energy 
production and fuel consumption, chemical manufacturing and cement. As it can be observed in 
Figure 1, the global CO2 emissions from fossil fuels and industrial processes were around 35000 
million tonnes per year (Mt/y) in 2010 and significantly growing.  
In Figure 2 the different stationary emission sources of carbon dioxide by industry sector can be 
observed. Stationary emissions sources include power plants and industrial manufacturing plants. 
Of the emissions from such sources, power plants contribute 54%, the cement industry 
contributes 15%, gas processing represents 12%, refineries represent 5% and others represent the 
rest. Global carbon emissions from fossil fuels, power plants, has doubled the amount between 
1970 and 2011 as it can be observed in Figure 1. The trend for carbon emissions from fossil fuels 
indicates that is going to continually increase if nothing is done.

1.3 Carbon mitigation  
There are different ways to address the carbon dioxide problem. Carbon capture and storage (CCS) 
and carbon capture and utilisation (CCU) both aim to capture CO2 from emission sources to 
prevent the release of it into the atmosphere. The carbon capture process could be the same for 
both technologies. However, the difference is what is done with this captured carbon. In CCS the 
carbon is transported to a proper long-term storage location [3]. In CCU, the carbon dioxide is 
converted into commercial products obtaining then some value and reducing the actual amount of 
carbon dioxide. 
1.3.1 Carbon capture and storage (CCS) 
Carbon capture and storage is the process of capturing waste carbon dioxide from large emission 
sources, such as those described in section 1.2. This is followed by the transportation of it to a 
storage site and disposal of it in a suitable place, for example underground geological formations, 
so that it will not enter the atmosphere. It aims to mitigate the large carbon dioxide emissions.  
While a promising long-term alternative, the storage technology is immature and the implications 
are not well understood. Therefore, it requires CO2 monitoring and verifications to ensure that the 
injected CO2 remains underground. In addition, there is the problem of potential leakage and 
earthquakes. In some countries CCS is not a viable option as their geological storage capacity is 
limited or in some cases only available offshore, thus increasing transportation and injection costs. 
This is the case, for example, for the UK, Norway, Singapore, Brazil and India [3]. 
Carbon capture and storage is a potential way to avoid CO2 emissions. However, in this way, the 
CO2 is not reduced; rather, it is just not emitted by keeping it underground. Furthermore, there 
are indirect carbon dioxide emissions for the carbon capture, transportation and injection. In 
terms of economics, CCS also presents obstacles, as it is an unprofitable activity that requires large 
capital investment amongst other costs.

1.3.2 Carbon capture and utilisation (CCU) 
Carbon capture and utilisation is an alternative to carbon capture and storage. Although the 
capture process is the same, the utilisation involves the transformation of CO2 to commercial 
products. One utilisation option is carbon dioxide conversion by a chemical reaction into other 
products. Thus, this CO2 is not emitted and it is converted into another product.  
Carbon capture and can turn waste CO2 emissions into valuable sustainable products, such as 
chemicals and fuels, and at the same time contribute to the mitigation of GHG emissions. Carbon 
capture and is a promising alternative to carbon capture and storage. Alternative to CCS, using the 
carbon dioxide captured in a conversion process produces a commercial product that can provide 
profit for the company. Moreover, the use of CO2 instead of some petrochemicals would be 
positive, since CO2 is a renewable resource, low in cost and non-toxic [1]. There are different 
opportunities for carbon conversion. Production of different bulk chemicals from carbon dioxide 
and through different paths are going to be studied. The following chemical compounds were

1.4 Motivations 
As the previous section shows, there is a need to increase sustainability, especially to reduce CO2 
emissions. Carbon capture and utilisation is going to be considered to address this problem.  A 
method to design utilisation processes is developed accounting for sustainability, economic and 
environmental issues. This method aims to promote the carbon capture in the industrial processes 
followed by a utilisation process or network. The CO2 can be reduced as it is being utilised and 
profit can be obtained or the investment and cost of operation for the CO2 treatment can be 
returned through the utilisation process. By following the methodology, a CO2 utilisation network 
will be proposed ideally with positive economic and sustainability impact for the company to be 
implemented. Thus, this method has the potential to promote the carbon emissions reduction in 
the source plants by presenting positive motivations to do that. 
Different research has been carried out on CO2 capture processes and conversion processes to 
produce liquid fuels and/or chemicals (methanol, formic acid, dimethyl ether, dimethyl carbonate) 
and some have been implemented in industrial plants. It is also important to study products other 
than methanol conversion process, as there is a huge amount of CO2 than can potentially be 
captured.

2 Carbon dioxide capture  
The different techniques for carbon dioxide capture in power generation are classified as: post-
conversion capture, pre-conversion capture and oxy-fuel combustion [3], which are shown in  
Figure 3 and described below. 
- 
Post-conversion capture: This involves the separation of CO2 from flue gas after the 
conversion of carbon based source to carbon dioxide. This capture method is the most 
versatile and can be applied to various processes across the different industry sectors. 
The capture can be done by absorption in solvents, adsorption by solid sorbents, 
membranes, cryogenic separation and pressure and vacuum swing adsorption. The 
most common separation process is absorption by monoethanolamine (MEA) [7] 
 
- 
Pre-conversion capture: This is the capture of CO2 generated as an undesired co-
product of an intermediate reaction of a conversion process. This could be applied to 
the production of ammonia and coal gasification in power plants. The involved 
separation processes could be physical solvents and membranes.  
 
- 
Oxy-fuel combustion: This method can be applied to processes involving combustion. 
The fuel is burned with pure oxygen to produce flue gas with high CO2 concentration 
and without NO and NO2. There is not the need for CO2 separation.

3.1.1 Step 1: Data collection 
It is important to collect the reaction data for all the possible carbon dioxide conversion routes. 
This includes the reaction itself, the catalysts used, the optimal reaction conditions, the reaction 
selectivity, side reactions, the product yield or conversion, raw material and product prices. These 
are all stored in a database, which also includes the global production of each of the products. The 
creation of this database is of special interest for the subsequent steps. 
3.1.2 Step 2: Select promising candidates 
The second step of the methodology consists of a first screening of the promising candidates. To 
shorten the list of candidates, the economic potential and the net CO2 emissions will be used as 
criteria. The potential impact that the implementation or substitution of the industrial processes 
by the carbon dioxide utilisation can have will be also studied. 
The economic potential has been considered as the first criterion for process selection. The reason 
is that the method is intended to motivate industries to reduce the carbon emissions by promising 
at the same time some economic benefits. Carbon capture and utilisation as an alternative to CCS 
presents economic advantage at the same time as atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration 
reduction.  
The economic potential is defined as the difference between the revenue from product sales per 
year and the raw material cost per year, as shown in equation (3.1). It is an indicator of process 
profitability.

The second criterion is the net CO2 emissions. The net CO2 emissions are defined as the overall 
balance of CO2 in the process. It accounts for the indirect, direct emissions and carbon dioxide 
utilised. Ideally, a process that has zero or negative net CO2 is desired.  
It is important to know that CO2 emissions can be classified as direct and indirect in a chemical 
process. Direct emissions are present in processes where CO2 is a product of the reaction. On the 
other hand, indirect CO2 emissions accounts for the carbon dioxide associated with the energy 
consumption. Thus, a process that has a negative net CO2 is a process in which the direct and

emissions that result if natural gas or another kind of fuel is needed to heat a stream. The CO2 
utilised would be the consumption of CO2 due to the reaction (i.e. the input minus any purge).

3.1.3 Step3: Rigorous simulation of promising candidates 
Rigorous simulation is going to be carried out in the third step of the method. All the selected 
candidates from the previous step are rigorously simulated. The potential for negative CO2 
emissions found in the previous step is going to be further investigated by this rigorous simulation 
and specific literature research. Each simulation is going to consider the whole process including 
separation and purification.  
3.1.4 Step4: Process evaluation 
From the rigorous simulation, the energy balances and mass balances are going to be analysed in 
order to calculate the rigorous net CO2 emissions.  
The indirect CO2 emissions will be calculated from the energy balances from Aspen following the 
same conversion factors as in step 2. The CO2 utilised is going to be calculated from the mass 
balance of the simulation. The CO2 utilised would be the difference between the fed CO2 and the 
various outlets of CO2 in the process (such as purge streams). Finally, this evaluation will identify 
targets for improvement.  
3.1.5 Step 5: Process optimisation 
Process evaluation has to be carried out in the fourth step in order to identify the hotspots and 
targets. From this, different improvements, such as heat integration or novel process 
configurations, are suggested and simulated in this step. 
3.1.6 Step 6: Propose CO2 based network 
A CO2 process network is going to be constructed from the output of step 4 and considering step 
5. From this general network, the promising CO2 based network solution will be proposed. This 
network ideally will have net CO2 negative, potential impact and economical positive 
opportunities.

3.1.7 Step 7: Economic validation 
To validate the proposed CO2 based network in the previous step, economic analysis will be 
carried out. This is of high importance because the aim of this methodology is to both achieve a 
negative net CO2 and present economic advantage to promote CCU. 
 
3.2 Tools 
Different tools were used to develop the methodology. These are Microsoft Excel, Aspen Plus and 
ICAS. 
Microsoft Excel is used for spreadsheet calculations of the CO2 emissions, economic potential, 
mass balances, energy balances and economic analysis.  
Aspen Plus is the software tool for process simulations, both simple and rigorous. In the simulation 
engine, it is possible to select different unit operations for the flowsheet creation. For that, 
process information is required as well as background understanding of the different units. 
 ICAS is used to obtain the VLE for the distillations and for the solubility curves.

The candidates for rigorous simulation are: 
- 
Succinic acid via glucose: It presented positive economic potential and the opportunity 
to have negative net CO2 emissions. The substitution of a small percentage of the 
succinic acid production (10%) could lead to a global succinic acid production of zero 
net CO2 emissions. Succinic acid via glycerol was discarded, as there was less literature 
for the process design available.

Product uses: Succinic acid is an important intermediate chemical witnessing a high

chemicals that are used in a wide range of applications, such as polymers, food, metals, 
pharmaceuticals, coatings, fibres, solvents, lubricating oils, diesel fuel oxygenates, and 
cosmetics. 
- 
Propylene carbonate: The production of propylene carbonate from carbon dioxide and

Ethylene carbonate presented the same possibilities and benefits as propylene

of this product. However, the entire process from ethylene oxide to dimethyl carbonate

Dimethyl carbonate direct synthesis from methanol: This is a quite new process and a

Product uses: DMC is an important building block for different organic syntheses

-   Fermentation  
M.succiniciproducens wild-type strain is the fermentation microorganism used for the glucose 
fermentation. The fermentation takes place at 37°C and atmospheric pressure by feeding glucose 
in 700g/l of concentration in water at 40°C, maintaining the glucose concentration in the culture 
medium in the range of 30-45 g/l. The fermentation takes 29h and succinate is produced with 
pyruvic acid, acetic acid and malic acid and other acids as by-products. The reactions path can be 
observed in Appendix A. The fermenter has been simulated as a black box using a yield reactor, 
specifying the yields provided in [24]. However, only the yields for succinic acid, pyruvic acid, 
acetic acid and malic acid were available. Thus, a correction factor was used to match the mass 
balance. The feed of nutrients and M.succiniciproducens were not taken into account for the 
simulation. The fermenter sterilization was simulated by heating up the streams to 130°C and 
cooling down to 37°C. 
-  Reactive extraction 
The exit of the fermenter, after removing the biomass, is sent to the reactive. The solvent used is 
tri-n-octylamine (TOA) diluted in 1-octanol because of the high viscosity and the corrosive 
property of TOA at a concentration of 0.25molTOA/kg [23]. TOA is selected because of its high 
extractability and because of the selective removal of the by-products. TOA only extracts the non-
dissociated form of carboxylic acid. The reactive extraction is carried out for the removal of 
contaminated organic acids and salts from the fermentation broth by the separation of the organic 
phase from the aqueous phase. In the reactive extraction, the succinate is converted to succinic 
acid. This was simulated with a separator. 
At the exit of the reactive extraction there is an extraction separation vessel were volatiles are 
vented. These volatiles are acetic acid, pyruvic acid, malic acid and a small amount of succinic acid.

Flash:  
The product from the reactor goes to the separation process. The first step is a flash to 
remove the unreacted carbon dioxide. The operating conditions to minimize the product 
losses are 25°C and 1.5 atm. This carbon dioxide will be recycled to the reactor.   
 
- 
Distillation column 1: 
The liquid outlet of the flash containing mainly methanol, dimethyl carbonate and water is 
sent to a first distillation column or predistillation. This step is to remove part of the 
methanol, which is in large quantities, and the remaining water to increase the DMC 
concentration. The stream obtained at the top of the distillation column will be the 
azeotropic composition (DMC/MeOH), which is a low boiling azeotrope. This azeotrope can 
be observed in Figure 13 at the composition of 86 mol% MeOH and 14 mol % DMC.

In the simulation with Aspen, dimethyl carbonate is set as the light key and methanol is 
set as the heavy key; this ensures that the azeotropic composition goes out the top and 
the bottom is primarily methanol. The operating pressure is 1.5 bar as the flash. Then, 
these results were used for the rigorous RadFrac distillation model. At the top there is a 
partial condenser with a CO2 vapour outlet to be recycled and the near azeotropic 
DMC/MeOH stream. At the bottom the rest of the methanol is obtained. 
 
- 
Extractive distillation: 
The near azeotropic composition obtained condensed at the top of the first distillation 
column enters the next separation step. This separation can be done by pressure swing 
distillation, membranes or extractive distillation. Extractive distillation has been chosen as 
it uses 71% less energy than pressure swing distillation and there is not much information 
available for the membrane separation of DMC/MeOH. According to the literature, the 
best solvent to be used is 2-ethoxyethanol and 4-methyl-2-pentanone (methyl isobutyl 
ketone, MIBK) [28]. A MIBK:Feed ratio of 0.8 was determined to be the optimal [27]. The 
==PAGE:54==

operating pressure was set to be 7 atm in order to ensure that the methanol remains in the 
liquid phase and it is not lost in the vapour with the remaining CO2. 
The simulation of the extractive distillation was carried out with the RadFrac model by 
feeding the stream from the distillation 01 (predistillation) and the solvent with the 
calculator of Aspen to ensure the solvent: feed ratio. At the top of the extractive 
distillation, it is desired to obtain the methanol and the carbon dioxide, whilst at the 
bottom the product and the solvent MIBK is wanted. Thus, the distillate and bottom to 
feed ratio were calculated according to the specifications and 79 stages were specified. The 
feed is at the stage 39 and the solvent is fed in the stage 10. The condenser is partial, so 
the carbon dioxide is leaving the top of the distillation as vapour whilst the methanol in the 
condensed phase. 
 
- 
Distillation column 3: 
The product stream from the extractive distillation containing the DMC and the solvent is 
sent to the solvent recovery column, which operates at 1.5 atm. The DMC is separated 
from the MIBK, which is recycled to the solvent feed in the distillation column 2. The 
simulation specifications are light key recovery DMC of 0.99 and heavy key recovery MIK of 
0.0005. Then, the DMC is obtained with 99.2% purity.

4.6 Proposed carbon dioxide network 
The production of propylene carbonate from process evaluation and methanol from previous work 
[19] showed negative carbon dioxide net emissions.  
The production of methanol from hydrogen and carbon dioxide presents negative net CO2 
emissions. Methanol synthesis gives the opportunity for high carbon utilisation (1.4 kg of carbon 
dioxide are used to produce 1 kg of methanol). The indirect CO2 emissions are 0.74 kg of carbon 
dioxide per kg of methanol. They are not that big because the process is not high energy 
demanding. By process optimisation, the indirect carbon dioxide emissions could be further 
reduced. This utilisation process has the potential to reduce the carbon dioxide emissions, also 
because it is a bulk chemical with high demand. However, as it was shown in Table 5 in section 
3.1.2, the production of methanol is not economically feasible, because of the high value of 
hydrogen as raw material and the low product price. However, the production of methanol 
through this path could be used to produce a higher value product to have positive economic 
potential at the same time than negative net CO2 emissions. 
The propylene carbonate production is already done industrially by this utilisation path, so 
producing the propylene carbonate by carbon utilisation would not have that much influence for 
carbon dioxide reduction. To have an impact in carbon dioxide reduction by the production of PC, 
more and new applications should be found for the propylene carbonate or to use it as an 
intermediate product to produce a higher value product will contribute to the reduction.  DMC is a 
higher value product that can be produced along with propylene glycol (PG) as a high value by-
product from methanol and propylene carbonate as raw materials [30] [31] [32]. DMC can also be 
produced from other processing paths, which uses raw materials that can be produced from CO2 
utilisation reactions, such as by the ethylene carbonate and the urea routes[18]. The simulations 
of these processes are available from previous work [18] and the values obtained will be used to 
calculate the carbon dioxide emissions, utilisation and net CO2. The processes for the production 
of propylene carbonate and methanol, already rigorously simulated in section 4.4.2 and from 
literature [19] were scaled up to connect them with the DMC production process simulation.

the potential for economic feasibility is going to be further analysed in terms of economics.

and price hotspots. Then, the global process is economically studied.

5 Discussion 
The output of stage 1 is a database of candidates for carbon dioxide reduction with reaction paths, 
global production, conversion and other data. The stage 2 consists of promising candidates 
selection from the database by applying specific criteria that will lead to the final problem 
solution. For that, the economic potential was the first screening criterion and secondly the net 
carbon dioxide restriction was applied. Solutions that offer both positive economic impact and 
negative carbon dioxide emissions are desired to help address the carbon dioxide problem. 
Candidates that offer higher carbon dioxide utilisation are of special interest; this means that to 
produce one kilogram of product a significant amount of carbon dioxide is fed. This occurs when 
the co-reactant has low molecular weight or the CO2 stoichiometry is higher. Then, the product is 
mainly composed by the weight offered from the carbon dioxide molecular insertion. For example, 
that is the case of methanol production from hydrogen and carbon dioxide. Although hydrogen 
has a stoichiometry number of 3 and carbon dioxide of 1, the molecular weight difference is bigger 
than the stoichiometry difference and presents a high carbon dioxide utilisation potential. 
In the first screening, only the indirect emissions until the reactor were considered in the 
calculations of the carbon dioxide emissions. However, it is possible that the most carbon dioxide 
emissions result from the downstream processing, as these can be energy intensive. These are 
difficult to incorporate in the early stages and therefore consider in later stages when because 
more process information is available. 
In stage 3, the rigorous net CO2 results are obtained. For that, rigorous simulation of the promising 
candidates for the carbon dioxide utilisation are carried out. In this stage, the carbon dioxide 
emissions associated with the energy requirements for the whole production process are 
incorporated. At the same time, more consistent results for the carbon dioxide utilisation are 
obtained, because the kinetic model is introduced in the simulation and the required purges are 
accounted for. The processes that have been simulated in this step are succinic acid via the 
bioprocess, DMC via the direct synthesis, and propylene carbonate production. 
The results of the rigorous simulation are used as input for the process evaluation, the 4th stage. 
The simulation results are high accuracy information for the carbon dioxide balance calculations, 
==PAGE:81==
Sustainable carbon dioxide capture and conversion process design 
81 
 
the net CO2 emissions. From these results, it can be observed that some of the promising 
candidates are no longer interesting for carbon dioxide reduction unless further process 
optimisation or better catalysts are developed. On the other hand, other candidates that resulted 
promising in step 2, in the rigorous simulation show negative net CO2 and thus, are of high interest 
for carbon dioxide reduction. Those are the production of propylene carbonate with -0.40 kg of 
CO2 per kg of product and the synthesis of methanol from hydrogen with -0.66 kg of CO2 per kg of 
product. 
Stage 5 is process optimisation. In this step, a study case amongst the ones rigorously simulated 
has been studied for process optimisation. From the carbon dioxide emissions and from the 
energy requirements, it could be observed that the compression is not significant in the overall 
results. However, the CO2 emissions and energy consumption for heat exchange was of big 
importance. The heat exchangers, whose function is to heat streams, account for carbon dioxide 
emissions. On the other hand, there were a large amount of energy to be removed from streams 
that needed to be cooled down. Thus, heat integration as process optimisation alternative was 
considered. After doing heat integration, the CO2 emissions and energy requirements were 
reduced around 50%. Nevertheless, the succinic acid production continued being positive net CO2 
and thus, not being suitable for carbon dioxide reduction. On the other hand, it may present lower 
emissions than the industrial process and may be of interest for that reason. Because small 
amount of carbon dioxide is utilised in this process, the energy requirements should be meaningful 
to achieve a negative or zero net CO2 emissions via the succinic acid production. Thus, further 
research should be carried out for the succinic acid and investigate the potential impact that this 
process could have if this was the actual running process instead of the maleic anhydride process. 
This step can be applied to all the promising candidates. 
In stage 6, a carbon dioxide based network is built. This includes results from the rigorous 
simulations and results from previous work. These processes were also combined to go to higher 
value products as a second stage product in order to enhance the economics and utilise more 
carbon dioxide (by combining two utilisation paths to create a secondary product). The methanol 
process utilises high amount of CO2 and presents good opportunities for carbon dioxide reduction. 
However, the results obtained in the economic potential estimations in step 2 show that is 
economically infeasible due to the high cost of the hydrogen (RM) and the low selling price of 
==PAGE:82==
Sustainable carbon dioxide capture and conversion process design 
82 
 
methanol. On the other hand, propylene carbonate does not utilise that much carbon dioxide but 
shows positive economic potential. However, PC is known to be already produced by this route. 
From those conclusions, the idea of combining the propylene carbonate process with the 
methanol process to produce a secondary stage product (DMC) has been studied. This alternative 
will take advantage of the high carbon dioxide utilisation that the methanol production offers with 
the economic feasibility of the propylene carbonate to produce a more valuable product DMC with 
a high value by-product (PG). These and other alternatives were included in the so-called CO2 
based network. From that, the process that encompasses the PC and MeOH production to go to 
DMC and PG was the selected network to the next step. 
The aim of the method is to help the carbon dioxide problem and the proposed way to address it 
is by CCU by offering both real carbon dioxide emissions reductions and economical motivations. 
Because of that, step 7 of the method is to do economic analysis of the proposed network to 
validate whether this is a solution to the problem or not. The first stage production of methanol 
and of propylene carbonate were evaluated individually. As it was shown in the stage 2 of the 
method, the economics are favourable for the propylene carbonate but not for the methanol. 
These analyses were done with the starting raw materials PO for the PC production and H2 for the 
MeOH at the selling prices. Then, the DMC production was carried out where both raw materials, 
methanol and propylene carbonate, were purchased from the market. That showed negative 
results because of the high purchasing cost of the PC. Lastly, the entire process to produce DMC 
with PG as by-product including the production of both the PC and MeOH was studied. In this 
process, the raw materials to be fed are the CO2, H2 and PO. This extended process showed both 
negative net CO2 and positive economics. A payback time of 3 years, IRRF of 34.52% and net 
present value of 588.86 M$ were obtained.  
For the production of DMC from the RM (carbon dioxide, hydrogen and propylene oxide), a 
sensitivity analysis was carried out. This sensitivity analysis consists of varying individually the 
prices of the products and of the raw materials. For that, the profit after taxes was calculated at 
different prices of DMC, PG, H2 and PO. This was done by fixing all the prices at their actual price 
and varying a single material. In Figure 23, how the profit varies by changing the prices can be 
observed. The different prices for the breakeven point can be calculated from the regression 
equations, as well as the potential to increase the profit by reducing the RM costs.

6 Conclusions 
It is obvious that something has to be done to reduce the carbon dioxide emissions problems. For 
that, a methodology to address the carbon dioxide emissions problem by carbon capture and 
utilisation technology has been developed. The objective of this methodology is to find a carbon 
dioxide solution that both has negative net CO2 emissions and that suppose a positive incentive for 
the companies to be applied (profit).  
The methodology consists of seven steps. In the first step, literature research is carried out to 
create a database that contain different possible carbon dioxide utilisation solutions. This large list 
is reduced in size by candidate’s screening in step 2. For the step 2 different criterion has been 
considered, such as the economic potential, the net CO2 and the potential impact for carbon 
dioxide reduction. The output of this step is a list of promising candidates to be rigorously 
simulated in step 3. Step 4 is process evaluation, the rigorous net CO2 emissions are calculated and 
from that and the potential of process optimisation (step 5) a CO2 based network possible solution 
is obtained (step 6). This proposal consists of the production of second stage product DMC with PG 
as high value by-product, from the starting materials (CO2, H2 and PO) via the intermediate 
products (PC and methanol). This network is then evaluated. It is both economically interesting 
(IRR of 34.52% and a profit after taxes of 14.6M€/year) and environmentally interesting in terms 
of net CO2 (-0.45 kg of CO2/kg of DMC). However, the actual DMC production process presents 
positive net CO2 emissions (+0.52 kgCO2/kg of DMC). That means that the proposed network 
compared to the industrial path would reduce the total carbon dioxide emissions by -0.97 
kgCO2/kg of DMC. 
The proposed method shows that there is the potential for carbon dioxide reduction by CCU with 
interesting economic incentives. The obtained outcome from the methodology also shows that 
there are other products rather than methanol or urea for carbon dioxide utilisation. This is also 
important because there is a high amount of carbon dioxide produced per year and so, it should 
be utilised in as many processes and amounts as possible. The research and thesis results pretends 
to propose a methodology and network for carbon dioxide emissions reduction. It means to show 
==PAGE:86==

that there is the opportunity for carbon dioxide emissions. Of course, this should be further 
validated and process optimisation should be carried out.

7 Future work 
Extensive literature research was carried out in order to create the database with feasible 
reactions and its information. However, more research should be done to find catalyst that with 
mild operating reaction conditions, high selectivity and conversions are reached. Furthermore, in 
the work done in [29] other thermodynamically feasible reactions that should kinetically be 
investigated to carbon dioxide utilisation are proposed. From the database, only the utilisation 
paths that presented extensive information were considered for further investigation.  
In addition, there is expected to be a lot of shale gas in the future [36]; the U.S. 
government's Energy Information Administration predicts that by 2035, 46% of the United States' 
natural gas supply will come from shale gas.  Shale gas is a hydrocarbon gas mixture that consists 
mainly of methane. Other hydrocarbons are natural gas liquids (NGLs) like ethane, propane, and 
butane, and it also contains carbon dioxide, nitrogen, and hydrogen sulphide.  Because of that, it 
may be interesting to start the proposed CO2 based network, from propane and producing the 
required propylene oxide instead of purchasing it. This should be studied for the possibility to 
make it a more economically attractive solution. The use of ethane to produce the ethylene oxide 
and then go to the first stage product ethylene carbonate to end up in the DMC production with 
ethylene glycol as by-product may be also of special economically interest. For that, including the 
production of the ethylene oxide and propylene oxide in the carbon dioxide network with its net 
CO2 calculations and economic analysis will expand the network and the obtained problem 
solution may change. 
As future work, it is also interesting to do process optimisation and intensification of the different 
processes in the CO2 network (the outcome of step 6) in order to improve the net CO2 values and 
have better economics. A CO2 based bio-refinery (a refinery with CO2 as feedstock) could also be 
proposed by combining more than one problem solution to produce a larger variety of products by 
having negative or 0 net CO2 emissions.

C. Industrial processes 
Methanol 
The most common industrial process for methanol is divided into two steps, first the syngas 
preparation from natural gas by the catalytic steam reforming. The second step is the catalytic 
methanol synthesis from this previously produced syngas. This is known to be a high energy 
consuming processes and does not utilise CO2 (it utilises the CO2 produced so the direct emissions 
are 0) because the raw material is natural gas or methane [48].  
The first reactor operates at 26 bar and 800K whilst the second reactor for the methanol synthesis 
at 50bar and 573K. 
Succinic acid 
Regarding the succinic acid, it is industrially produced through a petrochemical route. Liquid-phase 
maleic anhydride hydrogenation to succinic anhydride followed by the hydration to succinic acid. 
The maleic anhydride hydrogenation takes place at temperatures between 120-180⁰C with 
hydrogen pressures between 0.5-4 MPa. The CO2 emissions were calculated from the energy and 
mass balance provided in [49]. 
The simulation has been carried out at 150⁰C and 25 bar. 
 
Propylene carbonate and ethylene carbonate 
Propylene carbonate is an organic solvent produced by the reaction of propylene oxide and carbon 
dioxide in the presence of a homogeneous catalyst [50]. Thus, the net CO2 emissions would be the 
same as calculated before.  
𝐶3𝐻6𝑂(𝑃𝑂) + 𝐶𝑂2 →𝐶4𝐻6𝑂3(𝑃𝐶)                                                (𝐶. 1)

The same also for the ethylene carbonate that is formed from the reaction of ethylene oxide with 
carbon dioxide. 
(𝐶𝐻2)2𝑂+ 𝐶𝑂2 →(𝐶𝐻2𝑂)2𝐶𝑂                                                    (𝐶. 2) 
Dimethyl carbonate 
Dimethyl carbonate (DMC) can be produced through different reaction paths. In the 80’s, the DMC 
was produced via the reaction of methanol on phosgene with high yields. The reaction was usually 
taking place at 0C in an anhydrous solvent as toluene in presence of pyridine excess to shift the 
reaction equilibrium to the DMC formation [51]. However, this process was environmentally 
unfriendly due to the use of phosgene and other alternatives were implemented. From the 1996 
Bayer implemented and patented a DMC free phosgene process, the partial carbonylation path, 
which they still use. Thus, this has been the production path that has been taken as the base case 
that industry uses. The reaction for the DMC production can be seen in equation C.3. 
2𝐶𝐻3𝑂𝐻+ 𝐶𝑂+ 1

The optimal conditions for the reaction are temperatures between 120-180C and pressures 
between 12-22 bar. According to the revised article [18], a yield of 99.5% of DMC is achieved. For 
the simulation for the energy consumption the operating conditions for the reactor are 20bar and 
150⁰C.

J. Method of economic calculations 
The Method of Guthrie is used for the costs calculations. This method calculates separately the 
different costs of the plant. The total capital cost of the plant can be define as in the following 
equation. 
𝐶𝑇𝐶= 𝐶𝐹𝐶+ 𝐶𝑊𝐶                                                                        (𝐹. 1)

The fixed capital cost is defined as (F.2)

The different components of the fixed capital cost are: 
- CTBM: Total bare module cost for equipment  
- CSite: Cost of site development. Includes the purchase of land; grading and excavation of the site; and 
construction of all internal roads. walkways. and parking lots.  
- CB: Cost of the process buildings.  
- CC: Cost of contingencies. A factor to cover unforeseen circumstances.  
- CF: Cost of the contractor’s fees.  
- CAux: Cost of the auxiliary buildings. Includes administration office. maintenance shop and control 
rooms. warehouses. and services buildings.
