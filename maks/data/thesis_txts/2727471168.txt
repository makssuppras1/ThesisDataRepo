Abstract 
 
Bacillus cereus (B. cereus), a foodborne pathogen commonly found in dairy products, forms spores that 
contribute to its survival and persistence throughout the food chain. This comprehensive study aimed 
to investigate the growth and spore formation kinetics of psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains of B. 
cereus in dairy products, with a specific focus on permeate, a whey powder. Experimental 
investigations and mathematical modeling explored the factors influencing B. cereus growth and spore 
formation. Various dairy products were experimentally assessed for their ability to support B. cereus 
growth and spore formation. It was found that spore formation occurred when the concentration of 
vegetative cells reached a minimum threshold of ≥6 log10 (CFU/mL). The study thoroughly examined 
the impact of temperature on the growth and sporulation of B. cereus in permeate, revealing that 
lower temperatures significantly reduced sporulation rates and delayed the sporulation process. The 
time required for spore formation was five times longer at lower temperatures of 10 and 12.5oC 
compared to higher temperatures of 17.5 or 22.5oC for psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains, 
respectively. The Baril et al. (2012) model successfully captured the kinetics of spore formation and 
provided valuable insights into the influence of temperature on B. cereus spore formation in dairy 
products. Maintaining B. cereus concentrations below the threshold of 5 log10 (CFU/mL) is crucial to 
ensure product safety and prevent spore formation in the production line, as no spore formation was 
observed below the concentration of 6 log10 (CFU/mL). This study contributes to the scientific 
understanding of B. cereus spore formation in dairy products and provides insights for risk assessment 
and management in the dairy industry.

CHAPTER I 
Introduction 
 
The introduction of this master’s thesis (M.sc thesis) provides a concise overview of the 
relevance and scope of the study, focusing on understanding the kinetics that influences B. cereus 
spore formation in dairy products. It aims to provide a clear understanding of the research objectives 
and the significance of investigating the factors affecting spore formation in order to enhance food 
safety measures. This brief introduction serves as a foundation for the subsequent sections of the 
project, which delve into the experimental methods, data analysis, and findings related to B. cereus 
spore formation kinetics. 
B. cereus is a gram-positive, spore-forming bacteria commonly found in various foods, 
including cooked rice, pasta, vegetables, and dairy products (Batt, 2014). It has received significant 
attention as a foodborne pathogen due to its capacity to produce gastrointestinal disorders ranging 
from moderate diarrhea to severe vomiting and abdominal pains (Guinebretière et al., 2008). 
Foodborne outbreaks caused by B. cereus have been recorded worldwide, highlighting the need to 
comprehend this pathogen’s behavior in food systems, especially regarding dairy products. 
Dairy products such as milk, cheese, and whey powders promote the development and survival 
of B. cereus (Vidic et al., 2020). However, not all dairy products support the growth of B. cereus. Instead 
of providing favorable conditions for active growth, certain dairy products mainly serve as a medium 
for the survival and persistence of B. cereus spores. Pasteurized milk, for example, undergoes heat 
treatment that destroys vegetative cells, making it less conducive to developing B. cereus (Tirloni et 
al., 2022). However, B. cereus spores can persist in pasteurized milk, and under specific conditions such 
as improper storage or extended shelf life, the spores may germinate and proliferate (Bartoszewicz et 
al., 2008). 
On the other hand, certain dairy products, such as soft cheeses and cream-based desserts, can 
provide suitable conditions for both the survival and growth of B. cereus (Elafify et al., 2023). These 
products often have higher moisture content, lower acidity, and high nutrient availability, which create 
an environment that supports the growth of B. cereus. Under favorable conditions, B. cereus has the 
potential to undergo rapid multiplication and produce toxins that can lead to food poisoning symptoms 
(EFSA, 2016). The predominant type of disease caused by B. cereus varies from one country to another 
(Schoeni and Lee Wong, 2005). However, it is worth noting that B. cereus food poisoning is not 
classified as a reportable disease in any country (Jessberger et al., 2020a). As a result, official statistics 
regarding the total number of cases of this particular food poisoning are limited. Even if both 
syndromes were to be reportable, it is anticipated that there would be significant underreporting due 
to several factors. Firstly, individuals affected by B. cereus food poisoning often do not seek medical 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
assistance during the active phase of the illness. Additionally, the duration of the illness is relatively 
short, and patients tend to recover quickly after that (Batt, 2014). 
The bacteria can contaminate dairy products at various manufacturing phases, from raw milk 
to finished merchandise (Tirloni et al., 2022). Insufficient heat treatment, incorrectly high storage 
temperatures, and post-processing contamination can lead to the proliferation of B. cereus and the 
probable formation of heat-resistant spores. B. cereus spores that survive harsh conditions can 
germinate, multiply and produce toxins, representing a considerable danger to consumer health 
(Andersson and Granum, 1995). 
Understanding the dynamics of B. cereus growth and sporulation in dairy products is critical 
for effective control strategies and food safety. If B. cereus spores are present in the raw ingredients 
or, for instance, during the production of baby formula, there is a possibility of spore germination and 
subsequent growth into vegetative cells (Pei et al., 2018). Surviving spores can produce heat-stable 
enterotoxins, which pose a significant health risk when consumed by infants (Shaheen et al., 2006). 
Since children have immature immune systems, they are more susceptible to infections caused by B. 
cereus. 
Several kinds of research have investigated the growth kinetics and impact of various 
environmental factors on B. cereus spreading in dairy environments (Ryu et al., 2005; Baril et al., 2012; 
Gauvry et al., 2021). However, the spore formation process, which enhances the survival and 
persistence of B. cereus, remains relatively less explored and understood. These issues highlight the 
importance of preventing the presence of B. cereus in dairy products from ensuring consumer safety 
and maintaining the reputation of food companies. While heat treatments are a standard solution to 
destroy microorganisms, pasteurization of milk will not inactivate B. cereus spores and instead may 
function as an activator of spore germination (Vidic et al., 2020). Hence, it is crucial to implement 
preventive measures that inhibit the growth of B. cereus in various dairy products to ensure they meet 
consumers’ appropriate level of protection (ALOP). 
Spores are highly resistant structures that allow the bacterium to survive adverse conditions 
and potentially re-establish growth under favorable circumstances (Soni et al., 2016). Predicting 
microbial development in foods can contribute to successful solutions to avoid foodborne outbreaks. 
Understanding the growth and survival characteristics of B. cereus is essential for developing effective 
interventions to prevent foodborne illness and for designing food safety and preservation strategies. 
Various mathematical models are currently available to predict the sporulation of Bacillus species (Baril 
et al., 2012; Trunet et al., 2020; Gauvry et al., 2021). However, further investigation is needed for good 
prediction close to the growth boundary in food products instead of laboratory broth. Determining 
sporulation kinetics, either by specific studies or from growth factors (e.g., aw, cardinal growth 
temperatures, pH), could assist in identifying critical points in processing lines, leading to improved 
management of spore-forming bacteria in food products. The project will study two cocktails of, 
respectively, psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus isolates in order to develop the required 
models. The laboratory experiments will include chilled products such as cheese, whey concentrate, 
and beverages made from dairy or plants. The project employed existing modeling approaches to 
predict the conditions and rate at which B. cereus produces spores (Monod, 1949; Baril et al., 2012; 
Gauvry et al., 2021). The models will then be compared and, in some cases, modified to identify the 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
most accurate fit to the experimental data. The aim was to determine the most suitable model to be a 
predictive tool for future use. By identifying characteristics that impact B. cereus, such as temperature, 
this prediction model can enhance food safety and speed up product and process development while 
saving money and time. For instance, the predictive model may find the best temperature range during 
fermentation to prevent B. cereus growth and spore development while making dairy products like 
yogurt or find the temperature at which spores will not be produced during processing. The likelihood 
of B. cereus infection can be drastically decreased by implementing the advised temperature control 
measures and improving food safety. By optimizing manufacturing procedures, companies can 
eliminate the need for expensive trial-and-error approaches and extensive experimentation. Such 
optimizations allow for accelerated product development, streamlined manufacturing workflows, and 
improved resource utilization, resulting in significant time and cost savings. 
This M.sc thesis intends to fill this knowledge gap by experimentally quantifying and 
mathematically modeling the spore development of B. cereus in dairy products. This study aims to 
improve our understanding of B. cereus behavior in the dairy sector by exploring the variables that 
impact spore production, defining the kinetics of sporulation, and building mathematical models. In 
other words, the research seeks to characterize the kinetics of B. cereus spore formation in a range of 
dairy products (e.g., oat drinks, cottage cheese, whey powders/solutions), hence identifying the 
environmental factors that impact B. cereus sporulation in dairy products. The primary emphasis will 
be on investigating the effects and influence of temperature within a defined range (8°C to 22.5°C, 
incrementing by 2.5°C) on a specific product known as permeate. 
By addressing these objectives and challenges, this thesis will contribute to the scientific 
knowledge of B. cereus spore development in dairy products and give significant insights into food 
safety management and preventative tactics.

CHAPTER II 
Background theory 
 
This section will summarize the presently available knowledge of B. cereus and its spore formation 
and focus specifically on dairy products. It will investigate the sporulation process, factors influencing 
sporulation, and the influence of B. cereus spores on the safety and quality of dairy products. The 
review will also examine the models used to predict sporulation in dairy systems. Finally, it will identify 
information gaps and needs for future research to help us better understand B. cereus spore 
generation and management in the dairy sector. 
2.1. Introduction to B. cereus in dairy products 
2.1.1. Overview of B. cereus as a foodborne pathogen 
The B. cereus group, also known as B. cereus sensu lato (s.l.), is a collection of closely related, 
gram-positive, spore-forming, facultatively anaerobic bacteria that is now ascribed to the genus 
Bacillus (Caroll et al., 2021; Messelhäußer and Ehling-Schulz et al., 2018). According to Liu et al. (2015), 
until recently, a group consisting of nine officially recognized closely related species existed, namely: 
B. anthracis, B. cereus sensu stricto (s.s.), B. cytotoxicus, B. mycoides, B. pseudomycoides, B. 
thuringiensis, B. toyonensis, B. weihenstephanensis and B. wiedmannii (Liu et al., 2015). Furthermore, 
starting in 2017, the B. cereus group was expanded by including nine species discovered in marine 
sediments. These newly identified species are B. albus, B. luti, B. mobilis, B. nitratireducens, B. 
pacificus, B. paramycoides, B. paranthracis, B. proteolyticus, and B. tropicus (Liu et al., 2017). However, 
the B. cereus s.l. group species has expanded, with the number of validly published and recognized 
species reaching 29 by 2023. (Caroll et al., 2023). Isolates obtained from food and humans can be 
classified into two categories: mesophilic strains and psychrotolerant strains. Mesophilic strains of B. 
cereus exhibit robust growth at 37°C, while their growth is limited below 10°C. In contrast, 
psychrotolerant strains thrive at refrigeration temperatures but display weaker growth performance 
at 37°C, as Wijnands et al. (2006) documented. As a result, they can easily be isolated from foods even 
when they are refrigerated. Milk and dairy production conditions are linked to several lineages, 
including psychrotolerant strains (Guinebretière et al., 2008). 
Mainly associated with the dairy sector, B. cereus is a well-known cause of foodborne bacterial 
intoxications (Dietrich et al., 2021). Emetic and diarrheic syndrome are foodborne illnesses this 
endospore-forming bacterium can produce (Dietrich et al., 2021). Both conditions are often self-
limiting. The emetic toxin causes nausea and vomiting, while the diarrheal toxin leads to severe 
diarrhea (Ehling-Schulz et al., 2006). These symptoms can occur within 5 hours of consuming 
contaminated dairy products and last up to 24 hours (Table 1).

Despite being a well-known cause of foodborne disease, B. cereus infection is not frequently 
reported due to the organism’s typically mild symptoms. However, severe intoxications, requiring 
hospitalization and even resulting in death, are, nonetheless, on the rise (Dierick et al., 2005). 
Furthermore, B. cereus is more frequently identified as the cause of non-gastrointestinal illnesses, 
particularly in clinical settings (Schoeni & Lee Wong, 2005; Messelhäußer & Ehling-Schulz, 2018). 
B. cereus is mainly known for its role as a foodborne pathogen and as the causative agent of 
diarrheal or emetic symptoms. One of the notable virulence factors associated with B. cereus is the 
production of cereulide toxins. Certain strains of B. cereus can thrive in food items and produce heat-
stable cyclic peptides known as cereulide toxins (Schoeni & Lee Wong, 2005). Consuming food that is 
contaminated with B. cereus spores or vegetative cells can lead to the development of diarrheal illness. 
Cells develop in the stomach after ingestion and actively produce toxins. Though cytotoxin K (CytK) 
may play a role in diarrheal syndrome, hemolysin BL (HBL) and the non-hemolytic enterotoxin (NHE) 
are the primary toxins implicated (Jessberger et al., 2020b). Trimeric toxins HBL and NHE are released 
by bacterial cells separately and can connect outside the cell. The mono-component CytK and entire 
toxins both work by causing pores to develop on human cellular membranes (Jessberger et al., 2020b). 
The anaerobic or microaerophilic intestinal environment stimulates the generation of toxins, which 
lead to the lysis of human cells and the loss of microvilli, resulting in diarrhea (Tuipulotu et al., 2021). 
The disease often lasts 12-24 hours and involves stomach discomfort and watery diarrhea. Meat, milk, 
and vegetables are the most often implicated foods in this sickness, and the number of cells required 
in these to trigger the diarrheal symptoms is 105-107 CFU total Matthews et al. (2017).

2.1.2. Occurrence and prevalence of B. cereus in dairy products 
While B. cereus is expected in the environment, the bacteria is naturally prevalent in the 
intestines of numerous animals. Fomites, plants, freshwater, marine waters, and decomposing organic 
debris are the pathogen's main environmental reservoirs. Because of its ubiquity, this microbe may 
readily contaminate a wide range of foods, and it is challenging to get raw materials free of B. cereus 
spores (Johnson, 1984; Tirloni et al., 2022). The concentration of spore-forming bacilli in the soil is 
around 6 log10 spores/mL, while in human feces, it is 4 log10 spores/mL, suggesting a potential 
adaptation as gut commensals (Hong et al., 2009). B. cereus is frequently found in raw milk, albeit 
typically at low rates (100 CFU/mL) (Christiansson et al., 1999). However, there may be variations 
among dairy farms depending on their hygiene practices and housing systems. In the Netherlands, the 
average amount of B. cereus spores in raw bulk tank milk is 1.2 log10 per liter, and a maximum limit of 
3 log10 spores per liter is required in farm tank milk to ensure a shelf life of at least seven days for 
pasteurized milk (Heyndrickx, 2011). 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
B. cereus was detected in several kinds of cheese at a rate of 10.4% in Turkey, with counts as 
high as 3.8 105 CFU/mL (Yibar et al., 2017). In low quantities (10 CFU/mL), fresh ricotta cheese tested 
positive for B. cereus by 16% (Scatassa et al., 2018). Surprisingly, a recent study found B. cereus in UHT 
milk, which is not typically expected in this product type (Chaves et al., 2017). Possible reasons for this 
occurrence could be insufficient control during the UHT process or contamination after heat 
treatment. Depending on the product and regulatory requirements, the temperature is raised to at 
least 135°C or more during the UHT process. The duration of the heating phase typically ranges from 
2 to 10 seconds (Penfield and Campbell, 1990). This short exposure time at high temperatures is 
sufficient to destroy heat-sensitive microorganisms, including spoilage bacteria and pathogens, 
effectively. After the heating phase, the product is rapidly cooled to prevent overcooking or 
undesirable chemical reactions. The cooling process is carefully controlled to ensure good product 
quality and prevent the growth of any surviving microorganisms. (Penfield and Campbell, 1990).  
B. cereus is frequently identified due to soil contamination from plants used as primary food 
(cereals, particularly rice). The ability of the spores to survive heat treatments, such as cooking, 
determines the potential risk associated with consuming cooked meals kept at inadequate 
temperatures, allowing for the germination, multiplication, and synthesis of cereulide. Contaminated 
vegetables can also be utilized in food as ingredients (vegetables, spices), contaminating previously 
uncontaminated items. However, the possible list of contaminated items is extensive (Berthold-Pluta 
et al., 2019). 
B. cereus may be isolated at all stages of the dairy production chain (Tirloni et al., 2022). The 
prevalence and spread of B. cereus on the farm are linked to the contamination of soil, feed, and 
bedding that touch the cows’ udders. Spores are transmitted in the milk during milking and can survive 
pasteurization, which cannot be regarded as an effective barrier, otherwise functioning as a possible 
stimulator of their germination (Tirloni et al., 2022). In relevant environmental circumstances, the 
vegetative forms can multiply in the dairy substrate, most likely causing spoiling or posing a risk to 
customers. For instance, in a study performed by Tirloni et al. (2023), most B. cereus strains tested 
harbored toxin-encoding genes in different types of cheese and showed biofilm-forming ability. Finding 
virulence factors in the isolated strains shows that circulating B. cereus strains frequently produce 
toxins and enzymes, emphasizing the possibility of adverse effects of B. cereus contamination (Tirloni 
et al., 2023). Given that spores are present in raw milk, it is assumed that they will be present 
throughout the production process (Lin et al., 1998), with many sources of contamination converging 
simultaneously. The dairy production system is intricate, with dead ends, nooks, and traps that can 
be a permanent reservoir for bacteria that spoils food, such as valves, shafts, and gaskets (Donovan, 
1959). Milking equipment can also be a source of contamination (Donovan, 1959; Christiansson et al., 
1999), with spores adhering to and germinating in dairy equipment (e.g., tanks, pasteurizers, packaging 
machines), resulting in post-treatment milk contamination. An extremely hydrophobic outer coating is 
primarily responsible for the spores' ability to stick to food contact surfaces (including stainless steel) 
(Peng et al., 2001). For microbial germination and the subsequent formation of biofilms, ideal 
temperatures, high humidity, and a liquid or air interface are all necessary (Palmer et al., 2007).

2.1.3. Importance of studying B. cereus spore formation in the context of 
dairy food safety 
Research on B. cereus spore formation is critical in dairy food safety. The capacity of B. cereus 
to generate highly resistant spores offers a problem to the dairy business since these spores may 
survive severe processing conditions and germinate and develop under favorable conditions, resulting 
in foodborne disease (Huang et al., 2021). Understanding the factors that govern spore development 
is critical to avoid and control B. cereus contamination in dairy products. Temperature, pH, nutrient 
availability, and stresses experienced during processing and storage are some factors that encourage 
spore production (Baril et al., 2013; Gauvry et al., 2021). Scientists and food safety experts may create 
ways to reduce spore contamination, enhance processing procedures, and apply effective control 
measures by learning more about B. cereus spore generation kinetics. This understanding can help to

improve the safety and quality of dairy products, reduce the risk of foodborne diseases caused by B. 
cereus, and increase customer trust in the dairy business. It may involve optimizing temperature 
conditions to create unfavorable growth conditions for B. cereus spores. Therefore, by studying the 
factors that influence spore generation kinetics, in this report temperature, researchers can devise 
targeted control measures to inhibit spore formation and subsequent toxin production. 
Furthermore, research into B. cereus spore generation can assist in developing quick detection 
methods for spores in dairy products, allowing for prompt intervention and outbreak prevention. 
Studying B. cereus spore generation is critical for ensuring dairy food safety and public health. 
Although raw material contamination cannot be avoided, collection and storage processes can 
reduce the concentration of contaminating microorganisms. Decontamination procedures (e.g.,

significantly reduce the risk of milk contamination during milking. B. cereus levels should be kept below 
a threshold of 3 log10 CFU/mL in order to avoid spoilage (Saleh-Lakaa et al., 2017). 
2.2. B. cereus sporulation process 
Sporulation is the latest cellular response of bacteria to nutrient depletion (Burkholder & 
Grossman, 2000). As previously stated, the capacity of B. cereus to sporulate and create highly resistant 
spores (which can subsequently germinate) is essential when discussing foodborne infections. While 
heat treatments such as 80°C for 10 minutes can destroy vegetative B. cereus cells, spores can survive 
autoclaving and pasteurization (Ryu et al., 2005; Table 4). B. cereus spores are also hydrophobic, which 
allows them to attach to surfaces, making them difficult to remove during cleaning and 
targeting disinfectants (Matthews et al., 2017).

2.2.1. Spore structure 
Bacterial spores have a different structure than vegetative cells. A typical bacterial spore’s 
structure includes the exosporium, spore coat, outer membrane, cortex, germ cell wall, inner 
membrane, and core (Leggett et al., 2012). Fig. 1 depicts this structure: 
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
Fig. 1: Display of a B. cereus spore structure, including the exosporium, spore coat, outer membrane, 
cortex, germ cell wall, inner membrane, and core (Leggett et al., 2012). 
The spore’s outer structure (the exosporium) starts from the exterior and comprises protein, 
lipids, and carbohydrates. According to Leggett et al. (2012), this structure is adhering, and 
hydrophobic features are implicated in the pathogenicity of certain spores (See 2.1.1). The spore coat 
is then put into the exosporium (if present), acting as an initial barrier between the spore cortex and 
any more giant molecules. It is made up of multiple thin layers and is mostly protein. Following the 
spore coat is an outer membrane whose function is unknown (Leggett et al., 2012). The cortex is the 
following structure. It is made of peptidoglycan and has been recognized as possibly responsible for 
the core’s dehydration (Leggett et al., 2012). The next component, the germ cell wall, also includes 
peptidoglycan and becomes the cell wall when the spore germinates and outgrows (Leggett et al., 
2012). Finally, the spore core is in the center and includes the DNA, RNA, ribosomes, and most of the 
spore’s enzymes (Setlow, 2006). The spore core maintains the spore’s genetic integrity and facilitates 
the reactivation and growth of the bacterium upon favorable environmental conditions (Leggett et al., 
2012). Additionally, the core’s unique composition and structure contribute to the spore’s resistance 
to heat, desiccation, radiation, and other stressors, enabling the spore to endure harsh environments 
until suitable conditions for germination and bacterial growth are encountered (Beaman and Gerhardt, 
1986; Leggett et al., 2012). 
2.2.2. The life cycle of B. cereus as a foodborne pathogen 
B. cereus is a common soil bacterium that may quickly spread to plants and foods. It frequently 
appears in a wide range of food, such as cereals, dairy products, meats, and spices. Its ideal growing 
temperature lies between 28 to 35°C, with minimums of 4-5°C and maximums of 55°C (Gilbert et al., 
1974). The organism’s generation period is 18–27 minutes, corresponding to the maximum specific 
growth rate of 1.54-2.31 h-1. It can thrive in a wide pH range of 4.9 to 9.3 and up to 7.5% salt content 
(Batt, 2014).

Additionally, B. cereus can still be present in food that has undergone drying or other forms of

B. cereus has several phases in its life cycle, with sporulation being a significant transition point. 
B. cereus develops vegetatively as a rod-shaped bacterium in favorable circumstances. The bacteria 
reproduce by binary fission during their vegetative phase, quickly expanding their population. When 
exposed to adverse conditions, such as nutritional deficiency, high temperature, or other stressors, B. 
cereus experiences sporulation, a noticeable physiological transformation. This highly controlled and 
sophisticated sporulation process allows the bacteria to endure extreme environmental conditions. 
2.2.3. Key stages and morphological changes during sporulation 
Fig. 2: The sporulation and germination cycle in B. cereus (McKenney et al., 2013, Fig. made in

Sporulation starts with the activation of signaling pathways that are activated by environmental 
signals detected by the bacteria. In most species, sporulation is triggered by conditions not conducive 
to growth, such as the depletion of nutrients, change in pH, or temperature (Koopman et al., 2022). 
The initiation of sporulation is primarily determined by accumulating an adequate amount of the 
response regulator Spo0A in its phosphorylated state. When Spo0A is phosphorylated, it inhibits gene 
expression in post-exponential growth and activates genes essential for sporulation (Stephenson and 
Lewis, 2005). These signals set off a chain reaction that creates an asymmetrically positioned septum 
in the vegetative cell. This septum divides the cell into two compartments: the mother cell and the 
forespore. The forespore grows into the future spore, while the mother cell feeds and protects the 
growing spore (Fig. 2). 
==PAGE:18==
12 
 
The mother cell engulfs the forespore during sporulation, forming a double membrane 
structure. The coordinated activity of different proteins and enzymes is required for this engulfment 
process. Once absorbed, the forespore is enclosed by the cytoplasm of the mother cell, creating a 
sporangium. The sporangium experiences several morphological changes, including spore coat 
dehydration, thickening, and the buildup of calcium dipicolinate and tiny acid-soluble proteins, 
contributing to spore resilience and stability (Fig. 2). 
The mother cell lyses after spore development, releasing the mature spore into the 
environment. The resistance of the mature spore allows the spore to survive for lengthy periods until 
circumstances improve. When the circumstances improve, the spore can germinate, kicking off the 
process of returning to the vegetative development phase (Fig. 2). 
Understanding the B. cereus life cycle, particularly the change to sporulation, is critical for 
various reasons. First, it sheds light on B. cereus’ survival tactics in adverse situations, such as food 
processing or storage. On the other hand, this presents an opportunity to develop targeted control 
measures that address specific life cycle stages, thereby reducing the risk of B. cereus contamination 
and its potential to grow significantly. As a result, the incidence of foodborne illnesses associated with 
B. cereus can be minimized. Furthermore, researching the sporulation process can lead to the 
development of innovative detection technologies, as spores are frequently more tenacious and 
difficult to eradicate than vegetative cells. Resolving the complexities of B. cereus’ life cycle and 
transition to sporulation helps better understand this bacterium’s physiology, ecology, and influence 
on food safety. For instance, innovative techniques such as molecular-based detection methods or 
advanced biosensors could be designed to specifically target and identify B. cereus spores, enabling 
more accurate and timely detection (Ramarao et al., 2020). Furthermore, a comprehensive 
understanding of the sporulation process can inform the development of targeted interventions and 
control measures to prevent or minimize the formation and survival of spores, ultimately enhancing 
the safety and quality of food products and reducing the occurrence of foodborne illnesses caused by 
B. cereus (McHugh et al., 2017). 
2.2.4. Germination and outgrowth 
Spores are released by the mother’s cell as dormant cells. Exposing the spores to heat, pH, or 
chemical exposure may activate germination (Leggett et al., 2012). Germination transforms B. cereus 
spores from a latent, resistant condition to an actively developing vegetative cell. Germination 
happens as a result of favorable environmental circumstances, such as the existence of nutrients and 
appropriate temperatures. The activation of germination receptors on the spore surface, which sense 
and bind certain germinants, like amino acids, sugars, or ions, is involved in the process (Abee et al., 
2011).  
When the spore binds to the germinant, a series of processes occur within the spore, 
destroying the spore’s protective layers, including the spore coat and cortex. The spore expands and 
becomes metabolically active (Abee et al., 2011; Vohradsky et al., 2020). 
Following germination, the spore begins the outgrowth phase, during which it goes through a 
series of morphological and biochemical changes to become a fully functioning vegetative cell. The 
==PAGE:19==
13 
 
spore restarts metabolic activity during outgrowth, commencing protein synthesis, DNA replication, 
and cell division. The spore’s cellular structure undergoes significant modifications, including cell 
elongation, cytoplasmic restructuring, and the creation of new cellular components essential for 
growth and division (Abee et al., 2011; Vohradsky et al., 2020). 
The change from latent spores to actively developing vegetative cells is a highly coordinated 
and controlled process during germination and outgrowth (Koopman et al., 2022). It is managed by 
various factors, including germinant composition, the presence of germination receptors, signaling 
pathway activity, and the expression of genes involved in germination and outgrowth (Koopman et al., 
2022). 
Milk and dairy products are especially vulnerable to contamination since the spores can survive 
even in pasteurized and other heat-treated milk products, such as milk powder and cheese (Vidic et 
al., 2020). Although UHT effectively kills B. cereus spores in most consumer milk products (2 min at 
90°C, Byrne et al., 2006), there are some high-risk items like reconstituted infant foods (Berkeley et al., 
2002). Infants are more vulnerable to bacterial infections because their immune systems are 
underdeveloped (Ronan et al., 2021). Due to their complex processing, the powdered infant and 
follow-up formula cannot be sterilized using current methods (EFSA, 2016). Thus, a need for an 
understanding of B. cereus sporulation is required. 
2.3. Factors influencing B. cereus spore formation in dairy 
products 
2.3.1. Environmental factors 
Several factors, including dairy product type, storage temperature, and pH, can impact B. 
cereus proliferation and toxin generation in dairy products (Helmy et al., 1984). High-fat dairy products, 
such as creams, are more vulnerable to B. cereus contamination than low-fat dairy products (Berthold-
Pluta et al., 2019). Furthermore, dairy products maintained at warm temperatures for extended 
periods can create an ideal habitat for B. cereus growth and toxin synthesis. For instance, enterotoxins 
and emetic toxins have the potential to be produced at temperatures as low as approximately 10°C 
and 12°C, respectively (Tirloni et al., 2022). Therefore, maintaining proper refrigeration practices is 
crucial to prevent the persistent formation of toxins by B. cereus. 
2.3.1.1. 
Nutrient availability 
The depletion of nutrients required for vegetative cell development is one of the key factors 
influencing sporulation. Numerous studies have shown that starvation of cells leads to the sporulation 
of Bacillus species (Dawes et al., 1970; Grelet, 1957; Holmes et al., 1967), but little research has focused 
on the impact of simultaneous stresses caused by refrigeration temperature and nutrient deprivation 
on the sporulation of B. cereus. 
The availability of nutrients is critical for B. cereus spore development in dairy products. Spore 
development is more frequent in low-nutrient settings than high-nutrient environments since the 
bacteria respond to adverse circumstances by entering sporulation. Dairy products with poor 
==PAGE:20==
14 
 
nutritional content or those stored for an extended time might generate a suitable climate for spore 
production (Gopal et al., 2015). As milk is highly nutritious and has a near-neutral pH and high-water 
activity, it provides an ideal environment for the proliferation of microorganisms (Quigley et al., 2013). 
However, spores are usually present in raw milk due to air, soil, or water contamination. As mentioned 
earlier by Heyndrickx in 2011, B. cereus spores are usually already present in raw milk in low quantities 
(< 1 and up to 10–100 spores/mL) (Heyndrickx, 2011). 
A study by Ryu et al. (2005) suggested that nutrient availability and manganese impacted both 
test strains’ sporulation at 22°C. Compared to the rich medium (TSB), the low-nutrient medium (dTSB) 
slowed spore production and generated fewer spores. Manganese supplementation of dTSB, on the 
other hand, increased spore production (Ryu et al., 2005). Their study also observed that B. cereus 038-
2 and F3812/84 generated spores after reaching a stationary growth phase (Ryu et al., 2005). 
2.3.1.2. 
Temperature, pH effect 
The temperature considerably impacts the production of B. cereus spores in dairy products. 
Temperatures between 30-40°C are optimal for sporulation, but temperatures outside this range 
might impede or delay spore production (Baril et al., 2012). Lower temperatures can limit or inhibit 
sporulation, while higher temperatures stimulate the shift from vegetative development to 
sporulation. 
Baril et al. (2012) observed that lower temperatures delayed sporulation. The time to one 
spore per mL was tenfold longer when sporulation happened at 10°C and 20°C, respectively, than when 
sporulation occurred at optimal sporulation temperature (Baril et al., 2012). Their study demonstrates 
that environmental conditions have a comparable impact on quantitative changes in sporulation 
borders and rates as they do on changes in growth rate. The key findings of the experiments showed 
that temperature and pH were the most critical factors influencing time to first spore and ultimate 
spore concentration during sporulation, with lower temperatures and pH levels delaying the process. 
Environmental factors were shown to have a comparable impact on sporulation rates as on growth 
rates, implying that temperature, pH, and water activity limitations for growth might be valid 
predictions for sporulation boundaries in broth (Baril et al., 2012). 
Further investigations were performed in order to understand the relationship between 
growth, spore formation, and environmental factors. Gauvry et al. (2021) discovered that inadequate 
growing circumstances delayed the appearance of the initial spores and that spores emerged more 
synchronously (Gauvry et al., 2021). The authors propose that their model might be used to predict 
bacterial sporulation in food industry settings and identify food processing procedures that may 
enhance sporulation, such as improper cooling, inadequate heat treatments, and extended storage at 
improper temperatures (Soni et al., 2016; Burgess et al., 2010; Gilbert et al., 1974). 
Sporulation is more likely to occur in pH levels ranging from neutral to slightly alkaline (6.0-
8.0). Acidic environments, such as those in fermented dairy products, might hinder or delay 
sporulation, potentially limiting spore formation (Tirloni et al., 2017).

2.3.2. Other factors affecting growth and sporulation 
Oxygen can impact the development of B. cereus spores in dairy products (Abbas et al., 2014). 
Sporulation is most common in aerobic or microaerophilic environments. Anaerobic conditions, on the 
other hand, can impede or limit spore development (Abbas et al., 2014). Dairy product packaging and 
storage conditions impact oxygen availability. Abbas et al. (2014) examined the impact of anaerobiosis 
on B. cereus spores, commonly found in food (Abbas et al., 2014). They observed that, although spores 
produced in anaerobic conditions were more resistant to heat and certain chemical compounds, there 
was no difference in germination or size compared to spores produced in aerobic conditions. However, 
the anaerobically produced spores did have a damaged or detached exosporium, which could impact 
their survival and persistence in food. The research highlights the importance of considering the 
conditions under which spores are produced in food safety assessments (Abbas et al., 2014). 
Several other environmental conditions can influence B. cereus spore production in dairy 
products. The presence of specific ions (e.g., calcium, manganese) and water activity are examples. 
The addition of manganese increased sporulation in some broths in the experiments performed by Ryu 
et al. in 2014. Other studies focused on the effect of aw on spore formation. In 1977, Jakobsen and 
Murrell discovered that decreasing aw levels slowed sporulation, and the limiting aw value for the 
generation of mature spores was around 0.95 for glucose, sorbitol, and NaCl and approximately 0.91 
for glycerol (Jakobsen & Murrell, 1977). According to these findings, the aw of the sporulation medium, 
rather than the spore characteristics, controls the number of spores (Jakobsen & Murrell, 1977). 
Further studies later demonstrated that heating media’s water activity affects B. cereus spores’ heat 
resistance, with additional depressors, noticeably sucrose, boosting heat resistance (Coroller et al., 
2001). 
It is evident that various factors significantly influence the spore formation of B. cereus in dairy 
products. Nutrient availability, temperature, pH levels, oxygen availability, and other environmental 
conditions play vital roles in determining the occurrence of spore formation. Additionally, microbial 
interactions within dairy products can also impact the spore population. While these factors provide 
valuable insights into the conditions that promote or inhibit spore formation, further investigations 
are needed to gain a deeper understanding and develop predictive models. More research is required 
to elucidate the precise mechanisms underlying spore formation and to identify specific markers or 
indicators that can reliably predict the occurrence of spores in dairy products. Such knowledge will 
enable the development of targeted strategies and control measures to mitigate the risks associated 
with B. cereus spore formation, ensuring the safety and quality of dairy products. 
2.4. Modeling approaches for B. cereus sporulation 
Predictive microbial growth models offer a practical approach to assessing the growth and 
sporulation of B. cereus in broths under different storage conditions and processing factors (Baril et 
al., 2012; Gauvry et al., 2019). They help determine the critical factors that influence spore formation 
and provide valuable information for decision-making in food safety management. However, the 
accuracy and reliability of these models depend on the availability of accurate input data and the 
validation of model predictions through experimental observations. Continued research and validation

efforts are necessary to improve the precision and applicability of predictive microbial growth models 
for B. cereus sporulation in dairy products. 
These models consider various environmental factors that affect microbial growth and sporulation, 
such as temperature, pH, and water activity. By incorporating these factors, predictive models enable 
the estimation of B. cereus population dynamics and the prediction of spore formation in broths. They 
can provide valuable insights into the potential risks associated with B. cereus spore formation and aid 
in developing control strategies to minimize sporulation and ensure food safety. For example, the focus 
can be on conducting extensive experimental studies to collect data on B. cereus’s growth and

models establish the foundational understanding of the relationship between B. cereus growth and 
spore formation by incorporating essential characteristics and underlying principles. In contrast, the 
secondary models are more specific, accounting for multiple environmental factors and interactions 
not considered in the primary model. The two different approaches, although correlated, will be used 
in this report. The primary model will focus on describing B. cereus growth and spore formation, while 
the secondary model will analyze the effect of temperature on sporulation.

2.5. Knowledge gaps and future research directions 
2.5.1. Identification of gaps in current understanding of B. cereus spore 
formation in dairy 
Several information gaps persist despite considerable gains in understanding how B. cereus 
spores develop in dairy products. First, our knowledge of the specific environmental and dietary 
elements that cause B. cereus sporulation in dairy matrices is still lacking. Further research is needed 
to determine how complex dairy components like proteins, lipids, and sugars affect sporulation. 
Additionally, it is unclear how processing variables like heat treatment and pH modifications affect the 
kinetics of spore production in dairy products. Furthermore, it is crucial to investigate the impact of 
environmental factors, such as temperature, to enhance our understanding of the correlation between 
B. cereus growth and spore formation. By developing secondary models, we can gain insights into the 
behavior of the bacteria, which can aid in preventing foodborne outbreaks and food spoilage. 
==PAGE:28==
22 
 
2.5.2. Opportunities and need for further research to enhance knowledge 
and control strategies 
There are several possibilities for study to advance our understanding of B. cereus spore 
development in dairy products and our understanding of associated control methods. First and 
foremost, thorough research on the genetic control and molecular processes causing sporulation in 
dairy matrices of B. cereus is required. It will be helpful to understand better the unique gene 
expression patterns and critical regulatory elements that control sporulation to develop focused 
intervention techniques. Additionally, research into how processing technologies, such as cutting-edge 
preservation techniques and upcoming technologies, such as pulsed electric fields, ultrasound, and 
high-pressure treatments, affect the development of B. cereus spores can help to improve control 
measures. Additionally, investigating how natural antimicrobials and biocontrol agents may be able to 
prevent B. cereus from sporulating in dairy products may present intriguing new directions for the 
creation of secure control methods. 
2.6. Conclusion of theory background 
In conclusion, this theory background provides a comprehensive overview of B. cereus spore 
formation, highlighting its significance in food safety and quality. The review summarizes the current 
understanding of a variety of B. cereus sporulation-related topics, including the sporulation process, 
factors influencing spore production, and the effects of spores’ survival, germination, and outgrowth 
on the safety and quality of dairy products. The effects of temperature, pH, water activity, and other 
environmental factors on B. cereus’ growth and sporulation kinetics in dairy matrices have been the 
subject of several investigations. The research findings emphasize the importance of a more 
comprehensive understanding of the underlying processes and factors influencing spore production. 
This understanding is crucial due to the complex nature of sporulation in this significant foodborne 
illness. 
However, several aspects remain unknown, particularly concerning the specific triggers and 
regulatory systems that control sporulation in dairy matrices. Additional research is required to 
understand the impact of dairy components, processing conditions, and signaling on spore 
development. Furthermore, there is a need to explore genetic regulation, assess the effects of 
innovative preservation techniques, and investigate the potential of biocontrol agents to reduce spore 
formation in dairy products. These areas have been identified as promising avenues for future 
research. The study aims to provide insights into the spore formation behavior of B. cereus in dairy 
products at different temperatures, aiding the food industry in effectively managing spore presence in 
food products. Additionally, the study aims to determine the specific stage at which B. cereus 
undergoes spore formation. 
Combining experimental research and mathematical modeling strategies is essential for 
addressing these issues and improving our understanding. A more thorough comprehension of B. 
cereus spore generation in dairy products may be attained by integrating empirical data on growth and 
sporulation kinetics with prediction models. This integration will facilitate the development of effective 
control techniques and the identification of critical control points.

3.2. Challenge tests 
This project uses challenge tests to assess the growth and spore formation of B. cereus in 
selected dairy products. Key product characteristics, such as dry matter, pH, aw, NaCl content, and 
organic acids, are analyzed to comprehensively understand the selected dairy products. By applying 
available in-house predictive models for B. cereus, the product characteristics and storage conditions 
are utilized to predict the growth and sporulation dynamics of the bacterium. To evaluate the impact 
of temperature, a series of challenge tests are conducted using a cocktail of mesophilic or 
psychrotolerant B. cereus strains inoculated into the selected dairy products/ingredients. Factors 
including specific growth rate µmax (h-1), lag time (h), and maximum cell concentration Nmax (CFU/mL) 
obtained from these challenge tests serve as criteria for assessing the performance of the B. cereus 
growth models. Other parameters such as the maximum spore concentration Smax (CFU/mL), the time 
at which the first spore is formed t1s (h), and the spore formation rate µs (h-1) will also be monitored 
for assessing the performance of the available spore formation models for their ability to fit the 
observed data. The overall performance of each model will be tested using values obtained from the 
residual sum of squares (RSS) and the root mean square error (RMSE).

3.2.5. 
Detection and enumeration of spores 
This study’s total counts referred to the combined population of viable microorganisms, 
including vegetative cells and spores. The total counts were determined by analyzing unheated 
samples. Spores were defined as cells that exhibited resistance to a heat treatment at 80°C for 10 
minutes following the capillary method, as described by Baril et al. (2012). Therefore, to identify 
spores, a heating step is employed. To reach the heat treatment at 80°C for 10 min, a 1 mL suspension 
was subjected to heat treatment at 84°C for 13 minutes in a 1.5 Eppendorf tube immersed in a 
thermomixer (Eppendorf Thermomixer Comfort). Spore detection utilized selective (MYP) and non-
selective (BHI) media. The heat-treated samples are spread onto MYP and BHI agar plates, followed by 
incubation at 30°C for 24 hours and 25°C for 48 hours, respectively (Fig. 4). 
Sporulation abilities were assessed using Sporulation or No-Sporulation (S/NS) experiments at 
different temperature ranges. Various products, including oat drinks, cottage cheese, and whey 
powders (specifically WPC87 and permeate), were tested for S/NS experiments. Incorporating oat 
drinks, cottage cheese, and WPC87 served the purpose of conducting initial investigations and 
obtaining valuable insights into the behavior of spore formation in food products. Additionally,

cereus spores in dairy products 
Several methods have been developed to measure B. cereus spores in diverse samples, 
including dairy products. The plate counting approach, which involves diluting the material and plating 
it onto specific agar media, is one often employed technique. ISO 7932 is the recognized method for 
direct plate counting to detect and quantify the B. cereus group (Ramarao et al., 2020). 
 
Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption-Ionization Time of Flight (MALDI-TOF) will allow species 
identification of bacteria from fresh culture on agar plate based on their unique protein profiles (Fig. 
5). MALDI-TOF analyzes the mass-to-charge ratio of ionized protein molecules from a microbial sample. 
These ions are accelerated through a flight tube, and their flight time is measured. The resulting mass 
spectra are compared to a database of known spectra to identify the microorganism present in the 
sample (Singhal et al., 2015). MALDI-TOF is widely used in clinical diagnostics, food safety, 
environmental monitoring, and other fields where rapid and accurate identification of microorganisms 
is crucial (Pavlovic et al., 2013). The results obtained from the run of MALDI-TOF are shown in Appendix

3.4.3. 
Global fitting 
A global regression is a method that combines the primary and secondary models, providing a 
direct relationship between experimental factors and microbial counts (Martino and Marks, 2007). By 
employing secondary models fitted to the data, the global fit enables the assessment of the overall 
goodness of fit for the entire dataset.  
The global model incorporates both the primary and secondary models together. This means 
that each factor considered in the various primary models examined (Table 8-11) will be substituted 
with its corresponding curve regression when plotted against temperature. A final graph will then be 
created, plotting the observed values against the fitted values. This graph will provide an assessment 
of the goodness of fit of each model to the experimental data.

Based on the observed experimental results, it is evident that temperature plays a crucial role 
in various aspects of B. cereus sporulation. Specifically, temperature influences the time required for 
the final spore concentration to be attained (tmax), the time needed for the spore formation (tf), the 
probability (Pmax) at which the maximum proportion of cells that can sporulate at time tmax, and σ giving 
the scattering of the probability (Tables 21 and 22). Notably, spore formation sometimes occurs after 
the death phase of B. cereus growth for temperatures ranging from 12.5°C to 22.5°C, except at 8°C for 
the psychrotolerant strains, where no spores were observed even after 50 days. Moreover, based on 
the fitted data in Tables 21 and 22, tf is sometimes smaller than tdeath (10°C for psychrotolerant and 
17.5°C for mesophilic), suggesting that spores are being formed before the death phase of B. cereus. 
Additionally, only the psychrotolerant strains demonstrated spore formation at 10°C. The 
synchronization of sporulation among cells was less pronounced, resulting in a more scattered 
distribution of sporulation probability over time, particularly at 10°C with a scattering value (σ) of 150 
hours. The scatter of the probability seems to decrease as the storage temperature increases. This 
==PAGE:60==
54 
 
could be observed in the psychrotolerant data obtained with values decreasing from 150h at 10°C to 
24h at 17.5°C. However, such observations were not confirmed with the mesophilic strains. Indeed, σ 
is drastically increasing with the temperature reaching a very high value of 15388h at 17.5°C.  
The maximum spore concentration of the psychrotolerant strain at 17.5°C (5.19 log10 
(CFU/mL)) was approximately 30 times higher than at 10°C (3.68 log10 (CFU/mL)) (Fig. 25 and 22). 
Conversely, the maximum spore concentration of the mesophilic strains at 22.5°C (5.44 CFU/mL) was 
comparable to that of the mesophilic strains at 12.5°C (5.14 log10 CFU/mL) (Fig. 25 and 22). Initially, 
one might anticipate that this discrepancy is attributable to a significantly lower maximum sporulation 
probability (Pmax). While this holds for the psychrotolerant strains (0.0004 at 10°C against 0.014 at 
17.5°C), Pmax remains relatively consistent between the mesophilic strains incubated at 15°C and 22.5°C 
(0.06 and 0.06, respectively) (Tables 14 and 15). In other words, there is no clear correlation between 
Pmax and the final spore concentration. 
On the one hand, for the psychrotolerant strain at 17.5°C, the maximum sporulation 
probability was achieved relatively early in the incubation period (tmax = 150h) when the total spore 
concentration reached 5.13 log10 CFU/mL. On the other hand, at 10°C, the maximum sporulation 
probability occurred at 850 hours of incubation with a maximum spore concentration of 3.66 log10 
CFU/mL. 
At 22.5°C, the maximum sporulation probability was reached much sooner during the 
incubation (tmax = 42h) when the concentration of total spores was estimated at 5.44 log10 CFU/mL with 
the Gauvry model. On the other hand, at 12.5°C, the maximum sporulation probability was reached at 
200h of incubation when the maximum spore concentration was estimated at 5.14 log10 CFU/mL. The 
temperature, thus, could affect tmax. 
The time duration from the initiation of sporulation to the emergence of spores (tf) is also 
affected by temperature. Both psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains exhibited a similar trend, where 
tf values decreased with increasing temperature for the psychrotolerant strains; the time required for 
spore formation decreased from 178h at 10°C to 50h at 17.5°C. Similarly, for the mesophilic strains,

4.4. Secondary modeling for the effect of temperature 
4.4.1. Two-step approach 
The two-step approach in the secondary predictive modeling domain refers to a methodology 
that involves two distinct steps for developing predictive models. In the first step, primary models are 
created to estimate the growth and spore formation parameters of B. cereus under different 
conditions (e.g., storage temperature), as presented in the previous section (4.3). These primary 
models are based on experimental data and provide insights into the growth and spore formation 
behavior of B. cereus. In the second step, secondary models are developed using the output from the 
primary models as input variables. These secondary models are used to predict B. cereus growth and 
spore formation under specific conditions based on the information obtained from the primary 
models. By combining the primary and secondary models, the two-step approach enables more 
accurate and comprehensive predictions of microbial growth in various settings. 
Thus, this report investigated the effect of temperature on several spore formation factors. In 
order to do so, each value for each factor of the different models tested was plotted against 
temperature. The two-step approach was used for the maintenance factor, Gauvry’s model, and the 
modified Baril approach. Appendices D, E, and F include all graphs and data.

evaluated using the global fitting approach. As explained in section 3.4.3, a comparison was made 
between the observed data and the data predicted by the primary and the secondary model. This

psychrotolerant strains of B. cereus.

CHAPTER V 
Discussion 
 
5.1. Preliminary studies 
Due to the ability of B. cereus strains to sporulate in harsh conditions, it was assumed that the 
test strains of B. cereus used in this study would undergo sporulation despite unfavorable conditions 
for the growth of vegetative cells. A significant factor that influences sporulation is the depletion of 
essential nutrients required for the growth of vegetative cells (Koopman et al., 2022). Previous studies 
have indicated that Bacillus species sporulate in response to cellular starvation (Grelet, 1957; Dawes 
and Mandelstam, 1970). In addition, other factors, such as quorum sensing and environmental factors, 
e.g., pH and temperature, can also trigger the sporulation process (Gauvry et al., 2017). However, the 
combined effects of factors influencing spore formation of B. cereus have not been extensively 
researched. 
5.1.1. 
Oat drink 
In the case of mesophilic strains, B. cereus demonstrated the ability to grow and form spores 
in oat drinks stored at 16°C. After three days of incubation, the cells reached their maximum 
concentration and entered the death phase, coinciding with the onset of spore formation. 
The abrupt occurrence of the death phase in the bacterial population also coincided with a 
significant decrease in pH. This observation could be due to product contamination substantially 
altering the pH, causing a more than 1 unit pH reduction. This pH decrease could potentially impact 
spore formation and the final bacterial concentration. These findings indicate that B. cereus can grow 
and form spores in dairy products like oat drinks when stored at abusive temperatures such as 16°C. 
The influence of acidic conditions on B. cereus growth has been previously discussed by Mols 
and Abee (2003), who noted that exposure to acid conditions negatively affects the growth of B. cereus 
as the growth rate decreases while the lag phase increases. Additionally, the presence of organic acids 
intensifies the growth-inhibiting effects on the acid-exposed culture. 
5.1.2. 
Cottage cheese 
The experiment with cottage cheese performed at 15°C for both psychrotolerant and 
mesophilic strains displayed growth for two days before reaching values under the detection limit. In 
both situations, the bacteria did not have the time to enter their stationary phase. Therefore, no spores 
could be detected.  
==PAGE:69==
63 
 
These observations may be attributed to a noticeable and substantial decrease in the pH of 
the cottage cheese. The starting pH was already low for B. cereus proliferation (5.4) but dropped even 
further to as low as 4.6. Such pH values are considered below the growth boundaries for B. cereus, as 
indicated by Røssland et al. (2004). As they demonstrated in an experiment involving B. cereus NVH 45 
in milk, rapid acidification of the environment effectively inhibits sporulation in B. cereus. Achieving a 
pH of 5.0 quickly and maintaining it seems sufficient to prevent spore formation in the bacteria 
(Røssland et al., 2004). 
The observed results in the present study could be explained by the presence of lactic acid 
bacteria (LAB) in cottage cheese. LAB is commonly employed in producing fermented dairy products, 
including cottage cheese. These bacteria play a crucial role in fermentation by converting lactose (milk 
sugar) into lactic acid, contributing to the cheese's characteristic flavor, texture, and preservation (Yu 
et al., 2021). The rapid growth of a substantial LAB population could potentially hinder other 
organisms' growth through the preferential consumption of readily available nutrients and co-factors 
or simply by physically occupying the available space (Røssland et al., 2004). This study highlights the 
importance of lactic acid during the initial days of incubation in inhibiting the growth and sporulation 
of B. cereus (Røssland et al., 2004). Another study by Østergaard et al. (2014) showed LAB's effect on 
Listeria monocytogenes (L. monocytogenes) growth in cottage cheese. The LAB acted as an inhibitory 
effect on L. monocytogenes growth (Østergaard et al., 2014). When the pH reduction occurs rapidly 
enough to stabilize at pH 5.0, complete inhibition of B. cereus is observed as the opportunity for 
sporulation is effectively halted. Competition for nutrients may also play a significant role in these 
observations. 
5.1.3. 
WPC87 
The observations made in the oat drink experiment (section 5.1.1) also apply to WPC87. Both 
psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains of B. cereus displayed growth and spore formation. Notably, 
spores appeared simultaneously as the bacteria entered their death phase for the psychrotolerant 
strains. However, the mesophilic strain did not display the same pattern as spores stayed at a steady 
level throughout the experiment. Moreover, although the pH decreased throughout the experiment, 
it did not drop below a value of five, indicating a suitable environment for the growth and spore 
formation of both psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus, compared to cottage cheese. 
The death phase of bacteria occurs due to a combination of factors such as nutrient depletion, 
accumulation of waste products, exposure to toxins, and adverse environmental conditions (Finkel et 
al., 1997). The observations from the WPC87 experiment offer valuable insights into the dynamics of 
growth and spore formation in B. cereus. Sporulation was also found to be influenced by the culture 
and age of the bacteria (Bottone et al., 2010). While B. cereus is known to produce spores during 
vegetative cell growth (Dawes and Mandelstam, 1970), the present study observed that 
psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains of B. cereus produced spores after reaching a stationary growth 
phase. Similar findings were reported by Ryu et al. (2005), where the properties of B. cereus 
sporulation in broth were affected by the time required to reach the stationary/death phase of the 
bacteria (Ryu et al., 2005). 
==PAGE:70==
64 
 
The results of the WPC87 experiment suggest a close association between spore formation 
and the decline of vegetative cells during the death phase. The pH drop may also regulate bacterial 
growth and spore formation, possibly due to the production of certain acids during growth and spore 
formation (Magill et al., 1996). Further analysis and investigation are necessary to fully understand the 
underlying mechanisms and factors influencing the observed trends in the graph. It is worth noting 
that, in comparison to the study conducted by Ryu et al. (2005), the time for the first spore to appear 
was shorter in our experiment at 15°C, taking 20 hours instead of 24 hours as observed by Ryu et al. 
(2005) at 22°C for psychrotolerant strains (038-2, capable of growing at 12°C, and strain F3812/84, 
capable of growing at 8°C) (Ryu et al., 2005). 
5.2. Growth and sporulation abilities in Permeate 
Whey powders have gained significant importance in the food industry owing to their 
nutritional composition and functional properties. They are frequently employed as functional 
ingredients in food formulations, primarily to enhance protein content (Kumar et al., 2019). With its 
rich supply of high-quality protein and a complete amino acid profile, whey powder represents a 
valuable resource for fortifying protein levels in food products (Rodriguez-Lopez et al., 2022). 
Additionally, it improves flavor, providing a creamy and mildly sweet taste to a wide range of food 
items (Jeewanthi et al., 2015).  
The investigation on permeate revealed that both psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains of B. 
cereus exhibited growth and sporulation at temperatures of 12.5°C, 15°C, and 17.5°C. However, in the 
case of mesophilic strains, neither growth nor spore formation occurred at 10°C in permeate, while 
psychrotolerant strains displayed growth without sporulation at 8°C. These observations indicate that 
the temperature required for sporulation in B. cereus is higher than the minimum temperature 
necessary to grow psychrotolerant strains. Baril et al. (2012) that the temperature range for 
sporulation boundaries was found to be 5°C to 35°C for B. weihenstephanensis KBAB4 and 20°C to 50°C 
for B. licheniformis AD978. These boundaries closely align with the observed temperature ranges for 
optimal growth, which was 5°C to 37°C for B. weihenstephanensis KBAB4 and 20°C to 50°C for B. 
licheniformis AD978 (Baril et al., 2012). For Baril et al. (2012), as an initial approximation, it appears 
that temperature growth boundaries could potentially serve as an estimate for sporulation 
boundaries, although this estimation may be conservative (Baril et al., 2012). Similar behavior has been 
observed in the study conducted by Evans et al. (1997), where the storage temperature and spore 
formation of Clostridium botulinum (C. botulinum) influence their germination ability. This study has 
revealed variations in the germination capacity of C. botulinum spores based on factors such as the 
temperature at which they were formed, the temperature at which they were stored, pre-germination 
treatments (such as heat-shock), and the actual germination temperature itself (Evan et al., 1997). This 
comparison is meaningful as both C. botulinum and B. cereus are spore-forming bacteria and can cause 
foodborne illnesses, such as botulism for C. botulinum and diarrhea for B. cereus. These results 
highlight the significance of temperature management in inhibiting the sporulation of B. cereus in 
liquid food-processing environments. Controlling temperature effectively restricts the formation of 
spores, which is crucial for preventing the proliferation and potential risks associated with this 
pathogenic bacterium. 
==PAGE:71==
65 
 
In cases of temperature abuse during food processing or subsequent handling, there is a 
possibility of germination of spores, followed by vegetative cell growth and subsequent spore 
production. The onset of spore formation was delayed at lower temperatures, as evidenced by the 
time required for the first spore to appear. This delay was observed between 10°C and 17.5°C for 
psychrotolerant strains, taking ten days and two days, respectively. Similarly, the delay occurred 
between 12.5°C and 22.5°C for mesophilic strains, with five days and one day, respectively. These 
findings suggest a close relationship between delayed sporulation and storage temperature. This 
phenomenon has been previously documented by Baril et al. (2012), where the time required to 
observe one spore per mL significantly increased when the temperature decreased from 30°C to 10°C 
(Baril et al., 2012). These results imply that temperature may promote the sporulation of B. cereus at 
higher storage temperatures (Baril et al., 2012). 
The impact of temperature on the total spore concentration appeared to be unclear. The 
maximum concentration of spores varied between 3.66 log10 (CFU/mL) and 5.13 log10 (CFU/mL) at 
temperatures ranging from 10°C to 22.5°C. However, at 15°C and 17.5°C, fewer spores were produced 
compared to 12.5°C, which exhibited the highest spore concentration for the psychrotolerant strains. 
A similar trend was observed for the mesophilic strains, where the spore concentration at 12.5°C 
surpassed the maximum spore concentration observed at 22.5°C. These observations suggest that the 
storage temperature did not directly influence the maximum spore concentration. This finding aligns 
with previous reports indicating that the maximum concentration (Smax) of spores in B. subtilis broth 
cultures was not strongly affected by temperature (Gauvry et al., 2021). However, it has been noted 
that the maximum spore concentration is more strongly influenced by decreasing water activity 
(Gauvry et al., 2021). Similar findings were reported by Baril et al. (2012) for B. licheniformis AD978, 
where the final spore concentration was not significantly different between temperatures of 20°C and 
45°C (Baril et al., 2012). 
The Sporulation experiments established temperature ranges of 8°C to 17.5°C for 
psychrotolerant strains and 10°C to 22.5°C for mesophilic strains of B. cereus in permeate. Low 
temperatures, specifically 8°C for psychrotolerant strains and 10°C for mesophilic strains, were 
identified as the low-temperature boundaries for sporulating B. cereus, as no spore formation was 
observed in both cases. At 10°C, sporulation occurred only in psychrotolerant B. cereus, indicating that 
the temperature threshold for sporulation was reached for mesophilic strains. Similarly, at 8°C, no 
spore formation was detected for psychrotolerant strains, indicating that the temperature threshold 
for sporulation was reached. This observation aligns with the findings of Baril et al. (2012), where the 
temperature boundary for sporulation of B. weihenstephanensis was evaluated at 5°C, close to the 
temperature range for sporulation boundaries observed in the current project. The difference in 
temperature boundary can come from the different species evaluated in both experiments. 
This project did not determine the upper-temperature boundaries since the highest 
temperature tested was 17.5°C for psychrotolerant strains and 22.5°C for mesophilic strains. 
Sporulation was still observed at these temperatures, indicating the high-temperature boundary for B. 
cereus spore formation in permeate was not established. However, it is crucial to consider that the 
estimated boundaries may be underestimated due to the inherent spore detection limit of the 
experimental procedure, which is set at 10 CFU/mL. As a result, the actual boundaries for sporulation 
==PAGE:72==
66 
 
could be lower than the observed values. Furthermore, it is crucial to note that caution should be 
exercised when inferring sporulation boundaries based on the growth boundaries of Bacillus strains. 
Variations due to strain type, the physiological state of cells, and the media composition may introduce 
differences in sporulation behavior. 
This study reveals that the temperature range supporting sporulation in both psychrotolerant 
and mesophilic strains of B. cereus is closely aligned with the temperature range allowing growth. 
These findings are consistent with previous research. For example, in the case of B. sphaericus, growth 
occurred between 10°C and 40°C, while sporulation was observed only within the narrower range of 
10°C to 30°C (De Pieri and Ludlow, 1992). A primary model was proposed by Baril et al. (2012) to 
quantify the kinetics of sporulation, and it was observed that lower sporulation temperatures led to 
delayed spore formation. In the present study, at 10°C, the time required for one spore per mL (t1s) 
was five times longer compared to 17.5°C for psychrotolerant strains and approximately two times 
longer at 12.5°C compared to 22.5°C for mesophilic strains. 
However, it is noteworthy that the maximum sporulation rate decreased as the sporulation 
temperature decreased, indicating that higher temperatures resulted in more rapid sporulation. This 
trend could be linked to the maximum growth rate, which tends to increase with higher storage 
temperatures. A higher growth rate leads to a shorter time for the bacteria to reach their final 
concentration, thereby shortening the time required for spore appearance due to a higher specific 
maximum spore formation rate. Similar observations were also reported by Baril et al. (2012), where 
the maximum sporulation rate decreased with decreasing sporulation temperature (Baril et al., 2012). 
5.3. Primary models for sporulation kinetics 
5.3.1. 
Maintenance model 
The modified logistic growth model fits the data well using the maintenance approach. This is 
attributed to the model's consideration of the bacteria's time of death (tdeath) and the rate at which the 
bacteria are dying (kmax). The model's goodness of fit to the observed data was assessed using the root 
mean square error (RMSE) values, which indicate the accuracy of the predictions. In this study, the 
RMSE values obtained for the growth model were relatively low, ranging from 0.148 (8°C) to 0.300 
(15°C). These low RMSE values signify a strong agreement between the predicted and observed growth 
data, indicating that the model accurately predicts the growth of B. cereus in permeate with minimal 
errors. The close alignment between the predicted and observed data points highlights the precision 
and reliability of the model in capturing the patterns and trends inherent in the data. Overall, the 
model demonstrates high accuracy and reliability in making predictions regarding the growth of B. 
cereus in permeate. 
However, the inclusion of a tdeath and kmax in the modified logistic growth model is only 
significant for certain temperatures, such as 10°C, 15°C, and 17.5°C for psychrotolerant strains and 
12.5°C, 15°C, and 17.5°C for mesophilic strains, where a distinct death phase is evident. In these cases, 
the model effectively captures the declining bacterial growth phase. However, at temperatures like 
8°C, 12.5°C for psychrotolerant strains, and 22.5°C for mesophilic strains, the time of death becomes 
irrelevant as the bacteria reach their stationary phase and remain stable with kmax values 0. Therefore, 
==PAGE:73==
67 
 
assigning values to tdeath while fitting the modified logistic growth model becomes negligible at these 
temperatures. These observations are crucial, especially considering that the model for spore 
formation incorporates tdeath as the time at which spore formation begins. 
The maintenance model used for fitting spore formation also demonstrates a reasonable fit to 
the data by considering a single parameter, the maintenance coefficient (M, S/CFU/h). The model 
incorporates the time of death, a parameter fitted in the growth model, the initial concentration of 
spores (S0), and the maintenance factor (M). Overall, the model can provide a good fit, as evidenced 
by the relatively low RMSE values of 0.155 (15°C, psychrotolerant), 0.285 (17.5°C, psychrotolerant), 
and 0.222 (22.5°C, mesophilic). However, the model's performance varies across different 
temperatures, as indicated by higher RMSE values of 0.568 (10°C, psychrotolerant), 0.516 (12.5°C, 
mesophilic), and 0.430 (17.5°C, mesophilic). These results can be attributed to the fact that only one 
parameter was fitted in the equation, which allows for inevitable variability in the model's predictions. 
It is worth noting that applying the maintenance model for fitting spore formation in B. cereus 
is a novel approach, specifically for modeling spore formation in dairy products. The present study 
represents the first instance of utilizing this type of fitting in the context of B. cereus spore formation. 
Overall, utilizing the maintenance model and the insights gained from this study offer new perspectives 
on the spore formation dynamics of B. cereus in dairy products. 
5.3.2. 
Gauvry’s model 
Psychrotolerant strains of B. cereus demonstrated growth within a temperature range of 8°C 
to 17.5°C, while mesophilic strains exhibited growth within a range of 12.5°C to 22.5°C. As the 
environmental conditions became more challenging for growth, there was an increase in the lag phase 
before growth initiation and a decrease in the growth rate. These trends highlight the sensitivity of B. 
cereus to temperature variations and provide insights into its growth characteristics under different 
environmental conditions. Français et al. (2019) also observed that the B. cereus sensu lato strains' lag 
time was shorter when the incubation temperature was increased; at a temperature of 20°C, the lag 
time ranged from 1.2 hours to 1.7 hours, while at a temperature of 30°C, the lag time ranged from 0.5 
hours to 1.5 hours (Français et al., 2019). 
The observed differences in predicted Smax between conditions can be attributed to the 
combination of several parameters in Gauvry’s model. This notion is supported by the various models 
presented earlier in section 4.3.2, which highlight the importance of factors such as the maximum 
probability of spore formation (Pmax), the time at which the maximum probability occurs (tmax), and the 
scattering around that time (σ). These factors could contribute to the variations in predicted spore final 
concentration, as demonstrated by previous research conducted by Gauvry et al. (2019). 
At the higher temperatures tested for growth and spore formation, the appearance of the first 
spores was accelerated, resulting in higher concentrations of predicted spores. Specifically, 
psychrotolerant strains exhibited this pattern at 17.5°C, while mesophilic strains demonstrated it at 
22.5°C. These criteria, which reflect sporulation efficiency, are commonly employed to assess and 
characterize spore formation in studies related to food applications (Baril et al., 2012a; Carvalho et al., 
2010; Monteiro et al., 2005). 
==PAGE:74==
68 
 
Lower temperatures, on the other hand, had a limited impact on the predicted maximum 
concentration of spores. These findings align with previous observations regarding B. subtilis 
(Monteiro et al., 2005) and other species like Bacillus weihenstephanensis KBAB4 (Baril et al., 2012a). 
The effect can be attributed to the inhibitory influence of low temperatures on sporulation gene 
expression and the deceleration of enzymatic reactions (Budde et al., 2006). Consequently, even 
though the maximum proportion of cells undergoing sporulation was lower, more vegetative cells 
initiated the sporulation process. This phenomenon explains the higher level of predicted spore 
concentration observed at 12.5°C for both psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains. 
Although the model used for spore formation demonstrates an excellent fit to the observed 
data, several constraints had to be applied to ensure the fitted factors were meaningful. This 
observation contradicts the findings described in the literature, such as the study conducted by Gauvry 
et al. (2019) in broth, where the scatter for the probability exhibited narrower distribution as the 
temperature became more drastic for growth and spore formation. While Gauvry's model displayed 
an impressive fit to the observed data on spore formation in permeate, limitations still need to be 
addressed. 
One notable limitation is the relatively high number of factors that require fitting, which makes 
the model highly sensitive to even minor changes in parameter values. The scatter around the 
maximum probability (Pmax) often appears to lack meaningful interpretation, and the value of Pmax 
does not seem to correlate with any specific factor. Furthermore, while the model may offer a good fit 
for data in broth, its applicability to actual dairy products may be limited. 
These limitations suggest further refinement and investigation of the model to enhance its 
accuracy and applicability in real-world dairy product settings. Addressing these challenges would 
improve our understanding of spore formation dynamics and enable more precise predictions in 
practical food industry applications. 
5.3.3. 
Modified Baril’s model 
By fitting only three factors in the equation, the model effectively describes the data for spore 
formation. Although the total RMSE values may not be the lowest among the models tested, it is 
essential to note that this model does not account for the death phase of the cells. Instead, it assumes 
an infinite stationary phase. Despite this limitation, the growth model fits the data well and 
accommodates scenarios where the death phase is not explicitly observed. In the context of the spore 
formation model, it does not correlate with the time of bacterial death but with the time at which the 
maximum concentration of B. cereus is reached. From a mathematical standpoint (lowest spore RMSE 
values), the modified Baril's model demonstrates the best fit for describing the spore formation 
dynamics of B. cereus in permeate. Overall, this modified model balances the maintenance model and 
Gauvry's model, effectively capturing the spore formation kinetics.  
The low RMSE values indicate that the modified Baril’s model has good predictive power. It 
can generalize well to unseen data and make accurate predictions beyond the observed data points in 
dairy product simulations. This suggests that the relatively simple model of Baril is robust and can be 
used for future predictions or extrapolations for spore formation in dairy products. Furthermore, the 
==PAGE:75==
69 
 
low RMSE values imply that it effectively captures the complexities in the observed data. This is crucial 
for gaining a deeper understanding of the growth and spore formation of B. cereus. 
Although validation of the model at higher temperatures for growth and sporulation is 
essential, these findings suggest that the quantitative impact of temperature on both growth and 
sporulation is remarkably similar regarding lag time, the time for the first spore, and the sporulation 
rate. Therefore, as an initial approximation, knowledge regarding factors influencing the growth of 
spore-forming bacteria can also be utilized to ascertain favorable environmental conditions for spore 
formation and sporulation rates. During food processing, extended residence times of food materials 
can increase the likelihood of spore-forming bacteria being exposed to environments conducive to cell 
multiplication or spore formation in-line (Oomes et al., 2007). To prevent spore formation, it is 
recommended to restrict the niches where Bacillus sp. growth could occur. Determining growth and 
sporulation kinetics through dedicated experiments or by utilizing predictive models can contribute to 
identifying critical control points in processing lines and effectively managing spore-forming bacteria 
in food products. 
5.4. Secondary modeling for temperature effect 
5.4.1. 
Two-step approach 
The modified logistic growth model incorporated six distinct factors, while the maintenance 
model focused on a single factor, allowing for the examination of temperature's influence on growth 
and spore formation kinetics. Evaluating the temperature’s influence on growth and spore formation 
aligns with previous findings reported by Dey et al. (2020), who emphasized the significance of 
temperature as a determinant of bacterial growth rate. Their study demonstrated that the generalized 
logistic growth model yielded the lowest error, highlighting that the duration of growth primarily 
decreases with increasing maximum specific growth rate (Dey et al., 2020). Consistent outcomes were 
obtained when fitting the observed data from the permeate experiment using the logistic model. 
Therefore, for all growth models used, µmax increased linearly, following, 𝜇𝑚𝑎𝑥= (𝑏× (𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛−𝑇))

at increasing temperatures (Mejlholm and Dalgaard, 2009; Koukou et al., 2020). 
Regarding the impact of temperature on lag time, an interesting pattern emerges between 
psychrotolerant and mesophilic strains. For psychrotolerant strains, there is a decrease in the lag time 
as the temperature increases, as mentioned by Français et al. (2019). However, an opposite trend is 
observed in mesophilic strains, where the lag time slightly increases with higher temperatures. The 
opposite trend contradicts the commonly reported notion that lag time decreases with rising 
temperature (Duh and Schaffner, 1992). One possible explanation for this discrepancy is the absence 
of multiple data points before the growth phase of B. cereus (Appendices D, E, and F). 
Additionally, it is vital to consider the effect of specific conditions on the culture preparation 
of the mesophilic strains. In this study, the pre-culture of the mesophilic strains was stored in a water 
bath at 10°C before inoculation and subsequent growth at 30°C. In contrast, the psychrotolerant 
strains were kept on ice after the overnight growth at 15oC. As a result, the adaptation time for the 
psychrotolerant bacteria is relatively shorter, resulting in a shorter lag time.

The fitting of the maximum cell concentration (Nmax) was relatively straightforward as B. cereus 
exhibited growth within a narrow range of 6.5 log10 (CFU/mL) to 7.18 log10 (CFU/mL). Consequently, a 
linear regression model was selected to fit the data concerning temperature. This indicates that the 
maximum cell concentration was not influenced by temperature; instead, the time required to reach 
the maximum cell concentration showed an impact. Furthermore, the time of death (tdeath) was 
influenced by temperature, with decreasing values observed as temperatures increased. 
The factors of growth and sporulation were similarly influenced by temperature. These 
findings indicate a correlation between temperature and the sporulation factors, which play a 
significant role in determining the efficiency of the sporulation process. Specifically, the temperature 
appears to impact factors such as the scattering of spore formation probability (σ), the maximum 
proportion of cells capable of sporulation (Pmax), and the time (tmax) at which this probability is observed 
as reported by Gauvry et al. (2021). 
The influence of temperature on sporulation kinetics can be attributed to its impact on the 
time of maximum sporulation probability (tmax) and the time at which spores are formed (tf). In lower 
temperature conditions, both tmax and tf decreased linearly, consistent with findings Gauvry et al. 
(2021) reported in B. subtilis broth. However, contrasting results were observed for the maximum 
probability of sporulation (Pmax), which did not exhibit a transparent relationship with temperature, 
unlike the patterns described by Gauvry et al. (2021). In the study conducted in permeate, Pmax showed 
considerable variation among the tested temperatures, with values reaching their peak before or after 
spores reached maximum concentration. The scattering (σ) around Pmax also displayed exceptional 
values, occasionally exceeding 1000 hours, particularly for mesophilic strains. These outcomes deviate 
from the literature findings Gauvry et al. (2021) reported, where the maximum probability of 
sporulating increases with decreasing temperatures (Appendix E). 
Furthermore, the employed model used to estimate probability may possess limitations or 
oversimplifications that fail to capture the complexity of dairy products fully. These limitations may 
contribute to disparities between predicted and observed probabilities. Given the number of variables 
within the model (four), it is understandable that even minor parameter changes can significantly 
impact the model's sensitivity. 
The modified Baril's model includes two additional factors not considered in the previous 
models: the time for the first spore to form (t1s) and the sporulation rate (µs). The time delay (tdelay) and 
the time at which maximum cell concentration is reached (tmax) exhibited a similar pattern, decreasing 
exponentially with increasing temperature. This suggests that as the bacteria reached their stationary 
phase more rapidly, spore formation occurred more quickly. These observations were also noticed by 
Gauvry et al. (2021), where the t1s of B. subtilis also decreased with increasing temperature. Notably, 
a significant correlation is observed between the time of bacterial death (tdeath) and the time for the 
first spore to form (t1s). Generally, the values of tdeath obtained from the modified logistic model with a 
death phase closely correspond to t1s derived from Baril's model. 
It is worth mentioning that there is a strong connection between sporulation and growth 
processes. Previous studies have established a linear relationship between the maximum growth rate 
==PAGE:77==

and the sporulation rate (Baril, 2011; Dawes and Mandelstam, 1970), indicating that these two 
bacterial processes share common molecular mechanisms (Mendez et al., 2004; Reder et al., 2012). 
Furthermore, the boundaries for sporulation align closely with the boundaries for growth, and the 
optimal conditions for sporulation, characterized by rapid and high-concentration spore formation, 
coincide with the optimal conditions for growth. 
5.4.2. 
Global fit 
The lack of agreement between the observed and predicted data suggests that the model may 
be inadequate in explaining the effect of temperature on the spore formation. Other factors 
influencing the data may not be accounted for in the model. The limited inclusion of only one 
parameter, specifically the maintenance coefficient, in the fitting process for spore formation may 
contribute to the observed poor goodness of fit. The simplicity of the model and the absence of other 
influential factors could result in an inadequate representation of the complex spore formation 
process. Consequently, the model’s ability to accurately predict and explain the observed data is 
compromised. To improve the quality of goodness of fit, it may be necessary to incorporate additional 
factors or factors that capture the multifaceted nature of spore formation and consider their effects 
on the overall model. The poor quality of the goodness of fit also raises concerns about the reliability 
and accuracy of the model’s predictions. Further investigation and refinement of the model are 
necessary to improve its performance and enhance its predictive capability. 
The global fit of the modified logistic growth without the death phase for the growth of 
psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus showed good enough results compared to the previously 
mentioned. Seeing such results makes sense as the time of death is not considered in the modified 
logistic growth model used to fit the growth of B. cereus. Therefore, fewer parameters were adjusted 
to the model, leading to a less good quality of the growth model. Conversely, the global fit for the 
modified Baril’s approach showed amazing results in fitting spore concentration against the observed 
spore concentration. The model employed in this study demonstrates a strong goodness of fit to the 
observed data, as evidenced by the global fitting analysis. The model's ability to accurately capture the 
patterns and trends of sporulation in B. cereus suggests its potential applicability for future research 
in this field. However, before its widespread utilization, it is crucial to comprehensively evaluate the 
model's performance. Model evaluation could involve assessing its predictive accuracy, robustness to 
variations in experimental conditions, and generalizability across different strains or environments. 
Additionally, model validation using independent datasets would be crucial to ensure its reliability and 
validity. By conducting a thorough model evaluation, researchers can gain further confidence in its 
suitability for investigating the sporulation dynamics of B. cereus and its potential applications in food 
safety and quality control. 
The global fitting analysis of Gauvry's model for spore formation was not conducted due to the 
absence of clear temperature-dependent influences on specific factors within the model. Parameters 
such as Pmax and σ did not exhibit any discernible patterns or proportional relationships, even after 
various transformations (e.g., logarithmic or inverse transformations) were applied to the data. As a 
result, the data obtained for the global fit was inadequate, indicating that the global fit for spore 
formation would have yielded unsatisfactory results.

CHAPTER VI 
Conclusion and future 
perspectives 
 
In conclusion, this thesis has provided valuable insights into the growth and spore formation 
kinetics of B. cereus in dairy products. If the concentration of bacteria does not reach values above 6 
log10 (CFU/mL), no spore formation will be observed for either psychrotolerant or mesophilic strains 
between 8°C and 22.5°C. Through integrating experimental data collection, and mathematical 
modeling, we have enhanced our understanding of the factors influencing sporulation and investigated 
already existing predictive models to simulate spore formation kinetics in dairy products. The findings 
of this study showed that the modified Baril’s model presented the best fit to the observed spore 
concentration data with the lowest RMSE value of 0.260 for spores of psychrotolerant strains and 
0.378 for mesophilic strains. The developed mathematical model successfully captured the complexity 
of B. cereus spore formation and demonstrated its ability to predict spore formation kinetics. This 
model could potentially serve as a valuable tool for risk assessment and control strategies, aiding in 
producing safe dairy products. The integration of this model into food safety management systems 
could help in the identification of critical control points and the implementation of preventive 
measures to mitigate B. cereus spore contamination. Thus, the project contributes to the scientific 
knowledge of B. cereus spore formation and could have important implications for food safety and 
control strategies in the dairy industry. 
The experimental data revealed that environmental factors such as temperature plays a critical 
role in sporulation kinetics. Such observations were later confirmed through the secondary modeling 
proving the effect of temperature on time to the first spore being formed (t1s) and the spore rate (µs). 
Increasing temperature led to increasing µs and decreasing t1s. The results indicated the importance of 
controlling temperature during dairy product processing and storage to minimize the risk of B. cereus 
spore formation. 
While this study has advanced our understanding of Bacillus cereus spore formation in dairy 
products, several areas require further investigation. Firstly, validating the developed models using 
independent datasets considering various dairy matrices is essential to ensure their generalizability 
and applicability. Additionally, future research should explore the influence of other factors, including 
organic acids commonly present in dairy products, on spore formation dynamics. 
Moreover, to gain a comprehensive understanding of B. cereus spore formation, it is 
imperative to investigate spore formation kinetics at higher temperatures. Many dairy product 
==PAGE:79==
73 
 
processes, such as fermentation and high-temperature filtration, involve elevated temperatures, and 
the behavior of B. cereus spore formation under these conditions needs to be thoroughly examined. 
Furthermore, the inoculation concentration of bacteria may play a crucial role in determining 
the kinetics of B. cereus spore formation. Therefore, more extensive investigations are warranted to 
elucidate the impact of initial bacterial concentration on spore formation dynamics. 
In conclusion, while this study has made significant strides in unraveling B. cereus spore 
formation in dairy products, further research is needed to validate the developed models, explore the 
influence of additional factors, investigate spore formation at higher temperatures, and examine the 
role of inoculation concentration. This research could contribute to a more comprehensive 
understanding of B. cereus spore formation and aid in developing targeted control strategies in the 
dairy industry.

Appendix E: Raw data of Gauvry’s model application 
See attached file: Gauvry’s_model.xlsm 
The attached Excel file comprises the raw data and the primary Gauvry model fitting the growth and 
spore formation of psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus at every temperature tested (Sheet 8P 
until 22.5M). The file also contains the secondary models referring to the effect of temperatures on 
the different parameters of both growth and spore formation of B. cereus (Sheet µmax until tf). The 
sheets for the secondary models show the fitting of the cardinal model. However, this model type lacks 
meaningful interpretation as the value for Tmax, Tmin, Topt, and Yopt are unknown. The fitting of the 
cardinal model was, therefore, done to prove that temperature did not affect enough spore formation 
parameters to showcase the global fitting of Gauvry’s model. 
A model based on the cardinal model developed by Rosso et al. (1995) was employed to describe the 
influence of temperature on bacterial growth and spore formation following the next two equations: 
𝑌𝑚𝑎𝑥(𝑇°𝐶) = 𝑌𝑜𝑝𝑡× 𝐶𝑀2(𝑇°𝐶) 
𝐶𝑀𝑛(𝑇) = {
(𝑇−𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥) × (𝑇−𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛)𝑛
(𝑇𝑜𝑝𝑡−𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛)
𝑛−1 × [(𝑇𝑜𝑝𝑡−𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛) × (𝑇−𝑇𝑜𝑝𝑡) −(𝑇𝑜𝑝𝑡−𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥) × (𝑇𝑜𝑝𝑡+ 𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛−𝑛𝑋)]
, 𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛< 𝑇< 𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥
0, 𝑇≤𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛 𝑜𝑟 𝑇≥𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥
} 
In the context of this study, the growth or sporulation parameters (e.g., µmax, lag time, log10 (Pmax), 
tmax, or σ) are represented by the variable Y, while the environmental factor being examined, 
specifically temperature, is denoted by T. The maximum (Tmax), minimum (Tmin), and optimum (Topt) 
values of temperature at which the strain exhibited growth or sporulation capabilities are considered. 
Additionally, the shape parameter (n), set at a value of 2 in this study, describes the effects of 
temperature on the growth and sporulation parameters (Rosso et al., 1995; Gauvry et al., 2021).
