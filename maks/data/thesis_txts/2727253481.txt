Abstract
The goal of this paper was to assess the current status of electronic voting, which would be done in
multiple steps. The product of this paper at the beginning, was to only create a roadmap, and explore
the current situation of the solutions and the requirements that they fulfil. Some of the related work
papers were used to create a list for the requirement section.
However, before beginning there had to be created a methodology protocol to ensure that the re-
sults are reproducible. This was done because this paper aimed to be a systematic literature review
which would require a methodology section. The primary selection of papers went through the different
steps that were mentioned in the methodology section which was set up earlier. The final result was
140 papers which were assessed on which requirements they each fulfilled.
This information was then used to create two roadmaps, one for researchers, and another for solution
designers. The first roadmap which was made for researchers, was set up such that the requirement
that was least mature had the highest priority. The second roadmap which was made for solution
designers was set up based on the requirement’s dependency on one another, which in principle would
make it easier for the designer to design the solution.

Introduction
There are currently many proposed solutions for an electronic voting system. However, the majority
of countries around the world do not use any kind of electronic voting [1]. There can be many different
reasons for not implementing an electronic voting system. Electronic-based voting does have some
benefits which traditional ballot voting does not have. As an example, electronic voting is faster at
counting the votes, and it is less prone to errors compared to human counting [2, 3]. However, as
mentioned earlier it has yet to be widely adopted, which implies that there are reasons for avoiding
electronic voting. Some of these reasons might be that electronic voting has some issues which have
not been solved yet.
The goal of this thesis conducts a systematic literature review (SLR). The SLR will investigate and
explore the current solutions that exist out there, but before that the current domains must be defined
and each domain should also include a set of requirements. The solutions will then be evaluated on
how many requirements they fulfil in each of the domains, and lastly, a road map will be created
which describes the steps that are necessary to take to achieve or create a solution that meets all the
requirements for a given domain.
1.1
PICOC
The PICOC (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Context) criteria can be used to define
the SLR’s main objectives. The outcome can then be used as keywords in the search queries later.
PICOC is widely used in the medical field [4, 5, 6], and it is used to consider the components that will
be included in the research questions as well as the search queries [7].
Population: Population is the specific area that the research is conducted on, this can be a spe-
cific role, an application area, or an industry domain. The more specific the better, but the population
should not be too narrow because then there would not be enough relevant literature.
Intervention: Intervention is the solution that is used to address the issue. This could be a tech-
nology, a specific tool, or even a methodology. Defining the intervention is relevant because for each
issue there can be many different solutions which have nothing in common. An example could be
malware attacks on a company through email. The interventions could include educating the staff,
installing antivirus software, only allowing email from a specific sender, preventing the installation of
programs, etc. Looking at all the interventions for a problem is not feasible, therefore defining the inter-
vention, or at least the type of intervention, helps focus the scope of the SLR, without being too broad.
Comparison: Comparison is the other solutions to which the intervention solutions are compared.
This part is not always applicable. This SLR is applicable, and it will be used in the conclusion and
results, but it is not relevant for the search queries. This is because the literature does not have to
compare their solution to anything to be included. However, all of the solutions will be assessed at the
end of this SLR.
Outcome: An intervention can have many different outcomes. But it is important to specify the
relevant outcomes which will be used in the comparison. Moreover, it can also help to get a better
idea of whether an intervention has successfully achieved the outcomes that are defined by this SLR.
Context: Context is the environment in which the comparison is taking place. This can be rele-
vant if the solutions behave differently or produce different outcomes depending on the context and

Related works
For the duration of this subsection, this paper that the reader is currently reading will be referred to
as "this SLR".
The first study by Esteve et al. [1] is conducted by the IFES, and the study focuses on the internet
voting experience in a government context. More specifically the study focuses on the implementation
of internet voting in the Norwegian government. They investigate people’s trust in internet voting, as
well as the features of internet voting solutions. The study also looks at the role of the stakeholders. It
gets all its data by looking at other countries where internet voting has been implemented previously
and then investigates how that would look in the Norwegian government context. The tests were done
on local government elections to assess internet voting as a solution. This study touches on important
factors for the internet and electronic voting. However, this study focuses on human experience and
human behaviour, which is out of the scope of this SLR. Therefore, this study can be used to fill that
void for this SLR if the necessity arises.
The second study by Heinl et al.
[8] has the closest relation to this SLR. This study focuses on
classifying electronic voting solutions, where the solutions must be in an uncontrolled environment,
which means that the voter can vote from their own personal device or any other device. The paper
also evaluates the solutions on a set of requirements, which are derived from the German government
election requirements. This article is relevant to the current state of e-voting since it describes the cur-
rent solutions. However, this paper is not a SLR, since it does not fulfil the requirements of becoming
an SLR according to [13]. The article does not include a section for the methodology which makes it
not easily replicable. The article explores the same area as this SLR, however, it is doing so using a
different method, the goal of this SLR is to be replicable so that future readers can evaluate the whole
process and even use this information however it fits them. This means that, amongst other things,
the reader will be able to pick the part which is most useful for them and leave the rest or maybe
replicate this study for verification, or for updating purposes.
The study from Darmawan [9] focuses on e-voting adoption in government. The study is an SLR,
and therefore the focus is on other papers, that look at the adaption of electronic voting. This paper
has discovered that most of the adaption studies come from the USA and the study mentions that
this might make the results biased. However, the adaptation of e-voting is not the topic of this SLR,
therefore it is not in the same scope of this report. But it is still relevant because it shows an increase
in publications that studies the adaptation of electronic voting, which indicates that the topic of this
SLR is relevant.
The paper from Jafar et al. [10] focuses on scalable blockchain. The paper mentions that the problem
with blockchain is that it is not scalable and therefore it can be a problem when adopting blockchain

The paper mentions that it wants to outline research avenues for developing a scalable

areas.

Research questions
The research questions for this SLR are important. The reason is that the questions will define the
audience for this review [13]. The aim of this SLR is to have researchers and developers as its audience.

1.3.1
Problem Area & Methodology
The first goal of this SLR is to investigate where e-voting is currently standing, and the status of it.
The different domains where e-voting takes place must be established before investigating the current
state of e-voting.
A domain is a setting where the voting takes place, this could be in an office,
or in a government. Ideally, the status of the adaption of e-voting in each domain should also be
described. Moreover, the descriptions should include a reason for the adaptation or lack there off,
this is to determine if there is a consensus on the reason. In addition to that is the importance of
creating a methodology section to ensure consistency and transparency, this methodology will be used
for investigating the next research question.

RQ 1.1 What domains exists for voting?
RQ 1.2 What percentage utilize e-voting in their domain?
RQ 1.3 What requirements are essential for each domain?

1.4
Scope of this SLR
Electronic voting is a broad term which includes a lot of different methods of voting. A taxonomy
of electronic voting can be seen in Figure 1, this is based on the categorization of the U.S. Election
Assistance Commission and Mursi et al. [14, 15]. The taxonomy in Figure 1 is only expanded with
subcategories where it is relevant for this SLR. This SLR is concerned with both controlled and un-
controlled environments where the endpoint is either web-based or application-based. An uncontrolled
environment is defined as an environment where it is not controlled by the election responsible. In a
controlled environment, the elections might happen on a phone, tablet, or computer in a designated
location where voters can go to.
Uncontrolled environments are using the same endpoint (phone,
tablet, or computer) but it is location independent, and the devices could be personal devices or public
devices (e.g., library).
Figure 1: Taxonomy of electronic voting
The domains and the solutions are the main part of this SLR. The goal is to try and include as many
as possible in the first search round, then they are eliminated based on criteria which are pre-defined
in the methodology section. However, it might be possible that this SLR will miss some of the domains
and/or solutions. This is because it is difficult to create search strings which include everything without
leaving anything out. Moreover, it is not easy to verify something unknown, meaning that if a domain
or solution is missed in the literature search phase then it would not be possible to know, because
there is no indicator of what is missed by the search result.

Electronic voting requirements
This section is based on the paper by Helbach [16]. The document is in German but has been translated
to English with the help of translation tools, and an associate professor from DTU helped confirm
uncertainties in the translation. This section explores the different requirements that different use
cases have for electronic voting.
2.1
Election levels
There are three levels of elections as defined by [16]. The paper defines the three levels according to
the German government and their rules. However, it has been abstracted and made more generally
such that it can be applied to more than one government.
2.1.1
First-order election
First-order elections have the highest requirements, this is where the electoral systems are central for
democracy. Moreover, there will be adversaries with a lot of power and resources at this level. For
these reasons, this level must be protected with the highest level of security.
Some of the elections that this level includes are:
• Government elections
• The European parliament
• State parliament
• City/District/Municipal council
• City Mayor
Each country has different levels of official government elections. This list does not include them all,
but any election which is fundamental for the functionality of a country is classified as a first-order
election. The severity of the election is also defined by the government itself since an election for the
same position in two different countries might have different risk factors as well as different importance.
2.1.2
Second-order election
Second-order elections can be elections organised by the government where the general principles of
electoral law apply and, there are fewer pre-requisites and requirements than a standard election which
has more risk. The general principles are defined in subsection 2.2.
Some of the elections that this level includes are:
• In a meeting for representatives for the social security funds
• Staff council
• Church leader

2.1.3
Third-order election
Third-order elections are characterized by the fact that these elections do not have fundamental prin-
ciples defined. Instead, every club, association, or office defines their own rules and regulations for
the elections. This level also includes surveys and polls. This level does not have any real adversary,
therefore the security requirements are low.

2.2
Election principles
The election principles are derived from a paragraph from German law.
This subsection includes
several principles, which will further be described in greater detail.
2.2.1
Universality of choice
The first principle states that everyone should be able to take part in the election. This means that no
one should be averted from participating in any election as a result of organizational, functional, or any
other deficiency. Participation is defined as both as a candidate and as a voter. This can also be relevant
for electronic voting since it can be difficult for some people to use computer systems and computer
interfaces. Therefore, the interface should be easy to understand and easy to use. Moreover, the system
should not make it difficult for participating candidates to sign up for the elections. However, this
issue is related to the user interface, and not the design of the solution.
2.2.2
Direct election
German law defined this principle as the voting decision being made directly by the voter. This is true
for countries such as Denmark and Germany, however, in the United States they use electors that are
elected by the voters, and the electors make the final decision. But for electronic voting, this principle
is defined as the voting decision must not be changed after a voter has cast their ballot.
2.2.3
Free choice
This principle ensures that the voter has the right to vote, and the freedom to have their own opinion.
Additionally, the voter should not be pressured or influenced to make a specific voting decision. Fur-
thermore, the vote should not be cast prematurely or carelessly, in other words, incorrect entries must
be prevented. Up until the point where the vote has been cast, the voter should have the possibility
to change their vote as many times as they wish, without the changes being recorded, monitored, or
observed by any third party. The voter should be notified that their vote has been included in the
count after the vote has been cast. It should not be possible to change the vote after it has been cast,
neither by the voter nor by anyone else. A voter should have the possibility to cast an invalid vote, to
express their objection in this way.
2.2.4
Same choice
This principle is defined as each voter should have access to all the available voting types that are
provided, however, each voter should only be allowed to vote once and their votes counted exactly

==PAGE:13==

once, even if a voter can cast a traditional ballot and a vote online. There is more than one possible
method to handle additional votes. The votes could be combined, or one of the votes could be removed.
2.2.5
Secret voting
The freedom of choice in an election is safeguarded by its secrecy, which must never be compromised,
even when voter authentication is in place. The secrecy of voting must continue to be upheld in the
future. To ensure anonymity, the ballots or counted votes must be rendered untraceable to the voters
who cast them. Additionally, the election system’s data should never be utilized in any way that could
infringe upon the election’s secrecy.
2.3
Legal requirements
Legal requirements are relevant to most levels of elections. First-order elections involve the government,
and therefore they have to specify the legal requirements for the electronic voting systems that will be
used. However, it is also relevant for offices and companies because they have to comply with the law.
This can have an impact on the implementation of electronic voting if the law states that elections in
the office have to meet certain security requirements.
• Permanent confidentiality of vote: The confidentiality of a vote cast is defined as the vote
being separated from the voter and cannot be assigned to them in the future.
• Structure of the ballot: The structure of the ballot has to be the same for all voters and the
ballots should be indistinguishable. This requirement concerns the visual aspect of the ballot as
well as the data aspect of the ballot.
• Seperation of duty: The poll workers are provided by all parties and they can monitor each
other’s activities at the polling station. This ensures the citizen’s confidence in the traditional
electoral process.
• Freedom of choice and vote updating: A voter should be able to verify that their vote has
been correctly placed, and counted. However, the voter should not be able to prove their voting
decision to any third party. This is to prevent voter-buying and blackmailing.
• Public counting: In theory it should be possible for any citizen to make sure that the voting
box is empty at the beginning of voting, and that all votes have been counted. Moreover, if
a citizen chooses to sit throughout the voting day, they can ensure that no ballot stuffing has
occurred.
• Observation of elections: Third parties such as other countries should be able to observe,
examine, and assess the course of an election, to ensure that the election takes the correct course.
2.4
Political requirements
Democracy is the most established in most countries around the world [17], and the collective decision-
making of the citizen is an essential part of democracy. Moreover, the higher the participation rate
the better the citizens are represented in the government. Democratic systems are supported by tools,
whether it is ballot papers, e-voting, or internet voting. However, the tools have to be examined and
deemed neutral, before they are taken into use.

Societal requirements
2.5
Societal requirements
The democracy of any country is dependent on its citizen accepting the outcome of the election and
also trusting the process of the election. That is, the citizens have to trust the voting process before
it can be implemented. Therefore societal requirements are relevant to include to have a successful
voting system.
2.5.1
Trust and transparency
The voting process must be transparent such that it is easily understandable for the general citizen.
This is because the general citizen will not trust the process if it is not transparent and comprehensible.
Therefore information about the electronic voting system should be publicly accessible. Experts should
be able to check the source code of the voting system. Citizens’ trust and confidence in a system drop
greatly if experts express criticism of the voting system. There are some points that the paper mentions
that should be made clear to the voting citizen:
• How does the procedure work on an abstract level? (Describing the system as simple as possible)
• How are the electoral principles secured by the electoral system and are there residual risks? if
yes what are the risks, and how can they be minimized?
• How can the results of an electronic voting system, be verified manually, if necessary?
2.5.2
Verifiability and countability
The election results should be plausible, verifiable, and comprehensible.
It should be possible to
manually confirm the vote count of an electronic voting system. This would increase voters’ confidence,
because in case a party deems the results are improbable, then they could count the votes manually
and verify that the machines did not make a mistake. However, there should be made a distinction
between individual and universal verifiability.
• Individual verifiability: Every voter can verify that their vote has correctly arrived in the
ballot box and is correctly counted in the election results.
• Universal verifiability: If the system can be individually verifiable, and the voter can also
verify that all votes cast have been counted correctly.
2.5.3
Economic aspect
Citizens will be more accepting of a new voting system if there is a financial incentive for implementing
it. There are some points which should be addressed for an electronic voting system.
• How much does the introduction of an electronic voting system cost?
• Can the number of polling stations be reduced?
• What other costs arise from implementing such a system? (storage, maintenance, monitoring,
information, support, etc.)

2.6
Technological security requirements
The technological security requirements are based on two studies [16, 12], however, these requirements
are not the final requirements. These technological requirements are split up into two parts which have
been inspired by the Volkamer study [12]. This subsection includes requirements which were deemed
relevant for a software voting solution.
2.6.1
Polling requirements
The first part is at the polling phase where the security requirements are applicable while the voters
are casting their votes.
• Access control: Implementing access control to prevent intruders from accessing and tampering
with sensitive information.
• Tamper proof: The system should be tamper resistant, and tamper evident.
• Unauthorized votes (voter): Preventing a voter from voting multiple times.
• Unauthorized votes (external): Preventing external adversaries from ballot stuffing.
• Seperation of duty: Preventing one insider from abusing their power, possibly by only allowing
access to sensitive information if at least two different users are logged in.
• Secrecy: The casted votes must be secret, and the voter must be anonymous, meaning there is
no linkage between stored information and a voter.
• Secrecy after breakdown/malfunction: Preventing information leakage in case of breakdown
or malfunction.
• Availability: The voting system must be available and fail-safe (also against DoS attacks).
2.6.2
Tallying requirements
The second part is after the polling phase is done, which also includes the phase where the votes are
being tallied.
• Affecting the counting: Preventing an intruder malware from affecting the counting of votes.
• Integrity of the votes: Preventing tampering with the votes after they have been stored from
the polling session, and before the tallying count has begun.
• Authenticity: Verifying the authenticity of the votes before tallying. And the authenticity of
the voter and the election server must be ensured.
• Non-linkability of the votes and voter: Preventing linkability between voter and votes, by
examining stored votes.

2.7
The final table of requirements
These are the final requirements which will be used to evaluate the solutions. They are compiled
by considering the technological requirements, legal requirements, and election principles. However,
not all of the requirements from the previous subsections will be included, this is because these final
requirements are for the design of the solutions.
Whereas some requirements from the previously
mentioned sections fall out of that scope. Moreover, some of the requirements were inspired by these
papers [18, 12, 16].
SD Separation of Duty: Separation of duty is a principle that ensures different roles and respon-
sibilities are assigned to different individuals or entities involved in the electronic voting process.
It aims to prevent any single person or entity from having complete control or authority over
critical operations, reducing the risk of fraud or manipulation.
E Eligibility: Eligibility focuses on verifying the eligibility of individuals to participate in the
electronic voting process. It involves authenticating and validating the identity and qualifications
of voters to ensure that only eligible individuals are allowed to cast their votes.
An Anonymity: Anonymity protects the privacy and identity of voters. It ensures that individual
votes cannot be traced back to specific voters, maintaining the confidentiality of their choices.
Anonymity safeguards against coercion, vote buying, or any form of undue influence on voters.
VP Voter-Privacy: Voter-privacy involves protecting the privacy and confidentiality of individual
votes. It ensures that the content of a voter’s choice remains confidential and inaccessible to
unauthorized entities. Voter-privacy is maintained through encryption and secure transmission
protocols, preventing tampering or unauthorized access.
Ro Robustness: Robustness refers to the ability of the electronic voting system to withstand and
recover from disruptions or attacks. A robust system is designed to handle unexpected events,
technical failures, or attempts to compromise the voting process, ensuring the integrity and
continuity of the system’s operations.
VI Verifiability (Individual): Verifiability (Individual) allows each voter to verify that their vote
has been accurately recorded and counted. It provides a means for voters to confirm that their
choices are reflected correctly in the system, usually through a receipt, confirmation message, or
other mechanisms.
VU Verifiability (Universal): Verifiability (Universal) extends individual verifiability to enable
any interested party to verify the correctness of the overall election results. It allows external
entities to independently confirm that the final tally represents the aggregation of all individual
votes, providing transparency and trust in the electoral process.

Methodology discussion
This subsection is a discussion about the methodology section. The methodology section is based on
other studies and it is the foundation of this SLR, therefore it is important to highlight the implications
of this section by discussing the final protocol and its components.
The methodology consists of many different components, which define the process of the SLR to
ensure that the outcome is replicable, but also ensure that the work can be reviewed. However, the
main goal of this SLR is to explain the current state of the voting system and how the current solutions
fulfil the requirements of the different domains.
There can be many risks when conducting an SLR, and most of them can be mitigated in the method-
ology section. This is because the methods that are used can be a factor in increasing the risk or
decreasing the risk. Some of these risks include scope creep where the problem could be that the scope
either becomes too large or too narrow. This risk is highly dependent on the search queries since they
determine the number of studies that the SLR is based on. This risk can be mitigated by changing
the search query such that it includes or excludes more domains. Moreover, the set of IC and EC can
also be modified to include or remove more studies from the final set of studies.

Inclusion- and exclusion-criteria
The inclusion and exclusion criteria were established earlier, and this subsection will be removing the
papers which do not live up to the criteria. This step was split up into multiple steps to account for
the different criteria, that were established earlier.
The first step was to remove the duplicate papers. This was done using JabRef, which has a fea-
ture that can find duplicate papers. However, it was able to catch all of the duplicate papers, therefore
a manual search for the rest of the duplicate papers was also done to find the last duplicates. After
removing duplicates there were a total of 350 papers. This implies that there were a total of 189 dupli-
cate papers, where 111 were found by JabRef and 78 were found manually. The duplicate entries were
not removed instead they were merged with the other entries that were similar. When JabRef merges
entries it gives the user a choice of which parts to keep and which parts to remove. The database source
was merged such that each entry would have both database sources saved. This is because the entries
would have the exact same study and there was no objective reason to choose one database over another.
The next step was to remove papers based on the other criteria. This is because all of the other

4.6
Snowballing
4.6
Snowballing
Snowballing is a method of finding studies through existing papers, by looking at references. This can
either be forward snowballing if the studies reference the found paper, whereas backward snowballing
is looking at the reference list of the found paper. Only backward snowballing will be considered for
this SLR. Moreover, only papers that are deemed relevant will be included for the next step, there
were 95 papers which were included by this method. The studies can be seen in the next section.
5
Requirements fulfilled by solutions
The purpose of this section is to present the results, of the research which is each solution and the
requirements that they fulfil. Moreover, patterns of the results will be explored, and potential expla-
nations will be presented. An important detail is also the approach each solution has taken to fulfil
each requirement, which will be included to help understand the patterns of the results.
5.1
Results
The results are gathered by reading each of the studies and looking for each of the requirements. How-
ever, they should not just be mentioned in the studies, because the context matters. In some cases, the
studies mention these requirements, as future work, or as part of the work of other studies. Moreover,
synonyms of the requirements should be kept in mind when reading through the studies, this is in case
the original requirement was not mentioned. Then there were looked for protocols that would fulfil
the requirement if neither the original requirement nor a synonym was mentioned. The papers were
scanned over and the parts that were relevant were closely read through.
The research papers in this study were assessed using two symbols, a checkmark(✓) and a delta
symbol(∆).
A checkmark was assigned to papers that explicitly addressed a specific requirement,
where the requirement was mentioned in the context of their proposed solution. Furthermore, papers
that did not mention the requirement directly, but instead referenced a protocol that partially fulfilled
the requirement, were assigned a delta symbol to denote partial fulfilment. On the other hand, pa-
pers that merely mentioned the requirement without providing a contextual solution did not receive a
checkmark or the delta symbol.
The fulfilment rate of each of the requirements can be seen in Table 11.
5.1.1
Sub ID and snowballing
The snowballed studies come from primary studies. However, not all of the studies that are included
in the references will be snowballed and included in the next step. Moreover, the solutions that are
extracted by snowballing mainly come from papers which evaluate these solutions. As seen in Table 10,
the entries which have a sub-ID are solutions that are extracted from another study. The first entry
above these sub-studies which have a main ID is the paper which they were extracted from.
5.1.2
explanation of results
The results can be seen in Table 10. From looking at the results it is possible to spot that some of the
requirements are fulfilled by almost all of the solutions, whereas other requirements are only fulfilled by
a couple of solutions. This subsection will dive further into each of the requirements and the patterns
that were discovered when reading the solutions and their different approaches.

Seperation of Duty
Most of the studies did not include any kind of separation of duty, but few studies did describe a pro-
tocol that would be classified as separation of duty. There can be two reasons for the lack of fulfilment
of this requirement.
The first reason might be that most of the authors that use blockchain technology accept that
blockchain provides separation of duty on its own. Although blockchain does contribute to the sep-
aration of duty, it does not guarantee it. Because separation of duty is when the responsibility is
distributed amongst multiple authorities. Blockchain can distribute the ledger and the mining process,
but it does not distribute the administrative/controlling responsibility.
The other reason might be that the separation of duty should not be handled by the software, but
rather by the implementer. The reason for this is that it is possible to design the solution to pos-
sess the possibility to include more administrative authorities, but it would still depend on the final
implementer. Because the implementer could just have one authority control all of the duties that
the software has created. Then it will rely on the voter trusting that the implementing authority will
include other authorities which won’t collude and abuse their powers.
Ultimately this requirement was still kept as part of the requirements since it does need to be part
of the design phase of the solution to be possible in the end. If the solution does not allow the im-
plementer to separate the responsibilities then it will be difficult for the implementer to separate the
duties.
Eligibility
Nearly all of the studies fulfilled the requirement of eligibility, although not all studies included the
word eligibility. This requirement is not uncommon since it can be found on most systems that have
to identify their users.
Some of the studies were about a specific part/requirement of a bigger whole solution, therefore they
did not include the part that would fulfil this requirement.
Anonymity
Anonymity has a high rate of fulfilment, most of the solutions fulfilled this requirement. Some of the
solutions did use this term interchangeably with privacy, although the two requirements may seem
similar they still do have their differences.
Therefore, reading the definition from the studies was
important whenever anonymity or privacy was mentioned.
Voter-Privacy
Voter privacy is often fulfilled along with anonymity, however, it is not always the case sometimes it
is fulfilled on its own other times it is not. This requirement had the same issue as anonymity that it
would be used interchangeably, therefore context about the requirement would be read more closely
when this requirement was encountered.
Robustness
Robustness is not a requirement which can easily be fulfilled, because it involves the whole system.
Therefore, the whole solution needs to be assessed if it is robust or not. However, if there is one part

which is not robust, then the whole solution is vulnerable.
Some of the studies claim that their solutions are robust because they use blockchain, which is partially
true. Whilst blockchain certainly does help in making the solutions more robust, it does not guarantee
it. Blockchain mainly ensures robustness for the storage, more specifically it guarantees data integrity
and resistance to tampering. However, it does not guarantee it for the rest of the solution, which
includes encryption, underlying infrastructure, authentication, etc.
Verifiability (Individual & Universal)
Individual verifiability is a requirement which can help fulfil universal verifiability. Most of the studies
did use the term verifiability, which could represent either individual or universal verifiability, therefore
a closer reading was necessary.
Some studies mention that solutions are verifiable just by using blockchain, which can be true. How-
ever, if a blockchain uses permission participation then it is most likely not transparent to the public

5.2
Blockchain in e-voting
Blockchain can be used for electronic voting, which can have its advantages since it can provide sup-
port to fulfil some of the requirements. However, there are a lot of things to consider when using it
otherwise it will not help with any of the requirements.
Blockchain can help with transparency, verifiability, and auditability if the ledgers are made public.
Since the ledger consists of an immutable chain of blocks, and each block contains a set of transactions.
Immutability can also help with the integrity of transactions. This also makes it resistant to tampering.
These are some of the benefits that blockchain can provide, however, they still are dependent on the
final design as well as the implementation [200, 201].

5.3.1
Seperation of Duty
Multi-Party Involvement: Involving multiple independent parties in the system can help distribute
responsibilities and prevent any single entity from having excessive control. This can include external
organizations, regulatory bodies, or independent experts who participate in the oversight and verifica-
tion of the e-voting system.
5.3.2
Eligibility
Voter Registration: Voter registration is a process where individuals must register beforehand to
establish their eligibility. This can involve providing relevant identification and qualification criteria
such as age, citizenship, and residency. Voter registration allows for verification and validation of eli-
gibility before granting voting rights.
Identity Verification: This approach can involve verifying government-issued identification doc-
uments, biometric authentication methods, or other trusted identification mechanisms to ensure that
voters are whom they claim to be.
Database Integration: Integrate with relevant government databases, such as voter rolls or na-
tional identification systems, to verify eligibility. Cross-referencing voter information with authori-
tative databases can help authenticate the eligibility of individuals in real-time. This method also
includes verification by biometric data, since it would require that the government integrates an exist-
ing database for reference.
5.3.3
Anonymity
Encryption: Robust encryption techniques can be used to protect the voter’s information and their
votes. Encrypting the votes and associated metadata ensures that individual choices cannot be directly
linked to specific voters, providing a layer of anonymity.
Blind Signatures: Blind signature protocols are used to ensure anonymity. With blind signatures,
a trusted authority signs a message without knowing its contents, thus enabling the separation of a
voter’s identity from their vote.
Mix Networks: It is possible to utilize mix networks to enhance anonymity in e-voting systems.
Mix networks work by shuffling and encrypting messages multiple times before forwarding them, mak-
ing it challenging to trace the origin of any specific vote.
Homomorphic Encryption: Employing homomorphic encryption, which allows computations to
be performed on encrypted data. By using homomorphic encryption, votes can be encrypted and pro-
cessed without revealing their content, providing anonymity while maintaining the ability to aggregate
and compute results.
Randomized Mixing: Applying randomized mixing techniques, where votes are randomly mixed
and re-ordered before counting. This process makes it extremely difficult to associate any specific vote
with a particular voter, preserving anonymity.
Code vote: Code voting refers to a method where voters are issued unique codes or tokens that
they can use to cast their votes. Each voter receives a specific code or token that represents their
voting right or eligibility. These codes or tokens can be used to authenticate and validate the voter’s
Page 49 of 56
==PAGE:54==
5.3
Approach
eligibility during the voting process, ensuring that only eligible voters can participate. Code voting
helps maintain the privacy of voters by separating their identity from their vote choice.
Coin mixing: Coin mixing, also known as coin shuffling or coin tumbling, is a technique used to
enhance the privacy of transactions, including electronic voting transactions. In the context of elec-
tronic voting, coin mixing involves combining multiple votes in such a way that it becomes difficult to
trace the origin of individual votes. By mixing the votes together, the link between the voter and their
specific vote choice is obfuscated, providing increased anonymity and privacy.
Zero knowledge proof: Zero knowledge proofs are cryptographic protocols that allow one party
(the prover) to prove to another party (the verifier) that a particular statement is true without reveal-
ing any information beyond the validity of the statement itself. In the context of electronic voting and
anonymization, zero knowledge proofs can be used to prove the validity of a vote or the eligibility of a
voter without disclosing the actual vote choice or any identifying information. Zero knowledge proofs
ensure privacy and confidentiality while providing verifiability and trust in the voting process.
Secret share: Secret sharing is a technique used to divide sensitive information, such as encryp-
tion keys or passwords, into multiple shares distributed among different entities. In the context of
electronic voting, secret sharing can be employed to distribute the encryption keys or other sensitive
information necessary for secure and private voting. By dividing the secret among multiple entities, no
single entity can access the sensitive information alone, thus enhancing security and protecting voter
privacy.
Ring signature: A ring signature is a digital signature scheme that allows a member of a group
to sign a message on behalf of the group without revealing their identity. In the context of electronic
voting and privacy, ring signatures can be used to provide anonymity to voters. By employing a ring
signature scheme, a voter can sign their vote along with the signatures of other potential signers, mak-
ing it impossible to determine which specific voter’s signature corresponds to the signed vote. This
ensures that individual voters remain anonymous, protecting their privacy.
5.3.4
Voter-Privacy
Voter privacy is closely related to anonymity, therefore all of the methods that are used for anonymity
can also be used to fulfil the voter-privacy requirement.
5.3.5
Robustness
Scalability: Designing the e-voting system to handle a large number of concurrent users and high
voting volumes. This involves optimizing system performance, load balancing, and implementing mea-
sures to scale resources dynamically as needed.
Security Measures: Implementing robust security measures, including firewalls, intrusion detec-
tion systems, and access controls, to protect the e-voting system from external threats.
Establish
incident response protocols to effectively handle security incidents and recover from any breaches.
Load Testing and Performance Optimization: Conducting thorough load testing to simulate
high user volumes and identify potential bottlenecks or performance issues. Performance optimization
techniques, such as caching, query optimization, and resource allocation, can be applied to improve
the system’s responsiveness and robustness under heavy loads.
Page 50 of 56
==PAGE:55==
5.4
State of the art
Penetration Testing and Vulnerability Assessments: Regularly conducting penetration test-
ing and vulnerability assessments to identify and address security weaknesses in the e-voting system.
This helps uncover potential vulnerabilities, assess their impact, and implement necessary security
measures to enhance system robustness.
5.3.6
Verifiability (Individual)
Audit Codes: Assigning unique audit codes or identifiers to each vote that can be used by voters to
verify the presence and accuracy of their vote in the system. These codes can be cross-referenced with
publicly available information to provide verification.
Blockchain Transparency: Leveraging blockchain technology to provide transparent and auditable
records of individual votes. Voters can independently verify the presence and accuracy of their vote
by examining the blockchain’s public ledger.
5.3.7
Verifiability (Universal)
End-to-End Verifiability: Implementing end-to-end verifiability mechanisms that allow interested
parties to verify the entire voting process, from vote casting to tallying. This includes providing trans-
parency at each stage and cryptographic proofs that ensure the integrity and correctness of the final
results.
Blockchain Technology: Leveraging blockchain technology to create a transparent and tamper-
evident public ledger of all votes. Interested parties can independently validate the blockchain data to
verify the accuracy of the voting process and ensure universal verifiability.
5.4
State of the art
For this section, the state of the art will be a solution which is one of the newest that is in use today
as of writing this. The chosen solution is used today by the Estonian government, this is the second
version of the solution that they are using [155]. The paper Heinl et al. [8] has conducted an in-depth
review of the solution that Estonia uses. The paper by Heinl et al. assumes that the attackers are
able to achieve different kinds of attacks, where some of these attacks include, manipulating messages
on the communication channels, inserting ballots, controlling user devices, etc. Heinl et al. then goes
to setup four levels of attack one level for each of the order of elections and two for the first order of
elections. Both of the first-order election attacks conducted by Heinl et al. have the result that the
solution used by Estonia does not provide secrecy in any form. This is problematic since it is a solution
which is implemented and in use by the Estonian government. However, none of the other solutions
listed by Heinl et al. fulfils secrecy at the first-order election attacks.
The results from the survey paper Heinl et al. imply that there is a need for a solution that can
withstand the attacks which are set up in the paper.

Robustness: The first requirement is robustness since it is not covered by many solutions, even

systems more robust, but instead to create a method that can verify if a system is robust with the
other requirements in mind.
Separation of Duty: This requirement is ranked lower than robustness even though fewer solu-

This is because this requirement requires that the final implementer
implements the system in the same way it was intended by the designer of the system. This is because

that this requirement can be neglected by the final implementer, even if the solution is designed to
fulfil this requirement.
Verifiability Individual: This requirement is implemented by about half of the solutions that are
covered in this SLR, which implies that there are existing approaches which can fulfil this requirement.

impossible for the individual to verify their ballot has been correctly cast.
Verifiability Universal: This requirement has a higher fulfilment than individual verifiability, there-

dividual verifiability. To utilize the approach used in the individual verifiability for this requirement
is by repeating the approach used in individual verifiability for all the users to get universal verifiability.
Anonymity: This requirement does have a high fulfilment rate therefore it is not prioritised very
high. This requirement is dependent on individual verifiability for the same reason that individual
verifiability is dependent on this requirement. That is the conflict between the two requirements which
must be considered when researching these requirements.
Voter-Privacy: This requirement is prioritised lower than anonymity, although this requirement
has about the same fulfilment rate as anonymity. This is because this requirement is also covered in
other topics that are outside of electronic voting since this requirement is about confidentiality, and

7
Discussion
The goal of this SLR was to create a roadmap for electronic voting using a systematic methodology
to look through other papers. The methodology section had to be set up and researched before the
review of the electronic voting papers. There was conducted research of SLR papers and then thor-
oughly explained the methods that will be used in this SLR and set up a protocol which will be used
in the rest of this SLR. The results from the search query were filtered and their quality was ensured.
After that, the results were extracted and the information processed, to create two roadmaps, one for
researchers and one for solution designers.
This SLR covers a topic which has many solutions and reviews however, most of the reviews were
missing transparency which is provided in this paper. This reduces bias and provides a way for others
to conduct this SLR again such that it is updated with the newest papers of the time.
The lack of fulfilment in the separation of duty requirement was an unanticipated piece of infor-
mation. Seeing that it is a requirement which can help increase trust. However, it is understandable
after theorising that on the grounds that the system could be abused by just having the same authority
take all of the duties which the software solution has created.
7.1
Threat of validity
When writing this SLR some risks were identified before starting and in addition to that, there were
mitigation plans put in place to combat these risks. Most of the risks involved the scope. However, at
the time of reading this paper, those risks will be irrelevant.
An interesting risk which was also identified early on is the risk of bias. This is because it is dif-
ficult not to include some subjective information. This is an issue which has to be mitigated to ensure
that it will not impact the final results of this SLR. The mitigation plans specified that all claims
should be supported by sources or data. This was something that was considered throughout the
writing process. Another way to mitigate bias was to make sure that the process is as transparent
as possible. This will ensure that the reader is able to check if the process is done correctly and in
an objective manner. This is also a benefit since each reader might have a different level of tolerance
towards bias.
The last risk that is interesting and relevant to mention is that there might be papers which were
not included in this SLR. Although this is unlikely, it is still a risk which can happen. The problem is
that if a paper is missing and have not been covered, then that paper might contain a solution which
can have a large impact on the result of this SLR. This is something that would make the results of

7.2
Future work
Many issues surfaced while writing this SLR. These issues are mainly a lack of information or a lack of
unified agreement on some of the information, on certain topics. These topics were essential to creating
this SLR. Therefore, this subsection is dedicated to ideas of what the next paper could be about.
The requirements for electronic voting are not universally agreed on.
This does make sense since
many countries each have their own sets of rules. However, that is not the issue, the main issue is that
there is not a collection of all possible requirements for electronic voting, which could possibly rank
the requirement. Moreover, there is no distinction if a given requirement should be ensured by the
software or if it is the authority that has to ensure it, or if it is a shared responsibility. Therefore, it
would be a good idea to write about the different requirements, their definition, possible ranking them,
and most importantly addressing the responsible entity for implementing the requirement, because it
is not always the solution designer that has to do that.
Information about how to write an SLR was mostly provided by medical papers, which is not a
problem for creating a general setup. However, when diving deeper into SLR, it was understood that
not all of the methods were useful in all fields of science. Therefore, it would be a good idea to create
a list of methodologies which can be used for a SLR in the context of computer science, and then let
the reader pick the methodologies that work best for their case. Moreover, each methodology would
have to be thoroughly explained to the reader, such that there is no information missing.
8
Conclusion
The goal of this SLR was to create a roadmap, however, throughout the journey to answer that ques-
tion multiple other questions were also answered. This also includes the requirements for electronic
voting in government, and how other less security-demanding domains compare. It was then discovered
that the other domains could be dismissed since their security requirements would be a subset of the
government’s requirement (first-order elections).
Then the SLR methodology was set up, which is used to ensure that anyone can verify the work
of this SLR. This section created protocols which contained components that were then later used to
achieve the result presented in this paper. This consists of several steps which had to be thoroughly
explained to provide the ability to ensure that this SLR is verifiable.
In the end, the results were extracted from the papers, and the final product was a table that showed
the coverage and fulfilment of the different requirements, and the roadmaps. These two roadmaps will
help any individual trying to design a solution or create one himself, and researchers who are looking
for topics that can be researched. The roadmaps were then compared to see the similarities and differ-
ences. The final roadmaps were constructed with a certain priority in mind, where the priority differs
for each of the roadmaps.
