Abstract
Industrial biotechnology comprises the manufacturing of bulk chemicals and high-
value end-products from renewable feedstocks, thus it presents a valuable aspect
in the present transition from traditional, resource demanding manufacturing to
sustainable solutions. The non-conventional yeast Debaryomyces hansenii encom-
passes halotolerant characteristics that ensures its industrial applications, and hence,
its industrial importance. To enable industrial applications, a comprehensive and
holistic understanding of its behaviour and response to abiotic stresses is essential.
Through high-throughput screening methods using advanced robotics and automa-
tion devices, the present study enlightens intraspecies behavioural characteristics of
novel Debaryomyces hansenii strains’ response to Na+ and their ability to tolerate
abiotic stress in semi-controlled micro-bioreactors, and spot-test studies. A signiﬁ-
cantly improved performance under environmental conditions with abiotic stresses
was observed with the presence of Na+. A summative eﬀect of acidic environments
and high sodium content was revealed found on ﬁtness and stability of the strains.
Our discoveries align with previous ﬁndings, while inferring the halophilic behaviour
of Debaryomyces hansenii to include all strains in the study. Strain-speciﬁc diﬀer-
ential responses to the presence of sodium was realised, with some strains exerting
a more notable induction by sodium than the model strain (CBS767). Additionally,
the study provides indications of the use of Debaryomyces hansenii in industrial
bioprocesses that allows for use of lignocellulosic biomass and non-lignocellulosic in
feedstocks.

Resumé
Industriel bioteknologi gør det muligt at fremstille bulkkemikalier og højværdipro-
dukter fra vedvarende råvarer, og derfor er teknologien særligt værdifuld i overgangen
fra traditionel, ressourcekrævende produktion til bæredygtige løsninger. Den ikke-
konventionelle gær Debaryomyces hansenii er karakteriseret ved halotolerante egen-
skaber, der sikrer dens industrielle applikationer og dermed dens industrielle betyd-
ning. For at muliggøre industrielle applikationer er det afgørende at have en omfat-
tende holistisk forståelse af dens adfærd og reaktion på tilstedeværelsen af abiotiske
komponenter.
Gennem screeningsmetoder med høj kapacitet, avancerede robot-
teknologier og automatiseringsenheder belyser denne undersøgelse adfærdsmæssige
egenskaber ved Debaryomyces hansenii stammers reaktion på Na+ og deres evne til
at tolerere abiotiske komponenter i semi-kontrollerede mikrobioreaktorer og spot-
test undersøgelser. En forbedret ydeevne under miljøer med abiotiske komponenter
blev observeret ved tilstedeværelsen af Na+. En summativ eﬀekt af lav pH om-
givelser og højt natriumindhold blev ligeledes identiﬁceret på stammernes ﬁtness og
stabilitet. Undersøgelsens fund stemmer overens med den allerede etablerede viden,
der er om gæren, samtidig med at undersøgelsen udvider den haloﬁle opførsel af De-
baryomyces hansenii til at inkludere alle stammer, der er inkluderet i undersøgelsen.
Indikationer på diﬀerentielle stammespeciﬁkke reaktioner på tilstedeværelsen af na-
trium blev fundet. Nogle stammer udviste et tydeligere respons på tilstedeværelsen
af natrium end modelstammen (CBS767). Dertil blev der fundet indikationer af,
at Debaryomyces hansenii-baserede industrielle bioprocesser giver mulighed for at
inkorporere lignocelluløs biomasse og ikke-lignocellulose råvarer.

1
Introduction
Industrial biotechnology comprises the ability to manufacture bulk chemicals and
high-value products from renewable feedstocks. Rapid increases in innovative in-
dustrial biotech tools have reduced cost of production, thus creating competitive
advantages for biomass-derived products and making them commercially successful
[Li et al., 2014]. However, a number of hurdles still need to be addressed to fully re-
alise and obtain the full potential of industrial bioprocessing [Erickson et al., 2012].
Enabling high yield and reduced manufacturing maintenance, including reduced
sterilisation, remains a current obstacle in manufacturing biotechnological products
and it reﬂects the often costly processes. Deﬁciency of cost-eﬀective solutions decel-
erate the diversion towards a bio-based economy, thus continued research in host-
and process optimum is needed to maximise commercial success and competitive
advantages [Li et al., 2014]. Saline environments permit reduced sterilisation costs
due to their natural barrier toward microbial contaminants, for which the high salin-
ity is toxic. Hence, using saline environments in industrial bioporocesses oﬀer the
potential of minimising operational costs [Navarrete et al., 2020b].
The hemiascomycetous non-conventional yeast Debaryomyces hansenii (D. hansenii)
encompasses physiological properties that have generated an increasing interest in
the yeast [Prista et al., 2016]. Its unique and prominent halophilic and halotolerant
characteristics ensure its industrial applications, and hence, its industrial importance
[Chao et al., 2009]. It has proven to be able to tolerate levels up to 4.11M of sodium
[Chao et al., 2009]. However, growth inhibitions have been observed at concentra-
tions exceeding 2M NaCl [Navarrete et al., 2020a, Capusoni et al., 2019]. Addition-
ally, when D. hansenii has been grown in conditions containing 1M NaCl, protective
and non-detrimental eﬀects have been observed. Presence of stress factors, includ-
ing extreme pH conditions, oxidative stress or abnormal conditioning temperatures,
have proven to have a less detrimental eﬀect when added in co-existence with NaCl
[Prista et al., 2016]. At high temperatures with severe growth inhibitions, presence
1
==PAGE:12==
of salt has appeared to stimulate growth. Additionally, stress of extreme pH has
shown to be relieved by presence of 0.25M NaCl [Almagro et al., 2000].
Similar
results were demonstrated in studies where the observed behaviour in high salin-
ity conditions are explained as a dominant survival approach [Butinar et al., 2011,
Navarrete et al., 2020a].
Furthermore, D. hansenii is able to use a broad spectrum of carbon substrates, gen-
erally catabolised through respiratory metabolism [Nobre et al., 1999, Palma et al., 2007].
Industrial substrates often contain a variety of lignocellulosic biomass and non-
lignocellulosic feedstocks, which are composed of mixtures of hexoses and pentoses
[Navarrete et al., 2020b]. Hence, its ability to assimilate a broad spectrum also en-
courage further research in its application in industrial bioprocesses [Prista et al., 2016].
1.1
Literature Review
In a recent review by Prista et al.
(2016) the prospects of D. hansenii in food
and biotechnological applications, and how the yeast contains aspects fundamental
for application and success in industrial bioprocesses was enlightened. The review
illustrates the yeasts’ importance in basic and applied research illustrated, while urg-
ing for a holistic understanding of its behaviour and underlying molecular factors.
Concurrently, they appeal for studies of wild D. hansenii-strains isolated from nat-
ural habitats, to increase understanding of the yeast, while enabling identiﬁcation
of novel capabilities with biotechnological importance [Prista et al., 2016]. A study
conducted by Navarrete et al. (2020) demonstrated a beneﬁcial role of salt, with
sodium exerting a more signiﬁcant eﬀect, on cell performance of D. hansenii, validat-
ing the role of salt in its survival strategy and establishing its halophilic behaviour.
Simultaneously, the results sustained a summative eﬀect of acidic environments and
high salt content to have a positive inﬂuence on cell growth [Navarrete et al., 2020a].
1.2
Project Aim
This study aims to establish intraspecies behavioural characterisation of novel D.
hansenii strains through high-throughput screening methods using advanced robotics
and automation devices. By experimentally simulating the process control of batch

2.1
Halophilic and Halotolerant Characteristics
and High Gene Density of Debaryomyces
hansenii
Presence of excess salt in environments causes growth inhibitions of the major-
ity of fungal species and thus limits expansion and production.
Yet, some fun-
gal species exhibit highly salt-tolerant behaviours, enabling both survival and ad-
vancement in salty environments. These halophile and halotolerant species are able
to manage and escape ion toxicity and uphold water uptake, and hence maintain
viability [Prista et al., 2016]. Cellular adaptation to saline environments includes
control of ion movements and intracellular accumulation of osmolytes and polyols
[Ramos et al., 2004, Prista et al., 2016].
Despite a deﬁcit of a complete understanding of the mechanisms underlying the
halotolerant behaviour of D. hansenii, several salt-related genes have been identi-
ﬁed in D. hansenii. The accumulation of Na+ in the vacuole as an osmotic stress
response under high saline conditions and its link to the characteristic behaviour
of the strain [Montiel and Ramos, 2007, Navarrete et al., 2020b], has been related
to the gene annotated DhNhx1, a vacular sodium (potassium)/proton antiporter.
Hence, DhNhx1 has proven to be an important component for ion homeostasis in D.
hansenii and detoxiﬁcation of detrimental concentrations of sodium by sequestra-
tion of Na+ in the vacuole [Capusoni et al., 2019]. These ﬁndings are supported by
the behaviour of a Saccharomyces cerevisiae ena1-4∆nha1∆nhx1 mutant which has
proven able to partly recover its cation tolerance when transformed with DhNhx1.
Moreover, it demonstrated its ability to accumulate Na+ and K+ in the vacuole
[Montiel and Ramos, 2007].
DhKha1, a potassium/proton antiporter, too assists
in determining the cytosolic concentration of Na+ by sequestering Na+ in the Golgi
apparatus [Carcía-Salcedo et al., 2007, Navarrete et al., 2020b]. A summary of iden-

Production and accumulation of polyols, including glycerol, as response to osmotic
stress have been identiﬁed in D. hansenii, too [Reed et al., 1987]. Adaption to hyper-
osmotic stress has been linked to activation of components of the high-osmolarity
glycerol (HOG) pathway, which too targets genes (DhGPD1 and DhGPP2) cod-
ing for essential enzymes responsible for synthesis of glycerol [Prista et al., 2016,
Reed et al., 1987].
Among yeasts, D. hansenii reportedly has the highest coding capacity, measuring
79.2% of the genome, with an overall gene redundancy of 50% and 6906 detected
coding sequences [Souciet et al., 2009]. A large share of overrepresented genes are
involved in membrane function, signifying the importance of the membrane function
when compared to S. cerevisiae [Prista et al., 2016]. Notably, an eight to one ratio
of copies of a glycerol proton symporter (DhSTL1) has been identiﬁed in D. hansenii
to S. cerevisiae, emphasising the importance of glycerol transport in upholding os-
motolerance [Palma et al., 2007].
2.2
Oxidative Stress Sensitivity
D. hansenii has proven capable of both respiration and fermentation
[Aggarwal and Mondal, 2009]. However, a recent study by Navarrete et al. (2020)
demonstrated respiratory quotient values to remain below or equal to one, support-
ing ﬁndings of its favour of a respiratory process [Chao et al., 2009]. Thus, the yeast
2.2
5
==PAGE:16==
has been found to have a high respiratory and low fermentative activity. No pro-
duction of ethanol was detected under aerobic conditions, hence characterising the
yeast as crabtree negative [Aggarwal and Mondal, 2009, Navarrete et al., 2020a].
Aerobic organisms require oxygen for optimal generation of energy, while simul-
taneously constantly being exposed to oxidative stress caused by imbalance between
reactive oxygen species (ROS), a by-product of respiration, and its capability to read-
ily detoxify the reactive intermediates. ROS are highly mutagenic due to their ability
to damage cellular constituents, including DNA, RNA, and proteins. To maintain
cellular redox state and cellular constituents, several enzymatic and non-enzymatic
defence systems have evolved in aerobic organisms [Aggarwal and Mondal, 2009].
At increased levels of abiotic- and biotic factors, accumulation of ROS is increased
due to insuﬃcient defensive scavenging systems [Ramos-Moreno et al., 2019].
Under saline conditions, gene DhAhp1, which coded for a cytosolic alkyl hydroper-
oxide reductase, and its corresponding protein is regulated, contributing to its cor-
relation with salt resistance [Chao et al., 2009]. Overexpressing transformants of
DhAhp1 in both S. cerevisiae and D. hansenii exhibited decreased accumulation
of ROS under salt-induced stress, while a silenced DhAhp1 mutant of D. hansenii
resulted in lowered salt tolerance. This indicates DhAhp1 to serve as a chaperone
under salt induced stress by scavenging ROS and mediating oxidative stres signalling
[Chao et al., 2009, Prista et al., 2016].
2.3
Application of Debaryomyces hansenii in
Industrial Bioprocesses
Inadequacy of cost-eﬀective solutions decelerate the diversion towards a bio-based
economy, thus signifying the need for proﬁtable, stable solutions with potential to
accomplish market penetration [Singh, 2011]. The halotolerant and halophilic be-
haviour of D. hansenii enables the fermentation to be supplied with saline water,
as substitution for freshwater. Freshwater scarcity has increased prices, and conse-
quently increased production cost of industrial bioprocesses. Hence, switching to a
6

halophilic host permits a decrease in production cost and an alternative to saline wa-
ter. Additionally, signiﬁcantly decreased risks of contamination are achieved under
high salinity cultivation [Navarrete et al., 2020b]. Secondly, D. hansenii has poten-
tial for conduction of open fermentation processes with seawater, which bypasses the
need for intensive sterilisation processes, and hence decreases energy consumption,
while enhancing the competitiveness of the bioprocess industry [Li et al., 2014].
2.3.1
Broad Substrate Utilisation
D. hansenii is able to utilise a broad spectrum of carbon substrates, generally
catabolised through respiratory metabolism [Nobre et al., 1999, Palma et al., 2007].
Gene orthologues to S. cerevisiae have been identiﬁed in D. hansenii. Findings of
over-representation of orthologues involved in transport facilitation in D. hansenii
substantiate the phenotypic observations of its ability to respire a broad range of
substrates [Lépingle et al., 2000, Nakase et al., 1998]. While D. hansenii is able to
catabolise both hexoses and pentoses, slower and weaker growth has been observed
when assimilating pentoses [Nobre et al., 1999, Palma et al., 2007, Pereira et al., 2014].
Table 2.2 contains an overview of growth on a selection of carbon substrates
[Nakase et al., 1998].
D. hansenii can be cultivated in high concentrations of a broad variety of sub-
strates, implying its high chemo-stress tolerance [Breuer and Harms, 2006]. Com-
bined with its prominent ability to consume a wide range of carbon substrates, its
signiﬁcant ﬂexibility in feedstock utilisation is proven [Nobre et al., 1999]. To en-
hance competitiveness of bioprocess industries, waste-derived feedstocks are a poten-
tial driver for decreased operational costs [Li et al., 2014, Navarrete et al., 2020b].
While abundance and dependency are important considerations for waste-derived
feedstock, pretreatment requirements and conditions often determines the ability to
achieve successful implementation, due to the consequential impact on both process
cost and production of unwanted, inhibiting by-products [Navarrete et al., 2020b,
Singh, 2011].

2.3.2
Inhibitory Compounds of Conventional Feedstocks
Generally, conventional biomass is diﬀerentiated into two sub-groups: lignocellulosic
biomass and non-lignocellulosic feedstocks, wherein algal biomass and crude glycerol
belong [Li et al., 2014, Li et al., 2017].
Crude glycerol, a low value by-product, primarily obtained from biodiesel pro-
duction, contains salts, alcohols, fatty acids, and high concentrations of glycerol.
The compounds and concentrations thereof are known to interfere fermentation
processes and cause growth inhibition of some cell factory hosts [Li et al., 2014,
Navarrete et al., 2020b]. Main components of algal biomass are carbohydrates, pro-
teins, lipids, and salts, with galactose being the main carbon source. Hence, hosts
with halotolerant or halophilic characteristics are crucial for obtaining high produc-
tivity from cultivation on algal biomass [Li et al., 2017].
Lignocellulosic biomass is composed of a complex structure of cellulose, hemicel-
lulose and lignin. Thus, they require pretreatment to allow and improve an accessi-
ble sugar yield for subsequent conversion and production [Navarrete et al., 2020b].
8
2.3
==PAGE:19==
Pretreatment intent at increasing susceptibility towards enzymatic hydrolysis by
altering the structure of lignocellulosic biomass. Harsh condition are often used,
resulting in release of several inhibitory by-products eﬀecting microbial metabolism
in bioprocesses [Ask et al., 2013]. These inhibitory compounds include furan deriva-
tives, including furfural and 5-hydroxy-methyl furfural (HMF); phenolic compounds,
such as vanillin, and small organic acids. Vanillin is known to inhibit the activity of
DNA-dependent protein kinase catalytic subunit, required for non-homologous end
joining of DNA repair, while HMF and furfural have been found to severely aﬀect
the redox metabolism in S. cerevisiae [Ask et al., 2013, Li et al., 2017].
2.4
Current Limitations for Reaching the Full
Potential of Debaryomyces hansenii in
Industrial Bioprocesses
Absence of identiﬁcation and evolvement of eﬃcient and stable molecular tools
in D. hansenii, decelerate the application of the yeast in industrial bioprocesses
[Prista et al., 2016]. Genes accountable for the halophilic behaviour and osmotoler-
ance have been classiﬁed through heterologous expression in S. cerevisiae
[Almagro et al., 2001, Prista et al., 2005]. In D. hansenii, ambiguous translation of
CUG codon occurs.
Thus, heterologous expression in S. cerevisiae presents ob-
vious limitations, as D. hansenii translates the CUG codon primarily as serine
and not leucine [Massey et al., 2003].
Development of a genetic toolbox for D.
hansenii is hence crucial for reaching the full potential in industrial bioprocesses
[Prista et al., 2016].
2.4.1
Genetic Instability and Product Toxicity
Enabling the ability to engineer stable synthetic constructs is crucial to obtaining
competing productivity and yields of a D. hansenii based cell factory [Singh, 2011].
Furthermore, stable synthetic constructs are crucial for the viability of the produc-
tivity in large-scale production processes [Hewitt and Nienow, 2007]. Biomass yield
of S. cerevisiae increased by 7% when the vessel volume was decreased from 120
m3 to 10 liter [Hewitt and Nienow, 2007, Nevoigt, 2008]. Hence, microbial insta-

implementation of large-scale bioprocesses due to unsuccessful maintenance of sta-

Additionally, the choice of expres-

Overexpression of genes can lead

Experimental Strategy
Behavioural characteristics and assessment of the potential of the non-conventional
yeast, D. hansenii, in industrial bioprocesses with waste-derived feedstock and salt-
water supply, was enabled by analysis of carbon assimilation and inhibitor inﬂuence.
In addition, the importance and eﬀect of sodium on D. hansenii will be evaluated
through parallel micro bioprocesses.
3.1
Strains and Culture Conditions
To be able to infer reliable behavioural characteristics observed within the strains of
D. hansenii, the library was constructed from the total of D. hansenii strains from
the Institut for Bioteknologi (IBT) culture collection of fungi from the Technical
University of Denmark, Denmark (n=60). Strains from the collection have been
isolated from natural habitats. Detailed information concerning origin of the strains
can be found in appendix A. The strains from the culture collection have not been
sequenced, hence, it cannot be excluded that some strains are isolates of the same
strain. The model D. hansenii strain CBS767 (PYCC2968) was included in the
study too.
Several studies have reported clear diﬀerential eﬀect exerted by sodium or potassium
on the osmotolerance and halotolerance of D. hansenii, with sodium exerting a more
signiﬁcant eﬀect on growth and beneﬁcial role in harsh conditions [Navarrete et al., 2020a,
Martínez et al., 2011]. Hence, only sodium was included in the study.
3.2
Spot-tests
Pretreatment of lignocellulosic biomass yields products which can inhibit the follow-
ing bioprocess. The three major groups of inhibitors formed are furan derivatives
(furfural, HMF), phenols, and organic acids. Furfural has exerted inhibition on S.
cerevisiae at concentrations ranging from 0.5-2 g/L, while negative eﬀects on growth
rate has been reported on concentration < 1.0 g/L of HMF [Flores-Cosio et al., 2018].
11
==PAGE:22==
Vanillin concentrations ranging from 1-26 mM can be yield from lignocellulosic
biomass [Ito et al., 2020].
A considerable variation of the chemical composition of crude glycerol has been
reported. Methanol content has been found in concentrations ranging from 0.027-
14% [Hansen et al., 2009].
To have consistency and enable comparisons to other studies, inﬂuence of analogous
concentrations of inhibitors were investigated in this study. Presence of colonies and
diameter of colonies were used as indicator of carbon utilisation and stress tolerance.
3.3
Micro Scale Setups
To allow both economical and behavioural high throughput, while screening a range
of parameters in reasonable short time, a selection of strains were chosen for fur-
ther analysis in micro scale setups. To ensure detection of the variance at a 0.05
signiﬁcance level, 20 strains were included. Strains were selected based on their (1)
ability to utilise a broad spectrum of carbon sources, (2) substratum from which
they were isolated, (3) ability to endure inhibitors, (4) inhibition by supplemented
inhibitors. Furthermore, the selection criterias were established to reduce likelihood
of any bias caused by selecting isolates of the same strain, and not an original strain.
To achieve behavioural high throughput, parallelisation of serial parameters and
conditions in micro scale setups with high capacity were conducted. This experi-
mental setup allowed for statistical power, thus enabling establishment of tendencies
within the strains of D. hansenii. Fitness development and local growth rates were
used as indicators of growth development and stability.

Discussion
The global societal transition from chemical, conventional production of bulk- and
high value molecules to sustainable solutions is dependent on innovative bio-based
production. Developing cost-eﬃcient solutions, would assist in obtaining the maxi-
mal potential of industrial biotechnology, and thus, accelerate the transition
[Erickson et al., 2012]. The non-conventional yeast D. hansenii encompasses physi-
ological properties that have generated an increased interest in the yeast as a promis-
ing host in bioprocesses [Prista et al., 2016]. Its unique and prominent halotolerant
characteristics ensure its industrial applications, and hence, its industrial impor-
tance [Prista et al., 2016, Chao et al., 2009].
Debaryomyces hansenii’s Ability to Assimilate a
Broad Spectrum of Carbon Sources
While the salt-resistant, halotolerant behaviour of D. hansenii was established upon
discovery of yeast, and knowledge concerning behavioural characteristics further in-
creased exponentially when it was subjected to genomic exploration by genome
sequencing [Lépingle et al., 2000, Souciet et al., 2009], recent studies have demon-
strated its salt-including and halophilic behaviour [Navarrete et al., 2020a]
[Prista et al., 2016]. Similar realisations are obtained intraspecies from this study.
Medium-high concentration of sodium does not show a detrimental eﬀect on car-
bon assimilation capacity of the included strains in the study. Following the re-
sults of this study (ﬁgure 5.1), D. hansenii’s ability to assimilate a broad spectrum
of carbon sources [Nobre et al., 1999] can be extrapolated to other strains of the
same species. A varying growth was observed in the presence of arabinose and xy-
lose (table B), extending the reported slower and weaker growth when assimilating
pentoses [Nobre et al., 1999, Palma et al., 2007, Pereira et al., 2014] to be present
within strains of D. hansenii. An increase in capacity to assimilate pentoses was
33
==PAGE:44==
observed in the co-presence of 1M sodium, hence substantiating the halophilic be-
havior of D. hansenii and expanding knowledge of the behaviour intraspecies.
A very recent study conducted by Navarrete et al. (2020) demonstrated an increased
growth in the presence of up to 1M NaCl when compared to control conditions (no
salt). Despite decreased resolution due to the applied method in this study, the
observations were conﬁrmed by the high-throughput spot-screenings of carbon as-
similation and eﬀect of sodium presence (ﬁgure 5.1). In this, an improved growth
was observed when cultivated in co-presence with 1M sodium. At increased con-
centrations of sodium, slower growth was observed (ﬁgure 5.2c). However, after 144
hours, similar cell densities of the control were reached, supporting previous results
of an observed non-detrimental and non-inhibitory eﬀects of sodium on the pro-
liferation capacity of D. hansenii [Navarrete et al., 2020b, Prista et al., 2016] and
simultaneously extending the behaviour to be extrapolated intraspecies.
Increased Stability of Debaryomyces hansenii
Strains when Cultivated in Acidic Culture
Conditions
Whereas mutation-prone cells secure adaption to shifting environmental changes
for the evolving population, each underlying mechanism presents a probable threat
to the stability of the introduced synthetic system. Evolving sub-cultures of non-
producers is an increasing issue in long-term cultivation cell factory strains
[Hewitt and Nienow, 2007]. Generally, an improved stability of the library is ob-
served through the evolvement of the bioprocesses when sodium is available in the
culture media, ﬁgure 5.7. Importantly, the stability is more profound when culti-
vated in acidic culture conditions. In addition to a preserved stability in progression,
an initial higher maximum speciﬁc growth rate was observed for strains in acidic
conditions supplemented with sodium (ﬁgure 5.6b). A summative eﬀect of acidic
environments and high salt content has previously shown to have a positive inﬂuence
34
6.0
==PAGE:45==
on cell growth [Almagro et al., 2000]. The eﬀect was further sustained by Navarrete
et al. (2020). The positive summative eﬀect can be inferred within the strains of
D. hansenii based on the results obtained in this study, in which we were able to
show that all included strains amounted to higher maximum speciﬁc growth rates
in acidic environments supplemented with sodium (ﬁgure 5.6b). Subsequently, more
stable local growth rate proﬁles are seen at low pH and sodium concentration, fur-
ther sustaining a positive and protective eﬀect of sodium on the yeast.
Interestingly, the robust strain, IBT27, which showed broad carbon assimilation and
high endurance to supplemented inhibitors, especially when co-supplemented with
1M sodium, reached the highest maximum speciﬁc growth rate in conditions with
low pH and high sodium. Thus, IBT27 outperformed the remaining strains (ﬁgure
5.6b). However, at a pH level of 6 and supplemented with sodium, the maximum
speciﬁc growth rate was lower compared to that with no sodium (ﬁgure 5.6a). Hence,
this align our results with previous observations of improved performance in environ-
ments with abiotic stress, while further sustaining a summative eﬀect of acidic en-
vironments and high sodium content [Almagro et al., 2000, Navarrete et al., 2020a].
Additionally, indications of strain-speciﬁc diﬀerential responses to the presence of
sodium was realised, with some strains exerting a more notable induction by sodium
than the model strain (CBS767) (ﬁgure 5.6b).
Enabling a high level of process monitoring and parallelisation of serial parame-
ters and conditions in micro scale setups allow both economical and behavioural
high throughput. Thus, the setup facilitate screening a range of parameters in rea-
sonable short time while the application signiﬁcantly contributes to understanding
of bioprocesses. In addition, the power analysis indicates that the applied setup
allows for sample sizes large enough for detection of diﬀerential behaviour at a 0.05
signiﬁcance level (appendix E.1). Tracking local growth rates through the evolving
population provides important indications of the stability of the synthetic construct
[Rugbjerg et al., 2018]. Hence, the study provides a framework and similarity study
to be employed in future screenings and identiﬁcation of optimal candidates of D.
hansenii engineered to express a product of interest.

Debaryomyces hansenii’ Response to Stresses
from Lignocellulosic Biomass and
Non-lignocellulosic Feedstocks
Abundant and renewable lignocellulosic biomass has high potential as a substitute
for fossil resources to produce bioprocessed chemicals and materials, while elud-
ing less available and more expensive feedstocks [Li et al., 2017].
However, pre-
treatment of lignocellulosic biomass yields several inhibitory by-products, mak-
ing the use of lignocellulosic biomass as feedstock in some bioprocesses problem-
atic [Navarrete et al., 2020b]. Inhibitory compounds of pretreatment include furan
derivatives, including furfural and HMF and phenolics compounds, such as vanillin
[Ask et al., 2013]. We found sodium to relieve the abiotic stress caused by presence
of furfural and HMF. While the presence of sodium demonstrates a protective ef-
fect at lower concentrations of the furan derivatives, the protective eﬀect grow more
profound at increased concentrations (ﬁgure 5.3 and 5.4). This reinforces previ-
ously proposed results of Na+ not having a toxic eﬀect on D. hansenii, and that
sodium is able to protect the yeast cells against additional abiotic stress factors
[Ramos-Moreno et al., 2019, Navarrete et al., 2020a].
Furan derivatives were found to severely eﬀect the redox metabolism of S. cerevisiae
while less inhibition of energy metabolism was demonstrated [Ask et al., 2013]. A
study conducted by Ramos-Moreno et al. (2019) demonstrated that expression of
genes related to oxidative stress were induced in D. hansenii by high salt con-
centrations (with sodium enabling a better induction than potassium).
Hence
showing a direct link between high salt concentrations and ROS detoxiﬁcation.
Complementary to Ramos-Moreno et al.
(2019), a very recent study conducted
by Navarrete et al.
(2020) demonstrated an increased respirative activity of D.
hansenii when in presence of salts, especially sodium. The ability of sodium to en-
hance the respirative metabolism and induce expression of ROS detoxiﬁcation genes

[Navarrete et al., 2020a, Ramos-Moreno et al., 2019] may explain why the prolifera-
tion capacity of D. hansenii strains is inhibited by furan derivatives when no sodium
is available, but improved when sodium is present.
Contradictory, simultaneous presence of sodium and vanillin, known to inhibit the
activity of DNA-dependent protein kinase catalytic subunit, appears to have an op-
posing eﬀect resulting in hampered cell proliferation (ﬁgure 5.3 and 5.4). However,
depending on the choice of treatment and composition of lignocellulosic biomass, a
vast range of vanillin concentrations can be yielded, ranging from 1-26 mM
[Ito et al., 2020].
Thus, the varying tendency we observed at the highest tested
concentration of vanillin (ﬁgure 5.4) should be further investigated and expanded
to include increased concentrations. In addition, the observed summative eﬀect of
sodium on the cell proliferation in presence of furfural and HMF should be investi-
gated when extended to include both furan derivatives and phenolic compounds.
Equivalently, an improved proliferation capacity is observed at higher concentra-
tion of methanol, ﬁgure 5.4. The indications obtained from the spot inhibitor study
imply a robust behavior of D. hansenii intraspecies, with some strains, like IBT27,
outperforming the average.
The results obtained in this study illuminate behavioural characteristics of novel
D. hansenii strains in semi-controlled micro-bioreactors and spot-test studies, con-
tributing to establishing fundamental knowledge about the non-conventional yeast.
Successful implementation of industrial scale bioprocesses is restricted by limited
access to cost eﬃcient solutions [Li et al., 2014]. From the inhibitor study it was
found that D. hansenii-based industrial bioprocesses would allow for lignocellulosic
biomass and non-lignocellulosic feedstocks, and thus decrease costs while increas-
ing potential of market penetration. Furthermore, culture media and water supply
containing medium high salt concentration could be used due to its halophilic be-
havior, and thus bypass the need for extensive sterilisation processes. The halophilic
behaviour further supports innovative conﬁgurations of bioprocesses, including sea-
water-based and open continuous bioprocesses.

Conclusion
The high level of process monitoring and parallelisation of serial parameters and
conditions in the micro scale setups and spot tests allowed behavioural high through-
put, and, thus, to deﬁne behavioural characteristics of D. hansenii intraspecies. Our
ﬁndings align with previous knowledge of the yeast, while inferring the halophilic
behaviour of D. hansenii to include all strains of the IBT culture collection of fungi
from the Technical University of Denmark, Denmark. An improved performance
in environmental conditions with abiotic stresses was observed with the presence
of Na+. Signiﬁcantly, a positive summative eﬀect of acidic environments and high
sodium content was observed on ﬁtness and stability of the strains.
Simultane-
ously, the ﬁndings support previous reports of observed protective eﬀect of sodium
on growth, and further expand the eﬀect to be observed in presence of inhibitors
yielded from pretreatment of abundant lignocellulosic on non-lignocellulosic feed-
stocks. This signiﬁes its industrial applications, and hence, its industrial importance.
The strains included in this study exerted clear diﬀerential behaviour. Some strains
demonstrated more potent behavioural aspects beneﬁcial for implementation in in-
dustrial bioprocesses than the model strain CBS767. Wide carbon utilisation capa-
bilities and an improved tolerance towards abiotic stress was highly induced by the
presence of sodium in strain IBT27. Hence, an increased understanding of IBT27
should be obtained, as the strain might oﬀer capabilities and possibilities for im-
proved biotechnological processes, not oﬀered by the model strain (CBS767).
This study provides a framework for acquiring economical and behavioural high-
throughput screenings of strains. The framework allows for analysing strain-speciﬁc
diﬀerences, while enabling understanding of intraspecies behavioural tendencies.
The high capacity allowed by the framework, and proven by this current study,
enables our ﬁndings to construct a baseline of behavioural characteristics of D.
hansenii intraspecies. Importantly, while some level of diﬀerentiation was observed

within the strains of D. hansenii, the halophilic behaviour and sodium-induced op-
timal performance of D. hansenii can be inferred for all strains included in the
study.

Perspectives and Future
Experiments
Further research, including elaboration of the eﬀect of potassium on a statistically
signiﬁcant sample size of D. hansenii, should be conducted such that the exerted
eﬀect of potassium can be extrapolated intraspecies. In addition, a higher level of
behavioural resolution should be allowed when investigating inﬂuence of inhibitors,
possibly evaluated through parallel micro bioprocesses. Similarly, a holistic approach
should be applied in studying D. hansenii ability to function with lignocellulosic and
non-lignocellulosic feedstocks, incorporating analysis of sodium’s protective eﬀect,
as indicated in this study.
This study provides a framework for proﬁling local growth rates through the biopro-
cess. While the framework enables stability studies of the bioprocess, it also allows
for studying correlation between growth rates and production [Rugbjerg et al., 2018].
In absence of eﬃcient molecular tools, the full biotechnological potential of D.
hansenii and studies of production proﬁles are still to be obtained and investigated.
Development of capable molecular tools would also allow for better identiﬁcation
of elements responsible for the reaction to presence or absence of salts in the envi-
ronment, and, hence, to exploit and optimise the response from a biotechnological
point of view.

The ANOVA test suggest the variation among the conditions is less than the varia-
tion between the conditions (F-value=77.09, see table E.1). Hence, the likely-hood
that the observed diﬀerences between the condition means reﬂect general diﬀerences
is larger than what would have been expected to observe by chance. Additionally,
a p-value of < 0.05 was observed, causing a rejection of the null hypothesis as a
diﬀerence in means between groups are observed. The Tukey’s Test suggested sig-
niﬁcant diﬀerences (p < 0.05) between the diﬀerent pH levels, regardless of sodium
(table E.2). However, comparison of maximum speciﬁc growth rate of D. hansenii
in pH6 (mean = 0.216 h-1, standard deviation = 0.0767) was not signiﬁcantly diﬀer-
ent of that of pH6 with 1M NaCl (mean = 0.221 h-1, standard deviation = 0.0865).
Similarly, comparison of maximum speciﬁc growth rate in pH4 (mean = 0.0657 h-1,
