Abstract
This study aims to investigate the performance of electrochromic glazing compare with
traditional shading devices and passive cooling methods in terms of thermal comfort, vi-
sual comfort, energy consumption, and CO2 emissions. The study is carried out in col-
laboration with Prince Henrik’s School located in Frederiksberg, Denmark, which recently
installed an electrochromic glazing system supplied by Chromogenics from Sweden, pro-
viding a real-case scenario for the research. Indoor climate data was collected and com-
prehensively analyzed together with simulation results from IDA ICE. The research find-
ings that electrochromic glazing significantly improve indoor thermal and visual comfort,
particularly during peak solar radiation periods, effectively reducing cooling energy con-
sumption. However, its performance varies with different environmental conditions and
usage patterns. Through multi-objective optimization analysis, this study determined the
optimal combination of window sizes, shading device types, and passive cooling strate-
gies such as night ventilation, aiming to minimize CO2 emissions and operational costs
while maximizing daylight availability and occupant comfort. Ultimately, this research pro-
vides practical guidelines for architects and building consultants, facilitating effective se-
lection and implementation of electrochromic glazing and related strategies, thus support-
ing sustainable building practices in compliance with Denmark’s strict building regulations
and energy-saving objectives.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Currently, global climate change and environmental issues are becoming increasingly se-
vere, prompting countries worldwide to actively implement measures aimed at reducing
greenhouse gas emissions and decreasing building energy consumption. The building
sector, as a significant component of global energy usage, accounts for approximately
34% of the total global energy demand [1],resulting in a substantial proportion of CO2
emissions. Therefore, promoting sustainable development practices in the building in-
dustry, particularly enhancing the energy efficiency of building envelopes, has become
an international consensus.
To address this challenge, traditional shading and glass technologies are commonly
adopted in architectural design to enhance energy efficiency and indoor environmental
quality. Among them, common shading methods include external shading devices (such
as awnings and blinds) and internal shading systems (such as roller blinds and curtains).
For instance, the U.S. Department of Energy points out that installing awnings on south-
facing windows in summer can reduce solar heat gain by approximately 65%, and up to
77% for west-facing windows; in addition, in hot climates, external shading devices can
save up to 20% of energy consumption in buildings.[2] Meanwhile, Low-E (low-emissivity)
glass, with its excellent insulation performance, is also widely used. This type of glass re-
flects thermal radiation by coating a metal film on its surface, significantly reducing energy
consumption while maintaining visible light transmission. However, these traditional tech-
nologies usually have fixed or static characteristics and lack the ability to respond in real
time to changes in the external environment. Therefore, their shading or insulation effects
are easily affected by seasonal and climatic fluctuations, limiting their ability to provide a
comfortable environment throughout the year.
Against this backdrop, intelligent shading technologies with dynamic adjustment capa-
bilities are increasingly drawing attention. Electrochromic glass is an intelligent material
that can adjust its transparency and solar radiation transmittance in real time according
to changes in external environmental conditions, and has the potential to reduce cooling
loads while meeting natural lighting requirements. Prince Henriks School, located in Fred-
eriksberg, Denmark, serves as the collaborative partner in this study and has recently in-
stalled an electrochromic glazing system provided by Chromogenics from Sweden. This
innovative dynamic shading technology can adjust its transparency and solar radiation
transmittance in real-time according to external environmental conditions, thus balancing
daylight needs and potentially reducing cooling energy consumption.
However, due to significant geographical and climatic variations among different regions,
the actual performance of electrochromic glazing may vary considerably. Additionally, di-
verse application scenarios impose varying performance requirements on electrochromic
glazing. Therefore, it is essential to conduct an in-depth investigation into the compre-
hensive performance of electrochromic glazing compared to traditional shading methods
and passive cooling strategies under various control conditions, in order to clearly define
its applicable scope and performance advantages.

1.2
Problem statement and motivation
The built environment, especially in educational buildings, plays a critical role in achieving
energy efficiency, indoor comfort, and sustainability goals.In school buildings, indoor en-
vironmental quality (IEQ) is particularly important, as students spend a significant portion
of their day inside classrooms. A comfortable indoor environment not only influences the
physical and mental health of the students but also affects their cognitive performance
and the instructional effectiveness of the teachers.
Previous studies have shown that high-quality daylight conditions can significantly reduce
the incidence of myopia among students. For example, Kim et al. (2022) conducted a
controlled field study showing that children in high-daylight classrooms experienced sig-
nificantly less axial eye growth over a six-month period compared to those in low-daylight
environments, indicating a protective effect against myopia development.[3] Similarly, a
three-year randomized controlled trial by Guo et al.(2024) found that the introduction of
human-centric natural light systems in classrooms substantially reduced the onset of my-
opia in children in primary school.[4]
Beyond visual health, thermal comfort and indoor air quality also have a significant impact
on learning outcomes. Studies show that students tend to prefer slightly cooler indoor ther-
mal conditions, which support sustained attention and improved cognitive performance.
In addition, there is a positive correlation between indoor air quality and student attention
[5].
However, ensuring high levels of thermal and visual comfort often results in increased
energy use and carbon emissions. With growing expectations for indoor environmental
quality and the increasing urgency of climate mitigation, architects have become a signif-
icant challenge to balance thermal comfort, daylight access, energy efficiency, and CO2
emissions in building design. This is particularly complex in the context of school build-
ings, where high occupancy rates and stringent comfort demands must be met within
sustainable operating limits.
1.3
Aim and objectives
In this context, electrochromic glazing has emerged as a promising dynamic facade
solution that offers real-time adaptability to solar radiation while maintaining unob-
structed views, unlike traditional blinds.
Despite its technological maturity and com-
mercial availability, the actual performance of electrochromic glazing in real educational
environments—especially in Nordic climates—remains underexplored.
The primary objective of this study is to clearly identify the performance advantages and
suitable conditions for electrochromic glazing compared to traditional shading devices and
passive cooling strategies, specifically regarding indoor thermal comfort, visual comfort,
energy efficiency, and CO2 emissions. By conducting case studies and simulations based
on a real school project, this research aims to evaluate when and how electrochromic
systems outperform—or underperform—relative to other strategies. Furthermore, this
study seeks to address the multi-objective optimization challenge by identifying optimal
combinations of window sizes, shading systems, and passive cooling techniques, with the
goal of minimizing CO2 emissions while maximizing comfort and daylight utilization.
Based on this context, the core research questions of this study include:
• Does electrochromic glazing demonstrate superior performance regarding indoor
environmental quality and energy efficiency under different climatic conditions and
usage patterns? Under what circumstances might its performance be limited?

2.1
Literature review
Classrooms present a unique challenge in building design: they must provide a comfort-
able learning environment for students while minimizing energy consumption. The high
density of the occupants and the operation during the day mean that thermal comfort,
visual comfort, and energy efficiency are closely linked in classrooms [6].
Thermal comfort influences student health and performance – studies show that when
students are too hot or cold, their cognitive function and motivation decline, leading to
poorer learning outcomes.[7] [8] [9] In the 1970s, Humphreys conducted large-scale field
surveys in schools, residences, and offices across various climate zones, systematically
observing and measuring the “adaptive behaviors” in buildings worldwide for the first time.
His research particularly focused on indoor thermal comfort phenomena in naturally ven-
tilated buildings, and it laid the theoretical foundation for the development of the Adaptive
Thermal Comfort Model[10][11][12].
In the late 1990s, ASHRAE launched a comprehensive research project aimed at inte-
grating extensive field data on thermal comfort collected from diverse climates and build-
ing types worldwide, with the objective of systematically validating the Adaptive Thermal
Comfort Theory[13]. This project, based on the construction and analysis of a global field
survey database, led to the development of the “adaptive comfort temperature regression
equation,” which significantly advanced the field of thermal comfort research[14]. More-
over, it fundamentally reshaped building design practices, energy conservation strategies,
and indoor environmental standards—exerting profound influence particularly on the de-
sign of naturally ventilated buildings such as classrooms, office buildings, and residential
dwellings[15].
Since the 2000s, research on thermal comfort has further advanced, with increasing ef-
forts to integrate the traditional PMV/PPD model with the adaptive thermal comfort model.
[16] Additionally, growing attention has been given to the thermal comfort of specific
groups, such as adolescents and children[17]. Notably, Teli’s thermal comfort study on
British primary school children was the first to systematically validate the limitations of the
PMV model for younger populations, demonstrating that the adaptive model is more suit-
able for children in naturally ventilated classrooms[17].This research milestone extended
the scope of thermal comfort studies from adults to children and promoted the application
of adaptive models in educational buildings. Subsequently, Rupp, Vásquez, and Lam-
berts[16] conducted a comprehensive review of global thermal comfort research, system-
atically tracing the development of both the PMV/PPD model and the adaptive model[16].
Their work provided an authoritative synthesis and roadmap for scholars, standard-setting
bodies, and building designers, reinforcing the integration of these models in both re-
search and practice.
As research on thermal comfort models continues to deepen, scholars have increasingly
begun to explore the actual impacts of thermal comfort on student populations, particularly
its mechanisms of influence on cognitive performance and academic achievement. A field
investigation in Swedish primary schools found that for every 1°C increase in average
thermal sensation score, the rate of students’ complaints of discomfort increased by 14%.
[18] In a subsequent study conducted in Spanish universities, it was mentioned that a
temperature range of 23°C - 26°C could reduce students’ self-assigned major obstacles

Multi-objective optimization (MOO) in the building sector initially focused primarily on min-
imizing energy consumption, with commonly used methods including genetic algorithms
(GAs), linear programming, and goal programming. In 2001, Deb proposed the well-
known Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II)[22], which remains one of
the most widely applied and influential MOO algorithms worldwide. Deb systematically
introduced how evolutionary algorithms (EAs) can be applied to solve multi-objective op-
timization problems (MOPs), while also integrating and comparing several existing meth-
ods, such as VEGA, MOGA, PAES, and SPEA. His work significantly accelerated the
development of subsequent optimization algorithms.
During the development of MOO, its applications became widespread in the field of build-
ing thermal environment optimization, supported by increasingly advanced algorithms that
were integrated with various building energy simulation tools such as EnergyPlus[23],
TRNSYS[23], and IDA ICE[24]. Among these tools, GenOpt, a generic optimization pro-
gram developed by Wetter (2001), has been widely adopted for coupling with EnergyPlus,
TRNSYS, and other simulation platforms. It supports various optimization algorithms, in-
cluding particle swarm optimization (PSO), simulated annealing (SA), and generalized
pattern search (GPS), and has been frequently used in building energy performance opti-
mization studies [23].In IDA ICE, the AutoMOO module applies a genetic algorithm-based
multi-objective optimization approach, allowing users to optimize building performance
metrics such as energy use, thermal comfort, and indoor air quality within a graphical user
interface.[24]In recent years, optimization objectives have become more complex and
comprehensive, extending beyond conventional energy consumption and thermal com-
fort indicators to incorporate additional factors such as: Indoor air quality (IAQ);Detailed
optimization of sensible and latent heat loads;Daylighting; visual comfort; acoustic envi-
ronment;and carbon emissions.

example, He et al[25]. proposed a deep meta-modelling-based MOO method that com-

mal comfort, and indoor air quality, resulting in a predictive meta-model of building perfor-
mance[25]. Similarly, Han et al [26] introduced a multi-objective reinforcement learning
(MORL) method to optimize control strategies for complex building systems. They de-
veloped a multi-objective Markov decision process (MOMDP) to simultaneously consider

In terms of conventional glazing systems, such as double-layer (DL) glazing and low-
emissivity (LE) glazing, have improved building energy efficiency [34] [35] but cannot
adapt to variable solar radiation and indoor lighting needs, often increasing reliance on
heating, cooling and artificial lighting systems[36] [37]. This limitation has spurred the
exploration of advanced glazing technologies.
Since Deb’s seminal discovery of the electrochromic effect in WO3 thin films in 1969, [38]
electrochromic (EC) technology has progressed through several key milestones. Lam-
pert et al. developed WO3 / NiO multilayer devices in the 1980s, markedly improving
the switching speed and cycle life [39].During the 1990s, Granqvist’s group fabricated
fully solid-state EC devices, laying the groundwork for commercialization.[40]SageGlass
introduced the first commercial EC glazing in 2004[41],and the U.S. Department of En-
ergy’s 2010 “Smart Windows” programme further enhanced the technology’s energy per-

Electrochromic glass (EC glass), a smart glazing technology that dynamically adjusts

Research Gaps
Despite extensive global research on electrochromic glazing (ECG), shading devices, and
passive cooling strategies within educational buildings, there remains a substantial gap
concerning their integrated performance under Danish climatic conditions. Denmark’s
climate—characterized by mild yet variable summers, high levels of solar radiation, and
considerable diurnal temperature fluctuations—poses unique challenges and opportuni-
ties for implementing effective shading and cooling strategies.
Moreover, current international studies generally do not incorporate the stringent Danish
building regulations, such as BR18 and BR10, which specifically outline requirements for
energy efficiency, indoor air quality, ventilation rates, and overall indoor environmental
quality (IEQ) in schools. Consequently, existing findings from other climatic regions are
not directly transferable to Danish contexts without significant adjustments.
In addition, Danish schools exhibit distinct operational characteristics, including high oc-
cupancy densities during school hours and prolonged periods of vacancy during evenings
and nights. Such usage patterns demand specifically tailored design strategies to balance
thermal comfort, visual comfort, and energy consumption throughout the year. However,
integrated research addressing these operational profiles in combination with Danish reg-
ulations remains limited.
This study seeks to address these research gaps by comprehensively evaluating the inte-
grated performance of electrochromic glazing alongside traditional shading devices and
passive cooling approaches within a Danish school building. Through advanced paramet-
ric simulations and multi-objective optimization using IDA ICE software—calibrated with
real operational data from Prince Henriks School in Frederiksberg—this research aims to
establish an evidence-based framework to guide the design and optimization of shading
and cooling strategies that comply with local regulatory requirements, while effectively
addressing Denmark’s unique climate conditions.

Electrochromic (EC) glazing
3.4.1
Working Principle of Electrochromic Glazing
Electrochromic glazing is a type of smart glass capable of reversibly modulating its trans-
mittance and coloration under a low applied voltage (typically around 1–5 volts). A typical
electrochromic glazing assembly consists of multiple layers, including transparent con-
ductive layers, an electrochromic layer (commonly tungsten oxide, WO3), an electrolyte
layer, and an ion-storage layer (such as nickel oxide, NiO) [50]. When voltage is applied,
lithium (Li+) or hydrogen (H+) ions migrate from the ion-storage layer through the elec-
trolyte into the electrochromic layer, triggering changes in the crystalline structure of the
electrochromic material. This structural alteration reduces the glass’s transmittance and
darkens its appearance. When the applied voltage is reversed, the ions return to their
original layer, allowing the glass to revert to its transparent state [51].
The key advantages of electrochromic glazing include its dynamic control over both solar
heat gain and visible light transmittance. This dual functionality can significantly reduce
heating and cooling energy demands in buildings while also enhancing indoor visual com-
fort by mitigating glare [52].
Given its ability to intelligently regulate indoor comfort and simultaneously reduce build-
ing energy consumption, electrochromic glazing is well-aligned with the objectives of this
project, which focuses on indoor climate quality and energy efficiency. The following

3.5.1
Heating Setpoint
The heating system of this project is on all year round. However, seasonal control strate-
gies are implemented through time-based rules, which have clear priorities:
• From October 1st to April 30th: According to the Danish indoor thermal comfort
standard DS/EN 16798 Class II, the high-priority rule set the heating setting value
at 21°C.
• From May 1st to September 30th: Another high-priority rule was activated, setting
the heating point at 18°C, which is significantly lower than the typical indoor temper-
ature. This effectively shuts down the summer heating system, allowing it to operate
only under extreme weather conditions and avoiding accidental activation.
By correctly configuring the rule priorities in IDA ICE, the heating system operates in a
logically consistent and energy-efficient manner.

3.5.2
Cooling Setpoint
The cooling control strategy is valid from May 1st to September 30th and is implemented
using a schedule rule, clearly defining time-based priorities:
• Monday to Thursday, 07:00–22:00 (Rule 2, highest priority): Set at 23°C, mainly
used for teaching and office activities.
• Outside these times (22:00–07:00): The set value is reduced to 20°C as the night
pre-cooling strategy.
• Friday, 00:00–07:00 (Rule 4): The set value is 20°C, and 23°C for the rest of the
time.
• Sunday, 22:00–24:00 (Rule 3): The set value is 20°C, and 23°C for the rest of the
time.
• All other unallocated time periods (default rule): The default set value is 23°C to
ensure the continuity of the control logic.
This schedule ensures that cooling operation is closely aligned with occupancy and cool-
ing load requirements. By using rule priorities, the system switches automatically between
the 20°C setting during designated pre-cooling times and the 23°C setting during occu-
pied times. The result is a dynamic, energy-saving control that cools the building when
it is most effective (night or early morning) and maintains comfort during the day without
excessive use of the chiller. This approach is reflected in the simulation and contributes
to lowering peak cooling loads.
3.5.3
Interaction between Heating and Cooling Setpoints
To prevent conflicts and energy waste due to simultaneous heating and cooling opera-
tions during the summer season, a distinct temperature deadband strategy is introduced,
enforced through the rule priority structure:
• From May to September, the heating setpoint is fixed at 18°C, while the cooling
setpoint ranges from 20°C to 23°C.
• This creates a deadband of at least 2°C, within which no active thermal conditioning
is triggered. Indoor climate during this interval relies on building thermal inertia and
natural ventilation.
• The control logic is fully governed by IDA ICE’s rule priority system, ensuring that
only one rule is active at any given time, thus avoiding overlapping controls or ex-
cessive system cycling.
• Notably, the Sunday night pre-cooling period (22:00–24:00) functions as a thermal
inertia preparation phase for Monday morning, helping to delay cooling system ac-
tivation and smoothen peak load demands.
This strategy aligns with both the BR18 Danish Building Regulation and DS/EN 16798
indoor climate standards, ensuring that simulation results are both normative-compliant
and practically implementable.
In summary, the use of time-scheduled and priority-driven control rules enables season-
ally adaptive system coordination, significantly optimizing thermal comfort and energy
performance across all operation periods.

is to dynamically adjust the transparency of windows, thereby effectively managing the

Figure 3.11: P-control model for Radiation&Temp-based electrochromic glazing control in

3.6.1
Operating Principle of the Control System
The control system operates based on two essential input signals: indoor temperature
and solar radiation, as illustrated in the P-control model shown in Figure 3.11. These
inputs are processed through the following mechanisms:
• Temperature Control: A thermostat controller manages the temperature input with
a threshold set at 23 ◦C. When the indoor temperature exceeds this threshold, the
output signal is set to 1; otherwise, it remains 0.
• Radiation Control: A proportional controller (P-controller) processes the solar ra-
diation input. As solar radiation increases from 100 to 150 W/m2, the output signal
transitions smoothly from 0 to 1, indicating a gradual reduction in window trans-
parency.
Notably, the high and low setpoints of the P-controller correspond to
the threshold values defined in the Graphical Script model (see Figure 3.4 in Sec-
tion 3.3.2), which were used to establish activation limits for electrochromic glazing
operations.
The output signals from these two control sections are then combined through a product
controller, where the two values are multiplied. This design ensures that the EC glazing
remains fully transparent during nighttime when there is no solar radiation, regardless of
indoor temperature, while enabling adaptive shading under high radiation and tempera-
ture conditions.

Discussion
5.1
Summary of Key Findings
This study comprehensively evaluated the integrated performance of electrochromic glaz-
ing (EC glazing), traditional shading devices, and passive cooling strategies (specifically
night ventilation) under Danish climatic conditions, combining in-situ measurements with
IDA ICE simulations. The key performance indicators analyzed included thermal comfort,
visual comfort, energy consumption, and CO2 emissions.
The major findings are summarized as follows:
• Electrochromic glazing demonstrated excellent performance in reducing cooling
loads, particularly during high solar radiation periods in summer and transitional
seasons, significantly mitigating indoor overheating while maintaining good daylight
availability. However, under extreme design conditions with high window-to-wall ra-
tios (W2W Ratio > 0.35), the thermal control effectiveness of EC glazing becomes
notably limited. This limitation mainly arises from the relatively high visible light
transmittance (LT) and solar heat gain coefficient (g-value) of the current EC glaz-
ing products in their darkest state, which fail to sufficiently block intense solar ra-
diation. Consequently, indoor temperature control becomes less effective in such
cases. This suggests that the shading capacity of EC glazing requires further en-
hancement for applications in high W2W ratio designs, or alternatively, it should be
supplemented with additional strategies such as external shading devices or active
ventilation to achieve optimal thermal comfort.
• Within the target range of maintaining adequate daylight autonomy (sDA300/50% ≈
50%), the optimal W2W ratio typically falls between 0.24 and 0.28, varying according
to building orientation. Generally, south-facing facades require the smallest W2W
ratio to mitigate overheating risks, while north-facing facades can tolerate higher
W2W ratios to compensate for insufficient daylight. It is noteworthy that excessively
high W2W ratios, although beneficial for daylight access and exterior views, can
significantly increase overheating risks and overall energy consumption, requiring
careful consideration during design.
• There exists a typical trade-off among different shading strategies. EC glazing can
effectively balance daylight utilization, thermal comfort, and energy performance
through its dynamic modulation capability. In contrast, traditional shading devices
(such as external louvers and blinds) primarily focus on minimizing overheating but
often compromise indoor daylight levels and spatial visual quality.
• The multi-objective optimization results further indicate that under the current build-
ing typology and Danish climate, the combination of EC glazing with night venti-
lation offers the most favorable overall performance. This strategy can maximize
both visual and thermal comfort while effectively reducing CO2 emissions and an-
nual energy consumption. Such a combined solution shows promising potential
for wider applications in both new constructions and renovation projects of Danish
school buildings, particularly those targeting low-carbon and high-comfort design in
compliance with Danish building regulations (e.g., BR18) and indoor environmental
standards.

5.2
EC Glazing vs Traditional Shading
Simulation results indicate that electrochromic glazing (EC glazing) performs well in main-
taining indoor thermal conditions and reducing cooling energy demand, particularly under
the window-to-wall ratio (W2W Ratio) of 0.25. This W2W Ratio corresponds to the south-
facing facade scenario, where EC glazing achieves good daylight performance with spatial
daylight autonomy (sDA300,50%) approximately equal to 50%. The ability of EC glazing to
dynamically modulate solar transmittance allows it to adapt flexibly to seasonal and cli-
matic variations, effectively mitigating the excessive shading issues commonly associated
with traditional fixed shading devices.
However, when the W2W Ratio increases to 0.35—about 10% higher than the reference
value of 0.25—simulation results show that the specific EC glazing product used in this
study struggles to meet indoor thermal comfort requirements. The selection of 0.35 as
the high W2W Ratio scenario was based on preliminary experiments, which revealed that
at a W2W Ratio of 0.5, all shading strategies achieved nearly 100% daylight autonomy
(sDA300,50%), rendering further comparisons meaningless. Therefore, 0.35 was selected
as a practically relevant high-transparency scenario for performance evaluation.
Figure 5.1: Comparison of EC glazing performance indicators under different W2W Ra-
tios.
According to the technical specifications provided by the manufacturer, the electrochromic
glazing (EC glazing) product used in this study has a total solar transmittance (g-value) of
0.179, a direct solar transmittance of 0.129, and a visible light transmittance (Tv) of up to
32.3% in its fully darkened state. Although these parameters are lower than those in the
bright state, they remain relatively high under strong sunlight conditions, making it difficult
to effectively suppress solar heat gains. Furthermore, the glazing’s thermal transmittance
(Ug-value) remains constant at 0.514 W/m2 · K in the darkened state and lacks any light-
scattering function (diffusion factor of 0), further limiting its ability to regulate indoor thermal
conditions in buildings with large window areas.
It is important to emphasize that these performance limitations are not inherent to elec-

trochromic glazing technology itself but rather stem from the specific product model used
in this study. In practical applications, selecting EC glazing products with lower g-values
and Tv values in the darkened state may effectively overcome the aforementioned thermal
performance challenges under high window-to-wall ratio (W2W Ratio) conditions. There-
fore, the simulation results in this study primarily reflect the limitations of the selected
product rather than fundamental flaws in electrochromic glazing technology as a whole.
In comparison, traditional external shading devices—such as fixed external screens,
venetian blinds, and micro lamella shading systems—demonstrate superior thermal con-
trol performance in large window area designs. These devices physically block direct
solar radiation, effectively reducing indoor heat gains and significantly lowering overheat-
ing hours. Although such shading devices generally reduce daylight autonomy, they can
still provide more robust thermal protection in high-transparency spaces, reduce cooling
energy demand, and achieve outcomes that the selected EC glazing product alone cannot
match.
Specifically, under a window-to-wall ratio of 0.35, external screens exhibit the best perfor-
mance in reducing indoor overheating hours; however, this comes at the cost of the high-
est primary energy consumption (47.1 kWh/m2) and CO2 emissions (356.5 kg). Although
micro lamella shading also effectively reduces overheating hours (to 16.3 hours), it signifi-
cantly diminishes daylight autonomy (15.3%) and lacks dynamic adjustability, maintaining
a high shading rate throughout the year. In contrast, venetian blinds achieve the optimal
balance among thermal comfort, energy performance, CO2 emissions, and daylight uti-
lization. They limit overheating hours to just 6.8 hours, while also achieving the lowest
primary energy consumption (45.12 kWh/m2) and CO2 emissions (341.6 kg), with a day-
light autonomy of 21.33%. Therefore, considering thermal comfort, energy performance,
and visual needs comprehensively, venetian blinds offer the most balanced overall per-
formance, making them particularly suitable for school and office buildings with integrated
indoor environmental quality requirements.
These differences in the performance of shading strategies not only affect indoor comfort
and energy efficiency but also have direct implications for regulatory compliance. Accord-
ing to Danish building regulations and indoor climate standards, selecting an appropriate
shading strategy is essential to simultaneously meet thermal, visual, and energy-related
requirements. Therefore, understanding this context helps explain why selecting different
shading strategies according to varying conditions is essential.
In summary, while electrochromic glazing (EC glazing) remains suitable for buildings with
moderate window areas and high demands for visual comfort and balanced daylighting,
this study clearly identifies the performance limitations of the selected EC glazing prod-
uct in large window area buildings with a window-to-wall ratio (W2W Ratio) of 0.35. To
simultaneously comply with Danish building regulations and meet indoor comfort require-
ments, it is recommended to adopt hybrid shading strategies that combine EC glazing with
external shading devices, night ventilation, and other passive cooling measures. Such in-
tegrated approaches can provide more robust and reliable overall performance, ensuring
optimal indoor environmental quality and regulatory compliance.
Furthermore, it should be noted that applying more advanced electrochromic glazing prod-
ucts or further optimizing hybrid shading strategies may potentially alter the simulation
results, leading to improved building performance under similar conditions.

5.3
Impact of Passive Cooling Strategies
The evaluation of various cooling strategies in this study highlights the complexity of bal-
ancing thermal comfort, minimizing primary energy consumption, and reducing CO2 emis-
sions in educational buildings under Danish climatic conditions. The comparative analysis
clearly demonstrates the strengths and limitations of electrochromic glazing (EC glazing)
and conventional glazing systems when combined with both passive and active cooling
methods, such as night ventilation, standard air handling units (AHUs), and auxiliary heat-
ing/cooling systems.
The results indicate that the combination of EC glazing with night ventilation achieves a
relatively balanced overall performance, reducing annual overheating hours to 43, with a
primary energy consumption of 47 kWh/m2 and CO2 emissions of 350 kg. This strategy
demonstrates a good trade-off between thermal comfort and energy efficiency. However,
when auxiliary heating and cooling systems are further incorporated, indoor comfort can
be significantly improved, reducing overheating hours to only 15. Nevertheless, this im-
provement comes at the cost of increased energy use and emissions, with primary energy
consumption rising to 52 kWh/m2, CO2 emissions increasing to 389 kg, and heating en-
ergy demand increasing from 23.3 kWh/m2 to 31.9 kWh/m2.
In comparison, the combination of EC glazing with a standard AHU system completely
eliminates overheating (0 hours) but results in a notably high primary energy consumption
of 115.8 kWh/m2 and CO2 emissions of 383 kg, highlighting the evident trade-off between
extreme thermal comfort and environmental performance.
It is noteworthy that, although both electrochromic glazing and conventional glazing com-
bined with standard AHU systems can effectively eliminate overheating, simulation results
reveal that electrochromic glazing tends to exhibit higher overall energy consumption and
CO2 emissions under identical cooling strategies. This phenomenon mainly results from
the inherent optical and thermal properties of electrochromic glazing. Specifically, even
in its fully darkened state, the relatively high solar heat gain coefficient (g-value) of elec-
trochromic glazing reduces the potential for passive solar heat gains during the heating
season. Moreover, the year-round shading behavior of electrochromic glazing often limits
solar heat gains in winter, leading to increased heating demand. Consequently, although
cooling loads are comparable, the higher heating energy requirements associated with
electrochromic glazing under standard AHU operation contribute to increased annual pri-
mary energy consumption and CO2 emissions compared to conventional glazing.
Conventional glazing solutions, despite their lower technological complexity, exhibit se-
vere limitations under Danish indoor climate standards. When combined with night venti-
lation only, conventional glazing results in excessive annual overheating hours of 225. Al-
though this configuration achieves the lowest primary energy consumption (42.5 kWh/m2)
and CO2 emissions (315 kg), it fails to meet acceptable thermal comfort standards. When
auxiliary heating and cooling systems are incorporated, the overheating hours can be re-
duced to 162.9; however, thermal comfort remains inadequate, and the energy use in-
creases to 48 kWh/m2 with CO2 emissions rising to 356 kg. The combination of conven-
tional glazing with a standard AHU system eliminates overheating but leads to extremely
high energy consumption (119 kWh/m2) and CO2 emissions (1257 kg), along with an ex-
ceptionally high heating energy demand of 2640 kWh/m2, making it entirely unfeasible
under Denmark’s current regulatory framework.
Based on the above findings, this study strongly recommends adopting hybrid cooling
strategies, specifically combining EC glazing, night ventilation, and targeted heating/cool-
ing systems, as an effective and feasible solution. This approach provides the optimal

a suitable design strategy for educational buildings in Denmark.

5.4.1
Effect of Occupant Density
Simulation results show that increasing occupant numbers significantly enhance internal
heat gains, reducing heating energy demand from 55.41 kWh to 17.33 kWh. However,
this also leads to substantial increases in overheating hours (from 4.19 to 71.48 hours),
as well as in primary energy consumption and CO2 emissions. To maintain both energy
efficiency and thermal comfort while complying with regulations, the optimal occupant
density is identified as 24–26 persons.
5.4.2
Effect of Shading Threshold
Adjusting shading thresholds has pronounced impacts on thermal and visual perfor-
mance.
Lower thresholds (50–150 W/m²) effectively control overheating (38.3–52.7
hours) but result in reduced daylight autonomy near the regulatory limit (50%). Higher
thresholds (350–500 W/m²) provide better daylight performance but cause overheating to
exceed acceptable levels. A threshold between 150 and 200 W/m² offers the most bal-
anced performance, achieving overheating hours within 58.8–79.2 and maintaining sDA
around 50%.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
This study systematically evaluated the comprehensive performance of a specific elec-
trochromic glazing product in comparison with traditional shading devices and passive
cooling strategies in terms of thermal comfort, visual comfort, energy consumption, and
CO2 emissions, within the context of Prince Henriks School in Frederiksberg, Denmark.
The analysis was based on IDA ICE building performance simulations combined with op-
erational data and comprehensively investigated several key parameters that influence
building performance, including window-to-wall ratio (W2W Ratio), electrochromic glazing
illuminance switching threshold settings, occupant density, and various cooling strategies
such as night ventilation and air handling unit (AHU) operation.
The results indicate that under typical Danish school conditions with a moderate W2W
Ratio (approximately 0.25), the tested electrochromic glazing product demonstrated clear
advantages in balancing daylight utilization, reducing cooling loads, and improving ther-
mal comfort. Through dynamic modulation of solar transmittance, this product effectively
maintained comfortable indoor temperatures while preserving high daylight levels and vi-
sual comfort. However, under high W2W Ratio scenarios (such as 0.35) or conditions
with elevated internal heat gains, the thermal performance of the product was limited.
In these cases, the glazing alone was insufficient to meet Danish thermal comfort reg-
ulations, necessitating the combination with external shading, night ventilation, or other
passive cooling measures.
Moreover, both the switching threshold settings and occupant density showed signifi-
cant impacts on building performance. A moderate illuminance switching threshold (150–
200 W/m²) provided the most balanced performance, effectively controlling both daylight
autonomy and overheating while minimizing energy use and CO2 emissions. Similarly,
an occupant density range of 24–26 persons was identified as optimal for balancing over-
heating risks, heating energy demand, and regulatory compliance. Additionally, simu-
lation results confirmed that electrochromic glazing must be paired with passive cooling
strategies such as night ventilation to maintain both thermal comfort and energy efficiency
under high internal heat loads.
It is important to emphasize that the conclusions of this study are specific to the tested
electrochromic glazing product, which possesses particular optical and thermal character-
istics. These findings should not be generalized to all electrochromic glazing technologies,
as different products may exhibit significantly different performance outcomes.
6.2
Future Work
Based on the findings of this study, several directions are recommended for future re-
search to enhance the applicability and generalizability of the results:
1. Investigation of Various Electrochromic Glazing Products:
Future studies
should explore a wider range of electrochromic glazing products with different optical
and thermal properties, such as those offering broader transmittance ranges, faster
switching speeds, or advanced coating technologies. This would help to assess how
product-specific characteristics influence building energy performance and comfort
under varying conditions.

2. Application Across Diverse Climatic Regions: Since this study focuses on the
Danish climate, future research should extend to other climatic zones, including hot-
humid regions, cold climates, and areas with large seasonal variability. Such stud-
ies could provide insights into the broader applicability and effectiveness of elec-
trochromic glazing and shading systems worldwide.
3. Integration with Advanced Cooling Strategies: Further research is encouraged
to investigate the combined effects of electrochromic glazing with additional pas-
sive and active cooling strategies, beyond night ventilation. Examples include earth-
coupled cooling, displacement ventilation, or high-efficiency mechanical cooling sys-
tems. These studies can help identify optimal combinations of shading and cooling
approaches.
4. Comparative Analysis of Comprehensive Shading and Cooling Systems:
Future research should systematically compare a variety of shading systems—
including fixed external shading, operable shading devices, dynamic façades, and
advanced glazing technologies—in combination with various cooling strategies.
This would support the development of integrated solutions for maximizing thermal
comfort, visual comfort, and energy efficiency.

mended to integrate occupant behavior and adaptive control strategies into simula-
tion studies. This includes modeling occupant preferences, manual overrides, and
real-time automated controls, which may substantially affect the actual performance
of shading and cooling systems in practice.
Collectively, these future research directions would contribute to a more holistic under-

to enhance indoor environmental quality and energy efficiency across diverse building
contexts.
