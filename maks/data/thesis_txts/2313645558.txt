Abstract
This thesis deals with the application of topology optimization to turbulent ﬂows and
investigates the applicability of frozen turbulence assumption to topology optimization
problems. In order to achieve this outcome, thesis contains several branches of work.
For the ﬂow simulations, a 3D unstructured ﬁnite volume incompressible ﬂow solver is
developed. Solver utilizes segregated approach for the velocity-pressure coupling through
SIMPLE algorithm. Two popular RANS models namely, k −ω and Spalart-Allmaras
models are included into the developed solver for realizing turbulence modelling.
Automatic diﬀerentiation (AD) is utilized with the goal of realizing an eﬃcient frame-
work for the implementation of discrete adjoint solver with turbulence models. From
the carried out sensitivity validation study of the developed adjoint solver, it has been
demonstrated that frozen turbulence assumption produces inconsistent sensitivities.
Thesis conﬁnes itself to steady-state optimization of turbulent ﬂow problems. Through-
out the work, several 2D and one 3D optimization problems are investigated in which
the eﬀects of utilizing diﬀerent turbulence models and frozen turbulence assumption are
assessed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For the design of complex ﬂow systems, computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) can be con-
sidered as one of the most important tools available. Since, compared to experiments,
it oﬀers relatively inexpensive way of obtaining valuable information through virtual
ﬂow experiments in a computer environment. In the situations where no prior knowl-
edge is available on possible design improvements, the pursuit of achieving the desired
improvements on the design can easily become prohibitive considering large number of
ﬂow evaluations by trial and error without posing any optimization problem. To this
end, topology optimization can be utilized which is a method of material distribution
to obtain an optimum layout based on a chosen criteria even without deﬁning any ini-
tial shape prior to the optimization. Hence, it oﬀers vast implications for the practical
aspects of many industrial processes.
Topology optimization is based on the theory of constrained optimization. Optimum
material distribution is found by solving an optimization problem while satisfying the
considered design constraints and governing equations that deﬁnes the physics of the
problem. Solution to the posed optimization problem is most commonly realized with
utilizing gradient based optimization algorithms. In the process of optimization, material
distribution is achieved by deﬁning a density variable γ which is allowed to changed
between 0 and 1 (solid and ﬂuid, respectively) in the design domain.
This thesis concerns with the application of topology optimization for turbulent ﬂows
with high Reynolds numbers where the eﬀect of turbulence on the ﬂow is realized through
turbulence modelling. To this end, Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models are
utilized for the purpose of ﬂow simulation. Throughout the thesis, only incompressible
ﬂows are considered. For topology optimization, steady-state optimization problems are
investigated through the time-averaged steady-state ﬂow solutions from RANS models.

1.1
Motivation
It can be said that, considering both internal and external ﬂows, almost all of the
industrial applications which carry importance in terms of engineering purposes involve
turbulent ﬂows. However, so far topology optimization of ﬂow problems has been mostly
centered around laminar ﬂows. Hence, main motivation behind this thesis is the inclusion
of the RANS turbulence models to the topology optimization framework.
Although, some work has been done relevant to the topology optimization of turbulent
ﬂows such as [1], mostly the variation of turbulent model variables on the design pa-
rameter is neglected. This common way of approaching to the optimization problem
for turbulent ﬂows is known as frozen turbulence assumption. Even though, utilizing
this assumption simpliﬁes great deal of the problem description, it is known to produce
wrong sensitivities.
In general, topology optimization for turbulent ﬂows can be considered as a new research
ﬁeld. To the best of the authors knowledge, topology optimization for turbulence ﬂows
has not been done with exact sensitivities produced by discrete adjoint method via
eﬃcient utilization of automatic diﬀerentiation (AD). Hence developed methodology in
this thesis is seen as a contribution to the ﬁeld of topology optimization.
1.2
Aims and Objectives
Main goal of this thesis is the application of topology optimization to turbulent ﬂow
problems. To facilitate this goal, two initial objectives have been achieved within the
time frame of the thesis:
• Development of a 3D unstructured ﬁnite volume incompressible ﬂow solver fea-
turing eﬃcient segregated approach in the solution strategy (SIMPLE algorithm).
Solver includes two popular RANS models for turbulence modelling which are k−ω
and Spalart-Allmaras turbulence models.
• Development of a discrete adjoint RANS solver where exact sensitivities can be
obtained and diﬀerent turbulence models can be included with minimal eﬀort in
terms of development time.
With the development of ﬂow and adjoint solvers, the study is focused on the following
objectives:

• Assesment of the applibility of the frozen turbulence assumption for the topology
optimization of turbulent ﬂows.
• Investigation of utilizing diﬀerent turbulence models for topology optimization.
• Running several topology optimization problems including one three dimensional

Thesis Outline
Chapter 2 brieﬂy introduces the basics of turbulence theory that is relevant to the thesis
work. Following the introduction on the subject of turbulence modelling, utilized RANS
models have been presented. In Chapter 3, subsequent to in-depth introduction to the
unstructured ﬁnite volume formulation, solution algorithm used for Navier-Stokes equa-
tions and turbulence models are described in discrete setting. After the implementation
details of the developed ﬂow solver is discussed, Chapter 4 carries out the validation
study of the unstructured ﬁnite volume ﬂow solver.
In Chapter 5, brieﬂy, topology optimization and adjoint method for the calculation of
the required sensitivities are introduced. Diﬀerences between continuous and discrete
approaches concerning adjoint method are elaborated and discussed. Moreover, auto-
matic diﬀerentiation is introduced in the concept of discrete adjoint method. Finally,
methodology followed in the development of the discrete adjoint solver together with its
implementation details are presented.
Chapter 6 concerns itself with the veriﬁcation of the implemented discrete adjoint solver
by means of a sensitivity comparison with ﬁnite diﬀerences. Sensitivity check is done
for the adjoint solver including both k −ω and SA turbulence models. Furthermore,
Chapter 6 also investigates the eﬀect of making frozen turbulence assumption on the
calculated sensitivities.
Chapter 7 considers various topology optimization problems in order to complete the
thesis work. First problem is the optimization of a 2D channel bend, where the opti-
mization results from both k −ω and SA turbulence models together with their frozen
turbulence counterparts are presented. Secondly, the 2D U-bend problem is considered
where an additional parametric study of diﬀerent Reynolds numbers is carried out. After
the demonstration of an external ﬂow optimization concerning drag minimization, the
optimization of 3D channel bend problem is also carried out and presented.
Finally, Chapter 8 draws conclusions on the thesis.

Chapter 2
Turbulence
In this chapter, a brief introduction to turbulence theory is presented with highlighting
the important features of turbulence in the concept of this thesis.
For a complete
introduction to turbulence theory, the reader is referred to textbooks such as [2] and [3].
The presented theory deals with the steady boundary layers close to a smooth wall. For
topology optimization, it is important to understand and correctly model turbulent ﬂows
in the presence of a wall. Since, during optimization, solid mediums will be emerging in
the ﬂuid regime.
Furthermore, brief discussion of the modelling requirements presents the main reason
why for simulation of turbulent ﬂows, Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes equations are
employed for the thesis.
2.1
Basics of Turbulent Flow
It is a challenging task to describe turbulent ﬂow in a concise manner. Following the
statement given by [4], it can be described as, ”irregular motion in rotational ﬂows with
distinct statistical properties”. Determining if a certain ﬂow is turbulent depends on
the relative ratio of inertial to viscous forces which also identiﬁes the Reynolds number:
Re = UL
ν
(2.1)
Here, U is the characteristic velocity scale, L is the length scale and ν is the kinematic vis-
cosity of the ﬂuid. When Reynolds number is large enough, ﬂow becomes turbulent with
the developed instabilities through the interaction between convective (nonlinear) and
viscous parts of the Navier-Stokes equations. The resulting highly convective ﬂows with
diﬀerent scales of turbulent eddies are inherently three-dimensional and time-dependent

Another approach is to utilize large eddy simulation (LES) in which the large eddies
are simulated while the modelling is done only on the smaller scales which mainly acts

simulation is done approximately to the start of the viscous subrange with covering the

Stokes equations. LES approach results in a lowered mesh requirement when compared
to DNS which allows the simulation of very complex ﬂows. However, LES is as DNS,
fully time-dependent and three-dimensional which would make it very costly to employ
in an optimization procedure. Along with ﬁne spatial resolution, both LES and DNS
require ﬁne temporal resolution.

With employing RANS equations, only large scales are simulated (permanent eddies)
and the rest of the turbulent scales are modelled. RANS equations, realize a solution that
corresponds to an ensemble averaged ﬂow ﬁeld. Ability of simulation of turbulent ﬂows
with signiﬁcantly lowered mesh requirements (compared to LES) due to time-averaged
solution makes RANS the method of choice for many engineering purposes. Since, most
of the engineering interest lies in the mean properties of the ﬂow. Existence of steady-
state (or periodic) solutions with very high Reynolds number ﬂows is the reason that
RANS models are employed in the Topology Optimization framework that is developed
for this work.

Chapter 3
Finite Volume Discretization
In this chapter, ﬁnite volume formulation utilized in the developed fully unstructured
three dimensional incompressible Navier-Stokes solver is presented. Rather than solving
Navier-Stokes as a non-linear system of equations, segragated approach is followed for
the pressure-velocity coupling, meaning that the equations are linearized and solved suc-
cessively based on the SIMPLE [14](semi-implicit method for pressure-linked equations)
algorithm with using pressure correction. SIMPLE method and its variants are widely
used in computational ﬂuid dynamics due to their low memory requirements and the
ability of simulating both steady and unsteady ﬂows.
As an external library, PETSc [15–17] is utilized for its sparse linear solvers and for the
parallelization of the developed incompressible ﬂow solver. Following the ﬁnite volume
formulation and the solution procedure for Navier-Stokes, implementation details of
utilized RANS models (k−ω and SA models) are presented along with a short discussion
concerning the choice of element type for high Reynolds number ﬂow simulations.

Chapter 5
Adjoint Approach via Automatic
Diﬀerentiation
In this chapter, general constrained optimization problem in the concept of topology
optimization is introduced through which the diﬀerent methods for carrying out the
sensitivity analysis is presented. The term ”sensitivity analysis” implies the method
of obtaining the derivative of objective function with respect to some design variable
which is needed for gradient based optimization algorithms to be able to solve the
formulated optimization problem.
Following the formulation of the adjoint method
for the calculation of sensitivities, automatic diﬀerentiation (AD) is brieﬂy introduced,
which is the method of choice employed in this thesis for carrying out the sensitivity
analysis. Finally, the methodology developed for the calculation of adjoint sensitivities
are presented which carries the main goal of realizing an eﬃcient framework for the
development of discrete adjoint solvers where consistent sensitivities can be obtained and
diﬀerent turbulence models can be included with minimal eﬀort in terms of development
time.

AD tools can be used with two modes, namely forward (tangent) and reverse (adjoint)
modes. Forward mode provides derivatives in single direction and can be realized by
choosing one input j and making a forward sweep on the index i. In the case of having
only a scalar output (i = n + 1), in order to calculate the gradient vector, AD routine
has to be called n times in forward mode, which is not very eﬃcient considering in the
element based calculation of residual functions, number of independent variables greatly
exceeds the number of outputs. Whereas the reverse mode of AD is realized by choosing
an output i and making a reversed sweep on the index j. If we consider the case of a
scalar output function, gradient vector can be obtained in a single call to the reverse AD
routine. This thesis utilizes the reverse mode of AD for the calculation of the required
partial derivatives.
Practically, AD tools are implemented based on two diﬀerent methods, namely, source-
to-source transformation and operation overloading. Source-to-source transformation is
mostly utilized for programming languages such as Fortran and C. The method auto-
maticly produces a diﬀerentiated source code which includes additional statements for
the derivetive calculations. [50] uses source transformation AD tool TAPENADE [51]
in both reverse and forward modes to derive an exact adjoint solver based on SIMPLE
algorithm in order to calculate shape sensitivities.
Operation overloading approach uses high level features of object orriented programming
languages such as C++. Operations (real numbers) involved in the algorithms that are
to be diﬀerentiated are redeﬁned (overloaded) with a new type which contains both its
value and derivative. In order to apply the reverse mode of AD, operation overloading
approach tapes (records) the function evaluation priour to the gradient calculation with
all its values and mathematical statements. After which the reverse sweep utilizes this
tape. Although, overloading approach requires usually less modiﬁcation to the source
==PAGE:49==

code, care must be taken for memory usage with appropriately starting and ending
tapings. This thesis, utilizes AD with operation overloading since the developed Navier-
Stokes solver is written in C++. Eventhough, there are many AD tools available for C
++, throughout the thesis work, adept: automatic diﬀerentiation library [52] has been
chosen for carrying out the diﬀerentiation in reverse mode.
Application of AD with operation overloading can be seen in [53] where OpenFOAM
library [54] has been diﬀerentiated in a black-box manner.
Diﬀerentition with this
approach brings immense memory requirements for which usually check-pointing algo-
rithms [55] are exploited to reduce the memory consumption of tape.
Instead of diﬀerentiating whole code, this thesis utilizes reverse mode of AD selectively.
Meaning, AD is only used to calculate the required partial derivatives to form adjoint
equation (Eq. 5.17) and evaluate sensitivities (Eq. 5.18). This way, memory require-
ments are signiﬁcantly lowered. Although, the adjoint system needs to be solved in order
to calculate sensitivities, much faster execution time is obtained when compared to the
approach of diﬀerentiating whole code. Only requirement for the current methadology
is to implement additional residual and objective functions, which will be evaluated
and diﬀerentiated at convergence to calculate the required partial derivatives for ad-
joint approach. This treatment produces exactly consistent sensitivities and can easlily
be applied to any set of partial diﬀerential equations without much consideration. For
shape optimization problems, similar methadology of application of AD tools for deriving
discrete adjoint solvers can be found in [49, 56].

Topology Optimization
In this chapter, results from topology optimization of turbulent ﬂows will be presented
concerning several cases.
First case deals with 2D channel bend problem, where both k −ω and SA RANS models

minimization of drag is sought. The case also considers the eﬀect of blending lift max-

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In the presented thesis work, topology optimization is successfully applied to turbulent
ﬂow problems and the objectives of the thesis have been achieved. To facilitate this
outcome, within the time frame of this thesis, a ﬁnite volume ﬂow solver is developed
and veriﬁed against experimental data. An eﬃcient methodology is developed for the
utilization of automatic diﬀerentiation and applied successfully to develop a discrete
adjoint solver. Moreover, calculated sensitivities with the discrete adjoint solver of two
RANS models are demonstrated to be exact.
In the remaining part of the thesis work, several 2D topology optimization problems
are carried out as well as one optimization of a 3D turbulent channel bend problem.
The Assessment of frozen turbulence assumption is also considered and demonstrated
to produce inconsistent sensitivities. With the results obtained from the end-designs,
it is concluded that utilizing frozen turbulent assumption is not applicable for topology
optimization of turbulent ﬂows. The eﬀect of using diﬀerent turbulent models on the
end-design is also investigated. Even though, more in-depth study is needed, k −ω
model proved to be more robust. Also it has been found that, without a presence of a
local minima in the optimization both models approximately realizes same results.
