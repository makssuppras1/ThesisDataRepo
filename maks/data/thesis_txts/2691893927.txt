Summary (English)
As Internet of Things (IoT) devices become smaller, cheaper, and ubiquitous, there is
an increased risk of malicious attacks against them. Due to the limited resources of IoT
devices, it can be difficult to deploy complex security mechanisms and ensure that devices
deployed on IoT networks are not compromised by malware. To address this challenge,
several techniques have been developed for malware prevention and detection in IoT
networks. In this context, Remote Attestation (RA) is one promising technique that tries
to verify remotely and securely the legitimate state of IoT devices. There exists a multitude
of RA schemes with different goals and using different methods.
One category of RA schemes is called ControlôÙFlow Attestation (CFA). CFA schemes aim
to verify the controlôÙflow execution of one or many devices, ensuring their correct runtime
behavior. Despite various CFA schemes proposed in the literature, there is currently no
CFA scheme that attests distributed, asynchronous network of IoT services. In fact, there
are only two practical schemes that consider asynchronous IoT networks at all. In addition,
the only scheme that can verify the correct data exchanged between services can not
detect malware that affects the control flow of a deviceãs program without modifying the
program binary.
This thesis provides a systematic literature review on the topic of CFA in the context of
IoT. In addition, a novel protocol, ARCADIS, is proposed that can detect malware that
uses techniques that alter the controlôÙflow of asynchronous distributed IoT services, and
that can also verify the data that is asynchronously passed between these services. The
protocol is analyzed and simulated and shown to be practical for smaller networks.

become a ubiquitous part of everyday life and business. Nowadays, IoT devices are being
used in many cases to gather and process sensitive data, or used to perform tasks critical
to business processes or safety. This includes use cases such as smart factory automaôÙ
tion, wearables, and health technologies. This inevitably raises some questions about
the security of these devices and networks, and recent security research has shown that
in general, IoT devices are plagued with a multitude of security problems [1, 2, 3]. One
of the reasons for these security problems being so prevalent is the desire of IoT device
manufacturers to minimize the cost of manufacture and timeôÙtoôÙmarket to beat their comôÙ
petitors, and security often becomes an afterthought in the process. The pervasiveness
of these devices, coupled with the low emphasis on security and the potentially sensitive
and valuable data that is stored in them, has made the IoT sphere an incredibly lucrative
target for hackers.
Among many IoT attacks, IoT devices are increasingly becoming attractive targets for
botnet malware. A botnet is a collection of networkôÙconnected devices or computers that
a malicious actor has taken control of, often without detection. Due to the sheer number
of IoT devices around the world, it can be very lucrative for hackers to attempt to take
control of these devices in a stealthy way. When a sufficient number of devices have
been compromised, the attacker can then launch Distributed DenialôÙofôÙService (DDoS)
attacks on various targets worldwide. In 2016, a botnet that came to be known as Mirai

anyone to launch these types of attacks. A 2019 study also found that building a BaaS
platform on readily available Cloud Service Providers by exploiting trial accounts is very
easy [5]. The ease of building botnets and launching attacks these days further underlines
the critical need for improving IoT security.

To deal with the expanded attack surface in IoT networks, it has become crucial to imôÙ
prove the security of IoT devices. However, in many cases, improving the security of IoT
devices is too costly for manufacturers and thus infeasible. Many IoT devices are highly
resourceôÙconstrained and have severe battery limitations, so malware detection software
cannot run continuously. In addition, many IoT devices are deployed in harsh, remote
or otherwise difficultôÙtoôÙaccess physical environments, making security software updates
difficult or even impossible.
One promising technique which aims to detect the presence of malware on resourceôÙ
constrained devices is Remote Attestation. In this technique, a trusted third party (Verifier)
attests the legitimate software state of an untrusted device (Prover). A wellôÙresearched
method of detecting malicious activity using Remote Attestation is Static Attestation. Static
Attestation consists of verifying the static, unchanging parts of a Proverãs memory. This
is often achieved by computing a checksum of certain parts of the static memory, for exôÙ
ample, the program binary, and reporting that checksum back to the Verifier. The Verifier
can then compare the reported checksum to a precomputed database of valid checkôÙ
sums. This method is effective in detecting attacks such as program replacement, code
injection into the program binary, or modification of global constants or configurations. A
similar technique is already being used in cases such as secure booting of desktop PCs
and laptops in order to attest the integrity of programs launched during boot, where each
ãlayerã of software invoked during boot, starting with the bootloader, attests the integrity
of the programs in the next layer before they are executed. However, applying these
techniques to IoT devices and networks remotely presents various challenges, such as
resource constraints, hardware costs and connectivity.
A popular countermeasure of these types of static attacks is a Write or Execute (W ãX)
policy, also referred to as Data Execution Prevention (DEP). This policy marks certain
memory pages as either Writable or Executable, but never both at the same time. In this
way, an attacker may be able to inject new code into the memory, but cannot execute
the same code because the memory region cannot have both Write and Execute permisôÙ
sions. For the same reason, any executable code in the memory cannot be overwritten.
However, it has been shown that even with such protections in place, it may still be posôÙ
sible to mount successful code injection attacks [8], which is why Static Attestation is still
an important topic in IoT despite the prevalence of these preventative techniques.
Although Static Attestation is an important method of detecting malicious activity, it can
not detect attacks that target the more dynamic and changing parts of the memory, for
example the stack. An attacker can inject malicious code into the stack area of the device
and then force execution of that code. Static Attestation will typically not be able to detect
such an attack, because highlyôÙdynamic and changing parts of the memory, such as the
stack, are very unpredictable and thus it is very difficult or impossible to preôÙcompute valid
states to compare against. A sophisticated technique that attackers use and Static AttesôÙ
tation can not detect is ReturnôÙOriented Programming (ROP). This is where an attacker
uses soôÙcalled code gadgets that are already present in executable memory of the deôÙ
vice, e.g. in external linked libraries, to construct a malicious program flow pieceôÙbyôÙpiece.
This requires no changes to the actual memory on the device, except overwriting a return
address on the stack via e.g. a buffer overflow vulnerability. Attacks that leverage this
technique are notoriously difficult to detect and much research on the topic of detecting or
preventing these types of dynamic runtime attacks has been performed in recent years.
The group of techniques that aim to attest the dynamic parts of the memory is referred to

1.1
Problem Statement and Research Objectives
There are a multitude of Remote Attestation schemes that have been proposed to attest
IoT devices. Some of them focus on Static Attestation and attest only the program binaôÙ
ries loaded in a Proverãs memory. Other schemes go further and try to attest the dynamic
properties of a Prover, such as its current runtime state, execution path and control flow,
while a few attempt to attest both static and dynamic properties at the same time. Other
schemes focus on the attestation of whole networks of IoT devices instead of singleôÙdevice
attestation, called Collective Attestation. However, most proposed Collective Attestation
schemes are synchronous and require the halting of the normal operations of IoT devices
in the network while running attestation. Some recent RA schemes attest not only the inôÙ
tegrity of the Provers themselves, but also the integrity of the data that is passed between
the Provers to avoid malicious devices from affecting uncompromised devices, or prevent
manôÙinôÙtheôÙmiddle attacks during attestation. Achieving Collective Attestation and attestôÙ
ing the integrity of passed data in asynchronous networks is challenging because of the
nonôÙlinear nature of the communication. In asynchronous, eventôÙdriven IoT systems, the
ordering of events exchanged between devices in the network is unpredictable, so it is
difficult to formulate a concrete picture of the exact order of events. Nevertheless, it is still
important to remotely attest these types of networks, because otherwise there is a security
gap between more traditional synchronous IoT networks and asynchronous ones.
To the best of the authorãs knowledge, there is currently no Remote Attestation scheme
for IoT devices that can attest both the communication data exchanged asynchronously
between devices and the controlôÙflow integrity of the Provers. A controlôÙflow attack in
one device may result in that device sending corrupt data to other, nonôÙinfected devices,
which impacts their legitimate operations. Therefore, combining these features into a
single protocol is important in modern IoT networks.

2.1
Overview of Remote Attestation
Remote attestation is a broad research field which includes various approaches that rely
on different device architectures, security requirements, and assumptions. There are sevôÙ
eral different techniques and categories of remote attestation, which may or may not be
mutually exclusive. This section aims to give some necessary background information
regarding the topic.
Remote attestation is a security technique where an untrusted device, called the Prover,
can prove to a remote trusted party, called the Verifier, that the software running on the
device is legitimate and is behaving in a nonôÙmalicious way. This technique has promising
applications in various scenarios, such as Commercial and Industrial IoT, sensor networks
and cyberôÙphysical systems. A typical remote attestation protocol begins with the Verifier
sending a unique challenge C to a Prover, which then securely computes evidence of
its software integrity S over the challenge, and then responds back with the evidence R.
This evidence can take various forms depending on the type of remote attestation being
done. An illustration of a simple RA protocol can be seen in figure 2.1. One of the simpler
forms of the evidence is a hash checksum over the deviceãs program memory, which the
Verifier can then compare with a legitimate checksum that has been preôÙcomputed and
stored. Other forms of evidence can include e.g. the state of data variables, readings
from sensors, inputs from other devices or controlôÙflow paths taken during execution.
Figure 2.1: Simple RA Protocol
Remote Attestation schemes can be broadly categorized into three distinct categories:
softwareôÙbased, hardwareôÙbased and hybrid approaches. SoftwareôÙbased schemes are
implemented completely in software and require strong assumptions to achieve security.
These schemes often use very strict time constraints for the Prover to respond to the
Verifierãs challenge. The general assumption of softwareôÙbased schemes is that an adôÙ
versary that tries to bypass the attestation would require extra computations and execute
extra instructions in order to avoid detection, thus requiring longer time for computing

the attestation evidence than is permissible by the protocol, leading to detection. These
schemes are impractical for realôÙworld scenarios because networks are unreliable and
it is not possible to know beforehand how much time it takes for a message to traverse
between network nodes due to congestion, variable network topologies, and various imôÙ
perfections in the transmission medium. One of the first proposed Remote Attestation
schemes, SWATT [9], proposed in 2004, is purely softwareôÙbased. It relies heavily on
precise timings to achieve security, and even if the protocol itself is not practical for realôÙ
world use, its conception sparked a wide range of other more practical schemes to be
proposed. Another example of a purely softwareôÙbased Remote Attestation scheme was
proposed by Shaneck et al. [10] in 2005. This scheme aims to improve upon SWATT
by depending less on precise timings by increasing the allowable time deviation of the
attestation response while still maintaining security. Although the scheme still uses a
responseôÙtime constraint to detect an adversary, the constraint can be loosened considôÙ
erably compared to SWATT. To achieve this, the Verifier (called Attester in the paper)
sends a randomized attestation routine to the Prover that is protected by various means
of obfuscation techniques. The goal is not to prevent an attacker entirely from breaking
the scheme, but rather to make any attack on the scheme so computationally intensive for
lowôÙresource devices such as sensors that the timing window for a response can not feasiôÙ
bly be met. This includes techniques such as including fake instructions in the attestation
routine, selfôÙmodifying code, encryption, and pointer aliasing.
HardwareôÙbased schemes, such as ReDAS [11] and LOôÙFAT [12], use specialized hardôÙ
ware to generate the attestation evidence and do not require the same strong assumpôÙ
tions as the softwareôÙbased approach. These schemes typically employ an external coôÙ
processor that can either intercept instructions in the processor pipeline, and/or compute
the evidence in an isolated manner. These coôÙprocessors typically have their own memôÙ
ory that the device cannot access, where attestation evidence and keys can be stored.
However, these schemes are not always practical to deploy in the real world since often
these external hardware modules, such as TPMs, are just as, or even more, expensive
than the device itself.
Hybrid schemes, like SEDA [13], SANA [14] and DARPA [15], aim to combine softwareôÙ
based and hardwareôÙbased approaches into one that achieves security without the strong
assumptions of softwareôÙbased schemes and the prohibitive cost and logistical problems
of purely hardwareôÙbased schemes. A hybrid scheme assumes minimal hardware supôÙ
port for providing a trust anchor on the device to store keys and attestation evidence
and execute trusted code securely. Typically, this minimal hardware support consists of
ReadôÙOnly Memory to store the attestation routine and a Memory Protection Unit to seôÙ
curely store the attestation evidence, while the rest of the attestation procedure is realized
with software. Some processors provide such a minimal hardware trust anchor natively,
like ARMãs TrustZone extension. Works aiming to provide a hybridôÙbased RA prototype
include TyTAN [16], SMART [17] and TrustLite [18].
2.1.1
Collective Attestation
SingleôÙdevice remote attestation schemes focus on the attestation of single Provers. In
a singleôÙdevice approach, a Verifier issues a challenge to a specific Prover, which comôÙ
putes the attestation evidence and returns it along with the challenge to the Verifier. This
is different from Collective Attestation, where the Verifier attests a whole network of deôÙ
vices instead of one device at a time. Collective attestation schemes aim to improve the
efficiency of attesting a group of interconnected devices. Depending on the network topolôÙ
ogy being static or dynamic, collective Remote Attestation schemes can be either static
or dynamic. Collective attestation can also be known as Swarm Attestation, and the two

2.1.2
Static Collective Attestation
SEDA [13] was proposed in 2015 by Asokan et al. as, to the best of our knowledge, the
first attestation scheme for device swarms. The protocol is split into two phases, an offline
and an online phase. The offline phase is executed only once and is when devices are
introduced into the swarm and initialized with required keys and data. SEDA assumes
that the network is static, thus it constructs the network topology as a spanningôÙtree, in
which the devices have a parentôÙchild relationship. The online phase consists of two
subôÙprotocols called attest and attdev. When a Verifier wants to attest the swarm, an
attest message is sent to an arbitrary device in the swarm, which will then distribute the
message to its children, who will in turn recursively distribute the message to their own
children using the attdev message. Eventually, the initial device receives the accumulated
attestation result from the swarm and can report it back to the Verifier. However, this result
only contains the number of devices that passed attestation, and cannot inform the Verifier
of the identities of compromised devices.
The SEDA protocol was later extended by Ambrosin et al. who proposed SANA [14] in
2016. The goal of SANA is to achieve collective attestation under realistic assumptions.
While SEDA assumes that aggregator devices are trusted, SANA releases this assumpôÙ
tion by introducing a novel multiôÙsignature scheme, enabling untrusted aggregation of
attestation evidence. Essentially, the multiôÙsignature scheme allows the Verifier to detect
if a device that participates in the aggregation and forwarding of the attestation evidence
has maliciously attempted to alter the evidence in some way.
DARPA [15] was proposed in 2016 by Ahmad et al. as an extension to SEDA that makes
the scheme resilient to physical attacks. The scheme leverages absence detection to
detect if a device has been absent from the network for some time. It assumes that a
physical attacker would need to shut down or disconnect the device from the network for
a nonôÙnegligible amount of time to carry out a physical attack. This scheme will detect
that absence and flag the device as potentially compromised. It achieves this by using a
heartbeat protocol where each device regularly broadcasts a secure heartbeat message
to its neighbours. When a device receives a heartbeat from its neighbour, it securely logs
that the heartbeat was received, and forwards the heartbeat to its own neighbours. At
the time of attestation, these heartbeat logs are provided to the Verifier in addition to the
normal attestation report generated by the SEDA approach.
LISAs [19] was proposed in 2017 by Carpent et al. and is a scheme where each Prover
attests itself individually, and then collaborates with other Provers to forward their own and
othersã attestation evidence results. It introduces a new metric called QoSA (Quality of
Swarm Attestation) that aims to give some information about the quality of the attestation
of the network. This metric also opens the door to comparing the efficacy of multiple
protocols. In the same paper, LISAöÝ was also proposed as the asynchronous version of
LISAs, which will be described later in section 2.1.5.
2.1.3
Dynamic Collective Attestation
The aforementioned collective attestation schemes all assume the attested network is
static and connected. These schemes construct the network as a static spanning tree
which does not change during attestation. For dynamic networks with nodes that can
move, join or leave the network arbitrarily, the static RA schemes will not work. To reôÙ
lease this constraint, dynamic swarm attestation schemes were introduced. PADS [20],
proposed by Ambrosin et al. in 2018, solves the issue of attesting dynamic swarms of deôÙ
vices. This scheme leverages a distributed consensus mechanism to allow the devices

PADS, SALAD [21], was proposed by KohnhûÊuser et al. in 2018. It can mitigate physical

of dynamic networks is different from dynamic attestation, detailed in section 2.1.6.
2.1.4
Distributed Attestation
Centralized schemes are the most typical in Remote Attestation, where a centralized,
trusted Verifier handles the verification of the attestation evidence. Some have argued
that this presents a performance bottleneck, is a singleôÙpointôÙofôÙfailure and the assumpôÙ
tion that the Verifier has unlimited computing power and is always trusted are very strong
assumptions. To rule out these assumptions, decentralized schemes decentralize the
verification among the Provers, where each Prover acts as a verifier for its neighbours.
However, such schemes are more complex than centralized schemes and shift the reôÙ

with limited computational power and battery life. USôÙAID [22] is a distributed attestaôÙ
tion scheme where an unattended network is able to attest itself. Devices in the network
mutually attest each other, keeping a log of the result, which when aggregated together
provides an indicator of the health of the network. ESDRA [23], proposed by Kuang et
al. in 2019 is an Efficient and Secure Distributed Remote Attestation Scheme for IoT
Swarms. A credit value is assigned to each device and is dependent on each attestation
routine they are involved in. Nodes with low credit values are then reported to the network
owner who can then investigate further and take appropriate action. Note that Remote
Attestation schemes such as these with distributed Verifiers are different from the attesôÙ
tation of distributed services, such as SARA [24] and the protocol proposed in this thesis,
ARCADIS.
2.1.5
Asynchronous Attestation
Synchronous remote attestation schemes are the most common type of scheme today.
In a synchronous scheme, the communication between the Verifier and the Prover, or beôÙ
tween multiple Provers, is synchronous. This means that when a device makes a request
to another, for example a Verifier sending a challenge to a Prover, or a Prover forwardôÙ
ing aggregate swarm attestation evidence to another Prover, the device must stop and
wait for an answer before it can continue its normal operations. Asynchronous schemes,
on the other hand, utilize asynchronous communication constructs, such as message
queues, to communicate. This eliminates the need for a device to await a response, and
it can continue its normal operation immediately after computing its own local attestaôÙ
tion evidence. LISAöÝ [19], proposed in 2017, is a version of the aforementioned LISAs
scheme, where the collaboration between Provers happens asynchronously. SARA [24]
was proposed by Dushku et al. in 2020 and is the first proposed scheme that can atôÙ
test distributed IoT services asynchronously. Previous distributed services attestation
schemes [25] require the whole network to halt while computing the collective attestation
result of the network. SARA releases this constraint for asynchronous networks using the
publishôÙsubscribe paradigm, making attestation of asynchronous IoT services possible.
The difference between LISAöÝ and SARA is that SARA makes no assumptions about the
topology or connectedness of the device swarm, whereas LISAöÝ requires that the swarm
remains connected and quasiôÙstatic during attestation, though the topology may change
between any two attestations. QuasiôÙStatic topology means that the topology may change
during attestation, but in a way that does not affect the attestation itself, for example if a
link between devices disappears after sending a message, but then reôÙappears before
any other messages are exchanged between the same pair of devices.

2.1.6
Dynamic Attestation
Based on the memory regions considered by a Remote Attestation protocol, Remote
Attestation schemes can be classified into a static or dynamic category. This category
should not be confused with dynamic collective attestation, where the network topology
can change. Static schemes only attest the static properties of the software running on
the device, like the program binary residing in memory, while dynamic schemes take
into account the dynamic runtime state of the device, such as data variables or controlôÙ
flow information. ControlôÙFlow Attestation falls under the Dynamic category, since it foôÙ
cuses on attesting the correct runtime controlôÙflow of the program running on the device.
CôÙFLAT [26], proposed in 2016, is the first controlôÙflow Remote Attestation scheme for
resourceôÙconstrained devices. CôÙFLAT attests the controlôÙflow integrity of the software
running on a single device and can detect attacks such as ReturnôÙOriented Programming
attacks [27, 28] which do not modify the program code itself, and are therefore unable
to be detected by static remote attestation protocols. CôÙFLAT was later extended by LOôÙ
FAT [12] to address the issue of performance by leveraging specialized hardware comôÙ
ponents on the Provers to perform attestation evidence generation in isolation, and to
avoid requiring software instrumentation of the program binary (software instrumentation
is described in section 2.5).
2.2
Root of Trust
Remote Attestation schemes are designed such that a remote trusted party can ãaskã an
untrusted device for evidence of its integrity. An important problem that must be considôÙ
ered is that if a device is in fact compromised, how can the integrity of the evidence itself be
ensured and the measurements performed on the device to be trusted? As discussed in
section 2.1, remote attestation schemes can be broadly categorized into softwareôÙbased,
hardwareôÙbased and hybrid. While softwareôÙbased schemes often make use of strict time
constraints for the evidence to be reported to solve this problem, hardwareôÙbased and hyôÙ
brid schemes leverage a root of trust that is present on the device to provide a trust anchor
for securely ensuring the integrity of the attestation evidence.
There are several different techniques for achieving a root of trust on a device. The most
general way of achieving a root of trust is for the device to feature readôÙonly memory
(ROM) for securely storing the attestation routine, and a memory protection unit (MPU)
for securely controlling read/write access to attestation evidence and signing keys to
only be accessible by the attestation routine.
Some frameworks for achieving a root
of trust using these primitives have been proposed, including SMART [17], TyTAN [16]
and TrustLite [18]. In addition, some processors offer a root of trust implementation naôÙ
tively, such as ARMãs TrustZone extension [29] and Intelãs Software Guard Extension [30].
Trusted Platform Modules (TPMs) provide a standalone hardware rootôÙofôÙtrust. TPMs are
typically a chip that offers functionalities such as secure key storage, secure signing and
in some cases limited data storage capabilities. TPMs are widely used for larger systems
such as servers, personal computers, and larger embedded platforms such as Raspberry
Pi 1, however they are rarely used in IoT devices because of the cost of the chips themôÙ
selves often being too great, potentially even surpassing the cost of manufacturing the
IoT device itself.

2.4
ControlôÙFlow and NonôÙControl data attacks
Runtime attacks can be broadly categorized into two types, namely controlôÙflow attacks
and data attacks. ControlôÙFlow attacks aim to divert the controlôÙflow of a program away
from the intended controlôÙflow graph. This includes attacks such as ReturnôÙOriented ProôÙ
gramming (ROP), where the program is forced into executing existing soôÙcalled code gadôÙ
gets that are already present somewhere in executable memory, for example in linked
libraries. This is often achieved by exploiting a buffer overflow vulnerability in the tarôÙ
get program, such that the attacker can overwrite the return address of the current stack
frame with an address of a gadget, forcing the program to execute the gadget when the
currently running function returns. To launch a useful ROP attack, several gadgets must
be chained together. To do this, the attacker does not only overwrite the return address
of the current stack frame, but must also insert the addresses of other gadgets behind it
in sequence and ensures that all the gadgets end in a return statement. This way, when
one gadget finishes and executes a return, the next address that the attacker inserted is
popped off the stack into the instruction pointer register and the program is forced to run
the next gadget. Using this technique, the attacker can chain together almost any string
of instructions and can often force the program to do anything they want.

assumptions are outlined in detail in the CôÙFLAT paper [26].

2.6.1
Publish/Subscribe Mechanism
A wellôÙknown paradigm for asynchronous communication between services is the PubôÙ
lish/Subscribe paradigm. Using this paradigm, each entity in the system takes the role
of a Publisher, a Subscriber, or both. Publishers are responsible for publishing data to
topics. This data, in the context of IoT networks, is typically sensor data read from the
environment, and the topic may represent the type of sensor data. Subscribers are reôÙ
sponsible for subscribing to topics to receive the types of messages they are interested
in. Zero or more subscribers may be subscribed to any particular topic. A device can
also take on both the roles of a publisher and a subscriber simultaneously. In this case, a
device may be subscribed to a particular topic, perform some computations or generate
some data once a message is received, and then publish the result to other topics for other
subscribers to receive. Typically, there is an extra entity that is responsible for the filtering
and delivery of messages to topics, called the Broker. Subscribers will notify the Broker
that they are interested in a certain topic, and the Broker will then record that information.
The Publishers then publish messages to a Broker, which then handles the delivery to
any subscribers interested in the topic. Using a broker introduces a potential bottleneck
in larger systems and is a singleôÙpointôÙofôÙfailure. To combat this, the idea of distributed
brokers or even brokerôÙless publish/subscribe systems has been proposed, but systems
such as those present various challenges, one of which is the need for a fixed overlay

Gaining a common consensus about the order of events in a distributed system is not a

3.4
Snowball Sampling
Applying the exclusion/inclusion criteria results in a starting set of papers that are highly
relevant to the topic. However, it is unlikely that the initial query was able to find all of the
relevant papers on the subject and thus snowball sampling was performed.
Snowball sampling is an iterative process where the goal is to find more relevant papers
than the initial query can provide through references and citations. In each iteration, a
set of newly discovered papers is constructed and then the next iteration uses that set.
If no new papers are found, then the snowballing procedure is complete. The general
procedure used here is the one outlined in [38].
In each iteration, Forward Snowball Sampling is performed first on the iterative set of
papers. For each paper, its reference list is examined and the same inclusion/exclusion
criteria as above is applied. Any papers that survive the application of the criteria are
put into the next iterative set. Then, for each paper in the set again, Backward Snowball
Sampling is performed. Here, we find all the papers that cite the current paper and apply
the same inclusion/exclusion criteria on those, repeating the same process as for the forôÙ
ward sampling. To find papers that cite the current paper, the paper was found in Google
Scholar and the ãCited By Xã feature was used to get a list of citing papers. The iteration
stops when there are no more papers in the current iterative set to examine. The next
iteration then repeats the process for the newly created set of discovered papers. When
no new papers are found during an iteration, then the snowball sampling is complete. A
visual representation of this process can be seen in figure 3.1.
This process allows to identify a huge number of relevant papers and gives a very good
overview of the existing literature about the topic. It is important to strike a good balance
between inclusiveness and exclusion during the process. Using criteria that are too reôÙ
strictive will significantly lower the number of papers found, and also risks finding only a
small number of clusters of papers where the authors cite each other. Being able to esôÙ
cape the cluster to find other clusters is important for good coverage. On the other hand,
being too lax with the criteria will result in too many irrelevant papers to review, lowering
the efficiency of the procedure dramatically.
After 3 iterations, no more new papers could be identified. A total number of 15 new
papers were identified as relevant during the snowball sampling, bringing the total number
up to 24.

3.8.1
ControlôÙFlow Integrity
ControlôÙFlow Integrity (CFI) is a security property that is fulfilled only if the program in
question strictly follows the ControlôÙFlow Graph (CFG), as defined in the source code. A
program that fulfills the CFI property can be considered wellôÙbehaved regarding its conôÙ
trol flow. However, even though a program fulfills the CFI property, it does not guarantee
that malicious actors have not compromised the program. For example, an adversary
replacing a basic block (a node in the CFG) with malicious code will not break the proôÙ
gramãs CFI because the controlôÙflow has not been changed. A malicious actor may also
perform a soôÙcalled nonôÙcontrolôÙdata attack, where data is corrupted without deviating the
programãs controlôÙflow. Therefore, CFI does not guarantee that the program has not been
compromised ôÙ it only provides a way to detect that a programãs control flow has deviated
from the intended flow.
The earliest work, to the best of the authorãs knowledge, on ControlôÙFlow Integrity (CFI)
was done by Abadi et al. [39]. In this paper, the concept of CFI was first introduced and
several different techniques presented on enforcement of CFI using software instrumenôÙ
tation. An implementation of inlined CFI enforcement for a Windows operating system is
also presented and measured.
MoCFI [40] was proposed by Davi et al. in 2012 as a framework that provides a general
countermeasure against controlôÙflow attacks on smartphones using the ARM architecture
by enforcing CFI. The paper states that other CFI enforcement schemes do exist for x86ôÙ
based architectures (such as desktop PCs), and some adôÙhoc solutions exist for ARM
architectures, but to the best of the authorãs knowledge, MoCFI is the first general CFI enôÙ
forcement framework for ARM devices. The paper lists some challenges of achieving CFI
on ARM architectures. For instance, since ARM architectures do not have dedicated reôÙ
turn instructions, applications can directly access and modify the program counter. There
is also the challenge of ARM architectures using two instruction sets (namely ARM and
THUMB), and the processor can change between these instruction sets during runtime,
even interleaving them. MoCFI requires no knowledge of the original source code, and it
works by statically analyzing the program binary during load time, generating a CFG, and
then performing controlôÙflow validation routines during runtime by library injection and inôÙ
memory code patching techniques. For backwardôÙedge integrity, MoCFI uses a shadow
stack concept, a second stack kept in secure memory used to keep track of valid return
addresses. When a return address is popped from the stack to return control to a caller,
a return address is also popped off the shadow stack, and the two are compared. If they
do not match, a controlôÙflow deviation has happened. For forwardôÙedge integrity, MoCFI
uses a list of valid target addresses, computed during static analysis, that it compares
each jump instruction with to validate it. Although the scheme is not strictly designed with
IoT in mind, it is still interesting because it is the first general CFI enforcement scheme for
ARM processors, which are widely used by IoT devices. In addition, it requires no knowlôÙ
edge of source code and can be transparently applied to individual applications, which in
an IoT context is desirable since it can be deployed on systems that are already in the
field. However, it is unclear how this exact approach may be transferred to IoT devices
since it leverages some UnixôÙonly constructs, such as preloading shared libraries on load
time of an application using an environment variable. It is also unclear where e.g. the
shadow stack is kept such that it is not modifiable by an attacker, however as this scheme
is designed for ARM architectures, it could be possible to leverage TrustZone to provide
a trust anchor to keep secure data in.
HAFIX [41] was proposed by Davi et al. in 2015 as a fineôÙgrained backwardôÙedge CFI
system in hardware. The paper presents proofôÙofôÙconcept implementations on two proôÙ

applications, and the LEON3 microprocessor. The paper argues that the shadow stack
approach that is used by other schemes to detect backwardôÙedge controlôÙflow violations
has significant performance overhead, and can result in false positives for some language
constructs such as C++ exception stack unwinding. Instead of using a shadow stack,
HAFIX confines all function returns to active call sites, that is, a function may not return
to any place where a function has not been invoked in the current execution context. As
explained in the paper, ãwe force a return to target a callôÙpreceded instruction inside a

SOFIA [42], proposed by Clercq et al. in 2016, is a CFI architecture. This scheme preôÙ

with controlôÙflow dependent information during compileôÙtime. Doing this, if an attacker
attempts to mount a codeôÙreuse attack or to execute some injected code, at least one

program to crash. In addition to this, this scheme uses a Software Integrity mechanism

although different from the intended one, the Software Integrity mechanism will prevent
the instruction from being run. This scheme is hardwareôÙbased and upon detection of an
integrity violation, it reboots the processor to prevent execution of the malicious code.
CaRE [43] by Nyman et al.
was proposed in 2017 as a callôÙandôÙreturn enforcement

scheme for lowôÙend MCUs. Like most other CFI schemes, this paper focuses on preventôÙ

hardware since it uses ARM TrustZone technology as its trust anchor and operates directly
on binary code, thus requiring no source code.
Kuzhiyeli et al. [44] proposed in 2020 a CFI scheme suitable for safetyôÙcritical CyberôÙ

used to feed controlôÙflow information into the CFI monitor. Periodically, the applicationãs
current state is analyzed and the CFG is reconstructed and compared against the correct
CFG. The paper mentions that this scheme has a significant performance overhead, but
argues that this may be an acceptable tradeôÙoff for reliably enforcing CFI for safetyôÙcritical
systems that cannot be instrumented or invasively modified in any way.
3.8.2
ControlôÙFlow Attestation
These ControlôÙFlow Integrity (CFI) techniques can be applied to IoT devices to achieve
controlôÙflow integrity enforcement, but the main goal of these techniques is to prevent the
controlôÙflow hijack from happening in the first place. The way many of these techniques
realize this protection is to immediately shut down, reset or crash the device upon detecôÙ
tion of a controlôÙflow deviation [40, 42, 43, 45]. This is not ideal for many applications,
including safetyôÙcritical IoT devices and networks, because it effectively trades a controlôÙ
flow hijacking attack for a denialôÙofôÙservice attack. An attacker may no longer be able
to hijack the control flow of a process or device, but this enables the attacker to cause
unauthorized shutdowns instead. ControlôÙFlow Attestation aims not to prevent the occurôÙ
==PAGE:30==
3.8. FINDINGS
23
rence of a controlôÙflow hijack event, but rather to detect it so that appropriate action can be
taken either manually or automatically. For example, an infected device could be isolated
from the network, or a new device provisioned to take over the compromised deviceãs
responsibilities before terminating the compromised device.
CôÙFLAT [26] was proposed by Abera et al. in 2016 as the first complete controlôÙflow
attestation protocol. The Verifier is able to detect controlôÙflow deviations by computing
an accumulated hash of each possible controlôÙflow path taken in a controlôÙflow graph
beforehand in an offline phase. A hash engine computes a single hash value from the
aggregate of all the recorded instructions and securely sends it to the Verifier. Having
preôÙcomputed all valid hash values, the Verifier can then detect a controlôÙflow deviation
if it receives an unexpected hash value. The scheme assumes that provers feature Data
Execution Prevention (DEP) or some type of static attestation is run alongside the CôÙFLAT
protocol, and a trust anchor to provide an isolated measurement engine. This proposal
can detect runtime attacks that cause controlôÙflow deviations on single devices. Still, it is
not practical for complex software with large controlôÙflow graphs because the number of
valid hashes increases very quickly for complex software, leading to a state explosion of
possible valid hashes that need to be stored on the Verifier.
LOôÙFAT [12] was proposed in 2017 by Dessouky et al. to improve CôÙFLAT in some ways.
Instead of the softwareôÙbased approach of CôÙFLAT, requiring instrumentation of the runôÙ
ning code, LOôÙFAT instead leverages a hardware coôÙprocessor to record the instructions
taken. LOôÙFAT uses branch tracking functionality that is inherent in any processor pipeline
to record the instructions. This eliminates the need for software instrumentation and thus
eliminates some of the overhead of CôÙFLAT. In addition, this makes the attestation proôÙ
cess transparent to any legacy application, as long as the coôÙprocessor can be attached
to the device. Although it is a big improvement over CôÙFLAT, this approach requires speôÙ
cialized hardware to be installed on every device to be attested, and still does not solve
the state explosion problem on the Verifier.
ATRIUM [46], proposed by Zeitouni et al. in 2017, aims to solve the Time of Check Time of
Use (ToCToU) problem and targets bareôÙmetal embedded systems software. The ToCToU
problem involves the distance in time between attestation and malicious software being
present or executing on the device. That is, a sophisticated malicious payload may be
able to relocate itself to other devices while the infected device is being attested, or someôÙ
how hide, and then reôÙappear after a successful attestation. The scheme also allows for
a stronger adversary than previous schemes that has physical access to the deviceãs proôÙ
gram memory, aiming to detect some physical attacks on the Prover. The authors identify
and implement a memory bank attack on SMART [17], CôÙFLAT [26] and LOôÙFAT [12] that
those protocols cannot detect, but ATRIUM can. The memory bank attack assumes that
the attestation code and secret keys are stored onôÙchip, but the benign program code
resides in external memory. One way this attack can be carried out is by replacing the
original memory interface with a memory controller, and then direct instruction fetches to
either benign code when attestation is running, or malicious code otherwise. The authors
show different techniques to mount this attack on each of the aboveôÙmentioned protoôÙ
cols. ATRIUM is a hardwareôÙbased scheme that requires no software instrumentation
but does require a separate hardware module that runs in parallel to the processor on
the Prover and handles the attestation. The proposed protocol can successfully defend
against physical attacks where the adversary tries to manipulate the program at runtime.
In addition since every instruction run by the Prover is included in the hash generation,
it can prevent ToCToU attacks. However, requiring a separate hardware module makes
this scheme impractical for alreadyôÙdeployed IoT services, and increases the cost of the

LiteHAX [47] was proposed in 2018 by Dessouky et al. as a remote attestation scheme
that can detect both controlôÙflow attacks such as ReturnôÙOriented Programming, and
also nonôÙcontrol data attacks that do not deviate a programãs control flow such as DataôÙ
Oriented Programming. This approach is minimally invasive and does not require inôÙ
strumentation of code, modifications to the processor or extensions to the instruction set
architecture. This approach also mitigates the controlôÙflow path state explosion problem
by not generating a database of all the valid aggregate hash values that a legitimate proôÙ

the Prover and encodes it in a bitstream. This bitstream is then periodically sent to the
Verifier for analysis. The Verifier then stores the last received state of execution and conôÙ

by segment on the valid CFG, verifying that all controlôÙflow transitions are legitimate up to
that point. However, this approach only works for RISCôÙbased architectures, as it focuses
on the detection of memory access operation corruption. On RISCôÙbased architectures,
the only operations that can access memory are the load and store operations. In adôÙ

systemôÙonôÙchip design. Furthermore, a continuous online phase between the Verifier and
Prover is required, increasing communication overhead.
Conti et al. proposed [48] in 2018 as a collective remote attestation scheme which aims
to provide complete evidence of the integrity of a device in an IoT network. In addition, the
paper argues that it is not enough to only attest the software of single devices within the
network but also the data flow between the IoT devices forming an IoT service. For examôÙ

from another compromised device that forces it to perform an unauthorized or unexpected
operation. The proposal uses the CôÙFLAT [26] scheme to run controlôÙflow attestation on
single devices and extends it to support the verification of the runtime state of the whole
distributed service.
SALAD [21] was proposed by KohnhûÊuser et al. in 2018 as a protocol for highly dyôÙ

to attesting the network, where Provers within range of each other mutually attest each

shared attestation result value. The paper claims to mitigate physical attacks because an
adversary cannot fake valid attestation evidence for devices that are not physically comôÙ
promised. Because each device in the network has a unique key to sign the attestation
evidence, and the devices collaborate on the attestation, the paper argues that scaling
physical attacks to a large network is uneconomical and unfeasible. The authors of the
paper did not specifically design the protocol to perform either static or dynamic remote
attestation, and deliberately abstracted this away using a VerSW(vss) function located on
the Provers, which can be implemented to do any sort of attestation, including controlôÙflow
attestation. However, the authors did not provide any concrete implementation or ideas
on how to implement this function and rather focused on the issue of attesting dynamic
and disruptive networks.

DIAT [33], proposed by Abera et al. in 2019, aims to verify the correctness of data exôÙ
changed by devices in an IoT network . It attests both the generation and processing of
the data using controlôÙflow attestation and messages exchanged are authenticated along
with a proof of integrity. The software is decomposed into simple interacting modules,
and only those modules that process the data are attested, thus reducing the complexity
of the attestation procedure. This technique is named Modular Attestation in the paper.
They also aim to minimize the verification overhead by not requiring the Verifier to keep a
database of all valid paths taken in the CFG. Instead, the Verifier must only know the CFG
itself. This is achieved by designing an execution path representation based on multiset

and monolithic applications such as small embedded sensors are not the focus of DIAT.
MGCôÙFA [50], proposed by Hu et al. in 2019, probabilistically divides the program being

fineôÙgrained level, where the whole control flow inside the function is attested and comôÙ

only the calling and returning events inside the function are checked. To determine which
functions in the program are to be considered vulnerable, the authors make use of a
machine learning model to predict the vulnerable probability for each function. Then a
tweakable probability threshold is used to classify each function as vulnerable or normal.
The authors show that their scheme is successful in reducing the overhead for a certain
probability threshold, but do admit some drawbacks. For instance, this scheme is heavily
focused on functions as the base unit, so for languages such as Java that do not use
functions as their base unit, MGCôÙFA cannot be applied.

DOôÙRA [53] was proposed by Kuang et al. in 2020 as a remote attestation scheme that can

a static programôÙrewriting attack, a forwardôÙedge controlôÙflow attack and two constrainingôÙ

modified), and the other one being a nonôÙcontrolôÙflow altering data attack (a variable is
maliciously modified such that a valid, but unintended, path is taken in the CFG). This
scheme can detect controlôÙflow attacks, dataôÙoriented attacks, and also static attacks
such as code injection.

A widely used technique for ensuring backwardôÙedge controlôÙflow integrity in CFI schemes
is using a Shadow Stack. This technique involves keeping a secure copy of the stack of
return addresses. Each time a function is invoked and its return address is pushed on the
stack, the same return address is simultaneously pushed to the shadow stack residing
in secure memory. Once the function returns control to the caller by popping the return
address off the stack into the program counter, the shadow stack is also popped and
the addresses compared. If the addresses do not match, a backwardôÙedge controlôÙflow
integrity violation has been detected. This technique is effective in preventing ReturnôÙ
Oriented Programming (ROP) attacks and is used in both CFI enforcement schemes and
CFA schemes. However, this technique relies on every function call being matched by
a function return, which is not always the case in all languages. For example, C++ uses
a stack unwinding approach for exception handling, and using this technique in such an
environment would result in false positives [41]. This technique is not widely used in
CFA schemes, but is more often utilized in CFI enforcement schemes which monitor the
controlôÙflow path continuously. An example of a CFA scheme that does use a shadow
stack is [51]. In this scheme, a shadow stack is used due to the imprecision of backôÙ
ward controlôÙflow modeling using static analysis. Without a shadow stack, the Verifier
would need to match the callôÙandôÙreturn of the log, which would increase computational
expenses on the Verifier side by a lot, since this scheme continuously records the log.
A recent technique for achieving Remote Attestation is leveraging Formally Verified Proofs
of Execution, as defined and outlined in [55]. This technique lends itself well to very lowôÙ
power, resourceôÙconstrained and lowôÙcost devices like sensor motes, since the hardware
requirement for leveraging this technique is extremely minimal.
Most controlôÙflow attestation schemes for IoT devices today require software instrumenôÙ
tation of the program binary to be able to trace the controlôÙflow. CFA schemes which do
not use software instrumentation must instead rely on external hardware that can inteôÙ
grate or connect to the processor pipeline to intercept instructions. An exception to that
is Kuzhiyeli et al. [44], which uses the ARM CoreSight tracer/debugger to feed controlôÙ
flow information to the CFI monitor. While utilizing external hardware instead of software
instrumentation results in less performance overhead and is less invasive, it can be a
==PAGE:35==
3.9. DISCUSSION
28
prohibitively expensive investment to make, especially for IoT devices that have already
been deployed in the field.
Software Instrumentation is typically implemented with a technique called Trampolines.
Here, each relevant controlôÙflowôÙchanging instruction in the program binary is replaced by
an instruction that diverts the controlôÙflow to a soôÙcalled dispatcher, whose responsibility is
to save the execution state of the program, call the appropriate secure attestation routine,
then restoring the state of execution and handing control to the called function.
Being able to detect or prevent a controlôÙflow attack implies that the scheme can also
detect or prevent some types of static attacks where the program binary is modified. In
particular, if an adversary modifies the program binary in any way that either changes the
controlôÙflow of the program, or modifies the trampolines such that the detected controlôÙflow
path is deviated, any CFI/CFA scheme will detect this attack. However, if an adversary is
able to modify a basic block and avoid using any controlôÙflow altering instructions, CFI/CFA
schemes will generally not be able to detect the attack. Some schemes that record every
single instruction, like [46], will be able to detect this type of attack. Some schemes, like
[47], are also able to detect nonôÙcontrol data attacks which do not deviate the controlôÙflow
of the program, and if an adversary modifies a basic block such that some data is changed
without deviating the controlôÙflow, these kinds of schemes will also detect the attack.
It is clear from existing literature that the problem of attesting distributed IoT services
asynchronously is still open. Most ControlôÙFlow Attestation schemes are only designed
for singleôÙdevice attestation and do not consider attesting device swarms or networks as
a whole. The few schemes that tackle Collective Attestation [33, 21, 25] do not consider
asynchronous communication, and only one [25] considers distributed IoT services at all.
CFA in IoT is a topic that is gaining some traction and increasing interest. CFA in general
is a relatively new concept and as such there are not a lot of published works on the
subject, and even fewer that are applicable/designed for the IoT realm. The topic of CFA
in IoT has much research potential and should be studied more.

ARCADIS
Given the limitations of current static remote attestation protocols, this project proposes
Asynchronous Remote ControlôÙFlow Attestation of Distributed IoT Services (ARCADIS),
a Remote Attestation scheme which aims to enable asynchronous controlôÙflow attestaôÙ
tion of distributed IoT services. Overall, ARCADIS verifies the legitimate controlôÙflow of
every service participating in a network of distributed services, the correct order of service
invocation, and exchanged communication data.
This chapter presents the protocol details and defines the security requirements, system
model and adversary model. In addition, this chapter discusses how the protocol achieves
the defined security requirements, some limitations and how they can potentially be overôÙ
come.
4.1
Project Scope
The scope of this thesis is to propose a protocol that can remotely attest the legitimate
controlôÙflow of asynchronous IoT services. This includes attesting the legitimate operation
of each service, along with attesting the legitimate interactions between the services.
The focus is on verifying the controlôÙflow integrity of a network of distributed services,
i.e. protect against attacks that deviate the controlôÙflow or legitimate operations of the
distributed services. The goal is to attest distributed IoT services, so it is out of scope for
this thesis to perform a complete attestation of a group of IoT devices.
This project focuses on detecting remote software adversaries which exploit vulnerabilities
to perform runtime attacks. In this context, physical attacks are not considered in the
proposed RA approach. This assumption is in line with other stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart RA schemes
and is considered reasonable, since a majority of remote attestation schemes do not
consider physical attacks at all, and only a handful consider very limited types of physical
attacks.
4.2
System Model
This project considers distributed IoT systems that use asynchronous communication
mechanisms to exchange messages, commands, and data. In particular, the project conôÙ
siders the publish/subscribe paradigm as the communication mechanism among devices.
The publish/subscribe paradigm is popular in IoT due to the asynchronous nature of the
mechanism and the nonôÙreliance of static topologies. More detailed characteristics of the
publish/subscribe pattern are described in section 2.6.1.
1. Devices (D): A physical resourceôÙconstrained device that is involved in the IoT netôÙ
work. Each device may run one or more services.
2. Services (SRV ): A software module running on a physical device D. A service is an
entity that subscribes to topics and publishes messages to other services. A service
can be given some input. This input can come from the device itself (external input,
such as a sensor), a topic that the service is subscribed to (system input), or some
combination of both. The service then performs some computation and may or may
not publish the results to a topic for other services to consume. It is assumed that
publishing services always perform the publish as the last action of the program flow,
i.e., a service cannot publish any results during a computation (see section 4.5.2 for
ControlôÙFlow Attestation of Asynchronous Distributed IoT Services
32
==PAGE:40==
4.3. ADVERSARY MODEL
33
reasoning). This is a realistic assumption in the context of distributed IoT services,
since if a service needs to publish a result while still computing another result, it can
be split up into two distinct services.
3. Verifier (VRF): An external, trusted party that handles the verification of attestation
evidence. It is assumed that VRF is trusted, knows the legitimate controlôÙflow graph
of each individual service, and has preôÙcomputed all the valid hashes that can proôÙ
duce the execution of a given service. In addition, it has preôÙcomputed all legitimate
service flows and knows all legitimate interactions between the services. This asôÙ
sumption is realistic and used by other similar schemes [24], since publish/subscribe
protocols usually expose an interface for handling and observing the subscription
process.
4. Prover (PRV): A device D which the VRF challenges to prove its integrity. Each
PRV has the capability of handling an attestation challenge and publish its attestation
evidence.
5. Operator (OP): The owner and/or operator of the distributed IoT system. OP is
responsible for securely bootstrapping the software deployed on each Di and for
securely distributing keys between devices at the beginning of the operation of the
IoT system.
The protocol consists of two phasesëƒ the offline phase and the online phase. In the offline
phase, the network is set up and VRF takes a measurement of each SRV in the netôÙ
work and stores it in a database. VRF also stores the legitimate service interactions in its
database, such as the expected order of invocation. For the online phase, consider figure
4.1. VRF begins the attestation at time T0 by sending an attestation request message to a
publisher (step 1 ). The publisher computes its Local Attestation Evidence (LAE) by runôÙ
ning the CôÙFLAT [26] protocol to get a hash value representing the execution path taken,
and then computes the Global Attestation Evidence (GAE) by concatenating LAE with the
input received, output produced and its vector clock (step 2 ). GAE is then published
to the subscriber along with the output data (step 3 ). The subscriber now computes its
own LAE as the publisher did, and also computes its own GAE as before, except now
concatenating the previous GAE as well (step 4 ). This interaction can happen multiple
times, depending on how many services are present in the service flow. At a later time
T1, VRF sends a challenge to one or more subscribers (the last service in each service
flow) requesting the attestation evidence (step 5 ) and the subscriber responds with the
challenge and the complete evidence of the service flow (step 6 ).
4.3
Adversary Model
Two overarching types of adversaries are considered: A software adversary (ADVsw) and
a mobile adversary (ADVmob). An adversary with physical access to the device, ADVphy,
is not considered. This assumption is reasonable and in line with the vast majority of other
remote attestation schemes. ADVsw has complete control over the data memory of the
device and can launch attacks such as ReturnôÙOriented Programming (ROP) attacks. The
mobile adversary ADVmob is a powerful adversary that is able to move between different
devices and services in the IoT system in order to avoid detection. ADVsw and ADVmob
have three main goals in mind.
1. An adversary may launch an attack which deviates the controlôÙflow of the legitimate
program on the device. This may be any type of memoryôÙbased attack, such as
code injection or ROP.
==PAGE:41==

2. An adversary may attack a vulnerable service in order to maliciously affect the leôÙ
gitimate operation of another, less vulnerable service. This can be achieved for
example by publishing false or malicious data to the less vulnerable service, or to
act as a manôÙinôÙtheôÙmiddle to manipulate data that is inôÙtransit.
3. An adversary may listen in on the communication between devices and extract old
attestation messages in order to replay them later.
It is assumed that each device has a trust anchor that allows the hash engine to run in
isolation such that it cannot be disabled or modified by the softwareôÙbased adversary. This
can be a native offering, e.g. ARMãs TrustZone extension [29], or it can be as minimal as
some ReadôÙOnly Memory (ROM) to store the attestation routine and hash engine, and a
Memory Protection Unit (MPU) to restrict access to signing keys and other data to only
the attestation routine.
Physical attacks are not considered. Since ADVphy can invasively probe or replace the
whole memory contents, an attacker such as this is able to gain access to signing keys
and replace the attestation routine with one of their own. This assumption is reasonable
and in line with other RA schemes. The TimeôÙofôÙCheckôÙTimeôÙofôÙUse (ToCToU) attack
is not considered. An adversary may infect a device at some point, but erase itself or
return the device to normal operation before the attestation happens. To consider such
an adversary, continuous monitoring of the controlôÙflow must be performed, which is a big
drain on resources and battery life for resourceôÙconstrained devices. DenialôÙofôÙService
attacks (DoS) are not considered. An adversary can flood the network with messages and
prevent legitimate messages from getting through the network, or jam the radio frequency
of the communication medium such that the devices can not communicate. These types
of attacks are notoriously difficult to mitigate and are typically not considered in other RA
schemes.
4.4
Security Requirements
Considering the adversary model in section 4.3, ARCADIS should satisfy the following
security properties:
1. Trustworthiness ôÙ The integrity of each service being attested by the protocol should
be validated such that the service can be considered legitimate and trustworthy.

being maliciously affected by any preceding services.
3. Freshness ôÙ The protocol shall be able to detect submitted evidence that is not fresh,
e.g. precomputed evidence or replay.

4.5.2

evidence, for each legitimate path going from the start node to the end node. For larger
systems with more complex services, this quickly becomes unmanageable. An illustraôÙ

4.5.3
Attestation
Without loss of generality, we consider the attestation of a simple distributed service comôÙ
posed of only two servicesëƒ one publisher and one subscriber. The protocol algorithm is
depicted in figure 4.4. VRF begins the attestation procedure by sending an attestation
request message Ch to the publisher (step 1 ). This message is a challenge consisting
of the identifier of the publisher, a random nonce and a signature. Upon receiving the
challenge and successfully verifying it, the attestation can begin. The publisher initializes
its vector clock along with the Global Attestation Evidence (GAEp), which is initialized to
0, since there is no preceding service in the service flow. The publisher then reads input,
either from its environment or from another service, with the read() procedure and then
starts the attest() procedure (step 2 ). The hash engine procedure is borrowed from the
CôÙFLAT protocol [26], where an accumulated hash value is computed over the execution
path of the program. The publisherãs program is executed on the input returned from the
read() procedure. Due to the use of the CôÙFLAT protocol, where the program binary is inôÙ
strumented in such a way that an accumulated hash value is computed that represents the
controlôÙflow path the program has taken, the execution of the program does not only result
in some output, but also generates the Local Attestation Evidence (LAEp) for the publisher
as a side effect. This evidence is retrieved with get_evidence(). The publisher increments
its vector clock, and computes ü = SID||timestampp||LAEp||Outputp||Inputp||GAEprev,
which is the complete attestation evidence for the service P. Only VRF must have access
to this evidence, so it is encrypted with VRFãs public key, PKvrf and assigned to GAEp. P
then computes a message msgp = Outputp||GAEp||timestampp and signs it. This signed
message is then published to the subscriber (step 3 ).
Upon receiving this message, S verifies it and begins its own attestation routine if the
verification is successful. S begins by storing the input received from P, its vector clock
and the global attestation evidence. S then increments its own local vector clock usôÙ
ing the received vector clock and begins its own local attestation (step 4 ) with the outôÙ
put received from P as the input to its program. As with P, the local attestation eviôÙ
dence is produced as a side effect of the execution of the program, and is retrievable by
the get_evidence() function. S then increments its own vector clock by one, computes
ü = SID||timestamps||LAEs||Outputs||Inputs||GAEprev and encrypts this message with
VRFãs public key, assigning the result to GAEs. Note that in this simple example, the subôÙ
scriber does not publish its output anywhere, but at this point the subscriber could publish
its output to the next subscriber using the same method as the publisher, including the
accumulated global attestation evidence GAEs in the message.

Optionally, the subscriber at this point can decide to preôÙemptively publish the attestation
result back to VRF with publish_to_verifier(). In this case, S will increment its vector
clock, compute msg = Outputs||GAEs||timestamps, sign it with its own private key and
publish the result and signature back to VRF.
Alternatively, the subscriber may store GAEs and wait for an attestation request from
VRF consisting of the identifier of S, a random nonce R and a signature (step 5 ). If the
message is successfully verified, S computes a signature over GAEs and the random
nonce R and sends the attestation evidence GAEs, the nonce R and the signature back
to VRF (step 6 ).
4.5.4
Verification
Verification begins when VRF receives the encrypted attestation evidence (see figure 4.5)
from service S, which acts as a PRV. This can happen either by the preemptive publishing
of the evidence by a PRV, or by the VRF sending an attestation challenge to a specific
PRV. As the global attestation evidence that the VRF receives is encrypted with PKvrf,
the VRF can decrypt this data with SKvrf. Contained in the attestation evidence received
by the VRF is not only the timestamped local attestation evidence of the service S, but
also the timestamped local attestation evidence of each previously interacting SRV that
took part in the service flow, embedded within the previous attestation evidence strucôÙ
ture. By decrypting the GAEprev sections of the evidence structure recursively, VRF can
access the vector clock, local attestation evidence, input, and output of each service that
took part in the attestation. Using the vector clocks, VRF can accurately observe the
interactions between the participating services, and therefore reôÙconstruct the actual serôÙ
vice flow taken. Using this in combination with the local attestation evidence, input, and
output of each SRV, VRF can a) know the causal order of service invocation, and thus
attest that the order of service invocations is valid, b) the output produced by a publisher
was not tampered with before reaching its subscriber, and c) each individual service took
the intended controlôÙflow path locally, given the input to the service. Note that because
VRF attests the local attestation evidence of each service, the order of invocation, and the
data exchanged between the services, it does not have to keep a full database of all posôÙ
sible controlôÙflow paths throughout the entire distributed service. Instead, it is sufficient
that VRF keep only a database of 1) each serviceãs local valid controlôÙflow paths, and 2)
valid order of execution of each service.
Order of service invocation ôÙ Each service keeps its own timestamp, which is a vector

V CP is smaller than V CS if all pairs (j, k) in V CP have a value k that is less than or equal
to the corresponding k value in V CS, and at least one k value in V CP is smaller. That is,

4.6
Security Analysis
This section provides an informal discussion regarding the security requirements outlined
in section 4.4. As discussed in section 4.3, an adversaryãs main goals in attacking a
distributed IoT service are threefold. The first goal is to compromise one or more services
in the network, such that the service deviates from its legitimate operations and executes
code defined by the adversary, affecting a serviceãs trustworthiness. The second goal
is to affect the legitimate operations of one or more services by attacking a preceding
service. The third goal is to gain information about the structure of attestation messages
by listening to the communications, and extract attestation messages for use in replay
attacks later, threatening freshness of the attestation evidence. An adversary tries to
accomplish all of these goals without being detected by VRF.
Trustworthiness ôÙ A software adversary ADVsw attempts to launch an attack on a service
that deviates the controlôÙflow of the program. In ARCADIS, the attestation routine runs
inside hardwareôÙprotected memory, which is isolated and cannot be modified by ADVsw.
The hash engine of CôÙFLAT also resides within the hardwareôÙprotected memory and also
cannot be modified by ADVsw. The controlôÙflow tracing, enabled by the instrumentation
of the program binary as detailed in section 2.5, is also considered to be trusted since the
instrumentation was performed by the network operator in the setup phase. In addition,
CôÙFLAT assumes that the program binary is protected by other means such as Data ExeôÙ
cution Prevention (DEP) or Static Attestation. It is also in the adversaryãs best interest to
not modify the instrumentation in any way. Removing the instrumentation would result in
==PAGE:49==
4.7. DISCUSSION
42
the evidence saying that a node in the CFG has been skipped, which would indicate the
presence of the adversary. An adversary can attempt to modify the instrumentation such
that instead of invoking the trampoline that switches context to the attestation routine, it
instead invokes the adversaryãs own fake trampoline. This will not work, because the adôÙ
versary does not have access to any keys to sign the attestation evidence, so a fake hash
provided by the adversary will fail verification. Although ADVsw can launch an attack and
successfully deviate the controlôÙflow of the service, the final hash in the aggregate hash
chain generated by the CôÙFLAT protocol (LAE) will not match a valid hash that has been
measured and stored by VRF. Thus VRF can detect that the service has been comproôÙ
mised. If the hash reported (LAE) matches some valid hash in the database of the VRF,
it can be concluded that the controlôÙflow of the service has not been deviated, and thus
the service can be considered trustworthy.
Legitimate Operations ôÙ Included in the Global Attestation Evidence (GAE) that is reôÙ
ported to VRF is a timestamp in the form of a vector clock, the input to the service and the
output produced by the service. Assuming that ARCADIS can securely read the input
provided to the service and the output produced by the service, these data are securely
stored in protected memory that can not be modified by ADVsw. When VRF
requests
the attestation evidence from the last service of the service flow, this evidence includes,
for each service, the hash representing the program execution flow, the input received,
the output produced and the timestamp (vector clock) of the service. Using this data,
VRF
is able to reconstruct the precise service flow and identify any compromised serôÙ
vice. Using the timestamp of each service, VRF can also identify any services that are
potentially affected by the compromised service. In this way, ARCADIS can detect any
illegitimate operations of the services participating in the attested service flow. In addition,
ARCADIS can detect a mobile adversary, ADVmob that tries to evade detection. Due to
ARCADIS having access to all communication data exchanged during the service flow,
VRF can detect any attempt of ADVmob to relocate.
Freshness ôÙ An adversary can launch a replay attack to evade detection. This can be
done by sending preôÙcomputed attestation results in an attempt to fool VRF into acceptôÙ
ing the evidence as legitimate. In ARCADIS, the attestation evidence of each service
includes a timestamp (vector clock) which provides a way to see the logical causality
between the services involved. This allows VRF to construct a graph using these timesôÙ
tamps to represent the order of service interactions. In a legitimate service flow, this
graph is a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), where the vertices are the services and the
edges between services are the timestamps. Because a serviceãs vector clock is mainôÙ
tained in memory that is only accessible to the attestation routine, and that the attestation
evidence of each service is encrypted with the public key of VRF, the adversary is unôÙ
able to replace or modify the timestamp in the evidence being replayed. As such, upon
receiving the replayed evidence with an alreadyôÙused timestamp, a loop will form in the
constructed service interaction graph and VRF can thus detect a replay attack.
4.7
Discussion
In ARCADIS, the attestation evidence is encrypted with asymmetric cryptography, i.e.
publicôÙkey cryptography. Depending on the size of the network and the size of the service
flow, the attestation evidence can be large. For the Provers, having to encrypt the whole
attestation evidence, especially at the end of the service flow of a large distributed service,
is resourceôÙintensive. Some steps can be taken to mitigate this problem, one of which
is using symmetric cryptography instead of asymmetric cryptography for encrypting the
attestation evidence. This is further discussed in section 5.3.

Another possibility for mitigating this issue is to implement a different way of securely propôÙ

to propagate the attestation request, and its performance is promising. A possible future
work of ARCADIS is to modify it in such a way as to use an aggregate signature scheme
similar to SANA instead of using asymmetric cryptography for securing the attestation
evidence.
ARCADIS uses CôÙFLAT [26] as its measurement engine for measuring the valid execuôÙ

5.3
Discussion
It is obviously not ideal to encrypt the whole attestation evidence with asymmetric crypôÙ
tography. It would be more efficient to encrypt it with symmetric cryptography, but there
are a few challenges to overcome in order to achieve that. The first is that in order to do
symmetric encryption, VRF and PRV would have to share a symmetric key. This would
require secure key storage on PRV that is nonôÙaccessible to an attacker to be available.

In addition, to minimize the chance of the encryption key from being compromised, ideally
each PRV would have a unique shared key with VRF, which both increases the complexity
of the setup phase and also would require VRF to store as many keys as there are devices
or services in the network. This would also require the service identifier in the attestation
evidence to be in plaintext so that VRF can know which symmetric key to use to decrypt
the rest of the packet. This would would require some sort of message authentication or
signature scheme to avoid an attacker being able to modify the service identifier of the
evidence, or to encrypt the service identifier separately with asymmetric cryptography.
Another idea is to make PRV generate an ephemeral symmetric key for each attestation,
encrypt the evidence with the symmetric key, and then encrypt the key with the public key
of VRF. This would solve the issue of encrypting a large payload with asymmetric crypôÙ
tography as only a fixedôÙlength key would be encrypted, but introduces other challenges.
One is that in order to generate a cryptographically secure symmetric key, PRV would
need to feature a cryptographically secure random number generator. This is difficult to
achieve with software because of resource constraints, but may be reasonable for sensors
that monitor highôÙentropy sources. A key generator can also be provided by hardware,
such as a TPM, but that increases the cost of the device, which may be unfeasible.
The performance of the protocol is reasonable for smaller networks. The limiting facôÙ
tor on performance is twofold. 1) The complexity of the services within the network and
2) the number of services participating in the service flow. For 40 services or less, the
encryption and transmission overhead was observed to be under a second at the last
service of the flow (where each preceding service has less performance overhead), and
the performance overhead of the attestation of each prover was observed to be as low as
27,8 percent. This makes the protocol reasonable for application in smaller IoT networks.
However, the protocol is not suitable for deployment on timeôÙsensitive, large IoT networks,
where the time required for encryption and transmission of the attestation evidence beôÙ
comes unfeasible. Future works should aim to reduce the size of the attestation evidence

Conclusion
Attesting the controlôÙflow integrity in IoT services is an important activity in todayãs seôÙ
curity landscape. With the number of IoT devices being deployed in the world being
everôÙincreasing, and with IoT manufacturers competing in terms of device cost and timeôÙ
toôÙmarket, often coupled with extremely limited computational resources, the security of
these devices often leaves something to be desired. Therefore, it is important to find ways
of detecting and preventing malware present on these devices. Today, the most promisôÙ
ing techniques for achieving this seem to be Remote Attestation, both static and dynamic,
and ControlôÙFlow Integity (CFI) schemes.
This thesis has explored the current stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart of both CFI and RA in the category
of ControlôÙFlow Attestation (CFA) in the context of IoT. A systematic literature review was
performed on these topics and several CFI schemes and all Remote Attestation schemes
focusing on CFA were summarized, discussed and compared. Many different techniques
for achieving CFI and CFA were revealed and many schemes use the same types of techôÙ
niques to achieve specific things, such as a Shadow Stack, using a ãSecure Worldã context
for measurements, and using software instrumentation for execution tracing. However,
there does not seem to be a general convergence on any specific techniques or technoloôÙ
gies for use in CFI and CFA yet. Some of these techniques have the potential to become
more widespread than others due to their robust fulfilment of certain security properties, or
their simplicity or elegance. It seems like it could be beneficial to conduct more research
into these techniques in order to propose some standardization for which techniques to
use for fulfilling certain security properties and requirements in RA and CFI. However,
there are many promising schemes out there and it seems that the topic is steadily gainôÙ
ing more interest in the research community with more papers being published every year.
A remote attestation protocol, ARCADIS, was proposed and was shown to achieve ReôÙ
mote ControlôÙFlow Attestation of Asynchronous IoT Services. To the best of the authorãs
knowledge, this is the first Remote Attestation protocol that can asynchronously attest the
controlôÙflow of distributed IoT services. In addition, ARCADIS can also verify the correct
data exchanged between the services participating in the network. Currently, the only
other scheme to achieve this asynchronously [24] can only attest the static parts of the
memory, e.g. the program binaries, of Provers.
ARCADIS was implemented for Contiki OS and simulated using the Contiki simulation
software. Its performance was analyzed and it was shown that it is a practical protocol
for smaller networks of up to about 40 services. It was found that there is some room for
improvement with regards to performance overhead and some ideas for future works on
the protocol were presented.
6.1
Future Work
This project studied CFI and CFA security technologies with a particular focus on the CFA
schemes proposed in the IoT domain. It is possible that some CFA schemes that are not
specifically designed for the IoT domain, but are still very applicable in this context, may
have been missed. The future work of this thesis includes a complete exploration of CFA
schemes in both traditional and IoT systems. In addition, a more comprehensive study of
CFI schemes in both traditional and IoT systems can be done. The topic of CFI by itself
is vast and there are many papers that have proposed many different schemes that were
not included in this thesis in the interest of keeping the scope manageable.

6.1. FUTURE WORK
53
Although it has been shown that ARCADIS is practical for smaller networks, there is still
room for improvement for larger networks. In particular, future research activities will aim
to improve the encryption overhead for single Provers, using symmetric cryptography for
encrypting the attestation evidence that is sent between Provers instead of asymmetric
cryptography. In addition, a potential future work direction is on improving the signature
scheme used among devices. For instance, group signature or multiôÙsignature schemes
can be used for propagation of the attestation evidence.
Another future work is to make ARCADIS immune to nonôÙcontrol data attacks, which
currently remain undetected because they do not deviate the controlôÙflow in a way that
violates the ControlôÙFlow Graph.
