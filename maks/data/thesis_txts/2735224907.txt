ABSTRACT
1
Abstract
Developing new capable materials is necessary to further the green transition, and Advanced Ma-
terials (AdMs) are very relevant due to their unique properties and wide range of use. This study
assesses the behaviour and ecotoxicity of six selected AdMs in aquatic environments and the bioac-
cumulation potential of the metal-doped AdMs. The selected AdMs are three imogolites and three
spinel ferrites; the spinels are referred to as NRCWE materials. The AdMs behaviour in different
aquatic media was studied, focusing on Zeta potential, agglomeration, and sedimentation. Short-
term freshwater toxicity tests were performed on the six materials for key aquatic species, a 72-hour
test on R. subcapitata and a 48 hours test on D. magna. Ingestion and uptake experiments were
performed on Cu-doped imogolite and the three NRCWE materials using D. magna over a period of
72 hours. The results showed that all six materials were very polydisperse and unstable in dispersion,
with a high tendency towards agglomeration across all media and concentrations. The PDI suggests
that IMO-Cu and IMO-CH were slightly more stable at higher concentrations in ISO 8692; however,
the Zeta Potential did not support this. Of the six tested materials, the Cu-doped imogolite proved
the most toxic in the short-term tests towards both R. subcapitata, with an EC50 of 0.56 mg/L,
and D. magna, with an EC50 of 0.22 mg/L. Of the NRCWE AdMs, NRCWE-21 was found to be
the most toxic towards D. magna with an EC50 of 91 mg/L, while NRCWE-22 was the most toxic
towards R. subcapitata, with an EC50 of 24 mg/L. The toxicity of the AdMs was found to be related
to dissolved metal ions. In the ingestion and depuration test, 1.2 ngCu/organism remained at the
conclusion of the test, indicating that total depuration was not possible. The NRCWE materials
depurated almost all metals during the test setup. 0.19 µgFe/organism, 0.04 µgNi/organism, and
0.063 µgZn/organism remained in the daphnids after exposure to NRCWE-20, 0.25 µgFe/organism
remained after exposure to NRCWE-21, and 0.21 µgFe/organism and 0.025 µgNi/organism remained
after exposure to NRCWE-22. Some specific effects produced by IMO-OH proved challenging for the
standard test setup, so alternative methods should be considered when conducting short-term and
chronic tests for this material. Based on the data generated IMO-Cu should be classified as Acute
1 and possibly as Chronic 1 due to the evidence of bioaccumulation. NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22
should be classified as Chronic 3 if not rapidly degradable or a BCF ≥500 is determined. It is rec-
ommended to conduct further tests, such as acute tests with fish and chronic tests, to fully ensure
aquatic safety in regard to these materials.

BACKGROUND AND AIM

Background and Aim
Climate change is one of the biggest threats to the planet today. Widespread effects are seen all
over the world, global temperatures are rising, ice sheets are shrinking, sea levels are rising, and
intense heat waves are felt in many countries.[1] These changes are driven by human emissions, and
action is needed to reduce emissions and develop more sustainable ways of living, and the green
transition is seen as a way to achieve this. The green transition is the shift from an economy based
on fossil fuels and overconsumption of natural resources to a sustainable economy that relies on
low-carbon solutions and economically sustainable growth. To achieve this transition, consumption
must be limited without impacting the economy too much.[2] This can be done by bolstering the
green transition with guidance and legislation and promoting opportunities for new and innovative
solutions and materials to be used. These ideas, through the Green Deal[3] and the EU’s Chemical
Strategy for Sustainability[4], have led to the creation of the Safe-and-Sustainable-by-Design (SSbD)
Framework.[5] SSbD describes an approach focused on providing functionality while avoiding creat-
ing materials or services that may be harmful to the environment or human health and, at the same
time, avoiding a large environmental footprint over the lifetime of the material or service. The idea
is to minimise environmental footprint and use harmful materials early in the innovation process
instead of discovering the toxicity or impact of a material or service after creation.[5]
All are an important part of driving innovation within the EU, especially regarding materials such as
Nanomaterials (NM) and Advanced Materials (AdMs), needed materials to further the green tran-
sition. Due to their unique properties, these materials are seen as very attractive as there are many
practical applications that might help further the green transition. This has led to a rapid increase
in production of Engineered Nanoparticles. However, there is a lack of knowledge of whether these
novel materials are safe and sustainable or if they will hinder the green transition. The HARMLESS
Project is an EU project that seeks to rectify this and ensure that the novel materials that are a
part of or will be a part of the transition follow strategies set forth by the EU.[6] By generating new
data on novel AdMs it is possible to gain insight into whether or not these new materials can work
under the constraints set forth by the EU. In this thesis, six novel AdMs will be investigated as a
part of the HARMLESS project, and partners of the project will provide all six. The materials can
be divided into two categories, imogolites and iron-based NM. Imogolites are alumino-silicate clay
nanotubes with the potential to find use in plant protection products used in agriculture, leading to

to their physio-chemical properties. Iron-based NM have advantages such as properties like mag-

INTRODUCTION
3
Introduction
3.1
Nanomaterials and Advanced Materials
The European Commission defines nanomaterials as "a natural, incidental, or manufactured material
consisting of solid particles that are present, either on their own or as identifiable constituent particles
in aggregates or agglomerates, and where 50% or more of these particles in the number-based size
distribution fulfils at least one of the following conditions:
• One or more external dimensions of the particle are in the size range from 1 nm to 100 nm.
• The particle has an elongated shape, such as a rod, fiber, or tube, where two external dimen-
sions are smaller than 1 nm, and the other dimension is larger than 100 nm.
• The particle has a plate-like shape, where one external dimension is smaller than 1 nm and
the other is larger than 100 nm." [10]
Sometimes NM are also defined by things other than size, as it is with the definition from the Euro-
pean Chemical Agency (ECHA) who defines NM both by their size as a particle between 1 and 100
nanometres in at least one dimension, but also by the different characteristics they might possess,
when compared to the same bulk material, due to their increased specific surface area by volume.[11]
NM can exist in nature as natural compounds, often found in ash or soot particles from volcanic
activities or other forms of combustion[12], or they can be artificially created and are then referred
to as Engineered Nanoparticles (ENPs). Naturally occurring NM will not be further discussed here
as they are not a part of the scope of this project. ENPs are of interest as they can be used in
environmental remediation or clean-up to bind with and neutralise toxins. In theory, ENPs can
be engineered from nearly any chemical substance or material and can differ in size, composition,
shape, surface coating, and strength of particle bond.[13]
Advanced Materials (AdMs) are often defined as materials specifically engineered to exhibit en-
hanced or novel properties that improve performance over conventional processes or products. In
recent years, the term AdMs has also been used as a descriptor for nano-enabled materials; however,
AdMs are, unlike NM, not defined by size but only by their properties.[14] Therefore NM and ENPs
can be said to be encompassed by the term AdMs, and that AdMs is a broader umbrella term for
materials with enhanced properties.

==PAGE:14==
3
INTRODUCTION
NM can be described as a double-edged sword, as the properties that make them beneficial are
the same ones that cause concern. As production of ENPs grows, so does their release into the
environment. It is paramount that their toxicological effects are evaluated and mapped to ensure
the safety of living organisms and the environment. Currently, there are a lot of knowledge gaps in
regards to ENPs, and the huge diversity of the materials poses a great challenge when identifying
the inherent particle properties that determine their toxicity.[15] The smaller size and larger surface
area that gives ENPs their interesting properties make their behaviour and fate in the environment
difficult to predict. It makes them more reactive and enables them to reach new areas in biological
organisms, making it difficult to predict their movements and toxicology.
Another challenge presented by ENPs is their tendency to agglomerate in both wet and dry states.
Agglomeration is due to the adhesion of the particles to each other, mainly driven by Van der Waal’s
forces that become stronger at the nano-scale owing to the larger surface area to volume ratio. In
Brownian motion, agglomeration occurs as particles move about and randomly collide, forming par-
ticles of increasing size that may settle due to the gravitational effect. Agglomerates possess a high
mass and volume but lower density and slower diffusion rates than primary particles. This increases
the buoyant and gravitational forces acting on the particles, making them settle rapidly in solutions.
Thus key parameters from a toxicological viewpoint, size, size distribution, specific surface area, and
number concentrations, are altered when the particles agglomerate.[16]
Imogolites are a naturally occurring aluminosilicate nanotube, first discovered in the Kumamoto Pre-
fecture in Japan in 1962 by Yoshinaga and Aomine.[17] The name comes from the Japanese word
"Imogo", a brownish volcanic ash soil which is the parent material of natural imogolites. Imogolites
are not restricted to volcanic soil and have been found worldwide. Imogolites are both naturally
occurring and engineered due to their unique properties, making them interesting AdMs and a part
of this project’s scope. Structurally, imogolites are hollow nanotubes with an external diameter of
around 2.2 nm and an internal diameter of less than 1 nm. and with a varying length from sev-
eral hundred nanometres to micrometres.[18] The tube walls are formed by a curved di-octahedral
gibbsite-like sheet of Al-OH-Al groups, and the inner surface comprises O3SiOH.[19][17] The chemical
composition of imogolites are Al2SiO3(OH)4, and can also be written as (OH)3Al2O3SiOH. Imogo-
lites’ outer and inner surfaces have different properties, with the inner SiOH being more acidic than
the outer Al(OH)Al surface.[20]

3.2
Ecotoxicity in Aquatic Environments
Ecotoxicology is the study of how chemicals interact with organisms in the environment. Through
ecotoxicology, it is possible to see how different environments and organisms react to different chem-
icals. There are many different environments and many organisms relevant to this field of study,
making it virtually impossible to know everything about potential risks to different kinds of organ-
isms in different environments. Because of this, a set of indicator organisms are used as well as an
understanding of the physio-chemical properties of the chemicals that cause them to partition to
different organisms and environments.[24]
Aquatic toxicity refers to the effects of one or more chemicals on organisms living in aquatic envi-
ronments. Indicator animals used for aquatic environments represent the three trophic levels, with
plants or algae representing primary producers, invertebrates representing primary consumers and
secondary producers, and vertebrates representing secondary consumers. When doing ecotoxicity
15
==PAGE:17==
3
INTRODUCTION
tests in regards to aquatic toxicity both acute and chronic endpoints are relevant. Acute toxicity
deals with short-term exposure, while chronic toxicity refers to longer-term exposure.[25] Generally,
two endpoints are used in aquatic toxicity: acute and chronic. Acute aquatic toxicity testing is a
requirement in most EU chemical legislation, whereas chronic aquatic toxicity testing is required
when long-term exposure is expected or if the acute testing indicates risk. [25] Acute aquatic toxi-
city is defined as the intrinsic property of a substance to injure an organism in short-term aquatic
exposure to that substance.[26] Often it is determined using a 72 hour algal species EC50 test such as
OECD test guideline 201[27], a 48 hour crustacean species EC50 test such as OECD 202[28], or a 96
hour fish LC50 test like OECD 203[29]. Chronic aquatic toxicity is defined as the intrinsic property
of a substance to cause adverse effects to aquatic organisms during aquatic exposure, which are
determined in relation to the organism’s life-cycle.[26] Data for chronic toxicity is less standardised
than for acute toxicity, but OECD Guidelines such as 210[30] or 201[31] are used. These test are
determined not only by EC50 or LC50, but using relevant ECx and No Observed Effect Concentra-
tion (NOEC), and should be performed according to OECD Test guidelines.[26]
These guidelines are used for classifying substances according to the CLP classification system used
in the EU. Within CLP classification, danger to the aquatic environment is evaluated. It is composed
of three elements; the potential for rapid degradation, bioconcentration potential in fish, and short-
term toxicity to aquatic organisms (fish, daphnids, and algae).[32] There is one acute category, Acute
1 - Very toxic to aquatic life, which is based only on acute toxicity data, either the EC50 or the
LC50. If the EC50 or the LC50 value of any acute test, regardless of tropic level, is ≤1.0 mg/L, then
the substance is classified as Acute 1. For chronic toxicity, there are three categories, Chronic 1 to
3. However, the classification of chronic toxicity involves the biodegradability, the NOEC or ECX
of chronic tests, and the Bioconcentration Factor (BCF). First, it is determined if the substance is
rapidly degradable or not. If it is non-rapidly degradable and there is chronic toxicity data available,
it is given the classification Chronic 1 if the NOEC or the ECx ≤0.1 mg/L, and Chronic 2 if the
NOEC or the ECx ≤1.0 mg/L. If the substance is rapidly degradable and chronic toxicity data is
available, it is classified as Chronic 1 if the NOEC or the ECx is ≤0.01 mg/L, Chronic 2 if the
NOEC or the ECx is ≤0.1 mg/L, and as Chronic 3 if the NOEC or the ECx is ≤1 mg/L. In cases
with no chronic toxicity data available, and the substance is not rapidly degradable and/or BCF ≥
500, the acute data is used to determine the chronic classification. In these cases Chronic 1 is given
if the EC50 or the LC50 is ≤1 mg/L, Chronic 2 if the EC50 or the LC50 is > 1 mg/L to ≤10 mg/L,

classifications are applied individually, meaning a substance can be classified as Acute 1 and one of

3.2.2
Ecotoxicological Impact of Heavy Metals on Aquatic Systems
Poor freshwater quality is hugely impacted by pollution from heavy metals. Activities such as vehi-
cle emissions, agriculture, and domestic waste all contribute to the rise of heavy metal exposure in
the environment and among humans.[35] Heavy metals are classified as essential and non-essential.
Non-essential heavy metals have no known biological role in living organisms. Essential heavy met-
als, like Copper (Cu) and Zink (Zn), are already present in living organisms and are important for
organisms in quite low concentrations.[36] Their presence in the ecosystem becomes unsafe when
the concentration rises above the natural background observed in water.[35] Generally, heavy met-
als become toxic when the rate of uptake exceeds the rate of detoxification and excretion by the
species.[37] Heavy metals pose a risk in regards to both acute toxicity and chronic toxicity as they
are toxic, persistent and non-biodegradable, and also because they can bioaccumulate and biomag-
nify and thus be transferred through the food chain and pose great risk in the long-term.[35]
Cu and Zn are considered essential metals for all phytoplankton, while Ni is essential for prokaryotic
phytoplankton.[38] It is expected to find all three metals in nature to some degree, but they all
present significant risk at higher concentrations. Cu can easily accumulate in plants and animals,
and high BCF values have been observed in various species and trophic levels. It has been shown
that the toxicity and uptake of Cu in freshwater organisms are linked to alkalinity and hardness of
the water, with uptake and toxicity increasing with increasing alkalinity and decreasing hardness of
the water.[37] Zn toxicity follows some of the same trends as Cu, being that toxicity and uptake are
linked to the hardness and alkalinity of the water. Zn2+ is generally considered the ion primarily
responsible for the high toxicity. [39] Nickel (Ni) has been considered a Priority Substance under the
European Water Framework directive since 2008, and it is considered a human carcinogen.[40] Ni
has also been shown to be toxic to aquatic organisms and is very persistent, highly toxic, and non-
biodegradable.[41] In a study by Kahru et al. the toxicity of Zn+2, and Cu+2 ions on R. subcapitata
were presented. Both Cu+2 and Zn+2 are extremely toxic to R. subcapitat. With an EC50 value of
0.02 mgCu+2/L is the most toxic of the two, but it is closely followed by Zn+2 with an EC50 value
of 0.051 mgZn+2/L.[42] The toxicity of Ni on R. subcapitata is known to be less than Cu and Zn. A
study by Nys et al.[43] found the toxicity of dissolved Ni to R. subcapitata to be 0.63 mg/L Another
study by Lari et al. tested the toxicity of Ni+2, Zn+2, and Cu+2 ions on D. magna. Again Cu+2
was found to be the most toxic of the three, with an EC50 value 0.034 mgCu+2/L, followed by Zn+2
with an EC50 value of 0.32 mgZn+2/L. Ni+2 was the least toxic of the three, but with still toxic

3.3
Ecotoxicology of Nanomaterials in Aquatic Environments
The existence of Engineered Nanoparticles (ENPs) in aquatic environments can be traced to many
different sources. Accidental spills during transport or production, landfill leachates, waste incin-
eration, wastewater treatment effluent or sludge, but also from intentional releases such as the use
of ENPs for environmental remediation. As the the risk of ENPs contamination increases the need
to evaluate the toxicity of these materials on the environment. This led to the emergence of na-
noecotoxicology, which aims to study the environmental hazards of NM during the life cycle of a
nanoproduct.[45] Once ENPs ends up in aquatic environments, they can be affected by their sur-
roundings and undergo transformation processes such as dissolution, agglomeration and aggregation,
or transportation processes such as sedimentation. These are all important for the environmental
fate and behaviour of the ENPs.[46] Nanomaterials (NM) have different properties than their bulk
counterparts, due to the increased specific surface area and reactivity. This can lead to increased
toxicity and bioavailability. TiO2 and Al2O3 nanoparticles have been found to have a toxicity double
that of their respective bulk formulations. In contrast, CuO nanoparticles were up to 50 times more
toxic than particles from bulk CuO in regards to crustaceans. The surface properties of NM are
also a big factor in their ecotoxicity, as they influence the stability of colloidal suspensions or their
aggregation into larger particles, which can lead to sedimentation. Sedimentation is an important
sink for ENPs discharged into aquatic environments, and benthic organisms are key receptors for
these nanoparticles. Nanoparticles often form larger aggregates, which means only a small fraction
remains in the size ranges where direct uptake is relevant. This can lead to very dissimilar uptake
which impacts the toxicity.[42]
According to Connell[47] dissolution can be defined as "Individual molecules of the solute are sepa-
rated by the molecules of the solvent". Since ENPs are not individual molecules but rather multi-
molecular objects. This would mean that ENPs likely disperse in particulate form instead of dis-
solving in aqueous media. However, different ENPs might still be soluble to varying degrees as
molecules or ions may be released from the material surface, which then undergoes diffusion and
becomes dissolved in the media. This difference in solubility impacts the behaviour, fate, and effect
of the given ENPs. The physio-chemical factors that affect dissolution include chemical composition,
shape/curvature, size, surface area, crystal phase, and surface coating.

due to their topography and surface properties. In the immobilisation of daphnids, different ENPs
might cause immobilisation or effect mobility without being lethal to the organisms, causing EC50
values to be considerably lower if immobilisation is the focus rather than lethality.[49]

3.3.1
Agglomeration and DLVO
In the environment, small dispersed particles, like ENPs, will move according to Brownian motion.
This results in the particles being in constant and random movement, driven by their collisions with
molecules in the liquid they are dispersed in. As the particles move about they collide with one
another, which may result in agglomeration. Brownian motion has been suggested as a main driv-
ing force for agglomeration, as the particles may agglomerate upon collision if the attractive forces
between the particles are larger than the repulsive forces. Agglomeration differs from aggregation in
that agglomerated particles are weakly bound to each other, while aggregated particles are strongly
bound to one another. Therefore, agglomeration is generally believed to be reversible, whereas ag-
gregation is not.[46][50] However, in practice, it is difficult to distinguish between the two and as
such, only the term agglomeration will be used in this report.
DLVO theory (Deryaguin, Landau, Verway and Overbeek) describes the interactions between particle
interfaces in dispersion.[46] Particles will only remain dispersed if there is something preventing them
from becoming attached to each other during a collision. An example could be if all the particles
had the same electrical charge, then they would repel one another, and the system is then said to be
colloidally stable. According to DLVO theory, in any colloidally stable fluid, there are two opposite
forces: the van der Waals force that binds the particles together and an electrostatic double-layer
repulsion that drives them apart. Common methods to determine agglomeration include electron
microscopy techniques such as Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Transmission Electron
Microscopy (TEM), or spectroscopy techniques such as Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) and X-ray
Diffraction (XRD), or measurements of the Zeta potential which can be done via DLS. Measuring
the Zeta potential is an indirect way of measuring ENPs agglomeration. At a low Zeta potential, the

3.3.2
Surface Charge of Nanomaterials
Surface charge has been shown to impact the rate of uptake and toxicity in cases with NM. In
aquatic tests with D. magna exposed to 10 µg/L gold NM, the materials with a positive surface
charge were shown to be more toxic than the same particles with a negative surface charge.[52]
The effect of surface on aquatic toxicity is less studied in algae than other aquatic organisms, as
algae have different surface interactions with NM uptake than other eukaryotic cells due to their
cell walls. However, it has been shown that neutral and positive particles have stronger adsorption
to the cell wall of R. subcapitata. In a study by McKeel et al.[52], the effect of surface charge on
R. subcapitata was investigated using carbon dots. Carbon dots are generally known to be a low
toxicity NM. In their study, they showed that positively charged carbon dots were more toxic than
ones with negative zeta potential. After the algae was exposed to the positively charged carbon dot,

3.3.3
Test Organisms - D. magna and R. subcapitata
Daphnids are organisms that can be found in most bodies of freshwater. They are a type of small
crustacean, D. magna being the biggest type being up to 5 mm long.
Daphnids feed on small
suspended particles in water, mostly planktonic algae, and are filter feeders.[53] Daphnids are well-
established and widely used as model organisms for freshwater toxicity testing.
One reason for
their popularity is they are small in size and also inexpensive and easy to breed. Daphnids are
also considered a non-sentient species which removes the ethical implications of traditional animal
testing.[54] Most daphnids are cyclical parthenogens, meaning they are capable of both sexual and
clonal reproduction. When used as a model organism clonal reproduction is of use, as it allows for the
genetic background to be held constant. A constant genetic background allows for easy comparison
against a defined genetic background across different test setups.[55] Another advantage is their filter
feeding mechanism which ensures a high particle uptake and their transparent bodies.[54] Other key
aspects of their use as model organisms are the transparency of the outer shell, the length of their
life cycle, and their sensitivity alterations to their environment. Transparency of the outer shell
allows for a clear view of most of the internal organs, including the heart, which makes it easy to
see uptake as well as any internal reaction to the toxin being tested. The length of their life cycle
is useful as experiment setups can be short while still observing the effect of the tested material
through a full life cycle. And lastly, daphnids are very sensitive to alterations in the chemistry of
water and will show clear reactions to changes in temperature, oxygen reduction and alterations
of feeding patterns.[56] R. subcapitata are part of the Sphaeropleales, a dominant group of green
algae.[57] It is a planktonic species of algae that live in freshwater rivers, ponds, and lakes. The
cells are semi-circular and curved, with the diameter of the arc being between 4.8 and 10.8 µm and
the width between 1.6 and 4.4 µm.[58] Many species within the Sphaeropleales are important to
freshwater ecosystems, thus R. subcapitata is widely used for toxicological risk assessments.[57]

5 different concentrations of the test subject were chosen; for all but one, a sample range between
6.25 mg/L and 100 mg/L was used to determine toxicity. However, a lower concentration range of
0.125 mg/L and 1 mg/L was used for the Cu-doped imogolite. The range was determined based on
the reference tests described earlier. The necessary amount of stock solution for each concentration
was added to a 25 mL flask, while one flask was kept empty of the stock solution as a control.
Medium was added to each of the 6 flasks, filling them up while ensuring space for the inoculum.
The inoculum was then added in the calculated amount.
Then the flasks were topped up with
medium till the 25 mL mark. The contents of each flask were mixed well, and then transferred to a
beaker. 4 mL of each concentration, including the control, was transferred into 20 mL glass vessels,
3 replicas of each concentration and 5 for the control. The vessels were then labelled and put in the
fridge on a shaker table with adequate lighting. The shaker table was set to 200 rpm. Then 0.4 mL
was taken from each vessel and put into thin glass vials. These were labelled, 1.6 mL acetone was
added, capped, and then put into storage in a dark drawer.
After 24 hours, the glass vials in the fridge were removed, and samples were taken from each vessel.
The procedure was the same as described above, with 0.4 mL taken from each vessel and transferred
into thin glass vials, after which 1.6 mL of acetone was added. The vials were then labelled, including
the date, capped and put in a dark drawer. The glass vials were put back on the shaker table. After
48 hours and 72 hours, the procedure was done again. After the last samples were taken at 72 hours,
the contents of the glass vessels were disposed of. 4 days later, the vials were taken from the drawer,
and fluorescence was measured for each vial.

4.5
Ingestion and Depuration Test
Ingestion and depuration experiments were carried out with a 24-hour ingestion period, followed by
a 24-hour depuration period, and lastly, a 24-hour depuration period where the D. magna was fed
at the start. The experiments were carried out in suspensions of 1 mg/L for all NRCWE materials
and 0.1 mg/L for the Cu-doped imogolite. 5 D. magna neonates were placed in 100 mL beaker
glass containing 25 mL of suspension. Four control beakers without test material were included.
The beakers were incubated at 20 °C in the dark, and mortality was noted for each beaker at every
24h mark and the end of the test. The D. magna were sampled after 1, 2, 4, and 24 h. This was
done by sampling the mobile animals from three beakers at each sampling time. Immediately after
sampling the D. magna were rinsed with MilliQ water for approximately 30 seconds. After rinsing,
the animals were stored in 20 mL glass vessels for ICP-MS analysis. At the end of the 24-hour
exposure period, all animals in the remaining beakers were transferred to fresh Elendt M7 medium
for the depuration test. In the depuration test, the animals were sampled at the same time intervals,
1, 2, 4, and 24 hours after their transfer to clean media. After 24 hours of depuration, the animals
were fed R. subcapitata and then sampled at the same times as in the depuration and uptake period.
All sampled animals were stored in the dark at room temperature until ICP-MS analysis was carried
out.

5.1.1
Size Distribution and Agglomeration
Z-average and PDI for each material is shown in the tables below. As the Z-average is measured
as a diameter and assumes a near or total spherical formation of the nanoparticles, it is not an
entirely accurate parameter for size. However, it can be used as an indicator of agglomeration and
size distribution across the different media and concentrations. As for PDI, the highest value is
1.0, which indicates very varying sizes between the particles, with values above 0.7 indicating that
the solutions are not stable enough for accurate measurements using DLS.[60] Therefore, the results
obtained cannot tell the exact size or dispersion of the AdMs tested, but rather it can be used as
an indicator of how each material behaves across different concentrations, media, and in relation to
each other.
A general trend for almost all materials can be seen for the Z-average and the PDI as it decreases as
concentration increases. Most of the samples have a PDI above 0.7 in all three media and, therefore,
contain large particles and aggregates. The only samples deviating from this with PDI below 0.7
at multiple concentrations are IMO-CH and IMO-Cu in ISO 8692 media and NRCWE-22 in MilliQ
water. While IMO-CH shows low PDI at 6 and 12 mg/L in ISO 8692 media, the Z-average does
not markedly decrease along with the PDI, meaning it is likely that the AdMs are still prone to
agglomeration at higher concentrations, despite the lower PDI. IMO-Cu, however, shows a decrease
in Z-average from 1500 d. nm. to 310 d.nm. in ISO 8692 media as the concentration increases, while
also having a decrease in PDI from 0.97 to 0.34 at 0.75 mg/L and 12 mg/L, respectively. While the
IMO-Cu seems to have a more stable dispersion in ISO 8692 media at higher concentrations than
at lower, the results still indicate that aggregates and large particles are present, making it prone to
sedimentation.

6.1
Size Distribution, Agglomeration and Sedimentation
The overall observed trend of high Z-averages and PDI, along with Zeta potentials between ± 20
across all media and concentrations, suggests an unstable dispersion state. This also indicates that
agglomeration and sedimentation are present. The sedimentation can be seen very clearly for the
iron-based AdMs in Appendix D and is consistent with the literature as nanoparticles, especially
metallic ones, tend to agglomerate.[61] The Z-averages provided by DLS is therefore not an indica-
tion of the individual size of the particles, but rather the estimation of the sizes of the agglomerates.
However, as mentioned earlier, there is a lot of uncertainty associated with these measurements. As
no tested particles in this study are spherical in nature, imogolites are nanotubes, and ferrite spinels
are cubic in formation, the Z-average will be inaccurate even if the solutions were stable. However,
the high PDI also indicates that the solutions are too unstable for accurate DLS measurements. The
results are therefore mostly used to indicate trends and to see how the particles behave in different
media, concentrations, and in relation to each other, as mentioned earlier. Imogolites are known to
have an internal diameter of less than 1 nm. and an external diameter around 2.2 nm.[18], while
the ferrite spinels are said to have a diameter between 10-30 nm.[23] This is much smaller than any
Z-average value measured and further supports the likelihood of agglomeration in the suspensions.
For almost all materials the Z-average and PDI decreased as concentration increased, which indicates
higher stability at higher concentrations. This was, however, not supported by the Zeta potential,
as there is no major indication that the suspensions are more stable at higher concentrations. If
looking solely at the Zeta Potential, it indicates that the solutions for all six materials are the most
stable at the lowest concentration. However, a Zeta Potential value of ± 30 is needed to achieve even
moderate stability[46], so at their most stable, all are still considered to have unstable dispersion
and are prone to agglomeration. The PDI were above 0.7 for almost all materials across concen-
trations and media, further indicating that the materials are all very polydisperse and highly prone
to agglomeration, which can lead to sedimentation. For most of the AdMs, there seems to be no
notable difference in stability across the different media. However, for IMO-CH and IMO-Cu there is
some indication that they might be more stable in ISO 8692 media, which was further collaborated
by measuring them in 12 mg/L suspension for 24 hours. This observation is supported by a study
by Miao et al. that found that water hardness played a role as higher concentrations of Ca+ ions

ISO 8692 media, so a higher agglomeration in Elendt M7 is expected. It is worth noting that this

6.2
Short-Term Toxicity
Standard OECD Short-term toxicity tests are a simplified method for determining toxicity to aquatic
organisms over a short period of time. The method was developed to determine the toxicity of
chemicals and not AdMs, which presents additional challenges such as particle size and physical
effects. However, the data generated in this study provides insights into the aquatic toxicity of both
imogolites and NRCWE AdMs and highlights the specific challenges presented by these materials.
6.2.1
Short-Term Toxicity of Imogolites
All studied imogolites except IMO-CH showed a toxic effect on D. magna over a 48-hour period.
IMO-CH showed no substantial effect, and there is no evidence that it is toxic to D. magna at
concentrations below 100 mg/L. IMO-Cu was shown to be the most toxic of the three imogolites,
with an EC50 value of 0.22 mg/L. This is expected as Cu is known to be extremely toxic to aquatic

IMO-OH presented some challenging physical effects, as outlined earlier. In the first test, an EC50
value of 24 mg/L was found, but upon closer inspection of the animals, it was discovered that they
were not dead, merely incapacitated by the gel-like substance formed by IMO-OH when they came
into contact with the Elendt M7 media. Therefore, the experiment was performed a second time,
with eight test concentrations between 3.125 and 100 mg/L, in an attempt to determine the toxicity

this test, a much smaller number of animals were found to be dead, but all animals, except for those
in the very low concentrations, 3.125 and 6.25 mg/L, were rendered immobile by the gel. As it can

determine if an animal was immobilised or dead, making human error a big part of the uncertainty
of the results. Based on the results, there is an indication that IMO-OH would not be considered
toxic in the traditional sense, but the effects of the gel structure on D. magna in the long term could
be fatal as they are mostly unable to move.
All imogolites were shown to affect the growth rate of R. subcapitata. The Cu-doped imogolite was
once again the most toxic of the imogolites, with an EC50 value of 0.56 mg/L. In contrast to the
acute test performed on D. magna, IMO-CH inhibited the growth rate of R. subcapitata, though
not to the same degree as the Cu-doped imogolite. At 100 mg/L, a 45% inhibition was observed,
with the software R, estimating an EC50 value of 100 mg/L, meaning that unless it is proven to
have chronic effects, classification is unnecessary. However, since the test was not performed in a
concentration range where the EC50 could be observed, this value has some uncertainty. Based on
the confidence interval, the true EC50 value should be between 85 and 120 mg/L, as seen in table 10.
It is recommended that further studies investigating chronic toxicity should be performed to assess
if the compound is toxic to aquatic life accurately. IMO-OH also presented some physical effects in

to see if the results could be replicated. As can be seen in figure 7, it was not possible to replicate

pose a threat to the animals in a short-term setting other than making it difficult to determine
lethally versus immobilisation. However, in a chronic test setup, the daphnids will spend a lot of
energy trying to free themselves. The immobilisation might also hinder them from finding food, so
all the energy spent trying to move cannot be reclaimed, and they might die due to starvation. The
results obtained in this study indicate that IMO-OH is toxic, though the extent is unknown, and
that the physical effects might prove harmful to aquatic life in the long term. Based on these results,
using the OECD guidelines for both short-term and chronic setups is not recommended unless the
organisms can be shielded from the influence of the gel formation to determine the toxicity accurately.

Short-Term Toxicity of NRCWE Advanced Materials
Of the three NRCWE materials, only NRCWE-21 showed any toxic effect on the immobilisation of
D. magna. An EC50 value of 91 mg/L was found for NRCWE-21. However, it is worth noting that
the standard deviation is high, which makes this result unreliable. NRCWE-20 and NRCWE-21

and ingested the NRCWE materials; a clear colour change was seen, with the colour of the an-

animals had trouble swimming at higher concentrations, possibly due to an increased weight from
the ingested materials. All three NRCWE materials were shown to inhibit the growth rate of R.

growth rate between 25 and 100 mg/L. The EC50 value for NRCWE-20 presented in table 10 is

to unstable exposure. NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22 have similar concentration-response curves, as
seen in figure 8. An EC50 value of 37 mg/L was found for NRCWE-21, and 24 mg/L for NRCWE-22.

The overall toxicity of NRCWE-20 is still to be determined, however the data generated in this
study suggest no or low toxicity to aquatic life. As for NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22, both were
shown to be toxic to R. subcapitata, and NRCWE-21 toxic to D. magna. The data generated in this
study would suggest that both are somewhat toxic to aquatic life, and should be further studied
to determine if a chronic classification is needed. However, in cases where no chronic data exists,
acute data can be used to determine the CLP classification. Based on the data generated in this
study, NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22 would be classified as Chronic 3, if they were found to have
BCF value ≥500 or not rapidly degradable. The physical effect of the three materials seen on
D. magna could possibly be harmful to aquatic organisms, as restriction of movement was seen at
higher concentrations. However, it is unlikely that the AdMs would be present in the environment
in high enough concentrations that the effect would be a concern.
As mentioned in section 4.3, Zn ions are known to be more toxic towards both D. magna and R.
subcapitata than Ni ions, which could explain the overall higher toxicity of NRCWE-21. The amount
of metal present in both NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22 are similar, with NRCWE-21 containing 27%
Zn, while NRCWE-22 contains 25% Ni. Ni has been shown to be more toxic towards R. subcapitata
than D. magna[43][44], and could explain why a toxic effect for NRCWE-22 is seen towards R.
subcapitata and not towards D. magna. The higher toxicity observed towards R. subcapitata could
also be due to the metals affecting the algae’s photosynthetic activity, leading to decreased growth
as proposed by Skjolding et al.[15]. Dissolved metal ions could be an important mechanism for the
toxicity observed and are believed to be a major reason for the toxicity of metal NM,[66] and can
promote the production of radical oxygen species. A study by Wang et al.[67] found that the toxicity
of ZnO nanoparticles stemmed solely from the release of Zn ions. This supports the theory that
dissolved ions could be the cause of toxicity observed in this study. NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22
were found to be more toxic than NRCWE-20, and NRCWE-20 contains only around half of the
toxic metals, at 12% Ni and 14% Zn, while NRCWE-21 contains 27 % Zn and NRCWE-22 contains
25 % Ni. This is most likely the biggest reason why no toxic effects were found for NRCWE-20.
The amount of Fe in the AdMs is roughly the same, ranging between 46% and 48%, so it is unlikely
that it would influence the toxicity. Further study of the ion release of the NRCWE is recommended
to better understand the toxicity of the AdMs and to make definite conclusions about the toxicity
stemming from dissolved ions.

6.3
Bioaccumulation of IMO-Cu and NRCWE Advanced Materials
The bioaccumulation of Cu in the Cu-doped imogolite and Fe, Zn, and Ni in the NRCWE AdMs
were investigated using the ingestion and depuration test setup described in section 4.5. The D.
magna were exposed to 0.1 mg/L suspension of IMO-Cu, and 1.0 mg/L for each of the NRCWE
AdMs.
D. magna have been known to feed on particles between 0.4-40 µm[68], making it possible for ag-
gregates of IMO-Cu and NRCWE materials to be ingested during feeding. The results indicate that
some Cu accumulated in D. magna, the concentration of Cu in the final measurement, at 72 hours,
was 1.2 ng/organism. No clear depuration of Cu was seen until feeding was introduced, suggesting
that the most significant method of depuration is through excretion. This is in line with the litera-
ture; a study by Zhao et al.[69] found that while Cu does accumulate in D. magna there is a high
rate of elimination once food is introduced. The study found that excretion accounted for 82-94%
of the animals’ total loss of Cu. That an accumulation of Cu is observed could affect the toxicity
of IMO-Cu as it is possible if there is continued exposure and a large enough accumulation of Cu
occurs, for it to be hazardous. One study by Gajda-Meissner et al.[70] notes that Zeta potential
can influence the charge of NM. A positive Zeta potential can result in higher biosorption of NM in
daphnids due to the positive charge of the NM, while a negative one results in lower biosorption.
The study also found that a negative Zeta value made it more difficult for the D. magna to depurate
the NM, and they were shown to be more toxic than those with a positive Zeta value. The Zeta
potential of IMO-Cu at a low concentration (0.75 mg/L) was measured at -10 mV in Elendt M7
medium and could be part of the explanation for the high toxicity and bioaccumulation seen in this
thesis. The previous study by Zhao et al.[69] also found a correlation between the amount and type
of food fed to the daphnids and the amount of Cu elimination achieved. The study showed that high
elimination of Cu correlated with a high food concentration and that when fed the freshwater algae
Chlorella Pyrenoidosa less than 1% of the ingested Cu remained in the daphnids after 48 hours. This
could indicate that a large portion of the remaining accumulated Cu could be eliminated through
feeding if the animals were fed a higher concentration of algae and/or fed a different algae.
So, while there is some evidence of Cu accumulation after exposure to IMO-Cu, a large amount
is eliminated through excretion, and it might be possible to eliminate almost all through feeding.
However, even a low accumulation potential can affect the overall toxicity, as it can become a prob-

lem over time if the accumulation is of Cu ions and not particle accumulation in the gut. Cu has
been shown to have serious effects on D. magna in the long term. A study by De Schamphelaere et
al.[71] investigated the chronic toxicity of dietary Cu to D. magna. After being fed Cu-contaminated
food for 21 days, an accumulation 30 times higher than the control was observed, as well as a 38

As with the Cu-doped imogolite, ingestion of the particles is a possibility, and this makes bioac-

ability to accumulate heavy metals.[72] All three NRCWE AdMs followed the same general trend
with a quick uptake before reaching a plateau between 4 and 24 hours. After being transferred to
clean medium, a small amount of depuration was observed for all three in the first 4 hours, and then
quick and complete depuration of almost all metals once fed.
The fact that the ratio between Fe and the other two metals was not constant during the ingestion
phase for NRCWE-20 could indicate that Ni and Zn dissolve much faster than Fe and that primary
uptake is through ingestion. As the D. magna might continue to ingest the agglomerated particles,
the Fe concentration goes up. Since the concentration of Ni and Zn does not follow, it could be
because they have already dissolved or partly dissolved. This would be in line with established
literature, as Ni and Zn are known to dissolve quickly, with one study determining the complete
dissolution of ZnO to happen within 20 minutes.[73] However, this difference in the ratios between
the metals during uptake is only present in the experiment with NRCWE-20. For NRCWE-21 the
concentration of Fe remains around 2 times higher than Zn concentration, and for NRCWE-22 the
Fe concentration remains around 3 times higher than Ni concentration. It might be because Zn
and Ni are more tightly bound in NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22. NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22 have
similar chemical structures, while NRCWE-20 has more Fe and oxygen. However, this has not been
further looked into in this study.
When looking at the Fe and Zn concentrations in the medium when exposed to NRCWE-21, the
measurements were lower than the expected natural concentration for Fe but higher than the ex-
pected natural concentration for Zn, see figure 11. This could be due to uncertainties regarding the

precision of the measurements, sedimentation or contamination. A mass balance of the Fe present
indicates a loss of 73-82 % Fe when compared to the theoretical value if it is assumed that all Fe
introduced from the material and medium stays within the system. A likely explanation for this loss
is the NRCWE material’s tendency towards agglomeration and sedimentation, as demonstrated in
6.1 and Appendix D. An explanation for the higher observed concentration of Zn could be due to
depuration from the animals. The concentration of Fe and Ni present in the medium after exposure
to NRCWE-22 were also measured; see figure 12. Here the concentration of Fe was also lower than
the expected amount present in the medium. The mass balance of Fe, with the same assumptions
as for NRCWE-21, shows a loss of 73-82 % of Fe. The most likely explanation is, once again, sed-
imentation. This fits with the Z-averages and PDI of NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22, table 7 and 8,
which indicates that they are prone to sedimentation and what was observed and documented in
Appendix D. Tables 6, 7, and 8, as well as figure 4 indicate that NRCWE-21 might be a bit more
stable in Elendt M7 medium than the other two NRCWE materials. This would mean that it is
more likely to stay in the water phase and not in sedimentation and might account for the higher
concentrations of metals seen in the medium for NRCWE-21 compared to NRCWE-22. However,
it is very slight, and both materials are prone to sedimentation and have a large loss of Fe when
looking at the mass balances.
The fact that almost no depuration was seen until feeding was introduced indicates that the main
depuration mechanism is excretion, as shown with IMO-Cu. This corresponds with the literature, as
excretion has been shown to be a major depuration route for D. magna[74] . Final measurements of
the metals for all three materials are around the average natural concentration found in D. magna,
indicating that total, or near total, depuration of all three metals was possible. Based on the tests
performed, there is no indication of bioaccumulation over time for any of the three metals present
in the NRCWE materials. However, both Ni and Zn have been shown to accumulate in D. magna if
the exposure is constant[75][76]. Prolonged exposure can have an effect on the animals. A study by
Evens et al.[75] found a significant accumulation of Ni in D. magna after being fed Ni-contaminated
food for 21 days. A 33 % reduction in reproduction and a 9 % reduction in growth was also observed.
It is, therefore, important to keep the potential for bioaccumulation in mind if the use of the materials
could lead to higher or constant contamination in aquatic environments.

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to assess the behaviour of the six selected AdMs in aquatic environments,
provide data on their ecotoxicity to the freshwater species R. subcapitata and D. magna, as well as
quantify the ingestion and depuration in D. magna. All studied AdMs were very polydisperse and
unstable in dispersion in all tested aquatic media and concentrations. They all exhibit agglomera-
tion and sedimentation processes. For most of the AdMs, there was no difference in stability across
media, but the Z-average and PDI in ISO 8692 medium indicated IMO-CH and IMO-Cu to be more
stable, with 2300 and 0.38 for IMO-CH at 12 mg/L and 310 and 0.34 for IMO-Cu at 12 mg/L.
However, the Zeta potentials did not corroborate this.
The toxicity of the AdMs towards D. magna could be ranked as IMO-Cu (0.22 mg/L) > NRCWE-
21(91 mg/L). IMO-CH, NRCWE-20 and NRCWE-22 showed no toxic effect. IMO-OH showed a
toxic effect(24 mg/L and 1600 mg/L), but the OECD Guideline test was unsuitable for accurately
determining the toxicity due to the formation of gel which made it difficult to determine if immo-
bilisation was caused by toxicity or by the gel. The toxicity of the AdMs towards R. subcapitata
was ranked as IMO-Cu (0.56 mg/L) > NRCWE-22 (24 mg/L) > NRCWE-21 (37 mg/L) > IMO-
CH (100 mg/L). NRCWE-20 showed no continuous toxic effect on the inhibition of R. subcapitata.
Once again, IMO-OH proved unsuitable for the test setup, and the toxicity could not accurately be
determined, but a toxic effect was shown (6.8 mg/L and 5300 mg/L). IMO-Cu showed a tendency
towards bioaccumulation of Cu, with 1.2 ng/organism left after 72 hours, which could enhance the
toxic effect observed in short-term toxicity tests. No evidence of bioaccumulation could be observed
for any of the NRCWE AdMs. Zn and Ni have the potential to bioaccumulate, so this should be
kept in mind if large quantities are to be emitted into the environment.
The results obtained in this thesis indicate the toxicity observed for most of the AdMs is related to
dissolved metal ions. A physical effect was also observed for the NRCWE materials in D. magna at
large concentrations, which seemed to weigh the animals down. This physical effect, however, seems
to be concentration-dependent, making it unlikely that the levels of contamination in nature would
be high enough for it to be a serious concern. IMO-CH and NRCWE exhibited the lowest toxicity
levels of the selected AdMs suggestion potential as novel materials used in the green transition. In
accordance with the CLP classification, IMO-Cu should be classified as Acute 1. Since there is some
59

indication of bioaccumulation, it is possible that it should be classified as Chronic 1 as well. As
no chronic data exists, NRCWE-21 and NRCWE-22 should be classified as Chronic 3 based on the
acute data, but only if they are found to have a BCF value ≥500 or not be rapidly degradable.
It is recommended to study these materials further, as some data, such as acute data for fish and
chronic data, are currently non-existent. Generating more data on these materials will ensure that
decisions regarding the safety of these materials are as informed and safe as possible in regard to
their aquatic toxicity.

A
3,5-Dichlorphenol as a Reference Compound and Validity Crite-
ria for Acute Toxicity Tests
Acute toxicity tests were performed using 3,5-Dichlorphenol (C6H4Cl2O). The algal growth inhibition
test was performed with R. subcapitata and the acute immobilisation test were performed with D.
magna, the results can be seen in figure 13. All tests were performed in accordance with OECD
Standards following test guide OECD 201[27] and test guide OECD 202[28]. The average growth
rate of the controls for R. subcapitata were 1.1 d−1 which meets the validity criteria of a mean
growth rate above 0.92 d−1. The obtained EC50 value of 2.1 mg/L with a 95 % confidence interval
of [1.9; 2.3] falls within the the EC50 range obtained by others.[77] For the immobilisation test
with D. magna, all validity criteria were fulfilled, with less than 10% of the control animals being
immobilised. The obtained EC50 value of 3.8 mg/L with a 95% confidence interval of [0.7; 6.9] is
within the range found by others.[78]

B
Determining IMO-Cu Test Range Using CuSO4 ∗5H2O as a Ref-
erence Test and ICP-MS to Determine Cu Percentage
Acute toxicity tests were performed using CuSO4 ∗5H2O to have a frame of reference for Cu toxicity
on both R. subcapitata and D. magna, the results can be seen in figure 14. For R. subcapitata a
mean growth rate of 0.98 d−1 was achieved, fulfilling the validity criteria of a growth rate above 0.92
d−1. An EC50 value of 0.42 mg/L with a 95% confidence interval of [0.33; 0.52] were found. For the
immobilisation test with D. magna all the validity criteria were fulfilled with less than 10% of the
control animals becoming immobilised. An EC50 value of 0.74 mg/L with a 95% confidence interval
of [-; 1.5] were found. In addition to the acute toxicity tests, ICP-MS analysis was used on a sample
of IMO-Cu stock solution to determine the concentration of Cu in the imogolite. The percentage of
Cu in IMO-Cu was determined to be 3.2 %. The test ranges used for IMO-Cu were found based on
the reference test and the percentage of Cu in the imogolite.

Physical Effects of IMO-OH
Documentation for the physical effects produced by IMO-OH when in contact with Elendt M7 and

STABILITY OF IMO-CU AND IMO-CH IN ISO 8692 MEDIUM OVER TIME
F
Stability of IMO-Cu and IMO-CH in ISO 8692 Medium over Time
IMO-Cu and IMO-CH were tested in suspensions of 12 mg/L using DLS to measure for 1 minute ev-
ery minute for 24 hours. IMO-CH proved too unstable to measure for 24 hours, and the measurement
stopped after 735 measurements.
Figure 18: PDI and Derived Count Rate of IMO-Cu 12 mg/L suspension over a 24 hour period
Figure 19: PDI and Derived Count Rate of IMO-CH 12 mg/L suspension over a 12 hour period
The amount of particles counted went down for both imogolites over time. IMO-Cu seems to stabilise
with just below 10000 particles counted after 400 minutes. IMO-CH does not have as many counts
and seems to stabilise after 300 minutes. There is a big difference in the observed PDI over time.
IMO-Cu starts with a PDI near 1, but it quickly drops and seems to stabilise around 0.4. IMO-CH
stays at a PDI of 1 over time until it becomes too unstable to measure. These results indicate
that larger aggregates do sediment over time and that only smaller particles and aggregates stay
in suspension as the number of particles counted becomes smaller. This, combined with the PDI,
indicates that IMO-Cu stabilised over time, but IMO-CH seems to remain unstable even as larger
aggregates sedimentate.
