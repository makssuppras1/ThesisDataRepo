Abstract
Environmental issues and the ﬁnite nature of fossil fuels is driving the world towards renewable
energy sources (RES) such as wind and solar. However, the stochastic nature of RES has moti-
vated many countries to combine more than one renewable technology to support the grid. This
combination of more than one RES is known as hybrid power generation.
This thesis presents the development and investigation of a dynamic model and control of a hybrid
power plant consisting of a PMSG wind farm with a capacity of 120 MW and a PV power plant
with a capacity of 40 MW.
A big challenge that has arisen the last decades, is for a renewable power plant and hence a hybrid
power plant (HPP) to stay connected and support the grid during a grid disturbance. This ability
of the HPP is known as the Low-Voltage Ride Through. The present study focuses on investigating
the LVRT capability of the hybrid power plant and for that purpose a dynamic model and control
is built up in DIgSILENT PowerFactory, which represents a grid-connected hybrid power plant.
Diﬀerent control strategies are implemented for the appropriate operation of the HPP in steady
state, as well as in faulty conditions.
The proposed control strategies that refer to both the
generator-side and the grid-side components of the HPP are presented.
An advanced LVRT control is implemented to the grid-side converter of the model in order to
improve the LVRT capability of the hybrid power plant. Furthermore, a control for the PV power
plant is presented. Many simulations are done in order to test the performance of the HPP under
diﬀerent weather conditions.
Finally, the results of the simulations are presented and some useful ﬁndings arise from the study.
The results have been shown that the HPP has LVRT capabilities and can provide further support
with reactive power during a fault event in the grid. Some suggestions for further research in the
future are also provided.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this chapter an introduction on the project is presented. An overview of the deﬁnition of the
hybrid power plant, as well as the scope of the project are included. Finally, the structure of the
project is provided.
1.1
Backround and motivation
As the years go by, the rate at which the world consumes fossil fuels is increasing as the world’s
population increases. The fact that the reserves of fossil fuels are ﬁnite, as well as the environmen-
tal issues associated with the unstoppable use of fossil fuels, has led mankind to turn to alternative
sources in order to meet the demand and reduce the problems related to the depletion of fossils
fuels and the pollution of the environment. Renewable energy sources, such as wind power and
solar power are constantly replenished. Furthermore, they are considered as clean sources of en-
ergy with lower environmental impact than conventional energy technologies, which makes them a
reliable source of energy. However, the fact that power produced by renewable energy resources is
stochastic has motivated the combination of more than one renewable energy resources, which is
known as a hybrid power generation.
A hybrid power plant (HPP) consisting of wind and solar energy is proposed in this project in
order to increase the reliability of energy supplied to the grid. The main advantage of a hybrid
wind and solar PV system lies on the fact that these two individual technologies complement each
other in the sense that the weakness of one technology can be compensated by the strength of the
other.
Power system voltage stability is deﬁned as the ability of the power system to remain in a state
of equilibrium and maintain voltage magnitudes in all buses within speciﬁed operating limits,
according to the grid code requirements [Endegnanew et al., 2013], under steady state conditions

[Balu and Maratukulam, 1994]. Voltage instability problem has been a concern of high importance
in the electrical power system’s operation. Voltage instability occurs when a power system fails to
maintain the voltage at all buses within acceptable limits after the system is being subjected to a
disturbance.
1.2
Hybrid Power Plant
Two diﬀerent terminologies exist in the literature, i.e. hybrid power system (HPS) [Lazarov et al.,
2005], that has been broadly used until recently, and the newly introduced hybrid power plant
(HPP) [Dehghan et al., 2009].
A HPS consists of three major components; generation, a high voltage transmission grid and a
distribution system. It is designed for the generation and use of electrical power and it can be a
large centralized electricity grid or even a microgrid and incorporate more than one type of power
source. In that way a HPS can deliver electricity from producers to consumers, control energy ﬂow,
reduce the losses, and make the performance of the electric network more reliable and controllable.
In contrast to an HPS, the attention regarding the Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) is on the owner’s
point of view and it needs to be operated with the greatest possible reliability, capacity and
eﬃciency, along with minimum operating and maintenance costs. However, the fact that wind
power and solar power are non-dispatchable sources of electricity, which means that are unreliable
and unpredictable because of the weather and changes in environmental conditions [Mousa et al.,
2010], has made the need of combining these two renewable energy sources great.
In this thesis, the HPP consists of a wind power plant and a PV power plant while a battery energy
storage system is out of the scope. A combination of wind and solar energy, in order to overcome
the uncertainties that were mentioned above, seems to be a quite reliable solution [Dihrab and
Sopian, 2010], due to the following reasons:
• Overcome the possible reduced capacity factor of each renewable energy source (RES)
• Improve power quality
• Reduce grid’s instability due to the variability of RES
1.3
Aim and Scope
The overall objective is to investigate the capability of an HPP to provide voltage support in the
power system. In this context, this project develops a dynamic model in DIgSILENT, which is
a power system analysis software application for analyzing generation, transmission, distribution
and industrial systems.
The main focus of the thesis is to examine the eﬀect of a hybrid power plant, consisting of a Wind
Power Plant and a PV Power Plant in the power system, when a balanced fault is applied and the
power system faces voltage instability issues. The aim of the dynamic model is to develop a control

3
strategy that will enhance the Low-Voltage Ride Through capability of the hybrid power plant by
providing reactive power support in order to contribute to the voltage recovery and restore the
balance in the system. The present thesis attempts to answer the following research questions:
i. Is a hybrid power plant capable of preserving voltage stability in the system in steady state
operation? ii. Can a hybrid power plant provide voltage support to the network when the latter
faces severe grid faults?
The voltage stability studies will be examined in the time frame of 0-10 seconds, named the
transient voltage stability or short-term voltage stability. The short-term voltage stability can be
aﬀected by induction motors, DC-links, generators and excitation dynamics and static var compen-
sators (SVC). In recent years, the wind farm installations consist of variable speed wind generators
mostly, as they have the advantage of regulating their own voltage by producing reactive power,
through their power electronic converters in order to overcome such voltage instability issues on
short-term.
The main tasks of the project are as follows:
• Develop a dynamic model for the PV system
• Incorporate the developed model into an existing wind turbine model provided by the DTU
[Michalke et al., 2007]
• Investigate the performance of the HPP in normal operation.
• Investigate the performance of the HPP during fault conditions.
• Develop an LVRT control in order to improve the LVRT capability of the HPP.
1.4
Assumptions and Limitations
The present thesis is based on some speciﬁc assumptions and limitations:
• The present model is built up only for Low-Voltage Ride Through studies, without taking
into consideration the frequency support of the system.
• Economical aspects, i.e. proﬁt maximization of the HPP owner, are out of the scope of this
thesis.
• Marketing aspects are not taken into consideration.

Chapter 2
State of the Art
In this chapter, a literature review relevant to the use of each power generation individually, i.e.
wind and PV generation, as well as of a hybrid power plant for supporting the voltage of the power
system in case of fault, is presented.
2.1
Literature review
Hybrid power plant technology is still in its infancy. However, it is expected to expand rapidly in
the upcoming decades. The research regarding the HPP used for the voltage support of a power
system is mostly inspired from similar problems faced in each renewable energy technology (wind,
solar etc.) separately. The use of the models for the wind power and the solar energy generation to
support system’s voltage has been chosen without taking into consideration the challenges involved
with the implementation of LVRT capability. Speciﬁcally, the unanticipated diﬃculties regarding
the implementation of the LVRT capability related to the policy issues and the costs associated
with the enhanced LVRT techniques that are being installed are not the focus of the present thesis.
An understanding of short-term voltage stability and ride through capability of wind farms is
presented in paper [Singh and Chandra, 2008]. The modeling and the performance of a directly
driven, variable speed wind turbine based on multi-pole permanent magnet synchronous generator
(PMSG) under diﬀerent kind of disturbances, is presented. The proposed system uses a PMSG-
based variable speed wind energy conversion system consisting of two back to back converters. The
generator side converter is responsible for controlling the generator speed in order to achieve max-
imum power point tracking, whereas grid side converter is responsible for regulating the DC-link
voltage under grid disturbances. The simulation results show that smooth power transmission and
DC-link voltage regulation is achieved during disturbances, with the current and voltage harmonics
being kept within the acceptable limits.
==PAGE:18==
6
State of the Art
A detailed model that reinforces the LVRT capability of Permanent Magnet Synchronous Gener-
ators (PMSG) wind turbines during grid faults is presented in [Michalke et al., 2007]. Due to the
fact that the PMSG wind turbine’s full-scale frequency converter decouples the generator from the
grid, without being directly aﬀected by grid faults, it can be assumed that PMSG wind turbines
can perform fault ride-through and support the grid during faults more easily compared to the
conventional wind turbines. The control strategy that is developed attempts to provide a control
for both the generator-side converter and the grid or line-side converter (LSC), by keeping the DC-
link voltage and the stator voltage for the generator constant, and also independently controlling
the active and reactive power production of the turbine at the PCC. In order for overvoltages in
the DC-link to be avoided, a chopper is applied to the DC-link. Furthermore, a voltage controller
is implemented to the (LSC) in order to force the (LSC) to generate or to consume reactive power
in order to balance the grid voltage level. The author’s conclusion is that PMSG wind turbines
can improve the fault-ride capability signiﬁcantly leading to less power losses and a more stable
power system.
The authors from the previous paper extended their research by placing an oﬀshore active stall
wind farm nearby the PMSG wind farm, in order to assess whether the performance of the active
stall wind farm had improved or not. In [Hansen and Michalke, 2009], the same control strategy
as in [Michalke et al., 2007] is used, i.e. the generator-side converter is responsible for control-
ling the DC-link voltage and maintaining the stator voltage of the generator constant at its rated
value. Meanwhile, the task of the grid-side converter is to control the active and reactive power
in the grid voltage reference frame independently. A case study is carried out and it refers to an
oﬀshore PMSG wind farm connected to a power transmission system. After a set of simulations,
it is concluded that such a PMSG wind farm equipped with this control can enhance the fault-ride
capability of the wind turbine and at the same time it helps the active stall wind farm to stay
connected during the grid fault without the need of any extra devices.
In [Mali et al., 2014], the standards for interconnection of grid-connected wind turbine generators
during fault conditions are being discussed. Furthermore, the signiﬁcance of the Short Circuit
Ratio (SCR) is presented in this paper. In order to examine the way that wind turbines and grid
phenomena interact with each other, the characteristics of the Point of Common Connection (PCC)
are required. Diﬀerent solutions for LVRT applications have been proposed on [Mali et al., 2014],
such as a chopper resistor applied to the DC-link in order to burn the excessive energy during
grid voltage dips. Another interesting solution would be a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS)
with batteries connected to the DC-link in order to store energy, resulting in transient stability
improvements. After running some simulations, the authors concluded that WTGs equipped with
full or partial converters enhance strong FRT capability during the grid faults. The BESS acts as
a decoupler between the rectiﬁer and the inverter, protecting in that way the wind turbine system
by isolating it from the grid.
An overview of a method for the control of a 3-phase 2-stage solar converter during grid faults, is
presented in [Eydi et al., 2016]. The electronic power converter that is used in the photovoltaic sys-
tem consists of a DC/DC converter and a DC/AC inverter. Under normal conditions the DC/DC
converter is responsible for getting the maximum power extracted from the pv array, while the
inverter adjusts the voltage of the DC-link. During a grid fault, a voltage drop occurs and the grid
==PAGE:19==

side converter injects reactive power to the grid, the amount of which depends on the amount of
the voltage drop. Futhermore, the inverter injects active power into the grid in a way that under
a grid fault, the current of all the phases remains within their limits. The authors concluded that
the proposed method enhances the LVRT capability of the solar power plant.
A more advanced control strategy of PV system for LVRT capability enhancement is presented in
[Yang et al., 2014]. The main idea is to control the output power of the PV array through the
DC/DC converter depending on the voltage drop during the fault. In that way, the PV generation
system contributes to the balance of the grid voltage level. Compared to the conventional tech-
niques, the aforementioned method can also provide reactive power support by delivering reactive

3.1
Simulation software
The object of the thesis is the low-voltage ride through capability enhancement of a grid-connected
hybrid power plant. As mentioned in chapter 1, the present project makes use of the power system
simulation tool DIgSILENT, which has the ability to simulate load ﬂow, balanced steady-state

A large scale wind-solar hybrid power plant is described and investigated. The system consists of

3.2
Modeling of Type 4 Wind Turbine Generator
A variable speed wind turbine (Type 4) is used for the model. This particular technology uses a
multipole permanent magnet synchronous generator (PMSG) and a full scale power converter. A
representation of the aforementioned type of wind turbine is shown in Figure 3.1.

In contrast to the double-fed induction generator (DFIG) wind turbines, the PSMG wind turbine
completely decouples the generator from the network. The advantage of this decoupling lies on the
fact that the generator is not aﬀected by any change of the grid voltage. The power converter con-
sists of the generator-side converter, an intermediary DC link capacitor and the grid-side converter.
The generator-side converter acts as a rectiﬁer in order to convert the alternating current (AC)
to direct currect (DC). Furthermore, it is responsible for controlling the voltage at the DC-link in
order to be kept constant, through the DC link capacitor [Hansen and Michalke, 2008]. The DC
voltage is then converted again into AC by the grid-side converter, which is used as an interface
between the DC-link voltage and the grid.

This type of wind turbine has been quite attractive in the recent years due to the absence of

This thesis deals with the modeling of a gearless direct-driven variable-speed wind turbine using a
multi-pole magnet synchronous generator (PMSG) and a full-scale power converter.

3.5
Grid Code Requirements in Fault Response
Low Voltage Ride-Through capability of RES plays a key role on the transient stability of a power
system, since a possible disconnection of a large amount of distributed generation during a fault
would cause the power system to become unstable. Therefore, it is crucial for the RES to stay
connected to the power system when a fault occurs, according to the grid code requirements, as
presented in [Pertl et al., 2018]. Grid codes are technical regulations, which are deﬁned by ev-
ery country and supervised by the ENTSO-E, and facilitate a safe and secure operation of the
electrical grid. According to [Endegnanew et al., 2013], Wind Power Plants (WPP) with power
output greater than 1.5 MW must withstand voltage drops down to 20% at the point of common
connection (PCC) without being disconnected for at least 0.5s, as illustrated in Figure 3.7.

3.6
Unsuccessful LVRT Control strategies
When a grid fault occurs, a voltage drop at the terminal of the PMSG is observed. That voltage
drop leads to reduced active power from the grid-side converter delivered to the grid. However,
inertia causes the wind generator to continue to produce active power, leading to a power imbal-
ance in the system. This excess of active power that is created charges the DC-link capacitor and
consequently an overvoltage can be created at the DC-link. In order to avoid this problem diﬀerent
control strategies were implemented and investigated in PowerFactory.
Initially, a challenging control strategy presented in [Hansen and Michalke, 2009] was tested in
PowerFactory. According to that control strategy, the generator-side converter is responsible for
controlling the DC-link voltage, as well as for controlling and maintaining the stator voltage of
the generator US at 3.3kV, which is its rated value. On the other hand, the task of the grid side
converter is to control the active power and the reactive power in the AC-voltage reference frame,
i.e. d-axis current is determined by the active power, q-axis current is determined by the reactive
power. However, that control strategy has a drawback when it comes to the modeling of a hybrid
power plant with a PhotoVoltaic system, and speciﬁcally with the connection of the PV generator
with a DC/DC converter at the DC-link. Particularly, as already discussed in a previous section,
the DC/DC converter of the PV system is responsible for adapting the voltage at the PV panel
terminal in order to extract the maximum point of power. Therefore, the DC-link voltage needs to
be controlled by the grid-side converter, which is found to be not feasible with the aforementioned
control strategy.
Therefore, a diﬀerent strategy was implemented in order for the system to enhance the LVRT ca-
pability of the hybrid power plant. The generator-side converter was responsible for controlling the
==PAGE:29==
3.7 Proposed LVRT strategy for Hybrid Power Plant

active power P and the reactive power Q in the generator voltage reference frame independently.
That means that the reference of d-axis current is determined by the active power, whereas the
reference of q-axis current is determined by the reactive power. At the same time, the grid-side con-
verter was responsible for controlling and maintaining the DC-link voltage UDC within acceptable
limits [Endegnanew et al., 2013]. However, during the fault, reactive power from the hybrid power
plant to the grid is required, in order for the grid voltage to be recovered and the wind turbine and
the PV plant to stay connected to the grid. Since there is no exchange of reactive power from the
generator side to the grid side, due to the fact that the PMSG and the electric power network are
decoupled by the DC-link and no reactive power is transferred from the generation side to the grid
side, it would seem pointless for the generator-side converter to control and regulate the amount
of reactive power fed to the grid when the voltage drops during the fault.

DC-link

represented by a General Load (ElmLod) and is connected or disconnected according to the switch
position. The chopper is activated when the fault happens and the DC-link voltage exceeds the 1.1

and help for the grid voltage recovery. This capability of the HPP to stay connected to the grid
during the fault is known as Low-Voltage Ride Through.
The simulations are carried out by taking into consideration a grid fault that occurs at the terminal
where the external grid is connected (see Figure 3.6). The duration of the fault is 300 ms and then

Performance of the HPP under diﬀerent weather conditions
In this section, the performance of the hybrid power plant at 3 diﬀerent scenarios is presented.
Diﬀerent performance of the parameters of the HPP is observed, based on diﬀerent values of solar
irradiation and wind speed. The three scenarios are the following:
• Scenario 1: High Wind - Low Solar
• Scenario 2: Low Wind - High Solar
• Scenario 3: Low Wind - Low Solar
The ﬁrst scenario refers to high wind and low solar. In this scenario the value of the wind is
constant and equal to 12 m/s. On the other hand, some steps are applied in the solar irradiation
in order for the PV power plant to generate low active power. The solar irradiation is reduced
from 1000 to 700 W/m2 at t=10 seconds, after 20 seconds is reduced again from 700 to 300 W/m2
at t=30 seconds. The last step decreases the solar irradiation from 300 to 100 W/m2 at t=50
seconds, which preserves this value until the simulation is done, as illustrated in Figure 4.22a.
In the second scenario, the solar irradiation is kept constant at 1000 W/m2 throughout the simu-
lation. However, the steps are applied in the wind speed, reducing the value of wind speed from
12 to 8 m/s every 20 seconds with a step of 1 m/s. The ﬁrst is applied at t=0 seconds and the last
step is applied at t=60 seconds, as depicted in Figure 4.22b.
The last scenario refers to low wind and low solar, which means that the same steps that were
discussed above are applied in this scenario as well.
All these steps were applied because it is more realistic, mainly for the wind speed but also for
the solar irradiation to reduce gradually than to present a sharp decrease from high to low values.
Consequently, the simulation time is 100 seconds, in order for the system to have the time to
stabilize for every change that occurs to the model. In all three scenarios the simulations are
carried out and the comparison between the scenarios is made, with the Advanced LVRT control
being implemented in the model. Thus, the LVRT control strategy is not taken into consideration
in this part of the simulations. However, the performance of the Hybrid Power Plant in these
scenarios is not aﬀected by the control strategy of the grid side converter. Therefore, the changes
in the power output of the PMSG and the PV generator aﬀects only the voltage at the DC-link
and the energy that is burnt in the chopper, and not the reactive power support by the grid side
converter. The fault incident occurs at t=80 seconds and is cleared after 300 ms.

Chapter 5
Conclusions and future work
In the ﬁnal chapter a summary of the project along with the overall results of the work and a general
discussion are presented. Furthermore, ideas for future work are also provided.
5.1
Conclusions
The objective of the project has been to investigate the capability of a hybrid power plant to
provide voltage support in the power system. A study on the behaviour of a grid-connected HPP
in steady state and faulty conditions has been performed. Speciﬁcally, the aim of the project has
been to examine the Low-Voltage Ride Through capability of the Hybrid Power Plant in case of
a severe fault on a terminal of the system. Furthermore, the interaction between the wind power
plant and the PV power plant is evaluated. A set of studies have been conducted concerning the
interaction of the wind power plants or the PV power plants with the power system and their
ability to ride-through severe disturbances in the grid. However, the hybrid wind/PV power plant
is a promising and upcoming technology and it is expected to play an important role in the future
power systems.
In this study, the hybrid power plant consisted of a wind farm with capacity of 120 MW and a
PV power plant with capacity of 40 MW. The voltage stability studies were examined in the time
frame of 0-10 seconds, which are also known as short-term stability studies. The aim of the project
was to develop a dynamic model with the simulation tool of DIgSILENT PowerFactory and test
the ability of the power system to ride through a grid fault by staying connected to the grid and
contribute to restore the balance of the system. The following conclusions have been derived:
• The connection of the PV power plant to the DC-link has been implemented successfully.
• The steady-state operation of the individual units and consequently the overall simulation of
the hybrid power plant in normal conditions has been carried out successfully.
==PAGE:58==
46
Conclusions and future work
• The hybrid power plant provides reactive power support to the grid whenever the grid voltage
drops during a grid fault.
• The system fulﬁlls the grid code requirements for LVRT in Denmark, allowing for further
studies and improvements in the existing work.
The complexity of the model is relatively high due to the fact that the PV power plant is con-
nected to the DC-link. For that reason many control strategies were implemented and tested in
PowerFactory unsuccessfully. The tasks of this project were:
• to build a dynamic model in order to represent a hybrid wind/PV power plant
• to develop controls for both the PV power plant and the Wind power plant
• to develop LVRT studies for the Hybrid Power Plant
• to evaluate the performance of the hybrid power plant for changes in the weather conditions
• to obtain the inﬂuence of the onshore line impedance on the level of the voltage drop at the
20kV terminal during to the fault
From the present study some ﬁndings have arisen, which are presented and summarized. These
ﬁndings take into consideration the implementation of diﬀerent controls that were analyzed and
evaluated. More speciﬁcally:
• A dynamic model for providing voltage support to the grid has been built up. For that
reason a set of investigations of the LVRT and voltage support capability of the HPP has
been conducted and diﬀerent control strategies have been implemented without success. The
control strategy that has been found to be the most appropriate in order for the HPP to
operate properly is the one, where the generator-side converter controls the active power
produced by the PMSG wind farm and the stator voltage of the PMSG. At the same time
the grid-side converter controls the voltage at the DC-link in order for overvoltages to be
avoided. Furthermore, it is responsible for controlling the reactive power that is provided to
the grid.
• A PV control has been added in the model in order to avoid possible overvoltages at the
DC-link due to the power imbalance, during the fault. According to that control, the power
output of the PV power plant is regulated in order to produce almost zero active power
during the fault. In that way, the DC voltage faces less oscillations and also less energy is
lost at the chopper.
• An advanced LVRT controller at the grid-side converter, namely the Advanced LVRT control
has been implemented to assure that during the fault, the grid is supported with an additional
reactive current, fulﬁlling the reactive current requirements. The Advanced LVRT control
has improved the LVRT capability of the HPP, which is the objective of the project.

5.2 Future research
47
• The model has been tested for diﬀerent weather conditions in order to evaluate how the
voltage at the DC-link is aﬀected by changes of the wind speed and the solar irradiation.
According to the results, the DC voltage needs more time to stabilize, presenting larger
oscillations, whenever the wind speed changes in contrast to changes in the solar irradiation.
However, changes in solar irradiation result in the worst behaviour in terms of energy lost in
the chopper. On the other hand, changes at the wind speed results in less energy lost at the
chopper.
• The eﬀect of the grid fault on the voltage at the 20kV terminal for diﬀerent values of the
onshore line impedance has been tested. As it is expected for lower values of the Zline the
voltage drops signiﬁcantly, since the fault current ﬁnds less resistance to travel from the
terminal where the fault occurs to the measured terminal (20kV). Furthermore, the results
showed that the amount of fault current that can reach the 20kV terminal depends mostly
on the value of the reactance X of the line and not so much on the value of the resistance R.
From the above ﬁndings, it can be concluded that the dynamic model of the hybrid wind/PV power
plant that was built up in this project, has the ability to stay connected to the grid throughout
a voltage drop. It is essential that the park stays online in order to compensate for faulty grid
conditions and also for blackouts to be prevented. As it has been derived from this study, the
individual technologies of the hybrid power plant can complement each other, providing more
stability and reliability to the grid.
5.2
Future research
In order to improve the LVRT capability of the hybrid power plant even further, a battery energy
storage system (BESS) can be added at the DC-link bus. A big advantage of having a BESS is
that during a power imbalance due to a grid fault, the excess power can be stored to the battery
system for later use instead of being burnt at the chopper. That means that the rating of the
chopper can be reduced. On the other hand, when there is a lack of power from the renewable
sources’ generators, the BESS can provide the required power to charge the capacitor for the
voltage recovery.
Speciﬁcally, the battery is connected to DC-link though a bi-directional DC/DC converter in order
for the battery to get charged or discharged for regulating the DC bus energy. If there is a positive
power diﬀerence between the generator side and the grid side, leading to an overvoltage at the
DC-link, then the battery gets charged by transferring the excess power from the DC bus to the
battery. On the other hand, in case of negative power diﬀerence between the generator side and
the grid side, the battery transfers power to the DC bus through the DC/DC converter, helping in
that way to the DC voltage recovery. Some interesting ideas concerning the modeling of the BESS
are presented in papers [Serna-Garcés et al., 2018] and [Bollapragada et al., 2017].
There are many battery technologies that can improve the LVRT capability of the HPP such
as Lithium-ion batteries, nickel-cadmium batteries, lead acid batteries or even a super-capacitor
energy storage system, which is a very promising technology.
