Abstract 
The worldwide shipping industry holds about 90 per cent of the world trade. More than 
50,000 commercial ships are transporting goods of all kinds worldwide. The world fleet is 
registered in more than 150 nations and operated by more than one million seafarers of 
nearly all nationalities. It is easy to say that shipping is one of the most important pillars of 
the modern economy and without this industry the transportation of affordable food and 
raw materials would be impossible. Despite the countless advantages of this industry, there 
is also a major concern regarding carbon dioxide emissions. For that reason, in 2018 the 
International Maritime Organization (IMO) decided to adopt an initial strategy for the 
reduction of greenhouse gases by at least 50 per cent by 2050 compared to 2008 and to 
reduce the carbon intensity by at least 40 per cent by 2030. Many different candidate 
measures have been proposed by various sponsors at the IMO. The candidate measures are 
categorized into short-term measures (2018-2023), medium-term (2023-2030) and long-
term measures beyond 2030. The objective of this thesis is to describe and assess the latest 
submissions regarding short-term measures. The advantages and potential impacts of the 
two predominant measures are presented. Moreover, the carbon intensity indicator 
proposal is tested on nine different sea routes in both Tankers, Bulk Carrier and Container 
ships and the main assumption for compliance with this measure was the reduction of the 
operational speed. From the results, it can be judged which ship types could implement this 
measure but also what are the negative consequences for the rest of them. This work could 
be useful for the design of new strategies for the reduction of greenhouse gases.

Chapter 1-Introduction 
 
In 2018 the International Maritime Organization (IMO) developed an initial strategy on 
ship emission reduction, setting out a vision that reflects IMO's commitment to that and 
phasing out greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from international shipping as quickly as 
possible. To achieve this initiative, the IMO has divided this strategy into three different 
chronicle times. These chronicle times are divided into short-term measures (2018-2023), 
mid-term measures (2023-2030) and long-term measures(2030-). Furthermore, the IMO 
has approved a follow-up system to be used as a planning tool to full-fill the deadlines set 
out in the initial IMO Strategy. In particular, the initial GHG strategy foresees a reduction 
in global shipping's carbon intensity to reduce CO2 emissions per transport job on average 
across international shipping by at least 40 % by 2030, pursuing efforts to 70 % by 2050, 
compared with 2008. At this stage, different short-term proposals have been submitted 
from different countries, proposing different strategies for achieving the objectives. 
Specifically, short-term measures refer to the candidate measures that will be proposed 
from the members between 2018-2023. The most prevalent proposal will be in force in 
2023 and it will last until 2030.  Medium-term measures will be in force from 2030 until 
2050 and long-term measures will take effect from 2050 and onwards. 
 
1.1 Scope of the thesis 
 
The main goal of this project is the analysis of the aforementioned short term proposals, 
based on different criteria. Those criteria are the easiness of implementation, the 
enforcement, economic factors, geographical factors, and efficiency factors (if those 
proposals have a real impact on GHG reduction) and the impacts of those proposals for the 
developing countries, which primarily depend on shipping trading. In the sections below 
there is a detailed description of the thesis objectives and the structure of the thesis. 
 
1.2 Objectives 
 
The learning objectives of this project are listed below. 
• The presentation of the latest submissions in the IMO for greenhouse gases 
reduction, which is submitted from the different IMO member states and other

Chapter 2- Existing greenhouse gases reduction measures 
 
As mentioned above there are three unique narratives measures (short-term, mid-term and 
long-term measures), the International Maritime Organization will follow to accomplish 
the goals for greenhouse gases (GHG) reduction until 2050. Specifically, the initial short-
term measures that IMO is proposing, are listed epigrammatically below: 
• Improvement of the existing energy efficiency framework with a focus on Energy 
Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) and the Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan 
(SEEPM). 
• Development of technical and operational energy efficiency measures for both new 
and existing ships. 
• Setting-up an Existing Fleet Improvement Program. 
• Analysis of speed optimization and speed reduction as a measure. 
• Potential measures for the reduction of methane and other volatile organic 
compounds. 
• Consideration and analysis of measures to encourage authorities to develop more 
sustainable ports. 
• Research and development in the field of marine propulsion and on the sector of 
alternative low-carbon and zero-carbon fuels. 
In this chapter, there will be a  description of the Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) 
which is the basis of many short-term measures. 
 
2.1 Introduction to the EEDI 
 
The Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) was made mandatory for new ships by the 
Marine Environment Committee (MEPC) at its 62nd session in July 2011 and it the first 
legally valid protocol (IMO 2011a). The aim of the EEDI for new ships is the usage of 
more energy-efficient technologies like equipment or engines. he EEDI requires a 
minimum energy efficiency level per capacity mile (e.g., ton-mile) for different vessel type 
(Polakis,2019). The EEDI is a performance-based, non-prescriptive mechanism that leaves 
the choice of technologies to be used by the industry in the specific design of ships. As the 
required level of energy efficiency is reached, ship designers and builders are free to use 
the ship's most cost-effective solutions to meet the bylaws.

All the terms of the formula are detailed described below. 
• Vref: is the vessel’s speed measured in knots. The speed is affected by the following 
conditions: deep-water operations, calm weather including no waves and wind, 
loading condition corresponding to the ship’s capacity and total propulsion power 
(Polakis, 2019). 
 
• Capacity: for EEDI is measured in MT and there are different loading conditions 
based on the ship type. Specifically, the capacity for bulk carriers, gas carriers, 
refrigerated cargo carriers, LNG carriers, Ro-Ro cargo ships, Ro-Ro passenger 
ships, general cargo ships and combination carriers corresponds to the DWT at 
maximum load draft. Moreover, the capacity corresponds to 70% DWT for 
containerships and Gross tonnage (GT) for passenger and cruise passenger ships. 
 
• PME(i): is 75% of the engines maximum continuous rating (MCR) in (Kw). 
Specifically, for LNG carriers with diesel-electric propulsion is calculated as PME(i) 
=0.83 x MPPMotor/ ηi and for LNG carriers with steam turbine is calculated as 
PME(i) = 0.83 x MCR (steam turbine).

• PPTO(i): In the scenario that shaft generators are installed in a vessel, PPTO(i) is 75% 
of the rated electrical output power measured in (kW). If shaft generators are fitted 
to a steam turbine, 75% should be replaced by 83%. 
 
• PPT(i): In the scenario that shaft motors are installed in a ship, PPT(i) is 75% of the 
rated power consumption of each shaft motor measured in (kW), divides with the 
weighted average efficiency of the generators. If shaft motors are fitted to steam 
turbine 75% should be replaced by 83%.

and the necessary power for propulsion, machinery and accommodation. 
 
• CF: is the nominal factor for fuel conversion into C02 emissions and it is 
nondimensional. This factor can be determined both for the main engine and for the 
auxiliary engine fuel consumption. 
 
• SFC: is the certified-specific fuel consumption in (g/kWh) both for main and 
auxiliary engines of a vessel and can be obtained from the NOx technical file. For 
the ships which use engines with power output less than 130 kW, the SFC can be 
obtained by the ship manufacturer. Finally, for LNG-driven engines, the SFC is 
measured in (kJ/kWh) and must be converted to (g/kWh). 
 
• fw: is the weather factor which corresponds to a decrease in the vessel’s speed at 
specific sea conditions such as weight height, wave frequency and wind speed as 
determined in IMO (2012e). 
 
• feff(i): is the availability factor for innovative energy efficiency technology, as 
determined in IMO (2013 e).  
 
• fj: is the power correction factor, refer to IMO (2014 c). 
 
• fi: is the capacity correction factor, refer to IMO (2014 c).

2.3 Implementation phases of the required EEDI 
 
The required EEDI will is implemented in four different periods (phases).  Phase 0 was 
running from 2013 to 2014. Phase 1 was running from 2015 to 2019. Phase 2 is currently 
running, and it will last until 2024. Phase 3 will start from the year 2025 and onwards. 
Phase 0 (2013-2014) is taking account of the following new vessels. The vessels that their 
construction contract was established before Phase 0 and their delivery date is before 1 
January 2019. The ships that have a building contract before Phase 0 and their delivery 
date are on or after 1 July 2015 and before 1 January 2019. The vessels which are at a 
similar stage of construction on or after 1 July 2013 and before 1 July 2015 and the delivery 
date for them is before 1 January 2019. Finally, the ships which are at a similar stage of 
construction before 1 July 2013 and the delivery date for them in on or after 1 July 2015 
and before 1 January 2019 (Polakis, 2019). 
Phase 1 (2015-2019) is taking account of the following new vessels. The vessels that their 
construction contract was established before Phase 1 and their delivery date is before 1 
January 2024. The ships that have a building contract before Phase 1 and their delivery 
date are on or after 1 January 2019 and before 1 January 2024. The vessels which are at a 
similar stage of construction on or after 1 July 2015 and before 1 July 2020 and the delivery 
date for them is before 1 January 2024. Finally, the ships which are at a similar stage of 
construction before 1 July 2015 and the delivery date for them in on or after 1 January 2015 
and before 1 January 2024. 
Phase 2 (2020-2024) is taking account of the following new vessels. The vessels that their 
construction contract was established before Phase 2 and their delivery date is before 1 
January 2029. The ships that have a building contract before Phase 1 and their delivery 
date are on or after 1 January 2024 and before 1 January 2029. The vessels which are at a 
similar stage of construction on or after 1 July 2020 and before 1 July 2025 and the delivery 
date for them is before 1 January 2029. Finally, the ships which are at a similar stage of 
construction before 1 July 2020 and the delivery date for them in on or after 1 January 2024 
and before 1 January 2029 (Polakis, 2019). 
Phase 3 (2025+) is taking account of the following new ships. The new ships that have 
building contracts on or after 1 January 2025. The new ships that they are at a similar level 
of construction on or after 1 July 2025 and for the new vessels with a delivery date after 1 
January 2029.

2.4 EEDI Verification audits 
 
The verification of the EEDI is made by ship’s Flag Administration and recognized 
organizations (RO s) under the umbrella of IMO guidelines. EEDI survey and verification 
is conducted at two different stages the preliminary stage and the final stage. 
2.4.1 Preliminary stage 
 
At the preliminary stage, the verification is conducted during the initial design of the vessel 
and at the pre-construction period. At those stages, an application for an initial survey and 
an EEDI Technical File, which contains all the necessary data, should be submitted to an 
authorized verifier by the shipbuilder or the shipowner (IMO, 2014b). The EEDI Technical 
File should at least to include the following information: 
 
• Deadweight (DWT) or gross tonnage (GT in the case of passenger or Ro-Ro 
passenger ships. 
• The maximum continuous rating (MCR) of the main and auxiliary engines. 
• Design speed in knots. 
• The specific fuel consumption of the main and auxiliary engine. 
• Power curves in kW/knot. 
• Ship type. 
• A detailed description of the energy-saving equipment of a ship. 
• Value of the attained EEDI. 
• Values of the attained EEDI weather.

2.4.2 Final Verification of the EEDI 
 
For the final stage of the EEDI verification, a sea trial should be conducted in the presence 
of the authorized verifier. After the sea trial, the verifier will verify all the relevant 
parameters such as the power curve and the reference speed. This is a mandatory process 
before the final verification of the ship (Polakis, 2019). 
2.5 Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) 
 
The Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) is an operational method that sets 
out a cost-effective process for increasing a ship's energy efficiency. The SEEMP also 
offers a method for shipping companies to track ship and fleet output over time, using as a 
control device, for example, the Energy Efficiency Operational Indicator (EEOI). The 
EEOI helps operators to evaluate a ship's fuel efficiency in service and to determine the 
impact of any operational improvements, such as better travel preparation or more frequent 
propeller cleaning, or the implementation of technological measures such as waste heat 
recovery systems or a new rotor (IMO, 2012b).

Chapter 3- Overview of the latest IMO submissions 
 
An IMO online informal digital meeting was held in the first days of July 2020 as a 
replacement to the formal 7th Intersessional Working Group on GHG, which was 
scheduled for the March 2020 and postponed due to Covid-19 pandemic. The main topic 
of this meeting was the latest short-term measures submissions for the improvement of the 
operational energy efficiency of existing ships. The most prevalent proposals are presented 
in this chapter. 
3.1 Goal-based efficiency improvement measure for existing ships 
(EEXI) submitted by Japan-Greece-Norway 
 
The co-sponsors are proposing a goal-based energy efficiency measure for the utilization 
of the Energy Efficiency of the Existing Ships Index (EEXI). It is a goal-based approach 
because giving plenty of choices to shipowners and ship operators to choose from in order 
to achieve the energy efficiency improvements in existing ships. The base of this proposal 
is the Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) for new ships, which is the only mandatory 
approach for the reduction of GHG, that IMO has adopted so far. An analytical description 
of this proposal is represented in Chapter 5 of this study. 
 
3.2 Proposal for a mandatory operational goal-based short-term 
measure submitted by Denmark-France-Germany 
 
These countries are proposing a mandatory operational goal-based measure, where each 
vessel is given a specific carbon intensity reduction goal, and where shipping companies 
and crews are free to choose the methods to achieve it. The potential means to achieve this 
goal would be speed optimization, better network and routing design or hull cleaning. This 
proposal has been detailed analyzed in Chapter 4 of this master thesis project.

3.3 Mandatory energy efficiency indexing for existing ships 
submitted by the Central European Shipbuilders Association 
(CESA) 
 
A combinational technical and operation proposal developed by CESA. In the technical 
side, CESA propose the research and the impact assessment of new synthetic fuel, 
nanocoatings for the reduction of friction, the potential application of hydrogen or 
ammonia as fuel and carbon capture and storage technologies. On the operational side, are 
placed in favour of the power limitation of the ships. They are strongly encouraging the 
option that there should be a clear distinction among the technical and operational 
compliance options. They concluded that there should be a significant increase in the 
energy efficiency of both newbuild and existing ships and a comprehensive lifecycle 
assessment of alternative fuels should be conducted in the future.

Chapter 4- Proposal for a mandatory goal-based short-term measure 
submitted by Denmark, France and Germany 
 
For the 7th Intersessional Working Group on the reduction of Greenhouse Gases (a meeting 
that was postponed due to Covid-19), Denmark, Germany and France suggest a mandatory 
short-term "goal-based" operational initiative where each ship is allocated an individual 
carbon intensity reduction target, and where shipowners and crews are free to select the 
objectives to achieve it. This approach does not necessarily imply only operational means 
such as speed or trim optimization, travel routing, ship washing, and many more to be 
considered, but also technological measures such as power constraint, energy optimization, 
usage of alternative fuels, and so on. Besides, the compulsory operational goal-based 
strategy promotes creativity through the implementation of current and future technical and 
digital technologies, as well as improved integration of processes, both of which have a 
high potential for reducing emissions. Finally, this measure is based both on trust between 
the shipowners, operators and crew and based on control of the flag states and port 
authorities (ISW-GHG 7/2/9). 
4.1 The Concept of the proposed measure 
 
The definition of a compulsory short-term operational objective-based measure consists of 
many elements. The main idea behind the proposed measures is that every vessel must 
apply an annual required carbon intensity reduction factor Xr. 2008 will be the base year 
reference point for each ship, based on a Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) reference line, 
measured based on EEDI reference lines for different types of ships, adding various 
correction factors. Furthermore, it is suggested that the SEEMP of different kind of ships 
should include the annual required carbon intensity reduction factor (Xr) among others 
because the International Energy Efficiency Certificate (IEEC) is already issued based on 
the ship’s energy efficiency management plan. Moreover, these Countries are proposing 
also regular review and analysis of the measure based on various scientific data, to examine 
the need for any improvements to the measure. Finally, the compulsory short-term 
operational target-based measure will cover all ships of 400 gross tonnages and above 
(ISW-GHG 7/2/9).

4.5 Implementation and Enforcement of the proposed measure 
 
The implementation and enforcement scheme that co-sponsors are investigating, is based 
on different assumptions, as it can be seen on the next page. 
 
 
==PAGE:33==
 
Page | 33 
 
Assumption 1: A short-term goal-based measure aims to implement an average fleet-wide 
CI reduction of at least 40 per cent compared to a 2008 baseline, but also to lay the 
groundwork for the transition to alternative low- and zero-carbon fuels required to achieve 
the 2050 targets (ISW-GHG 7/2/2). 
Assumption 2: The rule will apply to any ship over and above 400 gross tonnages, except 
fixed and floating drilling rigs and other platforms. This conclusion is considered because 
the Initial IMO Strategy does not exempt any ship size from its GHG reduction targets. 
Assumption 3: Previous expenditure of capital in ships technological performance is 
meaningless unless the result is an actual decrease in fuel consumption and consequent 
CO2 emission reductions. This means that real reductions in ships ' fuel consumption, both 
new and current, reflect the efficacy of the capital expenditure and the degree to which it 
should be credited within a target-based measure 
Assumption 4: This measure should be implemented through legislation to Chapter 4 of 
MARPOL Annex VI. 
Assumption 5: The company's approach to the management of its ship's energy efficiency 
should be recorded in the SEEMP Section. 
Assumption 6: The implementation and enforcement scheme should build on the 
proposals included in MEPC 74/7/4. 
 
Moreover, regarding the build of the implementation and enforcement model Denmark 
posted the following elements: 
• The model will help the enhancement of strategies in technological, operational, and 
organizational terms. This will increase the degree to which businesses would take 
steps to ensure that their ships meet a mandatory CI goal (ISW-GHG 7/2/2). 
• The model will reward the companies, which had made efforts to reduce the Carbon 
Intensity of their vessels. 
• The model will encourage effective implementation internationally and a level playing 
field. 
• The model should encourage a culture of continuous energy efficiency improvement 
and should preclude or discourage the use of limited compliance strategies. 
• The model will tackle the challenge of charterers to control over the Carbon Intensity 
of a ship through a voyage. 
• The model will pursue consistency with potential initiatives to promote the adoption of 
low- and zero-carbon fuels or alternative frameworks for compliance.

4.6 Description of the proposed implementation and enforcement 
model 
 
The proposed model would introduce by amending MARPOL Annex VI and adding a new 
regulation 22B on the reduction of carbon intensity and related non-obligatory 
requirements for businesses and administrations. Moreover, this model would be consistent 
with any formulation of the CI reduction objective and do not rely on the selection of a 
single CII, noting that consideration of CIIs and proxies for transportation work in different 
fields remains subject to scrutiny. Additionally, periodic verification audits were carried 
out to ensure compliance with required CI reduction targets and would recompense 
additional investment in energy conservation and renewable fuels by allowing over-
performing ships to deliver excess output. Furthermore, the model will join an interface 
with alternate enforcement mechanisms if IMO will consent to the use of these 
mechanisms. Nonetheless, this is not discussed in depth in this study as alternate 
enforcement strategies, like offsetting, are not discussed compatible with the Initial 
Strategy's purpose. Finally, the model will guarantee compliance with the IEEC. The IEEC 
will cease to be valid under particular circumstances, including but not limited to a failure 
to achieve mandatory cumulative CI reductions during the IEEC validity period (ISW-
GHG 7/2/2). 
 
4.7 Annual Verification Audits 
 
An annual verification audit will be performed by the Administration or a properly 
approved organization on the first and second anniversaries of the initial verification. This 
proposal investigates three different compliance scenarios based on the level of how much 
of the carbon intensity reduction has been achieved. Specifically, the first case scenario 
base on the assumption that a ship has achieved carbon intensity reduction. In that case, if 
the annual CI reduction achieved is greater than or equal to the lower CI reduction limit 
needed for the year, the International Energy Efficiency Certificate (IEEC) will be 
endorsed. Moreover, where the achieved annual CI reduction is greater than the required 
annual carbon intensity reduction, the IEEC should be endorsed and annexed with a 
description of the achieved excess carbon intensity reduction. It can be moved into the 
following year and used to reduce the ship's required annual CI reduction (ISW-GHG 
7/2/2). 
The second scenario based on the assumption that the ship has not achieved the required 
annual carbon intensity reduction. In this case, the international energy efficiency 
certificate would be approved but this is a non-compliance with the provisions of 
==PAGE:35==
 
Page | 35 
 
Regulation 22B of Annex VI to the ship energy efficiency management plan. As a result, 
the audit administration will require actions to be taken from the ship operators, to avoid a 
reoccurrence. More precisely, the shipping company or the charterer is obligated to submit 
a CI action plan to boost the ship's energy performance to prevent reoccurrence in the 
following year and to produce an additional CI reduction before the renewal verification 
audit to mitigate excess emissions. The Administration will commit to the proposal within 
1 month of the verification audit.

Chapter 5- Concrete proposals to improve the operational energy 
efficiency of existing ships (EEXI), submitted by Japan and Norway 
 
In the 6th Intersessional Working Group on the reduction of Greenhouse Gases, Japan and 
Norway suggested a concrete goal-based measure which utilizes the Energy Efficiency of 
Existing Ship Index (EEXI). The principal idea behind this measure is the Energy 
Efficiency Design Index (EEDI), with the difference that this proposal includes the existing 
ships. This measure is goal-based, that means the shipowners and crews are free to select 
the objectives to achieve it, it has a big level of fairness and has been designed to be 
practicable, Implementable and verifiable (ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
5.1 Goal-based approach 
As mentioned above it is a goal-based measure, that means that the shipowner or the ship 
operator has many broad alternatives to achieve energy efficiency improvements in the 
existing ships. Moreover, it gives a lot of flexibility depending on the particular 
circumstances of each ship individually as long as such options are verifiable and 
controllable. On the other hand, if the measure was limited to a specific option, for 
example, speed reduction, it could obstruct technological progress and bring more 
uncertainty about the degree of GHG emissions reduction. Specifically, in the scenario of 
the mandatory speed limit for all ships, technological advanced and energy-efficient ships 
should slow down to the same level as inefficient ships. As a result, this measure would 
treat unfair energy-efficient vessels.  The figure below illustrates the contrast between 
mandatory speed reduction versus alternative options (ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
 
Figure 8: SPEED LIMITS VERSUS GOAL-BASED MEASURE (SOURCE: ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
There are many different options for a shipowner to comply with the measure. Some of 
them are to choose the shaft/engine power to an optimum level, to install energy-saving 
==PAGE:41==
 
Page | 41 
 
equipment aboard or to switch to alternative fuels such as natural gas or hydrogen. The 
figure below summarizes the overall concept of this policy and the alternative options that 
a shipowner has.

5.2 Fair treatment 
 
This measure is designed as a regulatory framework under MARPOL Annex VI, which is 
robustly and internationally applied under the principle of non-discrimination with no more 
preferential treatment irrespective of the flags. Furthermore, in introducing new measures 
to existing ships, specific consideration should be given to the efforts already made by each 
ship to ensure equity among the ships. Some current vessels may be designed for better 
environmental performance, whereas others may not. Such a difference between existing 
ships should be quantified and expressed in the regulation. To achieve the highest level of 
fairness among the ships this measure captures quantitatively and objectively such 
"efforts." Moreover, it introduces the same target of energy efficiency for all ships under 
each category (ship type and size). The figure below represents graphically the application 
of the same target for each ship type (ISW-GHG 6/2/3).

5.3 Practical, implementable and verifiable 
 
There are many different factors which affect the operational energy efficiency of a vessel. 
Some of them are technical other are business-related and others are external and 
unpredicted factors. Even though ships with the same design were operated on the same 
pathway, schedule and cargo, due to other external forces such as weather and sea 
conditions, their operational efficiencies will differ. The figure below shows these different 
factors which affect the operational efficiency (ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
 
Figure 11:FACTORS AFFECTING OPERATIONAL EFFICIENCY OF SHIPS (SOURCE: ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
==PAGE:43==
 
Page | 43 
 
EEXI assesses and monitors technological factors such as engine strength, equipment and 
fuels. Such factors have a major effect on the operating performance of the ship and can be 
recorded before the ship begins its operations. Since EEXI does not govern business 
operation or external forces such as weather and sea conditions on a prescriptive basis, 
ships will not be fined for factors outside their influence. Finally, unless the regulation is 
enforceable, effective compliance would not be assured, thus significantly distorting the 
level playing field of the international shipping industry. 
 
5.4 Structure of EEXI 
 
The structure of the EEXI has been developed under common principals of the EEDI (look 
Chapter 2 for more details) and consists of three policy pillars. The first step is the 
calculation of the existing energy efficiency of a ship (attained EEDI/EEXI). In case that 
the ship does not meet the required EEXI, then the ship should improve its energy 
efficiency. The final step of the policy is the verification and certification for each ship. 
The figure below represents the proposed structure of the EEXI (ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
 
Figure 12: PROPOSED STRUCTURE OF THE EEXI (SOURCE: ISW-GHG 6/2/3). 
The EEXI mathematical equation follows the EEDI formula's basic framework (see 
Chapter 2 for more details) and integrates the same correction factors. Consequently, the 
EEXI measurement is consistent with that of EEDI and thus ships subject to the 
requirements of the EEDI criteria may use their attained EEDI as an alternative to the 
attained EEXI without re-calculation or re-certification if the attained EEDI matches the 
required EEXI.

The improved energy efficiency of an existing ship should be verified by an authorized 
organization to clarify if the ship meets the required EEXI. As with EEDI on new vessels,

certification scheme. Specifically, the verifier has to request the technical file of the

(ISW-GHG 7/27). 
• The Deadweight (DWT) or the gross tonnage (GT) for passenger carriers without

5.7 Mechanism to achieve the nominal reduction 
 
The co-sponsors proposed two technical alternatives for the shaft/engine power reduction. 
The first alternative is the Shaft Power Limitation system (SHaPoLi) and the second one 
is the Engine Power Limitation system (EPL). The SHaPoLi system is tested and accepted 
with technical means for limiting the maximum shaft capacity. The EPL system is checked 
and approved program for technical means for limiting the maximum engine capacity. 
Moreover, the SHaPoLi system should include multiple sensors for measuring the 
rotational speed which is delivered to the propellers of the vessel, a data recording and 
processing instrument for data analysis and a computer unit for calculating and limiting the 
power that the shaft transmits to the propeller or propellers. For a mechanically controlled 
engine, a sealing device capable of physically locking the fuel index using a wire sealed 
mechanical stop screw, so that the crew cannot discharge the EPL without approval. 
Finally, For the electronically operated engine, a password-locked fuel index limiter that 
can lock the fuel index electronically so that the crew cannot remove the EPL without 
approval (ISW-GHG 7/2/7).

5.8 Impact assessment of the proposed measure conducted by the 
co-sponsors 
 
An initial impact assessment was conducted by the co-sponsors of the proposed measure 
taking into consideration eight different shipping routes. Based on the findings of this 
assessment the co-sponsors came to the following conclusions. The first finding is that the 
ships which will implement the Engine Power Limitation system (EPL), will reduce the 
shipping costs because the ships will use a smaller quantity of fuels. Moreover, they 
concluded that even with higher fuel prices and lower chartering cost the shipowners will 
benefit from the implementation of this measure. Another conclusion of this impact 
assessment study is that the insurance costs will increase because the journey from one port 
to another will increase too. Additionally, they believe that the improvements in the energy 
efficiency of a ship are strictly connected with the ship type, the shipping route and the 
specific type of cargo which is transported. All in all, the co-sponsors consider that 
compliance with the EEXI will not bring negative impacts on the current trading costs 
(ISW-GHG 7/2/8).

6.1 Tanker ships 
 
A tanker ship is designed to transport or store liquid or gasses in bulk. The three major 
types of tanker ships are oils tankers, chemical tankers and gas carriers (Muller, Gerhardt 
1998). Tanker ships can also transport vegetable oils, molasses and alcoholic spirits. 
Tankers are a relatively new concept which dates to the later 19th century. Before that, 
technology had failed to embrace the concept of transporting bulk liquids. The market was 
also not designed to carry or sell bulk goods, so most ships held a wide variety of different 
items in various holdings and traded beyond fixed routes. The oil industry initially used 
tankers to ship refined fuel from refineries to customers in bulk. It would then be collected 
offshore in large tanks and subdivided for distribution to specific sites. Tanker use caught 
on as other liquids were often cheaper to carry in bulk, store in dedicated facilities, then 
subdivide (Edmons, John, 2017). 
Small-coastal tankers: The capacity of those ships is less than 50.000 DWT, mainly used 
for transportation of refined products like gasoline. Typical dimensions of those ships are 
205m length, 29m beam and 16m draft.

6.2 Bulk carrier ships 
 
Bulk carrier ship is a cargo ship that is designed to transport bulk cargo like iron ore, coal, 
grains, steel coils, minerals and cement in its special storage barns. Today's bulk carriers 
are designed to carry very large amounts of cargo while maintaining all the necessary safety 
precautions. Moreover, bulk carriers accounted for the 21% of the world's merchant fleets 
and range in size from coastal carriers to Capesize ore ships able to carry 400.000 metric 
tons of deadweight (DWT). There are many different functional designs for this type of 
ships. Some of them can unload their cargo and some others depend on port facilities for 
loading and unloading. Finally, more than half of the world’s fleet bulk carrier ships have 
Greek, Japanese or Chinese shipowners and more than a quarter are registered in Panama. 
Small/Coastal Bulk Carriers: For this particular type of vessel, their length varies

6.3 Containerships 
 
Containerships are cargo ships, which carry their cargo using specially designed containers. 
Container ship capacity is measured in twenty-foot equivalent units (TEU). Typical loads 
are a mix of 20-foot and 40-foot (2-TEU) ISO-standard containers, with the latter 
predominant. Nowadays, about 90% of non-bulk cargo worldwide is transported by 
container ships, and the largest modern container ships can carry over 23,000 TEU. One of 
the biggest advantages of containerization is that the shipping expenses and time have been 
lowered and that, in effect, has enabled international trade to expand. 
Feeder Containership: These are medium-sized ships (up to 3000 TEU) bearing an 
average of 300 to 1000 containers and have a weight carrying capacity of around 10,000 
DWT. Feeder vessels collect shipping containers from different ports and transport them 
to central container terminals or transhipment hubs where they are filled for further 
transport to larger vessels. Over the years, organizations carrying containers on a pre-
defined route have formed feeder lines regularly. Feeder ships are mostly run by companies 
specialized in short sea shipping as well.

Chapter 7- Conclusions and limitations 
 
There is a big discussion among the members of the IMO regarding the adoption of the 
most feasible greenhouse gases reduction strategy. The biggest number of merchant ships 
will be affected by this strategy. Under an uncertain and volatile market, the shipowners 
and the ship operators will have to comply with the potential measures. It seems that the 
most two predominant measures for adoption are the Carbon Intensity Indicator (C.I.I) 
which has been submitted as a concrete proposal by Denmark-France and Germany and 
the Energy Efficiency of Existing Ships Index (EEXI) which has been submitted by Japan-
Greece and Norway. Those two proposals are summarized in this chapter. 
 
7.1 Carbon Intensity Indicator (C.I.I) 
 
The success of this measure is mainly based on the reduction of the operational speed of 
the ships. This reduction would also contribute to the significant reduction of GHG as it 
can be seen from the developed scenarios and the data analysis in Chapter 6. On the other 
hand, the speed reduction would have negative impacts on the global supply chains. 
Specifically, the small ships like small tankers and coastal bulk carriers are forced to 
operate with speeds that are very low and are dangerous in most of the cases, to meet the 
reduction limits. All ships have minimum permissible speeds, below which they cannot 
operate. These minimum speeds are functions of engine technology, older engines having 
higher minimum speeds than newer ones. So some of the very low speeds (esp. below 10 
knots) that are the results of this project calculations may be infeasible, meaning that some 
of the C.I.I reduction targets may not be reachable. A potential solution to this problem 
could be the replacement of smaller ships in terms of capacity with bigger ships but this 
solution could interrupt the whole supply chain. Specifically, increasing ship size, do not 
reject this as an alternative, particularly since average ship size has increased over the years. 
Problems may be in smaller ports that may not be able to accommodate larger ships and 
for ship owners being able to raise the capital to replace their smaller vessels.  Also, it 
would not be economically feasible for a shipping company to operate a ship half loaded. 
It would be beneficial for the co-sponsors to reconsider the reduction targets for the 
different ship sizes because it is obvious that bigger ships have a lower carbon intensity per 
transport work compare it with smaller size ships. It is recommended also the co-sponsors 
to research the impact of alternative fuels such as hydrogen or ammonia and then to 
reconsider the reduction factors for each ship type and size. 
According to the developed model, it can be seen that containerships are emitting the 
biggest quantities of carbon dioxide. This is based on the fact that ships sail at the highest 
speed compared to the other two categories. So the policymakers should focus more on this 
==PAGE:82==
 
Page | 82 
 
particular category in order to develop further their proposal taking into consideration also 
the actual CO2 emissions and not only the carbon intensity per transport work of them. 
Moreover, the bulk carrier ships tend to emit lower quantities of CO2 than tanker ships but 
according to the co-sponsors, the complying limits are more strict for them. This results in 
that coastal bulk carriers are forced to operate with very low and in many cases 
inappropriate speeds. This is clearly in the scenario of a coastal bulk carrier which is 
obligated to sails with a speed of 1knot in 2030 versus a feeder containership which has to 
adopt an operation speed of 10,6 Nm in 2030 to achieve the nominal reduction limits. 
Moreover, this measure does not take into account unexpected events like bad weather 
conditions, geopolitical incidents, unpredictable fuel prices, general fluctuation in the 
demand for goods and unstable diplomatic and trade relations between countries. All these 
factors could potentially disturb the regular operations of a ship meaning that this measure 
has been developed under ideal conditions. A real-life example is the trading ‘’war’’ 
between U.S.A and China, where the first country does not let many Chinese companies 
use it’s sea routes and ports. 
7.2 Energy Efficiency of Existing Ships Index (EEXI) 
 
Energy Efficiency of Existing Ships Index (EEXI) based on this study, seems to be a more 
concrete proposal compared to the Carbon Intensity Indicator. Firstly, it gives many 
different choices to the shipowners of how to achieve the energy efficiency of the existing 
ships. This proposal also includes besides the controllable factors that affect the ship’s 
efficiency such as design and navigation factors, the noncontrollable factors such as 
weather, sea current and economical changes. This gives more flexibility to the shipping 
companies to comply with the limits of the measure. Furthermore, the policymakers of this 
proposal suggest also their technical solution. This technical solution is the Engine Power 
Limit system (EPL) which is an affordable and easy to use mechanism. Finally, EEXI 
measure is closely related to EEDI so it would be easier the collection of the related 
technical data of every ship. Moreover, EEXI eliminates non-compliant ships in advance, 
ensures minimum standards for the entire fleet robustly and is trying to eliminate the 
influence of the external and uncontrollable factors. 
In my opinion, the Energy Efficiency of Existing Ships Index (EEDI) looks to be more 
concrete at the moment compared to the Carbon Intensity Indicator (C.I.I) proposal. The 
reason is that is easier and probably more financially feasible for a shipping company to 
make all the required actions to enhance the energy efficiency of an existing ship and there 
are a lot of alternatives to achieve it. Moreover, this proposal includes also the potential 
mechanism (EPL), so the shipowners can save some money from the research and 
development of new environmental-friendly technologies. Finally, there is not a final 
formal decision from the members of the IMO of which measure will be adopted, but its 
widely expected to adopt the EEXI. There is also overall agreement that EEXI needs to be 
coupled with the adoption of at least one of the other goal-based proposals. So it would be 
==PAGE:83==
 
Page | 83 
 
very helpful for the policymakers to conduct research and an impact assessment for the 
case that C.I.I will be an auxiliary measure in the EEXI framework.
