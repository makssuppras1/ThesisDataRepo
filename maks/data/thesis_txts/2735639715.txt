Abstract
This study conducts an in-depth exploration of polyethylene biodegradation, focus-
ing on the function of the katG enzyme and Ralstonia pickettii, motivated by a patent
(US20230167469A1) published by Mellizyme Biotechnology Ltd. The study commenced with
a focus on enzymatic degradation mediated by the wild-type katG, utilizing fluorescent and
non-fluorescent polyethylene microbeads to evaluate the degradation rates and effectiveness
of the katG enzyme. Despite challenges such as the hydrophobic nature of the microbeads
limiting enzyme interaction, significant insights were gained into the enzyme’s potential for
polyethylene degradation. Subsequently, the study progressed to evaluating the biodegrada-
tion potential of Ralstonia pickettii, indicating its possible role in polyethylene degradation.
This phase also acknowledged the distinct analytical complexities arising from the interplay
between microbial processes and the structural integrity of the polyethylene microbeads.
Furthermore, the investigation delved into the Galleria mellonella larvae’s gut microbiome,
identifying it as a promising source for isolating plastic-degrading microorganisms. Through
meticulous 16S rRNA sequencing and BLAST analysis, Ralstonia pickettii was confirmed
within the gut microbiome, emphasizing the necessity for refined detection methodologies
and tailored protocols for microbial isolation from the gut microbiome of the larvae. The
study encountered constraints related to the extended incubation periods typically required
for observing significant plastic degradation and proposed the utilization of microbeads to
potentially expedite this process while acknowledging the need for uniformity in microbead
size and the careful documentation of fluorophore usage. In conclusion, this research study
contributes valuable insights into the enzymatic and microbial pathways for polyethylene
degradation, highlighting the synergistic potential of the katG enzyme, Ralstonia pickettii,
and the Galleria mellonella gut microbiome. It underscores the complexities of biodegra-
dation studies and calls for further research to optimize degradation conditions, improve
microbial and enzymatic interaction with polyethylene, and develop effective biodegradation
strategies to mitigate plastic pollution.

1.1
Plastics: A Potent Environmental Pollutant
Plastic, a versatile polymer renowned for its durability and cost-effectiveness, has rev-
olutionized modern life [1] [2].
However, the exponential rise in plastic production and
inadequate waste management have led to profound environmental repercussions [3]. This
pervasive issue encompasses pollution across terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems, jeopardiz-
ing biodiversity and posing potential risks to human health [4]. The magnitude of plastic
pollution is staggering, with an estimated 11 million metric tons of plastic waste pouring
into the world’s oceans annually, resulting in an alarming accumulation of 86 million metric
tons in marine environments [3].
Figure 1: Trends in Global Plastic Production from 1950 to 2022. The bar graph
showcases the surge in worldwide plastic production, illustrating a steep increase from 1.5
million metric tons to 400.3 million metric tons. The most substantial rise occurs post-
2000, highlighting a significant increase in plastic output with potential environmental con-
sequences.
Microplastics, particles smaller than 5 millimeters, exacerbate the crisis, pervading ma-
rine habitats with concentrations reaching up to 1.9 million pieces per square meter [5].
This ecological catastrophe extends beyond marine ecosystems, infiltrating terrestrial land-
scapes and posing profound threats to biodiversity and human health [4]. Marine life bears
the brunt of plastic pollution, with over 800 species affected by ingestion, entanglement, or
habitat degradation, including numerous endangered species [2] [6]. On land, plastic waste
contaminates soil, water sources, and food chains, raising alarming concerns about the po-
tential health risks posed by exposure to plastic-derived chemicals [1] [7]. As plastics degrade
over time, they release a cocktail of hazardous substances, including endocrine-disrupting
compounds, further exacerbating environmental and public health hazards [1].

Polyalkene synthetic polymers have emerged as indispensable components in various
aspects of daily living, finding widespread application across the packaging, dental, and
apparel industries, thereby illustrating their multifaceted utility in everyday scenarios. The
advantages of utilizing these polymers over conventional materials are attributed to their low
density, water-resistant properties, thermal insulation capabilities, and prolonged resistance
to corrosion [8].

Each polyalkene polymer is distinguished by its distinct chemical structure 2, synthe-
sized via the polymerization of alkene monomer units (-CH2CH2-). This process gives rise
to a diverse array of polyalkene polymers, each engineered to exhibit specific mechanical
and chemical characteristics. The susceptibility of polymers to degradation is influenced by
a variety of factors, notably their molecular structure and the incorporation of functional
groups. To comprehend this aspect, it is essential to explore the mechanism of synthesis of
Polyalkene polymers. These polymers are predominantly synthesized via two principal mech-
anisms: condensation polymerization and addition polymerization. Polymers like polyamides
and polyesters are formed by condensation polymerization and are formed more slowly often
requiring heat and containing various functional groups (like esters and amides) as part of
the polymer backbone [9]. The inclusion of these functional groups renders the polymers
more prone to degradation in the presence of certain microorganisms and hydrolytic enzymes
[10]. Polymers like polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), polystyrene (PS), and polyvinyl
chloride (PVC) are usually made up of long chains of carbon atoms with few or no functional
groups. The absence of hydrolyzable functional groups makes them quite resistant to degra-
dation. Among these additional polymers, polyethylene (which is abundantly produced) and

1.3
Strategies and Challenges in Polyethylene Waste Management:
Navigating Towards Sustainable Solutions for Biodegradation
and Recycling
Following the exploration of polyalkene polymers and their ubiquitous applications, the
urgent discourse shifted toward plastic waste management. This section not only outlines
strategies to mitigate environmental plastic pollution, including innovative recycling and
biodegradation research but also addresses the critical barriers to effective waste manage-
ment. Key obstacles include the lack of technical expertise in handling hazardous waste,
underdeveloped recycling infrastructure, and a general unawareness of pertinent regulations,
all of which contribute significantly to the escalating plastic waste crisis [11]. Various ap-
proaches for managing plastic waste from households, industries, and municipalities encom-
pass landfilling, incineration, and mechanical, chemical, and biological recycling [11][12].
Initially, landfilling was a common practice for disposing of plastic waste, often without
any pre-treatment, leading to significant environmental challenges. This method resulted in
greenhouse gas emissions and the persistent nature of plastics in the environment, posing
substantial risks of land and air pollution due to their poor degradability[13]. Subsequently,
the situation was addressed by implementing landfill beds and conducting consistent ground-
water monitoring to detect any potential pollution. [11].
Since the early 2000s, incineration has become a prevalent method for processing plastic
waste, particularly from polyethylene, propylene, and polystyrene. This approach not only
facilitates the disposal of plastics but also allows for the extraction of significant energy.
Given the high calorific value of these materials, the energy derived from incinerating them
is comparable to that of conventional fuels such as gasoline, petroleum, and kerosene [11]
[14]. By 2050, it is projected that adopting certain measures could lead to the elimination
of up to 50% of the world’s plastic waste, suggesting a significant stride towards address-
ing this environmental challenge [15]. The combustion of these addition polymers (PE, PP,
and PVC) typically generates water and carbon dioxide. However, this process also emits
harmful substances, including toxic gases, greenhouse gases, and polycyclic aromatic hydro-
carbons, posing significant health risks to humans [11] [16]. To counteract the adverse effects
of burning plastics, a method was developed where the use of alumina significantly reduced
the release of harmful emissions such as carbon monoxide (CO) and polycyclic aromatic
hydrocarbons (PAHs) during plastic combustion [17].
The escalating crisis of plastic waste necessitates multiple recycling approaches to miti-
gate the environmental impact of plastic waste on the environment. There are three primary
recycling methods: mechanical, chemical, and biological. Each method offers a unique way
of converting plastic waste into valuable resources for use. Mechanical recycling transforms
used thermoplastics into new or similar products, involving sorting, processing, and remold-
ing steps. It’s a primary and secondary process that begins with collecting and sorting waste
plastics, which are then recycled into products nearly identical to the original. Despite be-
ing considered a ’green’ operation, mechanical recycling faces challenges such as high energy

1.4
Biodegradation of Polyethylene (PE)
This overview examines the biodegradation of polyethylene (PE) through three lenses:
the role of waxworms and their gut microbiome, microbial activity’s impact, and microbial
enzymes’ effects.
Studies highlight Galleria mellonella and Plodia interpunctella larvae’s
capacity to degrade PE, with specific bacteria like Enterobacter sp. from G. mellonella guts
showing promising results.
1.4.1
Role of Waxworms and Their Gut Microbiota in Biodegradation of Polyethy-
lene
Studies have demonstrated that larvae from Galleria mellonella and Plodia interpunctella
can break down low-density polyethylene (LDPE) without prior treatment [24, 25, 26]. These
larvae are capable of softening and metabolizing thin plastic bags, converting them into
polyethylene glycol, showcasing a natural process for degrading plastic materials. [27]. The
process through which larvae contribute to the biodegradation of polyethylene (PE) remains
underexplored, with a scarcity of concrete scientific evidence to validate existing theories
regarding their degradative mechanisms [28]. Recent research has investigated the biodegra-
dation of polyethylene (PE) by Enterobacter sp. D1, isolated from wax moth G. mellonella
guts [25]. The study utilized AFM and SEM analyses to demonstrate that strain D1 could
colonize and alter the PE film surface after 14 days of incubation, as evidenced by the
formation of carbonyl and ether groups identified in FTIR spectra and oxidative cleavage
products detected via high-performance liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry [25]. This
indicates strain D1’s role in PE degradation, though the exact mechanism, whether direct
larval activity or gut flora, remains uncertain. Another study involves the identification of

1.4.2
Microbial Degradation of Polyethylene
The investigation into microbial effects on polyethylene (PE) involved assessing seven
parameters related to biodegradation: surface functional groups, hydrophobicity/hydrophilic-
ity balance, crystallinity, molecular weight distribution, surface morphology, mechanical
characteristics, and mass balance[29, 30, 31]. These criteria were essential for understanding
the extent of microbial influence on the polymer’s degradation process. Studies have shown
that the biodegradation of polyethylene (PE) correlates with an increase in specific func-
tional groups, notably carbonyl, keto carbonyl, and vinyl bonds, as identified by FT-IR spec-
troscopy. The presence of these oxidized groups enhances PE’s hydrophilicity, facilitating mi-
crobial adhesion to the polymer’s surface, thereby augmenting its biodegradation [32, 33, 34].
Hydrophilicity is typically assessed by measuring a surface’s contact angle with water. A
lower contact angle signifies greater hydrophilicity of the oxidized PE surface, indicating
its affinity for water [35] [36]. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Atomic Force Mi-
croscopy (AFM) are pivotal for examining the biodegradation-induced alterations in PE film
surfaces [29]. Observations frequently include biofilm formation [37, 38, 39, 40, 41, 42, 43, 44]
and the intrusion of fungal hyphae [45, 46, 47, 48], leading to noticeable surface modifications
such as pits, holes, and erosions, indicative of the material’s degradation [29].
1.4.3
Role of Microbial Enzymes on Polyethylene Biodegradation
Plastic biodegradation is an intricate process that involves both abiotic factors and mi-
croorganisms working together. This collaboration leads to the breakdown of the polymer
into smaller fragments, enhancing the polymer surface’s accessibility. Such increased acces-
sibility allows extracellular enzymes to further fragment the polyethylene, intensifying the
degradation process [49, 50, 51]. The biodegradation of polyethylene is highly restricted due
to the lack of any hydrolyzable functional groups in its backbone structure [51] [52]. Re-
search has explored the potential of microbial enzymes, such as manganese peroxidase (MnP,
EC 1.11.1.13), lignin peroxidases (LiP, EC 1.11.1.14), and laccases (EC 1.10.3.2), known for
their lignin-degrading capabilities, to also degrade polyethylene [52] [53]. A laccase enzyme,
dependent on copper and sourced from the R. ruber strain C208, has been documented to
break down UV-pretreated PE films. The enzyme’s action on the amorphous regions of
PE films leads to the creation of carbonyl groups that are more accessible, alongside a no-
table reduction in the film’s weight, facilitating further degradation steps [54]. A significant
reduction (about 70%) was observed in a pre-oxidized high molecular weight polyethylene
sample, achieving up to 70% degradation within 15 days, which had been treated with the

1.5
Analytical Methodologies for Analyzing Polyethylene Biodegra-
dation
Analytical methodologies for analyzing polyethylene biodegradation encompass a range
of techniques designed to assess the structural and compositional changes that occur as
polyethylene is broken down. It examined changes in morphology and mechanical properties,
monitored weight loss and alterations in molar mass, measured the evolution of CO2 gravi-
metrically to gauge biodegradation, and utilized Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
for identifying shifts in functional groups and chemical structures.
1.5.1
Changes in Morphology and Mechanical Properties Concerning Biodegra-
dation of Polyethylene
The environmental degradation of polyethylene (PE), one of the most prevalent plastics,
presents a significant ecological challenge due to its durable nature. The process of biodegra-
dation addresses this challenge by leveraging microbial activity to break down PE into en-
vironmentally friendly compounds. During this process, microbial enzymes and microbes
can actively etch the surface of the polyethylene, a phenomenon that can be meticulously
observed using scanning electron microscopy (SEM), providing clear evidence of microbial
interactions with the polymer [58]. Furthermore, the formation of biofilms by microorgan-
isms on the PE surface, also discernible through SEM, plays a crucial role in facilitating the
degradation process [59] [60]. Additionally, the biodegradation of PE induces alterations in
its crystalline structure, changes that can be quantitatively analyzed using differential scan-
ning calorimetry (DSC) and X-ray diffraction (XRD) [61] [62]. These alterations significantly
impact the material’s physical properties. Specifically, the degradation process leads to a
weakening of the polymer chains, manifesting as a reduction in tensile strength and elasticity
[63, 64, 65]. For a comprehensive analysis of these transformations, detailed morphological
evaluations can be conducted utilizing SEM or atomic force microscopy (AFM). To further
understand the thermal and structural changes occurring within the degraded polyethylene,
DSC and XRD serve as essential analytical tools. Collectively, these methodologies offer
a cohesive understanding of the biodegradation dynamics of PE, highlighting the intricate
relationship between microbial activity and the polymer’s structural integrity.

1.5.2
Quantitative Indicators for Evaluating Polyethylene Utilization in Biodegra-
dation Processes
The evaluation of polyethylene (PE) biodegradation encompasses three principal analyt-
ical approaches: assessment of weight loss, analysis of molar mass variation, and gravimetric
measurement of CO2 evolution. Collectively, these methodologies furnish a detailed perspec-
tive on the degradation of PE by microbial activities, essential for a nuanced understanding
of its impact on environmental health. The quantification of weight loss in PE serves as a
direct metric of biodegradation, with the degree of mass reduction directly corresponding
to the activity level of degrading microorganisms. This method is pivotal for benchmarking
the biodegradation efficiency across different environmental settings, offering a straightfor-
ward comparison tool. Furthermore, the investigation into the changes in PE’s molar mass
through gel permeation chromatography (GPC) provides a window into the molecular dy-
namics at play during degradation. A decrease in molar mass signals the breaking down
of polymer chains, thereby shedding light on the efficacy and the specific pathways of the
biodegradation process. Lastly, the gravimetric analysis of CO2 evolution from PE degra-
dation serves as an indicator of the environmental footprint of the degradation process. By
measuring the conversion of the solid polymer into CO2, this approach not only tracks the
progress of biodegradation but also highlights its environmental ramifications. By weaving
together the insights gained from weight loss, molar mass reduction, and CO2 production,
a comprehensive and interconnected understanding of PE biodegradation emerges.
1.5.3
Assessment of Oxidative and Structural Changes in Polyethylene Biodegra-
dation Using FTIR Spectroscopy
FTIR Spectroscopy was utilized to detect new or modified functional groups, indicative
of oxidation in polyethylene undergoing biodegradation [66, 67]. The method revealed struc-
tural changes, like the appearance of carbonyl groups and alterations in carbon bonds [39]. A
decrease in absorbance within the 1710-1750 cm−1 range indicated the development of C––O
groups [68, 69, 70]. Comparative FTIR analyses of polyethylene pre- and post-exposure
to biotic and abiotic factors initially showed similar degradation patterns.
Initially, the
degradation mechanisms for both treatments appeared similar. However, diverging path-
ways emerged, discernible through new peaks at 1640 and 940 cm−1, associated with biotic
processes distinct from abiotic effects [71]. The FTIR spectral analysis indicated an initial
increase in the carbonyl index, likely attributable to oxidation from dissolved oxygen, an abi-
otic factor. With extended exposure to biological entities, there was a noted decline in the
carbonyl index, suggesting biotic degradation mechanisms, possibly through Norrish-type
reactions or esterification processes [72]. The insights gained from these alterations offer a
measure of the polymer’s degradation level and the biodegradation process’s effectiveness.

This research investigates the biodegradation of polyethylene (PE), which poses environ-
mental challenges due to their durability. Inspired by a patent from Mellizyme Biotechnology
Ltd addressing the challenges of plastic degradation, this research explores novel strategies to
overcome the inherent resistance of polyethylene (PE) to standard decomposition processes,
a resistance attributed to polyethylene’s inert nature and the absence of reactive functional
groups. This patent from Mellizyme Biotechnology (US20230167469A1) introduces a unique
approach for degrading polyalkene polymers like polyethylene using a KatG/EC 1.11.1.21
polyalkenase enzyme [83]. This method, which combines KatG with a peroxide-generating
enzyme and a plasticase enzyme, shows promise for converting plastics into biofuel, car-
boxylic acids, or other biodegradable materials. Notably, Ralstonia pickettii 27511 and katG
isolated from Ralstonia pickettii strain I2J has demonstrated significant degradation of var-
ious plastics, suggesting a potential breakthrough in tackling plastic waste [83].
In this research study, the katG enzyme derived from Ralstonia pickettii strain I2J serves
a central role. Functioning naturally as a homodimer, each monomer of katG contains an
active site for peroxidase activity, crucial for breaking down hydrogen peroxide. With a
heme-binding site integral for redox reactions, the enzyme’s structure is pivotal for initiating
the degradation of resilient polymers like polyethylene. This study leverages katG’s oxidative
capabilities to address the challenges of polyethylene degradation.
The underlying research study aims to validate the effectiveness of the KatG enzyme
and Ralstonia pickettii in polyethylene degradation, leveraging Ralstonia pickettii’s robust
environmental adaptability to explore the potential for extensive plastic breakdown. Ral-
stonia pickettii a Gram-negative, aerobic bacterium, known for its environmental adaptabil-
ity and ability to degrade pollutants, emerges as a promising candidate. The bacterium’s
adaptability across various environments and its evolutionary genomic characteristics under-
score its value in bioremediation, offering the potential for plastic degradation. By breaking
down complex plastic polymers into simpler, harmless compounds, this microbial approach
presents a promising strategy for addressing plastic pollution. Its role in biofilm formation
and resistance could be pivotal in enhancing plastic degradation processes.

contribution to polyethylene breakdown, considering the analytical challenges posed by

Materials and Methods
This section outlines the approaches and methodologies employed in this study to in-

2.1
Enzyme engineering, Purification, and analysis
This study embarked on the genetic engineering, expression, and evaluation of wild-type
and mutant KatG enzymes, including forms without signal peptides, for the assessment of
polyethylene degradation. Utilizing Benchling-designed primers and plasmids from Twist
Biosciences, the research focused on amplifying and assembling the necessary genetic con-
structs. These constructs were introduced into Escherichia coli strain DH5α for screening
purposes, and in E. coli BL21 for recombinant protein expression and purification.
Liquid cultivation of E. coli DH5α cells was conducted using 5 ml of 2X YT medium,
which consisted of 16 g/L tryptone, 10 g/L yeast extract, and 5 g/L NaCl. Similarly, liquid
cultivation of E. coli BL21 cells was conducted in 5 ml of Lysogeny broth medium, containing
10 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, and 10 g/L NaCl, and both mediums were supple-
mented with 5 µl of kanamycin (50mg/ml) prior to incubation at 37◦C and 250 rpm. Solid
cultivation was performed using Lysogeny Broth (LB) medium, containing 10 g/L tryptone, 5
g/L yeast extract, 10 g/L NaCl, and 20 g/L agar, supplemented with kanamycin (100mg/L).
The purification process was optimized through the ÄKTA system, particularly focus-
ing on the yield challenges associated with mutant proteins. The study employed fluorescent
polyethylene microbeads in enzymatic assays to monitor degradation rates, adjusting enzyme
concentrations to gauge the extent of polymer breakdown. Additionally, both fluorescent and
non-fluorescent microbeads were analyzed under high enzyme concentrations, with surface
characteristics examined via scanning electron microscopy (SEM) post-treatment. In sum-
mary, this comprehensive approach elucidated the potential of modified KatG enzymes in
polyethylene degradation, offering insights into developing effective strategies for polyethy-
lene biodegradation.

2.1.3
Sample Preparation and Protein Purification by ÄKTA Pure
The cell pellets were resuspended in a washing buffer (20mM imidazole, 100 mM NaCl,
50 mM Tris, and 10% glycerol) followed by the addition of 2 ml of protease inhibitor cocktail
(Sigma Aldrich: 04693132001). Subsequent sonication was performed at 80% amplitude for
six cycles, each lasting 30 seconds. The sonicated cultures were then centrifuged at 4◦C with
a speed of 4000 rpm for 50 minutes under cooling conditions. The resultant supernatant
was passed through a 0.5 um filter to remove cellular debris, aiming to enrich for a high
concentration of proteins in the filtrate.
The ÄKTA system was utilized for the purification of proteins through affinity chro-
matography. The purification process commenced with the preparation of the ÄKTA system,
involving the washing of buffer channels (A1, B1, Buff, and sample ports S1-S4) at a flow
rate of 10ml/min, followed by the washing of the 5 ml his-trap column at 1ml/min with
filtered MilliQ water. After ensuring the integrity of the 5 ml His-trap column setup without
leaks, the sample ports (S1-S4), A1, and the buffer channels were put into the washing buffer,
while the B1 channel was linked to the elution buffer (100 mM NaCl, 50 mM Tris, 300 mM
imidazole, and 10% glycerol). After a comprehensive washing of the ÄKTA system with the
washing buffer (20mM imidazole, 100 mM NaCl, 50 mM Tris, and 10% glycerol), all four
sample ports were carefully aligned with their respective samples, ensuring the absence of air
bubbles. This precaution is critical as the ÄKTA system employs an air-sensor mechanism
to halt sample uptake upon detecting air bubbles. The proteins were subjected to elution at
10%, 50%, and 100%. The purification of proteins was achieved via affinity chromatography,
leveraging the His-tag affinity of the proteins with the 5ml His-trap column. The purified
protein fractions were then run on SDS-PAGE to analyze the purified proteins from ÄKTA.
Following this, the fractions were subjected to dialysis, employing cellulose membrane tubing

In this section, we present the findings from our investigation aimed at validating the
capacity of the katG enzyme to degrade plastic.
This inquiry was inspired by a patent
highlighting a particular katG enzyme known for its potential in plastic degradation. To
assess this capability, we initiated our experimental approach by acquiring plasmids encod-
ing both the wild-type and mutant forms of the katG gene. Given the necessity to examine
the enzyme’s activity in the absence of a signal peptide, we employed polymerase chain re-
action (PCR) and Gibson assembly techniques to meticulously construct plasmid variants
of the wild-type and mutant katG genes devoid of their signal peptides. The subsequent
segments detail the experimental procedures conducted, the conditions under which they
were performed, and the analytical methods applied to interpret the data. Through rigorous
experimentation, we sought to provide empirical evidence regarding the plastic-degrading
efficacy of the katG enzyme.
Two plasmids were procured from Twist Biosciences: one encoding the wild-type katG
gene and the other encoding a mutant version of the gene characterized by two specific mu-
tations (Y221F and R410N). The first phase of our experimental approach was dedicated to
meticulously excising the signal peptide sequences from both the wild-type and mutant katG
plasmids. This critical step required a precise reconstruction of each plasmid, ensuring the
targeted removal of the signal peptide regions. Signal peptides are pivotal for directing en-
zymes to their appropriate cellular compartments; however, their influence on the enzymatic
catalytic function is not well established. Nonetheless, our research aims to investigate the
potential impact of the signal peptide on the catalytic efficiency of katG to determine if it
plays any role in modulating the enzyme’s activity.
Phusion PCR was conducted with distinct sets of primer pairs for the katG and mutant
katG genes, facilitating the selective amplification of the plasmid backbones while effectively
omitting the sequence encoding for the signal peptide. Subsequent electrophoresis on a 1%
agarose gel revealed two distinct DNA fragments for each version of the katG gene, with
sizes for the wild-type katG fragments being 3470 bp and 3995 bp, and for the mutant
katG fragments 3418 bp and 4047 bp, respectively. These DNA fragments were meticulously
excised from the agarose gel, purified, and subsequently subjected to Gibson assembly, a
process that enabled the seamless ligation of the fragments, thereby culminating in the suc-
cessful construction of the signal peptide-lacking plasmid variants. The two initial plasmids,
one harboring the gene for wild-type katG and the other for its mutant counterpart, along
with their respective versions engineered to omit the signal peptide, were subsequently in-
troduced into competent Escherichia coli DH5α cells through transformation. From this
process, successful transformants (specifically, 2-3 colonies) were selected and cultured in LB
broth supplemented with kanamycin, followed by the successful extraction and purification

3.2
Microbial degradation of polyethylene by Ralstonia pickettii
Now, that we have seen the potential of katG in degrading polyethylene, let’s explore the
potential of Ralstonia pickettii which is capable of producing katG concerning the biodegra-
dation of polyethylene and the mechanisms involved in it.
The Ralstonia pickettii strain K-288, an aerobic gram-negative bacterium that was ob-
tained from ATCC and originally isolated from a tracheotomy patient, characterizes itself as
an oligotroph, enduring in low-nutrient conditions [78, 79, 80, 81]. This resilience suggests
its potential for plastic biodegradation across various harsh environments, addressing plastic
pollution in less accessible regions.
LCFBM medium [74], lacking carbon but containing essential salts, served as the control
to evaluate microbial polyethylene degradation, using polyethylene as the sole carbon source.
This approach facilitates the determination of polyethylene’s biodegradability. Initially, the
experiment will monitor the biodegradation pace of polyethylene by R. pickettii, using fluo-
rescent polyethylene microbeads. These microbeads, when subjected to degradation, release
fluorophores, detectable by a microplate reader at specific excitation and emission peaks.
This process necessitates various control setups to discern the degradation rate accurately,
including evaluating the microbeads’ stability in water and the potential corrosive effects
of LCFBM medium salts on the polyethylene matrix, which could result in the leakage of
the fluorophore into the media.
The control set included E. coli DH5alpha cells, which
were chosen for their lack of known plastic-degrading ability, and ease of cultivation, and
to serve as a baseline in experiments with both fluorescent and non-fluorescent polyethylene
microbeads in LCFBM medium 22. This choice allowed for the assessment of potential flu-
orophore leaching from the beads which could contrast with experimental results, thereby
determining if observed fluorescence changes were due to actual degradation or due to pro-
duction of fluorescent metabolites. Similarly, among the experimental groups, there were
three distinct setups to analyze various aspects to accurately track degradation: fluorescent
polyethylene microbeads, non-fluorescent microbeads, and control with R. pickettii in the
medium 22. Enhanced interaction with fluorescent microbeads indicated potential polyethy-
lene degradation. An observed increase in fluorescence with non-fluorescent microbeads or
in the medium alone may suggest the generation of fluorescent metabolites by R. pickettii,
emitting light at wavelengths similar to those of the fluorescent microbeads.
Exploring the biodegradation of polyethylene over ten days, this study focuses on flu-
orescence intensity measurements from fluorescent polyethylene microbeads. These beads
emit at 528 nm (yellow-green spectrum) when their fluorophore is released, enabling the
quantification of released fluorophores by measuring fluorescence intensity. Fluorescence in-
tensity measurements conducted with the incubation of fluorescent polyethylene microbeads
confirmed their structural integrity and demonstrated minimal fluorophore leakage, indicat-
ing robust encapsulation of flurorphore within the polyethylene matrix.

Discussion
4.1
Evaluating the efficiency of biodegradation of polyethylene me-
diated by katG enzyme
The investigation commenced with the examination of the enzymatic degradation of
polyethylene by katG, a catalase and peroxidase enzyme. The study initially presented a me-
thodical and refined strategy for enzyme engineering, production, and purification processes.
Following the purification, the enzymes were then employed to evaluate the biodegradation
rate of polyethylene. The research utilized a patent that explored the potential applica-
tion of the katG enzyme for polyethylene degradation. This assessment involved fluores-
cent polyethylene microbeads (Cospherics), utilizing both fluorescent and non-fluorescent
polyethylene microbeads to monitor the biodegradation pace of polyethylene. This was a
novel and efficient method developed to monitor the degradation of polyethylene over ex-
tended periods. Two distinct types of polyethylene microbeads were employed to evaluate
multiple aspects of polyethylene biodegradation. The structural design of these microbeads
was particularly noteworthy; the fluorescent polyethylene microbeads were engineered to en-
capsulate a fluorophore within the polyethylene matrix. The underlying principle was that
any compromise, even minimal, to the integrity of the polyethylene matrix would result in a
proportional release of the fluorophore, with the quantity of fluorophore released escalating in
tandem with the extent of damage sustained by the polyethylene matrix. The non-fluorescent
polyethylene microbeads were fabricated devoid of any fluorophores or additional additives,
thus comprising purely polyethylene. These microbeads were employed to investigate alter-
ations in their structural morphology attributable to biodegradation induced by the katG
enzyme. Enzymatic assays were performed using fluorescent polyethylene microbeads at low
(200 µg/ml) and moderate (600 µg/ml) concentrations in the presence of hydrogen peroxide,
given that katG enzyme exhibits optimal functionality with hydrogen peroxide, leveraging it
as a substrate. This is attributed to the peroxidase activity within the katG enzyme, which
facilitates the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide, thereby catalyzing a series of oxidation
reactions. The assays included the use of LCFBM (liquid carbon-free basal medium) as a
control to evaluate the impact of salts on the microbeads and to confirm their structural
integrity and the experimental groups with the purified set of enzymes. The determination
of a significant alteration in fluorescence intensities indicated considerable degradation by
both the wild-type and mutant forms of the katG enzyme, including variants lacking the
signal peptide, underscoring their efficacy in polymer degradation.
Concerns were raised regarding the hydrophobic nature of the fluorescent polyethylene mi-
crobeads utilized in the study. Due to their hydrophobic properties, the microbeads tended
to remain on the surface of the water, limiting direct interaction with the enzymes intended
for degradation studies. This positioning hindered the enzymes’ ability to effectively en-
gage with the microbeads, as they were not fully submerged. A potential solution involved
rendering the microbeads more hydrophilic through the application of a surfactant, such
as Tween 20. However, this approach presented significant risks, as the surfactant might
induce premature degradation of the polyethylene microbeads, compromising their integrity

for analysis. Consequently, for this research study, we opted to introduce the hydrophobic

to purify. Although patent literature reported substantial degradation activity for the mu-
tant katG variant, such findings were not replicable in the current study. Consequently, the
research proceeded with the wild-type katG enzyme for subsequent degradation experiments.

measuring 22 µm in size, were subjected to incubation with both the wild-type katG enzyme
variants, with and without signal peptide, for a duration of 7 days. Following this incubation
period, the samples were prepared for SEM analysis to scrutinize any structural modifica-
tions. The examination unveiled minor depressions in areas where the enzyme appeared to
interact with the polyethylene matrix of the microbeads. Additionally, there were obser-
vations of some polymer layers detaching from the microbeads. Moreover, Aztec software
was employed for elemental mapping to ascertain potential oxidation on the polyethylene
matrix. This was crucial for confirming degradation, as oxidation serves as a significant
indicator. Given the SEM analysis was performed under high vacuum conditions, any pres-
ence of oxygen detected on the polyethylene surface could only be attributed to oxidation.
Additionally, the enzyme-treated polyethylene microbeads exhibited the deposition of salts
such as NaCl and certain elements potentially derived from enzyme deposition, such as sul-
fur and nitrogen found in amino acids within the enzyme. The control samples comprised
non-fluorescent polyethylene microbeads immersed in an LCFBM medium to evaluate the
influence of salts on the polyethylene surface. No discernible structural changes were noted;
however, significant salt deposition was observed on the microbeads’ surface. Consequently,
the oxygen peaks detected were often accompanied by salt particles such as potassium (K),
magnesium (Mg), and sodium chloride (NaCl). It was noted that oxygen peaks were not
observed in isolation, and there was no notable reduction in carbon content.

study proceeded to assess the degradation efficacy of Ralstonia pickettii. This bacterium,
previously shown to produce the katG enzyme under investigation, was identified as a poten-
tial candidate based on insights gained from the patent (US20230167469A1) that inspired
this research study. In a manner akin to enzyme assays, this evaluation employed fluores-
cent polyethylene microbeads (Cospherics), employing both fluorescent and non-fluorescent
polyethylene microbeads to monitor polyethylene biodegradation rates. This method, a novel
and effective approach, facilitated the monitoring of polyethylene degradation over 10 days.
Initial controls were established to ascertain bead stability in various media, while experi-
mental groups focused on biodegradation by Ralstonia pickettii, contrasted with Escherichia
coli as a baseline due to its known inability to degrade PE. The investigation extended to
the observation of embedded fluorophore leaching from the polyethylene matrix into the
media by these bacteria, indicating intricate interactions between microbial activity and
polymer integrity. Furthermore, the study delved into the role of flavin molecules, produced
by bacteria, in redox reactions and their fluorescent properties, which may overlap with the
fluorescence markers of the assay, posing a distinct analytical challenge in discerning mi-
crobial activity from polymer degradation. Despite this challenge, the experimental cohort
comprising Ralstonia pickettii alongside the fluorescent polyethylene microbeads exhibited
elevated fluorescence intensity compared to the control assembly featuring E. coli with fluo-
rescent polyethylene microbeads. This observation suggests some indication that Ralstonia
pickettii may possess the capability to degrade polyethylene, although such findings cannot
be definitively categorized as conclusive evidence. Morphological analysis was conducted to
gain a comprehensive understanding of the biofilm formation capacity of Ralstonia pickettii
and its interaction with the polyethylene matrix. This aimed to discern structural changes
and assess the biofilm’s interaction with the polyethylene matrix.
Three substrates were prepared to investigate potential structural changes in the polyethy-
lene matrix. The fluorescent and non-fluorescent polyethylene microbeads incubated in the
LCFBM medium served as controls and the experimental setup wherein they were incubated
with R. pickettii culture at a higher cell density for 10 days. Following the incubation period,
the samples were prepared for SEM analysis to examine any notable structural modifications.
Similar observations noted in the enzyme samples were also evident in the SEM samples.
The hydrophobic nature of the polyethylene microbeads resulted in incomplete submersion
in the culture medium, consequently limiting the extent of degradation observed. Analysis
of both fluorescent and non-fluorescent polyethylene microbeads treated with R. pickettii
revealed pronounced indentations and bacterial infiltration, causing detachment of polymer
layers. These structural changes were absent in the control samples, confirming that these
alterations are indicative of degradation induced by R. pickettii. In this study, the LDPE
(Low-Density Polyethylene) film was employed as a substrate to assess the formation of

The exploration into the biodegradation capabilities of Ralstonia pickettii, supplemented
by the catalytic actions of the katG enzyme, unveiled its potential role in polyethylene degra-

degrading entities. This gap prompted an investigation that harnessed the gut microbiota
from larvae known for plastic consumption, specifically Galleria mellonella and Plodia inter-
punctella which demonstrated visible damage to low-density polyethylene films. The quest
to discern the presence and activity of R. pickettii as a plastic degrading organism, led to
the procurement of Galleria mellonella larvae from MiniZoo, Copenhagen. The larvae under-
went a 72-hour cultivation across five distinct dietary regimes to ascertain the proliferation of
bacteria within the Burkholderiales order. After the incubation period, the larvae were ster-

by the sequencing of the V3-V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene, enhancing the identification
process of genus and species-level microbial constituents within the gut microbiome. The
ensuing challenges highlighted the need for improved documentation of culturing methods
and incubation conditions for these larvae, a bottleneck that could impede the isolation of
specific microbes involved in plastic degradation. The 16S rRNA sequencing of these gut
inoculum samples, processed using the QIIME2 platform, amassed over 673,296 sequences,
enabling an in-depth view of the gut microbiota’s response to various plastic-inclusive di-

incubation, particularly in groups fed with beeswax, polyethylene beads, and LDPE bags,
indicative of the potential presence of plastic-degrading organisms. Taxonomic classification
of the microbiota showed eight distinct features within the Burkholderiales order, and the
application of BLAST analysis against a comprehensive database of 16S rRNA sequences

Conclusion
A comprehensive analysis of polyethylene biodegradation mediated by the katG en-
zyme and Ralstonia pickettii was conducted, alongside investigating the potential of the
Galleria mellonella gut microbiome in screening for plastic-degrading microorganisms. The
study meticulously developed and refined methodologies for enzyme engineering, production,
and purification, subsequently applying these enzymes to assess the biodegradation rates of
polyethylene using innovative techniques deploying the use of fluorescent polyethylene mi-
crobeads.
This approach revealed significant insights into the efficiency of polyethylene
degradation, highlighting the potential application of the katG enzyme and the bacterium
Ralstonia pickettii in the breakdown of polyethylene. The investigation faced challenges such
as the hydrophobic nature of polyethylene microbeads limiting enzyme interaction and the
complexities of ensuring the effectiveness of microbial degradation of polyethylene. Despite
these obstacles, the study achieved notable outcomes, including the identification of sig-
nificant morphological changes and elemental alterations indicative of polymer degradation
through SEM and elemental mapping analyses. the meticulous examination of the Galleria
mellonella gut microbiome through 16S rRNA sequencing across various feeding regimes has
highlighted the possibility of extracting plastic-degrading microbes from wax worms and
verified the existence of Ralstonia pickettii. The identification of eight distinct taxonomic
features within the Burkholderiales order, along with the verification of the presence of R.
pickettii ATCC 27511 via BLAST analysis, demonstrated the effectiveness of the research
methodology used. Developing a targeted experimental method using primers specific to the
16S rRNA region of R. pickettii has greatly improved the accuracy of identifying bacteria
through PCR. This advancement confirms R. pickettii’s presence in the gut microbiome and
provides information on its related species, offering important knowledge about the microbes
involved in breaking down plastic. This study concluded that both enzymatic and microbial
approaches hold promise for polyethylene degradation, though it acknowledged the need for
improved methodologies to enhance the interaction between them and the polymer substrate.
The findings also suggested that future investigations should focus on optimizing conditions
for microbial and enzymatic degradation, including the consideration of surfactant use to
increase hydrophilicity and the potential impact of surfactants on polymer integrity. This
study contributes valuable knowledge to the field of polymer degradation and opens avenues
for future research aimed at mitigating plastic pollution through biological means.
