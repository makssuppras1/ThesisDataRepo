Abstract
According to a national survey "Den Landsdækkende Undersøgelse af Patiento-
plevelser" (LUP, 2017), patients in Danish Emergency Departments(ED) aren’t
satisﬁed with the amount of information given concerning waiting times. The
LUP survey has shown the same results for the past many years, with a little
to no indication of improvements. The problem continues to increase and is
according to Trout et al. (2000) one of the most important factors of patients
satisfaction in the ED. Waiting time equals overcrowding, which according to
Paul et al. (2010) leads to an increase in medical errors.
In this thesis, the problem of individual patient information is being focused on.
The main focus has been on how to provide an easily understandable solution,
through research, user experience technologies and machine learning. An intelli-
gent system was aimed to be developed, where information should be visualised
on a dashboard. Through the process, three iterations were completed. Firstly,
the focus was on optimising a previously developed design and validating it at
Hvidovre Hospital, as well as conducting an experiment on the current set-up.
The current set-up was tested to obtain a starting point for later experiments.
Secondly, based on the validation from the ﬁrst iteration, a patient priority
system was developed and validated at Hvidovre Hospital. In this iteration, a
second experiment was performed, with the objective to see if the developed
system had a positive impact on the patient.
Lastly, further, development was initiated, with a goal to improve patient satis-
faction, by showing estimated waiting time through the use of machine learning.
Concluded from the work of this thesis, the patient’s satisfaction increased when
personalised information, in regards to waiting time, was shown in the waiting
area. Based on a linear regression model, it was found that waiting time estima-

tions could be improved. We found that only 32% of overestimated predictions
were found to be within a 30-minutes range, which we believe to be a good
starting point for the prediction. As a result of the work, it is concluded that
further research is required in order to establish better predictions, which should
lead to a more trustworthy system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The emergency departments’ (ED) most important role is to provide care for
acutely injured and ill patients 24 hours a day. The number of patients cycling
through the hospital system varies a lot throughout the day; especially in the
ED, which in most cases can be considered the hospital’s front door through
which most new patients enter the hospital system. Throughout the day, the
number of people can increase radically, causing overcrowding. Overcrowding
have in many years almost been synonymous with for EDs, and most often
overcrowding equals long waiting time and less satisﬁed patients. This has led
researchers from various ﬁelds to investigate the problems within the EDs and
the overall patient satisfaction.
According to both Trout et al. (2000) and LUP (2017), it is the lack of infor-
mation regarding each individual patient’s current status and expected waiting
time that is one of the most unsatisfying factors for patients in the EDs.
Overcrowding does not only make the relationship between the patients and
care providers a little tenses at times, it also has negative eﬀects on the staﬀ,
e.g. high levels of stress, poor patient outcomes, low moral among staﬀand
increase in medical errors (Paul et al., 2010).
The overcrowding phenomenon in EDs has increased over time due to various
factors and will continue to do so in the future. The impact of overcrowding has
come to a point, where multiple articles address the problem as a crisis (Paul
et al., 2010) in regards to patients and the hospital staﬀ.
The focus in research for the past decade has, to the best of our knowledge, not

been used in practice and not been tested as a real-life system. The objective
of this thesis is to identify the problems the ED are struggling with at Hvidovre
hospital. We will develop a system, which can inform the patients of their cur-
rent status, and then we will examine whether our system has an impact on
patient satisfaction.
1.1
Thesis Target Group
It is assumed that the reader of this thesis has knowledge of computer science or
related ﬁelds at least at the level of a bachelor’s degree. No previous knowledge
about hospital ED ﬂow, triage or other hospital related context is required. The
reader will be introduced to all necessary concepts throughout the thesis.
1.2
Motivation
The topic of this thesis was chosen to get acquainted with the domain of health-
care and system development mainly focused on human-computer interaction
(HCI), which for both of us is one of our preferred areas. The reason we are
focusing on the ED is because of our personal experiences with EDs; the frus-
tration we have felt from hours of waiting not knowing when it was our turn,
while other patients skipped the line and came in before us for no obvious rea-
son. After reading the LUP (2017), we noticed that we where not the only ones
feeling that way. As mentioned in the introduction, the next obvious step is
to take all previous research into account and develop a system, which is easily
understandable for the patients and then test the impact of such a system. It
has motivated us to be able to contribute with research within this area, in order
to get a step closer to a fully functional system that can contribute to a better
understanding of what is going on in the EDs.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This chapter presents the literature review, which has been performed in a
systematical way. The structure has been based on research from the article
Creativity and Information Systems in a Hypercompetitive Environment: A Lit-
erature Review by Müller and Ulrich (2013). Müller and Ulrich provide guidance
towards making a systematic approach when writing a literature review, and this
is has inspired our own approach.
The primary objective of the literature review is to gain an insight into the
state-of-the-art solutions that exists in the EDs. Using the knowledge gained
in the literature review can help us to identify in which areas there is a lack of
material regarding research within the EDs.
The ﬁrst part introduces the state-of-the-art literature review, where a short
introduction is presented.
The second part presents the process of the literature search. Here detailed
descriptions of how the material has been obtained and the number of articles
the review is based on will be presented.
The third part involves the analyses of the discovered material, where a system-
atic approach of grouping articles together based on concepts within the ED
will be carried out.

2.1
Introduction
The hospital EDs have for years been associated with problems such as over-
crowding and frustrated, uninformed patients who have to wait for hours (Smith,
2017). This situation have some known damaging consequences for both the pa-
tients and the providers.
As of now, the current information in the ED does not provide any information
to the patients about their current status, both in terms of waiting time and
the ED’s prioritisation, which is highly requested in the market but has a level
of complexity that makes it diﬃcult to solve completely (Ahmad et al., 2015).
2.2
Literature Search
The literature search has been based on research conducted by Webster and
Watson (2002). Webster and Watson have written the article Analyzing the past
to prepare for the future: Writing a literature review, which is about including
certain points when writing a literature review. The article’s main focus lies
on how to ﬁnd relevant literature. From the article, basic knowledge has been
obtained and techniques like backward1 and forward2 searching have been used.
The search has been performed on the search engines Google Scholar and DTU
Findit. Besides searching using relevant keywords that relate to the problems
at the EDs, a year span has been applied as criteria. This span goes from 2010
to present time, and was applied while searching for articles.
For the search, the following search queries were used:
"Emergency department" AND "Waiting time" AND ("Visualisation" OR "Crowd-
ing" OR "Queuing theory" OR "Estimation" OR "Dashboard").
In the ﬁrst step of the search process, searching with the search databases from
Google Scholar and DTU Findit gave a result of 4792 hits in total.

2.3 Analysing the Literature

2.3
Analysing the Literature
According to Webster and Watson (2002), the next step in the process of struc-
turing the relevant articles is to use a concept-centric approach. This method
calls for the chosen articles to be sorted based on key concepts to get an overview
of the bigger picture in the EDs, because literature reviews in general are
concept-centric, as concepts determine the organising framework of a review
(Webster and Watson, 2002).
The discovered articles have been grouped into four diﬀerent components that
are each distinct from one another. The ﬁrst, the problem component, is for
articles that involve problems and issues such as crowding in the EDs. The
second, the solution component, consists of articles about solving the problems
in the EDs. The third, the patient component, includes articles that concern
patients, for example, conducted surveys targeting patients. The last component
is the queuing component, which includes articles about queuing theory and
estimations that have been conducted in the EDs.

Cooke (2002) performed a study which tried to verify that the use of fast-track

conducted over a period of ten weeks, where 13,918 patients went through the

(p<0.0001) (Cooke, 2002). Furthermore, the risk of waiting more than 1 hour
decreased by 32%.

2.5.1
Key Findings
After examining the articles, it was evident that one of the signiﬁcant challenges
in the ED was waiting time for low-acuity patients. The overall patient satis-
faction was low due to the lack of information presented by the provider, during
the wait and the wait itself. Another major ﬁnding was the lack of solutions
targeting the patients of the ED. Several solutions have been made trying to
solve the causes of crowding, but these solutions were only meant for the ﬂow
management and thereby the hospital staﬀ’s problem.
An additional ﬁnding was the combined lack of testing of a live system with the
ability to incorporate the visualisation for the patient and queuing theory with
the prediction of waiting time.
The three key ﬁndings discovered are listed below:
• Waiting time for low-acuity and non-informed patients.
• The lack of a solution targeted at patients waiting in the ED.
• System for visualising, predicting and testing of waiting times.
The ﬁrst challenge the ED faces is the waiting time for low-acuity patients
and the lack of information provided.
Both Trout et al. (2000) and Huang
et al. (2018) highlight the importance of communication and the need for more
transparency in the ED. They both suggest that the ED informs the patients
about their current situation, which will result in less frustrated patients and
increase patient satisfaction. Saunders (1987) study concluded that the ED was
more oriented towards the eﬃcient care of high-acuity patients and less so for
low-acuity patients, even though these constitute the majority of the patients
arriving at the ED. The current set-up has some consequences for the low-
acuity patients, such as long waiting hours and frustrations, which may result
in conﬂicts between hospital staﬀand patients or that patients leave before they
have been seen.

4.1
Lean UX
Lean UX is a way, process and mindset that grasps on Lean-Agile principles.
The focus of Lean UX lies on the practice under design and less on deliverables
than with traditional UX (IDF, 2018). The core idea of Lean UX is to collect
feedback from users as early as possible so quick decisions can be made.
Lean UX is about validating hypotheses.
It looks at a product as a set of
hypotheses to be validated rather than as a set of features to be developed
(Klein, 2013). When adopting the methodology of Lean UX, we do not assume
what the user wants but use interviews and research to develop hypotheses of
what the user might want (Klein, 2013).
Another essential concept of Lean UX is to validate at each step and make it
happen as early as possible. The reason for an early validation is that it is
better to fail fast on a new idea than to keep going, as that usually costs more

then decide to sign up for the idea if interested in the product. The owners of

with it based on the number of interested users. Other types of validation can
be achieved using interviews. Here, the end user is asked directly about the idea
or product, to see if any interest can be acquired.
Lean UX is agile, as the way of working emphasises an iterative process involving
measuring and improving continuously. Lean UX is based on three phases -
Build, Measure and Learn as seen in ﬁgure 4.1. A product is built based on
an idea and it is then possible to measure and learn from it in order to then
make the necessary changes based on what was discovered and validated before
moving on further.
Figure 4.1: The Lean UX Loop - Klein (2013)
Lean UX is also user-centered. According to Klein (2013), Lean UX borrows
heavily from User Centered Design (UCD), which is a process that focuses en-
tirely on the user and has existed for a long time.
Another concept of Lean UX is that it is data-driven.
According to Klein
(2013), the importance behind Lean UX is that new designs or features are
not automatically better in comparison to what came before it. Data plays an
essential role in deciding which design or functionality works the best for the
user. Whenever a design or feature change has happened, testing should be
performed, and data should be gathered from the users to validate the change
completely.

Lean UX is iterative and involves the three phases, Build, Measure and Learn as
stated earlier. An essential task within Lean UX is to create. It can be anything
from a landing page to a small design change or a functional prototype of a
product. In Lean UX, a concept deﬁned as Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is
practiced. The idea behind this concept is the development of the least possible
thing that enables the team to gather the highest value of validated learning
about customers with the least eﬀort (Maurya, 2017).
Before building an MVP solution, one or several hypotheses should be declared
based on prior research. Based on what was learned from the users, an MVP
solution should be created. After ﬁnishing the MVP, it must be continuously
reevaluated and, hypotheses should be validated or invalidated based on the
user’s feedback.

User Story Map
At the beginning of the chapter, a user story map was created to get a more
comprehensive understanding of the user journey and how the product should
work.
Introduced by JeﬀPatton, story mapping is a widely used technique
when adopting Lean UX and is introduced by JeﬀPatton. It provides a way to
visualise a product or service as a series of tasks which the target user completes.
A user story map is built using three blocks, goals, activities and tasks. Goals
reﬂect the desired outcome and can be explained as what we aim to accomplish.
Activities describe what the user is doing with the service or product. Lastly,
tasks describe the work that is needed to accomplish the goal. One approach
that the user story map brings is how easy prioritisation can be managed. Here
diﬀerent releases can be made and prioritised from top to bottom, so everyone
can see what is going to be made ﬁrst. The approach of prioritisation has been
utilised in this project.
As of now, the story map presents the task that is
planned for the ﬁrst release, but additional changes may be introduced in the
following iterations.

be created. Because of this, wireframing is well adapted in the start, of an idea,
when the product should be communicated, and the design and features should
be discovered.

6.3.3
User Testing
One of the essential practices in Lean UX is user testing. Using the input from
a bunch of users makes us capable of validating an idea or design in a matter of
minutes. In this iteration, three kinds of user testing were practiced. These are
interviews, surveys, and A/B testing.
Hospital staﬀand patients at Hvidovre Hospital were interviewed about the
initial idea, as well as the designs trying to validate the ideas that were created.
Furthermore, surveys were used as a tool to get statistics on the diﬀerent kinds
of answers that were received while interviewing the patients.
Lastly, A/B testing was used to test diﬀerent ideas within the designs that
were made. Here, the patients and staﬀwere shown the same feature, but with
diﬀerent styling, and had to choose the one they preferred. This technique was
especially useful for validating icons, phrases of text or colours that can have
diﬀerent eﬀects on some users.
6.4
Validating the Wireframes at Hvidovre
This section introduces the validation of the designs that have been conducted
at Hvidovre Hospital. To get a comprehensive result, the analysis was divided
into three phases. In the ﬁrst phase, some patients were interviewed regarding
relevant questions about the idea and design. Based on stage one, outcomes
were discovered, which led to a few changes to the design. In the second phase,
the optimised design was again shown to some patients. The primary objective
in this phase was to gather the user’s opinion about what information they
would like, and how it should be shown. In the last stage, a design expert from
a media bureau was questioned, trying to remove redundant elements in the
designs as well as adjust and ﬁne tune.
The number of patients that were asked during the interviews was kept at a
minimum due to what was learned in an article by (Nielsen and Landauer,
2000). This article is about the most optimal amount of users that should be
used when usability testing the designs. According to the report, Jakob and
Tom did a study where they discovered that the number of usability problems
found in a usability test can be linked to the number of test users. They saw
that when many users are testing a product simultaneously, many of them are
pointing out the same design ﬂaws multiple times. They concluded that the
optimal amount of users that should be asked should not exceed 5, as over 75%
of usability problems were already discovered by those ﬁve people. Based on this
research, each phase will aim at reclaiming at least 5-10 answers from patients
waiting at Hvidovre Hospital.

The following presents the choices of tools that have been selected for each of

dard languages HyperText Markup Language(HTML), Cascading Style Sheets(CSS)
and JavaScript(JS). It is our aim to develop a web application and therefore a
frontend framework should be chosen. Based on brief research on the most used
and popular frameworks, two frameworks have been chosen as presented below:
• Angular.
• Bootstrap.
Angular has been chosen among two other candidates known as React and
Vue.JS. These are according to a popular programming teacher (Hamadani,

A survey is a data collection tool used to gather information according to Cherry
(2018). Surveys are often used to collect personal opinions from study partic-
ipants. It is furthermore a relatively fast and cheap way of collecting data for
research purposes.
According to Penny S. Visser and Lavrakas (2018) the Repeated Cross-Sectional
Surveys were used to collect data at Hvidovre Hospital. Repeated Cross-Sectional
Surveys are used over time to notice the changes impact on the environment.
This approach has been used in both iteration 2 and iteration 3. The same sur-
vey was used at each test to be able to compare the results and see the impacts
of the changes applied to the environment set up in the diﬀerent iterations.
According to Penny S. Visser and Lavrakas (2018) one of the best methods to
measure changes is a panel survey. A panel survey includes the same group of
people tested in the same environment, two or more times over a period. This
is not a very likely scenario in an ED and would have to be organised in a way
which would not be able to replicate a patients state-of-mind from when they
were in the ED to get treatment. According to Messner and Waenke (2011), the
mood of study participants and their current life situation could have an impact
on the answers provided. This means that people at an ED are most likely not
in the best-state-of-mind based on their current situation at the ED. This fact
could have a negative impact on the answers received. The assumptions is made
that the LUP survey was conducted under the same circumstances and it will
therefore not be taken into consideration.

Machine learning is the science of self-acting computers that are not explicitly

and has since then only developed due to the digitisation and cheap computing
power according to Pyle and José (2015). Machine learning is nowadays used in
self-driving cars, practical speech recognition, eﬀective web search and a vastly
improved understanding of the human genome. The possibilities of machine
learning only increase over time due to the enormous size of data collected
every day.
Machine learning algorithms are referred to as "soft-coded", since they learn
by adopting from previously collected data and are not in the same sense as
"normal" coding "hard-coded". The adoption is referred to as training, which
implies taking in data samples and the machine learning algorithm will then
learn from the data. The algorithm will, during the learning, try to self-optimise
to produce the best outcome possible.
Machine learning can operate with large, noisy and complex datasets and still
be able to detect hard-to-discern patterns, which is useful in the ED with all
of the complexity.The tools used to do the actual machine learning are shown

Data Processing
One of the requirements building an intelligent waiting time system is the esti-
mation of waiting times. Being able to predict the time a speciﬁc patient have
to wait, can help the hospital presenting waiting times and by that inform the
patients much more than they do today at Hvidovre. Prediction also makes a
system more intelligent, as it can be used to automatically set a time to wait
for patients in a queue, without having the medical staﬀto interact with the
system.
The estimation of waiting times can be performed using various techniques, like
the use of queuing theory or the more recent approach using machine learning.
Queuing theory is the study of long waiting lines and queues, where mathe-
matically queuing models can be constructed to predict the queue length and
waiting time. This project will not be taken the classical approach of queuing
theory into consideration, but instead use machine learning to predict the wait-
ing time. The reason for this is that a dataset containing patient records have
been received from the chief physician at Hvidovre Hospital. The objective is

8.4
Estimating the Waiting Time Using Machine
Learning
Predicting the waiting time the patient has to wait is diﬃcult, because of the
complex environment at the ED (LouAnn H. Woodward, 2014). In real life, there
are many factors associated with an ED which can aﬀect the time a patient has
to wait. In the previous section, it was determined that ﬁve diﬀerent features will
be used to predict the waiting time. Depending on the nature of the problem,
three diﬀerent approaches can be chosen within machine learning. The three
approaches which can be taken are supervised learning unsupervised learning,
and semi-supervised learning. The approach is based on what kind of data is
used. The variations can be seen below.
• Unsupervised learning is used when the training data does not contain
any output data at all - also called unlabelled data.
• Supervised learning is used when the output data is deﬁned in the
training data - called labelled data.
• Semi-Unsupervised learning is a combination of both supervised and
unsupervised learning where the input data is partially labelled.
The
unlabelled parts are labelled based on the labelled part.
The data provided from Hvidovre Hospital is labelled and therefore the super-
vised learning approach is used. Regression and Classiﬁcation are both part of
the supervised learning. The main diﬀerence between the two is that the out-
put in classiﬁcation is categorical and the output of regression is numerical. The
regression approach was chosen to predict the estimated waiting time for each
patient in this project. Although regression was chosen, the classiﬁcation ap-
proach could also be used to predict if a patient can expect to get into treatment
within a predeﬁned target. This could be achieved by for example predicting in
which time interval the patient would be based on known data.
Before the selected regression models were used, the data was split into a training
set and a testing set. This was done in order to keep data, which could be used to
measure the performance of the training models. 20% of the data was randomly
chosen as test data, with the rest being used to train the models.
The ﬁnal step of data preparation was to detect whether part of the data should
be considered as categorical features. This was partly taken care of during the
data pre-processing section, where every feature was given a numerical value.
The method used to map the categorical features is called One-hot encoding.

for unexpected data points, that still should be cared for. Based on this fact, the
use of metric may depend on whether the data used contained many outliers.
Based on the dataset received, it’s hard to tell if a data point can be concluded
as an outlier. During the ﬁnal validation conducted at Hvidovre, it was dis-
covered that some patients were in need of a specialised doctor from another
department. Specialised doctors need to ﬁnd a time slot within an already ﬁlled
calendar, to consult the patient. This can potentially take a long time and may
cause the high data points in the dataset.
In ﬁgure 8.5, (Drakos, 2018) explains outliers in regards to error measurements.
Due to the fact that the executive consultant and superusers at Sundhedsplat-

the missing indicators of what kind of doctors the patients are waiting for makes
it diﬃcult concluding that the suspected data points are in fact outliers. Do
to the fact that outliers can’t be concluded, the Mean Squared Error has been
chosen as the most important performance metric.

Discussions Concerning Machine Learning Models
This section will focus on the ﬁndings from the data pre-processing and ma-

First, the data will be discussed and
subsequently the machine learning.
The ED is very complex as patterns can be hard to spot through observations
alone.
The ED collects a lot of data which can be used to illustrate these
patterns. A dataset containing a year of patient ﬂow data was received from
Hvidovre Hospital and analysed to obtain a certain level of understanding. Dur-
ing the process of understanding the data fully, multiple errors in the data was
found and conﬁrmed by the executive consultant and superusers of Sundhed-
splatformen.
The ﬁrst error in the existing data was the missing treatment
start time for fast-track patient, which indicates when patients are going into
treatment and are no longer located in the waiting area. According to the ex-
ecutive consultant, 80% of their "production" goes through fast-track, which
only is handles non-urgent patients, meaning that the majority of patients have
to wait. The missing treatment start time led to the removal of the fast-track

patients from the dataset, which potentially could have had a big impact on the
ﬁnal results due to the many missing patients.
The triage colours were the second big concern raised regarding the data. The
triage colours were manually typed into the dataset, which could have had a big
impact on the output as well. This could potentially create more noise in the
data then adding actual value to the prediction.
During the interview it was discovered that the doctors and nurses do not always
remember to close a patient in Sundhedsplatformen immediately after the con-
sultation is over. The same may occur in the waiting area if the staﬀforgets to
move a patient from waiting to treatment. It can have a big impact on the data
which becomes more unreliable each time this occurs. This is what happens in
many systems that needs manual action before updates are completed. This
may cause outliers due to long ﬁctive waiting times for one or more patients.
Outliers in this case known as data entry errors have a big impact on the predic-
tion. These type of errors are called multivariate outliers and can be found in a
n-dimensional space. Not all of them are necessarily errors, but simply patients
who have been waiting for a long time. These patients are hard to distinguish
between and is why outliers may still exist, but have not been removed from
the dataset.
To prepare the data for training and testing the features were chosen based
on a feature selection method. During this process it were conﬁrmed that the
triage did not add much value to the prediction.
The outcome might have
been diﬀerent with correct triage level and all fast-track patients. Based on
the interviews conducted at Hvidovre all staﬀinterviewed agreed that blue and
green patients waits the longest, but that is not the case based on the results
from section 8.3.3.
Cross-validation is another way of splitting the data into training and testing.
Cross-validation uses the folds, which is a way of splitting the data into N
numbers of splits and train and test on each of the folds and averages it all
together to the best ﬁt. The simple split function used in this project have its
limitation, as only some of the data are randomly chosen for the testing part. If
the testing part only includes data points that are only blue or yellow patients,
it may have an aﬀect on the further outcome. In retrospect the better choice
would have been to use cross-validation.
As seen in the previous result section, the diﬀerent models that were chosen,
were almost not able to predict better than the baseline. This may indicate
a complex problem and noisy dataset. It can be discussed if the choice of the
selected models were right for the type of problem, as several other possibilities
could have been considered.
Due to the time frame of the project, and the
quality of the data received, it was decided to focus more on integrating a
possible model into the system, so testing can be achieved.

8.8.2
Data Exchange
To make the developed waiting time system ready for production, the correct
data is needed and in the right format. This subsection examines how the possi-
ble data currently needed could look if provided by Sundhedsplatformen. Based
on the developed system, data is required when a new patient is registered and
whenever important updates take place like when patients are moved between
the queues or to treatment. To request for data two scenarios were considered.
The ﬁrst includes only a polling job, that every minute or two retrieves data
based on every waiting patient and every patient currently being treated. The
second option that can be considered is to include both a polling job, but also
use push events. A push event is something that can be triggered when criterion
is met. Events are for example used in the current developed solution, where
the JavaScript library Pusher.js was used.
Using events makes it possible to trigger requests for updates more frequently,
which ultimately leads to updates being shown whenever they happen. The
downside of the use of events is that a way of how to implement these needs to
be found, to make them work when Sundhedsplatformen is running. An exam-
ple of usage of the push events could be that every time a new registered patient
has been triaged and put in the queue, an event should be ﬁred, so a request
for new data can be made.

ments were discovered. A brief summary of the learning’s gathered from this
iteration are shown below:
• It was discovered that showing the waiting time to the patients, had a
better inﬂuence on patient satisfaction than showing nothing or the patient
priority alone.
• The triage colour was provided in the dataset but was found unusable due
to it didn’t depend with the waiting time.
• The time at day was found important, due to it was seen that the pattern
of patient arrival could be reproduced based on the data provided.
• It was shown that waiting time can be predicted and used in a system, but
still needed optimisation and more accurate data.
• The overall goal of predicting most patients within 30 minutes was not
reached and optimisations are needed.
• Informing patients at arrival still remained something that should be en-
hanced in the future.
• Patient understood the value of having a patient in treatment icon.
• It was discovered that optimisations were needed in the communication
and design, so patients that require special assistance are informed about,
why they have to wait longer than others.
• An API for Sundhedsplatformen was possibly discovered, but access was
needed which should be provided by the hospital, if the system should be
automated and operated independently.

Chapter 9
Discussion
Patient satisfaction at danish ED’s suﬀer from a lack of information in regards
to waiting time. Currently, no waiting time systems display the current situa-
tion for the individual patient at the EDs. Missing information has been a focus
for years due to more frequent occurrences of overcrowding and long waiting
time which increase annually. A solution to provide more personalised informa-
tion for the individual patient may improve patient satisfaction and provide a
better experience when visiting the ED for patients and staﬀalike. A Lean UX
approach was used as the overall strategy for completing the study, while an
agile approach was used in the daily work. Surveys, A/B testing and interviews
were used in the process of validating the developed solution.
The validations of the developed information system lead to the most important
ﬁnding during this thesis. It was discovered that patient satisfaction in regards
to waiting time information increases when the patient is provided personal in-
formation concerning their current status. The improvement was noticed both
while validating the priority system to an even higher degree when validating
the waiting time.
The problem was validated by testing the ﬁndings from LUP locally at Hvi-
dovre Hospital and compare the results, which were almost identical to the
LUP (2017). Furthermore, the solution developed was validated by conducting
patient and staﬀinterviews as well as surveying patients before and while the
system was running in the waiting area. The results correspond to what Krishel
==PAGE:140==

and Baraﬀ(1993) found back in 1993, namely that patient information had
a positive impact on patient satisfaction. The lack of information was clearly
pointed out by patients in the national survey LUP. The LUP (2017) survey also
states that the capital region scores signiﬁcantly lower in regard to informing
patients about the waiting time than the national average.
In section 5.2 the state-of-the-art in Denmark in terms of patient information
systems was researched by interviewing staﬀfrom diﬀerent EDs in Denmark.
Based on the ﬁndings it was discovered that multiple systems were tested, none
satisﬁed the needs of both the patients and the hospitals. Only one region had
a system with limited personal information. Along with the region of northern
Denmark, the region of southern Denmark were the only regions which scored
over the national average according to LUP (2017).
During chapter 6 the initial design for the system was validated through inter-
views and surveys by patients and staﬀat Hvidovre. Section 6.6 concluded that
a screen or monitor was the best way of showing information. Further, it was
found that patients were able to identify themselves on the screen based on the
design and see their estimated waiting time. Patients preferred to be shown a
higher waiting time than actually expected due to the frustrations potential un-
derestimations caused. During the development of the MVP, all these ﬁndings
were considered.
Discussions concerning machine learning were presented in section 8.4.4. It was
discovered that the data set received from Hvidovre Hospital has its severe limi-
tations and contained a lot of noise. It was found that a linear regression model
performed the best compared to random forest. As previously stated an overes-
timation of expected waiting time was preferred compared to underestimations.
The linear regression model performed 32% within 30 minutes above the actual
waiting time and 62% within above an hour. A study (LouAnn H. Woodward,
2014) concerning prediction of waiting time was found. The results of that study
indicate that they were able to predict within +/- 30 minutes with a precision
of 33% and within +/- an hour with 66%. Bear in mind that the results in this
thesis represents prediction mostly concerning overestimation.
It was found that a potential live implementation of the system with the ability
to retrieve data through Sundhedsplatformen was a possibility, just not feasible
with in the time frame of this thesis. The company behind Sundhedsplatformen
named Epic, provides an API for third-party integration with the system. The
data needed to make a live system is presented in section 8.8.

9.0.1
Limitations
This thesis has some limitations which need to be considered. The ﬁrst limi-
tations to be pointed out are found in the experiments conducted. Including
only ten patient’s per experiment may not provide an accurate indication of how
patients feel. Furthermore, the fact that the experiments were not performed
during busy hours may have inﬂuenced the results as well. The system was
not tested during peak hours due to Hvidovre Hospital preferred the testing
to be executed in the morning or at noon. Next, the system has only been
tested at Hvidovre Hospital, which means that the results only will apply for
Hvidovre and would have to be conﬁrmed elsewhere. Also the experiments were
conducted using the general information monitor, and this may have inﬂuenced
the question concerning the overall level of information, as patients may have
missed important information provided by that screen.
In addition, all patients were manually typed into the system during the ex-
periments and were not connected with Sundhedsplatformen. This might have
had an impact on the overall outcome due to the fact that it was only close to
real-time updates.
The dataset provided by Hvidovre Hospital limited the work on the prediction
model due to missing and incorrect data. This may have aﬀected the outcome of
the prediction model. The machine learning part of this assignment was limited
to concern only two regression models. Other prediction models combined with
a new dataset could have provided a better prediction result.
9.0.2
Future Work
This section provides information on the things that could be improved as a
natural extension to the thesis. Since this thesis is concerned with an actual
and serious problem in the EDs, there is still room for a lot of additional re-
search on the matter. Due to this fact, only the most signiﬁcant changes related
to the thesis will be discussed.
Throughout the thesis, design optimisations have been made as an iterative
process.
Based on ﬁndings from the last experiment conducted at Hvidovre
Hospital, it was found that some patients wait longer due the requirement of
additional specialists from diﬀerent departments at the hospital. This had an
eﬀect on a patient surveyed during the last experiment. The patient thought he
was being unfairly treated, as the dashboard indicated the patient had to wait
1-2 hours longer than some of the other patients.
A possible improvement to the design is to indicate in some way if a patient

is in need of a specialised doctor and that the waiting time is therefore uncer-
tain. Unfortunately. Unfortunately information like this was not provided, so
additional information from Sundhedsplatformen is required.
Another point that should be focused on in a possible extension of the thesis,
is the integration with Sundhedsplatformen. This is required if the system is to
operate on its own with minimal interaction from the hospital. It was discov-
ered that Epic had a sign-up form that can be used to request access. Instead
of accessing it manually, as was tried, the management at Hvidovre Hospital
should pull a few strings to make the process of getting access smoother and
faster.
Integrating with Sundhedsplatformen opens up additional research as more test-
ing is required to see how well the estimations of waiting time perform. Further,
an investigation of the data received from Sundhedsplatformen to the system
should be examined to see if updates are received correctly.
An important area where additional research can also be made is the integration
and testing of a mobile design. As illustrated in the user story map 7.1, one of
the releases indicated the creation of a mobile version. This release was rated
as not important for an MVP within the time frame and was therefore pushed
to potential future work.
Creating a mobile version makes it possible for patients to check their current
status in the queue on their phone. This allows the individual patient to do
other things like go to the shop while waiting.
Besides a mobile version, additional improvements can be made with the use of
general information. During each experiment, the general information screens
provided to the patients were replaced by the dashboard. Including a second
monitor in the waiting area for general information may improve the patients
view on the information level even further.
Due to the receptionists’ heavy workload, it was found that additional research
needs to be performed regarding information at arrival. A possible direction
that also relates to user experience is to provide extra information in the form
of a poster about the waiting time system.
Another way has been inspired

from the entrance to the waiting time dashboard. This could lead the patients
in direction of the screen. Furthermore, this technique is also helpful for the
receptionist, as a big part of their job is to guide people to their correct location
after arrival.

A major part of the intelligent system lies in the estimation of the waiting time.
This drives the overall system and should be as precise as possible. One im-
provement towards the estimation is that a new data set is needed with the
correct requirements. The data set received in early 2019 did not include what
was agreed upon and could not be used. Getting new data includes the pos-
sibility of using the fast-track patients, which may result in a better or worse
performance. Furthermore, the triage colour should be provided from Sundhed-
splatformen, and not entered manually by the executive consultant.
As seen with the results from the waiting time predictions, some predictions
were overestimated and others were underestimated compared to the actual
time. As it was found that patients preferred a longer estimated waiting time,
optimisations should be made, so the model will overestimate more often than
the current, where 32% estimated over within 30 minutes and 62% within 60
minutes. Based on discussions with Tue Herlue, an asymmetric cost function
should be decided and tested. The problem we have when estimating waiting
time for a patient is that if we underestimate too much, the patient may get
more frustrated and angry faster. Same goes if we overestimate too much, as
they then might also be annoyed. Because patients preferred a longer estimation
than the actual waiting time turned out to be, overestimation is to be preferred.
In the result used, overestimation and underestimation have the same cost, as 10
minutes over the actual is the same as 10 minutes predicted under. Introducing
an asymmetric cost function makes it possible, so for instance overestimation
has a lower cost than underestimation would have. This will have the impact
that the model will overestimate more than underestimate, which according to
what was learned is safer to do.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
In this project, the problem concerning the lack of information in the danish
EDs have been investigated. The focus of the thesis has been on providing infor-
mation to patients and testing wether it had an eﬀect on the level of information
and the patient satisfaction. This was achieved by developing a prototype so-
lution targeting patients which can show the waiting time for the patients at
Hvidovre Hospital based on predictions. Before the developing process began,
several interviews and surveys were conducted to validate possible designs and
problems patients might have while waiting. Next, the development was started
where patient priority and waiting time was some of the factors presented to
the patients. During the thesis, three experiments were performed in total, with
the objective of measuring the patient satisfaction based on what is presented
at the hospital.
The major ﬁnding discovered in the thesis was that personalised information
regarding waiting time at the ED had a signiﬁcant impact on the patient sat-
isfaction. The general information does not provide the same value as a per-
sonalised status for each patient, both in regards to priority as well as actual
waiting time.
Many others have provided ways of optimising the patient ﬂow within the ED
as well looked at the problem from a theoretical point of view. Few articles
describe the use of machine learning to predict the waiting time at the ED and
none seem to have accomplished the use and integration of a machine learning
model into a system which could be tested at an ED. This thesis contributes to

the ﬁeld of study by the development of a live system which was tested in a real
set-up at Hvidovre Hospital. The tested system was manually updated due to
missing integration with Sundhedsplatformen, which is therefore a possibility for
further development. The problem was addressed by developing an intelligent
system which tries to predict the individual patients waiting time and display
it in an easily understandable way.
