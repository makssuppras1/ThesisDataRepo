Abstract
The leading edge of a turbine refers to the part of the turbine blade that ﬁrst contacts
the air. Naturally, the leading edge of a wind turbine blade can be subjected to erosion
over time as a result of environmental factors. When wind turbines are in operation, the
tip of the blade can reach speeds of up to 330km/h which greatly exposes the leading
edge and increases the risk of potential damage and eﬃciency loss over time. In real
cases, the composite surface of blades (which erodes very fast) is coated with a more
resistant material, called Leading Edge Protection (LEP).
In the recent years, manufacturers innovated with new visco-elastic coating materials,
which better absorb the rain droplet impact. This results in a longer lifespan for LEP,
but the erosion still occurs.
In order to improve the coating materials and optimize the maintenance of wind tur-
bines, it is important to predict material durability. So we need to better understand
the material failure mechanisms, and identify which material properties control its
durability.
The purpose of this project is to build a method to measure experimentally the fracture
parameters of LEP material. These type of material are viscoelastic and hyperelsatic,
which makes testing quite complex because of very high strains and rate dependency.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Wind energy
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) identiﬁes the electriﬁcation
of transportation and industry as one of the most e↵ective measures to reduce global
carbon emissions [1]. In this context, global electricity demand is expected to increase
markedly by 2050, and under most scenarios, wind power is projected to supply a sub-
stantial share of this demand [2]. Wind energy has already become a central component
of the energy mix both in Denmark and worldwide, contributing signiﬁcantly to the
decarbonisation of the power sector [3]. Ensuring the cost-e↵ectiveness and reliability
of this contribution requires continuous improvements in wind turbine eﬃciency and
durability. A critical factor limiting turbine lifetime and performance is the progressive
damage of blade surfaces, in particular leading edge erosion (LEE) [4, 5]. It is the main
focus of IEA Task 46. [6]
1.2
Leading edge erosion
Modern wind turbine blades operate with tip speeds approaching 80 to 100 m/s, and
this value is expected to rise further as turbines increase in size [7].
At such high
velocities, rain droplets, dust, or sand particles generate high impact stresses at the
leading edge. These high stresses can form micro-cracks that can coalesce and lead to
material loss, denoted leading edge erosion.(see Figure 1.1) The composite materials
forming the structural core of the blades are particularly sensitive to these solicitations.
To mitigate erosion, a specialised leading edge protection (LEP) coating is typically
applied, consisting of materials with higher resistance to droplet impact [8, 5].
1
==PAGE:12==
Figure 1.1: a. Leading edge after di↵erent years in service, b. positioning of LEP
material,[9]
Despite the use of protective coatings, erosion of the leading edge progresses over time.
This degradation reduces aerodynamic performance and, in severe cases, may extend to
the structural laminate, leading to critical failures [4]. LEP coatings typically require
replacement every 5–7 years, while turbine lifetimes range from 20 to 30 years [10]. Such
maintenance operations are particularly costly for o↵shore installations, motivating the
need for predictive tools capable of estimating LEP lifetime and guiding maintenance
planning. In addition, accelerated understanding of erosion mechanisms supports the
development of new protective materials with extended durability.
1.3
Leading edge erosion testing
To characterise the erosion resistance of candidate materials, dedicated rain erosion rigs
have been developed (Figure 1.2)[7][5]. A rain erosion testing facility (RET) includes
a rotating rig where sample blades are subjected to controlled droplet impacts from a
nozzle system. The test conﬁguration allows control of impact velocity, droplet size,
frequency, ambient temperature, and humidity. By monitoring the area of the eroded
surface from images, one can estimate mass loss and surface degradation. The sedi-
ment analysis also give an idea of plastic pollution induced by the wind turbine blade
erosion. Knowing the amount of damage can be used to design the tip speed to an
acceptable erosion level. Although representative, these tests are time-consuming and
resource-intensive, as suﬃcient exposure is required to initiate and propagate erosion
damage. Replicability often demands multiple tests, increasing both cost and duration.
Consequently, complementary approaches are required to predict material lifetime with-
out relying exclusively on long-term experiments. Moreover, the RET results are not
directly measuring material properties, hence the need for a complementary approach.

1.4
Leading edge protection material
The development of LEP materials has evolved from rigid coatings to softer viscoelastic
polymers designed to deform under droplet impact and dissipate energy without under-
going permanent damage [7, 5]. These modern coatings exhibit pronounced viscoelastic
and hyperelastic behaviour, making their characterisation and modelling complex [10].
Previous investigations have highlighted strong dependencies on strain rate and tem-
perature, which inﬂuence erosion performance through time–temperature superposition
e↵ects [4].
For practical modelling, simpliﬁed constitutive laws such as linear elasticity are some-
times employed, providing analytical tractability while introducing quantiﬁable errors.
Evaluating these discrepancies is essential for assessing the predictive power of simpli-
ﬁed models compared to more advanced viscoelastic descriptions.
1.5
Idea of using an energy based approach
Observations of erosion patterns suggest that crack initiation and propagation are dom-
inant mechanisms in rain erosion of LEP materials [5, 4]. This motivates the use of
fracture mechanics concepts to quantify the energy required to propagate damage. If
a threshold energy release rate can be identiﬁed, it may serve as a predictive criterion
for erosion onset and progression.
1.6
Objectives of this thesis
The objective of this thesis is to establish a methodology for the measurements of
fracture mechanics parameters that govern fracture in LEP materials based on exper-
imental measurements. Both crack growth under static and cyclic loading should be
3
==PAGE:14==
characterised. However in this work, the initial focus is placed on static crack growth
tests, enabling the development and validation of the method. Once established, the
methodology can be extended to cyclic loading conditions, which are more represen-
tative of real in-service rain erosion. As mentionned, the material used for LEP are
hyperelastic and viscoelastic, making the modelisation more complex, but also the test-
ing procedure. Testing thin layer of material at very high strain induce diﬃculty to
clamp and ensure proper boundary condition. The theoretical tools for fracture me-
chanics are often not covering the blunting e↵ect we observe around the crack. The
study develop two di↵erent numerical procedure to extract from experiment two di↵er-
ent quantities : the crack growth, and the strain energy release rate.
In the present study, the following research question will be investigated :
As a starting point, what design starting point of the work will be the design of a
dedicated ﬁxture to ensure repeatable testing. Then we will investigate the criteria
that separate the stable crack growth from unstable mode. Identify the part of the
energy that is stored in the blunted area around the crack.

Structure of the report

Chapter 2
Background Research
This chapter provides some deﬁnition and concepts from Fracture Mechanics, and a
brief literature review of testing methods. Then we focus on the theoretical problem
as a starting point for experimental testing.
2.1
Facture mechanics fundamentals
The energetic approach for fracture mechanics was ﬁrst introduced by Griﬃth [12]. He
states that in a loaded linear elastic material with a crack, the crack propagates only
if the amount of energy released during a crack extension is higher or equal than the
energy absorbed by the fracture processed zone at the crack tip. Additionally, the crack
is stable unless the amount of energy released by the crack opening exceed the energy
absorbed by the surface creation [13]. The released energy is called the potential energy
release rate G in J/m2, and is the variation of potential energy (⇧) per crack surface
(A) change :
G = −@⇧
@A
(2.1)
Crack growth will thus happen when energy release rate G equals or exceeds a critical
value denoted Gc, which correspond to the work per unit area of the fracture process
zone. This energy approach can be connected by the stress-based approach with the
critical value of the stress intensity factor KIC which helps to understand how the
stress intensiﬁed on the tip of the crack. The crack propagates when the stress reach
the elastic limit of the material.

2.2
J-integral
2.2.1
Motivation for supplementing the approach
For many engineering polymers, however, inelastic deformation (nonlinear elasticity,
yielding, large-scale plasticity, viscoelasticity) near the crack tip contributes signiﬁ-
cantly to the energetics of fracture. Consequently, the assumptions underlying Grif-
ﬁth—linear elasticity and purely brittle cleavage—are no longer valid, and a fracture
parameter that remains meaningful in the presence of nonlinear material behaviour is
required. For this reason the J-integral was introduced as an energetic fracture pa-
rameter that (under broad conditions) extends the energy-release concept to nonlinear
elastic and elastic–plastic materials.[14, 15]

2.2.3
Path independence and physical meaning
Using the divergence theorem and equilibrium equations, Rice showed that J is path-
independent for (i) homogeneous materials and (ii) when there is no body force and no
singular sources inside the region enclosed by Γ, and (iii) when the loading is mono-
tonic and deformation near the crack tip remains proportional (i.e. no non-proportional
plastic unloading). Path independence implies that J evaluated on a convenient ex-
ternal contour (or from global load–displacement data) equals the energetic quantity
characterizing the crack tip ﬁelds (the internal value) — so the same J governs crack
advance. [14] On the Figure 2.2 the value of J calculated along red (internal) and blue
(external) path are equal. The part in light red at the crack tip is the fracture process
zone (FPZ). The FPZ is deﬁned as the region near a crack tip where inelastic processes,
such as plastic deformation and micro-cracking, occur.
Figure 2.2: Blunted crack, with two di↵erent path, internal (red) and external (blue),
and the fracture process zone (in light red)
However, according to Rice theory, only the part that is inside the fracture process zone
quantify the energy released by the crack opening. So the contribution of the blunting

phenomenon, i.e. the part of the red path that is outside the fracture process zone
must be deduced from the external Jext. Denoted JBlunting the integration of J along
the internal path, except for the fracture process zone, we have the equation with J
being the actual tearing energy of the material:
J = Jext −JBlunting
(2.4)
With this formulation, measuring the fracture mechanics behaviour of the material
accounts for quantify the tearing energy J0 that correspond to the beginning of crack
growth, and the tearing energy Jc that correspond to the transition from stable to
unstable crack growth.

Physically, for quasi-static crack advance J represents the energy available per unit
crack advance (per unit thickness in plane problems), i.e. the driving force for crack
growth; for linear elastic materials it reduces to the classical strain energy release rate
G.
In the linear-elastic regime the J-integral is identical to the energy release rate G. For
Mode I plane-strain conditions the well-known relation is

2.3
Limitation of the theory
In our study, the polymers shows time-dependent (viscoelastic) and large-strain non-
linear behaviour. When signiﬁcant viscoelasticity is present, the assumptions guaran-
teeing strict path-independence and the simple partitioning may be violated; careful
experimental protocol and/or rate-matched ﬁnite element simulations are required. For
quasi-static, monotonic tests at a ﬁxed rate where plasticity (or large nonlinear elastic
strains) is dominant but proportional, the J-integral framework (and J-R testing pro-
cedures analogous to ASTM) is still commonly used, with appropriate material-speciﬁc
calibration[16].

2.4
Limitation of traditional testing method
In classical fracture mechanics, several standardized specimen geometries are widely
used to evaluate fracture toughness, such as the compact tension (CT), single-edge
notched bend (SENB), or double cantilever beam (DCB) tests [17, 18]. These meth-
ods are well established for metallic and composite materials under predominantly
linear-elastic or elastic–plastic conditions. However, their applicability to highly com-
pliant, viscoelastic polymers is limited. First, the load and displacement levels required
to propagate cracks in such soft materials are often incompatible with the sti↵ness
of CT or SENB geometries, leading to signiﬁcant compliance of the test setup itself
rather than controlled crack advance [19]. Second, the DCB conﬁguration, commonly
applied to adhesive joints and composites, assumes small rotations and linear-elastic
beam theory; these assumptions are strongly violated when large strains and nonlinear
viscoelastic deformation dominate the response [20]. Moreover, time-dependent e↵ects
(creep and stress relaxation) can complexify the interpretation of critical energy release
rates measured in standard protocols.

3.4
Crack length measurement
The test was recorded with a classic reﬂex camera, with characteristics listed in Ta-
ble 3.2 (frame rate, resolution, low iso, lot of light).
A Python-based image analysis script was developed to quantify crack progression
during testing section A.2. The procedure requires user input to initialize the tracking
process. Because the material under investigation is highly compliant, the crack tip
undergoes signiﬁcant blunting during loading (see Figure 3.8a), and its shape changes
markedly in the early stages. For this reason, image acquisition for analysis must begin
only once the crack tip reaches a stable geometry. Consequently, the ﬁrst frame used for
analysis occurs after the onset of specimen displacement. Prior to this state, the crack
does not propagate, although the edges of the specimen move inward as load is applied,
which can cause an apparent displacement of the crack tip before actual opening. By
deﬁning the initial frame at a stable conﬁguration, these e↵ects are accounted for and
do not inﬂuence the measurement.
The algorithm proceeds as follows:
• The contour of the specimen in the initial state (Figure 3.8a) is selected by the
user to establish the video scale in pixels per millimeter.
• The user identiﬁes the ﬁrst frame in which specimen motion occurs, enabling
alignment of the video with the mechanical test data.
18
==PAGE:29==
• The user speciﬁes the ﬁrst frame for analysis, as described above.
• Rectangular regions around each crack tip are deﬁned (Figure 3.8b).
• For each subsequent frame, the displacement of the selected regions is tracked
using the cv2.matchTemplate() function from the OpenCV library.
• Within each region, the following processing steps are applied to determine crack
tip position:
– Noise is reduced by gaussian ﬁltering (Figure 3.8c). The ﬁlter size must be
suﬃcient to suppress local artifacts while preserving visibility of the crack
contour, 31 pixels is consistent for our test.
– A thresholding operation separates the specimen from the background,
threshold of 128 for the RGB value were consistent for the present analysis,
but need to be change for analysing specimen with speckles(Figure 3.8d).
– The outermost black pixel in the crack region is identiﬁed as the crack tip
(rightmost for the left crack, leftmost for the right crack).
– The crack length is computed as the distance between this pixel and the
specimen’s left edge.

3.5
Image Acquisition Parameters for DIC
Accurate digital image correlation requires careful selection of the camera parameters,
since both spatial and temporal resolutions strongly inﬂuence the reliability of the
displacement and crack measurements. In the present study, the acquisition conditions
were optimized according to the following considerations:
1. Spatial resolution
• Camera resolution and distance: The camera was operated at its maximum
resolution, and positioned as close as possible to the specimen while still
capturing the full region of interest (ROI). This conﬁguration maximizes
pixel density on the sample surface and thus enhances spatial resolution.
For measurements focused on a single crack, a smaller ROI was selected to
20
==PAGE:31==
further improve the resolution.
• Camera angle: To minimize scale distortions in the images, the optical axis
of the camera was aligned as close as possible to the normal of the specimen
surface.
2. Temporal resolution
• Frame rate:
The fracture experiments are displacement-controlled, with
maximum crosshead speeds of up to 10 mm/s.
Complete failure occurs
in less than 5 s, and the unstable crack propagation phase lasts less than
0.2 s. At a frame rate of 30 fps, only about six images capture the unstable
propagation, which is the minimum acceptable number of data points for
crack-tip tracking. While this was suﬃcient for the present work, higher-
speed cameras would allow a more detailed description of the transient crack
dynamics.
• Shutter speed: To avoid motion blur during the rapid crack advance, short
exposure times are essential. Tests showed that too long exposures blurred
the crack tip, whereas suﬃciently fast shutter speeds enabled clear identiﬁ-
cation of the specimen edges. The selected value represented a compromise:
short enough to limit blur but long enough to ensure adequate light capture.
3. Image quality
• Lighting and ISO: Adequate illumination is critical to maintain a high signal-
to-noise ratio, especially when using short exposure times. High-intensity
lighting was therefore directed onto the specimen (see Figure 3.9), which
allowed the use of a very low ISO setting and thus minimized sensor noise.
• Aperture (f-stop): The lens aperture was ﬁxed by the optics used. With the
strong external lighting, this was suﬃcient to achieve the necessary depth of
ﬁeld and image contrast.
Overall, the chosen conﬁguration maximized spatial resolution by exploiting the full
sensor capability and minimizing optical distortions, while ensuring temporal resolution
was just suﬃcient to capture unstable crack growth. The combined control of lighting,
ISO, and shutter speed provided adequate image quality for subsequent DIC analysis.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This chapter critically evaluates the experimental methodology and the obtained re-
sults, with particular focus on the limitations of the testing protocol and the interpre-
tation of the fracture mechanics framework applied to viscoelastic polymers.
5.1
Inﬂuence of gripping
During testing, complete elimination of specimen slipping could not be achieved. Nev-
ertheless, modiﬁcations to the clamping setup reduced the e↵ect suﬃciently to produce
an overall uniform displacement ﬁeld in the zone between the cracks. For non-cracked
specimen, DIC analysis on Figure 4.3 and Figure 4.4 shows that the the y-displacement
is almost uniform, except for the edges of the specimen and for a small region in the
middle. The inﬂuence of the edge is not surprising, as long as the plain strain condition
(no strain in x direction) is no longer valid. For the middle part inﬂuence, the main
hypothesis is that the grip surface that is in contact with the sample is not perfectly ﬂat
at this spot, because there is a joint between two metal pieces. For further experiment,
making this part from a single metal piece will be recommended.
For specimen with a crack (see Figure 4.1), we can measure on the image the level of
displacement and discompose the par that is due to slipping from the part that belongs
to deformation of the material. With a global displacement 31mm (210%elongation),
the real elongation of the part between the colored lines is 15,5mm, equal to 55%
elongation. For the other specimen with the extra polymer piece, elongation of the
specimen is 25mm, equal 150% elongation. These value are very dependent to the test,
and to the initial length of the specimen, which cannot be choose exactly because of the
the regions of the specimen outside the cracks exhibited negligible y-strains, indicating
minimal slipping in these zones . This observation raises concerns for the J-integral
45
==PAGE:56==
evaluation, since the integration path Γ includes the outer stress-free zones, and thus
the transition between stress free and plain strain zone, where the displacement value
changes.
5.2
Boundary e↵ects
As mentioned, the slipping phenomenon is a concern for the condition of uniform dis-
placement. This will have an inﬂuence on the J-integral value, as the Equation 2.10
is no long valid. However, calculation from experiments shows that the contribution
of this path on the remaining integral was very little compared to the other paths.
Previous work [22] already calculated the condition needed for the initial crack length
with respect to the specimen heigth. The condition a > H is needed to avoid inﬂuence
of side edge on crack zone deformation. This condition was not achieved on some ex-
periment showed in this study. But it was for the experiment used for the J-integral
calculation. This condition can be compared to the measurement of X displacement
showed in Figure 4.12. There is almost no displacement in X direction, in the middle,
and up to 3 mm of displacement on the the curve extrema, which is a few millime-
ter from the edges. The middle part with no lateral displacement correspond to the
plain strain area. The limit of plain strain area can be estimated from the curve to be
approximately 35mm. This value is the same for all value of global strain.
5.3
DIC performance at high strain levels
At the high strain levels observed in this study, the applied speckle pattern underwent
severe distortion. This results in a consistent drop on the pattern quality calculated by
the software, from 3.0 to 1.5. This may impact badly the precision of the calculation
made from the DIC data. The initial speckle size of approximately 0.3 mm increased up
to 1 mm in the y-direction while remaining unchanged in the x-direction (Figure 4.13).
Such anisotropic deformation challenges the standard correlation algorithms, which
typically rely on isotropic subsets.
A potential improvement would be the use of adaptive correlation windows, as suggested
in high-strain DIC studies [23]. Alternatively, non-square correlation window or pattern
redesign (e.g., with ﬁner or multi-scale speckles) may improve tracking reliability under
extreme deformations.

5.4
First results
The simple loading tests conduced in ?? give quantitative results of the material re-
sponse. The stress–strain response of the tested viscoelastic polymer exhibits a clear
non-linear character, with the curve Figure 4.5 showing two distinct regimes that can
be approximated as quasi-linear. The initial region corresponds to small strains, where
molecular chains undergo elastic stretching and rearrangement, allowing an estimation
of the apparent Young’s modulus. It is worth noting that the very ﬁrst data points
display a slight creep-like deviation, which is attributed to the specimen not being
perfectly straight or fully engaged at the onset of loading, rather than to the intrin-
sic material behavior. At higher strains, the slope decreases, reﬂecting the onset of
viscous ﬂow and time-dependent relaxation processes typical of viscoelastic materials.
This two-stage response is strongly rate-dependent: at a loading rate of 1 mm/s, the
apparent modulus is about 7.6 MPa in the initial 0–0.2 strain range and decreases to
3.13 MPa when extended up to 200% strain; in contrast, at a faster loading rate of
10 mm/s, the initial modulus increases to 9.18 MPa, while the longer-range modulus
remains nearly unchanged at 3.30 MPa.
These results conﬁrm that the early elas-
tic response sti↵ens at higher strain rates, whereas the large-strain viscous-dominated
regime is relatively less sensitive to rate. In addition, the area under the stress–strain
curve up to fracture allows an estimation of the strain energy density. This combina-
tion of rate sensitivity, staged sti↵ness, and measurable fracture energy highlights the
complex mechanical behavior of viscoelastic polymers and their dependence on both
microstructural relaxation mechanisms and testing conditions.
5.5
Crack growth regimes
The load–displacement and crack length–time curves (Figure 4.8) reveal two distinct
regimes of crack propagation. An initial slow opening phase (where crack tip speed
is around ⇠10mm/s) is followed by a faster propagation regime (very constant speed
⇠600mm/s), with the transition load showing good reproducibility across tests. Such
a two-stage behaviour is consistent with fracture observations in elastomeric and vis-
coelastic polymers reported in the literature [24].
Interestingly, the onset of rapid
growth was not always simultaneous for the two cracks in the specimen. This is pos-
sibly due to asymmetries in the boundary conditions or local stress loading.
The
heterogeneity of the material can also induce irregularities in crack growth.

5.6
Discrepancies between theoretical framework and ex-
periment
The theoretical simpliﬁcation of the J-integral described in chapter 2 is based on sev-
eral simplifying assumptions that are not fully satisﬁed in practice. In particular, the
paths Γ1 and Γ5 are assumed to be located in stress-free regions, suﬃciently far from
both the crack tip and the specimen side edges. However, in our analysis, we took into
account. In reality, the proximity of the crack to the specimen boundaries introduces
non-negligible stresses that violate this condition.
Similarly, the vertical paths Γ2 and Γ4 are assumed to coincide with the clamped
edges to ensure uniform displacements. However, once slipping occurs above the crack
tip, the displacement ﬁeld becomes non-uniform, leading to some contributions to the
J-integral. Even if slipping could be completely eliminated in an experiment, this con-
dition alone would not guarantee that the contribution of the vertical paths could be
neglected. Since the DIC correlation requires a square subset of approximately 40 pix-
els per side, each path must remain in a margin of at least 20 pixels away from the
grips in order to lie entirely within the surface component.
?? shows that the material behaviour deviate consistently from linear elastic model.
As this model is practically used for J-integral, it induce some errors in the real val-
ues. Including the hyper-elastic behaviour will require to measure experimentally the
response of the material, including bi-axial loading. Considering the rate-dependency
will also request to have a good model of the material response. In this study, all test
where conduced at a single global displacement rate. During the experiments, the slow
relaxation observed of the specimen parts after complete failure are typical visco-elastic
e↵ects.
5.7
Precision of J-integral measurements
The accuracy of J-integral values in this study is a↵ected by several factors, including
speckle pattern distortion, slipping at the grips, path selection, and image processing
artifacts. Each of these sources of uncertainty can introduce systematic bias, which
should be quantiﬁed in future work by sensitivity studies and repeatability tests. Mak-
ing comparison of the tearing energy measurement with analytical or Finite Element
solution would also be relevant.
According to the theory, the measurement of J on a close contour should equal 0. Com-
paring the measured value on Figure 4.15 with the vertical path is relevant to state if
the the whole contour measurement is close enough to zero. The latter value give an
order of magnitude of the energy level in the deformed state. Figure 4.17 show errors
in the order of 3%, with no clear variations with respect to the strain level.

5.8
J-integral measurements for a crack
One ﬁrst estimation of the tearing energy is based on the global displacement and
calculated with Equation 2.13. The results are probably overestimating the real ma-
terial parameters. As this method assume uniform displacement, the such calculated
displacement-based strain are higher than the local strain in the material, because of
the material slipping. The e↵ect of blunting was not taken into account either in this
calculation.
On the other hand a more precise evaluation of the fracture mechanics parameters
has been made with digital image correlation and local measurement of J. This result
reveal as well with a very big order of magnitude in di↵erence between the energies
required for crack onset and for unstable transition. The value of J0 from Table 4.1
seems very low compared to what we generally get for this kind of material. One reason
could be that the identiﬁcation of crack onset is very diﬃcult on the image, and and
be mistaken with the plastic deformation occurring around the crack tip. In this case,
the onset of growth is happening later, and with higher tearing energy. One other
reason could be the inﬂuence of the crack sharpness. We cut the cracks with a pair
of scissors which leave the crack not very sharp. The contribution of blunting will be
the di↵erence between the two curves on Figure 4.19, and represent more than 90% of
the energy measured with the external contour for the crack growth threshold J0, and
more than 75% of it for the stability threshold Jc. This very high contribution means
that the blunting e↵ect is to be taken seriously when realizing measurement of fracture
mechanics parameters. However, the calculation method, and particularly the selection
of the boundary of the fracture process zone is something the measurement could be
very sensitive to. Comparing these results over several experiment and quantify the
inﬂuence of curves choice is mandatory to quantify precisely this e↵ect.
5.9
Blunting contribution
A signiﬁcant fraction of the energy measured by the external J-integral is expected
to arise from the strain energy stored in the blunted crack tip region very visible in
(Figure 4.7d). This e↵ect, widely reported in elastomer fracture mechanics [25], compli-
cates the separation between crack-tip fracture energy and bulk viscoelastic dissipation.
Local path analysis shows that the contribution from the blunted area can not easily
be di↵erentiate from the fracture process zone contribution, which may limit direct
comparison with Jext values. Measuring and deducing the contribution of the blunted
area should be a way to isolate the real Fracture process zone absorbed energy.

5.10
Additional sources of error
Finally, additional error sources must be acknowledged. These include uncertainties in
manual contour deﬁnition for image analysis, variations in lighting and focus during
video acquisition, and possible misalignment in the initial crack geometry. Moreover,
the software use for DIC is a professional complex tool, and uses complex ﬁlter and
interpolation to perform the correlation, especially in the fracture process zone, where
data is incomplete and high strains make the correlation harder. While individually
small, these factors may collectively a↵ect the reproducibility of crack length measure-
ments and subsequent J-integral calculations.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Conclusions
This work addressed the characterization of fracture mechanics in viscoelastic poly-
mer materials, where conventional frameworks developed for metals and brittle solids
encounter fundamental limitations.
To overcome these challenges, a dedicated experimental methodology was implemented,
combining high-resolution video analysis with custom Python tools to extract crack
tip evolution and opening rates.
This approach enabled precise tracking of crack
growth under controlled loading, providing data that could be directly compared with
literature and used for calibration of viscoelastic ﬁnite element models. The mechanical
design aspects of the test rig and specimen ﬁxtures, ensured a decent alignment,
repeatability, and scalability of the setup. Finally, this approach was complemented by
the development of a DIC-based measurement procedure to experimentally determine
the J-integral from the strain ﬁelds on the specimen surface. Subsequent analyses of
the results allowed us to identify which regions of the strain ﬁeld must be considered
in the calculation.
The experimental tests conducted allows for a precise quantiﬁcation of the transition to
unstable crack growth. However, deﬁning precisely the onset of stable growth is more
diﬃcult because of a very small initial growth rate. This makes the estimation of J0
very sensitive to the experiment choices. Moreover, we were able to directly quantify
the contribution of the e↵ect of blunting, with up to 90% contribution for the threshold
determination.
51
==PAGE:62==
Together, these contributions provide a coherent framework for advancing fracture char-
acterization of viscoelastic polymers.
Future work will focus on extending the methodology to dynamic and cyclic loading
regimes, where time-dependent e↵ects are expected to play an even greater role. Pur-
suing the test campaign to provide a quantiﬁcation of the error sources inﬂuence, and
a repeatability proof.
