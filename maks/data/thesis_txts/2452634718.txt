Metaheuristics for an extension of the vehicle routing problem
by Christoph KOPPMANN
This report investigates the delivery of goods to customers in urban districts over a two week
planning horizon, while complying with time window preferences, different customer types,
lunch breaks and workload balance. The purpose is to review how existing literature suggests
to solve related problems and analyze how the delivery schedules of a case company should be
optimized using metaheuristic. An adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) was proposed
and extended to include simulated annealing and local search for further optimization. Com-
putational experiments indicated that total travel time could be reduced with more than 20%
compared to real life practices by the case company, while optimal and near optimal solutions
were identiﬁed. Furthermore, it was discovered that workload balancing could be achieved with-
out much sacriﬁce of total travel time. Also, idle time is beneﬁcial to obtain promising solutions.
Moreover, analyses of the characteristics of the ALNS illustrated that especially the worst re-
moval operator and regret insertion operator contributed signiﬁcantly to improve solutions. In
addition, the simulated annealing heuristic and a hill climber improved solution quality fur-
ther. Improvements which reduce operational time, fuel consumption and emissions. Hence
it is believed that the ALNS is a strong metaheuristic to beneﬁt both companies, society and
environment on a variety of related vehicle routing problems.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The optimization process of Vehicle Routing Problems (VRP) becomes ever more im-
portant in an increasingly interconnected and globalized world. In the latest issue of
the World Trade Statistical Review 2018 it is revealed that global trade volume has
increased by 4.7%. A pace which has not been seen since 2011. Setting this into per-
spective with the global export volume of around US$ 23 trillion, optimizing routes in
order to save fuel and time shrinks operating costs for any logistic company [WTO,
2018]. Adding to this comes the transportation of people which accounts for further
cost in terms of fuel consumption and time. Besides the great cost savings which arise
by optimizing vehicle routes, other maybe much more important aspects are environ-
mental and health. At a time where those with responsibility need to take action in
order to cut waste and pollution, ﬁght climate change and help to build a sustainable
planet far further than their own lifetime. But ﬁghting climate change to preserve the
planet while reducing cost by optimizing transportation routes should not be the only
motivation for government and companies. The most harmful pollutants are ﬁne par-
ticulate matter (PM). In urban areas the ambient air pollution (AAP) from PM, only
contributed by trafﬁc represents 25% according to [Karagulian et al., 2015]. Another
study found AAP to cause 3.2 million premature deaths accounting for around 6% of
all deaths worldwide. This comes with a cost of around US$ 5.1 trillion in 2015 [Roy
and Braathen, 2017]. With both the massive economical, environmental and health
factors in mind, optimizing vehicle routes is crucial and should be addressed by both
government and private companies, regardless of their size.
With the latter described mindset and desire to help both individual companies and
society in general the following thesis is developed. In short, an unique extension of
the VRP is researched, optimized and analyzed by various mathematical optimization
methods generally known in the ﬁeld of operations research. The extension is based
on a real life problem provided by a company reﬁlling vending machines. The name
of the company is kept anonymous throughout this report and refereed to as the case
company and its case problem. Thus the following research question is tried to be
answered:
"How does existing literature analyze problems related to the extended VRP introduced by the
case company and how can their delivery schedules be optimized using metaheuristic?"

1.1
Outline of thesis
In the following a short outline of the thesis is given to make the reader comfortable
with the structure and align expectations.
In the latter section a short appetizer and explanation on why this topic is relevant
for everyone is explained. Next an in depth problem description is given to lay the
foundation for the subsequent analysis. Based on the problem description a compre-
hensive literature review is carried out in order to reveal how similar problems are
solved, while providing valuable input to the construction of the mathematical mod-
els. Drawing from the learning’s in the literature review the methodology and methods
implemented are visualized. First a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) formulation is
described. This is afterwards extended by an Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search
(ALNS) algorithm in order to achieve better solutions within reasonable time. The
ALNS contains in short the following; a heuristic providing an initial solution, various
destroy & repair operators to improve the initial solution, an adaptive part choosing
which operators to use, a simulated annealing approach for greater search-space and
a local search for further optimization. Also the relevance and implementation of soft
& hard constraints and delta evaluation is given. Following the implementations of
the MIP & ALNS, their performance is tested, tuned, analyzed and compared by using
both artiﬁcial instances and real life case instances from the case company. Finally, key
takeaways and results are discussed and recommendations given based on the ﬁnd-
ings.
1.2
Problem description
The problem description outlines the case problem analyzed in this thesis. Its purpose
is to give a thorough idea of the framework, its restrictions, parameters and decision
variables to be addressed.
In its essence the case company faces a logistical problem. Namely which customers
to serve on which days and in which sequence, i.e. which routes should be driven
by a vehicle to serve all customers. The customers in this case problem are vending
machines which need to be reﬁlled. The objective for the case company is to minimize
route duration’s, while workload balance is important to provide routing schedules
with roughly equally long travel times each day. Thus the idea is to both optimize
travel time and provide good working condition using workforce management.
The case speciﬁc framework is based on a two week interval, with operations only
during weekdays. Hence delivery is possible from Monday to Friday in the ﬁrst week
and Monday to Friday in the second week. This results in a schedule of possible 10
operational days in total as visualized in Figure (1.1).

Each of the days in the schedule above are operated by one vehicle, with its starting

Next the customers are described. In general two different types of customers exist in
the case framework. The ﬁrst type is called weekly customers and they are represented
by customer 3 & 4 in the ﬁgure above. The characteristic of weekly customers is that
they need to be served exactly once each week, i.e. twice in total. Furthermore are they
required to be served during the same weekday in both weeks. Since customer 3 &
4 are served on Monday in week 1 they also need to be served at some point during
Monday in week 2. The second customer type is denoted bi-weekly customers and
they are represented by customer 1, 2 & 5 in Figure (1.1). Bi-weekly customers must
be served exactly once during the entire 10 days scheduling period. All customers are
allocated to the schedule in a way such to optimize the objectives desired by the case
company; total travel time and workload balance. The above case example is only for
visualization purposes and hence not optimized to minimize travel time or similar.
In addition to the above described scheduling process of customers, some other restric-
tions are necessary to be considered before allocating customers to speciﬁc days and
times. Important are restrictions with regard to delivery time windows. Each customer
may have preferences for delivery days. The reason behind is that some vending ma-
chines are placed within buildings which might not be eligible during a certain day
because of closure. This is for example the case for customer 2 on Monday, why cus-
tomer 2 is scheduled to be delivered on Tuesday instead. Furthermore, customers may
have desirable delivery time windows during a day and deliveries outside those win-
dows are not possible. This is the case for customer 4 in Figure (1.1). On Monday

workload balance while respecting the mentioned restrictions. Minimizing time indi-
rectly correlates to minimize distance as well. How this has been addressed and solved
in similar problems is analyzed during literature review in the next chapter. The ﬁnd-
ings then support Section 3 in order to construct a mathematical formulation for the
VRP case problem.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
In the following section a comprehensive literature review of existing contributions
related to the topic of the case problem analyzed in this thesis is given. Partly based
on these ﬁndings a construction of a mathematical model is then implemented later.
Furthermore it gives an understanding of other possible approaches.
In short, this literature review covers an assessment of vehicle routing problems with
partly similarities to the case problem. Based on this assessment different solution
approaches are reviewed, ranging from mixed integer programming (MIP) to differ-
ent heuristics and decomposition. Based on these ﬁndings a separate section for the
ALNS is reviewed since the literature suggest this algorithm to provide promising re-
sults. Furthermore a review of how multi-objective optimization can be used to achieve
trade-offs between different objectives is formulated. Lastly, a brief overview of the
most important and interesting ﬁndings in literature is given.
2.1
VRP’s related to the case problem, their characteris-
tics and applications
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) was ﬁrst introduced in a paper by [Dantzig and
Ramser, 1959] as a truck dispatching problem with the purpose to determine routes for
trucks between bulk terminals and service stations in the petrol industry. In general the
purpose of the VRP is to solve problems involving the visit of customers by vehicles
while often minimizing the total travel distance or cost [Christoﬁdes, 1976]. The VRP
originates from the Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP), with the main difference that
the TSP contains a single vehicle visiting all customers while the VRP might contain
multiple vehicles collaborating on visiting all customers.
Over the years many different variants of the VRP and TSP have emerged. In the
following three main problem variants associated with the problem discussed in this
paper are presented. The review and analysis of the articles cited should give an un-
derstanding of how to approach the case problem described in Section 1.2. The most
relevant problems reviewed are respectively concerning the Periodic Vehicle Routing
Problem (PVRP), the Consistency Vehicle Routing Problem (ConVRP) and the Vehicle
Routing Problem with Time Windows (VRPTW).
The PVRP was ﬁrst introduced by [Beltrami and Bodin, 1974] and is greatly related to
the problem discussed in this report. The periodic vehicle routing problem consists of
==PAGE:22==
6

a set of customers who require visits on one or more days during the planning hori-
zon. Once customers are assigned to a feasible visit option a VRP is solved for each day
in the planning period, with the objective to minimize total travel cost [Wilson, 2014]
[Coene et al., 2010]. The ﬁrst mathematical formulation for the problem is provided by
[Christoﬁdes and Beasley, 1984]. The formulation is generalized such that customers
are served k times during the planning period with a set of days on which they are
allowed to be visited. This is related to the problem described in this report, where a
set of feasible delivery days for weekly customers would be Monday week 1 and Mon-
day week 2, i.e. day 1 and day 1+5 or day 2 and day 2+5 and so forth. The usage of
PVRP has already early on been of great importance in real life applications. The ﬁrst
appearances, among others, were in the delivery of soft drinks to stores [Golden and
Wasil, 1987]. Other applications are the replenishment of inventory in grocery stores
in general. Trucks must satisfy the delivery to stores from a central distribution cen-
ter, with each store being replenished several times during a week and equal delivery
times for a store each day during the week [Gaur and Fisher, 2004].
However, contrary to the case problem described in this report the formulation of the
latter contains multiple vehicles with limited capacity and also no time windows. This
is different in the periodic delivery problem for vending-machine supply chains, which
is investigated by the periodic vehicle routing problem with time windows (PVRPTW)
[Rusdiansyah and Tsao, 2005]. An approach where both tour duration and time win-
dow constraints are implemented and customers are visited frequently with deﬁned
customer to day allocation combinations. However, also this problem deals with mul-
tiple vehicles and takes inventory cost into account as well. An interesting aspect if
balancing inventory and travel cost is needed.
The aspect of consistent delivery times for frequently visited customers as in the PVRP
is also utilized by the Consistent Vehicle Routing Problem (ConVRP), which was pro-
posed and deﬁned by [Groër et al., 2009]. The ConVRP concerns the identiﬁcation of
a set of optimal routes, a number of vehicles should utilize to visit customers, during
multiple time periods of a planning horizon. The consistency takes place because cus-
tomers require consistent service. If a customer requires service more than once during
the planning horizon, the difference between the earliest and latest vehicle arrival time
across all planning periods, must be within a given threshold in order to obtain service
consistency [Subramanyam and Gounaris, 2016]. Especially the providence of consis-
tent service to regular customers in order to achieve high customer satisfaction is an
attribute which in recent years has become essential. Other consistency considerations
for related problems are further discussed extensively by [Kovacs et al., 2014a]. Even
though the problem described in this report serves machines and service consistency
might appear less relevant, it is important to comply with the time windows given by
some customers. Since the time windows are consistent on a week to week basis sim-
ilarities can be drawn to the ConVRP. Other applications of great usefulness using the
ConVRP are industries concerning delivery and pickup services, where delivery and
pickup times have large inﬂuence on the performance of their customers and hence
their service satisfaction [Wong, 2008].
Deriving from the latter, another interesting approach worth reviewing is the vehicle
routing problem with time windows (VRPTW) as analyzed by [Bräysy and Gendreau,
2005]. As indicated earlier, the VRPTW, in contrary to the ConVRP, is not restricted

For purely VRPTW important application areas are deliveries to supermarkets, school

its uniqueness by combining many different aspects reviewed in literature into one
problem. Furthermore, customized features as lunch breaks and workload balance are
implemented. Because of the similarity to the previous described problems it is worth
analyzing the different solution approaches in literature in the next section.
2.2
Solution approaches for PVRP, ConVRP and VRPTW
The latter section gave an idea of why the PVRP, ConVRP and VRPTW are great start-
ing points to review and assess suitable solution approaches for the case problem de-
scribed in this report.
The ﬁrst approaches reviewed in literature are concering the use of integer program-
ming, as often in operational research problems. [Subramanyam and Gounaris, 2016]
suggests to solve the consistent travelling salesmen problem (ConTSP) by using vari-
ous MIP formulations. They are strengthened by valid inequalities to make the branch-
and-cut framework faster and reach provable optimality sooner by tightening the lin-
ear programming (LP) bound. Using this method instances with up to 50 customers
who require service over a ﬁve period planning horizon are guaranteed to be solved to
optimality.
However, many vehicle routing problems are NP-hard (non-deterministic polynomial-
time) and hence cannot be solved within polynomial time, meaning time increases ex-
ponential with the problem size [Lenstra and Kan, 1981]. This results in the search
for algorithms able to solve larger instances. Thus another paper suggests to solved
the ConVRP by formulating a MIP and implementing a record-to-record (RTR) travel
algorithm in order to solve very large-scale VRP’s [Lia et al., 2005]. The concept of
RTR is to create a set of template routes which can be used to build feasible routes for
each day by applying removal and insertion methods. This algorithm shows success-
ful customer service objectives with low total travel time, even when solving data sets
involving more than 1000 customers. Also, the study suggests that the objective cost
only increases by a few percentage while providing sufﬁcient service consistency to-
wards customers. A discovery which also has been proved by other researchers, when
vehicle capacity is not signiﬁcantly limiting the amount of customers possible to be
served from one vehicle [Luo et al., 2015] [Feillet et al., 2014]. However, disregarding
time window preferences from customers decreases the total travel cost signiﬁcantly
according to [Kovacs et al., 2015a].
==PAGE:24==
8
Chapter 2. Literature Review
Another study solved the ConVRP using a template based decomposition algorithm
to assign customers to vehicle routes. From the templates actual routes are determined
and a tabu search heuristic is used to optimize both templates and actual routes further
[Tarantilis et al., 2012].
Yet another approach is to solve the ConVRP by using a template-based adaptive large
neighborhood search (TALNS), a combination of RTR and the implementation of Large
Neighborhood Search (LNS) [Kovacs et al., 2014b]. The idea is to use a pair of LNS de-
stroy and repair methods to optimize predetermined templates. A template is e.g. a
set of customers who are served more than once during a planning horizon. Destroy
methods used in the TALNS are e.g. worst removal and related removal. The former
removes the customer which contributes the most to the total travel time, while the
latter removes for example close geographical related customers. The idea behind the
related removal operator was ﬁrst proposed by [Shaw, 1997] & [Shaw, 1998] in order
to create opportunities of interchange and cluster close by customers in the routing
schedule. Hence this destroy method is often referred to as Shaw removal. As an in-
teresting repair method the regret heuristic is implemented, which is further explained
later. The major ﬁndings were that implying tight maximum arrival time differences
and the possibility of allowing a delay in departure times from the depot, did almost
not increase total travel time cost. Another interesting study, solving the ConTSP using
a decomposition algorithm, found that by allowing vehicles to idle (wait) at customer
locations before serving the next customer could decrease up to 40 % of the cost which
is incurred by providing service consistency [Subramanyam and Gounaris, 2018].
Similar to the ConVRP also time window problems are solved using MIP formulations
and optimized further by heuristics to solve larger instances. The Travelling Salesman
Problem with Time Windows (TSPTW) is solved using an Adaptive Neighborhood
Search (ANS) [Mesquita et al., 2013]. Thereby the objective value is further optimized
versus the use of a purely MIP approach. Even though the TSPTW only solves one
route, which reduces the solution space and complexity signiﬁcantly, it still indicates
the usefulness of neighborhood search heuristics when dealing with multiple time win-
dows for traveling salesman and vehicle routing problems.
Turning from ConVRP and VRPTW to PVRP maps out the extensive work of PVRP
applications. Many exact and heuristic approaches have been suggested. In general,
one of the more inﬂuential solution approaches for the PVRP is tabu search. This was
ﬁrst introduced by [Cordeau et al., 1997] and later modiﬁed to address the PVRPTW
speciﬁcally by [Cordeau et al., 2001]. The idea of tabu search is to escape a local optima
in which a normal local search heuristic most certainly will be trapped. Thus does tabu
search accepts moves which are worse than the current global best solution and forces a
local search as e.g. the hill climber to descend. By descending new parts of the solution
space can be searched and hence new global best solutions found. Furthermore are
moves previous visited stored in memory, which prevents a revisit to those [M. and
JY., 2014]. Another method which produced signiﬁcant improvements of best-known
solutions is the variable neighborhood search (VNS) suggested by [Hemmelmayr et al.,
2009]. Similar to tabu search, also the VNS uses local search. When the local search
stagnates and no improvement to the current incumbent solution can be obtained, it
selects a new neighborhood in order to ﬁnd a new solution.
==PAGE:25==
2.3. Adaptive large neighborhood search
9
However, due to the size and complexity of many real life instances with a large num-
ber of customers, number of days and time constraints a natural choice is to develop an
ALNS. An approach taken by [Zajac, 2017] to solve the PVRP. The ALNS discussed in
the paper contains destroy and repair methods with the same concept as described ear-
lier in this section. Including random removal, worst removal, geographical oriented
removal (Shaw removal), greedy / cheapest repair and regret heuristics. The greedy
operator is among others used by [Pisinger and Ropke, 2007], [Ropke and Pisinger,
2006] & [Schrimpf et al., 2000] with the idea to insert customers which cause the low-
est increase in total travel cost. The idea of the regret operator is to consider several
insertion possibilities for each customer in a set of customers. The customer with the
greatest difference between the best and an alternative insertion possibility is chosen
ﬁrst. Thereby the loss’ of postponing these insertions to later iterations are minimized
[Trick, 1992]. Furthermore a local search procedure is implemented, which investigates
promising solutions to further improve the objective.
As the ConVRP, PVRP and VRP’s in general are solved efﬁciently by ALNS, also the
VRPTW is shown to be solved very promising using adaptive large neighborhood
search [Bent and Hentenryck, 2004] [Pisinger and Røpke, 2010]. Solving the VRPTW
by ALNS also uses destroy and repair methods similar to those described previously.
E.g. noticeable are worst & related removal, greedy & regret heuristics [Potvin and
Rousseau, 1993].
This section showed a fraction of the overwhelming amount of approaches to solve
the types of vehicle routing problems related to the one discussed in this report. First,
mathematical formulations as MIP were presented. These are important as a starting
point for many VRP’s. Followed by various heuristic methods as template based al-
gorithms, tabu search, ALNS, local search algorithms and ending with decomposition
approaches. Research has in the latest past especially given large neighborhood search
great attention. This metaheuristic embodies various other heuristics in its implemen-
tation and is proven to work very efﬁcient on VRP’s. Thus it deserves further attention
in the next section. Furthermore, many of the latter reviewed approaches does not
contain the randomness of different customer types as well as lunch break possibili-
ties and workload balance. Aspects which increases the complexity and give rise to
explore ALNS further.
2.3
Adaptive large neighborhood search
Besides the destroy and repair methods, which are described earlier, another important
and essential feature of the ALNS framework is the adaptive part of choosing between
multiple competing removal and repair heuristics. The framework is believed to be
more robust and able to adapt to various instance characteristics. This has been proved
successful on vehicle routing problems related to the ones described in the previous
section [Ropke and Pisinger, 2006]. The idea of the adaptive part is to keep track of
a score for each heuristic, measuring how the heuristic performs on a given instance.
The scores of the heuristics are updated given one of the following scenarios occur;
reward if new solution is global best, reward if new solution is better than current,
==PAGE:26==
10
Chapter 2. Literature Review
reward if new solution is accepted, "reward" if new solution is rejected. However, re-
ward is only applied if the current solution has not been seen before [Sacramento et al.,
2019]. By dividing the entire search procedure into segments of e.g. 100 iterations, the
probabilities for the heuristics to be chosen are updated accordingly to their scores at
iteration 100. Since a solution always depends on a removal and repair heuristic, both
must be updated with reward linked to the solution provided. It is shown, as time
passes, that the scores decreases, since more solutions have been searched already and
fewer improvements are made.
In addition to the ALNS framework it is suggested to extend it by a simulated anneal-
ing approach to solve large VRPTW instances [Pisinger and Ropke, 2007]. The idea
is to accept solutions which are worse than the current global best. The acceptance
occurs with a certain probability. This probability depends on a so called temperature
parameter. The aim is to ﬁnd new global best solutions by temporarily allowing worse
solutions. Initial this parameter forces a high probability of accepting worse solutions
in order to allow broad search within the solutions space. Eventually the temperature
decreases and worse solutions are only permitted with low probability.
2.4
Multi-objective optimization
Another important aspect of the case problem described in this report is the trade-
off between minimizing total travel time, distance and complying with workload bal-
ance.
This leads to the importance of multi-objective optimization.
An approach
which among others is addressed by [Kovacs et al., 2015a], by solving the Gener-
alized Consistent Vehicle Routing Problem (GenConVRP), a variant of the ConVRP
(non-generalized).
One of the main differences between the generalized and non-
generalized approach is how the consistency is implemented. While the non-generalized
problem restricts consistency by setting an arrival time difference which cannot be vio-
lated, the generalized problem assigns penalties in the objective function when arrival
time differences occur. The latter approach allows more solutions and hence is a less
restrictive model. This can be beneﬁcial in order to ﬁnd feasible solutions faster. Con-
trary to other literature, which use RTR travel algorithms, the GenConVRP is solved
using Large Neighborhood Search (LNS). This method should be more ﬂexible than
RTR because it operates on the entire routing plan, instead of predeﬁned template
routes.
Thereby the GenConVRP is slower ﬁnding good solutions, but eventually
might ﬁnd even better solutions than the RTR travel approach.
In addition to the GenConVRP the paper of [Kovacs et al., 2015b] suggests the multi-
objective generalized consistent vehicle routing problem (MOGenConVRP). This ap-
proach proves beneﬁcial when analyzing and ﬁnding the trade off between different
objective functions, as e.g. minimizing total travel cost and improving arrival time con-
sistency. By generating Pareto solutions it is shown that arrival time consistency could
be improved signiﬁcantly while total travel time only increased slightly. A similar ap-
proach is suggested by [Smilowitz et al., 2015] using multi-objective optimization for a
PVRP model. In order to balance workforce management (e.g. workload balance) and
travel cost it is of great interest to apply multi-objective optimization. Thereby success-
ful solutions without much sacriﬁce for neither travel cost or workforce management

3.3
ALNS framework
As known from Section 2 the limitations of the MIP model are the exponential grow-
ing run times with increase in customer size and the fact that no lunch break is ap-
plied. Hence this approach is not suitable for larger instances and only suitable for
benchmarking smaller instances. Thus a different approach is necessary to solve the
complex case problem, why the following presents the Adaptive Large Neighborhood
Search (ALNS) metaheuristic. First the framework is presented in this section to vi-
sualize the various methods included. Afterwards an extensive description for each
method is given in the subsequent sections.
Beginning with the framework, a visualization of the algorithm is shown in the ﬂowchart
in Figure 3.1. Although the pure ALNS does not include an initial construction heuris-
tic or a local search procedure, the framework visualized in the ﬂowchart below is
refereed to as the ALNS framework for the case problem going forward in this thesis.
The ﬁrst step of the algorithm is to read input data, corresponding to the data described
in the problem description and MIP formulation. Furthermore various parameters to
tune the algorithm are read. Next an initial solution (schedule) is made by a random-
ized greedy heuristic. Customers are added to an empty schedule as long as the con-
straints are complied. In case the constraints are violated, another insertion is tried. If
cycling occurs, i.e. no feasible insertions can be found, customers remain unscheduled.
As the cost of unscheduled customers is high, the algorithm seeks to omit the occasion
of unscheduled customers. Next the initial objective is calculated and printed together
with the initial solution.
Once an initial schedule is produced, the ALNS chooses which destroy & repair meth-
ods to use. Instead of checking validity of the constraints for the entire schedule only
the changed parts are analyzed by the delta evaluation. If constraints are complied a
new objective is calculated using the same principle as before, delta evaluation. If the
new objective is accepted by the probability function in the simulated annealing (SA)
method, either a new best solution is saved or an intermediate sub-optimal solution
kept. The sub-optimal solution is used to, hopefully, ﬁnd better global solutions as the
algorithm proceeds. Depending on the newly solution found the weight of the corre-
sponding destroy & repair method is updated. If there is time remaining the process is
repeated, else the current best schedule and objective is printed.
In order to optimize the solution further a local search hill climber is implemented.
Again delta evaluation is used, but contrary to the ALNS only solutions providing a
better objective are accepted. The local search continues until no more time is available,
which terminates the algorithm and prints the ﬁnal schedule and objective value.

3.4
Constraints and objective
In the following section the objective of the ALNS and the constraints limiting the
solution feasibility are described. Conscious that the objective and constraints of the
case problem already are reviewed in the MIP model, the following only presents the
constraints necessary to solve the problem using the ALNS framework. The constraints
necessary are divided into hard & soft constraints. The former must be complied with
at any time. The latter must be complied with as often as possible, depending on their
impact on the objective function. Each time the soft constraint is violated the objective
value will increase accordingly. The constraints are the following:
- Hard constraints:
1. Maximum Duration: The duration of a route cannot exceed the allowable
time limit where the vehicle needs to return to the depot (Pseudocode of
method is provided using delta evaluation in the next Section 3.5).
2. Bi-weekly vs. Weekly: A customer can only be delivered once a day. Bi-weekly
customers need delivery either week one or week two. Weekly customers
need delivery in both weeks on the same day, meaning with exactly ﬁve
days delivery interval.
3. Lunch Break: A break of 30 minutes must be respected between 11 a.m. and
13 p.m. on a day with deliveries (Pseudocode of method is provided using
delta evaluation in the next Section 3.5).
4. Time Windows: A customer can only be delivered within the time window
speciﬁed by the customer (Pseudocode of method is provided using delta
evaluation in the next Section 3.5).
- Soft constraints:
1. Workload Balance: Workload balance must be ensured as much as possible
throughout the ten days scheduling period. Each minute a day deviates
from the allowable workload balance threshold is penalized. The size of the
penalty is given by γ and analyzed during the result section. (Pseudocode
of the method is provided using delta evaluation in the next Section 3.5).
As discovered during literature review, a trade off between different objectives often is
important. Hence the ALNS, similar to the MIP model, optimizes both travel cost and
workload. The possibility of multi-objective optimization is enabled by the soft work-
load balance constraint. By not ﬁxing the workload balance, a broad solution space is
kept and the algorithm can explore a larger search space. This might enable the ALNS
to identify improved global best solutions. Furthermore, the objective is extended to
optimize idle time as well. Larger idle times might decrease the distance travelled, but
could lead to large travel times. Hence the idea is to identify appropriate trade offs us-
ing multi-objective optimization. The impact of both workload balance and idle time
in the objective is controlled by respectively the idle time penalty κ and the workload
balance penalty γ. They are used to determine interesting Pareto solutions in Section
4.4.

3.5
Delta evaluation
Based on the latter section which introduced the constraints and objective used in the
ALNS framework, the following describes how they are implemented using delta eval-
uation. Delta evaluation is crucial because the majority of time spend by a metaheuris-
tic is to calculate the change in the objective value and evaluate the various methods
and operations. Thus the idea is to only check constraints and calculate the change in
the objective on the parts of the schedule or solution which are changed by removing
or inserting customers, instead of analyzing the entire schedule from scratch each time.
By adding the change in the objective (delta) to the existing objective value, the new
objective value for the new solution is determined. Thereby a signiﬁcant amount of
fewer calculations are necessary and computational time can be saved. In the follow-
ing the delta evaluation is explained for the hard constraints ﬁrst and afterwards for
the soft constraint and objective function.
3.5.1
Maximum duration
The delta evaluation for this hard constraint calculates the travel time (distance + ser-
vice time + lunch break + idle time) and compares it with the latest possible return
time to the depot. If the return time is violated, false is returned and the move is
prohibited. The maximum duration constraint is used for both removal and inser-
tion heuristics since the new route duration must be determined. However, a removal
heuristic is not able to increase route length. The delta evaluation occurs by only de-
termining the change of removing/inserting a customer from/into a speciﬁc position
in the schedule, rather than recalculating the entire schedule from scratch. Thus if cus-
tomer three is inserted between customer one & two the delta difference is deﬁned by
∆= (distance[1][3] + distance[3][2]) −distance[1][2]. The delta for a removal opera-
tor is determined by subtract vice versa. Adding the delta difference to the previous
known route length results in a new route duration which can be compared to the
latest allowable return to depot time. This is visualized using pseudocode in Algo-
rithm 3.5.1, where ’positionIdx’ is the position in the schedule where a customer is to
be removed or inserted. However, this constraint can also be implemented into and
checked using the delta evaluation for the time window constraint and hence save

The idea of implementing a greedy heuristic is to obtain a good initial solution, in-

as possible inserted into the initial schedule reduced the time spent on inserting un-
scheduled customers during the destroy & repair heuristics. By further allowing ran-

Random Greedy Insertion
The greedy insertion method determines the two cheapest feasible insertion possibili-
ties, for the entire current schedule, among all unscheduled customers. In order to add
randomization to the initial solution only one of the two cheapest insertion possibili-
ties determined is inserted. This selection happens randomly. If the selected insertion
possibility is unfeasible, the corresponding customer is temporarily prohibited for se-
lection until a new greedy feasible solution is found. This validation process uses the
delta evaluation constraints presented in the previous Section 3.5. At some point no
customer might be able to be inserted into their cheapest insertion possibility due to
the hard constraints mentioned in Section 3.4. This would lead to cycling, but is pro-
hibited by instead terminating the random greedy insertion method and applying the
method described next.
Remaining Customer Insertion
Since the greedy heuristic, especially for larger instances, is not always able to ﬁnd a
feasible solution with all customers scheduled another approach is necessary. Thus
the customers remaining in the unscheduled customer list are tried to be inserted ac-
cordingly to Figure 3.2. For each of the remaining customers every possible insertion
is evaluated by temporarily placing the customer inside the already existing schedule.
Starting Monday week one at index one until the last index on Friday week two. As
soon as a feasible insertion possibility is determined the customer is placed, without
any consideration about the impact on the objective value. At some point there might
be one or multiple customers still remaining in the unscheduled customer list with no
feasible insertion possibility in the existing schedule. Each unscheduled customer is
penalized with a large value. Passing both schedule, objective and the unscheduled
customer list on to the heuristics in the adaptive large neighborhood search, the un-
scheduled customers will be inserted as soon as possible and the objective decreased
proportional.

Repair Heuristics
Random Insertion
The random insertion heuristic is similar to the random removal heuristic. The idea
is to be able to perform many iterations within a short time frame. Compared to the
other repair heuristics the method is rather simple and no further in depth elaboration
is provided here. For more information it is referred to the attached JavaScript or the
pseudocode for the random removal heuristic in Section 3.7.1. The main difference is
that customers are inserted instead of removed from the schedule.
Cheapest Insertion
The idea of the cheapest insertion heuristic is to determine the best position for a cus-
tomer within the schedule, leading to improved objective values. With the latter iden-
tiﬁcations less iterations should be needed to obtain promising solutions. However,
this approach is very time consuming since the best insertion possibility for each un-
scheduled customer is determined iteratively. Contrary to the random insertion a lot
fewer iterations are possible within the same time frame.
The construction of the cheapest insertion heuristic is a lot similar to the construction
of the worst removal heuristic, as the same constraints are validated and the determi-
nation of which and how customers are moved is related. Despite the latter and some
programming technical difference the main dissimilarities are visualized next.
Obviously customers are inserted instead of removed from the schedule. Hence cal-
culating the change in the objective occurs vice versa compared to the worst removal
heuristic. This is visualized in Figure 3.5. The total cost (distance and idle time) of
inserting customer 91 is determined as the difference between the cost before the in-
sertion (left illustration) and after the insertion (right illustration). Again, service time
is neglected while determining the cost since service time is constant regardless of
the position of a customer in the schedule. In the example below the longest day
duration has not changed and thus the workload balance penalty is calculated us-
ing delta evaluation only for the given day. Thereby the total cost change becomes
(6 + 7 −11) + (5 −15) = −8. The aim is to identify the customer with the lowest total
cost change. Once identiﬁed, the customer is added to the schedule if the time window
constraint is not violated. This process is repeated until the unscheduled customer list
is empty or no more customers can be allocated.
There is a trade-off between ﬁrst calculating the total cost change and then validating
the feasibility versus vice versa. Validating feasibility by checking the time window
constraints is time consuming. However, determining the total cost change is also time
consuming. The idea behind ﬁrst calculating the total cost change is to minimize the
number of times the time window constraint must be validated. If the current total
==PAGE:52==
36
Chapter 3. Methodology
cost change does not improve the overall cheapest total cost change known so far, the
time window constraint is not checked. This decreases computational time. Since it
is believed that the majority of removal or insertion moves are feasible, validating the
time window constraint ﬁrst and then calculating total cost change leads to an increase
in the number of calculations. Both the time window constraint and total cost change
would be assessed in the majority of moves.

Regret Insertion
In the following the regret insertion heuristic and the idea behind its implementation
is presented. Since this heuristic is very sophisticated an example is created and visu-
alized in Table 3.2. Four customers are about to be inserted. However, before deciding
which customer to insert ﬁrst, second, etc. their best and next best insertion possibility
is determined. This process is almost identical to ﬁnding the cheapest insertion, why
the framework used to ﬁnd the cheapest insertion possibility described in the previous
section is used. The insertion values for all four customers are calculated initially in
iteration one, as given in Table 3.2 below. Next the absolute difference between the
best and next best insertion is calculated. The idea is then to insert the customer with
the highest difference ﬁrst. Thereby the ﬁrst insertions might not be the cheapest, but
more expensive insertions at later stages are prevented. Example:
Cheapest customer ﬁrst: Insert customer 4 ﬁrst. Because of time window constraints the
cheapest possibility for customer 2 is not longer feasible, hence ﬁrst customer 1 & 3 are added
and ﬁnally the next best insertion for customer 2. This results in a total cost of −8 + 5 + 7 +
10 = 14.
Regret: Customer 2 with the highest difference in the ﬁrst iteration is inserted ﬁrst. Since this
insertion might inﬂuence the other insertion objectives, the schedule is recalculated. This results
in the values for the second iteration in Table 3.2. As seen, the cheapest possibility for customer 4
from the ﬁrst iteration is not longer feasible. Now customer 3 has the highest absolute difference
why customer 3 is inserted. Next the schedule should be recalculated again, but as no changes
occur this is not stated in Table 3.2. Hence the best insertion possibility in the second iteration
for both customer 1 & 4 are used. This results in a total cost of −2 + 7 + 5 + (−1) = 9.
It is seen that the regret heuristic provides a in total cheaper solution of 5, because it
prevents the expensive insertion of customer 2 at a later stage. The regret heuristic is
executed again until no customers remain or no feasible insertion can be found.

3.7.3
Specialized heuristics
Single Route Optimization
The purpose of the single route optimization heuristic is to destroy and repair cus-
tomers within a partial solution. The partial solution is limited by a single route (day)
and hence the customer to day allocation is predeﬁned. Thus a number of customers
are removed within a day and afterwards re-inserted. The number of customers re-
moved depends on the neighborhood size parameter nSize. The destroy method im-
plemented is based on the worst removal heuristic, while the repair method is based
on the cheapest insertion heuristic. Since only customers within a day are removed
and re-inserted, the destroy and removal methods are not as complicated as those de-
scribed previously. Thus it is not necessary to check whether a weekly or bi-weekly
customer is removed or inserted. If the single route heuristic does not provide a feasi-
ble solution, the last known feasible solution is restored.
The idea of using the single route optimization is to optimize the sequence of cus-
tomers served within a single day. Especially two beneﬁts are motivating the use of
this heuristic. First, travel time is minimized for a single day which minimizes the to-
tal objective. Second, decreasing route duration of the longest route beneﬁts the entire
schedule by providing better workload balance.
Besides using the single route optimization heuristic within the ALNS framework, it
can be used to provide benchmark results. The case company provides predeﬁned cus-
tomer to day allocations for existing routes. However, the sequence of customers vis-
ited still needs to be determined. This can performed by the day optimization heuristic.
Initially an empty schedule is created and based on the given customer to day alloca-
tion all customers are assigned to an unscheduled customer list. Since each customer
in the unscheduled customer list contributes with a large penalty to the total objec-
tive value, the single route optimization heuristic is programmed to insert customers
iteratively based on their cost.
Another approach implemented to optimize the sequence of customers served during
a single route or day is mixed integer programming. A MIP model minimizing the total

windows (TSPTW). With a known customer to day allocation the idea is to hopefully
ﬁnd optimal routes for each day. However, as instances increases the run times of the
MIP model increases exponentially. Hence this method is not suitable within the ALNS
framework, but can be used to determine single day routing schedules for benchmark
instances where fast run times are not essential. If desired, the MIP formulation solving
a single route, with predeﬁned customer to day allocations, can be found in Appendix
A.3. This is a simpliﬁcation of the MIP model presented previously in section 3.2.
Hill Climber
The idea of using a local search heuristic to further optimize the global best solution
provided by the ALNS was already discovered during literature review in Section 2.
This idea is also adapted to improve the performance of the ALNS by implementing a
hill climber. As the neighborhood of the hill climber is set to be very small, i.e. only one
customer is removed from the schedule, the heuristic is able to perform many iterations
within a short amount of time. Thus the hill climber is hopefully able to improve the
best ALNS objective by determining its local optima without increasing computational
time signiﬁcantly.

Since the aim is to ﬁnd a local optima, only improved solutions are accepted. The hill

Adaptive LNS
This section concerns the decision of which destroy & repair heuristics to choose in
each iteration of the LNS, inspired by the literature review in Section 2. The aim is to
choose the heuristics who perform best, which might be different for instances varying
in size and complexity. Furthermore, the choice of heuristics might depend on whether
to optimize distance, travel time, workload balance or a combination of them. Thereby
the metaheuristic becomes adaptive to changing circumstances. Hence the Adaptive
Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS).
The election of LNS heuristics occurs accordingly to a weight which is assigned to each
heuristic. To determine the weight of a heuristic the search procedure of the algorithm
is divided into segments. Each segment represents a number of iterations, e.g. 50,
given by the parameter λ. The weights are denoted by wh,s, which is the weight of
each heuristic h at segment s. The weight is recalculated at the end of each segment
using the following formula:

3.9
Simulated annealing
In order to broaden the solution search possibilities and prohibit the algorithm being
trapped in a solution from where it is difﬁcult to ﬁnd a new improved solution, the fol-
lowing introduces the use of simulated annealing. The principles behind are already
described in the literature review. However, as a brief recap, the idea is to temporary
allow worse solutions in order to discover new global best solutions. How this speciﬁ-
cally is used to support the ALNS framework is visualized using pseudocode provided
in Algorithm 3.9.1.
The probability of accepting a solution is calculated in line 3 in Algorithm 3.9.1 and
depends on two factors. First, the delta objective which is provided by the algorithm
shown in Section 3.5.6. Second, an external temperature parameter, T. The initial start
temperature is set such that a solution which is η % worse than the initial solution,
denoted z′, is accepted with a probability of 0.5. This is determined by Equation 3.21.
T = (z′ · η)
log(2)
(3.21)
The initial solution in the equation above does not include the penalty of unsched-
uled customers. The reason behind is that the unscheduled penalty is large and could
result setting a to high start temperature if the initial solution contains unscheduled
customers.
If the new solution is accepted it is saved as the current local solution (line 10). If the
new solution furthermore is better than the current global best solution, the new solu-
tion is updated to be the global best solution (line 11-14).
Besides updating the solution also three parameters are updated. Firstly, the tempera-
ture is decreased in line 4 by another parameter α. By decreasing the temperature on-
going the probability of allowing worse solutions decreases as well. This is desirable
as less diversiﬁcation is wanted towards the end, when an already promising global
solution is identiﬁed.
Secondly, another parameter is updated in line 5, namely the parameter which decides
the neighborhood size. Each time a solution is destroyed nSize customers are removed.
The amount of customers removed decreases with the size of β. Similar to the tempera-
ture, the idea is to only destroy smaller parts of the solution as the number of iterations
increases and a promising global solution is already identiﬁed. Thereby new suggested
solutions stay closer to the current to ﬁnd local and hopefully global optima.
The third parameter changes the size of the workload balance penalty (γ) by θ each
time a given number of iterations is reached, denoted by ζ. Thereby the impact of vi-
olating workload balance in the objective function changes. The idea behind this is to
initially allow the algorithm to explore solutions only with regard to travel time. This
could especially be interesting for instances where it is possible to discover solutions
with empty routes (days), in order to decrease objective values signiﬁcantly. Once the
latter occurs and the γ penalty increases also workload balance will be ensured.

Introduction
The last chapter gave a thorough description of the ALNS framework and the motiva-
tion behind using the various functions and heuristics. In this section the ALNS meta-
heuristic framework is implemented in Java to perform computational experiments on
various instances. First a brief description of the data instances is given, continuing
with the tuning of the parameters used in the ALNS in Section 4.2. Afterwards the
performance of the ALNS is compared to benchmarks provided by the case company
and artiﬁcial instances. Next an analysis and discussion of the functions and heuristics
within the ALNS framework is performed to further visualize the ALNS characteristics
and performance. The latter is extended using multi-objective optimization to analyze
the interrelations between distance, travel time and workload balance. All computa-
tional experiments are performed on a 2.50 GHz Core i5-7200U CPU.
4.1.1
Data instances
The following section describes the data instances used for the computational experi-
ments. Two types of data instances are used. The ﬁrst type of instances are provided
by the case company. These instances are anonymized data based on real life cases
where vending machines need to be reﬁlled. The second type of instances are artiﬁcial
instances. These are created to imitate the structure of the real life instances.
A sample of an instance is provided in Figure 4.1. Each instance contains a set of cus-
tomers and a depot from where the vehicle starts end ends its route (customer 0). Each
customer has a set of coordinates from which a distance matrix between all customers
is calculated. Furthermore, the customer type is speciﬁed to be either weekly or bi-
weekly. Next, each customer has predeﬁned time windows for delivery, where time
zero is 8 a.m. and time 720 is 8 p.m.. Thus if a time window ranges from 0 to 720 the
customer can be delivered at anytime during the day. Contrary if the time window is
stated as 0 to 0, then the customer cannot be delivered during that day in both weeks.
Finally, each customer requires a given service time. The real life data instances contain
a reﬁll depot from where the vehicle is reloaded with inventory, denoted by customer
1 in Figure 4.1.

ALNS performance and its characteristics
The latter section provided an analysis and discussion on the performance of the ALNS
in comparison to benchmark solutions and volatility. The following continues this
analysis with focus on internal performance of the implemented methods. First the
performances of the various destroy & repair methods are discussed in combination
with the adaptive heuristic, followed by an analysis of the simulated annealing ap-
proach. These analyses are based on solving instance one with lunch breaks and al-
lowing the elimination of routes.
Analysis of Adaptive Heuristic
An important aspect of the ALNS metaheuristic is the determination of which pair
of destroy & repair method to use in each iteration. In order to determine promising
solutions it is often not enough to limit the number of removal and insertion heuris-
tics to one. Different heuristics are able to solve different scenarios and thereby access
different areas of the solution search space. In the following it is analyzed how the dif-
ferent destroy and repair methods perform using the adaptive approach as formulated
==PAGE:76==
60
Chapter 4. Results
during the methodology in Section 3.8. Afterwards a brief analysis of the heuristics
without an adaptive layer is given.
Figure 4.8 visualizes the weights for each destroy heuristic as a function of the num-
ber of iterations when solving instance 1. However, the general patterns are similar
solving other instances as well. The patterns are revealed and discussed next. First
noticeable is the sudden increase of weights and the gradually decrease as the num-
ber of iterations increases. The initial increase is reasonable because the initial solution
can be improved signiﬁcantly during the ﬁrst iterations. Thereby the removal heuris-
tics are assigned high scores in the adaptive heuristic which translates into increasing
weights. As the solution quality increases and fewer improvements can be achieved,
lower scores are assigned to the heuristics and hence weights are gradually decreased.
This phenomena is obtained for all instances.
Next, the performance of the different removal heuristics is interesting to analyze. The
shortest route removal heuristic only performs well in the beginning of the large neigh-
borhood search. However, this is reasonable since the only purpose of this heuristic is
to eliminate routes. As this occurs rather quickly the removal heuristic is not able to
perform further afterwards. Among the other removal heuristics the worst removal
and bi-weekly removal operator are performing signiﬁcantly better than the others. It
is reasonable that the worst removal performs well since the worst parts of the current
solution are destroyed. Hence the subsequent insertion is most likely improving the
solution. Also to highlight, the performance of the bi-weekly removal heuristic. The
heuristic only removes bi-weekly customers and as a consequence only bi-weekly cus-
tomers are re-inserted. As bi-weekly customers are easier to move around the schedule
than weekly customers, this provides promising destroy & repair iterations which are
able to improve the solution quality. The Shaw removal operator performs decent,
destroying both weekly and bi-weekly customers. But because of the difﬁculties of
moving weekly customers around, the customers removed by the Shaw operator are
not always able to be re-inserted effectively. Lastly, it is reasonable to belief that the
random removal performs the worst as no logical removal approach is used.

Figure 4.9 illustrates the performance of the insertion heuristics. The patterns of a
sudden increase and gradually decrease in weights also occur for insertion heuristics,
with the same reasons as for the removal heuristics. Noticeable is the poor performance
of the random insertion heuristic. As the insertion of customers happens randomly the
likelihood of improving the solution is low, especially as the search procedure proceeds

4.4
Multi-objective optimization
The ALNS metaheuristic has been comprehensively analyzed and compared to bench-
mark solutions. These analyses are performed with the belief that travel time, distance,
idle time and workload are equally important. Thus idle time is penalized such that
each minute of idle time contributes with one minute in the objective. Also each minute
of workload difference exceeding the allowable is penalized with one minute in the ob-
jective. However, in reality this might not be the case since the decision of whether a
shorter total distance is preferable versus high idle times between customers or great
workload balance is subjective. Hence some might prefer to minimize the distance
in order to reduce fuel expenses and emission, even if this is associated with larger
idle and longer travel times. Furthermore, some might not care about great work-
load balance if this comes with further decreased travel times. In order to achieve
a trade-off between the different objectives the following analyzes the possibility of
multi-objective optimization. As it is not possible to obtain solutions using the MIP
model, the ALNS is used. By changing the impact of the parameters in the objective,
different solutions are produced. This is analyzed for respectively distance versus idle
time and the total travel time versus workload balance.
First the multi-objective of minimizing both distance and idle time is analyzed. Hence
the impact of the idle time penalty, κ, is changed iteratively. Beginning with κ = 0 for
the ﬁrst application, the value is increased by one each time until the idle time penalty
is large enough to prohibit idle time. For each value of κ the ﬁnal distance (includ-
ing service time) is determined together with the total amount of idle time. The sum
of both indicates the total objective value as calculated during the previous sections.
Any difference in workload balance is prohibited by the workload penalty γ. The out-
come of solving instance one using multi-objective optimization is visualized in Figure
4.12. The red dots represent the Pareto solutions with the red line indicating the Pareto
front curve for the given solutions obtained solving instance one. The Pareto solu-
tions are non-dominated, meaning it is not possible to ﬁnd another solution among
those obtained in the graph which can decrease either distance or idle time without
increasing the other. Thus the blue dots, above the Pareto front curve, represent the
dominated solutions. For each of those it is possible to ﬁnd another solution both opti-
mizing distance and idle time further. Depending on the individual preferences a good
trade-off solution is found on the Pareto front curve. If low distances are favourable
Pareto solutions towards the right should be chosen. Contrary, if idle time is desired
to be minimized, solutions to the left should be considered. The solution with the low-
est total travel time in Figure 4.12 has an objective of 4619 (including 14 minutes idle
time). In general, it seems that promising solutions with regard to the objective value
as calculated in the previous sections often include some idle time as many promising
solutions are clustered in the top left corner.

Discussion
Based on the latter computational experiments the following provides a valuable dis-
cussing of the main ﬁndings. First, the results are related and compared towards the
aims of the experiment, helping the case company to optimize delivery schedules. Sec-
ondly, the inconsistency and volatility of solution quality is discussed, followed by
identifying challenges and relate the implemented ALNS to existing scientiﬁc knowl-
edge. Lastly, future research improvements are suggested.
The aim of this thesis was to provide valuable information on optimizing delivery
schedules to the case company. The mixed integer programming model formulated to
solve the case problem had great difﬁculties solving even small instances. Instances
with more than 15 customers could not be solved to optimality. As the real life in-
stances from the case company contain more than 200 customers and more than 400
deliveries it is not suitable to use the MIP model for route optimization. Great results
on the other hand were obtained using an adaptive large neighborhood search ex-
tended to use simulated annealing and a hill climber at the end of the search procedure.
Based on the computational experiments performed with the ALNS the case company
should use it to optimize future delivery schedules. Furthermore, it is suggested to use
the possibility of eliminating entire routes. This gives great cost savings, both in terms
of total reduced driving distance and time savings. For the real life case instances it
is possible to eliminate two days, an equivalent of reducing the workload by almost
18 hours during a 10 days planning horizon. This relates to cost savings in the ob-
jective function of more than 20% compared to current case company practices. The
savings in workload can be utilized to expand the business and perform other tasks.
Thereby it is possible to balance the workload across all days even when eliminating
routes. In general, the computational experiment indicated that workload balance can
be achieved without much sacriﬁces of total travel time. This should be a great insight
for the case company, as they can achieve high quality delivery schedules without sac-
riﬁcing neither the desire to reduce total travel time nor neglecting great workload
balance. Beside the cost advantages for the case company, signiﬁcant advantages for
society as a whole can be achieved. As the company expands, more employees can
be hired. Furthermore, the decrease in driving distance contributes to reduce harmful
ﬁne particulate matter. Emissions which cause 6% of premature deaths worldwide,
mostly in urban districts. Also the environmental beneﬁts of reducing green house
gasses and reducing climate change should be an important decision to reduce the to-
tal travel distance of delivery schedules. Further suggestions for improvements to the
case company are to investigate the pre-deﬁned delivery time window preferences of
==PAGE:84==
68

customers. Already the literature review indicated that time window constraints ag-
gravate the solution quality. In the case problem customers requiring late deliveries
affect the solutions signiﬁcantly as workload balance forces all routes to have similar
duration length.
The second main ﬁnding of the computational experiment was the difference and
volatility of solution quality when solving the same instances repeatedly. The degree
of volatility depends on the circumstances, which is reasonable. Applying workload
balance across the entire planning horizon of 10 days results in an average deviation of
4%. This increases to almost 7% by allowing the possibility of eliminating routes, with
peak deviations of 45%. The reasons for large deviations among the solution qual-
ity were analyzed to be caused by ’complicated’ customers who occupy large parts
of the delivery schedule and variations in the number of routes which are able to be
eliminated. The variations caused by the former reason are difﬁcult to decrease as
this is caused by the data structure. However, one simple method could be to force
the randomized greedy heuristic determining an initial solution to insert complicated
customers at the beginning. Thereby the likelihood of unscheduled customers might
decrease, as the complicated customers already are part of the initial schedule. The
second reason is already tried to be minimized by the implemented shortest route re-
moval heuristic. However, this heuristic might not be able to exploit its full potential,
because it is chosen with equal probability as the other heuristics in the beginning
of the ALNS. The reason for this is that the large neighborhood search heuristics are
assigned equal initial weights, which leads to equal probability of choosing the heuris-
tics. Increasing the likelihood of choosing the shortest route removal heuristic in the
beginning of the search might result in less volatility among the number of eliminated
routes, as the same number of routes are eliminated repeatedly. Despite the partly
signiﬁcant volatility of solution quality, it is believed that this should not affect the
usefulness and utilization of the ALNS metaheuristic. In practice only one promising
delivery schedule is necessary every second week. Using the ALNS to solve the case
instances multiple times leads to reasonable run times for a practical implementation
and provides optimal and near optimal solutions.
Lastly, a discussion on how the implementations of this report relate to literature is
provided. First of all, the case problem described, analyzed and discussed in this re-
port is unique and no other problem with an identical case framework could be found.
Instead literature of related problems was reviewed and analyzed which provided a
broad perspective and indicated how to solve the case companies vehicle routing prob-
lem. Related problems were solved by various methods in existing literature, but with
the large neighborhood search repeatedly being applied across different problem char-
acteristics. Thus a LNS metaheuristic was favoured to solve the complex problem in-
troduced by the case company. Applying a pure LNS with only one destroy & repair
heuristic was not believed to be the optimal choice. Since the case problem consists
of different characteristics, different destroy & repair methods were needed to adapt
to case speciﬁc circumstances. Thus the use of operators are depended on whether
routes are eliminated or not. E.g. did the shortest route removal heuristic proved to be
beneﬁcial during the initial phase of the search procedure. Furthermore, the bi-weekly
removal operator proved to be strong to move and make bi-weekly customers adapt
around the weekly customers which are more difﬁcult to schedule. This suggests that

implementing a few customized heuristics also might be advantageously for other spe-
cialized vehicle routing problems in literature. Besides the custom constructed heuris-
tics, other heuristics well-known and suggested by literature were implemented as
well. During literature review the Shaw removal operator often showed very promis-
ing results. This was not the case during the computational experiments in this report.
The heuristic performed decent, but did not show the same usefulness as in literature.
One reason for this might be that the Shaw heuristic in literature often includes other
relatedness criteria than only geographical, as in this report. By introducing related-
ness in terms of customer type or time window preferences the Shaw heuristic might
be stronger and provide improved removal iterations. Another reason could be that
the other destroy methods performed speciﬁcally good on the vehicle routing problem
introduced by the case company. E.g. the worst removal operator performed signiﬁ-
cantly well, which not always has proven to be the case in literature. Great relatedness
to literature was indicated by the insertion operators. Hence the regret operator proved
to be the most promising repair heuristic, closely followed by the cheapest insertion
heuristic. Besides the adaptive part, literature suggested to allow temporarily worse
solutions in the hope for determining improved global best solutions. Implementing a
simulated annealing heuristic indicated to be promising as global best solutions often
were obtained by temporarily allowing the ALNS to search worse solutions. Also the
gradually decrease of the neighborhood size indicated to provide promising results. A
ﬁnding which could be beneﬁcial for other related research problems as well. Finally,
the usefulness of a local search heuristic to improve the global solution obtained by
a metaheuristic was also proposed by literature. However, only a limited amount of
literature applied this method as an extension to the ALNS. During the computational
experiment the implemented hill climber indicated performance increments of approx-
imately 1%. This does not seem signiﬁcantly in perspective with the total overall per-
formance increments of over 20% compared to current case company practices. But
with relatively fast run times of less than 60 seconds for the largest instances, the hill
climber provides great advantages for further optimization. Also, improving the ob-
jective by 1% becomes ever more important in a bigger perspective. Large companies
would be greatly satisﬁed with such increments, and reducing total urban emissions
or the total amount of green house gasses discharged, by one percent would have truly
great impact. Especially in comparison to short run times which a rather simple local
search heuristic as the hill climber provides.

Future research
The computational experiments, analyses and latter discussion reveled important ar-
eas for further improvements using the implemented ALNS. These are brieﬂy reviewed
in the following.
Derived from the discussion it could be beneﬁcial to further investigate how customers
with late time window preferences should be treated, as this could result in signiﬁcant
further reduction of travel time. Assigning each customer or each vending machine a
proﬁtability unit, which measure the revenue, trade-off solutions can be identiﬁed. E.g.
whether it is beneﬁcial to operate a vending machine or not could be determined by
==PAGE:86==
70

comparing the revenue to delivery cost (travel time) using multi-objective optimiza-
tion. Potentially this would cause ’complicated’ customers to be removed from the
search and result in both increased performance and less volatility.
Also, it would be interesting to investigate how the case company could implement the
metaheuristic in practice, adapt to new routing schedules and how the speed, s, and
workload difference, ξ, parameter used in the computational experiments compare to
current settings of the case company.
Furthermore, it could be interesting to extend the Shaw removal operator to include re-
latedness in terms of both geographical, customer type and time window preferences.
As more customers with similar time window preferences are removed and inserted
this might enable the ALNS to faster eliminate days and thereby reduce volatility. Also
the relatedness of customer types could potential replace the bi-weekly customer re-
moval operator.
An additional destroy heuristic in terms of a longest route removal operator could also
be suggested. Since the penalty of the workload balance in the objective depends on
the duration of the longest route, reducing this duration might determine promising
solutions faster and improve performance. However, it is important that the shortest
and longest route removal heuristic does not neutralize each other and hence become
ineffective.
Finally, a signiﬁcant amount of literature suggest to implement noise to the objective
function. The idea is to add randomness to the insertion heuristics such that they do
not propose the insertion possibility which is best locally. How and whether this inﬂu-
ences the implemented ALNS metaheuristic and enables a reduction of volatility could
be interesting to analyze in future research.
Lastly, more time could be spend on improving the delta evaluations. Especially for
larger instances the run times are high which makes computational experiments more
difﬁcult and time consuming. As the majority of time during the search procedure
is utilized by calculating new objectives and validating constraints, reducing the time
to determine this would decrease total run times signiﬁcantly. A proposal of how to
further improve delta evaluation would be to only re-determine the cost for customers
which are scheduled just before and after the newly removed or inserted customer.
Currently the cost of the entire day is calculated. However, removals and insertions
inﬂuence all subsequent customers because their arrival times changes. Also a change
in the longest day duration would require to recalculate the entire schedule because of
workload balance. Hence it is uncertain how much contribution to reduced computa-
tional time an improved delta evaluation provides.

Conclusion
The aim of this report was to investigate how existing literature suggests to analyze
vehicle routing problems related to the problem introduced by the case company. Fur-
thermore, the aim was to review, analyze and discuss how the case company should
optimize delivery schedules using metaheuristic. The case problem introduced by the
case company includes the delivery towards customers in urban districts during a two
week planning horizon, with the aim to minimize total travel time while obtaining
reasonable workload balance.
Literature proposed and repeatedly indicated the beneﬁts of using adaptive large neigh-
borhood search. A strong metaheuristic to search broad solution space with promising
results. The ALNS extended with a simulated annealing heuristic and a local search
for further optimization was implemented to solve the case problem. Using the ALNS
to solve real life instances indicated travel time savings of more than 20% compared
to current practices by the case company. This corresponds to reduce workload by al-
most 18 hours, which could be achieved by eliminating entire routes and consolidate
customers into fewer days. Also, promising solutions could be achieved without much
sacriﬁce of workload balance, while they often included idle time to comply with time
window preferences.
However, the disadvantage of eliminating days is increased volatility of solution qual-
ity. Deviations between the solutions produced during the computational experiments
were as high as 45%, with an average of 7%, as the cost difference is signiﬁcant whether
routes are eliminated or not. The process of eliminating routes was found to be trou-
blesome because of time window preferences and customers which were difﬁcult to
move around the schedule. Further computational experiments illustrated this should
not be a crucial disadvantage. The ALNS was able to determined optimal solutions
and near to optimal solutions for the real life benchmark instances when solving the
instances repeatedly with reasonable run times.
The heuristics of the implemented ALNS showed similar characteristics as those in lit-
erature. Destroy & repair heuristics using logical operators to improve the solutions
proved to be more effective than fast and random operators. Thus it was found that the
regret insertion operator improved solutions the most, while the worst removal opera-
tor performed the best destroy iterations. The adaptive layer proved to be essential to
eliminate routes and thereby improve solution quality. Also the simulated annealing

approach indicated to be necessary, as improvements were made after allowing tem-
porarily worse solutions. Finally, it is suggested that future literature should inves-
tigate the possibility of applying local search after the termination of a metaheuristic
to further optimize solution quality. Computational experiments visualized average
improvements of 1% using a hill climber after termination of the ALNS. In broader
contexts this could lead to great absolute advancements for both companies, society
and environment. Reducing emissions contributes to a decrease in the number of pre-
mature deaths in urban areas worldwide caused by ﬁne particulate matter and could
decelerate climate change to sustain a healthy planet for future generations to come.
Future research could imply the investigation of customers who have large inﬂuence

and decrease volatility. Also, in terms of general research, it would be interesting to
further analyze how the general large neighborhood search would perform on the case
problem compared to the sophisticated ALNS implemented in this report.
