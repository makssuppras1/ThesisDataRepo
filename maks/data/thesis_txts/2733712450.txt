Abstract
This study examines how pharmaceutical manufacturing companies, specifically Contract De-
velopment and Manufacturing Organisations, can balance financial and environmental consid-
erations in their inventory management strategies, specifically focusing on a waste-conscious
approach to material selection. Incorporating evidence from qualitative interviews, a survey, a
life cycle assessment and a cost-benefit analysis performed using data from a case company, this
study demonstrates that there are challenges that the pharmaceutical industry is facing in the
process of achieving a more sustainable manufacturing. Concretely, several issues such as in-
flexible contracts and communication challenges lead to more material waste and consequently
higher environmental impacts. The pharmaceutical industry is a highly regulated system with
limited capacity to alter processes at the company level. However, as a potential change that
the case company could make, two sizes of a single-use sampling system were compared in a
life cycle assessment. The results found that the impact categories with the largest impacts
were climate change, human toxicity cancer, and freshwater ecotoxicity. While environmental
impacts for the area of protection human health only decreased 0.3 person-year equivalents,
impacts on ecosystem quality was reduced by 4 person-year equivalents over the span of one
year. The material and manufacturing life cycle stage was found to be the main driver of
environmental impacts in most impact categories largely due to the metals, polycarbonate,
and polyethylene components of the sampling system. The second most impactful life cycle
stage was disposal. In addition, a scenario analysis revealed that environmental impacts may
be reduced by approximately 9 %, if the number of sampling systems that exceed their shelf
life is halved. Finally, a cost-benefit analysis found that lost revenue, which is the potential
revenue that the company could have made, was the main driver of financial losses due to excess
medicine being wasted, and potential savings of 10.8 million DKK - almost a 50 % decrease
over a 20 year time horizon were identified. In conclusion, this thesis found that a reduction in
size of the sampling system lead to both financial and environmental savings.

Executive Summary
The pharmaceutical industry is increasingly understood as having high environmental impacts,
partly driven by the large amount of material waste. As a means of reducing the amount of plas-
tic waste in the system, this thesis sought to evaluate the environmental and economic impact
of a single-use sampling system used in the production at a pharmaceutical company. An ini-
tial literature study revealed that research has found single-use systems to be more sustainable
than traditional systems, due to the decreased need for sterilisation between batches. However
as plastic pollution becomes an increasing global concern, the need to reduce single-use plastic
in the sector has increased. In response this thesis sought to investigate the research question:
”How can the heavily regulated pharmaceutical industry balance finances and environmental
considerations in their inventory management strategies?”.
Initial research using a mix of interviews and surveys revealed four key challenges regard-
ing inventory management, namely inflexible contracts with the customers, inflexible contracts
with suppliers, communication between departments, and old rules that persisted. The con-
tracts gave customers too much control over material selection which led to the case company
purchasing an excessive amount of materials. Lack of sufficient communication between de-
partments further amplified this trend, and outdated rules led to an increase in materials being
disposed of and a general misalignment of inventory levels.
A comparative life cycle assessment was used to calculate the environmental impacts of two
single-use sampling systems with varying size of drainage bags, following the guidelines of ISO
14040 and ISO 14044. Using the tool SimaPro with the impact assessment method IMPACT
World+, the analysis showcased potential environmental savings. Materials and manufacturing
were found to contribute the most to environmental impacts compared to other life cycle stages
followed by disposal. The impact categories with the highest impacts related to human health
were climate change, and human toxicity cancer.
For ecosystem quality it was freshwater
ecotoxicity and climate change. A reduction of approximately 25 % in environmental impacts
was found for ozone layer depletion, freshwater ecotoxicity, and freshwater eutrophication for
the smaller sampling system compared to the larger. For all other impact categories, potential
reductions in environmental impacts of ≤20 % were found. When relaying the results to the
global impacts of an average person, environmental impacts for human health would decrease
0.3 person-year equivalents, and impacts on ecosystem quality would decrease by 4 person-
year equivalents over the span of one year if the case company were to implement the smaller
sampling system.
Additionally, a scenario analysis found a potential of reducing the total
impacts by approximately 9 % if the amount of waste was halved.
Finally, a cost-benefit analysis was conducted of the two sizes of sampling systems, adding
a financial perspective to the environmental one. Using data from qualitative interviews and

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Climate change is recognised as the biggest threat to ecosystems and it is expected that cli-
mate change will cause 250.000 deaths per year in the period of 2030-2050 (WHO, 2023). As
temperatures rise globally, more extreme weather conditions are observed with an increase in
both frequency and intensity of events such as heatwaves, droughts, floods, wildfires, tropical
storms, and hurricanes. The health sector and its relation to climate change is complex, be-
cause with the growing population living under conditions worsened by climate change, there is
an increased need for a robust health sector that can accommodate and alleviate arising health
problems (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022). At the same time, the health
sector alone is estimated to be responsible for approximately 4.4 % of global net emissions which
is more than the automobile industry based per dollar revenue (WEF, 2022). A study in 2020
quantifying the resource consumption and pollution by the health-care sector in 189 countries
from 2000 to 2015 found that: ”health care is harming the environment in ways that, in turn,
harm health, thereby counteracting the primary mission of health care.” (Capon et al., 2020).
The healthcare sector mainly contributes to global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions through
energy consumption, transport, product manufacture, use and disposal. Mapping the GHG
emissions of the entirety of the sector reveals that 71 % of the emissions are primarily derived
from Scope 3 emissions, meaning the production, transport, and disposal of pharmaceutical
products and services in addition to hospital equipment, medical devices and instruments and
food and agricultural products (Health care’s climate footprint, 2019). Particulate matter for-
mation is likewise mostly found upstream in the supply chains (Capon et al., 2020).
Reducing GHG emissions is something that is increasingly adopted into the corporate
strategies of many pharmaceutical companies in line with achieving net zero emissions as set
out in the Paris agreement (United Nations, 2023). If this goal is to be achieved, large-scale
changes across organisations and supply chains are necessary and it will require a massive
collective effort.
However, one of the barriers the pharmaceutical industry does face when
trying to mitigate its climate impact is its need to comply with regulations of clean produc-
tion, which encourages the use of single-use systems (SUS). SUS dominate roughly 85 % of
the pre-commercial market for bioprocessing equipment (Langer & Rader, 2018). SUS are per

Almost 30 years ago, Sheldon (1994) reported that the pharmaceutical industry generated
the most waste per unit of product compared to other chemical industry sectors. Depend-
ing on the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API), a generation of between 25 and 100 kg of
waste per kilogram of API was seen. Since then, the adoption of green chemistry principles
have evolved, and a bench-marking study by Veleva et al. (2018) conducted on 34 global phar-
maceutical companies found that 41 % of the companies have publicly stated a commitment
to green chemistry, and that the most common sustainably goal across companies was energy
use/reduction followed by GHG emissions, water use and wastewater reduction. However, there
is still a much smaller focus on waste of raw materials, despite the vast amounts of materials
that are discarded due to their perishable nature or other factors, whilst the majority of aca-
demic research focuses on the production of pharmaceutical products, and organic solvents in
pharmaceutical process wastewater streams.
1.2
Statement of objective
The current study seeks to add to the literature on the environmental consequences of the
material waste generated by the pharmaceutical industry, by analysing data obtained from
a pharmaceutical Contract Development and Manufacturing Organisation (CDMO). The aim
of the study is to evaluate the environmental and economic impact of introducing a waste-
conscious approach to material management at a case company through the use of life cycle
assessment (LCA) and cost-benefit analysis (CBA). The reason for the study is that the case
company is seeing an increase in interest of environmental reporting from the customers indi-
cating a potential untapped competitive leverage point. Specifically the research question to
be examined is:
How can the heavily regulated pharmaceutical industry balance finances and environmental
considerations in their inventory management strategies?
The research question is addressed by examining the following four sub-questions:

Sub-research question 1: What are specific challenges for CDMOs regarding
adaptability of inventory?
The objective of this research question is to investigate the inventory challenges specifically
relevant for CDMOs. The nature of CDMOs requires a high level of adaptability in the types
of materials in stock as they must accommodate having to produce different medicinal products
according to their customers’ needs. This brings along several challenges regarding inventory
management, such as reactivity and deviations from standard materials.
The question will be explored through qualitative interviews and a survey at the case
company with different stakeholders in the departments of supply chain management, supply
planning, global sourcing and procurement and warehouse, under the hypothesis that challenges
regarding inventory management increase the amounts of waste.
Sub-research question 2: What is the environmental impact of a single-use
sampling system in medicine production?
The objective of this research question is to understand what the current environmental im-
pacts are for a single-use sampling system in the manufacturing of medicine at a CDMO. A key
element of any type of medicine production is the ability to test the medicine safely and con-
sistently at different stages in the production according to the ”good practice” guidelines and
regulations. In order for the case company to test their medicine they use single-use sampling
systems with five 250 mL drainage bags, that extracts medicine from a bioreactor and keeps it
in a closed system for testing purposes such as for endotoxin and bioburn tests. The standard
practise is to extract up to five times more medicine than needed for the tests, and the excess
medicine as well as the sampling systems consisting of plastic and steel are disposed of after
use. In addition to the sampling systems used, there are excess sampling systems that expire
due to exceeded shelf-life and must be disposed of without having been used.
To understand the environmental impacts of this situation an LCA will be conducted of
the sampling system. Input and output data will be obtained through qualitative interviews
with relevant stakeholders, such as operators in the manufacturing, IT support employees, the
departments of supply chain management and quantitative data in the IT systems. The mod-

2.1
Sustainability in the pharmaceutical industry
Worldwide pharmaceutical sales has increased significantly, in 2010 it was estimated that global
sales from pharmaceuticals were $693 billion (Brems et al., 2013), increasing to $1.48 trillion
in 2022 (Statista, 2023). Even though the industry is not considered ”dirty” in the public eye
compared to for example the automobile industry, with this growth the environmental impacts
of the industry have also increased substantially (Brems et al., 2013). The attention on environ-
mental sustainability in the pharmaceutical industry is a growing topic in scientific circles. A
testament to this is the amount of available research in the scientific database SCOPUS, which
has doubled in the past five years compared to the preceding five years 1. For the industry to
find a sustainable balance between the economic growth and the minimisation of environmental
impacts, it is necessary to achieve a synergy between the three pillars of sustainability being the
social, economic, and environmental aspects of sustainability. In practice, however, Hansmann
et al. (2012) identified that for a company trying to integrate the three pillars, there will often
be conflict between each pillar, as trying to balance them is difficult. Not least because no
company operates alone, but is part of an often complex supply chain.
Some studies indicate that in order for supply chains to progress to a recyclable closed-loop
system, there is a need of both forward and reverse logistics (Ishizaka et al., 2023). However
a challenge, as for all sectors moving to a closed-looped system, is that small changes to the
system may create disruption that affects the entire supply chain negatively. This problem is
amplified when considering pharmaceutical supply chains due to the many rules and regulations
securing high quality products, especially for supply chains operating across countries with
different regulations (Peachey, 2023). Additionally, the need for clinical quality products means
that most products have a limited life-span which increase the complexity of the supply chain

2.2
Single-use systems: Adoption and environmental is-
sues
An element of the problem related to sustainability in the pharmaceutical industry is how to
manage the amount of waste created. A study in 2018 estimated that 5.9 million tonnes of waste
are generated each year from healthcare facilities in the US (Voudrias, 2018). It is difficult to
quantify the share of waste being plastic, but plastic waste have been estimated to account for
30 % of all healthcare waste (Rizan et al., 2018), meaning that the US healthcare sector alone
produces 1.7 million tonnes of plastics every year. This number has likely increased since the
study, not least with the COVID-19 pandemic that saw a drastic increase in disposable plastic
items such as personal protective equipment and testing equipment (Global analysis of health
care waste in the context of COVID-19, 2022).
One of the reasons for the increase in plastic is the transition from the traditional manufac-
turing technologies to the use of single-use systems (SUS). Traditional technologies are based on
steel systems, which are sterilised between each batch using vast amounts of water and steam.
In the single-use systems, components are replaced between each batch, which eliminates the
need of sterilisation, but do require heightened levels of transportation and production of new
equipment. Situated in the pharmaceutical industry, biopharmaceutical manufacturing has seen
a change in the past 20 years from the manufacturing of large-scale blockbuster drugs to more
potent biologics that require smaller production volumes along with improved processes that
results in higher production yields, thus reducing the need for large, permanent infrastructure
(Langer, 2017). Due to the increased flexibility, and a reduction in both change-over time and
cleaning needs that traditional stainless steel systems can not compete with, the reliance on
SUS is ever growing.
In fact, Langer (2017) conducted a survey of 227 bioprocessing decision-makers on the
adoption of SUS which revealed that some single-use plastic products have almost reached

Christian B. Honor´e

market saturation while others are growing steadily and that SUS are being developed for
a variety of different purposes within biopharmaceutical manufacturing from basic tubing and
clamps to sampling systems, mixing systems, perfusion devices and even bioreactors. The high-
est adoption rates were seen within the field of scale-up/clinical production-scale at over 70 % in
all disposable categories. Study participants cited that the reduction in capital investments was
the main driver of adopting SUS, which fits the trend that smaller scale operations see higher
adoption rates than already established commercial production facilities. On the contrary, the
high cost of disposables was seen as a barrier for increasing the use of SUS, since the disposable
nature of SUS naturally means that they have to be replaced often. A key takeaway from the
study was that a majority of industry experts stated that they either agreed or strongly agreed
that there will be a fully disposable facility in operation in 5 years and the author argues that
the industry will see a wider adoption at commercial scale as regulatory agencies become ”more
comfortable with the performance of SUS”.
SUS and the traditional manufacturing systems have rather different environmental pro-
files. A study conducted by Pietrzykowski et al. (2013) compared the two systems over a time-
line of 10 years in an LCA to understand the differences. In the study it was identified that
the traditional system is much more polluting than the single-use system, due to the amount of
energy and water needed for the sterilisation process. This sterilisation outweighed the manu-
facturing and end-of-life for the two systems. The study also showed that the single-use system
improves both flexibility and quality, while also decreasing the costs of processing. However, as
both types of systems have their individual benefits depending on the process required, most
companies use a combination of traditional steel tanks, and single-use bags, connectors, filters,
and columns. Even though the study demonstrated that the end-of-life of single-use systems
do not pose significant environmental impacts compared to other life cycle stages, the build up
of plastic in the environment means attention should still be given to finding ways of reducing
the impacts. Recycling of plastic is certainly a potential way to reduce the problem, but this is
not a straightforward process with Blessy et al. (2021) highlighting the many challenges related
to sorting and cleaning of medical plastics as a key barrier to the widespread recycling of these
types of waste; along with the perception of SUS as more clinically safe, and concerns about
the use of once hazardous materials. Some argue that alternatives to plastics such as the re-
placement of petrol-based plastics with bioplastics could potentially alleviate the environmental
impacts (Jahnke, 2020) but this is a whole complex field of research of its own.
Some debate if the environmental issues of the pharmaceutical industry is attributable to
a lack of policies and regulations imposed to environmental emissions (Healthcare in Europe,
2023). Current regulations in the pharmaceutical industry are different from market to market,

2.3
The application of life cycle assessment in the phar-
maceutical industry
Quantifying the environmental impacts of the pharmaceutical industry is important in order to
increase the understanding of which processes are main contributors and to act as decision sup-
port when assessing alternatives ultimately leading to an efficient reduction of environmental
impacts. LCAs allow the practitioner to quantify and map environmental impacts throughout
the entirety of a product’s or service’s life cycle. A systematic analysis of research trends for
LCA application in the pharmaceutical industry revealed that the application of LCA in the
context of pharmaceuticals has seen an increasing trend from 2000 to 2022 (Sabour et al., 2023).
In addition, the review found that in recent years, research focus has been shifting to topics
such as environmental impacts, carbon footprint, wastewater treatment, human toxicity, and
active pharmaceutical ingredients. The study concludes that key topics are currently explored,
including: (1) the production of pharmaceuticals from renewable resources and the use of more
environmentally friendly routes for the production of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs);
(2) the use of organic solvents and solvents recovery; and (3) advanced treatment of pharma-
ceutical wastewater.
Research by Van Der Vorst et al. (2013) using an LCA to understand alternative synthe-
sis methods of Galantamine·HBr found a 41 % resource reduction potential by changing the
chemistry and by using a continuous flow reactor rather than batch production. Also using
an LCA approach, Ott et al. (2014) and Osorio-Tejada et al. (2022) analysed the different
environmental profiles of batch processing and continuous flow reactors. Ott et al. (2014) anal-
ysed 14 variations of batch and continuous processing and found that even though continuous
processing has a higher solvent and energy consumption, changing the catalyst and a yield en-
hancement outweigh the negative environmental burden. Osorio-Tejada et al. (2022) analysed
7 batch synthesis approaches and 2 continuous flow approaches in the synthesis of the API

to the batch approaches with one of the main reasons being an increased circularity in the

approaches are analysed with LCA.

be disposed of. The authors hypothesise that moving the production closer to the customers’
needs or demands via individualisation techniques could potentially lower the amount of waste

the hypothesis. One of the key problematic areas in pharmaceutical production is the use of and

higher environmental impact than the conventional solvents and as such were not recommended
under the circumstances analysed.

3.2
Life cycle assessment
Life cycle assessment is a structured method to analyse the environmental impacts of a product
or a service through a series of predefined steps. This study follows the guidelines set out in
ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 (ISO, 2006, 2008) which are the leading standards for conducting
an LCA (PR´e Sustainability, 2021). According to the standards, an LCA study consists of
four phases that the practitioner goes through iteratively, namely the goal and scope definition
phase, the inventory analysis phase, the impact assessment phase, and finally the interpretation
phase. Each phase will be briefly described in the following.
3.2.1
Phase one: Goal and scope definition
Defining the goal and scope is the first phase of the LCA, and even though the purpose of the
goal and the purpose of the scope are two separate things, both are included in this phase.
The aim of the goal is to define the framework for the analysis and unambiguously state the
following;
1. the intended application,
Page 12 of 73
==PAGE:24==
Astrid D. Rørdam and
Christian B. Honor´e
Technical University of Denmark
January 2024
2. the reasons for carrying out the study,
3. the intended audience, and
4. whether the results are intended to be used in comparative assertions intended to be
disclosed to the public.
The scope of the study must, according to the standards, contain the following information:
1. the product system to be studied,
2. the functions of the product system or, in the case of comparative studies, the systems,
3. the functional unit,
4. the system boundary,
5. allocation procedures,
6. LCIA methodology and types of impacts,
7. interpretation to be used,
8. data requirements,
9. assumptions,
10. value choices and optional elements,
11. limitations,
12. data quality requirements,
13. type of critical review, if any, and
14. type and format of the report required for the study.
3.2.2
Phase two: Inventory analysis
Once the goal and scope definition phase has been completed, the life cycle inventory (LCI)
analysis can be performed. There are several operational steps in this phase, such as prepa-
rations for data collection, data collection, validation of data, relating data to unit processes,
relating data to functional unit, data aggregation and refining the system boundary. All these
steps or a combination thereof results in a completed inventory. Depending on the situation
context defined in the goal and scope definition, the inventory analysis should contain allocation
including reuse and recycling of materials in the product system.

==PAGE:25==
Astrid D. Rørdam and
Christian B. Honor´e
Technical University of Denmark
January 2024
3.2.3
Phase three: Impact assessment
Life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) is a relative approach based on the functional unit defined
in the scope definition phase. This phase consists of mandatory and voluntary steps, depending
on the intended application of the study, and the goal and scope of the study. The mandatory
elements are the following:
1. selection of impact categories, category indicators, and characterisation models,
2. assignment of LCI results to the selected impact categories (classification), and
3. calculation of category indicator results (characterisation).
In addition, if relevant for the defined goal and scope of the study, there are optional
elements of the LCIA:
1. normalisation,
2. grouping,
3. weighting, and
4. data quality analysis (gravity, uncertainty, and sensitivity analyses).
3.2.4
Phase four: Interpretation
The final phase of the LCA is the interpretation and comprises:
1. identification of the significant issues based on the results of the LCI and LCIA phases of
the LCA,
2. an evaluation that considers completeness, sensitivity, and consistency checks,
3. conclusions, limitations, and recommendations.
As stated previously, LCA is an iterative method, and it will in most cases be necessary
to return to earlier phases as data is gathered and the system is built out.
3.3
Cost-benefit analysis
A cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is a systematic process based on measurable financial metrics
such as revenue and costs, which can be used as decision support for a variety of purposes.
This is done by quantifying and monetising the benefits of a specific action and subtracting
the costs associated with it. The benefits and costs can be tangible and intangible, as they can
Page 14 of 73
==PAGE:26==
Astrid D. Rørdam and
Christian B. Honor´e
Technical University of Denmark
January 2024
include factors like customer satisfaction, which may lead to a decline or increase in sales.
The steps of a CBA depends on the situation to be analysed, but should in all cases include
an identification of all costs associated with a given decision such as direct, indirect, intangible,
opportunity, and costs of potential risks. Likewise, all benefits associated with the decision
should be identified and quantified. Benefits can include higher revenue, intangible benefits,
and competitive advantages. Finally, the sum of all costs are subtracted from the sum of all
benefits. A positive result indicates that the decision is associated with more benefits than
costs, but does not necessarily mean that the decision should be made. In some cases, costs
and benefits may be distributed unequally geographically, temporally, or on different social
groups in a population. It may also be the case that another project would yield a higher net
present value (NPV) and would be more advisable for the company or organisation to pursue.
It is therefore necessary to qualitatively analyse the situation and not solely rely on the quan-
titative result. Additional steps include applying discount rates to account for inflation and
other potential unknowns, especially if there is a time-skewed realisation of either benefits or
costs.

A: Have an API that they want to design a production line for.
B: Have a product that is already on the market, but they are looking to either scale up or

just get a shopping list and we have no say in who the suppliers are or which materials to buy”.
The customers can demand that the case company purchase a specific material from a specific
supplier, even though the case company may already have a different supplier that provides the
exact same material. In some cases, this could lead to the case company having to purchase a
material even though they already have it in stock - only from a different supplier.
4.1.1.2
Consequence number two: Bulk plan changes
The customer can change their order amount close to the day of production start, meaning
that the case company will already be prepared and have ordered or purchased all required
materials and chemicals. ”Past term we have excess inventory because of bad contracts with
customers that allowed them to change the bulk plans, so already purchased material couldn’t be
used.” - P1. When a customer changes the number of batches they want produced or the timing
of the campaign, it is called a bulk plan change, and it has large environmental and financial
repercussions for the case company. An extreme example of this was a customer that cancelled
an entire campaign a few months prior to the date it was supposed to begin. The customer
still had to pay for the ordered product so the case company was not financially burdened by
the sudden change, but all materials and chemicals had been purchased and a large percentage
of these were unique to the campaign, meaning they had to be disposed of.

4.1.1.3
Consequence number three: Excess transportation of materials and chem-

Originally an American company, the case company has retained many of its ties to American
suppliers. Combined with the inflexible contracts, this introduces a risk of having to trans-

an employee in the Quality Control (QC) department. They mentioned that the case company
still use air freight to transport auto injectors from a production facility in Taiwan to Denmark,
a distance of almost 9,000 km (Fluent Cargo, 2023). This statement has not been confirmed

the auto injectors are a standard product since the details were not found to be critical for the
current analysis.
The supply network of the case company includes an external warehouse which is located
approximately 40 km away from the production facility. Transportation to and from this ware-
house will drastically increase in the near future, since the production facilities are currently
being expanded to almost double the current size and there are no plans to expand the ware-

] in the future because we are expanding the production facilities but not the warehouse capacity”
- P5. Several participants found this fact to be problematic with one highlighting that not only
will this require more transportation, but also more packaging:
”For instance, if one drum of sodium chloride is needed then it is repacked at [the external

wrapped in clear plastic to ensure it does not fall during transportation. Multiply that incident
by several more!” - P1.
In summary, inflexible contracts with customers lead to suppliers and specific materials
that may not be the most environmentally favorable, a risk of late bulk plan changes, excess
inventory, and excess transportation.

4.2
Life cycle assessment
To address sub-research question 2 and 3: ”What is the environmental impact of a single-use
sampling system in medicine production?” and ”What are the potential environmental gains of
a waste conscious approach to material-selection?”, a comparative LCA study was conducted
on the single-use sampling systems used at the case company. The analysis contain all the
required elements set out in the ISO standards 14040 and 14044 (ISO, 2006, 2008), however the
structure of the analysis was adapted to M. Z. Hauschild et al. (2018) to improve the readability
of the analysis.
4.2.1
Goal and scope definition
This section contains the goal and scope phase of the LCA study. The goal contextualises the
background for the analysis and explain all relevant information including the intended appli-
cation, method assumptions, and decision context. The scope provides a detailed description
of the product system including the functional unit, the life cycle inventory (LCI) modelling
framework, system boundaries, representativeness of the LCI data, and the basis for impact
assessment that follows in the next phase of the LCA.

4.2.1.1.1
Intended application
The intended application of this study is a comparison of two sampling systems, one of which

4.2.1.1.2
Method assumptions and impact limitations
Assumptions and limitations are unavoidable when conducting an analysis. There will be limi-
tations both to the data, but also to the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) method used. The
LCIA method chosen for the study was IMPACT World+ as normalisation is possible using
the IMPACT World+ normalisation factors for the different damage categories. A limitation
of using IMPACT World+, is that while 21 endpoint level indicators are included, an addi-
tional 23 are provided only as interim for sensitivity analysis, as they are still considered too
immature to be completely integrated. In addition, impact categories such as biotic resource
use (overfishing, unsustainable foresting), noise pollution, and the impacts of photochemical
oxidants on vegetation are not considered at all (Bulle et al., 2019). Another limitation of
using IMPACT World+ with the current version 2.0.1 is that the method only covers partial
regionalisation as newer implementations to SimaPro are not used in the inventory and will
give false impressions of contributions to the results (PR´e Sustainability, 2022).
Data availability was a limitation of the study, as primary data did not exist in a structured
format at the time of data collection. Therefore, qualitative interviews with managers and
employees at the case company were used to get an overview of the processes in the use,
transportation and disposal stages of the life cycle. The interviews were conducted with multiple
departments, see Appendix B for a complete list. In addition, the impact categories chosen as
the focus of the analysis limits the applicability of the results. It is important to be aware of
how changes to the system influence all impact categories to avoid problem shifting but the
most relevant categories were selected for process contribution analysis to further understand
the environmental profile of the sampling systems.

4.2.1.2.3
LCI modelling framework
The decision of whether the case company chooses to use 150 mL or 250 mL drainage bags for
the sampling system is not expected to have large structural changes on the market. Thus, the
decision context is at the company level, i.e. situation A (European Commision, 2010). Accord-
ing to the International Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) guideline, situation A suggests that
the LCI modelling framework should follow an attributional principle (European Commision,
2010). Thereby, multifunctional processes should preferably be handled by system expansion.
However, no multifunctional processes were identified in the value chain of the analysed system,
and thus system expansion was not performed. In reality, there may be some multifunctional
processes in the system, but due to lack of information it would generate insufficient evidence
of their influence to the system and they have therefore been left out. Some processes down-
stream can also have co-products that should be handled though system expansion. This is
the case for waste incineration at the end-of-life for some of the materials, as it generates heat
and power. However, the heat and power generation was not included in the study, presenting
a limitation. The attributional principle further suggests that background processes should be
modelled with average processes which was therefore done in the study.
4.2.1.2.4
System boundaries and completeness requirements
System boundaries. The analysis considered all life cycle stages from cradle to grave. The
processes identified were divided in the five life cycle stages: extraction of raw materials, man-
ufacturing, transport, use, and disposal as seen in Figure 4.3. In the figure the foreground
processes are indicated with a white color and background processes are indicated with a grey
color. The foreground processes are processes in which the case company has either direct
control or at least an influence in the activities. Transport was the only process modelled as
foreground due to an assumed influence of the case company. Contrarily, it is not possible
for the case company to directly influence the background processes. This was the case with
the extraction of raw material, the manufacturing processes at the supplier, and the disposal
processes. As prescribed by the decision context, background processes were modelled with
average processes due to the sufficiency of detail for this data.

4.2.1.2.5
Representativeness of LCI data
Technological representativeness: Data for the manufacturing processes should ideally come
from the commissioner and the supplier to properly reflect the manufacturing technologies
used. However, information about the manufacturing technologies was limited and was there-
fore estimated in corporation with an industry expert. Data for background processes should
ideally represent average technology.
Geographical representativeness: Two critical factors impact the geographical representa-
tion of the data. One being the manufacturing location of the supplier and the other being the
disposal site for the waste. For the manufacturing the supplier has a manufacturing base in
Germany and in Asia. As it was unclear where the product was manufactured, it was decided
to model the geographical representation as global average (GLO). The disposal stage was
considered part of the background system and therefore modelled as incineration in Denmark.
Data should therefore originate from the average for the main market in Denmark.
Temporal representativeness: The temporal representativeness of the manufacturing pro-
cesses was assessed using data from SimaPro. In SimaPro the manufacturing processes were
representative for the period 2011-2020, which was estimated to be a fair representation for the
manufacturing processes. Even though technology is subject to continuous improvements, it
was not assumed that the supplier uses technology which is newer than 2020. The end-of-life
processes should ideally be representative for a two-three year time horizon, spanning over the
sampling systems’ lifetime.

4.2.1.2.6
Basis for impact assessment
For the impact assessment the characterisation was done according to the ILCD’s recommended
practice (European Commision, 2010). The product systems were modelled in the software
SimaPro version 9.5.0.2 using the LCIA method IMPACT World+. IMPACT World+ relies on
a midpoint-damage framework covering 18 midpoint categories and 21 damage level categories
as well as the two areas of protection, human health and ecosystem quality. Figure 4.4, adapted
from Bulle et al. (2019) shows the framework. The figure is simplified to fit the scope of the
current study. Human health is expressed as Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALY) and covers
the impact categories climate change on human health, water availability on human health,
photochemical oxidant formation, ionizing radiation on human health, ozone layer depletion,
human toxicity cancer and non-cancer, and particulate matter formation.
Ecosystem quality is expressed as Potentially Disappeared Fraction of Species (PDF·
m2·year) and the impact categories associated with this damage category are climate change

are transported directly from G¨ottingen to the case company. Uncertainties were also present

combustible waste goes directly to an incineration plant and that confidential waste first has to
be sorted in a different location in Denmark. Afterwards it is transported to the incineration
plant. Due to the unknown processes at the sorting plant, all sampling systems were assumed
to go directly from the case company to the incineration plant.

4.2.2.5
Basis for sensitivity and scenario analyses
To test the sensitivity of the model to changes in input values, two sensitivity analyses and
one scenario analysis were conducted. Uncertainty analysis was deemed outside the scope of
the current study due to the limited amount of foreground processes and because no sensitive
parameters were identified.
4.2.2.5.1
Basis for sensitivity analysis 1: Transport distance
Transport was the only process modelled as a foreground process which means that it is the
only process the case company has control over. The distance of the transport was estimated
as a direct route from the supplier to the case company. However, most likely there will be
an alternative route in which the supplier will deliver products for other customers. For that
reason it was examined how an increase in the distance would affect the overall environmental
impact. For testing the effect of changes to the transport distance, the distance was increased
by 10 %. Equation 4.3 (M. Z. Hauschild et al., 2018) was used to calculate the normalised
sensitivity coefficient to asses if the transportation distance was a sensitive parameter for the
results:

baseline and ak is the value of the parameter that is tested for sensitivity, ∆IS is the change

parameter. In this study, a parameter was considered sensitive if XIS,k > 0.3, in accordance

4.2.2.5.2
Basis for sensitivity analysis 2: Impact method
The second sensitivity analysis was performed to identify if the results of the study were sensitive
to the impact method used. For the LCIA, the method IMPACT World+ was chosen. Another
impact method that is commonly used is ReCiPe 2016, which like IMPACT World+ has 18
midpoint indicators, but considers three areas of protection, namely damage to human health,
damage to ecosystems, and damage to resource availability (National Institute for Public Health
and the Environment, 2018). In addition, ReCiPe 2016 offers three different perspectives, the
individualist, hierarchist, and egalitarian (Bulle et al., 2019). Unlike IMPACT World+, ReCiPe
2016 does not include potential impacts from future extractions in the impact assessment,
meaning that certain impact categories will likely show lower impacts compared to other impact
methods (PR´e Sustainability, 2016). The model was evaluated using ReCiPe 2016 with the
hierarchist perspective to investigate the effects of the impact method on the results.

study was for long term impacts, this could be the explanation. In addition, toxicity in LCA
is generally a subject that is highly debated, as the results of the toxicity strongly depends on
the impact method chosen (Guide for interpreting life cycle assessment result, 2016). In the
current study, metals in the system were the main contributor to the high impact for IMPACT
World+, as the impact of the metals will not parish after regular disposal processes, which is
the case with organic material, but instead metals will continuously increase the impact in the
ecosystem and thereby to freshwater ecotoxicity.
It is difficult to conclude whether the magnitude of impacts are comparable for ecosystem
quality between the methods, as the units are not the same. The impact category global warm-
ing on terrestrial ecosystems was the highest contribution category for ReCiPe 2016, which is
similar to IMPACT World+, where it was long term climate change when excluding freshwater
ecotoxicity.
This analysis indicates the importance of acknowledging the effect the impact method can
have on the results since different methods may show different results. For the current study,
the overall conclusions were not deemed positively sensitive to the choice of impact method
since the only real discrepancy found is a commonly known concern. For the remainder of this
report, the results obtained with IMPACT World+ are considered the results of this study.

4.2.4.4
Completeness and consistency checks
4.2.4.4.1
Completeness checks
The current study represents a method of comparison between two sampling systems. Not
all relevant information was available as no data was measured by the case company or any
other relevant stakeholders. The manufacturing processes were assumed by a professor at the
Technical University of Denmark, which means that the transforming processes were omitted
due to lack of data. Instead market-based processes were used, which do not cover the actual
transport from the extraction of raw material to the refining and manufacturing of the sampling
system, but instead uses average transport. Furthermore, the processes cover an average use
of electricity for the technologies, which is not machine-specific to the supplier and waste
management companies. However, all actual processes that were identified were included and
modelled in SimaPro. All components of the sampling system were weighted, which gives a high
detail to the amount of materials used in the model. For the medicine, the API was excluded due
to limitations in SimaPro, presenting a completeness issue. Additionally, the current study only
covered the final ingredients of the medicine and not all chemicals, machines, and materials
used for the upstream processes of the medicine production due to lack of data. Including
these would expand the scope of the study but also enhance the level of detail and thereby
completeness. The transport from the supplier and the case company was measured with the
use of an online map, which may not represent the actual route, yet it was deemed the most
likely. For the disposal process it was identified from an interview which waste management
plant was used to handle the waste. However, as not all processes were identified in the disposal
stage, only incineration without heat and power generation was modelled. Including the heat
and power generation would improve the completeness of the model.
As a consequence of these completeness issues, concern should be given to the interpreta-
tion of the results. However, in accordance with the interest of the commissioner, the study has
fulfilled the objective of exemplifying how life cycle assessment can be used as a tool for iden-
tifying environmental impacts of products. For this purpose, the completeness of the current
study is deemed sufficient.

processes (GLO and RoW), as seen in Appendix F. This was due to the availability of data in
SimaPro and various assumptions. Changing the geographical representation of the processes
could change the results of the study, mostly due to transportation of the materials and the

4.2.4.5
Conclusions, limitations, and recommendations
4.2.4.5.1
Conclusions
1. The results of the study indicate small reductions in environmental impacts if the sampling
system uses 150 mL drainage bags compared to 250 mL. The small difference is due to
the structure of the system only being different in the size of the drainage bags. The steel
and polycarbonate used in the tank fitting contributes most to impacts related to human
toxicity and climate change and since the tank fitting is identical for both systems, the
environmental impacts are not much different.
2. For the area of protection human health, the modelled system contributes to environmen-
tal impacts mainly in the impact categories climate change and human toxicity cancer.
For the area of protection ecosystem quality, the system mainly contributes to environ-
mental impacts in the impact category freshwater ecotoxicity and climate change.
3. The materials and manufacturing stage entails the highest impacts across all life cycle
stages on the highest impacting categories, as it accounts for 56 % - 95 % of the total
impacts with the exception of freshwater ecotoxicity, where the disposal stage is the
highest contributor.
4. The use of stainless steel in the tank fitting of the modelled system accounts for 69 % -
71 % of the total impacts for the impact category human toxicity cancer.
5. The medicine being disposed of is not significant to the overall impacts of the system.
6. There is a potential approximate 9 % reduction of total environmental impacts, if the
number of scrapped systems are reduced by 50 % from 641 sampling systems to 320
sampling systems annually.

4.2.4.5.2
Limitations
The major limitations specific to the LCA in the current study are:
1. Transport being the only process that was modelled as a foreground process. This was
due to lack of first hand data for the remaining processes. Only having one process as
the foreground affects the level of detail of the study and for that reason it is difficult to
assess if the results are actual representations of the real world case.
2. Heat and power generation was not included in the model. Therefore, no deduction of
environmental impacts were accounted for and environmental impacts would be lower for
both cases if the heat and power generation was included.
3. The active pharmaceutical ingredient was left out of the model, meaning the medicine
was modelled more or less like saline. This omission was mainly due to a lack of im-
plementation possibilities in SimaPro. Furthermore, only the final buffer solution was
modelled meaning that other chemicals used upstream in the production were excluded.
The impacts would be higher if more chemicals were included.
4.2.4.5.3
Recommendations
A set of recommendations is provided for the case company based on the results of the analysis:
1. It is recommended to look further into the possibilities of implementing the smaller
drainage bags for the sampling system.
Even though the relative difference between
the two sizes may seem small, over time it will lead to a large impact.
2. It is recommended to reduce the amount of sampling systems that expire before they are
used, as this would result in lower environmental impacts across impact categories with
an average reduction of approximately 9 % annually if the waste is reduced by 50 %.
3. It is recommended to look into the possibilities of reducing the amount of stainless steel
in the tank fitting of the sampling system. Since this is not up to the case company, it is
recommended that they initiate a discussion with the supplier to look into the possibilities
of reducing the amount of steel.
The LCA demonstrated that there are potential environmental savings for the case com-
pany if they reduce the sizing of the drainage bags in their sampling systems, and further
savings if they reduce the amount of sampling systems that expire due to exceeded shelf lives.
However, environmental sustainability is not the only aspect to sustainability, and it would be
incomplete to only report on environmental concerns. Therefore, a cost-benefit analysis was
also performed, adding a financial perspective to the comparison of the two sampling systems
previously analysed in the LCA. This analysis is found in the next section.

4.3
Cost-benefit analysis
To address sub-research question 4: ”What are the financial impacts of changing the volume of
the drainage bags for sampling medicine?”, this chapter provides a cost-benefit analysis (CBA)
of the two sampling systems outlined as part of the LCA. One is a system that utilises 250 mL
drainage bags, which is currently being used in the medicine production of the case company,
and the alternative is the exact same system but with 150 mL drainage bags instead. Both
systems consist of a steel and polycarbonate tank fitting, five drainage bags, and ten silicone
tubes. The way the system is used is by connecting the tank fitting to a bioreactor, after which
a sample can be extracted, and the bag is cut and sealed so the medicine can be tested. Upon
completion of the tests, the bag including any remaining medicine and bleach is incinerated.
In presenting the CBA, this chapter presents the identified costs that could justifiably be
monetised, and the discounting of future costs. Then, all assumptions are briefly discussed,
followed by the results.
4.3.1
Methodology
Quantitative and qualitative data was collected in the IT systems and via interviews with the
case company and correspondence with the supplier of the sampling systems and the waste
management company. All data is found in Appendix J.
4.3.1.1
Costs
From the interviews it was identified that the costs of the two sampling systems could be divided
into two categories: the cost of loss of revenue and the disposal cost. The loss of revenue is the
amount that could have been earned on the disposed medicine. The disposal cost is the amount
paid to the waste management company for collecting and handling the waste. Additionally,
for the first year there will be implementation costs for the small system.
4.3.1.1.1
Cost of loss of revenue
The loss of revenue is seen as the potential revenue the case company could have made on
the amount of medicine they dispose of in the drainage bags. The case company add a 5 %
service fee on top of the production costs when invoicing their customers, so by disposing excess
medicine, they in principle loose those 5 % on the amount of medicine they dispose of. This
was calculated by multiplying the price of a batch with the service fee and dividing with the
total volume of that batch. See equation 4.4.

oversees the change.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This chapter provides an overview of the key results and situates the findings within the liter-
ature. Furthermore, it discusses disposal of plastics, the role of customer control for the case
company, and evaluates the appropriateness of methods and limitations to the current study.
5.1
Findings of the study
The study investigated the environmental impact and costs of two similar sampling systems.
The reason for including both analyses was to get an overview of the economic and environ-
mental factors influencing the choice of material for use in the production at a CDMO. For the
environmental comparison of the two systems, we chose to use IMPACT World+ as the impact
method. An awareness of the limitations and underlying assumptions pertinent to the chosen
impact method is important, as impact methods potentially generate different results. This
was investigated by Bulle et al. (2019), who compared IMPACT World+ with ReCiPe 2008
and found that the characterisation models included in the impact methods greatly influence
the results. For the current study, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to test the role of im-
pact methods, which indicated that using ReCiPe 2016 instead of IMPACT World+ resulted
in slightly lower but comparable impacts.
The environmental results found using IMPACT World+ showed that the highest impacts
were mostly attributable to the manufacturing and material life cycle stage with the exception
of freshwater ecotoxicity, where the disposal stage is the highest contributor. This is somewhat
in accordance with the findings of Pietrzykowski et al. (2013), who found that the production
stage entails higher impacts than the end-of-life for single-use systems. However, the study
grouped all upstream activities into ”supply chain”, so a direct comparison is challenging. Sit-
uating the findings further in the literature proved difficult, as comparable studies were very
limited. This validates the basis for the current study, namely the lack of academic focus on
material waste from the pharmaceutical industry.
The two analyses proved that there is both a financial and environmental gain from choos-
ing the smaller system. However, an important aspect of this finding was that both analyses
were conducted with multiple assumptions due to the availability of data, with the main as-
sumption being that only 100 mL of medicine is used for the tests.
The reduction of the

5.2
Disposal of plastics
The sales of medicine has drastically increased in the past few years, which has further in-
creased the use of single-use plastics in the pharmaceutical companies. From investigations and
interviews with relevant personal it was identified that policy makers and regulations indirectly
encourages the companies to keep the use of plastic, as the clinical requirements of the final
products increase. Currently most of the medical waste from the case company is considered
contaminated, as it is in contact with medicine. Literature found that contaminated waste is
being incinerated in most cases, as recycling requires the waste to be disinfected. Generally
studies indicate that single-use systems are less polluting than steel equipment, even though the
petrol-based materials do not have the ability to naturally degrade (Plastics Industry Associate,
2023). In fact, plastic pollution is a huge issue globally, with an estimated 11 million tonnes
of plastics entering the oceans annually in addition to an estimated 200 million metric tonnes
already circulating in marine environments (The Ocean Conservatory, 2015). These numbers
have almost certainly increased since 2015, which is when the estimations were made and stud-
ies indicate that pollution in the pharmaceutical industry has become an element of concern in
the past decade, due to the potential environmental consequences (Agar et al., 2014).
The results of the LCA showed that disposal of waste is the main driver of environmental
impacts in the category of freshwater ecotoxicity with impacts of 63 % and 71 % of total
impacts for the small and large sampling systems, respectively. If the case company and the
rest of the industry are to become more sustainable, changes need to happen to the methods
of handling the waste. Recycling materials is an obvious way to decrease the environmental
impact and secure a more sustainable manufacturing. Including more recycling would likely
reduce the impacts and in addition lower the demand of primary metals such as aluminium
and stainless steel. However, as Blessy et al. (2021) notes, there are challenges to the sorting
and cleaning of medical plastics, which is why Jahnke (2020) argues that an alternative could
be to use bioplastics. A further difficulty in moving to increased circularity however relates to
regulations (EFPIA White Paper on Circular Economy, 2020).
One problem with single-use systems is to what extend we as a society define something
as being single-use.
Glauser et al. (2016) debates the term single-use as with advances in
manufacturing technology, more disposable products has reached the market.
An example
was a breast pump labeled as single-use, which hospital leaders questioned, after which it
Page 63 of 73
==PAGE:75==
Astrid D. Rørdam and
Christian B. Honor´e
Technical University of Denmark
January 2024
was changed to single-patient. In some cases, disposables are more expensive in the long run,
however, in most cases it is a cheaper option than buying more expensive reusable supplies that
must be washed in between use (Glauser et al., 2016). For this reason it is critical to assess if
it is necessary to define a material as being single-use or if it is in fact possible to use again.
5.3
The role of customer control
The structural nature of a CDMO is to produce medicine that a customer either do not have
the resources to do themselves or to expand their production capacity. For this reason, CDMOs
are bound to comply with the customer choices both for supplier- and material choices. The
problem with this structure is that it is not always the most optimal supplier or most sustainable
material that is chosen to be used. From the survey four of the six participants addressed that
they think excessive customer control is a major influence in inventory management and the
sustainability of the company.
The optimal storage levels are not being used, as the case
company tries to fulfill the strict requirements from their customers, which results in much
material expiring due to not being used. Additionally, it was identified in the study that using
a different size drainage bag for sampling systems both reduces the environmental impact and
the financial impact. A more optimal solution therefore exists that would both uphold the
requirements for the testing purposes in QC and also the manufacturing processing. Despite
the LCA and CBA indicating clear environmental and financial benefits, a key consideration
of the implementation of the change is that the structure of the contracts between a CDMO
and their customer requires for the customer to approve any changes.
This is due to the
CDMO being bound by upholding the contracts with the customer. For this reason a change of
sampling system would require the case company to provide sufficient evidence that a change
is needed which could be both costly and timely. It should be noted that these factors and
all necessary interactions with the customers were not included in the current study, as it was
deemed outside the scope. The analysis conducted in this paper is only for one of approximately
2,000 materials being used at the case company. Doing a more detailed analysis of all materials
would likely demonstrate more sustainable choices for other materials as well.

current study

Specific limitations to the LCA has already been presented in Section 4.2.4.5.2. The overarching
goal of the current study was to holistically assess sustainability in a pharmaceutical CDMO.

The approach was to first assess what challenges were perceived to have the highest sustainabil-

but only one perspective was then further analysed, namely the vast amount of materials that
are continuously being disposed of. The details of how the case company would practically
approach changing the underlying causes of the excess inventory was not investigated, since
the purpose of the study was to show the environmental and economical consequences of the
problem rather than how to mitigate it. Thus, a substantial amount of work is still required for
the company to implement changes that would lower their environmental footprint and work
towards their goal of reducing emissions and other impacts. Still, the current study contributes
in educating the case company on their footprint and how methods such as the LCA and CBA
can be used to improve daily operations.
Whilst an LCA is an appropriate methodology to use, the results should be interpreted
carefully since they rely on many methodological choices and assumptions, which will reflect
the real world more or less accurately. Since a comparative approach was used, the relative
improvement between the systems can be concluded, but the study refrain from declaring the
results as being representative for the actual environmental impacts. For the economical as-
sessment, a simple version of a CBA was conducted. From interviews it was identified that the
case company’s customers cover all expenses for the production plus a 5 % service fee, which
made the calculations rather simple since that lowered the amount of costs significantly. In the
end only loss of revenue, disposal costs, and implementation costs were considered relevant for
the analysis and it could be argued that the calculations were thus too simplified.
In conclusion, this study demonstrates how two out of the three pillars of sustainability
can be used to assess a possible change thus ensuring a more holistic approach than if the focus
had solely been on either environmental or financial concerns. However, the third pillar of
sustainability, social sustainability, was excluded from the assessment altogether. The knowl-
edge needed to deploy social sustainability assessment methods was not something the authors
had experience in and thus, the sustainability assessment conducted in the current study lacks
the social perspective. A way the social aspect could have been included would have been to
deploy the sustainable livelihoods framework to assess how human, social, natural, physical,
and financial capital are all reflected in the case and to illustrate any potential differences in
the assessment of whether the case company should initiate a change to switch to the smaller
sampling systems. It could be argued that the survey and interviews work towards including
the social aspect by mapping out the implicit knowledge in each department, but that was not

the key purpose of the analysis.
In addition to the omission of social sustainability, the current study makes no attempt to
assess the absolute sustainability of the analysed case. By presenting the results and relative
differences, the reader may not fully understand the relation to absolute sustainability.
A
decrease in environmental impacts is naturally always positive - provided it does not shift
the problems onto another area or another pillar of sustainability - but it is not within the
realm of the normal reader to instinctively know which amount of CO2, DALYs, or person-year
equivalents is acceptable in the big picture. A scenario in which no environmental impacts occur
is a scenario with no production at all, and that is simply not feasible. A way to deal with this
gap is to shift the focus from eco-efficiency and comparative assessments that indicate relative
improvements to eco-effectiveness that relates a system’s environmental impacts to absolute
sustainability limits. These limits are dictated by the carrying capacity of the ecosystems also
known as planetary boundaries (Fantke & Illner, 2019; M. Z. Hauschild et al., 2020). Efforts
are being done to determine how to allocate allowable environmental impacts to create global
benchmarks but much work is still needed on this topic to increase the accuracy and reduce the
amounts of assumptions and estimations required (Nervil, 2022). For this reason, the absolute
sustainability perspective was left out of the analysis.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This study provides a qualitative and quantitative analysis in response to the research question:
”How can the heavily regulated pharmaceutical industry balance finances and environmental
considerations in their inventory management strategies?” and the main challenges to achieving
this balance.
Currently, there are no processes in place for quantifying the environmental
impacts of the decisions that are made regarding inventory management at the case company,
which means that there is room for improvement.
Literature revealed that single-use systems are beneficial for the sustainability of the phar-
maceutical industry, as it eliminates the need for water injection and steam to clean the equip-
ment, which is the main driver of the impacts in the traditional steel technology. However,
there are still sustainability issues related to the use of single-use systems, mainly due to the
plastic production and material waste. For the pharmaceutical industry, life cycle assessment is
mainly applied within the context of process design and wastewater treatment to comparatively
assess the environmental profiles of alternatives.
Interviews conducted with multiple departments at the case company gave indication to
customer control and internal communication being major issues in improving sustainability.
Customer control force the case company to purchase excess materials and chemicals leading
to higher environmental impacts. This could be indicative for the whole industry, as pharma-
ceutical companies must comply with many rules and regulations. The problem with excess
materials being purchased is further amplified by information not being properly shared be-
tween departments causing a bullwhip effect.
An life cycle assessment compared two similar sampling systems, which both serve the
function of sampling medicine, with the only difference being the size of the drainage bags.
The current system uses 250 mL drainage bags and the alternative case uses 150 mL bags. A
reduction at the midpoint level of approximately 25 % in environmental impacts was found
for ozone layer depletion, freshwater ecotoxicity, and freshwater eutrophication for the smaller
sampling system compared to the larger. For all other impact categories, potential reductions
in environmental impacts of ≤20 % were found. Aggregating the results to areas of protection
saw a 0.3 person-year equivalents reduction for human health, and 4 person-year equivalents
reduction for ecosystem quality for a year worth of sampling systems at the case company. The
relatively small difference is due to the main contributor to the environmental impacts being
the tank fitting, which is the same for both systems.

A cost-benefit analysis saw that while the small system entails implementation cost, there
is a potential saving of almost 50 % annually for the case company if they choose to implement
the smaller sampling system, savings that accumulate to 10.8 million DKK over 20 years. The
total costs were largely dominated by the cost of loss of revenue due to medicine being wasted.
6.1
Recommendations
Based on the results of the analyses and the discussion, a set of recommendations is provided
to the case company:
1. From the interviews conducted with multiple departments at the case company it was
discovered that there is a misalignment between the departments, as the communication
is limited, which impacts the inventory management. For this reason, it is recommended
for the case company to investigate these issues to gain a deeper understanding of the
challenges related to inventory management with the goal of aligning and improving
daily operations. This may be facilitated through workshops, in which employees can
share knowledge and experiences.
2. An important finding was the strict contracts between the case company and the cus-
tomers in addition to the ones between the case company and the suppliers. To improve
inventory management and reduce costs and waste, it is recommended to look into the
possibilities of changing the way in which contracts are negotiated. A higher degree of
flexibility would allow the case company to make choices that could reduce costs and
waste.
3. From the analysis it was identified that there is a potential environmental and financial
gain from investing in the smaller drainage bag. For this reason, it is recommended to
look further into the possibilities of implementing the smaller sampling system.
4. This study only investigated one material of the many materials used at the case company
and it is recommended to analyse more materials in stock using a combination of LCA
and CBA to get an environmental and financial understanding of the repercussions of
the decision-making on material selection and inventory management. Balancing the two
factors is important not least to obtain a competitive advantage.
