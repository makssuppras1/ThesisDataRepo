Abstract 
The state of the art in the additive manufacturing of H13 tool steel allows to achieve a maximum 
hardness of 52 HRC in the as-printed condition, which is not sufficient for some tooling 
applications. Therefore, hardening treatments potentially needed for the printed H13 need to be 
investigated. 
The Aim of this project was to enhance the hardness and wear resistance of 3D-printed H13 tool 
steel, by tailoring its microstructure via thermochemical treatments (gas nitriding and gas 
carburizing) and heat treatments (austenitizing followed by double tempering). 
These treatments were run with the process parameters that are suggested for the traditionally 
manufactured tool steel parts. The effectiveness of each of the processes was systematically 
examined throughout the project, by characterization of the H13 samples via Light Optical 
Microscopy (LOM), X-Ray Diffraction (XRD), Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), Energy 
Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS), Digital Microscopy, Wear Testing and Microhardness 
Testing. 
Gas nitriding increased the surface hardness of the samples up to 70 HRC, leading to a 80-90 µm 
thick hardened layer. In addition, it improved the wear resistance at the surface of the samples by 4 
times. 
Oxidizing the surface of the samples prior to gas nitriding showed to optimize the process, levelling 
out the thickness of the hardened layer.

1. 
Introduction 
At the time of its introduction – late 1980s – additive manufacturing was limited to polymeric 
materials and thus represented an alternative process in the making of prototypes in a more efficient 
way (rapid prototyping).[1] 
The developments that characterized certain fields of engineering (related to materials, control and 
software sciences) in the following decades made it possible to extend the process also to metallic 
materials, allowing the production of mechanically performing parts that could then be used not 
only as prototypes but also as final parts (rapid manufacturing).[2] 
Metal additive manufacturing, popularly known as metal 3d printing, has been revolutionizing 
engineering ever since. Many industries have been influenced by this revolutionary technique, 
leading to a worldwide soaring market with regards to additively manufactured parts and related 
services.[3] Therefore, AM is renowned as one of the most relevant disruptions brought by a 
general increasing tendency of digitization of the manufacturing sector – a phenomenon referred to 
as industry 4.0.[3] 
 
The moulding industry is one of the industrial sectors affected by this technology, due to some 
advantages that it potentially brings along. 
Most importantly, it allows the realization of complex geometries that would be unfeasible using 
traditional manufacturing methods. This geometrical freedom is particularly relevant for the 
molding industry, since it enables the manufacturing of dies featuring internal cavities and channels 
with a complex shape and an optimized location. In processes like injection molding, this may lead 
to conformal cooling during the process, resulting in higher quality products and reduced cycle 
time.[1], [4] 
Among other benefits induced by the adoption of 3d printing, are a reduction in the molds lead time 
and the possibility of create cheaper molds relatively to traditional mold-making methods. [4]  
Nevertheless, there exist some challenges that still need to be addressed and that arise from intrinsic 
characteristics of the process. 
The fast cooling rate typical of 3d printing may indeed lead to excessive residual stresses in the 
built parts, potentially causing geometrical inaccuracy or material failure during post process 
treatments or even during the process itself. [5] 
==PAGE:10==
Moreover, the amount of energy transferred from the laser system to the powder material has 
showed to be related to the amount of microstructural defects -such as porosity- characterizing the 
built parts.[6], [7] 
Residual stresses and porosity formation, together with microstructural inhomogeneity, heavily 
affect the final properties of the tools and are therefore of relevance for the tooling industry. 
Although post processing – namely stress relieving and Hot Isostatic Pressing - has been used to 
cope with these problems, there exist a need for further optimization of the AM in the tooling 
industry.  
Furthermore, the highest bulk hardness achievable with 3d printing of tool steels (around 52 HRC), 
not enough for some applications, highlights the need for experimental studies on the effect that the 
microstructure of the as-printed parts may have on the effectiveness of the surface hardening 
treatments that are usually performed on traditionally manufactured parts. 
 
This essay describes and explains the results of the experimental methods that were performed on 
3D printed tool steel in order to enhance its hardness (over 60 HRC as per external requirement) 
and wear performance.  
These properties were honed by tailoring the as-printed steel microstructure via thermochemical 
treatments and heat treatments commonly used on traditionally manufactured parts.  
The surface and bulk microstructure that were resulting from each step of the procedures were 
systematically assessed and related to the parts´ mechanical and physical properties.  
Microstructure defects and inhomogeneity, typical in additively manufactured products as described 
before, were found to have occurred also in the printing of the investigated parts. Their influence on 
the efficacy of the gaseous surface hardening treatments was assessed. 
AISI H13 steel, commonly used as base material in the tools designed for hot working, was the tool 
steel subjected to the experimental methods and was provided in as-printed condition by Danish 
Technological Institute.

2.1  
Selective Laser Melting (SLM) method: VED, porosity and temperature 
profiles [4]–[6], [8]–[12][7], [13] 
 
The parts subjected to this investigation were 3d printed with the so called Selective Laser Melting 
technique, which belongs to the powder bed fusion AM processes, together with Electron Beam 
Melting method.  
In The former one, a laser beams melts the powder, whereas in the latter an electron beam is used to 
do so. Moreover, unlike the EBM that requires vacuum in the building chamber, in the SLM the 
chamber is filled with inert gas in order to avoid oxidation of the metal. 
As figure shows, in the SLM after a layer of powder is selectively melted, the build piston moves 
downwards, a recoating blade or brush pushes another layer of fresh powder from the powder tank 
to the top of the previously built surface, and the laser scan process repeat. 
Powders used in the SLM of H13 are usually produced with Gas Atomization or Water 
Atomization.  
Figure 1: Illustration of the SLM method 
==PAGE:12==
The SLM process is characterized by a set of adjustable process parameters that determine the 
adopted printing strategy (or method).  
Among the most important process parameters, are the following (referring to figure): 
 
Scanning speed v is the linear velocity of the laser beam in the x-y plane 
 
Hatch h is the distance on the x-y plane between two consecutive and parallel laser paths 
 
Power P is the power of the laser 
 
Layer thickness t is the amount of the displacement along the z-direction - the building 
direction - of the build piston prior to the laser scanning 
 
The scan pattern is the set of directions - on the x-y plane - along which the laser moves: 
the laser direction of displacement may indeed change between two consecutive layers

First of all, an intuitive idea is that the higher the VED value the higher the amount of powder 
particles that are actually melt by the laser beam; vice versa, the lower the energy input, the higher 
the probability of finding some unmelt powder in the built microstructure. therefore at low energy,

dissolution, phenomena that lead to the  formation of near-spherical pores in the printed part. 
Studies showed that vaporization is more likely to occur at the bottom of the melt pools with

First of all, the energy directly transferred from the laser to a potential layer has an influence on the

H13 tool steel is alloyed with strong carbide forming elements such as chromium, molybdenum and 
vanadium in order for large volume fractions of fine alloying carbides to be generated upon cooling. 
These carbides precipitate with a dense distribution and interact with the dislocations hampering 
their movement, ultimately enhancing the mechanical properties of the steel. Among the improved 
properties, is the hardening of the matrix. This effect is therefore also referred to as precipitation 
hardening. 
Alloying carbides may form during solidification of the steel or when it is already in a solid state. 
The former ones are called primary carbides, the latter ones are called secondary carbides. The 
secondary carbides precipitating during tempering of the steel are called tempering carbides. 
The thermodynamic conditions for the existence of each carbide differ and depend on their type. 
The following species are usually found in H13 tool steel. In the notation, M and C respectively 
represent the metal and the carbon in the stoichiometry of the carbide. 
 
MC type mostly appears as primary carbides. They feature a FCC structure and in the H13 
they are usually rich in Vanadium (VC or 𝑉8𝐶7) and Molybdenum (MoC). Depending on 
the chemical composition, their precipitation temperature ranges between 1060 ℃ and 
1328 ℃.  
 
𝑀6𝐶 type is characterized by an FCC crystal structure and in H13 it is usually the 
Molybdenum-rich 𝑀𝑜6𝐶 carbide. Like the previous type, they are renowned to be primary 
carbudes, although they usually dissolve during tempering.  
 
𝑀7𝐶3 is a type of secondary carbides, usually rich in chromium and carrying an hexagonal 
unit cell.  
In H13, they usually precipitate at around 900 ℃, and at around 800 ℃, they are found to 
transform to 𝑀23𝐶6 type. 
 
𝑀23𝐶6 carbides are secondary carbides that in H13 are found to be rich in chromium 
content (𝐶𝑟23𝐶6). They possess a complex FCC structure and they are usually found as 
precipitates in a tempered martensite structure.  
 
𝑀2𝐶 may also be found in H13 as secondary and primary carbides. Usually rich in 
Molybdenum, they feature a hexagonal structure. When secondary, they are found to 
precipitate at around 880 ℃. 
==PAGE:18==
𝑀𝐶, 𝑀23𝐶6 and 𝑀6𝐶 types - respectively rich in vanadium, chromium and molybdenum - are found 
in a highest volume fraction in a tempered H13 tool steel microstructure.  
Primary carbides provided with a strong thermodynamic stability are more likely to resist the 
dissolution at the temperatures typical of the austenitization of steels, remaining in the form of 
rounded particles throughout the matrix. With regards to H13, as figure shows, carbide types as the 
𝑀7𝐶3 are likely dissolved at austenitizing temperature (around 1040 ℃), but stable types as the MC 
are difficult to dissolve at that temperature level.  
During austenitizing and following heat treatments, these non-dissolved carbides may then grow, 
depending on the thermodynamic of the phenomenon. Their coarsening may indeed be energetically 
more favorable than an alternative precipitation of fine particles in the matrix, and therefore may 
result in a decreased secondary hardening of the material and in a grain enlarging phenomenon.   
Microscopy of such a microstructure would then likely present lots of coarse carbides and a very 
small amount of secondary precipites.

2.3  
Microstructure tailoring [17], [19], [21]–[33] 
 
In this project, microstructure tailoring was achieved by performing two classes of treatments on the 
printed h13 tool steel. 
Firstly, a heat treatment was performed on the samples. It consisted in four steps: austenitizing, 
quenching, first tempering, second tempering.  
Secondly, thermochemical treatments - namely gas carburizing and gas nitriding - were performed.  
2.3.1  Heat treatment 
2.3.1.1  Austenitizing and quenching 
This phase represents an essential part of the treatment, since it provides the material with a hard 
and tough microstructure, characterized by fine grains and an oversaturated matrix. For this reason, 
it is commonly referred to as hardening step. 
austenitizing consists in heating the steel up to the equilibrium area of the austenitic phase and is 
kept at that temperature for the time needed for a complete austenitic transformation. Afterwards, 
the material is rapidly cooled down to room temperature – a process called quenching – in order for 
the fully austenitic phase to transform into a martensitic phase. 
Nevertheless, process parameters have to be carefully chosen, since the results of this treatment are 
highly dependent on them. 
With regards to H13 tool steel, the following facts should be taken into account for an optimal 
treatment: 
 
The formation of coarse austenitic grains during the process has to be avoided, since they 
may cause excessive distortion or even cracking upon quenching or following treatments. 
Large grains are also accountable for a softening of a steel. This coarsening phenomenon, 
which occurs when an excessively high austenitization temperature leads to the dissolution 
of all the carbides in the matrix, therefore sets an upper limit to the process temperature. 
For instance, the stable VC carbides in H13 are not dissolved at the typical austenitization 
temperature of this steel. Figure x shows indeed that for H13 steel there is no measured 
grain coarsening at temperature lower than 1060 ℃. 
==PAGE:20==
 
The hardenability and as-quenched hardness of the tool steel increase with the level of 
dissolution of the alloying elements in the matrix, which in turn is higher at higher 
temperature. Figure x shows this trend in the case of H13 hardness.  
 
At a higher level of dissolution of the alloying elements, the probability of retaining some 
austenite upon quenching increases. This occurs because when the amount of elements in 
solid solution is higher, the starting temperature for the martensitic transformation is lower, 
and therefore the amount of austenite that transform into martensite upon quenching is 
lower. This FCC phase, besides being softer than the martensitic one (see figure x), is 
metastable at room temperature and therefore its presence must be eliminated. 
 
Austenitizing soaking time must be enough for the austenitic transformation to fully occur. 
Although it depends on the part dimensions, for H13 tool steel it is suggested to be between 
15 and 30 minutes. 
 
Austenitizing soaking temperature is lower limited also by the range of the austenite phase. 
For H13, as figure shows, austenitizing temperature must be at least 1000 ℃.  
 
Quenching must not be characterized by an excessively high cooling rate, in order to avoid 
cracks due to martensitic transformation. Given the good air-hardenability proper of H13 
steel, quenching in a high speed gas circulating atmosphere is recommended for optimal 
results.

quenched microstructure. Figure x shows a Continuous Cooling Transformation (CCT)

2.3.1.2  Tempering 
Tempering is usually performed on the quenched microstructure, with two scopes: dissolving the 
retained austenitic phase and leading to a further hardening - called secondary hardening - of the 
material. 
It consists of heating the steel up to a temperature that ensure the precipitation of fine alloying 
carbides within the matrix, as well as the simultaneous dissolution of the retained austenite. This 
temperature has to be higher than the working temperature of the steel. 
These tempering carbides are the ones that provide further hardening of the steel and enhance its 
wear performance.  
It is usually done in up to three steps, each one followed by air cooling to room temperature.  
The locations which the carbides precipitate to affect the toughness of the tempered material, and 
they can be controlled with the process parameters.  
Certain combinations of process temperature and duration may for instance lead to a preferential 
carbides precipitation along the grain boundaries, lowering their resistance to failure and therefore 
increasing the chance for intergranular brittle fracture of the specimen during mechanical testing. 
With regards to a 2 hours double steps tempering, for instance, H13 shows a decreased impact 
resistance if tempered at temperature in the range 425 ℃ - 550 ℃, as figure shows. 
 
Figure 11: change of the impact strength of tempered H13 with various tempering temperature; the drop in toughness 
corresponds to the presence of retained austenite (highlighted area)

tempered material may be heavily decreased. Figure x shows this occurrence.

2.3.2  Thermochemical treatments 
 
Thermochemical treatments that involve the diffusion of atoms from a gaseous atmosphere to the 
material surface and later on to the bulk are also called surface hardening chemical treatments. 
Their purpose is to enhance the wear and corrosion resistance of the material, as well as increase its 
hardness. 
Among the variety of such treatments, gas nitriding and gas carburizing were performed on the 3D 
printed H13 tool steel. 
It is important to mention that the gas carburizing treatment was used in the project just to 
investigate the effect that a higher carbon content would have on the tempered microstructure, 
therefore not aiming for a local hardening at the surface of the samples. For this reason, it was 
followed by heat treatment.

The activity of nitrogen in the tool steel, which depends on factors like chemical composition of the 
steel and temperature, is the other chemical parameter that - together with nitriding potential and 
equilibrium constant - influences the effectiveness of the treatment. 
During the process, the diffusion of nitrogen as an interstitial solution continues until the maximum 
value of its solubility in the iron is reached. The locations at which this occurs are usually the active 
centers. Once the steel matrix is saturated with nitrogen, nitrides nucleate and subsequently grow 
and coalesce with each other forming a continuous layer, which is usually referred to as the 
compound layer.  
These nitrides occur usually either in the form of nitrogen-rich  𝜖-𝐹(2−3)𝑁  or  𝛾-𝐹𝑒4𝑁 nitrides. The 
Leher diagram helps in the prediction of nitrides formation in relation to the process temperature 
and chemical potential, in the case of pure iron. Although the precipitation of each type of nitride in 
case of an alloyed steel (such as the H13) may occur at different process parameters than the one 
measured in the diagram, the author - following previous studies - assumed negligible that 
difference in the material used in this investigation. The diagram is presented in figure x. The 
nitriding temperature range suggested for h13 tool steel is highlighted in the diagram. 
Figure 13: Leher diagram for pure iron; it is taken as a reference for the H13; the highlighted area is associated with the 
suggested temperature for nitriding of H13 
==PAGE:26==
This layer plays a very important role in the nitriding process, since it provides the steel with 
excellent wear ad corrosion resistance. Nevertheless, it is important to control the nitrogen 
concentration at the sample surface, since an excessive oversaturation would lead to an excessively 
porous and brittle compound layer, which would then turn into a negative result.  
Underneath the compound layer is the so-called diffusion layer, which is characterized by the 
presence of nitrogen both in interstitial solid solution with the matrix and in the form of nitrides. 
These nitrides, usually alloys of Chromium and Vanadium in H13 tool steel, give rise to 
compressive residual stresses in the surrounding matrix generating an increase in the local hardness 
of the material. 
This layer is therefore very important, particularly for H13 tool steel. 
Figure x shows the result of a scanning electron microscopy (SEM) performed on a gas-nitrided 
H13 tool steel, in which both the compound and the diffusion layer are visible. It is visible the 
typical elongated shape proper of the grains in the diffusion layer.

Studies on how to improve the efficiency of gas nitriding have been carried out.  
One of the significant factors that was found to affect the process outcome is the initial surface 
condition of the material. A rough surface along with the presence of pre-formed oxides on it 
indeed showed to have a positive impact.  
During the nitriding process, indeed, the iron nitrides previously formed with the oxidation are 
likely to be reduced. The products of this reduction - the metallic iron and the molecular oxygen - 
makes it possible to accelerate the ammonia dissociation rate: the former acts as an active site for 
the catalytic dissociation of ammonia and the latter reacts with the hydrogen forming 𝐻2𝑂 which is 
subsequently desorbed from the surface. This increased pace generates a higher nitrogen 
concentration gradient in the material, therefore ultimately improving the diffusion rate.  
Moreover, researchers found that the formed oxide lattice features a lot of crystal defects, which 
improves the rate of diffusion of nitrogen in the steel, leading to a relatively higher concentration of 
it in both the layers. 
A pre-treatment which ensures the creation of these oxides is usually therefore referred to as 
“activation of the surface”. The nanosized oxide stratum - which in H13 tool steel is composed 
mainly by 𝐹𝑒2𝑂3 as well as 𝐶𝑟2𝑂3 - is obtained heating the sample up to about 400°C in an open 
atmosphere furnace and keeping it at that temperature for around 1 hour.  
It is recommended that prior to the treatment the H13 tool steel is hardened (austenitized and 
quenched) and tempered at a temperature at least 25 ℃ - 50 ℃ above the nitriding temperature.  
As far as process parameters, a soaking temperature within the range 490 ℃ - 530 ℃ is 
recommended for H13 tool steel, with a process time ranging from 6 to 30 hours and that depends - 
among other factors - on the needed nitriding depth.

2.3.2.2  Gas Carburizing 
 
Gas carburizing treatments may be done in two different kind of systems. 
While in the industrial processes a mix of air and natural gas (namely methane) – previously mixed 
by a separate unit – is introduced in the furnace atmosphere as the carburizing gas, there exist the 
possibility to use furnaces that do not provide the mixing unit and therefore introduce the various 
gases using a separate piping system for each of them. 
The mix of air and natural gas that distinguish the former kind of carburizing is often referred to as 
endothermic gas, or more simply endogas.  
Even though such an atmosphere is characterized by the occurrence of tens of chemical reactions, 
only the following three are important for determining the rate of carbon transfer during the 
process: 
2𝐶𝑂→𝐶𝐹𝑒+ 𝐶𝑂2 
𝐶𝐻4 →𝐶𝐹𝑒+ 2𝐻2 
𝐶𝑂+ 𝐻2 →𝐶𝐹𝑒+ 𝐻2𝑂 
Therefore, based on the three reactions, gaseous components like CO or 𝐶𝐻4 are referred to as 
carburizing agents. The products of reaction 1 and 2, though, has an opposite effect, since they react 
with the carbon of the material. Therefore they are referred to as decarburizing agents. 
The ratio of these two components determine the carburizing potential in the furnace. The gradient 
between its value and the carbon content at the steel surface is the driving force of the carburizing, 
therefore the gas mixture of these two types of agents must be tailored in such a way that the carbon 
potential is kept high during the treatment. 
Nevertheless, the formation of soot on the surface of the sample represents the upper bound for the 
value of the carbon potential. The soot is a layer of impure carbon particles deposited on the surface 
of the material that has a negative effect on the outcome of surface hardening. Studies found that the 
occurrence of sooting is related to the amount of residual 𝐶𝐻4 in the furnace and it is mostly 
generated via the reaction x. Therefore, it is of interest to keep track of the amount of this 
hydrocarbon in the atmosphere during the typical industrial process.  
The presence of the decarburizing agents as by-products in the furnace is the reason why in 
industrial carburizing the atmosphere is usually enriched with additional hydrocarbons that are 
introduced in the furnace via a separate line.  
They are divided into unsaturated and saturated hydrocarbons. Belonging to the former category are 
==PAGE:29==
gases like 𝐶2𝐻4 and 𝐶3𝐻6, whereas 𝐶𝐻4 and 𝐶3𝐻8 are typical examples of gases coming from the 
latter division. Since saturated hydrocarbons are less likely to decompose in the atmosphere before 
coming in contact with the steel surface, and thus forming free carbon likely to cause sooting, they 
are usually preferred for carburizing over the unsaturated gases. 
These added gases indeed inhibit the oxidation of the surface by reacting with those agents, 
producing CO and 𝐻2 (see reaction) and therefore increasing the carbon potential of the 
atmosphere. 
𝐶𝐻4 + 𝐶𝑂2 →2𝐶𝑂+ 2𝐻2 
𝐶𝐻4 + 𝐻2𝑂→𝐶𝑂+ 3𝐻2 
The efficiency of this action depends on the chemical features of the hydrocarbon. 
As mentioned before, the saturated gases propane and methane are the most commonly used 
additions. Nevertheless, researchers found that propane enrichment has a better effect with respect 
to methane addition, since it enhances the evolution of the carbon potential in the furnace in a more 
efficient fashion.  
A chemical composition difference between the two candidates, and more specifically a different 
carbon to hydrogen atoms ratio of the two, is one of the reason for the higher efficiency. Such a 
ratio influences indeed the amount of carbon atoms that can be introduced in the furnace per 
hydrogen atom and it therefore affects the carbon potential in the furnace. 
The second reason has to be found in the thermodynamic of the occurring chemical reactions. 
Whereas natural gas directly reacts with endothermic carrier gases reducing 𝐻2𝑂 and 𝐶𝑂2 
concentrations, propane enrichment leads to an intermediate step of dehydrogenation, namely 
𝐶3𝐻8 →𝐶+ 2𝐶𝐻4 
The carbon produced in the reaction gives rapid raise to carbon concentration in the furnace and 
thus enhances the rate of carbon potential evolution. Nevertheless its unfortunate propensity to 
cause sooting, raise the problem of controlling the flow rate of propane during the treatment.  
On the other hand, the formed methane moles reduce the carburizing agents following the reaction x 
with a higher rate, since for every enriching mole of propane two moles of methane are generated. 
When a furnace that does not provide a mixing unit is used, the chemical reactions occurring in the 
atmosphere are different. In the following, a situation in which air is not introduced in the system is 
considered, in relation to what was used in the investigation. Additionally, a system that ensures a 
flow of propene, argon and hydrogen is considered, again in line to what was used during the 
==PAGE:30==
investigation. 
In such a situation, the occurring decomposition reactions are the following 
2𝐶3𝐻6 →3[𝐶] + 3𝐶𝐻4 
𝐶𝐻4 →[𝐶] + 2𝐻2 
and the total reaction is characterized by: 
𝐶3𝐻6 →3[𝐶] + 3𝐻2 
Like the previous type of gas carburizing, the carbon potential in such a system is given by the ratio 
between the partial pressures of the reactants and products of the carburizing reaction:

The last important parameter in the treatment is obviously the temperature. Carburizing is 
characterized by high process temperature, relatively to some other surface hardening treatments

Process optimization 
A thin foil of H13 (150 µm thick) was subjected to a carburizing treatment in which the propene-to-
hydrogen ratio was constantly increased by adjusting the flow rates of the two gases with the 
flowmeter.  
The size of this increment was very small, since sooting phenomenon had to be avoided. 
Specifically, every time the propene flow rate was increased of 1 ml/min, the hydrogen rate was 
decreased of 1 ml/min. 
Each propene-to-hydrogen flow ratio was kept constant for some minutes, in order for the chemical 
equilibrium to be reached in the furnace and in the material. Monitoring the weight gain of the 
sample, it was possible to understand when the equilibrium had occurred: equilibrium was indeed 
coinciding with a stop in the weight gain. 
The optimal set of flow rates was defined as the one leading to an acceptable carburization (that is, 
weight gain) without causing soot formation on the surface of the sample.

4.2.4  Carburized condition  
4.2.4.1  Microhardness measurements 
microhardness measurements performed on the carburized sample suggested that the combination 
of carburizing and heat treatment had led to an increase of C content throughout the thickness of the 
sample. The bulk hardness of the sample – 422 HV (average of 10 measurements) - was indeed 
considerably higher than the one characterizing the non-carburized tempered samples (see section). 
moreover, the hardness profile of the carburized samples did not show a clear difference in the 
hardness of the surface compared to the hardness of the bulk. The flatness of the curve – shown in 
figure x – therefore suggests that the carbon concentration in the sample was quite homogeneous. 
This may be explained by the relatively high surface-to-volume ratio of the sample, which ensured a 
relatively fast distribution of the carbon.   
 
 
These results may therefore contribute to strengthen the explanation, hypothesized by the author, 
for the low hardness of the non-carburized sample (see section).  
the main difference between the two conditions is indeed the form in which the carbon is found. 
in the non-carburized samples, the cooling conditions proper of the printing strategy may have led 
to an excessive formation of coarse MC-type carbides (rich in Molybdenum, Vanadium and 
Titanium) that depleted the steel matrix of the most part of the carbon. The lack of carbon may then 
have caused the secondary hardening to not occur upon tempering, leading to a soft microstructure 
typical of an annealed condition.  
on the other hand, the carburizing process enriched the steel with carbon that was not trapped in the 
stable MC-type carbides, that precipitated upon tempering in the form of fine secondary carbides 
leading to an increase of the hardness of the material.  
 
==PAGE:74==
The weight of the specimen after tempering was used to quantify the increase in the carbon 
concentration caused by gas carburizing. To simplify the calculations, the author assumed that the 
carbon content was homogeneous in the material. This assumption seemed reasonable, given the 
flatness of the hardness profile shown in figure x. 
the new carbon concentration in the sample was then calculated with the formula:

Comparing the XRD graphs with the non-carburized samples, it was possible to assess that the 
carburized treatment led to a broadening of the peaks. Since the broadening of a peak is generally 
due to a microstrain of the crystal lattice, the XRD results may therefore indicate that the 
carburizing led to an increase in the number of secondary carbides precipitated upon tempering.

5. 
Discussion 
As mentioned multiple times in the essay, the investigation aimed to enhance hardness and wear 
resistance of a 3D printed H13 tool steel by tailoring its microstructure.  
While no particular requirements were given on the wear performance that the potential application 
demanded from the steel, it was instead required a hardness of at least 60 HRC. 
The goal of the project was achieved, since by performing a gas nitriding on the tempered samples 
it was possible to achieve a surface hardness of 72 HRC, as well as an effective hardened case depth 
ranging from 60 µm to 90 µm depending on the type of sample. Additionally, the process allowed 
to improve the wear resistance - measured with the depth of the wear tracks - by 4 times. 
 
Even though the study undoubtedly showed that a 3d printed tool steel may be successfully tailored 
to meet the requirements of its potential application, it also clearly revealed what are the main side 
effects of the metal additive manufacturing on the properties of the printed parts. 
The various methods used for the samples characterization indeed suggested that porosities 
(whether it was due to lack of fusion or gas formation), microstructural defects and inhomogeneity, 
irregular element segregation and abnormal precipitation of secondary phases all affected the 
mechanical properties (macro and micro) and the microstructures that were resulting from the heat 
treatment of the parts. 
The project therefore confirmed the need for further optimization of the SLM technique, in order to 
ultimately eliminate all the above mentioned features from the printed products. 
Particularly, the author believes that the best critical approach to the problem likely implies holding 
the VED of the printing strategy accountable for the microstructural flaws and aberration. 
This belief arises from the findings that were observed during the development of the project and 
that resulted from the systematic comparisons that were made between the type-1 samples and the 
type-2 samples. The opinion is also strengthened by the results of some investigations that were 
performed prior to this project and that experimentally showed that there exist a VED value for the 
3D printing of H13 tools steel that potentially leads to an almost full dense as-printed 
microstructure.[35]  
 
==PAGE:79==
Given the comparison between this optimal value and the conclusions of this investigation, the 
author hypothesizes that the VED used for printing the samples may have been either too low (type-
1) or too high (type-2). 
Therefore the author would humbly advices to carry out a new printing process adopting a printing 
strategy that would tailor VED to the optimal value (100 𝐽/𝑚𝑚3), surely taking into account the 
limitation of the SLM equipment and certainly setting each parameter (scan velocity, layer 
thickness, hatch, laser power) to a reasonable value. 
 
In closing, although processes like HIP, stress relieving and hardening treatments may represent 
ways to cope with the side effects of 3D printing of tool steel, there clearly exists a need for further 
advancement on the technology (e.g. better process control and in-situ temperature measurements) 
designated to reduce the occurrence of those side effects, leading to printed parts that would not 
anymore need expensive and time consuming post-treatments before entering the market.

6. 
Conclusion  
The microstructure of 3D printed H13 tool steel was tailored using treatments and respective 
parameters that are usually recommended for traditionally manufactured H13. 
The two printing strategies resulted in two very different as-printed microstructures. 
The printing strategy indeed showed to have affected: 
 
Grain size 
 
Bulk and surface hardness 
 
Presence of retained austenite 
 
Shape of porosity 
 
Precipitation of carbides 
As a consequence, an identical standard heat treatment led to two different sets of tempered 
microstructures and mechanical properties.  
Type-1 tempered samples were characterized by:  
 
Bulk microhardness of 550 𝐻𝑉0,025; 
 
Elongated grains typical of tempered martensite 
 
Fine carbides (mostly of 0,2 µm size) 
 
Fine grains  
Type-2 tempered samples were characterized by:  
 
Bulk microhardness of 250 𝐻𝑉0,025 
 
Grain morphology typical of an annealed tool steel 
 
 Coarse carbides (with size up to 0,7 µm) 
 
Coarse grains 
 
==PAGE:81==
Nevertheless, an identical standard gas nitriding (with a pre-surface oxidation) performed on the 
tempered samples led to very similar nitrided layers, in terms of maximum achievable hardness, 
effective nitrided layer depth and wear resistance: 
 
Surface microhardness of 1300 𝐻𝑉0,025 (average over the two types of samples) 
 
Effective nitrided layer depth of at least 65 µm 
 
Improvement of wear resistance by 4 times (compared to as-printed condition)
