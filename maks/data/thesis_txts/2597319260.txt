Abstract
Accidents have over several years plagued the construction industry where different steel reinforced concrete structures
have experience critical conditions under a thermal load often with a fatal outcome. It is therefore important to improve
the way concrete is modelled at elevated temperatures in order to obtain a more accurate and thus reliable result. A prob-
lem with discretization of the concrete mesh is however present in current models, whereas a convergence problem is very
likely in ﬁnite element model (FEM) analysis.
The aim of this thesis is to construct a 2-dimensional reinforced concrete slab capable of predicting a structural response
when exposed to a thermal load. An additional aim is to identify problems with mesh convergency and limitations related
to the mesh size.
The geometry and material properties used to model the ﬁnal cross-section is based on a proposed tunnel where a rein-
forced concrete slab is creating a false ceiling in order to give a continuous surface to mount a ventilation system. The
slab is simpliﬁed down to a simple support at one end, and a simple support with a roller on the other end, with a free
span of 10.2 m. The mechanical loads applied upon the slab is considered as a uniformly distributed load, whereas the
ﬁre is based on a standard ﬁre. The mechanical and thermal nonlinearities of the concrete and reinforcement steel at am-
bient and elevated temperatures are similarly implemented into the material model. The ﬁnal 2D slab is validated through
deconstruction of the model into smaller sections namely a 1D steel model, 1D concrete model, 2D concrete model and a
steel reinforced 2D concrete model before the ﬁnal slab geometry is considered.
The global behavior of the concrete slab is investigated in a pushover analysis, along with a mechanical and thermal load
whereby a critical temperature is determined for the model. The ability of the model to capture the tensile and compressive
behaviour throughout each load step is similarly investigated whereby a discrepancy between the ultimate compression
and tensile strength is found in comparison to the stresses observed within the numerical model established in Abaqus.
The numerical model proved to be better at capturing the ultimate tensile force which is observed in multiple analysis
where the ultimate compression force only is approached at the failure point within the thermal analysis.

3
Introduction
3.1
Motivation of study
It is possible to decrease the travel time by constructing tunnels, but these tunnels must be protected adequately in order
to protect the users along with the structural system. Multiple incidents have occurred during the last years whereas this
protection has been proved insufﬁcient. This could for instance be the ﬁre originating from a truck in the Mont Blanc Tun-
nel that took the lives of 39 people, and inﬂicted millions worth of damage to the structure as shown in ﬁgure 1 [1]. This
accident among others forced the EU to issue a directive on tunnel safety, along with enforcing the need for a mandatory
risk assessment for all road tunnels longer than 500 m [6].
Figure 1: Mont Blanc Tunnel following the ﬁre in 1999 [1].
Many of the accidents that occurred have vehicles involved including heavy goods vehicles, whereas the high ﬁre load
imposed a high demand on the structure. Five large scale experiments were therefore conducted in the Runehamar tunnel
in Norway [7]. Publications have focused on the heat release rate [8], gas temperature at the ceiling [9], ﬁre spread and
ﬂame length [10]. Previous analysis concerning the structural system in a tunnel has likewise been conducted in relation
to impact and ﬁre load [11]. This investigation assessed the bearing capacity of the structure with varying ﬁre loads. These
analyses served to uncover the behaviour of concrete when exposed to ﬁre, but additional research is still required. “The
behaviour of concrete in ﬁre is not well characterised at present, and further research is required in almost every aspect of
this ﬁeld” [12].
Concrete is one of the most commonly used materials throughout the construction industry. The material have therefore
been the subject of numerous different research papers. Many of these papers have aim to improve or develop an accurate
formulation of numerical models using ﬁnite element method. The FEM method take an existing member or structure
and discretizes it into ﬁnite elements in which the structural response is evaluated. The ﬁnite element model is estimated
on the basis of a triaxial stress state on the basis of a given yield criterion. This criterion is deﬁned through tensile and
compressive stress-strain relations.
One of the problems that concrete structures imposes on the ﬁnite element method consists of the fact that localization
of stresses will form due to the material cracking in the tension zone or crushes in the compression zone. It is therefore
important to conﬁgure the tensile material model dependent on the discretization of the ﬁnite element model according to
the element size limitations in order to ﬁnd convergence for a changing mesh size and improved modelling of the concrete
plasticity and cracking which is one of the primary focuses in this thesis.

3.2
Objective and method
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the issue associated with modelling of reinforced concrete in a ﬁnite element pro-
gram, and propose a method that can be used when modelling a concrete slab member exposed to a standard ﬁre.
The ﬁnal slab member considered within this thesis is found within a proposed tunnel design. The tunnel design is a circu-
lar tunnel section with a suspended ceiling consisting of a reinforced concrete slab supported by a steel proﬁle suspended
from the top of the circular tunnel section down to the midpoint of the slab. The slab is similarly supported at each end
through an steel anchorage connection. The reinforced concrete slab is used to support a ventilation system suspended
from the slab. The speciﬁc details on the weight and the position of the ventilation system on the slab is unknown. It is
therefore assumed during the mechanical analysis of the slab that the ventilation system is a uniformly distributed load on
the surface of the slab and that this load corresponds to 60 % of the ultimate load of the slab obtained through a pushover
analysis.
The ﬁnite element program that will be used throughout this thesis is Abaqus. The reinforced concrete slab member will
be deconstructed into smaller problems that is analysed and validated. The validation of these simpler problems will be
done on the basis of analytical calculations or previously established numerical models.
Firstly, the elastic and plastic uniaxial compressive and tensile behaviour of concrete is investigated. The elastic function
is used to model the elastic behaviour and the smeared cracking plasticity model is used to model the in-elastic behaviour
of the concrete. Then the thermal degradation of Young’s modulus and the compressive and tensile strength and strain are
implemented in the numerical model and the behaviour is observed from uniaxial loading. From this the reinforcement is
introduced into the model and the interaction is modelled. The beam is then subjected to bending in a pushover analysis
and the behaviour is compared with analytical solutions. The next part of the validation is to ensure that the beam is able
to capture the tensile and compressive behaviour of reinforced concrete at elevated temperature. The global behaviour is
compared using static incremental and arc length method to solve a pushover analysis. Lastly, the one-dimensional heat
transfer from the standard ﬁre is added. The temperature proﬁle in the section is added to the nodes at the corresponding
depths. The global behaviour of the reinforced concrete beam is then investigated.
The validated properties from the conducted numerical analysis of the numerical model are transferred to the ﬁnite ele-
ment model used in the case study. The behaviour of the tunnel slab is modelled and analysed as a simply supported beam
exposed to a standard ﬁre.
The slab is investigated as a two-dimensional beam member with a width of 0.15 m and a height of 0.35 m. The width of
the slab chosen for the investigation is based on the spacing of the longitudinal reinforcement which is 0.15 m. The beam
has two layers of reinforcement. A reinforcement bar at the bottom with a cover layer of 55 mm and one at the top with a
cover layer of 35 mm. The beam is assumed simply supported based on the drawings of the tunnel cross-section and the
support generated by the centrally steel proﬁle is neglected.
The beam is subjected to a one-dimensional heat transfer of a standard ﬁre affecting the bottom of the slab. The top of
the beam is assumed isolated from the ﬁre. The edges of the beam are also considered isolated from the ﬁre as they are
connected to the surrounding tunnel structure which in the ﬁre scenario used does not take into account heating of the
surrounding structure. The duration of the standard ﬁre is set to 90 min in the numerical analysis to analyse the structural
behaviour of the reinforced concrete beam subjected to ﬁre exposure. The behaviour is investigated through a pushover
analysis and a case of 60 % ultimate load and ﬁre exposure at the bottom of the beam.
3.3
Literature review
It is investigated which relevant research studies are made in the ﬁeld of numerical analysis of reinforced concrete beams
and tunnel sections at elevated temperatures. In this section some research studies are presented along with the conclusion
of these.
Ozbolt et al. [13] developed a 3D numerical analysis of reinforced concrete beams exposed to elevated temperature fol-
lowed by mechanical loading as four-point bending. The 3D numerical prediction of load-deﬂection curves, temperature
2
==PAGE:12==
ﬁelds, strains and stresses and crack patterns are compared with experimental results. It is found that the 3D numerical
model is capable of reproducing the experimental results very well. This is concluded based on numerical results calcu-
lated compared with the experimental counterparts.
The performance of a concrete slab based on 6 full scale experiment subjected to a ﬁre curve corresponding to the English
BS476 and a more severe hydrocarbon ﬁre curve is investigated by Ali et al. [14]. The temperature proﬁle is in these
experiments measured at three different depths of the slab, whereas this is translated into a ﬁnite element model using the
DIANA software. The concrete model established within this investigation has the reinforcement modelled as embedded
reinforcement with a non-linear transient analysis. This results in this report manged to return numerical results compa-
rable to the experimental results in some test, but other produces diverged results.
Zhang et al. [11] developed a numerical model to predict the response of tunnel lining structure under impact and ﬁre
loading. This numerical model is designed to predict the impact resistance of the lining exposed to ﬁre loading and
explosion. The numerical results of the impact resistance and the structural ﬁre resistance are analysed. It is concluded
that impact resistance is signiﬁcantly decreased due to ﬁre exposure. It is also found that the ﬁre resistance is signiﬁcantly
reduced if the tunnel lining is impacted by a blast load prior to the ﬁre exposure.
3.4
Outline of thesis
The thesis is divided into two main parts. The ﬁrst part describes the theoretical background that contains and describes
every part of the numerical model used to analyse the case study of the tunnel slab. This include the theory of 1D heat
transfer and the application of this. It also covers the material model of concrete and steel used in the numerical model and
the functions used in Abaqus. Furthermore, it contains a description of the solution methods and the interaction function
used to create the interaction between the reinforcement and the concrete along with the rigid body function applied in the
cantilever beam used to create the ﬁxed support. The theoretical background also covers the elements used in the ﬁnite
model and their size limitations along with the load application.
The second part of the thesis consists of a validation of the individual parts in the numerical model. This include the ma-
terial behaviour at ambient and elevated temperature found through numerical analysis of the beam subjected to uniaxial
forces. Furthermore, it includes the pushover analysis used to investigate the behaviour of the numerical model subjected
to bending both at ambient and elevated temperature. It also includes the analysis of the behaviour of the reinforced
concrete beam at 60 % of the ultimate load and subjected to 1D heat transfer subjected to bending similar to that of the
case study. Lastly, the second part of the thesis consists of the case study and the associated analysis.

4.1.4
Reinforcement
It is not necessary to calculate a separate temperature curve for the reinforcement bar. This is due to the surrounding
concrete cooling the bar and ultimately the temperature of the bar is equal to that of the concrete at the same point
for a plain concrete cross-section. This is conﬁrmed through experiments up to 3 % reinforcement ratio for various
combinations of cross-section, reinforcement and concrete. The means that the temperature of the reinforcement bar can
be calculated as the temperature of the concrete at that point [3].

4.5.2
Smeared cracking model
Another way to create the non-linear material behaviour of concrete is through the smeared cracking model in Abaqus.
The initial part of the compressive and the tensile part of the material behaviour is linear and following this the compressive
behaviour is usually non-linear for concrete. The tensile behaviour can either be bi-linear or non-linear depending on how
17
==PAGE:27==
it is desired to model the softening of the tensile part of the concrete after the failure point is reached. The material
behaviour of concrete is displayed in ﬁgure 27.
Figure 27: Uniaxial behaviour of plain concrete [5].
In the smeared cracking model the cracking in the concrete is initiated as the failure surface is reached. The model does not
track the cracks in the concrete hence the name. This material model is usually used for continuous loading until cracking
or crushing occurs at the ultimate tensile and compression stress respectively. As e.g. the ultimate tensile strength is
reached the cracks are affecting the material behaviour at the associated integration point and is taken into consideration
in the constitutive calculations [5].
The smeared cracking model can be used to model all types of concrete structures but is mainly used for modelling of
concrete used in analysis of reinforced concrete structures where monotonic straining is applied to the structure.
4.5.3
Behaviour of damage plasticity and smeared cracking model
According to Chaudhari and Chakrabarti [21] the differences between the two concrete plasticity models have been noted.
It appears that the damage plasticity model is very mesh sensitive opposed to the smeared cracking concrete model. For
the smeared cracking model no difference is found when reﬁning the mesh whereas a small difference is found for the
damage plasticity model. It is found that the damage plasticity model overestimates the strength of the concrete for larger
mesh sizes whereas no change is found for the smeared cracking material model. According to [21] the smeared cracking
model is preferred due beneﬁts mentioned above. This way of modelling the concrete plasticity is chosen for the analysis
performed in this thesis based on the experiences in [21] and own experiences.
4.5.4
Failure ratios
In Abaqus it is required to specify the failure ratios which indicate the failure surface of the concrete model for different
parameters. The ﬁrst failure ratio deﬁned is the ratio between the ultimate biaxial compressive stress and the ultimate
uniaxial compressive stress. In Abaqus the default is 1.16 which is also used in this analysis.
The second failure ratio is deﬁned as the ”absolute value of the ratio of uniaxial tensile stress at failure to the uniaxial
compressive stress at failure” [5]. Abaqus defaults to 0.09 but in this thesis it depends on the type of concrete is used.
The third is the ”ratio of the magnitude of a principal component of plastic strain at ultimate stress in biaxial compression
to the plastic strain at ultimate stress in uniaxial compression” [5]. Abaqus defaults to 1.28 which is also used in this
analysis.
18
==PAGE:28==
The ﬁnal failure ratio used in this analysis is the ”ratio of the tensile principal stress value at cracking in plane stress,
when the other nonzero principal stress component is at the ultimate compressive stress value, to the tensile cracking
stress under uniaxial tension” [5]. Abaqus defaults to 0.33 which is also used in this analysis.

4.6.2
The arc length method
The arc length method proposed by Riks in 1979 is used in order to solve non-linear systems of equations where an
instability presents itself. This could for instance be at the point where a structure is unable to support an additional
loading applied upon it or when the nonlinear material considered becomes unstable. The primary difference between
the arc length method and the Newton-Raphson method is the ability to have the variation in displacement, ∆u, and load
vector coefﬁcient, ∆λ, unknown, where Newton’s method estimates the ∆u through iterations based on a given ∆λ. The
equilibrium equation for the arc length method can thus be written as:
R(u′, λ′) = Fint(u0 + ∆u) −(λ0 + ∆λ)q = 0
(30)
The equation above is normally not satisﬁed through this alone, whereas a correction needs to be added in order to obtain
equilibrium. This is done through the addition of δu and δλ as shown in the equation below:
R(u′, λ′) = Fint(u0 + ∆u + δu) −(λ0 + ∆λ + δλ)q = 0
(31)

4.7
Interaction
4.7.1
Embedded regions
A technique to embed elements in Abaqus is using the embedded region function. To use this function the embedded re-
gion must be deﬁned along with the host region. This embedded region could for instance be a reinforcement bar and the
host region could be a concrete element. The translational degrees of freedom are eliminated when using the embedded
element technique and the nodes are constrained to the host region if the element is within the host. It is possible for the
embedded element to have rotational degrees of freedom if it is allowed by the element deﬁnition. It is possible for e.g. a
beam element but not a truss element.
For the embedded element to function with the host region the translational degrees of freedom must be identical.
It is recommended by Abaqus to specify a set of host elements if it is not desired to constrain all nearby embedded nodes
in case the nodes are near discontinuities such as e.g. cracks.
When specifying embedded elements nearby host nodes are not included.
It is possible in Abaqus to deﬁne the geometric tolerance to ensure the embedded region is not beyond an acceptable tol-
erance set by the user. The placement of the nodes of the embedded elements relative to the host elements is determined
20
==PAGE:30==
by one or two tolerances. One tolerance is based on the average of the non-embedded element size in the model. By
default this is 0.05 as a multiplication factor of the element size. This is called the fractional exterior tolerance. This
can be adjusted if desired by the user. The second is the absolute exterior tolerance which by default is set to 0. If both
these tolerances are used the lesser of the two tolerances are chosen by Abaqus as the geometric tolerance. By default the
nodes of the embedded elements are therefore placed at the exact position of the geometrically placed embedded region
while using the absolute exterior tolerance of 0. If one or more nodes are placed outside the acceptable tolerance and thus
making it impossible to embed the elements an error message will be displayed to the user.
If the embedded nodes are placed near host nodes it is computationally efﬁcient to use the weight factor roundoff tolerance
to adjust the position of the embedded nodes. It must be noted that for large weight factor roundoff tolerance could see
the meshing change in an unintended way and must be reviewed by the user if a large factor is used.
If an embedded node is overconstrained Abaqus will display an error message. If a boundary condition is added to an
embedded node this boundary condition will be neglected and Abaqus will priorities the embedded region constraint.
4.7.2
Rigid Body
The rigid body constraint is used to model the ﬁxed support of the reinforced concrete beam. This constraint is modelled
in the interaction section of Abaqus. In this section the rigid body and its application is explained.
A rigid body is deﬁned as a collection of e.g. nodes that move according to a reference node during simulation. The
collection of e.g. nodes in the rigid body do not deform but can perform rigid body motion in accordance to the rigid
body reference node. The reference node must be speciﬁed in Abaqus otherwise the reference node is selected as the
global origin of the model by default. It is possible to add boundary conditions to the rigid body which is usually done by
specifying the boundary conditions at the reference node of the rigid body. From the boundary condition at the reference
node reaction forces and moments can be found and displayed making it easy to observe e.g. the global behaviour of a
structure. It is possible to apply boundary conditions at any of the nodes in the collection of nodes of the rigid body but
Abaqus will transfer it to the reference node of the rigid body.
The collection of nodes can be pin nodes which only has translational degrees of freedom or both translational and
rotational degrees of freedom. When selecting which type of collection of nodes in Abaqus, it must be know which
degrees of freedom the nodes have. For e.g. truss elements that only have translational degrees of freedom the pin nodes
must be chosen. For e.g. CPS8 elements that have both translational and rotational degrees of freedom the tie nodes must
be chosen. In ﬁgure 28 a visual representation of the collection of nodes and their behaviour is shown.
Figure 28: Visual representation of the collection of nodes and their behaviour.
From the ﬁgure it is shown that the rotation is directly applied to the nodes shared with the shell element for the tie nodes
whereas this is not the case for the nodes shared with the shell element for the pin nodes.

4.8
Structural and continuum elements
4.8.1
Truss element - T2D2
Truss elements are used within ﬁnite element models in two and three dimensional models. The degrees of freedom within
a truss element does not allow any moments or forces perpendicular to the center line to be supported.
The elements used to established the embedded reinforcement within the reinforced concrete model is of the type T2D2.
The naming convention of this element shows that it is a truss element deﬁned through the initial ’T’. It acts in a two-
dimensional space deﬁned by the ﬁrst ’2D’, and it has a total of 2 nodes.
The truss element used only has two nodes where only a vertical and horizontal displacement can occur. A linear inter-
polation is created in between these two end nodes, whereas a constant stress should be observed throughout the entire
element. The two-dimensional truss can be used to deﬁne a master surface for contact application where it should be
ensured that the direction of the master surface is outward normal to ensure proper detection.
Another element that could be used when establishing the reinforcement could be the T2D3 element, whereas an additional
node is applied at the center of the truss. This element is based on a quadratic interpolation for position and displacement.
The application of this type of element is often used to model a curved reinforcement cable. This could for instance be a
prestressed tendon restricted within concrete. A depiction of the T2D2 and T2D3 element can be seen below.
Figure 29: The truss elements T2D2 and T2D3.
4.8.2
Shell element - CPS8
The concrete is modelled through the use of a shell element of the type CPS8. This element has a total of 8 nodes deﬁned
by ’8’, and is a continuum plane stress element. All the nodes in the CPS8 element is located at the boundary, with one
at each corner, and one at the center-line of each edge. The out-of-plane displacement of the center point is assumed to
be equal to 0 if the model is limited to two-dimensional elements. It should be noted that out-of-plane displacement of
the center is neglected if the element is combined with a three-dimensional structure, whereas any displacements in this
direction needs to be restricted by the user directly if needed. Models that are limited to a two-dimensional plane should
therefore be deﬁned in the x-y plane, in order to avoid any limitations of the element. A depiction of the shell element can

4.10
Load application in Abaqus
There are two different methods that can be applied when establishing a distributed load upon a model namely, an element-
based and a surface-based method. The element-based load can be applied to element bodes, element surfaces and element
edges. Whereas the surface-based load is limited to the geometrical surfaces or geometrical edges [5].

4.10.1
Element-based loads
The application of element-based loads are speciﬁed through the use of element numbers or a general set containing the
elements. This application of distributed loads is very general, and this form of distributed load can be applied to all
elements distributed loads types and elements [5].
4.10.2
Surface-based loads
A surface-based load must be deﬁned through a surface or edge containing the element or face information. This load
application method is well suited for models where a complex distributed load is required. It is possible to use this
distributed load type for all elements whereas a valid surface can be deﬁned [5].

5
Study of reinforced tunnel slab
In Civil Engineering structures are often constructed using different kinds of material e.g. a composite beam made of
concrete and steel reinforcement. These materials may have non-linear material behaviour which makes it difﬁcult to
calculate by hand especially when structural members become complex with e.g. many layers of reinforcement.
Finite element modelling is a modern way to analyse the behaviour of structural members or larger structures. It is pos-
sible to do experimental testing to analyse how the specimen is behaving but it is often not cost efﬁcient if a good ﬁnite
element model is available.
A numerical model is made by creating a geometry of the specimen that is desired to analyse. Then deﬁning the material
properties and apply this behaviour to the desired part of the model. If the model consists of more than one structural part
the parts are assembled into a numerical model containing all structural elements and their individual material behaviour.
From this the geometry of the numerical model is discretized into ﬁnite elements and lastly the loading and boundary
conditions are added to the numerical model.
The numerical model can at this point be analysed but the user must choose which type of analysis is desired. Some are
fast and some can take days to run. For structural analysis the Newton-Raphson and the arc length method are commonly
used. These methods are described in section 4.6.
In this part of the thesis the build up of the numerical model is veriﬁed and described using numerical models that are
useful to describe e.g. speciﬁc material behaviour.

Conclusion
A preliminary analysis of a one-dimensional steel beam was created in order to verify the steel to be used as in the con-
crete reinforced model. This was done by creating material parameters in Abaqus for: Thermal expansion, plasticity and
Young’s modulus at ambient and elevated temperatures. A validation was then created through the use of plastic hinge
length and ultimate capacity at ambient temperature.
The model was then expanded to a two-dimensional concrete model subjected to uniaxial compression and tension,
whereas the compressive and tensile strength were calculated through the in-elastic strain and a constant fracture energy
deﬁnition respectively at ambient and at elevated temperatures.
A reinforced concrete beam was then created in order to verify the global behavior in bending. This analysis was similarly
used to investigate if the model was able to capture the plasticity through the height of the cross-section with the applied
mesh discretization.
A two-dimensional reinforced concrete slab was investigated in a pushover analysis in order to establish the bearing
capacity and determine an appropriate load case for a thermal analysis. The thermal load that was applied to the beam was
based on a standard ﬁre prescribe by Eurocode. The geometry of the 2D model was determined on the basis of a proposed
slab found within a tunnel section. The exact geometry and material properties used for the pushover analysis can be found
in ﬁgure 83 and table 14 respectively. A preliminary analysis of the minimum and maximum mesh discretization was then
conducted on the basis of the geometry and material properties in order to ﬁnd a valid mesh size which would allow the
material model to capture the tensile behavior of the concrete at ambient and elevated temperatures. The minimum and
maximum size limitations of the mesh can be found in ﬁgure 85. A mesh of ≈0.116 m consisting of the CPS8 element
type was used on the basis of the element size analysis because this element proved to be valid until ≈500 ◦C. The
vertical displacement at midspan was then plotted as a function of the thermal load in order to investigate the critical
temperature for the given load and cross-section along with the stresses and strains observed after the pushover analysis
after the mechanical load and after the thermal load application.
6.1
Suggestions to further work
A logical next step in modelling the tunnel slab is to expand this from a 2D beam model to a 3D slab model. This imple-
mentation will make it possible to more accurately model the real tunnel slab and the position of the ventilation systems
suspended from the slab. These ventilation systems are likely spaced out in a pattern that ensures sufﬁcient amount of air
ﬂow through the tunnel. With the known position of the ventilation systems the load can be applied as e.g. surface loads.
It is also possible to include the supporting steel beam shown in ﬁgure 71.
It is also of interest to model the boundary conditions of the tunnel slab to more accurately capture the boundary con-
ditions of the structure. In the model it is assumed that the slab is simply supported but from ﬁgure 82 the interaction
is shown as an anchorage connecting the beam and the surrounding tunnel structure. The connection is represented as
”Detail 1”. This detail must be investigated to understand how to properly model the anchorage.
In addition to the anchorage the surrounding tunnel structure could help capture the structural behaviour of the tunnel slab
more accurately. This can introduce hindered thermal expansion which increase the stress in the tunnel slab. Furthermore,
an analysis can be performed on the tunnel structure e.g. assuming ﬂashover has occurred in the tunnel.
In this model a standard ﬁre is used to capture the temperature dependency of the materials. The next step is to add a
realistic thermal model from e.g. a car ﬁre or hydrogen car ﬁre.
With the implementation of an updated thermal model the next step could be to expand the material model of concrete to
a material model that tracks the cracks in the concrete. This is one of the limitations of the smeared cracking model as this
model does not track the cracks as explained in section 4.5.2. This can introduce further material degradation of the steel
reinforcement as this cracking of the concrete in the tension zone leads to the reinforcement directly exposed to the ﬁre.

8.2

For veriﬁcation of a FE-model analytical calculations are often made. The analytical calculations are based on assumptions
and in this case Euler-Bernoulli beam theory. As previously mentioned the beam is ﬁxed at both ends with a point load
with a distance of 2 m from one of the supports. The behaviour of the beam is found using formulas derived from the
principle of virtual work and then found as follows:
• Loading is applied at a distance of 2 m from one of the supports.
• Firstly, yielding occurs at the support closest point load.
• Secondly, yielding occurs at the point load.
• Lastly, yielding occurs at the support furthest from the point load.

elastically using Euler-Bernoulli beam theory.

9.2
Nonlinear Geometry in the thermal and mechanical model
Nonlinear properties can be caused by two different sources within solid mechanics: Material nonlinear and geometrical
nonlinearity. Material nonlinearity has previously been explained, but in short can be summarized as the effect of material
parameters changing over time. Geometrical nonlinearity is on the other hand caused by changes in the geometry. The
nonlinearity is for both instances observed because of changes in the stiffness when an element is subjected to a load.
The affect of this depends on the deﬂections observed, it is often assumed that the deformations are small, whereas the
element will maintain the overall shape, whereas this criterion will be voided if large deformations are observed. The
complexity of the problem will thus change signiﬁcantly if large deformations are assumed, whereas the computational
time will increase. The increase complexity is caused by the variation in stiffness, whereby a the element stiffness matrix
needs to be constructed/updated for each time step. The following simpliﬁcations are allowed when a nonlinear geometry
analysis is conducted:
• Hooke’s law is valid, because the materials are assumed to be linear elastic.
• Nonlinear behavior is observed in the force-displacement relation and in the stress-strain relation.
• Large and small deformation are allowed, but rotations must remain small.
80
==PAGE:90==
• The forces are static.
These simpliﬁcations will have the following implications on the system.
• The equilibrium conditions are based on the deformed element shape.
• Superposition is not allowed.
• Displacement and stresses are found as nonlinear functions of the axial forces.
• The element stiffness is dependant on the external force.
The nonlinear geometry introduced in this part of the thesis will be based on a pinned to roller beam with a mechanical
and thermal load. This problem can be divided into four different stages.
1. The initial problem, where no loads a applied.
2. The ﬁrst phase, where a large axial force is generated by the thermal expansion, and only a small deﬂection occur
due to the mechanical load.
3. The mechanical force increases whereby a contraction to the initial ﬁrst phase is observed.
4. The mechanical force exerts enough force on the beam in order to cause the horizontal displacement to move past
the initial point.
