Abstract
The electrostatic deﬂection of the membrane is one of the most diﬃcult quantities to
determine when designing a microphone. Modeling the electrostatic force exerted on
a membrane can be quite straight forward when the force applied to the membrane
is uniform. However, this is not the case when the back electrode exerting the force
contains holes and/or slits, which is typically the case and thus makes the entire
modeling process much more complicated. In case of electret condenser microphones,
the charge distribution and thickness of the electret material are important parameters
to consider.
The report models the electrostatic forces generated by the electret
material and obtains the static deﬂection behaviour of the same, especially under
the presence of non uniform forces. Conclusions are drawn regarding the inﬂuence of
the charge height, variations of parameters such as maximum deﬂection and average
deﬂection as well as the validity of existing models in literature that predict the
deﬂection behaviour.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A condenser microphone is a highly elaborate pressure sensor that couples multiple
electrical, mechanical and acoustical phenomena in tandem. In particular, the sen-
sitivity of such a device is a combined result of numerous parameters, each of which
are ﬁne tuned to work properly together.
The electrostatic deﬂection is one of those parameters that is one of the
arguably diﬃcult quantities to determine when designing a microphone and this is
due to its close interaction with all the above phenomena. The static deﬂection has
potential inﬂuences over the sensitivity, the amount of internal noise as well as well
as level of distortion generated in presence of high sound pressure inputs.
Typically, simpliﬁed analyses and designing procedures include the modeling
of electrostatic forces that are uniform all over the membrane. In some cases, the
presence of static deﬂection is wholly ignored. This however is not the the true case
when the case when the backplate gets primarily modiﬁed, for example, adding slits
and holes with some goal of shaping the frequency response.
The project aims to develop numerical tools for estimating the electrostatic
deﬂection of a membrane when it is exposed to non-homogeneous back electrodes.
More speciﬁcally, with the case of an electret condenser principle which is an alter-
native to designing conventional condenser microphones. This report would thereby
provide some information on how the static deﬂection varies depending on the ge-
ometry of backplate based on a more accurate representation of the deﬂection shape
based on the generated electrostatic forces acting on the membrane.
This report ﬁrst models the electrostatic forces generated on a condenser
microphone’s diaphragm due to the presence of an embedded planar charge within
an electret material.
Existing analytical models/solutions for the displacement of
2D axisymmetric membrane normally involved a loading electrostatic force that is
uniform in nature. Another rework of the existing solution is proposed which is a slight
modiﬁcation of the same, in order to incorporate the inﬂuence of non uniform forces.
Likewise, ﬁnite element models were developed in order to provide a clearer picture
on the membrane displacement when making changes to the microphone backplate.
MATLAB and COMSOL softwares were used to achieve the simulation results. The
report is entirely revolved around the static deﬂection aspect of the microphone.

Chapter 2
Electret Condenser Microphones
2.1
Brief overview
Electret microphones are some of the most widely used types of condenser microphones
in the present day scenario, from cellular devices to audio microphones, although
modern cellular devices have found better use with the MEMS microphone design,
the latter which is a minor rework of the electret version. The electret was invented by
Gerhard Sessler and James West in 1961 at Bell Laboratories where they employed a
thin metallized teﬂon foil as the electret material [1], which would act as a permanent
charge source that in turn induced variable charge on an opposite facing stationary
electrode based on its movements.
The term electret is the electrostatic equivalent of a magnet, that is, it holds
a permanent charge for a very long period of time. Although a charge decay is to
be expected, this decay time has been observed to be in the order of several hundred
years [2] and hence, assumed to be constant under the purview of this report. The
nature of such a design makes it possible to employ the condenser capacitor principle
without externally polarizing the electrodes (membrane and backplate), as is the case
with a conventional condenser microphone. With this, electret microphones retain the
good characteristics of their externally polarized counterparts, such as a ﬂat frequency
response in the audio frequency range of humans, low distortion and the ability to
pickup transients accurately [3].
That said, electrets are relatively less sought after when designing high ﬁ-
delity measurement microphones because the charge decay might prove to be a vari-
able when it comes to repeatability of measurements over time. They can indeed be
used for accurate measurement purposes, however, they ﬁnd better use in designs that
are geared towards portable, high quality communication systems.
In the early days, foil electret designs were more common in which they
featured a layer of electret material on the diaphragm that followed the vibrations.
In modern designs, an electret material (such as PTFE or Teﬂon) is aﬃxed onto the
backplate of the microphone capsule instead. This newer design was considered for
the project.

2.2
Electret condenser principle
Figure 2.1: Simple 2D cross section of electret microphone capsule, with vertical
dimensions exaggerated.
A simpliﬁed cross section of the electret microphone is shown in Figure 2.1, with the
vertical dimensions exaggerated, in which the the dimensions dgap and delec represent
the width of the front air cavity/gap and thickness of the electret material respectively.
The relative permittivities for air and electret are ε1 and ε2 respectively. The electret
holds a planar surface charge σ inside it [3], which is a reasonable simpliﬁcation of the
volume charges that are normally present after the process of polarizing a dielectric.
The conventional condenser transducer has been discussed extensively in
literature by Leach [4], where the transducer is often modelled as a parallel plate
capacitor (ﬁxed backplate and a membrane with mechanical movement) which in
turn requires an external polarization voltage the charge the plates.
The output
voltage is inversely tied to the change in capacitance due to membrane movement,
or more speciﬁcally, the rate of change in the charge stored. The electret condenser
in comparison diﬀers in how this source of charges is provided. Instead of a ﬁxed
external supply of charges, the electret condenser has one of the parallel plates (in
this case, the backplate) to be a semi-permanent source of charges.
Electrets are formed when a dielectric material is injected with electrons or
exposed to an electric ﬁeld in order to polarize it. After polarization, this trapped
layer of electrons is responsible for the electrical ﬁeld generated in the air gap. The
trapped charge is immoveable, however, it induces moveable charges on the underside
of the membrane (σind) and some on the backplate interface respectively. Regarding
charges on the membrane, it is due to the membrane acting as a one-sided conductor
after being coated with a very thin conductive layer to form a capacitor with the
backplate. The number of charges on the membrane is dependent on dgap [3] and the
strength of the ﬁeld generated by the electret.
When sound wave impinges on the membrane, dgap changes periodically,
thereby changing the balance of electrical ﬁelds. In doing so, it changes the induced
surface charge σind on the membrane and generates a voltage Vo across load resistor R.
The open circuit voltage can obtained if R = ∞. The above is not entirely diﬀerent
from the externally polarized condenser principle, however, the distinction lies in the
fact that the source of charges is diﬀerent for each case and therefore the resulting
electrical ﬁelds are diﬀerent [3].

When there is no incoming acoustical input (static conditions), there would
be a small pull on the membrane towards the electret due to presence of electrostatic
forces. Unlike a piston or plate capacitor assumption, a circular clamped membrane
will be subject to varying electrostatic forces which are strongest towards the center
of the membrane. The deﬂection shape is best approximated by a Bessel function
of order 0 or a parabolic curve. The membrane deﬂection under static conditions is
studied in this report and for that purpose, the electrostatic force is modeled ﬁrst.
2.3
Modelling of electrostatic forces on diaphragm

distributed planar charge layer σ [3], which is present at a height h inside the electret
seen from the backplate. The electret is therefore made up of 3 interfaces: exposed

3.4
Finite element analysis
To solve a given engineering problem, a mathematical model is ﬁrst required to de-
scribe the system. Necessary assumptions may be included to simplify the procedure
for solving the problem. The governing mathematical model must describe the be-
haviour of the system with a set of diﬀerential equations that support some boundary
conditions.
The ﬁnite element method (FEM) approaches an engineering problem by
dividing the above problem’s domain (in this case, a microphone membrane) into
smaller continuous subdomains and each of these subdomains is called a ﬁnite element.
The governing expression is thereby applicable for each of these ﬁnite elements and is
solved individually for the smaller domains, after which these newly obtained solutions
are linked to build the solution of the problem. Shape functions are used to interpolate
between the individual solutions to build the overall result [9].
The FEM procedure yields an approximate solution for the problem.
A
diﬀerential equation results to zero when evaluated with its exact solution.
The
approximate solution instead yields a value very close to zero when substituted into
the diﬀerential equation.
This value can thought of as the diﬀerence, called the
residual, between an approximate and the exact solution. If the residual is very low
then the approximate solution is deemed to be accurate and vice versa [9].
24
==PAGE:26==

3.4.1
Accuracy of solution
The FEM’s solution accuracy is related to the number of elements used to divide it,
or in other words its mesh size. If the mesh size approaches zero, it suggests that the
FEM model uses almost an inﬁnite number of tiny subdomains or elements, and by
doing so, the exact solution for the system of equations is reached. In the engineering
world the speed of development and analyses is restricted by computational speed
and resources, therefore, a compromise has to be met where the real world problem’s
simulation should be reliable enough to interpret the results clearly but should not
take a long time to solve either.
The criteria for choosing an approximate solution is commonly referred to
as a convergence criterion and one normally tracks this criterion or parameter by
solving the FEM problem over diﬀerent mesh sizes (from coarse to ﬁne), such that
the error between the tracked parameter over the diﬀerent iterations is considered
negligible, after which the solution is said to have converged. A course mesh requires
less computational resources but may yield an innacurate solution. The choice of the
reﬁned mesh density is entirely dependent on the kind of problem being solved and
the end user’s design considerations.

Approximate solution for static deﬂection
For the PDE shown in Equation 3.5, if the problem domain was discretized and
converted to a ﬁnite element problem, the solution would be of the form:
η = [Ag · Tm]−1[Bg · pe]
(3.32)
where η is the array of calculated nodal deﬂections (the approximate solu-
tion) of the membrane, Ag is the global system or stiﬀness matrix, Bg is the global
input matrix, Tm is the static membrane tension while pe is the nodal electrostatic
pressure.
Ag is a sparsely ﬁlled matrix which contains the element contibutions along
the entire domain, in which it links the individual elements through their correspond-
ing nodal interconnections while Bg provides information on the input (source or sink
in the PDE) acting on the respective nodes. The system and input matrices will vary
accordingly depending on the choice of discretization of the PDE’s domain (as a 1D,
2D or 3D problem).
The 1D FEM model programmed in MATLAB followed the Galerkin’s method
of selecting a weighting function, such that the approximate solution is represented
by a linear combination of some trial functions. To put into perspective, the approx-
imate solution should satisfy the essential boundary conditions of the problem (in
the case of a microphone membrane, the rigid clamp and zero slope at the center).
This solution may not satisfy the diﬀerential equation exactly but will diﬀer by a
small residual amount. The residual is multiplied by a suitable weighting function in
order to minimize it. Galerkin’s method is one of the many ways to choose a suitable
weighting function.
The procedure followed for determining the approximate solution using this
method is detailed by Kwon [9].

3.5
Presence of holes and slits in microphone back-
plate
In practical scenarios, many diﬀerent designs of condenser microphones exist and de-
pending on the performance requirement, slits and holes are included in the backplate
of the microphone. This requirement could range anywhere from wanting a desired
frequency response (ﬂat or selective) or how much mechanical thermal noise is deemed
acceptable, for example. The purpose of this section is to give a brief overview on
many implications static deﬂection carries with itself.
Figure 3.3: Inside view of a typical measurement microphone by Br¨uel og Kjær [10].
Generally, modiﬁcations to the backplate are done depending on the design
requirements in order to attenuate the viscous-loss related damping resistance, which
is oﬀered by the thin air gap between the membrane and backplate. This may also
be achieved by either changing the membrane tension or backplate’s distance to the
diaphragm [10]. On the other hand, a condenser microphone’s electrostatic forces
acting on the membrane take into account the geometry of the backplate, as seen
earlier. Consider the unpolarized condenser design where the diaphragm is initially
ﬂat which soon experiences a pull upon applying some polarization voltage.
The
presence of holes and/or slits in the backplate would indicate a smaller eﬀective area
that is active, which dictates an overall decrease in the pulling forces. Consequently,

4.2
Estimating deﬂection due to non uniform elec-
trostatic forces on membrane
Equations 3.5 and its subsequent analytical solutions had been derived considering
that the loading electrostatic forces on the membrane would be uniform throughout
the air gap. Given that the backplate is ﬂat and clamped at the ends, this assumption
however only holds true in the very beginning when the membrane hasn’t deﬂected
at all. The parabolic deﬂection proﬁle of the membrane (as found in Equation 3.9) is
valid as long as the forces on the membrane still remain uniform after it deforms to
that shape.
This is because the air gap turns non uniform due to this deﬂection and the
resulting electrostatic forces between the backplate-membrane capacitor will change
depending on this ﬁnal air gap, as seen in Section 2.3. With its boundary conditions,
the membrane is clamped at the rims and therefore experiences the largest displace-
ment towards the center of the membrane than it does towards its clamp. This would
suggest that the generated electrostatic forces will be grow stronger as the membrane
displaces. Since the membrane is a one-sided conductor it carries free charges unlike
the ﬁxed charges in the electret material (a dielectric), it also suggests that the move-
able charges should be more distributed around the middle region of the membrane
due to the reduced air gap (from Equation 2.10).
4.2.1
Procedure
To observe the inﬂuence of non-uniform forces on the membrane, an iterative proce-
dure was employed to estimate the deﬂection proﬁle of the membrane when subjected
to such forces:-
1. The initial deﬂection d0 is calculated, when the membrane experiences uniform
forces F0 with a given laterally uniform air gap D0.
29
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 4. MODEL SIMULATIONS AND DISCUSSION
2. Based on the preliminary deﬂection, the new j′th air gap is obtained by sub-
tracting d0 from the initial air gap D0 such that Dj=1 = D0 −d0.
3. The corresponding set of electrostatic forces Fj=1 on the membrane due to Dj=1
are recalculated, and a newer deﬂection proﬁle dj=1 for the same is obtained.
4. In a similar fashion, the j′th air gap is given by Dj = D0 −dj−1.
5. The above two steps are repeated until a convergence criteria is satisﬁed. The
criteria in this case was that the diﬀerence dc between the area averaged deﬂec-
tion for each iteration should be very small. An alternative is to check for the
diﬀerence between the average air gap or maximum deﬂections of the membrane
in a similar fashion. Here, dc was chosen as 10−12 m.
The working of this procedure is trying to obtain the equilibrium position of the the
membrane, based on the loading electrostatic force balanced out by the restoring force
by due to Tm.
As the membrane deﬂects, the electrostatic forces Fe grow stronger by virtue
of the drop in air gap dgap. This would carry the implication that the electrostatic
forces would continue to increase indeﬁnitely until the membrane hits the backplate
as the air gap kept decreasing.
It should be kept in mind that this procedure does not guarantee any con-
vergence to any solution. For example if the initial air gap is too small as compared to
electret thickness, if the membrane’s tension is weak, if the embedded surface charge
is very strong or if the charge layer is put too close to the membrane, the membrane
would seem to deﬂect beyond the dimensions of the capsule indeﬁnitely. In the phys-
ical sense it implies the membrane has touched the backplate. Thus, a ﬁne balance
of the parameters of a microphone capsule governs the deﬂection behaviour.

It is easy to visualize with a ﬂat piston that its deﬂection would be entirely

As seen earlier, this behaviour was not true for an actual diaphragm which
is modeled as a vibrating membrane. For cases with rw < 1, there shall be an optimal
starting position of the slot of any given width, after which the deﬂections will exhibit
a rising trend with respect to rp. This is an eﬀect of the curved membrane deﬂection
combined with the axisymmetric geometry of the annular slot. If As grows when
moving outwards with rp for some ﬁxed rw, both ηavg and ηmax start to decrease
because there is a net drop in the eﬀective backplate area that generates forces. After
a certain point, electrostatic forces due to the electret present in the inner backplate
region upto rp (see Figure 5.1) will compensate for the lack of active forces over the
annular slot and hence, start pulling down the membrane more strongly as the slot
continues to move outwards.
The outermost region of the backplate (from R ≤r ≤a) will nevertheless
contribute to the deﬂection, however unlike that of a piston, its eﬀect is comparably
lesser than the region described by the innermost region (from 0 ≤r ≤rp). This is
largely due to the set of boundary conditions a membrane abides by, so if it experiences
the largest displacement towards the center, any change in the backplate near the
center will cause a signiﬁcant change in the deﬂection behaviour.
As such, arriving at a straightforward relationship between rw and rp which
gives these optimal ratios for minimum ηavg or ηmax is complicated, owing to the
slot area increasing with either rp or rw. This relationship is susceptible to change
depending on the nature of σ within the electret as well, which in this study was
assumed to be completely uniform and planar. Nonetheless, the positions can possibly
be achieved through numerical simulations like above in Figure 5.9a, where the desired
rp for any chosen rw is found by locating the local minima of the normalized deﬂection

Conclusions and future work
7.1
Conclusions
A somewhat comprehensive overview of the membrane deﬂection of pre-polarized
condenser microphones was provided. The working of a pre-polarized microphone is
entirely the same as that of a conventional condenser design, only that they diﬀer
by the mechanism by which they generate forces on the membrane. In conventional
design, there are no trapped charges and all charges on the electrodes are free to move
and provided by a separate polarization voltage, whereas in electret design, one of the
electrodes contains trapped charges while the other would contain free charges.
The importance of modelling non uniform electrostatic forces was understood
and their presence cannot be ignored due to the several implications they carry with
themselves, both within static as well as dynamic deﬂection, although the latter has
not been investigated in this project. The cause behind such behaviours was inevitably
tied to the unique set of boundary conditions a clamped membrane PDE comes with,
where a membrane is tighter towards the clamp and as a result it experiences the
strongest deﬂections, pulling forces as well as induced charge concentrations towards
the membrane’s middle. With these constraints, it suggested that the center region
of the backplate is the most important region where any change to it would cause the
strongest eﬀects on the deﬂection behaviour. These eﬀects were then studied through
inhomogeneous backplates.
The analytical expressions for static deﬂection existing in literature were
compared with diﬀerent FEM models and it is now understood that the existing mod-
els are quite suitable for verifying cases with uniform forces, owing to their simplicity.
They provide a quick understanding of the general behaviour of the microphone mem-
brane. However, the piston solution as well as parabolic solution (Model I) carry a
couple of strong assumptions which overlook the nature of non uniform forces that are
guaranteed to occur inside a general condenser microphone with a rigid, ﬂat backplate.
An alternative piecewise expression (Model II) for studying the behaviour
with axisymmetric microphones was derived, which was a slight rework of Model I
by just viewing the membrane in a piecewise fashion. Although Model II used the
same mathematical formulae as Model I to obtain the complete solution, it ended
up complicating the solution considerably and also pushed itself closer to the realm
of numerical methods. This was a strong indicator that non uniform forces should
be approached in a piecewise fashion and therefore, numerical models should be the
preferred choice, due to the presence of computational hardware and software available

these days. Model II’s usage can therefore be considered as an alternative benchmark
that one could compare deﬂections of axisymmetric FEM models of microphones
against and also possibly real world measurements.
As far as deﬂection behaviour with inhomogeneous backplates is concerned,
the study on annular designs revealed a fair bit regarding the importance of designing a
backplate for any condenser microphone, wherein the eﬀective area of the backplate as
well as the location where area reduction took place, both aﬀected the maximum and
average deﬂections in diﬀerent manners. It was observed that the boundary conditions
of a clamped membrane actually governed this aspect because they inﬂuenced the
nature of electrostatic forces owing to the variation in air gap, the latter which isn’t
possible with a piston model. Likewise, both the width rw and position rp of the
ring/slot had a much more impact on the a curved membrane deﬂection and were
deemed to be equally important. It was also found that any change in the induced
charge on the membrane corresponded to a similar variation in the static capacitance
of the microphone, and vice versa. Thus, in case either parameter among the two is
unknown then they can be obtained through the ratio ξ.
The study on backplate holes was found to be similar in nature to that of
annular backplates where the general observations from the former carried over to the
case with backplate holes. Minor diﬀerences were observed and this was largely due
to the nature of ring and hole geometries. The importance of parameters such as the
radius rh as well as position rc of the holes were also found to be equal.
7.2
Future work
The work presented in this project hopefully provides a base, using which one can
head into potential directions with the available information.
7.2.1
Validation with measurements
Although this project sought to investigate the behaviour of static deﬂections in elec-
tret microphones, it must be stressed that one cannot hope to completely study a real
world phenomena through simulations alone. Even if simulations potentially describe
a great deal about any desired physical phenomena, real world behaviour can always
prove to be diﬀerent. A necessary validation for these simulations becomes the next
logical step and therefore, a measurement setup for evaluating the static deﬂection is
required. A sample microphone capsule should be obtained, measured and simulated
like above before carrying out real experiments to observe the deﬂection. Or perhaps
a sample capsule can be manufactured based on the simulations observed and then
have its deﬂection measured.
The very process of measurement itself carries a host of issues that can poten-
tially stem from the measuring equipment itself. Nonetheless, a possible test method
was carried out by Warren et al.[13], who used a laser interferometer assembled on
an optical bench to measure static deﬂection in conventional condenser microphones,
with circular and annular membranes. This experiment can be reproduced for electret
condensers.

7.2.2
Modelling of charge distribution
A more comprehensive study into the nature of electret charge distribution can help
provide a more realistic picture on the electrostatic forces generated on the membrane.
The current study was conﬁned to an assumption of laterally uniform, trapped planar
surface charges which is a minor simpliﬁcation done in order to easily obtain the value
of the embedded charge after the manufacturing process. The above observations are
liable to change if the electret’s charge distribution was made to be non uniform as
well (like a gaussian distribution over the backplate for example).
A more realistic depiction of charges stored in an electret are in the form
of volume charges that have a charge centroid position located within the electret.
Generally any polarization of dielectrics leads to volume bound charge distributions
within the material. A number of experimental setups exist in literature that showcase
how to determine the position of the charge centroid [2].
7.2.3
Reﬁnement of numerical models used
Due to lack of good understanding regarding the mesh generator functions used within
the COMSOL software package, only basic mesh reﬁnement studies were performed
for the simulations with a 3D backplate with holes and the meshing parameters were
all individually tweaked on a trial and error basis, as long as they gave suitable results
with good agreement. Although adding more holes to the backplate indicates that
a ﬁner mesh needs to be used to better represent the geometries, the procedure to
achieve an optimal mesh can deﬁnitely be improved or streamlined keeping in mind
the solution accuracy and the time taken to solve the model. Likewise, the model can
also incorporate a volumetric charge distribution inside the electret as pointed out
earlier.
7.2.4
Dynamic deﬂection and distortion
The design of a microphone is entirely dependent on its use and end-user’s require-
ments. Although the non uniform nature of electrostatic forces and its inﬂuence has
been investigated on the diaphragm, information on static deﬂection alone cannot
provide a complete picture on the frequency response and performance of the micro-
phone because of the potential inﬂuence it carries. Since static deﬂection can aﬀect the
volume of the thin air gap in the front chamber, it aﬀects the damping loss provided
by the thin gap which in turn aﬀects the vibration of the diaphragm at its resonance
frequency. Another point to remember is that static deﬂection is only aﬀected by the
front chamber of a general microphone capsule while completely ignoring the back
chamber, the latter which is highly important when acoustical load is provided on the
microphone. A more complete model with both the front and back chambers should
be designed, which also takes into account the coupling with the visco-thermal losses
that occur between thin gaps.
Furthermore, the static deﬂection itself presents a source of non-linearity
in the movement of diaphragm about its mean position, as the vibrations will be
biased towards the backplate side due to the static pressure applied by the electret.
This again brings forth the question of the design goal of the microphone. Audio
microphones or speciﬁc types of measurement microphones are generally aimed at use
with high SPL environments, for example, an audio microphone held near a singer

can potentially be exposed to regular sound pressure levels as high as 140 dB since
they are positioned close to the mouth. Linear small signal models which model the
diaphragm as a piston may not guarantee accurate results at such inputs. Such high
pressures inﬂuence the diaphragm behaviour along with the non linearity presented
by the electrostatic forces, and thereby can lead to distortion/harmonics generated
in the microphone’s voltage output. Design goals like these can potentially beneﬁt
from having an accurate information on the static deﬂection and the behaviour of
capacitance with the induced charge on the membrane.
