Abstract
The goal of the thesis is to model, simulate and perform the process design and
the economic analysis of a biomethane plant that upgrades biogas biologically
using hydrogen from water electrolysis powered by excess electricity. The pro-
cess was implemented in PROII software and the plant capacity was chosen to
be equal to 100 000 tons of substrate per year.
The biogas and biomethane production were validated by real case Dansih
plants data and experiments on biomethanation. A biomethane cost of 0.97
e/Nm3 was estimated and the minimal price for NPV = 0 reaches 1.04 e/Nm3.
These values were calculated assuming a low-priced hydrogen of 1 e/kg. The
economic analysis showed that hydrogen related costs have a signiﬁcant con-
tribution to the capital investment and operation expenses. Therefore it is im-
portant to secure the supply of hydrogen from surplus of electricity. Compared
to conventional upgrading methods, biomethanation has higher cost however
it is possible to reduce the biomethane cost by selling the digestate as a fer-
tiliser or increase the plant capacity. A sensitivity analysis led to the conclusion
that maintaining high hydrogen and carbon dioxide conversion and thus high
methane content in the ﬁnal product is necessary for the economic viability of
the project.

Preface
This master thesis was conducted at the department of Environmental Engineer-
ing at Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in fulﬁlment of the requirement
for acquiring a Master of Science degree in Sustainable Energy, study-line Bioen-
ergy. The thesis was completed from February 10th to July 10th, and accounts
for 30 ECTS. The whole work in the thesis was under the supervision of Irini
Angelidaki, Panagiotis Tsapekos and Merlin Alvarado-Morales.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the Paris agreements in 2015 many countries have put efforts in order to
mitigate climate change. Denmark is one of the most active countries regarding
this target. Indeed, the government set the objective of a 100% fossil fuel free
energy system by 2050 [28]. To do so, the different energy actors have chosen
to develop mainly wind power [29]. However, the variability of such energy
source requires storage otherwise it will lead to substantial losses.
The second focus to achieve Denmark’s goal is biomass. Many coal-based power
plants are modiﬁed so it can be fed by biomass [30]. Another way to provide
renewable energy is through biogas production. This gas is produced by anaer-
obic digestion (AD), a biological process. Agricultural residues, food waste or
sludge are all feedstocks that can be used to produce this mixture of methane
(CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2). Thereafter, the biogas is either used in com-
bined heat and power plants (CHP) or upgraded to be injected in the gas grid
or used as a storage or vehicle fuel. The upgrading process consists of remov-
ing CO2 from the biogas to increase the caloriﬁc value of this renewable fuel
[5]. An alternative to CO2 removal is to convert the CO2 content biologically
using hydrogen, this is called biological methanation or biomethanation. The
surplus of electricity produced by wind turbines (or solar panels) can generate
hydrogen using water electrolysis. H2 transport and storage are very challeng-
ing, therefore converting it into methane, a gas much easier to handle, seems
like a promising path to improve biogas upgrading technologies and provide
another solution for surplus electricity storage. Research is still on-going to
improve biomethanation for commercial availability [9][31][5]. However, few
studies focusing on modelling the performance of this process and assessing its
economic feasibility are currently available in literature [18]. Regarding the
economics of biogas plants, the Danish parliament decided recently to change
the support scheme from 2020 [2].

This thesis investigates the modelling, the simulation and the economic via-

different scenarios were studied to identify opportunities or threats to improve
the process proﬁtability. Eventually, biological upgrading was compared to a
conventional CO2 removal method both from an economic and environmental
perspective, in this case greenhouse gas emissions (GHG).

2.1
Biogas production
2.1.1
Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is the process during which biogas is produced. It
is a biological process occurring in an oxygen free environment. The microbi-
ological organisms degrade the organic content in order to produce two end
products, the biogas composed mainly of methane and carbon dioxide and the
digestate which is rich in nutrients (N, P, K, etc). The AD needs to be operated at
relatively mild temperatures, from 37◦C (mesophilic conditions) to 55◦C (ther-
mophilic conditions). Organic feedstocks are generally used as for example,
animal manures, agricultural residues like wheat straw, sludge from wastewa-
ter plants or food waste from municipal solid waste.
The AD takes place following four main steps (ﬁgure 2.1):
• Hydrolysis: During this step, polymers (carbohydrates, proteins and lipids)
are converted to monomers e.g. simple sugars, long-chain fatty acids and
amino acids. Hydrolytic enzymes perform this process by splitting the
polymers with water molecules.
• Acidogenesis: The products of hydrolysis are degraded into volatile fatty
acids (VFA), alcohols, CO2 and H2. This conversion is accomplished by
==PAGE:24==
6
Background
fermentative bacteria through different fermentation pathways. NH3 and
H2S are also produced from amino acids during acidogenesis.
• Acetogenesis: The VFAs e.g. propionate, butyrate and valerate are con-
verted to acetate, H2 and CO2. A small partial pressure of H2 can cause
inhibition of the ﬁnal step called methanogenesis.
• Methanogenesis: In this last step, methanogens convert acetate and hy-
drogen to methane through 2 routes, acetoclastic methanogenesis and
hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis. The ﬁrst route uses acetate and the
second one uses H2. Around two-thirds of the methane is produced with
the ﬁrst route.

of biogas production.

2.4
Commercial upgrading technologies
Over the years, many CO2 removal technologies were developed, allowing bio-
gas upgrading to be used on more and more biogas plants. In the early stages of
the upgrading market, two methods were standing out, Pressure Swing Adsorp-
tion (PSA) and water scrubbing. Another technology is now gaining an impor-
tant market share, amine scrubbing. Membrane separation or organic solvent
scrubber are alternatives to the methods aforementioned however their market
presence is still minor as seen on ﬁgure 2.6 [4]. A description of the upgrading
market leaders is given below following the work of Bauer et al., Angelidaki et
al. and Beil et al. [4][5][8].
Figure 2.6: Evolution of the number of biogas upgrading plants from 2001 to
2012 [4]
• Water scrubbing: This process involves the use of 2 columns and is based
on the high solubility of CO2 in water compared to methane. In the ﬁrst
column the biogas is introduced at the bottom at high pressure (6 to 10
bars and up to 40◦C). The water is introduced at the top such that there
is a counter ﬂow of gas and liquid. The CO2 is dissolved in water and
then released from the water stream in the second column also called
desorption column. To do so, air at atmospheric pressure enters from the
column base and the off-gas that now contains the CO2 comes out at the
top. The methane losses are avoided by using a ﬂash unit between the 2
columns, it reduces the pressure to 2.5-3.5 bars. Both columns are packed
to extend the gas-liquid contact.
• Amine scrubbing: This technology involves an absorber as well and a
==PAGE:30==
12
Background
stripper. The separation process is based on the chemical binding of CO2
molecules with aqueous amine solutions. The biogas introduced at 1 or
2 bars from the bottom of the column is put in contact with an amine so-
lution. The exothermic character of the reaction heats up the liquid from
20-40◦C to 45-65◦C. H2S is also absorbed by the amine solution. The
amine stream is sent to a stripping column at a pressure of 1.5-3 bars and
heated by a boiler to 120-160◦C. The heat breaks the chemical bonds and
a stream rich in CO2 is created. This stream is then cooled in a condenser,
the condensate is recirculated to the stripper and the CO2 is released. As
the previous method, the columns have random packing for gas-liquid
contact enhancement.
The solution used can be methyldiethanolamine (MDEA), diethanolamine
(DEA) and monoethanolamine (MEA). Nowadays, it is common to use a
mixture of MDEA and piperazine (PZ) since it has a higher absorption ca-
pacity. One of the advantages of this method is that it removes to a certain
extent H2S nonetheless the solvents are toxic to human and environment.
• Pressure Swing Adsorption: The separation is done based on gases phys-
ical properties e.g. interaction between gas molecules and the adsorbent
materials. Common adsorbents are carbon molecular sieve, activated car-
bon or zeolites. They are characterised by high speciﬁc area. PSA consists
in 4 steps: pressurisation, adsorption, blow-down and purge. The ﬁrst
corresponds to the injection of compressed biogas (4-10 bars) in the ad-
sorption vessel that contains the adsorbent. CO2 is adsorbed while CH4
ﬂows through the column. When the adsorbent material is saturated, the
gas stream is released and the pressure within the vessel is decreased al-
lowing the desorption of CO2 and the regeneration of the adsorbent. The
stream containing CO2 is evacuated. The outlet gas that includes large
amount of biomethane is recycled in order to avoid methane losses.
An inconvenience of this process is the irreversible adsorption of H2S, thus
it should be removed before PSA upgrading. To comply with the continuity
of the process several adsorption columns, between 2 and 4, are installed.

gasses are present in biogas [5][35].

biomethane production more environmental-friendly. This type of systems are
still under development, only a few pilot and full scale plants operates currently

2.6.1
Anaerobic Digestion models
2.6.1.1
ADM1
The anaerobic digestion model number 1 (ADM1) is a structured model de-
scribing the different steps of AD with biochemical and physico-chemical reac-
==PAGE:36==
18
Background
tions. The depiction of this model is given by Batstone et al. [39]. The bio-
chemical steps are: disintegration i.e. homogeneous particulates disintegrate to
lipids, proteins and carbohydrates, hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and
methanogenesis as explained in section 2.1.1. Physico-chemical reactions de-
tail the physico-chemical state of AD e.g. pH and gas concentrations. To do so
the reaction equations describe acid-base reactions as well as gas-liquid trans-
fer. ADM1 is a dynamic mathematical model taking into account the kinetics
and inhibition phenomena during AD. ADM1 has been validated and employed
successfully many times for different substrates. [40][41].
2.6.1.2
Comprehensive model of anaerobic digestion bioconversion of com-
plex substrate
Another model was developed by Angelidaki et al. [42][43] and recently ex-
tended and validated by Kovalovszki et al.
[44], it can be referred as bio-
conversion model or BioModel. Compared to ADM1, the BioModel includes
stoichiometry , volatile fatty acids (VFA), long chain fatty acids (LCFA) and an
extended ammonia inhibition. It omits temperature dependence and hydrogen
inhibition. It was primarily developed to predict the process performance from
livestock manure and other complex mixture of wastes. As in ADM1, the sub-
strates is supposed to be composed of carbohydrates, lipids and proteins but
also VFA, LCFA and other components like ammonia. This model enables to
perform a dynamic simulation of anaerobic digestion but the stoichiometry pro-
vided can help to calculate the yield of each component after each step. Overall,
the BioModel describes 3 hydrolytic reactions and eight bacterial steps involving
19 chemical compounds.
2.6.2
Biological upgrading models
The biomethanation corresponds to hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis process,
a route that already occurs during AD. Hydrogenotrophic methanogens convert
hydrogen and carbon dioxide into methane. Hill [45] proposed a model includ-
ing reaction stoichiometry. This model takes into account cell growth and is
dynamic. The kinetics regarding hydrogen and ammonia provided by Hill can
be used the case of biomethanation. It was used for instance by Lovato et al.
[46] to model biomethanation except that the process was in-situ. Salman et al.
also modelled biological upgrading with biochemical reactions given by Angeli-
daki et al. and Rajendran et al. [47][43][48]. Other researchers applied ADM1
model for biological upgrading [49]. The model was extended for the study of
Bensmann et al. who were investigating in-situ biomethanation. Their study
was focusing on the dynamic modelling of biological upgrading.

2.6.3
Simulation using dedicated software
The aforementioned models need to be implemented in adequate software to
analyse the process performance. Chemical process simulators can therefore be
used. In the present study, PROII version 10.2 from AVEVA SimSci was used.
It is a steady-state process simulator for the analysis of chemical, petroleum,
natural gas, polymer and solid-based processes [50]. PROII offers a wide range
component libraries, thermodynamic methods and operation units like heat ex-
changers, reactors, distillation columns, etc. There is also the possibility for the
user to create their own components or their own operation units.
The model is implemented in the software as a conceptual ﬂowsheet contain-
ing all the units and describing every stream by its composition, temperature,
pressure, etc. A certain amount of speciﬁcations for the equipment can or has
to be supplied to the software. Once all the information is given, the software
runs a simulation and performs different calculations i.e. mass balance, energy
balance, heat transfer, vapor-liquid equilibrium, kinetics.
Other software exists for process simulation such as Aspen Plus or SuperPro
Designer. Aspen Plus is quite similar to PROII. A dedicated process simulation
model of anaerobic digestion was developed by Rajendran et al. on Aspen Plus
[48]. SuperPro Designer stands out since it offers speciﬁc simulation tools for
various industry sectors like pharmaceutical, biotechnology, food industry or
wastewater treatment [51].
2.7
Economic assessment of biogas and biomethane
production
This section aims at giving a short overview of economic feasibility studies of
biogas installations in literature and also have some input regarding cost-beneﬁt
analysis of biogas upgrading methods.
2.7.1
Biogas production feasibility
Numerous models and estimation methods have been developed for chemical
industry in order to assess the proﬁtability of a process. The interest for pro-
cesses based on biorenewable sources is very recent. Despite rising fossil fuel
prices, bioenergy and bio-based products are still occupying a small fraction of
the chemical, material, gas and other industry sectors. Because of the globally
negative impact of fossil-based processes, there is increasing research on the
economic feasibility of projects using biomass to show investment and political
decision-makers the attractiveness of such projects.
==PAGE:38==
20
Background
To do so new estimation methods were developed speciﬁcally for bioenergy and
bio-products. The book "Biorenewable resources" offers a general outlook of the
characteristics of plants using biomass whether it is biochemical processing or
thermo-chemical [27]. In the case of biogas production, the capital investment
estimation can follow the same method as for chemical processes, that is to say
a factorial approach. Nevertheless the values used need to be adjusted since
the equipment and supplementary units are delivered as a whole [52][53]. As
a consequence the costs are reduced compared to typical chemical plants. The
location and size of the plant should also be taken into account when calculating
the capital investment. For instance Amigun et al. investigated how to estimate
the capital cost of biogas installation in Africa and for different plant sizes [54].
This will be further discuss in section 3.6.2.
Many economic evaluation studies on biogas plants can be found in literature.
Their focus is either on the feedstock or co-digestion [55][56][57], either on
the digestate treatment and use [58], or the support scheme and plant conﬁg-
urations [59]. Usually, the process is ﬁrst modelled and simulated either using
a programming software or a simulator, mostly SuperPro Designer or Aspen
Plus. Then the economic analysis is performed considering the particularity of
the project. The proﬁtability is generally appraised using the net present value
(NPV) which reﬂects the value of a project at present time. Sometimes the
payback period (PBP) is also used as a metric. The PBP is the time necessary
to recover the investment of a project. Very few feasibility studies use more
economic indicators.
These studies are helpful for the anaerobic digestion process however a ma-
jority of them uses biogas for CHP. Therefore, the actual cost of biogas and
biomethane is not examined. The present study stands out since the biomethane
cost and price are investigated.
2.7.2
Biogas upgrading feasibility
As mentioned in the previous section, biogas plants feasibility studies do not
include upgrading process since often the biogas end use is production of heat
and power. On the other hand, studies focusing only on biogas upgrading have
emerged. Most of them are comparative analysis between commercial upgrad-
ing methods. The investment and operation costs are scrutinised regarding the
economy of scale i.e. the plant biogas production capacity. Both investment and
operation costs are reduced for larger capacities (ﬁgures 2.10, 2.11, 2.12 and
2.13).
The cost attenuation at high capacities is slightly different for each technology.

For now, there are few studies on biomethane production economic feasibility.
Vo et al. research group provided two studies on biological upgrading prof-

to amine scrubbing. More recently Bekkering et al. investigated the economic
feasibility of biomethanation for farm-scale plants [61]. Except from this last
study, there is no investigation on how to reduce the cost and therefore the
selling price of biomethane in literature.

do not use biogas as a CO2 source. The carbon dioxide comes from off-gases
or carbon capture from air [19]. PtG plants also include the electrolyser that
produces H2. Another noticeable difference is the methanation reaction con-

carbon dioxide economy analysis from those studies is valuable for biological
upgrading.

Regarding the model and simulation of AD, both ADM1 and bioconversion

3.2
Plant characteristics
This study investigates the mass and energy balance as well as the economic
feasibility of a biogas plant using an innovative upgrading method. In order to
depict accurately this type of plant, typical characteristics of anaerobic digestion
processes in Denmark were employed.
Denmark is the ﬁrst exporter of pork in the world, as a consequence around 35
million tons of manure are produced every year [22]. This places manure as
the ﬁrst option for feedstock in biogas production. Manure can be divided in
the following sub-groups: slurry, deep bedding/litter, liquid manure and solid
manure. In general, Danish plants feedstock is composed of 75% of livestock
manure [22][10]. The remaining fraction can be energy crops, organic waste or
algae. In this study, the remaining fraction consists in municipal waste biopulp
[62]. The composition in terms of carbohydrates, proteins and lipids were taken
from the BioModel 2.0 developed at DTU Bioenergy group. The composition of
municipal waste was assimilated to the given composition of industrial waste.
Both waste and manure have an organic matter lower than 20%, that is why
the digestion type in this case is wet [10]. There is an increasing number of
large-scale plants in Denmark, these plants treat from 50 000 to 500 000 tons
of substrate per year [22]. On average industrial scale plants treat more than
100 000 tons of substrate per year. Thus, a yearly substrate of 100 000 tons was
assumed.
The upgrading process will be based on the experiments described in section 3.1
which operate at thermophilic conditions, therefore the operating temperature
is 55◦C. For this operating temperature the corresponding hydraulic retention

Methodology
To implement this model on PROII a speciﬁc databank was ﬁrst created. Indeed
for the hydrolysis steps the insoluble (is), soluble (s) and inert (in) fraction of
carbohydrates, proteins and lipids did not exist in any PROII databank. Further-
more, depending on which fraction is considered the phase of the component
is either solid (insoluble and inert) or liquid (soluble). The properties of the
components created were adapted from similar components e.g. dextrose for
carbohydrates. Table A.1 in Appendix A details the assumptions on the different
components.
After adding the components, the digester was modelled using a conversion re-
actor. All the reactions were implemented in the reactor, a conversion efﬁciency
based on the degraded compound was attributed to each reaction. 80% conver-
sion for the hydrolysis step was assumed. Overall the conversion ratios range
from 80 to 90%. PROII can perform the conversion calculations based on the
amount of base component present in the feed stream or based on the amount
of the base component available for a speciﬁc reaction, these methods are re-
spectively called feed-based or reaction-based [50]. The reaction-based method
was chosen because certain components are not present in the feed stream or
in very small quantities. These components only appear after certain reactions
are completed e.g. soluble proteins that are present after insoluble proteins
hydrolysis and are a reactant for amino-acid degradation. The order in which
the reactions take place matters and was decided in order to avoid negative
ﬂowrates of certain components. In reality, the reactions of table 3.1 happened
simultaneously and continuously.
The composition of the feedstock was slightly changed compared to the BioModel
2.0 data as well as the conversion efﬁciencies to ﬁnd a realistic methane content
and yield. For this purpose the function "Study Case" on PROII was used. The
process simulation was carried out using the ideal thermodynamic method and
at ﬁrst the energy balance was not considered. Table 3.2 shows the ﬁnal com-
position of the feedstock based on BioModel 2.0 data and table A.2 in Appendix
A shows all the conversion efﬁciencies.
The outlet stream of the reactor consists of mainly liquid and vapour. To isolate
the vapour phase that corresponds to the biogas, a stream calculator was used.
The recovery of the different compounds in the overhead or bottom stream were
selected according to ﬂash calculations performed by PROII. The bottom prod-
uct composed of liquid and solid corresponds to the digestate. The ﬂowsheet of

The size of heat exchangers is determined from the area where the heat is trans-

For hydrogen, the storage method is different from the feedstock and the di-

consequence, hydrogen can be stored in a pressure vessel, for pressures lower

is low. 6 hours was considered as the storage capacity.

3.6.2
Capital Investment
The total capital cost is the sum of the ﬁxed capital investment and the working
capital investment. The latter is an extra expense needed to start up the plant,
it usually represents 15% of the ﬁxed capital cost.
==PAGE:64==
46
Methodology
The ﬁxed capital cost is estimated using a factorial method. This method con-
sists of multiplying the total equipment purchased cost by several factors that
are related to different items to build and start the plant for instance installation
costs, piping or electrical power. Lang (1948) proposed a method that compiles
all these factors in one. This factor called Lang factor (fL) can be directly multi-
plied by the total purchase equipment cost (TPEC) to appraise the ﬁxed capital
investment (FCI) of the project (see equation 3.21).
The Lang factor for chemical plants processing mainly ﬂuids is equal to 4.7
[13][88] and the detailed contribution of each aspect is available in many
chemical engineering design books. The Lang factor for anaerobic digestion
and Power-to-Gas (PtG) plants is not the same as for chemical plants, it is gen-
erally lower. This is due to the delivery of methanation, desulphurisation and
digestion units as complete systems, this entails fewer additional costs [52].
There are very few resources that breakdown the contribution to Lang factors
for biogas and PtG plants. The capital investment will be calculated only using
the Lang factor method. The entire plant was divided in 3 sections: anaerobic
digestion, desulphurisation and biomethanation upgrading. This separation al-
lowed to study the contribution of the biological upgrading to the total capital
investment and ultimately to the total product cost.
Tao et al. indicate a Lang factor of 1.80 for biofuels production from cellulosic
biomass while Amigun et al. determined a 1.79 Lang factor for biogas installa-
tions in Africa [53][54]. In this study the Lang factor is 1.80 for both anaerobic
digestion and desulphurisation because H2S removal systems are often inte-
grated in biogas plants even when biogas is used for combined heat and power
(CHP) production. As a matter of fact, H2S is very corrosive and can heavily
damage the turbines of a CHP unit.
Zauner et al. suggested a 2.62 Lang factor after studying the additional costs
related to engineering, materials and civil engineering, electrical needs, site
equipment etc. for PtG plant [52].
FCI = fL ∗TPEC

3.7
Sensitivity Analysis
Perform the feasibility analysis of the project results in a single evaluation or
a single value for each metric. A sensitivity analysis determines how different
values of a parameter affect a particular dependent variable here economic in-
dicators, under a given set of assumptions. For each parameter a backward and
forward value was considered, this provoked a backward or a forward pertur-
bation on the economic metrics (ROI, PBP, NPV, IRR). From these perturbations
the relative difference from the base case value was calculated.
Considering the methods used to approximate the ﬁxed capital cost, this pa-
rameter should be submitted to a sensitivity analysis. Furthermore it is in gen-
eral highly recommended to analyse the sensitiveness to the capital investment
[13][102]. For the ﬁxed capital cost, the advised backward and forward val-
ues are ±30% compared to the base one. The working capital value is more
uncertain therefore the backward change is -20% and the forward is 50%. For
the other parameters there is no pre-designed value. Table 3.9 summarises all
the backward and forward changes. The justiﬁcation of the chosen values is the
following sections.

The economic proﬁtability is an important parameter to look into during project

processes and products, the environmental performance should also be inves-

items, processes or services on the environment. The main focus of this thesis is
the simulation and economic analysis of AD and biogas upgrading, thus an LCA
was not conducted. However the GHG emissions of the process were estimated
and compared with the amine scrubbing process.
To carry out this analysis, compiled data from the Joint Research Center was
retrieved and used as a basis for anaerobic digestion GHG emissions with grid
injection as an end use [24]. The values for wet manure and biowaste were

feed i.e. 75% for manure and 25% for biowaste.

Bradbury et al. studies [25][26]. The biomethane injected in the gas grid is
assumed to have similar impact to NG for the rest of the life cycle e.g. trans-

avoided emissions are equal to 55.99 gCO2eq/MJ of biomethane produced by

4.1.2
Limitations and strengths of the model
As said in section 3.3, the model is stationary e.g. it describes a steady state.
Many parameters inﬂuence the performance of the process in terms of efﬁciency,
methane content and productivity. Despite yielding results that are validated by
real plants and experiments, this model has limits. The GRT is not a parameter
that can be entered on PROII, it is however possible to modify the reactor volu-
metric inﬂuent to simulate a different GRT. This change compared to reality will
not have any incidence on the efﬂuent except for the total volume and/or total
mass of the outlet. This is due to the use of a conversion reactor where utilisa-
tion efﬁciency of one base component is entered. Kinetics and other parameters
like inhibitors are not taken into account.
Certainly PROII simulator does not enable to model the performance changes
of the process but when properly simulated, it generates results that can be
quite close to real cases. Actually, PROII had other advantages for this simula-
tion. This software provides many units present in petroleum-based plants and
mining industry [32], in spite of that it was possible to achieve an accurate sim-

4.3
Sensitivity Analysis
From the previous section, it clearly appears that hydrogen related costs (stor-
age and price) have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the economics of the project. It
is now necessary to evaluate the extent of this inﬂuence and if it is possible to
diminish it. This is one of the goals of this section. Furthermore, the methane
minimum selling price determined earlier points to a minimum required subsidy
of 0.7 e/Nm3 of CH4 if the natural gas price is assumed to be 0.34 e/Nm3 (30
e/MWh). Nonetheless, the subsidy amount described on ﬁgure 2.5 reaches at
most 0.63 e/Nm3 (56 e/MWh). This is conﬁrmed by Börjesson et al. who re-
port a subsidy premium that can be as high as 0.67 e/Nm3 (60 e/MWh) when
accounting for CO2 avoided emissions credits [112]. The second aim of the sen-
sitivity analysis section is thus to judge how the minimum selling price can be
reduced.
As a reminder, the parameters that were examined are the biomethane price,
the FCI, the hydrogen price, the hydrogen storage purchase cost, the working
capital and the compressor purchase price. The response variables are all the
economic metrics that is to say NPV, IRR, PBP and ROI. The method used to
assess the sensitivity is detailed in section 3.7 and the backward and forward
changes for each parameter are present on each result graph.
First of all, the biomethane price is the parameter which has the most inﬂuence
on the economic metrics that is why it was plotted on a separate graph. Fig-
ure 4.11 shows that when CH4 price is decreased (i.e. undergoes a backward
change of -28%) the NPV, IRR and ROI decrease and the PBP increases, this
reﬂects a decline in the project’s feasibility. On the contrary, when the price
increases, the NPV, IRR and ROI increase and the PBP decreases indicating an
enhancement in the project’s feasibility. The proﬁtability of the plant heavily
relies on the biomethane selling price. This conﬁrms the hypothesis made ear-
lier which was that biomethane as the only income source is very important for
the project’s economic viability. A second income source will be welcomed to
ensure the project economic viability, the extent of this type of change is yet to
be determined and it will be studied in the scenarios section.
According to ﬁgure 4.12 to 4.15, the FCI is the parameter that has the most
incidence on all the indicators after biomethane price. Also, the NPV is the most
sensitive response variable with a maximum relative perturbation of approxi-
mately ±28%. The second parameter with extensive inﬂuence is the hydrogen
price. This corroborates the observations made before on the high contribution
of hydrogen to the total product cost and therefore to the plant’s proﬁtabil-
ity. The relative change is not extensive compared to the changes caused by the

Cost comparison with amine scrubbing

To examine the position of biological methanation on the market, a compar-
ison with a commercial upgrading process was performed. The selected pro-
cess is amine scrubbing. There are many studies on the performance of amine
scrubbers in the literature but there is very little economic analysis. The re-
sources about biomethanation upgrading are scarcer, only Vo et al. and Bekker-
ing et al. carried out a feasibility analysis concerning this innovative method
[60][18][61]. On ﬁgure 4.21 are represented the total product costs (i.e. in-
cluding AD and desulphurisation). Overall the estimated costs from the present
study are within the range found in literature.
Biological upgrading is more expensive compared to amine scrubbing. This is
because of hydrogen purchased cost. Amine scrubbing main expenses are re-
lated to energy need (high temperature, condenser, etc) and raw materials.
According to Vo et al., biomethanation consumes less energy than chemical
removal [18] thus lower costs are associated with utilities for biological up-
grading. Given this information and the signiﬁcant fraction of costs induced by
hydrogen, it appears that this last cost contribution is the main responsible for
the discrepancy between these upgrading methods. The results of this thesis
show that at high capacity and provided that hydrogen price is low, the cost of a
plant using biomethanation is close to the cost of a plant using amine scrubbing.
Despite a greater need for hydrogen, the biomethane volume produced is large
enough so that the cost stays low.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
A Danish biogas plant with biological upgrading using hydrogen from water
electrolysis powered by excess of electricity was examined in this thesis. The
plant was designed to match Danish standards AD installations. Biogas pro-
duction was modelled with a bioconversion model and the upgrading process
with hydrogenotrophic reaction stoichiometry. The whole system was simulated
using PROII software.
Mass and energy balances were calculated by the simulator, and the results
were used to specify the design of each unit and size them accordingly. From
the detailed process design and balances, the investment and operation costs
were estimated. Then the minimum methane price and maximum hydrogen
price were calculated for different proﬁtability levels.
Given the high cost of a plant using biomethanation and knowing the uncer-
tainty of certain parameters, a sensitivity analysis was performed to investigate
the inﬂuence of these parameters on proﬁtability. The sensitivity analysis also
gave information on the parameters to modify in order to improve proﬁtability.
Different scenarios were investigated to identify opportunities to enhance feasi-
bility or threats to the project viability. The costs calculated were compared to
amine scrubbing, a commercial upgrading process. The environmental impact
of the two processes was compared in terms of greenhouse gas emissions.
The bioconversion model gave realistic and reliable results for biogas production
as well as the model for biomethanation. PROII was a useful tool to implement
and simulate the models, however the steady-state feature entailed limited the
study of the process performance changes (with a different GRT for instance).
Additionally the gas-liquid transfer was not taken into account in the biological
==PAGE:105==
87
upgrading reactor modelling.
The conclusion of the economic analysis is the most important. The cost of
biomethanation is high compared to commercial upgrading methods, on the
other hand it performs better with regard to GHG emissions. This is mainly due
to the hydrogen related expenses for both investment and product costs. The
project is feasible for very low hydrogen price therefore the hydrogen supply
from surplus electricity must be secured. It is possible to reduce the cost and
the price of biomethane by selling the digestate as a fertiliser. Increasing the
plant size can also be a solution if the hydrogen is inexpensive. Indeed the
maximum hydrogen price for proﬁtability is lower in the case of a large plant
compared to a smaller plant. There is also a risk of unfeasibility from the process
performance i.e. if the methane content is reduced in the ﬁnal product.
From the conclusion and limitations of this study, it would be interesting to
focus future research on:
• Ensuring optimal performance from trickle bed reactor i.e. by looking into
adequate packing material, operation conditions or reactor conﬁguration
• Ensuring optimal performance at low GRT to avoid high capital cost
• Optimising the design and size of hydrogen storage for the plant operation
taking into consideration hydrogen production with electrolysis
• Cheap hydrogen security of supply by looking into the electrolyser design
and excess electricity availability. For instance given a certain location
and off-peak electricity the plant will be designed so that the offer for hy-
drogen exceeds the demand, thus the remaining hydrogen can be directly
injected in the grid. Another solution is to combine biomethanation with
conventional upgrading, consequently when there is a hydrogen shortage
biogas will be upgraded by CO2 removal
