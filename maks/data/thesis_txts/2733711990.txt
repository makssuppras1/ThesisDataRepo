Abstract
Lignocellulose from plant biomass is the most abundant renewable organic material on earth
and presents an obvious natural resource to exploit using green biotechnology. A promising
green approach is utilizing enzyme technology for the catalytic conversion of lignocellulose. Un-
speciﬁc Peroxygenases (UPOs) are exiting enzymes that are notably abundant in lignocellulose-
degrading fungi. They eﬃciently catalyze the insertion of oxygen into diverse substrates, and
while their natural function remains unknown, results of recent research make it tempting to
speculate if UPOs are involved in lignin degradation.
In this project, heterologous expression and partial puriﬁcation of two UPOs from the fungus
Chaetomium globosum were successfully carried out. The UPOs were characterized biochemi-
cally with a focus on their activity towards lignin substructures to lay the ground for determining
if and how these enzymes could enhance the applicability of lignin. Initially, a general character-
ization was performed to establish a fundamental understanding of the enzymes. This included
activity determination, pH optimum, and hydrogen peroxide dependency. Further characteriza-
tion of the expressed UPOs on gradually more complex lignin model compounds was performed
applying a variety of analysis methods to assess and elucidate the reaction outcomes.
The UPOs demonstrated the ability to oxidize diverse lignin substructures. Comparison of the
oxidation modiﬁcations catalyzed by the two UPOs revealed interesting di↵erences in catalytic
activity. Various structures have been proposed for the possible products of the UPO-catalyzed
reactions on monoaromatic and diaromatic lignin model compounds. Notably, the results for
the diaromatic compounds suggested that the UPOs can catalyze cleavage of the β-O-4 linkage,
the most prevalent linkage in the lignin polymer. Additionally, UPO-catalyzed oxidative mod-
iﬁcation on synthetic lignin was explored. The objective was to explore the activity of UPO
by conducting a thorough structural analysis of synthetic lignin treated with UPO using 2D-
NMR. Unfortunately, the obtained results were inconclusive and UPO activity on a polymeric
substrate could not be proven.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Lignocellulose from plant biomass is the most abundant renewable organic material on earth
and presents an obvious natural resource to exploit using green biotechnology. Lignocellulose
is a complex mixture of three biomacromolecules: the polysaccharides, cellulose, and hemicel-
lulose, entangled with the aromatic heteropolymer lignin [1, 2, 3]. The catalytic conversion
of lignocellulose holds the potential for green and sustainable production of biochemicals and
biofuels leading towards a future fossil-free society [4].
Over the years, various approaches have been proposed for the conversion of lignocellulosic
biomass. Their main objective has been to extract value from the polysaccharides, while the
lignin component has been considered a hurdle and an undesired secondary product. These
treatments often disrupt the native chemistry of lignin, leaving the biopolymer as an inert
low-value byproduct solely used to generate process heat [5, 6].
Today, it has become evident that to unlock the true potential of lignocellulose as an eco-
nomically compatible and sustainable resource, both the polysaccharides and lignin need to be
valorized. Therefore, current research within the conversion of lignocellulosic biomass focuses
on developing methods to gently extract native lignin from lignocellulose [7, 4]. Ideally, lignin
can then be exploited as a natural aromatic resource together with the polysaccharides.
A promising green approach is utilizing enzyme technology for the catalytic conversion of ligno-
cellulose while preserving the native chemistry of both lignin and the polysaccharides. Inspired
by natural lignocellulose-degrading organisms, scientists have directed their research to the
fungal kingdom, where degradation of lignocellulose is ubiquitous [8]. Various potential lignin-
modifying enzymes have been isolated in their secretome, but the precise mechanisms by which
fungi degrade lignocellulose and what enzymes contribute to this process remain ambiguous
[9, 10]. Therefore, the search for novel lignin-active enzymes, elucidation of their individual
roles, and their synergistic interactions remain the focus of present scientiﬁc research within the
biocatalytic conversion of lignocellulose.
An interesting enzyme family is Unspeciﬁc Peroxygenases (UPO) (EC 1.11.2.1).
UPOs are
only found in the fungal kingdom and are noticeably abundant in lignocellulose-degrading
organisms[8]. UPOs eﬃciently catalyze the insertion of an oxygen atom from H2O2 into a di-
verse variety of substrates. Much research has been put into their potential as a biocatalyst in
pharmaceutical and organic research, but their natural function in fungi remains unknown.[11].

1.2
Lignocellulose
1.1.1
Hypothesis and objectives
In this project we raised the hypothesis that UPOs catalyse oxidative modiﬁcation of lignin or
lignin derivitates from plant biomass. Such oxyfunctionalisation can potentially greatly enhance
the reactivity and applicability of lignin as a renewable and sustainable resource.
The overall aim of the project was to investigate this hypothesis by performing the following:
- Recombinant expression in Pichia pastoris of up to three di↵erent UPOs from Chaetomium
globosum and partial puriﬁcation of the proteins.
- A biochemical characterization of expressed UPOs with focus on their catalyzed oxidative
reactions on lignin substructures.
1.2
Lignocellulose
Lignin is the most abundant renewable source of natural aromatics as it accounts for roughly
30% of the organic carbon within plant biomass[12]. Lignin is present in cell walls of all vascular
plants and constitutes one of the three major components of lignocellulosic biomass together
with the polysaccarides cellulose and hemicellulose. These three biopolymers comprise up to 80
% of the total weight of raw plant materials. None of these components exist as independent
polymers but are intertwined in the cell wall to form a complex structure as illustrated in
ﬁgure 1.1 [1]. Lignin is considered to function as a resin, binding the lignocellulosic matrix
together by covalent crosslinking various polysaccarides, primarily hemicellulose and pectin.
These linkages are referred to as lignin-carbohydrate linkages (LCC). The main LCCs found in
wood are phenyl glycoside bonds, benzyl ethers and esters. The ester linkage is typically formed
between an aliphatic alcohol of the lignin polymer and glucuronoxylan, the main component of
hemicellulose [13, 14]. This composite structure of the cell wall serves as a protective and resilient
barrier for the plants towards biodegradation. As lignin is covalently linked to carbohydrates,
lignin derived from lignocellulose often contain a small amount of carbohydrates. Apart from
these components lignocellulosic biomass contains small amounts of pectin, ash and salts [3].

The proportion of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin in lignocellulosic biomass is generally dis-
tributed in a 4:3:3 ratio, respectively. Nonetheless, this ratio is subject to variation due to
signiﬁcant di↵erences in cell wall structure and chemical composition among plant species. Lig-
nocellulosic biomass can be broadly categorized into three main types: softwood (gymnosperms),
hardwood (angiosperms), and grass or herbaceous plants (graminaceous). Even within the same
species and across di↵erent components like wood and bark, variations in this ratio are observed.
The proportions can further be inﬂuenced by other factors such as growth conditions and the
development stage of the plant [15, 16].
1.3
Structure of lignin
Lignin is a complex aromatic polymer composed of methoxylated phenylpropanoid units. Lignin
forms a hydrophobic and amorphous heteropolymer exhibiting great variation in both chemical
structure and composition due to a low degree of order and high heterogeneity. The primary
monomeric precursors of lignin are three cinnamyl alcohols; sinapyl alcohol, para-coumaryl
alcohol and coniferyl alcohol, (ﬁgure 1.2). These monomeric units are also refered to as syringyl
(S), guaiacyl (G) and p-hydroxyphenyl (H) units, termed monolignols, when incorporated into
the lignin polymer [6, 17, 18].

Structure of lignin
Figure 1.2: Structure of the three precursors para-coumaryl alcohol, coniferyl alcohol, and sinapyl alcohol together with the

The in vivo synthesis of lignins in plants is believed to begin through enzymatically initiated
chemical reactions that includes oxidative polymerisation of the three precursors. Similair to
other polymers, lignins are constructed and assembled from their monomers, the monolignols.
The lignin polymerisation process is termed ligniﬁcation where the lignin macromolecule devel-
ops through free radical coupling of the monolignols [19]. Ligniﬁcation begins with dehydrogena-
tive dimerization of two precursor molecules and proceeds with additional cross-coupling of the
formed dimers or oligomers with adjacent radicalised monomers. The oxidative polymerisation
process occurs in the apoplast of the plant cell that constitutes the extracellular space between
plant cell membranes where the cell wall is subsequently formed. The oxidative polymerization
is catalysed by the oxidative enzymes laccases (EC 1.10.3.2) or peroxidases( EC 1.11.1.7) [20].
Oxidation of the precursors generates phenoxy radicals, that are relatively stable due to the aro-
matic ring and conjugated side-chains enabling single-electron resonance stabilisation, (ﬁgure
1.3. Subsequently, the lignin polymer grows through random radical-radical coupling between
the phenoxy radicals. Proteins are believed to contribute solely to the formation of phenoxy
radicals and not as speciﬁc directing proteins. Therefore, the ﬁnal structure of the lignin poly-
mer is predominantly inﬂuenced by the random process of radical coupling [21]. Nevertheless,
as in every chemical reaction, factors such as pH and the quantity of reactants still inﬂuence the
resulting linkages within the biopolymer.[5] Consequently, the actual structural conﬁguration
of the lignin macromolecule remains undetermined.

Structure of lignin
Figure 1.3: Generation of the resonance-stabilized phenoxy radical of p-coumaryl alcohol catalysed by oxidative enzymes
The linkages between the monolignols and the ratio of S, G and H units deﬁnes the chemical
structure of lignin. In principle, the coupling between the monolignol-radicals can happen in
many ways and generate a variety of substructures leading to a random structure of lignin [22].
Coupling between the precusors occur via inter-unit linkages and the oxidative polymerisation of
the monolignols is favoured at the β-position. Two types of linkages can be formed between the
precursors: ether bonds or carbon-carbon linkages, termed condensed linkages [12, 4]. Figure
1.4 depicts the most commonly found linkages between the monomers in the lignin polymer.
Coupling of two monolignols always includes the β-position of at least one of the precursors [23].
In native lignin, the most abundant linkage is the β-O-4 ether bond that often comprises more
than 50% [15]. Frequent C-C bonds formed by β couplings are β-5’, β-1 and β-β’, where β-5’
is the most common C-C bond in lignin. The inter-unit linkages 4-O-5’ and 5-5’ are potential
branching points of the lignin-polymers that only has been proven to occur between two growing
polymers and not between monomers [4, 17].
Figure 1.4: Overview of common inter-unit linkages in the lignin polymer [21].

ferent ratios of monomeric precursors and distribution of linkages. Consequently, there may
not exist two identical lignin macromolecules given the intricate complexity and diversity of
lignin together with variation in degree of polymerisation [22]. Native lignins typically consist
of 80-90% non-phenolic subunits and 10-20% phenolic subunits. Softwood lignins are primarily

1.4
Catalytic conversion of lignocellulosic biomass
1.4.1
Use of lignin in bioreﬁneries
The utilisation of lignin to value-added chemicals will ensure economic feasibility of bioreﬁneries
by overcoming the initial cost associated with processing of biomass. Additionally, the potential
conversion of lignin permits separation of these components aiding to the accessibility of the
polysaccarides to biological and chemical digestion [26, 7].
If all components can be fully
valorized thus the maximum exploitation of biomass can be achieved. Unfortunately, current
industrial processing of biomass, e.g. in the pulping industry, entails in structural modiﬁcation
of lignin. Common functional groups of the lignin polymer, such as ethers and hydroxy groups,
are modiﬁed resulting in non-phenolic substructures and condensed linkages. Overall, a more
recalcitrant and inert version of lignin is formed, referred to as technical lignin [15, 6].
In contrast, milder treatment of biomass could achieve degradation of lignocellulose while pre-
serving the native structure. With its distinctive chemical structure and properties, native lignin
has the potential to yield a wide variety of bulk and ﬁne chemicals, especially aromatics, for a
bio-based economy[4]. Enzyme-based methods are regarded as a key enabling technology for
biomass conversion where these valuable products could be derived from lignin substructures
and monolignols. [7, 26].
1.4.2
Enzymes involved in natural lignin modiﬁcation
The exact mechanisms of lignin degradation in lignocellulosic microorganisms are still not fully
understood. Since lignin is a heterogeneous and complex macromolecule it is logical that a
diverse set of enzymes with di↵erent redox potential and substrate speciﬁcity is engaged in
natural degradation of lignocellulose.
Enzymes reported to catalyse the degradation, modiﬁcation, or formation of complex carbo-
hydrates are described in the Carbohydrate-Active enZymes (CAZy) Database [27]. For hemi-
cellulose and cellulose, hydrolases are able to enzymatically cleave the glycosidic bond in both
polysaccarides whereas enzymatic lignin modiﬁcation is more challenging due to its heteroge-

CAZy database [9].

concert with hemi- and cellulolytic enzymes. The AA enzymes are predominantly found in

1.5.1
Characteristics
Unspeciﬁc peroxygenases (EC 1.11.2.1) are oxidoreducatases and belong to the family of fungal
peroxygenases. They catalyse insertion of an oxygen atom from H2O2 into a diverse variety of
substrates. UPOs are extracellular proteins that are exclusively found in the fungal kingdom

[32].
In 2004, Hofrichter et al. documented the discovery of the ﬁrst UPO from the white-rot fungus
- Agrocybe aegerita [33]. Subsequently, UPOs have been isolated in secretomes from several
fungi and UPO sequences were discovered in genomes throughout the fungal kingdom across
various phyla. Phylogenetically, UPO sequences split into two major protein families; family I
and Family II. Family I consist of short UPO sequences with an average mean size of 26 kDa
whereas family II consist of longer sequences and have a mean size of 44 kda [11].
Unspeciﬁc peroxygenases are heavily glycosylated heme-thiolate proteins and carbohydrates
may constitute between 10-40% of the ﬁnal molecular weight of UPOs [11]. As indicated by
the term ”hemethiolate protein”, the active site of UPOs consists of a prostetic heme group
(iron protoporphyrin IX) coordinated to the thio group of a cysteine residue. Typically, this
cysteine residue is ﬂanked by two proline residues that forms a conserved PCP motif in nearly
all UPOs, (ﬁgure 1.5).
In short UPOs, a conserved glutamic acid residue acts as an acid-
base catalyst in the beginning of the UPOs’ catalytic cycle [34]. The heme-thiolate structure
gives rise to a distinct UV–Vis spectrum for UPO proteins in the ground state with an intense
peak between 415 and 420 nm. The globular protein consists of ten characteristic ↵-helices,
approximately ﬁve very short β-sheets and a distinctive disulphide bridge that stabilizes the
C-terminal region. Among UPOs, the heme access channel can vary in amino acid sequence,
dimensions and geometry. The channel guides the substrate towards the ferric heme and the
di↵erentiation of the access channel among UPO is of relevance for substrate speciﬁcity [35, 36].

1.5.2
Reactions and catalytic cycle
UPOs perform both peroxidase and peroxygenase activity on various substrates resulting in a
wide range of catalysed reactions. Figure 1.6 gives an overview of the many catalyzed reactions
and the large substrate spectrum of UPOs. In the peroxygenase mode, UPOs catalyse diverse
oxygen insertion and examples of catalyzed reactions are hydroxylation of sp3-carbons, epoxi-
dation of sp2 carbons along with oxygenation of heteroatoms. The oxyfunctionalized substrates
have been proven to undergo further spontaneous reactions resulting in O- and N-dealkylation,
epoxide opening, deacylation, or aromatization. In the peroxidase mode, UPOs catalyse one-
electron oxidation into typical electron-rich substrates, such as phenols, forming reactive radicals
[37, 11].
Figure 1.6: Summarized scheme of reactions catalysed by UPOs [?]
Besides using H2O2 as electron acceptor and oxygen donor for oxygenation reactions, UPOs
catalyze H2O2 disproportionation via a catalase-like reaction at high H2O2 concentrations.
From two molecules of H2O2, one molecule of O2 and two molecules of water is formed by
the catalase-like reaction. The catalase reaction results in heme-destruction and thus enzyme
inactivation [38]. UPOs sensitivity towards high H2O2 concentrations is considered a major
limitation for their industrial application as biocatalyst.
The dual catalytic cycle of UPO is illustrated below in ﬁgure 1.7. The peroxygenase pathway
simulates the peroxide ”shunt” pathway of the better known monoxygenase cytochrome P450
while the peroxide pathways is similar to the ”classic” peroxidase cycle [34].

The resting state UPO consists of ferric-heme bound to water as a remote ligand. In the ﬁrst
step of both pathways, water is replaced by H2O2 followed by heterolytic cleavage of the bound
H2O2. In short UPOs, the negative charge of a deprotonated glutamic acid residue acts as
an acid-base catalyst in this heterolytic cleavage of H2O2 to form compound I. Both pathways
initiates from compound I which is an oxoferryl cation radical complex that has strong oxidant
properties enabling attacks on C-H bonds (sp3), C=C bonds (sp2), heteroatoms, and phenolic
OH-groups via radical formation as illustrated on ﬁgure 1.7. The pathways di↵ers regarding the
state of compound II. In the peroxygenase pathway compound II is protonated by the substrate
to form a hydroxide-ferryl complex in contrast to the peroxidase pathway where compound II
remains an oxo-ferryl complex. What speciﬁcally determines if a UPO follows the peroxygenase
or peroxidase catalytic route is not fully understood. Research indicates that it can depend on
the particular UPO, redox potential, substrate size, geometry of the substrate-binding site, pH
value, organic solvent and dimensions of the heme-channel [11].
1.5.3
Application and natural role of Unspeciﬁc peroxygenases
UPOs have received attention for their potential in industrial application because they eﬃciently
oxidize a variety of substrates using only H2O2 as cosubstrate. Since selective oxyfunctional-
ization of unactivated carbons are among the most diﬃcult reactions to realize in synthetic
organic chemistry, extensive research has been done on their possible application as oxidative
biocatalyst in organic and pharmaceutical synthesis. Beside their sensitivity towards H2O2, an-
other major bottleneck of the industrial application and characterization of novel UPOs is their
limited availability. Only a few UPOs are commercially available, and heterologous expression
is challenging and most often results in low yields (5-220 mg/L)[39].

In contrast, far less research has focused on investigating UPOs natural function which con-
sequently remains unknown. UPOs are abundant in lignocellulose degrading organisms where
their respective genomes posses several UPO encoding genes. For instance the genomes for
the known lignocellulose degrading fungi Chaetomium globussom, Agrocybe aegerita, Agaricus
bisporus have 4, 18 and 24 UPO encoding genes, respectively [40, 11]. For the latter fungus,
Agaricus bisporus, its secretome during 15 days cultivation on wheat straw as the sole carbon
source was recently studied by Duran et al [8]. Several UPOs were identiﬁed and at high abun-
dance when substrate deligniﬁcation was highest. Furthermore, the secretome was proven to
cleave the β-O-4 linkage of the dimeric lignin model, syringylglycerol β-guaiacyl ether (SBG)
as well as oxidising the nonphenolic veratrylglycerol β-guaiacyl ether (VBG) and phenolic gua-
iacylglycerol β-guaiacyl ether (GBG). In 2011, Kinne et al. also reported how the well studied
AaeUPO from Agrocybe aegerita was able to cleave the ether bond of VBG [41]. Finally, an
investigation of expressed enzymes of litter cultures at nine forest site found expression of UPOs
at all sites[28].
Based on these ﬁndings, it is tempting to speculate that UPOs are involved in lignin degradation.
In this project, we would like to investigate how the activity of UPOs are towards various lignin
substructures and related compounds. We did that by recombinant expression of three UPOs
from Chaetomium globusum, performing partial puriﬁcation of each, and by characterizing each
enzyme towards various lignin model compounds.
1.6
Chaetomium globosum
Chaetomium globosum is a ﬁlamentous fungus and belongs to the phylum Ascomycota. The fun-
gus commonly colonizes in soil, on plant materials and other cellulose- and lignin-rich substrates.
Chaetomium globosum is renowned for its excellent lignocellulolytic abilities and was one of the
ﬁrst ascomycetes investigated for its ability to break down lignocellulose [42, 43].
Genome
sequencing of Chaetomium globosum has revealed a substantial number of genes encoding var-
ious enzymes contained in the CAZy database including more than a hundred sequences from
the AA family. A complete list can be found in the CAZy database. Based on Chaetomium
globosum proﬁciency in degrading lignocellulosic biomass and its diverse range of expressed
lignocellulolytic enzymes, it was decided to explore three UPOs encoded by this fungus.

To determine activity and operational robustness of the expressed UPOs, the substrates 2,2’-
azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid (ABTS) and veratryl alcohol (VA), was em-
ployed for spectrophotometric assays. Upon one-electron oxidation ABTS forms a radical cation,
that is frequently used to measure laccase and peroxidase activity. While VA is a standard perox-
idase substrate as well, its redox potential exceeds that of ABTS [39]. The formation mechanism
of veratryl aldehyde by peroxidases and peroxygenases di↵ers. Peroxidases initially abstract an
electron from the aromatic ring, creating an aryl cation radical. This radical then undergoes
C↵-deprotonation and a second electron abstraction, leading to the formation of the aldehyde.
In contrast, peroxygenases hydroxylate C↵, resulting in the formation of a geminal alcohol that
is in equilibrium with veratraldehyde [33]. Additionally, VA is a secondary metabolite in fungi
and is thought to play an important role in fungal degradation of lignin [44].
The UPOs were further characterized on simple monoaromatics lignin model substrates: sy-
ringol, guaiacol and veratrol. The corresponding acid of coniferyl alcohol, ferulic acid (FA), was
also studied. As stated previously, FA is a common component of the cell wall and contains
several functional groups that UPOs are reported to oxidise, (ﬁgure 1.6).
As the β-O-4 linkage is by far the most abundant in the native lignin polymer, an enzyme able
to catalyze cleavage of this particular linkage is of great interest. Consequently, a wide array of
dimeric model compounds with varying levels of resemblance to the β-O-4 ether bond in lignin
have been utilised to screen for β-etherase activity [17]. In this projects we investigated the
UPOs activity towards the phenolic dimer guaiacylglycerol-beta-guaiacyl ether (GBG) and the
non-phenolic dimer veratrylglycerol-beta-guaiacyl ether (VBG). In a previuous study, Kinne et
al. found that the AaeUPO was able to catalyze cleavage of VBG with initial oxidation of the C↵
carbon [41]. The same, AaeUPO was proved to hydroxylate linear alkanes [45]. Therefore, the
expressed UPOs ability to catalyze hydroxylation of a completely aliphatic substrate, hexane,
was investigated.
In a ﬁnal study, it was investigated if the UPOs were able to catalyze oxidative modiﬁcation
on a more complex lignin model compound. Synthetic lignin was synthesized in vitro by us-
ing a biomemetic approach replicating the natural process of ligniﬁcation by dehydrogenative
polymerization[20]. This polymeric lignin model compound, termed dehydrogenation polymers
(DHPs,) are structurally similar to native lignin although likely with a higher percentage of
condensed linkages than native lignin [21]. The DHP were synthesized from coniferyl alcohol
using a laccase from myceliophthora thermophila.

3.3.9
UPO study on hexan
UPOs have been reported to catalyze hydroxylation on longer aliphatic chains, producing the
corresponding alcohol [45]. In this study, we investigated whether the UPOs could hydroxylate
the aliphatic substrate hexane to the corresponding alcohol, hexanol.
This substrate was not directly related to lignin but was included to determine whether the
UPOs could hydroxylate unactivated sp3 carbons, indicating a high redox potential of the
UPOs. The assays were conducted under two conditions: either with extensive shaking or in
a 10% acetone solution. Unfortunately, no hexanol could be detected by GC-MS under either
condition, and hence, it could not be proven that the UPOs could hydroxylate a completely

3.4
Further studies
The acquired results in this project give rise to many intriguing studies for future research. To
begin with, determining the thermal midpoint of the UPOs would enable us to assess their sta-
bility more comprehensively and determine the optimal reaction temperature for future assays.
A particular interesting approach is to exploit the large pH-range, in which ChaG571 remains
active. This could be utilized to conduct experiments investigating the potential e↵ect of pH on
product formation. Overall, there is an exciting future prospect of investigating the parameters
that inﬂuence reactions catalyzed by the UPOs.
It is further proposed to explore UPOs’ potential synergistic e↵ect with other known lignocel-
lulolytic enzymes. Finally, the mentioned optimisations points of the performed studies in this
project could be addressed.

Conclusion
4
Conclusion
The objective of this project was to heterologously express, partially purify and characterize
three selected UPOs, ChaG320, ChaG571 and ChaG721, from the fungus Chaetomium globo-
sum.
In conclusion, two out of the three UPOs, ChaG320 and ChaG571, were successfully
expressed in Pichia pastoris and were partially puriﬁed by IEX and SEC.
Initial characterization on standard peroxidase substrates, ABTS and VA, indicated a notable
di↵erence in peroxidase versus peroxygenase activity, pH optima and sensitivity to hydrogen
peroxide between ChaG320 and ChaG571.
After a fundamental understanding of the two UPOs was obtained, further characterization was
performed on lignin substructures with increasing complexity. Various analytical techniques
were applied to examine resulting products including LC-MS, GC-MS and NMR. The results
obtained in the project contribute to the following interesting ﬁndings:
◦The substrate to hydrogen peroxide ratio did not appear to decrease peroxidase activity.
Instead, it was proven to a↵ect rate of substrate conversion.
◦ChaG571 displayed highest peroxygenase activity, catalyzing insertion of oxygen to gen-
erate more polar and electron-rich substrates.
◦ChaG320 displayed highest peroxidase activity, yielding more polymerized and diverse
products.
◦Indications were found, suggesting that the UPO were able to catalyze cleavage of the
abundant β-O-4 ether linkage in diaromatic lignin model compounds. The obtained results
suggested that peroxidase activity was important for the cleavage to occur.
◦Utilising 2D-NMR for structural characterisation of UPO-treated DHP found no di↵erence
between control and enzyme samples. Thus, in the settings of this project, it could not
be proven that the expressed UPOs were able to catalyse oxidative modiﬁcation of a
polymeric synthetic lignin substrate.
Overall, the ﬁndings of this project revealed a notable di↵erence between the activity of the
two expressed UPOs. The UPOs displayed evident activity towards lignin substructures and
the obtained results hold great potential for further exploration.
