Abstract:
Take-away foods is a growing trend in Denmark and automated kitchen will be new
way to get take-way foods like burgers or sandwiches where it have also eliminated
any contamination coming from human handling. Although, the automated kitchen
is a new way of making food it still needs to interact with raw food. As raw foods
carry microorganism, there is a possibility for contamination of the kitchen with
pathogenic bacteria which could lead to cross-contamination. This present thesis
is looking into how L. monocytogenes and E. coli (VTEC) are behaving on the
knife, fork, pusher and two types of kitchen surfaces over time. The experiments
was three strains of Listeria innocua and three strains of a non-toxin producing
VTEC E. coli were chosen as biological indicators for the pathogens. The knife was
contaminated by cutting inoculated lettuce leaves where the fork and the pusher
pushed inoculated lettuce to be contaminated. The two types stainless steel were
investigated as kitchen surfaces. The kitchen surfaces were inoculated by drops of L.
innocua and E. coli cultures. Recovery of L. innocua and E. coli from the various
surfaces over time were carried out by a swabbing method. The results show that L.
monocytogenes and E. coli could live 24 hours after contamination on all the items’
surfaceses and the knife could cross-contaminate other foodstuﬀwhen contaminated.
The conclusion was thatL. monocytogenes and E. coli could survive after 24 hours
on any surfaces in the automated kitchen and therefore needs to have a cleaning
program that insures cleaning after every interaction with food.

Introduction
In Denmark the restaurant, cafe and fast food industry is growing. In the last 6
years up to year 2017 the revenue in the private food service market increased by 6%
every year and 9% of the Danes eat out on weekdays and 12% eat out on weekends
(LandbrugFødevarer (2018)).
The automated kitchen is a new way that the Danes can get take-way. An au-
tomated kitchen would make food like a burger or a sandwich without any human
contact which would decrease the amount of cost needed to salary and also eliminate
any contamination coming from human handling. It has the advantage that it can
be placed anywhere e.g. on a train station where a normal restaurant could have
problems with space to work in. It could also be used in super markets where the
customers could get food to go while they are buying groceries or placed on a festival
where it could make food in every hour of the day.
In the automated kitchen there will be raw food that the kitchen needs to cook,
therefore there is a possibility for the raw food to carry pathogen bacteria into the
kitchen and from there contaminate essential parts that will cross-contaminate the
product the kitchen is making. Therefore like any other food industry the auto-
mated kitchen needs a food safety program to protect the customers.
In this report the kitchen is making a burger and from that the report is look-
ing at two prominent bacteria, L. monocytogenes and E. coli, and how they are
behaving on some of the essential parts of the kitchen e.g. the knife that cuts the
food and the fork that carries the food from point A to B. To get information on
how to program the automated kitchen in a way so that it minimizes the happening
of cross-contamination, this report will investigate the behavior of L. monocytogenes
and E. coli.

2.1
Bacteria In The Burger
In this report the automated kitchen will be making a burger containing bread,
minced meat, cheese, tomatoes, cucumber and lettuce. The two prominent bacteria
found in these food items are Listeria monocytogenes and Escherichia coli (Zilelidou
et al. (2015),Sofos (2014)). This does not mean that these two bacteria are the only
ones that could be present, but in this report they will not be taken into account.
2.1.1
Escherichia coli
Escherichia coli or commonly known as E. coli is the largest variety of bacteria,
non-spore-forming Gram-negative bacterium (Percival and Williams (2013)) that
normally lives in humans and other warm blooded animals (Montville et al. (2012)).
The ones that are patogens to humans, have the O:H serotype. The O stands for
somatic antigenes located at the surface of the cell and linked to the lipopolysac-
charides and some of them can make Shiga toxins and H stands for ﬂagel antigenes
linked to proteins on the surface of the ﬂagella (Mead and Griﬃn (1998)). The
pathogen E. coli is categoried as (Montville et al. (2012)):
• Enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC)
• Verotoxigenic E. coli (VTEC/EHEC /STEC)
• Enteroinvasive E. coli(EIEC)
• Enterpathogenic E. coli(EPEC)
• Diﬀusely adhering E. coli (DAEC)
Because E. coli normally is in the intestines of animals it should come as no surprise
that cross-contamination from the gut and into the meat is one of the ways that
foodstuﬀis contaminated. But E. coli can also be found in diﬀerent vegetables like
potatoes, lettuce and sprouts (Montville et al. (2012)).

Shiga Toxins
Shiga toxins or verocytotoxins are toxins made by E. coli O157:H7. E. coli O157:H7
makes two types of Shiga toxins (VT1, VT2). The ﬁrst (VT1) is produced indis-
tinguishable from Shigella dysenteriae type 1 where the second (VT2) is more a
divergent molecule, where only 56 % aminoacid homology is the same as Shiga
toxin 1 (Mead and Griﬃn (1998), Karmali et al. (2010)). Shiga toxin is not enough
to make a person sick in itself. By using a plasmid and the Locus of enterocyte
eﬀacement, E. coli can trick the body to think that E. coli is a source of iron and
so it is released from the intestine into the body (Nataro and Kaper (1998)). On
Figure 2.1 the time frame of E. coli O157:H7 infection of the body (Mead and Grif-
ﬁn (1998)) is shown. The infection dose is around a CFU of 105 -106 to devolve a
reaction in the body Percival and Williams (2013).
Figure 2.1: Infection period with E coli O157:H7 by Mead and Griﬃn (1998)
The mechanisms which cause haemorrhagic colitis and haemolytic uraemic syn-
drome (HUS) are not fully understood (Nataro and Kaper (1998), Mead and Griﬃn
(1998)). On Figure 2.1 it can be seen that only 5 % of people getting infected by E.
coli O157:H7 devolve the HUS. HUS can be lethal Mead and Griﬃn (1998), as in
Denmark where two children died from an E. coli VTEC infection in 2019 (Meinecke
(2019), Krause (2019)).
Growth
E. coli is relatively good in acid environments. It can grow down to pH 4, but it
needs to be said that it is only possible if other factors are favorable for the E. coli
(Montville et al. (2012)). It can grow in temperatures from 100C to 430C (Marr
et al. (1964)).
2.1.2
Listeria monocytogenes
Listeria monocytogenes is one of the ten species in the Listeria genus. L. monocy-
togenes is a Gram positive facultative anaerobic and non spore forming bacteria. It
6
==PAGE:9==
is listed as a human pathogen bacteria due to the the illness listeriosis, caused by
L. monocytogenes. L. monocytogenes is ubiquitous in the environment and can be
found in soil and plants, furthermore also in animals and ﬁsh.
The primary way it is transmitted to humans is through consumption of contami-
nated foodstuﬀ(Montville et al. (2012), Ricci et al. (2018)).
This contamination is normally connected to cross-contamination from the pro-
cess environment, like slicing, cutting, slaughtering and other processing methods
due to the L. monocytogeness ability to form bioﬁlm and grow in a large range of
temperatures and pH-values. (Montville et al. (2012), Ricci et al. (2018)).
Listeriosis
If L. monocytogenes is consumed it will enter the body through the intestinal barrier
and be internalized by the macrophages, where it will replicate. Through the blood
the L. monocytogenes will be carried to the lymph nodes. After that they hit the
liver and spleen, where most of them will be killed. Those that survive will travel
to the brain and placenta through the blood. (Montville et al. (2012)).
In 2017 Listeriosis was the most severe zoonosis with the highest hospitalisation and
mortality rate based on the data from hospitals, where one out of nine died from
Listeriosis in EU (Journal (2018)).
Because it has a mortality rate this high, Listeriosis is a severe disease where the
symptoms vary from a mild diarrhoea, ﬂu to meningitis, septicemia, miscarriage and
premature labour (Ricci et al. (2018)). But because one of the essential genes for
L. monocytogenes that makes us sick is thermoregulated (Montville et al. (2012)),
and because the incubation period can vary between a day and several months, is
it very hard to locate the source of contamination and infection (Hansen (2014)).
It has been attempted to make a dose-response relationship, but due to the dif-
ﬁculty in getting data because of the long incubation and the retrieval of the L.
monocytogenes from humans, a lot of assumptions have been made for the assump-
tions model to work (Buchanan et al. (1997)).
But from a general perspective
there is risk of getting Listeriosis without consumption of relatively high levels of
L. monocytogenes. Around >104CFU is very low even for the people who are most
susceptible for L. monocytogenes (Buchanan et al. (1997)).
However there is a big diﬀerence between studies of how high the CFU of L. mono-
cytogenes needs to be to get Listeriosis. In the EFSA report (2018) they say that
intake of less than 100 CFU/g is not risky for healthy individuals, however people
who are susceptible to L. monocytogenes could be at risk, Ricci et al. (2018) ex.

Bioﬁlm
Bioﬁlm is in short terms an aggregation of cells. It can be many diﬀerent species
in a complex structure on a solid surface (Montville et al. (2012)). The way bioﬁlm
is formed can be described as a ﬁve step process (Myszka and Czaczyk (2011)) (an
illustration of this can be seen on Figure 2.2) :
1. An initial reversible attachment of planktonic microorganisms to a solid sur-
face.
2. A transition of reversible to irreversible attachment by a production of extra-
cellular polymers (EPS) by the bacteria.
3. The development of an early bioﬁlm architecture will form.
4. The development of microcolonies that lead to a mature bioﬁlm.
5. Dispersion of cells from bioﬁlm into the surrounding environment.
8
==PAGE:11==
Figure 2.2: A ﬁve step ﬁgure of how bioﬁlm is formed from Puiu (2017)
As said multiple times by now, for bioﬁlm to form it needs a surface, and studies
show that the properties of the abiotic surface have a big inﬂuence on the forming
of bioﬁlm due to its inﬂuence on initial cell attachment (Shi and Zhu (2009)).
Other studies have studied the roughness of the surface (more details is said about
roughness in the section Roughness of Stainless Steel) and its eﬀect on bioﬁlm. In
the studies from Howell and Behrends (2006) there was observed that there was
a certain increase in bioﬁlm on surfaces with high surface numbers (Howell and
Behrends (2006)). On Figure 2.3 it can be seen that bacteria is forming bioﬁlm in
the cracks of the surfaces.
There is also a correlation with the study from Mitik-Dineva et al. (2009) that say
that roughened materials provide a more favourable site for colonization of bacteria
(Mitik-Dineva et al. (2009)).

play for bioﬁlm to form. Stainless steel like Alloy 316 is one of the most common
chosen stainless steel in food process due to its strength, corrosion resistance and
wear resistance and it is relatively cheap. On top of that it is possible to get a
low roughness of the surface (Awad et al. (2018)). The roughness of a surface is

3.2
Inocula
Inocula are needed in all the experiments. It has been made by ﬁrst transferring
E. coli strains MS 17680, MS 21811 and MS 21816 and the L. innocua strains
MS21472, 1207 and 696 from a TSA with 5% calf blood agarplates to each its own
tube containing 10mL BHI for then to incubate for 24 hours at 37 0C. Thereafter
10 µL of the overnight culture was transferred to a 100 mL BHI broth bottle. This
was done to make the inoculum as standardized as possible. The bottle was also
incubated in 24 hours at 37 0C. To make the experiment as realistic as possible,
where the lettuce and other foodstuﬀwould be cooled, the broth was transferred to
a refrigerator at 4 0C for 24 hours after the overnight incubation.
For all the experiments, except the spectrophotometry experiment (Experiment Of
The Forming Of Bioﬁlm) two washes of the inoculum were done. It was done by
centrifuging at 3800 rpm for 10 min. After the centrifugation the BHI was replaced
with salt water and centrifuged again with the same parameters and the salt water
was poured out with new salt water. After that the washed inoculum was used in
the rest of the experiments. On Figure 3.1 a ﬂow chart over the inoculum is shown.

3.3
The Swabbing Method
Throughout all the experiments, again except the spectrophotometry experiment
(Experiment Of The Forming Of Bioﬁlm), a Swabbing Method was performed.
Because there was no consensus on how to retrieve the bacteria from surfaces, a
Preliminary Experiment on two diﬀerent kinds of cotton swab was done.
They
were selected based on the studies from (Tebbutt (1991), Zilelidou et al. (2014) and
Hansen (2014)). The two cotton swabs that were tested were a Roaly cotton swab
and a normal cotton swab.
The cotton swabs were tested on a single knife, prepared as described in section Pre-
liminary Experiment On The Knife one side of the knife to each side of the cotton
swabs. The method that has been used is:
1. First a tube was ﬁlled with 5 ml BHI.
2. After that the swab was put in the tube for 1 min.
3. The swab was thereafter used on the item (ca 2 min) and returned to the same
tube.
13
==PAGE:16==
4. A new dry swab was then used on the item (ca 3 min) and returned to the
same tube as the ﬁrst one.
5. The tube was then put in the refrigerator for 1 hour.
6. Thereafter the tube was vortexed at 3000 rpm for 30 sec. and a dilution was
made from the interval 10−1 to 10−4 and spread out on SSI red agar and
Palcam agar.
These six steps will in the rest of the report be called the Swabbing Method or in
short swabed.
After this preliminary experiment the Roaly Cotton Swab was selected to be used
for the rest of the experiments.

5.1
Knife
5.1.1
Preliminary Experiment Of The Knife
When looking at the Preliminary Experiment for both E. coli and L. innocua (Fig-
ure 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, 4.4 and 4.5) it can be seen that there was a big variation from
experiment to experiment. Sometimes the experiment got a really high CFU/knife
and sometimes there was nothing. When the knife was dipped more than once there
was especially next to nothing E. coli left on the knife. On Figure 4.4 it is only
because of the limit of detection that there is a bar where on the L. innocua there
was bacteria after the second dip but under the limit of detection on the third dip
(Figure 4.2). It suggests that the L. innocua is better at sticking on the knife. That
would also be in line with the study from Habimana et al. (2009) that has shown
that L. monocytogenes has a method to stick to the surface due the its properties
of making bioﬁlm.
Something in this experiment either takes oﬀthe bacteria or the bacteria want
to get oﬀthe knife. When looking at Figure 4.5 over the salt water it can be seen
that E. coli and L. innocua is migrating from the knife and over to the water when
it is dipped in the water. It could be that when the knife breaks the water surface
and are lowered down into the water it actually pushes the bacteria oﬀ. But if that
is the case, that will mean that the bacteria do not attach particularly well on the
knife and that way L. innocua was in higher amounts due to the fact that it can
make bioﬁlm. On the other hand it does not explain why it then after the third dip
is gone.
50
==PAGE:53==
Another explanation could be that there is so little food on the knife that when the
knife goes into the water the bacteria leave the knife. There was only 10% BHI in
the saltwater and that number was not based on an article but the educated guess
that the bacteria needed a little to survive on the knife. In the studies from Kim
and Frank (1995) they have shown that growth of L. monocytogenes is limited by
nutrient and water availability so it may be that it was too little nutrient and that
is why the bacteria did not want to be on the knife. To come closer to that further
research would be needed.
5.1.2
The Knife Cutting In Lettuce Leaves
The cutting lettuce should have had more parameters that was harder to control
than in the Preliminary Experiment, like pressure while cutting, the amount of
nutrients would vary from lettuce to lettuce and the fact the bacteria would not be
distributed equally. It was observed under the experiments that there were pieces
of lettuce leaves on the knife after the cutting, as can be seen on Figure 5.1. These
pieces were diﬀerent from experiment to experiment and in theory these leaves could
not only have bacteria on them but also provide nutrients to the bacteria. Because
there was no plan on how to remove them from the provider that made the kitchen
they were not removed.
Figure 5.1: V ¨ORDA knife with lettuce pieces
Despite that the cutting lettuce leaves experiment was much more similar than the
Preliminary Experiment. On Figure 4.13 the 24 hour experiment over E. coli and
L. innocua can be seen. It looks as if there is a slide increase of both bacteria at the
ﬁrst 3 hours, but between 3 and 4 hours there is a drop. After that until 6 hours
both bacteria’s bars are stable at the same amount but after 24 hours there is a drop
51
==PAGE:54==
in the E. coli bars where the L. innocua is almost the same as at 4, 5 and 6 hours.
From the results from ”Forming Of Bioﬁlm”(section 4.1.2) it is seen that L. innocua
do make bioﬁlm under this condition but E. coli do not so it could be the case
that the L. innocua had made a bioﬁlm and therefore survive better after 24 hours.
In the studies Kim and Frank (1995) they have shown that L. monocytogenes is
surviving 12 days after contamination on a stainless steel surface if there is nutrient
and water availability enough and in some cases with low amount of nutrient in the
ﬁrst 24 hours there can be a drop in the bioﬁlm of L. monocytogenes before it starts
to grow. In the studies from Wilks et al. (2005) it has been shown that E. coli can
survive after 28 days after contamination but in the ﬁrst 48 hours there was a 5 log
reduction. It needs to be mentioned that in the study Kim and Frank (1995) the
stainless steel type is not speciﬁed and in the studies Wilks et al. (2005) and Kim
and Frank (1995) the method of contamination is a lot diﬀerent from the method
used in this report.
The experiment with cutting multiple times showed two things. One; how the bacte-
ria change on the knife and two; how the bacteria migrated over to the fresh leaves.
When looking at Figure 4.14 the knives cutting for a second time after 3 hours there
is a drop on both E. coli and L. innocua.
What is interesting is that E. coli got a reduction in CFU after the second cut. The
CFU after 24 hours (Figure 4.12) is almost the same as the 24 hour with only the
contaminated cut (Figure 4.12). That could mean that after the second cut there
were nutrients enough to have the CFU stable for 24 hours because if the second
cut after 3 hour was following the trend from the time results on E. coli shown on
Figure 4.10 the CFU would drop lower than what it has.
L. innocua was more stable after 3 hours which has been discussed earlier. There is
a drop but not as much as the E. coli (Figure 4.14). This is further evidence that
the L. innocua is more attached than the E. coli.
After the third cut E. coli drops again and this time after 24 hours drops even more.
Why this happens is uncertain. It could be that the E. coli got so separated that
they had a harder time ﬁghting with the L. innocua for the nutrients and therefore
was dying. On the other hand L. innocua was growing after the third cutting so it
lines up that there have been more nutrients for the L. innocua to grow as can be
seen on Figure 4.8,4.9.
Looking at the lettuce on Figure 4.16 it can be seen that on the second cut af-
ter 3 hours there is a higher amount of E. coli than there is of L. innocua. After
the third cut it is almost the same. This shows that when the knife is contaminated
with L. innocua and E. coli it will transfer the bacteria to the foodstuﬀit cuts.
Other studies also has shown and made models where they show that the knife still

5.4
Cleaning Of The Automated Kitchen For L.
monocytogenes And E. coli
The start contamination concentration of L. innocua and E. coli in the experiments
were with a very high CFU, around 108 CFU/leaf. It is not likely that that amount
of bacteria will be present in a normal contaminated lettuce in practice. Looking
in the Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed (RASFF) database the cases with
lettuce contaminated with E. coli had a CFU/g from 5 · 102 −4 · 103 and for L.
monocytogenes it goes from 102 −1.5 · 103 CFU/g RASFF (2020). On top of that
the temperature of 30 0C is a worst case scenario which means that the temperature
could be lower in the kitchen especially at night. Nevertheless this still means that
if one of the items in the automated kitchen gets contaminated, these results show
that there is a risk of contamination from the items the ﬁrst 24 hours. L. innocua
that in practice would be L. monocytogenes could make bioﬁlm and make long term
contamination for the kitchens surface’s.
Looking at all the experiments E. coli and L. innocua have survived on the sur-
faces for 24 -48 hours and studies have shown that E. coli and L. monocytogenes
also can survive many days on a stainless steel surface (Wilks et al. (2005), Kim
and Frank (1995)). That must mean that the surfaces do not have a direct impact
on the bacteria as also shown in the study of Rodriguez et al. (2008). That study
shows that the surface roughness do not have an eﬀect on L. monocytogenes bioﬁlm
making.
Therefore some kind of cleaning of especially the knife, fork and pusher has to be
done. It is not possible to know when the items are contaminated by the foodstuﬀ
therefore the cleaning program should be made in a way so the items is disinfected
as much of the time as possible. Because the kitchen is in a closed cabinet a inline
cleaning with UV light is possible. UV-C light can be used to kill bacteria and
is already used in the industry but it is dangerous to humans so there is a limit
to how much it can be used Bak et al. (2009). Therefore the closed cabinet is an
56
==PAGE:59==
advantage. UV-C is not good at killing bioﬁlm but new research has shown that
UV-B light have a reduction factor up to 99% reduction on bioﬁlm on the bacteria
Pseudomonas aeruginosa Argyraki et al. (2018) if further research show the same
eﬀect on L. monocytogenes. could this be a way to minimize the risk between the
normal cleanings. UV Light has limitations, the light needs to hit the bacteria di-
rectly to kill them Bak et al. (2009). That means there must not be any shadow
zones and when looking at Figure 5.1 the lettuce would cover for the light and the
UV light will not be able to kill the bacteria so some kind of mechanical removal of
the residues on the surfaces would be needed to make UV eﬀective.
Regular cleaning at regular intervals with cleaning agent should also be imple-
mented.
It will be important that the cleaning agent do not damage the items
like what happened with the fork (Figure 5.3). It is also important how the cleaning
is done because if the kitchen would have some kind of hosing mechanism to clean
the kitchen and the hosing beam hits the bacteria colony in a closed cabinet the
bacteria would be spread as also is shown in the study of Burfoot et al. (2003). In
the results for the surface it has been shown that the bacteria can survive 48 hours
and in the studies from Kim and Frank (1995) and Wilks et al. (2005) it was shown
that L. monocytogenes and E. coli can survive even longer on the stainless steel and
aluminum surfaces and that could lead to cross-contamination.

6
Conclusion
This report has investigated how L. monocytogenes and E. coli behave on knives,
kitchen surfaces, forks and pusher from an automated kitchen after they were con-
taminated.
It can be concluded that the knife can have L. monocytogenes and E. coli living on
the surface 24 hours after contamination and have the ability to cross-contaminate
to other foodstuﬀ. Therefore to minimise that a cleaning of the knife should be done
to prevent the cross-contamination.
The treated kitchen surfaces have a higher Ra than the non-treated but there was
very little diﬀerence between the level of bacteria on the surfaces the ﬁrst 48 hours
after contamination and therefore the treated kitchen surfaces are as good as the
non-treated surfaces for food production when only looking at the bacterial behavior
after contamination.
L. monocytogenes and E. coli can also survive 24 hours on the fork and pusher
and due to their role where they touch every food item in the automated kitchen it
should be cleaned between every process to avoid cross-contamination.
Overall it can be concluded that the automated kitchen needs to have a clean-
ing program that minimizes kitchen items touching foodstuﬀbefore the item has
been cleaned and after it has touched the foodstuﬀit needs to be cleaned in some
way before it touches new food.
