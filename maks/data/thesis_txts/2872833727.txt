Abstract
The way district heating systems work is changing. Instead of having a few large plants,
we now often see many smaller sources of heat, such as heat pumps, spread out across
the system. This shift has made predicting exactly how much heat will be used more
challenging.
This thesis investigates whether applying minimum trace reconciliation to align forecasts
across temporal hierarchies from hourly up to 48 hours ahead can substantially improve
predictive accuracy. Initially, four forecasting techniques were tested: Recursive Least
Squares, AutoRegressive­Moving­Average with eXogenous inputs, Hoeffding Regression
Trees, and Online Support Vector Regression. Two distinct cases were analyzed to eval­
uate performance clearly. The first case covered winter months (December to February),
a period with high energy usage, while the second case examined transitional months
(March to May), capturing the shift from winter to spring. Results indicated that Recursive
Least Squares performed best during winter for nearly all forecast horizons. In contrast,
Hoeffding Regression Trees showed improved accuracy for longer forecasts during tran­
sitional months.
The goal was to see if reconciled forecasts adjusted using only statistical models per­
formed better or worse than those adjusted using only machine learning models. This
adjustment was based on historical data, aiming to align predictions consistently across
different time intervals. To test this method, the data from each Case was split into two
parts: one used to determine how best to make adjustments, and another to test if these
adjustments actually improved prediction accuracy in practice.
The results showed that during the transitional months from winter to spring, forecasts
adjusted using only statistical models consistently performed better across all prediction
horizons. On the other hand, when heat load consumption was more stable, forecasts
adjusted by machine learning models performed better for short­term predictions.
The overall findings verify the potential of forecast reconciliation to significantly improve
prediction accuracy.

Introduction
1.1
Overview
As global energy demands continue to rise, the shift towards cleaner and more sustain­
able solutions becomes imperative. Solar panels, wind turbines, and biogas systems are
no longer alternative options but essential components for securing a renewable energy
future. These renewable technologies are fundamental in reducing carbon emissions and
mitigating the impacts of climate change, ensuring a reliable and environmentally friendly
energy supply for the coming decades. However, their inconsistent output, such as solar
photovoltaic (PV) variability or wind intermittency, makes maintaining a stable power sup­
ply difficult. To counter this, energy storage technologies need to be integrated into the
grid.
Thermal energy storage (TES) systems play a crucial role in increasing energy efficiency
and integrating renewable energy in district heating systems [1]. District heating is a
method of delivering heat generated from a centralized location, such as a waste inciner­
ator, through a network of pipes to heat buildings and water for residential, commercial,
and industrial use [2]. TES systems store excess energy during off­peak hours in water
tanks, phase­change materials (PCMs), or solid­state thermal batteries [3]. By decoupling
energy generation from consumption, TES facilitates seasonal heat storage, improves the
efficiency of district heating and cooling networks, and enhances the integration of renew­
able energy sources.
The evolution of district heating systems has introduced new challenges due to the inte­
gration of diverse heat sources, such as heat pumps, and the decentralization of energy
generation [4]. These changes contribute to increasing variability in heat demand, requir­
ing more sophisticated forecasting and optimization strategies. Traditional forecasting
methods struggle to accommodate this growing complexity, particularly in decentralized
networks with fluctuating renewable energy inputs and dynamic consumption patterns.
Therefore, robust and adaptive forecasting approaches are essential to optimize TES uti­
lization and ensure efficient energy distribution.
Hierarchical forecasting and reconciliation are key methodologies for accurate heat load
predictions in decentralized district heating networks.
Hierarchical forecasting coordi­
nates predictions across different spatial or temporal levels, from individual zones to the
entire network, leveraging the system’s structure to capture both localized and broader
trends. Unlike traditional forecasting methods that produce isolated predictions, hierarchi­
cal forecasting integrates data from multiple levels, improving the precision of both area­
specific and aggregated forecasts. Reconciliation, the subsequent process, ensures that
forecasts remain coherent by adjusting them within the hierarchical structure so that the
total forecast aligns with its constituent parts. This harmonization improves TES oper­
ations by enabling more effective energy storage and distribution strategies, supporting
the integration of renewable energy sources through a flexible and scalable framework.
By accommodating the dynamic and nonlinear nature of real­world energy demand, hi­
erarchical forecasting and reconciliation facilitate resilient energy management practices,
ultimately maximizing storage efficiency and minimizing energy losses in district heating
networks.

1.2
Motivation
This thesis investigates how hierarchical forecasting improves heat load predictions in dis­
trict heating systems. Specifically, it develops and evaluates statistical, machine learning,
and reconciliation techniques for optimizing multi­level forecasting. By ensuring consis­
tency across different levels of the heating network, the study aims to enhance short­ and
medium­term heat load forecasting. A critical component of this research is the investiga­
tion of reconciled forecasts from both statistical and machine learning models, specifically
at the bottom level of the hierarchy.
1.3
Structure
The thesis is organized as follows: In Chapter 2, the theoretical foundation of this work
is established. Initially, the concept of Hierarchical Forecasting is introduced. Following
this, a discussion of the statistical and machine learning models applied in the analysis
is presented, explaining their theoretical background. The chapter ends by explaining
the reconciliation mechanisms employed to enhance forecast consistency across hierar­
chical levels. Chapter 3 moves into practical considerations. Here, the specific dataset
chosen for analysis is introduced, along with the structured framework within which model
predictions are evaluated. This chapter further details the methodologies employed for
multi­step ahead forecasting and investigates the implications of forecasting across differ­
ent temporal hierarchies. Additionally, it provides insights into the optimization strategies
applied to fine­tune model performance, followed by an explanation of the reconciliation
techniques used. The chapter wraps up with a description of the comparative analysis
strategy. In Chapter 4, the results derived from the models and analyses discussed in
the previous chapters are presented and interpreted. Finally, Chapter 5 summarizes the
results, and explores the reasons behind these outcomes.

Theory
Time series analysis is the process of statistical modeling of time series, i.e. data which is
sampled at different points in time over a period often with a constant increment between
the time­points, i.e. equidistant. Classical time series models for a single equidistant time
series use past values of the response variable (model output) as the predictors (inputs).
In this way, appropriate models describing the inherent auto­correlation structure of the
time series can be realized [5].
The field of load forecasting has evolved significantly, employing a range of methodolo­
gies that span from traditional statistical models to modern machine learning (ML) and
deep learning (DL) techniques. The primary objective of load forecasting is to anticipate
heat demand based on various influencing factors, such as historical consumption pat­
terns, meteorological conditions, seasonal variations, and socioeconomic data [6].
Early approaches to load forecasting were primarily based on statistical and time­series
models such as exponential smoothing, the autoregressive moving average (ARMA), and
its seasonal variant SARIMAX. These models have been widely used due to their mathe­
matical simplicity and interpretability [7]. However, they struggle with handling seasonality,
external factors, and require extensive historical data for accurate predictions [8].
Given the limitations of statistical models, machine learning techniques have been in­
creasingly adopted for load forecasting due to their ability to model complex nonlinear re­
lationships. Common ML techniques include K­nearest neighbors (KNN), decision trees,
random forests (RF), and support vector machines (SVM). While KNN and Naïve Bayes
(NB) are simple to implement, they struggle with computational efficiency and data depen­
dencies. RF and SVM handle high­dimensional data well but require careful parameter
tuning and can be sensitive to noise [9].
This chapter establishes the theoretical foundation for the background and methodologies
used in this thesis. We begin by discussing the characteristics of district heat load fore­
casting and the structure of temporal hierarchies. We then provide a detailed overview
of the statistical and machine learning models used to generate the base forecasts. Fi­
nally, we present the reconciliation process, emphasizing how each forecast is adjusted
to ensure coherency across different levels.
2.1
Heat Load Forecasting
Seasonal Heat Load Variation
Seasonal heat load variation is a well­documented and obvious phenomenon in district
heating systems. It primarily stems from the significant outdoor temperature differences
between winter and summer, coupled with the need to maintain relatively constant indoor
temperatures in buildings. Heat load can be categorized into physical and social compo­
nents. Physical heat load depends on environmental conditions such as temperature dif­
ferences and insulation quality. Distribution losses also qualify as physical losses since
they depend on the temperature difference between the district heating water and the
surrounding environment of the pipes. Other physical factors include wind and solar radi­
ation. Wind increases heat demand through infiltration, as warm air is replaced with cold
air that requires heating. Solar radiation decreases external heat demand in two ways:
by increasing the temperature of exposed outer walls (reducing heat flow from inside the
building) and by creating a greenhouse effect where solar radiation enters the building
but reflected long­wave radiation cannot escape through windows [10]. Social heat load

preparation, which significantly contributes to daily heat load variation as discussed be­

summer holidays, temporarily stopping hot water usage entirely. Additionally, in cities
where fresh water comes from surface reservoirs, the incoming cold water temperature
changes throughout the year, further increasing seasonal variation as the incoming water
is colder in winter than in summer.

Daily Heat Load Variation
Heat demands in district heating systems originate at the customer level and fluctuate
throughout the day. While district heating systems naturally smooth out some daily heat
load variations through geographical diversity (not all customers experience peak demand
simultaneously), noticeable daily variations still persist. Most of these variations stem
from social heat demands—heat requirements caused by individual and collective social
behaviors. In residential buildings, tenants typically sleep at night and don’t use hot water,
but morning bathroom routines create increased heat demand upon waking. Similar pat­
terns occur when people return home from work in the evening and use hot water. Night
setback modes in heating control systems, while not decreasing total heat use, increase
heat load variation similarly to time­controlled ventilation.
2.2
Hierarchical Forecasting and Reconciliation
Hierarchies
Hierarchical forecasting is an approach that systematically combines individual forecasts
into a unified and coherent prediction framework. Consider an example of heat load fore­
casting in a district heating system. Each building or local area of the network may have
its own consumption pattern, and forecasting each area separately may result in discrep­
ancies when aggregating these predictions into a total system forecast. Hierarchical fore­
casting overcomes this by explicitly reconciling individual forecasts across different aggre­
gation levels. The process uses shared information at different levels to capture correla­
tions and dependencies. As a result, hierarchical forecasting does not merely ensure con­

sistency but actively enhances overall forecasting accuracy, benefiting decision­making
through improved operational precision and efficiency.
Temporal Hierarchies
Temporal hierarchies offer a straightforward yet powerful approach to understanding and
forecasting time series data. The concept relies on breaking down a single series into sev­
eral non­overlapping temporal aggregations—such as hourly, daily, and weekly levels—
that each captures distinct characteristics of the underlying process. Lower­level aggrega­
tions tend to preserve high­frequency fluctuations and seasonality, while higher­level ag­
gregations highlight longer­term trends and cyclic behavior [11]. Historically, forecasters
dealt with data at a single resolution, often missing the nuances that appear when a series
is viewed through multiple time scales. Temporal hierarchies, however, embrace the idea
that a comprehensive understanding requires a multi­resolution perspective. In essence,
by examining a system at various aggregation levels, one can harness detailed insights
from high­frequency data alongside broader trends from aggregated data—a method that
is both simple and effective [12].
In the context of heat load forecasting, this multi­scale analysis is particularly beneficial.
Energy systems are influenced by rapid short­term fluctuations as well as slower, struc­
tural changes over time. Temporal hierarchies allow forecasters to capture both of these
aspects. By leveraging the intrinsic structure of the data, forecasts become more attuned
to immediate variations while still aligning with long­term patterns [12], [13]. For example,
a temporal hierarchy of four different levels ω = [24, 12, 6, 2] is shown in Fig. 2.3.

Forecast Reconciliation
A critical aspect of hierarchical forecasting is coherence. In heat load forecasting, coher­
ence implies that the sum of forecasts at the lower levels (e.g., individual buildings or
hourly forecasts) should exactly equal the forecast at an aggregated level (e.g., a district
or daily forecasts). Traditionally, coherence was achieved by simply aggregating indepen­
dent forecasts. More advanced methods now generate base forecasts independently at
each level and then adjust them via forecast reconciliation techniques to ensure consis­
tency across the hierarchy. Although these reconciliation procedures might not always

Hoeffding Regression Trees (HRT)
A regression tree is a decision­tree model in which the response variable Y is continuous
(or ordered) rather than categorical: at each node the data are split so as to minimize
the sum of squared deviations of Y from its mean in the resulting child nodes, and each
leaf then predicts the sample mean of Y in that region, yielding a piecewise­constant ap­
proximation of the underlying regression function. Traditional algorithms such as AID and
CART fit such trees in batch mode, exhaustively evaluating all possible splits to choose
the one that most reduces variance [21].
Hoeffding Regression Trees (HRT) are designed for incremental learning from high­speed
data streams, where data continuously arrives, making traditional batch methods unsuit­

Incremental Online SVR (Adding a Sample xc)
When a new training example (xc, yc) arrives, the goal is to fold it into the existing SVR
model in a few swift, controlled steps—never restarting the solver from scratch. The
procedure can be understood as “gently nudging” the new coefficient θc from zero up
or down until it meets the same Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) requirements as the rest of
the support vectors, while preserving optimality for all previously seen data [28].
First, one initializes θc = 0 and evaluates the current model’s prediction f(xc) to compute
the margin h(xc) = f(xc) −yc. If |h(xc)| ≤ϵ, the new point already lies inside the ϵ­tube
and can be parked in the “remaining” set without altering the model. Otherwise, θc must
move toward one of its bounds or into the margin­support regime: we choose a small
increment ∆θc in the sign that reduces the margin violation, and simultaneously adjust
the existing support­vector coefficients and the bias according to sensitivities obtained
from the inverse of the augmented kernel matrix over the current support set [29]. At
each micro­step, a careful bookkeeping mechanism examines all potential events­such
as the new margin hitting ±ϵ, the new coefficient reaching ±C, or any existing support
vector coefficient or margin crossing its own critical threshold—and selects the smallest
permissible ∆θc that triggers exactly one transition.
Applying this step updates every affected coefficient and margin, and reassigns a single
sample between the support, error, and remaining sets. By employing rank­one formulas
to update the inverse kernel matrix rather than full re­inversion, the algorithm maintains
efficiency. This process repeats until the new coefficient θc itself satisfies its KKT condition,
at which point the incremental addition is complete and the resulting model matches the
outcome of batch retraining on the augmented dataset.
Decremental Online SVR (Removing a Sample xc)
“Unlearning” an instance is carried out as the reverse of the incremental procedure: one
drives its coefficient back to zero in measured steps, ensuring that every other sample
remains KKT­optimal. If the sample lies in the remaining set (so θc = 0), it can be removed
immediately with no impact. Otherwise, the coefficient θc is gradually reduced toward zero
using small decrements ∆θc in the direction −sign(θc), while adjusting support­vector
coefficients and the bias by the same sensitivity relations used in the addition process. At
each iteration, the algorithm computes all candidate decrements that would cause either
the target coefficient to hit zero, an existing support­vector coefficient to reach a bound or
vanish, or any error/remaining­set margin to touch ±ϵ. The smallest such ∆θc is applied,
updating all coefficients and margins and moving exactly one sample between sets [30].
Whenever the support set changes, a rank­one downdate of the kernel­inverse matrix
keeps computations light. The process continues until θc reaches zero, at which point the

Data Description
The data used in this report consists of hourly measurements of heat consumption and cli­
mate for the period July 1995 to June 1996. The measurements of heat consumption are
supplied by Henrik R. Hansen, Vestegnens Kraftvarmeselskab I/S (VEKS), and consist
of the heat supplied from the VEKS transmission system to the local distributors over the
past hour. The unit of measurement is GJ, and since the measurement is related to the
past hour, the unit GJ/h will be used in this report. The climate measurements consist of
recordings of the air temperature in oC, wind speed in m/s, and global radiation in W/m2
as averages over the past ten minutes up to the full hour. The measurements are per­

performed two meters above ground level, which explains the relatively low recordings.
With the purpose of taking into account the different patterns of consumption for different
types of days, these are grouped into working, half­holy, and holy days. Half­holy days
include Saturdays.
For the case of the machine learning models, the dataset was scaled to a value range of
0 to 1 using min­max normalization. Time variables, including day of the week, hour of
the day, and week of the year, were mapped as cyclic features using a sine function and
subsequently scaled to the value range from 0 to 1. The cyclical transformation of the
time variables more accurately represents the recurring time features.

3.2
Multi­Step Ahead Forecasting
The objective of this phase is to produce heat load forecasts up to 48 hours into the
future. Such multi­step forecasts are necessary for optimal operation planning and man­
agement of thermal energy storage systems in district heating networks. Forecasts were
systematically produced on an hourly basis over a continuous three­month evaluation

Least Squares (RLS), AutoRegressive­Moving­Average with eXogenous inputs (ARMAX),
Hoeffding Regression Trees (HRT) and Online Support Vector Regression (OnlineSVR).
Each model used a direct online forecasting strategy, so individual dedicated models pre­
dicted heat load at each forecast horizon. In other words, instead of feeding the t + k–1
forecast into the t + k model (the recursive approach), we trained 48 independent one­
hour­ahead models—one for each horizon—each with its own tailored inputs and target.

3.3
Forecasting Across Temporal Hierarchies
The forecasting task spanned across a structured temporal hierarchy comprising six dis­
tinct aggregation levels: 1­hour, 2­hour, 6­hour, 12­hour, 24­hour, and the top level of 48­
hour aggregated heat load. Each upper level forecast aggregated heat load consumption
through a rolling window mechanism. Specifically, the size of this window corresponded
directly to the temporal resolution of the forecasted level. For instance, a forecast at the
6­hour hierarchical level targeted the aggregate sum of heat load consumption for the
subsequent six hours. A crucial methodological consideration was appropriately adjust­
ing the input features to match the aggregated targets at each hierarchical level. This was
managed through backward aggregation of historical inputs, aligning input data with the
nature of the aggregated forecast target. For example, at the lowest (1­hour) hierarchi­
cal level, the heat load at the previous hour (t −1) was directly used as a predictor for
hour t. At the 2­hour hierarchical level, the sum of heat loads at hours (t −1) and (t −2)
served as predictors for the aggregate consumption forecast for hours t and (t + 1). This
backward rolling window approach ensured the historical context matched the forecast­
ing task’s temporal resolution, enhancing the coherence and predictive capacity across
all hierarchical levels.
3.4
Model Optimization for Hierarchical Levels
During this thesis, model optimization was carried out systematically to ensure reliable and
accurate forecasts. Initially, for each case scenario, model parameters were optimized
offline using data from the previous month. This included critical parameters such as
the forgetting factor for recursive algorithms and various hyperparameters for machine
learning methods. It is important to recognize that parameter optimization procedures
varied significantly across different modeling frameworks, reflecting their distinct structural
characteristics.
To address this complexity, a structured step­wise, forward selection strategy was em­
ployed consistently across all base models (RLS, ARMAX, OnlineSVR, and HRT). This
process began by formulating a simple baseline model that relied solely on historical heat
load consumption data as predictors. From this baseline, additional explanatory variables
were methodically incorporated one at a time. These included ambient temperature, wind
speed, global radiation, and a categorical feature representing the type of day (working
days, half­holidays including Saturdays, and holidays including Sundays). The progres­
sive inclusion of these variables was systematically evaluated to ascertain their contribu­
tion to the forecasting performance, ensuring that each step improved model predictive
capability and coherence.

Recursive Least Squares
The Recursive Least Squares (RLS) optimization procedure aimed to enhance forecast ac­

This function evaluated the model predictions against observed data across a designated
optimization dataset. During optimization, intermediate parameter configurations and cor­
responding performance scores were optionally stored to monitor convergence progress
and facilitate analysis of the optimization path. The Nelder­Mead algorithm iteratively ex­

until convergence to a set of parameters that minimized the RMSE.
AutoRegressive­Moving­Average with eXogenous inputs
The optimization process for the ARMAX model was carefully structured to achieve ro­
bust forecast accuracy. Initially, the model was configured with preliminary parameters,
including orders for autoregressive, moving average, and exogenous terms. These pa­
rameters were then systematically optimized through a numerical approach, employing
the Nelder­Mead optimization algorithm.
The optimization targeted key model parameters, notably the forgetting factor (λ), the
autoregressive order, and low­pass filtering parameters for smoothing inputs such as am­
bient temperature, wind speed, and global radiation. Specifically, the forgetting factor λ
was constrained within the interval (0.9, 0.999) to ensure adequate responsiveness and
stability of parameter updates. The smoothing parameters (ta) for low­pass filters on in­
puts like ambient temperature (Taobs), wind speed (W.f), and global radiation (GR.f) were
optimized within the range (0.1, 0.95), balancing the trade­off between noise reduction and
preservation of meaningful signal variations.
Intermediate configurations and corresponding performance scores were recorded during
the optimization process, providing valuable insights into parameter convergence behav­
ior. This iterative numerical optimization process continued until a stable parameter set
was identified, effectively minimizing the RMSE and ensuring that the final ARMAX model
achieved optimal forecasting performance tailored specifically for each aggregation level
within the temporal hierarchy.

Online Support Vector Regression
The optimization process of the Online Support Vector Regression model was carried out
using Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO). This approach effectively tunes the model’s
hyperparameters, namely the penalty parameter C, the epsilon­insensitive loss parame­
ter ϵ, and the kernel coefficient γ, to achieve optimal predictive performance [27]. PSO
optimization was implemented through the PySwarms library. The optimization process
involved defining an objective function that evaluates the model’s performance through a
scoring function, such as Root Mean Square Error (RMSE). During each iteration, multi­
ple ”particles,” representing different combinations of hyperparameters, navigated through
the search space. Each particle’s position corresponded to a distinct set of parameters,
and the PSO algorithm iteratively updated these positions based on their predictive per­
formance.
The search was bounded to specific parameter ranges: C was optimized between (1, 7),
ϵ between (0.001, 0.01), and γ between (0.1, 0.6). These constraints guided the optimiza­
tion towards physically meaningful and computationally stable parameter values.
Throughout the optimization, the model was re­initialized and fitted separately for each
particle’s parameters. Each particle’s predictive accuracy was quantified by calculating
residuals between predicted and actual values, followed by computing the RMSE. To han­
dle potential issues such as model convergence problems or incompatible parameter sets,
a large penalty score was assigned to any configurations leading to errors or non­finite
RMSE values, ensuring the algorithm favored viable and stable parameter sets.

When evaluating heat load consumption, the winter months and the transitional period

4.1
Metrics
In order to evaluate the results of the methods numerical experiments were implemented.
One of the most commonly used metric in regression tasks to evaluate the difference
between predicted and actual values is the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE). It provides
a measure of how spread out the residuals (prediction errors) are, indicating the accuracy
of a model’s predictions. A lower RMSE generally indicates better model performance,
suggesting that the model’s predictions are closer to the actual values.
It is important to distinguish between in­sample and out­of­sample RMSE: the former is
computed on the data used to train the models, reflecting how well they fit historical ob­
servations (and in our case also providing the forecast errors on which the reconciliation
process is based), while the latter is calculated on held­out or future data, indicating true
predictive capability.

5
Final Remarks
5.1
Discussion
In this thesis, the base forecasts for each level of the hierarchy were chosen based on
the RMSE scores of each model at each node. However, relying solely on RMSE scores
might not lead to the best reconciled forecasts, because RMSE alone may not capture
all the important details. Taking a closer look at this selection method could help iden­
tify improvements and produce even more accurate reconciled forecasts. Furthermore,
the reconciliation step wasn’t done at the same time as the base forecasts; it was done
afterward. Ideally, reconciliation should happen right when the forecasts are made. If
we reconcile forecasts as they’re produced, we ensure that all forecasts are consistently
coherent, not just some of them, as was the case in this analysis.
The shrinkage parameter was fixed at 0.1 without a specific reason or analysis to support
this choice. Future research could improve on this by optimizing the shrinkage parameter.
In both cases and both scenarios (ST reconciled forecasts and ML reconciled forecasts),
reconciliation improved the results significantly. This improvement shows that the original
base forecasts were not very accurate and missed important information. The ARMAX
model naturally works as an iterative method when making multi­step predictions with
future external variables. However, in this thesis, the ARMAX model was implemented
directly for multiple steps ahead, due to the lack of future external inputs, and to keep
the comparison fair with other models. This choice unfortunately led to poor performance
from the ARMAX model. The OnlineSVR model also had performance issues, mainly
due to its complexity, which sometimes makes it unstable. A deeper look into how the
tolerances and sensitivities were set during the implementation could help produce more
stable and reliable predictions. In addition, the absence of Numerical Weather Predictions
likely affected the models’ performance. Since the forecasts extended up to 48 steps
ahead, relying solely on historical data probably reduced the accuracy. Including weather
forecasts in future work would likely lead to better and more reliable predictions. In this
thesis, the models were optimized based only on their performance for one­step­ahead
predictions. As a result, the parameters chosen for predicting longer horizons depended
only on the best settings for the first step. This likely affected the performance of the
forecasts, especially for machine learning models, which usually have more parameters
than statistical models. Optimizing each model separately for each prediction horizon
would probably lead to better overall results.
5.2
Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis has presented the utilization of four different online and adaptive
forecasting models focusing on the prediction of heat load consumption. This research
aimed to identify the differences in the performance of reconciled hierarchical forecasts
produced by statistical models with those produced by machine learning models.
This investigation first attempted to identify the most effective base forecasting models for
predicting heat load under varying seasonal conditions. The analysis revealed a distinct
performance difference between the models depending on the period. During the winter
months of December through February (Case 1), characterized by high and relatively
stable heat demand, the Recursive Least Squares (RLS) model consistently provided the
most accurate short­term forecasts for nearly all horizons up to 48 hours. However, during
Heat Load Forecasting for Thermal Energy Storage Systems Using Temporal Hierarchies
35
==PAGE:44==
the transitional months of March through May (Case 2), which exhibited greater volatility
and a clear downward trend in consumption, the Hoeffding Regression Trees (HRT) model
demonstrated superior performance, particularly for longer forecast horizons. This finding
reveals that no single model is universally optimal; rather, the choice of forecasting method
should be adapted to the specific characteristics of the demand profile.
A central hypothesis of this thesis was that forecast accuracy could be substantially im­
proved by enforcing coherence across a temporal hierarchy. The results support this
claim. By applying the Minimum Trace (MinT) reconciliation method, the initial base fore­
casts were adjusted, leading to significant reductions in error across all scenarios and
forecast horizons. As demonstrated in the analysis of both Case 1 and Case 2, the recon­
ciled forecasts consistently yielded lower Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) values than
the base forecasts, with improvements frequently exceeding 50%. This provides convinc­
ing evidence that leveraging the structural information inferred in temporal aggregations
is an essential strategy for enhancing the reliability of heat load predictions.
The analysis of the winter period (Case 1) revealed that during these months of sustained
consumption, the reconciled forecasts derived from the machine learning hierarchy (HRT
and O­SVR) showed better accuracy compared to the statistical hierarchy, for short­term
predictions. This suggests that when the underlying patterns of heat consumption are
more complex but stable, the ability of machine learning models to capture complex, non­
linear relationships provides an advantage.
When analyzing the transitional period from winter to spring (Case 2), the comparison
of those scenarios revealed an interesting result. The hierarchy constructed exclusively
from statistical models (RLS and ARMAX) produced reconciled forecasts that were consis­
tently more accurate across all prediction horizons than those generated by the hierarchy
of machine learning models. This suggests that during periods with structural change,
statistical models may provide better reconciled forecasts.
