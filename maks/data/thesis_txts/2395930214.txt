Abstract
The work in this project investigates possible degrading eﬀects occurring in a coated con-
ductor which have been cut to build an all superconducting persistent current system.
The work is based on the ﬁndings of a previous DTU student project where the potential
of using a second generation high temperature superconductor to generate a permanent
magnetic ﬁeld was investigated. The project succeded in building an all superconduct-
ing persistent current system by cutting a commercial available YBa2Cu3O7−δ coated
conductor from SuperPower Inc. [1] [2]. 24 hours after ﬁrst measurement the supercon-
ducting properties of the used coated conductor was completely degraded. In this project
possible causes of this degradation are investigated. The project focuses on the possible
eﬀects of exposing the cut coated conductor to a measurement cycle which includes sub-
merging the coated conductor in liquid nitrogen and subsequently drying it in atmosphere
leading to condense water gathering on the coated conductor.
The cutting procedure performed with a pair of scissors was seen to cause damage to
the edges in form of small areas where the YBCO material was broken oﬀthe substrate
and deformation in form of slip lines. An average of 0.20 slip lines per µm propagating
between (60 ± 5) µm and (105 ± 3) µm into the sample was seen.
Exposure to water at various temperatures for one hour showed a clear decomposition of
the YBCO layer occurring. A sample exposed to 90 ◦C water for 90 min was completely
decomposed leaving no YBa2Cu3O7−δ left to be detected with x-ray diﬀraction. Magnetic
measurements showed that exposure to 90 ◦C water for one hour decreased the critical
current density with (87.2 ± 0.5) % and the critical temperature with (7.3 ± 0.07) %.
Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy of samples exposed to water for one hour showed
a decrease in the relative barium concentration with increasing exposure temperature.
The decomposition occurred more at the edges of the samples than in the middle of the
sample.
Treatments where samples were submerged in liquid nitrogen and subsequently dried in
atmosphere was used to resemble a measurement cycle. From magnetic measurements
the critical current densities was seen to decrease between (11±2) % and (15±2) % after
the treatments. Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy showed that the relative barium of
the surface was slightly increased after the treatments.
The oxygen stoichiometry of YBa2Cu3O7−δ coated conductor samples was investigated
with x-ray diﬀraction scans prior to and after treatments with water and liquid nitro-
gen. No signiﬁcant change was observed in δ with respects to its eﬀect on the critical
temperature of YBa2Cu3O7−δ.

1.
Motivation for project
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) machines are used in hospitals all over the world
today [3]. MRI is a technology using high magnetic ﬁelds of typically 1.5 T-3 T to for
example visualize the interior of the body for medical diagnosis. The high magnetic ﬁelds
needed to perform MRI imaging are realized using superconducting wires wound into
very strong and large electromagnets, as shown in ﬁgure 1.1a [4], and are characterized
by their capability to conduct current at zero resistivity ρ = 0 and endure large applied
magnetic ﬁelds (further introduction to superconductivity is given in section 2). MRI
magnets technology is along with Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) magnets one of
the most successful commercial application of superconductors [5] [6]. Most commer-
cial MRI machines operate with a magnetic ﬁeld at 1.5 T or 3 T but MRI scanners with
ﬁelds as high as 7 T are commercial available [7]. The high magnetic ﬁeld technology
applied in commercial products as MRI machines relies mainly on the superconducting
alloy niobium-titanium (NbTi). NbTi is a type II low temperature superconductor (LTS)
with a critical temperature Tc = 9 K and an upper critical ﬁeld Bc2 = 15 T varying on the
precise material composition (critical parameters and superconductor classiﬁcation are ex-
plained in section 2) which along with the stoichiometric intermetallic compound Nb3Sn
paved the way for commercial application of superconductors [8]. The use of high temper-
ature superconductors (HTS) such as YBa2Cu3O7−δ (YBCO) and Bi2Sr2Ca2Cu3O10+δ
or Bi2Sr2Ca1Cu1O8+δ (BiSCCO) for MRI technology remains underdeveloped compared
to the LTS superconductors [9]. The HTS superconductors enable signiﬁcant improve-
ments in some aspects of the generation of high magnetic ﬁelds. Figure 1.1b shows the
upper critical magnetic ﬁelds as a function of temperature for diﬀerent superconducting
materials. This ﬁgure clearly shows the the potential for HTS material as BiSCCO and
YBCO.

1.0.1
State-of-the-art
As mentioned there are several challenges to overcome before HTS magnets can be re-
alized. Most MRI systems today operate using a large persistent ﬁeld. These persistent
ﬁelds are realized with LTS material as NbTi and Nb3Sn by joining the ends of a coil sim-
ply by pressing the material together [11]. Joining two ends of a YBCO coated conductor
is not as simple. Joint structures of YBCO introduces signiﬁcant ohmic resistance in the
systems which do not allow persistent mode [12] [13]. HTS persistent current systems
have been realised with losses down to 0.44 % per day at a temperature of 20 K despite
the ohmic resistances introduced by joint structures [14] [15] [16]. Another study has
succeeded in making a superconducting joint structure with no ohmic resistances using
a HTS material [17]. The process of creating the resistance free superconducting joint
requires fabrication of microholes in the coated conductor by laser and heating rates of
200 ◦C per minute peaking at a temperature of 850 ◦C where 18 MPa are applied to the
joining regions.
To avoid these joint structures and the demanding process of creating them without
introducing ohmic resistances, it has been attempted to create an all superconducting
persistent current system by cutting a coated conductors [18]. This procedure showed
that a superconducting joint of coated conductor is not a prerequisite in realizing a
permanent magnet. To create an all superconducting permanent magnet using a coated
conductor, the coated conductor is cut along the middle in the longitudinal direction
leaving a length of coated conductor intact in each end.
This procedure results in a
closed loop of coated conductor which can be winded into a coil. A sketch of the concept
is shown in ﬁgure 1.2.
The ﬁgure shows how an end is left intact for the current, shown with black arrows, to run
in a closed loop. The coated conductor is then winded in a coil so that a persistent current
generates a permanent magnetic ﬁeld. A previous DTU student project (2017) succeeded
in cutting a coated conductor and winding a coil. The unpublished work resulted in the
slowly decaying magnetic ﬁeld shown in ﬁgure 1.3 [1].
The measurement of the magnetic ﬁeld showed it to decay with 8.8 % per day. Very
importantly the study found that the set-up was no longer functional 24 hours after the
ﬁrst persistent current measurement. The reasons for the non functioning set-up was not
determined.

Hypotheses
To succeed in creating an all superconducting permanent magnet with a cut coated con-
ductor the degrading eﬀects must be known such that they can be avoided. The observa-
tions of the persistent current system from the DTU project’s unpublished work and the
knowledge regarding the superconducting properties of YBa2Cu3O7−δ and its relation to
oxygen stoichiometry leads to the following hypotheses that this project seeks to answer:
• How is the YBa2Cu3O7−δ exposed due to the mechanical damage caused by the
cutting procedure?
• What interaction occurs between the YBa2Cu3O7−δ-phase and water and how does
this aﬀect the critical temperature and critical current?
• Does a degrading mechanism occur due to a measurement cycle i.e. submersion in
liquid nitrogen, drying oﬀin atmosphere and drying with heat gun?
• Does a change in the YBa2Cu3O7−δ oxygen stoichiometry occur after the various
exposures?

2.
Basics of Superconductivity
Superconductivity was discovered in 1911 by Heike Kamerlingh Onnes [20] while studying
the electrical resistivity of mercury at temperatures approaching absolute zero. Within
some hundredths of a temperature of 4.2 K, the critical temperature (Tc), Onnes found
that the electrical resistivity of mercury abruptly dropped several orders of magnitude.
Lead and tin were soon discovered to also show superconducting properties. The techno-
logical potential of utilizing superconducting coils for generation of high magnetic ﬁeld
was investigated [8]. Magnetic ﬁeld as high as 10 T was expected but when trying to real-
ize such technology a new challenge emerged for superconducting materials: The critical
current (Ic), the upper limit of superconducting current capable of ﬂowing in the self ﬁeld
of a coil before switching to normal conducting state. The extraordinary properties of
superconducting materials were a subject of much attention which led to the next break-
through. Meissner and Ochsenfeld studied the magnetic properties of superconductors
and in 1933 they showed that when a superconductor at a temperature above Tc is applied
an external magnetic ﬁeld, and then cooled below Tc, the superconductor would expel
the penetrating magnetic ﬂux. This phenomenon is know as the Meissner eﬀect and was
deﬁned as a new thermodynamic state, describing when a superconductor cooled below
its critical temperature acts as a perfect diamagnet with susceptibility χ = −1. This
occurs due to shielding currents arising at the superconductors surface inducing a ﬁeld
which cancels out the externally applied ﬁeld inside the superconductor. Two fundamen-
tal properties are therefore associated with the superconducting state: Zero resistivity
ρ = 0 and perfect diamagnetism with χ = −1 in the Meissner state. These are shown in
ﬁgures from Fossheim [8] 2.1a and 2.1b respectively.

Another deﬁning parameter when considering the applications of superconductors, besides
Tc and Ic, is the critical magnetic ﬁeld (Hc). The critical magnetic ﬁeld is the maximum
applied ﬁeld a superconductor can sustain without becoming normal conducting again.
Diﬀerent materials display diﬀerent behaviours when their critical ﬁeld is surpassed, which
is elaborated in section 2.1. All three critical parameters depend on one another and
together they produce the critical surface for a superconducting material as shown in
ﬁgure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: A sketch of the critical surface. When one of the critical parameters, Tc, Jc or Hc are
surpassed the superconductor switches to normal state.
The large interest in the topic of superconductivity resulted in several metals and alloys
were discovered to have a superconducting phase. Despite the eﬀorts all materials showed
a generally low critical temperature for the metals and alloys therefore classifying them
as low temperature superconductors (LTS).
In 1957 a quantum theory for superconductivity was presented by Bardeen, Cooper and
Schrieﬀer hence called the BCS theory [21]. The theory explains the superconducting
state as a macroscopic coherent matter wave constituted by electron pairs. This electron
pairing is accomplished by a phonon mediated coupling.
The concept is sketched in
ﬁgure 2.3.
When a electron moves through a material it attracts the positive nuclei
creating an area of excess positive charge, a disturbance, which can be seen as a phonon.
When another electron interacts with the disturbance from the ﬁrst electron, it can be
regarded as a phonon induced attraction.
Since the positive nuclei are heavier than
the electrons they move slower.
This means that the ﬁrst electron that creates the
phonon, is gone before the second electrons interacts with the excess positive charge
which therefore saves Coulomb energy. The fact that heavier isotopes move slower than
light ones, means the induced attraction is smaller, and if this mechanism is the true
cause of superconductivity, it would be expected that heavier ions decrease Tc, known

2.1
Type I and type II superconductors
Superconductors are divided into two types depending on their interaction with an ex-
ternally applied ﬁeld surpassing Hc or Hc1 depending on the material:
• Type I: If an applied ﬁeld surpasses the critical ﬁeld Hc, the Meissner state breaks
down and the superconductor switches abruptly to normal state and is fully pene-
trated by the magnetic ﬂux.
• Type II: If an applied ﬁeld surpasses the ﬁrst critical ﬁeld Hc1 the Meissner state
breaks down and the superconductor enters a mixed state allowing partial pene-
tration of magnetic ﬂux. As the applied ﬁeld is increased the superconductor con-
tinuously allows more ﬂux penetration until the second critical ﬁeld Hc2 is reached
and the superconductor is fully penetrated by magnetic ﬂux and switches to normal
state.
A phase diagram illustrating this diﬀerence is shown in ﬁgure 2.4 [8].

like a normal conductor. To prevent the motion of the ﬂux vortices a pinning force Fp
is introduces in the superconducting material. If Fp > F the ﬂux vortices are at rest, if
Fp < F the ﬂux vortices will move. The current density applied resulting in F becoming
equal to Fp is the critical current density Jc [8].
Type II superconductors can be further classiﬁed by considering the pinning force acting
on penetrating ﬂux vortices. The pinning force is the force barrier which a ﬂux vortex
must overcome to move freely in the superconducting material.
When a ﬂux vortex
experiences a local energy minimum in the superconductor material it is at a pinning site.
Such a local energy minimum could be due to a defect in the superconducting material.
If a superconducting material has no local energy minima in the form of impurities or
defects it is considered a soft superconductor. In a soft superconductor no pinning force
acts upon the ﬂux vortices. A hard superconductor is a superconducting material where
the ﬂux vortices experience a pinning force due to defects or impurities in the material.
Hard superconductors of type II are therefore the most practical used.

2.1.1
Ginzburg-Landau theory
Another great step towards describing the mechanism of superconductivity was taken in
1950 with the publishing of the Ginzburg-Landau theory [24]. The theory is based on
the semi-phenomenological theories derived by the London brothers in the 1935. The
ﬁrst London equation describes the frictionless acceleration of superconducting electrons,
and states that an electric ﬁeld can start a superconducting current but is not needed to
sustain it. The second London equation introduces the penetration depth (λL). When an
external magnetic ﬁeld is applied to a superconductor screening currents arise opposing
the external ﬁeld and cancel it out it out inside the sample. The screening current cannot
exist only on the surface otherwise a ﬁnite current should exist in a layer of zero thickness.
The penetration depth is the depth a screening current penetrates a superconductor.
Another characteristic length scale, the coherence length ξ, was derived from the ﬁrst
equation of the Ginzburg-Landau theory. The coherence length describes the length over
which the superconducting electron density is suppressed to zero which corresponds to
the radius of a ﬂux vortex. From the two characteristic length scales a ratio κ is deﬁned.

Vibrating sample magnetometer
Vibrating sample magnetometer (VSM) is a characterization technique that measures
the magnetic moment of a magnetised sample. VSM is a contact free method to perform
magnetic measurements, which means that small samples can be investigated in a non-
destructive way. In VSM a sample is magnetised either permanently or by applying a
controlled external magnetic ﬁeld. The sample is then oscillated inducing an AC signal
in a pair of pick-up coils. The amplitude of the detected AC signal is proportional to
the magnetic moment of the investigated sample. A VSM set-up basically consist of an
electromagnet for applying the external ﬁeld, sets of pickup coils to detect the induced
voltage signal and a sample-holder capable of oscillating in a temperature controlled
environment. A sketch of the VSM set-up is shown in ﬁgure 3.4 adapted from [44].

3.4.2
Critical temperature measurement
The critical temperature of a sample is determined by measuring the real part of the AC
susceptibility χ′ as a function of temperature. If a sample is superconducting then χ′
goes to −1 as the sample switches from non-superconducting to superconducting phase
being perfectly diamagnetic. A measurement of the relation between AC susceptibility
and temperature in a superconducting material is shown in ﬁgure 3.6.
Figure 3.6: Relation between the real part of AC susceptibility and temperature [46]
The critical temperature can be determined by analysing diﬀerent point of χ′ - T relation.
The temperature at χ′ = −0.5 is the critical temperature Tc. The width of the critical
temperature ∆Tc is the diﬀerence in temperature between χ′ = −0.1 and χ′ = −0.9.
In this project Tc of samples are measured at a constant magnetic ﬁeld of 0.5 mT oscillated
at 17 Hz and a temperature varying from 100 K to around 55 K.

3.5
X-ray diﬀraction
X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) is a characterization technique utilized to measure structural
parameters in a crystal lattice to identify crystalline phases [48]. An XRD set-up basically
consist of a x-ray source and a detector with an adjustable angle between them. XRD
uses the concept of elastic scattering of x-ray photons by atoms in a periodic crystal
lattice. X-rays incident on periodic crystal planes are reﬂected so that the incident angle
equals the angle of reﬂection. Only a small fraction of the incident radiation is reﬂected
by a single crystal plane. The diﬀracted x-ray beams are detectable when the reﬂections
from several of parallel crystal planes interfere constructively. This occurs in accordance
with the Bragg relation in equation (3.7)
nλ = 2d sin(θ)
(3.7)
where λ is the wavelength of the x-rays and n is an integral number, d is the distance
between two lattice planes, θ is the angle between the normal of the reﬂecting plane
and the incident x-rays. At certain angles θ all the reﬂections from parallel crystal planes
interfere constructively as shown by the sketch on ﬁgure 3.7a [48]. The θ - 2θ constellation
used is shown in ﬁgure 3.7b.

3.6
Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy
Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS) is a characterization technique used to in-
vestigate the elemental composition of a sample. EDS characterization is performed by
bombarding a sample with electrons from an electron gun, as situated in a Scanning Elec-
tron Microscope (SEM). The primary electrons from the electron gun hits and interacts
with the sample within an interaction volume depending on the density of the sample and
the acceleration voltage of the primary electrons. Part of the primary electrons excites
electrons from the samples atoms thus called secondary electrons. Secondary electron
vacancies from the innermost shells leaves the sample atoms unstable leading to electrons
from outer shells decaying to the vacancies. As shown in ﬁgure 3.8 the innermost shell
is the K-shell, followed outwards by the L-shell and M-shell, the decay of an electron
from the L-shell to the K-shell is a Kα decay [50]. The excess energy from an electron
decaying from an outer shell to an inner shell is emitted as a x-ray. The energy (or the
wavelength) of the emitted x-rays are characteristic for each element [48] by analysing
the emitted x-rays a samples surface element composition can be determined.
Figure 3.8: The ﬁgure shows the possible decays of electrons from
outer shells leading to the emission of characteristic x-ray [50].
EDS analysis of YBCO samples allows for determination of the particular elements
present and their relative proportions. EDS makes it possible to map the intensity and
distribution of the found elements over a scanned area. In this project samples microstruc-
ture are analysed using SEM and their relative element proportion and distribution with
EDS to investigate how the surface of YBCO coated conductor changes after exposure
to diﬀerent environments.

4.1
Damage from cutting
The damage caused by cutting the samples with a pair of scissors was estimated using
SEM imaging. All edges of the cut samples were imaged using SEM. Random sections
of edge was chosen near the middle of the samples to avoid possible eﬀects caused by
being near the corners. The corners were also investigated. Figure 4.1 shows chosen SEM
images of the investigated samples.
The damage caused by cutting the samples was determined by analysing the amount of
slip lines per µm created on the edges and how far into the samples they had propagated.
The angle of the slip lines with respect to the edges was also investigated. Of the six
investigated edges 4 showed between 0.16 slip lines per micrometer and 0.18 slip lines per
µm and the last two showed 0.25 slip lines per µm. The average was found to be 0.20
slip-lines per µm. Because of the very damaged edges the amount of slip lines per µm were
obtained from 30 µm into the samples over a length as shown with the blue line in ﬁgure
4.1b. The slip lines were measured to propagate between (60±5) µm and (105±3) µm into
the samples. The angles of the slip lines with respect to the edges were also measured.
They showed that the slip lines propagated with angles between (40 ± 2) ◦and (56 ± 1) ◦
into the samples. This deviation is well explained by the fact that the cutting procedure
does not cut the samples perfectly orthogonal with the transverse or longitudinal direction
of the coated conductor. The results found by the SEM image analysis of the damage
caused by cutting the samples deviate a lot. This deviation was expected due to the
inaccurate cutting procedure and can be explained by both irregularities in the scissors
surface leading to large mechanical stress in regions and the fact that the same force was
not applied to the sample throughout the cut.
The purpose of the investigation, of the damage caused by cutting the samples with a pair
of scissors, was to conﬁrm that the edges became fractured, and that slip lines caused by
these fractures propagated into the sample increasing the surface area which was exposed
to the surroundings. It is clear from the SEM image results that the actual eﬀective areas
which were superconducting was decreased signiﬁcantly. The SEM images in ﬁgure 4.1c
and 4.1d of the corners show that the YBCO layer was completely fractured and even
broken of the samples in areas. These corners further increased the exposed areas and
decreased the eﬀective superconducting area.

Discussion
In this section the obtained results are discussed and compared to literature.
5.0.1
Damage caused by slitting
The damage caused by cutting the coated conductor with a pair of scissors was investi-
gated. It was found that the procedure caused damage to the edges of the YBCO layer
in form of slip lines propagating between (60 ± 5) µm and (105 ± 3) µm into the sam-
ple and averagely 0.20 slip liner per µm. The procedure clearly decreased the eﬀective
superconducting area both in the form of YBCO that had been removed in small areas
and by the deformed edges. The amount of damage caused to the edges during the cut-
ting procedure is clearly increasing the surface area which is exposed to the surrounding
environments during the experimental treatments due to slip lines propagating into the
YBCO material. Minimizing this damage could decrease the interaction seen between
YBCO and water.
The damaged edges caused by the cutting procedure decreases Jc due to the loss of
eﬀetive superconducting material. The loss could be decreased by minimizing the extent
of the damage caused to the edges. In the previous DTU student project that build an
all superconducting persistent current set-up it was found that Ic had decreased 22.6 %
more than if a perfect cutting causing no damage was performed [1]. Another cutting
procedure is proposed in a study were a coated conductor was cut using a diamond wheel.
The decrease in Ic was found to be 82 % partly due to a 10 % material loss [18]. Cutting
a coated conductor with a diamond cutting wheel with ethanol continuously applied as
lubricant, to avoid degradation by heating or vibration, has also been used in a study that
succeeded in building an all superconducting persistent current system [59]. The system
could be loaded with 37 A and run in persistent mode so the cutting method can be
successfully used. Studies show that the damage caused by the cutting procedure could
be minimized using a laser. A study comparing the damage caused to a coated conductor
done by cutting mechanically or with a laser clearly shows that the eﬀective width of the
coated conductor and therfore also Jc was decreased more using the mechanical method
than the laser method [60]. The decrease of Jc can be avoided if the means are there.
Decreases in Jc of only 1 % has been achieved on a 8 m long piece of coated conductor
from SuperPower Inc. by using a cleanroom procedure [61]. The downside of such cutting
procedures is the means necessary to perform it. If the damage caused by the cutting
procedure should be decreased during testing of an all superconducting persistent current
system a laser cutting method is proposed.

Interaction between YBCO and water
The results of the YBCO samples exposed to water showed that an interaction between
the YBCO-phase and water does occur.

Conclusion
This project has investigated how certain exposures interact with samples cut from a
coated conductor commercial available from SuperPower Inc. The project was based on
the ﬁndings of a previous DTU student project where an all superconducting persistent
current system was successfully build using a cut coated conductor. 24 hours after the ﬁrst
measurement the superconducting properties of the coated conductor persistent current
system had degraded completely. Therefore it has been investigated how YBa2Cu3O7−δ
interacts with water and if the superconducting properties are aﬀected by a measurement
cycle.
Cutting the coated conductor with a pair of scissors was seen to remove small areas of
the YBCO layer at the edges. The cutting procedure created averagely 0.20 slip lines
per µm along the cut edges which propagated into the samples between (60 ± 5) µm and
(105±3) µm. The eﬀective superconducting material was clearly decreased by the cutting
procedure thus decreasing Jc. The cutting procedure is cheap and eﬃcient but could be
replaced by a more expensive laser cutting procedure if less damage is wanted.
It can be concluded that the interaction between YBCO and water does decompose the
YBCO phase. The results correlated with the proposed interactions in literature where
water penetrates YBCO material along the b-axis. It was seen that barium was leached
from the structure and probably dissolved in the water as Ba(OH)2. The eﬀect on Tc and
Jc was clear in the form of a decreasing values with increasing exposure temperature. It
was seen that Jc decreased up to (87.2 ± 0.5) % for a sample exposed to water at 90 ◦C
for one hour. The decrease in Tc and Jc was not observed for samples where the silver
layer was still on. This indicated that the silver layer still protected the YBCO material
from exposure despite the cutting damage.
Samples exposed to liquid nitrogen treatments resembling a measurement cycle showed
various results. No decrease was observed in Tc and the resistivity measurement were also
inconclusive. A decrease of Jc between (11 ± 2) % and (15 ± 2) % was observed for the
samples. A small increase of relative barium concentration on the surface was observed
from EDS scans. This could be due to a decomposition caused by condense water which
would correlate with the discussed interaction with water where barium is leached to the
surface.
It was investigated if the oxygen stoichiometry δ of the YBa2Cu3O7−δ samples changed
due to the water exposure and liquid nitrogen treatments. When exposed to the liquid
nitrogen treatments δ was both decreased and increased though the changes were to small
to aﬀect Tc signiﬁcantly. It was found that δ slightly increased when exposed to water.
The change was again very small and not signiﬁcant in means of aﬀecting the Tc.

7.
Outlook
The investigations performed in this thesis was based on a degradation process observed
in a previous DTU study (2017) [1]. This project focused on exposing samples of coated
conductor to environments resembling those met in a measurement cycle and investigate
their degrading eﬀects. The degradation observed in the previous DTU project could
also have been caused by a fault in the process of unloading the persistent current system
or other current related issues.
A study investigating if a further degrading eﬀect is
caused by any current related mechanics as quenching should be conducted to support
the ﬁndings in this thesis.
A less damaging cutting method could be considered. The cutting procedure using a
pair of scissor is cheap and eﬃcient but damaged the coated conductor and caused loss
of superconducting material. Using a laser cutter procedure could decrease the damage
caused but is more demanding and expensive than using a pair of scissors. If the all
superconducting persistent current system using coated conductors is to be used for MRI
machines the cutting procedure must be performed with the least amount of damage
caused to ensure throughput.
To avoid the degrading eﬀects seen in this project a study investigating the eﬀectiveness
of diﬀerent protecting layers could be performed. Other studies have reported succesfully
adding curable epoxy as protecting layer [59] or applying a rubber solution of butadiene
and styrene copolymer [63] though not with any explicit argument for the choices. The
coated conductor could also be protected by adding a silver layer after the cutting pro-
cedure. The procedure is more demanding than experimenting with epoxy but if an all
superconducting persistent current system is feasible for MRI machines this could be the
ﬁnal solution to avoid exposure of the YBCO layer.
