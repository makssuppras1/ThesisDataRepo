Abstract
This thesis presents a detailed numerical investigation of hydrogen–diesel dual­fuel direct injection
(H2DDI) combustion in a constant­volume combustion chamber (CVCC). Large­eddy simulations
were performed using Simcenter STAR­CCM+, employing a validated 3D geometry based on ex­
perimental work by Rorimpandey et al.. The objective was to assess the fidelity and sensitivity
of sub­model selections, numerical schemes, and physical parameters in reproducing dual­fuel ig­
nition, flame development, and heat release. Inert, pilot­only, and dual­fuel configurations were
simulated, with particular focus on ignition delay time (IDT), apparent heat release rate (AHRR),
and flame evolution. A comprehensive series of mesh and model sensitivity studies was conducted,
including evaluation of droplet breakup models, subgrid­scale turbulence formulations, species dif­
fusivity, and gas equations of state. Results show robust agreement with experimental IDTs across
three injection strategies, and the simulations capture key qualitative combustion features, includ­
ing ignition kernel development and flame lift­off lengths. The overprediction of AHRR peaks
is attributed to simplified injection modelling, which neglects transient injector dynamics. Nev­
ertheless, the final model configuration achieves a balance between computational efficiency and
physical realism, providing a validated framework for further parametric studies and optimisation
of H2DDI strategies.

Introduction
Realising hydrogen’s full potential as a transportation fuel requires a deeper understanding of its
combustion characteristics, such as low lift­off lengths, high laminar flame speed, and low min­
imum ignition energy, as highlighted in works such as [1, 2, 3, 4]. One promising strategy is
hydrogen­diesel dual­fuel direct injection (H2DDI), first proposed by Yip et al. [1] and further
developed by Rorimpandey et al. [5]. Numerical modelling plays a crucial role in advancing this
understanding by offering insights beyond the reach of experiments alone. However, generating
valid, reliable, and cost­effective results remains a significant challenge, particularly for novel
combustion modes like H2DDI, where prior research is still limited and the computational cost is
significant [1, 6, 7].
1.1
Motivation and Context
Numerical models can enhance our understanding of combustion characteristics in systems with
complex reactive flow behaviour, such as hydrogen ignition and combustion [2, 4]. Experimental
investigations often require extensive planning and setup to obtain the desired measurements [8,
9]. By contrast, numerical simulations offer the advantage of extracting detailed flow and chemical
information through post­processing techniques after computation, thus eliminating the need for
costly prior planning.
This capability is beneficial for developing internal combustion engines (ICEs) that run on un­
conventional fuels such as hydrogen. Hydrogen possesses a range of beneficial physicochemical
properties to ICEs. If these properties are effectively controlled, they can support highly efficient
combustion. Table 1.1, reproduced from Yip et al. [1], compares critical fuel properties of hy­
drogen with those of compressed natural gas (CNG), gasoline, and diesel. The most noteworthy
attributes of hydrogen for ICE applications are highlighted in bold.
Beyond its carbon­free nature, hydrogen exhibits several favourable characteristics, including a
high lower heating value (LHV), fast laminar flame speed, and a wide flammability range, all of
which promote efficient combustion [10, 11]. However, despite its high LHV (representing energy
per unit mass), hydrogen’s volumetric energy density is more than 3000 times lower than that
of diesel, requiring substantially larger storage volumes. Furthermore, the same characteristics
that enhance its combustion performance also introduce significant challenges for integration into
conventional ICEs. Notably, hydrogen’s low minimum ignition energy increases susceptibility to
abnormal ignition events, such as pre­ignition and knocking, often triggered by engine hot spots
or residual gases. Its short quenching distance elevates the risk of backfiring in port fuel injection
(PFI) systems and contributes to greater heat losses at the cylinder walls.
Emission regulations in hard­to­electrify sectors, such as international shipping, are becoming in­
creasingly stringent, necessitating alternatives to conventional fuels. The International Maritime
Organisation’s (IMO) 2023 revision targets a 20–30% reduction in greenhouse gas (GHG) emis­
sions by 2030, alongside a 5–10% uptake of zero­ or near­zero­emission fuels and technologies
[12]. In response, hydrogen­fuelled internal combustion engines (H2­ICEs) have gained traction
as a viable decarbonisation pathway, supported by growing research and investment [3, 13, 14].
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA) [15], hydrogen and hydrogen­based fuels
are not only expected to play a significant role in maritime decarbonisation but are also essential
for putting the sector on track to meet net­zero emissions by 2050. Over 50 pilot and demonstra­
tion projects for hydrogen bunkering infrastructure were already underway as of 2023, signalling
growing momentum. Ongoing research considers both hydrogen­only and hydrogen­enriched,

Dual­fuel combustion in compression­ignition (CI) engines is another promising near­zero­emission
technology, particularly as a transitional solution in hard­to­electrify sectors. Such engines enable
the use of various alternative fuels, thereby reducing CO2 emissions [16]. In a dual­fuel CI system,
two fuels are directly injected (DI) into the combustion chamber: one, typically diesel, serves as
the pilot fuel to provide an ignition source, while the other, often gaseous, acts as the main energy
carrier. Diesel is favoured as the pilot due to its low auto­ignition temperature (see Table 1.1);
once it auto­ignites, it supplies sufficient energy to ignite the main fuel. Consequently, interest in
dual­fuel CI combustion is growing, especially in sectors where electrification is difficult [1, 3, 4,
14].
Combining hydrogen with diesel in a dual­fuel CI engine offers a pathway to zero­carbon combus­
tion while mitigating hydrogen’s tendency for abnormal ignition events, through improvements to
precise timing control by DI systems [1, 5]. However, hydrogen’s high adiabatic flame tempera­
ture presents a challenge in the form of elevated NOx emissions relative to other alternative fuels
[3, 4]. A comprehensive understanding of the underlying combustion mechanisms is essential to
enable the widespread adoption of hydrogen dual­fuel CI technology in industries with high GHG
emissions, such as international shipping.
1.2
State­of­the­Art
This section presents a comprehensive and critical review of existing studies on H2DDI. The sec­
tion examines engine configuration and constant­volume combustion chamber (CVCC) investi­
gations. The objective is to present what has been explored regarding injection and combustion
characteristics and highlight areas requiring more work.
1.2.1
Studies with Engine Configurations
Motivated by the need to overcome challenges associated with hydrogen PFI, as previously noted,
Liu et al. [17] carried out the first study of H2DDI in a single­cylinder CI engine. By maintaining

a constant total fuel­energy input, they varied hydrogen substitution and injection timing, while
fixing combustion phasing, to investigate performance and emissions trade­offs. Their results
demonstrated that intermediate hydrogen injections produced the highest indicated mean effective
pressure (IMEP) and apparent heat­release rate (AHRR), albeit at the cost of increased NOx. Con­
versely, later injection timings reduced smoke and combustion noise significantly, with an optimal
compromise at 40∘CA bTDC (40∘crank angle before the top­dead­centre) for balanced efficiency,
reduced smoke and marginal noise, despite a moderate NOx rise.
Following this work, Liu et al. [18] achieved a 90% hydrogen substitution by energy in a retrofitted
single­cylinder engine, utilising DI for both hydrogen and pilot diesel. They investigated injection
control strategies using a hydrogen single­hole injector. Findings support their previous optimal
hydrogen injection timing at 40∘CA bTDC, which minimised NOx while preserving the same com­
bustion phasing as the diesel baseline. At this 90% hydrogen fraction, the 40∘CA bTDC timing
improves the IMEP and reduces CO2 emissions by 85.9%. Further, two distinct combustion modes
were identified; earlier injections favoured premixed combustion, resulting in higher IMEP, ef­
ficiency and NOx, whereas later timings led to mixing­controlled combustion. Although rising
hydrogen fractions generally elevated NOx, very late injection timings were shown to reduce NOx
below diesel levels, however, at the cost of engine performance.
Building on these findings, Liu et al. [19] investigated the influence of split hydrogen injection
timing and two injector geometries (multi­hole versus single­hole), alongside varying hydrogen
energy splits. In their split­injection strategy, the first hydrogen injection occurred between 180–
60∘CA bTDC and the second between 90∘CA bTDC and TDC. Their results indicated that, although
the multi­hole injector enabled higher hydrogen substitution, it exhibited only marginal changes in
performance and emissions due to mixing limitations. Moreover, an increased first injection por­
tion with this geometry reduced CO2 but concurrently raised NOx owing to enhanced premixing.
In contrast, the single­capped injector substantially enhanced combustion by directing hydrogen
toward the piston bowl. Late injection timings achieved mixing­controlled hydrogen combustion,
comparable power output and a substantial CO2 reduction. Variations in the split ratio influenced
combustion behaviour: a higher proportion of hydrogen in the first injection favoured premixed
combustion, whereas a greater share in the second injection led to mixing­controlled combustion.
Overall, the split injection strategies demonstrated high energy efficiency (up to 90%) and achieved
a notable 77% CO2 reduction relative to the diesel baseline.
Apart from this research group at the University of New South Wales [17, 18, 19], Lee et al.
[20] conducted an experimental study supplemented by zero­dimensional chemical­kinetics sim­
ulations in CHEMKIN and one­dimensional simulations using the Musculus­Kattke spray model
[21]. Their work in a single­cylinder heavy­duty optical engine explored the phenomenology and
underlying mechanisms governing pilot fuel ignition and the subsequent combustion of a premixed
hydrogen­air charge. This contrasts with previous studies targeting diffusion­flame combustion.
Lee et al. [20] highlighted a complex interplay between physicochemical processes, showing that
higher premixed H2 concentrations substantially delay pilot­fuel ignition, necessitating longer in­
jection durations. Their analysis emphasised that pilot injection strategies are important for main­
taining local pilot­fuel concentration and mitigating variability arising from stochastic pockets of
rich pilot­fuel mixture within fuel jets. Increased premixed hydrogen delays ignition onset, leading
to numerous misfires unless pilot strategies are carefully controlled.
Contrary to combining experimental and numerical approaches, Wang et al. [22] conducted a
purely numerical investigation of an H2DDI engine. They validated three­dimensional Unsteady
Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes (URANS) simulations in CONVERGE against the experimen­
tal data from Liu et al. [17]. They analysed charge stratification by varying hydrogen injection
timings at a fixed 50% hydrogen substitution. Their findings indicated that the highest thermal

Although H2DDI cases generally exhibited reduced efficiency and increased NOx compared to
diesel, the study revealed a strong correlation between NOx production and high temperatures or
near­stoichiometric conditions. Later hydrogen injections enhanced stratification, thereby mitigat­
ing NOx formation.
Similar numerical work by Ramsay and Ranga Dinesh [23] investigated engine performance us­
ing H2DDI versus CH4DDI (methane–diesel DI) in ANSYS Fluent. Their 3D URANS simu­
lations identified four primary phases of non­premixed combustion: gaseous­jet ignition delay,
free­jet combustion, wall­jet combustion and late combustion. They found that hydrogen exhib­
ited a shorter ignition delay than methane and, during the free­jet phase, achieved a higher peak
AHRR due to a wider flame and faster jet penetration. In the wall­jet phase, hydrogen maintained
a higher AHRR than methane, attributed to a smaller quenching distance, greater jet momentum
and a higher rich­flammability limit. During late combustion, hydrogen displayed a more rapid
decline and a lower AHRR level in the power stroke, suggesting earlier fuel consumption. Over­
all, hydrogen combustion produced higher NOx emissions yet significantly reduced carbon­based
emissions compared to methane.
One section of Treacy’s PhD thesis [24] focused on numerical simulations of an H2DDI engine,
supported by experimental data from Scania CV AB. These simulations, conducted using CON­
VERGE CFD, aimed to improve understanding of fuel­air mixing, in­cylinder flow and combus­
tion processes. Unlike earlier studies employing separate injectors, Treacy used a single multi­hole
injector for both hydrogen and diesel, with independent control over each fuel’s start of injection
(SOI). A parametric study of ignition delay times (IDT) revealed that the highest thermodynamic
efficiency (54.9%) was achieved with the earliest split injections at high rail pressures. However,
these conditions also produced the highest NOx emissions [25].
Taken together, these works focus on optimising injection strategies, injector geometries, and fuel­
air mixing to improve efficiency and reduce emissions. They combine experimental, numerical,
and hybrid approaches to understand the combustion characteristics and trade­offs between NOx
and CO2 emissions at varying hydrogen substitution levels and injection timings. Common find­
ings show that early injection improves efficiency but raises NOx, while later injection reduces
NOx at the cost of performance; injector design and split strategies further influence this balance.
However, all studies are conducted in engine configurations, a dynamic system that introduces in­
creased complexity through cylinder movement. The next group of studies remove this uncertainty
to provide a more static and accessible system through CVCCs.
1.2.2
Studies with Constant Volume Combustion Chambers
Building on earlier hydrogen­jet experiments by Yip et al. [26, 27], Rorimpandey et al. [5] per­
formed a detailed investigation of H2DDI combustion in a CVCC equipped with two single­hole
injectors oriented convergently; one for n­heptane (pilot fuel) and one for hydrogen (main fuel).
They explored how the sequence of injections, dwell time between pilot and main fuel injections,
and ambient temperature (780­890 K) influence jet­jet interactions and the resulting combustion
of n­heptane and hydrogen. Their results show that when the diesel pilot is injected before hy­
drogen, the burnt diesel products mix and cool as dwell time increases, thus prolonging the jet­jet
interaction required to ignite the hydrogen jet. In contrast, when hydrogen is injected before the
diesel pilot, the degree of hydrogen­air premixing before pilot­fuel ignition dictates how combus­
tion propagates through the hydrogen jet [5].
Continuing this work, Rorimpandey et al. [28] studied the effects of fuel injection durations and
ambient oxygen (O2) concentrations (10­21 vol.%) on ignition behaviour and flame structure.
Their findings indicated that lower O2 levels necessitated longer interaction times between n­
heptane products and the hydrogen jet for ignition to occur. At higher O2 concentrations, flames

They also demonstrated relationships between injection duration, flame lift­off behaviour, interac­

To advance numerical modelling capabilities, Lucchini et al. [6] extended the transported probability­
density function (PDF) method by coupling Eulerian Monte Carlo Field (EMCF) with Flamelet
Generated Manifold (FGM) to simulate H2DDI combustion in a CVCC. Their EMCF+FGM model
was validated against experimental AHRR and high­speed schlieren imaging from Rorimpandey
et al. [5]. It successfully reproduced flame structures and AHRR trends under various injection
strategies, accurately predicting both diffusion­governed hydrogen ignition by the pilot and pure
hydrogen diffusion flames when the pilot preceded the main fuel injection. The model further re­
vealed how increasing dwell time shifts combustion from diffusion­controlled regimes to partially
premixed regimes, influencing cyclic variability and combustion stability [6].

the peak AHRR during the premixed hydrogen phase increased with dwell time, indicating longer
mixing before ignition [24].
Generally, CVCC experiments and simulations have explained how injection sequence, dwell time,
O2 concentration and injector geometry dictate flame stabilisation, lift­off and hydrogen ignition.
However, the literature on the topic is scarce, indicating that the area of research is in its infancy. A
deeper understanding of the mechanisms dictating the observations made in the covered literature
is imperative to achieve widespread adoption of H2DDI in dual­fuel engines.
1.3
Scope and Objectives
Given the limited understanding of injection and combustion mechanisms in H2DDI systems, this
thesis focuses on pilot–main jet interactions and resulting combustion phenomena. Simulations
are conducted in a CVCC geometry using Simcenter STAR­CCM+ to isolate and analyse these
phenomena under well­controlled conditions. Experimental data reported by Rorimpandey et al.
[5] are employed as validation benchmarks to ensure that the numerical results can be compared
directly to established measurements.
1.3.1
Objectives
The objectives of the thesis are to:
1. Establish a methodology for modelling injection and combustion in H2DDI configurations
within a CVCC, including mesh generation, boundary condition specification, injector ge­
ometry representation and solver configuration in Simcenter STAR­CCM+.
2. Identify and assess the influence of physical sub­models, such as droplet breakup and evap­
oration for n­heptane, turbulence modelling and model parameters, on predicting injection
dynamics and combustion regimes.
3. Integrate a reduced chemical kinetic mechanism to balance species­prediction accuracy, cov­

ficiency.
4. Validate non­reacting (injection­only) and reacting (combustion) simulations against exper­

tify model fidelity.
1.3.2
Expected Outcomes and Contributions
By validating simulation results against established experimental benchmarks, this study clarifies
the capabilities and limitations of current combustion models in reproducing dual­fuel ignition
and heat release behaviour. The expected outcomes include quantification of model fidelity for
H2DDI and combustion in a CVCC, identification of important sub­models or parameters that
require refinement to capture pilot–main interactions and flame propagation accurately, and rec­
ommendations for adjustments to modelling approaches to enhance predictive capability. These
contributions provide a validated framework for dual­fuel CFD studies under controlled conditions
and may inform future engine research on dual­fuel strategies.
1.3.3
Scope
This work is confined to single­cycle combustion events in a CVCC. Ambient pressure, tempera­
ture and gas composition are fixed at engine­relevant conditions as provided by the experimental­
ists. Heat loss to chamber walls is included to account for hydrogen quenching effects. Turbulence
will be modelled through LES. The n­heptane spray is modelled with droplet breakup and evap­
oration sub­models, while hydrogen is treated as a gaseous injection. Only the final section of
the injector is represented, and needle movement is excluded (the arguments for this are presented
in Section 3.2. The initial ambient conditions assume a perfectly homogeneous and quiescent gas
field at the SOI. The final study aims to capture the full combustion evolution; however, secondary
analyses may focus on early n­heptane combustion or subsequent hydrogen combustion. The in­
jection strategy is restricted to a pilot injection of n­heptane followed by a main hydrogen injection
(subsequently referred to as pilot­main fuel injection), in contrast to the sequences used by the ex­
perimentalists, as they also employ a main­pilot sequence. Model outputs include qualitative flow
visualisations, AHRR and vapour penetration length (VPL) for validation purposes. Although this
work does not undertake direct extrapolation to real engine cycles, it aims to provide a foundation
for modelling H2DDI in engines and can be extended to similar dual­fuel cases.
1.4
Structure of the Report
The thesis is organised into six chapters from theoretical foundations to conclusions. Chapter 2
establishes the theoretical foundation and modelling strategies used to simulate the coupled phe­
nomena of fuel injection, turbulence, multiphase interactions, and combustion in the H2DDI sys­
tem. Chapter 3 describes the numerical setup in detail, including mesh generation, boundary­
condition specification, injector geometry representation and solver configuration in Simcenter
STAR­CCM+. A series of parametric and sensitivity studies are conducted to tune model pa­
rameters and sub­model selections so that the AHRR and VPL match the experimental data from
Rorimpandey et al. [5]. Chapter 4 presents the validated simulations for three injection strategies:
Case I hydrogen injection 0.07 ms before the pilot, cases II and III with pilot injection 0.93 ms and

2.2
Large Eddy Simulation
Several classes of turbulence models exist, the most prominent being algebraic models, turbulence
energy equation models, and simulation­based approaches. The first two aim to close the Reynolds­
Averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) equations by modelling the Reynolds stress term. Algebraic
models employ the Boussinesq eddy­viscosity hypothesis, expressing the Reynolds stress as the
product of an eddy viscosity and the mean strain rate. Turbulence energy equation models retain
this formulation but compute the eddy viscosity based on additional transport equations, typically
for the turbulence kinetic energy and its dissipation. For detailed discussions of these approaches,
the reader is referred to standard turbulence modelling references such as Wilcox [34] and Sumer
& Fuhrman [35], as they are not further addressed in this thesis.
Simulation­based approaches constitute the most advanced tier of turbulence modelling. These
include DNS, Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and hybrid techniques such as Detached Eddy Simu­
lation (DES), which will not be further mentioned in the present work. Figure 2.1 illustrates how
DNS, LES and RANS differ in the portion of the turbulent kinetic­energy spectrum they resolve.
DNS resolves every dynamically relevant scale, LES resolves the large energy­containing eddies
and part of the inertial range while modelling the remaining sub­grid­scale (SGS) motions [35].

2.3
Multiphase Flow Modelling
The injection of n­heptane into a gaseous hydrogen–air mixture results in a multiphase flow, where
distinct thermodynamic phases (liquid and gas) interact within the computational domain. The
present work focuses on capturing the evolution of the liquid phase from injection through atom­
isation, evaporation, and ultimately combustion. Accurately resolving this sequence requires a
modelling framework capable of describing both the dispersed liquid phase and its interactions
with the surrounding gas.
2.3.1
Eulerian–Lagrangian Framework
Two fundamental descriptions exist for fluid motion: the Eulerian and Lagrangian frames of ref­
erence. The Eulerian description assesses flow variables at fixed spatial locations, while the La­
grangian reference tracks individual fluid particles as they traverse the domain [30, 31].
Since the direct resolution of each droplet on an Eulerian mesh is computationally infeasible, these
approaches are often combined. The gas phase is treated using the Eulerian formulation, while

the liquid droplets are modelled as a group of droplets, called parcels, in the Lagrangian frame.
These parcels each represent a statistically significant number of physical droplets with identical
properties (size, velocity, temperature). The Lagrangian phase is coupled to the Eulerian through
the conservation equations Eqs. 2.1­2.3. The equations’ source terms are exchanged between the
Lagrangian and Eulerian phases to ensure conservation of the equations [9, 31].

way around. In the two­way coupling, they affect each other. For the present work, the two­
way coupling is used as the n­heptane affects the surrounding continuous phase through mass,
interphase momentum, and heat transfer [31].

3.1
Experimental Case Specifications
Fig. 3.3 shows the CVCC configuration used in the experiments of Rorimpandey et al. [5], which
serves as the benchmark for the present modelling work. The chamber is a 114 mm cube (Fig. 3.3a),
equipped with two converging injectors arranged to produce a 12∘jet intersection approximately
28 mm downstream of the nozzle exits (Fig. 3.3b). The hydrogen injector is converted from a
multi­hole to a single­hole configuration using a cap insert with a 0.58 mm orifice, as shown in
Fig. 3.3c and described by Yip et al. [26].
Fig. 3.1 presents a conceptual overview of the involved physics studied in this work. A short,
high­pressure injection of liquid n­heptane forms a conical spray that undergoes atomisation, break­
up, and evaporation. The resulting vapour mixture auto­ignites and initiates combustion, which
propagates into the surrounding gas. Hydrogen is injected at reservoir pressures exceeding twice
the chamber pressure, forming an under­expanded jet with shock structures and high velocities.
This enhances turbulence in the shear layer and promotes mixing with both the ambient gas and
the n­heptane [5, 28].
To generate the desired high­temperature and high­pressure ambient environment, a premixed com­
bustion event is initiated before fuel injection. As shown in Fig. 3.2, this results in a rapid pressure
increase, followed by a cooling phase due to heat transfer to the chamber walls. Once target con­
ditions are reached, fuel is injected and auto­ignition occurs [45, 26].
The experimental work includes multiple dual­fuel injection strategies, with variations in injection
order (diesel­first or hydrogen­first), dwell time between the two injections and ambient conditions.
The ambient conditions are achieved through the preconditioning event, described in Chapter 2,
resulting in core gas temperatures of 890, 820, and 780 K and pressures of 5.20, 4.60, and 4.45 MPa.
However, only the former ambient temperature and pressure will be used for the validation process
in the present work. Table 3.1 summarises the ambient and fuel injection parameters used for
validation from the reference experiments.
For the present study, three injection strategies are selected for validation. Case I corresponds to
hydrogen injection occurring 0.07 ms before diesel, while in Case II and Case III, diesel precedes
hydrogen by 0.93 ms and 1.93 ms, respectively. The reported IDTs for both fuels are listed in
Table 3.2, given relative to the SOI of the first­injected fuel in each case.

3.3
Inert Main Fuel Injection
The simulations using the initial setup (Section 3.2) are conducted under inert conditions to ensure
that the fundamental jet dynamics are accurately captured before introducing combustion effects.
This approach enables a systematic evaluation of individual physical phenomena and their influ­
ence on the jet development, before assessing their combined impact on the combustion process.
3.3.1
Mesh Settings
The mesh is generated using the trimmed cell mesher, with a base mesh size set to 2.00 mm for
the full domain. However, to account for the significant difference in resolution requirements
between the main fuel injector and the CVCC, a cylindrical volumetric refinement is imposed. This
region, extending 14 mm axially from the injector inlet and with a radius of 2.00 mm, is refined
to a minimum cell size of 0.125 mm. To resolve the internal nozzle flow, a further refinement is
applied to the injector core, reaching a minimum cell size of 0.0625 mm. Near­wall gradients are
captured using a prism layer mesh with a total thickness of 0.03 mm. The resulting mesh is shown
in Fig. 3.6, including a close­up of the in­nozzle region with the prism layers at the valve needle
end.
In addition to the prescribed volumetric refinements, AMR is applied based on the local velocity
field and the mass fraction of hydrogen. The detailed formulation of the AMR criterion is provided
in Section A.1 in the appendix.

The final inert­phase investigation concerns the EOS used to describe the relationship between

• IEOS­1: Ideal gas law
• IEOS­2: Redlich–Kwong (RK) real gas model
Their influence on jet development is evaluated through the VPL, spreading angle, and radially

The results indicate that the VPL predictions for both gas models are in close agreement with the
experimental data, with IEOS­2 offering a marginally better match, particularly during the early
injection phase. In terms of spreading angle, IEOS­2 predicts a slightly larger angle than IEOS­1
at early times, consistent with the observed improvement in VPL prediction. The axial velocity
profiles reveal that IEOS­1 predicts a higher velocity close to the jet centre.
These differences are attributed to IEOS­2’s ability to capture real gas effects, notably the non­ideal
thermodynamic behaviour of hydrogen. Specifically, hydrogen exhibits a negative Joule–Thomson
(JT) coefficient under the present conditions, meaning it heats upon expansion and cools upon
compression, opposite to the behaviour of most gases. This leads to different thermal responses in
the jet core, which the ideal gas model fails to capture. For this reason, employing real gas models
that account for such effects is recommended when modelling high­pressure hydrogen jets [48].
The effects of this are also studied in Section 3.5.3 with combustion.

3.5
Reactive Dual Fuel Injection
The preceding sections established baseline behaviours for the dual­fuel case. Inert hydrogen sim­
ulations (Section 3.3) closely match the experimental VPL and are largely insensitive to model
variations, except for the real gas EOS, which produces a sharper velocity gradient at the jet core
relative to the ideal gas model. Reactive pilot­only cases exhibit elevated AHRR peaks, but when
using the mass flow profile from Treacy et al. [24, 50], the integrated energy release aligns well
with experimental data. This motivates an assessment of hydrogen combustion sensitivity to vari­
ations in both Lagrangian and continuum­phase parameters.
For all dual­fuel simulations, the AHRR is directly compared to experimental measurements to as­
sess predictive accuracy. IDTs are determined by the same method outlined in Section 3.4 for both
fuels. The following subsections organise the sensitivity studies by numerical settings, injection
conditions, flow physics, and chemical modelling.

3.6
Final Model Configuration
The preceding sensitivity studies indicate that most parameter variations exert limited influence on
key simulation outputs. In the inert case, only the choice of the equation of state slightly affects the
hydrogen jet’s velocity profile. Nonetheless, both the spreading angle and VPLs remain in close
agreement across all cases.
In the reactive pilot case, the peak AHRR is substantially underpredicted, whereas the total heat
release matches experimental data more closely. This discrepancy suggests that while the cumu­
lative energy release is reasonably well captured, diagnostic uncertainties, unidentified underlying
characteristics cause incorrect combustion development. For the dual­fuel case, simulation results
exhibit higher sensitivity to the droplet size distribution, even though the vapour phase distribu­
tions at 0.2 ms appear similar between the baseline and coarser configurations. Additionally, solver
variability must be considered when interpreting case differences.
Combustion chemistry is represented using the reduced Seidel mechanism, comprising 56 species
and 206 reactions. The pilot injection profile follows the mass flow history reported by Treacy et

5
Discussion
Chapter 3 demonstrated the low sensitivity of the model to a broad range of numerical and phys­
ical parameters. Based on these results, the simulation setup was refined according to the most
influential factors and applied to three experimental cases.
5.1
Interpretation of Results
In comparison with the experimental data, the simulations reproduce the key trends in AHRR and
flame behaviour. This includes qualitative agreement in lift­off length and general flame develop­
ment, which indicate the location and extent of reaction zones. While some discrepancies remain,
the overall modelling framework demonstrates reliable predictive capability.
5.1.1
Apparent Heat Release Rate and Ignition Delay
As discussed in Section 3.4, the energy input from the injected n­heptane can be calculated directly.
A similar estimation can be made for the hydrogen injection. With a total hydrogen mass of 5.28 mg
delivered over a hydraulic duration of 3.3 ms, the average injection rate is 1.6 mg/ms. Using a LHV
of 119.7 J/mg (as given in Table 1.1), the resulting average heat release rate is 191.5 J/ms.
Because hydrogen is injected at a steady rate, the flame is expected to stabilise near this release rate,
unlike a premixed case where the fuel is already available in the domain. This is also supported by
the initial spike in AHRR following the IDT of the hydrogen, as described by Rorimpandey [5].
As seen in Section 4.1, particularly in Fig. 4.1b for Case II, the simulation begins to approach this
steady combustion regime.
Due to computational cost, Cases I and III were not simulated long enough to fully develop the
steady­state phase. However, the trend observed in Case II suggests that the model is capable of
predicting this phase accurately. Provided that similar convergence would be observed in the other
cases, the current model can be considered reliable for predicting steady hydrogen combustion
under the given CVCC conditions.
As shown in the experimental results in Section 4.1 by Rorimpandey et al. [5], all cases exhibit a
pronounced premixed combustion peak following hydrogen ignition. The magnitude of this peak
can be qualitatively linked to the hydrogen residence time before ignition, defined as the interval
between the hydrogen SOI and its corresponding IDT. A longer residence time allows for greater
accumulation and mixing of hydrogen with ambient air, leading to a larger quantity of premixed
reactants at the time of ignition. Case I, which has the longest residence time, exhibits the highest
peak AHRR, while Case II, with the shortest delay, produces a noticeably smaller peak. Case III
lies between the two. The total injected energy, calculated over the injection duration before IDT
using the LHV of hydrogen, is summarised in Table 5.1 for reference. This supports a general
relationship: longer residence time results in greater premixed hydrogen mass at ignition, yielding
a more energetic premixed combustion phase [51].
When comparing the simulations to experimental data, the expected trend in premixed peak mag­
nitude remains vaguely discernible, but significant deviations are evident. For reference, Table 5.2
summarises the injected hydrogen energy prior to ignition in the simulations. Case I produces
the highest simulated peak and also shows the smallest deviation from the experimental value. In
contrast, Case III exhibits both a large peak and the greatest discrepancy. Case II falls in between.
While hydrogen residence time appears to govern the variation in premixed peak magnitude across
the experimental cases, it does not fully explain the observed simulation­to­experiment difference.

One underlying reason for these discrepancies, especially in Cases II and III, is the way hydrogen
injection is modelled numerically. As described in Section 3.2, hydrogen enters the domain through
a constant mass flow inlet fixed at 1.6 mg/ms. This approach neglects the transient dynamics of
injector opening, which are present in the experiments. As a result, more hydrogen accumulates
in the system before ignition than would realistically occur. While Table 5.1 reflects an idealised
case assuming constant flow in the experiments, the actual transient behaviour would reduce the
premixed hydrogen mass, lowering the expected energy release at IDT.
In contrast, Table 5.2 reflects the injected energy in the simulations under the constant­flow as­
sumption. The resulting over­prediction in hydrogen mass explains the overestimated premixed
peak magnitudes, particularly in short dwell time cases. Interestingly, despite the same modelling
limitation, Case I shows good agreement with experimental peak magnitude. This may be due to
its longer residence time, where the total injected mass before ignition is already large. In such
cases, the effect of neglecting injector transients becomes relatively less significant than in Cases II
and III, where shorter delays amplify this modelling error.
The discrepancy observed in Case I may also be partially attributed to an overprediction of pilot
combustion heat release as the two fuels are combusting concurrently. The modelling of the n­
heptane pilot shows notable deviations from the experimental data. This is particularly evident
in Case III, where the simulated ignition delay is significantly shorter and the peak heat release
considerably higher than observed experimentally. In Case II, the simulated IDT aligns well with
measurements, though the peak AHRR remains overpredicted. Interestingly, the simulations for
Cases II and III exhibit nearly identical pilot combustion behaviour. This is consistent with ex­
pectations: since the pilot is injected into an identical ambient field before hydrogen injection
begins, the local conditions governing ignition and flame development are effectively the same.
However, the experiments do not reflect this similarity, suggesting variability in the measured pi­
lot behaviour that is not reproduced in the simulations. This divergence implies that, while the
model is internally consistent and reliably predicts the pilot IDT, it may systematically overesti­
mate the associated heat release rate. One possible explanation arises from the sensitivity study in
Section 3.5.3, where reducing the Schmidt number to 0.21 resulted in a lower pilot peak for the
coarser AMR configuration. This indicates that pilot combustion may be more sensitive to species
diffusivity than initially expected, particularly concerning the mixing of evaporated fuel vapour
82

with ambient air.
In the inert case (Section 3.3.2), a kinetic theory­based diffusion model was tested but showed
no significant effect. Applying this approach in reactive cases would likely be prohibitively ex­
pensive computationally. Alternative methods, such as specifying binary diffusion coefficients or
component molecular diffusivities of the combination of gas components, may be a better means
of accurately modelling the molecular diffusion rather than setting the bulk molecular diffusion by
the Schmidt number of hydrogen into air.
A comparison with the simulations conducted by Treacy et al. [24, 29] provides further context for
evaluating the present model. Notably, the early ignition of the pilot observed in Case III is also
present in their work, while Case II shows good consistency in pilot ignition timing. However,
their predicted pilot AHRR peak more closely matches the experimental magnitude than in the
present study. One key modelling difference is the use of the KH–RT secondary breakup model in
Treacy’s work. Yet, as demonstrated in the sensitivity analysis in Section 3.5.4, this choice alone
does not appear to significantly influence ignition or heat release. Consequently, the source of this
improved pilot fidelity remains unclear.
In contrast, a major discrepancy arises in the hydrogen behaviour. Treacy’s simulations do not
exhibit the distinct premixed combustion peak observed in the present work. This can likely be
attributed to their application of a 0.3 ms ramp on the hydrogen mass flow at injection onset.
For Case I, this ramp duration exceeds the residence time between the hydrogen SOI and its IDT,
thereby suppressing premixed accumulation. Additionally, the hydrogen mass flow used by Treacy
is slightly lower (1.56 mg/ms) than the 1.6 mg/ms reported by Rorimpandey et al. [5], further
reducing the total premixed energy prior to ignition. As their simulation progresses beyond the
initial transient phase, it reaches a stable combustion rate consistent with the theoretically estimated
steady­state value of 191.5 J/ms.
These comparisons reinforce the conclusion that the premixed peak in the present model is a result
of simplified boundary conditions. Incorporating realistic hydrogen ramping, as in Treacy’s work,
would likely mitigate this overprediction, whilst that of their work seems excessive due to the lower
gradient of the premixed combustion.
5.1.2
Combustion and Flame Evolution
Qualitative comparisons in Section 4.2 indicate good agreement between simulations and experi­
ments in flame development. However, the experimental sequences represent trial runs with un­
controlled variations in injection and timing, limiting the value of direct temporal comparisons.
Nonetheless, spatial trends offer useful insight. In all three simulations, the hydrogen ignition
kernel forms consistently upstream of the 20 mm grid line, as shown in Figs. 4.4, 4.7, and 4.10.
In contrast, schlieren images in Figs. 4.2a, 4.5a, and 4.8a suggest ignition occurs further down­
stream, typically between the 20 mm and 40 mm grid lines. This consistent upstream ignition in
the simulations implies that the n­heptane pilot acts as a robust and early ignition source across all
injection strategies. The observed discrepancy likely stems from experimental non­idealities, such
as injector variability or ambient fluctuations, not present in the numerically controlled conditions.
Investigation of the pilot n­heptane ignition kernel in Figs. 4.3 and 4.9 shows that Cases II and III
feature nearly identical ignition locations, while Case I ignites later and further upstream in the pilot
jet. This suggests that pilot ignition occurring before hydrogen injection promotes more favourable
ignition conditions for the pilot. As seen in Fig. 4.2c, ignition takes place in a fuel­rich region with
equivalence ratios exceeding 2, well separated from the hydrogen jet. This trend is also evident
in Fig. 4.6, representing the frame closest to pilot IDT in Case II, and can reasonably be assumed
for Case III due to their similar evolution. These results indicate that the presence of hydrogen
prior to pilot ignition alters the flow field and mixing behaviour unfavourably, delaying ignition.

5.2
Modelling Uncertainties and Limitations
Generally, it may be observed that the model performs well for determining the characteristic tim­
ings and reaching the expected behaviour as the hydrogen flames become diffusion­governed rather
than the available premixed hydrogen with air after the ignition. This is also observed for the pilot
fuel, which ignites at the expected time and robustly provides an ignition source for the hydrogen.
However, the model is not able to capture the correct magnitudes of the heat released by the initial
combustion after the ignition of the fuels. Some methods to further study this have been introduced
in Section 5.1.1 and 5.1.2, such as introducing a ramp for the hydrogen, and investigating the effect
of component­wise diffusion specification, to improve the hydrogen and pilot initial combustion
phases, respectively.
As shown in Section 4.3, the mesh quality in the hydrogen jet region is inadequate, with LES𝐼𝑄
values falling below the recommended threshold of 0.8. Nevertheless, this does not appear to
affect the AHRR field or the observed flame development within the domain. It is possible that
such effects are not yet prominent during the early stages of combustion but may become more
significant as the solution becomes increasingly diffusion­governed by the hydrogen from the jet.
Extending the simulation time would help clarify this and potentially reveal inaccuracies arising
from excessive modelled turbulent viscosity.
Another investigation that was considered in Section 3.4, but not in Section 3.5, is the cone angle of
the pilot injector, which was determined not to affect the pilot combustion. However, its effect on
the combustion of the hydrogen is uncertain. From the pilot­only investigation on the cone angle

6
Conclusion and Further Work
This chapter summarises the main outcomes of the thesis, evaluates the extent to which the stated
objectives were achieved, and outlines limitations of the current modelling approach. Finally,
directions for further work are proposed to support continued development and application of the
model.
6.1
Summary
This thesis established and validated a comprehensive numerical modelling framework for H2DDI
combustion in a CVCC, addressing all four objectives introduced in Section 1.3.1:
1. Establish a modelling methodology for H2DDI in a CVCC: A 3D simulation domain
replicating the geometry and boundary conditions from Rorimpandey et al. [5] was imple­
mented in STAR­CCM+ for both inert and reactive cases. Mesh independence was con­
firmed via convergence of the VPL, spreading angle, velocity profiles, and AHRR. Signif­
icant ignition metrics such as delay, location, and flame evolution were validated against
experimental data.
2. Assess the influence of physical sub­models: Species diffusivity and droplet size most
significantly influenced ignition timing and flame development in select conditions, while
overall sensitivity remained limited. The real­gas EOS improved hydrogen jet representa­
tion, and sub­grid scale turbulence models had a negligible impact.
3. Integrate a reduced chemical kinetic mechanism: The reduced mechanism by Seidel et
al. [44] for n­heptane accurately captured the IDTs of both fuels within experimental uncer­
tainty. Hydrogen flame evolution under steady­state conditions aligned with experimental
trends within the mechanism’s validated temperature range.
4. Validate simulation results: Model predictions were compared to experimental AHRR
curves and schlieren imaging, as well as prior simulations by Treacy et al. [24, 29]. The
model showed strong agreement in ignition delay and flame lift­off behaviour. Although
it overpredicts the initial heat release relative to Treacy’s, it more accurately captures the
transition to steady­state combustion and identifies ignition delay times within experimental
uncertainty.
The modelling strategy successfully reproduced the key features of dual­fuel hydrogen–diesel com­
bustion with high fidelity and robustness, providing a solid foundation for future studies on injec­
tion timing, ignition control, and model refinement.
6.2
Further Work
Several options have been identified to extend and improve upon the present modelling framework:
• Refine pilot cone mesh resolution: The fixed volumetric refinement level of 0.125 mm was
chosen before the potential dependence of IDT on pilot mesh resolution was identified. In­
troducing a third refinement level would help confirm convergence and improve confidence
in ignition timing predictions.
• Incorporate transient injection profiles: The constant hydrogen mass flow boundary con­
dition seems to lead to overprediction of premixed energy and initial AHRR peaks. Intro­
ducing a ramped or experimentally validated injection profile would reduce this discrepancy
and improve early flame predictions.

• Refine diffusion modelling: The species diffusivity was found to influence both pilot and
hydrogen combustion. Implementing component­wise molecular diffusion or binary diffu­
sion coefficients may provide a more accurate description than the current bulk Schmidt
number formulation.
• Extend simulation time: Cases I and III were not simulated to steady state. Longer simu­
lation durations would confirm the model’s capability to predict the late combustion phase
and fully resolve flame stabilisation behaviour. In particular, extending the simulation time
may reveal whether the inadequate mesh quality in the hydrogen jet region, identified by
LES𝐼𝑄< 0.8, leads to accumulated errors in flame development due to excessive modelled
turbulent viscosity.
• Investigate injection parameters: While pilot cone angle and droplet size showed lim­
ited influence in isolated pilot studies, results suggest these parameters may affect hydrogen
ignition under dual­fuel operation. A parametric study for each is would provide a better
understanding of these effects.
• Assess hydrogen injection temperature effects: The sensitivity study was limited to Case I.
Extending this analysis to Cases II and III would clarify the role of injection temperature un­
der low­residence­time conditions.
• Quantitative thermochemical analysis: Constructing 𝑇–𝜙diagrams overlaid with OH
mass fraction at ignition times would enable quantitative analysis of the ignition mecha­
nisms, improving the analysis of the present work.
• Explore injection strategy optimisation: Use the validated setup to conduct parametric
studies on alternative injection sequences and timings, aiming to optimise ignition timing
and heat release profiles for optimised emissions and work.
• Reduce computational cost: Further optimisation of meshing and time­stepping strategies
may significantly reduce computational time, enabling faster iteration and broader paramet­
ric exploration of ignition strategies.
