Abstract
As decarbonization progresses among different sectors, low­ and medium­heat temper­
ature industries has been comparatively slow in replacing their energy sources due to
the maturity and predictability of fossil fuels. For this cause, this study aim to perform a
techno­economic analysis in two stages. It first objective is to evaluate two thermal en­
ergy storage (TES) technologies – pressurized water storage (WTES) or packed­bed rock
storage (PTES) – in conjunction with solar collector field and deduct which heating system
is more economically viable depending on the capacity and end consumer load. Its sec­
ond objective is to perform sensitivity analyses on specific variables to see the resulting
effect on the TES system. These objectives are referred to as “research objectives”, and
are later revisited towards the end of thesis as the results are presented.
Before the results, however, this study investigates and applies the relevant theoretical
principles into a simplified and representative schematic of a final system. The system is
then evaluated pertaining to its degrees of freedom, after which mathematical equations
and numerical values are added. Economical constants are derived to calculate CAPEX
costs, as well as levelized cost of storage (LCoS) and heating (LCoH). Once operational,
the model is then tested and verified for several different variables and ranges.
The initial results does not provide a wide enough range of values to answer the first
research objective, so a new approach is taken where the relation of the main techno­
economic variables – solar fraction, LCoS, LCoH, and storage capacity – are evaluated.
This approach shows that WTES is dominant in delivering the most heat to the end user,
but the PTES is considerably cheaper. However, PTES is not cheap enough that its LCoS
and LCoH is lower than that of WTES, unless for relatively high storage capacities.
After the primary research objective, the sensitivity analyses investigates relations and
trend between various ranges for several different variables. Following those presented
simulations, the last few pages are dedicated to a discussion and conclusion about the
study as a whole.

1
Introduction
In this chapter, a brief overview is given of the background and relevance of thermal
energy storage (TES) systems. Their relevance for this thesis is discussed, after which
primary and secondary research objectives are listed and explained. Past, current, and
future research areas related to the topic are then summarized, followed by a short outline
of the thesis structure.
1.1
Background
As the effects of global warming are growing more severe [1], efforts to limit greenhouse
gas emissions continue to be one of the most pressing matters discussed worldwide.
Despite the increased call for urgency, the challenge of mitigating the effect of global
warming has proven complex, as the world’s different sectors offers a plethora of various
circumstances and challenges.
In the industry sector, efforts have been made to identify and categorize energy usage by
temperature intervals. When so doing, approximately half of the energy spent are found
to achieve temperatures below 400 °C – collectively termed “low­medium temperatures”
[2] – as is visualized in Fig. 1.1. These kinds of industries have been comparatively slow
in reducing their emissions due to their dependence on the predictability and reliability of
fossil fuel­powered boilers and burners [3]. Although replacing these energy sources with
heat from renewable energy sources does remove the related carbon emissions, it also
removes the required reliability and security of heat­on­demand.

In order to increase the efficiency from these mixed energy systems, a storage method
should be considered as solar energy occasionally can deliver larger amounts of energy
than needed. One of the most common forms of storing energy is via pressurized water
tanks (WTES), which utilizes the high specific heat capacity in water to store and release

energy at temperatures above water’s normal boiling temperature of 100 °C. However, de­
spite the low cost of the heat transfer medium, the tanks themselves are costly due to the
pressure this storage type requires, which renders them inadequate for larger quantities
of heat and larger energy demands.
There are however alternatives to pressurized water tanks, such as packed rock­bed stor­
age (PTES) that store energy in small rocks rather than water. This method does not
need to operate under pressure and could, due to its half­spherical shape, prove more
cost­efficient and thus competitive than pressurized water tanks, which would speed up
the decarbonization of the low­medium temperature industry sector.
1.1.1
Primary research objectives
The purpose of this techno­economic analysis is to investigate WTES and PTES by sim­
ulating their respective heat flows in order to deduct which system is preferred from an
economical perspective. Given that the half­spherical volume of PTES increases expo­
nentially to the power of 3
 V = (2/3) · π · r3
with its radius rather than exponentially
to the power of 2 as WTES
 V = h · π · r2
, the hypothesis is that PTES should prove
more economically attractive beyond a certain volume or capacity. The main question of
this thesis is therfore to find the break­even point at which PTES is more economical for
a given consumer load (if the hypothesis holds true), and to find a correlation between
these break­even points and the“solar fraction”, i.e., the percentage of energy that can be
supplied to the industrial consumer from each TES. To summarize this research objective:
The objective of this thesis is to find the solar fraction where one storage type is more
economical than the other, given the load profile of the end consumer.
To achieve this kind of comparison, a year­based transient model is made in MATLAB
where available heat is calculated from solar irradiance data for three different locations:
Copenhagen, Rome, and Madrid, as their yearly direct normal irradiation are different
enough to highlight interesting simulation trends. Once this irradiation is calculated to
available thermal heat, it is absorbed and delivered by a glycol­water mixture which trans­
fers the heat in either liquid­to­liquid heat exchangers (HE) for WTES, or air­to­liquid HE
for PTES. An industrial consumer with an accompanying load profile is added which con­
trols when stored heat should be discharged. The discharged heat is then collected by a
steam accumulator tank that delivers heat in the form of steam and receives it back after
the energy of the steam has been extracted. (Additional components, such as pumps,
valves, and expansion vessels are assumed but not modeled in detail beyond facilitating
adjustable flow rates, flow redirection, and rupture prevention.)
The multiple components in this simulation will allow for a high degree of freedom for
the model which will enable a detailed simulation and investigation on each variable’s
effect on the final result. However, it is not desired to make a hyper­realistic model of all
components included, as the scope and resources for this project are primarily focused on
producing a “good enough” model to extract sufficiently accurate and comparable data,
rather than to showcase a true­to­life model which would take considerably more time and
resources to develop.

Secondary research objectives
Alongside the primary research objective, other potential areas of focus are considered
for investigation. These are subject to the available time and resources after the primary
research objective is achieved, and are listed as follows:

1.2
Current research
Research on the fundamental principles of heat transfer has existed for centuries, as
studies relating to heat transfer modeling only dates back to 1929 [6], with additional
focus on heat transfer involving packed material beds surfacing in the early 1930’s in
connection with the mining industry [7]. These publications laid an important foundation,
but ultimately lacked the capability to predict variation in inlet temperatures, which weren’t
actively researched until the early 1980’s with the surge of higher computational power
[8]. From that point on, research on PTES grew rapidly and was claimed to be superior
to pressurized water tanks already by 2005 [9].
Today, TES research can largely be divided into two categories:
• Numerical studies, where a mathematical and/or digital model is constructed which
simulates TES behavior, from which conclusions are drawn.
• Experimental studies, where a real­life TES, ranging from small­scale prototype to
full­scale TES, is constructed from where data is gathered to use for conclusions.
For either category, it should be noted that the high variability of TES modeling can make it
difficult to examine and deduct the technological progress of new discoveries when com­
paring them side­by­side. In other words, different research teams might omit specific
physical phenomena to allow for in­depths examinations. Additionally, they might also
base their research in different geometries, standards, or system characteristics, thus
eliminating common­ground comparisons. Despite the difficulty in determining exact sci­
entific progress this way, valuable discoveries and trends can still be identified, but should
be applied to other systems with caution.
1.2.1
Numerical studies
Four numerical studies are selected to high­light interesting and relevant findings for the
purpose of this thesis. Andreozzi et al. [10] investigated a high­temperature PTES with
focus on the flow rate of the heat transfer fluid, as well as rock porosity. They utilized air

as the heat transfer fluid and steel as the storage medium, and concluded that the fastest
charging rate was achieved with a porosity of 0.40 and a flow rate of 0.2 kg/s.
Zanganeh et al. [11] studied the thermal performance by using a transient two­phase
heat transfer model with air as heat transfer fluid and rock as heat storage medium. By
investigating several variables such as output temperature and thermal efficiency, they
found that the PTES efficiency increases when the diameter­to­height ratio of the tank

Ortega­Fernández et al. [12] simulated general thermal behavior for a PTES with air

pressure drop and efficient heat exchange are for the packed bed material.
Elouali et al. [13] tested and compared four different numerical models to evaluate thermal
performance behavior. Their study found a strong relation between porosity, particle size,
and mass flow rate to the overall thermal performance of the PTES. Notably, smaller
particle sizes combined with lower fluid mass flow rates showed potential to increase the
efficiency for PTES systems.
1.2.2
Experimental studies
As for experimental studies, investigations on rocks suitable as TES heat media has been
conducted in Finland [14], Wisconsin [15], South Africa [16], and Morocco [17] [18]. These
studies have investigated the thermal properties such as specific heat capacity, thermal
diffusivity, thermal conductivity, and degradation of local rock types to find suitable alter­
natives. As the cost of crushed rock is usually highly dependent on transportation costs,
finding rock from local producers ensures low prices and improved cost­competitiveness.
In the majority of these investigations, suitable materials was indeed found locally, proving
that a low CAPEX cost for TES media isn’t necessarily restricted to an exclusive geograph­
ical location. Further, these studies also highlighted the importance of non­thermal rock
properties, such as density and porosity.
Not only local rock has been investigated as a TES media: concrete [19] [20], sand [21],
sodium chloride (NaCl) [22], ceramic pebbles [23], and even steel industry by­products
[24] have been researched, and have in most cases delivered satisfactory results. How­
ever, some materials such as concrete and sodium chloride show some unwanted behav­
ior in forms of cracking or corrosion, showing that although alternative, rock­like media can
be successfully used, they should be thoroughly investigated to make sure they do not
impact their systems negatively.
Besides storage medium research, lab­scale TES and PTES experiments have also con­
tributed to a better understanding of TES principles put into practice. In a study focused
on the extraction of heat and thermal stratification from Mulane and Havaldar [25], it was
found that the TES performance is affected negatively by high temperature differences in­
side the storage tank to the extent where large temperature differences should be avoided
if possible, partly due to the high destratification rate in the high temperature region.
On a similar topic, Almendros­Ibáñez et al. [26] conducted a study where they compared
fluid bed TES with PTES and found not only that PTES are indeed suitable for both high
and low temperature applications, but that increased thermal stratification play a key role
in PTES as this improves solar thermal collector efficiency.

2.1
Mind model
In the mind model, the fundamental theory, principles, and phenomena related to TES
systems and components are described, with focus on the relevance to the model and

2.1.1
System overview
The overarching purpose behind TES charged by renewable energy is to combat the in­
herit volatility in the renewable energy source. When this is successful, the zero­emission
nature of the renewable energy source makes it an objectively better heat source to tra­
ditional fossil fuel heating. Against other heating solutions, TES still offers significant
advantages due to its efficiency, operational temperature and economy:
• Against systems that convert heat to electricity, TES has an inherit advantage as
the efficiency for converting heat to electricity is relatively low at about 35­40 % [32].
Converting heat to heat however, which TES utilizes, can approach 100 % according
to the second law of thermodynamics, and is only hindered due to losses by heat
convection, conduction and radiation.
• Against non­TES electricity to heat­solutions such as battery­powered heat pumps,
TES can deliver higher temperatures up to 1 000 °C [4], as compared to heat pumps
which struggle to provide more than 200 °C [33]. However, battery packs do provide
a superior energy density compared to normal rock (a rock storage needs a ∆T of
1 700 °C to have the same energy density as a modern battery with 400 Wh/kg),
but TES is less complex and thus more realistic to implement as its storage media
is comparatively free and requires less scarce materials.
Despite their efficiency and operating temperature range, all TES must navigate heat
charging, storage, and discharging in an effective and safe manner, taking multiple phys­
ical phenomena into consideration. To do this, heat (or electricity, in which case it is
converted into heat) is gathered and transported to a storage through a number of valves
and heat exchangers, then gathered in a storage appropriately dimensioned with respect
to the quantity of heat as well as discharge characteristics. The heat is later discharged
– typically in the form of heated air, water, or steam – to the consumer on demand.
2.1.2
Solar charging
There are many methods through which solar irradiation can be converted to heat. In
the most common cases, the heat is generated when a heat transfer fluid flowing through
a solar collector is exposed to sunlight, which increases its temperature. The solar col­
lector itself can have many shapes, such as flat­plate, evacuated tube, parabolic trough,
or parabolic dish, depending on what configuration is most applicable for the scenario.
8
Techno­economic analysis of novel thermal energy storage systems for solar heating applications
==PAGE:23==
Their installation can also vary, as some solar collectors have solar tracking equipment,
rendering them capable of tracking the sun along one or sometimes two axes for optimal
heat absorption. Calculating the usable heat from solar collectors therefore depends on
both solar irradiance data, the installed solar collector model, their layout configuration
and the heat transfer fluid. Merely using a DNI value is misleading, as such an approach
excludes effects like solar collector tracking mechanisms, solar collector efficiency, and
even shadows cast by solar collectors on other collectors for low solar altitude angles
during mornings and evenings.
Additionally, solar collectors typically operate under some kind of pressure as heat transfer
fluid temperatures can reach and surpass boiling temperatures, which can cause uneven
heat transfer capabilities as the fluid shifts between a liquid and gaseous state. By raising
the pressure, the fluid instead maintains its liquid phase due to its increased saturation
temperature (further described in section 2.1.5).
Common heat transfer fluids are water, air, propylene glycol­water mixtures, and silicone,
due to their relatively good thermal properties and availability. Air and water, for example,
is universal and virtually free, with water having a higher heat capacity but also a narrower
operating temperature range. Glycol­mixture and silicone on the other hand has the ad­
vantage of a wider operating temperature range as their boiling point is higher and their
freezing point is lower. However, care should be taken with glycol­mixtures to ensure
that it is not ethylene glycol­water mixture, which is toxic. Silicone is not without potential
issues either, as it requires more energy to pump due to its high viscosity, and due to it
being prone to leakage, even through smaller holes [34].

2.1.4
Storage dynamics
A storage typically move between four phases (see also Fig. 2.4):
• Phase 1 (Empty): there is no available heat in the storage (the storage is at room
temperature). Note that a storage can be empty even though solar collectors might
generate heat, as long as the generated heat is lower than the system load and
by­passes the storage.
• Phase 2 (Charging): once the incoming heat to the system is higher than the load,
the surplus heat is accumulated in the storage.
10
Techno­economic analysis of novel thermal energy storage systems for solar heating applications
==PAGE:25==
• Phase 3 (Max capacity): once the storage temperature reaches the temperature of
the heat transfer liquid, the storage loses the ability to absorb incoming heat and is
therefore said to be at max capacity. Any heat not absorbed by the load or storage
at this point cannot be utilized by the system, and counts as wasted energy.
• Phase 4 (Discharging): when the incoming heat decreases below load levels, heat
is transferred from the storage to the load source until the storage is empty and at
phase 1. Should the load cease while the storage still contains heat, the storage
remains partly charged and is subject to heat loss to the surroundings, primarily
through conduction and convection.

2.1.5
Saturation temperature and pressure
The saturation temperature is the temperature of a liquid said to be saturated with thermal
energy, i.e., the temperature of a liquid when any addition of thermal energy results in a
phase transition from vapor to gas. This temperature changes depending on the pres­
sure surrounding the liquid, with an intuitive example being the saturation temperature of
water for atmospheric pressure, which is 100 °C. The saturation temperatures of different
pressures can easily be accessed in various steam tables.
Saturation temperature is a key principle for storage tanks that intend to contain water
(or any liquid) above its atmospheric saturation temperature, as the pressure must be
considered when sizing tank wall dimensions. It is also of importance to consider pressure
variation, as pressure varies depending on the heat and mass in­ and outflows. Valves and
seals in such tanks should be carefully investigated to protect against leaks, as leakage
causes pressure drops which lowers the saturation temperature to where the fluid rapidly
boil and expands, causing further rupture and possibly steam explosions.
A recommendation is to leave a fraction of the tanks full capacity unfilled if it will be oper­
ating close to its saturation temperature so that vapor and steam can form without risking
the structural integrity of the tank, as gas is several times more compressible than liquids.
2.1.6
Steam storage and discharge
The steam is usually stored in and discharged from a steam accumulator, which receives
a saturated water­steam mixture from the HEs and stores it under pressure as described
in section 2.1.5.
Steam is generated in the accumulator when it has reached its saturation pressure but
is still charged with additional heat from the HEs. This heat converts the saturated water
depending on the latent heat of vaporization, which decides how much energy is needed
for converting saturated water into steam mass.

2.2.1.2
Neglected phenomena
The neglected phenomena for this thesis:
• The effects of pressure are not included in this study, aside from the prerequisite of
having a high saturation temperature in the WTES tank and the steam accumulator,
and its impact on steam discharge. (This negligence is represented by expansion
valves in Fig. 2.6 and 2.7).
• The effects of thermal expansion or contraction due to changing component tem­
peratures are neglected.
• The solar collector’s operating temperature limit is neglected, enabling the collector
to operate under any temperature. (However, a PID­limit of 160 °C is set as a signal
to affect the flow rate and thus decrease temperature.)
• The heat transfer fluids are not modeled as having any heat loss while transported
in pipes between components.
• The pumps are not modeled to have any practical upper flow rate limitation.
• The heat transfer fluids are assumed to not change state from liquid to gas during
high temperatures.
• Leakage and related effects from any component (such as mass balance impacts)
are neglected.
• Fouling is neglected for all heat exchangers.
• Thermal stress is neglected for all heat exchangers.
• Heat exchangers cannot operate in reverse, i.e., should the typically hot fluid side
be colder, heat will not travel from the typically colder side to the warmer.
• Decreased storage heat capacity due to degradation from repeated charging and
discharging of the storage medium is neglected.
• The PTES thermocline is assumed to be perfectly horizontal, i.e., atypical thermo­
cline behavior in edges and corners are not considered for the simulation.
• Neither thermal conductivity, diffusion, nor thermal gradients are considered for the
PTES media. Biot numbers are therefore not utilized.
• Heat transfer between PTES particles (convection, conduction, and radiation) are
not included in this model.
• The thermocline region for both the WTES and PTES are assumed to have zero
width, meaning that the tank is perfectly stratified and real­life destratification is
thereby neglected.
• Non­linear discharging for PTES – where the flow rate has to be increased towards
the end of a discharging cycle to maintain a constant output temperature – is ne­
glected.
• The phenomena where rocks expand and re­organize causing uneven rock distri­
bution in the PTES tank is neglected.

Model characteristics
The following assessments are made regarding the appropriate model characteristics,
given the required nature of the model:

• The model will rely heavily on derived equations which operate on a basis of ad­
ditional formulas with further derived values and calculations. A large portion of
the model will therefore be based on First Principle theory. However, the model
will also require many empirical values such as UA­values for heat exchangers and
irradiation data input. As a result, the model will be a grey box model.
• The model is highly subject to time (simulated by time steps) and as such, results
from a previous time step will affect the current one. This inertial momentum indi­
cates that the model must be a dynamic model, rather than a steady state model.
• As time is highly relevant and gradual changes in the model are required for both
troubleshooting, increased insight, and verification, the model needs to be a dis­
tributed model. (Otherwise, only the starting and end values could be extractable.)
To summarize, the required model is concluded as being a distributed and dynamic model
with grey box characteristics.

heat not be requested, the water­glycol is redirected to the bottom HE without bypassing
the top HE. Later, when heat is requested again, heat is discharged from the storage
unit through a HE to the steam accumulator tank. Finally, once enough steam has been
accumulated, it is delivered to later be returned as water.

2.2.4
System components
In this section, the components needed for the systems in Fig. 2.6 and 2.7 are discussed
theoretically as well as in relation to each component’s system implementation.
2.2.4.1
Solar collectors
In order to calculate the usable heat from the solar collectors, data is imported from the
software Meteonorm for three locations:
• Copenhagen (latitude: 55.676098, average DNI: 1007 kWh/m2 [40]
• Rome (latitude: 41.902782, average DNI: 1687 kWh/m2 [40])
• Madrid (latitude: 40.416775, average DNI: 2027 kWh/m2 [40])
For each of these three locations, the Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI), Global Tilted
Irradiation (GTI), Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI), Diffuse Tilted Irradiance (DTI), beam
radiation, and temperature is gathered alongside the hour of the day and of the year.
The solar collector used in this thesis is the Absolicon SC T160 parabolic trough collector,
from which data such as η0, a1, a2, and Kb is collected. This solar collector tracks the
sun along one axis and uses propylene glycol­water mixtures up to 40 % for heat transfer
fluid [41]. It has an operating temperature limit of 160 °C, but this is largely neglected (see
section 2.2.1.2).
Solar collector field variables and data such as the number of collector rows and collector
spacing is provided by Absolicon and are listed alongside the rest of the solar collector
variables in section 2.4.1.
2.2.4.2
Heat transfer fluid
There are three different heat transfer fluids used for this thesis: propylene glycol­water
mixture (40 %), air, and water.
The propylene glycol­water mixture is applied to the solar collector loop, as this medium
has a higher boiling point and lower freezing point than water while maintaining relatively
good thermal properties. By modifying the glycol percentage, the specific heat capacity,
density, and temperature of the mixtures change. Then, by multiplying specific heat ca­
pacity with density, a curve as shown in Fig. 2.8 can be deducted, showing that from a
heat transfer perspective, lower mixture percentages improve the heat capacity because
more and more of the fluid is water. As mentioned, however, this also affects the freez­
ing and boiling temperatures to the point where a 0 % mixture would be unsuitable for
medium­heat temperature ranges. Therefore, a trade­off is needed and 40 % has been
set with this in mind, as it offers an acceptable trade­off and is also the highest glycol per­
centage allowed for the solar collector. Although the resulting boiling temperature (104
°C, [42]) is still below operating temperature, the solar collector loop is under pressure to
prevent boiling.
For the TES storage loop, water is used for the WTES and air for the PTES. Water has
the benefit of low cost and high heat capacity, while air is commonly used for PTES as
this allows for non­pressurized storage within acceptable thermal conductivity.

For the steam accumulation and delivery loop, water is used for largely the same reasons
as air, namely low cost and high heat capacity, in addition to the creation of steam as the

2.2.4.3
Heat exchangers
The HEs selected for this thesis are plate­type liquid­to­liquid HE for the WTES, and shell
and tube­type air­to­liquid HE for the PTES. The U­ and A­values for each HE have been
estimated on the basis of relevant empirical tables. An efficiency factor is also applied to
account for any static imperfections in the HE.
The modeled exchange of heat for all HEs is done simplistically by calculating the potential
energy difference and dividing it between the fluids based on their mass flow rates (see
Eq. 2.166a, 2.168a, and 2.179a for WTES). As such, this approach follows the basic
principles of a parallel flow HE, but without using explicitly related formulas.
2.2.4.4
WTES tank
The WTES tank is cylindrical and charged top­to­bottom, meaning that the hotter fluid
enters in the already hottest zone and “pushes” the cold water downwards (contributing
to stratification). The tank operates under a constant and absolute pressure of 5 bar(a) in
order to increase the boiling point of the water to 160 °C [44]. This temperature is deemed
an in­between temperature of the estimated temperature range from the solar collector
loop (160­200 °C) and the desired steam output temperature (120 °C), which is a necessity
as a low storage output temperature of 120 °C disable heat discharge, and a high output
storage temperature of 180 °C will disable any charging from the solar collector loop. As
temperature difference plays an essential role for heat transfer, an average temperature
between the solar collector loop and steam accumulator loop is deemed most suitable.
As for heat loss modeling, the WTES is modeled as being made out of three materials: a
steel hull, insulation, and an aluminium cover.
2.2.4.5
PTES tank
The PTES has the shape of an upside­down half­sphere (similar to a bowl) and follows
the same principles pertaining to top­to­bottom charging nature, temperature ranges, and
heat loss as outlined in section 2.2.4.4. However, PTES differs from WTES in a few
areas. Due to the high melting point of the rock storage medium, it can be operating
Techno­economic analysis of novel thermal energy storage systems for solar heating applications
17
==PAGE:32==
at atmospheric pressure, which greatly improves the simplicity of its construction, further
minimizing CAPEX costs. Additionally, the fraction of empty storage space to rocks, called
porosity, must be determined to model the heat transfer. Finally, the thermocline itself,
although conceptually similar to the WTES, is more reactive for higher a state of charge
(SOC) as the concave bottom of the tank causes the thermocline to rise and sink quicker
than for a WTES.
As for heat loss modeling, the PTES is modeled as being made out of a concrete shell
and insulation, with a thin layer of aluminium on top.
2.2.4.6
Steam accumulator
The desired output temperature for the steam is 120 °C which equals a constant absolute
pressure of 2 bar(a) for the steam accumulator. When the load is applied to the model,
and if there is sufficient steam available (decided by a set margin), it is discharged to the
consumer and later returned to the steam accumulator loop as cooled water, before it is
feeding into the HEs again.
A steam discharge limiter is also applied to keep the steam discharge more constant
and less volatile. The limit is set as a static value which can be adjusted and optimized
empirically.
As for heat loss modeling, the steam accumulator is modeled as being made out of three
materials: a steel hull, nsulation, and an aluminium cover.
2.2.4.7
Pumps
To represent pumps, PID­controllers are implemented to regulate the flow rates through­
out the model. As PID­controllers can be very complex due to multiple layers of regulation
(P­, I­, and D­regulation), a basic approach is taken, as mathematically described in sec­
tions 2.3.2.8 and 2.3.3.7.
2.2.4.8
Other components
There are additional components which are not coded, namely two­ and three­way vales
and expansions valves. These are not included in this model beyond being included in the
previous schematics to represent the ability of the model to implement logical operators
and prevent rupture due to over­pressure.

2.4.4
Loading profiles
Pyearload from table 2.8 is applied across five different loading profiles incorporated into
the model:
1. Minimal ­ roughly 40 hours per week for 45.6 weeks.
2. Low ­ roughly 56 hours per week for 45.6 weeks.
3. Moderate ­ roughly 80 hours per week for 45.6 weeks.
4. High ­ roughly 112 hours per week for 52 weeks.
5. Maximal ­ exactly 168 hours per week for 52 weeks.
The load profiles were scheduled so that weeks without load were put in the beginning of
the year in order to utilize the charged heat by the end of the year.
Notably, as the yearly load was spread out between the number of hours, the load profile
with the fewest hours have a higher hourly load than the load profiles with more hours.

gives a “smoother” flow rate, but the slower reaction also extend the period with higher
temperatures.

As has been established throughout this study, the WTES and PTES are similar as they
build on the same simulation principles. The behaviors of the PTES model is therefore
not too different from those of the PTES model, which is why the PTES model will not be
discussed in the same level of detail as in the previous section.
There are however a few interesting distinctions:
• The solar collector loop and PTES loop flow rates are much higher, which is due to
air being the transfer medium for the PTES, and as such has a lower heat transfer
rate due to air’s lower heat capacity, as well as the lower U­value for liquid­to­air
heat exchangers (see Fig. 2.16).
• There is a delayed spike for the solar collector loop temperature (see Fig. 2.17),
likely due to not optimized PID­parameters for the flow rate (see the impact of PID­
controllers reaction on the flow rate and temperature in the previous section). This
spike is carried over to the PTES, likely because of – again – the delayed and/or
unoptimized reaction time of the PTES PID.
• The PTES is much slower at discharging heat, leading to it being near­fully charged
throughout the year. As a result, more energy is wasted. Additionally, the heat loss
is larger for the PTES tank than for the WTES tank, which can be explanied due to
the PTES having a larger mantle area due to its geometry (compare Fig. 2.19 with
Fig. 2.13).

4
Discussion
At its most fundamental level, this thesis study set out to decide the technical feasibility
and economical attractiveness of WTES and PTES systems in order to decide which TES
would be preferable given a specified circumstance. Although the initial approach was
rooted on solar fractions being continuous – which was later proved to be incorrect –
it is still accurate to say that the mission of the thesis was not in vain, given the vast
amount of information the model was able to supply. In all, the model and the subsequent
simulations helped form a richer understanding of the techno­economic picture, which
could be argued, is more valuable. To structure this understating, this chapter is split
between areas of improvement, direct comments on the extracted results, and relevance
for the global scene.
4.1
Areas of improvement
However, due to the large number of assumptions and simplifications, the results should
not be taken as immovable facts before verified with physical tests and experimentation.
Perhaps the most challenging of these simplifications is the exclusion of pressure from the
heat transfer fluid and heat exchanger, which subsequently cause extremely and practi­
cally impossibly high mass flows which severely challenge real­life verification. For this
detail, a sister model based on H = U + PV rather than Q = Cp ˙m∆T could serve as a
good reference in order to evaluate this assumption. Alternatively, this model could also
be reworked to account for pressure.
Another aspect of this study that has great potential for improvement is the PTES itself.
Multiple phenomena related to it and its heat storage media, such as thermal conductivity,
diffusion, and thermal gradients, were excluded from the model due to time constraints but
could certainly have caused the model to produce more realistic results. Better performing
heat transfer fluids with higher heat capacity could also have been investigated, along with
more realistic thermocline behaviors – especially for PTES as its sharp upper geometry
induce convection and conduction flows unique for that storage type.
Looking at the system schematic (see Fig. 2.6 and 2.7), things could also have improved
on a more systematic level. Real­life system have more intricate networks of valves to en­
hance the performance, but additional heat exchangers can also be included to mitigate
the real­life effects of thermal stress and improve efficiency. This is a principle already il­
lustrated in the schematics of this study, as the mass flow through the first heat exchanger
is rerouted to the second heat exchanger instead of going directly back to the solar collec­
tor. With this method, the heat transfer rate limit is “by­passed” as the fluid gets a second
chance, of sorts. There’s nothing to prevent this do be done a third or fourth time expect
rising CAPEX costs, but since the heat output and efficiency can be expected to increase
from this (it forms a case of further investigation).
Speaking of costs, the constants used to calculate the CAPEX for WTES and PTES can
massively be improved. These constants were extrapolated from another set of empirical
equations, which therefore carry with it a large degree of uncertainty. A better approach
would have been to gather first­hand costs of PTES examples for a statistical approach,
or better yet, to gather information about each construction material and form a volume­
cost relationship. This would have enabled the results to project the future intersection
point between WTES LCoH and PTES LCoH as alluded to in Fig. 3.5.

These remarks being made, there are still plenty of possible areas in this model to explore,
investigate, and improve. Crafting a realistic model will in the end always fail to some
degree, as mimicking all physical phenomena present is near impossible. The purpose
of a model is rather to give an idea to prevent its user in starting from square 0, which this
model despite its possible flaw, still achieves.
4.2
Direct comments on results
Even before assembling a preliminary table with values, a digital model – like the one in
this study – must go through a rigorous testing and evaluating process to guarantee that
its modelling has been executed correctly. As modeling progresses and more and more
lifelike phenomena are included, the results simultaneously grow more complex to where
distinguishing errors becomes progressively more difficult. Given that many researchers
include and exclude their own sets of phenomena, this shows the inherit difficulty with
verifying you results against other works, as was the case with this study. The only viable
approach which remained after a while was to verify the model against itself to catch any
deviating results or unexplainable trends, which is less than ideal. Despite this suboptimal
approach, two certain trends were identified in this model from the verification:
1. The heat loss was found to be much lower than anticipated. Heat loss is often men­
tioned as a major obstacle against upholding a relevant temperature for a tank, but
the model showed a heat loss on the range of 100 000 to 1 000 000 times lower than
the heat content of the tank (see section 2.5.1). Regardless of whether this discrep­
ancy depends on an extraordinary set of circumstances or a mere miscalculation, it
is worth looking into for further studies.
2. The PID­controller has a major impact on the behavior of its system. Its inclusion
is necessary to have a dynamic response, but it’s integrated three­part nature (four
if the error criteria is included) quickly increased the complexity for the model to
where many simulations were required to find an adequate trade­off between an
over­sensitive response prone to resonance, and a laxer reaction with delayed flow
rate adjustment – leading to incremental heat increment and temperature runaway.
Constructing a script to automatically determine an ideal starting value and interval
could have been an effective solution but was disregarded as this likely would have
increased the simulation time tremendously.
Verification aside, extracting and understanding the final results for the primary research
objective proved to be an unexpected challenge due to the unexpected output as men­
tioned earlier in this chapter. Although it could theoretically be possible to achieve a solar
fraction of 1 for a TES system with small enough load, the question immediately rise
whether that storage capacity is relevant for a solar collector­powered TES, and whether
the resulting LCoS and LCoH remains cost­competitive, which is not guaranteed. The im­
pact of every single variable isn’t entirely known either, and meticulously tweaking them
one after the other to find the optimal combination for the optimal solar fraction is such a
monumental task that a certain level of solar fraction inefficiency has to be accepted.
Another perplexing solar fraction question is related to its tendency to converge for WTES
capacities of around 200­300 MWh, and 400­500 MWh for WTES, regardless of consumer
load. The trend showing larger storages possessing lower solar fractions is not entirely
intuitive either, as a larger storage filled with more heat logically would release it and in­
crease its solar fraction. Comparing Fig. 3.3 and 3.4 with 3.5 hint at the converging nature
might be related to the global minimum seen for the levelized cost, as both phenomena

takes place during roughly the same respective storage capacity interval. Notably, the de­
velopment for the WTES is much more dynamic for lower storage capacities than PTES
as well, which in addition to the previous points raised cements the solar fraction­storage
capacity relation as a intriguing area which could certainly be explored further.
As for the secondary research objectives, the original seven objectives were gradually
dropped and merged into other parts of the evaluation as the study progressed. By the
time of evaluation, three remained together with three new additions which were easily
processable to their nature. A seventh one ­ rock characteristics including density, spe­
cific heat capacity and PTES porosity – was initially investigated, but since the model
determines tank volume by storage capacity and these precise variables (see Eq. 2.135
and 2.239), changing them only affects the volume of the tank, not the mass which would
used for solar fractions and levelized cost­related calculations later. Although this ap­
proach enables an accurate comparison between WTES and PTES, the exclusion of the
proprieties was arguably one of the largest oversights of this study.
For the six secondary research objectives that were analyzed, a number of interesting
trends and phenomena were detected:
• As for the numerical range of investigation – larger sensitivity ranges for small values
would have been advisable to visualize more distinguished impacts, and a higher
resolution could have led to a greater understanding, especially for the load profile
development (see Fig. 3.9 and 3.10), as well as the heat exchanger U­value de­
velopment (see Fig. 3.15). It would be of extra interest for the U­value sensitivity
analysis in particular, as its curves go from near­linear to taking a sharp turn at some
point before reaching the U­value of heat exchanger 1, hinting that there might be
a limiting factor in the intermediate range for heat exchanger 2 and 3 as the initial
levelized cost curves showed no signs of convergence.
• More locations should be added for the latitude sensitivity analysis (see section
3.2.1), as the results from the three investigated location are not decisive enough to
form a consistent trend across the DNI range. This could either be because of an
error in calculating the solar collector heat (even though such an error would affect
all three locations and thus not been a cause for the distinguishable, case­by­case
deviations seen in the results), or extremely volatile weather. This latter reason
echoes the theory outlined in the mind model in section 2.1.4, but again, more loca­
tion data would be needed to specify either reason. Should the latter reason be the
case, however, it would show that locations are secondary to coincidence in terms
of variables deciding the quantity of extractable heat from solar collector fields.
• The last, odd behavior for this chapter is the increasing levelized cost and decreas­
ing solar fraction for larger solar collector fields, as shown in Fig. 3.11 and 3.12.
A reason for this could be that the larger heat influx charges up the TES faster,
which leads to less energy being transferred in heat exchanger 2, thus raising the
temperature of the heat transfer fluid in the solar collector loop, which transfer more
energy direct from the solar collector field to the steam accumulator tank via heat
exchanger 1. This could explain the LCoS and LCoH figures (and the more rapid
rate of development for the TPES), but not directly the solar fraction trend. It’s a
curios phenomenom and perhaps the most counter­intuitive of them all ­ heat usu­
ally don’t decrease when more solar collector fields are added. Whether a realistic
phenomenom or a modeling error – it does go to show the high level of complexity,
manifesting itself in system interdependence and obscure heat transfer behaviors.

4.3
Global relevance
When considering this study in a larger, more global perspectives, both problems and
possibilities make themselves known pretty quickly. Since many other TES studies fo­
cuses on just the TES storage to investigate round­trip efficiencies, having a predefined,
larger system with solar collectors, accumulator tanks and heat exchangers strengthens
the avenue of research where TES units are seen and investigated from a more holistic
perspective. If TES is supposed to become a real­life solution, then more research in this
direction needs to be done, which is what Gautam and Saini encouraged in their 2020
study [27]. Studies like this can therefore hurry up the TES research focus on practical
application, as few real­life scenarios will be satisfied by a TES storage alone, regardless
of efficiency.
To pick one area in particular, heat exchangers are not common to discuss in connection
with TES storages but could benefit immensely from more research. As the TES heat is
usually for short to medium periods of time and straight from storage to consumer, there
really is not a great demand for heat exchanger, but with the increase climate ambitions, it
could absolutely be an are which attracts more attention, which would be highly beneficial
for heat transfer fluid that are not ideal to their poor heat exchanger equivalents,
As for the more problematic side – this study suffers from the same issues as does many
other similar studies, namely those of a very particular set of phenomena, assumptions,
and circumstances which complicates comparison to other studies. Given the neglect
of pressure and simplistic approach, this is perhaps extra true of this study. However,
a possible mitigation against this verdict is found in the secondary research objectives.
Since these simulations deviate slightly from the more holistic and unique system view
and focuses on thermal heat transfer on a more atomistic level, their related results could
be easier to test in other experiments to either confirm or invalidate its discoveries.
To conclude – it is hard to know in which direction the future goes, but if this study has
shown anything, it has shown that interesting and unintuitive trends can be found even in
relatively simple models. As such, there are likely many correlations yet to discover both
theoretically and practically within the realms of TES heat transfer, and with the rise of
AI, inflation, global warming, and more ambitious climate plans, new discoveries could be
made within heat transfer design which might improve solar fraction limits and levelized
costs beyond what might unthinkable today. Regardless, the ongoing wealth of research
does show that this is a field of research that is poised to continue, and it has been an
exciting topic to be a part of.

Conclusion
This study performed a techno­economic analysis by examining the relationship of solar
fractions, levelized cost of storage (LCoS), levelized cost of heating (LCoH), and thermal
energy storage (TES) capacity for two heat storage technologies: pressurized water tank
energy storages (WTES) and packed rock­bed energy storages (PTES). This was done
by modeling a simplistic solar collector plant in MATLAB, including solar collectors, heat
exchangers, PID­controllers for pumps, the TES tanks, and a steam accumulator.
The goal was to decide the break­even point where PTES were more economically viable
for a given solar fraction than WTES dues to its geometry and low CAPEX cost. How­
ever, the initial results were not conclusive enough to decide this matter. Instead, the
approach shifted to examining the general relationship between said variables but based
on the peak storage capacity instead. This approach provided enough results to form
conclusions about the relations between the variables as originally desired, which are
summarized in these three takeaways:
1. The results speak in favor for WTES for moderate capacities between 10 to 5 000
MWh, after which the LCoH of PTES surpass the WTES. The LCoS comparison fa­
vors WTES for a longer interval, which is not determined but estimated through trend
projections of around 100 000 MWh. Both TES however show a global minimum at
around 200­500 MWh of storage capacity.
2. Achieving a high solar fraction depends more on end consumer load than TES ca­
pacity, as lower load increases the share of renewable energy that the system. In
accordance with the LCoS and LCoH advantage of the WTES, the WTES reaches
higher solar fraction that the PTES. Notably, the solar fractions converge to a set
value after around 500 MWh of storage capacity.
3. The CAPEX costs for PTES are far lower than for WTES for higher storage ca­
pacities (>1 000 MWh), showing that the economic advantage of WTES is strictly
connected to its heat delivery. In other words, the economic disadvantage of PTES
is due to its lack of heat delivery, which is partly because its lower heat exchanger
(air­to­liquid as compared to liquid­to­liquid) transfer rate and specific heat capacity
of its heat transfer medium.
Additionally, specific variables with various degrees of impact on the results were made
subject to sensitivity analyses. From these secondary investigations, some of the trends
found was:
• A location’s average yearly DNI (mostly based on latitude) is not conclusive enough
to establish a trend in extractable solar collector heat.
• The timing and frequency of consumer load cycles can severely impact the solar
fraction of either TES system.
• Increased solar collector area increases LCoS and LCoH and results in a lower solar
fraction.
Given this study’s approach to model a simplified schematic, several related phenom­
ena has been neglected, proposing some components for further modeling and analysis.
Chief among these is the PTES tank itself, as it did not include inside thermal gradient,

thermal conductivity, or any irregularities in its thermocline. The heat exchangers were
also modeled like simplified parallel­flow heat exchangers, and the cost figure for either
TES tank were largely composed of empirically derived constants.
Despite these imperfections and others like it, due to the comparative nature of the model,
it model still serves well as a tool to compare the different TES, as any minor incorrect
assumption is included for both comparisons and will not therefore cause one TES to
appear favored before the other. However, rigorous testing of the model has taken place to
ensure it can model the relevant heat flows as accurately as possible given the resources
for this study.
