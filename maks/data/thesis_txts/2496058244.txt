Abstract (EN)
The majority of today’s literature regarding period elongation, deﬁnes the period
elongation as the change from initial elastic period to the predominant period
of the response signal of the structure when subjected to a ground motion.
This study mainly focuses on determining the time-history period evolution of
a SDOF system subjected to a seismic ground motion. To achieve this, a robust
method was established that allows to extract and process the full general system
matrix from OpenSEES for each time-step during a nonlinear transient analysis.
Following three simple steps in this method, the full general system matrix can
be separated into a time-history evolution of stiﬀness, mass and damping matrix.
This method is carefully validated by comparison of analytical results.
An extensive investigation of a broad variety of SDOF systems with modiﬁed
Ibarra-Medina-Krawinkler deterioration model with peak-oriented hysteretic re-
sponse is the main work of this study. The investigation includes SDOF models
with 11 diﬀerent initial elastic periods, each with three diﬀerent yield strengths
and 3 diﬀerent deterioration levels, including both stiﬀness and strength deterio-
ration. Each model combination are subjected to 100 individual ground motions
to determine the period evolution and residual reloading and unloading periods
with use of the established method. A total of 9900 analysis indicates that resid-
ual reloading period elongation ratios (TResRe/Tel) are greatest for structural
systems with the lowest initial elastic period (Tel = 0.2s), and decreases from
stiﬀto moderate initial elastic period systems (Tel = 0.3 −1.0s), and obtains
a constant and lower elongation for ﬂexible systems with long initial elastic pe-
riod (Tel = 1.0 −3.0s). The average residual unloading period elongation ratios
(TResUn/Tel) are found to be signiﬁcantly smaller than the residual reloading
period elongation. The average unloading period elongation is between 2.5-20%
of the corresponding reloading period elongation, and is depending on the initial
elastic period. The hysteretic deterioration capacity has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence
on the period elongation. Moreover, the force reduction factor, which controls
the yield strength of the system, has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the period elon-
gation. Also ground motion characteristics such as mean period and signiﬁcant
==PAGE:4==
ii
duration has a minor but signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the period elongation.
Furthermore the period elongation during seismic sequences (multiple ground
motions) are investigated. Two examples of seismic sequences were selected, one
from the Coalinga 1989 earthquakes and one from the Chi-Chi 1999 earthquakes.
The period evolution during a seismic sequence is found to be dependent on the
intensity of the main-shock vs. the aftershocks. A preliminary investigation
of a 6 degree-of-freedom reinforced concrete frame shows a clear change in the
mode shapes when the system is deteriorated under a seismic excitation. Mode-
shape 1 and 6 remained almost unchanged, while mode 2-5 showed signiﬁcant
changes. All periods for the 6 modes elongated but the period elongation ratio
were largest for the mode with longest elastic period and smallest for the mode
with the shortest elastic period.

Abstract (DK)
Størstedelen af nutidens literatur vedrørende periodeforlængelse er deﬁneret som
forlængelsen af fra den elastiske begyndelsesperiode til den overvejende periode
af kontruktionsresponset når den udsættes for jordrystelser iform af et jords-
kælv. Dette studie fokuserer hovedsagligt på at bestemme tidsevolutionen af
perioden af en model med én frihedsgrad udsat for jordskælv. For at opnå dette
er der udviklet en robust metode der gør der muligt at eksportere og evaluere
den fulde generelle system matrise fra OpenSEES for erhvert tidsskidt igennem
en ikke-lineære dynamisk analyse. Følger man tre simple procedure beskrevet
af denne metode, kan man fra den fulde generelle system matrise bestemme
tids-evolutionen af stivheds-, dæmpning- og stihedsmatrisen. Denne metode er
veriﬁceret ved sammenligning af analytiske resultater.
En stor undersøgelse af bred samling af én-frihedsgrads modeller med den modi-
ﬁcerede Ibarra-Medina-Krawinkler nedbrdnings model med top-orienteret hyste-
reserespons er hovedproduktet af dette studie. Undersøgelsen inkluderer én-
frihedsgrad’s modeller med 11 forskellige elastiske begyndelsesperioder, hver
med tre forskellige ﬂydestyrker og tre forskellige nedrydnings niveauer, indehol-
dende både styrke- og stivhedsnedbrydning. Enhver af disse modeller er udsat for
100 forskellige individuelle jordskælv for at bestemme periode tids-evolutionerne
og de residuale gen- og aﬂastnings perioder ved hjælp af den beskrevne metode.
Et totalt antal af 9900 analyser indikerer at residual genlastnings periodefor-
længelsesforholdet (TResRe/Tel) er størst for systemer med den mindste elasti-
ske begyndelsesperiode (Tel = 0.2s), og faldende fra stive til moderate systemer
med elastiske begyndelsesperiode (Tel = 0.3 −1.0s), og har en konstant og
mindre forlængelse for ﬂeksible systemer med lang elastisk begyndelsesperiode
(Tel = 1.0 −3.0s). Den gennemsnitlige residuale aﬂastnings periodeforlængel-
sen (TResUn/Tel) er signiﬁkant mindre end genlastnings periodeforlængelsen.
Den gennemsnitlige residuale aﬂastnings periodeforlængelse er mellem 2.5-20%
af den korresponderende genlastnings periodeforlængelse, og er afhængig af sy-
stemets elastiske begyndelsesperiode. Hysterese-nedbrydnings-kapaciteten har
en signiﬁkant eﬀekt på periodeforlænglesen. Endvidere har kraft-reducerings-
==PAGE:6==
iv
fakotren (R), som kontrollerer ﬂydestyrken af systemet, også en signiﬁkant eﬀekt
på periodeforlængelsen. Også jordskævskarakteristika som gennemsnitsperioden
og den signiﬁkante varrighed har mindre men stadig signiﬁkant betydning for
periodeforlængelsen.
Endvidere er periodeforlængelsen under jordskælvssekvenser (et muliplum af
jordskælv) undersøgt. To eksempler på jordskælvssekvenser er samlet, Coalinga
1989 og Chi-Chi 1999 jordskælvene. Periode forlængelsen under et jordskælvs-
sekvens er afhængig af intensiteten af hoved-skælvet sammenlignet med efter-
skælvene. En forundersøgelse af en rammekontruktion med 6 frihedsgrader viser
en klar ændring af sviningsformer når systemet nedbrydes under ikke-lineære
respons. Svingningsform 1 og 6 viste mindre forandringer imens svningsform
2-5 viste signiﬁkante forandringer. Alle perioder for de 6 frihedsgrader viste en
forlængelse, men periodeforlængelsesforholdet var størst for perioden tilhørende
frihedsgraden med den længste periode, og omendt mindst for perioden tilhø-
rende frihedsgraden med den korteste periode.

Preface
This Master Thesis was prepared at DTU, department of Civil Engineering in
fulﬁlment of the requirements for acquiring a Masters degree in Engineering
(cand.polyt.).
My personal motivation for this study started during my exchange in the fall
2016 where I studied at University of British Columbia (UBC) in Vancouver,
Canada. At UBC I followed three structural dynamic courses. As a result of
a subduction fault lying just oﬀthe coast of Vancouver, the seismic design was
a great part of all the structural dynamic courses at UBC. The seismic aspect
and inﬂuence on structural dynamics caught my interest.
Lyngby, 26 January-2020
Victor Ambo Boserup

Chapter 1
Introduction
Structures and civil infrastructures, being located in earthquake prone areas, are
frequently subjected to earthquake-induced strong ground motions that may de-
teriorate the performance of the structures and degrade their integrity. Indeed,
the earthquake excitations may lead the structural system to perform in the
nonlinear regime, the latter being usually associated with structural damages.
It is, thus, of high importance to assess accurately the nonlinear behaviour of
structures that will allow quantifying their damage potential due to the earth-
quake motions. Along these lines, the current MSc thesis project focuses on
using advanced numerical analysis methods, supported by the robust theoretical
background of structural dynamics, in order to assess with increased accuracy
the nonlinear performance of simpliﬁed structural systems. Additionally, this
MSc project will pursue tracking of the changes occurring in structural and dy-
namic properties of the structures during their excitations from ground motions.
Especially the period elongation due to deterioration of strength and stiﬀness is
of interest for to this study.
Literature on the subject of period elongation often quantiﬁes the period elon-
gation as the elongation from the elastic period (presumed initial state) to the
predominant period of the structural response subjected to a given seismic ex-
citation. This deﬁnition of the period elongation (based on the predominant
period) is a good indicator of how the response of the structure is shifted in the
frequency domain as the stiﬀness deteriorates. However the frequency content
of the load will inevitably inﬂuence the predominant period that is based on
the recorded response of the structure. This method is the only option when
investigating the period of existing buildings, where the recorded response is
the only available input for an analysis of the dynamic characteristics. To pre-
vent the inﬂuence of the frequency content of the ground motion to inﬂuence

the predominant period calculated by the response, the response needs to be
uncoupled with the input ground motion. Also the predominant period, or the
response spectra from a recorded acceleration response of the system, does not
inform of the evolution of the period, nor the residual inelastic period.
When investigating computer-modelled structures, and thereby simulating the
structural response, there is the possibility of tracking the actual instantaneous
stiﬀness and period of the structural system during the excitation. The modern
ﬁnite-element (FE) software Open System for Earthquake Engineering Simu-
lation (OpsenSEES) allows the user to extract the full general system matrix
also known as generalised stiﬀness matrix of the system. This matrix includes
information of the stiﬀness, mass and damping of each element of the system
for each time-step, and thereby the evolution of these aforementioned structural
parameters.
1.1
Objectives
The objective of this study is to track the evolution of the period during the
response of the system when subjected to strong ground motion accelerations.
By establishing a robust method of extracting and processing the full general
system matrix from OpenSEES to track the time-history change of the stiﬀness
matrix of the structural system it is possible to determine the period evolution
of the system. When the period at each time step are determined, the residual
period can be evaluated.
This study focus on the residual elongated period. The residual period is es-
pecially of interest since this indicates the total period elongation during the
non-linear response of the system. It is important to mention that if the period
is derived at each time step, only considering the stiﬀness and mass matrix, this
period would not consider the hysteretic damping. At the end of the response
the hysteretic damping is insigniﬁcant since the response is very small, and thus
can be neglected when when determining the residual period.
Furthermore it is of interest to investigate the inﬂuence of diﬀerent structural
properties and ground motion characteristics on the period elongation, i.e. force
reduction factor, material deterioration level, mean period, moment magnitude,
signiﬁcant duration, epicentral distance and soil class of ground motions.
In this study the focus will be on single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) systems
subjected to individual ground motions, however a preliminary study of SDOF
systems subjected to seismic sequence will investigate the inﬂuence of accumu-

Furthermore it is of interest to verify the developed method of extracting and

1.2
Literature Review
Period elongation of a structural system during seismic excitations is a result
of damage of the structural system, i.e. strength and stiﬀness degradation, and
increased damping from non-linear behaviour (hysteretic damping) [11], [8], [7].
When a structure is damaged, e.g. yielding of reinforcing steel bars or soften-
ing of concrete, the period elongates during the ground motion. However the
inelastic period can fully or partly be recovered during and after the end of the
excitation [11]. This is explained by concrete cracks closing and consolidating
of surrounding soil, regaining stiﬀness of the global system.
A ductile structural system dissipates energy from the response. The dissipated
energy can be quantiﬁed though the hysteric cycles in the response. The ductil-
ity can therefore be considered as equivalent damping (hysteretic damping). The
eﬀect of ductility on the response is similar to the eﬀect of damping; elongation
of the response period and reduction in the vibration amplitude [11].
In modern building codes (e.g. EC8-part 1 [6] and ASCE/SEI 7-10 [37]) the
period range of interest, hence where period elongation is expected, is between
0.2Tel −2.0Tel for EC8 and 0.2Tel −1.5Tel for ACSE (where Tel is the elastic
fundamental period of vibration). This range of periods described in the codes
is used for the spectra-compatibility criteria when choosing accelerograms for
the time-history analysis. The upper bound of these ranges is determined from
the elongated fundamental period and also accounts for the uncertainties in
predicting elastic fundamental period [8].
There are diﬀerent methods used in the current literature to determine the pe-
riod elongation under a ground motion excitation. The most common method
is based on the predominant inelastic period from acceleration response analy-
sis (analysis in the frequency domain, i.e. Fourier analysis) [11], [27], [8], [30].
Other methods to determine the period elongation exists in the literature: i)
Secant stiﬀness corresponding to the maximum displacement response of the
structure, i.e. the slope of the curve drawn to the maximum displacement point
on the capacity curve transformed to acceleration-displacement response spec-
trum format [26]. ii) predominant period from the post-excitation free vibration
denoted as the post-excitation period [30].

The study of E.I. Katsanos & A. G. Sextos (2015) [27] investigates SDOF sys-

The study of L.

System for Earthquake Engineering Simulation (OpenSEES) using a tri-linear
backbone curve with pinching, stiﬀness and strength degradation. Both [27]
and [8] investigates a wide range of structures with diﬀerent elastic fundamental
periods ranging between 0.1s - 3.0s.
During 6 foreshocks, the mainshock and 7 aftershocks from the Pollino seismic
swarm, the period elongation was monitored of a 3 storey reinforced concrete
building and reported by Gallipoli et al. (2016) [13]. An important observation
in this study was that the fundamental period measured in the foreshocks was
restored in the ﬁrst aftershock, just 3 hours following the mainshock. Hence
no permanent period elongation was detected, this matches the on-site dam-
age inspection, there were no visible damage indications of the building. The
elongation of the fundamental period is based on the shift of the predominant
period from the acceleration response spectra for the foreshock and mainshock.
The paper does not discuss the explanation for the restoring period.

The eﬀect of period elongation, and change of dynamic characteristics is related
to the discussion of seismic sequences. A great majority of studies generally
agrees that seismic sequences (multiple earthquakes in a sequence) has an de-

inter-story drift, ductility demand etc. ([19], [20], [16], [42], [41], [12], [22], [21],
[23], [43], [10], [1], [14], [2], [15], [9], [39], [33], [18], [21], [22]). While only a
single paper concludes that the eﬀect of multiple earthquakes does not inﬂuence
the peak ductility demand ratios signiﬁcantly [17].
Hatzivassiliou & Hatzigeorgiou (2015) [22], a study regarding structual damage,
maximum displacements, permanent displacements and inter-storey drift ratios

the displacements occurring in the top of the structures for the seismic sequences
are higher than those for the corresponding single seismic events. This clearly
implicates that the eﬀect of multiple earthquake have an degrading eﬀect on
the structural system in terms of damage such as stiﬀness and strength degra-
dation. These studies regarding seismic sequences does not directly mention
period elongation, but only indirectly in terms of stiﬀness degradation.

Software for Numerical
Analysis
To carry out nonlinear time-history analysis of ﬁnite element (FE) models sub-
jected to seismic excitations the use of a robust software for numerical analysis
is paramount.
Open System for Earthquake Engineering Simulation (OpenSEES) is a open
source software framework for developing applications to simulate the perfor-
mance of structural and geotechnical systems subjected to earthquakes. The
framework is written in C++ and can be used by creating applications (scripts)
in the Tcl programming language and running these applications in OpenSEES.
In this chapter a set of essential OpenSEES commands are described.
The
described commands are especially important to this study, and includes how
OpenSEES maps the degrees-of-freedom of the model and how the current full
general system matrix is extracted during the transient analysis. Furthermore
this chapter includes a brief description of material objects available in the
OpenSEES framework that are used in this study.
3.1
Mapping of Degrees-of-Freedom
The numberer command deﬁnes the mapping between equations and degrees-
of-freedom [32]. The ’numberer Plain’ command lists the degrees-of-freedom
(DOF) and equations in the system ﬁrst after nodes then DOF’s at that node.

3.2
Full General System
The system command is used to construct a full general linear system of equation
object [32]. The ’system fullGeneral’ system setting requires more memory than
every other solver and takes more time in the actual solving operation than any
other solvers. However it is required if the user is interested in looking at the
full general system matrix i.e. the generalised stiﬀness matrix of the system.
3.3
Extract Full General System Matrix
The printA command prints the current full general system matrix i.e. the gen-
eralised stiﬀness matrix of the system [32]. In order to use this command, the
system must be fullGeneral. The command must be called after an analysis has
been executed. Depending on the model, the output is either a transformed
stiﬀness matrix or some transformed combination of stiﬀness-, damping- and
mass matrix.
The transformation depends on the constraints and the con-
straint handler. If the use of a ’plain’ constraint handler, the matrix will not be
transformed. However the plain constraint handler will only be able to can only
enforce homogeneous single point constraints (ﬁx command) and multi-point
constraints constructed where the constraint matrix is equal to the identity
(equalDOF command) [32].

With the use of the Finite Element software OpenSEES, the evolution of the
inelastic dynamic characteristics, such as inelastic natural frequency and in-
elastic modeshapes, in which a structure experiences during a strong ground
motion, can be determined. When using the ’fullGeneral’ system setting in the
OpenSEES model, the user is allowed to extract the full general system matrix
directly from the Newmark integration method with the function ’printA’. See
Chapter 3 for more information of OpenSEES functions.
For a static analysis the function ’printA’ will directly return the stiﬀness matrix
of the system, since the mass and damping are not present in the system of
equations.
For a transient analysis the ’printA’ function will return the full general system
matrix, consisting of both stiﬀness, damping and mass matrix, see Equation 2.7,
here revisited:

analysis, since the system have no velocity nor acceleration.

excited both by a simple cyclic sine curve and the El-Centro strong ground

for the general system matrix and that the method proves accurate values for the
stiﬀness of the system for each time step in a the non-linear transient analysis
with damping.

Chapter 6
Analysis Framework
It is the purpose of this study to investigate the trend of residual period elon-
gation of single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) systems with varying initial elastic
periods, strengths and deterioration levels. This chapter describes the analysis
framework for this investigation, here within details about diﬀerent model and
ground motions.
The framework consists of 9900 analysis with diﬀerent models and ground mo-
tions, the variations are listed here:
• 11 models with diﬀerent initial elastic period of vibration, varying from
Tel = 0.2 - 3.0.
• Three diﬀerent force reduction factors (R), that determines the strength
of the system and the level of non-linearity in the response.
• Three diﬀerent hysteretic deterioration models, low, high and very high
deterioration levels. Representing reinforced concrete structures that have
diﬀerent levels of deterioration to hysteretic (cyclic) loads.
• One hundred strong ground motions.
The SDOF system adopted for the analysis framework represents the inelas-
tic behaviour of a yield zone in a reinforced concrete member or connection.
Both an inelastic rotational spring (see mechanical model in Figure 6.1a), or
a simple translational spring model (Figure 6.1b) using the modiﬁed Ibarra-
Medina-Krawinkler deterioration model would achieve this. Both models would

6.2
Material Model with Hysteretic Deteriorat-
ing
The modiﬁed Ibarra-Medina-Krawinkler deterioration model with peak-oriented
hysteretic response is adopted to simulate both strength and stiﬀness deterio-
ration ([28], [24]). This advanced hysteric model includes both deterioration of
stiﬀness and strength. It have been proved to accurately model the inelastic
behaviour reinforced concrete beam-columns [28]. Read more on the modiﬁed
Ibarra-Medina-Krawinkler deterioration model with peak-oriented hysteretic re-
sponse in Section 3.4.2.
A database consisting of ﬂexual load tests of 200 RC specimens, is background
for a extensive study to calibrate the cyclic moment-rotation relationships (or
more generally force-deformation) of plastic hinge regions [28]. For this analysis
framework, the calibrated model parameters from Lignos and Krawinkler (2008)
[28] are adopted as basis for the SDOF model to reﬂect a realistic structural
deteriorating behaviour of reinforced concrete.

7.3
Residual Period Elongation
In this section the reloading and unloading residual periods for the 9900 anal-
ysis are reduced into average residual periods by averaging the values for the
100 ground motions for each combination of force reduction factors (R-factor),
deterioration level and elastic initial period. Remember the residual reloading
and unloading period was deﬁned in Chapter 7.2. Inﬂuences of diﬀerent model
and ground motion characteristics are investigated, namely the inﬂuences of:
1) Force reduction factors
2) Deterioration levels
3) Mean period of ground motion
4) Moment magnitude of ground motion
5) Signiﬁcant duration of ground motion
6) Epicentral distance of ground motion
7) Soil conditions
The results are presented in plots with the variation of the 11 diﬀerent initial
elastic period systems on the x-axis and the residual period elongation ratio
on the y-axis.
The residual period elongation ratio is the residual inelastic
period divided by the corresponding initial elastic period (TRes/Tel). Both the
reloading and unloading residual period elongation ratio are presented as results
of this study. By presenting both the reloading and unloading residual periods,
regardless of the fact that the reloading period elongation is most dominating,
ensures the full picture of the systems period elongation.
Along with the average residual periods of the 100 diﬀerent ground motions, the
standard deviations are computed to present the deviation of the data points.
The standard deviations (STD) is plotted in a similar matter as the residual
period elongation ratios. It should be emphasised that the results presented
in this study are valid for the chosen backbone curve and hysteric deteriora-
tion parameters of the analysis framework. The material model parameters are
chosen in order to represent the real ﬂexual behaviour of reinforced concrete
structures, which is why 200 RC ﬂexual experimental tests from Lignos (2008)
[28] are background from the choice of parameters.

In the previous analysis of the SDOF system the diﬀerent models were sub-

with the initial elastic stiﬀness (no damage) when subjected to the ground mo-

ﬁrst seismic ground motion excitation. Creep, temperature and shrinkage de-
formation during construction and serviceability state loads will all contribute
to cracking of the concrete, this will compromise the initial uncracked elastic
stiﬀness and damage the structure.
It is therefore interesting to discuss the
accumulation of damage, or more speciﬁc the history of stiﬀness deterioration.

main-shock followed by two aftershock ground motions in a sequence.

for the western United States) database. The selected seismic sequences are:
i) Coalinga 1989 earthquakes and ii) Chi-Chi 1999 earthquakes. The ground
motions in each sequence are recorded at the same recording station and in the

Chapter 10
Conclusions
The main work of this study is the investigation of period evolutions during the
inelastic response of SDOF systems subjected to strong ground motion accel-
erations. With the establishment of a robust method to extract and process
the full general system matrix from OpenSEES it is possible to determine the
period evolution of the system. From period evolution of the system the residual
period elongation can be determined.
An extensive analysis was carried out involving 11 diﬀerent initial elastic period
SDOF systems, each with 3 diﬀerent force reduction factors and 3 deterioration
level (99 diﬀerent SDOF systems). The modiﬁed Ibarra-Medina-Krawinkler ma-
terial model with peak-oriented hysteretic response was adopted to model the
3 diﬀerent hysteretic deterioration levels. The hysteretic deterioration param-
eters, deﬁning the deterioration capacity of the systems, were carefully chosen
in accordance with Lignos (2008) [28] to represent the true ﬂexual behaviour of
reinforced concrete. Each SDOF were subjected to 100 selected strong ground
motions and the residual reloading and unloading period elongations were de-
termined.
The conclusions from this extensive investigation residual period
elongation of SDOF systems are the following:
i) The average residual reloading period elongation ratio (TResRe/Tel) is
greatest for very stiﬀstructures with a the shortest initial elastic period
(Tel = 0.2s). The period elongation ratio decreases from stiﬀto moderate
initial elastic period systems (Tel = 0.3 −1.0s), and obtains a constant
and lower elongation for ﬂexible systems with long initial elastic period
(Tel = 1.0 −3.0s). Thus the residual reloading period elongation ratio is
sensitive to the elastic period for short to moderate elastic period systems
==PAGE:132==
106
Conclusions
(Tel = 0.2 −1.0s), but is unaﬀected for ﬂexible systems with long initial
elastic period (Tel = 1.0 −3.0s).
ii) The average residual unloading period elongation ratio (TResUn/Tel) is
signiﬁcantly smaller than the residual reloading period elongation. The
unloading period elongation is between 2.5-20% of the reloading period
elongation, and is depending on the initial elastic period.
iii) The strength and hysteretic deterioration level of the structure both have
signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the residual period elongation. The force reduc-
tion factor (strength of the system) has a strong inﬂuence on the residual
reloading and unloading period elongation, weaker systems (all variations
of initial elastic period) cause greater period elongation. The hysteretic
deterioration level has a very strong inﬂuence on the residual reloading
and unloading period elongation, systems (all variations of initial elastic
period) with higher deterioration level (less hysteretic deterioration capac-
ity) leads to greater period elongation.
iv) Ground motion characteristics, in particular: mean period, moment mag-
nitude and signiﬁcant duration all inﬂuences the residual reloading and
unloading period elongation. A longer mean period in general leads to
a greater residual period elongation.
Especially the low and moderate
elastic period systems (Tel = 0.2 −1.0s) are strongly inﬂuenced by the
mean period. A greater moment magnitude generally results in a greater
residual period elongation. Similarly a longer signiﬁcant duration results
in a greater residual period elongation.
v) Both the epicentral distance and soil category has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence
on the residual reloading and unloading period elongation ratio, how-
ever these characteristics are directly related with the mean period of
the ground motion: Far-ﬁeld ground motions generally have lower fre-
quency content, whereas near-ﬁeld ground motions have high frequency
content.
Similarly dense soil (soil class C) eﬃciently transmit shaking
with high-frequency (short-period) energy content but tend to attenuate
low-frequency content (long-period) energy content. Less dense soil (soil
class D) transmit high-frequency motion less eﬃciently but tend to amplify
the low-frequency energy content.
Secondly it was investigated how the period elongates during a seismic sequence
(a sequence of ground motions). Two examples of seismic sequences were se-
lected, one from the Coalinga 1989 earthquakes and one from the Chi-Chi 1999
earthquakes. Moreover a brief investigation of the change in modeshapes was
carried out. The modeshapes were tracked for each time step in the transient
response of a 6 degree-of-freedom 2D frame subjected to a seismic excitation.
==PAGE:133==
107
The ﬁndings from the study of seismic sequence and MDOF systems are the
following:
vi) The eﬀect of multiple ground motions (seismic sequence) proved to have a
inﬂuence on the evolution of the reloading period of the SDOF system. The
residual reloading period after the main-shock would further elongate after
the two aftershocks for both the Coalinga and Chi-Chi seismic sequence.
However the evolution of period was quite diﬀerent for the two seismic
sequences: The greatest part of the ﬁnal period elongation of the Coalinga
seismic sequence happened during the main-shock, whereas the period
elongation was greatest during the ﬁrst aftershock of the Chi-Chi seismic
sequence.
vii) There is a clear change in the mode shapes when the system is deterio-
rated under a seismic excitation. For the 6-DOF 2D reinforced concrete
investigated, mode 1 and 6 remained almost unchanged, while mode 2-5
showed signiﬁcant changes. All periods for the 6 modes elongated but
the period elongation ratio were largest for the mode with longest elastic
period and smallest for the mode with the shortest elastic period.

==PAGE:135==
Chapter 11
Further Studies
This chapter discuss branches of possible further investigations on the broad
subject of deterioration of structural systems subjected to seismic excitations
with use the developed method. The developed method of extracting the current
stiﬀness-, damping- and mass matrix for each time-step during a non-linear
transient analysis makes it possible to monitor the structural health of any
SDOF or MDOF system during a single or a sequence of ground motions (seismic
sequences). A list of further studies is proposed:
i) Investigation of the change in modeshapes for MDOF systems. An exam-
ple of this is illustrated in this project to demonstrate the possibilities of
the developed method.
ii) Investigation of the period elongation of a detailed MDOF system model.
For this investigation the computational eﬀort necessary would be much
greater. The NxN full general system matrix for a large detailed model
would result in great cost in computational time when saved and post-
processed, but the method is universal and works for any size model size.
For this to be successful it is of great importance that the DOF numbering
can be tracked to the output full general system matrix by OpenSEES.
iii) More elaborate investigations into the period elongation of single- and
multi-degree-of-freedom systems subjected to seismic sequences, using the
developed method. This could uncover more details of the impact of seis-
mic sequences.
iv) Investigation of the asymmetric stiﬀness deterioration of structural sys-
tems. For SDOF systems subjected to ground motions, the residual reload-
==PAGE:136==
110
Further Studies
ing period (reloading stiﬀness) is diﬀerent depending on the loading di-
rection (positive/negative). Investigations of asymmetric vs. symmetric
stiﬀness deteriorated structure, and the eﬀect on the response and period
elongation could uncover novel results.
v) A more detailed study on the evolution of the stiﬀness (period) of the
structure though-out the response. There might be trends and general
behaviour of the stiﬀness deterioration during the response. However the
established method evaluating the period evolution based on the extracted
stiﬀness matrix at a given time-step, does not include the hysteretic damp-
ing.
