Abstract
Density estimation of high dimensional datasets plays a crucial role in unsupervised learnôÙ
ing tasks. The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the use of tensor factorizationôÙ
inspired methods to model densities using mixtures of latent states. The thesis primarily
focuses on Canonical Polyadic, Tucker, Tensor Train, Tensor Ring, and Tensor Grid facôÙ
torization, but also proposes a framework for arbitrary directed acyclic graph models. The
models are trained fully observed benchmark datasets, but also on heterogeneous and
partially observed data. The resulting models are compared against the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart
flowôÙbased density estimators and produce competitive results for fully observed homogeôÙ
nous datasets. It is shown that the models are invariant towards data missing at random,
and the models are capable of fitting heterogeneous data without dequantization or similar
techniques to turn discrete data into continuous data.

Introduction
Density estimation is the act of estimating the probability density function of some probôÙ
ability distribution given samples drawn from said distribution. Density estimation is an
unsupervised learning task and consists of generative statistical models. Informally, a
generative model is capable of generating new samples. Formally, generative models
estimate the joint probability between some data, X, and labels, Y , as P(X, Y ), and just
P(X) in the case with no labels. The importance of density estimation stems from the
nature of data available for learningëƒ unlabeled data is drastically easier to obtain than laôÙ
beled data. Density estimation has many uses, as probability density functions often are
the building blocks for machine learning algorithms and deep learning models. Some apôÙ
plications besides pure density estimation include outlier/novelty detection, classification
tasks, dataset summarization, and imputation.
Plenty of methods for density estimation have been proposed, all of varying complexity.
A simple nonôÙparametric density estimation method is Kernel Density Estimation [RosenôÙ
blatt (1956), Parzen (1962)], which overlays kernels on top of training observations to
generate a smooth estimate for the probability density function. This is useful if all that
is needed is a smooth histogramôÙlike approximation with the ability to sample for lower
dimensional data. The method suffers from the curse of dimensionality, which only makes
it appropriate for simple tasks.
Variational approaches introduce models that utilize latent variables to explain observed
samples, that provide greater flexibility at the cost of complexity when it comes to learnôÙ
ing. In recent times, deep generative neural approaches have been the way to go, with
approaches such as generative adversarial networks (GANs) [Goodfellow et al. (2014)]
and variational autoôÙencoders (VAEs) [Kingma et al. (2013)]. GANs and VAEs in particuôÙ
larly have had impressive results for learning distributions of natural images for generative
purposes, but with many major limitations hindering practical useëƒ neither model can calôÙ
culate exact likelihoods, and GANs suffer from training instability [Salimans et al. (2016)].
A recent approach that has proven stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart performance for density estimation
related tasks is Normalizing Flows (NF). NF is a family of generative models that utilize
change of random variables to turn a simple distribution to a complex distribution through
the use of applying bijective mappings sequentially. New samples can be drawn from
NF and density estimation is exact, and both can be done efficiently depending on the
mappings used.
Many deep generative models require plentiful amounts of data to converge and produce
proper results. This limits the modelsã applicability in many realôÙlife scenarios, as data
often is only partially observable or in limited quantities. Recommendation engines are
one of the areas where data usually is partially observed, e.g., when a doctor recommends
treatment they do it based on answers given to a few select questions out of many potential
questions. Likewise, recommendation engines will mostly have no way of knowing all
preferences of a user, but only a tiny subset, and have to somehow estimate the missing
values or provide an answer with limited knowledge. Questionnaire data in particular is
often heterogeneous and contain mixes of continuous, count, categorical, and missing
data, which often requires transforming discontinuous data to continuous data through
dequantization or embeddings.

Neural networks are often used to construct efficient representations of highôÙdimensional
functions, such as probability density functions. Similarly, tensor networks are powerôÙ
ful representations of highôÙdimensional tensors that can represent highôÙdimensional data
accurately using combinations of lower dimensional tensors. Examples of such networks
are Tensor Train/Matrix Product State [PerezôÙGarcia et al. (2006), Oseledets (2011)], TenôÙ

[Hitchcock (1927), Harshman et al. (1970), Carroll et al. (1970), Kiers (2000)], and Tucker
decomposition [Tucker (1966)]. In this thesis, a range of tensor factorized density estiôÙ

aim to be alternatives to GANs, VAEs, and NF by having exact likelihoods and efficient
sampling, while also being more resilient towards partially observed data, and simple to
use with heterogeneous data.
This thesis will explore utilizing tensor factorization methods for probability density estiôÙ
mation, with a focus on the benefits that the tensor network structures bring. In particular,
the thesis will explore
ãÂ how well the different tensor factorization methods estimate densities of benchmark
datasets,
ãÂ the ability to estimate densities of heterogeneous data without processing everything
into continuous features,
ãÂ how the tensor factorization methods perform when data is missing at random,
ãÂ multiple imputation using the tensor factorized models,
ãÂ and how the ordering of features have an impact for models utilizing matrix product
states.
1.1
Related works
As mentioned, the most popular approaches for density estimation are GANs, VAEs, and
models based on NF, and many papers have been made to improve upon them. This
section will introduce the biggest ones that either introduce great improvements in the
architecture, or aim to solve problems related to handling partially observed data.
1.1.1
Generative Adversarial Networks
GANs were originally introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014) and have since exploded
in popularity. As the architecture consists of training a generator and a discriminator, it
suffers from mode collapse, which happens when the generator and discriminator train
at different paces and end up overôÙoptimizing for a specific configuration. Salimans et al.
(2016) propose ways to hand perform more robust training of GANs.
Li et al. (2019) introduces MisGAN, which is a GAN for missing data. The MisGAN arôÙ
chitecture consists of two generatorôÙdiscriminator pairs that generate or discriminate full
observations or binary masks for missing observations. The discriminator for the observed
data is oblivious to missing data, as missing values are replaced by some constant value.
MisGAN is improved upon by Li et al. (2020) with an approach called PôÙBiGAN, which
stands for Partial Bidirectional GAN. PôÙBiGAN aims to match the joint distribution of inôÙ
complete data with a joint distribution of generated masked outputs based on a latent
variable and indices sampled from the dataset being trained on.
1.1.2
Variational Auto Encoders
Kingma et al. (2013) introduced VAEs, which are especially popular for image generation
and other highôÙdimensional tasks, as the variational part allows precise control over laôÙ

decoder, with the difference being that the bottleneck is variational.
Mattei et al. (2018) propose a way to train deep latent variable models such as VAEs by
maximizing an objective called the missing data importanceôÙweighted autoôÙencoder (MIôÙ

1.1.3
Normalizing Flows
Normalizing Flows are a family of generative models with tractable probability distribuôÙ
tions, where both sampling and evaluation of probabilities can be exact, unlike GANs or
VAEs. Normalizing Flows were initially introduced by Tabak et al. (2010ëƒ 2013), but first
gained traction within variational inference when Rezende et al. (2015) demonstrate the
ability to create extremely rich posterior approximations that can contain the true posteôÙ
rior distribution in the asymptotic regime, and within density estimation when Dinh et al.
(2014) achieves results competitive with the then stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart while having tractable
evaluation and sampling. Continuous normalizing flows [Chen et al. (2018)] and FFJORD
[Grathwohl et al. (2018)] build upon the normalizing flows architecture by instead calculatôÙ
ing the likelihood as a function of time and integrating from start to finish, where FFJORD
in particular has the benefit of allowing unconstrained Jacobianãs due to utilizing trace
estimates for efficient calculation.
Masked Autoregressive Flow (MAF) [Papamakarios et al. (2017)] is another model that
will be compared against, which implements normalizing flows using an autoregressive
model that has a tractable and invertible Jacobian. MAF is a generalization of Real NVP
[Dinh et al. (2016)], which proposed using realôÙvalued nonôÙvolume preserving invertible
transformations for normalizing flows. Richardson et al. (2020) introduce Monte Carlo
Flow (MCFlow) models, which is a normalizing flows based architecture meant for training
with missing data. MCFlow works by first naively imputing missing data, after which the
estimate is passed through the model and updated. The process is then repeated until
convergence, where the input is the output of the previous epoch.

Density Estimation
Probability density functions are the baseline for many mathematical models, with the
Gaussian distribution being the most frequently used distribution. Probability densities
have a wide range of uses and can be used for classification, data clustering, dimenôÙ
sional reduction, and fieldôÙspecific applications such as noiseôÙfiltering or pricing financial
derivatives. The central problem is that probability density functions in most cases are
unknown. This is where density estimation comes in: if we are given a set of independent
and identically distributed observed samples xi, i = 1, . . . , N, then we wish to estimate
the unknown underlying probability distribution. The current stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart estimators
are based on normalizing flows, which will be the main focus for this section. However,
first previous methods such as GANs and VAEs will be briefly introduced.

Normalizing Flows
A normalizing flow is a transformation of a simple base probability distribution into a more
complex distribution by letting it ãflowã through a sequence of differentiable and invertible
mappings. The density of an observed sample can then be evaluated by transforming it
back to the latent simple baseline distribution and computing the product of the inverseôÙ
transformed sample under the latent distribution and the changes in volume that originate
from the sequence of inverse transformations. This method also allows us to sample from
the latent simple distribution and then apply the transformations to obtain a sample from
the complex distribution.

Feature ordering
As briefly mentioned in the Tensor Train section, the order of features matters for density
estimation as soon as the graph for the model has a depth greater than 1. The order
of features is a nonôÙtrivial problem for arbitrary DAPGM models, however if crafting a
model from scratch it is likely that an optimal order is known beforehand. For the trainôÙ
like models based on matrix product states, it makes sense that the best performance
is obtained when the features with most mutual information are close to each other in
the train. In Oseledets (2011) it is observed that the Tensor Train factorization has lower
ranks near the edges of the train and larger ranks near the center, and that most of the
expressive power originates from the center cores. While it isnãt transferrable 1ôÙtoôÙ1 to
density estimation, it also makes sense that the most important features are grouped
together.
In order to test the hypothesis that choice of feature ordering has an impact, a couple
of methods are proposed to determine betterôÙthanôÙrandom ordering for trainôÙlike models.
Two methods are proposed, both of which are based on (an estimate of) mutual informaôÙ
tion, where one selects features greedily and the other is based on hierarchical clustering.
Estimating the mutual information requires deciding on a binôÙsize for discretizing the inôÙ
tegrals used. As the purpose just is to decide on an ordering and not to actually use the
mutual information, it is fine for an over or underestimation as long as it is proportional to
the real mutual information, which means the binôÙsize is a hyperparameter with little to no
importance as long as it is not too large (too few bins).

4.8.2
Hierarchical clustering approach
The hierarchical clustering is very similar to the greedy approach, except it is possible
to customize the behavior a bit by changing the linkage method. The approach utilizes
a completely standard hierarchical clustering algorithm, with the only difference that the
distance metric is D(U, V ) = 1ãNMI(U, V ) instead of traditional measures. As hierarchiôÙ
cal clustering usually clusters individual observations together, we in this case consider
the transpose of our dataset, of size M û N, which can be interpreted as M elements.
The clustering can then be performed, and the leaf nodes can be arranged in optimal leaf
order, which minimizes the distance between adjacent leaves. The leaf order can then
be used as feature ordering.
The ordering depends on the linkage chosen, and the linkage chosen here will be single
linkage such that the distance between clusters is the minimum distance between clusters.
This makes sense, as weãre just interested in keeping them ordered close to elements with
the lowest distance.
The method is given in algorithm 7 as rough steps, as everything can be easily impleôÙ
mented using packages such as SciPy with well documented algorithms.
Algorithm 7 Hierarchical Clustering Mutual Information Ordering
Require: A dataset X ãRNûM, where N is the amount of observation and M is the
amount of features.
1. Turn each feature into a singleton, i.e., cluster of single element.
2. For each pair of clusters, u and v, calculate their distance D(u, v).
3. Merge the pair of clusters that have the smallest distance.
4. Repeat from step 2 until a single cluster is left.
5. ReôÙorder leaves in optimal leaf ordering.
6. return leaves as accessed in inôÙorder traversal.

Experimental results
While TFDE models are powerful, they are not expected to outperform stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart
flowôÙbased models such as FFJORD, but should still be competitive, when it comes to
plain density estimation. The key areas of interest is instead the easeôÙofôÙuse with arbitrary
datasetsëƒ the major strength is that TFDE models are capable of fitting on heterogeneous
datasets that are only partially observed. The experiments will thus focus not only on raw
density estimation expressive power, but also fitting models on datasets with randomly
dropped values. The fitted models (with no dropped values) will also be used to perform
imputation to emphasize the strengths of using a graphôÙbased model.
All training runs are logged using Weights & Biases and can be found at
https://wandb.ai/marcboeg/tensorôÙfactorizedôÙdensityôÙestimation/table.
Datasets
The models will be evaluated on some benchmark datasets, that have received the same
preôÙprocessing from Papamakarios et al. (2017) and Grathwohl et al. (2018). An additional
heterogeneous dataset will also be used, which hasnãt been subject to any preôÙprocessing,
meaning continuous features donãt have zero mean and unit variance.
BSDS300
The BSDS300 dataset is a segmentation dataset consisting of 300 natural images [Martin
et al. (2001)]. In order to properly compare with other work, we utilize the same preproôÙ
cessing as Papamakarios et al. (2017), Grathwohl et al. (2018), and originally Uria et al.
(2013). The data in question are random 8û8 image patches from the BSDS300 dataset,
which have random uniform noise added to pixel values, and then rescaled to be in the
range [0, 1].
UCI Datasets
Four of the benchmark datasets used are from the UCI machine learning repository [Dua
et al. (2017)]. The datasets used are POWER, GAS, HEPMASS, and MINIBOONE. The
datasets are preprocessed by subtracting the sample mean from the data and each feaôÙ
ture divided by its sample standard deviation. DiscreteôÙvalued attributes and attributes
with greater than .98 correlation have been dropped.
CIFAR-10 & MNIST
The CIFARôÙ10 dataset consists of 32 û 32 color images in 10 classes, with 6000 images
per class [Krizhevsky et al. (2009)], and MNIST is a database of 70,000 handwritten digits
stored as 28 û 28 monochrome images [Deng (2012)].
The same preprocessing as Dinh et al. (2016), Papamakarios et al. (2017), and GrathôÙ
wohl et al. (2018). The preprocessing consists of adding random uniform noise, and then
rescaling pixel values to [0, 1]. Papamakarios et al. (2017) and Dinh et al. (2016) transôÙ
forms the pixel values to logit space when performing the density estimation and performs
horizontal flips on training examples, which has been skipped as Grathwohl et al. (2018)
only adds random uniform noise and rescaling.

The CreditCard dataset is a heterogeneous dataset with continuous attributes and count
attributes from Kleiber et al. (2008). It contains data on credit history for a sample of

6
Discussion
The TFDE models all show promising results, even though the models havenãt been fineôÙ
tuned to perfection through hyperparameter sweeps. Even without optimizing the feature
ordering, the models provide results comparable to the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart flow based density
estimators such as FFJORD or MAF, with models that are fairly small. TFDE models
based on tensor networks inspired by Tensor Train factorization perform particularly well
on image data, such as BSDS300, CIFARôÙ10, and MNIST, and tabular data with strongly
correlated features.
Density Estimation & Feature Ordering
As mentioned, when only considering density estimation, the TFDE models will likely not
be able to outperform flow based models. The density estimation performance is competôÙ
itive, but depends on the ordering of features. Random ordering already has competitive
performance, but ordering tailored to the specific model will increase performanceëƒ this
can be a vice or a virtue depending on perspective, as a betterôÙthanôÙrandom ordering can
be found fairly easily for the Tensor Train model, but be computationally infeasible to find
for arbitrary network models on highôÙdimensional data.
TFDE models are thus not meant as a replacement for current stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart methods
for density estimation, but rather proposed as an alternative if working with data that is
partially observed or heterogeneous.
Heterogeneous Data
TFDE models assume that the distributions of individual observable features are indepenôÙ
dent of each other, and that the parameters of the distributions instead depend on interôÙ
connected latent random variables. This allows each feature to be modelled by different
distribution types, which makes it trivial to create a density estimator for heterogeneous
models. E.g., count data can be modelled by Poisson distributions instead of treating the
data as continuous. Fitting flowôÙ or VAEôÙbased models on discrete features is often done
through dequantization, where random noise is added to the discrete value. The perforôÙ
mance of this depends on the distribution the random noise is sampled from, with the
best performance coming from variational dequantization [Ho et al. (2019)], which uses a
flowôÙbased distribution for the random noise.
Missing Data
The biggest strength of the TFDE models is that they are invariant towards missing values
(assuming missing values are spread uniformly among features, and there are enough
samples). In a scenario with a model that isnãt complex enough to overfit a dataset, the
test loss basically remains unchanged as the missingness increases. This is only possible
as the model is able to train using samples with missing features, which allows it to update
parameters given all the observed features, without being colored by choice of imputation
method. Training GANs, VAEs, or deep flow models require actively making a decision
with how to handle the missing data, with the easiest solution being either discarding the
samples or imputation, which either introduces a bias or decreases learning potential.

Imputation
While imputation isnãt the main purpose for TFDE models, as any density estimator with
a tractable likelihood can be used for imputation, imputation is still used to show the
strengths of having the latent random variables connected based on tensor networks.
The results show that the structure of the model network has a meaningful impact on imôÙ
putation power, as the latent states can be estimated from the observed data. The nature
of the missing values greatly impacts the performance of imputationëƒ randomly distributed
missing values can be imputed fairly well by Tensor TrainôÙlike models, while long chains
of missing values ãperpendicularã to the model underperform. The Tensor Grid model
counteracts this by having a lattice structure at the cost of complexity.
Figures 5.9 to 5.11 showed some randomly selected samples from the MNIST dataset to
perform imputation on. While the Tensor Train model produced reasonable results in the
random and vertical case, it struggled to impute properly in the horizontal case. ImpleôÙ
menting the more expensive and generic imputation algorithm given in algorithm 5 can
likely overcome this limitation at the cost of complexity. Customizing the feature ordering
would also help with overcoming this issue, as the pixels would be scrambled.
Next steps
Currently, the implemented TFDE models vary with limitations. The CP, TT, and TR have
no limitation to how highôÙdimensional the modelled distribution can be besides hardware
limitations. As the pure fullyôÙconnected Tucker model scales exponentially with dimenôÙ
sionality, it isnãt suited for highôÙdimensional data anyway, so it isnãt an issue that it is
limited to at most 25 dimensions. However, the TG and DAPGM implementations both
suffer from the 25 dimension limitation, while the models presumably can be useful even
for very highôÙdimensional data. Ironically, the limitation stems from having to perform
complex tensor contractions, which currently are implemented in the log domain as eleôÙ
mentwise sums and logôÙsumôÙexp reductions.
The logôÙdomain tensor contraction is currently done through the use of NumPy broadôÙ
casting1 semantics, however, this requires creating highôÙorder tensors where majority of
dimensions have length 1, which is limited by PyTorch being restricted to 25thôÙorder GPUôÙ
tensors (64thôÙorder CPUôÙtensors). The solution to this is fairly simple, but would require
additional bookkeepingëƒ as tensor operations can be described using Einstein summation
notation, it is possible to use optimized einsum algorithms [Smith et al. (2018)] for the conôÙ
tractions. Einsum engines are limited by alphabet size, as the equations are written using
strings, however this can be overcome keeping track of which dimensions have been conôÙ
tracted and using multiple einsums until done. This will allow for complex TFDE models to
work on highôÙdimensional datasets as well and speed up calculation of likelihoods, which
in turn allows faster imputation.
Currently, features are assumed independent and are modelled by univariate distribuôÙ
tions. An interesting next step would be to extend the framework to allow modelling sets
of features as dependent through multivariate distributions, which could greatly improve
performance for models on datasets, where some features are known to be related.

7
Conclusion
Various Tensor Factorized Density Estimation models have been proposed in this thesis,
which all have tractable exact logôÙlikelihoods and can be sampled from efficiently. The
models take inspiration from tensor networks to represent a graph of latent random variôÙ
ables, that distributions of features then depend upon for parameter selection. As the
feature parameters are independent of each other and only depend on the latent states,
the models can be constructed using arbitrary distributions for the different features. This
makes it possible to easily create heterogeneous models with different assumptions for
how each feature is distributed.
As a result of distributions being assumed independent, the models can be trained with
nearly no impact from missing values. This invariance is due to the modelsã ability to
marginalize missing features away, in contrast to most other density estimation methods.
The models have also been tested for imputation and are compared to the most common
single imputation method: mean imputation. The trainôÙlike models show promising reôÙ
sults on the image datasets with unconditional imputation, while the Canonical Polyadic
model performs worse than mean imputation. For the HEPMASS dataset, the trainôÙlike
models underperform the Canonical Polyadic and Tucker models, which perform roughly
equivalently to mean imputation.
For plain density estimation, the models perform competitively. The trained models mostly
get outperformed by flow based models, but at times produce similar or better results,
while the models havenãt been fineôÙtuned.
The feature ordering has a large impact on model performance for Tensor Train, Tensor
Ring, and Tensor Grid models. A greedy algorithm and algorithm based on hierarchical
clustering have been proposed to order features based on their mutual information. This
ordering has consistently better density estimation performance for the Tensor Train and
Tensor Ring models, while having mixed results for the Tensor Grid model, and being
irrelevant for Canonical Polyadic and Tucker models.
