Abstract
In recent years, Variable Stiffness Robots (VSR) have gained significant interest due to
their inherent compliance and adaptability. Among the various existing actuation systems,
twisted string actuation remains underutilized despite its potential advantages. This re-
search focuses on characterizing a twisted string-actuated VSR specifically for Unmanned
Aerial Vehicle (UAV) applications, employing simulation and experimental validation to
address two critical challenges: (1) stiffness modulation and (2) impact force minimization.
A MATLAB Simscape simulation was developed, incorporating first 2D then later 3D
bending dynamics. For the bending, an average error of 1.705 degrees was found.
The study also explores the trade-off between stiffness, speed, and impact forces, identi-
fying a near-linear relationship that enables optimization for collision resilience—critical
for applications such as UAVs, where minimizing damage from impacts is essential. Ex-
perimental validation revealed a 30% initial discrepancy, prompting adjustments in sensor
sampling rates and contact modeling, ultimately reducing errors to 15.85% on average.
As a secondary contribution demonstrating the technology’s broader potential, we imple-
ment an Artificial Neural Network (ANN)-based inverse dynamics model (97.7% accuracy)
capable of predicting the forces needed for bending - a feature applicable to both UAV-
integrated and standalone VSR systems.
This work advances the understanding of twisted string-actuated VSRs, providing a found-
ation for future improvements in simulation accuracy, adaptive control, and real-world
deployment.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Over the past centuries industrial progress has been exponentially growing [1].This is
mainly due to the invention of the steam engine in the 18th century, which sparked the
beginning of the first industrial revolution [2]. This revolution enabled man, for the first
time, to be aided by machines. For example, it transformed the way agriculture was done
by highly improving its productivity, turning workers to other fields [3]. This progress,
and the subsequent second and third industrial revolution have led to humans living in
better conditions than ever before: life expectancy rose to 72 years in 2020, compared to a
measly 30 years in 1820 [4]. People also have been living better lives, as extreme poverty
declined from an estimated 76% of the population in 1820 to 9.78% in 2020 [5].
The progress of course continues, as we are now in the midst of the fourth industrial
revolution, also known as industry 4.0. The current industrial revolution is marked by the
use of smart machines and automation [6]. Robots have become more and more utilized in
different industries, leading to a growing need for their capacities. Expectations for what
a robot can do have indeed risen, but many of those expectations cannot be met due to
the rigid structure of traditional robots.
For example, grasping delicate or soft objects is a typically complex action to carry out
with a rigid-linked robot, due to their lack of compliance [7]. For the same reason, rigid-
linked robots can be unsafe for human interaction, limiting their adoption in sectors such
as healthcare - despite the industry’s strong interest in investing in advanced technologies
[8]. Soft robots, which are characterized by an actuation that is integrated along the whole
link instead of only at the joints [9], offer a solution to those problems. Soft robots that can
modify their stiffness, called Variable Stiffness Robot (VSR), do not experience the lack of
compliance from which rigid-linked robots suffer [10]. For those reasons, the soft robotics
demand is rapidly growing, resulting in a field full of innovation [11]. The demand for soft
robots rises everywhere: in the previously mentioned automation and health-care industry
(respectively Figure 1.1a and Figure 1.1b), but also in aerial applications (Figure 1.1c),
for example, and more surprising, in applications like deep-sea exploration (Figure 1.1d).
1.2
Problem statement & Research questions
As mentioned above, there exist many applications for soft robots. In this study, a VSR
prototype, built by Dr. Fumagalli and shown in Figure 1.2, will be characterized. The
VSR was designed to be used on a Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) for a package delivery
system. Traditionally, this requires precise knowledge of the location and a lot of control.
Using a soft link, rather than a stiff one, could ensure safe physical interaction with both
the environment and the humans.
It is also important that the design is simple and
cost-effective. A twisted-string actuation system fits the criteria [7].

2.1
Variable Stiffness Robots
In this study, a twisted string actuated robot will be used. Twisted string actuation is a
kind of length variable tendon actuation, of which other kinds exist. One kind of length
variable tendon soft robots are Shape Memory Alloy (SMA) [10]. SMA’s are very popular
in current soft robots [17] [18], largely due to the fact that they are capable of very high
deformation (300% in spiral shape [10]). There are however drawbacks to SMA’s. For
example, some of them need high temperatures to operate, which can exclude the possible
use-case of interacting with humans [7]. Aside from this, they have very complex and
non-linear behavior and if one wants to use the spiral form, small differences on how the
spiral is made can have big and unpredictable impacts on the results [17].
Another way of creating a variable tendon is by having a tendon-sheath actuator on which
one can pull. This does require a spool, which takes up more space than only the motor
needed for a twisted-string actuator. The difference in space needed is quite significant, as
can be seen when comparing a twisted-string actuator and a tendon-sheath actuator for
an exosuit, as shown in Figure 2.1. Despite this major drawback, one might still opt for
a tendon-sheath actuator if the linear velocity is important, but when it comes to force
outputs and effective torque inputs, the twisted-string actuator performs better [19].
The most popular actuation method for soft robots, pneumatic actuation, which require
compressors or air sources, can also take up a lot of space. This also results in a lower
power-to-weight ratio [20].

2.2
Learning-based control
The control of soft robots has been evolving considerably in recent years. The simplest
technique, which is still very current, is to use a (piecewise) constant curvature assumption
to predict the kinematics of the robot [26] [27]. This assumption allows for simple com-
putation of the kinematics, and a lot of continuum robots fulfill the assumptions needed
to use it [27]:
• The manipulator is uniform in shape and symmetric in actuation design.
• External loading effects are negligible.
• Torsional effects are minimal.
Due to the nature of the vertebrae and the later inclusion of gravity in this project, the
contact forces would be in opposition to those conditions and could thus not be used. This
model is still the most popular, despite the fact that ANNs have shown better results, as
showed ever since the first paper on using them for soft robotics [28]. Other models, such
as the Cosserat theory, give promising results when having to account for external forces
[18]. The added complexity of the analytical model is however not worth the trade-off
in computational and sensing cost [27]. The trade-off between simplicity and accuracy
is in fact hard to balance, leading researchers to start turning to model-free alternatives,
and more specifically to learning-based alternatives. The reason for this is that ANNs
have proven to perform better than their analytical alternatives; in [29], a Multi-Layer
Perceptron (MLP) and a Jacobian-model method are compared to compute the statics
of cable-driven soft robot arm.
The results are clear: the Jacobian-method, based on
analytical computations, has less than half the accuracy of the MLP (43.81% versus 91%).
Not only this, but the Jacobian method also takes longer to compute. More recent results,
showcase the same conclusions: in [30] (2022), the kinematic model found from using
a back propagation model results in a lower position error than the analytical model: a
decrease from 3.09% to 2.46% is obtained. The paper refers to pneumatic actuation, where

the use of neural networks is more common than for other soft actuators, due to the high
non-nonlinearities in the system [27]. ANNs giving highly promising results for pneumatic
actuation [31], do indicate potential for other soft robots.
For tendon-based actuation, the constant curvature model is still largely favored over a
model-free approach [27]. When ANNs are used, MLPs are favored due to their simplicity

It is also interesting to note that in [33] other, more complex types of neural networks
were tested, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gate Recurrent Unit (GRUs),
which are types of Recurrent Neural Network (RNNs). RNNs are a type of neural network
where the output of a neuron can be send back as the input from a previous layer, as
illustrated in Figure 2.3. This added complexity allows to remember the prior inputs,
which is essential for sequence relationships, such as in speech where the words must be
understood together and not simply separately [34]. An issue with RNN is the vanishing
gradient problem, where the weights become too small and the backpropagation essentially
reduces to zero. This is due to the fact that the backpropagation is now a backpropagation
trough time, and when the time is excessively long, the values of the weights become
too small [34].
The LSTM and the GRU both improve on this.
The LSTM uses an
added memory that serves as a long-term memory,which decides which information should
be forgotten and which should be remembered by using 3 gates, similar to switches,a
mechanism that helps overcome the issue of the long-term dependecy of the weights [35].
GRU uses a similar model to LSTM, but with less parameters, as it only needs 2 gates
[36]. Both methods are computationally effective compared to a basic RNN, but in the
case of a tendon-based actuator, they display no significant differences with MLP, despite
a training time that is more than 250 times bigger [33].

A drawback to the Artificial Neural Network approach is the number of data required
that is much larger than what would be needed for an analytical method. Generating
this amount of data from tests on the robot can have negative consequences because the
properties of soft robots tend to change over time, due to usage. Generating all the data
from the robot itself will accelerate the aging of the robot greatly [37]. The process is also
very time-consuming, which is why synthetic data can serve as a valuable alternative [16].
Different methods can be used to generate the data: Finite Element Method (FEMs) are
a popular option [38] [39], and so is the use of simulations. Simulations have indeed been
proven to generate similar results to real-world data when fed to an ANN [40]. Recent
From simulation to reality: a variable-stiffness robot

research also indicates that transformers can build upon existing data to generate accurate
synthetic data, therby reducing the need for real-world data [41].
ANNs can be implemented either online or offline, with both methods having their re-
spective advantages and disadvantages. Online models, which are also called incremental
learning, are models that will deal with smaller batches of data, as they update more
frequently. The model is not trained before its use, but rather during it. Enabling the
model to learn ”on the fly” allows for a model that is better suited to sudden changes and
has a bigger adaptability [42]. It is however limited in the accuracy it can obtain due to
the small size of their learning batches, but this does mean that less data is needed, which
can be beneficial [43]. In offline models, also known as batch learning, the ANN is trained
for a bigger batch of data, and the resulting weights are remembered for when the ANN
is needed and there is no further learning during its use. This means that offline models
are generally more accurate to the training data, except for applications that are unpre-
dictable [43]. They are also highly dependent on the training data, which, if they don’t
resemble the problem well enough, will simply stay a bad approximation, while the online
model could correct this mistake [42]. Given that the task at hand will, while complex,
not experience unpredictable nor wild changes, and given the importance of accuracy, it
is thought best to go for an offline model.

Chapter Summary
In this chapter, a literature review was made, which was split in two main sections. Firstly,
Variable Stiffness Robot were studied. From this, it appears that twisted string actuation
can have significant advantages over other soft actuation methods: it takes less space
than a tendon-sheath actuator, is less complex to put in place than a SMA and is more
eﬀicient than a pneumatic actuator. Despite this, there a few studies about the use of
twisted-string actuation in robotics, and none with non-flexible materials.
Secondly, learning-based control for soft robots was reviewed. A majority of control for
soft robotics is still done with analytical models, mainly with the (piecewise) constant
curvature assumption or the Cosserat theory. Both perform worse than Artificial Neural
Network (ANN), which, even in their simplest forms generate better results. A reason
that ANN’s are not widespread yet might be the amount of data needed, which, if all
generated from the robot itself, will age the robot and can worsen non-linear behavior. To
avoid this, FEM and simulation, as well as transformers are possibilities. The possibilities
of having the network online or offline were also discussed. Given the need for accuracy
in this application and that the system should not experience unpredictable changes, an
offline model was deemed suitable.

Kinematics and (inverse) dynamics
When referring to kinematics, one is referring to the study of the motion of mechanical
body/bodies without considering the mass or the forces [44].
In robotics, the inverse
kinematics are often used, as they transform a Cartesian point in space into a set of joint
angles, and subsequently make it possible to find the region of space the robot can reach
and eﬀiciently move the end-effector into a desired position [45].
In this study, the dynamics will be analyzed, and from that the inverse dynamics will be
used. Contrary to kinematics, dynamics include mass and forces. The inverse dynamics
refers to the finding of the necessary forces to execute a certain motion [46].

The data is typically divided in a training set, a validation set and a test set. The training

MLP
A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is a forward neural network (Figure 3.2) that utilizes

MLP’s are characterized by their simplicity and their ability to handle data that is non-

3.3.2
CNN
A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is also a type of forward neural network, but
is more complex than a MLP, as it is based on kernel optimization. It excels at pattern
recognition and is, for example, used in image processing. CNN’s can handle large amount
of data while maintaining a high accuracy.
A CNN consists of three kinds of layers:
convolutional layers, pooling layers and a final fully connected layer [55].
The convolutional layers return feature maps, as they extract features by using filters, also
called convolutional kernels, which are grids of weights. Those weights are originally set at

Stiffness
The previous simulation did not incorporate any displays of the stiffness. Now that the
pulling force was added, it is interesting to compute the stiffness. The definition of linear
stiffness (3.1) and the definition of rotational stiffness (3.2) are used to compute each of
the main stiffness (Kxx, Kyy, Kzz, Kθxθx, Kθyθy and Kθzθz) at the end-effector.
Those stiffnesses are displayed in a scope found at the opening screen of the simulation
(Figure 4.16). The stiffness is only computed in steady state to avoid the fluctuations of
the angle θ and this is done by verifying that the velocity of the robot is close to zero. This
is why in Figure 4.16b, where an example of the computed stiffness for varying pulling
forces is given, the stiffness is discontinuous. The sudden spike is at 2.5 seconds, which is
when the new force is applied, resulting in a new ratio when computing (3.1) and (3.2).
The spike is thus what was expected, as is the general behavior; only the x, y, θz stiffness
show any changes, which corresponds to a 2D bending system.

Impact force simulation
With the simulation having been validated for 2D bending, it is now possible to add the
additional necessary DOF, as shown in Figure 4.17. The additional DOF is a rotational
DOF, which allows to rotate the base at 360 degrees.
To do this, an extra revolute joint was added to the simulation. Its motion is defined by
an integrator, which is dependent on the chosen speed. The output angle is monitored
so the simulation can stop after the wall has been hit, which is when the VSR reaches
90 degrees. This value was chosen to have an easy reference when executing tests on the
real-world VSR. The simulation uses a value of 90.05 degrees to ensure that the position
is reached, as shown in Figure 4.18. This precaution was taken to ensure that there is no
penetration of the wall and it also leads to the optimize the simulation running time. The
parameters of the ”Spatial Contact Force”, which computes the contact force, are later

4.2
Artificial Neural Network for bending inverse dynamics
While the simulation provides the dynamics of the VSR, figuring out the inverse dynamics
is not possible this way. To resolve this, an ANN is used. The goal is to find the required
inputs (pulling force and end-effector force) for any desired position and speed.
The
ANN explores the inverse dynamics for the case without the additional DOF, as that case
employs direct motor control. However, the ANN build could be expanded to more DOF
if needed and/or relevant.
4.2.1
Prior work
The ANN used in this study will be based on the ANN from a previous master’s thesis on
a VSR [54]. In the thesis, the ANN consists of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
which can extract all the features, including their time dependencies, followed by a Multi-
Layer Perceptron (MLP) which processes the extracted features to generate an output.
The general structure of the model will not be changed, as it achieved good results for a
similar use case.
Before reaching the CNN, the data is pre-processed with the following steps:
1. Both the input and output data are normalized to have a zero mean µ and an unit
variance σ2, as to reduce error and calculation times [61].
2. The input data is processed into sequences of 50 data points, as to improve the
learning eﬀiciency. A step size of 1 is used, which leads to overlap. Mathematically,

though; the model that will be used does not use an explicit time parameter. The solution
for this is to recompute the data to have a constant time step. Given that the original
time steps of the simulation are small with a mean step of 0.87ms, it is assumed that data

general structure of the ANN is not modified. However, the system being studied is less

avoid the influence of noise on the accuracy.

was chosen because it has been proven to reduce noise significantly better than the more

Chapter Summary
In this chapter, the simulation for the impact force when hitting a target was tested against
the real life robot. This resulted in a 30% error. To remedy this, changes were made, both
on the real-life configuration as in the simulation.
On the test set-up, the sampling rate of the loadcell used to measure the impact force was
changed from 10SPS to 80SPS. Although this allowed for more accurate results, speeds
above a pin value of 160 were cut off from the range, as results suggested the sampling
rate was too small to properly measure their impact force.
In the simulation, the bending was expanded to take a 3D input, now allowing to both
account for gravity and the bending when hitting the wall. This was done by replacing
the two revolute joints between two bodies with a spherical joint connected to a Cartesian
joint.
This method has the added bonus of removing the artificial connection bodies.
It does create algebraical loops, but this can be quickly resolved using delays inside the
simulation.
The contact force is represented by a spring-damper, for which the constants were found
empirically from new measurements made for varying speeds and pulling force in the new
test set-up. An average error of 15.87% was found.
The pulling force and the speed are both varied separately in the simulation, leading to
similar trends to experimental results.
A simulation with 3000 varying speeds and pulling forces was run, leading to find that
there is a close to linear relationship between the impact of speed and pulling force on the
impact force. This finding could be affected by the assumption of linear contact that was
made.
The optimal configuration (lowest possible impact force for highest speed and lowest
pulling force) can be found with a trade-off optimization.

Discussion
The study aimed to address three key research questions regarding the characterization,
optimization, and inverse dynamics of a Variable Stiffness Robot for potential UAV applic-
ations. The findings provide insights into the accuracy of the simulation, the relationship
between stiffness, speed, and impact forces, and the feasibility of using an Artificial Neural
Network (ANN) for inverse dynamics prediction.
The initial MATLAB Simscape simulation was first analyzed with only the possibility
of bending and achieved a high accuracy with an average error of 1.705 degrees when
comparing to test results. While this is a satisfactory error, it is hypothesized that im-
provement would be possible by both running more tests and redesigning the load cell
configuration to have a more steady reading. More data would allow for more precision
in the polynomial relationship in Equation 4.3.
The simulation was then extended to include an additional degrees of freedom (DoF) and
contact dynamics, by adding a base rotation with a revolute joint, which mirrors the added
motor to the test set-up. Experimental validation revealed discrepancies. The comparison
between simulation and experiments showed a 30% error, prompting modifications in both
the test set-up and the simulation. On the side of the test set-up, a major improvement
was the increase of the load cell sampling rate from 10 SPS to 80 SPS. This improved
measurement accuracy, but limitations persisted at higher speeds (beyond a pin value of
160).
In the simulation, the transition from 2D to 3D bending dynamics was made possible by
the replacement of the revolute joints with spherical and cartesian joints. This transition
allowed the removal of the artificial connection bodies, which, together with the inclusion
of gravity, improved simulation realism. However, algebraic loops were now introduced,
but those were resolved using delays. The spring-damper were emperically tuned, yet an
average of 15.85% on measurements remained. This suggests that while the simulation
captures general trends, nonlinearities in real-world contact dynamics may require further
refinement.
The study identified a near-linear relationship between impact force, speed, and pulling
force. This finding aligns with the assumption of linear contact mechanics but may not
fully capture high-speed or high-force scenarios. The trade-off optimization between speed
and pulling force to minimize impact forces is promising for UAV applications, where
collision resilience is critical. However, the linearity assumption may limit accuracy in
more complex impact scenarios, warranting further investigation into nonlinear contact
models.
For the 2D bending dynamics, an ANN was created to study the possibility of computing
the inverse dynamics. The ANN successfully predicted pulling and end-effector forces from
displacement and speed inputs with high accuracy (97.72% with filtering). This demon-
strates the feasibility of data-driven inverse dynamics modeling for the VSR. However,
this model was not extended to the new test configuration, due to the nature of the test
set-up leading to direct motor control.
Although the study provides insights on the accuracy of the Matlab simulation, the pos-
sibility of minimizing impact forces upon contact and and the potential of the VSR as
stand-alone system, future works could include the use of non-nonlinearities to model the

Both in the context of the UAV’s and as a stand-alone robot, the simple mechanism of
twisted-string actuation has a lot of potential.

Conclusion
In this study, a novel VSR with a twisted-string actuation was simulated and validated by
means of experimental testing. The focus was on the developing of a simple but accurate
dynamic model, as well as on studying the impact dynamics.
The simulation was made in MATLAB simulink. Rather than simulating the internal
string mechanism, it is represented as a 1 DOF VSR with the use of revolute joints at the
contact points between elements. The goal was to have a simplified representation of the
real-world VSR, while still being kinematically accurate. To verify said accuracy, different
measurements where the VSR was bended were made. 85% of those measurements were
used to find a relationship between the simulated joint stiffness and the real-life pulling
force, while the remaining 15% were used for testing the relationship.
A satisfactory
average error of 11% was found, which is the equivalent of 1.70 degrees. This error is
satisfactory with regards to the simplifications made.
After the validation of the simulation, it was extended to add a second degree of freedom.
This DOF was rotational, and permitted to study the behavior of the VSR when coming
into contact with a rigid target.
The impact force was also experimentally verified, as to ensure the parameters for the
spatial force between the VSR and the target wall were correct. A bigger mistake was
found, 30%. This could be due to the low sampling rate of the load cell, namely 10SPS.
The sampling rate was raised to 80SPS, with limitations being present but overall improv-
ing accuracy. The model was also changed from 2D bending to 3D bending by replacing the
revolute joints with a spherical joint combined with a cartesian joint. The error dropped
to 15.85%.
The study identified a near-linear relationship between impact force, speed, and pulling
force. This finding aligns with the assumption of linear contact mechanics but may not
fully capture high-speed or high-force scenarios. The trade-off optimization between speed
and pulling force to minimize impact forces is promising for UAV applications, where
collision resilience is critical. However, the linearity assumption may limit accuracy in
more complex impact scenarios, warranting further investigation into nonlinear contact
models.
The ANN successfully predicted pulling and end-effector forces from displacement and
speed inputs with high accuracy (97.7% with filtering). This demonstrates the feasibility
of data-driven inverse dynamics modeling for the VSR.
The broader implication of this study is that, due to its easy construction and low price
point, the use of twisted-string actuation could become more commonplace in robotics.
With the help of the simulation and the ANN, the VSR could be expanded to more DOF,
thus expanding its applications as well.
In summary, this work provides a foundation for VSR characterization and control, with
immediate applications in UAV collision resilience. Future research should focus on refin-
ing contact dynamics models, expanding experimental validation, and exploring further
control strategies.
