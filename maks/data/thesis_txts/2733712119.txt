Abstract
The foundation costs of offshore substation (OS) constitute a substantial expense, which could
be optimised by reducing the mass of the OS’s topside. This thesis investigates one of the
heaviest components, the transformer, by focusing on the overall mass potential reduction
when comparing 3-winding transformers (3WTs) and 2-winding transformers (2WTs).
The project commences with a thorough literature review focused on discerning the common-
alities and distinctions in modelling the mass of the core and windings in large 2WTs and
3WTs. Subsequently, the second phase involves elaborating models in Python to estimate the
mass of steel in the core and copper in the windings. Furthermore, the study evaluates various
parameters, including the cost of the active part, positive leakage reactance, and both no-load
and load losses. The third step consists of collecting essential data for mass, no-load, and cost
functions.
Manufacturer datasheets are collected and used for testing the Python model. Despite the
limited amount of datasheets, these allow us to evaluate results concerning active part mass,
no-load and load losses, and positive leakage reactance. The values for mass, no-load, and
load losses estimated with the Python models are within the same order of magnitude as the
datasheets. Disparities in winding modelling, assumptions regarding leakage flux in selected
analytical methods, and the omission of magnetic shunts make the positive leakage reactance
results deviate significantly from the tender datasheets and industry standards. For this rea-
son, they cannot be compared, and the leakage reactance is excluded from the comparison
between the two transformer types.
In light of the findings, implementing of 3WTs is a potential approach to diminish the loss, cost,
and mass of the active part in both present and future offshore wind farms. The benefits vary
depending on the type of steel used in 3WTs. Specifically, 3WTs with a core of conventional
grain-oriented electrical steel (CGO) seem less attractive than those with high permeability
grain-oriented electrical steel (HIB). While 3WTs with HIB outperform 2WTs in every category,
3WTs with CGO surpass 2WTs only regarding the cost of active parts and no-load losses.

Preface
This project offers me the opportunity to explore a new type of transformer, specifically 3WT.
Additionally, the emphasis on estimating the mass of power transformers provides a unique
chance to delve into the design considerations for transformers operating at high power and
voltage ratings. These two research areas intrigue me and have motivated me to pursue this
project. Moreover, collaborating with Ramboll give me an enriching opportunity to gain in-
sights into industry challenges in offshore wind and transformers.
The research framework was initially broadly defined, as shown in subsection 11.1, focusing
on investigating the advantages and disadvantages of 2WTs and 3WTs at offshore substations.
The primary aspect of comparing the two transformer types was their mass. However, the in-
clusion of other considerations in the comparison varied among the supervisors. In conclusion,
the selection strategy included evaluating the practicality of integrating these considerations
based on the dimensions of 2WTs and 3WTs obtained from the Python function.

Introduction
1.1
Motivations
In many countries, winning auction bids is a prerequisite for operating an offshore wind farm
(OWF) [1]. These auctions foster competition among operators to provide the most competitive
electricity prices [2]. This drive compels operators to consistently seek solutions to decrease
the overall cost of the OWF.
As a cost-saving measure, the foremost strategy is to reduce Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) [3].
An OS structure consists of two parts, which are the topside and the foundation. A potential
approach to realise a reduction in CAPEX involves decreasing the overall mass on the foun-
dations of the offshore substation (OS). The OS’s location within the OWF is depicted in the
diagram below:
Figure 1: Grid connection system of OWF [4]
The load borne by the foundations of the OS is significant. In Hornsea 2 OWF, the total topside
mass of OS components, including high-voltage transmission elements like transformers, is
8000 tons [5] [6]. Reducing the mass of topside components on the OS lowers costs as it neces-
sitates less steel and concrete for foundations.
Examining the economic impact of heavier and bulkier components on the OS in relation to
their cost, the CAPEX for the structure surpasses that of the electrical system [7]. More pre-
cisely, the cost of the electrical system on the OS is estimated at 45000 £/MW, while the cost of
the foundations is 60000 £/MW.
One of the heavy components of the OS is the transformers. While 2WTs currently dominate,
recent projects are opting for 3WTs. High voltage alternating current (HVAC) OS at Afland-
shage is proposed to incorporate one 3WT of 300 MVA, depending on the necessity of con-
structing the OS [8]. Additionally, each of the three high voltage direct current (HVDC) OSs at
the Dogger Bank OWF, with an individual capacity of 1200 MW, will be equipped with 3WTs
[9].

2.2
Geometry of the core
The shape of a transformer depends on its rating. Small transformers typically use a rectan-
gular cuboid, while larger power transformers utilise an approximately circular shape with
several steps [19]. Rectangular cores have limitations due to winding deformation caused
by short-circuit forces. When the core is rectangular, the windings also take on a rectangu-
lar shape, which performs poorly under high short-circuit forces, as shown in the following
illustration.
Figure 5: Effect of short-circuit forces on the circular and rectangular shape of winding [20]
The above figure shows that the rectangular shape of windings does not maintain its shape as
effectively as the circular shape. This occurs because rectangular windings experience stronger
forces in the middle and weaker at the edges, while the forces are uniformly distributed for cir-
cular windings [21][22].
The core’s shape is designed to closely resemble a circular form by employing varying widths
of laminations. Increasing the number of steps in the core includes cutting and assembling a
greater variety of lamination widths. For instance, a visual representation of a 7-stepped core
is provided in the following image.

2.3
Laminations in the core
According to Ampere’s law, the alternating current in the winding generates a magnetic field
around it. As the current and magnetic field strength decrease, the magnetic flux density in
the core diminishes, known as demagnetisation. Conversely, as the current and magnetic field
strength increase, the magnetic flux density strengthens, which is termed magnetisation. In the
following picture, the reaction of a magnetic material during magnetisation can be visualised.
Figure 7: Magnetic domain configurations during several stages of magnetisation [25]
As observed in the graph, the size and shape of magnetic domains alter due to the movement
Page 7 of 81
==PAGE:22==
January 28, 2024
2

of domain walls. These walls encounter obstacles, like impurities, during their motion, leading
to hysteresis losses.
A major consideration is to have a magnetic material with low losses during the magnetisa-
tion and demagnetisation. This requirement is fulfilled by selecting a soft magnetic material.
Another condition is high resistivity to minimise eddy currents. Electrical steel satisfies these
two requirements by containing iron and a silicon content ranging from 1 to 4% [26].
Two different types of electrical steel regarding the grain orientation exist: grain-oriented elec-
trical steel (GOES) and non-oriented grain electrical steel (NOES). GOES is anisotropic, mean-
ing the optimal magnetic properties are only available for one magnetisation direction. On the
other hand, the NOES is isotropic, which signifies that the magnetic properties are the same
regardless of the magnetisation direction.
As the transformer is a static device, the field magnetisation direction is constant. The magnetic
properties, such as permeability, will be at their optima by aligning the laminations in GOES
with the field direction. It is possible to use the benefits of higher permeabilities, lower losses
in the rolling direction [27], and higher maximum flux density [28][29] of GOES compared to
NOES. One of the reasons behind these differences is the larger grain size of GOES compared
to NOES [30] [31]. The below sketch shows the several magnetic domains inside the grains of
one sheet.
Figure 8: Schematic diagram of magnetic features of GOES sheet [32]
Enlarging the grains will decrease the number of grains and grain boundaries within one sheet.
During the movement of magnetic domains, the main domain wall pinning site is the grain
boundaries [33]. Larger grain sizes lead to fewer pinning sites, subsequently reducing hystere-
sis losses.
GOES is more expensive than NOES due to its special manufacturing process. However, it is
compensated by the better magnetic properties of oriented steel [34]. In the end, at an equiv-
alent rating, transformers with GOES are cheaper and smaller due to higher maximum flux
densities than NOES.

Type of winding
A key criterion of winding is its ability to withstand high mechanical forces during short-
circuits. As the power rating increases, the current carried by windings and, thus, the forces
exerted on them rise. In LPTs, mechanical constraints limit the use of only two types of wind-
ings [35] such as:
• helical winding for a small number of turns and a high current
• disc winding for a high number of turns and a low current
Both winding types can resist high forces due to mechanical support [36] from key spacers at-
tached to sticks on the press-board cylinder around the leg, as represented in the next sketches.

2.7
Cooling and insulating medium
To preserve insulating papers and pressboards [46], a cooling medium for the core and the
windings are required. This medium assists in dissipating the heat generated by:
• resistive losses in the windings
• hysteresis loss [47], caused by the friction of molecules in the core during the alignment
of magnetic domains with the field
• induced eddy currents in the core and other metallic parts, such as the tie plates and the
tank
There are two types of transformers classified by the cooling medium employed:
• dry-type transformers using air
• oil-immersed transformers using mineral or synthetic oils
Upon review of major manufacturers [48] like Hitachi Energy and Siemens Energy, dry-type
transformers such as HiDry [49] and GEAFOL [50] fall short in meeting the required power
and voltage ratings for the OS. HiDry is rated at 72.5 kV/40 MVA, while GEAFOL stands at 52
kV/50 MVA.
The primary reasons for selecting oil for higher power and voltage capability lie in its superior
heat transfer capacities and dielectric strength. For instance, at 40°C, the specific heat capaci-
ties of naphthenic transformer oil and air are 1875
J
kg:K [51] and 1007
J
kg:K [52], respectively. A
higher specific capacity means that the substance absorbs or releases more heat energy when
the temperature increases or decreases by 1 K. Thus, naphthenic transformer oil will extract
heat more effectively than the air.
Moreover, oil with paper exhibits 20 kV/mm breakdown strength, while dry air has only 3
kV/mm [53]. The significant discrepancy in breakdown strength highlights the oil’s capability
to withstand higher voltages without experiencing breakdowns.

3.1
Number of limbs
In our analysis, a single 3WT replaces two 2WTs for transmitting an equivalent amount of
power. This doubles the power ratings of 3WT compared to the studied 2WT. As indicated in
Equation 29 and Equation 44, the rise in power ratings directly correlates with an enlargement
in the window’s cross-sectional area, consequently increasing the window and core heights.
In addition, these equations emphasise that the increase in height linked to power ratings is
more significant in 3WT compared to 2WT. Thus, transportation height limitations [62] [63]
have a more significant impact on 3WT than 2WT. To address this concern, large 3WT in our
analysis employ a 5-limb core design [64], as shown in the next figure.

[65]
The figure above demonstrates the reduction in the core’s height, marked by the red arrows.
This decrease arises from the smaller diameter of the top and bottom yokes in the 5-limb core.
The extent of this height reduction depends on the ratio (Fy) between the cross-sectional areas
of the main yokes and limbs. This aspect will be further elaborated in section 3.2.
3.2
Cross-section of yokes and limbs
The cross-section of the main limbs in both the 3-limb and 5-limb cores is determined by Equa-
tion 7. However, there is greater freedom in determining the cross-sections for the yokes in
both types. Additionally, there is flexibility in determining the sizes for the cross-sections of
return limbs and yokes in the 5-limb core.
In 3-limb core, a larger cross-section of yokes compared to limbs is specifically used for NOES
transformers in the low-power range [66]. This practice aims to mitigate transformer loss and

no-load current [67] [68] [69]. However, for larger transformers using GOES, expanding the
yoke’s cross-section to reduce losses and no-load current is unnecessary. Consequently, in our
context, the cross-sections of the yokes and limbs in the 3-limb core are identical [70][13].
In a 5-limb core, the core height decreases maximally when the main yoke’s cross-section
slightly exceeds 50% of the main limb’s cross-section. This limit is determined by the divi-
sion of flux between the return yokes and the top main yoke. In practice, the flux behaviour
in these paths is non-sinusoidal and difficult to predict [71][72]. The non-sinusoidal flux of
the return path has been observed in some case as an undershoot, causing an overshoot at the
upper main yoke. This resultant surge heightens the flux density. To counteract this effect [65],
the main yokes’ cross-section needs to be larger than that of the return path.

5
Electrical performance of the transformers
The mass and cost of materials for 2WT and 3WT have been assessed in the previous section.
In order to evaluate the quality of the transformers from an electrical perspective, the following
properties of each design will be appraised:
• no-load losses in subsection 5.1 and subsection 5.2
• load losses and efficiency in subsection 5.3
• positive leakage reactance in subsection 5.4
The transformer’s efficiency is paramount due to its economic impact and physical influence
on the cooling system. More losses generate more heat, which needs to be removed by more
oil. Furthermore, the power losses represent a loss in revenue from sales of power from the
OWF.
The positive leakage reactance is a significant parameter for the OWF’s grid analysis and
the transformer’s mechanical design. Indeed, it reduces the mechanical forces during short-
circuits and is used to estimate the short-circuit current during 3-phase faults.

6.1
Frequency
The frequency depends on the location of the offshore wind farm. Most countries have a grid
frequency of 50 Hz with some exceptions at 60 Hz, such as Brazil, South Korea and the United
States of America.
6.2
Voltage ratings
The line voltage rating of the collection system depends on the ratings of the wind turbines.
Larger wind turbines lead to higher output voltages. The present output voltage at the wind
turbines is 66 kV [117]. Although, a change towards 132 kV is expected in the future [118].
The voltage of the export cables depends on the total capacity of the OWF. The capacity of an
export cable between 132 kV and 245 kV is usually rated between 300 and 500 MW [119] [120].
Most of the current export cables are used at 220 kV, which is less costly than system with
lower export voltage [121]. However, Nexans proposes export AC cables at 400 kV capable of
transmitting 1 GW [120] [122].
6.3
Type of connection
The commonly used connection for a step-up transformer is ∆−Y [123]. ∆and Y are used on
the lowest and highest voltage sides, respectively.
Firstly, the Y connection suits better than ∆connection for HV windings. The phase voltage
magnitude in the Y connection is
√
3 less than the phase voltage, while the magnitudes for
line and phase voltages are identical in ∆connection. For the insulation of the windings, this
characteristic of the Y connection will lead to less insulation for Y windings than ∆windings.
Secondly, ∆connected windings are usually used to low voltage side of the transformer with
high current. In the ∆connection, the phase currents are equal to the line current divided by
√
3, while the line and phase currents are equal to each other in the case of Y windings. By
using ∆connected windings, each turn’s cross-section of each turn can be decreased and so
less copper is used. This leads to a more economical design.

Another error source could be our equal height representation, whereas the transformer from

The leakage flux shows a more radial pattern in asymmetric height windings compared to
symmetric ones. The height difference creates more space where the flux lines can diverge. As
the analytical formulas are derived from estimating the leakage flux [145], the asymmetry in
winding height will also cause a difference in the leakage reactance compared to a representa-
tion with symmetric windings.
Since the analytical formulas assume annular winding configurations, the distinct features of
helical windings tend to be overlooked. For example, in disc-type winding, the flux is only
radial and axial [105] [106]. However, in helical windings, an extra component in the leakage
components proportional to the current has been observed in the azimuthal direction [146].
As the windings’ current increases with the windings’ rating by looking at Equation 13, the
proportion of azimuthal leakage flux will also increase. This leads to less accurate formulas as
the rating of transformers increases.
With the winding layout unspecified in the tender datasheet and the most accurate method,
Finite Element Modelling, beyond the scope of this master’s thesis, the primary cause of leak-
age reactance discrepancies remains elusive. Nevertheless, the significant differences between
our values and the tender datasheet are likely a result of incorrect winding modelling and the
approximation of the leakage flux in analytical formulas.

DISCUSSION
9
Discussion
The primary challenge in this analysis is to generalise a highly customised component. Starting
from 20 MVA, solutions become tailor-made, according to Siemens [152]. Drawing from their
prior designs, manufacturers can swiftly offer a preliminary design, incorporating broader
physical domains such as thermal and mechanical. In contrast, our model mainly addresses
the electromagnetic domain. Moreover, the limited availability of public data complicates ac-
quiring information for our model. For example, the building factor for a 5-limb core at high
power ratings is rare. This lack of data compels us to make simplifications, such as neglecting
the impact of frequency and flux density.
Some simplifications in the calculation, such as in the voltage per turn, seem suitable for the
early design stage of the transformer. In the verification of our model, the Kt value for the
2WT is within the range specified in Table 7, while the Kt values for the 3WT slightly exceed
the limits (0.58 and 0.73). Moreover, the overall layout of the active part of 2WT and 3WT pro-
posed is correct. The design has been validated by confirming the number of limbs, type, and
arrangement of windings for both types through a comparison with the tender datasheet.
One of the goals of this project is to develop a method that achieves the minimum computa-
tion time while maintaining accuracy. These two criteria can conflict as certain outputs may be
challenging to attain through straightforward formulas. For example, the different methods
for leakage reactance have been selected due to their simplicity and low computation time.
The high percentage deviation between our results and the tender datasheet in the leakage
reactance is expected from the selected methods and our winding modelling.
Firstly, our winding modelling has revealed high discrepancies in both load losses and leakage
reactance. This underscores the necessity for further refinement in the modelling of the wind-
ing. To investigate the underlying causes of these discrepancies, obtaining a detailed winding
layout from the manufacturer is crucial for comparison with our model.
Secondly, the assumptions made by these methods may no longer be acceptable for the trans-
formers under study. Indeed, it appears that many researchers prefer alternative methods,
such as Finite Element Modelling (FEM), when dealing with higher power and voltage rat-
ings. The highest power and voltage ratings applied in the selected approaches in a scientific
publication were observed at 31.5 MVA and 132/33 kV [74]. In this context, the Rogowski
method demonstrated a deviation of 0.41%, while Kapp’s method exhibited a 6.31% discrep-
ancy compared to FEM. Nevertheless, the design can significantly differ from the ratings under
study, even at this power and voltage level. Therefore, it is strongly recommended to employ
more sophisticated techniques, such as FEM, for the simulated designs in Python in order to
assess the accuracy of the formulas in our context.
Thirdly, another element not considered in this preliminary design can impact the leakage
reactance. Magnetic shunts are often used in large power transformers [153] to reduce leakage
reactance [154] and heat of metallic parts, such as clamping structures. An illustration of the
position of magnetic shunts is shown in the next picture:

The transformer’s leakage reactance has been observed to be directly proportional to the shunt’s

To enhance accuracy, it is recommended to use more up-to-date data, as the sources for the

is advisable to determine insulation clearances using the basic insulation level (BIL) outlined

However, finding a more modern empirical relation for the window space factor of large power
transformers appears more complex. Collaborating with a manufacturer could assist in devel-
oping updated curves for the window space factor.

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
This thesis thoroughly examines the preliminary design considerations for large 2WTs and
3WTs, with a specific focus on the material and geometry of the core and winding. This inves-
tigation enables the assessment of the dimensions of the core and winding and forms the basis
for estimating key parameters such as mass, cost, losses, and positive leakage reactance. How-
ever, the results obtained for leakage reactance using the chosen methods deviate significantly
from the manufacturer’s datasheet. These discrepancies stem from a combination of inaccura-
cies in winding modelling, precision in leakage flux representation within analytical formulas,
and the omission of magnetic shunts in our model. Consequently, the leakage reactance has
not been further incorporated into comparing of 2WTs and 3WTs. The comparison of the two
transformer types is thus based solely on mass, cost, and losses.
The results of 2WT and 3WT models and datasheets for mass and losses are in the same order
of magnitude, suggesting a general validation of the models. However, the core modelling
appears more precise than the windings. The core is heavier than the windings, and a reason-
ably close estimation of the active mass part implies a reliable assessment of the core mass.
Additionally, the no-load losses associated with the core are more accurate than the load losses
from the winding models for 2WT.
Based on the models’ results, 3WTs emerge as a compelling alternative to 2WTs. Switching
from 2WTs to 3WTs can lead to an overall improvement in the active part mass, efficiency,
and cost. However, the extent and presence of these improvements depend on the selection of
core material for the 3WT. Notably, a single 3WT or two 3WTs with CGO have the potential
to replace two or four 2WTs, maintaining mass but achieving reductions in losses and cost.
Moreover, one or two 3WTs with HIB can replace two or a minimum of three 2WTs with im-
provements in mass, cost, and losses.
The escalation of mass reduction with higher power ratings of transformers has been observed
when using 3WTs instead of 2WTs. As offshore wind farms move towards larger capacities
in the future, the appropriateness of 3WTs is forecasted to increase. Additionally, the expecta-
tions of increasing voltage levels on each side of the transformers have a marginal impact on
improvements. This ensures their continued relevance in the evolving landscape of offshore
wind farms.
Additionally, the mass reduction facilitated by 3WTs contributes to optimising foundation siz-
ing, resulting lighter overall foundation mass. The overall impact is a decrease of raw mate-
rials such as copper, steel and sand. This minimised use in raw materials positions 3WT as a
straightforward solution for environmental sustainability in OWFs. Many proposed alterna-
tives emphasise transitioning to recycled materials and integrating renewable energy into the
manufacturing process, requiring investments from various suppliers [158]. Compared to the
previous propositions, the mature production of 3WTs facilitates a streamlined approach to
implementing a solution for carbon neutrality.
