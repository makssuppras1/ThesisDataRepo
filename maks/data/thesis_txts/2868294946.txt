Abstract
Sewage sludge biochar could be a promising alternative to sewage sludge (SS) directly applied on agricultural
soils, as pyrolysis eliminates a wide variety of inorganic and organic pollutants, including PAHs and PFAS.
However, the legal basis of SS biochar use is still ambiguous in the EU due to concerns over its high (heavy)
metal content. This thesis investigated the leaching and sorption behaviour of SS and the subsequent biochar
for Al, As, Ca, Cd, Cr, Cu, Fe, K, Mg, Mn, Na, Ni, P, Pb, and Zn. The nutrients were included as they are
of interest considering the potential fertilizer effect and the eutrophication risk.
Figure 1: Visual abstract of the thesis project, depicting higher
equilibrium dissolved nutrient and metal concentrations in sewage
sludge suspension than in biochar ones.
The
aims

to

of
SS

biochar,

the

exposure

The respective solid-water distribution
coefficients (KD) were also determined,
both through direct measurements of
solid and aqueous equilibrium concentrations
and a novel method relying on a dilution
series, described by Ter Laak et al., 2005.
It was also investigated how much of the

The experiments revealed that biochar suspensions had reduced dissolved concentrations as compared
to SS suspensions. The concentrations in the biochar samples were mostly below regulatory limits, except
for arsenic. Although there were significant differences in the KD values obtained by the two methods, an
increase in coefficients from SS to biochar was observed for most elements in both experiments. It was found
that the reductions in dissolved concentration could be mostly explained by the increase in KD. However,
other mechanisms were also at play, such as volatilization during pyrolysis and precipitation. Overall, the
results unveil biochar as a safer alternative to SS as a soil amendment due to the reduced (heavy) metal
leaching. Nutrient leaching, however, is also reduced, which may call for supplementation with additional
fertilizers. These findings contribute to the scientific basis for future conclusive regulation of the use of sewage
sludge biochar in Denmark and the European Union.

Introduction
As the International Biochar Initiative (IBI) defines it, biochar (BC) is “a solid material obtained from
thermochemical conversion of biomass in an oxygen-limited environment" [1]. Building on 38000 years of a
tradition spanning several continents, biochar is a form of intentionally produced pyrogenic organic matter
(PyOM), which is a natural byproduct of activities involving fire use, such as cooking or metal production
[2]. Modern biochar production normally takes place in a controlled pyrolysis plant, under oxygen-limited
conditions and at higher and more constant temperatures, up to over 750°C. The initial biomass (referred to
as feedstock) for biochar can vary greatly, but some type of organic waste material is usually used, such as
woodchips, cereal husks, manure, food waste, or sewage sludge (SS) [2].
Biochar production through pyrolysis can be a promising solution for degrading contaminants such as
chlorinated hydrocarbons, pharmaceuticals and personal care products (PCPPs), antibiotic-resistance genes
(ARGs), pesticides, microplastics, and even the per- and poly-fluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) present in the
sludge [2]. Furthermore, pyrolysing organic waste matter has the benefit of capturing carbon dioxide while
producing a value-added product. Biochar has various potential uses across several sectors. A tremendous
research effort surrounds its soil improvement properties, both for improving crop yield in agriculture and for
the remediation of polluted soils [2]. Further uses include energy recovery or mixing it into animal feed [2] [3].
The focus of this thesis is biochar produced specifically from sewage sludge.
The physicochemical
characteristics of biochar can be heavily influenced by the feedstock of choice, and SS biochar does have some
unique properties compared to the other types [4]. It is typically richer in phosphorus, nitrogen, and potassium
than biochars of different origins, which makes it a good candidate for agricultural applications [4] [5].
However, a key safety concern is the remaining intrinsic contaminant concentration, leaching in soil and
subsequent uptake of these by biota when biochar is applied. Despite the promising removal rates of organic
contaminants, heavy metals are usually concentrated through pyrolysis, as most of them remain in the solid
matrix, which loses mass during the process. While certain metals (e.g. Cu, Zn) are essential for plant growth,
and may therefore be a welcome addition to agricultural soils, heavy metals and certain metalloids can pose a
serious health hazard even at relatively low water concentrations [2]. Similarly, the nutrients within biochar,
their bioavailability, and their retention in biochar are of interest - partly due to the soil-improving and plant
growth-enhancing potential, but also because of the possible eutrophication consequences.
The aim of this project, as further specified in Section 3, is to provide a more comprehensive picture of
sewage sludge biochar’s leaching of native nutrients and metals compared to the feedstock, focusing on the
change in sorption properties. With this in mind, our initial hypotheses regard the following:
• The dissolved concentrations of nutrients and heavy metals in biochar suspensions are expected to
be reduced compared to SS suspensions of the same dry weight, foreseeing that biochar has higher
distribution coefficient (KD) values due to the physicochemical transformations of the matrix during
pyrolysis.
• The dissolved concentrations of nutrients and heavy metals in biochar suspensions, at the experimental
dilutions and pH levels, do not exceed Danish regulations limits for inland surface water defined by
1

BEK 796 from 2023, "Bekendtgørelse om fastlæggelse af miljømål for vandløb, søer, overgangsvande,
kystvande og grundvand" [6].
• The found KD values are comparable to those determined in previous studies on biochar’s KD values
for the studied nutrients and metals.

Literature Review
2.1
Introduction to Biochar
2.1.1
The Origins of Biochar
The history of fire use by humans may date back to as far as 1.5 million years ago, and fire’s natural
byproduct, charcoal, has been used for several thousands of years for purposes such as drawing, smelting, or
treating medical symptoms [2] [7]. Therefore, the production and use of PyOM have accompanied much of
humanity’s history, and biochar is a new addition to this long-standing tradition. The term biochar was first
introduced around the year 2000 and has been used in its current meaning since 2006 [2]. Originally, biochar
was mainly discussed in the context of the Amazonian Terra Preta soils, but from around 2010 onwards, the
purposeful production and application of biochar has gained significant scientific and commercial interest [2].
Fast forward to the present day, biochar is the topic of a rapidly expanding volume of scientific literature,
several biochar production facilities are operated around the world, and even respected organizations and
certification schemes have been established such as the International Biochar Initiative and the European
Biochar Certificate (EBC) [2] [8] [9].
2.1.2
Production of Biochar
As mentioned before, biochar is produced from biomass through pyrolysis, defined as the thermochemical
decomposition of the feedstock under oxygen-limited or completely oxygen-free conditions [2]. Typically,
solid (biochar), liquid (bio-oil) and gaseous end products are obtained through pyrolysis, and by varying the
exact conditions, the balance can be shifted in either direction.
Slow, fast, and flash pyrolysis can be distinguished in terms of the duration of the pyrolysis process. As
its name suggests, slow pyrolysis takes the longest, usually between 5 and 30 minutes [10]. It operates with
temperatures typically no higher than 500°C and a heating rate of 0,1-1°C/s. This method is closest to the
traditional means of pyrogenic organic matter production and it favours the production of solids rather than
oil and gas [10]. On the other hand, fast and flash pyrolysis are heavily shifted towards bio-oil production,
with over 60% of the end products being liquid. Fast pyrolysis involves heating at a rate of 10-200°C/s to
an 800-1250 °C temperature maintained for 1 to 10 seconds. Flash pyrolysis differs from this not so much in
the achieved maximum temperature (900-1200 °C) but in the very high heating rate of up to over 1000 °C/s
and the very short duration of no more than a second (hence the name) [10].
Besides the duration of the pyrolysis process, other variables can also be adjusted to fit the desired
outcome. These include reactor type (e.g., fixed bed or fluidized bed) and position (horizontal or vertical),
2
==PAGE:10==
feedstock type and particle size, continuous or intermittent operation, heating method, reactor pressure, and
carrier gas flow [2] [10]. However, it was found that the three parameters influencing biochar yield the most
are the exact pyrolysis temperature, residence time, and feedstock type [10]. Overall, the composition of the
feedstock biomass may be the single most important factor determining biochar properties [11] [12].
A very wide range of biomass types can be suitable as feedstock for biochar production. A traditional
option is either soft- or hardwood biomass in various forms, such as bark, shavings, twigs, woodchips, or even
logs [2] [11]. Grasses and other non-woody leafy plants may also be used, such as switchgrass (Panicatum
virgatum) or water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes), for example. A large group of potential feedstocks is
agricultural crop wastes, such as straw and/or husks of cereal crops, as well as other biomass from the
cultivation of soybeans, sugarcane, peanuts, mangoes, etc. [11]. Finally, various types of biosolids may also
be used, including paper mill sludge, manure from animal farming, and sewage sludge from wastewater
treatment facilities [11].
2.1.3
Uses of Biochar
While cave art is sadly a less common pastime today than it once used to be, some of the most prevalent
uses of biochar are similar to those of general PyOM throughout history. For example, biochar is used as
soil amendment both to increase crop yields through improved nutrient availability and water retention,
as well as remediation at contaminated sites [2].
Another important use is energy production through
the pyrolysis process, which yields thermal energy and energy-carrying gases and bio-oil. Besides, energy
recovery can happen through the combustion of biochar in the presence of oxygen, although biochar exhibits
sub-optimal heating values [2] [3] [13]. Further currently applied or emerging uses include mitigating water
and air pollution, reducing carbon dioxide emissions, thermochemical catalysis, incorporation in concrete and
asphalt in construction, as well as enhancing feeds in aquaculture and terrestrial animal production [2] [3].
However, this thesis mainly bears in mind the agricultural use of the material due to the growing interest of
governments to improve nutrient circularity by using SS biochar.
2.2
Properties of Biochar
Biochars are characterized by different physical, structural, micro- and nanochemical, macromolecular, and
nutrient properties [2]. While some of these, such as sorption properties, as well as nutrient and heavy metal
content are later discussed in-depth, this section aims to give a general overview of biochar’s basic properties.
2.2.1
Particle Size, Specific Surface Area, and Porosity
The particle size of the produced biochar depends on both the particle size of the feedstock and the maximum
heating rate during pyrolysis. The higher heating rates are applied during pyrolysis, the more the particles
tend to shrink [2]. This also means that biochar particles from fast pyrolysis are typically smaller than those
that have undergone slow pyrolysis [2].
Biochars are highly porous materials and therefore have large surface areas, reaching up to hundreds
of m2g−1 [2].
Their porosity, again, is highly dependent on the heating intensity during pyrolysis and
3
==PAGE:11==
the feedstock material; more precisely, on its content of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin, which decompose
during pyrolysis to different extents [14]. The pores can be classified according to size as macropores above 50
nm diameter, mesopores between 50 and 2 nm, and micropores under 2 nm [2] [15]. The primary mechanism
of pore formation is the volatilization of the feedstock’s cells’ inner contents [16]. With further heating,
smaller pores may form in the remaining cell walls, and eventually, smaller pores may also collapse into each
other, forming larger pores [16]. The porosity thus tends to increase with temperature and residence time, at
least when pyrolysis temperature is between 350 and 650 °C [17]. Higher porosity, and especially the presence
of more macropores improves biochar’s water-holding capacity and its potential as a habitat for microbes.
Additionally, higher porosity biochar may also be useful as a sorbent for contaminants in environmental
remediation applications [17]. It is worth noting, however, that the increase in surface area compared to
the original feedstock is mainly observed in biochars produced at temperatures above the decomposition
temperature of cellulose, approximately 350 °C [15]. Furthermore, there is an upper bound at 600-700 °C,
after which pore formation ceases, and porosity and surface area reach their maximum [14].
2.2.2
Aromaticity, Surface Functional Groups and pH
Along with pore formation, the carbon structure of biochar also develops according to the pyrolysis temperatures
used. At low temperatures, there is no significant change in surface area and the largely amorphous (due to
the lignin and hemicellulose) organic carbon structure of the feedstock material is maintained [2]. However,
as temperatures increase, more and more of the amorphous organic carbon is replaced by aromatic ring
structures. These are first largely isolated, but with increasing pyrolysis temperatures they can start to form
sheets, then turbostratic crystallites. Eventually, biochar could achieve a perfectly crystalline structure once
all the amorphous carbon is eliminated. Nevertheless, this is rarely the practice case, and most biochars are
composites: made up of amorphous pyrogenic carbon, turbostratic crystallites, and pore space [2].
The aromatic rings, however, still have an important influence on biochar’s properties. Aromatic structures
are more thermodynamically stable, which can be advantageous when biochar is used for soil amendment or
remediation purposes [2] [14]. This is because aromaticity is one of the factors that influence electrostatic
interactions responsible for biochar’s adsorption properties (Section 2.4) [2] [12] [18]. Aromaticity depends
on both the feedstock and the pyrolysis temperature. The extent of aromaticity increases steeply especially
between 200 °C and 500 °C, with some further increase until reaching a maximum at 700-800 °C - some
biochars may become perfectly crystalline, made up entirely of aromatic structure at this stage [14]. The
reason behind the temperature dependence is that volatile compounds are lost, and hydroxyl, alkyl, and
ester groups are broken down to a greater extent with higher pyrolysis temperatures. This is also shown in
a reduction of H/C and O/C ratios in biochar [2] [19].
2.2.3
Elemental Composition
As mentioned above, the feedstock material loses hydrogen and oxygen as it transforms during pyrolysis, with
the resulting biochar having an increased carbon content compared to the feedstock [14]. The development
of carbon content is affected by the quantity of carbon in the feedstock, as well as the pyrolysis temperature.
Both the H/C and O/C ratios in biochar decrease steadily with treatment temperature, with the steepest
4
==PAGE:12==
drop occurring between 200°C and 400°C [14].
The highest pyrolysis temperature biochars may achieve
compositions of over 95% carbon. After carbon, the second-most abundant element in biochar is oxygen,
followed by hydrogen, and a relatively lower concentration of nitrogen [14]. For very high-carbon biochars,
oxygen content can be reduced to below 5% and hydrogen content to below 1-2% [14].
Besides these, several nutrients and metals can be found in biochars in lower quantities. Even though
their concentrations are relatively low, the release of these and potentially remaining organic pollutants into
the environment from field applications is a plausible concern.

2.3.2
Specific Properties
Given that sewage sludge biochar is the specific type studied in this thesis, it is noteworthy to mention
that there are additional properties specific to the biochar of this feedstock, all while the general properties
discussed before apply.
The pH of SS biochar is normally alkaline and is expected to fall in the pH 6.5 - 12.0 range [23]. Its surface
area and porosity ranges are in line with biochars of other feedstock [3], providing good water retention
capacity. In terms of elemental composition, SS biochar has a higher H/C ratio and a lower carbon content
than many other types of biochar [3]. It is richer in nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur, potassium, iron,
and calcium [11] [24] [25]) and minerals than most biochars originating from different sorts of biomass, which
is an advantage in agricultural use [5]. Additionally, relatively volatile metals like As, Hg, and Cd are partly
removed from the solids through pyrolysis. Nonetheless, there is an up-concentration of other heavy metals
because of the general mass reduction of the solid phase during pyrolysis. Consequently, leaching of these
pollutants from biochar is still a common concern, although several studies reported that biochar acts as a
sink rather than a source of heavy metals [2]. To gain further insight into this, sorption and its mechanisms
must be understood first.
2.4
Sorption
Sorption is a process in which a chemical with the potential to be sorbed, known as a sorptive, is removed
from the solution and becomes associated with a solid phase, called a sorbent, thus becoming a sorbate.
This process is environmentally significant due to its effects on transport processes, chemical bioavailability,
and degradation [26]. It also influences a change in the behaviour of the chemical depending on whether
it is in the gas or liquid phase, adsorbed, or absorbed [27]. Therefore, sorption is considered among the
most important chemical processes governing the retention of nutrients, pollutants and other chemicals in
the environment [28].
6
==PAGE:14==
The process can be subdivided into two types: 1) adsorption when the chemical accumulates at the
solid-liquid interface, attaching to the two-dimensional surface, and 2) absorption when the sorbate accumulates
inside a three-dimensional matrix [27].
Furthermore, when adsorption occurs and continues through the
formation of an expanding three-dimensional solid, it is called precipitation [28] [29].
This study works
with inorganic chemical species that get released into the water via solid-liquid interface interactions of the
high surface area sorbent. Therefore, adsorption is the prevalent type of sorption discussed both in further
literature and within this project.
As physicochemical processes, adsorption, absorption, and precipitation can be reversible or irreversible.
This reversal, known as desorption, is controlled by the same mechanisms that initiate sorption. However,
the kinetics may vary since the process involves overcoming the chemical (chemisorption) and physical
(physisorption) forces that initially caused the chemical to be sorbed. For example, physical forces such
as long-range electrostatic interactions are not very specific and have low adsorption energy. On the other
hand, chemical forces such as short-range electrostatic forces and covalent bonds possess higher adsorption
energy and are more specific [30].
2.4.1
Describing sorption
At equilibrium between a solid and a solution, the relationship between the total sorbate concentration, Cs
(e.g. mg kg−1), and the sorptive (solution) concentration, Cw (e.g. mg L−1) describes a sorption isotherm
[27]. This concept plays a vital role in understanding and predicting the sorption interaction between the
environment and the chemical. The isotherm can take a variety of forms, depending on the quantity, affinity,
and saturation of the binding sites both on the surface and within the sorbent (Figure 3) [27].
Figure 3: Some of the observed sorption isotherm behaviour. Adapted from Schwarzenbach et al. [27].
Linear sorption (Figure 3a) is the consequence of constant affinity of the binding sites throughout the
concentration range. This happens in the case of an abundance of sites due to low concentrations of sorptives,
or when (in the case of certain organic sorbates) the overall sorption is primarily governed by partitioning
into a uniform phase [27]. The second type of behaviour observed in most sorption processes is a saturation
response (Figure 3b and c). There is linear sorption at low concentrations, but as sorptive concentrations
increase, fewer binding sites remain available, or their affinity decreases, leading to a maximum capacity
point [27]. Finally, the superposition of two or more sorption behaviours can occur in heterogeneous blends
of sorbents, resulting in a mixed isotherm. For example, when an adsorbent with limited high-affinity sites
dominates sorption at low concentrations, followed by partitioning at higher concentrations (Figure 3d) [27].

Finally, to comprehend the factors influencing adsorption-desorption equilibrium concentrations of nutrients
and metals in the solid and liquid phases, it is essential to discuss the mechanisms by which the physical and
chemical properties of the sorbent interact with the sorptive.
2.4.2
Factors Affecting Sorption
2.4.2.1
Sorptive Size and Ionic Potential
As mentioned before, individual properties of the chemical, like sorptive size, make a difference in the
importance of each mechanism relative to the sorptive.
When dealing with inorganic sorptives that have identical charges and concentrations, the size of the ions
affects their sorption preference. Generally, a lower ionic potential (IP) for a given valence, which is the ratio
of the ion’s charge to its radius (IP = Z/r), typically enhances the ion’s affinity for a sorbent. This happens
for two reasons: the first is that it takes less energy to remove a water molecule from the hydration sphere
of a large ion compared to a smaller ion with the same charge. This gives larger ions an energetic advantage
in forming inner-sphere bonds with the sorbent. Second, as the ionic radius increases, the electron cloud
extends further from the nucleus, making it easier to re-shape and form bonds with the sorbent [28].
2.4.2.2
pH
Another factor that changes how sorption mechanisms work is pH. A change in solution pH modifies the charge
of sorptive and sorbent, vastly impacting the electrostatic interactions and ion exchange capacities [28]. For
example, a solution with lower pH and higher ionic strength generally reduces the sorption of metal cations
onto biochar by ion exchange, due to the increased competition from other cations [32].
Given all of this, it is apparent that metal and nutrient adsorption and removal are tied to increases or
decreases with pH. To visualize this, the point of zero charge (pHP ZC) is a useful concept to discuss. The
pHP ZC of biochar refers to the solution pH at which its surface net charge is zero. When solution pH is >
pHP ZC, biochar is negatively charged and binds to metal cations. On the other hand, when pH < pHP ZC,
biochar is positively charged and binds metal anions [32].
2.4.2.3
Ageing
The long-term presence of biochar in the soil can lead to two types of ageing, the first of which may cause
leaching resulting from increased contact time between pollutant, matrix, and solution. The second involves
significant changes in the physical and chemical properties of biochar, as well as its electrostatic charge
characteristics [2]. These alterations affect the primary mechanisms through which nutrients interact with
biochar. Over time, for example, the ageing of biochar can lead to increased hydrophilicity, enhancing its
ion exchange capacity [33]. Ageing also affects the competitive adsorption of metals, which is the reduction
of one sorptive’s affinity to the sorbent (diminishing KD) due to a more suitable species competing for the
same binding site. Furthermore, the modifications to the sorbent, introduced by ageing, alter the relative
contributions of each sorption mechanism [34].

2.4.3
Sorption Mechanisms
The sorption mechanisms between biochar, nutrients, and metals are diverse. Common mechanisms for both
nutrients and metals include electrostatic interactions, ion exchange, and complexation [12] [32]. For metals
specifically, precipitation and chemical reduction are additional mechanisms that influence sorption processes
[32]. On the other hand, nutrient sorption mechanisms include covalent bonding, partitioning, hydrophobic
interactions, and ligand exchange [2]. However, the importance of each mechanism varies significantly between
different inorganic sorptives because of their inherent properties. Adsorption is influenced by the sorptive
size / ionic potential, pH and ageing, as discussed above.
2.4.3.1
Electrostatic Interactions
To describe the mechanisms ruled by electrostatic interactions, it is useful to categorize the constituents
of these reactions according to charge. Sorptives can be divided into three groups: anionic, cationic, and
uncharged sorptives. Meanwhile, the binding sites on sorbents have the corresponding negative, positive and
uncharged categories [28]. Sorptives accumulate preferentially in the surface sites of the oppositely charged
sorbent, in an inner layer that several mathematical models, such as Helmholtz or Stern, can describe.
Meanwhile, a diffuse layer of both cations and anions shields this inner layer and undergoes diffusive exchange
with the bulk solution [28].
Sorbates in the inner layer that form ionic or covalent bonds with the sorbent by losing one or more
of their surrounding water molecules are considered inner-sphere adsorption complexes. Those ions in the
diffuse layer that retain their hydration spheres and are only associated by electrostatic interactions are
considered outer-sphere adsorption complexes [35]. Chemicals sorbed in the inner-sphere are more difficult
to desorb, thus they are less mobile than ions sorbed in outer-sphere complexes, which are vulnerable to
leaching [28] [30].
In biochar, the surface aromatic structures have delocalized π electrons that can form bonds with organic
pollutants and mineral nutrients by acting as electron donors or acceptors [2] [12] [18].
These aromatic
moieties, and other functional groups featuring C=O ligands and C=C bonds, with abundant π electrons
create π-π* donor-acceptor interactions that bind electron-deficient metal cations [36] [37] [38]. This is similar
for nutrients with a positive charge (e.g., NH+
4 ), that get attracted to the negatively charged surface in [2].
2.4.3.2
Ion Exchange
A sorbent can retain positively or negatively charged ions by exchanging them between the outer-sphere
complexes, or diffuse layer, and the bulk solution [30]. This is a nonspecific adsorption process named either
cation exchange capacity (CEC) or anion exchange capacity (AEC) [2] [12]. In general, CEC is related to
negatively charged acidic functional groups and π electrons. AEC, on the other hand, is associated with
positively charged sites on oxonium and basic functional groups [2].
Sorption of metals happens with the release of cations originally associated with the surface of the biochar,
including H+, Na+, Ca2+, K+, and Mg2+ [38] [39] [40]. For heavy metals, this process is also considered
outer-sphere complexation, which is weak, rapid, and reversible [12].
10
==PAGE:18==
2.4.3.3
Complexation
This type of adsorption is specific and involves a variety of chelate-forming surface functional groups from the
sorbent donating electrons to the metal ion, strongly immobilizing it [2] [12] [32]. This process is favoured by a
large surface area and an abundance of oxygen-containing functional groups [41]. For example, complexation
with phenolic, carboxyl (-COOH) and hydroxyl (-OH) groups has been proposed as the major mechanism
for Cd2+, Cu2+, Ni2+, and Pb2+ sorption in biochar [42].
2.4.3.4
Precipitation
In the context of biochar, precipitation can be described in two separate forms. The first and most important
for immobilization happens when intrinsic reactive species in the feedstock form embedded insoluble compounds
during pyrolysis. For example, calcite and hematite were observed to be formed after pyrolysis of sewage
sludge [2] [24].
The second form of this mechanism is a release of mineral components that alters the soil-water solution,
which may lead to precipitation of metal ions, turning them into bound or unbound insoluble compounds [12].
These minerals come in the forms of hydroxide, phosphate, sulfate, and carbonate salts, precipitating with
heavy metal ions [36]. Additionally, biochar increases soil pH, augmenting concentrations of hydroxyls that
react with cationic metals to form hydroxide precipitates [32]. As specific examples, high phosphate-containing
biochar is known to precipitate Pb [32] and other metals. Some studies report immobilization of Pb, Cu, Cd,
and Ni due to precipitation as insoluble phosphates [38].
2.4.3.5
Chemical Reduction
Biochar contains redox-active oxygen-containing functional groups, such as quinone and hydroquinone groups,
which play a crucial role in the redox transformation of sorbed metal species [12]. Quinone groups can act
as electron acceptors, while hydroquinone groups can serve as electron donors, creating a dynamic redox
environment. This redox activity not only aids in the stabilization and immobilization of heavy metals but
also can produce less harmful metal species by modifying their oxidation state.
2.4.3.6
Covalent Bonding
Moving from metal sorption to nutrient sorption, the basic concept of a covalent bond plays a fundamental
role. This chemical bond, where two or more atoms share electrons equally, is referred to in the context of
sorption mechanisms as inner-sphere adsorption. Therefore, it produces a stronger binding than electrostatic
interactions and is less prompt to leaching [2].
2.4.3.7
Partitioning
Partitioning is a process that applies mainly to organic sorptive and involves two main steps: diffusion
and solubilization. In the first step, sorptives move into either the pores or the organic matter found in the
11
==PAGE:19==
noncarbonized part of biochar. Then, in the second step, these compounds dissolve within the organic matter
matrix of the biochar, enhancing sorption [43] [44] [45].
2.4.3.8
Hydrophobic Interactions
Hydrophobicity is the phenomenon where nonpolar molecules tend to cluster together in aqueous solutions
to minimize their interaction with polar water molecules. Hydrophobic interactions can facilitate nutrient
translocation to plants by biochar. This is due to its porous structure and high surface area, which can
adsorb nonpolar organic compounds and nutrients [2]. These interactions help retain nutrients within the
biochar matrix, reducing leaching and making them available for plant uptake over time.
2.4.3.9
Ligand Exchange
A ligand is a molecule that binds to a central metal atom, forming a coordination complex. For instance,
organic matter released from biochar can act as organic ligands [46]. These ligands can undergo chemical
reactions and be exchanged with other ligands like ammonium or phosphate, leading to nutrient sorption by
biochar [2].
2.5
Heavy Metals and Metalloids in Biochar
As mentioned before, heavy metals can concentrate in (sewage sludge) biochar, proving to be an obstacle to
its widespread agricultural application [47], due to their potential to harm humans and the environment.
Cadmium, chromium, copper, mercury, nickel, lead, and zinc are the most toxic and environmentally
hazardous. Similarly, aluminium and the metalloids arsenic and antimony are a concern due to their toxicity
to plants and humans [2]. However, some of these, such as copper, zinc, and nickel are also essential to plants in
low concentrations. Therefore, their presence as a beneficial plant growth supplement or as a phytotoxicant
is a concentration-sensitive question [2]. Furthermore, while heavy metals are in general concentrated in
biochars by a factor of up to 2.5-3.5, there are exceptions to the rule.
Arsenic, mercury, and cadmium
present in SS are not expected to concentrate significantly from pyrolysis temperatures of 400-500°C. At
higher temperatures (>600°C), these elements are likely to volatilize to a great extent [47] [48] [49] [50]. This
section will provide an overview of the presence, characteristics, and interactions of the (heavy) metals and
metalloids (selected for this thesis) in SS biochar.
2.5.1
Factors Influencing Heavy Metals Sorption and Content in Biochars
While numerous organic contaminants can be efficiently removed during pyrolysis provided that the temperature
is sufficient, most metals typically get concentrated in biochar or are, at best, partially removed in high-temperature
conditions.
This implies that for the overall heavy metal concentrations in biochar, feedstock type is a
crucial variable, as most of the original heavy metal content will remain in the solid fraction. Furthermore,
maximum pyrolysis temperature can significantly influence the concentrations of certain metals, such as the
aforementioned cadmium, arsenic, and mercury [47] [49] [50] [51]. Besides these, the presence and type of

desorption [47].

2.5.2
Mobility, Bioavailability and Toxicity
Combining the theory from above and literature results, the mobility of heavy metals in biochar - or the
percentage of total content that can leach out of the solid - depends on the biochar’s feedstock material, the
maximum pyrolysis temperature, the properties of the metal in question, and the pH during the leaching
experiment [47] [55] [56] [57]. Besides these, other factors that are harder to control in laboratory settings
can play a role, such as chemical and mechanical weathering or microbial interactions [47].
When in sediments or water-saturated soils, heavy metals and metalloids are partitioned between four
"compartments": they are either found in the solid phase, bound to the surface of the solid phase, form organic
and inorganic metal complexes in solution, or are present as free ions in solution [2] [58] [59]. Out of these,
only the free metal ions in solution can be taken up by living organisms, or are, in other words, bioavailable. [2]
[58] [59]. These four phases typically reach equilibrium relatively soon after shifts or disturbances. However,
this also means that plant uptake of heavy metal ions in solution, for example, can lead to further desorption
of heavy metals into the liquid phase [2].
Biochar leachate containing metals that are also micronutrients (e.g. Cu, Fe, Zn), along with the other
nutrients, could aid plant growth and enhance crop yields.
However, due to variations in heavy metals
leaching from biochars based on the experimental factors mentioned above, there is no guarantee that all
biochars will be beneficial for plants under all conditions - they may even be phytotoxic due to excessive
heavy metals leaching.
On the topic of ecological risk, it was found that cadmium poses the highest risk in several types of biochar
leachate, despite its overall low concentrations [55] [56].

2.5.3
(Heavy) Metals and Metalloids Present in Biochar
2.5.3.1
Aluminium
Aluminium may be present in SS biochars at high concentrations if alum or other aluminium-containing
flocculant were used in the WWTP processing the feedstock sludge [47] [61].
While not a heavy metal, Al present as Al3+ in the soil is highly phytotoxic.
Fortunately, biochars
were found to have a high association capacity for aluminium [62]. The dominant mechanisms of aluminium
adsorption to biochar are binding to the organic fraction and complexation with oxygen-containing functional
groups [63]. Al3+ can bind to the −COO−and −O−groups in biochar, forming Al(OH)2+ and/or Al(OH)+
2
[62] [63]. For aged, oxidized biochars, however, electrostatic interactions between Al3+ and the negatively
charged surface were found prevalent [63].
2.5.3.2
Chromium
For chromium, biochar concentrations of 15 to 1313 mg/kg dry solids have been found, covering a wide range
and varying highly depending on location and biochar type [60]. It is a low volatility heavy metal, meaning
that most of the Cr from the feedstock remains in the solid phase after pyrolysis [60]. In natural waters,
chromium is most often found in the valence states of Cr(III) and Cr(VI), even though chromium’s actual
valence states range from -2 to 6 [64]. Cr(VI), in particular, is highly soluble in water, which is a concern
due to its carcinogenic and ecotoxic properties [4] [65] [66]. Cr(III), on the other hand, is not only about
300 times less toxic but is also a micronutrient that may play a role in carbohydrate and lipid metabolism
in humans [4] [64] [65]. Nevertheless, Cr(III) disposal can still pose an environmental risk, as it can also be
oxidized to Cr(VI) under environmental conditions [4] [64]. The proposed sorption mechanisms of Cr(III)
by biochar are the following: 1) complexation with functional groups that contain oxygen, 2) electrostatic
16
==PAGE:24==
attraction to the negatively charged biochar surface, and 3) cation exchange [4]. For sorbing Cr(VI), the
two main mechanisms are 1) electrostatic attraction, and 2) reduction to Cr(III), which then undergoes
complexation with surface groups of the biochar [4]. For Cr(VI), the second mechanism was found to be
dominant in biochar, especially in the case of biochars produced at low temperatures (250-350°C) that are
rich in oxygen-containing functional groups [4]. On the other hand, native chromium content in biochars is
expected to increase with pyrolysis temperature [50] [60].
2.5.3.3
Manganese
Even though it is a micronutrient, manganese occurring as Mn2+ in natural waters can present a human
health risk at high concentrations [67]. Dangerously high concentrations in drinking water reservoirs can even
happen naturally if manganese gets released from the sediment when anoxic conditions are present in the
bottom waters [67]. It is proposed that remediation by Mn adsorption to biochars could be viable but there
is limited information on the exact mechanisms involved [67]. Zhao et al. found precipitation and cation
exchange to be dominant but it depended on the biochar type which of the two mechanisms accounted for
most of the Mn sorption [67].
2.5.3.4
Nickel
Nickel has four oxidation states (+1, +2, +3, +4) but it mainly occurs as Ni2+ [68]. Ni is a suspected
carcinogen and it is a low-volatility heavy metal, mostly remaining and accumulating in the solid phase
through pyrolysis regardless of pyrolysis temperature [47] [60] [69].
Higher nickel concentrations can be
observed in food waste biochars in particular, possibly because metal items are sometimes accidentally
discarded together with food [47].
When used as a soil amendment, even adding 0.5-2% biochar to soil
has been shown to effectively reduce the bioavailability of Ni [68]. Ion exchange and electrostatic interactions
are suggested to play an important role in the adsorption of Ni to biochar, but complexation and precipitation
have also been observed [68]. A newer study by Wang et al. identified precipitation to be the main mechanism
of Ni immobilization by biochar, however, the prevalence of the exact mechanisms seems to be still generally
debated for nickel [70].
2.5.3.5
Arsenic
Arsenic is a metalloid that is toxic by inhalation and ingestion, as well as ecotoxic, and it may also have
carcinogenic and mutagenic properties [71]. Arsenic can occur in various oxidation states (-3, 0, +3, +5),
in natural aqueous environments mostly as As(III) - arsenite - and As(V) - arsenate [4]. Arsenic, as one of
the most volatile elements from the considered metal(loid)s starts to volatilize at approximately 500°C in the
presence of organic carbon [47] [72]. However, Ca or Fe, if present, can react with As, and form non-volatile
arsenic-containing substances such as Ca(AsO2)2 or FeAsO4 [73]. It has been proposed that the addition
of CaO and Fe2O3 during pyrolysis inhibits arsenic volatilization and traps it in the char, decreasing the
contamination of the oil and gas phases [73]. On the other hand, in soils, dissolved organic carbon (DOC)
may compete with As for sorption sites [74]. Additionally, phosphate and As(V) are chemically analogous
17
==PAGE:25==
and can be taken up from the soil through the same transport system in plants [2] [75]. These can result in
increased As leaching when the concentrations of DOC and/or P in solution are high [2].
In terms of arsenic sorption to biochar, complexation and electrostatic interactions are the dominant
mechanisms while precipitation and reduction play a lesser role [4]. Just as for chromium, biochars produced
at lower temperatures and rich in oxygenated functional groups tend to be more suitable for sorbing As [4].
2.5.3.6
Cadmium
According to certain sources, cadmium is more volatile than lead, but less than arsenic during pyrolysis, and
it is expected to start volatilizing at temperatures above 500-650°C [73] [5] [47] [72]. Other sources observed
that the thermal volatilities of heavy metals at 850°C are related as Cd = Hg > Zn > As > Pb > Mn >
Ni > Cr > Cu and that cadmium starts volatilizing significantly at around 400°C already [76]. Around
850°C, up to 100% cadmium emission ratios can be expected during either fast or slow pyrolysis, with a
slight variation depending on the feedstock [77].
In the environment, cadmium is mostly present as Cd2+ in aqueous solution, and at high pH, especially
at pH>11, it occurs in a hydrolyzed form [4]. In terms of sorption, the sorption of Cd (and Zn) can be
heavily affected by the presence of competitively sorbing metals Cu and Pb [78]. For cadmium, the decrease
in sorption in the presence of competitive metals can be as high as five-fold [78]. The exact mechanism of
its sorption to biochar was found to depend on the cation exchange capacity of the biochar. For high CEC
biochars, cation exchange seems to be the dominant mechanism of Cd sorption, while for low CEC biochars
complexation was found to be prevalent [4]. However, for biochars produced at low (200-350°C) temperatures
that are rich in soluble phosphate and carbonate (e.g. manure-based), precipitation could be the primary
sorption mechanism [4].
2.5.3.7
Lead
Lead is a substance of very high concern (SVHC) due to its reprotoxic and ecotoxic properties as well as its
capacity to cause organ damage upon repeated exposure [79]. While not as volatile as As and Cd, lead is also
one of the more volatile heavy metals. It is expected to start volatilizing at 700-850°C, the exact temperature
is likely also dependent on the biochar type [47] [72]. During sewage sludge pyrolysis at 850°C, for example,
a lead emission factor of up to 60-90% could be observed [77]. Biochars typically have a high sorption affinity
for lead, which relative to other heavy metals can usually be described as Pb > Cu > Zn > Cd > Ni [78] [80].
This could, in principle, indicate that biochar amendments could help remediate lead-contaminated soils, but
it has been found that the application rates for this would have to be well above 1-2% [78].
As mentioned in the context of cadmium sorption, Pb and Cd sorb competitively, and their sorption
behaviours are similar.
Typically mainly cation exchange, and, to a slightly lesser extent, complexation
account for most of the lead sorption to biochars [4]. However, as for Cd, in the case of low-temperature
produced biochars with higher concentrations of soluble phosphate and carbonate, precipitation becomes
dominant [4].

2.6
Nutrients in Biochar
As previously stated, pyrolysis of SS eliminates organic pollutants and produces biochar that retains macro
and micronutrients, compared to other feedstocks. This could make the char useful for agriculture and land
reclamation because said nutrients are eventually released. However, adding too much of a nutrient can
be detrimental to agriculture and the environment due to their toxicity in higher concentrations (e.g. Cu,
Zn) [81], or their potential for eutrophication (e.g. PO3−
4 , NO−
3 , NH+
4 ) [2]. For this reason, N, P, K, S, Ca,
and Mg are frequently measured in studies. However, less information is available for Cu, Fe, Mn, as well as
Zn, and almost nothing is known about B, Cl, and Mo [2]. This section will give a general overview of the
nutrients studied in this project, as well as their interaction with biochar.
2.6.1
Nutrients in Biochar
2.6.1.1
Nitrogen
Nitrogen content in biochar is low as higher pyrolysis temperatures lead to lower N content due to volatilization
starting at 400°C [82]. The remaining nitrogen can be preserved by forming heteroaromatic ring systems with
carbon [83] [84], but it becomes unavailable due to the strong covalent bonding [2]. However, according to
Ippolito et al. 2020 [11], biochar from manure and biosolids has 2.5 times more total N than biochar from
wood feedstock. Additionally, manure/biosolid biochar is higher in NH+
4 content [11]. This is because sludge
tends to have a higher protein content than plant feedstocks [2].
A portion of the total N is available in the form of ammonia adsorbed to the biochar surface [85], or as
nitrate, which is released by ion exchange [2]. This means that, at least in the short term, a higher CEC
promotes higher N retention [86]. In the case of NH+
4 , it has the possibility of undergoing ligand exchange or
surface complexation with negatively charged surface functional groups that facilitate cation adsorption [87].
As for NO−
3 , it is also reported to be retained in the pores, increasing its residence time [88] [89].
2.6.1.2
Phosphorus
There are elevated levels of total P and extractable P in manure and biosolid biochar compared to other
feedstocks [11]. This is because humans and animals consume excess phosphorous that gets excreted and
accumulated in manure and biosolids [2]. The total and available concentrations of this non-volatile nutrient,
along with potassium and calcium, concentrate with higher pyrolysis temperature due to mass loss from the
feedstock [11]. Nevertheless, P compounds volatilize at 760°C, and there is a sharp decline in concentration
for biochars pyrolysed at >800°C [90].
Even though this nutrient has elevated concentrations in biochar, the material may still reduce soluble P
leaching (and therefore loss) in soils. Adsorption of phosphorus happens via electrostatic interactions, ligand
exchange, or covalent bonding [91] [92], and increases the maximum P retention of soil. Once phosphorus
has been adsorbed, or when dealing with intrinsic concentrations, it is reported that a slow release of the
nutrient is caused because this content has intermediate stability, or due to the adsorbed P not forming
19
==PAGE:27==
a stable crystalline phase [93] [94].
Furthermore, ageing may induce the formation of organo-biochar or
organo-mineral-biochar complexes that retain and exchange soluble P [95] [96].
In the case of insoluble P, biochar may influence phosphorous precipitation by altering pH, or by sorbing
chelating molecules that form complexes with metal ions that otherwise precipitate P [92]. Also, the release
of some cations from biochar may induce precipitation. In alkaline soils, phosphorous precipitation occurs in
the presence of Ca2+, while in acidic conditions it is regulated by Al3+ and Fe3+ [2].
2.6.1.3
Potassium, Calcium, and Magnesium
Most biochars have relatively high K content as they form part of the ash fraction. Even though manure
and biosolid biochar do not have the highest content of this nutrient, they can have elevated potassium
depending on the diet of humans and animals. This, and the soluble salts Ca and Mg, get excreted when
they are consumed in excess [2]. Similarly to N and P, manure/biosolid biochar shows higher calcium and
magnesium content compared to other feedstocks [11]. Additionally, these elements are volatilized at equal
or higher temperatures as P [90], meaning they concentrate in the matrix. However, only 20% of the K is
extractable, which is lower than the other types of biochar [2] [11].
The previously mentioned salts are allocated in the pores, connected to acidic functional groups and
chelates, or bound to ion exchange sites [97].
This means that in the presence of water, the nutrients
solubilize out of the matrix or undergo ion exchange into the solution [2].
2.6.1.4
Micronutrients
Manure and biosolids biochars are reported to have higher total concentrations of micronutrients (Fe, Cu, Zn,
B, Mn, Mo, Co, Cl), because of incomplete assimilation from animal diets, or waste from municipal/industrial
sources. Of these, Cu, Mn, and Zn show greater available concentrations compared to other biochar types,
but Fe shows less availability [11].
Iron
Iron content in SS feedstock may be high, independently from the diet, due to the wide use of iron salts
for flocculation, P removal, and sulphide control [24] [25]. Iron present in the feedstock is observed to form
stable oxides like hematite after pyrolysis [24]. Meanwhile, the remaining Fe(II) species undergo oxidation
with ageing to form Fe(III) (hydr)oxides [2]. This element is of interest in this context due to its effects on
biochar physicochemical properties. Li et al. [24] report, in the presence of iron, a decrease in CEC as well
as availabilities of nutrients, especially P, Ca, and Mg.
Copper
Usually present as Cu2+, this micronutrient exhibits a toxic potential to both terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems
[2] [98], raising interest in materials such as biochar, that show immobilization of copper and other heavy
metals [38]. The main mechanism is cation exchange, observing proton release when this metal gets immobilized.
But, at higher carbonized fractions, cation exchange gets outweighed by other mechanisms [38]. For example,
20
==PAGE:28==
copper and other transition metals undergo inner-sphere covalent bonding caused by d-orbital electron sharing
with surface O- and N-donor ligands [99].
Zinc
While zinc (present in aqueous solution as Zn2+) is an essential micronutrient for both humans and plants,
it is also ecotoxic and capable of causing acute and chronic damage to aquatic organisms [2] [100]. This
highlights how crucial it is to ensure a healthy balance in terms of environmental zinc concentrations in
agriculture and beyond.
SS biochar was found to be a relatively good sorbent for zinc (and lead) in aqueous solution [101]. For
example, Shah et al. found that up to over 95% of the Zn2+ can be removed from a 50 mg/L zinc solution in
less than 90 minutes by adding 25 mg/L SS biochar [101]. The percentage of removal was, however, heavily
pH-dependent; a sharp increase in % of Zn2+ removed was observed between pH 2 and pH 8, with a further
slight increase at an even higher pH [101]. The main mechanism of zinc sorption by biochar is cation exchange,
followed by precipitation to a lesser extent in biochars based on SS or animal biomass [101] [102]. In the
cases of plant biomass biochars, however, precipitation, and complexation were found to be dominant [103].
2.7
Biochar Effects on Soil
Given its structural properties, biochar application to soil has been reported to improve the water holding
capacity [2]; consequently, typically an improvement of the available water content (AWC) is expected. For
example, an average of 28.5% has been observed with application rates between 30 and 70 t ha−1 in coarse
soils [104]. Furthermore, the soil structure gets improved by lowering bulk density, improving aggregation [2],
and improving infiltration capacity [105]. This suggests that biochar can improve rain-fed agricultural crop
yields and reduce costs in irrigated systems, but more long-term studies need to confirm these [2].
Biochar has a liming and buffering effect when used as a soil amendment [2], and it is an important
mechanism explaining how biochar increases crop yield [106]. Agricultural soils tend to acidify due to nutrient
and proton exchange between crop and soil. Therefore, an additive such as lime or biochar is needed to
maintain an appropriate pH. However, adding these materials to soils on the higher end of the optimal pH
spectrum may lead to detrimental effects by creating an excessively basic environment [2].
There is also interest in the effects that biochar has on soil microorganisms and fungi. However, the
complexity of the system and interactions leads to no unanimous conclusion on whether it is beneficial or
not [2].
Finally, it is crucial to highlight that ageing plays a major role in modifying the properties of biochar,
leading to a different physicochemical response in the soil. In literature, it is reported that generally [2],
ageing in soil leads to lower pH [107], higher hydrophilicity [108], an increase in CEC [109], a decrease in
total C [107] and an increase in the ratio of aromatic to aliphatic C [110].
2.7.1
Interaction with Nutrients
Biochar’s retention of nitrogen does not have enough longevity for it to function as a slow-release fertilizer
[111]. Added to this, the intrinsic low concentrations of the nutrient mean that additional fertilizer is to be
21
==PAGE:29==
used for better crop yield [2]. Applying biochar can result in both the immobilization and mineralization
of N by microbes. However, the extent to which biochar affects the microbial N transformations in the soil
is challenging to predict because it is influenced by several variables, including soil redox potential [112],
cropping systems, fertilization types, and biochar properties [2].
There is variability in the availability of P when biochar is applied to soil. This is because it depends on
endogenous concentrations, biochar alteration of soil properties, adsorption, and pretreatments [2]. Regardless,
as with nitrogen, biochar may play a better role when applied in conjunction with phosphorus fertilizers.
Finally, as mentioned in section 2.6, the elevated concentrations of total K and, subsequently available
K, are beneficial for plant growth. In coarse-textured soils, biochar can help with the retention and reduced
leaching of K via ion exchange [2] [113]. In most other soils, however, a high content of these nutrients does
not improve retention and may even promote leaching [2] [114] [115].
2.7.2
Interaction with Heavy Metals
In the report by Yuan et al. [116], biochar was shown to decrease the bioavailability of heavy metals in
acidic, coarse, and medium-textured soils. However, it was increased for alkaline and fine-textured soils. The
authors also reported that heavy metal bioavailability decreased within less than a year, but it increased in
long-term ageing experiments. Furthermore, they state that biochar addition stabilized heavy metals in pot
experiments, but mobilized them in the field, due to exogenous factors like acid rain. This ties in with their
conclusion that pH changes are the primary factor influencing heavy metal bioavailability in biochar-amended
soils [116].
It is important to note that the environmental fate of metals can not be described only by sorption.
There is a variety of changes that metal ions can undergo when exposed to the environment. For example,
in agricultural soils they can undergo hydrolysis, group in colloidal particles, form organic or inorganic
complexes, adsorb, precipitate, or coprecipitate with other metal oxides, regardless of the presence of biochar
[2] [117]. As for the metals present within the biochar, they may experience acid, ligand, and reductive
dissolution, similar to other minerals in soil [2].
In summary, biochar amendment has the potential to improve agricultural soil or remediate contaminated
soils. Nevertheless, the intrinsic nutrient and metal content of biochar should be kept in mind, as well as
external factors such as pH which may worsen the state of the soil. This level of complexity is part of the
reason why the regulations for using SS biochar are still dubious.
2.8
Regulations on Sewage Sludge Biochar
The European regulatory landscape around biochar is yet to be fully developed. For now, it can be difficult
to navigate, as a lot can depend on local regulations - even on the municipality level - and the relevant
legal documents may not even directly mention biochar. It is also heavily dependent on not only pyrolysis
conditions criteria but also the feedstock materials used. The use of sewage sludge biochar, in particular, can
appear as a grey zone. While SS is rich in recoverable nutrients and its use on agricultural soil is permitted
in some countries, SS biochar is often surrounded by greater precaution than other biochar types.
22
==PAGE:30==
The EU regulation 2019/1009 on the permitted agricultural fertilizers states that pyrolysed SS can not
be used either in itself or as a fertilizer component on agricultural soils in the EU [118]. It acknowledges,
however, that pyrolysis products of plant or animal biomass meet sufficient quality criteria as fertilizers [118].
As of early 2025, Denmark still lacks a general legal basis for biochar for agricultural use [119]. The
legal document that could, at least partly, cover SS biochar is BEK 1001 from 2018 (Bekendtgørelse om
anvendelse af affald til jordbrugsformål), which does not mention biochar but describes the legal use of SS on
agricultural soils. According to the document, SS from public WWTPs (or private ones that handle household
wastewater) can be applied on agricultural soil without special pre-approval provided that it first undergoes
sufficient sanitization [120]. By this, they mean thermal treatment at ≥70°C for at least an hour, and these
criteria could be, in principle, met by biochar production by pyrolysis [120]. However, it is important to
point out that it does not directly consider the pyrolytic transformation of SS into biochar here, it simply
states the requirements for the safe use of SS. Additionally, it lists threshold concentrations for selected heavy
metals and organic pollutants in the dry waste matter (including dry SS).
Paradoxically, the use of biochar based on several other feedstocks, such as plant biomass, is in general
not permitted in Denmark, only in certain cases, after acquiring a municipal environmental permit [119].
BEK 931 from 2024 (Bekendtgørelse om jordbrugsvirksomheders anvendelse af gødning) on fertilizer use in
agriculture, on the other hand, lists biochar as an exception from restrictions on fertilizers but it does not
discuss biochar any more in depth [121].
Lastly, the use of SS biochar on agricultural soils in Denmark would also be affected by the limit values
on phosphorus use. Application of an average of 30 kg P/ha/year is permitted on actively used Danish
agricultural soils, which would translate into approximately 800 kg of SS biochar per hectare per year [119].
In conclusion, a clear and more complete regulatory background is desirable for SS biochar on both the
EU level and in Denmark, as the ambiguity of the current situation discourages biochar production and use
despite its potential advantages. The project this thesis is part of has the objective of supporting these future
regulatory decisions with environmentally relevant data on SS biochar’s sorption and leaching properties.
3
Aim
The general objective of this project is to study the leaching and sorption behaviour of sewage sludge and the
subsequent SS biochar in aqueous suspensions. All experiments rely on the native concentrations of nutrients
and metals in the solid matrix, which seeks to emulate environmentally relevant conditions similar to how
the material is practically used. This means that no spiking of chemicals is needed, resulting in relatively
low water concentrations that enable the assumption of linearity of the relationship between solid and water
concentrations Cs and Cw. Therefore, the change in leaching from SS and from biochar is investigated, and
the distribution coefficient (KD) of the nutrients and metals for both SS and biochar are determined and
compared through two different methods.
The specific aims of the project are summarized as follows:
• Determine appropriate conditions to reach equilibrium in sewage sludge and sewage sludge biochar
suspensions. This includes time, pH and solid-to-water ratios.
23
==PAGE:31==
• Experimentally determine and compare the dissolved metal and nutrient concentrations in the suspensions
at equilibrium between the water and the solid phase.
• Determine the distribution coefficients in biochar and sludge for selected metals and nutrients, and
assess to what extent the change in KD values affects the dissolved concentrations (and to what extent
are they due to different reasons). KD values will be calculated from dissolved concentrations in a
series of suspensions at different dilution factors, as well as through the direct method of measuring
concentrations both in the solids and the water at equilibrium.
4
Materials and Methods
4.1
Terminology
Dissolved concentration: The concentration of metals or nutrients in the liquid phase of samples, without
distinguishing between freely dissolved ions, complexes in solution, and metals and nutrients sorbed to very
small suspended solid particles. The context of this thesis mainly refers to the aqueous concentrations from
the equilibrated SS or biochar suspensions, which were determined by ICP-MS, ICP-OES, TOC, or CFA
analyses.
Native concentration: The concentration of metals or nutrients found in the solid compartments described
in Section 2.5.2. This includes irreversibly bound chemicals embedded within the solid matrix or bound
by covalent bonds to the surface, as well as reversibly sorbed ones that may dissolve into water.
Exposure reduction: Reduction of the dissolved nutrient or metal concentrations in water at equilibrium
with the solid (CH2O⇄Matrix), most of which can become available for plant uptake, leached to groundwater,
or become run-off to surface waters, affecting flora, fauna, and humans.
4.2
Sampling
Samples of sewage sludge and SS biochar were received and ready for use. The SS had been frozen in an
air-tight glass container and then defrosted in a refrigerator shortly before the experiments. The biochar was
stored in a closed plastic container in a refrigerator.
The dewatered activated sludge collected at the inlet of the pyrolysis plant originated from the Fårevejle
dewatering plant, Denmark, operated by Odsherred Forsyning. The Fårevejle plant receives SS from the
Rørvig, Havnebyen, and Abildøre wastewater treatment plants.
In all three WWTPs, the aluminium
chloride-containing PAX-215 is used for phosphorus removal.
AquaGreen’s fully automated commercial pyrolysis plant is located by the Fårevejle treatment plant. The
SS biochar had been collected in a carefully time-matched manner, ensuring that the sampled biochar was
a product of the sampled ingoing SS. During AquaGreen’s process, the dewatered sludge is first dried and
then heated up to approximately 600°C within the pyrolysis plant, which is followed by 10 minutes at above
650°C [50]. The residence time during the slow pyrolysis is approximately 20 minutes and the total duration
24
==PAGE:32==
of the process from inlet to finished product takes 2.5-3-5 hours [50]. The produced biochar was not pressed
into uniformly shaped pellets.
4.3
Analytical methods
Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) elemental concentrations of Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu,
Zn, As, Cd, Pb, and Y were analysed by ICP-MS (7850x, Agilent Technologies) with an internal standard
of Yttrium. The samples had been acidified to 2% HNO3 and 1% HCl (v/v%) before the analysis. The
instrument was equipped with a platinum-tipped skimmer and sample cones, a double-pass Scott spray
chamber – operated at 2 °C – as well as a Micromist nebulizer. Some of the elements - Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni,
Cu, Zn, and As – were analyzed in He collision mode with a 5 mL/min helium gas flow, while the rest of
the elements – Cd, Pb and Y – were analyzed in no gas mode. Data processing was carried out through the
MassHunter 5.2 Workstation Software (v. D.01.02.).
Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES) elemental concentrations of Na,
Mg, K, Ca, Al, and P were determined by ICP-OES (Avio 200, PerkinElmer) with an internal standard of
Yttrium. The detector of the instrument was operated in radial mode, and detection was achieved at 589.592
nm for Na, 285.213 nm for Mg, 766.490 nm for K, 317.933 nm for Ca, 396.153 nm for Al, 213.617 nm for P,
and 238.204 nm for Fe. The samples had been acidified to 2% HNO3 and 1% HCl (v/v%) before the analysis.
The instrument was equipped with a baffled, cyclonic spray chamber as well as a Micromist nebulizer. Data
processing was carried out through the SyngisticTM for ICP Software v. 2.0 from Perkin Elmer.
Continuous flow analysis (CFA) was carried out with an automated Skalar SAN ++ Continuous Flow
Analyzer. The instrument uses segmented flow analysis (SFA) and colourimetric methods to determine the
concentrations of NH3, PO4, NO2, and NOx in aqueous solutions.
The 8-12 mL liquid samples in 12
mL plastic vials were analysed soon after sample preparation, using the SAN ++ instrument’s 15-minute
automated program. Data processing was carried out using the Skalar SAN ++ FlowAccess V3 software.
Total organic carbon (TOC) and total bound nitrogen (TNb) sample analysis was carried out by a
multi-N/C 3100 flow injection TOC analyser (Analytic Jena). The combustion temperature was 750-800
°C with O2 as carrier gas at a flow rate of 160 mL/min. The values of total carbon (TC) and inorganic
carbon (IC) were determined by analysis with a non-dispersive infrared spectroscopy (NDIR) detector, and
from these TOC was derived following the formula TOC = TC −TIC. TNb analysis was carried out by a
chemiluminescence detector (CLD). Data processing was done through multiWin 4.13.
4.4
Experiment 1: Preliminary Experiment
This experiment aimed to provide a basic understanding of the leaching of native metals and nutrients from
SS biochar at different pH levels and time steps, all in comparison to SS. The elements chosen to analyse
were Al, As, Ca, Cd, Cr, Cu, Fe, K, Mg, Mn, Na, Ni, P, Pb, and Zn. These were selected as they were
expected to be present at detectable dissolved concentrations under the experimental conditions and were
either important plant nutrients or pollutants of concern (or both).

phase ratios were chosen in a way so that the dissolved concentrations would likely be low enough to be

Data Treatment and Presenting Results
Experimental results were received and organized using Microsoft Excel, where means and standard deviations
of triplicates were calculated using the internal functions. The means of triplicates were plotted in all graphs
with the standard deviations featured as error bars. In the case of triplicates where one or two of the sample
results were below the quantitative limit, the respective quantitative limit was used in calculating the mean
and standard deviation in the triplicate. However, triplicates with all three values below the quantitative
limit were not used (unless explicitly stated, in exceptional cases, e.g. in Figure 19).
All plotting and fitting were done using Dotmatics GraphPad Prism 10. The model fits used the least
squares nonlinear regression, with a custom equation for the dilution factor range of 0 to 20,000. All the
non-linear scales displayed on the graph axes are 10-base logarithmic. For further visual guidance and ease
of comparison, identity (y = x) lines and a factor of 10 identity lines (y = 0.1x and y = 10x), and in one
case, in Figure 11, a factor of 2 identity lines (y = 0.5x and y = 2x) were added where relevant. It is worth
noting that Prism does not feature error bars when the standard deviation is very low - which is the reason
behind any missing error bars.
The results on native nutrient and metal concentrations and on determined KD values were primarily
compared with the 2024 study "Heavy Metals in Pyrolysis of Contaminated Wastes: Phase Distribution
and Leaching Behaviour" by Sørmo et al. [47]. This study was chosen for comparison as it is recent, among
others uses dewatered sewage sludge and digested sewage sludge as feedstocks, employs a longer than one-day
equilibration time (72 h), and features several pyrolysis temperatures and pH levels, some of which match
the experimental conditions in this thesis relatively well.

possible that just like several other biochar properties, the biochar-water distribution coefficients are also
affected by the exact choice of feedstock and pyrolysis conditions. Furthermore, for more precise results, not

only reaching equilibrium conditions would be necessary but better accounting for the non-leachable metal
and nutrient content of the solids and using a higher solids ratio for a more homogeneous sample would also
be beneficial.
Overall, however, Table 5 provides a clear picture of KD increase through pyrolysis. Even in the cases
where the results for biochar have up to 60% relative standard deviation, it is likely that the KD had increased
meaningfully, as the change is of 1-2 orders of magnitude. These results indicate that the pyrolysis of sewage
sludge may indeed help decrease the exposure of flora and fauna to heavy metals and an excess of nutrients
in agricultural applications. To investigate this further, the last experiment was aimed at a more direct
demonstration of exposure reduction and the determination of KD through a second method, which does not
rely on the measured solid phase concentrations.

5.4
Comparison of Methods for Obtaining KD
Even though the previously described cases offer a plausible explanation for exposure reduction, there is
significant difference between the distribution coefficients obtained by the direct method (Table 5) and the
dilution method (Tables 6 and 7). Furthermore, for Fe and As, Table 5 shows that the coefficients are not
similar.
Nevertheless, both experiments 2 and 3 show that there is indeed an exposure reduction, together with
a difference in KD values for SS and BC. Therefore, to assess how these two values are related, regardless
of the method to obtain them, Figure 23 shows the exposure reduction factors against KBC,H2O/KSS,H2O
from Tables 8 and 9.
This figure illustrates how well a reduction between CH2O⇄SS and CH2O⇄BC is explained by a change
in KBC,H2O. When data lands in the identity line, it can be said that the increase in KBC,H2O explains the
exposure reduction. In contrast, data far from identity demonstrates that other factors are dominant in the
reduction of concentrations.
Observing Figure 23, it is evident that most of the chemicals studied are close to the identity line, with all
the direct method results (method I) close to the factor-of-two identities. This reveals that exposure reduction
can be explained by an increase in the distribution coefficient caused by the physicochemical transformation
of sewage sludge into biochar during pyrolysis.
The deviations from the identity line could be explained by the three cases described before. For example,
chromium up-concentrated in the solid and the KD increased ten times. Since it undergoes various types of
sorption mechanisms, such as complexation and cation exchange, Cr can be grouped as case 2).
Nevertheless, as seen with arsenic, the suspension systems are complex, and a combination of improved
sorption, precipitation, and/or other mechanisms each play a role for every individual chemical. For instance,

5.5
Regulatory Perspectives
When comparing the observed dissolved metal and nutrient concentrations to regulatory standards, an insight
was made that any metal or nutrient leaching from biochar would most directly affect groundwater. Since
Denmark is largely reliant on groundwater for its drinking water supply, this could eventually impact drinking
water. Therefore, in addition to the initially chosen Danish surface water thresholds, relevant drinking water
standards from both Danish and EU regulations were included in the comparison. This was also advantageous
as these described limits for more substances of interest than the Danish surface water quality regulation
45
==PAGE:53==
alone. An excerpt of the four regulations used combined in one table can be consulted in the Appendix
(Table 11).
With this in hand, and looking at Figures 18 and 19, the biochar shows promising results for Al, Cr, Cu,
Mn, Ni, Zn, Fe, and Na since the concentrations are below the limits. The least diluted biochar suspensions
did exceed the most stringent arsenic limits with an average of 6.21 µg/L (Danish surface water and tap water
standards) but were still below the EU drinking water limits (10 µg/L). The same cannot be stated about the
sewage sludge suspensions, where the mean dissolved arsenic concentration of the lowest DF samples reached
50 µg/L. This highlights the importance of the exposure reduction in sewage sludge through pyrolysis.
Fortunately, the concentrations of cadmium and lead were below quantification limits for all samples
except for the most concentrated SS suspensions (0.20 µg/L and 2.14 µg/L, respectively). While no insight
could be gained on the exposure reduction this way, it is reassuring nevertheless, as Cd was found to be
responsible for the highest potential ecological risk from heavy metals in biochar and lead was also found to
pose considerable risk [56].
It is worth noting that the KD values for nutrients also seem to increase with pyrolysis, as shown in
Table 5 and Table 7. While field experiments would be needed to confirm it, this could suggest a reduced
nutrient availability from biochar as compared to SS, but higher retention. Therefore, supplementation with
higher-nutrient fertilizers may be necessary, as other sources have also suggested (Section 2.7.1, [2]). It
could, nevertheless be used as a soil amendment for its pH buffering, water retention, and soil structure
improvement properties.
The compiled water standards, along with individual sludge and biochar plots for each element can be
seen in the Appendix (Table 11 and Figures 25-28).
6
Conclusion
Returning to the initial aims and hypotheses, the experimental results from this thesis were able to provide
insight into the native nutrient and metal sorption and leaching behaviours of sewage sludge biochar, confirm
an exposure reduction between sewage sludge and SS biochar leachate, as well as demonstrate an increase in
KD for several of the studied elements.
The time frame needed to achieve equilibrium conditions with relatively high certainty (as determined
from the development of pH and dissolved concentrations) was an equilibration time of 168 hours at a dry solid
concentration no higher than 18 g/L, at unadjusted (or minimally adjusted) pH, while mixing continuously
at a temperature of 10°C.
When comparing the dissolved nutrient and metal concentrations between equilibrated SS- and BC-containing
samples, lower concentrations were observed in the BC samples in all cases regardless of equilibration time,
and often by an order of magnitude, as shown in Figure 13. The exact exposure reduction (CH2O⇄BC/CH2O⇄SS)
for the studied elements, displayed in Tables 8 and 9, were correspondingly well below 1. For Fe, Zn, and
NH3, even 3-4 orders of magnitude reduction were observed at the lowest dilutions. Meanwhile, the exposure
reduction was more subtle for sodium, and potassium, which indicates the sorption of metals by ion exchange
causing the release of Na and K.
46
==PAGE:54==
In all cases from the third experiment, except for As at the lowest dilution, the dissolved concentrations
in the biochar suspensions were below the most stringent relevant water quality criteria.
In the sludge
suspensions, on the other hand, several exceedances were observed (e.g. Al, Mn, Zn). While these exposure
reduction factors indicate that the pyrolysis of sewage sludge is indeed an effective way of mitigating heavy
metals leaching risk, they also confirm that biochar alone may not be sufficient for P and N supplementation
in agricultural soils.
The KD values for the studied elements were determined for both SS and BC by measuring equilibrium
concentrations in the solid and liquid phase, as well as by the method relying on dilution series as outlined by
Ter Laak et al., where it was possible [31]. The results obtained through the two results were not comparable,
often differing by several orders of magnitude. Which of these results is correct is still unconcluded, but two
partial explanations for the differences were identified: An overestimation of method I caused by including
irreversibly bound metals and nutrients in the solid concentrations; and incomplete data for curve fitting
introduces uncertainty to the obtained values in method II. Additionally, the KD values based on data from
Sørmo et al. are again different, and comparable studies using unmodified sewage sludge biochar are scarce.
Nevertheless, both methods revealed that exposure reduction can be explained by a general KD increase
during pyrolysis, with the second method showing exceptions that provided further insight. A total of 3
cases were distinguished: 1) similar KD, where exposure reduction is mainly due to the volatilization of the
material (e.g. As); 2) a noticeable increase in KD through pyrolysis leading to exposure reduction (e.g. Mg);
3) slight to no KD change or volatilization, exposure reduction due to forming insoluble compounds that
remain with the solid phase (e.g. Fe). Of these, the second case was found to be the most common. However,
it was also identified that there are deviations from the complete identity between exposure reduction and
the change in KD. These differences could be attributed to the complexity of the suspension system, having
a combination of pyrolysis removal, improved sorption, precipitation, and other mechanisms.
6.1
Suggested Improvements
Although the use of native concentrations makes these experiments environmentally relevant, laboratory
conditions do not faithfully replicate the soil to which sludge and biochar would be applied. For this reason,
field or at least column experiments would be useful for obtaining a closer approximation. Besides, the results
have certain limitations, as already indicated previously. As mentioned in Section 4.1, unfiltered samples
resulted in the inability to distinguish between freely dissolved concentrations, complexes, and chemicals
bound to suspended solids. Therefore, the water equilibrium concentrations reported here could be higher
than reality. This could be solved by filtration with a filter better suited for sludge suspensions. Furthermore,
the nature of the experiments cannot distinguish between the oxidation states of the metals sorbed, leading
to uncertainty about the toxicity of the leachate. This would require further analytical steps.
Overall, leaching experiments involving soil would be necessary to understand how the results of this
thesis could change with unstable conditions, a different pH, and ageing with longer equilibration times.
Additionally, filtration and the further analysis of the oxidation states of sorbed and dissolved elements as
well as the sorption mechanisms could provide a more accurate insight beneficial for risk assessment.
Despite the limitations, this study provides a scientific understanding of the reduced leaching and improved
47
==PAGE:55==
sorption behaviour of sewage sludge biochar compared to its feedstock.
With these results, we hope to
help reduce the uncertainty surrounding sewage sludge biochar use, contributing to a basis on which future
regulations can be built.
