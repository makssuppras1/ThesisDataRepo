Preface 
This master’s thesis is prepared by Huri Nur Kocak who is studying civil engineering at the Technical 
University of Denmark in Lyngby. The start date of the project is April 3rd 2018 while the due date is 
September 3rd 2018.  
The project is a structural analysis of Building 310 at DTU, which is a laboratory and office building. 
The structural analysis takes place by using traditional hand-calculations and the structural software 
programs StruSoft and Robot. The results are then compared and discussed.  
The purpose of the project is to analyze the differences between traditional – and software 
calculations and to furthermore compare the differences of the software programs.  
In order to fulfill the purpose, the building is structurally analyzed with both methods by looking at 
the horizontal – and vertical – load distributions of the building. Furthermore, static documentations 
of vertical structural members are analyzed.  
The project consists of three parts:  
1. The primary report, which consists of text, important results and calculations.  
2. The appendix, which consists of calculation examples, excel sheets, verification notes from 
the software programs and relevant bearing capacity tables.  
3. The drawings, which consist of elevations of the building and structural plan drawings of the 
floors plans.

Abstract 
This paper is associated with the master’s thesis civil engineering studies.  
The project is composed by Huri Nur Kocak (s123480) who is a civil engineering student at The 
Technical University of Denmark. The project is worth 30 ECTS points and extends over a semester. 
The project is sectioned into four parts; the main paper, appendix, and drawings. 
 
The purpose of this paper is to compare the structural software programs StruSoft FEM-design and 
Autodesk Robot Structural analysis by making a structural analysis of building 310. The results of the 
programs are compared to structural analysis results obtained with hand-calculations. All calculations 
to the static analysis are based on the rules and standards from Eurocodes. 
Building 310 is a multi-storey laboratory and office building located in the Technical University of 
Denmark.  
It has been built to facilitate research laboratories and offices for students and researchers of the 
University.  
The construction consists of concrete and steel elements which includes: slabs, beams, columns, walls 
and footings. 
The vertical and horizontal loads have been found in Ultimate Limit State (ULS), where the critical 
loads have been applied to the software programs.  
The wind load was found to be the critical horizontal load in the lateral direction, while the seismic 
load was found to be the critical horizontal load in the longitudinal direction. Therefore the wind load 
has been applied in order to analyze the laterally stabilizing walls of the building whereas the seismic 
load has been applied to analyze the longitudinally stabilizing walls of the building.  
During the calculations of the vertical loads, three areas of the building were assessed to have the 
most critical loads for each column-type of the building. Calculations were performed in hand and in 
Robot and StruSoft.  
Robot had an impressive 0,32% mean deviation (from the hand-calculations results) whereas StruSoft 
had a 14,37% mean deviation (from the hand-calculations). 
During the calculations of the horizontal load distribution, two methods were used in the hand-
calculations in order to gain a wider comparison range to the results obtained in the software 
programs.

1 Introduction  
This project deals with the structural analysis of Building 310 with the use of traditional and software 
calculations. The calculations are made with respect to the Danish Eurocodes and their associated 
annexes. The project describes the construction and their elements.  
Both horizontally – and vertically stabilizing elements will be picked out, after which the loads that 
affect the building will be determined and calculated. Afterwards, the loads will be put in different 
load combinations in ultimate limit state in order to determine the critical design loads. 
The building and the design loads will then be drawn in StruSoft and Robot and the results from the 
programs will be discussed and compared to each other and the hand-calculations.

Building 310 is a 7-storey (including the basement floor) building located in DTU Campus, Lyngby.  
As shown in figure 1 the building is placed between building 307, building 309 and building 306.  The

5 Static system 
As the building is affected by vertical and horizontal loads, each case is associated with a main static 
system.  The main vertical system is identical everywhere in the building seen from a global 
perspective, though it varies when seen from a local point of view. Therefore there will be two sections 
explaining the vertical static system, a main (global) system and five different local systems.  
5.1 Vertical static system 
The vertical load of the building consists of permanent loads and variable loads. The permanent loads 
consist of the self-weight of the construction members and the dead-load (installations, floors, 
windows etc.). The variable load varies, depending on what the floor is used for. In the case of building 
310, it is used for office – and laboratory work. To simplify the calculations, it has been chosen to 
consider all of the indoor variable loads as if the building only were loaded by laboratory category 
load. Below is a figure showing the load distribution in the main vertical static system.   
 
Figure 5: The vertical static system 
==PAGE:17==
Page 9 of 123 
 
Figure 5 above shows building 310, which consists of three frame constructions. The outer frames are 
identical and have the same dimensions, while the middle frame is approximately half the size of the 
outer frames. The columns of the frames are simply supported in the bottom. Furthermore, the frame 
structure is carried out in concrete and steel elements and extends from the foundation edge to the 
top floor. The slabs are simply supported with beams and walls, which explains why the supports are 
shown as hinges in the frames.    
5.2 The horizontal static system  
The horizontal loads of the building consist of wind load and seismic load. For the stability of the 
building, it will be the slabs and the stabilizing walls, which will counteract all the loads.  
In case of horizontal load, two cases are considered: one where the load attacks across the building, 
and one where the load attacks along the building. Below there are descriptions and figures, which 
illustrate the horizontal static system in both cases.  
5.2.1 
Stability across the building  
The horizontal stability system across the building consist of seven concrete walls all parallel to the 
gables. The thickness of the walls vary between 200 mm and 250 mm.  
 
Figure 6: Horizontal system across the building 
The wind load is absorbed into the façade and distributed to the nearest slabs. The slabs then 
transmit the loads to the marked walls in figure 6 and figure 7, which all have a stabilizing function. 
The process is described in more detail in the section on stability analysis.  
Figure 6 shows the horizontal stability system across the building.

Stability along the building  
The longitudinal stability system consist of five walls (including some parts of the façade) all parallel 
to the building façade. The thickness of the walls vary between 200 mm and 250 mm.  
 
Figure 7: Horizontal system along the building 
Similarly, the walls, parallel to the facades, stabilize when the force attacks along the building (wind 
load onto the gable).

7 Load combinations 
Based on existing consequence classes, it is estimated that building 310 is under high consequence 
class, CC3. This assessment is based on the descriptions of the different classes of consequences, 
among other factors, that the top floor is 12 m above terrain. 
CC3 occurs when the consequences of a possible failure involves high risk of loss of human life or the 
economic, social or environmental consequences are very high. The high consequence class means a 
KFI value of 1,1 is used for the calculation of the vertical loads. 
7.1 Vertical load transfer - Ultimate limit state (ULS) 
ULS is the ultimate boundary state, where the building will collapse if exceeded. The condition will 
occur by:  
 
Material fracture  
 
Large and growing deformations  
 
Local break caused by collapse of a construction  
The building has been checked for the following ULS load combinations:  
1. Live load dominating  
𝑞𝑑= 1,0 ∙𝐾𝐹𝐼∙𝑔𝑘+ 1,5 ∙𝛼𝑛∙𝐾𝐹𝐼∙𝑞𝑘+ 1,5 ∙0,3 ∙𝑆𝑘+ 1,5 ∙0,3 ∙𝐾𝐹𝐼∙𝐹𝑤

9 Stability analysis 
The following sections deal with the horizontal stability of the construction, including transfer of the 
horizontal forces in the transverse- and longitudinal-direction of the building. For each direction, a 
calculation example is given.  Each load transfer is performed with the load combination STR2B, which 
is the critical combination with regard to horizontal load transfer.   
The laterally stabilizing walls are referred to as Yi while the longitudinally stabilizing walls are referred 
to as Xi. 
The results and excel calculations for those remaining walls can be found in appendix B, sections 2.8 
and 2.9  
Section 9.2 deals with the hand-calculated stability of the building.  
Sections 9.3 and 9.4 deal with stability in StruSoft and Robot, where the obtained results are discussed 
and compared to the results from Section 9.2 – Stability with hand-calculations.  
9.1 Stabilizing walls  
 
By a stable building is meant a structure that is resistant to the horizontal forces it is exposed to. The 
horizontal forces usually occur in the shape of wind loads, seismic load or soil loads. In the case of 
building 310, the wind load and the seismic load have been assessed dominant, why the calculations 
are performed with respect to those. 
The wind load is taken by the façade which is clamped to the individual decks. The load then transfers 
from the façade to the nearest deck. The decks work as an entire unit which also is characterized as a 
plate. The plate then transfers the force to the stabilizing walls, which by diaphragm action transfers 
the force down to the foundation. In order to confirm the stability of the building, the stabilizing walls 
must be examined for uplifting, overturning and sliding. In the cases where the requirements of the 
three scenarios are not met, the dimensions of the walls are changed or reinforcement is usually 
added to the elements. Furthermore, post-tensioning can be performed on the wall, if it is not possible 
to change the dimension nor the reinforcement amount.

11 Discussion  
The Autodesk giant, Robot Structural Analysis or the Scandinavian found StruSoft FEM-design?  
The results obtained in both programs are somewhat acceptable when compared to results achieved 
in hand-calculations. Robot generally has smaller deviations compared to StruSoft. This is mostly due 
to the fact that Robot provides an option to turn-off slab meshes, as opposed to StruSoft. The turn-
off of the mesh option is an advantage when performing a vertical load transfer, as the un-meshed 
slab structures distribute the vertical loads exactly the way the user wants it (without distributing to 
structural elements not meant to be vertically bearing). It is observed by tests and results, that StruSoft 
distributes vertical loads to elements not meant to be vertically bearing. This situation would likely be 
avoided if StruSoft also provided an option to turn-off the mesh of the slab elements.  
Furthermore, it is observed that both Robot and StruSoft distribute applied horizontal loads based on 
more factors than the lengths of the stabilizing walls. Based on the comparisons between results by 
the programs and hand-calculations, it seems like StruSoft is distributing horizontal loads based on 
the torsional centrum method with small differences, whereas the mechanism of the horizontal load 
distributions differs in Robot. When performing the lateral horizontal load distribution in Robot, the 
obtained reactions are closer to the reactions calculated in hand based on the torsional centrum 
method, while the reactions obtained from the longitudinal load lean towards the reactions calculated 
in hand, based on the plastic distribution.  
StruSoft provides tools to check the local stability of the stabilizing walls, while in Robot the user has 
to open tables with results of connection forces and manually insert the obtained values into stability 
equations. The process is especially difficult and extremely time craving due to the results being 
presented in a table with node numbers and element numbers as the only indicators to which element 
is dealt with.  
During the static documentations of the structural members, it is observed that the programs design 
columns differently. Robot is on the safe side, taking moment contributions from every direction into 
consideration whereas StruSoft designs the column based on the critical loads only. Furthermore, it is 
observed that StruSoft has relatively highs deviations in the maximum moments of the beams while 
Robot has minimal deviations. This is caused by the slabs in StruSoft being meshed and not in Robot. 
As mentioned earlier, the mesh on the slabs makes the program distribute the loads differently than 
what is normally calculated in hand.

12 Conclusion  
Both software programs seem to work properly in their own ways. Robot is an older program created 
by AutoDesk who has also created widely used programs like Revit and AutoCad. The knowledge of 
the company behind the program already gives an insurance that great results will be achieved. Robot 
does not lack any important features, though a local-stability tool for the shear walls is missed. 
Furthermore, an option to have the resultant of wall-reactions shown could be another feature to add. 
Robot is fast when making calculations whereas StruSoft can take some time to calculate when it 
comes to constructions with various elements and load combinations. Furthermore, Robot provides 
an option to turn-off the mesh from the slabs. The interface of Robot can be challenging when using 
the program the first few times, for example: the user will not be able to know how to hide/show 
specific elements, without googling it. Furthermore, it can be difficult to have an overview of the 
obtained results, as they are presented in a table. The results obtained in Robot are generally precise 
and expected – especially when dealing with vertical loads.  
StruSoft FEM-design is a new program created in Scandinavia. It feels like a tailored program for a user 
with a civil engineering degree from a Danish university, whereas Robot is more universal. The user 
interface of StruSoft is simple and easy to learn. Furthermore, the local stability tools are precise and 
simple. Compared to Robot, StruSoft has less precise results when it comes to vertical load transfer. 
This is obvious, when comparing the mean of all deviations calculated in section 8 – Transfer of vertical 
loads:  
Mean deviation, vertical load transfer:  
StruSoft = 14,37%  
Robot = 0,32% 
When it comes to horizontal load distribution it is observed that StruSoft uses the torsional centrum 
method, whereas Robot uses a mix between the plastic distribution and the torsional centrum method. 
This statement is based on the relevant mean deviations for each method in the respective load 
directions:  
Lateral load – plastic:  
 
StruSoft = 30% 
Robot = 25% 
Lateral load – torsional:  
 
StruSoft = 5%  
Robot = 11% 
Longitudinal load – plastic:   
Strusoft = 83,2% 
Robot = 25%  
Longitudinal load – torsional: 
StruSoft = 24% 
Robot = 51%  
It is concluded that the results obtained from Robot are generally more stable in the horizontal 
stability area, as they don’t vary as much between the two methods like the results from StruSoft do. 
Furthermore, it is concluded that Robot provides more precise results in vertical transfer, compared 
to the results obtained in StruSoft.
