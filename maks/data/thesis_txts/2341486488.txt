Abstract
The manufactures of foodstuff are highly concerned about living up to their customers quality
standard through quality control programs. Sensory evaluation is the most common method
for assessing the quality of vegetable oil as it is regarded as the most reliable analysis due to
the complexity of ﬂavour perception. However, sensory evaluation is a costly analysis due to
the fact that it requires several well-trained people and is a time-consuming task, if valid and
reliable results are to be obtained. The deterioration of quality in food, especially vegetable
oils, is mainly cause by lipid oxidation and results in an accumulation of volatile off-ﬂavours.
These volatiles can be measured with chemical analysis. If a correlation between the sensory
properties associated with quality deterioration and a chemical analysis can be established, it may
be possible to replace a sensory panel with the chemical analysis, which is easier to standardise
and maintained.
Therefore, the aim of this study was to investigate if a correlation could be established between a
descriptive or an industrial sensory evaluation and the measurement of volatiles by automated
Dynamic Headspace - Gas Chromatography, Mass Spectroscopy in reﬁned sunﬂower and rape-
seed oil by the used of multivariate data analysis. The two oil types were stored at ambient
temperature as well as 40 ◦C and 50 ◦C to accelerate lipid oxidation.
The obtained results show that there is a potential for using DHS-GC/MS in combination with
chemometrics to predict the overall ﬂavour perception, which was anti-correlated with content of
volatiles. The best obtained model for rapeseed oil had a RMSECV of 0.447 and a r2 of 0.779.
Moreover, a Principle Component analysis showed that it was possible to discriminate the
two oil types by their content of volatiles when stored at 40 ◦C for 30 Days; sunﬂower had a
higher content of 1-pentanol, pentanal and hexanal, whereas rapeseed had a higher content of
1-penten-3-ol, heptanal, E-2-butenal and 2-ethyl furan.
Furthermore, PCA models of the individual oil types showed that it was possible to discriminate
sampling day based on their volatile compositions, however, the discrimination was better when
oils were stored at 50 ◦C than 40 ◦C.

1 Introduction
Manufactures in the food production industry are very concerned about the quality of their
product. The quality of an ingredient will often affects its pricing, as its quality is a key factor for
the end product [27]. Low quality products generally costs less money and therefore giving less
proﬁt. Furthermore, if an ingredient of poor quality is released, it can have major consequences
for the company’s reputation because it could result in discomfort by ingestion.
The quality of foodstuff can be deﬁned based on its sensory properties, the safety of the product
and its nutritional value as well as having a suitable shelf life. In the vegetable oil industry a
reﬁned oil of high quality is an oil with bland or neutral ﬂavours, that would be light-colored and
stable [26].
Vegetable oils make up 79% of oil and fat consumption world wide [54]. They are popular
due to their relative high content of unsaturated fatty acids compared fat of animal origin and
especially the content of the essential fatty acids: linoleic and linolenic acid [4]. However,
the high content of unsaturated fatty acids makes it susceptible toward lipid oxidation [35].
Lipid oxidation is playing a signiﬁcant role in the deterioration of quality in edible oils, par-
ticularly by producing off-ﬂavours and hereby changing the sensory properties of the oils[35].
Humans have extremely low threshold of these off-ﬂavours, therefore, products produced with
oils of low ﬂavour quality becomes unpleasant to consume and resulting in a short shelf life.
However, lipid oxidation not only causes off-ﬂavours, it also reduces the nutritional value by de-
composing the essential fatty acids and generating toxic compounds which affects the safety [35].
To ensure the quality of oils, sensory evaluation (SE) is often integrated in the quality control
(QC) programme in the industry. The purpose of SE is mainly to look for the presence of
off-ﬂavours. As a matter of fact the sensory panel is often regarded as the most important
part in QC since SE, in general, is considered to be the best method for measuring ﬂavours
in food [bog?] [32]. This is due to the fact that human perception of ﬂavours is an integrated
experience, which cannot be measured by any instruments, as an instrument is able to measure
only individual compounds [34]. Furthermore, some humans have a higher sensitivity toward
off-ﬂavours than instruments [34].
In AAK, a large vegetable oil manufacture, the sensory panel has the ﬁnal word regarding the
release of a product [1]. Therefore, it is of uttermost importance that the sensory panel performs
valid and reliable results. For example, if the sensory panel determines a product to be false
negative (Type II error) in containing an off-ﬂavour, it should result in a stop of the release as
they will release a product with an off-ﬂavour, which could damage the company. Likewise,
if the sensory panel determines a product to be false positive (Type I error) in containing an
off-ﬂavour they will throw out a batch, which in reality is okay, leading to money lost.
However, to get valid and reliable results SE is time-consuming and a costly affair [15]. Suf-
ﬁcient and proper training is the most important parameter for SE as it both increases validity
and reliability[32]. A new panelist requires training between 40-120 hours, or said in another
way, half a year when internal employees are used as panelists [32],[1]. Not only is it time
consuming train a future panelist, but maintaining a well-trained panel also takes time and money.
Furthermore, the panel needs to be of a certain size to reduce bias[32]. It is beneﬁcial having
extra panelists, that can be called upon if some of the regular panelists catches a cold or is
stressed at work, and no longer can participate in the SE. However, often in the industry only a
few people are part of the sensory panel. This makes SE vulnerable.
Another issue by using SE is that a lot of information is stored in peoples heads. When panelists

==PAGE:15==
stops, all the information is lost and cannot just be replaced, it takes time to learn how to
differentiate attributes and using intensity scales.
Therefore, the industry is searching for easy, fast and cost efﬁcient analytical methods that can
replace SE or at least parts of it. Today, peroxide value (PV), para-anisidine value and free fatty
acids (FFA) are used in the oil industry to evaluate oil quality. In a former project by Michail
Pavlou [not published] it was shown that PV did not correlate with the assessment approved/not
approved of the oils at AAK. However, the analysis does not tell anything about volatiles present
in the oil, which is the principle cause for off-ﬂavours. The dynamic headspace methods linked
to a gas chromatography and a mass spectrometer have been shown to be the one of the most
suitable instrumental methods for studying volatiles and have a sensitivity down to part per
billion [24], [48].
Before it is possible to replace SE with instrumental techniques, a relationship between them
needs to be established. Therefore, attention has been turned in an attempt to correlate instru-
mental and sensory data by using multivariate data analyses as Principle Component Analysis,
which is good for pattern recognition, and Partial Least Square regression, which is a methods
for building correlation models [28]
1.1
Objectives
The overall aim of this project is to investigate the correlation between sensory properties and
content of volatiles in sunﬂower and rapeseed oil. This is looked upon to investigate the possi-
bility of using volatiles as a quality control parameter to assess the ﬂavour stability; either as a
precautionary measurement giving warning when the sensory panel should evaluate an oil, as a
support to the sensory panel or as a total replacement of the panel in the future.
The correlation between volatiles and sensory properties is assessed by investigation of the
sensory properties and volatile compounds developed during storage at ambient and elevated
temperatures. Principle Component Analysis is conducted with the aim of exploring the underly-
ing structure and patterns of the data for later use in building a Partial Least Square regression
model; Is it possible to discriminate the oil types by their content of volatiles?, Is it possible from
day 0 to discriminate between days based on content of volatiles or their sensory properties?.
This obtained knowledge contributes to the understanding of lipid oxidation in the two oil types.
Finally, Partial Least Square regression is conducted with the aim of correlating the sensory data
with volatiles; Is there a correlation? Can the total ﬂavour intensity giving by the sensory panel
at AAK be predicted from the volatiles?
1.1.1
Hypothesis
The hypotheses are:
• Increasing temperature will increase lipid oxidation, leading the formation of more volatiles
in the oils.
• There will be a difference in the volatile composition between the two oil types due to
differences in fatty acid composition.
• The higher the content of volatiles in the oils, the less grade is given by the sensory panel.

2 Theory
2.1
Vegetable oil
This section contains a general description of lipids and minor components that can be present
in vegetable oils. Furthermore, a composition of rapeseed and sunﬂower oil is given. Lastly,
colloidal structures are touch upon.
Vegetable oil makes up 79% of the edible oil consumption world wide [54]. Oil function
as a carrier for the fat-soluble vitamins; A, D, E and K, provides energy and is a source of the
essential fatty acids linoleic, omega-6, and linolenic acids, omega-3 [4].
Vegetable oils can be deﬁned as fats from plants, which are liquid at ambient temperature [2].
Most vegetables oils are obtained by pressing seeds, by hexane extraction or a combination of
both, resulting in crude oil and a protein rich mixture [39], [18]. Crude oil consist of around
96% triacylglycerols and some minor components, where some are desirable (Tocopherols,
sterols and polyphenols) and some are undesirable (phospholipids, FFA, chlorophyll, metal ions
and oxidation products) [54]. The exact composition depends on the species, environmental
conditions and the processing of the oil.
A triacylglycerol is a molecule consisting of glycerol backbone where to three fatty acids are
attached, see Figure 2.1.

A Fatty acid is a carboxylic acid with an unbranched hydrocarbon chain of at least 8 carbon
atoms, but a carbon chain length of 16, 18, 20 or 22 carbon atoms is most common for edible
oils [2], [61]. Figure 2.2 shows the three most abundant fatty acids in vegetable oil; Oleic acids
(C18:1n-9), Linoleic acid (C18:2n-6) and Linolenic acid (C18:3n-3).
Generally, fatty acids are classiﬁed into three groups according to their degree of saturation;
saturated, monounsaturated or polyunsaturated. Saturated fatty acids have a straight hydrocarbon
chain. On the other hand, unsaturated fatty acids contain one or more double bonds with either a
3 of 62
==PAGE:17==
cis or trans conﬁguration. Natural oils commonly have cis conﬁguration, but trans conﬁguration
can be present in processed oils as a consequence of high processing temperature [2]. The
presence of cis double bonds gives a bend in the structure, leading to a less closely packaging
of the fatty acids. This is the reason for the lower melting point of oil with high content of
unsaturated fatty acids [8].
As three different fatty acids are attached in the glycerol molecule, it is possible to do many
combinations, which leads to a mixture of different triacylglycerols. Saturated fatty acids are
preferably situated at the sn−1 position, and polyunsaturated fatty acids at more protected sn−2
position [39], [40], [19].
Two popular oils are rapeseed and sunﬂower oil, which are the third and fourth most consumed
vegetable oils in the world just after palm and soybean oil [53].
2.1.1
Rapeseed oil - Canola oil
Rapeseed oil is extracted from seed of the Brassica genus [14]. Rapeseed oil makes up 15.6%
of all vegetable oil consumed worldwide, which makes it the third most consumed vegetable
oil worldwide [53]. Original rapeseed has a high content of eruric acid (C22:1n-9), but as it has
shown to cause heart problems, cultivars with low content of eruric acid have been breed [14].
In the new breed, eruric acid is mainly replaced by oleic acid (C18:1n-9) [40].
Rapeseed oil is sometimes called canola oil. Canola oil is a rapeseed oil made from a species
with less than 2% eruric acid in its fatty acid proﬁle and less than 30 µmol glucosinolate [40].
Glucosinolates are compounds consisting of a glucose unit, sulfur, nitrogen and a sulphate group,
that give rise to pungency [8].

2.1.3
Minor components in Vegetable oil
Summarising from above, edible oils contains numerous minor components that impacts the oil
quality.
Triacylglycerol can undergo hydrolysis generating diacylglycerol (DAG), monoacylglycerol
(MAG) and free fatty acids (FFA), catalysed by lipase, extreme pH, heat and moisture [5], [6].
Furthermore, FFA can catalyse the hydrolysis of TAG leading to an accelerated increase of FFA
in oil [6]. These components are amphiphilic as they both have a hydrophilic and a hydrophobic
part, making them surface active. Being surface active means that the compound reduces the free
energy, the tension, of surfaces or interfaces, which makes it favorable for them to self-assemble
at interfaces [29]. Besides catalysing hydrolysis FFA act as pro-oxidants, accelerating lipid oxida-
tion and give rise to acidic ﬂavour [7], [25]. Therefore, FFAs are highly undesirable in edible oils.
Vegetable oils contains phospholipids (PL) originating from the plant cell membrane, which was

2.2
Lipid oxidation
This section begins with a general description of lipid oxidation and the mechanisms followed
by the oxidation of the three main fatty acids in vegetable oil. Next lipid oxidation in bulk oil
9 of 62
==PAGE:23==
will be described. Lastly, two well used chemical methods for investigating lipid oxidation will
be touched upon.
As the name, lipid oxidation, indicates, it involves the reaction between fatty acid and oxygen.
As seen in Section 2.1, Vegetable oil have a high content of unsaturated fatty lipids that contains
allylic groups, which are highly susceptible towards radical attack.
Lipid oxidation give rise to off-ﬂavours, known as rancidity, as well as a decrease in nutritional
value due to oxidation of essential fatty acids and vitamins, and generates toxic compounds.
Therefore, lipid oxidation is the major cause of deterioration of oil quality.
Lipid oxidation can be divided into three main mechanisms: autoxidation, photooxidation
and enzymatic oxidation. The most abundant mechanism is autoxidation, also known as the
free radical chain reaction, but when edible oil is exposed to light photooxidation becomes of
importance [7].
2.2.1
Autoxidation
Typically, autoxidation is depicted as a free radical chain reaction, driven by repeated abstraction
of hydrogen atoms by radicals. A radical is a molecule that has one or more unpaired electrons,
making it reactive towards abstraction of hydrogen atoms.
Autoxidation is an autocatalytic reaction, meaning once started the reaction is self-propagation
and accelerating [47]. This does not means, it is a spontaneous reaction, which it is not. Oxygen
in its ground state cannot react directly with double bonds as it is spin forbidden due to opposite
spin conﬁguration [35]. Therefore, an initiator is required for initiation. Metals, heat, light
(photooxidation), lipoxygenase (enzymatic oxidation), heme proteins and porphyrin, chlorine
radical from disinfection and ozone can function as initiators, mainly by decomposition of
hydroperoxides [47], [50].
Redox active metals undergoing one-electron transfer such as iron and copper are regarded as
the most important initiator in edible oils. Metals is ubiquitous present in food as it is almost
impossible to complete remove traces of metals and they are often introduced during processing
from the equipment. This is also the case for edible oils. Metal catalysed the decomposition of
hydroperoxides see Subsection 2.2.3, which has been formed during processing [47], [35].
Classically, the free radical chain reaction is divided into three steps; initiation, propagation and
termination.
Initiation is the formation of a lipid radical by an initiator which removes/abstracts a hydrogen(H),
see Equation (2.2).
LH initiator

(2.2)
The abstraction of H is speciﬁc occurring at bis-allylic and allylic hydrogens as these hydrogens
have the lowest bond energy, bis-allylic hydrogens being lowest with a bond energy of 272kJ/mol
compared to 322kJ/mol for allylic hydrogens and 410kJ/mol for hydrogen in the chain [47], [4].
It is also the reason for the more polyunsaturated a fatty acid is, the more prone/susceptible it
is toward oxidation. It has been shown that there is a linear relationship between the number
of bis-allylic groups and oxidation with a two-fold increase in reaction rate for each active
bis-allylic group in a fatty acid [35].

As the hydrogen abstraction is quite speciﬁc, it is predictable which isomer of hydroperox-

Oleic acid
Oleic acid has one double bond at position 9 to 10. It means the hydrogen is abstracted either at
the the allylic position 11 or 8. To stabilise the radical the electron is delocated over three C atoms
as seen in Figure 2.7. Then oxygen will attack at the electron deﬁcient points resulting in the for-
mation of a mixture of four hydroperoxides in almost equal amount: 8-OOH, 9-OOH, 10-OOH
and 11-OOH. Upon abstraction the double bond in shifted one carbon into a β-position from the
site of abstraction. trans-conﬁguration is always the dominant conﬁguration at 9-OOH and 10-
OOH, whereas it becomes dominating at 8-OOH and 11-OOH only at elevated temperatures [35].
Linoleic acid
Linoleic acid has two double bond at position 9 to 10 and 12 to 13, resulting in bisallylic
hydrogen atoms at position 11. It means a H is abstracted at position 11 leading to the formation
of a pentadienyl radical, which is resonance stabilised by delocalisation of the electron between
both double bonds, as seen in Figure 2.7. Then oxygen attacks at the electron deﬁcient points
generating of a mixture of two conjugated diene hydrogenperoxides: 9-OOH (cis, trans or trans,
trans) and 13-OOH (cis, trans or trans, trans). As for oleic acid, the conditions determine the
conﬁguration, whether is it cis, trans or trans, trans [35].
Linolenic acid
Linolenic acid contains two bis-allylic positions, 14 and 11, where hydrogen abstraction can
occur. This leads to a pentadienyl radical between carbon 9 and 13 or 12 and 16. Again oxygen
attacks the end carbon positions of the pentadienyl radical, which are the electron deﬁcient
points, see Figure 2.7. It leads to a mixture of four peroxyl radicals which upon hydrogen
abstraction results in four conjugated dienes hydroperoxide isomers: 9-OOH, 12-OOH, 13-OOH
and 16-OOH. Again the conﬁguration depends on the conditions. However, they are not found in
equal amount as 9-OOH and 16-OOH are formed four times more frequently than 12-OOH and
13-OOH. The reason is found in the tendency of 12-OOH and 13-OOH to undergo the competing
internal 1,3 cyclisation reaction[35].
In cyclization the peroxyl radical abstracts a hydrogen atom from the cis β,γ double bond creating
a new radical on the 9 or 16 position depending on if 12-OOH or 13-OOH was the initial peroxyl

2.2.2
Photooxidation
Photooxidation is another important oxidation mechanism in edible oil [7], which occur when the
oil is exposed to visible or UV-light. Edible oils contain the type II sensitizer chlorophyll. When
exposed to light it become excited as it absorbs its energy. The sensitizer can then either returns
to ground state by emission, internal conversion or undergo intersystem crossing generating
an excited triplet state sensitizer [7]. This triplet state sensitizer can react with triplet state
(ground state) oxygen, transferring its energy whereby singlet oxygen is generated through and
the sensitizer return to ground state [7] Singlet oxygen is highly reactive, 1500 times more
reactive than ground state oxygen, and capable of reacting directly with either of the carbon
atoms in a double bond in unsaturated fatty acids via a concerted ’ene’ addition resulting in equal
formation of hydroperoxides at both ends of the original double bond [11], [40], [47]. Following
the formation of hydroperoxides, double bond migration and transition to trans-conﬁguration
occurs resulting in the formation of both conjugated and nonconjugated hydroperoxides [7].
When singlet oxygen reacts with oleic acids only 9-OOH and 10-OOH are formed. Linoleic acids
with singlet oxygen generates conjugated 9-OOH and 13-OOH, and non-conjugated 10-OOH
and 12-OOH. Singlet oxygens reaction with linolenic acid results in the formation of conjugated
9-OOH, 12-OOH, 13-OOH and 16-OOH, and non-conjugated 10-OOH and 15-OOH [35].
Photooxidation is not effected by temperature and level of unsaturation to the same extent
as autoxidation [11]. The rate of autoxidation of oleic, linoleic and linolenic acid have been
reported to be 1:40 to 50:100, whereas the relative reaction rate of singlet oxygen is 1:1.4:1.9 for
oleic, linoleic and linolenic acid, respectively [7]. To minimise the inﬂuence of photooxidation,
samples need to be kept in darkness.
As different hydroperoxides are generated by autoxidation and photooxidation, it will also
lead to slightly differences in the breakdown products even though they are degraded by the
same mechanism. This can be used to investigate which oxidation mechanism that has been
dominating.

2.2.4
Lipid oxidation in bulk oil
Oxidation of edible lipids are inﬂuenced by exposure to light and heat, accessibility and con-
centration of oxygen as well as its type, the fatty acids composition and content of the minor
components especially; metals, free fatty acids and chlorophyll acting as prooxidant accelerating
oxidation, phospholipids, and tocopherols and carotenoids acting as antioxidants inhibiting
oxidation [7].
Antioxidants are compounds that hinder or delay lipid oxidation [35]. There are two types of
antioxidant; primary antioxidant (free radical scavenger), that inhibit oxidation by donating
a hydrogen atom to the radical, or secondary antioxidant, which include metal chelator, that
inactive metal ions, and deactivate singlet oxygen or oxygen scavenger [35].
Temperature has a huge effect on the oxidation mechanism and a temperature enhancement may
change the balance between different pathways [47]. Therefore, it cannot be expected that the
same oxidation products are developed at different storage temperatures of the oils.
Accumulating evidence shows that lipid oxidation in bulk oil takes place in the associated
colloids as reverse micelles, which acts as nano-reactors leading to an increased interaction
between the lipids and the prooxidative water-soluble components [5], [6]. It is supported by the
polar paradox which states that hydrophilic antioxidants are the most potential in bulk oil. In
the past this has been explained by that hydrophilic antioxidant would accumulate in the air-oil
interface, though air is less polar than oil, and in reality evidence does not show a concentration
of antioxidant at the air-oil interface [5]. New evidence point to that the hydrophilic antioxidant
are able to accumulate at the water-lipid interface of the associated colloids where oxidation
17 of 62
==PAGE:31==
could be more prevalent [5].
As stated in Subsection 2.1.5 FFA are surface active leading to a migration to the water-lipid
interface. This creates an anionic surface which attracts the positive metal ions. Likewise,
hydroperoxides are surface active and migrate to the interface where the metals are present
leading to an increased interaction between the hydroperoxide and the metal, thereby increasing
the decomposition of hydroperoxides. Phospholipids are also surface active and it has been
shown that they increase the concentration/accessibility of tocopherol at the site of oxidation, to
inhibit oxidation [5]. A proposed mechanism oxidation in bulk in can be seen in Figure 2.9.

The ability of surface active components to decrease surface tension, increases the diffusion
rate of oxygen from the head space into the oil accelerating oxidation by facilitating the access
of oxygen [7]. Therefore, controlling the minor surface active components may lead to better
oxidative stability of the oil.
Furthermore, the concentration of oxygen in oil depend on the oxygen partial pressure in the
headspace; higher oxygen partial pressure leads to a higher concentration of oxygen in the oil
[7]. As long as oxygen is present in adequate amounts, the rate of oxidation is independent of
the oxygen concentration. However, at higher temperature oxygen concentration becomes more
important as the high temperature reduces the solubility of oxygen [7]. Therefore, the size of
head space and the surface-to-volume ratio of an oil is of importance during long storage periods
in closed containers.
As mentioned in Section 2.1, vegetable oil mainly consist of TAG. It has been shown that
unsaturated fatty acids in position 1 or 3 oxidised faster than when present at the sn-2 position
in the TAG [4]. Furthermore, the binding of the fatty acids to the glycerol backbone makes it
possible for the oxidised fatty acids to undergo inter- and intra-molecular reaction leading to the
formation of large molecules [47].

Measurement of lipid oxidation
The mechanism producing oxidation products are very dynamic. Analytical methods, used to
investigate oxidation, can change the oxidative proﬁle of a matrix if harsh conditions are applied,
especially when elevated temperature is used [46].
2.2.5.1
Measurement of hydroperoxides - PV
Peroxide value (PV) is the most used chemical quality indicator of oxidation [][ch3]. However, as
seen above peroxides are unstable and will readily decompose into secondary oxidation products
and the correlation with sensory evaluation has been inconclusive [62].
As the rate of (2.4) is low, an induction period (IP) will often be seen when PV is plotted
against storage time. The induction period will decreases with increasing temperature as the
decomposition of hydroperoxides increases [30]. When hydroperoxides start to accumulate an
increase in PV will be seen. If the rate of hydroperoxide decomposition is greater than the rate
of formation a decrease in PV will be seen[50].
Nevertheless, it is a good indicator for measurement of early stage of oxidation [21]
2.2.5.2
Dynamic headspace (DHS) - Gas Chromatography Mass Spectroscopy (GC-MS)
Dynamic headspace coupled with gas chromatography is a method used to investigate volatiles
generated in lipid oxidation. Dynamic headspace GC is divided into four steps [35];
• 1) Purging of a liquid sample with nitrogen in a heated vessel to facilitate the release of
volatiles
• 2) Trapping vaporized volatiles into a tube containing/packed with a sorbent material such
as Tenax R⃝
• 3) Desorption of volatiles from the trap/tube by elevated temperature and the transportation
by a carrier gas into the inlet of the Gas Chromatography (GC)
• 4) Separation of volatiles by GC
Then a gas chromatograph separates compounds of a mixture as a function of time based on
the boiling point and the polarity of the compounds. Compounds with a lower boiling point
and a low molecular weight as well as less bound in the food matrix volatiles ﬁrst [21]. The
column is coated with a stationary phase and compounds that has the lowest interaction with the
stationary phase will elute fastest and therefore reach the detector ﬁrst. This compound will be
seen early in the gas chromatograph. Therefore, only compounds that can be vaporized without
decomposition are seen in this methods [52]. Aldehydes, ketones, alcohols and hydrocarbons
are the most suitable volatiles for determination by DH-GCMS [35].
The sorbent material packed in the tube determines which volatiles are trapped in the tubes
and what is released to the GC for analysis. Tenax R⃝is a porous polymer of 2.6-diphenyl-p-
phenylene oxide and binds compounds with a chain length between ﬁve and 11 carbon atoms
[48]. Tenax R⃝GR is a variant where 23-30% graphite has been added to increase retention time
and the ability to absorb lower boiling compounds [48].
The gas chromatograph is often linked to a mass spectrometer (MS). In MS the compounds are
bombarded with electrons which transforms the compounds into positively charged ions. Then,
these charged ions fragments are separated according to their mass-to-charge ratio, which can be
19 of 62
==PAGE:33==
used for identiﬁcation though a library [52].
The advantage of this method is a high sensitivity of parts per billion (ppm), however if sorbent,
purging time and ﬂow rates do not match the compounds under investigation some of the com-
pounds may not be trapped and some may not be desorbed [48]. Another disadvantage is the
need for application of heat and it is a time consuming analysis.
2.3
Sensory evaluation of ﬂavours
In sensory evaluation (SE) humans are used as "instruments" to measure the perception of
sensory properties such as ﬂavours in food [32]. It is a method that is used for detection and
measuring differences in production and often part of quality control programs [62].
Flavour can be deﬁned as a combination of taste and odor [28]. Taste is non-volatile chemical
stimuli that is registered by receptors on the tongue and other oral surfaces. The ﬁve recognised
tastes are; sweet, bitter, acid/sour, umami and salty. Odours or aromas are volatiles chemical
stimuli that can be registered by receptors in the olfactory epithelium located in the nasal cavity
either through snifﬁng before the food is put in the mouth (orthonasal route) or through the mouth
during eating (retronasal route) . Furthermore, there is oral feeling factor that gives rise to terms
as astringent, metallic, burning or cooling feeling, which also might affect ﬂavour perception
[32], [28].
It means, the human perception of ﬂavors is a mixture of multiple senses. This is one of the
reasons why sensory properties are difﬁcult to measure by chemical analysis and why sensory
evaluation is a main method for assessing oxidative stability and acceptability of foods [24].
However, it is believed that odours have a larger inﬂuence than taste in determination of the
overall ﬂavor perception [55].
Sensory evaluation methods
There are several methods of sensory evaluations. It is often divided in to analytical tests where a
trained panel is used as an instrument to measure difference is a product including discrimination
tests and descriptive analysis hedonic tests where the preference and acceptance by consumers
are looked upon [32].
In quality control a variant of Descriptive Analysis (DA) is often used as it is not enough just
to know if there is a difference in the production. There are also an interested in knowing what
the difference is. In DA the trained panel detects, recognise and describe the attributes and their
intensities making it both a qualitative and quantitative measurement [45].
It requires a vocabulary that fully can describes differences in the product. Furthermore, the
vocabulary needs to be speciﬁc concrete term everybody in the panel understands and which
are not redundant. Typically, a vocabulary consist of 5-9 attributes, to keep it more simple
[15]. Typical words used to describe lipid oxidation in oils are bland, nutty, green, rancid, ﬁshy,
metallic or cardboardlike, old or stale as well as painty [24],[35].
Different scales can be used to evaluate intensities. Category scale consist of a limit set of word
and number. Line scales overcome the disadvantage of the category scale less accuracy by having
a line, typically of 15 cm, on which the panelist makes a mark where the panelist has evaluated
the intensity to be. This is more detailed and accurate, however it is more difﬁcult to use. Finally,
a magnitude estimate can be used. Here the ﬁrst sample in the session are giving a number of
20 of 62
==PAGE:34==
free choice, then all following samples are assigned a number relative to the ﬁrst sample.
Many companies have developed their own grading system, which is often a mix of several meth-
ods, typically based on the product quality. These systems are focused on product off-ﬂavours
from deterioration and taints from contamination to minimise complexity [45].
Panelists needs to be trained in the given vocabulary as well as the rating or grading system. This
is done by using samples that covers the expected range of variation in intensities of ﬂavours/off-
ﬂavours that could occur in reality. Discrimination tests are often used in training of the panel.
The most used are Triangle test, where three samples are given where of two are similar and one
is different and the purpose is to determine which of the sample are added [32].
Factors inﬂuencing perception and panel performance
Flavour perception is inﬂuenced by many factors, both physical factors as temperature of the
samples and the surroundings during evaluation, and psychological factors as mood and motiva-
tion. These factors can create unwanted noise in the data [32].
First of all ﬂavour perception is inﬂuenced by the availability of the volatiles to the olfac-
tory epithelium which depends on the ﬂavour release from the sample. Flavour release depends
main on the volatility of a compound and the resistance to mass transfer between phases, which
is affected by the composition and physical state of the food matrix as well as the structure and
concentration of the volatiles [59].
The surroundings can be a disturbing factor during the evaluation. To limit inﬂuence from
surroundings, the sensory evaluation should be conducted under controlled conditions which has
been designed to minimise bias and disturbances from the surroundings [32].
It is most optimal with a room fully dedicated to the SE where temperature, humidity and light
intensities can be controlled. Furthermore, it needs to be a place that is odour-free, so external
odours do not interfere with the ﬂavours in the sample under investigation. In addition, it should
preferably be located in a quite area as SE requires full focus from the assessors [32].
Another factor affecting perception is how the sample is prepared and served. The serving size,
containers and temperature of the sample should be standardised to minimise sample-to-sample
variation. For example increased serving temperature will increase the release of ﬂavours for
oils, so if the serving temperature of the oil is not the same it will affect the perceived ﬂavour
intensities [32], [59].
The sequence in which samples are served also have an inﬂuence in the perception. Samples
served in the begin of a session are usually scored more correctly than samples served later in a
session due to fatigue [32]. Therefore, it is important to ﬁnd the optimal number of samples that
can be assessed in a session. A way to expand the number for samples that can be evaluated in a
session is to expectorate instead of swallowing the samples as it is not so draining. It is also the
preferred one in QC where a lot of samples need to be tested. To minimise bias, samples should
preferably be served blind coded with a three digit number in a balanced randomised order [32].
This means, that samples should be presented more than once in a session in a random order.
However, there is a risk of a carry over effect from samples to samples. For example, if the
previous sample had a high intensity of rancid, there is a risk of the sample under investigation
to be rated higher than if it was served after less rancid sample. This is termed the contrast effect
[32]. Therefore, it is important to rinse with water or eating a neutral biscuit between samples.
Another bias in relation to the serving order is the group effect which is the opposite of the
21 of 62
==PAGE:35==
contrast effect; if a bad sample is presented in a group of good samples the sample will be rated
better than if the samples were normally distributed in the range[32]. The time of the day where
the SE takes place also inﬂuence the favour perception. It should not be just after a meal where
people are satiated or just before a meal where people are hungry[32].
The validity and reliability of SE depends on minimising the above mentioned factors and
thoroughly continues training of panelists as well as a detail, clear standardised protocol. If
panelists are not well trained their judgement will be based on preferences in stead of the used
evaluation system whereby the results becomes bias. Bias can also be reduced by increasing the
number of panelists [32]. Preferably a panel should be used for sensory evaluation instead of
one person. However, the more trained the panelist are the less people is needed in the panel.
A general rule is that a panel should consist of minimum ﬁve highly trained panelist, if less
trained a panel of 8-12 assessors are recommended for quality grading [45]. Furthermore, it
is important to monitor the panelist performance to keep track of when further calibration is
needed. In addition the results form the monitoring can be used as feedback to the panelists for
motivation as interested panelists always are more interested in performing a proper job.
The choice of panelists will also affect the panels performance. Humans have different sensitivity
to different chemical stimuli and some stimuli can be detected down to part per trillion [16].
First of all it is important that the panelists are able to detect stimuli giving rise to off-ﬂavours in
a given product. When a descriptive analysis are performed the panelist also need to be able to
describe the off-ﬂavours and their intensities [32].
The use of either internal or external assessors should be determined. The industry most often
uses internal assessors, that have other main tasks in the company. It may give rise to lack of
focus in the session if the assessor is stressed by the other tasks.
Sensory evaluation can be a valuable tool in quality control if the panel consist of well trained
experts that follows a clear evaluation protocol. However, training to maintain valid and reliable
results is a time consuming and expensive process and this become the biggest hurdle for sensory
evaluation.
2.4
Chemometric - Multivariate data analysis
In chemometrics, multivariate data analysis is used to extract information from complex ana-
lytical data sets by ﬁnding latent variables that reduces the overall number of variables. It is a
mathematical tool for ﬁnding patterns, groupings and correlations in large data sets by using
all the measured variables simultaneously, instead of only using one variable at the time as in
univariate data analysis [13]. This is of interest in the context of analysis on sensory evaluation
as most phenomena as ﬂavor perception depends on several factors that inﬂuence each other and
by using all variables hidden phenomena, latent variables, can be visualised. However, it will
only be successful if relevant data is present [28],[13].
Principle Component Analysis (PCA) and Partial Least Square regression (PLS) are two popular
methods for analysing relationships in food [28], [62]. PCA is an explorative qualitative method
used to ﬁnd patterns in a given data set as well as detection of outliers and correlations between
variables[13]. For example, it can be used for distinguishing between different types of oils,
the oxidative stability of an oil or to assess the performance of panelists, [43]. On the other
hand, PLS is a supervised method for ﬁnding correlations between two data sets and quantitative
predictions [12]. For example it is one of the most used method for ﬁnding correlations between
22 of 62
==PAGE:36==
sensory data and instrumental data [43]. As it is a supervised method, meaning the user needs
to have a priori knowledge of the grouping of samples, PCA is often performed before PLS is
conducted [12].
2.4.1
Principle Component Analysis (PCA)
PCA takes a data set with multiple dimension, variables reducing it to fewer dimensions by calcu-
lating latent variables. These are called Principle Components (PC) and are linear combinations
of original variables, that explains most of the original variance in the data set [13]. Variance is
deﬁned as a measure of how far a set of numbers are spread out [13]. This is possible because
the dimension each variable span in a data set is not of equal importance [13]. In fact, it is often
possible to explain 75-90 % of the total variance in a data set consisting of 25-30 variables with
only two or three PCs [32]. In other word PCA is making a new coordinate system. This makes
it easier to investigate patterns as fewer plots can be looked upon.
The ﬁrst principle component (PC1) lies along the direction of maximum variance in the original
data swarm as seen in Figure 2.10, so it explains as much as possible of the variance in the
original data set [13].
The second principle component (PC2) is orthogonal to PC1, as they need to be uncorrelated, and
have the direction of the greatest variance orthogonal to PC 1 [13]. PC3 will then be orthogonal
to both PC1 and PC2, and simultaneously have the direction of the third largest variance [13].
This continues until an appropriate amount of PCs has been calculated, but the maximum number
of PCs are samples minus one or equal to the number of variables. This means that each PC
represents a smaller and smaller variance. Typically, the PCs are calculated by the NIPALS
algorithm, which will not be explained as it is out of the scope of the project [13].
Figure 2.10: Visualisation of how principle components are found in Principle Component Analysis
Choosing the right number of PCs is important as using too many components increases the risk
of interpreting noise, the model is over-ﬁtted, whereas if too few components are chosen there is
a risk of leaving signiﬁcant parts out, under-ﬁtting [13], [3]. The correct number of components
are usually determined from the plot where the explained variance is plotted against PCs. In this
plot the ﬁrst local maximum is looked after on the validated line. After that point, the model
starts to model noise. The fewer PCs containing the necessary information, the more robust the
model would be and the explained variance is a measure of how much of the variation in the
23 of 62
==PAGE:37==
original data has been explained by the model [13].
Points in the data swarm/real space are the projected orthogonally onto each PC as seen in
Figure 2.10 [13]. The distance from each point to the PC is termed the residual and is the
unexplained part of the data by the speciﬁc PC. The distance from the projected point to the
origin is called the score. The further away from the origin, the higher positive or negative score
depending on the direction [13]. In Figure 2.10 the green dot has a high score in PC1, but a low
score in PC2, whereas the yellow dot has a large score in PC2 and a low score in PC1. The red
dot has a low score in both PC1 and PC2. In a score plot two PCs are plotted against each other
and here it can be seen how well the samples are distributed and it is here patterns, grouping and
underlying trends are revealed. Samples that are close to each other are similar [13]. If groups
are well separated it may indicate that a model for each group will be appropriate.
To interpret the score plot a loading plot is used. This can reveal why samples are similar and
what discriminates them from other groups.
Each PC can be represented as a linear combination of the unit vectors of variables in the original
space. The coefﬁcient of each unit vector is called loadings [13]. Therefore, loadings describes
the relationship between the original variables and the PC.
Besides the use for interpretation of the score plot, the loading plot is used to identify which
variables that inﬂuence the model; variables close to the origin have low inﬂuence whereas
variables farthest away from the origin have a strong inﬂuence [13]. Furthermore, it can be used
to investigate which variables that are correlated, that is variables close to each other, and which
variables that are uncorrelated, that is variables in opposite directions [13].
Data is always mean centered, meaning the data swarm is placed in the middle of the coordinate
system, to help the PCA model focus on the variation between the samples. This is accomplished
by subtracting the mean of each variable from the data [13].
When variables do not have the same intensity scale, the variables are normalised by division
with the standard deviation, also called scaling. This makes the variables unit-less and thereby of
a prior equal importance [13].
Outliers are samples which are different from the main data. It is important to remove outliers
because they could disturb the model as PCA assumes that all samples represent a homogeneous
distribution [36]. So when outliers are present, the model will describe the variation between
the outlier and the rest of the samples and not the variation between the samples as wanted [36].
However, removing false true outliers, which contains information, will lead to a model that do
not describe all the hidden patterns in the data [12].
For PCA there are two types of outliers; extreme samples with high score compared to all
other samples, which can be spotted in a score or Hotelling’s T 2 plot and samples with high
residual spotted in a residual plot, which are not well described by the model. However, often
the inﬂuence plot is used to spot outliers as the residuals are plotted against the Hotelling’s T 2.
Samples with a high residual should always be removed, on the other hand samples with high
Hotelling’s T 2 score has the same pattern as the ones described by the model, but they have a
high inﬂuence on the model. Removing these outliers makes the model more robust, but might
miss explaining some parts. As a general rule, not more than 1-2% of data should be removed
[44].

2.4.2
Partial Least Square regression (PLS)
PLS is described as a regression extension of PCA, where the covariance between two data
matrices, X and Y, is investigated [44]. The covariance is a measure of the linear association
between the two matrices or how two variables changes together. A large covariance means
a strong linear dependence between the two variables, they are correlated, whereas a small
covariance means that the variables are not very dependent [12].
PLS can be looked upon as two PCAs; one of X and one of Y. First, a PCA is performed on
Y to describe it by scores and loading, or weights as they are called in PLS. A PCA variant is
made on X. But instead of looking for maximum variance in X the score and loadings are made
in such a way that the ﬁrst score in X has maximum covariance with the score in Y. It means
that the PC must be rotated in X to maximise the linear relationship between X and Y. When the
scores from X and Y are plotted against each other it can be seen if there is a good correlation
between the two data set, a good linear ﬁt will be seen with a r2 of 1. This means that in the
future Y can be predicted from X [44], [13].
It is desired that a model can be generalised, meaning ﬁnding systematic variation that would
not only be speciﬁc to the samples the model is built on, but instead tells something about the
"population" it represent. To test if a model describes the systematic variation or the noise it is
necessary to perform cross-validation (CV) [3], [44].
Models needs to be cross-validated (CV) to ﬁnd the average uncertainty that can be expected on
predicted samples in the future. In cross-validation usually 10-30% of the data is temporary taken
out and a model is calibrated. Then these 10-30% of data is predicted and put back into the model.
This is repeated until all samples has been taken out and predicted once. When having replicates
in a data set it is important to ensure that all replicates are taken out at one time. Otherwise the
CV will not be independent and give an over-optimistic result [3], [44], [36]. Calibration and
validation scores should in theory behave similarly, however, there will always be a general shift
of validated samples having higher residual and leverage. If a sample behaves differently in
the calibration from validation, it is an indication that something is wrong with the reference [44].
Besides looking at the r2, the performance of a PLS model is evaluated by Root Mean Square
Error (RMSE). It is a measurement of the total error or the average error in the y-values. It
describes the standard deviation of difference between predicted and reference data; RMSEC is
a measurement of how well the calibration describes it self, RMSECV is a measurement of how
well samples that are taken out in in cross-validation can be predicted, and Root mean square
error of prediction (RMSEP) is how well a model describe a new data set [36], [44]. It means
the lower RMSE value the better is the model to predict samples with unknown y-values. The
RMSE can be divided in the bias, the systematic error, and the standard error [3].
The precision of a PLS model is improved with the increasing number of relevant X-variables.
Therefore, it can be important to do variable selection if some variables are revealed to be
irrelevant [44].

3.4
Primary oxidation products - Peroxide value (PV)
Peroxide value(PV) was measured to get an indication of the initial oxidation in the oils by
measuring the oxidation by the peroxides of ferrous, Fe2+, to ferric ions, Fe3+. The ferric
ions reacts with thiocyanate generating a reddish complex, Fe(SCN)3, that can be determined
photometrically at 500 nm [49]. It has been assumed, that mainly peroxides from the lipid
oxidation will oxide the ferrous ions.
Approximately 30mg oil was weighed into test tubes with ground glass neck joints and sealed
with a lid. 10 ml of chloroform/methanol was added, overlaid with nitrogen and mixed. Next
50 µl thiocyanate-solution is added, overlaid with nitrogen and mixed, followed by addition
of 50 µl Fe(II)chloride-solution, overlaid with nitrogen and mixed. Then the samples were
incubated for 5 minutes in darkness at room temperature. The absorbance of each sample was
measured on a UV-1800 spectrophometer (Shimadzu Corporation, Kyoto 604-8511, Japan)
with a sipper-system at 500 nm. Before each series of measurement, two blank samples with
chloroform/methanol solution was measured.

following equation:

PV was performed by duplicates by a lab assistance for all sampling days. It should be notes

The development in PV during the storage of the two oil types at 20 ◦C and 40 ◦C can be seen in

From Figure 4.1 it can be seen that PV increased during the storage period for the samples stored

In Figure 4.1 it can be seen that rapeseed oil stored at 40 ◦C has is an induction period of
approximately 15 days. However, the statistical analysis showed that only Day 30 had a PV
signiﬁcant different from all the other days. Petersen et al. (2012a) found signiﬁcant changes in
PV already after 8 days of storage in darkness at 40 ◦C in a conventional rapeseed oil. However,
the initial PV of the oil used in that study was higher, 1.5 meq O2/kg oil compared with 0.14
meq O2/kg oil in the present study, and may explain the difference since more oxidation already
had occurred.
In addition, it can be seen that PV in sunﬂower oil stored at 40 ◦C increases already after 1 day
of storage, but ﬁrst signiﬁcant after Day 13 where the recommended PV of 10 meq O2/kg oil by
35 of 62

Codex Alimentarius (CODEX-STAN 210-1999) has been exceeded. Moreover, it is noted that PV
at Day 13 is slightly higher than PV at Day 15, though not signiﬁcant. Day 13 was one the codes
that had been thawed and is a reasonable explanation for the difference. Furthermore, it seems
like PV starts stagnate after Day 20 as there is no signiﬁcant difference between Day 20 and
Day 30. However, this was the other code that had been thawed, which could have accelerated
the oxidation and not an actual stagnation. This is supported by Crapiste et al. (1999) who ﬁrst
found a stagnation after 80 days in a pressed sunﬂower oil stored at 47 ◦C and did not found a
stagnation through out the storage period of 100 days when the sunﬂower oil was stored at 30 ◦C.
Furthermore, they found that it did matter whether 22.5g, 45g or 90g of oil were ﬁlled into
100ml bottles as the sample with only 22.5g had higher PV than 45g which had a higher PV than
90g. The content of 65g oil at Day 1 as well as 2 for the chemical analyses was divided in two
reducing the content of Day 13 and 20 to approximately 35g. This reduction could have had a
signiﬁcant inﬂuence on oxidative stability of these codes together with the "harsh" treatment
of thawing. Lee et al. (2007) found an induction period in sunﬂower oil stored at 40 ◦C of 15.3
days which is in accordance with the result obtained in this study. Furthermore, they found an
induction period at 25 ◦C of 87.3, which likewise is in accordance with the observation of no
changes in PV during the storage at 20 ◦C in the present study.
Comparing the content of PV in the two oil types, it can be seen that the increase in PV is much
greater for sunﬂower than rapeseed oil although the difference between the oil types ﬁrst be-
comes signiﬁcant at Day 13. It has been found that hydroperoxides in rapeseed are immediately
decomposed into volatiles, whereas in sunﬂower oil there need to be a certain concentration of
hydroperoxides before decomposition occur [7]. Another reason for the difference between the
oil types could be their oxidative stability. Due to the fatty acid composition sunﬂower oil is
believed to be less oxidative stable than rapeseed oil, which these results supports. However, the
oxidative stablity is not only affected by the fatty acid content, but the presence of pro-oxidants
and antioxidants have a large inﬂuence in the stability of oils. As the total composition of the
oils is not known it cannot be conclude that sunﬂower oil, in general, is less oxidative stable than
rapeseed oil, but as AAK often reports problems with sunﬂower oil, it may be true.
The PV result for Storage Experiment 2 is similar to the results obtained in Storage Experiment
1 as expected, since oils from the same batch has been used together with the same storage
conditions. However, the statistical analysis showed that there is a signiﬁcant change already
between Day 0 and 1. However, the PV values are quite low and therefore the uncertainty of the
measurement is high.The result for PV in Storage Experiment 2 can be found in Appendix E
The observed increase in PV for the samples stored at 40 ◦C indicates that lipid oxidation has
occurred in the samples. In addition the results indicates that no oxidation has occurred in the
oils stored at 22 ◦C, however to conclude further the results from the volatile analysis needs to
be looked upon as the hydroperoxides decomposes into secondary oxidation products. If the rate
of hydroperoxides formation equals the rate of hydroperoxides decomposition there will be no
observable changes in PV.

By the used of a 10-member trained expert panel, Jelen et al. (2000) found that low level of
volatiles correlated with most desirable samples and samples with a high content of volatiles

In another study be Przybylski et al. (1993) they investigated the relationship between total
content of volatiles and pleasantness as well as overall odor in conventional rapeseed oil stored
at 60 ◦C for up to 12 days. The pleasantness and overall odor were evaluated at 50 ◦C by a
10-member trained panel on a 15 cm line scale after four training sessions. They found that both
pleasantness and overall odor had a correlation coefﬁcient of 0.9 with total content of volatiles
analysed by GC.
These studies in combination with the present study shows that there is a potential for using
the composition of volatiles for assessing the the overall ﬂavour perception and if not replacing
sensory evaluation it can be used as a precaution measurement.

5 Conclusion
A Principle Component Analysis of the development of volatiles during a storage at 40 ◦C for 30
Day in darkness showed that it was possible to discriminate the two oil types by their content of
volatiles; sunﬂower had a higher content of 1-pentanol, pentanal and hexanal, whereas rapeseed
had a higher content of 1-penten-3-ol, heptanal, E-2-butenal and 2-ethyl furan.
From PCA models of the individual oil types it was concluded that samples could by discrim-
inated by their oxidative state based on the content of volatiles already from early oxidation,
however, the discrimination was better when oils were stored at 50 ◦C than 40 ◦C. Therefore, it
was concluded that increasing temperature accelerates lipid oxidation, which was supported by
the signiﬁcant larger content of volatiles developed during storage at 40 ◦C than at 20 ◦C.
Furthermore, it was concluded that the total ﬂavour intensity increased during storage and that it
could be correlated with the use of attributes used for sensory evaluation at AAK.
Finally, it was concluded that there is a potential for using DHS-GC/MS in combination with
chemometrics to predict the overall ﬂavour perception, which was anti-correlated with content
of volatiles. The best obtained model for rapeseed oil had a RMSECV of 0.447 and a validated
r2 of 0.779.

6 Perspective
In the study it was found that there is a potential for correlating ﬂavour intensities with formation
of volatiles.
Furthermore, the study showed that a panel working in sensory evaluation every day have dif-
ﬁculties in producing calibrated result. This reduces the reliability and validity of the results,
whereupon important choices are taken. Therefore, an establishment of correlation between
sensory and instrumental data will beneﬁt the industry.
The next step is expanding the number of samples in the model and continuing the development
of the models. Especially, it would be interesting to build a model over a whole production year,
whereby the seasonal variation in the raw material could be taken into account. As the time pass,
more and more data will be included in the model, leading to a more robust model.
When more data are available it might be possible to predict the speciﬁc sensory attributes.
The next step is to investigate if speciﬁc volatiles or group of volatiles correlates to speciﬁc
sensory attributes in the different oil matrices. However, this is a time consuming process. This
of interest for the industry, as knowing which volatiles causing speciﬁc unwanted off-ﬂavours
the the industry can investigate what in the production chain are responsible for the quality
deterioration. Then actions for preventing the quality deterioration can be investigated.
The used method for analysis volatiles are time consuming, taken approximately one and a half
hour per sample, but it was seen that the volatiles used in the model had a maximum retention
time of 31 min. Therefore, it might be possible to design a program which is more suitable for
the speciﬁc purpose and thereby shorting the analysis time. However, it would be much more
convenient if spectroscopic methods as NIR or IR could be used. These methods are fast and can
be included on-line in the production.
Another interesting experiment rising from the study is an investigation of the inﬂuence of
freezing, thawing, freezing, thawing, on the lipid oxidation. It was found that samples that had
been thawed an extra time could be discriminated from the other [data not shown], however the
samples analysed were contaminated from previous runs and it is not know if contamination had
effected the used variables.
6.1
Recommendation to AAK
If further investigations conﬁrms the correlation between observations by the sensory panel and
volatiles and it proves to be a high correlation, it is recommended that AAK adapts the methods.
This could either be as a fail safe control or as a warning system to inform the panel when further
sensory evaluation is needed. The money saved on the daily sensory evaluation can then be used
for training of the panel, to improve their calibration and thereby the validity and reliability of
the panel.
It is recommended that the evaluation includes replications of one or two samples, so the perfor-
mance of the panel and the individual panelist can be assessed in each session.
Furthermore, it is recommended that a more clear and detailed protocol for evaluation is devel-
oped to reduce bias from uncertainties and misunderstandings.
Lastly, it is recommended to extend the intensity scale, even though it will makes the evalaution

more complex, however it is believe that the company will be able to get more useful information
from the evaluation, which could be used for ﬁnding the cause of deterioration in the production.
