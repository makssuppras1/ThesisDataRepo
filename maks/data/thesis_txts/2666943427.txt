Abstract
Rechargeable magnesium batteries exhibit highly favourable potential attributes like high energy
densities and low material cost. A major challenge in this ﬁeld, is designing cathodes that allow
for reversible and easy migration of Mg2+ ions within the cathode as this is often hindered by
strong electronic interactions with the lattice. An example of such a cathode candidate addressing
this issue is vanadium tetra sulﬁde VS4. Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations were
used to generate magnesiated VS4 atomic data, which was used to train SchNet graph neural
networks for prediction of forces and energies in MD simulations of VS4. Uncertain areas in
the learned potential energy surface resulted in large force predictions during simulations which
eventually led to a structural breakdown during MD. A 0.3 ns stable simulation trajectory was
obtained with 1000steps
min highlighting the possibility of obtaining stable nanoscale simulations.
This also demonstrated the signiﬁcant computational eﬃciency increase of this method compared
to traditional DFT. To solve the stability issue it is likely that additional magnesiated VS4 data
addressing the magnesium-sulphur bonds has to be generated, for example through an active
learning scheme. If the stability issues are solved, nanoscale MD trajectories for magnesiated VS4
can be obtained at extremely high step rates of approximately ≈1000 steps
min for small structures
and ≈300steps
min for larger structures. These trajectories can then be used to deduce kinetic
properties of magnesium within the VS4 cathode.

Introduction
The emergence of lithium-Ion batteries in the 1990s was a revolutionizing turning point in
modern technology. Attributes like great cycle characteristics, high operating voltage and great
energy-to-weight rations paved the way for Lithium-Ion Batteries (LIBs) to be the dominant
technology for portable devices and consumer electronics the last decades. In the face of the
coming transition to green energy driven technology, the need for proper storage capabilities have
never been greater. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA) the storage capability
worldwide in 2017 was 158 GW, most of which was pumped-storage hydroelectricity (≈95%) [43].
The worldwide wind energy capacity alone, reached 546 GW in 2017. There is clearly a need for
storage technologies that can handle the excess amounts of energy and this demand can simply
not be satisﬁed solely by Li-Ion batteries or other current technologies. In terms of batteries,
due to issues with cost (scalability), energy densities and life-lengths there are extensive eﬀorts
to explore other post-lithium technologies.
Figure 1: An overview of magnesium battery technologies compared to current lithium tech-
nologies as presented by the E-MAGIC project [79]. E-MAGIC is a 4-year FET Proactive project
aimed at researching magnesium battery technologies.
One such alternative can be found in the ﬁeld of multivalent batteries, which has gained traction
in recent years. Here multivalent ions like magnesium (Mg2+), calcium (Ca2+) or aluminium
(Al3+) are used as the charge carrier ions in rechargeable batteries. This has the advantage
of increasing the amount of electrons available per atom which leads to high energy densities.
Magnesium in particular is a highly abundant material, and exhibit high theoretical capacities
of ≈3800 mAh/cm3 which is almost ﬁve times higher than commercial LIB graphite anodes
(≈770 mAh/cm3)[73, 109]. Using a magnesium as metal anode is not limited in the same way
as lithium, as it is known to have dendrite free deposition which allows the exploitation of

Currently, the most reliable way of obtaining accurate material properties on the atomic scale
is through ab initio Density Functional Theory calculations. However, these are extremely
time consuming which limits the scale at which such calculations can be performed. With the
emergence of machine learning as a computational tool the last decade, a wide new range of new
possibilities for material screening and property predictions have opened. Recently, ML methods
have been used to predict atomic forces and energies in order to drive molecular dynamics
simulations [21, 119] . These methods aim to decrease the gap between the accuracy of ab initio
and the eﬃciency of classical methods to obtain MD trajectories of previously unobtainable
scales (nanoseconds).
The aim of this project is to apply such ML methods in the form of a graph neural network
to perform molecular dynamics (MD) simulations of VS4 at the nanosecond scale. This will
address the issue of ion kinetics within multivalent battery cathodes and hopefully provide useful
insights into the migration of magnesium within the VS4 cathode material. Ultimately, the goal
is to obtain successful nanoscale MD simulations from which phenomena like diﬀusivity, phase
separation and intercalation potential at various concentrations can be deduced. In order to
achieve this, DFT calculations will be used to generate magnesiated VS4 structures which will
be used as training data for a graph neural network. The trained graph neural network will then
be used as a calculator for predicting energies and forces to drive MD simulations.
The study starts with the theoretical background for magnesium batteries and ML methods
which include concepts from the generals ﬁelds of multivalent batteries and neural networks for
property predictions. Then comes a description of methods applied in the project which involve
DFT, GNNs and MD simulations. At the end, the results from the collective application of these
methods will be displayed and discussed.

Theoretical background
This section aims to provide a fundamental walk-through of the physical concepts that are used
in this project as well as the ﬁeld of multivalent rechargeable batteries and machine learning
based force ﬁelds. The walk-through is split into two sections. The ﬁrst half revolves around
the physics of Rechargeable Batteries (RB) as well as the various RB types with a special focus
on Multivalent Rechargeable Batteries and magnesium cathodes. The second half moves on to
exploring Machine Learning (ML) methods for modelling of materials at the atomic scale.
2.1
Battery fundamentals
A galvanic cell is the foundation of a full ﬂedged battery and can be seen in its most basic form in
ﬁgure 2. It is made up of the key components: anode, cathode, electrolyte and a semi-permeable
barrier.
Figure 2: The fundamental structure of a basic galvanic battery cell, compromised of an
electrolyte solution, an anode and cathode and a semi-permeable barrier [80].
The fundamental concept of a galvanic cell is to connect two electrodes through an external
circuit to have the materials in the two electrodes react chemically and to use this reaction to
produce or store energy. To achieve this there must be a chemical driving force driving the
reactions. This force comes from a diﬀerence of chemical potential of the two electrodes and
can be expressed as the diﬀerence in the Gibbs standard free energy of the reactants and the
products [42]. This force creates a transport of electrons and ions between the electrodes. The
electrons from the anode move through the external circuit while the ions move through the
electrolyte. Thus, the anode is the electrode responsible for supplying electrons during discharge.
This happens when a material is oxidized at the anode which yields an electron and an ion that
move to the cathode via separate pathways. The barrier indicated on ﬁgure 2 is there to separate
the two electrodes and to promote ionic transport between the electrodes. At the same time it
should prevent electronic transport as this would cause a short-circuit of the cell. By having a

There are many terms related to batteries and it is important to be able to distinguish between
them. The electrochemical capacity of a battery depends on the amount of charge available
for exchange between the electrodes. The amount of charge available can either be increased
by simply increasing amount of electrode material used, or by using materials with diﬀerent
electrochemical properties. Formally, the charge capacity can be written as
Energy =

Edq
Here E is the electrochemical cell voltage and it is the potential diﬀerence of the redox couples
at each of the electrodes, while q is the amount of charge. Thus, the total energy a battery
can deliver is the integral of the voltage times the charge capacity [42]. Simply increasing
the amount of electrode material to gain more energy is not a viable option in most practical
applications. Therefore, one has to ﬁnd materials with high energy densities. The energy density
is for a battery can be deﬁned as volumetric or gravimetric energy density. Volumetric energy
density refers to the energy per unit volume (Wh/liter) and gravimetric energy density refers
to the energy per unit of mass (Wh/kg). The ideal case would be to have a battery exhibiting
high values in terms of both mass and volume, but this is seldom the case as there is often a
fundamental trade-oﬀbetween the two.
In a RB, the behaviour of the anode and cathode change depending on if the battery is in a
charging or discharging state. Thus, to avoid confusion the discharge state is used as the basis
when discussing electrode behaviour from this point forward.
2.2
Conversion and intercalation type batteries
There are many ways of designing the cell presented in ﬁgure 2 and there are two major ways
of performing the transfer of charges in a battery which is decided by the type of electrode. In
general, there are two major types of electrodes, conversion and intercalation. Conversion type
electrode materials are based on the conversion of some compound to another compound. With
a Li battery as reference, the conversion type reaction can be written as seen in equation (1)
[129].
TMaXb + (b × x)Li+ + (b · x)e– ⇋aTM0 + bLicX
(1)
Here TM represents a transition metal and X an anionic species. Conversion reactions are
often able to yield higher capacities compared to intercalation which will be discussed below.
The drawback of conversion type batteries is that the conversion reactions often are prone to
high volume expansion, low ion mobility and hysteresis [129, 86]. For this reason, intercalation
type electrodes have been the preferred choice for rechargeable batteries for most commercial
applications.
The intercalation type batteries rely on a diﬀerent mechanism and they are used in the well
known LIBs. For such a battery the electrodes consist of host material that can store ions
in its structure without undergoing any major structural changes itself. Instead of forming
new compounds, the charge carrier ion is stored within the anode and cathode material and
is transferred between the structures during charge/discharge. The next section discusses a
concrete example of a intercalation type battery, namely a regular Li-ion intercalation battery
which can be seen as a reference for magnesium batteries as these type of batteries represent
state-of-the-art rechargeable batteries.

2.2.1
Lithium-ion intercalation battery
Lithium-ion rechargeable batteries are a heavily researched and well understood technology and
in many ways form the reference point for which new batteries are designed. These batteries are
based on intercalation and this mechanism is illustrated for a LIB in ﬁgure 3. Graphite is by far
the most common choice of material for the anode while the cathode is often some type of metal
oxide. The most common technologies involve oxides of manganese Mn, cobalt Co, nickel Ni
or aluminium Al as the cathode. The energy density of these variations vary from 100 to 170
Wh/kg with cell voltages around 4 V. The high cell voltage arises from the high diﬀerence in
chemical potential for the metal oxides with lithium and graphite. In terms of the electrolyte,
current materials used are often a form of organic solvents with one lithium salt, usually LiPF6
[48, 29].
Figure 3: Simpliﬁed ﬁgure of a lithium-ion intercalation battery. The electrode separator is not
indicated on the ﬁgure [37].
In the discharged state, the graphite is empty and the metal oxide contains all the Li+ ions. When
a voltage is applied to the electrodes the Li+ ions migrate out of the metal oxide. This migration
is crucial for cell performance in both the charging and discharging state as the trapping of ions
within the structures can severely inhibit cell performance. Nevertheless, the Li+ ions from the
oxide migrate through the electrolyte and through the separating barrier into the graphite layers
as shown in ﬁgure 3. During discharge, the reverse process occurs and as such, this battery is
simply Li+ ions moving in and out of the crystal and layered structures [48]. As an example, the
chemical reactions taking place in a cobalt based LIBs are given in equations (2) and (3).

At this point it is relevant to discuss why LIBs are widely used, what advantages they have and
why there is extensive research surrounding alternatives to these. The main advantages that Li
ion batteries oﬀer are [77]:
• High working voltage.
• Relatively high energy density.
• Great cycle characteristics.
• With a carbon anode the formation of dendrites is prevented.
Despite these positive characteristics there is a major issue in scalability. As mentioned, the
availability of lithium is much less compared to other compounds, this is an issue when trying
to fulﬁl the increasing energy storage demands coming from for example wind or solar energy
[88, 129]. Another issue is not so much related to the actual battery but rather why a graphite
intercalation anode is used instead of a metal Li anode. A metal electrode with Li has a higher
volumetric and gravimetric capacity which would certainly be preferred over the lower density
counterpart in graphite based anodes. However, these are more prone to dendrite formation
during the charge/discharge process in a RB [88]. Dendrites are related to non-uniform surface
changes on the metal anode which causes a decrease in mechanical stability of the battery and
can lead to internal short-circuits [48]. This issue is avoided with graphite intercalation at the
cost of energy density resulting in a safer battery which was the original motivation behind LIBs
[108].
From here, the focus is turned towards the multivalent options to lithium. These are known for
being less prone to dendrite formation and their divalent nature pose a compelling incentive for
further research [88].
2.3
Multivalent rechargeable batteries.
To have a rechargeable battery, the ﬂow of ions from one electrode to the other should be
reversible. This imposes some restrictive requirements on the design choices and materials when
designing such batteries. There are a vast amount of materials and conﬁgurations available
for this matter. To understand the motivation behind researching Rechargeable Magnesium
Batteries (RMBs) and the current challenges in developing this technology, it is key to realize
the purpose of each of the components and to recognize the requirements for these components
for use in a rechargeable magnesium battery.
First it is relevant to discuss the limiting factors for the electrode reactions. If the ﬂow of either
reaction component stops, the reaction is halted [42]. In an open-circuit, the electrons can not
move through the external circuit to reduce the cation at the cathode. Likewise, if there are no
positive ions arriving at the cathode, then there is no driving force on the electrons. This means

Multivalent Rechargeable Batteries (MRBs) in general, ion transport. Coulombic interactions

2.4.1
Calcium
Calcium, similarly to magnesium, forms divalent ions (Ca2+). The abundance of calcium is
twice that of magnesium and it is the ﬁfth most abundant element in the earths crust [75]. The
huge abundance of calcium means that this technology would have a serious cost advantage
compared to other technologies it ever became a serious competitor to current state-of-the-art
RBs. Research based on these batteries is more recent as the electrodeposition of calcium was
thought to be impossible due to the nature of the SEI layer forming on the calcium anode. As
mentioned, the SEI layer for lithium anodes was crucial for reversibility as it allowed reversible
reactions by blocking further decomposing of the electrolyte and by conducting Li+ ions. For
calcium this was initially shown by Aurbach et al. to not be the case in 1991 [3]. The main
reason being that calcium mobility in the SEI layer was poor. To have calcium dissolution there
would have to be a breakdown of the SEI layer. It was suggested that some combinations of
solvents could justify further research of calcium in primary batteries, not secondary.
Despite all this, in 2016 Ponrounch et al. presented a calcium based battery, that was able to
perform successful plating and stripping of calcium on the electrodes during cycling. This was
done using well known aprotic organic electrolytes in EC:PC (Ethylene Carbonate–Propylene
Carbonate) solvents. Thus, paving the way for rechargeable calcium batteries. They studied
both Ca(ClO2)2 and Ca(BF4)2 and found Ca(BF4)2 to be the best electrolyte candidate. It was
found that this electrolyte created a SEI layer permeable to Ca2+ thus contradicting the ﬁndings
by Aurbach et al. A cyclic voltammogram for this electrolyte can be seen in ﬁgure 5. The ﬁgure
shows a stable cycling of plating/stripping of calcium which supports the notion of rechargeable
calcium batteries. However, this was only achieved at an operating temperature of 100 °C which
is not ideal for most practical use cases. Clearly this technology is in a very early phase. It
was not thought to be viable until recently and future research will focus on ﬁnding suitable
cathode materials that allow for reversible Ca2+ insertion to obtain proof of concept calcium
batteries.

2.5
Magnesium batteries
This section moves on to the discussion of magnesium batteries which is the subject in this study.
Magnesium does not suﬀer from a high reduction potential as aluminium does with a reduction
potential of -2.4 V vs Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE). It also exhibits great volumetric
capacity (ﬁg 4) and allows for dendrite free deposition, which is a major issue with Li metal
anodes. The challenges of various magnesium batteries are discussed below, together with an
overview of the current state-of-the art cathode materials and electrolytes for Rechargeable
Magnesium Battery (RMB) application.
The challenges of RMBs are similar to those of the previously discussed multivalent options.
Comparable to calcium batteries, one has to ﬁnd suitable electrolytes with electochemical
compatibility towards the electrodes to allow reversible magnesium deposition at the metal
anode [18]. In contrast to Li, the formed SEI layer is for magnesium often of a passivating
nature instead of conducting towards Mg2+ ions [69]. Early research on magnesium batteries
revealed that solutions of simple magnesium salts such as Mg(ClO4)2 and Mg(BF4)2 did not allow
reversible magnesium deposition in solvents such as acetonitrile, propylene carbonate and N,N-
dimethylformide [97]. The formed SEI layers were found to have extremely low Mg-ion mobility
which would eﬀectively diminish the capacity and eventually lead to cell failure. Furthermore, it
has been reported that the when water impurities were present in the non-aqueous electrolytes,
passivating MgO or Mg(OH)2 ﬁlms would form for even concentrations at ppm level [40, 24,
127].

2.5.1
Pioneering work and Chevrel phase
One of the ﬁrst functional magnesium batteries was made by Aurbach et al. with a work
published in 2000 [4] presenting a proof-of-concept RMB, which was based on inital work made
by Gregory et al in 1990 [38]. Three possible electrolytes for RMB were presented. These were
based on tetrahydrofuran(THF) as solvent and Mg organohaloaluminate salts. Namely, BuMgCl,
Mg(BPh2Bu2)2 and Mg(AlCl2BuEt)2. All three solutions allowed reversible Mg deposition but
Mg(AlCl2BuEt)2 showed the best stability at the anode during cycling. They also veriﬁed that
no surface passivation layer was formed. The cathode proposed in the same paper was a Chevrel
Phase (CP) Mo6S8, that still serves as the current benchmark for magnesium battery cathodes
[73]. The average working voltage of the cathode was 1.2 V vs Mg +
2 /Mg and the maximum
reversible capacity was 130 mAhg−1 displaying stability over thousands of cycles.
Chevrel phases are a series of compounds with superconducting properties and the general
formula MxMo6S8 which was ﬁrst reported by Chevrel et al. [32]. Here M can be a wide range
of metals and in this particular case the metal is magnesium. The MxMo6S8 has a structure
which consists of stacking sulphur cubes with Mo arranged in a octahedral shape inside the
cubes shown on the right in ﬁgure 6. The magnesium is able to sit between the cubes and move
reversibly within the structures due to favorable kinetics which are explained by the electronical
conﬁguration of the orbitals. The octahedral Mo structures exhibit metallic bonding with highly
delocalized orbitals which allow for easy accommodation of electrons within the Mo6 structures
[18, 73]. In 2015 Thole et al. investigated the change in electronical structure of Chevrel phase
during Mg intercalation [116]. A Bader analysis was performed revealing that the sulphur atoms
(the outer cube structure in ﬁg. 6b) eﬀectively screen the 2+ charge on the magnesium. This is
due to fact that the Mo6S8 units are intrinsically deﬁcient of 4 electrons and when two magnesium
ions arrive, electrons are transferred in to stabilize the Mo6S8 structure which creates a highly
localized electronic screening. This allows for easier Mg2+ diﬀusion. However, this favourable
electronic structure, is a trade-oﬀfor lower cathode voltage (lower capacity) which is a major
limitation to Chevrel phase cathodes [116, 62, 73].

2.5.3
Magnesium-sulphur conversion batteries
A diﬀerent segment of the research community has its focus turned towards conversion type
cathodes for RMB with sulphur being the cathode material. In fact, as one can see, sulphur had
been a key component in both the state-of-the-art CP and spinel structures. The Mg-S batteries
are based on the redox reaction between magnesium and sulphur displayed in equation 4.
S + Mg2+ + 2e– →MgS
(4)
The combination of magnesium and sulphur has an electronegativity diﬀerence of 1.7 V and oﬀer
theoretical energy densities of 1330 Whkg−1 and 2500 WhL−1 [93]. The low volumetric density
on the sulphur side is compensated by the higher volumetric energy density of magnesium, which
together with the possibility of dendrite free deposition is why the Mg-S couple is of great interest.
Recent papers have presented coin cells displaying capacities between 400 to 900 mAh/g at
50-100 cycles [126, 132, 128].
The study of Mg S for rechargeable magnesium batteries is also at an early stage and has
yet to solve several limitations. A major issue lies with the electrolyte. There is a search for
electrolytes that can eﬀectively transfer magnesium ions and display electrochemical stability
towards sulphur cathodes [56]. Common electrolytes for Mg batteries is of an nucleophilic nature.
However, sulphur is an electrophilic element, which means that commonly used nucleophilic
electrolytes for Mg systems are incompatible with the sulphur electrode. This has prompted a
search for non-nucleophilic electrolytes and a reversible Mg S battery with a ﬁrst compatible
non-nucleophilic electrolyte was presented by Kim et al in 2011. [51]. This electrolyte was
a Mg salt enhanced by Lewis acid Mg2Cl3 HMDSAlCl3 and variations of this is a common
electrolyte approach. A newer common approach is ﬂuorinated alkoxyborate-based electrolytes
and Zhao-Karger et al. reported a capacity of 200mAh/g for such an electrolyte, highlighting its
potential [131].
In terms of the sulphur cathode, there are also issues with the product of the magnesium and
sulphur reactions at the cathode. Sulphur is during discharge converted to various forms of
polysulﬁdes [122]. An example of the Mg-S reaction evolution during discharge is seen in ﬁgure
8. The intermediate polysulﬁdes (PS) will dissolve into the electrolyte, inhabiting performance
by what is called the ”shuttle” eﬀect [27, 20]. The dissolved PS can migrate to the metal anode
and cause a side reaction, which reduces the PS and ”steals” electrons from the outer circuit.
The reduced PS can then recombine with another PS back at the cathode, getting oxidized,
which eﬀectively creates a cyclic process. This leads to capacity degradation and low Coulombic
eﬃciency. In addition to this, the products formed at the cathode, namely MgS2 and MgS, are
electrochemically inactive which can inhibit reversible charging/discharging.

2.6
Molecular dynamics
Molecular Dynamics (MD) is a computational technique used to generate dynamical trajectories
of atomic systems by solving Newtons equation of motion numerically. Properties associated
with small atomic structures are not enough to characterize a macroscopic system. What one
can determine experimentally about material properties, for example for a VS4 battery cathode,
depends on ensemble averages over large atomic structures at certain temperatures or pressures.
MD allows the computation of such ensemble averages by time evolution of an atomic system
based on the forces between the atoms. To achieve force-ﬁelds of high accuracy it is possible to
perform so called Ab-Initio Molecular Dynamics (AIMD) where MD simulations are coupled
with DFT calculations to calculate energies and forces. However, this simulation method is
prohibitively expensive to perform [119, 19]. A newer approach aims to use ML to learn such
force ﬁelds based on ab initio training data and this is further discussed in section 2.8.

system is allowed to exchange energy with the environment (at a temperature T) and the volume
is kept constant. A ﬁnal example is the NPT ensemble where the pressure and temperature is
kept constant [12].

2.9.4
Graph models
There exists many diﬀerent graph-based models that are used for various applications. In 2015,
Duvenaud et al. presented a NN for learning molecular ﬁngerprints based on graph representations
[28]. The work aimed to design a diﬀerential ﬁngerprint based on the state-of-the-art for molecular
ﬁngerprints, Extended Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP) [96]. This allowed for optimization of
the ﬁngerprints outperforming the regular ECFPs. Another MPNN was presented by Battaglia
et al. in 2016 [8]. This model was designed to perform atomistic simulations by decomposing
a graph problem into objects and relations and learning the dynamics of the objects in the
graph based on this. The building blocks of the model was standard deep learning multilayer
perceptrons (MLPs) and matrix operations. The model could very accurately predict next-step in
the dynamics simulations but was not designed to be able to share learning across relations.
Models speciﬁcally designed to be able to predict molecule properties are discussed in the
following. An example of this is the molecular Graph Convolutions (GC) model by Kearnes
et al. [50] which was similar to the work by Duvenaud et al. The basis of this model was what the
authors deﬁned as Weave modules. These modules consisted of operations on atom and atom-pair
layers. The atom layer would contain an n-dimensional vector associated with each atom and
the pair layer would contain an n-dimensional vector for each pair of atoms. The output of this
module would be a molecule-level representation of features which could be treated as a regular
multitask-problem. A comparison reavealed that the GC model slightly outperformed the neural
ﬁngerprints presented by Duvenaud et al. on predictions on three separate datasets. Another
example is the Gated-Graph neural network(GGNN) presented by Li et al. in 2016 [64].
The model used gated recurrent units (Cho et al. [23]) and ﬁt well within the MPNN framework
as the authors deﬁned a graph-level readout function directly.
In addition to proposing the MPNN framework, Gilmer at al. also proposed their own model,
enn-s2s, was inspired by the GGNN mentioned above. The model used an edge network message
function given by

and a readout function based on a set2set model from Vinyals et al. [120], hence the denotion
enn-s2s. Here A(evw) is a neural network that maps the edge vector to a square matrix.
This model displayed a strong inductive bias towards learning molecular relations to accurately
predict molecular properties. It was shown to outperform other previous baselines for predicting
properties by a signiﬁcant margin.
Finally, there is the SchNet MPNN presented by Sch¨utt et al. in 2017 which is one of the best
performing MPNNs to date [101]. The main breakthrough in this model was the application
of continuous-ﬁlter convolutions, enabling smooth energy predictions. The model was designed
speciﬁcally to be able to predict energies and forces which is ideal for molecular dynamics

To obtain accurate values for energies and forces in the molecular structures it is relevant to
apply the ab initio Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations. Ab initio methods are based
on the ﬁrst principles of quantum mechanics to calculate material properties, not incorporating
empirical parameters [49]. The ﬁrst principles approach starts from the absolute base of what
is known for condensed matter systems. They are all made of atoms with positively charged
nuclei and negatively charged electrons. The interactions among the electrons and nuclei is what

problem for many-body systems which can lead to computationally expensive calculations.

The ultimate goal of DFT is to be able to calculate energies and it is is therefore useful to write

EV is the potential due to the ﬁxed nuclei, ET is the kinetic energy term, EJ is the energy from

exclusion principle, which states that two electrons of same spin state can never be found at
the same place, and as a result the electron repulsion energy is aﬀected [61]. ET , EV , EJ are in
many methods calculated in identical fashion. It is the exchange-correlation term that separates
for example DFT from Hartree-Fock methods.

approximating the correlation energy to the same accuracy. This means that errors appearing
in the correlation approximation can not be compensated by the known exchange energy used.
Therefore, one wants to ﬁnd approximations where the two terms are treated in a similar manner

Two prevailing ways of approximating the exchange-correlation functional is the local-density
approximation (LDA) and the other is methods applying corrections to the LDA. The essence
of LDAs is to consider an inhomogeneous electronic gas as locally homogeneous, hence the
name. And then use what is denoted as the exchange-correlation hole which is deﬁned for a
homogeneous electron gas and known to a high accuracy. The exchange-correlation hole refers to
the fact the probability of ﬁnding an electron at a certain position (r1) given that there is an
electron at another position (r2) decreases when the distance between the two electrons decrease.
Local energy densities are then calculated at all points r within a certain volume by integrating
the value of the electronic density at the points r.

In general, the exact wavefunction of an electronic system is extremely complex. However, it
can be approximated by a simpler wavefunction and other electronic structure methods use
a self-consistent ﬁeld method (SCF) where an approximate Hamiltonian is selected and used
to solve the Schrodinger equation iteratively to obtain a more accurate set of orbitals each
iteration until the results converge. Hartree-Fock methods are examples of this [45]. There exist

There are many semiempirical methods available and most are based on neglect of diatomic

diﬀerential overlap in electronic interaction integrals. Eﬀorts have been made towards improving

method is eﬀective at modelling chemical systems as well as predicting heat of formation2 but

An example of a program incorporating all these methods is the Molecular Orbital PACkage

concepts used are mainly based on Hartree-Fock methods, which as opposed to the DFT method
discussed in section (2.11), treats the electron-electron interactions by a more crude approximation.
Therefore these methods suﬀer in accuracy compared to DFT [7]. As such, this calculator can
be used to obtain approximate solutions to the forces and energies of atomic structures. This
with the beneﬁt of a speed up in computation time.

Method
This section presents the methods used for obtaining the results in this project. The main tool
used was the SchNet model with edge updates and a detailed description of how the model was
used is therefore performed. Additional methods include density functional theory calculations
for generation of training data, as well as molecular dynamics simulations with the trained
SchNet models.
Computational practicalities
All processes were performed with the Python programming language and the data was handled
within the Atomic Simulation Environment (ASE) [58]. Calculations were done both on the
LSF 10 cluster at DTU Compute4 and the Niﬂheim cluster at DTU Physics5. LSF 10 was used
for training the majority of the ML models, and for performing MD simulations due to the
availability of GPUs. As of January 2021, new GPUs were also installed on Niﬂheim. These
were applied for the last part of the MD simulation experiments. Niﬂheim was used mainly for
data generation in the data generation phase due to the availability of strong multicore CPUs
suitable for DFT calculations.
4.1
Data generation
The initial VS4 atomic data was provided by Piotr Jankowski, Postdoc at DTU Energy. The data
consisted of DFT-generated MgxVS4 unit cell structures in the form of ASE atoms objects6. The
number of magnesium in the cells ranged from one to twelve magnesium. A detailed description
of the data will be performed in the results section 5.1.
To get an accurate and robust model, thousands of VS4 structures were generated in addition
to the initial data. This process had two phases. The ﬁrst phase involved taking empty VS4
structures and enriching them with 1-12 magnesium. Then, these structures were relaxed within
ASE to obtain stable low force and low energy structures. The second phase involved taking the
optimized structures and performing ab initio DFT calculations to obtain a better representation
of the minimized structures and their forces and energies as DFT calculations are known for
their high accuracy. The methods used for the two phases are described below.
4.1.1
Phase 1
The workﬂow for generating the initial minimum structures is seen in ﬁgure 23. In this part
the goal was to obtain minimized structures with diﬀerent magnesiation. The ﬁrst step was to

of the many-body system. To help stabilize the algorithm the linesearch version of BFGS was
applied.

increased computational eﬃciency to obtain approximate minima structures quickly, which were
then reﬁned by accurate DFT calculations in Phase 2. An important note in regards to the
MOPAC calculations was that the MOPAC calculator parallelized poorly across the requested
CPU cores. Only 40 % of the CPUs were utilized on a 16 core node. Therefore, it was necessary
to submit two separate MOPAC calculation jobs to the same node, each job requesting 8 of the
16 cores. In this way, the core utilization was maximized and it prevented the jobs from beeing
suspended by the superuser.

stochastic gradient descent algorithm Adam (Adaptive moment estimation) with learning rates

previous experience, this amount of memory could lead to some limitations on the hyperparameters
to prevent the calculations from running out of memory at reasonable batch sizes. It was found
that 32 GBs were suﬃcient for all hyperparameter optimization on the VS4 datasets. Furthermore,
the training could only run for 24 hour sessions before they had to be restarted due to time
constraints in the LSF 10 cluster. The model was saved periodically when a certain criterion was
met. In the original code this criterion was MAE(energy) < MAE(energy)best, meaning that the
model was saved if the mean absolute error was the lowest so far. This became an issue when
the force weight parameter was altered to be favoured towards forces as this led to the energy
error not necessarily improving over the course of a whole training session. Therefore, the runner
script for SchNetE was altered to also check the condition MAE(forces) < MAE(forces)best which
ensured periodic saving of the model when training on high force weight.
Due to a lack of available GPUs and queue priorities in the HPC cluster at DTU, only a few
models could be trained at a time. Therefore, the hyperparameter optimization process was long.
Some models had to be restarted up to 10 times before converging in terms of validation error
and loss.

4.2.2
SchNetE hyperparamaters
It is key to properly explain what hyperparameters were available in the SchNetE model and
what they signify, such that the reader can properly understand the diﬀerent elements in the
hyperparameter optimization and the thought process behind the chosen experimental directions.
In addition to the ones described below, there were the standard hyperparameters for NNs; batch
size (32 as default) and learning rate (10−4 as default).
Interaction steps: Decided how many times the message was passed in the interaction block.
Increasing this value would increase the perceptive ﬁeld of an atom and a wide range of values
were tried for this parameter (3-15). Interactions will be used in short as a description for this
parameter.
Force weight: Was the weight on the force loss for the SchNetE model (equation 24). A higher
value meant that the model would mainly optimize of the force loss during training. Some models
where trained with a weight of 0.5, but most models were shifted towards higher weights on the
force loss (0.90 −0.999).
Atomwise normalization(AN): Decided whether normalization constants were based on a
graph-level output or on an atom-level. This parameter should always be enabled if one wants
to use the model for predictions on structures of diﬀerent size than the training data.
Edge updates: This was an option to enable or disable the update on the edges during training.
Some models were trained with this turned of, but the vast majority were trained with edge
updates. When disabled, the SchNetE model reduces to the regular SchNet model.
Node size: Decided the size of the hidden state h containing information about nodes (atoms).
Increasing this would increase the complexity of the model. Usual values were 64 and 128.

As the errors on forces and energies have been crucial for model training and will be analyzed
extensively in this section, it must be clear what the errors on forces and energy speciﬁcally
signify. For energy, the errors are obtained by comparing the total energy of the structures to
the target energy of each structure. Thus, the energy errors are obtained by comparing the
total energy of a structure with the target value that was calculated by DFT. The forces on the
structures are given as arrays with three components (x,y,z) with the number of rows equal to
the number of atoms in the structure. The error is then obtained by comparing the obtained
force array with the target force array. In this computation the force errors are divided by the
number of nodes (atoms) per structure. This means that the force error values are the average
errors on the forces per atom. Furthermore, the term energy will be used to refer to the total
potential energy of a structure unless otherwise speciﬁed.

5.3.1
Phase 1
In Phase 1, 205 stable minima structures with low forces and low energies were obtained by
performing semi-empirical quantum calculations with MOPAC. As described in the method
section, this was done by placing magnesium atoms in empty VS4 structures and then relaxing
the atoms into low energy conﬁgurations. At ﬁrst, optimizations with BFGS and MOPAC would
stagnate at forces above 1 eV/˚A, never reaching the optimization cutoﬀof 0.05 eV/˚A. The
algorithm would move all atoms, and simply bounce the atoms back and forth on the energy
surface without going towards the minimum. Therefore, the vanadium atoms were ﬁxed to their
positions for the optimizations as this was expected to make the structure more rigid allowing
the magnesium to be relaxed without distorting the whole lattice. This was found to be a great
improvement on the eﬀectiveness of the algorithm and the calculation converged to the cutoﬀ
force.

Verifying DFT calculations
It was desirable to check the generated data for any discrepancies in the DFT calculation. This
was done to prevent the inclusion of data from an optimization where the convergence towards a
certain optimized structure was suddenly disrupted, leading to signiﬁcant change in the structure.
This could lead to noise in the training data.
The energy minimization process for the both the atomic relaxation and the full cell relaxation
was investigated by plotting the energy as a function of the optimization steps. If there were
any sudden jumps in the energy curves then the structures resulting from this calculation were
discarded. This jump was observed from some optimizations in Phase 1 with the MOPAC
calculator. For each of the 205 input minima structures, the energy was plotted for each step in
both optimization with Calculator 1 and cell relaxation with Calculator 2. This resulted in
410 plots and all of them were accepted as no structural changes were observed in the energy
curves (see ﬁgure 85 in appendix C).

5.5
Generation of additional MD data
The models discussed until now, were all trained on gen set which was simply relaxation data
from Calculator 2. Another dataset was made to enrich the dataset further. It was decided to
obtain DFT driven MD trajectories to hopefully provide additional robustness for the models.
These MD trajectories were obtained by performing MD on the last structure of each of the
205 full cell relaxations. This was done with the Langevin thermostat at 600 Kelvin. As these
cells had already been optimized, these DFT calculations only had to optimize on the atomic
positions during MD and therefore Calculator 1 was used. However, the more accurate SCAN
functional was used instead of PBEsol. Due to time limits it was not feasible to wait for all
205 calculations to ﬁnish as each calculation would take up to 48 hours. 38 calculations were
therefore put on hold and the trajectories from the other 167 calculations were collected and
combined with the gen set data from Phase 2. This became what was labelled the gen setMD
dataset.
The distribution of the generated MD data can be seen in ﬁgure 39. The force distribution
reveals that the generated data had forces centered at approximately 1.8 eV/˚A. All of this data
was included in gen setMD such that the model could learn higher energy states appropriate
for MD simulations.

5.7
Molecular dynamics simulations
The results from the data generation and the model training on the generated data has been
discussed. Subsequently, the trained models were applied as ML calculators to predict energies
and forces for molecular dynamics simulations. This section will show the results and insights
acquired during these experiments. Multiple models were tried as calculators for these simulations
and various approaches were tried to obtain reasonable results. First, some initial considerations
for performing the MD simulations is discussed.
5.7.1
Finding MD approach
The MD simulations were to be performed with the 1200 atom VS4 structure discussed in the
methods section as basis. Initially, the idea was to use do minimahopping on such magnesiated
large structures with the trained models as calculators. Then the minimas obtained from these
calculations would be passed for MD simulations.
No models that had trained on the extra MD data (gen setMD) were available at the start of
these simulations. Therefore, the best available model at the beginning in terms of validation
error, was the one seen in ﬁgure 37 with 15 interaction steps and 128 node size trained for over 3
million steps on gen set.
This model was applied as calculator for a minimahopping simulation on a large VS4 cell with
2 magnesium per unit cell. To clarify, this way of describing the magnesium contents of the
large supercells will be used extensively. 2 magnesium per unit cell indicate that the supercell
was created by expanding unit structures containing 2 magnesium and the total amount of
magnesium in the large cell is therefore signiﬁcantly greater than 2.
The ﬁrst part of a minimahopping simulation consists of relaxing the atom into lower energy
states. The result from this optimization is shown in ﬁgure 45. It is clear that the algorithm
stagnates quickly and is unable to ﬁnd a minimum as the force cutoﬀcriteria was 0.05 eV/A. The
minimum force obtained was 0.145 eV/A. This is likely an expression of the intrinsic prediction
error in the model. For this model the minimum RMSE on forces for the validation set was
approximately 0.03. It is likely that the model reached its PES ”resolution” limit at 0.145 eV/˚A
and could not optimize further.

5.8
Ensemble calculators for MD
In an ensemble approach, multiple models are used to predict forces and energies, which form a
prediction ensemble. Then, the statistical properties of this ensemble can be used to strengthen
the predictive power of a model. The forces and energies used to adjust the atoms during MD
are then calculated based on the predictions of multiple models instead of one. The idea was
that this could possible help stabilize the simulations to prevent the blow-out. If this would
not be the case, the ensemble statistics could provide insights into calculator behaviour at the
breaking points.
There are many ways of forming such a model prediction ensemble. A popular method it the
bootstrap method. Here the models are trained on diﬀerent datasets that are obtained by
randomly sampling the whole dataset with replacement [85]. For this project a simpler method
was used where 5 identical models were trained on the same datasplits and then relying on the
inherent stochasticity of the model weights to form the ensemble. The 5 models were designed
to have lower complexity as simpler models were expected to converge faster which was desirable
to obtain faster results due to time limits. The hyperparameters of the ﬁve trained models can
be seen in table 12 and the corresponding learning curves are presented in ﬁgure 60. Here it is
important to notice that the models were trained with the updated model that had implemented
smooth cutoﬀ. The ﬁve models were then used as inputs to an ensemble calculator. The output
of the calculator would be the mean force and mean energy predictions of the ﬁve diﬀerent
models.

5.9
Visualizing high force predictions
During the execution of some simulations it was discovered that two separate MD simulations
behaved similarly at the breaking point. One simulation was a regular MD simulation while the
other was a minimahopping simulation. Both simulations were performed on the same starting
structure with 6 magnesium and with the same model (Model4). Figure 67 displays the two
simulation structures right at the point where the forces were starting to blow up with black
circles indicating the atoms to focus on. Please note that the green magnesium atom experiencing
the large force is in fact right next to the yellow sulphur atom at the opposite side that also has
a large force. The atom could not be wrapped around due to the bug with the wrap() method
for the ML calculator discussed previously. If wrap was applied, the forces would disappear from
the visualization.
Surprisingly, the same Mg-S atom pair marked with black circles caused the breakdown in both
cases even though it was two diﬀerent simulations. This consistency for diﬀerent simulations was
interesting as it highlighted the model had not learned to predict forces and energies for this
speciﬁc conﬁguration.

Discussion
Message passing steps and ﬁlter cutoﬀ
A large amount of SchNetE models were trained with a lot of variety in hyperparameters. One of
the main parameters that was changed, was the number of messages passing steps (interactions)
in the model, and values as high as 15 was tried for this parameter. Previous studies with
this model often applied interaction steps in the range 1 to 6 [101, 46]. Trying higher number
of interaction steps was useful to see how the learning curves responded to this and if there
would be a signiﬁcant change. However, it did not appear that there was any signiﬁcant eﬀect
of having the large number of interactions steps. This is in accordance with what was stated
in the original SchNetE paper where a conclusions was that merely increasing the interactions
steps did not improve the performance of the models they applied. This was for predicting
properties on molecules in the QM9 dataset. The eﬀect of changing the cutoﬀdistance for the
ﬁlter functions was not studied in detail, only a few models were tried with lower cutoﬀdistance.
However, increasing the cutoﬀsigniﬁcantly increases computational complexity and 5 ˚A should
be suﬃcient to capture the relevant environment for interactions as the VS4 interchain distance
is 5.83A.
MD with SchNetE
An interesting point for comparison, is the fact that the authors behind the regular SchNet
model have created a framework for performing simulations with their model within Python,
namely SchNetPack [103]. The MD module in this package is marked as experimental as seen
here16. This package shows examples of simulations up to 10 000 fs that were performed with
the SchNet model. Additionally, the authors of the regular SchNet model were able to obtain
1.25 sins path integral molecular dynamic (PIMD) trajectories for the fullerene C20 molecule
[102]. As the SchNetE model reduces to the regular SchNetE model when edge updates are
disabled, it is a proof-of-concept that underlines that this speciﬁc model should be capable of
performing nanoscale MD.
The results showed that this was not easily obtained as the simulation stability was unreliable. In
the literature where nanoscale MD with ML force ﬁelds have been obtained, the structures used
for MD have often been single molecules. The MD has in these cases been used to study bond
frequencies of a molecule, for example aspirin [22, 119, 21]. These examples are fundamentally
diﬀerent from what has been tried here for a battery cathode. The unique chain like ﬂexible
structure of the VS4 together with the migration of magnesium between the chains, constitute a
more complex system where the simulation is more likely to see conﬁgurations far from what
was learned from the training data. Additionally, there was not found any examples in the
literature of successful ML driven MD on such large 1200 atom structures as was performed in
this study. In retrospect, it should have been tried to perform MD on the small structures ﬁrst
before trying large scale simulations. The eﬀects causing the breakdown was more prevalent in
larger structures as the probability of arriving at unseen conﬁgurations increases when adding
more atoms. The issue was also easier identiﬁed when working with small cells.
The major issue with the simulations was the high force predictions originating from magnesium
sulphur atoms moving too close. Unke et al. state that curvatures in the learned PES in ML
force ﬁelds may diﬀer by many orders of magnitude across the structure which will be an issue
for obtaining low prediction errors across all regions [119]. If such regions are not sampled well
enough the model is not able to properly extrapolate the energy in these areas. When atoms move

Active learning with ensemble uncertainties
A way of populating the training data in these uncertain regions would be through adaptive
sampling, also by extension known as active learning. Here, an uncertainty criterion is used to
evaluate whether the model prediction becomes unreliable and if that is the case, the conﬁguration
is sampled and DFT calculation is performed to obtain accurate forces and energies in this region
[85]. The uncertainty criterion is often based on some ensemble approach as was tried for the
MD simulations in the project. The ensemble of models was obtained by training ﬁve models on
the same datasplits. In hindsight this was probably the incorrect approach as the variance on
the predictions of the model ensemble resulted in being extremely low, even in the high force
regions. This would be a poor uncertainty measure and was not particularly useful for gaining
uncertainty distributions of the ensemble. Instead, a bootstrap technique on the training data
could be used or even simply using diﬀerent datasplits for training the ensemble models [6]. This
would probably make it easier to identify when structures are moving into uncertain regions
during MD. Combining such ensemble methods with adaptive sampling during MD would make
a strong starting point for further improving the simulations.
A concrete solution to the simulation problem would be to manually perform adaptive sampling
on the already obtained simulations. One could identify the breaking point conﬁguration for all
the simulations performed in the project and then do DFT optimization on all these breaking
point structures and retrain the SchNetE model with the obtained reference calculations. It
is likely that this would signiﬁcantly improve simulation stability and prevent the large forces
from Mg-S interactions from breaking the structure to some extent. This at the cost of weeks of
DFT calculations. It is possible that other discrepancies in the PES are discovered after Mg-S
interactions have been learned and these would then have to be solved by setting up an active
learning scheme.
In terms of computational eﬃciency, the results were consistent with expectations from theory
and the literature. In the literature the increase in computational eﬃciency was expected to be
3-4 magnitudes higher when using ML force ﬁelds compared to DFT [103, 34, 118, 119]. For the
simulations that were able to run stable for 0.1 and 0.3 ns on small cells, the steps per minute
were 3 magnitudes higher. Also, as a ﬁnal note, Unke et al. [119] mentions that the training
data for ML FFs should have data consistency and the same level of theory should be used to
calculate the data. This is in agreement with the choice made to only include SCAN functional
DFT data and not mix it with the data obtained with PBEsol.

Conclusion

7
Conclusion
In this study, approximately 50 000 magnesiated VS4 structures were generated by ab initio DFT
calculation and used as training data for a SchNetE graph neural network model that was trained
to predict the forces and energies of the VS4 structures. Extensive tuning of hyperparameters
was performed and it was discovered that atomwise normalization had to be enabled for the
SchNetE model to be able to predict energies for larger VS4 structures. The best models had 7
interaction steps and 128 node size and were able to obtain validation errors of RMSE ≈0.05 eV
for energies and around RMSE ≈0.05 eV/A for forces when converged.
Subsequently, the SchNetE model was used as a calculator for forces and energies to drive
molecular dynamics simulations on small VS4 unit cells and large 1200 atom VS4 supercells. The
MD process was studied extensively in order to obtain long and stable simulations. As the MD
simulations progressed, large force predictions by the SchNetE model caused a huge inﬂux of
kinetic energy in the structures during MD where the structures eventually broke down. An
ensemble approach was tried where ﬁve models were used to form an ensemble prediction to
possibly stabilize the simulations. This revealed that sudden large force predictions were made
by the ensemble at the breaking point of simulations. This led to the discovery that these large
forces emerged when pairs of magnesium and sulphur atoms were moved too close to each other
during MD. To improve upon the model, more training data in this region would have to be
generated.
Despite the eventual breakdown of all simulation, it was demonstrated how ML driven MD
simulations could be performed with approximately 1000 force and energy predictions per minute
for small structures. This was a magnitude 3 faster than to DFT driven MD. One simulation
with a small VS4 unit cell containing one magnesium was able to run for 24 hours before breaking,
providing a trajectory of 0.3 ns length with a 0.1 fs time step. Although stable 1 ns MD
simulations were not obtained, these results were promising as they demonstrated how nanoscale
simulations were obtainable if the large force prediction problem was solved.
Perspectives and Future Experiments
The main take away from the simulations was that the models had simply not learned enough of
the interactions between sulphur and magnesium atoms. To further advance the method, more
data on magnesium-sulphur bonds has to be generated and preferably in combination with an
active learning scheme.
At the end of the project, four atomic structures were provided by Juan Lastra, Associate
professor at DTU Energy that only contained a few magnesium, sulphur or vanadium atoms.
These were created to address the interactions between the atoms directly. Future improvements
on the model would involve performing MD simulations on these structures in order to give the
SchNetE model more data speciﬁcally related to these interactions.
Hopefully this will lead to a breakthrough that will enable MD simulations at the nanosecond
scale. If this is the case, many experiments can be performed to derive kinetic Mg properties.
MD simulations could be run at diﬀerent concentrations for both large and small cells in order
to obtain information of the VS4 charge and discharge process. Phase separation and diﬀusion of
magnesium within the structures are also properties that would be obtainable from successful
simulations. It could also be interesting to investigate model inﬂuence on the accuracy of such
obtained properties in terms of using either complex or simple SchNetE models.
