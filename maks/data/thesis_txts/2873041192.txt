Abstract
As offshore wind turbines continue to grow in size, their natural frequencies decrease
and approach the dominant frequencies of wind and wave excitation. This increases
the risk of resonance and amplifies the importance of accurately modeling soil–structure
interaction and damping behavior. This thesis investigates the influence of nonlinear
soil behaviour on the dynamic response of offshore wind turbine foundations using a
hyperplasticity framework. Three different soil models: linear, hyperelastic, and hys-
teretic are implemented within the aeroelastic code HAWC2 to evaluate their impact on
system dynamics. Damping is inherently present in the hysteretic model, and equivalent
damping ratios are estimated from its response. These damping ratios are then used
to calculate Rayleigh damping coefficients, which are applied to the linear and hyper-
elastic models to ensure a consistent comparison. Simplified load cases with harmonic
excitation at the tower-top are used to isolate the effects of soil behavior. Results show
that the hyperelastic model leads to significantly larger displacement amplitudes as the
non-linearity increase and a notable shift in natural frequency due to reduced tangent
stiffness, indicating a potential for increased fatigue loads. The hysteretic model cap-
tures permanent deformations and moderate frequency shifts. In contrast, the linear
model assumes constant stiffness and neglects non-linearity and leading to a simplified
response. These findings highlight the importance of including nonlinear and hysteretic
soil behavior in offshore foundation modeling.

Since the adoption of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development in 2015, which

mental degradation (United Nations 2015). Offshore wind energy plays a pivotal role in
meeting these goals, particularly SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy) and SDG 13 (Cli-

wind sector not only supports decarbonisation efforts but also contributes to economic
growth, job creation, and technological innovation, aligning with SDG 8 (Decent Work

projected to install over 20 GW of new offshore wind capacity between 2025 and 2030,

2024). According to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), onshore and
offshore wind are expected to account for over 20% of total power generation by 2030
and 35% by 2050 (IRENA 2019).
Offshore wind energy has several key advantages over onshore wind. At sea, the wind

1.2. Offshore wind turbine foundations
1.2
Offshore wind turbine foundations
Foundations can contribute up to 35% of the overall cost of installing a wind turbine
(Byrne et al. 2003). Existing design standards for foundations are often seen as overly
conservative, which can result in inefficient or unnecessarily expensive designs. Enhanc-
ing these design approaches could reduce the cost of monopile foundations and offer
substantial savings at the project level. Figure 1.4 presents various types of support
structures used for offshore wind turbines (OWTs), including monopile, gravity-based,
jacket, tripod, and floating foundations. Most offshore wind turbines (OWTs) are sup-
ported by monopile foundations, which are wide-diameter, hollow steel piles. In Europe,
monopiles account for approximately 78.4% of all installed OWT support structures.
Other types, such as jackets, gravity-based foundations, tripod structures, floating spars,
floating semi-submersibles, and tension-leg platforms, make up 12.77%, 5.25%, 1.78%,
0.27%, 0.11%, and 0.04% of installations, respectively (WindEurope 2024).
Figure 1.4: Illustration of typical foundation types and applicable water depths (DHI
Group 2021).
The design of monopile foundations for offshore wind turbines (OWTs) is primarily
governed by lateral loading.
OWTs are subjected to a combination of wind, wave,
current, and operational loads, which interact with the structure’s dynamic response
and impose both horizontal forces (H) and bending moments (M) on the foundation, as
shown in Figure 1.5. An essential requirement for offshore wind turbine foundations is
their stiffness under lateral loads caused by wind and wave forces during operation, as
this significantly influences the natural frequency of the entire structure. The soft-stiff
design is typical for OWT structures where the dynamic load effects are minimised by
designing OWT with its fore-aft frequency in the band between the rotor frequencies
(1P) and the blade passing frequencies (3P) as illustrated in Figure 1.6.

1.3. State of the art
1.3
State of the art
The purpose of this chapter is to present an overview of various methods proposed in
the literature for analysing soil-structure interaction and to assess their applicability to
wind turbine systems.
1.3.1
Current design practice
Monopile foundation design is typically done using the conventional p–y curve method
(API (2011), DNV GL (2021)). It is based on the generalized Winkler model (Winkler
1868), which represents the pile as a beam on a series of nonlinear lateral springs that
simulate the soil’s resistance, as illustrated in Figure 1.7. These springs, distributed
along the pile’s length, relate the local lateral resistance (p) to the corresponding lat-
eral displacement (y) through semi-empirical relationships, often derived from a limited
number of experimental tests. This method has its origins in the oil and gas industry
and has been widely adopted for laterally loaded piles. The conventional p–y curves for
sand, originally proposed for cohesionless soils by Reese et al. (1974), were derived from
two instrumented pile tests conducted at Mustang Island, Texas. For cohesive soils, the
curves were similarly developed from field experiments reported by Reese et al. (1975).
However, these methods were originally developed for long, slender piles with high L/D
(where L is the embedded length and D is diameter) ratio and rely extensively on em-
pirically derived parameters. High L/D ratio typically results in flexible behaviour of
the pile, and monopile diameters have significantly increased in recent years, while the
corresponding L/D ratios have decreased. Therefore, a pile exhibiting relatively rigid
behaviour will experience negative deflection at the pile toe as shown in Figure 1.8.
DNV GL (2021) recommends to validate p −y curves for piles with D > 1 m by e.g.
FE analysis (7.6.2.6). Furthermore, conventional p −y curves are typically formulated
as non-linear elastic (hyperelastic) models, which are unable to capture the foundation
damping observed in offshore wind turbines (Page et al. 2018).

1.3.2
The Pile Soil Analysis (PISA) project
The PISA project was undertaken to better understand the behaviour of monopile foun-
dations and to reduce the conservatism inherent in existing design approaches (Beucke-
laers 2017). It employed advanced numerical modelling techniques, which were validated
through an extensive field testing campaign. The modelling primarily focused on accu-
rately capturing the monotonic response of the foundation up to failure, while extensions
to soil layering and cyclic loading are possible (Burd et al. 2020). The PISA design model
offers a one-dimensional (1D) representation of a monopile foundation subjected to a lat-
eral load, H, applied at a height h above the seabed (referred to here as ”ground level”),
as shown in Figure 1.9. The design approach developed in the PISA project builds upon
the traditional p −y method found in current design standards, where lateral soil resis-
tance is described as a function of lateral displacement along a one-dimensional beam.
This method follows the Winkler foundation model, assuming that the soil response at
any depth depends solely on the local displacement and rotation of the pile. The PISA
method enhances this framework by incorporating distributed moment curves, as well
as base shear and base moment curves, as illustrated in Figure 1.10. The monopile is
modelled using Timoshenko beam theory, which enables an approximate incorporation of
shear strains in the structural analysis. The model can provide performance predictions
that closely align with those from equivalent 3D finite element analyses. However, the
current version of the PISA design model is limited to monotonic loading. It is possi-

1.4. Thesis outline
1.4
Thesis outline
Chapter 1 (Introduction) discusses the limitations of current design guidelines when
applied to monopile foundations for offshore wind turbines.
Chapter 2 (Theoretical framework) outlines the fundamental concepts behind the de-
velopment of a soil model that captures key aspects of offshore monopile foundation
behavior, such as hysteresis. The chapter introduces two main modeling approaches:
the kinematic hardening model using extended Masing rules, and the thermodynami-
cally consistent hyperplasticity framework. The model output from the hyperplasticity
framework is presented and discussed.
Chapter 3 (Implementation within commercial software) describes how linear, hyper-
elastic, and hysteretic soil models are implemented within commercial software. The
one-dimensional soil response of a simple beam is compared across the three models.
Chapter 4 (Benchmark Simulation) presents benchmark simulations of a monopile with
soil springs, validated against natural frequencies and unit displacement results provided
by Wood Thilsted.
Chapter 5 (Simulations) presents global damping estimations from decay tests for the
hysteretic model. These estimated damping values are then applied to the linear and
hyperelastic models. The responses of all three models are compared under several load
cases, followed by a discussion of their respective stiffness characteristics.
Chapter 6 (Conclusion) summarizes the key findings of the thesis and discusses possible
directions for future work.

Chapter 2
Theoretical framework
This chapter presents the theoretical background for developing a soil model that cap-
tures cyclic behavior and hysteresis, essential for evaluating offshore wind turbine founda-
tions. Two approaches are discussed: kinematic hardening, focusing on extended Masing
rules commonly used for cyclic loading, and hyperplasticity, a thermodynamically con-
sistent framework that inherently reproduces hysteresis. Since hyperplasticity naturally
satisfies the extended Masing rules (as will be demonstrated in subsection 2.2.3), it elimi-
nates the need to implement them separately. Consequently it is used as the basis for the
constitutive model for hysteretic response in this thesis. It is important to note that the
objective of this thesis is not to assess whether hyperplasticity is the most appropriate
modeling approach in general. Instead, the focus is to compare the dynamic response of
offshore foundations using three different soil models, implemented within an aeroelastic
solver: hysteretic, hyperelastic, and linear. Figure 2.1 illustrates their fundamental differ-
ences through typical load-displacement responses. The linear model exhibits a constant
stiffness with no energy dissipation. The hyperelastic model captures nonlinear stiffness
but follows the same path (backbone curve) during loading and unloading, resulting in
no hysteresis. The hysteretic model, on the other hand, shows both nonlinear behavior
and energy dissipation, producing hysteresis loops in the load–displacement curve. This
figure provides a conceptual foundation for the subsequent model comparisons.

elastic models.

Hyperplasticity
The hyperplasticity framework is a rigorous and consistent approach that allows the
development of constitutive models which are guaranteed to obey the laws of thermody-
namics. The approach described comprehensively by Houlsby et al. (2006), which is the
roots of the method developed by Ziegler (1977, 1983). The framework can be used in
the development of both rate-independent and rate-dependent systems. It uses internal
variables (α) to describe the loading history which is convenient to interpret it as plas-
tic strain. The constitutive behaviour of models often described in stress-strain (σ, ϵ)
space, however, different variables such as load-displacement (p, y) and moment-rotation

In this thesis, HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code, version 2) is
used for the implementation. It is a time-domain aeroelastic simulation tool developed
by DTU Wind Energy for modeling the dynamic behavior of wind turbines, including
both onshore and offshore systems. The structural component of the code is built on a
multibody framework, in which each individual body is modeled as a collection of Tim-
oshenko beam elements (Larsen 2024). HAWC2 is operated by executing the HAWC2
program through the Windows Command Prompt, using a text-based input file with
the .htc extension. This file contains the necessary simulation settings and parameters
required to run the model. The coordinate system of HAWC2 is shown in Figure 3.1.
An external system dynamic link library (ESSYSsoil spring.dll) is developed to model
the soil spring reaction. This DLL comprises three distinct soil spring models: (1) linear,
(2) hyperelastic and (3) hysteretic. Based on the user-defined input, the DLL computes
the soil spring reaction corresponding to the selected model until force convergence is
achieved at each time step. The overall structure of the implementation is shown in
Figure 3.2. For each spring along the pile in the soil, the backbone curve is saved as a
.dat file where the first row is the force in Newton and the second row is the displacement
in meters. In the part of the HTC file, for each spring the external system is defined
with 2 inputs: spring type and path to backbone curve data. (ESSYSsoil spring.dll)
reads the backbone curve and computes the kinematic hardening parameters. In each
iteration, HAWC2 feeds (ESSYSsoil spring.dll) with force and displacement vectors.
Tangent stiffness and spring force is calculated depending on the soil model and passed
back to HAWC2. Iterations continue until the force converge between the DLL and
HAWC2. It should be noted that for hysteretic model, the internal variable α is saved
and passed to the next time step when the convergence is achieved for the previous time
step. Conversely in hyperelastic model α is not saved and passed to the next time step
so that it always follow the backbone curve.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Results show that the hyperelastic model leads to significantly larger displacement am-
plitudes and a noticeable shift in the natural frequency as non-linearity increases due to
reduced tangent stiffness, potentially resulting in higher fatigue loads. The hysteretic
model demonstrates smaller frequency shifts. Also it captures permanent deflections,
which may be significant when loads caused by out-of-verticality are of concern.
In
contrast, the linear model maintains constant stiffness and does not account for stiffness
change or energy dissipation, leading to simplified system behavior. Unlike the linear and
hyperelastic models, which require the assignment of Rayleigh damping parameters, the
hysteretic model inherently includes soil damping through its formulation, eliminating
the need to externally define a damping ratio.
6.1
Future work
In this thesis, only p −y curves were used to model the lateral soil response. How-
ever, the hyperplasticity framework allows for the extension of the soil model to include
additional nonlinear components such as m −θ curves for rotational resistance, t −z
curves for shaft friction. Additionally and an S-y curve can be added to model tip re-
sistance at the pile base. Incorporating these elements would enable a more complete
and realistic representation of soil-pile interaction under combined loading. In addition,
advanced soil behaviors such as gapping between the pile and surrounding soil and pore
water pressure effects can be integrated. Furthermore, more comprehensive simulation
scenarios involving simultaneous wind and wave loading could be conducted to better
reflect operational conditions. This would allow for the assessment of both fatigue limit
state (FLS) and ultimate limit state (ULS) loads, enabling a more robust comparison of
foundation behavior across different soil models and improving design reliability.
