Abstract
A simple model of the retarding Lorentz force pro-
duced by eddy currents which is induced in a con-
ductive disc through a static magnetic ﬁeld has been
derived from theory described in the literature study.
A heat transfer model has also been implemented in
order to estimate the maximum allowable current that
can pass through the electromagnet which will pro-
duce the magnetic ﬁeld.
The maximum braking force has been calculated based
on four values for the current utilized in the electro-
magnet, in order to produce the necessary magnetic
ﬂux density to induce eddy currents in the conductive
disc, thus slowing it down.
In order to evaluate and validate the models, a test-rig
has been built locally at the Technical University of
Denmark along with the electromagnet and a loadcell
assembly. This is done in order to obtain the neces-
sary data for validating the model.
A parameter study has been carried out in order to
gain knowledge about the inﬂuence of speciﬁc param-
eters on the model, and how the outcome can be al-
tered.

4. INTRODUCTION
4
Introduction
During the 19th century, there were several physicists that formulated mathematical laws
that laid the foundation of today’s technology. Starting in the early 1800’s, the German
mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss formulated Gauss’ Law (also known as Gauss’ ﬂux
theorem) which describes the relationship between the distribution of electric charge to
the resulting electric ﬁeld. In 1813 Gauss formulated his law for magnetism, which states
that a magnet can not have a monopole, and even though a magnet can be cut in half,
two new magnets with their respective north- and southpoles are created. Although these
laws are denoted Gauss, other physicists also discovered the laws independently.
Somewhere around the same time, a French physicist and mathematician, André-Marie
Ampére discovered the relationship between the magnetic ﬁeld in a closed loop and the
current.
In 1820, a Danish physicist H.C. Ørsted made a somewhat random discovery that led
to one of the most well understood physics, electromagnetism. Some years later in the
1830’s, a british physicist Michael Faraday conducted a series of experiments that would
eventually lead to Faraday’s Law of Induction. However his experiments were not ac-
cepted by the wide scientiﬁc community due to the lack of mathematical expressions.
Later in the 19th century, the Scottish scientist James Clerk Maxwell, combined the
diﬀerent laws in a set of mathematical expressions described as Maxwell’s Equations. To-
day, these equations have been extensively studied and are considered to be among the
most important discoveries of physics.
4.1
The Eddy Current Phenomenon
Eddy currents are used in various applications and have been known for a long period of
time. In most applications, eddy currents are undesirable since they account for ohmic
losses which is seen in transformers and other electronic equipment with alternating cur-
rent (AC). The ohmic losses are dissipated as heat.
There are some applications where eddy currents are used, such as induction heating,
metal detectors and electromagnetic braking [1].
Induction heating is generated by time varying magnetic ﬁelds using alternating cur-
rents in a conductor. Metal detectors are generating a static magnetic ﬁeld which induces
eddy currents in nearby conductors. Finally electromagnetic braking uses both kind of
the mentioned magnetic ﬁelds.

4.2.1
Analytical Modeling of Eddy Current Brakes with the Application of
Time Varying Magnetic Fields
This article ([8]) studies the behaviour of eddy currents on time varying magnetic ﬁelds.
The methodology is similar to the one used in other articles, however the main focus is
determining if an AC can be used to acquire a higher braking torque compared to direct
current (DC).
An aluminum disc is used and the magnetic ﬁeld is produced by an electromagnet with
a varying magnetic ﬁeld. Maxwell’s Equations are used to derive an expression for the
braking force and other terms used in the article. The model is compared to a FEM model.
The results showed that the diﬀerence in braking torque is marginal at low velocities,
but increases at higher velocities, and so the conclusion is that there is a great enhanced
braking torque when applying AC instead of DC.
The article is useful in the extend that some of the applications and methodology utilized
in the article can be transferred to the problem described in this thesis, although the
problem in this thesis is simpliﬁed in the extend that only DC is applied.
4.3
Motivation
Electromagnetic brakes have the potential to solve a variety of problems that exists in
traditional mechanical hydraulic brakes, such as pollution, faster response time, reduced
complexity, contactless operation etc. They also have some limits that are mostly con-
nected to the velocity.
At lower velocities the braking torque decreases rapidly, and so the motivation of this
thesis is to investigate how the braking ability develops over a wide range of velocities.
In order to do so, a model of the retarding force, the Lorentz force, is made and compared
to experimental data. In order to simplify the approach, a static magnetic ﬁeld is applied
from DC instead of AC, which is used in a previous study [8].

5.2
Magnetic Fields and Properties
5.2.1
Introduction
Magnetic ﬁelds are created by charges in motion, which can create magnetic ﬁelds. Mag-
netic ﬁelds in permanent magnets are created by the spin of electrons in atoms. The net
sum of the spin from the electrons are what creates the magnetic ﬁeld, and this stays
permanent. In most of the materials, these electron spins tend to cancel out one another,
so that the net sum of the magnetic ﬁeld is not present on a large scale. However this
will not be elaborated further since this thesis will only involve magnetic ﬁelds induced
by currents moving in coils.

Skin Eﬀect
When increasing the frequency e.g. in an AC application, or eddy current brake, the
current tends to go from a homogeneous distribution throughout the conductor to the
surface, making the distribution of current uneven within the conductor [1]. The result
is a change in the internal inductance, and as the frequency goes up, the skin eﬀect will
be even more signiﬁcant making the inductance go to zero. As mentioned earlier, the
inductance is a material’s ability to induce an EMF, and so when the skin eﬀect occurs,
the induced EMF (which "drives" the eddy currents) will go towards zero.

6. THEORETICAL ANALYSIS OF HEAT TRANSFER
6
Theoretical Analysis of Heat Transfer
When the electromagnet has to be designed, some considerations to the heat transfer and
maximum allowable temperature of the windings have to be made in order not to overheat
and thereby melt the lamination of the wire, making the current travel on the surface of
the windings instead of going around in the loops.
6.1
Heat Transfer
According to Amperes Law, the magnitude of the magnetic ﬂux is determined by the
passing current and permeability of the material. In theory the higher the current the
higher the magnetic ﬂux, but there is a practical limit to the current since high amounts
of current will dissipate a high amount of heat due to the resistivity in the wire. In this
section, the maximum allowable current passing through the wire in the electromagnet
will be calculated with respect to a maximum allowable temperature. The wires are lam-
inated, and this lamination will melt at high temperatures, leaving the current to pass
directly through the electromagnet and not in loops. This will decrease the magnetic ﬂux
rapidly, which is undesirable.
The task is approached as a heat transfer problem where the radiation and convection
from the heat dissipated in the wire will be calculated with respect to a maximum allow-
able temperature of the wire of 60◦C. The following assumptions have been made in order
to simplify the problem:
• The density, speciﬁc heat capacity and radiation properties are constant.
• The speciﬁc heat capacity for iron is quite low, and so it is assumed that the tem-
perature change in the iron core and copper wire will be instantaneously.
• The temperature is completely uniform in both the iron core and the wire.
• It is assumed that the heat ﬂux happens in a room which has a much larger surface
than the surface of the electromagnet (Tsurface >> T).
The ﬁrst assumption is quite good, although the temperature will vary and change some
of the boundary conditions. The change is marginal due to the low temperature diﬀerence.
The second assumption is based on the fact that eventually, the core of the electromagnet
will reach a steady-state, having a temperature equal to that of the windings of wire.
Since the speciﬁc heat capacity is low, a temperature change in the windings will rapidly
change the temperature within the core material. The temperature is considered to be
uniform, and this assumption is quite good considering the symmetrical geometry.
The last assumption is not only correct, but also true - it is made in order to ensure
that the convection from the core to air does not change the temperature of the surround-
ing air signiﬁcantly.

Assumptions
Two assumptions are made in order to simplify the approach to determine the acceleration
over time.
• The friction provided by air is zero.
• The mass moment of inertia from the Hooke’s joint is neglected.
For the friction provided by air, the geometry of the rotating parts allows only a minimum
of inﬂuence from the surrounding air since all shapes that rotate are cylindrical.
The mass moment of inertia for the Hooke’s joint is very complex to estimate, so it
is assumed to be zero. The losses for the bearings in the test-rig will be included. On the
web page for the bearings (SKF), data for the bearings are available with a calculator in
order to estimate the frictional loss for the bearings used on the test-rig. The data can
be seen in appendix D.
Since the speciﬁc bearings used in the test-rig are unknown, an appropriate bearing type
has been chosen based on the geometry.

7.5
Partial Conclusion
The time for bringing the disc to a stop is quite long. This depends only on one factor in
the end: The magnetic ﬂux density induced by the electromagnet, which can be increased
by two factors: The current and the number of windings. Since the number of windings
can not physically be changed once they have been wired to the electromagnet, the only
option is to increase the current - however the current is limited due to a maximum allow-
able temperature in the wires. There are however a few options to increase the current,
which will be described in section 9.
It can be diﬃcult to measure the temperature increase in the disc due to the cooling
when the disc is spinning, and so to establish a more accurate result, the calculations
should include a heat transfer model which accounts for the cooling. As for Figure 22,
the braking time for the four diﬀerent amperage proﬁles are considered to be acceptable.

9.3
Partial Conclusion
From the diﬀerent plots presented in this section, it is clear that the behaviour of the
braking force are somewhat expected.
From Table 12 the maximum force for experiment #2, #3, #4 and #5 seems to fol-
low the expression for the Lorentz force from 5.6.
From the experiments, there also seems to be a co-relation between the angular velocity
and the maximum braking force. The braking force is directly proportional to the angular
velocity of the disc. However this is only the case up to a certain angular velocity limit
- increasing the angular velocity above this limit will not increase the braking force, but
decrease it.
The loadcell picked up some vibrations from the setup, especially during the stop of
the AC motor in the initial seconds of the experiments. This could have been reduced
more if the load-cell assembly construction was made more stiﬀ, and perhaps also have a
design where the natural frequencies was kept out of the investigated RPM.
The heat transfer theory seems to be quite accurate considering the deviation in the
initial conditions. The heat transfer on the electromagnet was tested when the experi-
ments was not on-going, in order to avoid any circulating air from the spinning disc cool
down the electromagnet. This could induce an error in the measured temperatures, since
the heat transfer model has not taken this "cooling" into account.

The most important parameter in the Lorentz force expression is the conductivity of the
material, so in order to maximize the braking force, the conductive disc has to be a good
conductor.
For the heat transfer model, the surrounding air temperature had the most signiﬁcant
impact on the model, and so in order to increase the current, the surrounding tempera-
ture should be decreased. Another option could be to introduce forced convection (e.g. a
cooling fan) so the convective heat transfer coeﬃcient could be increased.
Depending on the application, a parameter analysis can be used to examine how the
diﬀerent parameters should be prioritized, and drive towards an optimal solution that
accounts for cost, material selection, complexity, robustness and size of the brake.

12. FURTHER DEVELOPMENT AND APPLICATIONS
12
Further Development and Applications
The conducted experiments still have many interesting topics to investigate, and sugges-
tions for future work are presented here.
12.0.1
AC vs. DC
The experiments were carried out using DC on the electromagnet. The use of AC has not
been investigated, and an interesting topic to pursue could be the use of AC in order to see
if the behaviour of the braking curvature in the plots is equal to that of DC. The frequency
as a function of braking force could be investigated, but requires additional power supplies
that can handle AC and a well established theory to handle the inductance.
12.0.2
Maximum Magnetic Flux Density
The electromagnet was subjected to an upper limit of the current at around 4.41 A (5.39
A for the corrected model), and in order to make the heat transfer model relatively simple,
only one layer of copper wiring was added. The amperage could be increased rapidly by
increasing the diameter of the wire as well as adding multiple layers of wire in order to
increase the turns (which was also directly proportional with the magnetic ﬂux density).
However the behaviour of the heat ﬂux would be quite diﬀerent and more complex to
simulate if several layers was added, and additional cooling would be necessary.
12.0.3
Permanent Magnets
Permanent magnets could be interesting to use, as they have a high magnetic ﬂux density
compared to their size. Electromagnets can also generate very powerful magnetic ﬁelds,
but are limited by the size, since they would have to be equipped with a cooling system,
have many windings and a rather large current have to be used which would also require
a large power supply. A substantial reconﬁguration of the loadcell assembly would have
to be made in order to have a mechanical system to push the magnets back and fourth
between the conductive disc.
12.0.4
Natural Frequencies
A suggestion to look more into the natural frequencies that occur could be interesting. A
way to increase the interval of the natural frequencies is to add stiﬀness to the loadcell
assembly (but new natural frequencies would be induced).

12. FURTHER DEVELOPMENT AND APPLICATIONS
12.1
Applications
Various applications in the industry have electromagnetic brakes, especially within the
railroad industry. In Figure 65 a picture of electromagnetic brakes are shown for a train
[7].
Figure 65: Electromagnetic brake for a train.
There are various advantages when using electromagnetic brakes, such as no noise and
pollution, robustness, no moving parts, very low maintenance cost and a compact design.
In the railroad industry one other signiﬁcant advantage is the neglects of cooling in the
system since the heat is dissipated in the rails. The two main downsides of electromag-
netic braking are the lack of braking force at low- and high speeds, and so an intermediate
range of speed can be used - braking outside of this range requires traditional mechanical
friction braking.
If electromagnetic braking should be used in cars, a traditional mechanical braking system
would still be needed. When cars are parked, a mechanical brake make sure that the car
can not move, even on elevated grounds. Electromagnetic brakes can not withhold the
same force when the car does not move, and so a hybrid brake would have to combine the
two braking technologies if it should be commercialized.
An area of use could be in the aircraft industry, where the braking is usually only ap-
plicable when the aircraft is landing and most of the speed has been reduced prior to
landing. Larger aircraft with turbofan engines use thrust reverser in order to reduce the
speed when on ground, but for smaller aircraft, only disc brakes are used. In Figure 66 a
rendering of the existing braking system on a SAAB T-17 is presented.

13. DISCUSSION
13
Discussion
13.1
Discussion of Theoretical Results
The validation of which the electromagnet did indeed produce the magnetic ﬂux den-
sity of 0.135 T was not investigated, however the results in general for the model were
within an acceptable deviation, and so the strength of the electromagnet was perhaps
also acceptable. Assumptions regarding the geometry and neglecting the fringing and
ﬂux "leakage" were based on a presumed relatively small impact in the overall model, and
so the obtained expression and calculated values of the magnetic ﬂux density have been
deemed to be accurate enough for this application.
The expression for the Lorentz force was made with some assumptions that can vary
signiﬁcantly depending on the conditions.
A more accurate model could be obtained by increasing the accuracy of values and pa-
rameters for materials and make a more thorough analysis of how good (or bad) the
assumption of neglecting the leak eﬀect of the magnetic ﬂux density. The model did only
account for the eﬀects happening in the vicinity of the electromagnet, and not the whole
disc, which also have a consequence.
The heat transfer model has been validated and has an acceptable deviation.
There
were some parameters that were hard to apply to this speciﬁc problem, and so table
values were obtained and applied to the model. Such parameters were the convection
heat transfer coeﬃcient h and emissitivity ϵ. The parameter h is determined by several
conditions, such as the surface geometry of the electromagnet, the nature of the ﬂuid
motion and an assortment of ﬂuid thermodynamic and transport properties ([3] and [11]).
The applied value of h is based on a table value which can be seen in Appendix F, and
has been obtained in the boundary layer of free and forced convection. The emissitivity
can also be hard to determine as it depends on the radiative properties of the speciﬁc
surface. However since the contribution of the radiation is low, this parameter is not
considered to have a large impact on the model when compared to the convective heat
transfer coeﬃcient.
The mechanical model had some good assumptions, and the results turned out to be
quite accurate (when the contribution from the Lorentz force was removed) with an ac-
ceptable deviation of 0.6 % . The mass moment of inertia from the Hooke’s Joint was
neglected, and so the braking time would increase a bit. The contribution from air was also
neglected which would decrease the braking time, and so it look like the two contributions
almost cancels out.

13. DISCUSSION
13.2
Discussion of Experimental Results
The experiments could be optimized with regard to accuracy and eﬃciency of the Lab-
VIEW program. As mentioned in section 8, some parts of the experiments were done
manually which introduces errors and a responsetime in the measurements which could
be removed. The program should include a full cycle, so the diﬀerent parts of the manual
work were done by the program.
The Lorentz force is considered to be quite accurate for lower RPM. There seems to
be a tendency in the deviation of the braking time for higher magnetic ﬂuxes (higher am-
perage values) but this can not be determined on the data and assumptions discussed in
this thesis. The reason for this is unknown, and the magnetic ﬂux from the electromagnet
was not validated. If there is a deviation in the magnetic ﬂux that increases at higher am-
peres, this would be accumulated in the model and could explain why the model deviates
at higher amperage values and would be the only explanation since the friction from the
air and ball bearings was the same for all experiments as well as the initial angular velocity.
For the heat transfer model the steady state will take a long time to reach since the
windings have to heat up the electromagnet.
The average time for each temperature
measurement (on all four amperage values) was approximately one hour, and so if the
experiments were performed in a more controlled environment with a longer measurement
time, and experimentally obtained values for the convective heat transfer coeﬃcient then
the accuracy of the temperature would be increasingly better.
For the loadcell, the speciﬁcations and tolerances have a rather large impact on the mea-
surements. With a load range up to 500 N and a tolerance of ± 0.5 N, this particular
loadcell was perhaps not the best suited for this kind of operation. Measuring forces
between 0-6 N will inevitable induce relatively large deviations. A loadcell in the range
of 0-10 N would have been preferred, but since the forces were unknown at the time of
the design of the loadcell assembly, this loadcell was chosen.
From the results in section 9, there seems to be some issues with the tachometer sen-
sor, where the sudden drop at the very beginning of the experiment does not ﬁt in well
with either the theoretical model or the expected outcome. What could have been ex-
pected was a more smooth and even decrease at the beginning of each experiment. One
other thing to notice is that the drop happens within the ﬁrst couple of seconds, around
the same time the AC motor stops. This could mean that the AC motor does not only in-
ﬂuence the loadcell as seen in section 9 but also has a signiﬁcant impact on the tachometer.
For the dissipated heat in the disc, the expected result was not considered to be very
accurate because of the cooling of the moving disc from the surrounding air. However it
was considered an important topic to include because it proved that the braking power
did indeed heat up the conductive disc.
The results for the braking force at higher angular velocities were not expected. There
is a theory that could explain the behaviour of the braking force during the initial sec-
onds where it would increase, reach an optimum at a given angular velocity and then
decrease to zero. The theory could be that the skin eﬀect inﬂuences the braking force,
thus decreasing the inductance at higher angular velocities which increases ohmic losses
and decrease the current to be going near the surface of the conductive disc. Additional
investigations would have to determine if this is the case.
