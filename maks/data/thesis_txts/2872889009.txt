Abstract 
 
The rise of the creator economy has led to an increasing number of individuals building 
careers around content production on platforms such as Instagram, TikTok, and 
YouTube. However, many creators, particularly solo entrepreneurs and small teams, 
face challenges when it comes to managing their content and business workflows. 
Existing digital management tools are often overly complex, poorly optimised for 
mobile use, or not tailored to creators’ specific needs. 
This study explores these challenges through a multi-phase research process, 
combining initial market research, qualitative interviews with content creators, and 
quantitative surveys. The findings revealed consistent frustrations with tool usability 
for organising ideation and content. Based on this insight, a mobile-first minimum 
viable product (MVP) was developed. It focuses on the essential features of project 
organisation, such as to-do lists, note-taking, and reminders. 
The MVP was validated through a feedback loop involving surveyed creators, who 
largely confirmed the value of the tool’s simplicity and its alignment with their 
workflows. The study concluded there is a meaningful gap in the market for a 
lightweight, user-friendly management solution designed specifically for content 
creators. Recommendations for future work include improving visual design, 
expanding ideation support, and exploring further integrations, while continuing to 
involve users in the product development process.

1. 
Introduction 
 
While the influencer space rapidly grows, many of its opportunities remain unexplored. 
Therefore, content creators have been identified as the primary target users for the 
solution developed in this study. Content creators are individuals who produce and 
share original material, such as videos, text, or images, online to educate, entertain, 
influence, or engage audiences (Vrbat, 2023). Influencers are a subset of content 
creators, working primarily on social media platforms like Instagram, Facebook, 
TikTok, or YouTube (Vrbat, 2023). However, in this study, the terms “influencer” and 
“content creator” are used interchangeably, as the emphasis is specifically on the 
influencer segment. 
This project has been developed by a team called Lunary, currently consisting of two 
people: Klaudia Tóth and myself. The activities and outcomes described in this paper 
reflect my individual contributions to product and business development and market 
research. Included are decisions made jointly with the co-founder regarding business 
design. 
1.1. Background 
The content creator economy is rising, involving an ever-increasing number of creators 
(Yec, 2022), who aim for a voice on digital social platforms. However, this career 
choice does hold its own list of challenges.  
Successful content creation involves much more than just writing a caption or 
recording a video. It typically starts with generating and refining original ideas based 
on the audience and goals, then moves on to planning and organising the work 
(Tague, 2025). After that, creators produce the actual content, whether that’s visuals, 
text, audio, or video, before revising and polishing it to fit the brand and purpose 
(Tague, 2025). Finally, the content is optimised for platforms, search engines, and 
distribution (Tague, 2025). This complicated process helps ensure the content is both 
effective and engaging, but it needs great planning to follow. In order to achieve 
success, a structured content strategy is vital to engage their target market and to 
==PAGE:8==
 
8 
make progress towards their desired outcome (Kajander, 2019). Lack of a strategy 
leads to failure for many creators (Kajander, 2019). 
To successfully follow a strategy, most people utilise online management tools. Many 
creators talk on their platforms about their challenges related to finding the right tool 
and how time-consuming it is to set up a good system to manage their content and 
their business. For solo entrepreneurs, this is especially true and will be discussed 
further in 2.1.1. Problem Research section. 
One popular tool for managing a content creator business is Notion (The AI Workspace 
That Works for You. | Notion, n.d.), but it is often highlighted that the biggest problems 
with using this software are the long time it takes to get familiar with the tool and the 
usability of its mobile version (Raymond, 2023). The problem of the steep learning 
curve is not specific only to Notion, but it seems to be a general short coming for digital 
management tools, as well as the fact that they tend to add complexity to projects 
instead of making it easier for influencers to focus on their content creation (Kolosky, 
2025). On top of these problems, management software is often designed for large 
corporations for profitability, leaving solo entrepreneurs and small teams with few 
options or forcing them to adapt to a software that is designed to be used by corporate 
teams. 
1.2. Motivation and Research Question 
As discussed in the previous section, individual or small content creator teams face 
several problems when using digital management tools: 
● Steep learning curves 
● Poor mobile usability 
● Features that are not tailored to their specific needs 
Meanwhile, the number of creators on major social platforms continues to rise, and 
the market itself is experiencing rapid growth. According to the data presented in 
section 2.1.3. Market Opportunity, the creator economy is projected to grow 
significantly over the next few years, with millions of people already monetising content 
on platforms like YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok. This expanding market makes 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
content creators a valuable and growing user group, increasingly relevant to include 
in user-centred product development. 
Social media often receives criticism for its negative effects, such as contributing to 
FOMO (fear of missing out) and harming mental well-being (Barari, 2023). However, 
it is important to recognise that it is not inherently harmful.  
A study conducted at Harvard found that mindful or routine use of social media can 
positively impact social well-being, mental health, and even self-rated physical health 
(Bekalu et al., 2019). Simultaneously, social media influencers (SMIs) are also 
becoming a powerful force in digital marketing. Research by Vrontis et al. (2021) 
shows that SMIs positively affect consumer attitudes and behaviours towards the 
products they promote.  
These mixed perspectives show that social media is a complex, impactful space, and 
one that is not disappearing anytime soon. Given its scale and societal influence, 
understanding the needs of content creators is increasingly relevant. 
Despite their growing importance, little is known about content creators’ tool usage 
and unmet needs when it comes to managing their workflows regarding content and 
business activities.  
This study aims to answer the following research questions: 
1. What are the content creators’ pain points in the digital content management 
process? 
2. What solutions are available that simplify the content management processes, 
and how are the content creators' relationships with them? 
3. How can a content workflow management prototype be developed to effectively 
support the creators?

2. Market Research 
 
2.1. Initial Market Research 
Before starting an extensive and time-consuming development process, it is important 
to assess the targeted market and validate whether there is sufficient space and 
demand for a new product. Therefore, the goal of this initial market research is to 
understand the problem, current solutions, and potential opportunities in the market 
for a management tool targeted at content creators. 
This section provides an overview of the key problems faced by content creators and 
identifies potential market gaps that could be addressed by a new solution. 
2.1.1. Problem Research 
In the initial phase of the market research, the characteristics of the market and 
targeted users are utilised. The opinions and experiences of content creators can be 
first assessed through their online presence before getting in contact with them. Long-
form videos posted on social media platforms, primarily YouTube, are reviewed in this 
section to identify recurring themes and common pain points mentioned by creators 
regarding their content management process. The list of the main videos reviewed can 
be found in Appendix 1: Reviewed Video Resources. This approach provides an initial 
overview of the problem space and helps guide the subsequent research phases of 
this study. 
Many creators approach the topic of how they manage their workflows, motivated by 
requests from their viewers. Although content about specific tool reviews and tutorials 
to set them up can be found, many of these videos have a common theme, namely 
the struggle to find a productive setup, which forces the influencers to switch between 
tools and methods of managing their content workflows. (Case 2, 3, 4)  
A pain point often mentioned is overcomplication, which leads to spending an 
extensive amount of time on the management platform (Case 8). This is often the 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
reason for returning to simpler organising methods, such as pen and paper or notes 
on a mobile device. (Case 1, 2)  
Another type of video provides templates created by creators for management on one 
of the biggest project management platforms, often Notion. While this can be a good 
approach, the initial time spent creating these templates and even understanding them 
often discourages other users from integrating them. (Case 3) 
Lastly, some influencers also talk about specific tools targeted for content scheduling. 
While these are viable options for certain platforms, common downsides include high 
prices, limited features, and a lack of integrations. These pain points frequently appear 
in these discussions. (Case 5) 
Based on these findings, it is evident that there is no solution that currently dominates 
the market, and many creators and aspiring influencers are still seeking better ways 
to manage their workflows. 
2.1.2. Competitor Analysis 
Content reviewed about the problem inevitably features the current solutions used by 
creators. Thus, this section focuses on the potential competitors and the differentiation 
of the product developed through this project. 
The competitors have been split into two main categories: project management tools 
and content scheduling tools. This yields an improved focus on their type of business 
and a better picture of the overall market. 
Project Management Tools 
The following competitors were identified in the project management tools category: 
● Notion: Notion passed 100 million users worldwide (Notion Statistics 2025: 
Company Information (Updated), 2025). Notion is one of the most popular tools 
among content creators and is often mentioned as the best option (Insider, 
2025). It features many guides and templates showcased in online content to 
help users set up their systems. On the other hand, it is also one of the most 
frequently mentioned tools in discussions about overcomplication (Case 1, 2). 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
● Asana: With over 2.5 million weekly users (Asana, n.d.), Asana is also found as 
a favoured tool among influencers (Case 6). However, its primary focus is 
corporate users, with more than 169,000 companies using Asana for project 
management (Asana, n.d.). This makes it the primary project management tool 
for corporations and larger teams (Case 8). The learning curve can be 
extensive and overwhelming (Case 9). 
● Trello: Trello provides its service to more than 50 million users (Middleton, 
2025) and is frequently mentioned in tutorials for setting up content 
management workflows (Case 7). Although it is less popular than Notion, the 
pain point of "spending more time managing than working" seems to be less 
frequently connected with Trello. This tool is limited to simple visual task 
management but may be a better fit for smaller creation teams and individuals 
(Case 10). 
● Jira: Over 30,000 companies have adopted Jira to support their projects 
(Atlassian, n.d.). Thus, Jira’s primary market lies clearly in the corporate world, 
and it is infrequently chosen by individual users or content creators. 
● ClickUp: Similar to Notion, ClickUp is a growing platform with 10 million users 
worldwide (ClickUp, 2024). It is mentioned by creators, some of whom have 
integrated it into their workflows. However, it also gets the critique of 
overcomplication (Case 4, Case 8). 
● Monday: Monday is growing in market share, yet smaller than the previously 
mentioned tools. Currently Monday serves more than 245,000 users (Customer 
Stories | monday.com, n.d.). Despite starting as a great tool, with trying to 
integrate more functionalities, users started to mention overcomplication as a 
problem with Monday (Case 8). 
None of these tools were designed specifically with the content creator user segment 
in mind. Therefore, to nuance the picture, a second competitor category was identified: 
Content scheduling tools. 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
Content Scheduling Tools 
A smaller, more specific part of the content management process, content scheduling, 
is targeted by a different group of tools. The main competitors in this segment include: 
Hootsuite, Metricool, Buffer, Loomly, SocialPilot, and Sendible. 
These products in market terms represent more direct competition to a new tool 
specifically for content creators. However, they focus exclusively on content 
scheduling, leaving a gap in the market for a tool that targets the management process 
more comprehensively. Creators also frequently criticise the costs and limitations of 
these tools, particularly regarding poor integrations with their preferred platforms, likely 
a restriction imposed by the platforms themselves (Case 5). 
Summary 
In summary, analysing the competition in management tools for content creators 
revealed an opportunity to target influencers with a more comprehensive tool to 
manage their content creation workflows. 
2.1.3. Market Opportunity 
The number of social media users is increasing, with more people seeking 
entertainment like niche tips and even educational content on these platforms. In 2025, 
social media sites are expected to reach 5.4 billion users (Number of Worldwide Social 
Network Users 2028| Statista, 2025). To reach potential customers is one of the main 
reasons why companies develop a presence on these platforms. It is also why 
individuals and entrepreneur teams choose to start a career as social media content 
creators. 
In 2022, more than 50 million considered themselves creators for these 5+ billion users 
on social media platforms (Yec, 2022). The global digital content creation market is 
valued at 32,26 USD billion in 2024 and is expected to reach 117,51 billion USD by 
2034, exhibiting a compounded annual growth rate (CAGR) of 13,8% during 2025–
2034. (Polaris Market Research & Consulting Inc., 2025). 
While the content creation market is valued at 32,26 billion USD, the project 
management market was valued at 6,59 billion USD in 2022 with a CAGR of 15,7% 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
between 2023 and 2030 (Project Management Software Market Size Report, 2030, 
n.d.).  
These numbers suggest a huge market of creators with a total addressable market 
(TAM) of 32,26 billion USD. This includes all tools, services, hardware, software, 
agencies, and others in the content creation market. From this, assuming a 
conservative 15% of this is management tools, based on the project management 
market, the serviceable addressable market (SAM) can be estimated to be around 
4,84 billion USD. Influencers and social media personalities take up around 38,8% of 
this market (Market.us, 2025). Based on this, the portion of the SAM relevant to the 
target audience of this project is approximately 1,88 billion USD. From this, assuming 
a conservative initial market share of 0,5% of the relevant SAM, the serviceable 
obtainable market (SOM) is estimated at approximately 9 million USD. 
Considering the market size, the potential TAM, SAM, and SOM, the realised gap in 
the market, and a trend in sharing management workflows on social platforms, the 
market seems to be quite attractive for a new target product.

provide a balanced view of the opportunity:

● Difficulty to reach potential 
customers

Even though a new product can offer significant differentiation compared to existing 
competitors, the market for project management tools is quite saturated. Additionally, 
the product team currently has limited connections within the content creation sector, 
making it difficult to reach potential customers. They may be difficult to reach, as many 
creators already receive high volumes of messages. Initial research also revealed that 
many creators prefer to use free options currently available on the market.  
Furthermore, poor integration of existing tools with social media platforms (often due 
to platform restrictions) is an inherent limitation and risk to consider. Finally, a content 
management tool is required to handle a substantial amount of user data, which raises 
important considerations regarding data privacy and security. 
To further validate the opportunity and to better understand creators’ specific needs 
and preferences, the next chapter presents the results of qualitative interviews and 
quantitative surveys conducted with content creators.

3. Market Validation 
 
3.1. Qualitative Research - Interviews 
While previous research analysed publicly available content from creators' channels 
to identify potential challenges, the method lacked the depth needed to uncover 
personal experiences and context.  
Two in-depth interviews with smaller creators strived to gain a more nuanced 
understanding of how creators interact with content management tools, including their 
behaviours, motivations, pain points, and habits.  
3.1.1 Why Qualitative Research 
Qualitative methods focus on the “why” and “how”. They are an outstanding tool to 
understand human experiences and what these mean to people. (Fournier, 2023) 
With a deeper understanding, a theory can be explained further, developed, or 
extended to a bigger group. This can be obtained, for example, by realising the right 
questions to ask in quantitative research. (Fournier, 2023) 
3.1.2. Interview Introduction 
The interview structure needs to be developed carefully to gain the insights needed 
for the research and kept short enough for creators to accept the invitation, as they 
tend to have a very busy schedule.  
The questions used in the interviews can be found in Appendix 2: Interview Structure. 
3.1.3. Interviewee 1 
The first interviewee was a creator primarily focusing on TikTok and recently Instagram 
for collaborations, creating content on topics like healthy eating and dietetics. Online 
views range from ten thousand to hundreds of thousands, depending on the content 
itself, and they have more than 46.000 followers. 
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
From an insightful conversation, the following were collected: 
● Creation process: The interviewee is constantly trying to come up with new 
content ideas and uses quick notes to write them down as they appear. 
Researching current trends and content that interests viewers can be frustrating 
for them, as it is easy to get lost in consuming content without making progress. 
It also creates pressure when seeing other creators covering the same topic in 
a better way. Another pain point in their process is editing the material. As they 
try to post content every day, this part can become overwhelming. 
● Management tools: The influencer mentioned using notes on their phone, 
digital sheets, pen and paper, and an online calendar. They have tried some 
new tools, mainly online time planners, but they are very used to their current 
process, so changing tools is difficult for them and not desired either. 
● Desired features: The interviewee expressed that help with ideation would be 
very useful for them. They would like to have creative topic ideas and reminders 
for each day, as well as notifications to guide them to the next steps of the 
process, such as filming and editing. A new tool should allow collaboration with 
others and be easy to use and navigate. 
3.1.4. Interviewee 2 
The second interviewee was a space-tech educational content creator, posting to 
TikTok to their Hungarian audience and Instagram to reach international audiences in 
English. They have around 22.000 followers on TikTok. 
The second interview concluded with the following insights: 
● Creation process: Considering their target audience, the interviewee chooses 
a topic and writes a script. They struggle to always come up with new ideas and 
often miss trends because they are too slow and do not have time to keep up. 
They also mentioned the difficulty of figuring out how to catch people’s 
attention. Their main pain point in the process was editing, which they have now 
delegated to an assistant. 
● Management tools: The interviewee described using a variety of tools in their 
workflow, including phone or tablet notes, the drawing application Concepts by 
==PAGE:18==
 
18 
TopHatch (n.d.), digital spreadsheets, and Jira. They recalled having previously 
tried another tool, though they could not remember its name, but found the 
experience so poor that it discouraged them from exploring further options. The 
tool was not helpful, and they received an excessive number of persistent 
emails and notifications that could not be disabled. They also experimented 
with Notion in a team setting but found it overly complex and not worth the effort 
to maintain. Overall, they expressed frustration with having to rely on multiple 
separate tools for different tasks such as note-taking, project management, 
editing, and subtitle creation. 
● Desired features: The interviewee said that support with ideation is one of their 
most desired features. They expressed interest in receiving insights into 
trending topics and audience interests. Additionally, they would like more 
meaningful feedback on the performance of their content, beyond basic metrics 
such as likes and views. It is often unclear whether a post’s success is due to 
the topic, timing, or other factors. They also noted a preference for being able 
to manage and access all aspects of their process in one place, particularly 
ideation and performance analysis. 
3.1.5. Interviews Summary 
Although the interviewees work on different topics and have different goals in their 
content creation journey, there were notable similarities in the insights gathered: 
● Mental overload and frustration: Both interviewees mentioned the burden of 
constantly coming up with new ideas and staying on top of recent trends and 
audience interests. 
● Editing is a burden: The creators highlighted the post-production work as a 
pain point, and particularly, editing their content as a source of stress.  
● Usage of mobile devices, especially notes: An important insight was the 
devices used in the influencers’ management process. Both creators rely on 
their mobile phones or tablets, especially during the early creative stages, to 
take notes or sketch ideas. 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
● Desired features: Both interviewees expressed a desire for support with 
ideation and staying up to date with trends to reduce the burden of research 
and constant brainstorming. 
Some additional differences and insights: 
● Attitude towards new tools: The first creator is generally reluctant to change, 
due to the mental effort of adjusting to new systems. The second influencer was 
more open to trying new tools but often abandoned them early on due to their 
complexity. 
● Desired features: Notifications, reminders, and collaboration were mentioned 
only by the first interviewee as desired features. However, the second 
interviewee also mentioned these functionalities as part of their current toolset. 
Analysis was highlighted only by the second creator. Further market research 
is needed to confirm the demand for these features more broadly. 
Although the personalities and content areas of the two creators differ significantly, 
there were many similarities in their responses, particularly in terms of core features 
used and desired, such as note-taking, ideation, and trend tracking. While some 
differences emerged, the underlying needs and functionalities aligned. Based on these 
findings, the qualitative research fulfilled its purpose. The quantitative phase sought to 
validate the scale of the problem and the level of demand.

3.2. Quantitative Research - Surveys 
For the quantitative research, two surveys were conducted, each with 30 participants. 
The first survey was launched after the ideation phase. Although it resulted in some 
useful initial insights, as the market research and qualitative interviews progressed, a 
need for a more problem- and market-focused survey arose. 
A second survey was developed with refined questions related to pain points and the 
use of existing management tools. Some questions from the first survey, such as those 
regarding tool cost, were repeated to increase the total data sample size. 
The following sections present the preparation and analysis of the survey data. While 
some diagrams are shown in the section, the rest can be found in Appendix 3: Survey 
Preparation and Analysis. 
3.2.1. Survey Design and Distribution 
The surveys were designed to better understand the pain points and preferences of 
content creators. The goal was to either support or challenge the findings from the 
market research and qualitative interviews. As a result, the surveys focused on 
frustrations, liked and disliked features, and the importance of various aspects of 
content management tools.  
The questionnaires were distributed through a platform called Prolific (Prolific | Easily 
Collect High-quality Data From Real People, n.d.), which is designed for recruiting 
participants for academic and user research studies. The targeted participants were 
motivated by a small financial reward for the time spent answering the questions. This 
amount was covered by the co-founders of the project. 
As previously mentioned, reaching content creators directly can be challenging and is 
considered a development risk. By using a platform like Prolific, quality responses and 
fast targeted distribution were ensured, providing a strong foundation for the 
quantitative part of the research.  
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
3.2.2. Data Preparation 
To ensure the data validity and the reliability of the results, the analysis started by 
cleaning and preparing the data gained from the questionnaires. 
First, invalid or missing data needed to be discarded, therefore, empty and duplicate 
entries were removed, resulting in a 6% reduction in the sample size, with four 
responses discarded from the total of 62. Additionally, the irrelevant columns were 
dropped, such as timestamp and long-form text responses requiring manual review. 
Afterwards, some columns were optimised for analysis and compatibility between the 
two surveys.  
● The cost of the tools needed to be converted to the same currency, namely 
euros, to the planned location for the product launch. After the conversion, high 
outliers were identified, suggesting that some participants had reported annual 
rather than monthly pricing, likely due to unclear question wording. These 
values were adjusted accordingly. 
● The follower counts were free-text in the first survey, therefore, they were 
categorised into brackets used in the second survey to enable consistent 
comparison. 
3.2.3. Participant Profiles 
To better understand the findings, the surveyed group was first assessed. Earlier 
research indicated a market gap in management tools for individual and small content 
creator groups. Additionally, being based in Europe and having familiarity with this 
region could make Europe a natural choice for market entry. However, by examining 
the demographics and characteristics of the creators who participated in this research,

5. Technical Design 
 
5.1. High-Level Technical Planning 
For rapid prototype development, tools and methods that were familiar, easy to use, 
and suitable for delivering the planned product were chosen. This led to the high-level 
architecture shown in the following figure. 
 
Figure of High-Level Technical Diagram 
As described in 5.2. Minimal Viable Product (MVP) section of this study, a mobile-first 
approach has been decided for this project. Developing the user interface, this project 
utilised one of the most widely used frameworks in mobile development, React Native 
(Statista, 2025). Its popularity, strong community support, and the availability of 
various libraries helped make the development easier and faster. 
Developing the backend, the feature software utilised a Node.js backend with NestJS 
framework and TypeScript. There were multiple factors behind this decision: Node.js 
is a very popular, fast, and easy-to-learn backend solution (Node.js — Introduction to 
Node.js, n.d.) with extensive community support. NestJS is a robust and opinionated 
framework (NestJS - a Progressive Node.js Framework, n.d.) with built-in support for 
modularity, which is essential for a scalable application. Choosing TypeScript over 
==PAGE:36==
 
36 
JavaScript was based on its type safety, which enhances application security and code 
quality. These choices allowed more focus on the business logic development, as the 
structure and safety were supported by the chosen tools. 
The communication between the frontend and backend happens through REST 
(Representational State Transfer) APIs (Application Programming Interfaces). This 
approach allows for a uniform interface for data transfer and an independent structure 
(IBM, 2025), enabling a future web application to be built on the same backend logic. 
To deliver consistent value to users, some data needs to be stored. The nature of the 
data and its relationships suggest a relational database, which also aligns with my 
technical expertise. I also needed to consider future growth. For these reasons, 
PostgreSQL was selected due to its reliability and flexibility (IBM, 2025a). 
To make the product accessible to users, a cloud provider was needed to host the 
backend and the database. Amazon Web Services (AWS) was selected because it is 
currently the largest cloud provider, offers extensive features, and includes a free tier 
(Richter, 2025), making it well-suited for hosting the application. 
Keycloak (Team K., n.d.) was chosen to handle user authentication. Writing a custom 
authentication system would have been time-consuming, and due to the time 
constraints of the project, along with the high security standards and free service of 
Keycloak, I decided to use this service in the project. 
5.2. Minimum Viable Product (MVP) 
Designing a product that brings value to users and justifies the founders’ invested 
efforts can be challenging. The Lean Startup principle offers a solution to this 
challenge, centred around developing a version of the product that is complete enough 
to demonstrate value to customers, in other words, an MVP that can be tested and 
validated (Moogk, 2012). 
5.2.1. Initial Feature Selection 
Based on the problems and preferences identified in the qualitative interviews and 
quantitative surveys, a core set of features was selected to form the basis of the MVP. 
==PAGE:37==
 
37 
These features aim to provide focused value while keeping the product lean and 
manageable for initial development and validation. 
According to the second survey, users value organising projects the most in their tools. 
Therefore, the application enables users to organise everything under projects. A 
project can include to-dos, free-drawing notes with the option to add text, and even 
other projects. To-dos and notes were selected due to their popularity in the second 
survey, where 44,8% of respondents selected each of them. Participants in the first 
survey also stated to-do lists as their most desired feature, and project organisation 
was popular there as well. 
Another widely appreciated feature was reminders, which were mentioned in the 
interviews and favoured by more than 40% of respondents in both surveys. For the 
initial product, a local notification feature has been developed to allow users to receive 
reminders for their daily to-dos. 
Lastly, essential functionality was added to ensure the product's basic usability and 
security: signup, login, logout, and profile deletion. 
While post-scheduling was also considered important, appearing among the top 
responses in both surveys, this feature was excluded from the MVP due to its 
complexity. Post scheduling adds significant risk by relying on external APIs provided 
by social media platforms, as discussed in 2.1.1. Problem Research section of this 
study.

5.3. Technical Feasibility 
As the MVP has already been developed, it confirms that the current technical setup 
and chosen tools were appropriate for creating a working prototype within the planned 
time frame and with limited resources. However, some technical limitations and risks 
have also been realised during the development, which are important to consider when 
planning further steps. 
5.3.1. Risks and Limitations 
As mentioned earlier, one of the main technical risks lies in integration with social 
media platforms. While this feature is important to many content creators according to 
the surveys, the social media APIs can be unreliable, restricted or difficult to get access 
to. The limitations from the platforms’ side, such as requiring specific permissions or 
limiting functionality, can delay or even block the feature altogether. 
==PAGE:39==
 
39 
Data security and privacy are also areas that need attention. Although Keycloak is 
used for secure authentication, additional measures might be required in the future, 
especially to comply with laws like the GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) if 
the application expands to a larger user base. 
Although the features included in the MVP have been chosen based on user input, 
their current functionality has limitations, such as a small non-expandable canvas for 
notes or the limited attributes on the to-dos. This can be a risk when trying to reach 
new users and convince them to change from their current tools. 
Lastly, offline access is another limitation, which is currently not supported in the MVP, 
and scalability needs to be validated as well for future product development. 
5.3.2. Potentials 
Despite the limitations, the current setup also has several technical strengths and 
opportunities. The chosen stack provides a good base for scaling the application and 
adding new features over time. The backend is built in a modular way, allowing easier 
expansion and also making it possible to move towards microservices later if needed. 
Another benefit is that the current API-based architecture supports future development 
of a web application without major changes to the backend logic. This gives flexibility 
in platform support moving forward. 
There is also technical potential to introduce more advanced features based on earlier 
research, for example, more advanced reminders connected to the calendar or an idea 
generator tool. 
Finally, setting up a user feedback loop and adding basic user analytics can be part of 
a future version, helping the team understand which features are used the most and 
what users struggle with, so improvements can be made based on actual data.

7. Discussion 
 
This section brings together the different stages of the study to reflect on what was 
learned, what it means in practice, and what could be done next. The following 
subsections outline the key findings, practical implications for product development, 
limitations of the current research, and directions for future work. 
7.1. Findings 
The findings of this study have been built up step-by-step, starting from the market 
research, then explored in qualitative interviews, supported or challenged in surveys, 
and finally validated through targeted feedback on the MVP demo. 
In the market research, one of the clearest trends was that many creators struggle 
with overcomplicated tools, which often take more time to use than the time they save. 
As a result, creators frequently switch between those tools or give up entirely, returning 
to simpler methods like pen and paper or mobile notes. The competitor analysis 
reflects these findings, with trends of upcoming tools and frequent complaints about 
complexity. Additionally, the growing markets of social media, content creation, and 
project management revealed a promising opportunity to research these problems 
further and potentially enter the market with a solution tailored to creators’ needs. 
This observation was partly confirmed in the interviews, where one participant 
mentioned that existing tools are often too complex, leading them to prefer sheets and 
physical notes. However, new insights also emerged: both interviewees mentioned 
struggling particularly with the ideation phase of content creation and expressed a 
need for support in this area, along with access to trend information. They also 
emphasised the importance of mobile devices in their workflow and described editing 
as a burdensome task. 
The surveys helped shape the initial understanding of the respondents’ ideal tool and 
frustrations in their content management process. Similar themes reappeared to the 
interviews along with some new complaints: creators struggle with generating new 
ideas, posting regularly, following trends, reaching new audiences, gaining attention, 
==PAGE:44==
 
44 
writing scripts, and being unique. These findings confirm that creators want tools to 
support the early stages of their work, especially ideation, even though editing was not 
highlighted as strongly. Many survey respondents stated that complexity or pricing of 
current tools is a barrier for them, confirming the initial market research findings. 
Organising ideas also appeared among the top frustrations. 
Another point of alignment between the first survey and the interviews was the need 
for a mobile-first solution. Although results in the second survey were mixed between 
mobile and computer preferences, the interviews and additional research confirmed 
that quick and flexible access is important in the content creation process. From the 
tools mentioned by the survey respondents, mobile notes are frequently used. 
The most valued features across both surveys were to-do lists, note-taking, reminders, 
project organisation, integration, post scheduling, and calendar functions. These 
matched what the interviewees shared about the importance of quick note-taking, and 
they also revealed new priorities. The MVP was designed around these core features, 
with more complex functionalities planned for future development. The product 
structure was kept minimal and mobile-first, reflecting both the qualitative and 
quantitative findings. 
The surveys also revealed new aspects of the competitor analysis. A new competitor 
appeared in the responses, namely Canva (Canva, n.d.), while Trello seems to be less 
popular among content creators, despite its focus on smaller teams. Many 
respondents were distributed between other tools, but Notion proved to be popular, 
confirming the earlier competitor analysis. This suggests more extensive user 
research to gain a better understanding of the current market, what the current tools 
are used for, and how to win market share among the available tools. 
Finally, the feedback survey for the MVP demo confirmed that the direction was 
aligned with user needs. A majority of participants appreciated the clean, minimalistic 
design and confirmed the value of organising their work in a simple way. Many said 
they could imagine using the app in their content creation workflow, mostly for task 
management, idea storage, and client/project tracking. Although some mentioned that 
the visuals could be improved, the overall feedback showed that the product 
addressed real needs in a simple and accessible way. With further development, most 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 
participants said they would be open to including the tool in their content management 
workflow, which reflects well on the findings about creators’ usual reluctance to switch 
tools. 
7.2. Practical Implications 
The results of this study have practical value for both the continued development of 
this project and similar tools designed for content creators. First, the findings suggest 
that a mobile-first, lightweight application that helps creators organise their thoughts 
and daily work without overcomplicating things can fill a noticeable gap in the market, 
especially among micro-influencers and solo entrepreneurs. This aligns with the 
market research and was echoed in the interviews and survey results, which 
repeatedly pointed to frustration with overly complex tools. 
From a product design perspective, focusing on a few essential features such as to-
do lists, project organisation, notes, and reminders, rather than attempting to build a 
large all-in-one tool, appears to meet user needs more effectively. The positive 
response to the MVP confirmed that simplicity and usability are highly valued. 
From a business perspective, the pricing sensitivity highlighted in the surveys supports 
the choice of a freemium strategy. This model lets creators try out the core functions 
of the app without having to commit right away. If they find it useful, they can later 
choose to pay for additional features. This approach is especially relevant, as the 
market research showed that many creators feel current tools are not worth the cost.  
Lastly, the feedback loops and early testing methods used in this project show the 
value of co-creation. Involving creators throughout the design and validation process 
led to more relevant feature selection, stronger alignment with real user problems, and 
early adopter interest. These practices may not only improve product-market fit but 
also enhance long-term user engagement and retention. 
7.3. Limitations 
This project had several limitations. First, the number of participants in the surveys 
and the MVP feedback round was limited, which may affect the generalisability of the 
findings. This is especially important in relation to the opinions shared, as many were 
expressed by only a few respondents, therefore, it is insufficient to draw strong 
==PAGE:46==
 
46 
conclusions about the broader market. Additionally, all users were micro-influencers, 
so larger creators or teams were not represented in the sample. 
Due to time and resource constraints, not all features could be implemented or tested 
in real usage. For example, ideation support, collaboration, and integrations are 
planned for later stages but have not yet been developed. Feedback on the MVP was 
gathered through a demo version, which means real-world usage patterns, such as 
long-term engagement and retention, could not be measured. 
Finally, while early testers provided valuable insights, more extensive and long-term 
testing would be needed to fully understand how the product performs over time and 
across different user contexts. 
7.4. Future Work 
There are several stages for future work. The first is to improve the existing features 
slightly by adding colour options and more visually engaging elements, since this was 
highly requested and a relatively low effort to implement. Enabling more customisation 
on the home page so users can prioritise their most valued features, and ensuring 
smooth functionality throughout were also mentioned by creators as important factors, 
therefore prioritised. Lastly, based on user input, strengthening the application's 
security is also essential. 
The second stage is developing and testing features focused on ideation, as this was 
a key pain point mentioned by creators. Implementing AI-based ideation support, 
content suggestions, and scheduling optimisation would be a natural next step. This 
could be extended with data gained from social platforms, helping to keep up with 
current trends both in broader terms and specific to the creators’ niche, or advice on 
how to reach new audiences. 
Although collaboration and integrations with social platforms, such as post scheduling 
and insights from previous posts, were also highlighted in the surveys, these are more 
complex and involve greater risks. Therefore, they are better suited for later 
development stages, when reliable access to external platforms is ensured. 
Additionally, for the future, an important step is to onboard users through targeted 
marketing in order to collect sufficient feedback for ongoing development.

model over time.

8. Conclusion 
 
This study set out to explore the challenges faced by content creators in managing 
their workflows and to develop a prototype that responds to their most pressing needs.  
Through a combination of market research, qualitative interviews, surveys, and user 
feedback, the research identified a clear gap in the current tool landscape: existing 
platforms are often too complex, not mobile-friendly, or not tailored to the specific 
workflows of smaller creators and influencer teams. 
The research revealed that creators often feel overwhelmed by the tools they use, 
frequently abandoning them in favour of simpler methods. Yet, they still express a 
desire for support in organising their tasks, generating ideas, and keeping up with 
trends. The findings suggest that creators appreciate minimalistic and intuitive tools, 
and many are open to new solutions with clear value. 
Based on these insights, a mobile-first, lightweight MVP was developed, focusing on 
project organisation, to-do lists, reminders, and free-form note-taking. The technical 
setup enabled rapid prototyping and laid the foundation for future scalability. The 
feedback gathered from early testers confirmed that the tool addresses real pain points 
and was aligned with creators’ needs, while also highlighting areas for improvement. 
The study was limited in time and resources, hence, the approach taken was centred 
around co-creation and iterative validation, which offered a solid foundation for 
continued development. Future work should focus on enhancing existing features, 
implementing ideation support, and preparing for more complex integrations. 
Additionally, broader testing and market outreach will be essential to fully realise the 
tool’s potential and refine its long-term strategy. 
In conclusion, this research contributes to a better understanding of the workflow 
challenges faced by content creators and presents an early-stage solution built with 
and for them. While further development is needed, the results provide promising 
evidence that a focused, user-centric tool can create meaningful value in a growing 
and increasingly relevant market.

Freelance graphic designers often manage multiple clients and creative projects using 
scattered tools. Current methods involve switching between sketching apps, task 
managers, and communication platforms, resulting in workflow inefficiencies, missed 
deadlines, and reduced creative focus. 
The tool addresses these issues by offering a unified workspace where designers can 
organise projects visually, manage tasks per client, set deadline reminders, and 
personalise their dashboard to prioritise what matters most. This streamlined approach 
enhances clarity, focus, and delivery quality.

● The freelance graphic design market is valued at 47 billion USD in 2024, 
projected to reach 83,6 billion USD by 2034(CAGR 5,9%).

market, with Asia-Pacific as the fastest-growing region.

there remains a gap for integrated, design-friendly project management solutions, 
highlighting a clear opportunity for this tool.

increase the risk of missed deadlines and inconsistent episode planning.
