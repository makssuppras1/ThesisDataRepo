Resumé
Denne rapport beskriver et projekt, hvor et navigeringshjælpemiddel til svagtseende, der ønsker at løbe,
blev udviklet. Hjælpemidlet kan opdage forhindringer foran løberen, når den svagtseende løber på en lukket
løbebane. Ved at udvikle et hjælpemiddel til svagtseende løbere, kan der fjernes en barriere for svagtseende,
der lider af svær tilgængelighed til motion og træning. Når træning er besværligt for svagtseende at udføre
af frygt for skader, medvirker det til, at deres sundhed og livsstil forværres. Projektet viser lovende resultater
for et hjælpemiddel til at advare om forhindringer, men kræver yderligere udvikling for at være brugbart
for en svagtseende. Hjælpemidlet kan opfange små forhindringer som eksempelvis tasker på 16 meter
og mellemstore forhindringer på størrelse med en stol på 24 meter. Når hjælpemidlet er monteret på en
løber, kommer hjælpemidlet til at kigge for meget ned i jorden, når kroppen bliver lænet frem. Det kan
derfor have svært ved at opfange forhindringer i tide. Hjælpemidlet er derfor ikke robust og konsekvent
nok endnu til at kunne bruges til at erstatte en løbeguide. De vigtigste forbedringer, der mangler, er et
etui til elektronikken og en brugergrænseﬂade til svagtseende. Udviklingen er foretaget i ﬁre stadier: En
pilottest, en indledende prototype, en lokaliseringsprototype og en afsluttende prototype. I hvert stadie er der
foretaget eksperimentelle forsøg for at afsøge den videre udvikling. Projektet blev startet og afsluttet med
forsøg med en svagtseende bruger. I pilottesten bliver den minimale søgeafstand bestemt til at være 16 meter.
Hjælpemidlet bruger en laser afstandsmåler, der når kroppen bevæger sig under løb, skanner vejen foran
løberen for forhindringer.

Abstract
This report describes the process of developing a prototype for a navigational aid for a visually impaired
user to use while running. The navigational aid is restricted to detecting obstacles while running on a closed
track like a running court. By developing an aid for running when visually impaired, a major barrier for
exercising is removed for the visually impaired, when the risk of injury can be lowered. Making it easier to
exercise if visually impaired, could help improve the health of the visually impaired community. The ﬁnal
test shows that is possible for the aid to locate an obstacle and warn the user of it. At 16 meter the aid is
capable of detecting small obstacles at the size of a bag. At 24 meter it can detect medium-sized obstacles
at a size of a chair. During testing it was found that the aid had a tendency to scan too much towards the
ground, making it difﬁcult to detect obstacles. The aid is not reliable or robust enough for ﬁnal release and
requires more work before being usable by a visually impaired user. Most notable is the need for a casing
for the device and a user interface for a visually impaired user. The development was performed in four
prototyping development iterations: A pilot test, an initial prototype, an obstacle localisation prototype and
a ﬁnal prototype. Each phase was concluded with experimentation; testing the capabilities and limits of
the prototype. The project started and ended with testing with a visually impaired user. The pilot test, with
the visually impaired user, showed that obstacles should be detected at a minimum distance of 16 m. A
laser range ﬁnder, utilising the natural body movement when running, scans for obstacles in front of the user.
Obstacles are then located by recording the motion of the sensor while scanning the path ahead.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The project described in this thesis concerns the development of a prototype of a navigational aid for
the visually impaired (VI). The prototype is developed using the rapid prototyping method[7], where the
development and documentation is built in smaller iterations, with a large degree of user involvement. The
VI are, because of their limited vision, restricted in their mobility. They learn to navigate known routes with
the help from a white cane and maybe a guide dog. The known routes are often subject to changes, e.g.
roadwork, construction equipment and parked cars or bicycles. When visiting unknown or unfamiliar areas
they require the help of a seeing person. However, they are often only granted a limited number of hours for
help from the public. These hours are used on shopping, events and some are saved for special occasions
such as vacations. It would ease their burden if they had access to an aid that could warn them of obstacles.
In 2008 the two greatest challenges for the independence of VI individuals was access to printed materials
(books, signs, maps etc.) and the stress related to safe and efﬁcient navigation.[4] In recent years, with
the emergence of smartphones, many of the travel aids have compacted into solutions on the smartphones.
The developed navigational aids are many, but few electronic obstacle avoidance aids are used by the VI
community. In a survey article from 2010 about electronic travel aids for visually impaired, 17 different
travel aids were reviewed and rated.[3] The authors’ conclusion is that none of the aids reviewed meet all the
criteria for a great travel aid that is reliable, robust and with an overall good performance. According to their
analysis an electronic travel aid should be:
1. Free hands: The user should not be required to use his hands for operating the aid, because the hands
are needed for holding the white cane.
2. Free ears: The user is already impeded on the loss of one sense. A travel aid should not interfere with
the user’s ability to hear the surroundings.
3. Wearable: By utilising wearable technology the travel aid becomes part of the person’s outﬁt.
4. Simple: The aid should be easy to use, and not ﬁlled with unnecessary features or require extensive
training to use.
A common mistake in the development of electronic travel aids for VI users is not taking into account
the gap between which features a user wants, a mobility instructor’s knowledge about mobility and how the
engineer is developing the aid.[3,4] This needs to be considered when designing a navigational aid.
The commonly used navigation aids for the independent VI is the white cane and for some a guide dog.
The white cane is used for mobility and for making the surrounding world aware of the VI user. With the

white cane the VI user scans the path ahead for obstacles while walking. The guide dog helps with ﬁnding
paths around obstacles and indicating where the kerbs are when crossing roads. Both the white cane and
the guide dog requires the VI user to know the route. When travelling via unknown routes and/or without a

The objective of this project is to design a successful navigational aid with obstacle detection capabilities.
The aid must fulﬁl the criteria stated above and a VI user must ﬁnd that the device helps with the navigational
problem. The project will only examine the VI in Denmark, as it is difﬁcult to attest to the VI demands in
other countries.
The methodology for developing the prototype is following the rapid prototyping (RP) method.[7] RP is
an approach where the prototype is developed in small iterations. The process is shown in Figure 1.1 and
starts with a project deﬁnition. In the project deﬁnition process the users are included. The second step is
Figure 1.1: Process of rapid prototyping, by ASTM International
tool selection. Good RP tools allow quick changes, permits several solutions, encourage the developer to try
different solutions and are available now rather than being proposed for future use. In RP by selecting the
right tools, the development should ﬂow faster and encourage experimentation with the solution.
The next part of the process is the RP loop. This is a cycle of analysis, synthesis, construction and
evaluation, where the prototype approaches the required system step by step for each iteration. In the analysis
the user’s needs and required system behaviour is investigated one question at a time, and for each cycle the
analysis becomes more detailed and the understanding of the requirements grows. Synthesis and construction
is the process of design and implementing changes to the prototype. Features are added or changed one at a
time and each iteration should lead to a testable prototype. The evaluation is important, since it is here the
user and developer determines if the prototype fulﬁl the user’s needs, which features should be kept and
which should be removed. When the user deems the device capable of meeting their needs, the system can

3
either be redesigned or directly implemented into an operational system. The documentation for the project
is built during the RP loop with each iteration.

The chapters are summarised below.

CHAPTER 2
Analysis
The Problem
The difﬁculties by having a visual impairment is hard to imagine for a normal seeing individual.
Imagining how it would be to navigate through a never ending obstacle course covered in darkness or heavy
fog or doing other otherwise mundane tasks is impossible. A seeing individual would normally fumble and
stagger around, even in their own home, when the house is covered in darkness. If a VI wants to partake in
the same hobbies and exercise forms as seeing persons, they often need assistance.
Normally a VI user has a white cane and maybe a guide dog to aid them in their daily mobility. The
training of a guide dog is very strict and expensive, and the blind user has to be careful not to disrupt the
dogs training. Navigational aids for the VI can be divided into three categories.
1. Travel aids
a) Obstacle detection
b) Path planning
2. Orientation aids
3. Position locating aids
The white cane and the guide dog are included in the travels aids. Orientation and position locating aids are
today found in smartphones, where GPS, maps and synthetic speech can help a VI user to navigate unknown
areas. The aids used in the daily mobility are very hard to compete with since the training is built up over
several years. Especially the white cane is very hard to improve in a way that the added features give any
new or better beneﬁt. It is therefore more interesting to examine the cases where these aids cannot be used.
The traditional aids can not be used when running. The cane has a tendency snag on objects, and when you
are running the handle of the cane often ends up hurting the person using it. Guide dogs can not be used
either, since they are not trained for running, and doing so could interfere with the dog’s normal training.
The smartphone is easy to take along on a run, so the need of navigational aids are in the area of travel aids.
When a VI user wants to run, it requires the aid of a running guide. The VI runner is connected to the
guide with a short elastic rope (see ﬁgure 2.1). The guide can then direct the runner with spoken warnings
and by the rope. The main task of the guide is to steer the VI runner around obstacles and stay on the track.
The help from the runner’s guide is difﬁcult to replace, especially if the path is unknown or difﬁcult to
navigate. But by requiring a seeing person as guide is also a major barrier for VI who wants to run. The
Danish research centre for Welfare released a report about the barriers for visually impaired in 2010.[1] One

The Test User
The test person during this project is the authors older brother, Mark Sonne Nielsen. Mark is "practically
blind" with a vision of 1/60 on the best eye and also has a reduced ﬁeld of view.
"Practically blind" is a level of vision impairment in the danish system that allows for some light sensitivity.
To be labelled as "practically blind" one of three conditions has to be fulﬁlled:
• Have a vision of equal to or less than 1/60
• Have a ﬁeld of vision less than 10 degrees on the best eye
• Have a vision less than 6/18 and a ﬁeld of vision less than 20 degrees
==PAGE:27==
7
The vision fraction is understood as follows: a person can at 1 or 6 meter see the same feature as a person
with normal vision can see at 60 or 18 meter, respectively.
Mark is however able to determine some colours and contrasts. He can for example sense the lines
separating the lanes on a running court, but other lines and shades sometimes trick him and he becomes
unsure if it instead is an obstacle. He started losing his vision when he was in his teens, but he has managed
to ﬁnish an education as assistant sound technician. He is very active socially and volunteers in vision
impairment associations. In addition to vision impairment, Mark also has a disease called ataxia which
inﬂuence his motoric and sensing capabilities. This will inﬂuence any test result, when compared to other
VI.
Mark is very active physically; he runs long distances three times per week. He ran his ﬁrst marathon in

Before the development strategy for an obstacle detection aid could be chosen, I needed to examine the
hypothesis that a visually impaired runner needs an aiding device for obstacle detection. To examine the
hypothesis, I conducted a pilot test in the form of a Wizard of Oz experiment. A Wizard of Oz experiment

By simulating a ﬁnished product, it was possible to test how the user would use and respond to the aiding
device. With the pilot test I also wanted to examine the issues visually impaired users experience and test
some of the parameters needed for the device development, such as the minimum detection distance.

CHAPTER 4
Initial Prototype
After making the initial product investigation with the user, it is time to make the initial prototype. The
initial prototype will be a basic device with a Teensy 3.2 microcontroller as the backbone and the Lidar Lite
v3 distance sensor. The microcontroller will then turn on a vibrator if the measured distance is below the
minimum distance of 14 meters found in the pilot test.
4.1
Analysis
In the interview with the user after the pilot test, the user requested an aid for exercising on a running
track, which could warn him about obstacles on the track. The visually impaired user can sense the white
stripes separating the lanes, but have issues detecting obstacles on the course and distinguishing shadows
from obstacles.
There are two general solutions to detect an obstacle at a distance. The ﬁrst is vision/camera based and
the second is based on measuring the distance to the obstacle[12].
The use of strategy with a vision based pathﬁnding and collision warning is cheap in sensor cost,
but is very computational heavy. The requirement of a powerful processor will increase the cost, power
consumption, and also the size of the computational device. The light conditions will inevitable change
depending on time of the day, season and direction of the runner. Any camera based solution would be
very sensitive to the light conditions, and when the light condition changes, the ability to detect a possible
obstacles correctly is changed.
Measuring the distance to the obstacle can be achieved by using a time of ﬂight measuring method. The
most common technologies uses Infrared (IR), Ultrasound, Radar and LIDAR laser scanner. Common for all
three technologies is they send out a sound or electromagnetic wave that will reﬂect when it hits an object
and return to the sensor. By knowing the propagation speed of the wave and by measuring the time from the
wave emitting until it returns, the distance can be calculated simply by Distance = 1
2Velocity · Time.
The IR and ultra sonic distance sensors have a maximum range of 5 and 8 meters respectively, which is
not enough for the intended application. Also these sensors have been around for decades, and although
several people has invented obstacle detection aids using these technologies, they are not used by the visually
impaired community in Denmark. The most noteworthy aids are the K-sonarcane, NavBelt and Miniguide[3].
The best range and resolution is achieved with radars, but the technology is too expensive and large for
this project.

LIDAR is a more expensive sensor system than IR and ultrasound sensors, and it uses a laser range
ﬁnder to measure the distance to object around the scanner. LIDAR is used currently in most models of
autonomous cars, which together with the increasing interest in drones has pressed the size and price down
for these sensors. The price is from 150 USD and up depending on range and resolution of the measurement.
Therefore it is the type of sensor chosen for this project.
The pilot test showed that the device needs to detect obstacles at a minimum distance of 16 m. The
runner uses 10 m to stop from receiving the warning. Fast runners has a speed of 4 m/s and the device should
be able to have a 1.5 second delay of the warning, resulting in the minimum detection distance of 16 m.
4.2
Design and Implementation
The prototype needs a laser scanner, to detect obstacles. The laser scanners are traditionally expensive,
but Garmin announced during this project the release of a cheap laser range ﬁnder called the Lidar Lite v3[6].
The Lidar Lite is a small, safe and cheap laser ranger ﬁnder made for UAV’s (unmanned aerial vehicles)
and other robotic uses. The Lidar Lite has a maximum range of 40 m, well beyond the minimum detection
distance of 16 m, and an accuracy of ± 10 cm. It has a sample speed up to 500 Hz, and the default is 50
Hz. For the Lidar Lite to work a microcontroller is also needed. The choice of microcontroller falls on the
Arduino micro, which uses 5 V for its signals. This means I do not need a voltage regulator for connecting
the Lidar Lite.
The Lidar Lite is connected with the microcontroller through I2C, pulse width modulation (PWM) can
also be used. PWM was experimented with, but was ﬁxed to a sampling speed of 50 Hz. If the Lidar Lite
should be able to be tweaked it requires a I2C connection.
The program is written in the arduino develeopment environment, and the programming language is a
C/C++ dialect for Arduino. For the Lidar Lite it uses the co-released arduino library. In ﬁgure 4.1 a diagram
for the implemented program is shown. The arduino program is divided into a microcontroller setup and
loop. The setup is run only on start-up, and it is here the microcontroller is set for I2C and the Lidar Lite
is set up in default mode. After the setup the microcontroller enters an eternal loop. It is in this loop the
majority of the program is. By reading the status byte at address 0x01, bit 0 is a busy ﬂag for the Lidar Lite
and if it is high the sensor is busy. By creating a condition where data is only read from the sensor if it is not
busy, it frees up the microcontroller to do other tasks while it awaits the sensor. When the data is retrieved
from the sensor it checks if the distance measured is below the minimum detection distance.

5.1
Analysis
In the test of the initial prototype above, it was shown that the distance measurements does not allow a
user to positively locate an obstacle or distinguish obstacles from the ground. It would quickly become a
nuisance with all the false positives, and the device would not be used. This next prototype iteration needs to
resolve this issue.
With the choice of a laser range ﬁnder for detecting obstacles, it is an obvious choice to use a laser
scanner localisation strategy, which is often used in mobile robotics, for localising obstacles. By rotating the
laser range ﬁnder it would be possible to make distance measurements with a planar scan of the surrounding
environment. When running on a track, the user is not interested in every object around him. He is only
interested in objects on his current path, and not next to the track. If the device should only scan in the
current lane, the scanning angle can be limited. By limiting the scanning angle it is also possible to increase
the resolution, since all the samples from the range ﬁnder is distributed on a smaller angle. I therefore
determine the scanning angle based on the desired resolution for the aid, which again is based on the desired
minimum size of the obstacles and the detection distance.
Before a motor, for converting the range ﬁnder into a laser scanner, can be selected, I need to establish
a parameter for the size of objects which should be detected. My test user, Mark, reported during the
interview[10], that objects he has collided with is footballs, bags, baby prams and people. The narrowest
object I observed on the running court when making the pilot test with Mark was the lighting poles placed
around the stadium. The lighting poles have a diameter of 10 cm. To ensure that the pole is an object
and not noise, there has to be 3 samples distributed on less than 10 cm. The resolution needed is then

5.2
Design and Implementation
5.2.1
Design
The aid needs to know the angle of the sensor relative to the heading. Another solution from the ﬁeld
of robotics is the use of an inertial measurement unit (IMU) . An IMU measures the acceleration and the
change in angle (angular rates) of the IMU with an accelerometer and a gyroscope, respectively.
The accelerometer can be used to ﬁnd the orientation of the IMU relative to the ground, by calculating
the angle of the gravity vector relative to the accelerometer’s XYZ-axes.
The gyroscope can be used to calculate the rotation around the gyroscope’s axes by integrating the
measured angular rates. In most cases the axes of the gyroscope and the accelerometer are aligned such that
the gyroscope measures the angular rate around the accelerometer axes.
The issue with using an IMU is that it is prone to errors. The accelerometer will experience acceleration
vectors in other directions than the forward running direction, especially when it is mounted on an running
user who bounces up and down. The accelerometer can also not be used to calculate the heading of user
because the heading is the rotation around the gravity vector.
The gyroscope has an issue of integration errors. Small errors in the measurements will when integrated,
over time increasingly deviate away from the true angle. The accelerometer will on average provide the
correct angle, but it takes time to change an average. The accelerometer signal will be very noisy due to
the fact that any acceleration other than the gravity will change the resulting angle. On the other hand the
gyroscope will be good at measuring the small angle changes, but will over time be incorrect.
By using sensor fusion a more reliable angle can be found. A simple, but effective sensor fusion
technique, is the use of a complementary ﬁlter. A complementary ﬁlter creates the fused signal by weighing
the two signals that are either low- or high-pass ﬁltered. In practice it is simply the addition of a low-pass
ﬁltered signal to a high-pass ﬁltered signal, as is shown in ﬁgure 5.1. The solution is therefore to apply ﬁlters
to the IMU signal. By adding a low-pass ﬁlter to the accelerometer the high frequency noise is removed, and
by adding a high-pass ﬁlter to the integrated gyroscope data, the slow deviation from zero will be removed.
By fusing the outcome of the two ﬁlters a more reliable angle can be found for the two tilting angles (θpitch
and θroll) of the IMU.

5.3.1
Method
The approach to the obstacle location test is similar to the initial prototype test. The prototype was
mounted in a ﬁxed position on a tripod for stability. The prototype has been installed in a cardboard box for
easier mounting and protection. The test setup was placed in the middle of lane 4 at a running court with 6
lanes, where each lane has a width of 1.22 m (see ﬁgure 5.4 for the mounting and 5.7 for image of the test
site in Lyngby).
Figure 5.7: Test setup on running court in Lyngby
By measuring obstacles at different distances, it is possible to ﬁnd the detection limits for the device.
The further away an obstacle is, the smaller the area of the object in the ﬁeld of vision of the device will be.
Objects at long distances are therefore harder to detect, since the peak in the returned signal might become
lower than the threshold for the sensor. The angle between object and ground also diminishes at greater
distances (described in detail above). A small angle between the ground and an obstacle makes it hard to
distinguish the ground from the object, which will give more false positives.
Three obstacles were placed in the lanes of the running court and detected at three different distances. A
bag was placed in lane 6, a chair was placed in lane 4 and a person was placed in lane 2. The prototype was
tested at distances of 16, 24 and 30 m to the three different obstacles (see ﬁgure 5.8). 16 m is the minimum
distance the device should function at to allow enough travel distance to come to a halt when running. As
discussed in the pilot test, the minimum distance for stopping was 14 meters, the additional 2 meters were
added to accommodate varying reaction times for different users. 24 m and beyond is the desired normal
operating distance. For fast running speeds of 4 m/s (14.4 km/h or 14.2 minutes/km), an obstacle detected at
24 meter gives 2 seconds to change course before the ﬁnal stop warning is given at 16 m.

5.3.2
Test Results
From the experimental results, I conﬁrmed the hypothesis of using an IMU to locate the obstacles, by
correlating data from distance sensor with the IMU. The obstacle detection is further improved by removing
the samples taken when the sensor was pointed down towards the ground (negative pitch). The pitch of the
sensor is the rotation around the x-axis of the gyroscope and accelerometer. Positive pitch is up, and negative
pitch is down relative to the horizon. The obstacle can be localised by the sideways movement of the sensor
(yaw). The yaw is the rotation around the gyroscope and accelerometer y-axis. The y-axis of the gyroscope
and accelerometer is pointing down in the mounting, the data has been corrected for readability such that a
positive yaw is counterclockwise and negative yaw is clockwise. The yaw however is not very precise for
large angles due to the implemented high-pass ﬁlter on the yaw rotation, giving it a tendency to return to
zero.
Before plotting the data, the pitch, roll and yaw was normalised to the average of the 10 ﬁrst samples of
the data set. This was needed because there were small mis-alignments of the IMU attached to the range
ﬁnder as well as the range ﬁnder with respect to the horizon. The roll of the sensor was ignored, because it
does not increase the knowledge about an obstacle. By ignoring the roll of the sensor it also simpliﬁes data
handling, plotting the data using spherical coordinates.
At 16 m it was possible to distinguish a small obstacle the size of a school bag from the ground. A
medium sized obstacle in the form of a chair and a large obstacle (person) was observed easily in the data.
At 24 m the bag disappears into the ground data and the chair was only detected in about half the samples.
At 30 m it was not possible to detect any of the three obstacles.

between the obstacles and the maximum downward pitch before the ground begins. These angles are used for
reference in test results. As mentioned above, the width of the lanes are 1.22 m. For the sampled distances of
16, 24 and 30 m the angles between the centre of the obstacles are then 4.3, 2.9 and 2.3°, respectively. The

The IMU makes it possible to locate small objects at 16 m, and mid-size to large objects at 24 m. It can
also be shown from the test results that by using the pitch of the sensor, it is also possible to remove the

such change would either have an negative impact on the sampling speed of the range ﬁnder or the detection
sensitivity. Decreasing the sampling speed signiﬁcantly, lowers the ability to detect smaller objects. It would

6.1
Analysis
In the previous chapter is was determined that it is possible to locate an obstacle from the yaw, pitch and
distance readings. The obstacle can be located by comparing the yaw with the distance to it. To ensure the
obstacle is not a ground reading, the device can ﬁlter out most of the the ground readings by looking for
measurements that has a pitch above 0°. This of course, only allow the observation of obstacles which are
placed higher than the placement of the device. The precise angle for limiting the warnings can then at a
later time be tweaked, to allow small negative angles. The angle should change depending on the distance to
the obstacle, since close obstacles require a larger angle.
The aiding device should be designed to be placed at the chest. By placing the aid around the chest, it
impedes the runner the least, compared to around the arms and legs. An aid attached to the chest still makes
periodic scans without the user taking active part in the task of looking for obstacles. If the aid is attached to
the head, the user might be able to use it to actively look for obstacles. However the body is designed to
dampen motion throughout the body, such that the head is moving as little as possible when running. The
result is that the aid might loose the ability to look for obstacles in a large enough arch in front of the runner.
A device which can be placed on the head, will require further testing.
Before I am able to test the functionality of the prototype on myself or especially with a VI user, The
device needs to be able to warn the user of obstacles. A single vibrator has been attached to the device.
According to the results from the pilot test, warnings using sound received the highest score of 1.7, while
warnings in the form of vibrations on the chest came third with the score 0.7. The vibrator makes a distinct
buzzing sound, when activated. It is therefore not necessary to add a buzzer or other sound devices.

Software
The changes to the software are limited in this iteration. The main (and only) new feature is the warning
system that needs to be implemented. As mentioned above, the warning system consists of a vibrator motor
that needs to be enabled at the right time. The warning should start if the device records an obstacle at
a distance below 16 m. However, the Lidar Lite uses the distance of 1 cm for zero and inﬁnity values.
These values appear when the laser beam is not reﬂected back to the sensor and should not give a warning.
Furthermore, the warning should not be started if the sensor is aimed down toward the ground. The warning
should only be given if the distance is between 1 < Distance < 1600 cm and the pitch >= 0. The warning
is an activation of the vibrator connected to the PWM pin 4 on the Teensy 3.2. The pin is set as an output
during the setup phase and is activated as an analog output. The detection of an obstacle can be just one
sample, and if the next sample is not an obstacle, the vibrator would not be able to start before it is turned off

6.3.1
Method
The ﬁnal prototype was tested in two parts: A performance test and a user evaluation test. The ﬁrst part is
a performance test, where the prototype is used as intended. The device is attached to a runner, while he runs
two series on a running court. The track is traversed in one series where there are no obstacles and another
series where there is an obstacle. The test run is a 50 m run in a straight line. During the performance test,
the data is logged on a connected computer so the motion of the sensor can be showed together with any
detected obstacles. The performance test is executed with the author as the runner.
The second test is a user evaluation test, where an intended VI user tried the ﬁnal prototype of the obstacle
detection aid for VI runners. To be able to document a changed running behaviour in the user, the user’s
running times are documented for all tests of a distance of 50 m. The user ran four repetitions of four
different tests. The ﬁrst and second tests are without the aiding device, with and without obstacles. For the
third and fourth test, the user runs with the obstacle detection aid, with and without obstacles. After the test
runs, the test user is interviewed about the experience.
The chosen obstacle during these tests is a person. The reasons I have chosen to use a person are because
a person is the most likely obstacle to ﬁnd at a running track and a person can also function intelligently.
This is particularly important when performing the user evaluation test with a VI user. If the tested user
does not stop, then by having a person as an obstacle, the obstacle can take evasive manoeuvres. This is to
prevent any injuries to the test user. The author’s brother Mark has once again agreed to assist during the
user evaluation test.

appear at the edges of the scanning arc. This indicates that the sensor moves in a U-shape, where it pitches
up at the edges of the scan.
The obstacle detection feature can be seen to function by the obstacle detected after the run (shown with
blue markers). This is the stands at the stadium, which is detected when turning around after the run. These
are only possible obstacles, since they are beyond the 16 m, where the device would warn the runner. The
other data sets in this series show similar results, where no obstacles are detected, but with a constant low
pitch during the run.
The distance plot would, if there was an obstacle, show the data points getting closer to zero as I approached
the obstacle. What is seen in the data set is, the data points comes closer when the sensor is pitched forward,
and after wards move away from the sensor slowly.
Despite the sensor is angled down towards the ground it is possible to conclude by looking at all the data
together that there is no obstacle. This is however an advanced analysis I am currently not able to implement
in the prototype.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and Future Developments
7.1
Conclusion
The project started with an investigation of the most pressing navigational needs of the visually impaired
(VI). Because of the current effective tools already used by the VI for daily mobility (the white cane,
smartphone and guide dogs), the navigational aid needed to be developed was found to be most useful for
running purposes. When running the VI do not have access to any aids and rely on having a running guide.
A navigational aid that allows the runner to be free of the guide would increase the independence of the user
when practising on a closed track. The help of a navigational aid for running removes a barrier for the VI
who wants to run, and it could lead to an increased health level among the VI.
The initial problem was investigated with a pilot test where a VI user was included in the testing. The
pilot test showed that the user could beneﬁt from an obstacle detection aid. The test user stated that he would
be interested in such an aid, since it would allow him to practise more often. The pilot test showed that the
aid needed an auditory and tactile warning and that it should be mounted on the chest.
The next step was to decide how an obstacle detection aid should function. The chosen design incorpo-
rates a microcontroller and a laser range ﬁnder for detecting a obstacle in front of the runner. The laser range
ﬁnder functions as a laser scanner when mounted on the body of the runner. The test showed that it was able
to detect objects up to 30 m away. It was however not possible to distinguish obstacles from the ground or
background, nor was it possible to locate where the obstacles are.
To solve these problems an IMU was included in the prototype to record the movement of the device.
By knowing the angle and the distance to the obstacle, it is possible to locate obstacles and distinguish
them from the ground. The prototype is now able to locate obstacles. A ﬁeld test, where it was mounted
on a runner, showed that it is possible to use the natural body movement during a run to scan the path for
obstacles. The ﬁeld test also showed that the aid scanned down into the ground when running, but was still
able to detect obstacles.
The device unfortunately failed during the user evaluation. The test user was very positive about the prospect
of the device, but could not rate its functionality. When trying the prototype aid, he felt he got a sense of his
surroundings, and would appreciate even the slightest warning. Even so, he would prefer a running guide at
any time. The prototype is still in an early development stage and it is not consistent in the obstacle detection.
However, the intended users are very interested in the product. The ﬁnal assessment of the project is that
it shows potential, but it can not currently aid a VI user running. It would require more work as discussed
below.

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE DEVELOPMENTS
7.2
Future Developments
While the prototype shows potential, it is not in a state where it would consistently aid a VI user. The
reliability is a key element for a device, since the user has to rely on it to avoid injuries when running.
The next immediate step in the prototype development is to investigate a better solution for removing
ground readings. This could be done by making the pitch limits self-adjustable based on when it receives
inﬁnity readings. A construction of a proper housing for the device would also be in the immediate future for
the device. A housing would make it easier to wear the prototype correctly, and also protect the electronic
components from shocks and shakes during the run which can destroy the device.
The steps towards a ﬁnished product would require a VI friendly user interface, including warnings for
e.g. low battery, wrong mounting on the body, calibration. The test user requested that the product would
be able to guide a runner around an obstacle if possible. Such a path planning feature would require an
improvement to the obstacle warnings and storage for obstacle data.
When the VI user is ﬁnally able to avoid obstacles on the running track with high reliability, it would be
time to investigate if more features should be included to improve the running experience and increase the
desirability of the product.
The outlook for turning the product into a business venture is not promising. The possible customer
segment is small, which would make the price for such a device higher than the customer would be willing
to pay. Another solution could be to release the product as open-source, allowing the interested owner to
hire somebody to construct the device for his personal use. If this project should turn into a start up, the
aid needs to be turned into an all-round navigational aid, like a robotic guide dog. This would increase the
possible customer segment to the about 50.000 visually impaired in Denmark.
