This M.Sc thesis is an original work of Astrid Bjoern Doennem (M.Sc. Architectural Engi-
neering). The thesis has been prepared over five months with Anders Björnfot as supervi-
sor, during the period of September 25th 2023 to Feburary 25th 2024 at the Department

Abstract
Despite construction and demolition being the largest waste stream in Denmark [1], and in-
troducing requirements for standardized demolition plans being an action item established
in the Danish Climate Act to lessen waste and better use natural resources [2], Design
for Disassembly (DfD) is a small field. Currently, there are very few projects across the
industry where DfD is considered (Interview, Structural Engineer 1, A).
DfD is an ”approach to the design of a product or constructed asset that facilitates disas-
sembly at the end of its useful life, in such a way that enables components and parts to be
reused, recycled, recovered for energy or, in some other way, be diverted from the waste
stream” [3].
This paper investigates how to optimize DfD by identifying parameters necessary to eval-
uate DfD and by establishing a context-based framework to evaluate them. Research into
more wide-ranging topics within the current state of the industry and changes needed to
implement DfD successfully were also carried out.
A state-of-the-art literature review was conducted to establish the parameters required
for a DfD Framework for Evaluation. The method applied for generating weightings of
the identified parameters was inspired by the BREEAM Methodology. To gain a more
practical view of the industry, a case study and interviews with six experts, one of which
answered questions specific to the case study, were also conducted.
The research advances the field of DfD in building design by providing a starting point for
discussions and further work to identify parameters for optimization and evaluation of DfD,
as well as some insight into how the industry must adjust to enable DfD inclusion more
broadly in design. The resulting framework may form the basis which DfD of a project can
be rated against and function as a guiding tool in early design decisions to optimize DfD
in timber buildings.
Keywords: design for disassembly, circular economy, wooden structures, life cycle as-
sessment.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Design for Disassembly (DfD) is an ”approach to the design of a product or constructed
asset that facilitates disassembly at the end of its useful life, in such a way that enables
components and parts to be reused, recycled, recovered for energy or, in some other
way, be diverted from the waste stream” [3]. It may provide a solution to the world we
currently live in, often functioning linearly. Materials are commonly taken from the earth
and designed for singular uses before they are discarded, without consideration of po-
tential new lives. Although governments recognize the urgency for change in the ways
the world operates through adaptation of plans such as The Paris Agreement [6], The 17
Sustainability Goals [7], and The Climate Act in Denmark [2], a final solution has not yet
been found, and global improvement remains imperative to combat climate change.
In Denmark, construction and demolition is the largest waste stream [1], which also makes
it a focus area in which change may have a great impact. Designers are incorporating
concepts of circular economy, a principle in which products and materials are circulated at
their highest value [8]. Many green building standards, ratings, and certification systems
exist, but there are not yet any widely accepted for DfD, and few of the most recognized
building certification systems even even consider it as a part of their rating. Considering
facilitating disassembly as a method at the end of a product or constructed asset’s life
means it may be given a second life, and a great number of other benefits that may tran-
spire from incorporating DfD in design, there is great value in investigating and developing
DfD concepts. Luckily,
”the industry is becoming more and more
aware that [only] planning construction
elements to be easily produced and installed
is no longer sufficient ”(Interview, Structural
Engineer 3, A).
Considering the industry is constantly evolving and changing, including ”the relationships
between owner, contractor, architect, and engineer” (Interview, Structural Engineer 4, A),
adjusting the implementation of DfD in building design to enable optimal outcomes within
prevailing industry norms is detrimental to its success.
The research undertaken as a part of this master thesis is not just interesting for profes-
sionals within the built environment who desire to incorporate DfD in their designs, but
also for clients wishing to extract the most value out of the resources on their projects,
tenants who desire a built environment that may adjust to their needs, waste collectors
who desire a reduced pressure and quantities, forests handlers desiring resilient practices,
and governments desiring a more circular economy. While not necessarily by direct con-
sequences, the whole world is ultimately affected by the strain we’re experiencing on
resources and climate change. Any potential change in the construction industry, as one
of the largest waste streams, may therefore impact everyone.
This project will investigate principles necessary to optimize DfD, primarily of timber build-
ings. The goal is to create an assessment tool of DfD parameters, which may be used

to assist in early design decisions. The general idea is that the assessment tool will not
be material-specific and will account for the context of the design to make it applicable to
all new construction. Broad concepts related to DfD will be explored to give an extensive
understanding. A combination of a literature review, a case study, and interviews has
been conducted to conclude.
Inspiration may be taken from existing frameworks to create an evaluation method for DfD.
BREEAM, the world’s leading science-based suite of validation and certification systems
for a sustainable built environment, has created a weighting methodology [9]. The method-
ology may be adjusted to provide a single and comparable score for DfD. There is much
knowledge available in the literature on what these parameters may be, such as in the
design guides by Phillip Crowther [10], Mark Webster [11], and Guy Brad and Nicholas
Ciarimboli [12], which all identify themes and principles for DfD. The Danish Standard
DS/ISO 20887:2020 also defines principles, requirements, and guidance for designing

been written. Speaking to people with experiences in the field may reveal this, along with
a more holistic understanding. Including knowledge from a case study and interviews will
likely contribute to providing a more practical solution.
The research is expected to contribute to knowledge within the field of sustainable build-
ing design by advancing the understanding of DfD as a means of enabling a circular
economy. By identifying specific parameters, the research is also expected to lead to
actionable recommendations for design implementation. Although the research aims to
identify parameters necessary to evaluate DfD and establish a framework for evaluation,
this framework is expected to need additional refinement, work, and inputs before it may
be implemented through the industry. The thought-provoking discussions that ought to
take place throughout the partition of the study will likely also contribute to bringing aware-
ness to the importance and potential of DfD. Although the research conducted is only an
overview and beginning to establish norms for a wider adaptation of DfD principles, pub-
lishing the results aims to encourage further discussion and collaboration, which may
positively impact the industry.

Limitations to the work include a limited exploration of theory, in which the researcher has

much larger.
The assumption is that all identified parameters may be given a potential score of 1-5. In

3.1
Design for Disassembly (DfD)
DfD is an ”approach to the design of a product or constructed asset that facilitates dis-
assembly at the end of its useful life, in such a way that enables components and parts
to be reused, recycled, recovered for energy or, in some other way, be diverted from the
waste stream” [3]. In order to ensure that buildings are circulated at their highest value,
either through biological or technical cycles, the structure should be designed for disas-
sembly from the beginning. If not, the building might not be able to undergo the optimal or
desired change at the time of disassembly, or pursuing a sustainable disassembly path
might be more complicated than if the strategy for end-of-life was implemented in early
design to guide the solution. To do so, ”a major cultural shift is needed in all trades, which
recognizes the need for construction elements to be more separable” [20]. Research
and standards are emerging throughout the industry, but there is still much that needs to
change if DfD is to become a universal consideration of design successfully.
In their handbook on DfD, The Technological Institute identified the five most impactful
factors for the success of DfD to be [16]:
1. Better education of professionals in DfD
2. Give more points for deconstruction ability in sustainability assessment (like DGNB)
3. Laws, which require a deconstruction plan of the building, in relation to receiving the
building permit
4. Public law to see goals for the material recovery and reuse
5. Early involvement of professional demolishers, in connection with the design of the
building
But what is successful DfD? As a broad concept, DfD includes considerations from all
project team members. Determining what to educate professionals, what to give points

3.2
Circular Economy
3.2.1
Overview
The circular economy is about getting the most value from our resources. In a truly circular
economy, material never becomes waste, and nature can regenerate itself. It is a resilient
system in which the production of linear products with singular uses is stopped, and we
stop producing waste that cannot have a second life. It is underpinned by the transition
to renewable energy and materials, and gives the tools to tackle climate change while
addressing social needs. Three design principles drive the concept [21]:
• Eliminating waste and pollution: In nature, there is no waste; waste is actually
a result of design choices, choices taken without asking what happens at the end
of this product’s life. We must shift our mindset and treat waste as a design flaw,
including a specification for the material to re-enter the economy at the end of its
use to enable a circular economy. Products may be maintained, shared, reused,
repaired, refurbished, remanufactured, and recycled.[22] DfD are design choices to
reduce waste and pollution.
• Circulate products and material: The design principle circulating products and
materials at their highest value means ”keeping materials in use, either as a product,
when that can no longer be used, as components or raw materials. This way, nothing
becomes waste and the intrinsic value of products and materials are retained”[8].
The Ellen MacArthur Foundations think of it as two fundamental cycles: the technical
and the biological cycle as seen in Figure 3.1 [8]. The technical cycle is related to
a product’s series of lives. Key implementation methods include maintenance, re-
usage, refurbishment, manufacture, and recycling. On the other hand, the biological
cycle refers to returning material to the earth through processes such as composting
[8].
Wooden structures may circulate through both cycles, as wood is biodegradable.
The life cycle for wooden building materials includes ”Growth of trees, the harvest
and processing of woody biomass, the manufacture and assembly of wood-based
products, the utilization and maintenance of the building, and the disassembly and
end of-of life management of the wood materials”[23] Numerous studies have been
conducted to investigate the performance of wood-based products, and the general
conclusion is that wood-based materials have a potential to significantly reduce the
life cycle environmental impact when compared to non-wood based materials [23].

• Regenerating nature: We must shift the focus from extraction to regeneration.
Farming practices that allow nature to rebuild soils and increase biodiversity by re-
turning materials to the earth support the natural system, which has regenerated for
billions of years. [24] For wooden structures, this concept is highly related to the
forest industry cycle of plating, growing, and chopping trees. In a report investigat-

a sustainable build as the answer to, and definition of, a sustainable construction
[25]. A message from politicians, architects, developers, and the wooden industry
is clear: Wood is a part of nature’s natural system; it is regenerative and carbon
neutral [26].
Timothy Searching, a researcher from Princeton University in the US, as well as
other scientists around the world, are, however, questioning this and pointing out
new and overlooked calculations uncovering overlooked consequences for the cli-
mate from building with wood. It is only beneficial to build in wood as long as there
is a balance in which new trees are built. Although the forest is renewable, it takes
years for newly planted trees to absorb as much CO2 as the old would have done,
and thereby increasing CO2 emissions for years to come. Focusing on only using
environmentally certified wood may guarantee that the lumber is actually sustain-
able and truly contributes to a regenerating nature.[26]

Life Cycle Assessment
”Life cycle assessment is a tool that is used to determine and evaluate the environmen-
tal loadings and impacts of a particular product or process” [29]. Environmental impact
across the life cycle depends on relevant inputs and outputs. Inputs may be raw materials,
energy or water, and outputs may be emissions, waste, or other releases. An LCA does
not consider economic and social aspects, although the same principles may be used to
evaluate them. [29] In the book Life Cycle Assessment in the Built Environment [29], DfD
in the built environment is recognized as one of eight design strategies for a sustainable
built environment. If one desires to evaluate the life cycle impact of DfD and the range
of technical outcomes in the built environment, as seen in Figure 3.2, the five scales of
inputs and five scales of outputs may be used to evaluate the environmental impact in a
framework similar though a methodology similar to that for LCAs.
3.2.3.1
LCA in Denmark
In Denmark, LCA Collect is commonly used for LCA calculations. It is a web-based plat-
form that gathers all information needed to document the climate influence of a construc-
tion per the BR18 requirement, which are the legal requirements applicable to buildings
in Denmark. This documentation is a requirement for building permit applications from
the 1st of January, 2023. Benefits include a uniform method of digitally determining life
cycle analysis and the ability to gather statistics using a common platform. The platform
is compatible with LCAbyg, which focuses on the Danish construction industry to fit the
Danish context. Both are freely available. [30]
LCAbyg may be used to prepare documentation for DGNB certification for reference val-
ues and climate bonus points. It also allows you to simultaneously model more than one
scenario and make comparisons at the component level or renovation scenarios. [31]
LCAbyg can be used in three phases of design [31]:
1. Early LCA: Where comparison functions are available to for example compare con-
struction parts and types in the early phases.
2. Introductory LCA: Where you may use generic data from the LCA material library
to represent your building model.
3. Documenting LCA: Where your calculation or product-specific EPD data may be
used. EPD Danmark is an organization that gives environmental declarations per
the European Standard EN15803, which ensures a third-party verification of Danish
construction material [32].
Although LCA is currently not considering DfD in depth for calculations, aligning the design
decisions for DfD with the preexisting and required framework of LCA calculations may
simplify the inclusion of it into projects for designers and promote its incorporation across
the industry.

Green Building Standards And Evaluation Systems
In an effort to improve practices within the built environment and push towards sustainable
practices, a wide range of Green Building Standards and Certification Systems have been
created. These help guide, demonstrate, and document efforts to deliver sustainable,
high-performance buildings. There are hundreds of green product certifications in the
world. Determining which standards, certifications, and rating programs are most credible
and applicable can be challenging. [37]. Especially since requirements may either be
prescriptive, performance-based or outcome-based for code compliance [38].
Prescriptive: indicates specific requirements, such as levels of stringency of mechanical
properties, that can be quantified in tables. While that path dictates specific requirements
that must be met, it does not account for other potential factors that may improve perfor-
mance [38].
Performance Based: are designed to meet particular results and use baseline mea-
surements from which certain systems must perform. More details in building design are
required than for the prescriptive path. However, the approach is more flexible as it does
not limit the techniques or methods used [38].

agents affect the service life of building components, including mechanical, electromag-

Building as a Set of Components
In his book ”How Building Learn,” Stewart Brand proposes that buildings adapt best when
constantly refined and reshaped by their occupants. In the 6S Shearing Layer Model seen
in figure 3.12, he looks at the building as a set of components that evolve at separate
paces. The layers are site, skin, structure, services, space, and stuff. Based on findings
from his historical study, he identifies several social and technical benefits of designing in
this layered manner for adaptation, addition, and relocation [48].

While this report focuses primarily on the DfD of the structure, it is important to note the
structure’s relationship with all building components or systems, especially as the different
components have different potential lifetimes and will require alterations between layers
throughout the building’s life, not just within a layer. By allowing the layer’s lifetime to
be independent of those it attaches to, one may leverage the full potential of the layer.

skin, which has 30-60 years. Once the skin, or facade, needs replacement, one may be
forced to demolish the structure if the interface between the two does not allow them to
be separated. The structure may, per the lifetime estimates of the 6S shearing model,

Material Considerations for Timber
Unique to wood, when compared to many other construction materials, such as steel
or concrete, are the many special properties one must pay attention to in design and
especially in DfD. Unfortunately, despite the high potential for end-of-life reuse, salvaged
timber often does not end up in structures but instead for flooring and furniture [11] or
roofing.
3.7.1
Advantages
Some of the main advantages of timber as a construction material include include [49]:
• High Strength to Weight Ratio: Timber has a low density, and a high strength-to-
weight ratio relative to most other building materials/
• High degree of Strength and Stiffness
• Insulating Properties: Having both low thermal and electrical conductivity
• Durability: If properly treated, detailed, and built, timber statures can be highly
durable, Extensive research and experience over years has lead to comprehensive
knowledge on how to achieve durable design.
• Aesthetically Pleasing: Naturally available in many colors, patterns, shapes, and
sizes, wood is very versatile and aesthetically pleasing.
• Associated with good indoor climate: Current results are sometimes contradict-
ing, but wooden construction and building materials seem to be related to satisfac-
February, 2024
33 of 74
==PAGE:46==
tion and comfort in a complex manner. ”Wood is also promoted as a material that
can increase the sense of the presence of nature indoors and thereby increase the
comfort and well-being of occupants” [50].
• Overcoming Size Limitations: Developments in composite and engineering prod-
ucts, such as various lamination techniques, lessen libations from shapes, forms,
and sizes. Manufacturing and transportation boundaries now rule. [51].
3.7.2
Disadvantages
While it does not need to limit the ability of wood to be designed for disassembly, additional
considerations include (cite lectures anders)
• Anisotropic: Strength and Stiffness properties are highly dependent on the grain
direction and tend to be low in the directions perpendicular to its fibers.
• Affected by moisture content: Both strength and stiffness properties are nega-
tively affected by the moisture content, up until the fibre saturation point of about
27%. As most timber (in the UK) is air-dried to a moisture content between 17-23%
[51], properly protecting the building through the use of robust moisture manage-
ment techniques to protect the wood from decay and insect damage is very impor-
tant.
• Inhomogeneous: As a material of organic origin, natural in-homogeneity occurs.
Corrective factors are used to compensate, but many elements will be oversized due
to the erroneous strength assumptions. The size and frequency of knots distorts the
fibre around them and abnormal wood tissue formation from wind forces is referred
are only a few of the many factors that causes the highly varied structure in the
natural characteristics [51].
3.7.3
Structural Reuse of Timber
A challenge to increase the reuse of construction and demolition waste is the reluctance to
use products without certification of tested performance, particularly in a structural capac-
ity. Often there is very little information on where the product has come from and its length
of use in a particular application. This means that the ‘worst case scenario’ is normally
applied to the potential reuse applications. Testing of performance could also require the
destruction of samples to mitigate possible risks of further use. [33]
Although there are many challenges for structural properties unique to the reuse of wood
when compared to other materials, several wood-specific non-destructive test methods
are available that can be performed while maintaining structural integrity and reducing
the need for destructive testing. While non-destructive testing can never stand on its own
and requires destructive methods to calibrate the results, ”with sufficient knowledge of the
material and wooden structures, it is possible to reuse the wooden structures” [52]. There
are non-destructive test methods available for several mechanical properties (structural),
durability characteristics (environmental), and geometric properties (dimensional). Some
of the test methods include [52]:
• Visual Inspection: preliminary to evaluate conditions such as rot, tears or cracks,
signs of insects, direction and distance between wood fibers
• Rot test: physical test of resistance and resilience
• Moisture content: measuring of electrical resistance in the material between two
points.
• Mould growth: determined by surface samples as an indicator of moisture content

4.4
Relevance
Table 4.5 is a modified version of a similar table in DS/ ISO 20887:2020[3] and identifies
the appropriateness of the principles for disassembly based on the expected service life
and ownership of the building. The table aims to guide design to the correct DfD strategies,
which differ depending on the context of the intended design life before disassembly. A
numeric score has been given for the level of relevance, one to three, depending on
whether the relevance is low, medium, or high.
As an example, for short-term service lives of less than 20 years, building relocation has
a high relevance score of 3, as the Danish building code requires the design life to be
over 50 years. It is, therefore, highly likely that end-of-life scenarios for building relocation
may be desirable. Note that although a low score 1 was assigned for material recycling,
it is not excluded from the following calculations. It is given a lower weight for relevance
than building relocation. Conversely, for long-term service life ownership, we may look at
buildings such as public libraries or monuments expected to remain unaltered for a long
time. The design may be more unique, and it is more relevant for easy material recycling
to take place rather than building relocation.

5.1.2
Case Study Findings and Analysis
While the case study wasn’t explicitly designed to address every research question, it has
yielded several relevant insights to all the research questions, as follows:
1. What parameters are necessary to evaluate the Design for Disassembly?
Results: The parameters chosen to focus on in this project include: large plate el-
ements (1), joints (2), standardization of solutions through highly industrialized and
precise prefabrication (3), minimizing waste (4), and avoidance of adhesives and
paint (5).
Analysis: As the projects implement DfD as a solution to sustainable construction
practices, the main design considerations and solutions taken are a strong indicator
of relevant parameters to be included in any evaluation created for the evaluation of
DfD. The project may also serve as a sample model of how DfD may be implemented
in the built environment to promote reusability.
2. How can these parameters be evaluated in the same framework?
Results:For the case study, DfD was not evaluated per a standard or transparent
framework. Although BREEAM-NOR Excellent certified, there was no points re-
ceived for DfD.
Analysis:Despite being recognized in the industry as a frontrunner for DfD design
in timber buildings, the lack of ability to measure its deconstructability presents a
challenge in labeling the project a success and even more so in comparing it to
other projects. While the project has demonstrated notable achievements in pro-
moting DfD principles through implementing innovative and smart design strategies,
the absence of a quantifiable matrix for deconstructability makes evaluating its suc-
cess difficult. This underscores the need for standardized measures and evaluation
criteria in DfD projects to gauge their effectiveness and impact accurately.
3. What is the industry’s current state and how is DfD currently implemented?
February, 2024
51 of 74
==PAGE:64==
Results: Completed in 2022 and recognized as The Wooden Building of the year
2022”, the project stands as a premier example of Wooden Construction and is a
great state-of-the-art example for completed projects.
Analysis: The project does not represent the industry as a whole but shows how
clients may desire sustainable, demountable, and innovative buildings and how that
may be achieved in the current built environment by implementing DfD in design.
4. What design approach best supports the implementation of DfD?
Results: Notable design approaches include the initial project description from
stakeholders to include DfD, active participation from stakeholders throughout the
process, a broad feasibility study in which several strategies are investigated, pre-
fabricated elements, dedicated developers, good collaboration with the general con-
tractor, and the certification of the sustainability assessments BREEAM-NOR Excel-
lent.
Analysis: The results indicate that a comprehensive approach in which collabora-
tion and participation with stakeholders, developers, and general contractors best
support DfD. It also seems that including DfD requirements from the beginning of
the project and conducting a broad feasibility study is key.
5. What change is needed to implement DfD more successfully across the indus-
try?
Results: The project team had to overcome the ”this is how we always do this”
mentality. New concepts with uncertainty were found to be expensive.
Analysis: The project team overcame this by working with a dedicated developer
and good collaboration with the general contractor. If DfD concepts are pushed,
with time, the concepts will not be new anymore as timber producers and other
stakeholders build experience, which will likely result in easier implementation.
Overall, the case study served as a valuable contribution to the existing literature, provid-
ing practical and meaningful answers to the research questions.

The interviewees’ responses have been structured to align with the research questions
below. Interview answers from the Architect have not been included, as they were pre-

elements, and careful design of the partitioning of the elements.
SE 2 believes the response would vary significantly depending on the perspective

ement viability and detailing. Cost implications and overcoming local construction
practices were also mentioned.
SE 3 emphasized basic requirements for structural integrity and meeting local codes
and for structural connections to be dry and demountable.
SE 4 believed choosing the correct details was essential from a design perspective
but thought other aspects, like fire requirements and acoustics, were also necessary

in the slab to minimize holes in the CLT slab was also stated as important.
SE 5 thought the main factor worth noting was the connections.
Analysis: A range of answers arose by asking this question openly, without defining
DfD first, yet all answers aligned well. Two interviewees mentioned requirements to
align with local codes such as structural integrity (1), fire (2), and acoustics (3). It is
interesting how none of these directly impact the ability of a building to be disassem-

and likely mentioned as meeting them may be some of the challenging aspects of

The interviews were not aimed at answering this question. However, answers high-
light that considering the relative importance might be necessary. Some parameters,
like connection design, which was identified by four of five interviewees and thought
by SE 5 to be the primary worth noting, should perhaps have a higher weighting
than other parameters in any created framework.
3. What is the industry’s current state and how is DfD currently implemented?
Results: SE 1 states that DfD is seldom considered in projects in the industry and
that there are few instances of material reuse. However, the industry has already
changed much throughout his career, developers and builders now asking more fir
CLT as opposed to concrete and steel structures.
SE 2 on the other hand, works in a firm in which DfD is incorporated in any project
where it is technically possible.
SE 3 has seen topics of sustainability, timber structures, and DfD take more promi-
nent positions both in the industry and his company throughout his career. Con-
sultants, as well as clients, are today more aware than ever that de-signing in the
framework of a circular economy is paramount to a successful project. He has sev-
eral samples of projects that can be demountable, and the concept is embraced in
many architectural concepts. There are some examples of disassembly and reuse
of components, but he points out how since it is a new concept and the design
life of a project is 50 years, it will be 50 years before we see projects ready to be
demounted.
SE 4 has seen an increase in timber requests since 2017, when he started working
with timber. He notes how in Norway, it is mostly the public buildings that are built
with timber (schools, kinder gardens, nursing homes, student housing, etc.), but we
did have some buildings in the private sector, too, like apartment buildings, office
buildings, cabins, etc. Unless there is a clause to include DfD on a project, they do
not consider it by their own initiative.
SE 5 has seen mass timber becoming WAY more prevalent in the United States,

She’s seen many projects also explore it to speed up the construction sequence

Results: SE 1 and SE 5 did not speak to this question.
In SE 2’s experience, the biggest challenges are usually related to clients and con-

and increased complexity of detailing that can require stricter coordination between
manufacturers, construction workers, and other subcontractors.
SE 3 believes good planning should not just consider construction elements to be
easily produced and installed but also careful demounting, and therefore, a culture
of “kit of parts” design, construction, installation, and demounting. He believes one

the client to have everyone on board. The architect will then make design choices

SE 4 found it important to go into DfD structures already in the early stages of the

increasing design time with readjustments.

needed between consultants, clients, manufacturers, construction workers, and other
subcontractors is key. Including a DfD philosophy early in the project is detrimental,
allowing all following design decisions to fit within the parameters and reducing de-
sign time compared to the later inclusion of a DfD philosophy. The hesitancy from
clients and contractors regarding increased costs and complexity indicates a need
for education and awareness about the long-term benefits despite concerns about
initial challenges.

6
Discussion
Valuable insight was gained through the conduction of the research to answer the two
primary research questions, which focused on parameterizing the design to evaluate it
and ultimately design most efficiently for DfD. This chapter discusses the answers to the
research questions found from theory, the case study, and interviews, to converge findings
to one answer. The research findings have also been critically reviewed, recognizing
areas for improvement, and where the methods conducted did not result in as clear and
final answers as desired.
1. What parameters are necessary to evaluate the Design for Disassembly?
The state-of-the-art conducted in Chapter 4, which resulted in 57 parameters nec-
essary to evaluate DfD, is a valuable contribution to the field of DfD design. It is a
much more extensive list of parameters than what existed in the literature before. It
was unexpected for so many parameters to be important and for the span of topics
to be so broad. Yet, there are undoubtedly more that could be identified.
The case study provided some insight into five relevant parameters, as listed in
Subsection 1, but neither contradicted those identified in the framework.
As for the interviews, the answers challenge the framework more. Parameters iden-
tified by interviewees, as listed in 5.2.1, related to structural integrity (1), fire (2), and
acoustics (3) should not be included in the framework, despite being important for
the project, as they do not impact the DfD ability of the design, although they are
indeed challenges to overcome that may be even more difficult for a project in which
DfD is incorporated. The remaining five parameters that arose from the interviews
fit well into the framework.
2. How can these parameters be evaluated in the same framework?
The BREEAM Methodology for generating category weightings [9] proved a very
good framework to apply that accounted for the seriousness, relevance, and poten-
tial, to generate a normalized scoring method. This is a big step for the evaluation of
DfD, much more encompassing than any previous attempt at scoring found through
the research.
The case study had not evaluated their DfD in any framework and had, in fact, not
even received points for including DfD for their BREEAM/NOR Excellent certification,
which indicates much room for improvement.
The interviews suggest that the current potential for each parameter being 0-5 points,
independently of any weighting, might need adjustment to account got the relative
importance of parameters. Connection design, for example, could, and probably
should, be given more points than other categories. Further investigation is needed
to determine this.
The remaining three questions aim to answer how the industry must adjust to enable DfD
inclusion in design considerations.
3. What is the industry’s current state, and how is DfD currently implemented?
The case study proved a great example of a completed DfD project, but to truly
determine the industry’s state, a much more extensive review of the project would
have to be conducted.
56 of 74
February, 2024
==PAGE:69==
The interviews gave much more valuable insight, as the professionals were able
to reflect not just on their own projects but also on their knowledge of the broader
state of the industry. Although some specific design solutions were mentioned, the
answers mainly addressed the general state, one in which design in timber and the
implementation of DfD are becoming more and more prevalent, although there is
still much room for improvement.
4. What design approach best supports the implementation of DfD?
The literature review identified DfD as a means of contributing to the circular econ-
omy. Some of the identified strategies for best implementation included:
4.1 Evaluating DfD depending on the life-cycle scenario [10]
4.2 Planning and procurement practices that promote the incorporation of reclaimed
products and materials [33].
4.3 Aligning DfD strategies with the construction industry trends, such as using
artificial intelligence (AI), LCA assessments, BIM, project planning tools, and
simulation tools [36].
4.4 Create unambiguous rules and better traceability for construction and demoli-
tion waste [2] through transparency and awareness [13] and EPDs [43].
4.5 Deciding to include DfD early in a project [14].
4.6 An integrated design process (IPD) that encourages functional integration [14]
and cooperation with stakeholders from the construction and demolition sector,
including the public authorities [19].
4.7 Material considerations, including strength testing, to make it possible to reuse
the structure [52].
The case study provided some identical approaches but also further suggested:
4.8 To conduct a broad feasibility study in which several strategies are proposed
to answer the task. (Interview, Architect 1, A)
The interviews also mainly confirmed items identified as a part of the literature review,
but one additional consideration was suggested
4.9 Fostering a culture of “kit of parts” design, construction, installation, and de-
mounting. (Interview, Structural Engineer 3, A)
The most important factor, identified through all three research methods, seems to
be deciding Strategy 4.5, to include DfD in design early, at the beginning of the
project.
The items are not ranked in order of importance, and there may be many more
important considerations; these are just some of the main approaches identified.
More investigation into the approaches is needed, especially for items suggesting
industry change, such as ”aligning DfD strategies with the trends in the industry”
and ”creating standards and testing to promote reuse” [33]. Others, like ”Deciding
to include DfD early in the project,” may be implemented by project teams within the
current frames of the industry.
The framework is not a complete evaluation of the sustainability of a project; it is only
of the DfD. There are many considerations that greatly affect the overall sustainabil-
ity rating, which are not included, such as operational energy and social sustain-

ability. The framework for evaluation may, therefore, not function as a stand-alone
evaluation of sustainability.
5. What change is needed to implement DfD more successfully across the indus-
try?
To allow for the industry-wide application of the design approaches addressed when
answering research question three, ”What design approach best supports the imple-
mentation of DfD?” much development and change is needed. The literature review
identified some items as:
5.1 Develop the existing LCA-tools for buildings into design-tools [2] that include
DfD considerations.
5.2 Standards and testing of products to promote reuse [33]
5.3 Introduce requirements for standardized demolition plans [2] and for DfD certi-
fication as a means of ”considering the project as a whole” [55].
5.4 Create unambiguous rules and better traceability for construction and demoli-
tion waste [2] through transparency and awareness [13] and EPDs [43].
The case study further identified that we must
5.5 Overcome the ”this is how we always do this” mentality. (Interview, Architect 1,
A)
And the interviews cast light on the following additional items in need of change:
5.6 Educational efforts to promote sustainable practices. (Interview, Architect 1, A)
5.7 Creating a database with optional solutions (Interview, Structural Engineer 2,
A), especially for connection details and the ability to partition panels (Interview,
Structural Engineer 1, A).
The results show that there is significant change needed within the industry to allow
design approaches that best support the implementation of DfD and, ultimately, the
optimization of DfD, which will result in the highest scores in any applied framework.
In its current state, the industry has unreasonably many challenges for design teams
to overcome. Initiatives should be taken to reduce them by addressing the items
above. By doing so, DfD as a design strategy may contribute significantly to creating
the circular built environment we need to move towards.
6.0.1
Critical Review of Research
All questions asked for the case study and interviewees should have been aimed more
specifically at the research questions. A working session in which the professionals could
give feedback on the specific parameters might have allowed their input to be less generic
and with more depth, providing more value. The overall research methods were aimed
at addressing broad topics. Still, a need for more specific research, such as individual
parameters and items needed for change in the industry, should be conducted to gain
more specific answers for action as opposed to overarching themes.

Conclusion
The method applied to address the research questions in Section 1.3 resulted in valuable
insight from theory, case studies, and interviews. The created framework in Table 4.14
provides designers with a structured method for evaluating and improving the efficiency of
the Design for Disassembly (DfD), tailored to the specific design context of their projects
by accounting for the seriousness, relevance, and potential specific to the project context.
It serves as a practical solution to address the challenges within the building sector by
encouraging the circulation of products and materials through the implementation of 57
specific parameters that can be assessed through a normalized scoring method inspired
by the BREEAM Methodology for generating category weightings [9].
By committing to the identified DfD principles, we can create a more sustainable built
environment that meets the needs of today and is resilient and resource-efficient for future
generations, contributing to solving the urgent need to move from a linear to a circular
economy by providing universal guidance and requirements, aimed at solving construction
and demolition as the largest waste stream in Denmark. While all interviewees agreed
that DfD of timber buildings and sustainable solutions is becoming more prominent, there
is a divide in the understanding of how widespread the incorporation of DfD is across the
industry (Interviews, A), indicating room for improvement.
Through insight gained from the case studies and interviews, it is evident that while the
parameters identified in theory are crucial, broader concepts such as design approach
and stakeholder involvement are detrimental to the successful implementation of DfD in a
project. The most important factor, identified through all three research methods, seems
to be deciding to include DfD in design early, at the beginning of the project. Other im-
portant design approaches are evaluating DfD depending on the life-cycle scenario [10],
an integrated design process [19], planning and procurement practices that promote the
incorporation of reclaimed products and materials [33], and aligning DfD strategies with
the trends in the construction industry [36].
In order to enable these design approaches, much change is needed including industry
development of existing tools to include standards and testing for DfD [33], requirements
for standardized plans for DfD [2], waste traceability for DfD [2], educational efforts to
promote DfD (Interview, Architect 1, A), a database with solutions for DfD (Interview, Ar-
chitect 1, A), and certifications for DfD. Collaboration between various stakeholders from
the construction and demolition sector, including the public authorities, is also required to
change the industry [19].
Continuous research and development efforts should be taken to refine and expand the
evaluation criteria in response to evolving technology and sustainability standards. Ulti-
mately, a change in mindset and cultivation of a culture of responsibility throughout the
industry are needed to implement DfD as standard practice.
The research advances the field of DfD in building design by providing a starting point for
discussions and further work on the identification of parameters and evaluation, as well
as some insight into how the industry must adjust to enable DfD inclusion more broadly
in design. To conclude, this study shows that DfD may be evaluated in the framework
by assessing specific parameters and aims from Table 4.2 and scores per Table 4.14.
The framework may form the basis to rate the DfD of a project against and function as a
guiding tool in early design decisions to optimize DfD in timber buildings.

Future Works
Although the research provided valuable insight into optimizing the Design for Disassem-
bly (DfD) of timber structures, there is still a great need for, and potential in, further re-
search within the field to enable the most efficient and impactful DfD across the industry.
Some of the topics worth investigation that unfolded as the result of the conducted inves-
tigations include:
Refinement of Evaluation Framework: The potential score for all parameters has been
set to five, but there is potential for improvement through a more meticulous process
of thoughtful refinement. While the current weighted parameters reflect the varying se-
riousness and relevance, updating the potential score to demonstrate the different con-
sequences of including them, may result in a more accurate weighting. More input from
professionals and testing of the framework to ensure it functions as intended and produces
reliable results that are accurate and precise by assigning correct DfD scores would help
in identifying flaws and weaknesses, potentially leading to some changes to verify it meets
the intended purpose.
Further Case Studies: Conducting a more comprehensive and systematic review of
more case studies to identify success factors and reasons for different DfD solutions be-
tween projects may result in more knowledge about optimizing solutions.
Design Solutions: Further exploration of what design solutions may look like is needed
before DfD may be adopted more widely. Based on the identified parameters, finding
answers to questions such as: What structural kit-of-parts is optimal for DfD? What con-
nection allows for maximum flexibility in reuse? And what member sizes give the most
flexibility in grid layout for current and future uses? is essential before industry-wide adap-
tation is possible
Pushing Industry Change: Further research should be conducted to uncover how to
push industry-wide change best. As standardization is one of the main success mea-
sures of DfD, there is a need for industry-wide adopted solutions to move away from the
highly individualized industry, with unique solutions to all projects, to one that is more stan-
dardized and industrialized to optimize the use of all material resources. Solutions may
include government interference, inter-company collaboration, and stricter requirements
from clients.
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA): There is great potential in including DfD considerations in
the LCA of construction. Integrating the evaluation of DfD with required LCA calculations
and LCA tools may be a method of accelerating widespread industry focus and inclusion
of DfD concepts. Investigation into how this may be done is recommended.
Leveraging Technology: There is also great potential in leveraging parametric modeling
and technology for optimization in design. A script may be created that is optimized based
on a selected weighting of all parameters to result in the optimal design. Accomplishing
this does, however, require much more data and information to be included in the script on
things such as availability of sizes, construction methods, costs, uses, etc., and will likely
prove very difficult if it is to reflect the true complexity of decision making and optimization
that applies to DfD.

A.1
Architect 1
1. How were stakeholders and the projects involved in the DfD process and
decision-making?
The initial project description from the stakeholders was to design a sustainable, de-
mountable and innovative building. They initiated a broad feasibility study in which
we proposed several strategies and details to answer to the task. The stakeholders
actively looked for re-useable components from other buildings throughout the de-
sign process and helped in fixing demountability as one of the main concepts in the
tender documents.
2. What parameters did you find necessary to evaluate the Design for Disassem-
bly?
First and foremost, avoid adhesives and paint wherever possible. Mechanical con-
nections and dry installation were stated as a general prerequisite for detailing the
building. Screed was to be avoided, instead a raised data flooring was introduced.
Thus, we guarantee not only the possibilities for demounting the building but also
flexibility in use and adaptability over the years to come.
3. How did you determine the impact of design decisions on DfD?
We checked all our decisions and proposals from the work site against the ease
of demounting the details. Nearly equally high, we ranked the exposure of timber
surfaces toward the interior due to its important effects on a healthy and stable indoor
climate.
4. Was a plan made for disassembly, and if so, may I see it?
I am not sure if we are allowed to share those documents. Since the building is
largely constructed of prefabricated elements, the disassembly is basically a back-
ward reading of the installation documents.
5. What challenges did working with DfD concepts present?
We had to challenge and overcome the ”this is how we always do this” mentality
in all phases of the project. Even from timber producers. New concepts create
uncertainty, and uncertainty tends to be expensive. Luckily we had a dedicated
developer and a good collaboration with the general contractor. Nevertheless, the
process needed a lot of attention to not fall back on ”regular” details.
6. Why were dowels and x-fits chosen, and (a) what challenges did they cause
from a structural view?
Both those connectors, as well as wooden nails for fixing interior and exterior wood
panels, were chosen to a) reduce the amount of carbon-intensive steel in the building
and b) ease disassembly and facilitate re-use since no metal residues will remain in
the materials.
The challenges were small.
The column/beam connection has very little shear
forces, the dowels take small shear forces but mainly are to keep the columnn and
beam locked in place. The X-Fix connectors had big advantages when installing
the plates, and they drag the elements slightly together when being hammered in.

None. They can be drawn out, drilled out, or sawn over.

Design for disassembly is a topic that must be taken into consideration from the

sion of the concept of DfMA (Design for Manufacture and Assembly), the industry
is becoming more and more aware that planning construction elements to be eas-
ily produced and installed is no longer sufficient if the building will be demolished
afterward.
Good planning must also cater to careful demounting, and therefore, a culture of
“kit of parts” design, construction, installation, and demounting is a must-have to be
future-ready.
4. What factors do you consider important when working with DfD?
In our line of work, the basic requirement is the structural integrity of the building
and the satisfaction of all the minimum requirements dictated by the local codes of
practice.
To ensure DfD will be a reality, all structural connections must be dry and demount-
able. For example, it is common to have vibration problems on CLT decks. These
can be resolved by using a mass concrete topping-up on the timber deck. But cast-
ing concrete on timber will prevent any opportunity for disassembly and reuse also
affecting circularity, therefore alternative solutions must be found (for example use
a membrane between concrete and timber or change the structural system).
Timber is particularly prone to a Dfd approach since the connection can be screwed
or even dowelled together, meaning easier to demount. Steel connections are gen-
erally torqued in a way that makes it complex to demount. Concrete structures
(precast or in-situ) are even more complicated or virtually impossible to demount.
5. Do you have a sample project in which DfD was successfully implemented in
design?
Several projects can be demountable, and we see the concept being embraced in
several new architectural concepts.
See, for example, The Circular Building and Reversible Building in the Netherlands.
6. What challenges did working with DfD concepts present?
At times this can be challenging as designing for disassembly limits your options.
When working with DfD, we always need to find solutions to problems that will al-
low for easy disassembly. For example, when studying the robustness of a timber
structure, to ensure the alternative load path, part of the structural elements will nec-
essarily need to carry a very high load. This must be justified by connections that
need to be robust and demountable.
7. Have you worked on a project where structural parts were successfully reused?
It is a relatively new concept, and if you think that the design life for a project is gen-
erally 50 years, it will take 50 years before we see projects ready to be demounted.
However, there are some examples of disassembly and reuse of components. In
the DeFlat Kleinburg in Amsterdam, a renovation projects, part of the old exterior
panels were disassembled, refurbished and reinstalled, this allowed for the reuse
of the façade. Another example is the Bijlmerbajes, where a former prison complex
was transformed in a mixed-use development; parts of the existing building were
reused.

8. How do you determine the impact of design decisions on DfD?
One of the most important factors is the collaboration with the consultants’ team
and with the client to have everyone on board. The architect will then make design
choices (like materials, for example) that are aligned with the intent to realize a
project in the framework of DfMA and DfD.
At the beginning of a project, we define a philosophy, a path that will allow us to
complete the project with specific parameters. We try to include DfD as one of the
parameters along this path. As this is established in the beginning of the project,
any design decision will be framed to fit within the parameters established.
9. Are there standardized DfD solutions, or do they vary with the project?
The concept of DfD is that you do not need standardized solutions; you need to
carefully choose the right connections and move away from the business-as-usual
procedure, for example, avoiding wet connections. There are proprietary solutions
that a specific fabricator can offer in their catalog and could be used in the framework
of DfD. Typically, these have specific design parameters within which they can be
used. In some cases, the catalogues will not satisfy our requirements, in other words
you need to have an ad-hock study of the specific DfD solutions.
10. What is needed to implement DfD more successfully across the industry?
It depends on how we define success. I can provide my opinion in this regard.
If success is implementing more disassembly across the industry, promoting the
idea of a circular economy and sustainable development is a great start to raise
awareness in the industry (training). At the same time, fostering a culture of collab-
oration between the various disciplines involved in a project will help successfully
implement DfD (see question 8).
If success means that we need to make sure that the material is disassembled and
reused, we could implement a system of elements traceability (say we give an ID to
a specific timber beam and we then follow its history throughout its lifetime).
Another strategy could be embracing the so-called cascading theory, whereby a
structural element can be used as something else (not necessarily a structural ele-
ment ). For example, timber planks could be reused in the furniture industry, and
that could be implemented for steel and concrete as well.
The technology could be of great support. The current tools used in the industry (I’m
thinking mainly BIM and design tools) could incorporate plug-ins that assist in imple-
menting DfD, like visualizing the number of identical elements, highlighting problem
areas, and proposing a visual solution to the assembly/disassembly process.
11. . What changes do you foresee in the industry in the coming years? Predict-
ing the future is not one of my talents, but I can try to make an educated guess.
Sustainability is an excellent focus for the construction industry. I can imagine, and
hope, that there will be an advancement in the sustainable production of construc-
tion materials and that there will be a greater focus, where possible, on re-purposing
existing structures other than constructing ex-novo. In bullet points, I believe that
the main development areas in the future of the construction industry can be listed
as follows:
• The continued growth of the prefabricated methods of construction (modern
methods of construction to be broader): they are usually faster, safer, and
cheaper, and we can potentially integrate them with the DfD philosophy.

1. How has the industry changed throughout your career?
Since we started working with Timber (January 2017), we have seen an increase
in timber building requests. In Norway, it is mostly the public buildings that are built
with timber (schools, kinder gardens, nursing homes, student housing, etc.), but we
did have some buildings in the private sector, too, like apartment buildings, office
buildings, cabins, etc. What I see that is a big problem is that many companies take
on the design of timber structures, but they do not have the proper knowledge to
design structurally efficient buildings.
2. How much do you currently consider DfD in your projects?
If there is not a clause in the project to have a DfD system, then we don’t consider
it by our own initiative. We do try to have the joint detailing such as they could
be reused, but sometimes when having exposed timber elements due to the fire
requirements and production time, we do not have the opportunity to think about
reusable components/solutions.
3. How are project stakeholders involved in the DfD process and decision-making?
We had 2-3 projects where the owner wanted to have a building that could be dis-
assembled or even moved and assembled again (for example, pop-up shop) the
already designed structure. Unfortunately, just 1 project has been finished; the oth-
ers stopped; one stopped because of foundation costs – bad soil, and we still don’t
know why they stopped.
4. What parameters are necessary to evaluate the Design for Disassembly?
From the design side, it’s essential to choose the correct detailing. Another aspect
is the fire requirements of the building and the acoustics of it since it is a timber
building.
5. Do you have a sample project in which DfD was successfully implemented in
design?
Yes, Hasle Tre office building is designed for disassembly.
6. What challenges do working with DfD concepts present?
Mostly the detailing of the element, to choose the correct type of fixing between
elements that can be used again or do not destroy the elements. Another possibility
is cutting the end of beams/columns if shorter elements can be reused later. It is also
important where all the technical (electrical, ventilation, sprinkler, pipes) installations
are placed. If there are a lot of holes in the CLT slabs, that reduces the possibility
of reuse.

If you wonder about reusing timber elements, unfortunately, we have not been in-
