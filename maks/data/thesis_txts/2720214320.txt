Abstract
Today the inner work of most Neural Networks (NNs) is regarded as a black box.
The increasing
demand for applying NNs and their potential within society, enhances the need to explain their out-
put. Furthermore, explainability will contribute to increasing model performance and control, building
trust, and accountability. A global explanation of a NN’s classification class can be obtained using the
concept-based explainability method: Testing with Concept Activation Vectors (TCAV)[1].
This thesis presents the method TCAV developed for computer vision and applies it to Natural Lan-
guage Processing (NLP). The TCAV method is a quantitative way to examine the high-dimensional
internal state of a neural network. First, the components of the method are presented theoretically along
with an attempt to reproduce one of the originally published results for image classification. Based on
the challenges associated with the reproduction, TCAV results are further analyzed to understand and
examine the behavior of the concepts across the internal state.
The explainability method is adapted and implemented to the domain of text classification.
First,
common methods for handling and preprocessing text data are described. This is followed by an intro-
duction to Transformer-based NLP models, by which the Transformer architecture and its components
are introduced. While NLP consists of many different tasks and models, this thesis considers a pre-
trained A Robustly Optimized BERT Pre-training Approach (RoBERTa) model and fine-tunes it for
the downstream task of sentiment classification. This forms the context for the analysis of TCAV within
NLP for which several concepts are explored as potential explanations for the target sentiment class.
Additional analyses identified that the final layers are approximately linear transformations, which en-
tails that the TCAV method is unsuitable for these layers. Further work should be conducted to devise
a qualified testing method for these.
It is of general interest to be able to quantify whether a user-defined concept is important to a classi-
fication network. The TCAV method’s ability to explore and generate insights for the target class of
interest is limited by the provided concepts, and concept retrieval is non-trivial. This thesis presents
an approach to finding contextually relevant concepts in an objective and eﬀicient manner. The key
idea is to use Wikipedia articles and their internal relations to create a Knowledge Graph (KG) that de-
fines relevant concepts and their corresponding data. This enables a user to investigate several concepts
within the same topic and generate labeled data to test their relevance against the target sentiment class.
Keywords:
Natural Language Processing, Transformer model, Sentiment Classification, Concept-
based Explainability, TCAV, Knowledge Graphs, Extracting Concepts

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) are found at all levels of our society today from
Google Translate and e-mail filters to Robo-advisory within wealth management. Even more potential
applications are to come, some with higher stakes than others. Examples of high stake applications are
autonomous vehicles[2], diagnosing patients[3, 4], and security applications[5]. All applications come
with a lot of opportunities but also challenges, and there is a concern about which implications they
might have on society, especially the high stake applications. In situations, where decisions are based
on a ML model and will ultimately affect a person’s life, there is a growing need for understanding how
and why the model made the decision and with that, an increasing focus on bias[6] and trust[7] of ML
models. Using ML models in EU from 2018 in contexts where lives are affected, e.g. in medicine, law,
or even possible self-driving vehicles, the user and the affected citizen have the ”right to explanation”.
This means that the user can demand an explanation of the decision made by an algorithm. It is argued
that this law will constitute large challenges for the industry, but nevertheless, it will also create an
extensive demand and great potential for explanation methods within ML and AI[8].
1.1.1
Explainability
Some models in ML have the characteristic property of interpretability, where the reasoning for a
given output can be deduced from the model itself. An example of an interpretable model is decision
trees, which by the design of the model are interpretable and give an explanation from their easy-to-
understand procedure, but they are limited by the complexity of problems they can tackle and are
therefore often inadequate. Complicated computational tasks within ML require more complex models
which can be obtained with Deep Learning (DL) methods. The success of DL methods e.g. Deep
Neural Network (DNN) originates in the combination of large parametric spaces and eﬀicient learning
algorithms[9]. These latter spaces are also what make people regard DNNs as black-box models as such
spaces comprise millions, billions, or even hundreds of billions of parameters[10] and up to a thousand
layers[11, 10, 12]. These models are not interpretable due to their design.
NLP in healthcare presents some unique and inspiring opportunities to e.g. retrieve information and
support clinical decisions. However, if critical and important decisions made by such black-box ML
models are to become an everyday working reality, the need for explanations arises concurrently. This
demand for explanation comes from various stakeholders[13], and it is important to be able to provide
trust in high-risk domains such as healthcare[14].
A great thing about explainability in the context of ML, is that it is not a necessity to make the
model work. It can be used as a separate building block when testing the model. One could view the
explanation as insurance, like with a car, the seat belt is not a must to make the car run, but it sure
is favorable if the car crashes. The same goes for the explanation, the ML model can for sure run
without, but if something goes wrong, it is desired to be able to give an explanation. The explanation
is important to gain accountability, to know which factors created the decision, and thereby, enhance
control of the model. Furthermore, explanation will extend the understanding of how the model works,
e.g. if it has biases, and thereby makes it easier to fine-tune and improve performance[15]. Besides using

1.1.1.1
Local and Global State-of-the-art Methods
To attain explainability in ML, one can be limited to solely use interpretable models, which will pro-
vide the explanation by the passive characteristics of the model, but will lack in performance when the
problem-complexity increases [17, 18]. Another option is to post-process a given model, but with the
drawback of potentially needing to modify and retrain the model. The growing demand for explaining
ML models, to ensure that they e.g. comply with government regulations and industry standards[8],
require methods that can be utilized without the need for modifications or retraining of an entire model.
There are two ways to regard an explanation; using a local or global viewpoint. A global explana-
tion helps to provide knowledge about which features or concepts in the model contribute to an overall
prediction of e.g. a class. A local explanation gives insight into each individual prediction. An example
of obtaining a local explanation is by using Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME)
being an interpretable surrogate model e.g. a linear regression model, that explains the predictions
of any classifier with a local approximation[19]. To simplify, it manipulates the original input data,
where the new data set only contains part of the original attributes. The new data is then classified,
and through the absence or presence of different key attributes, the impact on the classification can
be measured. Meaning that LIME gives an explanation for each individual prediction. One drawback
of LIME is that it has shown not to be robust, where minor alterations in input data can cause the
explanation to differ substantially[20].
Another example is SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP)[21], computing the marginal contribu-
tion of each feature in the model to provide a local and global explanation. The explanation for the
SHAP method is proved to be consistent in its explanations as compared to LIME. That being said,
LIME and SHAP are both techniques that rely on input perturbations. Besides being very computa-
tionally time-consuming, they have also shown to be unreliable and not detect biases in classifiers[22].
Popular local explanation methods within image classification are saliency maps[23, 24, 25]. The
output of these explainability maps usually visualizes the importance of each pixel for a given classifica-
tion. A limitation of this method is that it is only a local explanation. To obtain an explanation for a
whole class, each picture in a given class is manually examined to make a class-wide explanation. Fur-
thermore, it has been shown that saliency methods can be manipulated without impacting the model
prediction[26, 27].
With the pitfalls of feature-based explanations, research has focused more on providing explanations by
means of human-defined concepts. It has been shown by Zhu et al. that human-understood concepts
can be learned with the examples of a zebra and Van Gogh[28]. Research also shows how it is possible
to reveal the important concepts in an image instead of focusing on appointing importance to individual
pixels or features[29]. However, the method proposed by Zhou et al. can only identify concepts from
the dictionary applied[29]. Kim et al. goes beyond this in “Interpretability Beyond Feature Attribution:
Quantitative Testing with Concept Activation Vectors (TCAV)”[1]. Here, the TCAV explanation is
feasible beyond the feature attributes and only requires data for the proposed concepts and random
counterparts. The explanation is obtained on basis of a linear classification between the concept and
its random counterpart. Linear classification is a proven approach, where it has been shown that it
is not the individual units but entire vectors that have the semantic information of the input encoded
in a NN [30]. In addition to that, meaningful directions from the layers in a NN can be learned by
means of a simple linear classifier [31]. The TCAV method benefits from being a global explanation and
thereby provides an explanation for a whole class. Additionally, it can be used directly on a trained NN
without any retraining or modification of the model. TCAV has been used in several settings such as
histopathology for breast cancer[32], medical imaging[33], and cardiac magnetic resonance imaging[34].
Regardless of the setting, this method always depends on the user to provide relevant concepts to get

1.1.2
Natural Language Processing
Human languages often referred to as natural languages, contain large diversity in vocabulary, words
with various meanings, and speakers with different accents. Words are often assembled together, key
details are omitted and words are mispronounced. However, humans tend to look through these varia-
tions and generally, understand each other. Our skillful use of languages is a huge part of what makes
us human. Therefore, the desire of giving computers the ability to understand and speak our languages
has been a major task in AI and has led to the creation of NLP. It is at the core of tools we use every
day, e.g. search engines, translation software, and voice assistants.
The origin of bridging the communication gap between computers and humans was seen during World
War II by the idea of having an automatic translation between Russian and English sentences. This
project was involved as the Georgetown-IBM Experiment in 1954 and became the foundation of Machine
Translation (MT) research[37]. In 1966, the Automatic Language Processing Advisory Committee (AL-
PAC) (committee evaluating the progress of NLP research) reported slow and ineﬀicient progress in
the project resulting in funding for MT being drastically reduced[38]. However, the IBM staff of the
Thomas J. Watson Research Center claimed that the true obstacles of MT lay in the relative impotence
of available computing power. As computational power increased and statistical methods were proven
to work, IBM became leading in the development of successful and complicated statistical MT mod-
els[39].
Natural language modeling is nothing new. In 1983 Salton and McGill made the Introduction to mod-
ern information retrieval introducing a vector retrieval method[40]. This was followed by the extension
Latent Semantic Indexing in 1994[41], but the first natural language model within NN was proposed in
2001. It replaced the classical approaches such as N-grams in order to predict the next word in a text
based on the previous[42]. Recently, the feed-forward neural networks were replaced by Recurrent Neu-
ral Network (RNN) to learn the sequential data[43] and later, the Long Short Term Memory (LSTM)
network for language modeling was developed as an extension of RNNs. Here, the long-term connections
were found to be more precise than standard RNNs[44]. The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
was additionally started to get applied to language and enabled the possibility to run states in parallel
as opposed to RNNs[45, 46]. Several combinations of these main types of NNs have been proposed since.
As stated in the previous section, ML models are increasing in complexity, the same goes for NLP
methods, so the need for understanding such models and explaining their decisions is increasing too.
Questions about model behavior arise: When does it perform poorly? Why does it make such predic-
tions? What happens under a controlled change in the input? The research workflows are constantly
advanced into deeper and more complicated models which drive the need for an explanation of NLP
models in order to trust the output decisions.
1.1.2.1
State-of-the-art NLP models
In 2017, a new state-of-the-art architecture was proposed to replace the current RNNs and CNNs.
This groundbreaking architecture was introduced by Vaswani et al. and referred to as the Transformer
model[47]. The model follows an encoder-decoder structure with stacked self-attention layers. Hereby,

the recurrence mechanism was eschewed and instead replaced solely by an attention mechanism to
draw global dependencies between input and output. This architecture is the go-to model for solving
different problems, e.g. text classification and machine translation. Enhancements of the Transformer
architecture were later proposed where the approach was to utilize the multi-layer Transformer-Decoder
together with a LSTM baseline model[48] and further as stand-alone Transformer-Decoder architecture:

GTP-2 and GTP-3 were later proposed with more parameters and became the largest

corpus solving one problem and used to solve a different, yet related problem. The idea behind pre-
trained transfer models arises, as labeled datasets for specific NLP tasks are usually limited and quite
small. Instead, various unlabeled data sources can be utilized for pre-training. As long as the dataset
is large enough, the data sources can be unrelated to the specific task. The pre-training is very time
and resource consuming and usually, such tasks require multiple GPUs and several hours or even days
of training. The obtained model checkpoints of these pre-trained models then serve as a starting point
for fine-tuning and thus, this approach saves a lot of computation as these pre-trained models can be
used in various NLP downstream tasks.
1.1.3
Knowledge Graphs
Building upon the revolutions of the 1990s, many great inventions came in the wake of the World Wide
Web[52], which created a global information infrastructure. The first project succeeding in combining
knowledge and data on the Web was the Semantic Web[53], later leading to the invention of the data
structure Linked Data with examples of Freebase[54] andWikidata[55]. The big breakthrough came
with Google’s announcement of Google Knowledge Graph in 2012[56] followed by KG announcements
from companies such as Amazon[57], LinkedIn[58], Microsoft[59], and many more[60].
All product
announcements on KG shared the common factor; representing data in the form of a graph. KGs have
continuously been developed and used especially to deal with the dominating nature of the Web[60].
The graphs supply an intuitive abstraction for a given domain and provide a link between domains,
where each node represents entities of interest which can be objects, concepts, or events, and edges
represent relations between the entities.
A KG is machine-readable and also allows a machine to
uncover knowledge in an organized manner[61].
1.1.3.1
Utilization of KGs
Exploring state-of-the-art for KGs within NLP, it is mainly utilized as background knowledge[62],
whereas within AI, they are generally used to obtain explanations. The latter is often in the context
of a specific model. Within the domain of AI, multiple attempts have been performed to utilize KGs
for obtaining better explanations. In “A framework for explainable deep neural models using external
knowledge graphs,” a KG is aligned with already known concepts to let the model exploit patterns
beyond the signals that are given through the training data. It should be noted that a lot of work has
been performed in aligning training data and KG and that this study is still on a hypothesizing level
about improving the explainability and robustness of the model[63]. There are multiple examples where
KG are exploited as background knowledge, e.g. the use of ConceptNet to explain objects in an image
with captions[64].
The KGs represent a valuable form of domain-specific, machine-readable knowledge. Here, the con-
nected or centralized datasets available could possibly serve as background knowledge for producing
concepts of relevance and obtaining labeled data. KGs have shown to be a potential research line that
should be explored. However, one also has to bear in mind that the need for filtering data when wanting
to identify relevant data is a huge task[61].

2.1
Data preprocessing in NLP
The study and development of NLP cover the possibility for computer systems to be able to interpret
speech and text as humans naturally speak and write it. Prior to any modeling involving text, the first
task is to perform some basic text processing where converting the text into regular expressions plays
a large role. This involves performing text normalization, which includes the tokenization of text input
into so-called tokens that are later embedded.
2.1.1
Tokenization
To be able to handle the extensive vocabulary in natural language corpora, the text is tokenized into
a numeric representation to obtain a narrower vocabulary. This tokenization involves chopping up a
character sequence into a sequence of tokens. In language processing type is an element of the vocabu-
lary and token is an instance of the type in a text.
Tokenization is the main task when preprocessing text data. The first step is to split the text into
initial tokens and e.g. remove HTML elements in the data. There are multiple ways to split the initial
tokens such as tokenization on white spaces. Alternatively, the text can be split into single characters
with spaces treated as part of a token. Meaning that a character will be encoded in two different ways
when it is at the beginning of a sentence or not.
After splitting into the initial tokens, the next step is to create a new vocabulary. The encoding
used for RoBERTa is the byte-level Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE). BPE is a data compression technique,
where the most frequent pair of consecutive bytes of data is replaced with a single unused byte. BPE
was first introduced in 1994[65]. The method was later converted from merging frequent pairs of bytes
to merging character sequences used for word segmentation in neural machine translation[66]. From the
initial tokens, a set of all types and their occurrence in the training dataset is determined and BPE then
creates a base vocabulary. This base vocabulary comprises all symbols appearing in the set of types,
where merge rules are learned to create new symbols from an existing pair in the base vocabulary. This
merging is repeated until the desired vocabulary size is obtained. In this way, the method results in
a vocabulary where the most used words are represented by a single token, and rare words are broken
into multiple tokens, resulting in a typical vocabulary size ranging from 10 000 to 100 000 tokens. The
byte-level refinement of the BPE used in RoBERTa was introduced by Radford et al. It works with a
base vocabulary size of 256 and a final vocabulary of 50 256 tokens[67]. Building a vocabulary from an
alphabet of single characters turns out to be beneficial when having even rare words being decomposed
and tokenized, thereby avoiding unknown tokens.

2.1.2
Embedding
The key breakthrough in deep learning in order to represent words and documents is vector embedding.
Having the text tokenized, the model still requires the inputs to be numeric rather than strings to
perform mathematical operations on them. Thereby, the next step in data processing is to associate
each token with an embedding where strings are converted into vectors of real numbers. There are many
ways to create such embedding vectors and most deep learning frameworks offer a pre-trained embedding
provided by the associated research group. This enables the user to use an existing vocabulary.
A common way to ensure that all inputs have equal lengths is to utilize padding and truncation.
Hereby, sequences above a fixed threshold are truncated whereas sequences shorter than the threshold
are padded to obtain the appointed length.

Models in NLP
In the following, an introduction of the proposed Transformer model including an overview of its com-
ponents is given. The Transformer led to huge development within ML models and is one of the most
common transfer learning architectures. A milestone in the NLP community was achieved when re-
searchers from Google AI published BERT based on the Transformer. The second part of the section
focuses on BERT and the optimized method RoBERTa, and how it can be fine-tuned for the downstream

2.2.1
Transformers
In 2017, the Transformer model was introduced by Vaswani et al. in Attention Is All You Need. This
work proposed the model architecture that eschewed recurrence by entirely relying on the attention
mechanism to draw global dependencies between input and output[47]. With further development, the
Transformer structure became state-of-the-art in machine translation, sequence- and language modeling.
As the title indicates, the attention mechanism in the proposed Transformer model plays an important
role.
In general, the model consists of stacked self-attention and point-wise fully connected layers
inserted into multiple encoders and decoders. In order to understand how the Transformer works, the
following will introduce what self-attention actually is, leading to the introduction of the multi-head
attention mechanism that is used in the model. Lastly, a brief overall description of how the Transformer
works including its remaining components.
2.2.1.1
Self-Attention
The core idea behind self-attention is to compute a weight distribution on the input sequence, assigning
higher values to contextually relevant elements[68]. The mechanism of self-attention can be described
as mapping a query with keys and getting weights for the values. Such weights can be understood as
the relevance between the query and each key. The values are then reweighed using the weights, and
lastly, the output is computed as a weighted sum of the values. The weights assigned to each value are
computed by a compatibility function of the query with the corresponding key.
Having the overall description of how the self-attention mechanism works, an example of the compu-
tation of the mechanism is given to understand it in more detail. The before-mentioned query, key,
and value are vectors created for each of the encoder’s inputs, i.e. the embedding of each token. The
said embedding is multiplied by three matrices, W Q, W K, and W V that are learned during training,
resulting in the query, key, and value vectors. A visualization of the queries, keys, and value vectors
and their corresponding weight matrices are seen in Figure 2.1. Here, the embedding of the first input,
x1, is multiplied by the weight matrix W Q producing the associated query vector, q1. Using the same
approach creates a query, key, and value vector for each token in the input sentence.

2.2.1.2
Scaled Dot-Product Attention
The compatibility function used in Attention Is All You Need is the scaled dot-product. The input
of the compatibility function consists of the query vector, q ∈Rdk, and the key vectors, ki ∈Rdk for
i ∈[1, L] where L is the number of input tokens, as well as the value vector of dimension Rdv. The
result is scaled by the dimension of the key vector, √dk, leading to more stable gradients. The scaled
dot-product of the query and keys is computed in order to find the most similar key in regard to the
query. Using the softmax function, the closest query-key pair will have the highest value. The result
from the softmax is then multiplied by the value vector.
Continuing the example given in Figure 2.1, the first token in the input sentence is scored against
the remaining tokens. Thus, to compute the self-attention on the first token, the dot-product of q1 and
k1 is computed and referred to as score one, by which the second score is the dot-product of q1 and
k2. These scores are scaled and passed through the softmax operation ensuring normalized scores that
are positive and sum to one. Each value vector v1 and v2 are then multiplied by their corresponding
softmax output of scores one and two. The idea is to keep the values of the relevant tokens intact and
tone down irrelevant tokens. The last step in the self-attention computation is to sum the weighted
value vectors, v1 and v2, producing the output of the first token.
In practice, the self-attention mechanism is computed on query, key, and value matrices instead of
vectors. These matrices are created by packing the input embeddings into a matrix X, having each
row corresponding to a token. The embedding matrix is multiplied by the trained weight matrices,
W Q, W K, and W V .

Figure 2.2b. Here, the output of the softmax function is referred to as P having the same dimension as
the value matrix. The self-attention is computed by multiplying P and the value matrix V resulting
in Z. Formally, the output formula for the self-attention computation is given by

2.2.1.3
Multi-Head Attention
It is often not enough to obtain the contextual relevance of a token using a single attention function.
This is accommodated using the so-called multi-headed attention mechanism which expands the model’s
ability to focus on different positions in the input. The multi-head attention contains multiple scaled
dot-product attention heads operating in parallel. Thereby, non of the heads will share any weights,
i.e. the multi-head attention consists of multiple sets of query, key, and value weight matrices, whereas
the input embeddings stay the same. Each of these weight matrices is randomly initialized and after
training used to project the input embeddings into different representation subspaces. Having h heads
will result in h different self-attention output matrices previously referred to as Z. These h matrices
are concatenated into a single matrix. However, to accommodate equal dimensions between the input
embeddings and the output of the multi-head attention, a dense linear layer is utilized. Thus, the
concatenated matrices are multiplied by an additional weight matrix W O that is jointly trained with
the model.
To sum up, multi-head attention allows to jointly attend to information from different representation
subspaces. Formally, the mechanism is given by
MultiHead(Q, K, V ) = Concat(Z1, . . . , Zh)W O,
(2.2)
where Zi is given by Equation 2.1 for i ∈[1, h]. W O is a learned matrix used to scale the concatenated
matrices.

output sequence. This is done by masking future positions. This masking, combined with offsetting
the output embeddings to the right by one position, ensures that the predictions for position i only
depend on the known outputs at positions less than i. The residual connections are likewise utilized

2.2.2
Transfer Learning
It turns out that the Transformer is very suitable for transfer learning. The idea of transfer learning
is to train a model on one task, and then make use of the acquired knowledge to fine-tune the model’s
performance on a downstream task.
Downstream tasks in NLP is a supervised learning task that
makes use of a pre-trained component or model. One of the front runners within transfer learning was
Generative Pre-Training (GPT)-1 improving on the state-of-the-art for multiple languages understand-
ing tasks[49]. To evaluate performance for transfer learning, a multi-task benchmark such as General
Language Understanding Evaluation (GLUE) can be utilized[70].
2.2.3
BERT
To overcome the limitations of the left-to-right architecture, Devlin et al. only use the encoder part of
the Transformer in their work which introduces the BERT model[51]. BERT has a multi-layer bidirec-
tional Transformer encoder architecture. The language model is trained using two strategies: Masked
Language Modeling (MLM) and Next Sentence Prediction (NSP). The objective of MLM training is to
mask a word in a sentence with the task-specific token [MASK], and train the model to predict the word
based on the context of the non-masked words. The objective of NSP training is to train the model to
predict whether two sentences have a logical, sequential connection or not. The model was trained to
minimize the cross entropy loss on these tasks.
The original version of the BERT architecture consists of 12 encoder layers where a single encoder
layer has 12 self-attention heads being the multi-head attention. The default input length is 512 tokens
including the [CLS] and [SEP] tokens. The classification token, [CLS], is the first token in the input
sequence which is commonly used as an information carrier for classification tasks. Sentence pairs are
packed together into a single sequence. Here, sentences are separated using the separator token [SEP].
The token embedding method used in BERT is referred to as WordPiece, a technique developed
by Schuster and Nakajima[71]. Additionally, segment and position embeddings are passed as input.
Segment embeddings are basically the individual sentence number encoded into a vector. Thereby, it
informs whether a particular token belongs to e.g. sentence A or B. Position embedding is the position
of a word within the input sequence, assigning each position a unique representation. Combining the
token, segment, and position embeddings result in the input embedding.
The great performance of BERT is a result of the pre-training procedure. The pre-training data
corpus is based on the BooksCorpus consisting of 800M words and English Wikipedia consisting of
2.500M words[72, 51]. BERT was proven by dominating the GLUE benchmark within language tasks
like classification, named-entity recognition, parts-of-speech, and question/answering. Generally, BERT
is suitable for sentence-level and natural language understanding tasks, as opposed to e.g. GPT which
is better suited for text generation and language translation tasks. This is among others due to the
difference in training objectives, where GPT has been trained with a causal language modeling objective.

2.2 Models in NLP
13
2.2.4
RoBERTa
RoBERTa is a replication study of the BERT model[73]. Liu et al. measures the influence of the size
of training data, training objectives, and hyperparameters. This study resulted in a model achieving
state-of-the-art scores on GLUE and other multi-task benchmarks.
The RoBERTa model is trained using only the MLM objective. However, the strategy of masking is
done differently than for BERT. The masking is dynamic during training whereas, in BERT masking
is performed only once at the data preparation also known as static masking. With MLM, the model
learns to predict internally masked text, this method allows for training in a self-supervised fashion and
the power to utilize unlabeled data. A further advantage of MLM is the ability to learn a bidirectional
representation of the text as seen in BERT as well. The NSP task was removed as the performance was
similar or slightly improved on downstream tasks without it. Again, a cross entropy loss is used as the
objective for the prediction of the masked tokens.
2.2.5
Cross Entropy Loss
Loss functions for different classes of problems can be derived using the maximum likelihood approach.
A general NN defines a parametric function f(x, β). Given a set of fixed network parameters, β, the
function maps the input values, x, to the output, y. Using the maximum likelihood approach, the

2.2.6
Fine-tuning
The following will introduce the approach of fine-tuning. The task of fine-tuning a network is to tweak
the parameters of an already trained network so it adapts to a new task. The idea of utilizing an
already trained model is based on taking advantage of what it has already learned without having to
start training from scratch. Generally, it takes a lot of time and computational power to train such a
deep network successfully, and thereby, it is beneficial to have a model already trained on very large
amounts of data for tasks with possibly thousands of classes. Fortunately, many research groups share
their models, which have trained on millions of documents (as seen with BERT published by Google AI)
for many hours on powerful GPUs or TPUs. Fine-tuning in NLP refers to the procedure of retraining
a pre-trained language model on a downstream task using a domain specific dataset. Thereby, the
weights of the original model are used as starting point and then updated to account for the task and
characteristics of the domain data.
2.2.7
Sentiment Analysis
Sentiment analysis is a classification and widely used NLP downstream task. In sentiment analysis,
there are mainly three levels of classification: document, sentence, and aspect. At the document level,
the input is a document, a basic information unit, meaning that the document only handles one topic.
The sentiment analysis objective is to classify the document as expressing e.g. positive or negative
sentiment. At the sentence level, the objective is to classify the sentiment expressed in each sentence.
Because each sentence not necessarily has an opinion encoded, the first step would be to classify if
a sentence is opinionated or not, known as subjectivity classification.
Another option is to have a
classification problem with three classes: positive, negative, or neutral[75]. It should be noted that
there is no fundamental difference between document and sentence level sentiment classification, as
sentences can just be viewed as short documents[76]. At the aspect level, the goal is to obtain the
opinions of all aspects of the entity. Here entities and their aspects need to be identified to classify each
aspect’s sentiment. One entity can have multiple sentiments. Sentiment analysis can also be used to
study emotion analysis, hate speech, sarcasm detection, and more.
2.2.8
Accuracy
Throughout this thesis, accuracy is used as the metric to train and evaluate the obtained models and
results. Working with two classes, positive and negative, there exist four different combinations of which
class an observation belongs to and how a proposed model will predict it. These are given by
• True Positives (TP): Number of observations that are in fact positive and correctly labeled as
positive by the proposed model.
• False Positives (FP): Number of observations that are in fact negative and incorrectly labeled as
positive by the proposed model.
• True Negatives (TN): Number of observations that are in fact negative and correctly labeled as
negative by the proposed model.
• False Negatives (FN): Number of observations that are in fact positive and incorrectly labeled as
negative by the proposed model.
Accuracy is a common metric that evaluates how much the classifier predicts correctly. Thus, it is given
by
Accuracy = TP + TN

2.3
Testing with Concept Activation Vectors
Getting explanations of NN models are in high demand. The main aim for explaining NNs is to utilize
them at their full potential. Explainability provides accountability, trust, compliance, and a possibility
for an increase in performance. For this reason, Kim et al. introduced the technique TCAV to be able
to explain the internal state of a NN[1].
The TCAV method tests whether a given concept is relevant for the target class. It requires data for
the proposed concept and random counterparts. The explanation is obtained on the basis of multiple
CAV testings between the concept and its numerous random counterparts. An example from the paper
is having zebra as the target class and the concepts are zigzagged, dotted, and striped. An overview of
the method is summed up in the following steps:
1. The data representing the class, concepts, and random counterexamples is provided by the user.
a) The example uses image data which are represented through embedding vectors.
2. A model, by which an explanation is desired, is provided by the user.
3. The CAV is defined by training a linear classifier to separate the inputs of the concept and the
random inputs.
4. The sensitivity of the model’s prediction is computed using the CAV. Hence, the TCAV score is
defined as the number of inputs from the target class that are positively influenced by the concept.
The original figure from “Interpretability Beyond Feature Attribution: Quantitative Testing with Con-
cept Activation Vectors (TCAV)” explaining the method can be seen in Appendix A, Figure A.1. The
theory of the above-mentioned steps is given in the following sections.
2.3.1
Concept Activation Vectors
Prior to introducing the CAVs, the idea of a concept is given: A concept needs to be human-interpretable
and defined by a set of examples that represent the concept, and have a random counterpart. In NLP,
a concept could be a set of documents representing the concept of a woman in some way or another.
Furthermore, a set of random counterparts is a set of documents that are randomly chosen to represent
’everything’ else, i.e. all other concepts.
In the following, a NN model has x ∈Rn as input and a feed-forward layer l with m neurons. The
function fl is introduced, it takes x as input and returns the output of the activation function from
layer l (such that fl : Rn →Rm). The layer of the NN is flattened so width, height, and channels
become a vector of m = w × h × c activations.
Given a set of documents IC representing the concept C and a set of random documents IR rep-
resenting the random counterexamples R. The activation output fl(x) of layer l, is used to train a
binary linear classifier to distinguish between the activations of the two sets: {fl(x) : x ∈IC} and
{fl(x) : x ∈IR}. Thus, the classifier is used to obtain the vector orthogonal to the decision boundary
separating the two sets known as the CAV, vl

3.1
Terminology Clarification
The following will establish common ground in defining what the terms explainability and interpretabil-
ity in regard to machine learning stand for, and how the terms will be used throughout this thesis. It is
done to not mix up the two terms which are often used interchangeably throughout the literature and
research.
3.1.1
Interpretability
Interpretability in the context of ML and as we will use it throughout this thesis; defines the ability
to provide cause and effect from a given model in understandable terms in the context of a human.
The interpretability comes from the design of the given model and can be described as relating to the
passive characteristics[9].
3.1.2
Explainability
A common point of understanding is established for the term explainability. In the context of ML, ex-
plainability is the knowledge of the representation and importance of a node to the model’s performance.
Explainability is the active characteristic of a given model, with the intent of clarifying the internal
functions of the model. Explainability is related to what could be called post-hoc explainability, where
any given model has the possibility to be explained, contrary to interpretability which comes solely
from the design of the model. The mentioned definition is in the context of the audiences who need to
grasp the explanation, and the definition of the concept Explainable AI from will be used:
Given an audience, an explainable Artificial Intelligence is one that produces details or
reasons to make its functioning clear or easy to understand [9].

The first step in a typical NLP workflow is to tokenize the desired dataset. Meaning to convert a line

a word will be encoded in two different ways if it is at the beginning of an input sequence or not. In
our setting a space is represented with ˙G. The input is complete sentences where the start and end

(classifier) token. Besides the beginning and end tokens, the vocabulary also includes the special tokens;

vocabulary, <pad> is used for padding to obtain the desired length of each input sequence. <mask> is not
utilized as it is only used in pre-training when training the model for MLM task. The final vocabulary
size of the tokenizer used for the RoBERTa model is 50 265.

3.2.3
Pre-trained Model
The utilized RoBERTa model is trained on a union of five datasets; the BookCorpus[72], a dataset
on unpublished books, the English Wikipedia[92], CC-News[93], a dataset of English news articles,
OpenWebText[67], and Stories[94]. This union of datasets adds to a total of 160GB. In comparison,
BERT was only trained on data with a total of 16GB.
The information on the training can be found in the associated model card provided by HuggingFace.
Hereby, implementation essential information is provided with the aim of being able to discover, repro-
duce, and share models in a transparent way. The model card contains information about intended use
and limitations, along with biases and ethical reflections as described by Mitchell et al. proposing the
framework of model cards[95]. The cards also feature training parameters, experimental information,
datasets used for training, and evaluation results. The training of the RoBERTa-base model was on
1024 V100 GPUs for 500 000 steps with a batch size of 8 000 and a sequence length of 512. The optimizer
used was an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 6e−4, β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.98, and ϵ = 1e−6, a weight
decay of 0.01, warm-up for 24 000 steps followed by linear decay of the learning rate[89].
3.2.3.1
Configuration
The following is the configuration of the RoBERTa model provided by Transformers and utilized in this
thesis (V.4.23.1)[96]. The configuration is based on BERT implemented by HuggingFace[97, 98].
• Hidden size of 768
• Number of hidden layers is 12
• Number of attention heads is 12
• Intermediate size is 3072
• Hidden activation is Gaussian error linear units (GELU)
• Hidden dropout probability is set at 0.1
• Attention probability dropout ratio at 0.1
• Maximum sequence length is 512
• Epsilon used by the normalization layers is 1e−12
Here, hidden size denotes the dimension of the encoder layers. The number of hidden layers is the
number of layers in the Transformer encoder and the number of attention heads is for each attention
layer in the Transformer encoder. The intermediate size is the dimension of the feed-forward layer in
the Transformer encoder.

3.3.2
Generating concepts using KG
In the last example, we use ”KG to generate concepts” as seen in Figure 3.2.
The idea is to use
Wikipedia articles and their internal relations to create a KG that defines relevant concepts and their
corresponding data. Wikipedia is one of the largest repositories of human knowledge in the world, it is
free, online, and an obvious resource to take advantage of. Wikipedia articles and their relations are
given in lists outlining their relations. A Wikipedia list resembles a KG by which an article refers to an
entity by hyperlinks and the listing refers to the semantic relationships, i.e. edges between the entities.
Throughout this thesis, Wikipedia lists will be referred to as KGs. Using the already preprocessed
Wikipedia dump (introduced in section 3.3.1) and a list of related articles, it is possible to explore and
collect sub-concepts all related to a main concept of interest. Besides having retrieved concepts and
their corresponding (labeled) data, the approach also enables a user to represent a main concept in a
relevant and objective manner. Thereby, the approach is also used to expand the horizon by increasing
the range of knowledge and understanding of what a main concept consists of.
As an example we will consider the main concept sports: A non-expert user might not know every
branch of sport and thereby, not know the whole extent of the proposed concept. Hereby, the use of
the Wikipedia list can be considered as an objective and not least informative way to generate concepts.
The approach is given as follows: Find a list of related articles in Wikipedia. Following the example, the
sports concept is represented using: ”List of sports”2. The first instance of a branch of sports is acrobatic
sports, by which sports like ballet and breakdance are sub-categories. To represent acrobatic sports as a
concept, the user should look for the corresponding article: Acrobatic Sports, to obtain representative
data. If such an article exists, the user must ensure that the article contains enough data to represent
the concept. If that is not the case, or Wikipedia does not contain the corresponding article, the user
must search for all articles in the list being sub-categories to acrobatic sports and concatenate these
into a concept, acrobatic sports. To ease the explanation of how to use KGs, a sketch of ”List of sports”
is provided in Figure 3.7. Here, different levels are introduced to indicate the sub-categories of a KG.

Our implementation of the TCAV method within a NLP setting can be found in our GitHub repository
along with the requirements for running the code3. As the datasets commonly contain more samples
than required, the desired number of samples from each dataset is randomly chosen. The desired num-
3https://github.com/gaunby/master_project

ber of samples for the concept and the random counterpart is at least 100. For the target class, at least
300 samples are desired when computing the TCAV score (Equation 2.17). A sample is a sentence from
the applicable dataset. For reproducibility, the seed has been fixed to initialize the random number
generator for sampling the datasets.
The CAV, between a given concept and random counterpart, is computed using scikit-learn’s linear
classifier, SVM, with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training with regularization, α = 0.01, and
maximum number iterations at 5 000[88]. The input data is split into 33% test and 67% train data.
The accuracy of each classifier is also computed. To be able to get the representation for the concept
and random counterpart in the desired model layer, fl(x), the function register_forward_hook from
PyTorch has been utilized[110]. The gradient of hl,k(fl(x)) as denoted in Equation 2.16 is computed
using the function register_full_backward_hook from PyTorch[111]. It is desired to obtain an expla-
nation for each of the 12 encoder layers in the fine-tuned RoBERTa model. For each of the encoders,
the hidden state of the last layer is chosen as a representation. This is a dropout layer placed in the
block denoted Add & Norm in Figure 2.3. As this layer is only utilized during training, we in practice
get the output from the previous layer, a normalization layer.
The sensitivity and TCAV score are computed as described in Equation 2.16 and Equation 2.17, respec-
tively. The testing is run 500 times for the concept each time with a different random counterpart. It
is also run 500 times with different sets of random data, one acting as a concept and one as a random
counterpart. Here, the set of random data is randomly chosen for each run. The 500 tests are conducted
to obtain enough data points to perform a t-test with Bonferroni correction. Working with a significance
level at α = 0.05 and a Bonferroni correction being the number of layers tested (12 encoders) times the
number of different concepts tested.
3.4.1
Evaluating Multiple Results in KG setting
As the KG setup initiates the possibility of creating several concepts based on the desired abstraction
level (Figure 3.7), the evaluation should be adapted to only consider the most relevant concepts in a
manageable way for the user. The resulting TCAV scores are pro tempore evaluated qualitatively using
visual tools such as histograms and bar charts (as seen in section 2.3). Therefore, it is of interest to
present an ideal way to evaluate the TCAV results when having multiple concepts.
Working with data at a deeper level of abstraction, a wide range of concepts will be tested.
It is
too much to comprehend all at once, hence the analysis is restrained to only consider and evaluate the
five most relevant concepts. Here, ’most relevant concepts’ refers specifically to the five concepts having
the highest mean of TCAV score across all layers. This approach of using the mean is chosen as the
appropriate screening method, as we want the overall most describing concepts. By using this approach
it is avoided to choose a concept as the most relevant on the basis of a single high TCAV score.
Proceeding, we end up having five concepts being the overall most descriptive for the target class.
It should be noted that ’overall most descriptive’ is in the context of the tested concepts. However, the
actual means of TCAV scores across all layers might be considered before starting the analysis of the
most descriptive concepts. If the means of the TCAV scores for the top five concepts are high, they are
generally found descriptive for the target class. On the other hand, if the means of the TCAV scores
are closer to 0.5, the results are not so evident after all. In this case, it is necessary to take a closer
look into the TCAV scores of the single layers. At last, if the mean of the TCAV scores is below 0.5,
the considered concept is overall counter-descriptive for the target class. This being said it is always
encouraged to examine the TCAV scores at the individual layers.

4.2
Introduction to TCAV results
An effort is spent on implementing and experimenting with the proposed method to gain insights into
the sentiment classifier using the TCAV method on different concepts. A TCAV result for a given layer
can unfold in three different explainability scenarios: A significant concept with a high TCAV score
denotes the concept being evidence in favor of the target class, whereas a low, but still significant, score
results in the concept being a counterexample of the target class. Lastly, there is the case of having an
insignificant TCAV score as a non-determined concept. To avoid misconception between significant and
non-significant layers in the visualization of the TCAV results, the indication for a non-significant layer
is a cross, ’×’, placed in the absence of the TCAV score at 0.5. As proposed in section 2.3, distribution
plots of the TCAV scores were found very helpful as support in understanding and examining the
behavior of the concepts across layers in more detail. This approach is likewise utilized in the following
analysis. The experiments were run on the high-performance computing (HPC) cluster at DTU, and
the model used is the fine-tuned RoBERTa model with the simplified classification head.

4.6
Discussion
A discussion of the results is given. Here, we will go through considerations regarding the data and
model used throughout the thesis. Further investigation of the TCAV components is conducted. This
results in new findings pointing out the limitations of the proposed method. Additionally, trends in the
TCAV scores and the average classification accuracy across concepts are considered.
4.6.1
Considerations of Data Inputs
The data used for training the downstream task of sentiment classification consists of sentences. Each
contains a review of a movie including a label determining the sentiment. As seen in Table 3.2, the
applied sentences were consisting of tokens in a range of 4 to 67. Thus, it might be beneficial to use
documents instead of sentences as input for fine-tuning the RoBERTa model, to fully utilize its capabil-
ities. Documents are data consisting of several sentences. The used tokenizer is able to handle inputs
with a maximum of 512 tokens before truncating the input, which we currently do not take advantage of.
Based on the idea of having a fine-tuned model trained on documents instead of sentences, it is likewise
essential to discuss the approach of creating concepts using the KGs. Here, it should be considered that
a single sentence might not be representative of the given concept. That is, extracting a sentence from
a Wikipedia article might not always contain facts related to the concept of interest. Consequently, it
is of interest to use documents to represent a concept instead of only a single sentence. The same ap-
proach of searching for different articles could be used, however instead of retrieving only one sentence,
more data should be requested to utilize the tokenizer’s full potential. Unfortunately, this suggestion is
challenged by the limitation of data in Wikipedia articles. This approach might result in having fewer
concepts to test using the TCAV method, however, it might be possible to extract data related to the
concept (using data at Level II or III as proposed in section 3.3.2).

Taking a closer look at the tokenizer example in Table 3.1, it is interesting how the word ’Been’ is not
represented as a single token. This might be due to the RobertaTokenizer being trained on books and
Wikipedia data, where the syntactic structure is different compared to spoken and social media lingo.
Conversational English and social media often use ellipsis, which is the omission of one or more words.
The sentence is still understood when using ellipsis, but the left-out words must be supplied to make
the construction grammatically complete. This difference in how the language is used might have an
effect on the model. One might consider adapting the tokenizer to be more domain-specific and thus,
better at recognizing commonly used words in the specific domain. Said difference in data might also
have an effect when computing the classification boundary used to create the CAVs. Here, the data
used to train the model, both for the pre-training (books and Wikipedia) and for the fine-tuning (movie
reviews), can differ slightly from the data retrieved to create concepts from e.g. Twitter data.
4.6.2
Suggesting a Simplified Model
From the t-SNE visualizations in Figure 4.3 and C.4, the behavior of linear separation in the t-SNE
domain as early as in the 6’th layer, arises the question: Is the chosen RoBERTa model too complex,
and uses unnecessarily expenses on computation during training? This is in regard to the downstream
task, which might be too simple to solve for the capabilities of the model. Thus, one might want to test
a distilled version of the RoBERTa model. A distilled model is a faster, smaller, and cheaper alternative
that mimics the original network in some cases with lower performance than the original model. It is
an option if its performance is similar to the original model.
4.6.3
Bi-modal Distribution
Looking at the distribution of random TCAV scores in the last encoder layers for the RoBERTa model
in Figure 4.7 and 4.9, a bi-modal distribution is seen. The modes are located at zero and one, meaning
that a random CAV produces exclusively positive or negative sensitivities. This trend is seen in both
the random tweet dataset and the random Wikipedia dataset. The bi-modal distribution is also noticed
in the distribution plots of the random TCAV scores in the later layers in the GoogLeNet model in
Figure 2.6. This bi-modal distribution seems to be a general tendency across networks in the later
layers close to the classifier module. As formerly stated, this bi-modal distribution of TCAV scores is a
concerning behavior when the testing is based upon an assumption about normally distributed scores.
Wanting to understand what might cause the bi-modal behavior for the TCAV scores in the last layer
of the model, it is desired to examine the components used to compute a TCAV score (Equation 2.17).
As the number of samples in the target class set, Ik, is fixed throughout the layers, the sensitivity is the
only component of interest (Equation 2.16). The sensitivity for layer l and input xi for i ∈Ik consists
of the gradient, ∇hl,k(fl(xi)), and the CAV for a defined or a random concept. For convenience, the
gradient is here denoted gi. Both the CAV and the gradient are vectors of size 768. To visualize mul-
tidimensional data, PCA has been chosen as it is useful for increasing interpretability while preserving
information[112].
Using PCA, the CAVs for the concept woman and its corresponding random concept, along with the
gradients for the positive and negative sentiment class are visualized in Figure 4.11. Each vector has
been normalized to a unit vector before computing the PCA. In the figure, it can be seen how the
gradients of the two classes individually cluster more and more. In the last layer, it is seen how the
gradients for each class become almost a single dot. This indicates that there is a very small variance
between the gradients in each class. Furthermore, it indicates that the gradients are close to constant
in the last layer for each class. This means that these gradients do not differ with changes in input,
i.e. two different positive sentiment inputs will result in the same gradient for the last layer. In other
words, resulting in a constant gradient across all inputs for a target class. Formally,

If a concept is well-defined, the result is an approximately constant CAV. In layers with a constant
gradient, this will lead to all statistics being either approximately 0 or 1, shown as a uni-modal dis-
tribution. On the other hand, if the concept is poorly defined or just random, the CAVs will not be
constant across runs and the TCAV scores will be either 0 or 1 with a 50-50 probability. In the later
layers where the gradient is constant, this shows as a bi-modal distribution.
Having a well-defined concept in a layer where the gradient is constant, one should be careful about
interpreting the resulting TCAV score. This is because the TCAV score does not report anything about
the magnitude of the angle between the concept and the target class. The angle could be negligible
small, but as long as the CAV stays constant across runs, the result would be a final (t-tested) TCAV
score of either 0 or 1. Thus, in layers where the gradient is constant, the t-tested TCAV score only
indicates whether the concept is well-defined or not, and the score itself is not useful for discussing the
relevancy of the concept.

Average Classification Accuracy Trends
Further investigation of the before-mentioned trends is performed to obtain additional insights into
behaviors across concepts. We will have a closer look at the performance of the classification boundary,
separating the concept from the random counterexamples in each layer. This classifier is used to pro-
duce the CAV (being normal to said hyperplane) used to compute the sensitivity. This is done as we
assume that it requires a fairly high classification accuracy to have a meaningful CAV.
This investigation is again performed across all tested concepts, having a total of 56 concepts. Part
of the proposed TCAV method contains performing several classification tasks separating a concept
from different random counterexamples, resulting in 500 tests and thus, resulting in 500 accuracy mea-
surements for each concept. To look into the performance of these classifiers, the average of these 500
accuracy measures is computed for each of our 56 concepts. The distribution of average accuracy for
each concept in each layer is visualized using box plots in Figure 4.13. Thus, every observation seen in
the plot corresponds to the average classification accuracy across 500 tests for one concept at the given
encoder layer. Trends across the encoder layers are noticed as the average accuracy for all concepts
increased from layers 2-4, where it is very clear that for most concepts the classifier is performing well.
This increase is followed by a decrease in layers 6 and 7 by which the average accuracy increases to-
wards the last encoder layer. This increase in accuracy is found odd as the classifiers are not expected
to separate the concept and random data in these last layers to this extent as these layers are trained to
distinguish between sentiments. Additionally, low average accuracy measures for certain concepts are
observed, yet following the same trend as described. From reported numbers in Appendix D, Table D.1
it is found that these are tweet observations. It can be seen that the classifier performs better in the
later layers than layers 4 and 5. This indicates that the tweet concepts are easier to classify closer to
the sentiment classification layer.
Using the t-SNE visualizations of the embeddings in the hidden layers seen in Figure 3.5 and 3.6,
it was noticed how the woman concept was generally more clustered compared to the hate concept.
Looking into the embeddings of the woman concept for layers 3-5, it is seen how the concept seems
linearly separable from the random data in this domain. The average classification accuracy in these
layers is likewise found relatively high (0.73 for layer 3 and above 0.9 for layers 4 and 5 reported in
Appendix E, Table E.1). On the other hand, hate is harder to separate from the random data in the
corresponding layers (Figure 3.5). This reflects in the average classification accuracy being much lower
compared to woman (ranging from 0.55-0.64 reported in Appendix D, Table D.1). This reinforces the

4.6.6
Results in KG
Having that the KG setup for selecting and testing relevant concepts in the NLP setting is our own
invention, multiple approaches could have been explored. In this thesis, the most relevant concepts are
selected using the average TCAV scores. This has been done with the aim of getting the concepts that
overall are most positively correlated with the target class. It could be argued that some layers are of
more interest than others, and the KG TCAV analysis should only consider layers for which the TCAV
method is valid. Furthermore, the analysis could have been altered to only consider single layers, e.g.
selecting the top most relevant concepts for each layer. It is also questioned if five is the most suitable
number of concepts to further investigate, or if the number should vary depending on the investigated
concepts.

CHAPTER 5
Future work
New questions have arisen throughout this thesis. Utilizing the TCAV method to get concept-based
explainability of a given NN has given rise to investigate deeper into the understanding of the results.
The deeper we got into the analysis, the more questions and ideas for further investigations we had.
Ideas and areas we think require further attention have been suggested in the following.
5.1
Sentiment Classification
In this thesis, the RoBERTa model is fine-tuned for the downstream task of sentiment classification using
a simple linear classification head on top. To grasp if this model is too complex and computationally
heavy for the downstream task, it is suggested to train a linear classifier on each of the encoder layers.
This is to train a classifier to distinguish between the activations produced by the two sentiments for
each encoder output.
The classification accuracy for each encoder would give an indication of the
obtained ’knowledge’ of sentiment throughout the network, and if the sentiment classification could be
performed with e.g. fewer encoders. Furthermore, it would inform about whether a distilled version
of the RoBERTa model might be suﬀicient to classify sentiment or not. A distilled model is a faster,
smaller, and cheaper alternative that mimics the original network, but in some cases, it results in lower
performance than the original model.
5.2
TCAV method
The discovery of having distinct uni- and bi-modal distributions of TCAV scores in some layers give
rise to exploring new testing methods. This is suggested as the proposed t-test is not appropriate for
testing between concepts and random concept TCAV scores in these layers. Thus, future work should
strive to identify a more suitable testing method.
It is likewise interesting to dive deeper into defining if a concept is relevant to the target class. As
discussed previously, having a well-defined concept in the later layers does not denote anything about
having a relevant concept. Hence, the relevance of a concept could be further investigated by consider-
ing the magnitude of the angle between the gradient and the CAV when computing the TCAV score.
In this way, the later layers might be more interoperable.
Considering the approach of producing random concept CAVs within the TCAV implementation, it is
noticed how a linear classifier is trained on two random subsets with the aim of producing a random
vector, hence obtaining a random CAV. This procedure seems unnecessary as a random vector could
easily be obtained without the need for training a classifier. Therefore it is suggested to implement the
TCAV method where the random CAVs are computed using randomly generated vectors in the size of
the desired hidden layer. This approach will save computational costs and time.
==PAGE:65==
5.3 Evaluating Multiple TCAV Results
55
5.3
Evaluating Multiple TCAV Results
Using the KG approach to retrieve several concepts, one way to find the ’most relevant concepts’ to
the target class was suggested. Further investigation of how to navigate in having lots of concepts and
results, should be considered. It could be argued that having a few zero or one TCAV scores (from the
last layers), would gain too much impact on the average TCAV score and thereby distort the conclusion.
Thus, it would make sense to make further exploration of the ideal approach to capture and convey
multiple TCAV results at once.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis has considered the concept-based explainability method TCAV in a NLP setting.
The
TCAV method yields an explanation of the internal states in terms of human-friendly concepts. To
address issues in finding all contextually relevant concepts used to quantify the explanation of the NNs
prediction, the thesis presents a KG based setup to generate concepts of interest.
In the attempt to reproduce one of the originally published TCAV results for image classification,
challenges occurred and laid the basis for investigating the behavior of the TCAV scores across the
internal states. From this it is suggested, when applying the TCAV method it should include an exam-
ination of the scores to ensure fulfillment of the assumptions for the testing method.
The pre-trained RoBERTa model has been utilized on the downstream task of sentiment classification,
with the aim of using the TCAV method to obtain linear explanations of the classifications. To do so,
directional derivatives are used to quantify the model’s sensitivity and together with a CAV obtain a
TCAV score. Multiple tests are performed by training multiple CAVs to obtain several TCAV scores
with the assumption that the scores are normally distributed upon which statistical tests are performed
to obtain a final explanatory TCAV score. The score indicates the relative importance of a concept in
relation to the target class.
The sentiment classification model has been tested on several concepts.
It is shown that huge
amounts of labeled text data ease the task of obtaining concepts in the text domain. Furthermore, our
experiments have shown that the classification of various concepts in internal states of the deep learning
model is also applicable to text data. Testing reveals that the concept of gender is evidence in favor
of the negative sentiment target class. Thus, the classification of negative sentiment has gender as an
important factor. Furthermore, the concept of man was found neutral in regard to both the positive
and negative target classes. Overall, experiments of the sports concept were shown to be evidence for
the positive sentiment classification rather than for the negative sentiment class.
Investigating the TCAV explanation and its directional derivative, it was found that the final layers
of the RoBERTa backbone just before the linear classifier were nearly linear. From this, TCAV scores
in these last layers were resulting in bi-modal or distinct uni-model behaviors, i.e. not normally dis-
tributed as assumed for the statistical test. This is a concern for the usability of the TCAV method as
the testing is based on a t-test which is no longer valid for these layers. This entails that the TCAV
method is not applicable to hidden states of layers showing linear tendencies.
The approach of using Wikipedia KGs to generate contextually relevant concepts as input to the TCAV
method was proven to be a suitable method. The KG setup allows retrieving annotated data and enables
investigating several concepts within the same topic. Wikipedia is utilized as a knowledge graph due to
its vast size and since its articles are related by hyperlinks, thus creating a semantic graph. Using the
KG setup it is possible to define relevant concepts as well as their model representations, i.e. vectors. It
has shown to be very beneficial as Wikipedia contains data for even niche concepts. It is possible that
a concept does not include a suﬀicient amount of data, i.e. its main article is very short. Such concepts
are instead defined in terms of the articles of related sub-concepts. The KG setup can be viewed as
a knowledge portal, suitable for retrieving concepts to test against the target classes using the TCAV
method.
