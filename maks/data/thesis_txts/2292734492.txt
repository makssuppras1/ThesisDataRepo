Summary  
This is a research thesis in master level of 30 ECTS which comprises an 
elaborated package of well drilling processes, focusing only on the important 
concepts, e.g. drilling bits, casing, stability, and well controlling etc. The thesis is 
named ’’important concepts within oil well drilling procedures’’ divided into 
two parts. The first part is a literature research including some numerical 
estimation (exercises), while the second part is a purified numerical part in 
which a specific case study has been analyzed. 
The principle aim of this thesis is to provide students with knowledge regarding 
oil well drilling procedure as a course reading material and might also be 
considered as a reference for further studies.  
The numerical problems in both parts such as adjusting the drilling window 
regard to the formation, mud pressure and fraction pressure will be analyzed 
and visualized using suitable software in a proper way. Moreover, other 
requirements such as the input data of executing this work will be provided and 
additionally the words written in cursive letters are described either directly 
afterwards or on the glossary appendix.

1. Rotary Drilling  
The objective of this concept is aimed to introduce the major modern life rotary 
drilling 
components, 
rotary 
drilling 
process 
and 
the 
basic 
fundamentals 
operation procedures. The rotary drilling is considered a modern life standard 
oil well drilling method that essentially based on a motion of a rotating drilling 
bit digging down through the earth’s crust. The advantage of spinning bit is to 
crush and penetrate even the hardest rock type, subsequently the modern well 
drilling is being much deeper than before.  
The force needed to deepen a well is provided by the weight of pipes placed 
over the drilling bit, while the rotation is given at the surface by rotary 
equipment which rotates the drill string, which in turn transmits the rotation 
energy to the bit. When a well is being drilled deeper by the action of the 
rotating drilling bit on the rock formation, new pipes are required to be added 
to the drill string. The small crushed pieces of rock, ‘’cuttings’’ resulting from 
that action is transported to the surface by mud which refers to the fluid that 
circulated constantly from the surface into the drill sting down to the bottom to 
be returned back carrying cuttings through the annular space formed between 
drill string and the well wall. Once reaching the rig surface, the cuttings are 
removed and in this way the mud is treated to be reused.  
The mechanisms of performing bit rotation, pumping mud, hoisting and drilling 
directional controlling, separating and treating cuttings and all other operations 
are performed at the drilling rig.

1.2.  Drilling Team, Language and Units  
There are four types of oil and gas wells based on objectives in two different 
environments, exploration well (wildcat), appraisal, development and injection 
well in either onshore or offshore environment. Wildcat wells are the very first 
ones drilled to discover oil and gas in an unexplored area, usually using a big 
diameter bit for lithology crushing in big sizes to help geologists determine the 
underground formations. Prior that both seismic and geological study must be 
carried out in order to determine the best location for the wildcat operation. 
This study is performed by a company’s geological team, while the reservoir 
team is responsible for locating the development wells. The drilling team is 
responsible 
for 
planning 
and 
execution 
of 
the 
operation 
including 
cost 
estimation and contingency plans. In other words, to perform a drilling activity 
is not an easy task and it may involve many specialists, number of companies, 
engineers, expertise and technicians. The drilling operation is depending on 
several crucial factors, e.g. the type of well being drilled, its purpose, location, 
complexity of the operation, local and Oil Company’s regulation that owns the 
exploration right in the area and its health, safety and environment (HSE) policy 
is of major importance. It is not uncommon that two or more companies 
forming a consortium for the venture. Normally in that case is one company 
which is the operation partner, whilst the other company is the financial 
partner. Nevertheless which one is an operational decisive is usually referred to 
the joint operation agreements (JOA). The drilling operation itself will be 
carried out by a drilling contractor that may be hired specifically for a certain 
well to perform the operation according to well program using the equipments 
and procedures specified in the contract. In order to ensure the operational 
standards being followed by the drilling contractor, the operation team has at 
least one representative, usually a drilling engineer who has responsibility of 
ensuring correct operational performances.  
During well drilling various operations will be performed depending on the type 
of well and its complexity such as drilling fluid, drilling bits, directional drilling, 
casing and cementing operation etc. Therefore the operator needs to involve a

1.3.  Drilling Rigs 
Drilling rig in general is a structure where all necessary executions of an oil well 
drilling operation required. It is mainly varied in size, drilling capacity and 
environment in which it can operate. Nevertheless the basic drilling rotary 
process is the same for all types of rigs. Rigs can stratify into two main 
categories, land and marine rigs Figure 1. Land rigs are essentially classified to 
mobile, conventional and helitransportable rigs that can be transported when 
disassembly by helicopter to be operated in non road infrastructure and on 
jungle area. Mobile rigs seem easy to be dismantled and moved from one 
location to another, while conventional rigs are less mobile and also required a 
standard derrick build in the location before the rig arriving. Mobile rigs in turn 
classifies into portable mast (derrick) and cantilever derrick. The portable mast 
is raised and lowered as a whole and it is less time consuming to assemble in 
comparison 
with 
conventional 
rigs, 
while 
cantilever 
derrick 
is 
assembled 
horizontally on the ground and raised vertically by rig hoisting system. 
Portability and maximum operation depth are two important factors when 
choosing the type of land rigs. Operation depth is related to derrick loading 
capacity which is varied between less than 3,000 ft up to 30,000 ft. Each rig has 
specifications which can be properly prepared and compared between well 
contactors and information to select the right rig before drilling operation 
starts.

Marine rigs are offshore drilling rigs which considered nowadays is an 
important segment of oil industry which has been undergoing significantly 
technology development during modern times. The portability and maximum 
water depth are also main criteria of selecting the right rig before drilling start. 
There are some offshore rigs operate in very shallow water, whilst there are 
some operating in very deep water. Marine rigs can be bottom supported or 
floating vessels. Submersible and barge rigs are basically bottom-supported rigs 
that contain vessel compartments which can be flooded with water to be sunk 
to desired location. Both considered operating in a very shallow water depth, 
e.g. submersible operates at depth of 8 to 20 ft, while barge cannot be used 
when wave height exceeds 5 ft. At the end of the drilling operation, water can 
be pumped out from the compartments allowing rig to float again to the 
surface to be moved o another location.  
Platform are considered to be bottom-supported rig where mainly used to 
extract economically precious reserves in the reservoir by drilling a number of 
wells, perhaps 100 wells directionally from the platform to the reservoir. Wells 
can be drilled from platforms using either self-containing or tender assisting 
rigs. The approach is very expensive, however, if the platform is sufficiently 
large, drilling equipments and personnel can stay on the rig otherwise, a 
floating tender is in charge instead. Water depth is the main criterion for 
installation of such large structure because in very deep water area would be

1.4.1. 
 Rig Power System 
Drilling rigs are usually demanding high power supply, in which can be 
continuous or intermittent power. Most rig power is consumed by hoisting, 
==PAGE:15==
12 
 
fluid circulating system. The total power requirements for most deep water rigs 
are between 1,000 to 3,000 hp (750-2,200 kW/day), while for shallow water 
required as little as 500 hp. This kind of power consists of a prime mover that 
transmits the power to the end-use equipment. The current drilling industry is 
using diesel engines as prime movers sources which can generate power from 
500 hp to 8,000 hp that can be transmitted by one of the following systems.  
 Mechanical drive 
 Direct-current (DC) generator and motors 
 Alternating-current (AC) generator 
 Silicon-controlled rectifier (SCR) 
The reason why using diesel fuel instead of gasoline is because of diesel 
generates more power, runs safer, it is cheaper and easier to store. In modern 
rigs AC-SCR transmit system are common. The SCR system is used to covert AC 
power to DC, however, the diesel-electric system is used so that the main rig 
engines are set to generate electricity power. The mechanical drive transmit 
system is used to transmit the power from rig engines to the operating tools, 
e.g. mud pumps, drawworks and the rotary system. Normally the drive system 
consists of gears, chains, belts and shafts etc. Occasionally, in big rigs and only 
for safety reason of avoiding kicks and well blowout, backup generators are 
installed on a rig in case of the main power system fails to work properly [4, 5]. 
1.4.2. 
Rig Hoisting System  
The hoisting system is one of the most important rig equipments which consist 
of tools responsible for lowering and raising drill strings, casing, cement and 
other equipment into or out of the well, i.e. it provides a vertical movement for 
example, pipes in the borehole. Hoisting system is also providing the required 
weight which has to be adjusted on the drilling bit. The most recognizable 
routines made by hoisting system are called making a connection and making a 
trip. Making a connection principally refers to the process of adding a new 
section of drill pipe into the well, while making a trip refers to the process of 
removing the drill strings out from the well for appropriate bottom assembly 
changing such as changing a dull or tearing bit and then lowering the drill string 
==PAGE:16==
13 
 
back again into the well. In that case the process of pulling out the drill strings 
called (trip out) and when it is lowering down again called (trip in). The main 
items in the hoisting system are classified as following Figure 2 [4, 5]. 
 Drawworks 
 Block and tackle 
 Derrick and substructure 
 Ancillary equipments such as hooks, elevators and bails 
 
Figure 2: presents the main items of the hoisting system. [6] 
1.4.2.1. Drawworks 
The drawworks is the equipment that exploits energy from the power system to 
provide a force to the drill line, i.e. it reels in the drill line on the drum to lift the 
drill pipe, whereas it allows the drill line to be spooled out as the pipe is 
lowered into the well Figure 3. The drawworks must have an efficient braking 
and cooling systems. Brake system is used to control the heavy pipe loads, 
while cooling system is used to dissipate friction heat generated during braking. 
The hydrodynamic and electromagnetic brake systems are commonly used on 
the most of the rotary drilling rigs.  
==PAGE:17==
14 
 
 
Figure 3: presents the drawworks and the block and tackle arrangements. [6] 
1.4.2.2. Block and Tackle 
The block and tackle is primary a link between the drawworks and the load that 
will be lowered into or raised out from the wellbore. Generally it consists of the 
crown block, the traveling block and the drilling line Figure 3. The main purpose 
of the block and tackle arrangements is to provide a mechanical advantage 
which in turn makes the handling of large loads easier. The crown block consists 
of multiple pulleys called sheaves for which increasing the pull capacity of the 
hock. The crown block is a stationary and always located on the top of the 
derrick. The traveling block is free to move but it also consists of sheaves 
applicable with the drilling line, hooks, bails and elevators attached to the 
bottom for latching the pipe. Both blocks have a certain number of sheaves 
varied between 4 to 12 depending on the maximum load and the depth of the 
well. The drilling line connects to the drawworks and the deadline anchor so it 
pulled through the crown and traveling block sheaves so the traveling block can 
be raised or lowered if necessary [4]. 
1.4.2.3. Derrick and Substructure 
The derrick and substructure plays an important role in drilling operation due to 
the derrick providing the height necessary for hoisting system to raise and 
lower the drill pipes. It also supports a thousand of feet of drill stem below it. 
The greater height of the derrick, the longer of the pipe section can be handled 
and the faster the drill string can be inserted or removed from the hole. 
==PAGE:18==
15 
 
The substructure provides the necessary height required for the blowout 
preventer stack in the wellhead below the rig floor. 
1.4.2.4. Ancillary Equipments 
Ancillary equipments are additional tools attached to the hoisting system with 
the overall function to make the mechanism of the pipe raising and lowering 
easier. Hooks, elevators, bails, slips, tongs and deadline anchor as are used. The 
Hook is a huge device placed under the traveling block from which the elevator 
bails or swivel is suspended. It is designed to carry a load between 100 to 650 
tons when lifting or lowering into the wellbore. Elevators are gripping tools 
from which the drill string is lifted from the drilled well, while slips are attached 
to hang the drill pipe on the rig floor when it is not attached to the hook and 
traveling block. The tongs are usually two devices which used to un-screw or 
screw pipes together. One tong is used to screw the pipes and another backup 
tong used to keep the rest or the string from turning. Bails are mainly two 
cylindrical shaped from which supporting the elevators during the hoisting 
operation. The deadline anchor is a device to which the deadline is attached to 
the derrick substructure and sometimes called also as deadline tie down 
anchor. Supply reel provides the system with sufficient rope or line, i.e. stores 
the line in which being pulled and spooled around a disk on the dead anchor 
then being lifted to the crown block and spooled between the crown block and 
traveling block sheaves several times. Ones the numbers of sheaves are 
achieved the line being run at the drawwork and clamped on both the 
drawworks drum and on a dead anchor [4].  
1.4.3. 
Circulating System 
The fluid circulating system is a process providing hydraulic power to the 
drilling fluid so that it pumped down through the drill string, travelling all the 
way to the bottom of the borehole passing through the bit orifices and then 
return to the surface through the annulus, carrying the cuttings resulted from 
the action of the bit against the rock. The main components of the rig 
circulating system are as follows Figure 4 [4].

1.4.3.1. Circulating Fluid 
The drilling circulating fluid is usually referred to as the mud, which can be 
water based or oil based with additives, e.g. gel to increase mud viscosity. The 
drilling fluid has different functions during circulation such as lubricating and 
cooling down the rotary blades, lubricating the drill bit, cleaning the rock 
surface around the drill bit, returning back the cutting to the rig surface through 
the annulus, reducing friction between the outside of the drill string walls and 
the side of the borehole, preventing well caving and exerting back the 
hydrostatic pressure. One critical function of the drilling fluid is to curb so called 
kicks. A kick happens when the hydrostatic pressure of the drilling fluid is low to 
overcome the formation pressure and subsequently can lead to a well blowout. 
Therefore the hydrostatic pressure of the drilling fluid normally has to be kept 
slightly greater than the pressure in the formation. By adjusting pressure two 
phenomena might be generated, the overbalanced drilling and underbalanced 
==PAGE:20==
17 
 
drilling. Overbalanced drilling is when the pressure of the drilling mud is greater 
than the pressure in the formation, while the underbalanced pressure is when 
the situation is in opposite [4].  
1.4.3.2. Mud Pump 
The mud pumps are aimed to circulate the drilling mud down the drill string 
and return back to the shale shaker, i.e. pumps are designed for pressure 
output. Therefore high flow pressure is required to circulate heavy mud in deep 
wells and to optimize borehole cleaning below the bit. The most currently used 
mud pumps in the industry are duplex and triplex positives-displacement 
pumps [4].  
1.4.3.3. Mud Pit  
The mud pit is a structure designed for storing and handling the mud during 
circulation process. The drilling operation during early days used earthen pit to 
store and handle the mud which has been replaced by steel pits in modern 
systems. During drilling operation the discharged fluid from the well that carries 
cuttings enters the pit and is subjected to several treatment processes including 
equipments such as shale shaker, sand trapper, degasser, centrifuges and mud 
cleaners. A fluid with a high percentage of solid particles may clog the 
formation’s channel and decreasing reservoir permeability. Therefore it is 
vitally important to ensure that mud is treated properly before re-entering the 
system for reused circulation [4].  
1.4.3.4. Shale Shaker 
The shale shaker is an important equipment during execution of the solid-
control operation because it removes the major solid particles from the 
circulated fluid out of the well and also remove other large cuttings which may 
in turn clog other equipments. The device comprises various techniques, e.g. 
screening, setting, centrifuging or dilution. Once the cuttings and solid particles 
are removed the mud is pumped into cyclones for cleaning and removing of the 
finest solid particles, whilst the removed solid particles would be gathered into 
another structure called contaminant-removal equipment for final disposals.

Geologists may use removed cuttings from the shale shaker for analyzing the 
formation rocks. 
The rotary hose allows rotation of the pipe providing drilling mud to be injected 
into the borehole, while the swivel is a mechanical device placed directly under 
traveling block and directly above the Kelly, that has the ability to rotate the 
Kelly and drill string as well as allowing introduction of the drilling fluid into the 
wellbore [4, 5]. 
1.4.4. 
Rotary System 
The rotary system is mainly responsible for all equipments that provide bit 
rotation down in the borehole for rock crushing and well deepening process. 
The rotary system originally consists of rotary table, swivel, Kelly and Kelly 
bushing, drill string, drill collar and drilling bit, i.e. the bottom hole assembly 
(BHA). The rotary swivel is playing double important roles in drilling process as 
a connection between circulating system and rotary system. The upper section 
of the swivel has a bail for connection to the traveling block, while the 
gooseneck of it provides downward pointing connection of the rotary hose for 
which drilling fluid can be injected into the wellbore. Swivel does not rotate self 
but it allows everything below it to be rotated.  
The Kelly is the first section of the pipe below the swivel. The Kelly is 
approximately 40 ft long and has a square or hexagonal outside shape and 
hollow throughout to provide a passage of drilling fluid. Square shape is 
cheaper than hexagonal but hexagonal is most commonly used shaped which 
provides strongly grip when turning.  The outer surface of the Kelly is engaged 
to the Kelly bushing which fits into the master bushing as a part of the rotary 
table. The powered gears in the rotary table rotate the master bushing and thus 
the Kelly bushing which in turn rotates the Kelly and everything below it. During 
drilling operation a new drill pipe is prepared in a place called rat hole to be 
added below the Kelly down into wellbore. The recent drilling rigs are replacing 
Kelly, Kelly bushing and rotary table with topdrive Figure 5.

The rotation of the drill string might be achieved by hydraulic or electric 
motors. The topdrive is different from the Kelly system because of the topdrive 
is able to travel up and down the derrick, the drilling is performed with stands 
of pipes using 2 to 4 pipes instead of one single joint used in the conventional 
method with Kelly rotation. The swivel in the topdrive is substituted with a 
power swivel that exhibits the same function as the normal swivel do, but with 
a transmission rotation system instead of rotary table. Besides time saving 
advantages with the topdrive system and due to that the drill string rotates 
faster problems such as stuck pipe might be avoided during tripping process by 
using topdrive systems rather than the Kelly system [4, 6].

The drill string is the chain of pipes that connect the surface equipment with 
the drilling bit. It rotates by either the Kelly rotary table or by the topdrive 
system. Generally the drill string presents only the column of the pipe, although 
the term is loosely applied including drill collar. The drill string has functions of 
transferring rotation provided on the surface to the drilling bit, conducting mud 
system down around the bit and also putting the weight on the drilling bit that 
represents the force required to execute drilling operation. The drill pipes are 
classified as API system in length ranges. Range 2 which is 30 ft is the most 
commonly drill pipe used in oil industry. 
The bottom-hole assembly (BHA) is made up of the drill collar, drill stabilizer 
(jars, pressure disconnect) and drill bit. The drill collar is a thick-walled, heavy

several teeth or cutters and diamond bits [4, 5].

2.1.  Drilling Mud Sorts and Compositions  
The most basic drilling fluid is called normal or natural mud is composed mainly 
of a liquid, dispersed solid and dissolved solids moreover, some additives can 
also be added to the mud as well as weight material such as barite to increase 
mud density and hence hydrostatic pressure. The liquid can be water (fresh or 
saline), oil (diesel or crude oil) and synthetic fluids or mineral oil, while 
dispersed solids usually are colloidal particles which are suspended in various 
sizes. Dissolved solids are normally salts which have an important effect on the 
colloidal particles. The normal mud is generally used in drilling of shale or sand 
formation where there are no unexpected conditions likely to occur. The special 
mud is another type of mud made up of adding some material to the normal 
mud in order to solve some specific problems. It is used to increase drilling 
efficiency for example, abnormal borehole conditions with long salt sections or 
at high formation pressures or might also be used to accomplish specific 
objectives, e.g. faster penetration rate. The special drilling fluid and its additives 
can be stratified as following presented [8].  
 Lime Base Mud: A ratio of 2 lb caustic soda, 1.5 lb quebracho and 5 lb 
lime added in that order to 1 barrel of mud. The advantage is the mud 
will go through highly viscous stage and tolerate high contaminant salt 
sections, while the disadvantage is the tendency to solidify in high 
bottom-hole temperature conditions. 
 Lime - Treated Mud: Similar to the lime base mud with less lime portion 
as an attempt to overcome high temperature gelatin problem. 
 Emulsion Mud - Oil in Water: The advantages of adding oil to natural 
mud are that the mud in this case being stable, easy to maintain, 
reducing 
down-hole 
friction, 
low 
filtration 
required 
and 
faster 
penetration rate achievements. 
 Inhabited Mud: Large amount of salts added to the mud to increase pH 
rate in order to achieve desired results. It is designed to reduce the 
==PAGE:26==
23 
 
amount of formation swelling caused by filtration. The drawback of this 
mud is that high salt concentration may destroy the natural clay 
properties as well as the difficulty of increasing mud viscosity. 
 Gypsum Base Mud: An inhibited mud with large amount of calcium 
sulfate. Typically 2 lb of gypsum is added to 1 barrel of mud. The 
disadvantage is that the remaining of the abrasives in mud as well as the 
mud retains gas. 
 Oil Base Mud: Oil replacing water in natural mud. The oil base mud have 
a good lubricating property and high drilling rates can be used but  
expensive to produce, generally dirty to work with and principally the 
mud viscosity various with formation temperature. 
 Inverted Emulsion: Water in an oil emulsion which has the oil base mud 
pros but it is a cheaper and less stable mud compared to the others. 
 Salt Water Mud: To be used partially or fully salt saturated with no filter 
cake building properties and easy to lose in a porous formation. 
 Silicate Mud: Made up of sodium silicate and saturated salt water which 
has an effect on shale formation but it is corrosive, expensive and gives 
poor logging results. 
 Low Solid Mud: Keeps amount of clay in the mud at a minimum which 
promotes faster and safer drilling. The mud works with large mud 
pumps and high mud volumes. Excessive dilution and production cost 
can be a problem. 
2.2.  Drilling Fluid Categories and Classifications 
Nine distinct categories can be used to classifying drilling fluid systems used in 
drilling engineering operations. Five of them represents drilling fluid based 
using freshwater and one with a saltwater system. Two categories include oil or 
synthetic based systems and the last one is for pneumatic base system such as 
air, mist, foam and gas. The nine categories are described as follows [7, 8]: 
1. Non-Dispersed Mud System: A freshwater based mud that may consist 
of natural mud and other lightly treated systems. Generally used in 
shallow wells or top-hole drilling procedures. 
==PAGE:27==
24 
 
2. Dispersed Mud System: A freshwater based mud mainly used in deep 
wells or where problems with viscosity occur. The mud is dispersed with 
deflocculants or lignosulfonates.  
3. Calcium Treated Mud System: A freshwater based mud system used to 
inhibit the hydration of the formation clays or shale by adding some 
components, e.g. calcium, magnesium, hydrated lime and gypsum. 
4. Polymer Mud System: A freshwater based mud system made up of 
polymers which are long chains, high molecular weight compounds that 
are used to increase viscosity, flocculate clays, reduce filtrate and 
stabilized borehole. 
5. Low Solid Mud System: A freshwater based mud system which controls 
the solid contents and type. The total solid should be in range of 6% to 
10% by volume and clay should not be greater than 3% by volume. The 
primary advantage of this mud system is to improve the rate of 
penetration. 
6. Saturated Salt Mud System: This mud system is mainly a saltwater 
based mud which has a chloride content of 189,000 ppm. Usually 
saltwater has chloride contents ranging from 6,000 to 189,000 ppm. The 
lower value is normally called a seawater system. If the mud system 
made up of freshwater, then the sodium chloride needs to be added 
excessively. 
7. Oil Base Mud System: Oil base mud is commonly used for high 
temperature wells, deviated wells and where sticking pipe and hole-
stabilizing is a problem. It consists of two types of systems. 
a) Invert Emulsion Mud System: where water is the dispersed phase 
and oil is the continuous phase (water in oil mud). Water usually 
increases the system viscosity. 
b) Emulsion Mud System: where oil is dispersed phase and water is 
the continuous phase (oil in water mud). Oil usually decreases the 
system viscosity, therefore emulsifier such as fatty acid amines 
derivatives, high molecular weight soap are added to control 
rheological properties in both cases. 
==PAGE:28==
25 
 
8. Workover Mud System: Also called completion fluids that are usually 
highly treated brines and blended salt fluids. This system is designed to 
minimize formation damage, to be compatible with acidizing fluids (acid 
soluble) and to reduce clay or shale hydration. 
9. Pneumatic Mud System: Air, mist, foam and gas mud systems are lower 
than hydrostatic pressure and stratified into four categories as follows: 
a) Dry Air Drilling: which presents by injecting dry air or gas to the 
wellbore to remove cuttings until water is encountered. 
b) Mist Drilling: the process is followed the dry air drilling directly by 
injecting a foaming agent to the air stream which is mixed with 
produced water and cuttings. 
c) Foam Drilling: uses chemical detergent and polymers to remove 
cuttings in fast-moving air stream. 
d) Aerated Fluid: is a mud system injected with air to reduce 
hydrostatic

2.3.  Drilling Fluid Circulation Loop  
In order to achieve all essential purposes of a drilling fluid and to prevent 
problems during drilling operation, the fluid has to be pumped out through the 
system then circulated to the surface for treatment and reuse. The main 
component of drilling circulation loop is the mud pump that has ability to pump 
out the mud to be circulated through the system Figure 6. The mud pump is 
considered to be the heart of the system which provides pressure and fluid flow 
to be traveled through the entire system. It is recommended that mud pump 
runs at 120 SPM. The drilling fluid is stored in a mud pit after mixing and being 
prepared in mud mixer which consists of several large tanks, each volume of 
typically 60 m3. The mud pit volume in modern offshore platform is 
approximately 200 m3 with a surface area of typically 200 m2. After leaving the 
pump, the drilling fluid is transported through the stand pipe passing through 
the rotary hose and swivel to the topdrive or through the Kelly, Kelly bushing 
and rotary table. Thereby the fluid entering the drill string passes through the 
==PAGE:29==
26 
 
bottom-hole assembly. At the end of the drill bit the fluid shoots out with great 
force through bit nozzles to clean both the drilling bit itself and from under it 
and travels back through the annulus carrying the drill cuttings up to the 
surface.  
At the surface the returned fluid includes cuttings and are going through 
several cleaning stages at the mud cleaning unit starting with shale shaker and 
solid removal processes, e.g. desander, centrifuge, mud cleaner, settling pit etc. 
The aim of those operations is to remove cuttings and other undesirable 
material from the mud and to clean and retreat the mud to be reused.  
Most unwanted solid contents and cuttings are collected in a place called 
reserve pit. Moreover, the mud mixer ensures that drilling mud return to the 
system with the right compositions according to the measurements. This will be 
done by adding mud additives which have influence on mud density and 
viscosity [4, 7, 9].

drilling mud. For example, the cutting removal rate is dependent on the fluid 
viscosity, gel strength and fluid velocity, while fluid density (mud weight) has a 
major effect on the well control in terms of avoiding kicks and blowouts.

Mud Density  
Fluid density is defined as the mud weight per unit volume which can be 
expressed in pound per cubic foot (lb/ft3) or in SI unit as (kg/m3), despite the 
most common units used in North Sea are pound per gallon (lb/gal) or ton per 
cubic meter (ton/m3). The density of the fluid is important to maintain well 
control because it affects the hydrostatic pressure in the well. The hydrostatic 
pressure has to be greater than the pore pressure to prevent inflow of 
formation fluid. For instance sometimes drill engineers are exaggerating with 
excessive mud density and hydrostatic pressure for safety drilling reason but 
this can cause dramatic disadvantages such as the wall of the borehole 
fractures, resulting in that the fluid is lost into formation, increasing drilling 
operation cost and reducing the drilling penetration rate [9].

Viscosity is the second most important parameter after the density that has 
significant effect of mud circulation and cutting removal. It can be understood 
as a fluid resistance to gradients in flow velocity resulting in shear stress in the 
fluid. It depends on cuttings concentration, quality of the cutting themselves 
and the dispersed solids of the mud. There are different techniques and 
proposals of measuring the viscosity made by for example, Newton, Bingham, 
Fann VG viscometer, power law model and the Herschel-Bulkley model. 
According to Newton’s model the viscosity can be measured as a ratio between 
shear stress and shear rate of the fluid. Newton proposed that shear stress 
responds linearly in relation to the shear rate, while Bingham plastic model 
presents the correlation between shear stress and shear rate when it is not 
linear. The fluid viscosity needs to be adjusted during drilling circulation 
processes for efficient results, so when mud is dense, the viscosity need to be 
less and in contrary when mud is less dense, the viscosity needs to be higher.

2.4.4.3. Imposed Pressure 
The imposed pressure is the pressure generated during well ‘’shut in’’, when 
the annular preventing and choke are closed and small amount of mud are 
pumped into the wellbore using cementing units. The pressure by pump will be 
imposed and increased through the system. There are three types of test can 
be conducted in order to detect this type of imposed pressure, Leek-Off Test 
(LOT), Pressure Integrity and Fracture Test. 
Another imposed pressure can be detected during well ‘’shut in’’ is the pressure 
imposed by formation. When formation pressure exceeds hydrostatic pressure, 
the pressure differential between them will be imposed through the system 
which can be read at the surface by two different readings types, the pump 
pressure reading and the casing (choke) pressure reading [8].

3. Bit Technology  
Bits are the tools that provide wellbore deepening and rock formation cutting, 
therefore it is important to give an overview of available bit types and its 
application.  
The drilling process of rotary bits can be generated by various actions, e.g. 
scraping, chipping, gouging, or grinding the rock formation. The bit technology 
has to be satisfied with the specific rock formation and with the drilling 
expectation conditions. The following will be discussed, (1) the most available 
bit types used today, (2) the required criteria of selecting the appropriate bit for 
a specific drilling application and condition, (3) the general bit operational 
technology, (4) the factors that affecting wear bits and penetration rate, (5) Bit 
classification systems.

3.1.  Bit Types  
The first invention of a rotary bit was from a patent by Howard Hughes in 1909 
that described a rotary roller-cone bit with two rotation cones. The bit had a 
tendency of so called ‘’balled up’’ in soft shale formation and thus it redesigned 
in 1920s with meshing teeth. Thereby the tri-cone bit used for hard and soft 
formation process was introduced with cutters designed in 1930s. The first 
three-cone bit using tungsten-carbide inserts (TCI) in the cutting structure was 
introduced by Hughes Tool Company in 1949. The fixed-cutter bits are also 
evolved early 1900s which were using synthetic diamond to provide scraping of 
the surface.  
In this section we will introduce the most common, modern rotary drilling bits 
in use today such as roller-cone bits and fixed-cutter bits in detail. Moreover,

3.1.1. 
Roller-Cone Bits 
The roller cone bit usually has one or more cones that contain cutting elements 
usually refer to inserts that rotates about the axis of the cone as the bit rotated 
at the bottom of the borehole. The roller cone bit can be put into two 
categories, milled tooth bit and inserts bit category. The difference between 
them is how the cutting elements are built. For example, in milled tooth bit the 
cutting elements are milled from the steel that making up the cone, while in 
insert bit the cutting structure is a series of inserts press into the cone. The 
drilling action of the roller-cone bit depends on several parameters as follows 
[4, 8]: 
3.1.1.1. Cone Offset 
The offset of the bit is the measurement of how much bit-cones are moved so 
that their axes do not intersect with the centerline of the borehole. This will 
increase drilling speed in the most formation types but also promotes faster 
teeth wear in some abrasive formations. For soft formation cones offset are 
==PAGE:40==
37 
 
varied between 0.375 and 0.5 inch, while in hard formation varied between 0.0 
and 0.325 inch [4, 8]. 
3.1.1.2. Journal Angle 
The journal is the load-carrying surface on the bearing on a bit, while the 
journal angle is the angle between the cone rotation axis and the plane 
perpendicular to the axis of rotation of the bit, i.e. is the angle at which the 
journal is mounted to a horizontal plane Figure 8. This is an important 
parameter in terms of designing roller-cone bits because the journal angle 
varies from one rock type bit to another as a combination with bit offset for 
example, a hard formation require large journal angle with no offset. The 
journal angle in soft formation is about 33o to promote greater cutter action 
due to greater teeth depth, despite in medium formation the journal angle is 
varied between 34o-36o for decreasing cutter action, whilst in hard formation 
the journal angle is usually greater than 39o in order to minimize the cutter 
action [4, 8]. 
 
Figure 8: illustrates the Journal angles in roller-cones bit. [8] 
3.1.1.3. Teeth Shape and Cutting Structures 
The shape of the roller-cone bit teeth has influence on the drilling action and 
the bit cleaning flow rate. Steel teeth cutting structure are mainly used in milled 
cone bit with three design classifications, teeth spacing, tooth hardness and 
tooth angle Figure 9. For soft formation the teeth are preferred to be hard, at 
few numbers and widely spaced as well as a small tooth angle at 39o-42o for 
greater penetration rate (ROP) reason, but when the rock formation become 
harder the teeth numbers increase with the moderate spacing and depth. The 
tooth should be stronger with increased angle 43o-46o and reduced offset to 
==PAGE:41==
38 
 
prevent tooth breakage. As the rock type becomes much harder the bit has 
many teeth with a close spacing. The cutting structure is strong, small with 
tooth angle of 46o-50o and zero offset. This type of bit is usually used for 
crushing action. For a given WOB the wide spacing between inserts and teeth 
results in high penetration rate as it is relatively high loading on them, while 
closely spaced inserts and teeth reduces penetration rate as loading is reduced, 
therefore there are always a correlation between formation type, inserts and 
teeth spacing with regard to penetration rate Table 3  [4, 8].

3.1.1.4. Bearing System 
The roller-cone bit has various types of bearing systems such as friction bearing 
and roller bearing systems. Roller bearing system is referred to as anti friction 
bearings frequently used in large bit sizes. The advantage of roller bearing 
systems is the ability of working properly even after seal failure with higher 
revolution per minute (RPM), but it is really expensive to produce.  
The friction bearing system is referred to as journal bearing which is used 
basically in small bit sizes. The advantage of journal bearings is the high load 
distributing and handling, but it has a drawback when failure especially after 
seal failure. Generally the bearing system of a roller-cone bit is composed of the 
following [8, 12]. 
 
 
==PAGE:42==

 Roller-ball –roller bearing.  
 The seal which retains the lubricant and prevent the drilling fluid and 
abrasive from entering the bearing cavities. 
 The shirttail which is hardfaced to protect the seal. 
 Lubricant used to minimize friction and wear. 
 The reservoir is the lubricant supplier to the bearing. 
 The vent which equalize pressure surrounding bearing seal. 
3.1.2. 
Fixed-Cutter bits 
The fixed-cutter bits are generally bits without any moving parts, i.e. no bearing 
in comparison with roller-cone bits which is an advantage especially in small 
wellbore size where no place is available for roller-cone bits. Usually the fixed-
cutter bits require high RPM and low WOB and in general usually classifies to 
two groups, PDC and diamond bit group. The polycrystalline diamond compact 
(PDC) bit does not have any bearing and it has not the benefit of self cleaning 
action between the teeth. Therefore it includes small holes called nozzles use to 
flush the cuttings from under the bit in order to prevent ‘’balling up’’. The 
drilling mechanism of PDC bit is shearing, therefore requires less energy, less 
WOB and high rotation speed in comparison with roller-cone bits. The PDC bit 
has different shapes but the major profile used in industry is flat, shallow cone, 
tapered or double cone. The WOB is distributed homogenously on bit’s cutter 
elements in flat and shallow con bits. 
The diamond bit has no bearing system either but it is very resistant to abrasion 
and often used in abrasive formation due to the surface set (cutters) are made 
of natural diamond Figure 10. The diamond bit is generally made up of natural 
diamond, impregnated and thermal stable polycrystalline (TSP) elements. The 
drilling mechanism of a diamond bit is grinding, therefore it requires high 
rotation speed and low WOB. Despite the high wear resistance the bit is very 
sensitive to the vibration and shock, thus this must be taken in consideration 
when using the diamond bit. Fluid circulation must be very efficient in order to 
prevent overheating and balling up the bit [4, 8].

3.2.  Bit Selection and Operation  
The selection of the right bit is one of the most common challenge that 
encounter drilling engineers who evaluate bits between not only different bit 
categories but between same types of bits because choosing the wrong bit 
results in dramatic cost and time consumption. There are many parameters to 
be considered prior choosing the right bit such as the formation failure while it 
is plastic or elastic, formation type, WOB requirement, rotary speed, bit 
aggressiveness and wear resistance, desired ROP and cost per foot etc.  
There are always correlation between ROP and WOB  for a given formation for 
example, the penetration rate is always high with high weight on bit when using 
roller cone bit with long teeth and large cone-offset in soft formation rock, 
while the ROP, RPM and  WOB are always less when using fixed-cutter bits.   
The roller-cone bits typically require gouging/scraping action when operating in 
soft formation where the cutting is mostly large and ROP application is high, 
while in hard formation the bits require chipping/crushing operation instead, 
where ROP is low applicable and high WOB requires fracturing the rock. These 
actions are governed by the cones roll and skid. For soft formation actions 
(gouging/scraping) require significantly high degree of skid, conversely for hard 
formation actions (chipping/crushing) require that cone roll approach ‘’true 
roll’’ with very little skidding. 
The operation action for the PDC bit is shearing and the cutting is sheared off 
with an angle relative to the plane of the thrust. In shear the energy requires to 
==PAGE:44==
41 
 
reach the plastic rapture is less than that required by compressive stress, thus 
the PDC bit require less WOB than roller-cone bits.  
The diamond bit removed the rock with a grinding operation action as the rock 
removed with very shallow depth of cut. The depth of cut is determined by the 
rock strength and the WOB, therefore the diamond bit requires less WOB than 
the roller cone bits. In spite of an average weight is between 350-750 pounds 
per square inch of bit area, it requires relatively high rotating speed, e.g. 200-
1000 rpm especially when down-hole motors are used. The diamond bit should 
be avoided in some drilling situations at which the bit can be damage, breakage 
or burned as follows [4, 8]: 
 Very hard broken formation: because it cause severe loading on bit 
resulting in diamond breakage and short bit life. 
 Formation contains chert or pyrite: because chert or pyrite breaks into 
large pieces under diamond bit which may result in bit damage. 
 Reaming long section in hard formation: because the hydraulic cooling 
and cleaning system is extremely poor during reaming which results the 
diamond ‘’ burning’’ or damage in the gauge area. 
3.3.  Bit Circulating Operation 
As mentioned in drilling fluid concept 2 the fluid flows from under the bit and 
returns to the surface through the annular, subsequently when the drilling fluid 
reaches the bit flushes through holes drilled on the dome of the bit. Typically 
one to three holes are used and this is called regular circulation. The mud 
passes through the bit fluid channel trough the drill holes over the cutters and 
then to the bottom of the bit to flush away cutting from under the bit.  
Jet circulation bit has a different pathway in the sense that it flushes fluid in a 
great flow velocity. The drill fluid passes the fluid channel of the bit at high 
velocity with minimum pressure losses to the jet nozzles which have different 
shapes to increase fluid flow velocity in order to flush cutting away from under 
the bit.

3.4.1. 
Roller-Cone Bits Classification 
This system is an industry standard classification which describes milled-tooth 
and insert-roller-cone bits. The coding system in this classification is based on 
bit design and application related-criteria Figure 11. The classification is a four 
character design and application related-code. The first three characters are 
always digits and the fourth is always alphabetic. The first digit describes the bit 
hardness series which defines the general formation characteristics in eight 
categories. Series 1 to 3 refers to the milled-tooth bit and series 4 to 8 refers to 
inserts-cone bits. The higher the series number, the harder or the more 
abrasive rock type is. Series 1 represents the easiest drilling formation type 
(very soft) for milled-tooth bit, while series 3 represents the more abrasive 
drilling formation type for milled-tooth bit. Series 4 represents the easiest 
drilling formation for insert-type bit, while series 8 represents the hardest 
drilling formation for insert-type bit.  
The second digit in IADC classification system describes the bit type milled-
tooth or insert-type bit and describes also the degree of the formation hardness 
in four categories. Unfortunately the rock hardness is not clearly identified by 
the IADC system. The meanings of soft, medium and hard formation are 
subjective and refer to the driller interpretations.

  It isolates a certain formation during well drilling and provide safe place 
for installing the BOP device. 
 Enable safe controlling of formation pressure. 
Casing has a major function of conducting well fluid from the formation to the 
wellhead in a safety manner but it is the most expensive part in the well drilling 
procedure. It costs almost 20% of the average cost of a completed well owing 
to the greater well depth increasing the number and the size of tubing requires 
to complete a well successfully. Principally casing can be divided into five basic 
types as follows [4, 8, 13]: 
Conductor Casing  
The conductor pipe is the first string of casing to be used with a depth variation 
between 10-300 ft. The outer diameter of the conductor pipe size ranges from 
16-36 inches. This pipe must be sufficient large in order to allow other string 
casings to be installed through it. The purpose of this casing is to circulate the 
drilling fluid to the shale shaker without any erosion of the formation below rig 
foundation. The conductor casing also protects subsequent casing strings from 
corrosion and may be used to support some of the well load structurally. 
Conductor prevents also soil and other material from entering the borehole. 
Surface Casing 
The surface casing is the second string in which the amount of it depends on 
the depth of unconsolidated formation. Surface casing is set in the first 
competent formation. Normal size of the outer diameter is between 133/8 - 20 
==PAGE:50==
47 
 
inches. Surface casing depth is usually between 300-5000 ft and because the 
temperature and pressure increase with depth, different grade of surface 
casing is required to handle well conditions. The main purposes of this casing 
are: 
 Protect fresh water formation (aquifer) and seal off unconsolidated 
formation, in other words, prevent caving in. 
 Provide a safely installation of the blowout preventer (BOP). 
 Support the ‘’build’’ sections for deviated wells. 
Intermediate Casing 
This type of casing is set directly after surface tubing and implicitly depends on 
the size and the grade of the surface casing required withstanding the 
subsurface conditions. It is usually required in deeper wells that penetrate 
abnormally pressured formation conditions, therefore it sometimes refers to 
the ‘’protective or drilling’’ casing. The outer diameter of the intermediate 
string is typically between 95/8 - 133/8 inches. Intermediate casing provides 
protection of the hole against caving in weak rocks and allows using drilling 
fluid of different densities for controlling of lower rock units. 
Liner  
Liner is a string of casing that usually does not reach the surface. It hangs from 
the base of the intermediate casing and reaches the bottom of the hole. In case 
of liner being extended to the surface is called ‘’production liner’’ which is 
generally connected to the surface with a tieback casing string Figure 14. The 
symbol

of the liner in next casing string, often seal the annulus between the liner and 
casing, usually called ‘’packers’’. The major advantage of liner is the cost 
reducing due 
to 
hanging 
requirement 
instead 
of 
casing

Sometimes also liner attached to so called intermediate liner in which the 
production liner would be hanged on it.

Production Casing 
This kind of casing is mainly the last casing section set in a wellbore. These 
strings are run to isolates production zones through which the production fluid 
travel to the wellhead. The strings are set in across or within reservoir rock 
where well completion (perforation and hydraulic fracture) take place and 
resulting oil and gas flows through it. The size of the production casing depends 
significantly on the production rate (ROP), the higher barrel per day produced 
the larger inside diameter of the string. Commonly used size is between 3-7 
inches (outer diameter).

4.3.  Casing Setting Operation 
Casing is a steel pipe ‘’string’’ with a specific length placed in a wellbore. It 
includes joints that the drill crew puts together of two pipes to be run into well 
bore with connection threads called coupling or collar. This will be done by 
using a multi speed power casing tongs.  
The wellbore needs to be drilled to a prescribed depth where the first casing 
will be located and cemented then the drilling operation resumes through 
cementing at the bottom to the next desired depth Figure 16. This operation is 
repeated until production casing is placed into a wellbore. However, there are 
some casings nomenclatures that need to be taken in consideration before 
concluding casing operation and prior cementing. These nomenclatures are 
listed below with brief descriptions [5, 16]. 
 Guide Shoe  
Guide shoe is a heavy rounded base steel and concrete fitting that the 
crew installs at the end of the first casing joint to go in the hole. It guides 
the casing to the centre of the hole and minimizes problems associated 
with casing sticking from ledges. The device has an opening at the head 
to allow cementing going through to the annulus. 
 Float Collar/Float Shoe 
It is a device or a component that installed on the second or third joint 
above the guide shoe on which cement plugs land at the primary 
==PAGE:56==
53 
 
cementing procedure. It has a check valve for which closes under casing 
running and opens during cementing. The valve allows the cement to 
flow downwards through it and prevents the upwards flowing into the 
casing string. 
 Centralizers 
They are components installed around casing strings in order to provide 
an opening between the string wall side and the wellbore wall. Another 
function is to prevent casing strings from leaning against the side of the 
hole. In other words, they reduce string drag and sticking. Keeping the 
casing away of the wall of the hole also ensures that cement will 
surround uniformly the outside of the casing and bonded securely to the 
hole.

intervals depending

scratchers rotating and reciprocating, i.e. moving up and down.

5. Cementing  
Generally the term cementing in well drilling operation is a process of mixing 
cement slurry (powder cement, additives and water) at the surface of the rig 
and pumped it down in a well by hydraulic displacement to the desired 
==PAGE:57==
54 
 
location. The process follows directly after each casing and cement hardening. 
Nevertheless when cement is hardened or ‘’set’’ it become a rigid solid for 
which has several important functions on well course such as: 
 Providing support for the casing.  
 Preventing the movement of formation fluid through annulus outside 
casing.  
 Protecting casing from corrosion.  
 Sealing of troublesome zones. 
The drill engineers are concern to chose and evaluate the best cement 
compositions and cement placement technique require for each specific well 
conditions for example, a deep well with high pressure and temperature 
(HP/HT) may require several casing strings to be cemented properly in place 
before the well being drilled and completed successfully. The primary cement 
composition, additives, the method of placement, field required calculation 
such as hardened time, volume of slurry and water requirement will be 
discussed [4]. 
5.1.  Cement Compositions 
The main ingredient in the most types of drilling cement is Portland cement 
which is a mixture made by burning of limestone and clay. The name Portland 
comes from the solid mixture resembling the rock quarried on the Isle of 
Portland, off the coast England and was chosen by the inventor Joseph Aspdin. 
When Portland cement mixed in water becomes cement slurry which can be 
pumped easily and hardened readily in underwater environments. The principal 
compounds resulting from the manufacturing process of cement are four 
crystalline compounds (clinkers). These crystalline compounds are in anhydrous 
form but when water is added become hydrous formed and called cement 
slurry. These clinkers are presenting as follows: 
 Tricalcium Silicate (3CaO-SiO7 or C3S) 
 Dicalcium Silicate (2CaO-SiO2 or C2S) 
 Tricalcium Aluminate (3CaO- Al9O3 or C3A) 
 Tetracalcium Aluminoferrite (4CaO-Al2O3-Fe2O3 or C4AF)

5.2.  Cement Additives  
Cement additives are substances that either can be dry blended to the cement 
before mixing with water to make slurry or can be dispersed in the mixing 
water at the job site. Additives have several functions and effects on the slurry 
such as controlling slurry density, setting time and viscosity etc. some of them 
have more than one function for example, additives that controls slurry density 
are the same additives affecting setting time and viscosity. However, the

 Accelerator Additives 
This is a chemical additive used to speed up the normal reaction time 
between cement and water to shorten thickening time of the cement 
and to save time on the rig. Adding such additives are determined by 
drill engineers according to a specific well conditions and applications. 
By adding accelerators the normal cement hardening is reduced in 
which saving operation time subsequently cost on the rig and avoiding 
so called ‘’waiting on’’ before resuming drilling operation. 
Mostly 
accelerators are adding to cement for cementing a shallow well with low 
temperature below 100oF. For example, the minimum compressive 
strength requires for resuming drilling operation is 500 psi which takes 
1-2 days with neat cement but by using accelerators can be developed in 
4 hours.

 Extender Additives 
In weak formations the density of the slurry obtained by mixing cement 
with normal amount of water will usually be too great for the formation 
fracture strength. Therefore extenders are added to reduce the density 
of the slurry and subsequently reducing hydrostatic pressure on the 
formation. This can be achieved by adding, for example, more than the 
normal amount of water to the cement to make the mixture lighter. This 
process also tends to reduce the overall cost of the cement, despite it 
can increase thickening time and decrease compressive strength. In 
other words, longer ‘’waiting on’’ required before resuming drilling 
operation.

 Pozzolan  Additives 
Pozzolans are either naturally or artificially siliceous and aluminous 
mineral materials added to Portland cement for density and viscosity 
control. These material may be either volcanic or a clay high in silica 
content combined with the calcium in the Portland cement results in the 
formation of calcium silicates that possess cementation properties. 
These materials are usually at low costs in comparison to the overall 
cost of cementing using Pozzlans. However, only a slight density 
reduction can be achieved owing to the specific gravity of the Pozzlans is 
slightly less than Portland cement. The density achieved by using these 
materials is about 13 PPG, while Portland density is approximately 15.7 
PPG. Common Pozzolans are diatomaceous earth and fly ash. 
When additives are added to the cement designates, the mixture as a ratio 
between the additive and the cement with the total always equal to 100 
percent. For example, Pozzolan, or fly ash is added then the ration can be 
expressed as 50:50 if it is equally divided. The first number is for the amount of 
additive and the second is for Portland cement. Moreover, when other 
additives are added such as accelerators, retarders and gel (bentonite) the ratio

5.4.  Cement Placement Technique  
The cementing procedures start directly when the casing string has been set to 
the planned depth or alternatively for a wellbore stability reason. The 
conventional method of placement is called cement casing in which uses for 
single-stage primary cementing. The method starts with tripping out drilling 
string and casing string is made up to be set down into the wellbore. Once the 
casing is set at the bottom, the borehole must be circulated to remove gelled 
drilling fluid and make it mobile. A pre-flush may be now used to thin and 
disperse drilling fluid particles that stick on the wall of the borehole so that it 
can be readily removed. Chemical wash that contains surfactant and mud 
thinners is another term used for pre-flush.  
A spacer is pumped into the casing before pumping the cement slurry. A spacer 
is a volume of fluid pumped ahead of the cement slurry and behind drilling 
fluid. It enhances the removal of gelled drilling fluid and makes better cement 
bond. Those washing procedures are important to ensure that the casing and 
borehole is in a good cleaned condition before performing the cementing job. 
Then the crew releases the bottom wiper plug located in the cementing head 
which separates the mud from cement slurry to be landed on the float collar or 
casing shoe. Thereby the cement is mixed with water and appropriate additives 
may be added before the slurry being pumped down into casing string. The 
slurry pushes the bottom wiper plug down using a high pressure cement 
pumping unit. When the bottom plug seats on the float collar, the pressure 
builds up and breaks its diaphragm to allow cementing going through the float 
collar’s one way valve. The float collar or casing shoe has one way valve which 
allows the cement slurry to be continued trough annulus and prevents slurry of 
backing up the casing. When the calculated desired volume of slurry is pumped 
down casing, the crew then releases the top wipe plug which is a solid core that 
pushes down by drilling mud to meet the bottom plug on the float collar. When 
the top wipe plug reaches the final destination, the pressure builds up sharply 
indicating that the job is completed and the pumping unit needs to shut down.  
==PAGE:66==
63 
 
After shutting down the pumping unit, the crew waits on for resuming drilling 
operation to the next planned depth. This is depends on the type of cement, 
type of additives and the drilling depth. Sometimes requires another or multi 
stage cementing at the same depth for reasons presenting below. 
 Cement is not required between widely separated intervals. 
 Cementing the high pressure gas zone and water producing horizons. 
 Down-hole 
formation 
unable 
to

the

exerted by long cement column. 
The technique is based on using the conventional primary cementing method of 
the lower portion of the casing and then cementing the upper portion by using 
staging cementing tool such as conventional 2 Trip and inner string tool. Most 
staging cementing is two, although multi staging is also possible [4, 5, 16].

6.1.  Wellbore Instability Causes 
Wellbore instability (WI) can be classified into three cases, mechanical failure, 
chemical effects and a combination between mechanical and chemical effects. 
Generally the wellbore instability can be recognized when the 
borehole

low strength rock, while chemical failure is usually due to the interaction 
damage between the rocks, typically shale (75% of sediment is shale) and the 
drilling fluid which causes the shale to swell and weakening which results in 
collapse of the wellbore.

Formation Integrity Test (FIT) is often called ‘’limit test’’ in which tests the 
strength, the integrity of the formation and the cement bond around the casing 
shoe. This can be done by increasing the bottom-hole pressure (BHP) to the 
designed pressure. It is often conducted to ensure that the formation below the 
casing shoe will not be broken while drilling the next section with high BHP. It 
has many purposes such as determining the fracture gradients around the 
casing shoe, knowing the optimum mud and equivalent mud weight for next 
drilling section and minimizing the risk of lost circulation in drilling troublesome 
zones. The most advantage of using (FIT) is that does not create a fracture in 
the formation and the integrity of the well will therefore be intact, despite the 
pressure determined by (FIT) is not corresponding the fracture pressure and

hence it does not give any information about principle stresses around the 
wellbore.

the volume, pressure, time and then repeat pumping process until reaching the 
maximum pre-determined pressure. Thereby the pump should be stopped and 
BOP should be opened again. The pressure will be plotted versus the mud 
weight volume Figure 21. If there is no abnormality, the curve will be almost a 
straight line [20, 23].

6.5.   Rock Failure Criteria 
The stability of a wellbore depends mainly on the mechanisms that cause 
collapsing in the wellbore or in the area around. However, different lithologies 
around wellbore may fail in different matters, e.g. sandstone perhaps fails in 
shear mechanism, whilst clay may fail owing to plastic deformation.  
==PAGE:80==
77 
 
Principally there are several mechanisms that cause rock collapse and wellbore 
instable such as tensile strength, plastic deformation, shear and erosion failure 
creep failure and so on. Therefore there are many rock failure criteria are used 
to determine the formation breakdown in the vicinity of the wellbore within 
regard to those mechanisms such as Von Mises, Tensile and Griffith failure 
criteria.  
Because the Mohr-Coulomb criteria is the most commonly used criteria in shear 
failure mechanism and because it is a correlation between in-situ stresses, 
redistributed stresses, formation pressure and mud weight, it will be explained 
in details in this section. Other failure criteria are mentioned as follows [19]:

  Hoek Brown Failure Criteria 
  Druker –Prager Failure Criteria 
  Mogi-Coulomb Failure Criteria  
  Ewy Modified Lade Failure Criteria

7. Well Control  
Well control is a category where kicks and blowouts are killed and prevented. It 
is one of the most important categories in well drilling procedures, because of 
uncontrolled occurrence may result in life loss, waste of nature reserves, and in 
worst case the well may be totally lost. Therefore it is important that drilling 
crew understand how a wellbore could be controlled and what procedures are 
needed to be followed when a disaster blowout occurs. In this chapter the 
causes of kicks, general signs of kicks, controlling procedures, calculation of 
some necessary kick parameters and different kick kill methods will be 
discussed. 
The equipment is used to prevent kicks and blowouts is called blowout 
preventer stack (BOPs). A preventer stack is usually consisting of an annular 
preventer located on the top followed by one or more ram-type preventer 
Figure 24. It is a vertical component which is rated with different pressure 
==PAGE:83==
80 
 
capacity, e.g. 3,000 psi, 5,000 psi, 10,000 psi and 15,000 psi. The pressure rating 
of BOPs can be found in API bulletin D13. 
The annular preventer is a sealing objective which is able to seal over an empty 
hole with a circular cross section by applying the hydraulic pressure on its 
piston. Then the ram-type preventer is closed as an effect of the applied 
hydraulic pressure. The advantages of the BOPs are, (1) able to close the top of 
the hole, (2) control the release fluid and permit fluid pumping and (3) allow 
movement through it. 
The BOPs is basically located below the rig surface basing upon the surface 
casing in onshore site, whereas in offshore site is recommended to be installed 
on the bottom of the ocean, thus this is called the subsea BOPs [4, 8].  
 
Figure 24: illustrates typical preventer stack arrangement used in the industry. [4] 
7.1.  Causes of Kicks 
A kick is defined as undesirable influx of the formation fluid into the borehole. If 
the influx is not controlled, it will lead to blowout occurrence. Normally there 
are three things must happen simultaneously when a kick is occurred and if any 
one of those things is missing, the well cannot be kicked. First a mobile fluid 
must be in present in the formation adjacent to the borehole. Second the 
formation permeability must be high around the borehole to ensure a flow into 
the borehole. Third the pressure exerts in the wellbore, i.e. the pore pressure of 
==PAGE:84==
81 
 
the formation is greater than the mud hydrostatic pressure. There are 
nonetheless five other reasons describing why kicks occur as follows [4, 8]: 
 Insufficient Mud Weight 
Kicks can occur due to the mud weight that is circulating while drilling 
does not overcome the formation pressure. The mud density has to be 
greater than the pore pressure to solve this problem but less than 
formation fracture pressure. 
 Failure to Keep the Hole Full 
The majority of kicks are happened during tripping process, when the 
reduction of the pressure occurs owing to the mud pump shuts down 
prior to tripping. This makes the ECD is equal to the pore pressure, 
therefore kicks occur. By using, for example, a trip tank which contains a 
calibration tool that monitors the entered mud volume into the 
borehole, the problem can be solved. The tank can be located above the 
preventer and the mud can be entered exploiting the gravity. 
 Swabbing  
Swabbing pressure is only considered for the formation below the 
drilling bit, when the drill string is pulled out. It has been considered as a 
negative pressure that has an effect of reducing the hydrostatic 
pressure. 
Reduction 
of 
the 
hydrostatic 
pressure 
results 
a 
kick 
development 
throughout 
the 
borehole. 
Swabbing 
pressure 
can 
be 
controlled by controlling for example, the drill string pulling speed, mud 
properties and borehole configuration. 
 Poor Well Planning 
Both mud and casing program have a great bearing on well control and 
any poor planning of mud weight circulation or casing setting depth may 
cause a kick.  
 Lost Circulation 
If the mud density is much greater than the pore pressure, it exceeds 
the lowest formation fracture pressure and causes borehole fracture. 
==PAGE:85==
82 
 
This fracture allows the drilling fluid to permeate into the formation 
rocks which is called lost circulation. 
7.2.  Recognition of Kicks 
A number of kicks detections can be observed at the surface which can be 
interpreted by crew members for taking a proper controlling procedure. These 
recognitions may be observed while drilling, during connection and while 
tripping. Some of those keys are presenting below [4, 8].  
 Flow-Rate Increase 
The mud weight is pumped with a constant pump rate into the 
borehole, so any increasing of the flow rate means that the formation 
fluid flows into the wellbore and causes the flow rate increase.  
 Pit-Volume Increase 
Pit-volume increase is a sign of a kick occurrence in two mechanisms. 
First due to the flow-rate increase as mentioned above. Second due to a 
kick contains gas and its expansion in the annulus will increase the flow-
rate and pit-volume.  
 Continuing Flow in the Well While Pumps off 
When the rig pump is off and the flow from the wellbore is continuing, it 
is a sign of progressing kick. Nevertheless there is an exception in case of 
a slug, when the mud in the drill pipe is heavier than that in the annulus. 
 Pump-Pressure Decrease While Pump-Stroke Increase 
It is a kick sign, when the drilling fluid has been displaced some distance 
up the annulus. The pump-pressure decreases due to the fluid in 
annulus become less dense, therefore the pump speed may rapidly 
increase to balance the mud weight drop.  
 Improper Hole Fill up on Trips 
When pulling up the drill string, the mud level decreases due to the mud 
displacement. If the mud volume is not balanced throughout the 
borehole, a kick may be indicated. 
 
 
==PAGE:86==
83 
 
 Drilling Break 
Drilling break is an abrupt increase in bit penetration rate. This is not a 
direct sign of a kick, but it is a warning sign to a possible kick. Suddenly 
increasing of the (ROP) is also a sign of the formation rock has been 
changed. Therefore it is recommended to break drilling operation and 
follow check analysis for the new formation. 
 Mud Weight Decrease 
If the mud weight is reduced in the flow line, it is probably a kick sign. 
The reduction may occur due to the influx of the formation fluid into the 
borehole or gas expansion in the annulus. The mud weight drop is a 
crucial sign that indicates kicks occurrences and should be taken in 
highly consideration. 
7.3.  Shut in Procedures 
When kick signs are observed at the surface, the steps of well shut-in must be 
taken seriously without any differences between ‘’small flowing’’ or ‘’full 
flowing’’ because of both can rapidly develop to a blowout. The well shut-in 
procedure is a prescription varied from one company to another and mainly 
depended on the type of the drilling rig. The general shut-in steps in case of one 
or more kick signs observed are presenting as follows: 
 Immediately raise Kelly or top-drive until a tool joint is above the rotary 
table. 
 Stop the mud pump. 
 Close the annular preventer (located on the top of the BOPs). 
 Notify company personnel for shut-in execution. 
 Read and record the shut-in drill-pipe pressure (SIDPP). 
 Read and record the shut-in casing pressure (SICP). 
 Determine the require BHP to balance a kick.  
 Determine the mud weight to balance the formation pressure.  
The SIDPP and SICP are the most important reading pressures during execution 
of the well shut-in, because they are used exclusively in wellbore kick killing. 
SIDPP can be read as a direct measurement of BHP gauge at the surface, but

When a kick encounters, the kick killer procedure initiates immediately by 
shutting-in 
the

Moreover, 
some

concerning

kick killing method. General there are many methods used in industry for well 
control such as: 
==PAGE:88==

 Constant Bottom-Hole Pressure (BHP) 
 Pressure Balance 
 Circulating and Weight 
 Reverse Circulation 
 Dynamic Kill 
 Bullheading 
 Lubricate and Bleed 
 Volumetric 
The criteria of choosing among those methods are different from one method 
to another, though the constant BHP is widely used in kick and well control 
industry. The constant BHP method is stratifies in three sub-categories, driller’s, 
engineer’s and concurrent. Therefore this method will be solely considered and 
broadly presented as follows [8]: 
7.4.1. 
The Driller’s Method 
The driller’s method is usually called ‘’two circulations’’ that is required to kill 
the well by holding the BHP constant. The first circulation, Initial Circulating 
Pressure (ICP) is circulated with the original mud weight with aim of removing 
the fluid influx from the wellbore through the annulus up to the surface. 
However, the pump must be leveled up to the killer rate by holding the casing 
pressure constant. This can be done by slightly opening the choke manifold 
Figure 24. When the pump is reached the kill rate level, the choke manipulates 
the ICP on the drill pipe.  
The drill pipe pressure holds then constant to maintain constant BHP which is 
normally equal to or slightly greater than the pore pressure. It holds constant 
until the total influx removes from the annulus Figure 25. Then the choke is 
closed, the pump is stopped and the well is shut-in. The SIDPP value will be 
equal to the SICP otherwise there are some influx still remaining in the annulus. 
==PAGE:89==
86 
 
 
Figure 25: illustrates the ICP of kick killer on the Driller’s Method. [27]  
The final circulating pressure (FCP) is prepared by estimating the KMW to be 
circulated through the wellbore. The casing pressure must be held constant 
until the kill mud is reaching the bit. Thereby the drill pipe pressure is held 
constant until the kill mud weight is totally circulated out at the surface Figure 
26. Then the pump is shut down, the chocked is closed and the well is shut-in. If 
the well is successfully killed, both the SIDPP and the SICP will be zero in 
reading gauges, otherwise there is still some influx in the wellbore and hence 
the well is not successfully killed [9, 28].  
 
Figure 26: illustrates the FCP of kick killer on the Driller’s Method. [27]  
7.4.2. 
The Wait and Weight Method 
The wait and weight method is called the engineer’s which is more effective 
method of killing kicks than the driller’s. It is based only on the FCP, i.e. the ICP 
is eliminated. Nevertheless the shut-in procedure is the same as in the driller’s 
method. When a kick occurs, the well is shut-in and the reading value of SIDPP 
is necessary to estimate the kill mud density. The casing pressure is held 
constant until the mud pump rises up to the kill rate level. The BHP can be 
maintained in two sections.  
==PAGE:90==
87 
 
The first section is from the surface down to the bit and the second is from the 
bit up to the surface again. The choke manifold is regulated in a way as the drill 
pipe pressure decreases linearly while pumping down the kill mud weight in the 
first section Figure 27.  
 
Figure 27: illustrates the FCP in the first section with relation to drill pipe pressure. [28] 
Once the FCP with the KMW comes out of the bit through annulus, the BHP is 
maintained by holding the drill pipe pressure constant in the second section. 
This can be done by slightly opening the choke. The well can be shut-in, when 
the kick has been totally percolated at the surface and some amount of kick 
fluid have also been displaced Figure 28. Thus if the kick is successfully killed, 
the pressure gauges of the SIDPP and the SICP will be read zero [8, 28]. 
 
Figure 28: shows the FCP in the second section with constant drill pipe pressure. [28] 
 
==PAGE:91==
88 
 
7.4.3. 
The Concurrent Method  
The concurrent method is the most complicated and unpredictable among 
those three methods. It is used only in rare cases for example, when a severe 
kick of 1.5 PPG or greater with large influx and potential problem of lost 
circulation. This method is neither one circulation pressure nor two, whereas it 
is a several circulations with different mud densities. The difficulty in this 
method is to determine the mud density that is being circulated and its relative 
depth, despite the well shut-in is the same manner as that outlined previously.  
The method can be initiated immediately after a kick is receipt and all kick 
information has been recorded. The mud pump is weighted up slowly to the kill 
rate level as the mud density weighted up too until reached the designed kill 
mud weight. Each pump rate increment is concerning a mud weight increment 
in one completed circulation. The choke is regulated according to the different 
drill pipe pressure conditions after each completed circulation. However, when 
the final kill mud weight circulation is reached the bit, the drill pipe pressure 
should be held constant until the kill mud completely displaces on the surface 
Figure 29 [4, 8].

Glossary Appendix  
Annular space 
The space around a pipe in a wellbore or the distance between 
the outer wall of a pipe and casing often calls annulus. 
Bails 
Two cylindrical bars are supporting the elevator in a hoisting 
system which is attached to the hook. 
Bailer 
Long cylindrical container, used to remove sand, mud or oil from 
a wellbore. 
Blowout 
An uncontrolled flow of gas, oil or other fluid occurring by 
uncurbed kicks. 
Circulating fluid 
A fluid pumps constantly from a rig to a hollow drill string to 
force cuttings circulating to the surface.  
Derrick 
A large loading beam structure usually of bolted constructions, 
the standard derrick has mainly four legs.  
Drill string 
The column or string of drill pipe neither including drilling collars 
nor Kelly, but the term is loosely applied for them. 
Drill pipe 
A heavy seamless of 30 ft long tube use to rotate drilling bit and 
allow circulating drilling fluid. 
Drilling bit 
A cutting tool used in oil and gas wells which consists of cutting 
and circulating elements.  
Drilling line 
A wire, connects the crown block with the traveling block used to 
hoist or lower drill pipe into or out of the well. 
Deadline 
The drilling line from the crown block sheaves to the anchor, it is 
dead because it is not move. 
Drag 
Resistance to motion caused by the casing contacting the 
wellbore. 
Drill stem 
All members in the assembly used in rotary drilling from swivel to 
the bit, Kelly etc. 
GPM  
Gallons per minute. 
Kick 
A fluid entry such as water, oil and gas because the pressure 
exerted by the column of the drilling fluid is not greater enough 
to overcome the pressure exerted by the fluid in the drilled 
formation. 
Lignosulfonates 
A sulfonated lignin is water-soluble anionic polyelectrolyte 
polymers. They are byproducts from the production of wood pulp 
using sulfite pulping. 
==PAGE:112==
 
 
Mud  
A liquid which is circulated through the wellbore during rotary 
drilling operation.  
Overbalanced drilling Safety drilling when the mud pressure (WP) is greater than 
formation pressure. 
Over gauge 
A larger hole in diameter than the bit size caused because the bit 
is not properly stabilized or insufficient weight loaded on it. 
Quebracho 
A Spanish name for a very hard wood tree species. 
Rat hole 
A hole in the rig prepared for a new drill pipe to be connected by 
Kelly to the drill string. 
Reservoir 
An oil and gas bearing formation rock which contains a trap. 
ROP 
Rate of penetration.  
Slug 
A heavy mud used to push down the lighter mud in the drill pipe 
before pulling the drill pipe out of the borehole. 
Substructure 
A foundation on which the mast or usually drawworks sit which 
contains space for well control and the blowout preventer. 
SPM 
Strokes per minute. 
TVD 
A vertical measurement from the surface to the last point in 
wellbore. 
Trip margin 
An increment increase in mud density needed to overbalance the 
formation pore pressure and compensate the reduction in 
effective mud weight caused by tripping operations. 
Trapped pressure 
The pressure in the drill pipe or in the annulus greater than the 
needed amount to balance the BHP. It can be trapped for a gas by 
migrate through annulus and expand or by shutting-in the well 
before pumps have stopped running. 
Underbalanced drilling Unsafely drilling when the pressure in the drilled formation is 
greater than the mud hydrostatic pressure.   
Under gauge  
A smaller hole in diameter than the bit size when the bit wears 
and worms in abrasive formation and balled in. 
Well caving 
A rock fracturing and collapsing around the borehole.
