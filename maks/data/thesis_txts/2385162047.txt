Abstract
It is important for any design process to consider the prototyping time and cost reduc-
tion. 3D printing technologies offer not only these advantages but also the potential to
manufacture complex structures. In recent years, there has been an increasing interest
in 3D printed antennas.
The aim of the project is to design an antenna for which the combination between
the high frequency, structure complexity, and gain will result in state of the art for
3D printed antennas. Some specifications for the design are based on applications for
intercubesat links.
First, a deep study of the state of the art was carried out in order to determine which
technologies has been used for 3D printed antennas over the recent years.
Then, some antenna candidates were studied and compared according to the specifi-
cations derived. The designed prototypes were based on reflector antennas, since they
can provide the compactness, gain, and frequency bandwidth required. Symmetric- and
offset configurations were considered during the design process, as well as single- and
dual configurations.
Later, the configuration with better potential performance was optimized. The final
prototype was a dual offset Gregorian reflector.
The optimization process was divided in different stages. First of all, a study of
the dimensions of the feed was carried out with CST. Then, since the avoidance of the
blockage depends on the feed itself, these dimensions were included in the optimization
process of the geometry of the reflector.
The final design includes a rectangular to circular waveguide transition as well as
a bend of the circular waveguide. This introduced another degree of complexity in the
antenna, which makes the test of the validity of the technology more challenging..
Once the antenna was optimized, the design and optimization of the support struc-
ture for mechanical interface was carried out, since the antenna was measured in the
DTU planar near field measurement facility.
The last step was to measure the reflection coefficient and the radiation pattern of
the antenna. Data processing and a comparison between the measurements and the
simulations were carried out.

Resumen
Título: Diseño e impresión en 3D de antena en la banda de 60 GHz.
Autor: Rubén Tena Sánchez.
Supervisores: Olav Breinbjerg (Professor, Ph.D) y Oleksiy S. Kim (As-
sociate Professor, Ph.D).
Para cualquier proceso de diseño es crucial considerar los tiempos de fabricación y
la reducción de costes. Las tecnologías de impresión 3D presentan las ventajas anterior-
mente mencionadas y además presentan el potencial de fabricar estructuras complejas.
En los últimos años, ha habido un incremento en el interés por antenas impresas con
este tipo de tecnologías de impresión 3D.
El objetivo del proyecto es diseñar una antena para la cual, la combinación entre
complejidad, frecuencia y ganancia resulte en el estado del arte para antenas impresas
en 3D. Algunas de las especificaciones para el diseño están basadas en aplicaciones para
comunicaciones entre cubesats.
En primer lugar, se realizó un estudio del estado del arte para determinar qué tec-
nologías se han utilizado en los últimos años para la fabricación de antenas mediante
impresión 3D.
Posteriormente, un análisis de posibles soluciones para la configuración de antena
fue llevado a cabo. Los prototipos diseñados se basaron en reflectores, debido a que ésta
antena proporciona la ganancia, tamaño y ancho de banda necesarios de acuerdo con
las especificaciones. Para el diseño se tuvieron en cuenta configuraciones simétricas y
descentradas, como también configuraciones simples y dobles.
De las configuraciones estudiadas, aquella que proporcionaba mejor rendimiento po-
tencial fue la elegida. El prototipo final fue un sistema Gregoriano descentrado.
El proceso de optimización fue dividido en diferentes etapas. En primer lugar, se
realizó un estudio de las dimensiones del alimentador con CST. Tras ello, debido a
que el bloqueo de la radiación del reflector principal depende de las dimensiones del
alimentador, éstas fueron incluidas en el proceso de optimización de la geometría del
reflector.
El diseño final incluye una transición de guía de onda rectangular a circular, como
también una curvatura en la guía de onda circular. Esto introdujo otro grado de comple-
jidad en la antena, lo cual supuso un reto más en el análisis de la validez de la tecnología
3D utilizada.
==PAGE:6==
iv
Resumen
Una vez el proceso de optimización finalizó, se diseñó y optimizó la estructura de
soporte de la antena necesaria para la interfaz con el sistema de medida, dado que la
antena se midió en el sistema plano de medidas en campo cercano de DTU.
Por último, se midió el coeficiente de reflexión y el diagrama de radiación de la
antena, y se llevó a cabo un análisis de los datos y comparativa entre las medidas y las
simulaciones.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Overview
Over the last decade, the idea of using 3D printers to turn a digital file into a physical
component has been increasingly propagated and applied in the industry. These manu-
facturing techniques which have seen many applications in the world of mechanical and
structural design now see further application for antenna manufacturing. Some of its
advantages include the potential for designing complex structures that were not feasible
before due to high cost or difficulties in the traditional manufacturing process.
3D printers are becoming more reliable and accurate.
Provided that this trend
continues, they could become a real alternative to conventional manufacturing techniques
where more human interaction and slower product development cycle is necessary.
The technologies based on 3D printing are also known as Additive Manufacturing
(AM). The most common AM technologies that are currently being used for antenna
design will be discussed in Chapter 2 along with different antenna designs for each
technology.
AM transforms digital models into physical ones by building them up in layers. In
AM, the object is fabricated by controlling the placement and adhesion of successive
layers.
A wide range of materials can be used for 3D printing.
These include met-
als, plastics, ceramics, paper, or biomaterials. They can be supplied in different forms
(powder, filament, pellets, granules, resin, etc.) depending on the particular technol-
ogy. Additionally, they can be treated using different AM processes, the most common
are Fused Deposition, Stereolithography, Material Jetting, Binder Jetting, Powder Bed
Fusion, Directed Energy Deposition and Sheet Lamination [1].
Figure 1.1: 3D Printing process
To 3D print an object the first thing that is needed is a digital model created in
a software modelling tool such as SolidWorks. From the CAD model, an STL file is
generated. This file is then used in another slicing software that divides the model into

cross-sectional layers. These slices are sent to the 3D printer that fabricates the object
by printing the layers sequentially on top of each other.
Essentially, the aforementioned 3D printing technologies can be classified in four
categories of processes which will be used throughout this document:
• Extrusion
• vat photopolymerization
• Powder based technologies
• Sheet lamination.
As opposed to traditional manufacturing methods, AM presents advantages such
as low-cost, time-saving, mass reproduction, and arbitrarily complex designs. Hence
making it suitable for projects where the cost, time and weight are critical factors.
The 3D printing technologies developed in recent years have triggered new innovation
in antenna and microwave components at high frequency. Nevertheless, the efficiency and
applicability of the different AM technologies have yet to be tested at high frequencies
as will be seen in the following section.
For the specific case of antenna and microwave components, the metallization of
the components becomes a critical factor that directly influences the quality of the
component. Essentially the components can either be 3D printed and then metallized or
they can be directly printed in metal. Some examples of this distinction will be further
detailed in Chapter 2.
1.2
Intercubesat communications
The market of satellite telecommunications can particularly benefit from the break-
through provided by 3D printing. These enable simplified, high performance and lower
cost user terminal front end components as well as lightweight spacecraft payload.
Within the satellite market one can find cubesats. Each cubesat is a miniaturized cu-
bical structure that has a side length of 10 cm and a mass of no more than a few kilograms.
The size of the cubesat is a standard defined as 1 cubesat unit (1U) = 10 × 10 × 10 cm,
see Figure 1.2.
A feature that make cubesats different from other satellites and suitable for 3D
printing is that their design is oriented towards lower cost and rapid development. These
characteristics have led to the proliferation of cubesat constellations as well as simpler
intersatellite communications.
Intersatellite links provide direct connectivity between two or more satellites, elimi-
nating the need for intermediate ground stations. In the past, a large satellite system
was associated with a large economic budget.
Hence, the development of CubeSats
makes these satellite constellations feasible for more applications.
The communication layer of inter-satellite links (ISLs), is one of the most important
aspects as it is critical for ensuring the autonomy of the system.

A 2016 European Space Agency invitation to Tender (”Cost-effective high-gain cube-

between cubesats[2]. These specifications will be used as a reference for the design of
the antenna in this Thesis.
1.3
Objective
From Section 1.1 and Section 1.2 it can be seen that there is great potential in inter-
satellite communications with a series of requirements that are satisfied by 3D printing
better than with other technologies.
The 60 GHz unlicensed spectrum band (57-66 GHz) has received a lot of attention
due to its capability to facilitate short-range and high-speed communications. Thus, a
3D printed directional antenna is a promising candidate in order to combine high-speed
applications within a compact device.
To sum up, the goal of this project is to design, manufacture and test a 3D-printed
antenna operating at 60 GHz.
Some specifications for the antenna will be designed
for intercubesat communications. The ambition of the project is that the combination
of a complex antenna structure at a high frequency will result in new state of the art
developments for 3D-printed antennas.
The requirements for the design are based on the ones specified in ESA’s ”Cost-
effective high-gain cubesat antennas” note [2]:
• Gain larger than 10 dBi.
• Mass reduction of 50%.
• Frequency range from 57 to 66 GHz (IEEE 802.11ad Wireless Gigabit standard).
• The antenna design shall be compliant to 10x10x10 cm.
Low profile design is
preferred since there are other components in the CubeSat.
A reflector antenna will be proposed as a solution for these requirements. The re-
flector antenna will provide the performance desired in terms of compactness, high gain,

CHAPTER 2

This section provides an overview of the recent developments in the state-of-art for 3D
printing antennas. The research is based fundamentally on desk research and focused
mainly on documents published from 2014 up to present time.
To collect the information needed, the academic digital library ”DTU findit” has
been used. The following keywords were used as search criteria:
• Rapid prototyping antenna
• Additive manufactured antenna
• 3D printing antenna
• Printed antenna
• Stereolithography/Material Extrusion/Selective Laser Sintering/Selective
Laser Melting/Electron Beam Melting
For the assessment of the current state of the art in 3D printed antennas, a detailed
description will be provided of the different AM processes as well as the set of antennas
that have been manufactured using the different technologies.
2.1
Material Extrusion
In these sort of processes, 3D printing consists on building up objects in layers by
outputting a semi-liquid material from a computer-controlled nozzle. A wide variety
of materials can be extruded but the most widely used are thermoplastics. The most
common choices for the material are ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene) and PLA
(PolyLactic Acid).
This material, so-called filament, is laid down in different layers until it forms the
3D shape. At the beginning of the process, the filament is slowly fed to the print head
and heated to a temperature that varies between 180°C and 250°C. Consequently, the
filament is melted and then extruded through a fine nozzle.
==PAGE:22==
6
2 State of the Art: 3D printing technologies for antennas
In Figure 2.1, it can be seen that the material is deposited onto the platform where
the filament rapidly cools. The print head moves along the platform until it traces out
the different layers.
Figure 2.1: Material Extrusion technology
The best industrial printers can extrude layers that are 0.1 mm thin with an accuracy
in the other axes of about 0.1 mm. One important practical aspect of this technology
is that objects manufactured using thermoplastic extrusion may warp, shrink, or curl
while they are being printed because of the different cooling down rates of each part of
the material. Furthermore, objects manufactured via thermoplastic extrusion may have
stepping (which is important for curved surfaces).
Material Extrusion requires additional structures to be added to support overhangs
or initially orphan parts. This is one of the most important aspects to take into account
when 3D printing a structure.
The study of the literature about 3D printing antennas has shown that it is among
the most widespread technologies for manufacturing antennas. This technology has been
used in the recent years for a wide range of frequencies as well as different fashion of
antennas.
Additionally, Table 2.1 shows that there is a wide variety of antennas fabricated with
this technology. It is important to note however that most of these antennas operate at
frequency bands below V-band.
One of the limitations of this technology is the metallization of the antenna. Nickel
based aerosol spray has been used in [19], demonstrating satisfying results and significant
reduction in the weight of the antenna. Direct print additive manufacturing with silver
ink has been used in [2][8][21][23] and [22] where the conductivity achieved with the
silver ink was the limiting factor in the final performance of the antennas.
In [4] the feasibility of 3D printing has been tested for a conductive carbon-PLA
composite in comparison with a non-conductive PLA, obtaining a better performance
with non-conductive PLA. The material used to paint the antenna for the non-conductive
PLA was Bare Conductive’s Electric Paint. Carbon based conductive black paint was
used as well in [3], the simulations and the measurements are in good agreement.

2.2
Powder Based Technologies
Powder bed fusion is referred to all 3D printing technologies that build objects in
layers by using a heat source to selectively stick together successive layers of powder
(build material). Hence, this technology includes laser sintering (LS), selective laser
melting (SLM), selective laser sintering (SLS), selective heat sintering (SHS), direct metal
laser sintering (DMLS) and electron beam melting (EBM) among others. The powder
material can be a polymer, ceramic, or metal. That is one of the main differences with
respect to ME where the most used material is thermoplastics.

2.2.1
Selective Laser Sintering
In this technology a layer of powder is rolled across a powder bed, then a laser beam
traces out the cross-section that corresponds to the first layer. The heat produced by
the laser selectively makes the granules from successive layers to fuse together. A visual
description of the process is shown in Figure 2.3.
One example that uses this technology can be found in [13]. The antenna was first
printed in plastic. To metallize the antenna, several layers of copper paint were applied
(one was applied with a brush and another was sprayed). It showed that the conductivity
increased with each consecutive layer.
In [14], SLS was used to print the antenna in plastic but in this case polycrystalline
copper was used to metallize the structure, resulting in a better performance compared
to coating with conductive copper paint.

2.2.2
Selective Laser Melting
This technology uses a very high power laser to entirely melt metal powder granules in
order to form object layers. More specifically, the laser melts a certain portion of the
powder material and then cools it down to form the solid portion of the structure.
The application of direct 3D-printing of metals by SLM was tested in [12]. A phased
zone plate operating at 530 GHz was designed in order to characterize its reflection
after 3D printing. Post 3D-printing process was applied in the sense that the plate was
sandblasted at 600 kPa with a particle mixture that is 70% garnet and 30% glass, and
particle sizes in the range from 90 to 400 µm, in order to smooth the surface. They
concluded that the main losses in the reflection were caused by the roughness of the
surface.
2.2.3
Electron Beam Melting
The process followed is the same as shown in Figure 2.3. In this case, the printer builds
metal objects layer-by-layer in a vacuum, with the electron beam making passes of each
object layer. In the first pass, the power bed is pre-heated to an optimal temperature.
During the second pass, the outline of the object layer is melted while the following
passes melt the bulk of material inside the outline.
In EBM the electron beam moves around via electromagnetic deflection, instead of
using mechanized mirrors.
One of the advantages of EBM over laser-based technologies is that 100% solid com-
ponents can be achieved.
In [27] two horn antennas where fabricated with EBM without post-processing steps
to improve the surface roughness. It was shown that the surface roughness had an effect
of 1 dB loss when the surface roughness was 39.7 RMS.

2.2.4
Other Powder Based Technologies
In [18] a Volcano Smoke Antenna was fabricated using two approaches. In the first one
the antenna was printed in steel and after (due to the low conductivity of steel) it was
covered with tape for soldering to a SMA connector. In the second approach the antenna
was electroplated with copper. The same process was used in [10] where the antenna
was printed in steel and then electroplated.
The challenges found were that the glue in the copper was introducing air gaps
between the steel and the copper tape. Furthermore, insufficient electroplated copper
over the steel caused some discrepancies in the results.
In the particular process used in [18] and [10] the 3D printer deposits small drops of
glue onto layers of stainless steel powder, one layer at a time, until the print is complete.
Then the model is removed from the printer. At this stage of the process, the objects
are very fragile, similar to wet sand. The models then go through an infusion process
that replaces the glue with bronze, creating a full metal product.
2.3
Vat Photopolymerization
Vat Photopolymerization uses a light source to solidify successive object layers on the
surface or base of a vat of liquid photopolymer. There are many technology variations,
but we will describe the most used ones: stereolithography (SLA) and photopolymer
jetting (PolyJet). The objects fabricated with this technology are very accurate and are
smoother than the ones created with ME. Like with ME this technology needs additional
structure for overhanging parts.

2.3.1
Stereolithography
Stereolithography is a vat photopolymerization process that builds objects in layers
using a StereoLithographic Apparatus (SLA). Objects are created by a laser beam that
traces out and solidifies each successive layer on the surface or base of a vat of liquid
photopolymer.
A UV laser beam traces out the shape of the initial object layer on the surface of the
liquid. This cures the object, and the build platform lowers just a little. More liquid
photopolymer then either naturally flows over the top of the first object layer, or is
forced across it by a mechanical mechanism that skims the surface of the vat, and the
next object layer is traced out and set solid by the laser. This process is repeated until
the whole object has been printed.
Most conventional stereolithographic systems can produce parts with an accuracy of
0.025 mm in X and Y axes and 0.05 mm minimum thickness. In 2.4 a description of the
process of this technology can be seen.
Figure 2.4: Stereolithography process
This technology has been tested in [9] where the antenna was first printed by stere-
olithography with a tolerance of 0.2 mm. Then chemicals were used to make the surface
receptive to a metal plating. Finally, all the surfaces were copper plated.
In [28] a ring-focus dual-reflector antenna and spline-profiled smooth horn antenna
were fabricated with Stereolithography and then Cu-plated, see Figure 2.5. The gain of
the dual-reflector antenna is approximately 27.3 dBi at 14 GHz, and the diameter of the
aperture 20 mm, which leads to an aperture efficiency of 62.46%. The radiation pattern
measured and the simulation of the radiation pattern are in good agreement.

In figure 2.6 the Photopolymer Jetting process can be seen. The print head moves across
the build platform depositing one layer of liquid photopolymer. This is then set solid
with UV light also emitted from the print head.

At 60 GHz a reflectarray Figure 2.7 has been fabricated [6]. Copper coating was

frequencies the metallic losses of copper do not affect significantly the performance of
the antenna. Furthermore, due to the small skin depth at this band, only the sputtering
process was enough to get reasonable results, with 1.2 dB gain difference between the
simulations and the measurements.

Sheet Lamination
This technology sticks together sheets of paper, plastic or metal foil. One special case
of this technology is ultrasonic additive manufacturing (UAM). This technology creates
objects out of layers of metal tape that are welded together using high frequency (20,000
hertz) vibrations. It can reach resolutions of 0.1 mm.

2.5
Conclusion
We have presented a comparison of the most common 3D printing technologies used for
antennas manufacturing. It has been shown that the main challenges are the surface
roughness and the metallization of the antenna, that can cause losses and non-desired
effects in the radiation pattern. More specifically, a common disadvantage observed for
the radiation pattern of the 3D printed antennas was an increment in the side lobe level;
a worse radiation efficiency was observed as well.
From the study, the most promising technologies for high frequency, high gain, small
antennas are vat photopolymerization- and powder based technologies. These technolo-
gies are the most accurate among the different possiblities. With vat photopolymeriza-
tion, the good performance of the antennas has been proven as well as the flexibility
offered for the design. Powder based technologies offer the possibility of 3D print metal
components which make the antenna more robust.

From Chapter 2, it is clear that designing high-gain, high-frequency, small-size antennas
is a real challenge using 3D printing.
In this section, the characteristics and performance of different reflector antenna con-
figurations will be discussed , with the goal of reflecting the advantages and drawbacks
of these antennas for the specifications shown in Chapter 1.
The solution will be based on curved reflector antennas due to the high gain and
good performance that can be achieved in terms of bandwidth and cross-polarization.
Therefore, this section is intended to define which configuration achieves the best overall
performance.
3.1
Geometry of the antenna
Classical reflector antennas such as paraboloid, hyperboloid and the ellipsoid are based
on cone sections.
Cone section curves are originated by the intersection between a cone and a plane.
The angle α is called the half-angle and goes form 0◦to 90◦. The angle β is the smallest
angle between the cone axis and the plane intersecting the cone axis at a distance δ from
the vertex. If rectangular xz-coordinate system is used so that the origin is at the cone
axis and it is oriented to make the intersection curve symmetric with respect to z, the
curve equation can be described as follows:
x2 + z2 cos β (tan2 β −tan2 α) −2zδ cos β tan2 α = δ2 tan2 α
(3.1)
The parameters α, β and δ define the different curves. If δ differs from zero, the
intersection will be a hyperbola (0◦< β < α), a parabola (β = α) or an ellipse (α < β
≤90◦).
If now the cone section curves are rotated around the symmetry axis (z-axis), the
rotationally symmetric surfaces, the paraboloid, the ellipsoid, and the hyperboloid are
generated. Each of these surfaces have two focal points. Therefore, a field radiated by a
source in one focal point will either be focused in the other focal point or be interpreted
as radiated from it.

The design of reflector antennas is based on the use of quasi-optical design techniques
of Geometric Optics. For this approximations to hold, there are some assumptions that
must be satisfied:
• The radius of curvature of the reflector is large compared to the wavelength. Thus,
the surface of the reflector is locally flat for each ray.
• The design does not take into account any deformations (diffraction) from the rim
of the reflector.
• Electromagnetic waves travel in straight lines, called rays.

3.4

From the last designs, it is clear that the blockage and the additional effect of the struts

to reach the maximum aperture efficiency of the reflector.

Dual offset configurations is the alternative proposed to avoid blockage and minimize the
cross polarization observed with the single offset antenna. Since the dual configuration
has two reflectors, there are two degrees of freedom to play with, so the performance of
the antenna can be optimized.
Offset Cassegrain or Gregorian antennas are the different configurations that can be
designed. The sketch of both antennas is depicted in Figure 3.19.
For the Gregorian antenna, no subreflector blockage above the vertex of the parabola

CHAPTER 4
Feed Antenna
Horn antennas are widely used as feeds for reflector antennas. There are some necessary
characteristics for a feed antenna:
• Ideally they should generate uniform amplitude and phase distribution over the
main reflector without spillover (losses). In practice, it can not be realized.
• Rotationally symmetric radiation pattern.
• Phase center of the feed at the focal point of the reflector.
• Low cross polarization.
• Low sidelobe level.
• Good performance over the desired operational bandwidth.
Corrugated horn antennas fit with the specifications presented above. At high fre-
quencies, the corrugations are difficult to manufacture, and other possibilities of feed
antennas that achieve a good performance can be found, like Potter and linear section
horns.
For the purpose of this project, and considering the complexity of the offset Grego-
rian reflector for 3D printing, a smooth-walled horn antenna will be designed. Designing
another fashion of antenna would introduce another degree of complexity for the manu-
facturing process and the smooth-walled horn antenna is a simple model for testing the
validity of the technology.
Therefore, either pyramidal or conical horn antennas could be designed for feeding
the reflector antenna. Electrically, both antennas would provide similar characteristics.
One of the important differences between both horn antennas is the SLL. As can be seen
in [1] (pp 672-673,688-689) the conical horn antenna presents a lower value for the SLL
when both the pyramidal and the conical horn are illuminated with the fundamental
TE10 and TE11 modes respectively.
The design of the feed for this project will be based on a smooth-walled conical
horn antenna. Although the antenna will be designed for linear polarization, circular
polarization could be used as well.
Since it this difficult to find coaxial to circular waveguide transitions, a coaxial to
rectangular waveguide transition will be used for this project (a better explanation of
this transition will be done in Section 4.2).

4.2
Rectangular to circular waveguide
transition
The physical parameters that will be considered for the design of the transition are the
length of the rectangular to circular segment (L) and the effect of the section of circular
waveguide (Lc) on the reflection coefficient (S 11). The geometry of the transition can be
seen in Figure 4.3.
The program used to design the transition was CST. Transient solver or frequency
solver could be used in CST to resolve the problem. The chosen one was the frequency
solver because it provides broadband results faster than the transient solver. In addition,
it uses the built-in adaptive frequency sweep. In that sense, an error threshold of 0.001 dB
in the S 11 parameter was used as a condition for the convergence. The last means that
the solver automatically refines the mesh until the difference between two iterations for
the reflection coefficient in all the frequency range is smaller than 0.001 dB.The time
domain solver was used as well to double check the results.
The process followed to design the transition was based on the loft function of CST.
Thus, the idea was to create two faces, one with the dimensions of the rectangular
waveguide and another one with the dimensions of the circular waveguide. Then, the
loft tool is applied to connect the two faces. There is an important smoothness setting
that allows to transform one face into another more or less smoothly. This parameter will
determine the kind of spline function used to join both faces. Therefore, the smoothness
setting will be critical in the design process to achieve the best transition.
Considering that the transition should be as smooth as possible to minimize reflec-
tions and assure a good transition between the TE10 mode and the TE11 mode, the par
sweep function was used in CST to compare the performance of the antenna when differ-
ent lengths and smooth settings are applied. At this stage of the design, the transition
is supposed to be tilted, so the minimum length of the transition that provides good
results in terms of reflection coefficient will be the best solution in terms of blockage.
Figure 4.4 depicts the different results of the performance of the transition for differ-
ent combination of parameters. This sweep was made for a fixed Lc = 0.32 cm, that is
half the wavelength in the rectangular waveguide at 61.5 GHz. This value was chosen
as a reference.

5.3
Circular waveguide bend
With the aim of improving the horn antenna, a bend of the circular waveguide will be
designed. The length of the bend will be chosen as the minimum that provides a good
reflection coefficient in order to ease the optimization of the horn antenna.
The design of the bend was made using a loft between two faces in CST. The ideas
was to define two sections of circular waveguide with a certain distance between then,
after that a rotation is applied to one of the waveguide sections. The center of rotation
is half the distance between both faces to make the loft smoother, moreover the angle
of rotation is 20.72◦.
Figure 5.11 depicts the design process.
The sections of waveguide were fixed to
0.32 cm since with this length the reflection coefficient was acceptable. In addition, high
order modes are sufficiently attenuated.
After that, a sweep was made to find a good trade off between minimum distance
between the two waveguide sections, L1, which means minimum height after rotation,
and good reflection coefficient. The final value of L1 is 0.3 cm. Although high order
modes are attenuated (see Figure 5.11), the structure is not symmetric with respect to
the offset plane where the rotation of the section of waveguide was made. Nevertheless,
this asymmetry is not very large due to the smoothness of the transition and the effect
on the final radiation pattern of the antenna will not be critical as will be shown in the
next section.
5.4
Final horn design
It was showed that a total dimension of the transition and horn antenna of 2.37 cm
was enough to ensure avoidance of the blockage. Since the approach has changed, the
bend now limits the dimension of the horn antenna when optimizing.
The distance
between sections of waveguides before the rotation is 0.3 cm, since the rotation is done
with respect to the center of the sections, an estimation of 0.2 cm of restriction due to
the bend is done. Moreover, it is necessary to add the 0.32 cm of circular waveguide
section. Finally, the maximum dimension of the horn when optimizing is approximately
2.37 −0.32 −0.2 = 1.85 cm.
With this restriction, a new conical horn was designed. The sketch of the conical
horn can be seen in Figure 5.12. The final radius of the conical horn is 0.42 cm, and

6.1
Mechanical considerations for the
support structure
The antenna will be measured in the DTU planar nearfield measurement facility. There-
fore, it is necessary to consider the requirements of the facility in order to make the
antenna fit properly with the facility limitations.
After checking the limitations and necessities of the measurement facility, some draw-
ings where made and in cooperation with the workshop, the final structure was designed.
In the design process of the support structure the next considerations were made:
• The structure should be stiff enough to support the whole system (main reflec-
tor, subreflector and feed system), and at the same time minimize the possible
reflections of the signal that may perturb the radiation pattern.
• The support structure should consider the mechanical interface with the coaxial
to rectangular transition as well as the interface with the support frame where the
antenna will be mounted in the planar scanner.
The support frame of the planar scanner for the antenna under test (AUT) can be
seen in Figure 6.1. It is important that the final support structure allows the connection
with this frame and at the same time enable the collocation of the absorbers.

in order to understand the effect of the structure on the final radiation pattern.

computer did not allow such heavy simulation in terms of RAM memory. However, it

can be seen in the figure how the structure designed provides the stiffness necessary and
at the same time minimizes the reflections (it can be seen in the width of the support).

6.2
3D printed reflector antenna
According to the analysis made in Chapter 2 powder based technologies are one of the
candidates for 3D printing antennas at high frequency with high gain. For this particular
project, the technology chosen is Selective Laser Melting. It was already introduced in
the first chapter that the main challenges of 3D printing technologies are the roughness
and the metalization process. The technology SLM was chosen due to the fact that
direct metal printing is possible, avoiding in that way post-processing after printing the
antenna. The material chosen was aluminum.
The Teknologisk Institut in Aarhus was the company in charge of the manufacturing
process. The tolerance from the printers is equal to ±0.2 mm. The most critical part of
the 3D printing process are the overhanging parts of the design. It was not possible to
find a printing direction for which support structures did not affect either the subreflector
or the horn antenna.
Figure 6.8 shows the final printing direction of the antenna. The support structure
(red and yellow parts in the figure) are removed after printing. It can be seen that the
most critical part will be the subreflector since the surface after removing the support
structure may be very rough.
One possible solution could have been to design a geometrical structure to avoid the
overhanging parts of the antenna and therefore the risk of roughness. Nevertheless, due
to limitations in time it was not possible.

The distance between the AUT and the probe was 2.5 cm, which represents 5 wave-

trade off between minimizing multiple reflections and valid region of the far field. The
size of the scan surface was 300 mm by 300 mm. Some pictures of the set up for the
measurements can be seen in Appendix C.
The radiation pattern comparison between the simulations and the measurements
can be seen in Figure 7.5. The valid region of the far field goes from θ = −76.6◦to
θ = 76.6◦. These results are interpolated, the row data is presented in Appendix B.
The peak directivity measured is 33.05 dBi which is in good agreement with the
simulated one, 33.08 dBi. It means that the final aperture efficiency achieved by SLM
for 60 GHz is 63.12%. It is clearly observed that there is a shift in the main beam of
the antenna. This could be due to different factors:
• Manufacturing errors in the antenna that may create a phase shift in the aperture
illumination of the main reflector.
• Manufacturing errors in the structure that fix the antenna to the frame of the
measurement facility.
• Misalignment between the probe and the AUT.
To evaluate the phase shift, a UV plot of the radiation pattern has been carried out.
In Figure 7.6 the measured directivity of the antenna can be seen in a 2D plot. The
range of θ represented in the figure goes from −8◦to −8◦.
According to the UV radiation pattern, the maximum of the radiation pattern is at
ϕ = 178◦, θ = 1◦.
It would be necessary a deep analysis of the radiation pattern of the antenna to
understand the source of this beam shift. Source reconstruction with the data in the
near field could be a way to determine whether the beam shift is due to manufacturing
or measurement errors.
In addition to the shifting of the main beam, the cross polarization of the antenna
presents discrepancies with respect to the simulations, mainly in the E-plane.
The
cross polarization is intended to have very low levels, therefore any error either in the
manufacturing process or measurement of the antenna will be critical.
The cross-polarization could be due to the roughness of the surface of the antenna.
Nevertheless, it can not be concluded that this is the reason, since the antenna could be
also tilted with respect to the AUT and it could explain these values.
One way of addressing the problem could be calculating the axial ratio of the electric
field. It can be seen in Figure 7.7 that the value of the axial ratio is high for the maximum
of the radiation pattern. Since a high value of cross polarization is measured for this
direction, it seems that the antenna could be rotated with respect to the AUT and this
may be causing these discrepancies in the measured cross polarization. Equations (7.2)
and (7.3) were used for the calculations. In the equations δ represents the different in
phase between the components θ and ϕ of the electric field whereas Φ represents the
rotation angle of the polarization ellipse.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
8.1
General conclusions
This work began describing the wide range of antennas that have been designed using
different rapid prototyping technologies. However, none of these antennas were designed
and tested with metal 3D printing at high frequency (V-band), with high gain in a
compact size. Throughout this Master Thesis, the design, simulation, manufacturing
and testing of such an antenna has been carried out.
A possible application for this design would be Intercubesat communications. For
this particular project, ESA’s “Cost-effective high-gain cubesat antennas’’ invitation to
tender has been used to define the specifications of the design.
The comparison between the different reflector configurations, showed that the max-
imum aperture efficiency that can be achieved using an ideal Gaussian feed is in the
vicinity 70 −80%.
The offset dual configuration was the antenna design which showed an optimal trade-
off between directivity, side lobe levels, and cross polarization. More specifically the off-
set Gregorian reflector antenna is the one with better electrical properties and therefore
the choice for the final design.
Other configurations could have been simpler to design for 3D printing. However,
the aim was to test the performance of AM when evaluating the most promising antenna,
even though it meant adding some degrees of complexity when testing the technology
itself.
The analysis of the impact of the feed design in the radiation characteristics of the
antenna showed that it is a critical element of the antenna system. The design of the
feed affects the illumination of the main reflector in amplitude and phase, additionally
it can present undesired effects due direct radiation of the feed. Furthermore, for the
frequencies of operation, the feed will be the limiting factor on the system, as it shown
in the simulations.
On the other hand, different feeds could have been considered in the design to opti-
mize the performance of the antenna. However, the system was already rather complex
using the smooth-walled conical horn antenna as feed and having to design a rectangular
to circular waveguide transition and a bend.
The values measured for the reflection coefficient show a good matching in the entire
band of operation. However it was not possible to get rid of the undesired effects of
the coaxial to waveguide transition. Hence, it was not possible to determine the effect
of the roughness of the surface, and only the ripples in the reflection coefficient can be
==PAGE:122==
106
8 Conclusion
attributed to this rough surface.
Regarding the radiation pattern of the antenna, a good agreement with the simula-
tions for the copolar component can be observed. The beam shift is not very pronounced
and since the alignment process is not perfect and the support structure of the antenna
was not completely parallel to the measurement coordinate system, it can not be con-
cluded that this depointing of the maximum is due to manufacturing errors.
Furthermore, the cross polarization presents some discrepancies with respect to the
simulations. A study of the axial ratio of the polarization of the antenna indicates that
it could be due to a slight rotation of the AUT with respect to the probe.
The directivity measured for 60 GHz is very close to the value obtained in the sim-
ulations since only a difference of 0.03 dB was observed. The aperture efficiency of the
design is 63.12%, which demonstrates the potential of this manufacturing technology.
On the whole, the overall performance of the 3D printed antenna is in good agreement
with the simulations, despite the errors that may be induced by other factors already
pointed out. Finally, it is seen how the advantage of SLM over other 3D printing tech-
nologies relies in the direct metal printing of the antenna, which reduces the complexity
of the designing process. A deep analysis of the antenna, like for instance an analysis of
the gain could determine whether this direct metal printing technologies are preferred
to other technologies or not.
8.2
Future work
In this Thesis the analysis of metal 3D printing has been carried out for high gain and
high frequency compact antenna. The investigation carried out in this document could
be extended by focusing on the following research lines:
• Post-processing of the near field and far field measurements in order to determine
possible effects due to manufacturing errors when 3D printing or surface roughness.
• Post-finishing of the structure to test the improvement in the performance of the
antenna.
• Manufacturing of the antenna with other technologies like SLS or SLA in order
to compare and determine the advantages and disadvantages of metal 3D printing
over such technologies.
• If the 3D printing results are satisfying enough, more complex designs for feed
systems could be carried out, in addition to reflectors shaping, in order to improve
the overall performance of the antenna and test the potential of the technology.
