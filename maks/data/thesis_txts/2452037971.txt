Abstract
Historically arch bridges have been used for construction, recognizing the statically beni’¼ücial arch
shape, that can carry vertical loads, without being exposed to severe bending moments. In the recent
years the arch shape is seldom seen as the construction becomes complex and demanding compared
to ’¼éat beam elements. The pearl chain method provides a solution to this where ’¼éat pre-fab elements
are combined together in an arch-shape by a post tension cable.
The method has already proved e’¼ācient for small span bridges. The purpose of the study have
been to investigate if the pearl chain method can be used to construct multi-span bridge, with spans
up to 100m. Initially 4 bridge designs were proposed and through investigations on construction,
material consumption, CO2-emissions and bearing resistance it was indicated that an open-spandrel
arch bridge would be the best solution to comply with the pearl chain method and a major span.
Through a preliminary static analysis an optimal design of the arch was found to be a two-hinged,
circular shaped arch with a rise-span ratio of 1/8. In the design phase it was seen that arch elements
of 10.3m was optimal and the spandrels should be connected near the connection of the arch-elements,
leading to the proposed arch design:
In an ULS-analysis it was shown that arch elements of (3.5mx1.5m) provided su’¼ācient bearing ca-
pacity towards bending moments and buckling. Additionally the axial compression from post tension
forces and the arch thrust induced a su’¼ācient bearing capacity towards shear forces.
Due to the size of the bridge it was found that some of the usual bene’¼üts of the PC-method is
removed. As the arch is likely to be constructed by formwork, the big arch elements seems costly to
transport and the arch must be constructed with the use of a tension-tie system.
In the end it has been shown, that the pearl chain method can be used to create arches covering large
spans. However, further detailed analysis on the di’¼Ćerent bridge types and more practical experience
with the pearl chain method must be gained, before concluding whether the pearl chain method
introduces any bene’¼üts compared to the existing bridge construction methods.

Introduction
The following project will serve as a thorough investigation of a segmental bridge, based on the
pearl chain method. The idea is to analyze the applicability of the pearl-chain method, when used
in a multi-span bridge with large span. In this project the proposed Kattegat-link will serve as a
reference for the analysis carried out.
The Pearl Chain Method is a way to create arch-shaped structures with several ’¼éat elements that
are post tensioned together. The method introduces an e’¼ācient transportation of ’¼éat elements and
a fast assembly of otherwise complex structures. This thesis will be based on existing knowledge and
experience with the Pearl Chain Method, combined with theory of arches, prestressed concrete, etc.
When constructing major-span bridges, several design types are typically considered in the pre-
liminary phase. Through considerations regarding construction, material consumption and CO2-
emissions, overall bearing resistance and economy, a certain design is considered optimal and is
veri’¼üed through a thorough analysis. This thesis will follow a similar process.
Initially 4 bridge types are considered and through an analysis on construction and material con-
sumption, the two bridge types that seem most advantagous are selected. Through a preliminary
analysis of the two bridge types, general material consumption, CO2-emissions and dimensions is
analyzed and one bridge type is chosen the most advantageous design. This bridge will then be
investigated in a thorough analysis from which a ’¼ünal design of cross section properties and bridge
probertied will be proposed.
As aforementioned this master thesis will deal with a range of subjects within the theory of arches,bridges,
concrete, prestressing and construction methods. In order to clarify the layout of the report, a short
explanation of the proccess throughout the thesis follows in ’¼ügure 0.1.

1.1
Pearl Chain Method
The basic concept of pearl-chain reinforcement is to compress elements together, by a post tension
cable through a series of pre-fabricated elements. The method can be used to create varying shapes,
vaults, domes, ribs and arches. In the present thesis, the focus will be on pearl-chain arch bridges.
In general, multiple ’¼éat elements will be post tensioned together, forming the shape of an arch.
Curved ducts are cast into each element, making the post tension cable follow a desired arch shape.
The elements used are ’¼éat and the appearance will be that of an arch-polygon, as depicted in ’¼ügure

Connecting Pearl Chain Elements
In between pre-fabricated elements a strong mortar will be cast, which will serve two purposes. The
strong mortar will achieve a su’¼ācient compression strength for post-tension faster than regular con-
crete, and secondly it creates a strong connection between the pre-fab elements.

in the joints as well
After assembly the ducts are ’¼ülled with grout that provides an anti-corrosive environment to the

Last, it should be mentioned that the post-tension systems are vulnerable from a construction per-

the cables. Utilizing the PC-method to create arches introduces many on-site connections between
the ducts of each arch-element, so in the construction phase great focus must be on avoiding dust
and dirt from entering the arch.

However statically a 3-hinged arch lack a measure of reserve-strength. A lowering of the arch crown,
will increase the arch thrust, hence also the outward reaction forces - which further increases the
distance between supports, and thereby additionally lowering the crown. An e’¼Ćect that becomes

2-hinged Arches In two-hinged arches a shortening of the arch axis will increase the positive bending
moments in the arch, but reduce the arch thrust, so counteracting the tendency. And an increase in
distance between supports will usually lead to a decrease in bending moments and arch thrust aswell.
The hingeless arch is very sti’¼Ćand is usually subject to smaller de’¼éections than other arches. It has
similar abilities as the 2-hinged arch when it comes to the reserve strength. However large forces will
be transferred to the supports, thus requiring a solid foundation/supporting system.

5.
Discussion
5
Discussion
Generally it has been shown that spans similar to the Kattegat Link are possible to construct with
the use of the PC-method. However, some concerns about structural stability and actual e’¼Ćect of
the PC-method, remains unsolved. The following section will highlight some of the most important
concerns and discuss the process and method of the thesis.
5.1
Process and Method
In the preliminary analysis it was indicated that an open-spandrel arch was the optimal bridge type,
to comply with the PC-method. However, the selection process was based on assumptions of weight,
construction methods and CO2-emissions. And it is acknowledged that a more thorough analysis
could prove a di’¼Ćerent bridge type to be optimal.
An example of this is the rigid Box-girder and Truss-girder bridges, that would yield a possibility
to avoid a tension-tie system. Furthermore the girder bridges implies using smaller arch elements,
which could cause a more cost-e’¼ācient transport of elements and could yield a possibility to connect
the arch elements without formwork, because the moments from a two-hook lift would be reduced.
Additionally, the truss-girder bridge was assumed constructed with steel trusses, which showed to
induce a signi’¼ücant increase of embodied CO2. A design with concrete trusses could be investigated
in order to lower the embodied CO2, however this was not done as concrete sections in pure tension
seemed complex to construct.
5.2
PC-method and arch
Up until now the PC-bridges investigated have been of small-medium spans and the general knowl-
edge about the method is based on smaller spans. Therefore there is a general question of how the
PC-method is used with a span of 100m.
As the elements length is of 10m there will be a chance of misalignment between the elements, which
will introduce an eccentricity between the elements. Additionally ’¼üve ducts must be connected pr.
mortar joint and a slight misalignment in this connection, will decrease the e’¼Ćect of the post tension
cables, decreasing the overall bearing capacity of the arch.
Therefore it is likely that PC-bridges of this scale, must be carried out with great precision on the
construction work. Which is likely to increase the construction cost.
5.2.1
Static behaviour
In the preliminary analysis, the bearing resistance of the arch proved to be su’¼ācient. The large axial
compression in the arch proved to create an acceptable resistance towards shear forces and bending
moments, in both arch elements and in the mortar joint.
The arches where designed as wide arches that are stable towards out of plane buckling alone. This
was decided to avoid extra connections from sti’¼Ćeners and to have a strong arch when standing alone
during the construction phase. However, an investigation to construct the arch with slender arches
Page 61 of 115
==PAGE:69==
connected with sti’¼Ćeners would likely reduce the arch dimensions signi’¼ücantly. And is a discussion
topic to increase the functionality of the open-spandrel arch bridge.
Because the arch need to transfer all forces from deck and spandrels, the arch is subject to rather
severe bending moments inducing a large cross-section of 3.5x1.5m and in general the open-spandrel
arch did not result in a light element. Therefore it is questionable if an Open-Spandrel arch is an
advantageous bridge type when spans are this long and the tra’¼āc load is described by a class150
tra’¼āc load.
There have not been focus on the horizontal resistance of the bridge piers and their possible move-
ment. When neighbouring arches are loaded di’¼Ćerently from tra’¼āc, this will induce a di’¼Ćerence in
the incoming horizontal force, that the pier must transfer in bending. Therefore the bridge piers
must be designed with an additional bending capacity, compared to piers supporting ’¼éat beams.
This might induce movements of the piers, which will induce additional bending moments in the
arch. This concern can lead to increased capacity of the bridge pier to avoid deformation, or of
the arch to resist the additional moments created by the movements of its support. This concern
however, complies to all arch-bridges and not speci’¼ücally with the PC-method.
5.2.2
Construction
The general step-by-step method creating the open-spandrel arch, seems very applicable with the
PC-method and like a good pre-fab construction method. However, some concerns still remains,
which are discussed in the following section.
The tension-tie system will induce extra construction work and more importantly the systems cannot
be removed until the entire arch is placed, which might also have a negative impact on the tra’¼āc
below the bridge.
It was seen that a two-hook lift induces large moments in the arch. The existing PC-arch bridges
have all been connected standing sideways, and then rotated in the air into an up-right position
to avoid the use of formwork. Because of the arch-size it seems that the best way to connect the
arch-elemnts is by using some sort of formwork, exempli’¼üed by a steel structure in this thesis.
As the arch elements are constructed in rather large dimensions (10.3x1.5x3.5)m, it is questionable
if transportation of the elements is cost-e’¼ācient. A more cost-e’¼ācient method could prove to have
a production facility near the site, to reduce transportation issues.
In the design phase it was found bene’¼ücial to connect the arch and spandrels near the connection
of the arch elements. This was based on keeping internal bending moments to a minimum and to
have the arch elements geometrically identical. However, this introduced varying distance between
the spandrels and therefore a demand for the road deck to be produced in di’¼Ćerent lengths.

6.
Conclusion
6
Conclusion
The increased puplic focus on environment and CO2-emissions, might force the building industry
to implement green solutions in a faster pace than usual. The pearl chain method can help reduce
the material consumption, by using optimized structural shapes and fast construction, if used in
appropriate structures.
Based on the analysis in this thesis, it has been demonstrated that major spans could be created with
the PC-method. However, the e’¼Ćectivity of the PC-method seems reduced when spans becomes this
large and the investigated open-spandrel arch do not necessarily reduce the CO2-emissions, compared
to other bridge types.
Based on initial analysis it was demonstrated that: a back’¼ülled arch will become too heavy for such
a major span and the box-girder arch seemed too complex to comply with the PC-method. In a
preliminary analysis the truss-girder arch proved to be a rigid and light weight construction. How-
ever, the steel trusses resulted in large amount of embodied CO2 and the general construction of the
truss-girder seemed more complex than the open-spandrel arch. So the ’¼ünal analysis was carried out
on the open-spandrel arch.
Through a preliminary static analysis the optimal design of the arch was found to be a two-hinged-,
circular shaped arch, with a rise-span ratio of 1/8. It was found that arch elements of 10.3m and
spandrels placed 400mm from the connection between arch elements, was optimal. This resulted in
