Abstract 
The rise of additive manufacturing (AM) has made it possible to produce the designs found through 
topology optimisation (TO). This thesis is investigating the possibilities of designing printable 3D 
structures. The focus is on compliant mechanisms (CM) that are meant to deform, unlike the typical 
minimum compliance problem. The main benefit of CM is reduced number of parts needed in a 
mechanism.  
For analysing large deformations, nonlinear geometry has been implemented and verified with long 
cantilever beam example. The material nonlinearity is also added for more physically accurate 
deformation of mechanism compared to a linear case. The material model chosen for analysis is a 
modified neo-Hookean material. In addition to usual SIMP scheme, interpolation scheme for fictitious 
domain is also exploited to reduce the risk of numerical instability. Robust formulation is used to enforce 
length scale and improve manufacturability. 
The test problems used to demonstrate the results are cantilever beam, force inverter and newly 
developed bottle opener. The problems were run on parallel framework UTOP, which is based on 
PETSc. The benefit of this framework is possibility to use unstructured mesh, which is demonstrated 
with the new bottle opener optimisation problem. 
The benchmark problem of the cantilever beam under a large load yieldexpected result, but continuation 
of 𝛽 in projection filter should be slowed down for achieving crisp design. The result is also mesh-
dependent, but more stable than with a smaller element size. This is not seen as a setback, because 
the code can still be verified. 
It is seen that CM problems with parameter sets used for linear analysis are not physically admissible 
and cannot be solved with nonlinear methods. The same problem arises with both the force inverter 
and the bottle opener. A set of parameters is provided for testing the sensitivities of force inverter. The 
inverter is also used to demonstrate the impact of passive domains and usefulness of robust 
formulation.  
The bottle opener problem gave reasonable results and the possibility to use unstructured mesh is 
welcomed for connecting TO and AM. 
Keywords: additive manufacturing, topology optimisation, geometrical nonlinearity, material 
nonlinearity, compliant mechanism, hyperelastic material.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Scarcity of raw materials, rising prices and the environmental impact of production processes is shaping 
the future of manufacturing. Additive manufacturing (AM) (or 3D-printing) is a relatively new technology 
that, according to ASTM, includes seven different ways to fabricate an object layer-by-layer. Compared to 
traditional manufacturing procedures, there are fewer geometrical limits and less machines needed for 
direct production from a 3D-model. The range of materials suitable for AM compared to materials used in 
traditional manufacturing is smaller but growing. 
AM is seen as one option for moving towards industrial sustainability. Replacing traditional subtractive 
manufacturing (SM) like milling or cutting with AM reduces material waste as the unused substance is 
reusable. Since less machines are needed, production can be brought closer to customers and supply 
chain shortened. Minimising the number of parts in an assembly is another way to simplify logistics. This 
means reduction in transportation, assembling time and stocked inventory [1]. Weight of structures and 
material usage can also be improved by using different infills. There are still problems with metal powders 
as significant amount of energy is needed for producing the substance and later to melt it in the AM system 
[2]. This can be reduced by increasing powder recovery which is estimated to be at 95-98%. 
How to use AM to its full potential? By letting go of constraints coming from SM and focusing on the purpose 
of the part. Topology optimisation (TO) can be used for that. This is an iterative process used to distribute 
material in a predefined domain by minimising or maximising a selected objective and following problem 
setup [3]. The setup may include boundary conditions, loads, passive domains and limit on material volume. 
Design of the structure in the domain is closely monitored and changed based on sensitivities of objective 
function. One of the most popular methods of TO is density approach, where continuous design variable is 
assigned to each element in Finite element method (FEM) problem.  
The common goal of structural TO is increasing the stiffness and reducing displacement. Examples of such 
rigid structures are bridges and beams. Maximising the displacement at chosen points results in compliant 
mechanisms (CM) e.g. gripper [4] or force inverter [4]–[6]. In [4], the CM is described as mechanism that 
depends on flexibility of all its members instead of being divided into rigid parts, springs and joints. The 
benefit of CMs over traditional rigid versions comes from decreasing the number of elements. Having less 
parts in an assembly results in fewer contact surfaces, smaller wear of the system and therefore longer life 
expectancy with less maintenance. The expected deformation of CM is assumed to stay in elastic region 
and internal forces can be exploited to restore original shape. On the other hand, designer must be cautious 
about the stresses in the deformed part that must not surpass the yield strength. 
Research on CM in TO started almost 30 years ago with simple crunching mechanism in 1994 [7]. A brief 
overview about the beginning is given in [4], where the CM problem is optimised for continuum-type 2D 
design domain with the assumption of linearly elastic response. Even though the material can be assumed 
to undergo small strains, the loaded structure may reach large displacements (rotations). Geometrical 
nonlinearity is implemented for that in [8]. 
Material nonlinearity was also accommodated into structural analysis at around same time in [9], where 
path-independent hyperelastic material model was assumed. The St. Venant–Kirchhoff model used there 
has problem of collapsing in compression. Better alternatives like modified versions of the model or 
compressible Neo-Hookean materials are presented and compared in [10]. 
Structural TO is used to separate design domain into void and solid features. The stiffness and other 
material parameters of an element in a mesh that are dictated by density, have very low values in the non-
material part of structure. These elements may behave in unwanted way and endanger the convergence 
of the whole system. An interpolation scheme that treats solid and void elements differently is utilised for 
prevention [3]. The main idea is to divide the elements into geometrically linear and nonlinear category 
based on the element stiffness. 
With the increase in computational resources, the design domain with third dimension has become more 
common for continuum type mechanisms. MATLAB code for 3D design domain solving linear minimum 
compliance, CM and heat conduction TO problems was made available for students in 2014 [11]. A faster 
and more compact version for solving linear minimum compliance problem is presented in [12] from 2020. 
==PAGE:9==

Filters based on Helmholtz-type differential equations have been tested on 3D-structures with holes in [13]. 
Geometrical nonlinearity is combined with CM problem in [5]. 
1.2 Objective 
The purpose of this project is to explore the 3D mechanism designs considering geometrical and material 
nonlinearity using PETSc-based optimisation framework with unstructured mesh. How material and 
geometric nonlinearities affect the morphing functionality is still an open question and deserves further 
study. A good way to test implemented code is by solving a CM problem. Besides already known problems, 
a new one is created with unstructured mesh. 
TO and AM are perfect fit and popularity of both topics has increased over last decades. Using unstructured 
meshes expands the domain shape design possibilities. As mentioned before, three-dimensional models 
are more expensive and have gained popularity only in the last years. Utilising the parallel framework with 
numerical library of Portable and Extendable Toolkit for Scientific Computing (PETSc) enables to speed up 
the calculation time. Its suitability for TO has been investigated in [14] and tested further in [15]. A more 
practical application of PETSc and unstructured mesh is demonstrated in [16]. Same system, including the 
Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) [17] for solving the material distribution problem is taken as the basis 
for this project. 
A robust formulation from [18] is applied to improve manufacturability within chosen tolerance. Even though 
the precision of AM has increased, there are always deviations from the perfect shape and implementing 
this decreases the chances of scrapping details.  
1.3 Outline 
Section 2 goes through the governing equations for finite element formulation which includes geometrically 
nonlinear elasticity and modified neo-Hookean material model. The optimisation problems chosen for the 
project are also presented there. The TO formulation with used filters, robust formulation and interpolation 
schemes is described in section 3 with numerical implementation. Section 5 presents the results and 
discussions. Conclusions are given in section 6.

2 Problem modelling 
This chapter explains, how TO problems are solved with the assumption of geometrical and material 
nonlinearity. The main part of structural analysis is based on Finite Element Method (FEM). Compared to 
linear case the FEM calculations yield more accurate results but require more steps.  
The assumption is used to solve three topology problems: cantilever beam with a line load, force inverter 
and a bottle opener. First two are well known benchmark examples that have been used for years. The 
third one is a fresh challenge used to explore the possibilities of a nonlinear solver and practicality of CMs.  
2.1 Geometrical and material nonlinearity 
Linear problem means that relation between input load and output deformation can be described with a 
linear function. This works only in case of geometrical linearity for small deformations and material linearity 
applicable to certain materials. An example of geometrical linearity can be small deflection of a cantilever 
beam solved with Euler beam theory. Material linearity can be seen among many steels (DIN EN 
10088:2014) and aluminium alloys (DIN EN 485:2016) before the relative deformation reaches 0.2%. After 
that, material behaviour becomes nonlinear and then plastic. 
The linear analysis is simple, because the history of deformation is not considered. The stiffness matrix is 
computed using only material parameters, density and undeformed mesh element dimensions. However, 
there are couple of aspects that are not considered. Firstly, loading the structure causes stresses, but 
stiffness related to the stress is not considered. Leaving out the additional stiffness caused by stress in the 
structure means that theoretical deformation is greater than and different from reality. Another problem is 
directly related to deformation of the structure. Change in cross-section area affects true stresses. Since 
the linear analysis uses original area, the stresses can exceed the yield strength. 
It is safer to assume geometrical nonlinearity, which allows to find solutions for more problems e.g. bending 
of a long beam, a snap-through structures seen in [19] or path-generating mechanisms like in [20]. 
Geometrical nonlinearity can also be used for generating the microstructure of auxetic material with 
negative Poisson’s ratio like in Figure 2.1. Having the correct deformation field is crucial in achieving desired 
material parameters. 
 
Figure 2.1 Microstructure of material with negative Poisson's ratio of -0.8. Vertical symmetry is used in 40x40 design 
domain. Taken from [21]. 
Assumption of nonlinear material is necessary for materials with nonlinear elasticity curve such as rubber. 
It can survive substantial elastic deformations without becoming plastically deformed. Compression is 
another reason to use material nonlinearity for numerous materials. Increase of cross-section area reduces 
stresses inside the feature, but that is not considered in linear analysis. Even the older nonlinear models 
have problems with that and hyperelasticity should be used to get more realistic results.

2.2 Finite element method 
Finite element method is the basis for deciding the material distribution. Most deformed elements will be 
the first to become solid.Design domain is divided into isoparametric hexahedral elements with eight nodes. 
By combining number of nodes to three degrees of freedom in spatial coordinate system, the size of 
elemental displacement vector is 24x1. Hexahedral element like in Figure 2.2 is positioned in global 
coordinate system “xyz” and has its own local reference coordinate system “𝜉𝜂𝜁”. 
 
Figure 2.2 Hexahedral element with local coordinate system before and after distortion. The 8 nodes of element are 
marked with red dots. 
The isoparametric formulation allows hexahedrons to have other shapes than rectangular. Since there are 
no nodes in the middle of the edges, the faces and edges cannot be curved. This problem can be solved 
by using finer mesh. Shape functions are used to describe, what happens inside the element, and to move 
that information between physical and reference element. The matrices needed for strain-displacement 
matrix 𝑩 are calculated at reference level. 
As explained before, FEM as the approximate method depends heavily on mesh element type and size. 
The analysis is less likely to give correct results for a structure with large elements. Even though the strains 
and stresses are typically calculated in several Gauss points inside the element, the density of sample 
points is simply too low for accurate predictions. On the other hand, a mesh with too small elements 
consumes significantly more resources while the accuracy of the FEM analysis does not improve 
proportionally. The sufficient element size can be determined with convergence studies, which is not done 
here. The size of the elements needed for sufficiently accurate results is bigger than the resolution required 
for acceptable TO designs. 
More about isoparametric hexahedral elements can be read in chapter 6 of [22], which also has most of 
the steps needed for implementation. Some equations are taken from [23] that focuses more on 
nonlinearity. Body forces are not considered to simplify the calculations and the analysed structure is 
assumed to have stresses small enough that the plastic deformation is avoided while loaded.

A cantilever beam from [3] is used to test the implemented code for minimum compliance problem. One

Figure 2.6 Design domain and boundary conditions for minimum end-compliance of a cantilever beam. 
Force inverter in Figure 2.7 is one of the most common CM problems used already in [21]. The idea is to 
create a mechanism, where red and green areas move in the opposite directions. For example, pushing 
the red rectangle in the positive x-directions moves the green rectangle in negative direction and closer to 
the former area. The typical design domain for this problem is a cube, but double symmetry marked here 
with grey sides can be used. The dimensions are therefore 256×128×128 mm. The symmetry forces 
restrictions to the domain as the movement in the direction normal to the plane is not allowed while the 
displacement in the other two directions is. The hatched corner of rectangle marks the support fixed in 
every direction. Since both force and displacement are inverted, the problem is also called displacement 
inverter. 
 
Figure 2.7 Design domain and boundary conditions for maximum output displacement of a force inverter with larger 
impact areas. 
The spring between two red squares indicates a simple actuator, defined by force and stiffness. No specific 
material has been chosen for most of examples. The used parameters are 𝐸𝑚𝑎𝑥= 1 MPa and 𝜈= 0.3. 
Other variables are given in results chapter or in example descriptions. Two meshes with different input, 
output and support area sizes are used for this problem: 25% and 12.5% of domain side length per contact 
area. The contact areas are defined as surfaces on passive domains shaped as a cube.

3 Topology optimisation formulation 
A density-based structural topology optimisation is considered as the basis of the whole project. Topology 
of a structure is described elementwise with density ρe that can have value in the range of: 
0 ≤𝜌𝑒≤1.  
(3.1) 
The upper limit “1” marks that element is solid and lower limit “0” is for void. The typical use of colours for 
different densities is black indicating solid and white marking void elements. The intermediate variants that 
have not reached one of the two desired values are called grey areas. These grey elements in the mesh 
can be interpreted as areas of material with different microstructures. It is argued in [21] that all materials 
have some kind of structure, in some cases it is just on an atomic level. Therefore, there is no certain way 
to separate structures from materials. It is more like a range of materials with increasing cell size. 
The unfiltered density field with element 𝜌𝑒 does not represent the physical design that should be used for 
manufacturing. Instead, the filtered density field 𝝆̃̅ with 
0 ≤𝜌̃̅𝑒≤1.  
(3.2) 
is the base for optimisation. It is used to determine weight, stiffness or some other material parameter of 
the element as ratio to maximum. 
The procedure of nonlinear TO for CM can be divided into steps 
1. Read in the mesh files for all cores and multigrid levels 
2. Initialise load, design, additional stiffness and output vectors, change=1, number of iterations i=0 
3. While: change >0.01 and i < iteration limit 
a. Filter the design field vector 𝝆𝑖 
b. Solve FE problem with filtered densities 𝝆̃̅𝑖 
c. Calculate filtered sensitivities based on filtered densities 𝝆̃̅𝑖 
d. Reverse the filtered sensitivities to be suitable for 𝝆𝑖 
e. Write filtered design and deformation fields obtained from FEM 
f. 
Get the new design field 𝝆𝑖+1 with MMA 
g. Calculate the change for next loop 
h. If continuation is used, update parameters 
4. Post processing 
5. Write final design field with old deformation field 
The final change in design is assumed to be small enough to use old deformation field. Post-processing 
means that after the problem has converged, a sharp threshold filter is applied to the design. This step is 
voluntary and more important when design would be manufactured. In other cases, the non-discreteness 
can be an important indicator for evaluating the results.

3.2 Interpolation schemes 
When the design obtained through TO is manufactured, elements with intermediate or minimum density

and can cause numerical problems. Interpolation schemes are used to reduce influence of these elements.

3.3 Sensitivity analysis 
The essence of TO is solving a mathematical function. It starts with a guess which is then used for 
calculating an objective. Likely, the first guess is not correct and needs to be modified. Derivative of the 
function is used to find the right direction towards optimum. In TO, this process is called sensitivity analysis. 
The objective function of CM in (3.7) is based on a displacement field. However, it is common to consider 
only design variable as the basis of TO and interpret the displacement field as a function of the variable 
that in this project is density. Equilibrium equation (2.3) in linear analysis can easily be used to understand, 
how displacements and density are linked. For nonlinear analysis, where (2.5) is combined with (2.4), the 
connection is not that straightforward. In both cases, the function’s dependence on deformation means that 
chain-rule should be used.

3.4 Filtering 
It is very important to get manufacturable results after applying TO to a design problem. When design 
domain is discretised as a coarse mesh, results are typically more robust and easier to physically produce. 
On the other hand, shape and size of the mesh element can have a strong influence on optimised topology 
and FEM results. The main goals are: 
1. Manufacturability – smoother borders are preferred 
2. Crisp black and white topology – design without grey areas 
3. Mesh-independency – apply length scale 
4. No checkerboards – must be avoided 
To improve the producibility, the optimised model based on a coarse mesh needs post-processing that can 
prolong the design process and increase cost of development. Second problem of topology with too big 
elements is derived by regions with smaller loads. In that case the feature does not need to be as thick and 
==PAGE:23==
16 
strong as the rest of structure. This is represented by grey areas. Both problems can be relieved by 
introducing finer mesh, but that leads to other difficulties. Some features become too small and difficult to 
manufacture. This is also named mesh-dependency. The minimum thickness limit applied to features in the 
design is called length scale. Even worse is having checkerboard-like patterns, which show that material is 
needed in some way, but artificial stiffness from the lone solid elements tricks the programme into believing 
that structure is strong enough. 
To avoid these 4 issues, filtering is used. Both filters implemented here are based on changing density not 
sensitivity. A good paper for going over the basics is [31]. It includes a similar, but longer list of requirements 
to a suitable filter and introduces multiple available options. 
3.4.1 Density filter 
When a smaller region of design domain is investigated, the element in there is assumed to have density 
analogous to its neighbours’. The size of the element’s neighbourhood is defined by the distance from its 
centre. Whether the other elements are in the neighbourhood, is determined by their central coordinates.  
These regions are used to even out the differences in densities with a filter. Density filter is introduced in 
[9], where density of an element is recalculated as weighted average of other elements in the 
neighbourhood. The filter is applied to design field before FEM part. After computing the displacements, 
the sensitivities are modified to reverse filtering, and applied to the original design field. Since the density 
of an element is distributed over the domain, the total volume of the material is not affected and the 
unfiltered field can be used for volume constraint check. 
This filter significantly improves the topology. The radius of 1.2 times the element side length used in [32] 
is the common minimum. The closest neighbours that share a surface with the filtered one are considered. 
However, the minimal filter radius might not be sufficient to clear topology from all the small features and 
impose length scale to finer mesh. Too big filter radius can dilute the solid elements into void and blur the 
topology. The typical maximum is 10%, but 12,5% has also been used. 
The main filter used in this project is based on solution of Helmholtz partial differential equation (PDE) [13]. 
This can be used for filtering either density field or sensitivities, but only former is employed here.  
3.4.2 Threshold projection filter 
The main problem with density filter is that it creates new grey elements. Most of them become part of solid 
or void features during TO but applying the length scale has its own consequences. The intermediate values

3.5 Continuation methods 
Even though TO may take hundreds of iterations to converge, essence of the final design is revealed in 50 
or less depending on the nature of the problem. Moving from global to local topology needs more precise 
and stronger filters or interpolation schemes. Exact procedure can change the optimal design and must be 
planned thoroughly. For example, updating the parameters can be connected to convergence with a setup 
or just number of completed iterations. 
Precision is mostly related to density filter, where decreasing the radius reduces impact of distant elements. 
The importance of using right filter radius at the right time is explained in 0. Larger radius imposes length 
scale and smaller allows to connect solid features into one structure. Continuation method is commonly 
applied by starting with 10% of smallest dimension of the design domain as the filter radius [32]. This is 
slowly decreased to around 1.2 times the element size. 
Sharp Heaviside function hastens the convergence and ensures crisp binary designs but applying it too 
early might change the design. Figure 3.6 b) represents the typical continuation of threshold filter by 
increasing 𝛽 after every 30 iterations from 1 to 64. Increasing projection parameter over time allows the 
design to mature. Even though the binary design is achieved, the topology has changed and is less robust 
than without continuation. The hinges are thinner and in more locations.

3.7.1 Nonlinearities 
Geometrical and material nonlinearity are both using displacement field as input. First for stiffness matrix, 
second for elasticity tensor and stress matrix. This requires an iterative process, where external loads are 
divided into smaller increments. In this project, the load increments are equally sized. Even though the 
splitting increases computational time, the chance of reaching convergence with given limit on iterations 
grows. This is also seen in the decrease of iterations needed for solver to reach convergence. 
After finding displacement field, final stiffness matrix is used for linearised subproblem (3.20) for sensitivity 
vector. Displacement field is then written to a log file for plotting and cleared for next optimisation iteration 
as suggested in [32].  
The nonlinear solver SNES is used for equilibrium equation: 
𝒓(𝒖) = 𝒇𝑒𝑥𝑡−𝒇𝑖𝑛𝑡= 0     →       𝒇𝑒𝑥𝑡= 𝒇𝑖𝑛𝑡. 
(3.30) 
The 𝒇𝑒𝑥𝑡 is a predefined constant vector and 𝒇𝑖𝑛𝑡 is a function depending on displacement field. Using 
SNES solver has several steps: 
1. Setup of solver 
2. Setting the function to calculate internal forces – SNESSetFunction(); 
3. Setting the function to calculate tangential stiffness matrix – SNESSetJacobian(); 
4. Solving with external forces as one of the arguments – SNESSolve(); 
5. Getting information about solving 
It is also possible to use vector of zeros in solving, but it is difficult to move the external forces vector to 
SNESSetFunction();, which has a limited amount of arguments. 
3.7.2 Framework 
The whole project is built onto previously developed system that uses PETSc – Portable, Extensible Toolkit 
for Scientific Computation. This a fully parallel open-source numerical library that can be used well for TO. 
Dividing the computational workload between several cores allows to increase the number of elements in 
design domain which leads to better resolution and larger problems. PETSc can be used with codes written 
in Fortran, C/C++ and Python. An improved version of this framework, Unstructured Topology Optimisation 
(UTOP) with unstructured grid formulation developed in DTU TopOpt group is written in C++. Unstructured 
mesh means that the design domain is not pure combination of rectangular shapes. For example, there 
can be spherical cut-outs. 
It is recommended to use UTOP in Linux system. The example code used for introducing the framework is 
given in [30]. The code has mostly been written in personal computer because Visual Studio keeps track 
==PAGE:27==
20 
of every change in there. Same programme in the DTU’s gbar is tracking only commits and changes done 
during the session. DTU’s hpc cluster has been used for final figures and results presented here. 
MMA is the preferred solver for optimisation part of code mentioned in several references like [6], [16], [32]. 
This a stable and efficient algorithm that also works for large scale TO problems. The time spent on MMA 
is notably shorter than how much FEM takes. Like the multigrid solver and filter mentioned in previous 
chapters, the MMA is already implemented in PETSc.  
3.7.3 Load and stiffness 
The loads of inverter and bottle opener are implemented as tractions over surface and are given per 1 mm2. 
The stiffness and load for cantilever beam are given per node and total size depends on number of elements 
used. When switching between different grid levels, the input parameters must be checked to stay 
consistent. Safest option is to not change mesh used for TO. 
3.7.4 Robust formulation 
The standard setup of MMA in the UTOP framework is using only one function as objective. This is replaced 
for robust formulation with three objectives of eroded, intermediate and dilated design field. The new 
command line is “MMA mma(mesh.getGlobalDesignVariableSize(), m, x, at, ct, dt);”. 
The “m” marks number of constraints (4) and “at” connections with 𝑧 in (3.29) solver ({1, 1, 1, 0}). The rest 
can be taken from old code. MMA is used for updating design field with command mma.Update(x, dfdx, 
gx, dgdx, xmin, xmax);. Since objectives are treated as constraints, the sensitivities vector “dfdx” 
contains only zeros. “gx” is an array of four constraint values and “dgdx” a vector with sensitivities for all 
four constraints. This vector is created with VecDuplicate(); function, specific for PETSc framework. 
To write the sensitivity vectors into one, vectors are converted to arrays.  
Sometimes the projected design fields differ a lot from each other and yield topology very different from not 
robust version. This can be avoided by using average of objectives for first iterations. It is also good idea 
to check the asymptotes used for MMA. 
3.7.5 Mesh 
The input domain meshes are created with Cubit that exports files in Exodus II format. This programme is 
good for generating more complex design domains with passive domains. The minimum side length of 
mesh element is usually 1 unit, used here as 1 mm. The design domain defined in Cubit is remeshed 
several times for the multigrid solver, another method for improving computing time. Element size is usually 
doubled for new coarser grid level as seen in Figure 3.9. The design domain is then divided between cores 
by running a driver from UTOP framework. Most important parameters for that procedure are numbers of 
cores and multigrid levels. 
 
Figure 3.9 Three multigrid levels from finer to coarser. Meshing in the Cubit usually starts with finer mesh. 
The main reason to start meshing with smaller element size is to avoid unnecessary irregular element 
shapes. Even though the UTOP framework is meant for unstructured mesh, the information moves better 
in multigrid solver when the nodes have same coordinates. For that reason, passive domains are sized 
based on the coarsest mesh. When unstructured mesh is created, it is recommended to divide domain into 
smaller volumes for that time. Example code and meshes for bottle opener are given in Appendix F.

4.1 Cantilever beam 
The problem seen in Figure 2.6 is solved with geometric nonlinearity, modified neo-Hookean material and 
interpolation scheme for fictitious field. The aim is to convert 2D problem from [3] to 3D and use it to verify 
the implemented code. By increasing the load, design should become more asymmetric until the loaded 
nodes are connected to the rest of structure by just one bar. This is caused by extreme deformation of the 
structure while optimising and using only end-compliance as objective. The loaded nodes are so far from 
the original place that it is better to just strengthen the rest of the structure and use the least amount of 
material for connection to load. The continuation of penalisation factor has been applied by starting with 
𝑝= 1 and adding 0.05 at every second iteration until 𝑝=2. Then the same increment is added at every fifth 
iteration until 𝑝= 3 at 140th iteration. After that the 𝛽= 4 in projection filter is doubled at every 10th iteration 
until 𝛽= 64. The optimisation is continued until 250 iterations has been reached. The rest of parameters 
are given in A.2.

4.3 Bottle opener 
Bottle opener is a new CM problem similar to force inverter. All the contact areas are on one plane of 
symmetry and movement is parallel to the plane. Compared to other problems, this is more practical. 
Material parameters 𝐸𝑚𝑎𝑥= 1.6 GPa and 𝜈= 0.35 are chosen for ABS. Minimum input force is based on 
experiments. The total force needed for opening a wine bottle is around 280N. The design domain is only 
one quarter of the total problem and input area is 128 mm2. This means that pressure applied to 1 mm2 for 
pulling out the wine cork should be at least 0.55 N. Additional force is needed to deform the opener itself. 
Hence 1 N/mm2 is chosen. 
Most of the parameters for this problem have been defined by the choice of material and experiments. The 
two sets of chosen parameters are given in Table A.3. Main difference is the stiffness used for void 
elements. First set uses the ratio of 10−3𝐸𝑚𝑎𝑥 and second 10−9𝐸𝑚𝑎𝑥. It was good to see that disconnection 
from passive domains was not as big obstacle as for force inverter. When void stiffness was reduced, the 
volume expanded instead of diminishing. To decelerate the growth rate of solid domain, input and output 
stiffness were reduced too.  
Continuation of 𝛽 starts when 80th iteration is reached. The value is doubled after every 40th iteration until 
𝛽= 64. Its influence on convergence of objective and volume difference from limit are seen in Figure 4.17 
and Figure 4.18.

5 Conclusion 
The nonlinear geometry and modified neo-Hookean material model have been implemented successfully. 
The analytical part of code has been verified for solving nonlinear problems by looking at cantilever beam 
under extreme deformation. The beam was very long and used to check geometrically nonlinear part of the 
FEM code. The slight difference from analytical curves can be explained with the beam being thicker than 
in the original paper. 
Another cantilever beam was shorter and used for checking the combination of interpolation scheme for 
fictitious field, nonlinear geometry and nonlinear material. When 3D-problem is solved as 2.5D-problem 
with only one element in z-direction, it is possible to get results such as seen in earlier works. The objective 
in convergence study for that design domain is quite stable until 𝛽-continuation started. It might be fixed by 
using slower continuation scheme, where importance of grey elements is reduced at relaxed pace. Mesh 
element size has also significant influence on convergence. The mesh discretised as in [3] is better than 
mesh with half the element size. On the other hand, design is then mesh-dependent and should not be 
used for manufacturing real part. Another difficulty is asymmetry that could be solved by splitting the beam 
in the middle and solving only half assuming symmetry. The density filter radius of 12.5% is very useful for 
reducing noise in the void areas of design domain. 
Force inverter problem was run with different parameter setups. First was taken from earlier works to 
compare results and discuss the effect of passive domains generated using Cubit programme. It was shown 
that size of passive domain influences results in different ways and can yield unsuitable designs. Using 
robust formulation improved the results and reduced impact of domain size and shape. It is suggested to 
try passive domains with different shapes to use full potential of the UTOP framework. Second parameter 
set proved that physically admissible stiffness can be used for void elements without disconnection 
problems. Third set of parameters was chosen with nonlinear analysis in mind. Even though the perfect 
parameters could not be found, sensitivity check was used to verify the code with nonlinearities 
implemented. 
The bottle opener used two sets of parameters, where main difference was stiffness of void elements. Most 
of the parameters were determined by chosen material’s parameters and using experimental data. When 
stiffer void elements were used, the design included a bar connecting loaded nodes. When void elements 
have lower stiffness, the bar is replaced with strong and stiff handles. 
The problems with both CMs were similar. It is very difficult to find parameters suitable for nonlinear solver. 
The load and stiffnesses should be physically admissible, but still large enough to enforce connections 
between designed structure and passive domains. The stiffness of void elements is very important as it 
makes the TO process more sensitive towards the input parameters. 
The author sees potential in the combination of developed code and AM. It is good to see that with the 
growth of some features in chosen CM the effectiveness of infill is recognised, and features end up being 
empty. Depending on the chosen AM process, this helps to save on material consumption compared to 
SM. The bottle opener problem was a good and simple example to prove that.
