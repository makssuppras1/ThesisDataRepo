Abstract 
The purpose of this study was to contribute to the methodology of Absolute Environmental Sustainabil-
ity Assessments by providing an overview of the existing approaches to operationalising the Planetary 
Boundaries (PBs) and other Environmental Sustainability Boundaries (ESBs) relevant to climate 
change. The list of identified existing operationalisation approaches is the PB-based Life-Cycle Impact 
Assessment (LCIA) method, the Planetary Boundary Allowance impact assessment method (PBA’06), 
the carrying capacity-based normalisation references, biocapacity, Remaining Carbon Budget (RCB), 
and the emissions pathways scenarios C1 and C2. These six operationalisation approaches were applied 
to the case study of EU27+UK consumption footprint by the European Commission Joint Research 
Centre (EU-JRC) using six different allocation principles, namely Equal per Capita (EPC), Grandfa-
thering (GF), Economic Throughput (ET), Rights to Development (RTD), Biocapacity (BC), and Ca-
pacity (CA).  
The study showed that the operationalisation approaches differentiated much on the characteristic of 
being either static or dynamic. The static approaches can be used for setting emission targets and the 
dynamic approaches can be used for setting targets for the year of reaching net-zero emissions and 
show what the trajectory to getting there could be like. The application of the static approaches showed 
that the EU, in most cases, does not comply with the thresholds relevant for climate change. The appli-
cation of the dynamic approaches showed that the year of reaching net-zero is very uncertain depend-
ing on which allocation principle is used. Therefore, the year of reaching net-zero emissions varies too 
much for a meaningful conclusion to be made. Though, if the EU wants to take a precautionary ap-
proach to the uncertainty, the European Green Deal goal of reaching net-zero emissions in 2050 is not 
ambitious enough. When choosing an operationalisation approach several aspects should be considered 
including the objective vs Area of Protection (AoP), steady-state, the metric of the control- or opera-
tionalisation variable, and uncertainty of the data needed.

1 Introduction  
During the past decade, there has been an increasing focus on the need for moving away from relative sus-
tainability and toward absolute sustainability (Bjørn and Hauschild 2015; Bjørn et al. 2016; Hauschild 2015). 
Relative sustainability is useful in comparative Life-Cycle Assessments (LCAs) where the aim is to examine 
whether product A or product B is the better choice when considering their environmental impacts. Absolute 
sustainability aims to examine if a product or system is sustainable in an absolute context: instead of compar-
ing an environmental impact to other products or systems’ environmental impact, it is compared to a bound-
ary for that impact (Bjørn et al. 2020). These types of assessments are called Absolute Environmental Sus-
tainability Assessments (AESAs) and are a boundary-based approach to assessing environmental sustainabil-
ity. The boundaries used in AESAs are the so-called Environmental Sustainability Boundaries (ESBs) (Vea 
et al. 2020). An important example of ESBs is the planetary boundaries (PBs) which are defined for nine dif-
ferent environmental concerns with the objective of not pushing the Earth System out of its Holocene-like 
state (Rockström et al. 2009; Steffen et al. 2015). The aim of the PBs is to avoid human activities' interfer-
ence with the Earth’s System in such a way that irreversible changes occur.  
The boundary-based approach to sustainability also takes up an increased focus in the political agenda and is 
being incorporated into policy goals. Already in 2014, the EU published their “Environment Action Pro-
gramme to 2020” which had the title Living well, within the limits of our planet in which they described con-
cerns about the tipping points and planetary boundaries, and the need for promoting research efforts on the 
area (European Commission and Directorate-General for Environment 2014). Internationally, the Paris 
Agreement, announced in 2015, writes:  
“[…] aims to strengthen the global response to the threat of climate change, in the context of sustainable 
development […] by: Holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 °C above pre-
industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels, 
recognizing that this would significantly reduce the risks and impacts of climate change.”  
(United Nations 2015: Article 2.1.a) 
The Paris Agreement was a result of COP21 in Paris in 2015, and 55 countries signed the agreement in 2016 
(UNFCCC 2015). Subsequently, the EU launched the European Green Deal in 2020 which holds concrete 
goals for the EU to achieve the Paris Agreement, which includes a greenhouse gas (GHG) emission reduc-
tion of at least 55% compared to 1990 levels and climate neutrality (i.e. net-zero emissions) of Europe by 
2050 (European Commission 2020). These two goals are also a part of the “Environment Action Programme 
to 2030” (Council of the European Union and European Parliament 2022). These are all examples of ESBs 
having gained relevance in policymaking. Though, there is a difference between environmental impacts 
when taking a consumption-based perspective and a production-based perspective. Environmental policies

Operationalisation in absolute impact assessment methodology within LCA is a problem which has been 
identified by several studies (Clift et al. 2017; Sala et al. 2020, 2013). In an AESA carried out by Sala et al. 
(2020), assessing the European production and consumption against the Planetary Boundaries (PBs) 
==PAGE:7==

(Rockström et al. 2009; Steffen et al. 2015) operationalised through Carrying Capacity-based normalisation 
references (Bjørn and Hauschild 2015), they identified a need for exploring more alternatives to integrate the 
concept of PBs into Life-Cycle Assessment (LCA).  
In this study, the aim is to provide an overview of the existing methods for operationalisation of ESBs and 
assess their implications in LCA and policymaking. This is done through application to a case study which is 
the 2019 consumption footprint of EU27+UK which has been calculated by the European Commission Joint 
Research Centre (EU-JRC) through a process-based LCA approach (Sala and Sanyé-Mengual 2022). The 
consumption footprint describes the consumption-based impacts of the EU on several impact categories. In 
this study, only the impacts on the impact category of climate change are included. A more detailed descrip-
tion of the methodological choices and background data of the consumption footprint can be seen in the sup-
plementary material S1 Section C. The study will follow three steps: (1) a literature search leading to the 
identification of existing operationalisation approaches, (2) analysis of the identified approaches through ap-
plication to the case study, and (3) comparison and discussion of the approach’s implications in LCA and 
policymaking. Through these steps, it will be examined if the EU's consumption footprint is compliant with 
the ESBs for climate change and if the political goal of climate neutrality by 2050 is ambitious enough for 
the EU. This study will specifically contribute to the methodological choice related to the quantification of 
the carrying capacity, identified by Bjørn et al. (2019), by providing an overview of the existing methods for 
quantifying the carrying capacity and other absolute measures.  
One of the other methodological choices in an AESA is the choice of allocation principle, used for allocating 
the boundary to the assessed system. In this study, several allocation principles are chosen to assess the un-
certainty of the results, but it is not the primary focus of this assessment. The scope of this paper is only on 
the impact category of climate change. Though, it should be noted that all impact categories should be con-
sidered in an AESA to know if the system of assessment is truly absolutely sustainable.

The aim of this literature review is not to provide an exhaustive list of every existing operationalisation ap-

• 
Absolute environmental sustainability assessments: The environmental sustainability boundaries

mental sustainability assessments are included. This excludes LCA studies that are not absolute.  
• 
Non-LCA studies on environmental sustainability boundaries: Operationalisation of environ-
mental sustainability boundaries is not always defined in LCA studies. Therefore, studies on envi-
ronmental sustainability boundaries without LCA-perspective are included.

2.2 Analysis of operationalisation approaches  
Each approach was analysed using the framework developed and proposed by Vea et al. (2020). The frame-
work consists of five components I: Objective, II: Boundary principle, III: Impact and accepted levels, IV: 
Scientific estimate, and V: Uncertainty principle, see Figure 4.  
The framework was initially developed to analyse boundary approaches but will in this study be applied to 
operationalisation approaches. To make the framework relevant for the operationalisation approaches as 
well, the Operationalisation Principle was described instead of the Boundary Principle for component II 
(see Table 1 for a definition of operationalisation principle). The following aspects were included in compo-
nent II: which threshold it is based on, how the approach is applied, and whether the approach is static or dy-
namic. In step IV, the response and control variables were defined together with their location in the driver-
pressure-state-impact-response framework (DPSIR). In this study, it was chosen to also describe the opera-
tionalisation variable, and its location (see Table 1 for definition). The understanding of what each location 
in the DPSIR framework covers will be based on the report from the EEA (European Environment Agency et 
al. 1999).

2.3 Allocation principles 
To apply the operationalisation approaches to the case study it needs to be set up as an AESA. Bjørn et al. 
(2019) identified four assessment steps and six methodological choices. Step 1, 2, and 4 is included in any 
sustainability assessment: (1) definition of activity and (2) quantification of environmental pressure, and (4) 
presentation of results. The third step is unique for an AESA as it concerns the absolute boundary: (3) com-
parison of pressure to allocated carrying capacity (Bjørn et al. 2019). The choice of operationalisation ap-
proach is associated with choice number three between steps (1) and (2) in the AESA framework. The choice 
of allocation principles is the choice number five and is between steps (2) and (3), which will be elaborated 
in the following. A further elaboration of the steps and choices in the AESAs, included in this study, is pro-
vided in the supplementary material S1 Section B.  
Most ESBs for climate change are defined on a global scale because the impacts affect globally regardless of 
the geographical location. To know if a system complies with an ESB it is necessary to examine how much 
of the Safe Operating Space (SOS) it occupies to determine the Share of Safe Operating Space (SoSOS). 
This is done through allocation of the SOS. Allocation can be handled in several ways by applying different 
allocation principles. An allocation principle is defined as a principle that assigns a boundary to an anthropo-
genic system or process (Bjørn et al. 2020). It is thus a way of making a global boundary applicable at a sub-
global level, which is a necessary step when measuring progress towards a boundary from a sub-global point.  
There is currently no standardized method for allocation, even though the choice of allocation principle often 
has a larger influence on the conclusions than any other factor in an AESA (Ryberg et al. 2018a).  
Allocation involves both ethical and political aspects, and therefore this study will include a selection of dif-
ferent allocation principles. In literature on AESAs the different allocation principles are discussed in terms 
of justice principles, i.e., distributive justice theories or ethical norms (Lucas et al. 2020; Lucas and Wilting 
2018; Bjørn et al. 2020; Ryberg et al. 2020). These principles differ from each other on seven dimensions 
namely: currency (what should be distributed), pattern (rules of distribution), target (to who e.g., countries, 
people, etc.), geographical scope (e.g., global, national, regional, etc.), temporal scope (timeframe e.g., infi-
nite, a person’s lifetime, etc.), constraints (if the theory is the same as another but with a clause), and clauses 
(if it is meant to be combined with other principles) (Ryberg et al. 2020). In the literature the following jus-
tice principles were identified:  
• 
Egalitarianism: Equality of resources and welfare. 
• 
Luck egalitarianism: Equality of resources and welfare but with personal responsibility as a con-
straint. 
• 
Prioritarianism: The worse off are weighted higher.  
• 
Utilitarianism: Maximizes the sum of welfare. 
• 
Difference principle: Maximizes resources of the worse off. 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
• 
Sufficientarianism: Priority to those below a defined sufficiency level. 
• 
Libertarianism: Equality based on basic rights. 
• 
Acquired rights (also called sovereignty and inegalitarian): not a distributive justice theory but a 
term used to cover some widely used allocation principles which are based on the already established 
usage of the ecological space. 
A review of methods for sharing the planetary boundaries carried out by Ryberg et al. (2020) showed that the 
allocation principles used on national scale AESAs from 2013 to 2019 are based on either egalitarian, ac-
quired rights, or prioritarian (Ryberg et al. 2020). Therefore, a selection of allocation principles that are 
based on these three justice principles is applied in this study. The chosen allocation principles are equal per

3.1.1 The PB-LCIA  
The Planetary Boundary Life-Cycle Impact Assessment (PB-LCIA) approach is the only approach included 
in this paper where the boundary is not operationalized through changing the metric of the boundary control 
variable but instead changes the metric of the LCIA result. The characterisation factors (CFs) used for cli-
mate change in a conventional LCA is typically the GWP100 which converts elementary flows, relevant for 
climate change, to CO2-eq. With the PB-LCIA, Ryberg et al. (2018b) have enabled characterisation of the 
LCIA results directly to the same metric as the planetary boundaries, by the development of CFs with an 
Area of Protection (AoP) which is the same as the objective for the PBs. The PBs for climate change are en-
ergy imbalance in the top of the atmosphere (radiative forcing) or atmospheric CO2 concentration which will 
be used in this study. Thus, this approach is static. The characterisation factors are estimated based on the 
uncertainty principle of best estimate, but the planetary boundaries which they are comparable with are de-
fined based on a precautionary principle. The uncertainty principle associated with this approach is therefore 
a mix of the two. 
3.1.2 The PBA’06  
The per capita allowance impact assessment method, referred to as PBA’06, is defined by Gabor Doka. The 
first edition is from 2015 and the latest edition was published in 2016. The objective of this approach is to 
combine the life cycle inventory results with the planetary boundaries, which they have done by assigning 
each of the planetary boundaries to a per capita allowance and then converting to the metric of conventional 
LCA indicators to define a set of CFs for an LCIA method. For climate change, the CF can be used in combi-
nation with GWP100 to include all GHGs.  
For the conversion between CO2-eq and W/m2, Doka refers to the absolute global warming potential 
(AGWP) from Forster et al. (2007) which describes the radiative forcing efficiency for a small perturbation 
of CO2 for 100 years. Doka states that this value can be used to convert LCIA results derived from GWP100 
in the unit of CO2-eq into absolute radiative forcing and the other way around. In this study, this approach 
will be used to convert the PB of radiative forcing into the unit of CO2-eq. Thus, this approach is static. The 
uncertainty of this approach is a mix of the uncertainty of the PBs and the AWGP. The AGWP has an uncer-
tainty range of +/- 15 % (Forster et al. 2007). See supplementary material S1 Section E.2.2 for the calcula-
tion of the carrying capacity using this approach. 
3.1.3 Carrying Capacity-based normalisation references 
Carrying Capacity-based normalisation references (CC-based NRs) are developed as an alternative to the 
conventional NRs (reflecting the current level of impact per person) and are compatible with the GWP100 
characterised LCIA results of climate change (Bjørn and Hauschild 2015). The CC-based NRs are based on 
several thresholds which are relevant for the impact categories in an LCA. The NRs for climate change are 
based on radiative forcing of +1 W/m2 (Rockström et al. 2009) and global average temperature increase of 2 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
C (UNFCCC 2015). They refrained from using the planetary boundary of atmospheric CO2 concentration 
because it only considers CO2. 
They converted these thresholds to carrying capacities of each greenhouse gas by steady-state calculations 
i.e., the steady-state annual emission leading to compliance with the thresholds. This gives two values for the 
yearly allowed emission of greenhouse gasses globally, to not exceed the threshold, which makes this ap-
proach static. See supplementary material S1 Section E.2.3 for an elaboration on the models used for the cal-
culation of the carrying capacities. The value based on the 1 W/m2 threshold is more restrictive because it 
corresponds to an approximately 1.5 C threshold. The NRs are based on the uncertainty principle of best 
estimate.  
3.1.4 Remaining Carbon Budget 
The remaining carbon budget (RCB) is by Rogelj et al. (2019) defined as “the finite total amount of CO2 that 
can be emitted into the atmosphere by human activities while still holding global warming to the desired 
temperature limit.”. This definition implies that there is a level of RCB that will limit global warming to 
avoid transgressing a certain temperature. The RCB is generally estimated based on the near-linear relation-
ship called the transient climate response to cumulative CO2 emissions (TCRE). This near-linear relationship 
between cumulative CO2 emissions and CO2-induced global temperature change has been established by sev-
eral studies (Matthews et al. 2009; Meinshausen et al. 2009; Tokarska et al. 2016; Zickfeld et al. 2009).  
The RCB chosen for this study is defined by the IPCC (Canadell et al. 2021, chap. 5). The RCB is provided 
for the TCRE percentiles 17th, 33rd, 50th, 67th and 83rd, for temperature increase from 1.3-2.4 C, using nor-
mal distribution. There is no recommendation on which one to use. In this study, the 50th percentile of the 
TCRE for the temperature increase threshold of 1.5 C is chosen. Since the RCB is defined as a total value, it 
needs to be distributed between the remaining years of the assessed timeframe. The RCB can be both static 
and dynamic depending on how this distribution is done. Equally among the remaining years and linear re-
duction are included in this study. Furthermore, for the RCB to be relevant when assessing GHG emissions, 
it is necessary to convert it to a GHG budget. In this study, two methods are used namely conversion based 
on current emission contribution and linear regression between cumulative CO2 and CO2-eq emissions based 
on IPCC 6th Assessment Report (AR6) scenarios. These methods are elaborated in the supplementary mate-
rial S1 Section E.2.4. The RCB for CO2 only is also included. See Table 5 for the RCBs and their static 
yearly distribution. See Figure 6 for dynamic linear trajectory.

3.1.5 Emission pathways

sion pathway is a part of a scenario, which can be both a qualitative narrative and/or a quantitative projection

technology, lifestyles, policy, and consequences on energy use, land use, and emissions (Riahi et al. 2022,

3.1.6 Biocapacity  
Biocapacity is the only approach where the boundary metric is compatible with the Ecological Footprint 
framework for quantification of impacts. The ecological footprint is defined as the demand that human activ-
ities have on the biosphere (Borucke et al. 2013). Biocapacity is defined as the amount of productive land 
and sea area available to provide the ecosystem services that humanity consumes (Borucke et al. 2013). They 
are both defined in the unit of global hectares (gha). The types of productive land covered by the biocapacity 
in the ecological footprint methodology are cropland, grazing land, fishing ground, and forest. The land 
productivity type of forest land is assumed to cover both the demand of timber products and CO2 emissions. 
Therefore, the forest land capacity needs to be allocated to CO2 emissions and timber products respectively 
to be relevant for climate change only. The calculation for EU27+UK is elaborated the supplementary mate-
rial S1 Section E.2.6. 
The biocapacity cannot be compared directly with the case study by EU-JRC because they do not calculate 
their ecological footprint. The EU27+UK ecological footprint is therefore retrieved from the Global Foot-
print Network (Global Footprint Network and York University Ecological Footprint Initiative 2018). The 
latest available data is from 2018 for most of the EU27+UK countries, except for Cyprus and Malta, where 
the latest available data is from 2008. The biocapacity is based on the best estimate uncertainty principle due 
to the use of world average yield rates. In principle, this approach is dynamic because the biocapacity 
changes through time due to changes in the climate or land use. However, there is no defined trajectory for 
biocapacity. Hence, it will be treated as a static approach in this study.

4 Discussion 
4.1 Implications in LCA 
As described earlier the static vs. dynamic is one of the characteristics that differentiate the operationalisa-
tion approaches the most. The static approaches are more easily incorporated into LCA as they can be used 
as NRs. The dynamic approaches provide a trajectory of the emissions from now until the end of the century 
which cannot be used for normalisation.  
For static approaches, there is a large difference in how they are defined in terms of steady-state. Steady-state 
is important because the definition of carrying capacity implies that it is a steady-state impact which com-
plies with a threshold. The static version of the RCB is a cumulative emission divided with the remaining 
years of the century, meaning that the RCB runs out after the year 2100. Thereby, the static RCB cannot ac-
tually be considered as a carrying capacity. Therefore, if the static RCB approach is used for normalisation it 
should be communicated that this value is only valid for this century and that the emissions should be 0 after 
the year 2100.  
The CC-based NRs are based on a steady-state calculation and thus reflect the level of sustained yearly emis-
sions which will lead to compliance with the two thresholds that they are associated with. Though, because 
they are using GWP100 to calculate each of the substances carrying capacity in the unit of CO2-eq the values 
are artificially low for substances with longer lifetimes due to the disregard of impacts occurring after 100 
years (Bjørn and Hauschild 2015) which can end up overestimating the normalised results.  
The PBA’06 method for converting W/m2 into CO2-eq is not based on steady-state but on the AGWP from 
Forster et al. (2007), which is also based on the 100 years time limit. If it is also the case here that the 
amount of CO2 contributing to radiative forcing is artificially low due to the disregard of the impacts after 
100 years, the AGWP will be artificially high which will overestimate the carrying capacity calculated when 
multiplied by the 1 W/m2 resulting in an overestimated normalised result.  
The PB-LCIA method provides the level of steady-state atmospheric CO2 concentration and radiative forcing 
from a continuous annual emission of substances, thus they are based on steady-state. The PB-LCIA is not 
limited to the duration of 100 years and thus includes impacts occurring after 100 years. Figure 8 shows that 
the results using the CC-based NR for radiative forcing and the PB-LCIA approaches are at the 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
approximately same level which might indicate that they are close to being equally compliant with the PBs. 
The results using the approaches PBA’06 and CC-based NR for temperature increase are more similar to 
each other even though the boundary for PBA’06 is based on a more restrictive threshold than the CC-based 
NRs. This could indicate that the PBA’06 boundary is overestimated. The biocapacity is not steady-state as it 
is not defined based on any accepted level of impacts that can be sustained and the capacity varies each year 
depending on the land use change within the region of assessment.  
Another important aspect in the context of LCA is the objective of the boundaries. Each of the carrying ca-
pacities is defined based on a threshold which is associated with an objective of the boundary. The boundary 
objective is by Vea et al. (2020) defined as “What should be protected to achieve the related environmental 
goal”. Thus it corresponds to the LCA term AoP which for conventional LCAs are human health, natural 
environment, and natural resources (Hauschild et al. 2018). The AoP concept is elaborated in the supplemen-
tary material S1 Section B. The AoP of conventional LCAs covers a broader range of impacts than the objec-
tive of the PBs and the temperature threshold, and therefore more impacts than necessary are included in a 
conventional LCA. The AoP of the PB-LCIA is the same as the objective of the PBs, meaning that it will 
only provide information on how the assessed system contributes to the occupation of the PB SOS (Ryberg 
et al. 2018b). The CC-based NRs are steady-state based and are therefore more in line with the objectives of 
the boundaries but Bjørn and Hauschild state that for impact categories which affect more than one AoP (e.g. 
climate change) the lowest NR should be chosen. The other operationalisation approaches boundary objec-
tives are not aligned with the conventional LCA AoP, and the commissioner of the AESA should therefore 
clearly communicate the larger risk of uncertainties if choosing one of these operationalisation approaches. 
4.2 Implication in policymaking 
The different characteristics of the six operationalisation approaches included in this study have different im-
plications in policymaking. Since policymaking requires a broad mass of people to understand and agree on 
decisions it is important that the communication of the boundary, and how to track the progress towards stay-
ing below it, is relatively straightforward or translatable. Therefore, the metric of the communicated bound-
ary is important. The term greenhouse gas emissions has become more common in politics in general, but it 
is a location in the impact pathway that is further away from the actual impact that people can see and feel 
and may therefore lose importance and understanding with some people. Therefore, it can be argued that 
LCIA methods, like the PB-LCIA, will be easier to communicate because it moves the interpretation closer 
to the visual impacts. However, the consequences of the terms atmospheric CO2 concentration and radiative 
forcing might be difficult to understand for people that are not familiar with the PBs. Further development of 
the PB framework could be to expand with an indicator for temperature increase to make it easier to com-
municate in policymaking. The control variable of temperature increase is something most people know and 
can understand the consequences of.  
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
The static and dynamic operationalisation approaches have different implications in policymaking. The static 
approaches can be used for setting a single value target for greenhouse gas emissions which is easy to incor-
porate because the global carrying capacity does not change. A disadvantage of the static approach is that the 
defined carrying capacity is usually very low and can be demotivating for the regions, companies, individu-
als etc., which can result in them giving up.  
The dynamic approaches cannot be used as a foundation for setting a target for yearly greenhouse gas emis-
sions, but they can give an idea of how much change is needed to stay below a threshold. Furthermore, it can 
be used to investigate which year the emissions should reach net-zero emissions. In dynamic approaches, the 
near-term reductions needed are smaller compared to the reduction needed when using static approaches, 
which can make it easier to postpone the reductions. Though, the fact that it is not the end emissions but the 
total cumulative emissions which are important to comply with the threshold, might be difficult to communi-
cate. Thus, it is not enough to just reach the emissions shown at the end of the trajectory. The area below the 
trajectory must be considered. 
The boundary of biocapacity is different from the others due to the different unit and definition. This ap-
proach is more conceptual and illustrative. Thus, this might not be as suited for policymaking as the other 
approaches but could serve as an effective communicative device in public discourse.  
The current policy goals are being defined for the production-based impacts rather than consumption-based 
impacts which are due to the complications of setting reduction goals which also concern impacts in other 
countries. Policymakers must start considering the consumption-based approach to defining goals as well. If 
only the production-based emissions are accounted for, the EU's emissions can decrease if current EU activi-
ties are moved outside of the EU. This will result in emission reductions in the EU but not globally, i.e., 
problem shifting. Of course, tracking the production-based emissions can help identify where reductions 
should happen within the EU, but even though emission reductions from EU-based activities are reached, an 
increase in consumption will often increase emissions. Therefore, policymakers must take responsibility for 
the consumption-based emissions as well.  
4.3 Uncertainties  
The individually defined uncertainties of each operationalisation approach, as identified by their source, were 
included. Though, the uncertainty identified for each operationalisation approach is defined based on very 
different approaches to uncertainty evaluation. The advantage of including their individual uncertainties is 
that the uncertainty range of the results is based on the actual variation of the operationalisation approaches. 
Some of the uncertainty ranges are defined based on a range without any information on the probability dis-
tribution in this range (pathways, PBs and PBA´06) and some of the approaches do not provide an uncer-
tainty range at all (CC-based NRs and biocapacity).  
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
The uncertainty analysis of the static approaches showed that the conclusion is relatively robust because only 
two combinations’ uncertainty range resulted in the EU being compliant with the assessed threshold. For the 
dynamic approaches, it showed that there is a large variation in the results when including uncertainty, which 
affects the conclusions. The variation of the year of reaching net-zero is so large that it is difficult to con-
clude if the goal of 2050 is ambitious enough. There is especially a large variation between the operationali-
sation approaches when using the rights to development and capacity principles for allocation. For rights to 
development, the allocated trajectories vary from approximately current emissions to negative emissions al-
ready in 2030. For capacity, the large variation in the BAU data washes out the meaning of the allocation 
principle and makes it impossible to use as a foundation for conclusions due to the large negative values. It 
should be noted that the scenario data of emissions, GDP and populations are co-variate parameters and in-
formation on the probability range of the parameters in an integrated system is needed in the future for a 
more meaningful uncertainty range of the allocated trajectories.  
A qualitative interpretation of the uncertainty suggests that the largest uncertainty contributors are the sce-
nario data used for allocation and the choice of allocation principle. The reason for the large uncertainty of 
the scenario data is that it involves both C5, C6 and C7 classifications, which are based on different SSP 
storylines and temperature increase levels. The uncertainty associated with the choice of allocation principle 
is large because of the different justice principles that they are based on and their level of complexity and 
data need.  
4.4 Is net-zero in 2050 enough?  
Based on this study alone, it is difficult to conclude if the 2050 goal is enough due to large uncertainty 
ranges. The trajectories showed that if no fast reductions happen soon the year of reaching net-zero emis-
sions will move closer as we run out of RCB. If the current annual emission level is maintained, the RCB 
will run out faster and then even more drastic reductions are necessary. I.e., the longer we wait, the harder it 
gets. The emission pathways show very late years of net-zero emissions which are enabled through a trajec-
tory which is not linear but more exponential-like, keeping the emissions from cumulating above a certain 
amount. 
A meta-study recently published by Matthews et al. (2022) describes that many regions of the world have the 
goal of net-zero emissions by 2050 but that this level of current targets will lead to exceedance of the 1.5 C 
global temperature increase, which will most likely not be possible to return to this temperature level during 
the second half of the century. Furthermore, if nations want to avoid reliance on uncertain future carbon re-
moval technologies they should change their net-zero goal year to 2040. Though, the resources and mitiga-
tion needed to quickly reach net-zero emissions vary among nations which is why they argue that the wealth-
iest and most industrialised nations should reach net-zero before 2040 (Matthews and Wynes 2022). The 
study by Matthews et al. (2022) uses RCB estimates which are lower than the ones included in this study due 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
to an integrated approach to uncertainties (Damon Matthews et al. 2021) which underlines the importance of 
the inclusion of uncertainties when making conclusions.  
5 Conclusion 
This study has provided an overview of six existing approaches to operationalise the PBs and PB-related 
ESBs, namely the PB-LCIA, PBA’06, carrying capacity-based normalisation references, biocapacity, re-
maining carbon budget, and emission pathways. These approaches have been analysed through application to 
the case study of the EU27+UK consumption footprint of 2019. The analysis showed a significant difference 
in the characteristics of the operationalisation approaches especially static vs. dynamic, and that the choice of 
operationalisation approach will affect the results in an AESA. The application of the static approaches to the 
case study showed that the EU does not comply with the thresholds for climate change. This conclusion did 
not change when considering uncertainties. The application of the dynamic approaches showed too large un-
certainties to make any meaningful conclusion on whether the year 2050 is ambitious enough for the 
EU27+UK to reach net-zero emissions. It did show that if a linear reduction trajectory is aimed for, the year 
of reaching net-zero emissions needs to happen sooner than if an exponential-like reduction trajectory is 
aimed for. If the current emission level is maintained, the remaining carbon budget will run out soon, and 
more drastic measures will be required to extend the durability of the RCB.  
For the context of LCA, it should be considered if the objective of the boundary and the AoP of the LCA 
method is aligned. Though there is currently only one existing LCIA method which is aligned with the PB 
framework, and the control variables of the PB-LCIA might be difficult for setting policy targets due to the 
metric being more distant from impacts that most people understand. If the AoP is not aligned with the ob-
jective of the boundary, the carrying capacity chosen should be based on steady-state calculations since this 
is closer to the objectives. The trajectories are not useful in LCA the same way carrying capacities are, as 
they cannot be used as NRs, and their objective is not aligned with the LCA AoP. Though, they can be useful 
in policymaking to setting goals for the year of reaching net-zero emissions and how to get there.  
No matter the operationalisation approach or allocation principles chosen, the choices made must be trans-
parently communicated both in the AESA and in policymaking. Based on the discussion it is recommended 
that policymakers also consider the consumption-based approach to both the quantification of impacts and 
definition of policy goals to avoid problem shifting.

B. Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessments  
Where Relative Environmental Sustainability Assessments seek to investigate the environmental impact of a 
product or system relative to other products or systems’ environmental impact, an AESA seeks to investigate 
the environmental impacts of a product or system relative to absolute boundaries of the assessed impact 
categories. I.e., instead of comparing the environmental impacts of products and systems to each other, they 
are assessed against absolute boundaries which will indicate if the environmental pressure is sufficiently low 
for it to be considered environmentally sustainable (Bjørn et al. 2019). Bjørn et al. (2019) identified four 
steps in an AESA: 
1. Definition of activity 
2. Quantification of environmental pressure  
3. Comparison of pressure to allocated carrying capacity 
4. Presentation of results  
In the following, each of these steps will be elaborated on in the context of this study. Furthermore, six 
methodological choices between assessment steps, also identified by Bjørn et al. (2019), are elaborated. 
B.1 Definition of activity  
This step is a part of any sustainability assessment and is where the activity of assessment is defined. In this 
study, the consumption footprint of the EU27+UK is the activity of assessment. A further description of the 
consumption footprint of the EU27+UK is seen in Section C.  
Before moving to step two, four choices must be made (Bjørn et al. 2019).  
1. Choice of approach to setting system boundaries (territorial or consumption-based?). 
The choice here is consumption-based so it will be compatible with the case study.  
2. Choice of environmental sustainability objective of the boundary. 
In this study, three objectives are operated with. See Table 2 in the paper.  
3. Choice of carrying capacity quantification (Choice of operationalisation approach).  
Several operationalisation approaches for the quantification of carrying capacities and trajectories are 
assessed in this study. See the list of operationalisation approaches in Section 3.1 in the paper. 
4. Choice of approach to data collection. 
The approach to data collection is based on a representative basket of products which is a choice made 
by the EU-JRC and is elaborated in Section C. 
==PAGE:40==
8 
 
B.1 Quantification of environmental pressure 
This step is also a part of any sustainability assessment and where the environmental impacts are quantified. 
This can be done in several ways.  
In this study, four methods for quantification of the environmental pressure are considered, namely: 
elementary flows of CO2 before characterisation, LCA using conventional LCIA methods, LCA using PB-
LCIA method, and ecological footprint. In the following, a brief description of the LCA methodology is 
provided, including a description of the difference between conventional LCIA methods and the PB-LCIA 
method. Then a brief description of the ecological footprint framework will be provided. 
Before moving to step three, the fifth choice must be made (Bjørn et al. 2019):  
5. Choice of allocation principle. 
The allocation principles equal per capita, grandfathering, economic throughput, rights to development, 
capacity, and biocapacity are included in this study. They are elaborated in Section 2.3 in the paper and 
Section F in this supplementary material. 
B.1.1 LCA 
There are 4 steps in an LCA, following the ISO 14040/14044 structure (Hauschild et al. 2018; ISO 2006a, 
2006b): 
1. 
Goal and scope definition: description of the reason for the assessment, the functional unit, 
methodological choices, system boundaries, reference flow, etc.  
2. 
Life Cycle Inventory (LCI): gathering all information on the life cycle of the product/system of 
assessment and quantification of all physical elementary flows of the product/system associated with 
the reference flow fulfilling the functional unit. 
3. 
Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA): quantification of the environmental impacts associated 
with the inventory modelled in step 2. This involves choices of impact categories, characterisation, 
normalisation, weighting etc.  
4. 
Interpretation: interpretation of the results and if the assessment answers the questions defined in 
the goal and scope. Furthermore, sensitivity and uncertainty analysis are typically carried out in this 
step.  
This process is iterative and may be repeated many times.  
There are two differences between the conventional LCIA methods and the PB-LCIA method: the position 
on the impact pathway and the AoP. These two differences affect the LCA methodology in especially two 
areas, namely the metric of the indicators and the definition of the functional unit.  
==PAGE:41==
9 
 
When quantifying the environmental impacts both midpoint and endpoint indicators can be used. In a 
conventional LCA, the endpoint indicators are representative of the different AoPs (Hauschild et al. 2018, 
chap. 10). The AoPs are the objective for quantifying the environmental impacts. The midpoint indicators are 
groups of environmental impact categories in which different substance flows can be gathered according to 
their contribution to these categories (Hauschild et al. 2018, chap. 10). Characterisation is the step in LCIA 
where the elementary flows are translated into either midpoint indicators or endpoint indicators. In 
conventional LCIAs the characterisation method for the midpoint indicator of climate change is the GWP100 
which converts all greenhouse gasses to the unit of CO2-eq based on their global warming potential (GWP) 
from a pulse emission over 100 years (Hauschild et al. 2018, chap. 10). For the endpoint indicators, further 
characterisation is needed but is not relevant in this study. See Figure S1 for the typical midpoint and 
endpoint indicators.  
 
Figure S1: Midpoint and endpoint indicators. The figure is from the book “Life Cycle Assessment – Theory and Practice” 
(Hauschild et al. 2018, chap. 10). 
Because the PB-LCIA is based on the PB framework the AoP for the PB-LCIA is the same as the objective 
for the PBs namely “Keeping the earth system from being pushed out of its Holocene-like state” (Rockström 
et al. 2009). There are no defined midpoint or endpoint indicators in the PB-LCIA framework since there is 
only one set of indicators. The PB-LCIA framework provides eleven indicators for seven Earth System 
processes (Ryberg et al. 2018b). The PB-LCIA characterises the elementary flows, based on the steady-state 
level of the control variables that each substance results in if maintained at that level. The indicators of 
impact categories of the PB-LCIA are the same as the metric of the control variables of the PBs. Figure S2 
shows an illustration of the characterisation in the same format as in Figure S1. 
==PAGE:42==
10 
 
 
Figure S2: Characterisation in the PB-LCIA framework. The illustration is adapted from Hauschild et al. (2018, chap. 10) to fit the 
framework of PB-LCIA (Ryberg et al. 2018b) 
In a conventional LCA, the impacts of the life cycle of assessment are integrated over the time duration of a 
finite number of life cycles. Therefore, the functional unit defined should express “for how long” the impacts 
should be integrated over. In the PB-LCIA framework, the impacts are integrated over the annual life cycles 
that will continuously fulfil the functional unit. Therefore, the functional unit must be defined with 
continuous time duration in a PB-LCIA. See the illustration of the difference in LCI approaches in a 
conventional LCA and for the PB-LCIA in Figure S3 from Ryberg et al. (2018b). 
 
Figure S3: Representation of the difference in modelling the LCI using conventional LCA and PB-LCIA. The illustration is from 
Ryberg et al. (2018b) 
B.1.2 Ecological footprint 
The ecological footprint is an indicator of the biologically productive area needed to provide for the 
competing demands of humans. Five demands are included in the ecological footprint framework, namely 
space for food growing, fiber production, timber regeneration, absorption of CO2 emissions from fossil fuel 
burning, and accommodating built infrastructure (Global Footprint Network 2021). The framework assumes 
==PAGE:43==
11 
 
that all products and materials carry an embedded amount of bioproductive area which is necessary for the 
production and sequestering of waste associated with the products and materials.  
The area needed to provide for these five types of demands is calculated using yields of primary products 
namely: cropland, forest, grazing land, and fisheries. These area types are converted from hectares to global 
hectares by their biological productivity (Borucke et al. 2013). See Figure S4. 
 
Figure S4: The National Footprint Accounts accounting framework. The illustration is from Borucke et al. (2013) 
The ecological footprint of consumption is calculated as the ecological footprint of production plus the 
ecological footprint of import minus the ecological footprint of export (Global Footprint Network 2021). The 
core data needed for the ecological footprint calculations are provided by the National Footprint and 
Biocapacity Accounts (NFAs), and the calculations are based on United Nations or affiliated data sets 
(Global Footprint Network 2021). 
For this study, the carbon uptake land is used. See the calculation in Section E.2.6.1. 
B.2 Comparison of pressure to allocated carrying capacity  
This step is unique to AESAs as it is in this step that the quantified environmental impact is compared to an 
absolute measure. In this study, the quantified environmental pressures are compared to one of the 4 absolute 
measures: an allocated PB threshold, an allocated carrying capacity, an allocated emission trajectory, or an 
allocated biocapacity. These are dependent on which operationalisation approach is used, which is elaborated 
in Section 3.1 of the paper.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:44==
12 
 
The last and sixth choice must be made before moving to the fourth step: 
6. Choice of aggregation of the results. 
Since 6 different operationalisation approaches are included, no aggregation is made to avoid loss of 
information. 
B.3 Presentation of the results  
The presentation of results is also a part of any sustainability assessment, but the presentation of results in an 
AESA might focus on other aspects compared to other sustainability assessments. The results are presented 
differently for the static and the dynamic approaches. The static approaches are shown as normalised results 
where the value should lie between 0 and 1 for the environmental pressure to comply with the threshold. The 
dynamic results are presented as their allocated trajectories and by the year of reaching net-zero emissions. 
See the presentation of the results in Section 3 of the paper. An analysis of the uncertainties associated with 
each operationalisation approach is included for both static and dynamic approaches, see Section H. 
C. The case study 
The EU-JRC operates with five lifecycle-based indicators (Sala et al. 2020): 
1. Domestic footprint: process-based LCA with a production perspective  
2. Consumption footprint bottom-up: process-based LCA with a consumption perspective (inclusion 
of import, and exclusion of export)  
3. Consumption footprint top-down: Estimation of consumption footprint using Environmentally 
Extended Multi-Regional Input-Output (EEMRIO) based LCA applied on products.  
4. Final consumption I/O footprint: Estimating the final consumption by applying EEMRIO-based 
LCA on sectors.  
5. Consumer footprint: estimating the impact of consumption by using process-based LCA on a 
representative basket of products. 
The case study of this assessment is the second indicator namely the “consumption footprint bottom-up”. 
The EU-JRC uses the Environmental Footprint (EF) LCIA method for their process-based LCAs, but they 
have recently included the PB-LCIA method as well which enables the quantification of the environmental 
impacts in the same metric as the PBs. For this assessment both the consumption footprint bottom-up 
indicator calculated by the EF-LCIA and the PB-LCIA method is used. The functional unit of the process-
based consumption footprint in 2019 for EU27+UK is:  
- 
EF-LCIA “the consumption of the EU27+UK in 2019” 
- 
PB-LCIA: “annual consumption of the EU27+UK”

C.1 Inventory  
The consumption footprint is the calculated environmental impacts of the EU consumption, based on 160 
representative products, grouped into areas of consumption referred to as a basket of products (BoP). These 
BoPs are: food, mobility, housing, household goods, and appliances. These five BoPs represent the EU 
consumption by different coverage of the Classification of Individual Consumption by Purpose (COICOP) 
expenditure. By rough estimation mobility and housing are fully covered, food covers 89%, household goods 
cover 79%, and appliances cover 81% (EU-JRC 2022). The consumption footprint of the BoPs has been 
upscaled according to their coverage of the COICOP categories (Castellani et al. 2019; EU-JRC 2022).  
Figure S5 shows an overview of the number of products in each consumption category and the overall 
assessment process by the EU-JRC.

As seen in Figure S5 the production, import and export are calculated for each of the total 160 products using 
statistical data and scaling factors that fit their coverage of the COICOP categories.  
C.2 LCIA method 
The two LCIA methods that the EU-JRC has applied which are included in this assessment are the EF 3.0 
and the PB-LCIA.  
The EF 3.0 method includes 16 impact categories namely climate change, ozone depletion, eutrophication 
(marine, freshwater, and terrestrial), acidification, land use, water use, particulate matter, photochemical 
ozone formation, human toxicity (cancer and non-cancer), ionising radiation, and resource use (fossils, and 
mineral and metals) (Sala et al. 2020). The environmental impact category of climate change is the only 
impact category considered in this study. The climate change result is characterised using the GWP100 CFs 
(European Commission 2013). The control variable is GHG emission, and the unit is CO2-eq. This LCIA 
result can be derived from the EU-JRC consumption footprint platform (European Commission 2020). The 
==PAGE:46==

LCA results, before characterisation, used in this study were provided by the EU-JRC directly and were used 
to extract the result for just CO2 to compare with the RCB of CO2 only. 
The PB-LCIA includes CFs for seven Earth System processes namely climate change, stratospheric ozone 
depletion, ocean acidification, biogeochemical flows, land-system change, freshwater use, and atmospheric 
aerosol loading (Ryberg et al. 2018b). Both control variables, atmospheric CO2 concentration and radiative 
forcing are included and have the units ppm and W/m2 respectively. This calculation is not yet available on 
the consumption footprint platform and was therefore provided directly from the EU-JRC as well.  
To be able to include the ESB biocapacity in this study, the ecological footprint of the EU27+UK was 
needed. The ecological footprint indicator is not included as one of the EU-JRC indicators for the 
consumption footprint, and therefore the ecological footprint was downloaded from the Global Footprint 
Network database (Global Footprint Network and York University Ecological Footprint Initiative 2018). The 
latest data available is from 2018 for all countries except Cyprus and Malta of which the latest data is from 
2008. To include only indicators relevant for climate change, the carbon uptake land needed to absorb the 
CO2 from fossil fuel emission burning was calculated, see Section E.2.6.1.

E.2 Calculations and details 
In this section, more details on the operationalisation approaches are provided as well as formulas used for 
the calculation of the ESBs associated with the approaches.  
E.2.1 PB-LCIA  
Ryberg et al. (2018b) state that there are two fundamental differences in the conventional LCA indicators 
and the control variables of the PBs that make conversion problematic. The first difference is in impact 
pathways between their positions; the LCA indicator and the PB are in two different metrics and there are 
most likely different cause-chain mechanisms that lead to the impact on each of these metrics. The second 
difference is that the defined area of protection is different from the objective of the PBs which influences 
the calculation practices. Therefore, they developed a set of new characterisation factors to use in LCIA, that 
would accommodate these differences. The PB-LCIA characterisation factors for climate change are based 
on the steady-state level of radiative forcing and atmospheric CO2 concentration which is the result of 
constant annual emissions at a certain level. Therefore, when using the PB-LCIA method the functional unit 
should be defined with a continuous and constant time duration rather than a specific timeframe. 
These characterisation factors (CFs) are calculated based on the cumulative emissions of each greenhouse 
gas’ effect on the change in radiative forcing or atmospheric CO2 concentration respectively by using the 
RCP2.6 scenario modelled using the MAGICC6 model by Meinshausen et al. (2011).  
The operationalisation approach PB-LCIA is compatible with the PBs in their original metric. The planetary 
boundaries are a set of defined boundaries for nine different impact categories originally formulated by 
Rockström et al. (2009). Each boundary has one or more control variables defined together with an 
associated threshold. Further development of the framework was published by Steffen et al. in 2015 
containing updates for most of the PBs and some new proposals (Steffen et al. 2015). Ryberg et al. (2018a) 
defined a so-called natural background level for the planetary boundaries, which is the level of the control 
variables from before anthropogenic impacts interfered with the Earth System process (pre-industrial levels). 
The natural background level is subtracted from the PBs to estimate the full safe operating space because the 
PB-LCIA CFs are defined based on the change from pre-industrial levels. See the PB values for climate 
change in Table S2.

E.2.3 CC-based NRs  
This approach includes the estimation of carrying capacities, based on two different thresholds, which can be 
used as normalisation references in an LCA. One is defined based on the control variable of average global 
temperature increase with a threshold of 2 C. The other is defined based on the control variable of radiative 
forcing with a threshold of 1 W/m2 which is one of the PBs for climate change. For both thresholds, they 
used the Absolute Global Temperature Change Potential (AGTP) for a sustained emission change (Shine et 
al. 2005) for non-CO2, and the GEOCARB III model (Berner 2001) for CO2. Two different models were 
used for CO2 and non-CO2 gasses because CO2 takes a much longer time to reach steady-state after a change 
in emissions. The timeframes they used for the steady-state calculations were 1 million years for the AGTP 
model and 1.95 million years for the GEOCARB III model (Bjørn and Hauschild 2015). After calculating 
substance-specific carrying capacities for each threshold, they calculated a single carrying capacity for each 
threshold by using the GWP100 CFs and averaging all substance-specific carrying capacities with a 
weighting according to substance contribution in 2010 (Laurent et al. 2013). 
E.2.4 RCB 
The definition of the RCB is compliant with the term avoidance budget (Rogelj et al. 2016) which describes 
the amount of carbon that leads to avoidance of the threshold. The term exceedance budget describes the 
amount of carbon that leads to the threshold being reached. 
==PAGE:54==
22 
 
In literature, there is a relatively large spread in the estimates of the RCB which can both be due to the type 
of RCB that is being defined (avoidance or exceedance) and how the uncertainties are included. Dickau et al. 
(2022) have identified 5 important uncertainty aspects when defining the RCB namely; TCRE (physical 
climate and carbon cycle), non-CO2 forcing, unrepresented Earth System feedbacks, zero-emissions 
commitment, and non-CO2 emissions pathway (Dickau et al. 2022). The RCB chosen for this study is 
defined by the IPCC AR6 WGI (Canadell et al. 2021, chap. 5). This RCB is defined by using strictly TCRE 
and they do not provide a way to assess the full set of uncertainties as a single distribution, therefore only 
uncertainties related to physical climate, carbon cycle and the unrepresented Earth System feedback of 
permafrost carbon feedback are included. The non-CO2 contributions were estimated based on the median 
value of the scenarios assessed in the SR1.5 report by the IPCC (Canadell et al. 2021, chap. 5). 
To be able to apply the RCB in assessments where more greenhouse gasses are included than just CO2 it was 
necessary to convert the RCB from CO2 to CO2-eq. Afterwards, it was necessary to distribute the RCB over 
the remaining years. Methods for both are elaborated in the following.

greenhouse gas budget were found in the literature, namely the current emission mix and linear regression.

E.2.5 Emission pathways  
As described in the paper, emission pathways are a part of a scenario. The IPCC operates with reference 
scenarios, which are the scenarios reflecting e.g., current policies or no-climate policies, and mitigation 
scenarios which is exploring different options for policies and are typically derived from a reference 
scenario. Scenarios can be used as the basis for integrated assessment models (IAMs), which is a model type 
that can assess complex systems with many variables. IAMs are generally driven by economics and are thus 
compliant with the typical cost-effective variables defined in a reference or mitigation scenario (Riahi et al. 
2022, chap. 3). IAMs can both be complex and simple depending on the assessed system but in simple 
systems, the model will make assumptions, which will increase the level of uncertainty. 
There are multiple scenarios included in this study in one way or another. For the trajectory that does not 
lead to exceedance of the threshold (the pathway operationalisation approach), C1 and C2 are used. When 
allocating the trajectories, scenario data is used for some of the allocation principles. For the equal per capita 
and economic throughput principles, the population and GDP trajectories, respectively, from C1 and C2 
scenarios are used. For the rights to development and capacity principles BAU scenarios are used, which are 
the classifications C5-C7 according to the IPCC (Riahi et al. 2022, chap. 3). Rights to development use the 
BAU emissions trajectories and the capacity uses both BAU emissions, GDP, and population trajectories. 
See Table S4 for a list of the scenario classifications and their compatible SSP. A description of each SSP 
storyline is provided below the table. If no uncertainty is included the median value of all scenario data is 
used. When assessing the uncertainty, an uncertainty range of the scenario data of 5%-95% percentiles is 
used because this is the range that the IPCC show in their WGIII contribution when introducing the C1-C8 
classifications (Riahi et al. 2022, chap. 3).

E.2.6 Biocapacity 
The biocapacity and the ecological footprint are already in the same control variable but to be relevant for 
climate change only, it is necessary to calculate the carbon uptake part of the biocapacity and ecological 
footprint. This method is described in the following. 
E.2.6.1 Calculation of carbon uptake land 
The ecological footprint is quantified through six different product flow types namely: agricultural products, 
livestock products, fishery & aquaculture, timber products, CO2 emissions, and built-up surfaces. CO2 
emissions, which is the only product flow that is relevant for climate change, can through a carbon uptake 
factor be converted to the equivalent carbon uptake land necessary to sequester the emissions.  
Biocapacity is defined through 4 different land productivity types namely: cropland, grazing land, fishing 
ground, and forest. The carbon uptake land, which is the land productivity type that is relevant for climate 
change, is currently assumed to be only forest land according to the ecological footprint methodology, and 
the land productivity type of forest land also assimilates the biocapacity timber products (Borucke et al. 
2013). Therefore, the forest land capacity needs to be allocated to CO2 emissions and timber products 
respectively if they should be used separately. This is done by sharing the biocapacity for forest land based 
on the share that the CO2 part of the ecological footprint has, relative to the sum of the forest- and CO2 part 
of the ecological footprint. See the formula in (6) and Table S5 for results for each country in the EU27+UK.

F. Allocation principles 
In this section, the formulas and data used for each allocation principle together with their advantages and 
disadvantages are presented. For all allocation principles, the data concerning future years used for allocation 
of dynamic approaches are much more uncertain and less available.  
F.1 Equal per capita (EPC) 
This allocation principle is based on the justice principle of equality between all individuals, but it neglects 
the current existing inequality (in both responsibility, resources, needs development etc.) between countries 
(Klima- og Omstillingsrådet 2022). Due to the current level of inequality, this allocation principle might be 
less relevant and less realistic. 
An advantage of this principle is that it is easy to apply, and the data need is very low and widely available. 
It is a principle that most people can understand and are able to apply and does not require much time. It 
does not distinguish between impact categories, as it assigns the same share for all impact categories. See 
Table S6 for information on the data used.

F.2 Grandfathering (GF) 
Grandfathering is a principle that is not based on an actual distributive justice theory, but rather on the 
concept of acquired rights, which in this allocation principle means that the rights to emissions in the future 
are acquired through the emissions that have happened in a base year. Thereby, it assumes that the share will 
be constant in the future. In contrast to the EPC, this principle is not based on the idea of equality between

individuals (Klima- og Omstillingsrådet 2022), but will actually maintain the current inequality. Therefore, it 
can be questioned if it can be considered ethically responsible to use this allocation principle.

when allocating. In this study, the consumption-based approach is used which will give the EU a larger share 
due to the larger impact from consumption-based impacts than production-based impacts (see Figure S12 in 
Section G).

F.3 Economic throughput (ET) 
Economic throughput is an allocation principle that also relies on the concept of acquired rights, but in terms 
of contribution to the global GDP. This is similar to GF in terms of ethics as it does not consider equality 
between individuals but is maintaining the current inequality. Also, by using this principle, the EU will get a 
larger share than many other regions because the EU has a relatively large GDP.  
There are two types of GDP data available which can both be used in this allocation principle, namely the 
Market Exchange Rates (MER) and the Purchasing Power Parity (PPP). The MER express the GDP where 
demand and supply are balanced for international currencies (OECD 2006). The PPP express the GDP where 
the different cast of goods and services in different countries are balanced. For this study, the GDP PPP is 
used (OECD 2006).  
The ET principle is also easy to apply, easy to understand, and not time demanding. The data necessary for 
the allocation is easily accessible but it cannot distinguish between impact categories. See information on the 
data used in Table S8. 
This principle applies to all the ESBs included in this study. See formula (9).

F.4 Rights to development (RTD) 
This allocation principle is based on the prioritarian justice principle, which is a variation of the egalitarian 
principle but with a prioritisation according to different criteria. In this allocation principle, the prioritisation 
is made based on countries’ right to development which allocates a larger share to developing countries than 
developed countries, giving developing countries room to grow. It is based on the thought of all countries 
converging towards the same development level and does thus consider current inequality. Though this 
==PAGE:64==
32 
 
principle will often result in very small or negative shares to the countries with large environmental impacts 
and therefore the reduction required might seem unrealistic to implement.  
Instead of allocating the SOS as was done with the principles EPC, GF, and ET, this principle allocates, the 
global reduction needed. Therefore, the SoSOS can be negative if the allocated reduction is larger than the 
current impact or scenario impact of that year. The RTD principle relies on impact data and RCI data. The 
RCIs are based on either only CO2 emission or GHG emissions, and it is provided for both production-based 
and consumption-based approaches (Holz et al. 2019). Though, information on the emission data behind the 
RCIs is not available. See information on the data used in Table S9. 
The RTD principle is not as intuitive to apply as some of the simpler allocation principles due to the 
inclusion of BAU and RCI. The data for the RCIs are only available up until 2030, and therefore the dynamic 
approaches can only be allocated so far by using this principle. Furthermore, the consumption-based BAU 
emissions data of the EU27+UK is not available but is estimated based on past emissions, see Section G. It 
can distinguish between impact categories but BAU data on other impact categories than climate change may 
be hard to find, and the RCIs are, as described, only based on emission data. Therefore, the principle is only 
applicable to ESBs in the metric of either CO2 or GHG emission. Thus, not applicable to the planetary 
boundaries for atmospheric CO2 concentration and radiative forcing, and ecological footprint.

F.5 Capacity (CA) 
This allocation principle is also based on the prioritarian justice principle. In this allocation principle, the 
prioritisation is made based on countries’ capacity to reduce. It is based on the rationale that the most 
resourceful countries must carry the largest reduction burden and does thus consider current inequality to 
some extent (Klima- og Omstillingsrådet 2022). 
The CA principle is more complex and difficult to understand than the other principles because of the three-
step calculation and relatively large data need. See information on the data used in Table S10. The principle 
can distinguish between impact categories but the missing data on BAU for other impact categories than 
climate change may again cause issues.  
Where the RTD principle allocates the absolute global amount needed to reduce, the CA principle allocates 
the global reduction percentage which is done by using the sub-global GDP/capita relative to the world 
GDP/capita. This can create issues by allocating shares that do not necessarily add up to one, especially if the 
global reduction need is large. For example, a country with a very high GDP/capita compared to that of the 
world, will almost always get a negative share assigned whereas a country with a very low GDP/capita will 
not be able to get a larger share than their BAU emission. As a consequence, the shares summed up can end 
up being much lower than 1. BAU scenarios are always uncertain because there are assumptions about the 
future involved, which should be kept in mind when using it for allocation of trajectories.   
Three formulas are needed to do the allocation, see (11)-(13). The calculation is based on the EU's GDP per 
capita relative to the world GDP per capita. Furthermore, in (11) and (13) BAU emissions are needed, but for 
the static approaches, this will be emissions of 2019, thus reflecting current capacity. This principle applies 
to all ESBs included in this study.

F.6 Biocapacity (BC) 
This allocation principle is, like grandfathering and economic throughput, based on the concept of acquired 
rights, but in terms of biocapacity. This acquired rights principle is different from EPC and GF because it 
distributes the ESBs based on each region's available biocapacity which is also included as an ESB in this 
study (see Section E.2.6). Countries with large areas of productive land get larger shares when using this 
principle. Though, it might be less relevant because the world is so interconnected in terms of trade of 
produced goods and services. 
This principle is easy to understand and apply due to the simple formula and the easily accessible data 
needed. It cannot distinguish between impact categories, but it applies to carrying capacities in all metrics 
(EEA and FOEN 2020). The Global Footprint Network defined the available biocapacity for each country, 
which in this study was summed up for EU27+UK. See information on the data used in Table S11. Because 
this study only assesses climate change it was chosen to use the carbon uptake land for allocation as well to

make the allocation share more relevant. This might be possible for other impact categories as well but will

G. EU27+UK business-as-usual emissions  
For the BAU GDP and population of the EU27+UK, the data from the IPCC AR6 Scenario Explorer is used. 
The AR6 Scenario Explorer only contain BAU emissions for the world, and therefore the BAU emissions for 
EU27+UK need to be found elsewhere (Byers et al. 2022). In policymaking, the production-based impacts 
are usually the impact referred to. Therefore, the emission projections based on current policies are only 
available for production-based impacts (Carbon Transparency Initiative 2016; EEA 2021), which cannot be 
used as a consumption-based projection because historically the tendency for production- and consumption-
based impacts of the EU27+UK is that the production-based impacts have slowly decreased while the 
consumption-based impacts have slowly increased. See Figure S12 for the historical consumption- and 
production footprint of EU27+UK from the EU-JRC footprint platform (Sala and Sanyé-Mengual 2022; 
Sanyé-Mengual et al. 2022).

The consumption-based footprint includes emissions occurring outside of the EU, and therefore to estimate a 
BAU emission trajectory, assumptions based on other regions’ future policies would be necessary. These 
assumptions will always be very uncertain, and they are outside of the scope of this study. Therefore, for this 
assessment, the BAU emissions used for the allocation principles RDT and CA will be based on the current

year with the ratio of CO2 relative to CO2-eq from 2019. Note that this approach is associated with a high 
level of uncertainty which is always the case when estimating future conditions.

H.3 Interpretation of uncertainty associated with dynamic approaches 
Even though the uncertainty of the dynamic approaches has a large influence on the range of the trajectories 
for the EU, they all suggest a large decrease in yearly emissions from now. The upper range of the C2 
pathway suggests a smaller yearly decrease up until 2030 but will decrease rapidly afterwards. The CA 
principle applied to any of the operationalisation approaches suggests large negative emissions and a very 
large variation of the trajectories.  
The GF and BC principles are not dependent on scenario data for allocation due to the use of base year 
instead, and therefore Figure S23 and Figure S27 are the same as Figure S29 and Figure S33. Both EPC and 
ET are using scenario data for allocation but the allocated trajectory range including the uncertainty of the 
scenario data is not very different from the allocated trajectory range when only including the uncertainty of 
the ESBs. There is a quite large difference in the uncertainty of allocated trajectories when considering both 
RTD and CA principles, which is expected since they use BAU scenario data while the others use C1 and C2 
scenario data. Allocation principles like RTD and CA show that including uncertainty when allocating based 
on BAU data is important. Figure S26 and Figure S32 show that the inclusion of the uncertainty of the 
scenario data makes a very significant difference for the CA principle. Figure S32, where the uncertainty of 
the scenario data for allocation is included, shows large variations and a very negative lower bound of the 
trajectory. The very negative trajectory is caused by the inclusion of uncertainty of the BAU scenarios which 
according to the IPCC is C5-C7. C5, C6 and C7 are very different, and therefore there are large uncertainties 
associated with the use of these scenarios as BAU.  
The allocated C2 trajectory, using CA allocation, calculates the emission in 2098 to be -14176 Gt CO2-eq 
which is very unrealistic. The reason why this value is so largely negative is a combination of two factors. 
First, the global SOS of -13 Gt and a BAU emission of 0.3 Gt calculates via equation (11) a global reduction 
of a factor of 40. Second, the BAU in this case says that the EU GDP/capita is 38 times larger than the 
world's average GDP/capita, calculated in equation (12). These two factors combined in equation (12) 
calculate that the EU should reduce their emissions by a factor of -1523 compared to BAU in 2098 which 
allocates a yearly emission of- 14176 Gt CO2-eq in 2098 via equation (13). The large uncertainties of the 
BAU scenario data make the CA principle irrelevant because negative emissions of that magnitude are 
unrealistic.
