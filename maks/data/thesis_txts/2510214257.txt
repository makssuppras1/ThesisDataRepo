Multi-spectral satellite derived bathymetry in Greenland
Abstract
In this study an inverse model that retrieves water depth, optical properties and other characteristics
of coastal ocean environments based on multi-spectral satellite imagery from the Sentinel-2 mission is de-
veloped and tested in the Arctic. Particular challenges in the Arctic region include: sea ice, low sea ﬂoor
albedo, murky turbid water, and low light intensity. A set of criteria for a successful application of the
satellite derived bathymetry method was developed, including methods for data screening and analysis, as
well as necessary satellite data pre-processing steps. To asses code performance, multibeam data supplied
by the Danish Geodata Agency is used in the validation procedure of the inversion scheme for select test
sites. The forward model is based on a semi-analytical model of the radiative transfer equation in the ocean.
A nonlinear inverse problem was posed and a solution by linearisation and implementation of an iterative
inversion scheme was developed for which the reference model was corrected with a regularised damped
Least Squares estimate. Model convergence was achieved based on the criteria of minimizing the norm of
the residual, but the results did not capture any true physical variation, and the parameter retrieval was not
overall successful.

Introduction
Figure 1
The purpose of this study is to develop an inverse model that retrieves water depth, optical properties and
other characteristics of the ocean environment from multi-spectral satellite imagery collected by the Sentinel-2
mission. The project is conducted to assist bathymetric mapping of coastal areas in the Arctic for navigational
purposes. Bathymetric surveying in Greenland is challenging due to harsh conditions, such as hazardous rocks,
logistics, and dependence on season due to extensive sea ice cover. Hydrographic surveyors with the Danish
Defense are mainly using multibeam systems by ship, with high accuracy and reliability, but with limited survey
coverage. Any means of assisting the current surveying approaches could show great potential in Greenland.
Satellite Derived Bathymetry (SDB) methods have shown potential for hydrography in some areas of the world,
for instance in monitoring the Great Barrier Reef in Australia. The main focus of the study is the development
of an inversion code for retrieval of water depth, optical properties and bottom type based on the work of
Lee et al. (1998) and using visible bands in multi-spectral satellite imagery from the ESA Sentinel-2 satellite
mission. Particular challenges in the Arctic region include: sea ice, low sea ﬂoor albedo, murky turbid water,
and low light intensity.
Figure 1 illustrates the challenge posed in satellite derived bathymetry. In order to retrieve the water
depth, optical properties and other characteristics of the ocean environment, from reﬂected solar radiation,
the interactions of the light along its pathway in the water column, through the air-water interface and in the
atmosphere must be studied, before the quantity that can be measured by satellite, namely the remote sensing
reﬂectance, can be used for retrieving the water depth and other variables of interest. In satellite based ocean
optics, it is a challenge that the signal measured by the satellite is actually a composite of many interactions

and and not just reﬂections from the ocean surface or ocean bottom.
In this study, a semi-analytical radiative transfer based model is implemented in an inversion scheme. First,

To asses code performance, multibeam data supplied by the Danish Defense will be used in the validation

Theory
The theory section is divided into two parts. In part I a theoretical framework is developed around Mobleys
organizational chart for optical oceanography seen in ﬁgure 6. The aim is to build up an understanding of how
electromagnetic radiation interacts with a water body. Firstly, the radiometric variables are deﬁned. Secondly,
the apparent optical properties (AOPs) are deﬁned, as well as their relation to the radiometric variables. Then
follows a deﬁnition of the inherent optical properties (IOPs) in water, and a description of the main scattering
and absorbing constituents in sea water. Subsequently, the radiative transfer equation (RTE) is formed based on
the IOPs together with the necessary boundary conditions. Lastly, in the semi-analytical (SA) model developed
in Lee et al. (1998) and Lee et al. (1999) the remote sensing reﬂectance, an AOP, is expressed as a function
of water column height, H, bottom albedo, ρ, absorption coefﬁcient, a(λ), and backscattering coefﬁcient,
bb(λ). These variables can be estimated by minimizing the difference between modelled and measured remote
sensing reﬂectance. The variables can subsequently be used to retrieve in water constituents, such as chl-a
concentration, colored dissolved organic matter (CDOM), through their empirical relationship with a(λ) and
bb(λ). However, this study primarily aims to estimate water column height and bottom albedo for bathymetric
charting.
Part II describes the damped least squares inversion method applied.

3.1.5
Semi-analytical model
In optically shallow water the signal measured by the satellite is a composite of light reﬂected of the surface
of the ocean, light scattered from the water column and light reﬂected from the bottom. These interactions
must be expressed separately in order to retrieve the water column height and bottom albedo from the bottom
reﬂection part of the signal, or optical constituents from the water column signal. In Lee et al. (1998) and Lee
et al. (1999) a semi-analytical model for remote sensing reﬂectance is developed and tested in different oceanic
environments for the purpose of deriving water depth, bottom characteristics, and in water optical constituents.

4
Data and method
This section contains a detailed overview of the data sets and data analysis methods used in this study. In
section 4.1, the data screening procedure leading to the choice of Sentinel-2 satellite imagery is outlined. In
the following section 4.2, all necessary pre-processing steps for the Sentinel-2 imagery are described. Section
4.3 states the initial value and allowed range for the inversion model parameters from a selection of appropriate
sources. And lastly, in section 4.4, in-situ multi-beam data acquisitioned by the Danish National Defense and
accessed through the Danish Geodata Agency is presented along with the validation procedure.
4.1
Data screening procedure
The data screening process speciﬁc to this study, aims to ensure that the site and data chosen is optimal for the
SDB method applied. Below are listed the criteria that governed data selection.
Firstly, the number of spectral bands available should match the number of physical parameters estimated
in the model inversion. As mentioned in the theory section (ref), the modelled remote sensing reﬂectance is
a function of 2 variables to be estimated, and therefore at least 2 spectral bands in the visible spectrum are
needed, reﬂectance in each band contains a portion of signal from the ocean ﬂoor.
In the inversion scheme, initial values for the 2 variables in the model vector are needed as input. The
suitability of the starting guess depends on the availability of in situ measurements of in water constituents,
height and albedo, and this is taken into consideration in the acquisition selection.
The acquisition should be at low tide, to increase the chance that an adequate amount of signal from the
ocean ﬂoor reaches the satellite. The correction for the tidal offset can be performed at a later stage, but this
necessitates that the tidal information is available for the selected location, which is another data screening
criterion, but which may prove difﬁcult in the Arctic region.
In the Arctic, successful SDB application is also dependent on seasonality. Acquisitions in summer are
preferable, in order to lessen the extent of sea ice cover. In summer phytoplankton growth is at its peak, this
increases absorption of light in the watercolumn and is a disadvantage, but it is an unavoidable trade-off, as the
scene must be free of sea ice.
To further increase the probability of measuring a signal from the ocean ﬂoor, cloud coverage at the time of
acquisition should be minimal to limit atmospheric inﬂuence. It is likewise preferable to choose an acquisition
where the wind speed is low, as this will lessen the effect of waves and subsequent white caps, glitter on the
surface, and wind driven turbidity within the water column.
The SNR of the bands should be acceptable. The study Kudela et al. (2019) speciﬁes SNR recommenda-
tions for the visible bands. SNR should be larger than 400 for open water and >1000 for coastal areas, when
used in water optical constituent retrieval models.
The satellite imagery selected should include atmospheric correction, and bottom-of-atmosphere reﬂectance
should be available.
The spatial and temporal resolution of the satellite imagery and the resulting resolution of the estimated
parameters should meet the requirements of the end user. The most optimal end product, is a bathymetric

4.2.2
Sea masking
Only signal return from the ocean and water bodies is of interest, and land has to be masked out of the satellite
imagery. Short wave infrared radiation will generally be absorbed in the ocean, and little or no return is expected
from ocean and water bodies in this part of the spectrum, making them easily distinguishable from land. The
masking procedure is based on the black pixel assumption, where the water-leaving radiance over the ocean
is assumed to be zero. Using SWIR bands at 1640 and 2130 nm, the study Shi, Wang (2009) found that this
assumption is generally valid even for extremely turbid waters.
The pixels in the B12 band that contain reﬂectance values above a near-zero threshold of 0.001, are con-
sidered to be ’land pixels’ and their indices are used to mask out land areas in all remaining bands. A visual
example of the land-masking procedure can be found in section 6.1, where reﬂectance distribution of the B2
band before and after masking is displayed for each product, P and Q.
4.2.3
De-glinting
In the sea masking procedure described above, a threshold was imposed in the SWIR band on all reﬂectances
above a certain value, as these where assumed to be pixels containing land. However, not all pixels that have
been masked out fall into this category. By imposing a threshold on the high reﬂectance values in the SWIR,
pixels containing highly reﬂective artifacts such as sun glint and reﬂection from white caps may also have been
partially removed from the data. Imposing a threshold is a very simple procedure for de-glinting and more
reﬁned methods do exists and will be brieﬂy discussed in section ref.
4.2.4
Removing outliers
Additionally, a simple test for removing outliers is performed using the 3σ or empirical rule. As can be seen
in the ﬁgures 20a, 20b, 20c and 20d in result section 6.2 the distribution of ocean reﬂectance in the bands is
positively skewed. For this reason a logarithm transform is performed before the empirical rule is applied, and
all data points outside 3σ are discarded.

Geostatistical signal to noise ratio
The signal to noise ratio is an important tool for evaluating band perfor-
mance. In order to know if any observed change in reﬂectance is due to physical phenomena and not a result
of noise, information on noise is required for each band. The level of noise on its own does not say much, as
the signiﬁcance of the noise level also depends on the strength of the signal. The signal-to-noise level which is
the ratio of the signal mean to its standard deviation conveys more information about data quality. Wavelength
dependent noise is introduced into the signal as a result of physical interaction with the atmosphere, random
noise or variability within pixels. By applying the geostatistical method from Curran, Dungan (1989), and
following the application of the method in Kudela et al. (2019) the SNR can be found using a semivariogram.
A semivariogram calculates the spatial auto-correlation of the data, i.e. the correlation of a signal with a de-
layed copy of itself. First, a semivariogram is constructed for each band with the MATLAB (Mathworks Inc.)
package variogram. Then, using the package variogramﬁt, a theoretical variogram is constructed based on the
distribution of the data. The Gaussian distribution is integrated in the variogramﬁt function and can be selected
among other distributions as input, depending on the distribution of the data. A logarithm transform is again
used, such that the data better resembles a Gaussian, this is done prior to using the variogram function. The
theoretical semivariogram is used to estimate the nugget, sill, and range. The nugget variance, determines the
noise, and the SNR is estimated by dividing the mean signal in each band with the square root of the nugget
variance: SNR = mean signal / √nugget. This method is only applicable if the data is stationary, isotropic, and
does not change spatial resolution. To fulﬁll these requirements, the open water region in the Q- and P-products
has been chosen for the SNR estimation. Results can be found in table 4 in section 6.3.

4.4
In situ multibeam data
Multibeam echo sounding is used for bathymetric charting. A sound signal is transmitted from the bulk of the
ship, reﬂected of the ocean ﬂoor and received again by a censor. The signal is transmitted in nadir direction. If
wave motion causes the signal to propagate off-nadir a motion censor on the transmitter/censor will allow for
angular dependence corrections. The path length can be found based on the speed of sound in water, and it is
also necessary to have knowledge of water conditions for accurate water depth retrieval.
The multibeam data was acquisitioned by the Danish Defense and accessed through the Danish Geodata
Agency. The data was collected in Sissimiut habour and at Anders Olsen Sund in the period 2005-2017 during
7 different hydrographic surveys. The data was delivered in 10 meter resolution, but was re-sampled to match
the resolution of the satellite imagery, i.e. 60 meter. The data comes with an International Hydrographic
Organisation (IHO) order, which specify the usage and uncertainty of the data. All data was supplied in IHO
order 2. The uncertainty is depth dependent, for a reference depth of 20 meters the error is ±1.1 meters for
IHO 2. The data has been corrected to height at lowest astronomical tide, as is common practice for nautical
data, International Hydrographic Bureau (1998).

5
Inverse model
The inverse model takes 6 measured remote sensing reﬂectance values, Rrs,d, for every pixel in the Q- and P-
products as input, and performs an iterative process, where the model vector containing the start guess of water
column height and albedo is tweaked with the regularised Least Squares solution in 28 for every iteration. The
output of the inverse model is a 2 element vector containing the estimates of the H and ρ.
5.1
Testing inverse model
The inverse model was brieﬂy tested for the the remote sensing reﬂectance values in the ﬁrst pixel entry of the
optically shallow area in the Q-product.
To determine the appropriate number of iterations to use, the number of iterations was varied between 1 and
10, for each run the model convergence criteria was considered ||δm|| << ||m||. Additionally, the B parameter
in the particle backscattering parameterisation in eq. 15 was varied between values 0.3, 1.0 and 5.0, to simulate
normal to highly turbid waters and the resulting effect on the modelled remote sensing reﬂectance. Figures 15a-
17b show, that the model easily converges, but that the modelled remote sensing reﬂectance generally higher
than the measured. The normal turbidity inverse model run, where B = 0.3 and only one iteration is made
performed best, that is the modelled Rrs came closest to the measured Rrs, whilst still fulﬁlling the model
convergence criteria ||δm|| << ||m||. Therefore, this conﬁguration was chosen in the result section 6.4, where
the inverse model was run on the entire OS area in the Q- and P-products.

Discussion
Understanding the theoretical background of the semi-analytical model has been very time consuming, and has
left little time to thoroughly test how variations in model parameters affect the end result, and to evaluate the
result itself. Only the turbidity parameter, B, was varied and its effect on the modelled remote sensing was
evaluated. But why does the forward model overestimate the remote sensing reﬂectance as was seen in section
5.1? Chl-a concentration and amount of CDOM was assumed to be constant in our implementation of Lee’s
model, and the studies from the cruises conducted in Knagerlussuaq and Godthåbsfjord in section 4.3 showed,
that they vary by proximity to glacial outlets, and this simpliﬁcation probably affected the model results.
The model has shown itself to be very sensitive to the initial values in the reference model. The model
converges very easily, but does not create meaningful results. Since the inverse model does not retrieve the

Conclusion
In this study, remote sensing reﬂectance from the ocean has been modelled through the implementation of a
semi-analytical model of the radiative transfer equation in the ocean. A nonlinear inverse problem has been
posed, and the retrieval of water depth and bottom albedo has been attempted by linearisation of the nonlinear
inverse problem. An iterative inversion scheme was developed for which the reference model was corrected
with a regularised damped Least Squares estimate for every iteration. Model convergence was achieved based
on the criteria of minimizing the norm of the residual, but the results did not capture any true physical varia-
tion, and the retrieval was not successful. The choice of starting values are very important, and more a priori
knowledge may be necessary for successful retrieval of water depth and bottom albedo.
A set of criteria for a successful application of satellite derived bathymetry based on the semi-analytical
approach has been developed in the course of the project, and can be used as a guideline for SDB implementa-
tion.
It was stated in the introduction, that for satellite based ocean optics, it is a challenge that the signal
measured by the satellite is actually a composite of many interactions and and not just reﬂections from the
ocean surface or ocean bottom. It is important to note, that however well tuned and implemented an inverse
model could be, it will only ever be as good as the input satellite imagery and the reﬁnement of the imagery
pre-processing.
A recommendation for future studies would be to focus ﬁrst on the atmospheric radiative transfer model
and subsequent atmospheric corrections to the satellite imagery, as well as more extensive testing of the input
satellite data and the information contained in the different bands.
