Abstract
In case of disruptions in the passenger railway operations, trains are often replaced
by buses in order to recover the disruption. This thesis is about planning of
replacement buses for Arriva Danmark A/S, who operates trains in Jutland.
Currently the planning of the buses is performed by planners, who manually create
timetables and rotation plans for the replacement buses. The manual planning
process is time demanding and very dependent on the experience of the planners.
This project examines optimization models to automatize the planning process.
Five diﬀerent models are developed and create the basis of a ﬁnal optimization
tool in Excel. The tool is developed, such that a planner can type the locked
section, the disruption period, the maximum allowed waiting time and the model
number. Afterwards the planner only have to hit the optimize button to achieve a
timetable and rotation plan of the bus ﬂeet. The tool provides solutions for the
locked section and the solutions might need to be manually modiﬁed in order to be
well synchronized. The test results of the models ignoring rex-buses show that the
tool provides solutions, that are as good as the manual planning solutions or even
better. When using rex-buses in the planning, the tool provides good solutions
with reference to the assumptions of the model. A ﬁnal conclusion on the
comparison of the solutions depend on the prioritization between cost and
passenger service. All tested disruptions from the Arriva train network can be
solved within approximately 5 minutes with a gap under 1.5% using the developed
Excel tool. Hence the planning time is reduced and the cost and passenger service
is optimized
Key words: Disruption management, passenger railway operations, replacement
buses, timetables, optimization tool, Arriva.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In case of disruptions in the passenger railway operation, a section of the rails can
be closed. In these situations the trains must be replaced by buses in order to
maintain the traﬃcal passenger service. This project is about developing a tool to
plan the replacement buses with respect to timetabling and bus ﬂeet rotation.
This is conducted in collaboration with Arriva Danmark A/S, who also provides
the data. Currently the recovery of the disruptions are solved manually by trained
planners within Arriva. The goal of the tool is to make the best possible timetable
of the replacement buses, such that a minimum bus ﬂeet is needed. This is
achieved using optimization models to sculpture the problem. The models should
be adjusted to ﬁt the amount of passengers, the maximum waiting time for the
passengers and the bus travel time between the train stations. The aim of the
project is that the tool can be used in the daily planning at Arriva in order to
create the most optimal timetables, when recovering the disruptions in the
passenger railway operation.
The rest of this chapter includes a motivation for the project in Section 1.1, a
more detailed description of the problem in Section 1.2 and at last an overview of
the literature within this research ﬁeld in Section 1.3. Chapter 2 describes the
current manual solution method used in Arriva, when planning the replacement
buses. Chapter 3 describes the data provided by Arriva as well as the data, that
was created for this project. Chapter 4 contains a detailed description and
development of the optimization solution methods used to solve the problem.
Chapter 5 explains the traveling salesman problem and how it is connected to the
models from Chapter 4. The implementation of the program and the ﬁnal Excel
tool is explained in Chapter 6. The experimental results of the implementation are
presented and analyzed in Chapter 7. Chapter 8 discusses some of the possible
further improvements of the project. Finally the thesis will be completed with a
conclusion in Chapter 9.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
Currently the planning of replacement buses is manually executed. This process is
time demanding and strongly depending on the experience of the planner. The
manual solution method can be imprecise, if the planner has no or little experience.
By developing a tool that can partly automatize and optimize the planning
process, the goal is to achieve better solutions than the manual planning is able to
using less time. The tool should produce solutions, that potentially has several
advantages with respect to economical aspects, passenger service and scalability.
The following list contains some of the possible goals of developing and using the
described tool.
• Smaller bus ﬂeet and fewer drivers.
• Minimal transit times for the passengers.
• Good synchronizing between replacement bus and train.
• As few changes as possible, meaning that the bus/train service is easier to
use.
1.2
Problem Description
The problem of this thesis consist of creating good timetables for replacement
buses in case of disruptions. These timetables should be as close to the original
train timetables as possible, ensuring enough bus capacity to satisfy the passenger
demand and lead to a minimum bus ﬂeet size. This description of the problem
could be formulated into the following problem formulation
• Given a disruption in the passenger railway operations, which should be
recovered by replacement buses.
1. What are the requirements for a good timetable?
2. How should a timetable and a rotation plan for the replacement buses
be found?
3. What does a timetable look like?
4. How many buses are needed in order to run that timetable?
5. How have the timetable and rotation plan improved compared to the
existing ones?

requirements for a good timetable should be settled. It would be ideal if the
timetables of the train and the bus could be identical. Since a diﬀerence in travel
times between a train and a bus exist, this is impossible in most cases. The
timetable for the bus should be as similar to the train timetable as possible. The
approach used in this project, is to set the departure of the bus at the ﬁrst station
as close to the departure of the train at the ﬁrst station. When taking a look at
Figure 1.4, one can see that the departures at the ﬁrst station are the same. If the
ﬁrst station of a locked section lies in the middle of a train line, the departure time
of the bus should also be aﬀected by a transit time, which is the time available for
the passengers to change from train to bus. In order to make the departure time
from the ﬁrst station ﬂexible, but still close to the departure time of the train, a
maximum allowed waiting time is introduced. The maximum allowed waiting time
is deﬁned by the maximum diﬀerence between the departure time of the bus and
the arrival time of the train at the ﬁrst station. The ﬁve elements: Start and end
stations, start and end time and maximum allowed waiting time depends on the
disruption and should be provided as input to the problem.
A good timetable also takes the bus ﬂeet into account. Since more buses means
more cost in form of rental, driver salary, fuel and maintenance, it is preferable to
minimize the total amount of buses used. Even a slight change in a timetable can

ﬂeet. This can be shown by a small example. Consider two trains driving in the

1.2.1
Operational bus planning
The process of traditional bus planning can be divided into four main processes.
Figure 1.8 shows the outline of the operational planning process. The ﬁgure is a
simpliﬁed version of Ceder [2007a]. The marked subprocesses are the ones handled
in this project.
According to Ceder [2007a], the main processes are often performed sequentially in
the presented order. This thesis gathers all marked subprocesses in one
comprehensive process. This is described further in Chapter 4. Network route
design consists of creating the routes of the buses. This includes line planning
and stop patterns. In this project, the train lines are given as input and the
stopping patterns are only relevant when handling rex-buses. Timetable
development consists of creating the timetables of the buses. This includes
setting the frequency or the headway of the buses and construct the timetables. In
this project, the headways are given, since the the replacement buses should follow
the same headway pattern as the trains. Vehicle scheduling consists of planning
the buses given a timetable. This includes using deadheading trips, shifting the
departure times and constructing the rotation plans. All three subprocesses are
considered in this project. Crew scheduling consists of planning the shifts of the
bus drivers and assigning them to speciﬁc buses. This includes crew assignment
and crew rostering. Crew scheduling is not part of this thesis.

about planning of buses in general and discusses the similarities between the
existing work and planning of replacement buses in this project.
1.3.1
Disruption management
In general disruption management or recovery management is the process of
recovering an operation, when a disruption appears. The recovery is performed on
the day of the disruption, eg. at departure of a train. According to Clausen et al.
[2001], operations research (OR) can be used to recover disruptions within many
ﬁelds in industrial companies to public sectors. OR is used to produce alternative
plans in case of disruptions. A few examples of application is the airline industry,
production management and logistics.
The airline industry seems to be a pioneer within disruption management, since a
lot of work is published within this ﬁeld. Fewer work has been made within the
railway industry and even less within bus operations. Disruption management
within the airline industry can be decomposed into three recovery assignments:
Aircraft recovery, crew recovery and passenger recovery. The crew can be divided
into cabin crew and ﬂight crew. The three assignments have a great impact on
each other, and solving one of them might create irregularities in another one. The
problem can be solved combining all three recovery assignments, but this is very
time demanding. Jarrah et al. [1993] present one of the ﬁrst models within aircraft
recovery. Kohl et al. [2007], Clausen et al. [2010] and Song et al. [1997] are about
disruption management within the airline industry and focuses on one, two or all
three components. Vaaben [2012] examines disruption management within the
airline industry as well as the liner shipping industry with a view to fuel
considerations.
1.3.2

Within disruption management in the passenger railway transportation, Groth
et al. [2009] describes the main subproblems as: Timetable adjustment, rolling
stock re-scheduling and crew re-scheduling. Evans [2011] creates a combination of
an existing tool for recovery within the airline industry and train timetabling
software. Using the solution method for trains, includes a challenge with respect to

1.3.3
Planning of bus transportation
The outline of the traditional operational bus planning was seen in Figure 1.8 in
Section 1.2.1. The ﬁgure showed four main processes with a total of nine
subprocesses. This section focuses on the subprocesses handled in this project.
This include stopping patterns, timetabling and vehicle scheduling. A comparison
of the traditional bus planning elements and the approach of this thesis is
presented in the end of the section.
Stop patterns
Ceder [2007b] looks at optimum stopping locations, which is about deciding where
to place the stops of for instance a bus line. But whether a bus should stop at a
station or not is more relevant to this project. Jong et al. [2012] examine a
decision support system for generating optimal stopping patterns with focus on
minimizing the in-vehicle time of the passengers. The article includes both an
exact mixed integer programming model and a generic algorithm approach. Since
the the train network of Arriva is not very big, one could imagine that an exact
model could be suﬃcient.

Timetabling
Ceder [2007c] describes methods for bus timetabling in general, Ceder [2007d]
handles timetabling regarding maximum passenger load and Ceder [2007e] explains
a heuristic approach for synchronizing buses. The amount of meetings at a bus
stop is maximized. According to Desaulniers and Hickman [2007] good quality
service is more important than making money for the public transit companies.
This implies that synchronization and a small passenger waiting time is very
important to consider besides the size of the bus ﬂeet. Newell [1971] minimized the
total passenger waiting time based on a smooth passenger demand function.
Later Osuna and Newell [1972] tried out dynamic programming and strategies for
holding back or dispatching buses. Furth and Wilson [1981] maximized the
ridership beneﬁt while still minimizing the total passenger waiting time. At the
same time, the headways was based on a standard headway. Ceder has in general
made numerous articles about timetabling, of which Ceder et al. [2013] is one of
the newest found. In the article, two heuristics for creating timetables with even
headways and even load at the hourly max-load point using diﬀerent bus sizes is
developed.
Vehicle scheduling
Ceder [2007f] focus on ﬁxed vehicle scheduling, which includes bus rotation
planning as a max-ﬂow problem and ﬁnding a minimum bus ﬂeet. The chapter
also describes how to improve the minimum bus ﬂeet size using deadheading
techniques and deﬁcit functions. Ceder [2007g] look among others at diﬀerent ﬂeet
reduction procedures for variable vehicle scheduling. Desaulniers and Hickman
[2007] describes both vehicle scheduling and duty scheduling as well as the
integration of the two problems. The motivation of the integrated problem is, that
a good vehicle schedule does not necessarily implicate a good duty scheduling. The
models minimizes cost while respecting all operational constraints and driver
regularities. Desaulniers and Hickman [2007] also mention, that timetables may be
changed in case of infeasible or too expensive vehicle schedules.
Comparison to this project
In this project, the total passenger waiting time is also minimized, while also
minimizing the size of the bus ﬂeet. It is assumed that all passengers arrive at the
station at the planning train departure time even though the majority of the
passengers probably arrive earlier than this time. The headways, and hence also
the frequencies, are not considered, since the bus timetables are based on the train
timetables. Meaning that the already scheduled train timetables deﬁne the
headways. The passenger load is considered when determining the amount of

Manual Solution Method
This chapter describes the current manual solution method for planning of
replacement buses in Arriva. Figure 2.1 describes the current solution method
shortly step-by-step. The sections 2.1 and 2.2 show two real-life examples of
inserting replacement buses in case of track works.
1. Identify the locked section.
2. Identify involved trains.
3. For each involved train, do the following:
• First station: Insert a bus with departure time equal to the departure
time of the train added with a transit time.
• Next stations: The bus should have a departure time equal to the
departure time from the previous station added with the bus travel
time between the two stations.
4. Check if this timetable is well synchronized with the timetable outside
the locked section.
5. In case of a bad synchronized timetable for the replacement buses, the
timetables can be adjusted.
6. Calculate how many buses are needed for the job.
7. Check whether a slight change in the time table can result in fewer buses.

In point 1 the locked section is identiﬁed by simply looking at Figure 1.10 or
similar train networks. Knowing the two ending stations of the locked section, it is
easy to identify the stations in between. I point 2 the involved trains are identiﬁed
by looking at a timetable and comparing the departure times of each train by the
time interval of the disruption. The departure times of the replacement buses are
set in point 3. The ﬁrst station means the ﬁrst station of the given bus. That is, it
could be either the ﬁrst or the last station of the locked section. If the ﬁrst station
of a bus is also an ending station of a train line, no extra transit time is needed.
Otherwise a few minutes is needed for the passengers to change from train to
replacement bus. The transit time varies from station to station. For the rest of
the stations the departure times are depending on the bus travel time between the
stations. This bus travel time should include unloading and loading of passengers
at the station. In point 4 it is examined whether the timetable is well synchronized
with the timetable outside the locked section or not. If the waiting time between
the arrival time to the last station of a bus and the departure time of the next
train is not too large, the timetable is well synchronized with the planned
timetables outside the locked section. If the bus timetable is not well synchronized,
both the bus timetables as well as the train timetables outside the locked section
can be adjusted in point 5. The timetables are adjusted by pushing the timetable
by a constant amount of minutes. The timetables can be pushed in either
clockwise or counter-clockwise direction. The push can be applied to one or more
of the timetables. In point 6 the amount of needed buses are found. If a bus can
be used to replace more than one train, this is beneﬁcial. A slight change in the
timetable may result in fewer amount of buses needed. This is examined in point 7.

Model 1
The most simple case of the problem is when each planned train is replaced by a
group of bus trips. All bus trips in a group are departing at the same time and
stopping at all stations in the locked section. The group of bus trips can be seen as
one large bus trip with unlimited capacity. See Figure 4.2 for an illustration of the
driving pattern of the bus trips. The object of this problem is to ﬁnd a departure
time and an arrival time for each bus trip replacing a train, such that a minimum
of buses are needed. Since all bus trips replacing one train departs at the same

deﬁning that a train is replaced by a group of bus trips, the problem diminishes to
revolve around the departure and arrival times of all groups of bus trips. For
simplicity it is assumed, that all trains should be replaced by groups of bus trips
containing the same amount of bus trips. Thus each group of bus trips can be
considered as one very large bus trip with a capacity large enough to handle the
maximum amount of passengers. The departure time of a bus trip replacing train t
should lie in the interval [δt, δt + w], where δt is the earliest departure time of the
bus trip replacing train t, which is deﬁned as the planned departure time of train t
from the ﬁrst station of the train added with a transit time used for changing
between train and replacement bus. w is the maximum allowed waiting time for
the passengers. The arrival time of a bus trip replacing train t should be equal to
the departure time of the bus trip added by the travel time between the ﬁrst and
last station of the train. This travel time includes stopping at all stations in
between as well as unloading and reloading passengers. The departure and arrival
times should be set, such that a minimum amount of buses are needed. This is
ensured by maximizing the number of times a bus can be used to replace two trains
directly after each other. That is, one recycle corresponds to one bus performing
two bus trips directly after each other. In order to model the these restrictions, the

4.6
Model 5
This model distinguishes a lot from the previous models. In model 1 to model 4 all
bus trips should stop at all stations in the locked section. In model 5 rex-buses are
allowed. That is, some of the bus trips are allowed to skip one or more stations in
order to arrive faster at the last station. Figure 4.8 shows the driving pattern of
the bus trips in model 5. In praxis, each bus could display which kind of bus it is.
Figure 4.8: Driving pattern of a normal bus trip and a rex-bus trip. The straight
red line is the track and the curved green and blue lines are the road, that the
buses drive on.
If a passenger wants to go to the ﬁnal station, it will be fastest to take a rex-bus.
If the passenger wants to go to a station, where the rex-bus does not stop, the
normal bus is the best choice.
For each train, at least one bus trip should stop at all stations. This is to ensure
that a travel between any two stations of the locked section can be made. Consider
a case with a locked section of four stations. Two bus trips are needed at station 1
and 4 and one bus trip is needed at station 2 and 3. Figure 4.9 shows an example
of replacement buses, where it is not possible to travel between station 2 and

The objective is the same as for model 4; the number of recycles are maximized
and the actual waiting time is minimized. In model 5, the departure times of all
stations in the locked section are deﬁned. This is new compared to model 4, where
only the departure time of the ﬁrst station as well as the arrival time of the last
station was deﬁned. For the last station of a bus trip, the departure time actually
denotes the arrival time, since the bus trip is not going any further. In model 5 it
is also interesting whether each bus trip is stopping at a station or not. Thus a
new binary variable hbs is introduced. The variable is equal to 1 if bus trip b ∈B
stops at station s ∈S. One station index is suﬃcient, since the sequence of the
stations and the direction of the bus trip is known.

The objective function (4.47) maximizes the number of recycles in the plan and
minimizes the total actual waiting time. Having a solution, where the the number
of recycles are maximized, does not necessarily mean that the waiting time is
minimized. The two objectives are controlled by the weight c. The larger c gets,
the more inﬂuence will the waiting time have on the optimal solution. That is,
when c is large, the model will ﬁnd a solution with a very little waiting time rather
than a solution with a large amount of bus trip recycles. Constraints (4.48) ensure
that the bus trips stop at mandatory stopping stations. Constraints (4.49) make
sure that enough bus trips are stopping at each station in order to fulﬁll the
passenger demands. Constraints (4.50) ensure that at least one bus trip will stop
at all stations for each train, by forcing the ﬁrst bus trip replacing each train, to
stop at all stations. Constraints (4.51) and (4.52) deﬁne the departure times for
each station in the locked section. The departure times are deﬁned from the
departure time of the previous station and the bus travel time between the two
stations. Constraints (4.51) deﬁnes the departure times of bus trips going in
direction A and (4.52) the departure times of bus trips going in direction B. Both
constraints make sure that the departure times are not restricted when the bus
trip is not stopping at the given stations. Constraints (4.53) investigate the
possible recycles of the bus trips, including the extra travel time for DH-trips.
Constraints (4.54) and (4.55) make sure that each bus trip can only be performed
by the same bus directly afterwards at most one other bus trip. Constraints (4.56)
ensure that two bus trips cannot be performed by the same bus, if they replace the
same train. The domain of the decision variables is deﬁned in (4.57) - (4.60).

The TSP and bus planning
The models from Chapter 4 are similar to the multiple traveling salesmen problem
with time windows (m-TSPTW). This chapter explains the traveling salesman
problem (TSP), some of the extensions of this and the connection to the
MIP-models developed in this project. Section 5.1 introduces the general traveling

5.1
The traveling salesman problem
The traveling salesman problem (TSP) is a well known problem within operations
research. J¨unger et al. [1995] describes very well, what the problem is about:
”A traveling salesman wants to visit each of a set of towns exactly once,
starting from an returning to his home town. One of his problems is to
ﬁnd the shortest such trip.”
The TSP is NP-hard and (n −1)! possible solutions exist, where n is the number
of towns. Consider a directed graph G = (V, A) consisting of nodes V and arcs A.
Each arc (i, j) ∈A has an attached cost cij, which is often representing a distance.
Figure 5.1 shows a small example of a graph with the given conditions. Now the
TSP is about ﬁnding a Hamiltonian cycle in G, such that the cost or distance is
minimized. Dantzig et al. [1954] ﬁrst formulated a model for the symmetric TSP.
This thesis will focus on the asymmetric TSP, and even though the formulation
from Dantzig et al. [1954] could easily be applied to the asymmetric problem, a
formulation from Miller et al. [1960] is used.

The traveling salesman problem with time windows
The traveling salesman problem with time windows (TSPTW) is an expansion of
the general TSP. Each town has an individual time window in which the salesman
must visit the town. The example from Figure 5.1 is now expanded to include a
time window for each node as well as a travel time for each arc. This is seen in
Figure 5.3. The time window is given as [ai, bi], where ai is the earliest time to
visit town i and bi is the latest time to visit town i. The travel time is denoted τij.
When formulating the TSPTW as a mathematical model, a new variable is needed.
ti is the time, where the salesman visit town i. First of all ti should be limited by
the time windows. Second of all, the visiting sequence should still make sense.
This is ensured, if tj is at least equal to ti + τij, if node j is visited immediately
after node i. Hence the sequence variable ui is no longer needed.

Limitations
The three most important limitations of the program is:
• The disruption must be within the same day.
• Only trains stopping at all stations in the locked section are considered.
• The locked section can cover at most one train line.
79
==PAGE:94==
CHAPTER 6. IMPLEMENTATION
That the disruption must be within the same day, means that the start and end
time should be between 00 : 00 and 23 : 59 and the start time should be smaller
than the end time. This is due to the compositions of the train timetables. Each
timetable ﬁle contains the timetable for one train line in one day. If the disruption
period stretches over two days, the earliest trains are departing later than the
latest trains, since they depart at the next day. This is not handled in the program,
and a planner will have to divide the disruption into two disruptions. When
having two disruptions, the program will provide two diﬀerent rotation plans. The
planner must connect the two rotation plans manually. Since all of Arriva’s train
lines have a break in the middle of the night, this should not be a big problem.
The second limitation is, that only trains stopping at all stations in the locked
section are considered. Some trains might only stop at the stations in one half of
the locked section, and some of the trains might skip one or more stations in
between the locked section. These trains will not be considered, even though they
also should be replaced by buses. The reason for this limitation lies in the
optimization models. They simply do not handle diﬀerent end stations of the
replacement buses. The most simple solution was to ignore such trains, but
another solution could be to replace such trains by buses stopping at all stations,
even though the train does not.That is the stopping pattern of the buses will with
certainty be diﬀerent from the stopping pattern of the trains. The best but most
advanced solution would be to expand the models, such that diﬀerent end stations
was allowed. This would include considering travel times and distances between all
stations in the locked section.
The program can identify the locked section from any two end stations, even if
they do not belong to the same train line. The ﬁrst problem is, that the program
only reads a timetable for one train line at each run. That is, the timetable will
not cover all stations in the locked section. The second and most signiﬁcant
problem is that only trains stopping at all stations are considered in the program.
No train in the Arriva network stops at two stations, which are not from the same
train line. When providing such a locked section in the Excel tool, the program
will terminate as usual, but the solution will be empty, since no trains are involved
in the disruption.

For the data provided by Arriva, the program gets good solutions within a 300

Future work
During the work period of this project a number of various improvements have
come to mind. Some of the improvements could easily be implemented if more
time were available and some of the improvements are more complex. The
suggested future work has been divided into three subgroups: Data, program and
model. The possible improvements are discussed in the following sections.
8.1
Data improvements
The input data could be improved by using more accurate data and by
automatizing some of the processes, when reading the data ﬁles. The following
describes three suggestions on how to improve the data.
Depot travel time and distance as input
Currently the travel time between the depot and any station of the locked section
is ﬁxed to 30 minutes. In reality, it is more plausible that the travel time diﬀers
according to the station and the depot. To improve this data, an individual travel
time could be assigned to each station of the total network. A completely diﬀerent
assignment is to choose the depot. The same points are valid for the distance.
Travel times and distances from Google Maps
Google Maps provides routes between any two given locations, including the travel
time. Since Google Maps also takes real time traﬃc into account, it would be ideal
to make a program, that could extract the travel times and distances between all
81 stations in the Arriva train network. This data would be more accurate
regarding weekdays and time of the day. Using travel times and distances from
99
==PAGE:114==

Google Maps, the program would achieve better timetables for rex-buses, since all
direct travel times and distances would be known.
Automatized preprocessing of data ﬁles
The data ﬁles provided by Arriva needed some preprocessing in order to work as
input to the program. Due to time limits of the project, the preprocessing was
performed manually. In order to make the process smoother when adding or
updating a data ﬁle, the preprocessing could be automatized in Excel.
Implemented macros could remove unwanted spaces and irrelevant lines and
replace the Danish letters with letters more suitable for programming languages.
8.2
Improvements to program
The program has several limitations. By reducing the limitations, the manual work
of the planners will also decrease. The following describes some of the
improvements to the program.
One direction disruptions
The program currently handles disruptions in both driving directions of a locked
section. In real life, it could easily happen that only one track is involved in the
disruption. In such cases, the models should only handle trains driving in one of
the directions. It would mean that fewer bus trips exist and it would only be
possible to recycle buses by performing DH-trips. The feature could be
implemented as input from the Excel tool.
Push timetables
Since the timetables may not be well synchronized with the timetables outside the
locked section, the planners sometimes push the timetables with a certain time
constant. The timetables are pushed, such that passengers, traveling to a station
outside the locked section, will have a low transit time between replacement bus
and train. Currently the planners can use the solution from the program as an
initial solution and make the push modiﬁcation manually. The push modiﬁcation
could be implemented as a function in C++ and the enabling of the function could
be an input in the Excel tool.
100
==PAGE:115==

Trains driving in a part of the locked section
Currently a large limitation is the set of involved trains. Only trains stopping at
all stations in the locked section are considered in the program. This is strictly for
simplicity reasons. A replacement bus should stop at all stations in the locked
section unless it is a rex-bus. In both cases all buses should stop at the end
stations of the locked section. The travel pattern of a train only driving in a part
of the locked section is not modeled. If allowing such trains to be replaced by
buses as well, the program should be able to track the ending stations of the trains.
This means, that a bus trip having ﬁnal station at the station before the last one,
has to make a DH-trips between those two stations in order to be recycled. This
makes the program much more complicated, since all variables should contain
stations as well.
More than one train line involved in the disruption
Due to the previous limitation, the program can only handle disruptions covering
one train line. The locked section can be identiﬁed for more than one train line at
the time, but no trains will be handled, since no trains cover more than one train
line. If the previous improvement was implemented, the program could also be
extended such that a bus could perform bus trips for diﬀerent train lines. This
means that all timetables should be read before optimizing, which increases the
running time slightly.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
This thesis has examined the planning of replacement buses and suggested and
implemented an Excel tool for ﬁnding timetables and rotation plans. A good
timetable requires low cost and a good passenger service. Arriva currently uses a
cost, that depends on the size of the bus ﬂeet and the operating time. The new
proposed cost presented also depends on the distance. Good passenger service
includes a low waiting time between arrival of the train and departure of the bus
and easy access between all stations.
The recovery of a disruption is solved by developing a mixed integer program,
modeling the timetable and rotation plan. The MIP-model is implemented in
C++, solved with CBC and called from Excel. The user of the Excel tool can
decide the restrictions of the solution method by choosing between ﬁve models.
Model 1 models one bus trip for each involved train. Model 2 ensure that each
involved train is replaced by an individual amount of bus trips. Model 3 enables
dead heading trips in order to achieve solutions with a smaller bus ﬂeet. Model 4
minimizes the waiting time weighted with the number of recycles. Model 5 enables
rex-buses, which makes it possible to achieve better solutions by having direct
buses. A timetable contains departure and arrival times for all bus trips and all
stations in the locked section. The number of recycles determines how many buses
are needed to run a timetable. The number of recycles are decided by the models
and make up the rotation plan of the bus ﬂeet.
For the data set with only one involved train, the timetable created by the
program is the exact same as the one made by the Arriva planners. For the data
set with four involved trains, the timetable created by the program verge the one
made by the Arriva planners. The new timetable is better with respect to the size
of the bus ﬂeet, but slightly worse with respect to the waiting time. When solving
the problem using rex-buses, the program creates diﬀerent timetables than the
105

ones found by the Arriva planners. This is due to the assumptions of model 5. The
program provides solutions with one bus stopping at all stations and possible
rex-buses. This results in a smaller bus ﬂeet than the one found by the Arriva
planners. For all tested data sets, the program can ﬁnd a good solution to the
problem within a 300 seconds time limit with a gap from the optimal solution of at
most 1.5%. That is, the Excel tool provides good solutions and possibly better

Problem formulation
In case of disruptions in the passenger railway operation, a section of the rails can be closed.
The train stations in this section is called a locked section. In this case the trains must be
replaced by buses in order to maintain the transportation service. For this an estimated
amount of the passengers traveling in the locked section as well as the planned timetable
for the trains are known. The job is now to calculate how many buses, that are needed
where and when. This should be adjusted to ﬁt the amount of passengers, the maximum
waiting time for the passengers and the bus driving time between the train stations. The
project should result in a tool, which given some input data (locked section, time period
of disruption and maximum waiting time) provided by a planner should return a complete
timetable for every bus needed.
Motivation
The disruptions in the passenger railway operations can be planned or sudden. A planned
disruption could be caused by e.g. a planned track work. From 2010 to 2013, there was at
least 13 planned track works on the Danish railways1. A sudden disruption could be caused
by e.g. accidents or signal errors. In cases, where it is not possible to run trains, replacement
buses must be inserted in order to maintain the transportation of the passengers. Currently
this problem is solved by an experienced planner, who creates the timetables manually from
a set of bus driving times. With a tool as mentioned above, the planner can simply type in
the locked section, the time period of the disruption and a maximum waiting time for the
passengers. The goal is to make a tool, that can make faster, cheaper and more accurate
timetables for the replacement buses.
