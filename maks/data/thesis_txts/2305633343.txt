Abstract 
 
Ammonium is major issue for drinking water treatment in Denmark. In many drinking water treatment 
plants rapid sand filter are used to remove ammonium by oxidizing it to nitrate. Ammonium oxidation 
involves two kinds of microbes, which oxidize ammonium to nitrite and in second step nitrite to 
nitrate.  
The operation and the design of rapid sand filter are mostly done by rule of thumb. A mathematical 
model, which covers the important nitrification processes, can help to reach a better understanding of 
the processes and gain a tool for estimating the ammonium, nitrite or microbe concentrations for 
different operating conditions.  
For this reason a 1-D model is chosen, which is developed to simulate startup of a rapid sand filter and 
involves a space competition method for ammonium and nitrite oxidizing microbes as well as growth 
limitation approach by using the Monod equation. The limiting compounds are ammonium, nitrite and 
oxygen.  
The model is calibrated and validated with data measured in pilot columns are used.  
The results show that the model can predict ammonium concentration over 70 cm depth and 23 days 
and estimate the filter efficiency for ammonium inlet concentrations ranging between 0 and 1.3 mg/L.  
For nitrite, the model can estimate correctly the close concentration range, but not the trend over time 
and depth.  
The measured microbe concentrations in contrast differ critically from predicted results. Only the 
ammonium oxidizing microbes can partly be estimated. 
In addition, the estimated Monod constants are low in comparison to literature. 
The findings indicate that the main nitrification process can be predicted by respecting only a limited 
number of involved processes. On the other hand it shows that other nutrient limitations and filter 
properties cannot be completely neglected. 
Finally, a the model is a good tool to estimate the ammonium concentration as well as to gain a better 
understanding on nitrification processes in rapid sand filters.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 The rapid sand filter 
 
The water supply in Denmark is based on groundwater (99%). The major tasks are ammonium, iron, 
manganese, methane and hydrogen sulfide removal as well as maintain an acceptable pH value 
hardness and alkalinity. For filtration process mostly rapid sand filtration by gravity-feed or 
pressurized filters are used (Bækgaard 1997).  
The main purpose of the rapid sand filter is to remove particles (Gujer 2006; Gray 1994)   
But, a rapid sand filter used as biological filter can be employed to remove ammonium and degradable 
organic matter (BOM). For groundwater, the biological filter mainly is utilized to remove iron, 
manganese and ammonium. (Lee 2014).  
Microbes populate the filter material and oxidize and incorporate substrate and compounds to growth. 
This leads to an increase of biofilm mass in the filter and to a precipitation of metals, such as iron and 
manganese. (Gujer 2006; Lee 2014) 
To ensure an adequate performance of the filter, it has to be assured that sufficient microbes are 
present within the filter. The growth of the microbes is slow in an environment with low nutrition 
concentration. Therefore, a new setup filter needs some time till it is operational. (Lee 2014) 
Since the filter gets clogged after some time the filter has to be backwashed. 
After the backwash, the filter needs also a little time to deliver again water with satisfying quality. The 
biofilm needs to be reestablished. As well as some remaining particles have to be washed out.  (Lee 
2014) 
The rapid sand filter has been used for decades for drinking water purification (Gujer 2006; Rittmann 
& Mc Carty 2001). A lot of experience was gained on this field. However, the design and operation is 
still done on rule-of-thumb methods. (Gujer 2006; Lee 2014; Lopato et al. 2011) Therefore, to 
improve setup of sand filters as well as the operation more deterministic approaches are needed.  
 
During the last years several research project have been conducted at DTU, which are connected to 
rapid sand filters. A selection of project is given below. 
Lee (2014) focuses on dynamic operating condition of the filter as well as the increase of nitrification 
by adding phosphorus and the influence of backwashing on the efficiency of the filter, while Lopato 
(2011) lays the emphasis on the heterogeneity of the sand filter especially considering nitrification and 
evaluates diagnostic tools for rapid sand filters. 
Finally, Tatari Smets and Albrechtsen (2013; 2016) investigated the bio kinetics and activity 
stratification in a biological rapid sand filter using a bench-scale column. 
To supplement this research, a mathematical model, which estimates the ammonium oxidation, is 
useful for conclusions about design and operation and it supports the aim to use gained knowledge for 
improvement of the water treatment process.  
 
1.2 Ammonia oxidation 
 
Ammonium is oxidized first to nitrite and in a second step to nitrate. The overall reaction is considered 
as one with a low energy yield. (Rittmann & Mc Carty 2001) Microbes are responsible for the 
ammonium and nitrite oxidation in the sand filter. (Lee 2014; Rittmann & Mc Carty 2001)  
Ammonium is oxidized to nitrite by Ammonia-Oxidizing Bacteria and Archea (AOB and AOA) and 
nitrite is oxidized to nitrate by Nitrite-Oxidizing Bacteria (NOB). (Lee 2014; Rittmann & Mc Carty 
2001) AOB, AOA and NOB involve many different species (Rittmann & Mc Carty 2001).

1.3 Mathematical Model 
 
A mathematical model can be described as a systematic attempt to translate the conceptual 
understanding of a real-world system into mathematical term (Eberl et al. 2006) Therefore, 
mathematical model for a biological rapid sand filter can help to understand processes in or point out 
phenomena, which are not understand. Further, a mathematical model allows predictions about 
behavior of the filter which is not covered by experiments.  
The processes in rapid sand filter are many and complex. Therefore the “golden rule” of modeling is 
that a model should be as simple as possible, and only as complex as needed. (Eberl et al. 2006) This 
means that simplification must be made. 
Therefore, a model must be found or constructed, which can describe the ammonium removal over 
time and on similar type as the experimental setup, but still covers the main processes. 
The model of Queinnec et al. (2006) has these features. It combines hydraulic model using a plug-flow 
with multi species biofilm model. The species modeled are AOB and NOB. AOBs grow by oxidizing 
ammonium to nitrite and NOBs prosper by oxidizing nitrite to nitrate. The growth of the microbes is 
restricted by a competition for space. Compound transport processes in the biofilm and within the 
biofilm are neglected.  
 
1.4 Aim 
 
The aim of this master thesis is to describe and predict the data collected during the PhD thesis of Lee 
(2014) with the model of Queinnec et al. (2006). The model will be used to predict 1-D datasets of 
pilot scale columns, since the pilot column can mimic the full scale filter and the results cover accurate 
the nitrification process. (Lee et al. 2014) 
At the end, the modeling process should lead to a better understanding of the processes in sand filters. 
In addition, the models should support the decision finding in operation and design of rapid sand 
filters. 
Several steps are needed to reach this goal. First, the model will be calibrated with observed data of 
Lee et al. (2014; 2016) 
In a next step, the strengths and weaknesses of the models will be documented.  
This will include a comparison between parameters found in literature and the estimated parameter. 
The model is validated with data, which wasn’t used for calibration.  
With knowledge gained out of this process, the model characteristics such as spatial and temporal 
resolution, accuracy, sensibility of parameters and model constrains will be documented.  
In addition, the assumptions made for the model are discussed and tested if they are reasonable and 
sound. Finally, the model should lead to better understanding of the processes in the sand filter and 
show where operation and design could be improved.

3 Experimental setup and data 
 
The data to be described is found in (Lee 2014). The data available are obtained from column 
experiments as well as from a full scale filter.  
 
3.1 Full scale filter 
 
The full scale filter in this paper is rapid sand filter used for drinking water production. The main 
focus in this paper lies on rapid send filter in Denmark around Copenhagen (Islevbro). 
Data from full scale filter are taken from setup, where all processes are involved from a transport-, 
chemical-, microbiological and operational point of view. 
On the other hand, it is more difficult to sample all parameters (chemical and microbial ones) in a full 
scale filter, because most filters are not constructed for research and since the regulations for drinking 
water are very strict, the sample possibilities and time are limited.  
In Figure 2 the conceptual model of rapid sand filter can be seen. 
 
 
Figure 2: 2-d conceptual model of full scale filter; a) Outlet filtrated water; b) Row water Inlet c) sand pack of the 
filter d) Inlet of the back wash water e) Out let of the backwash water f) Inlet of the backwash  of the back wash air if 
applicable. 
 
3.2 Column filters 
 
Two different column filters with different scales are considered in this paper; the pilot scale column 
and the bench scale column 
 
Pilot scale columns can mimic the full scale filter. Filter material from a full scale filter is filled into a 
column according to the layers of a full scale filter. The column has the same depth like the full scale 
filter. (Lee et al. 2014) 
The pilot scale filter has the advantage to be run independently from the drinking water treatment 
facilities. This means also that the effluent water can be disposed and therefore the also experiments 
can be execute, which lead to an effluent above the guidelines. On the other hand the column can be 
fed with the same row water, which also contains fluctuation in water composition. (Lee 2014) 
For sampling, sampling ports are installed along the depth of the column. From these sampling ports 
concentrations of components as well as microbial samples can be taken.  
The pilot scale filter can be modeled as a plug-flow type. (Queinnec et al. 2006)  
 
Bench scale columns are of similar setup than pilot scale filter with similar advantages, but they are 
smaller, therefore they only can represent a certain depth of the full scale filter.

3.5 Experiments in the column 
 
Different experiments where conducted in the pilot column. For this paper, two experimental setups 
were considered.  
 
For the first experiment, which is explained in (Lee et al. 2014), the pilot column was operated like the 
full scale filter. After it could be shown that the pilot column can mimic the full scale filter, the 
ammonium load was increased for short time periods (6h), to observe how the filter adapts to short 
time changes in ammonium load. The short times shifts were done under different flow conditions.  
 
For the second experiment, which is shown in (Lee et al. 2016), the ammonium load was increased 
over a long period, to see how the filter adapts to the new situation. Samples were taken to see how the 
removal Rate increases over time.

4 Modeling 
 
In this section the used models and implementation are explained. In addition the calibration and 
validation is explained.  
 
4.1 General modeling approach  
 
The pilot column can mimic the full scale filter. (Lee et al. 2014) This offers the opportunity to model 
the processes in 1-D, which simplifies the model and reduces the influence of transport processes and 
focus can be laid on the biochemical processes.  
Therefore, a 1-D filter model is the most suitable to use to calibrate the parameters of the substrate 
reaction and microbial growth. Since, the microbe concentration changes, within the depth of the filter 
a model must be found, which combines a biofilm model with flow model. After the parameters are 
calibrated, the model can be validated and used for prediction. On later state the model can be applied 
to more dimension and new substrates (phosphorus) can be introduced.  
 
4.2 1-D models 
 
Different experiments were conducted in pilot columns. During these measurements profiles of AOB, 
NOB, ammonium and nitrite were taken (Lee et al 2014, 2016). Therefore, a model is chosen, which 
can simulate the behavior of column, with the measurements taken. Since the profile data and different 
time steps are available, the model must be able to compute results, which are different in time and 
space of the filter. The model of Queinnec et al. (2006) contributes the requirements to describe this 
data.  
4.3 Implementation into Multiphysics 
 
To solve the equations the program Multiphysics is chosen, because it can solve the differential 
equations dynamically and with the finite element approach.   
The column is modeled as string of nodes, which represent the compartments shown in figure 4. 
Further, the PDEs are ODEs are implemented, which are explained below. 
The compound and microbe mass inlet it is given by fixed water flow multiplied with concentration 
set with a Dirichlet boundary condition. 
At each depth the initial concentration can be defined, which is used to set concentration of the AOB 
and NOB in the biofilm.  
 
4.4 Model of Queinnec et al. (2006) 
 
The model of Quenniec et al. (2006) respects the 4 compounds and 2 different microorganisms. 
The compounds are ammonium, nitrite, nitrate and oxygen. The compounds are transported by 
advection in z-direction. Diffusion is neglected.  
The microorganism consists of AOB and NOB. The microorganisms are present in the bulk liquid as 
well as in the biofilm. Only the microorganisms in the biofilm are considered to be active. The 
microorganisms in the bulk liquid are transported the same way as the compounds.  
It is a 1-d model can compute the distribution of concentration of microorganism and compound 
concentration over time and space. The model is calibrated and validated with compound 
concentration profiles. In Queinnec et al. (2006) the inlet concentration of the microorganism in the 
bulk liquid is known for some experiments, but not data is given for microbes inside the filter. 
To regulate the mass of AOB and NOB, an upper limited of space for microbes is applied. The 
microbes have to compete for space within the biofilm. The excess of microbe mass is considered 
inactivated or detached.

4.4.1 Conceptual model 
 
In figure 4 the conceptual model of Quenniec et al. (2006) is shown. The concept is explained with the 
figure and the explanations below: 
 
 
Figure 4: Conceptual model of Quenniec et al (2006) at steady state 
 
For numerical discretization, the 1-D column is divided in many infinitesimal small parts (dz), which 
are represented by nodes in multi physics. The compounds mass flow is expressed through arrows. 
Advection is the only transport mechanism between the different compartments (dz). Diffusion is 
neglected, since the mass inflow is considered as constant over a long time. Therefore, it is assumed 
that a steady state situation is established.  
 
At left side the compounds and microbes enter the system, which is regulated by defining the water 
flow and compound / microbe concentration. At the right side the compounds and microbes leave the 
system again. Since the bulk liquid flow is given as constant, the outlet concentration can be compared 
to the inlet concentration, by dividing the mass flow by the water flow.  
 
The particular compartments are considered to be completely mixed. Therefore, the mass-transfer 
boundary layer and the concentration gradient within the biofilm are neglected.  
 
The modeled AOBs and NOBs in the bulk liquid do not react with the compounds, only the microbes 
in the biofilm execute reactions. The only process, they are involved, is a filtration process, which 
means that they get transferred from the bulk liquid to the biofilm.

4.5 Calibration Method 
 
The calibration is done, by considering two different processes; the utilization of substrates and the 
growth of microbes.  
 
An important criterion for the chosen model and data is the time scale. The reaction rate and the 
diffusion velocity of components are much faster than the growth of microbes. Therefore, results can 
very, dependent on the observation window. For an experiment, in which no change in biomass is 
expected, because the duration is not long (not more than a day for the bio filter setup), the variables 
such as the amount of biomass can be described as constant or in other words as frozen in time. If the 
inlet concentration of the compounds are constant till the concentration in the system are almost stable, 
the conditions can be described as steady state. (Eberl et al. 2006) 
 
Therefore, to calibrate the model, two data sets of the column experiments with different time scale are 
taken from Lee (2014). One data set was taken for time dynamic and one for the steady-state 
calibration. The origin of the date is explained in chapter 3.Data.  
 
4.5.1 Parameter value determination  
 
As explained in the chapter 2. Processes, almost all parameter are based on estimations and many 
parameters are correlated. Therefore, it has to be decided, if a parameter is taken for calibration or if 
value from literature should be taken. 
 
Calibrated are the parameters involved in growth, compound utilization, detachment and inactivation.  
With the exception of the oxygen Monod constant is not calibrated, since the concentration for oxygen 
is remaining high in the filter and the vast majority of the cases, the electron donor is the rate-limiting 
substrate. (Rittmann & Mc Carty 2001) 
 
The initial microbe concentration is either converted from measurements or calibrated a long with the 
other parameters.  
The yield is considered to be more certain than the parameters related to growth, decay, detachment 
and inactivation. Thus, these parameters are picked from literature. 
The decay rate is calculate from equation (37) with data of Lee et al. (2014), respecting the depth 
where no ammonium is present, during observation period (depth 33 and 46 cm). It is assumed that no 
detachment takes place during this period 
Parameters describing the model setup such as porosity, temperature and dimensions are directly taken 
from Lee (2014). The model setup data is shown in chapter 3. Data.

concentration. Therefore, it has to be defined how the microbe numbers are converted into mg-COD/L 
and how the concentration shape is defined.

150000 cell copies per gram dww sand. This value can later be evaluated by the results of time

4.5.4 Quasi-steady state calibration 
 
The calibration aim is to determine an additional parameter set to the time dynamic calibration for the 
ammonium removal. 
 
Since the approaches for Michealis-Menten and Monod are similar for steady state, the calibration 
results can be interpreted as estimation for growth and activity, whereas the time dynamic calibration 
connected to growth. Therefore, a comparison of parameters obtained from a steady-state and a time 
dynamic setup can contribute to a better understanding of the application of the model. 
 
The maximum specific rate of ammonium utilization (𝑞̂𝑁𝑆), represented through the parameters: YNS 
and μNS,max and the Monod constants are obtained with this calibration step. 
In 𝑞̂𝑁𝑆 only μNS,max is calibrate. It is assumed that the growth rate is less deterministic ordained.  
But, the calibrated growth rate can also be interpreted as calibration for yield. However, with this 
calibration method both parameters cannot be calibrated at the same time, since they are correlated. 
 
To estimate the rate of ammonium utilization at steady state the short-up shift experiments have been 
used. (Lee et al. 2014)  
The ammonium concentration shift ups were made for 6h, but modeling time is 1d. This is made to 
assure quasi-steady in the model.  
 
Two different calibration approaches are used. The first approach respects only measured data, which 
leads to short profile. The second approach uses extrapolated AOB data. Thus, the whole profile can 
be used.  
 
The first calibration approach is executed with AOB and ammonium data collected from the pilot 
column at the depth 7, 20, 33 and 46 cm from the top of the column (short profile). The AOB 
distribution can be seen in figure 8 in in section 4.7.5 AOB concentration shape over the filter. The 
ammonium concentration in at 7 cm is used as inlet concentration into the system.  
 
The second calibration approach is conducted over the whole pilot column (long profile). For 
ammonium the measured data over whole profile is used, while for the AOB concentration equation 
52 is used with the numbers shown in figure 5.

4.6 Validation  
 
To prove, if the model can estimate the filter behavior, the model results are compared with 
experimental data not used for calibration. For validation additional experimental data from Lee 
(2014) is used.  
 
4.6.1 Time dynamic  
 
First, the model is validated on data, which was taken after the time period used for calibration. This 
comparison shows how well the model can estimate further growth and can be considered main aspect 
to validate the model performance. 
 
4.6.2 Quasi-steady state 
 
To validate the calibration, which uses the steady state approach, ALR and ARR from the short shift 
up experiment (equation 40 and 41) are applied. The model is run for different standardized 
ammonium influent concentrations and the effluent concentrations contribute to the comparison. 
 
4.6.3 Application on the time dynamic calibration on the steady state data 
 
According to Prosser and Nicol (2012), the growth kinetics (Monod equation) and activity (Michaelis-
Menten equation) are not necessarily the same. Therefore, ALR, ARR and NRR are applied to show 
this relation. This validation is done similarly to the quasi-steady state validation. The calibrated 
model is run for different influent concentration after one day.

4.7 Sensitivity  
 
The model represents a complex system with many processes involved. Therefore, the model is based 
on many simplifications.  
 
First, the available data for calibration is limited in quantity as well as in kind of data. Thus, the data is 
not sufficient to provide all information needed for system with this high degree of freedom.  
Almost all parameters in the model are based on estimations. Therefore, the correlations and the 
impact on the result of each input variables and each parameters must be shown. 
 
Second, the model can be calibrated by many different ways depending on the number of parameter 
used for calibration and the way they are used for calibration. Therefore, several sensitivity analyzes 
are done to find important parameter, to reach an efficient calibration. 
 
Finally, the model should work for different observation time and inlet concentration. The sensitivity 
analyzes can support the finding for parameter, which lead to consistent model. 
 
4.7.1 Impact of parameter (Morris method) 
 
With the Morris method it is possible provide qualitative sensitivity results with limited computational 
effort. With the approach, a regular n-dimensional grid is setup from the model space, where n equals 
the number of parameter. As well as the elementary effect is associated to each input factor is defined. 
With randomly generating different parameter values, a distribution of the elementary effects for each 
input factor is obtained. From the distribution of the elementary effects, the sensitivity of each 
parameter can be obtained by applying mean and standard deviation. (Campolongo, Cariboni & 
Saltelli 2007) 
The results of this sensitivity approach can be read as follows.  
First, the mean (µ) shows if the parameter has a positive or negative output on the model. Second, a 
high mean of absolute values (µ*) indicate that the parameter is important. Finally, a high standard 
deviation suggest that the µ* is influenced by other parameters. 
(Campolongo, Cariboni & Saltelli 2007) 
 
4.7.2 Correlation of parameter or variables 
 
Correlation of parameter or variables can be shown by Monte Carlo simulations. In this paper 1000 
simulation runs are used for one simulation. The parameters for the simulation are uniformly 
distributed. With a color code (dark red lowest cost function value and dark blue highest cost function 
value) the goodness of result can be presented. (Saltelli & Annoni 2010) 
An alternative way is used if only two parameters or variables or parameters are compared. The cost 
function representing the goodness of result is plotted as raster. The raster is determined by the regular 
variation of the parameters or variables. (Queinnec et al. 2006)

5.6.5 Conclusion for modeling  
 
The parameters are in many and different ways connected to each other. Also modeled time period 
influent concentration plays also an important role. Therefore, the several points have to be respected 
during the calibration. 
 
For the steady state approach, several profiles must be used for calibration, since μNS,max and KNS are 
dependent on the this constrain. KNS and μNS,max are correlated but influence the fitting result 
differently. Therefore they have to be calibrated at the same time.

parameter are time and inlet concentration dependent.

6 Discussion 
 
The discussion is divided in four parts. First the possible model alternations are discussed. Second, the 
results of the time dynamic calibration are debated with literature. Third, the result reliability of the 
quasi steady state calibration is discussed. Finally, different parts of the model results are discussed 
from an overall point of view. 
 
6.1 Possible model alternations  
 
For parameters and terms related to microbe growth and substrate utilization no changes are 
proposed. The calibration results show that the major processes are captured. Theoretically, for the 
quasi steady state approach the growth rate and yield could be merged to the utilization rate. But, 
results could not be compared to the time dynamic setup and it would be more difficult to compare the 
results to literature.   
 
Filtration of AOB and NOB from Bulk liquid contributes for less than one per mil as source for 
microbes of all modeled places within the filter. Therefore, this process is unimportant for the grow 
process. The process is mainly important to populated sterile filters as it is the case in Queinnec et al. 
(2006). But this is not the case for this study.

be removed and equations 50 and 51 altered.

6.5 Monod Constant 
 
The calibrated Monod constants are very low in comparison to the literature values and for the steady 
state setup the result improves with a lower Monod constant. This means, if the Monod constant losses 
influence the results improve. This effect can have several reasons. To most important arguments are 
expressed in the two points below. 
 
First, Rittmann & Mc Carty (2001) report that K values can go often as low as µg/l, if mass transport 
is not included and simple substrate donor are used.  
Within one depth element (dz), the model can be explained as numerical 1-D model (after Eberl et al. 
2006) with many simplifications (see figure 4 in section conceptual model). The transport boundaries 
layer is neglected as well as transport processes in the biofilm. Also ammonium and nitrite can be 
considered as simple electron donor.  
Further, no chemical interaction takes place in the biofilm and the surface morphology or thickness is 
not considered.  
It can be constituted that the statement of Rittmann & Mc Carty (2001) is correct for the model 
approach and that many processes are lumped into the Monod equation and the Monod constant, 
which may lower the Monod constant even more.  
Second, according to Prosser and Nicol (2012) Monod kinetics is best quantified in chemostats. 
In the model of Queinnec et al (2006) the compound and microbe concentrations are not constant over 
the filter. Therefore, additional processes in terms of transport processes within the filter affect the 
Monod constant. 
Third, for low ammonium inlet concentrations, no ammonium is present in the deeper parts of the 
filter. With Monod constants found in literature, the model could not predict ammonium 
concentrations, which reach 0 mg/L, since the growth rate would get very small with low 
concentrations. This accounts for the fact that microbes are less active in low concentrations. 
An explanation could be that Eubacteria are 10 times higher than nitrospira and 100 times higher than 
AOB. Since the nitrogen in ammonium doesn’t need to be reduced. The intake of ammonium is the 
most energy efficient way for cell synthesis. (Rittmann & Mc Carty 2001). Thus, in lower parts of the 
filter eubacteria remove the last remaining ammonium concentration and change to other nitrogen 
source if ammonium exceeds. This effect has may no big influence on filter parts with high 
ammonium concentration.

6.6 Yield 
 
The yield was not calibrated, since it is taken as one of the most deterministic parameter. But the 
chapter for yield influenced by the energy transfer efficiencies, which are estimations.  
The sensitivity is not analyzed in this paper, but equations 43, 45, 47 and 48 show that the yield can

cannot be defined as totally deterministic and uncertainties remain.  
 
6.7 Microbe population in deeper parts of the filter 
 
The measured data of Lee (2014) show that an AOB population is present in lower parts of the filter

The model does not explain how the AOB could establish them self in regions where no growth 
substrate is found as well as the decay rate is very low.  
An explanation can be that backwashing leads to redistribution of microbe. Another possibility is that 
AOBs use another energy and growth source.  
 
6.8 Transferability of model results

not all limitation and filter properties can be modeled, the calibrated model is maybe not able to

It is know that phosphorus can be the limited nutrition (Lee et al 2016). But also other parameters such 
as the pH can influence to nitrification. For the transport process into the cell, it is important if 
ammonia is charged. (Madigan et al. 2006) Therefore, the pH can also have an influence.

7 Conclusion 
 
The model can simulate the main process connect to nitrification in the active part of pilot column 
filter with 70 cm depth. The measured compound data can be estimated in ammonium influent range

The model can predicted ammonium profiles accurately in trends over depth and time, while the nitrite 
concentration range can be estimated, but not the trends over depth and time. 
The AOB concentration at the start of a process can be estimated the later development is not 
captured.  
Therefore, the model can successfully execute time dependent estimations over spatial distribution of 
ammonium concentration. As well as, predict the approximate nitrite concentration over time. 
 
As well as, the model is good tool to predict ammonium concentrations for steady state and over time 
and it can give a good guess on the nitrite concentration. 
In addition, the model can support better understanding of microbe growth processes, while the model 
should not be used to estimate microbe concentrations.  
Overall, the model can contribute to better understanding of nitrification processes. 
 
However, the results bear some uncertainties, since the model covers only the main processes taking 
place in the filter. Many processes are lumped in each other. Some processes are overestimated, while 
other processes are neglected or underestimated. This contributes to the fact that parameters are lower 
than expected and proposed by previous studies. 
Especially, the Monod constants are low; therefore the substrate affinity of the microbes is high. Thus, 
the concentration course over the filter is influenced highly by other processes, such as compound 
transport processes or microbe distributions. 
 
The sensitivity analyses show that many parameters are correlated and depend on constrains such as 
ammonium inlet concentration are calibration time period. Therefore, if the model should be 
recalibrated, several measurement profiles are necessary and constrains should be set for the modeling 
period. In addition, if certain research aim is given, additional research for the parameter especially 
important for this aim should be executed, since the model is highly dependent on approximation.  
 
From the model results several points can be concluded, which support previous findings made over 
the sand filter or show possible new findings. 
First, the model overestimates ammonium removal at the top and the bottom of the filter. Thus, 
characteristics, which are not dependent on microbe growth, such filter material properties and 
microbe activities, change over the course of the filter. 
Second, the estimated results and parameter are similar for quasi-steady state and time depended 
fitting. Therefore, the activity and growth velocity of activated microbes are similar. 
Third, the high measured AOB concentration in the deeper parts of the filter cannot be explained by 
the growth proposed by the model, since no ammonium is present in these parts of the filter for a long 
time. Therefore, it be concluded that either AOBs can take another energy source or that they are 
redistributed during the backwash 
Finally, the estimated AOB concentrations are much lower than the all measured concentrations after 
long period with high ammonium influent, which confirms the experimental finding that, many 
measured microbes are not active. 
 
For the filter operation it can be concluded that the filter development depends highly on the initial 
microbe concentrations. The time till no ammonium is found in the effluent varies significantly.   
It takes a long time to establish the filter if low initial concentration is present. Therefore the startup 
time could shorten if the new filter material could be enriched with nitrifying microbes.

8 Outlook 
 
The model could be enlarged and changes to gain new knowledge about processes, which are not 
considered in the current model.  
First, phosphorus can be added as new compound. Implementation would be similar to Oxygen. This 
extension offers the possibility to add a phosphorus Monod constant in to the Monod equation. So, the 
limitation of phosphorus can be accounted for. Since phosphorous is found to be a main limitation and 
much measured data exists, the implementation would lead to new knowledge about the system.  
Second, the current model does not respect dispersion and diffusion. To examine sharp non-steady 
concentration fluctuation and development within the filter, they must be included. This change would 
lead to better understanding of non-steady processes, such as fast but short changes in ammonium inlet 
concentrations.  
Finally, filter material properties could be include into the model such as surface area or variation in 
porosity. This would lead help to define the sensitivity of the filter to material changes.  
 
To gain new information about the design and the operation of the filter, the model could be 
transferred from 1-d to 2-d or 3-d. The current transport term could be enlarged for other directions. 
The findings could lead to knowledge about the importance of the raw water inlet facility.  
 
Since the high abundance of AOB in the lower part of the filter cannot be explained, a model approach 
simulating the redistribution during the backwash could be developed.. This would lead to better 
understanding of the AOB development over the filter. 
 
Finally, almost all parameters are based on assumptions, which are taken from literature or from 
measurements, which bear uncertainties.  
Since the processes are too complex and involve too many parameters for an accurate deterministic 
description, emphasis must be laid on certain processes.   
The author and the authors of the secondary literature emphasis on certain effects and therefore the 
authors view have also an influence on the results. Despite, the results is meant to be deterministic and 
independent from the authors.   
Therefore, broader literature research could lead to a new view on the findings and improve the 
results.  
Also, the model could be run with other parameters and other initial conditions to see if similar results 
could be reached or to discuss the difference between the new and previous results.
