Abstract
Due to society’s present and future demand for sustainable and green energy resources,
the offshore industry calls for faster and more accurate simulation tools to develop and
optimize structures at sea. These tools are based on mathematical formulations and nu­
merical discretization methods that ­ due to the rapid increase in computational resources
­ looks to become the primary and dominant go­to option when considering engineering
tasks in the ocean.
The main objective of this thesis has been to develop and validate a new 2D/3D numer­
ical model that solves the linear radiation problem in terms of an impulsive and pseudo­
impulsive time domain formulation. This model serves the purpose of efficiently simulating
wave­induced response and loading on floating offshore structures by calculating the hy­
drodynamic added mass and damping coefficients.
The weak Galerkin formulation of the governing potential flow equations was derived and
discretized using the spectral element method to enable geometrical flexibility, efficient
computational scalability, and high accuracy. An explicit 4­stage 4’th order Runge Rutta
scheme performed the time integration combined with a global CFL condition on the tem­
poral discretization.
To complement the new numerical models, the thesis also included topics such as: Struc­
tured and unstructured meshes, affine and curvilinear elements, 2D and 3D mesh gen­
eration using MATLAB and Gmsh for a variety of element types, wave generation for nu­
merical wave tanks, non­reflective and periodic domains, continuous and discrete Fourier
transformation, linear wave propagation and analysis in 2D and 3D, eigenvalue stability
analysis of discretizations, pressure computation using finite difference approximations,
and a novelty investigation and discussion of unresolved energy in free surface quantities.
To demonstrate the legitimacy of the developed models, various validation tests were
carried out by comparing against known benchmarks and analytical solutions for different
floating structures, such as a cylinder, a barge, a simplified spar buoy, a sphere, and
a box. In general, excellent numerical results were obtained, ultimately confirming the
correctness of the model implementation.

Introduction
The offshore industry is undoubted of considerable economic and ecological importance
in Denmark and likewise in the rest of the world. Designing structures at sea requires
a very detailed, in­depth analysis of the physical environment to ensure a safe, well­
functioning, and sustainable result. This engineering field can ­ more or less ­ be divided
into three main categories depending on the structure: 1) forward­moving structures, such
as ships, 2) fixed structures, like oil platforms, bottom­mounted wind turbine foundations,
and pipelines; and finally, 3) floating structures, suchlike wave energy converters, sea­
weed farms, and wind turbine foundations. Especially the latter category can be of great
importance in the future in the scope of green and sustainable energy resources. For in­
stance, will floating wind turbine foundations allow for the establishment of energy farms
in greater water depths, where bottom­mounted foundations would be too expensive to
install [2]. And even further development of wave energy converters in combination with
plant­based nutritional alternatives from seaweed farms can be helpful in the challenge
of using fewer fossil fuels [3].
When considering the off­shore environment and structures herein, the primary forces are
induced by the water waves, thus the single­most­important physical phenomenon to un­
derstand. Traditionally research within off­shore engineering used to be based on experi­
ments either as on­site studies and in large laboratory wave flumes and tanks. However,
in the last couple of decades, mathematical numerical computer models have gained a
lot of interest and importance, primarily due to the increase in computational capacity.
This ultimately enables the numerical methods to model and resolve the physics of dif­
ferent fluid flows with fair accuracy and within a reasonable time frame. Using computer
models to simulate real­life flow scenarios has a clear advantage over physical experi­
ments, which is the ease and flexibility to change the flow settings, thus saving a lot of
time and resources. Despite the recent progress, we are still facing computational chal­
lenges when high accuracy is required featuring very complex settings. These settings
could be varying bathymetry, the complex geometry of structures, extreme flows such
as very steep waves, and the resulting interactions between all of the aforementioned.
Hence, calling for the application of time­efficient and high­fidelity numerical methods and
models, leading to improved simulation opportunities for off­shore environments!
1.1

2
Literature Study
Remark: The following part serves as a literature study, essentially highlighting where
and how this project fills a scientific gap within the research field of waves and wave­
structure interactions. This part will be very concise compared to the rest of the thesis
and assumes the reader to have quite some knowledge about this particular field. As a
result, it can be beneficial to read the literature study before and after reading the thesis.
The study of waves and the interactions between waves and floating structures can be
done in different ways as it has been for many decades. One way is to use numerical
methods and mathematical formulations that mimic the physical environment. The most
precise ­ but also complicated and computational expensive ­ mathematical formulation is
the complete set of Navier­Stokes Equations (NSE); however, often, assumptions are ap­
plied to the fluid flow to simplify these governing equations. When assuming impressibility,
invisibility, irrotational motions, and small amplitude waves and structure movements com­
pared to the wavelength and structure size, respectively, one arrives at the well­known
linear potential flow formulation for free surface gravity waves in either the time­ or the
frequency­domain. This enables a linear decomposition of the problem into a diffraction
problem (scattering of incident waves) and radiation problems (wave radiation due to un­
steady body motions) that then can be modeled independently [5]. Compared to the NSE,
the linear potential equations will not be able to simulate nonlinearities, overturning and
breaking waves, or the viscous effects herein or near structures and the seabed. How­
ever, in off­shore environments, it is sufficiently accurate to discard these phenomenons
for the most parts. The linear potential flow model will only be a fraction of the NSE­
based ones in term of computation cost, thus much more prune to enabling long­time and
large­scale wave simulations.
Considering wave­structure interactions, this has traditionally been solved numerically us­
ing the Boundary Element Method (BEM) where the Boundary Value Problem (BVP) is
recast as an integral equation that then is to be solved ­ in a discrete sense ­ on the domain
boundaries. With the projection onto the boundaries, the formulation becomes of one less
dimension. This essentially means that every boundary node receives information from
the entire fluid domain, hence resulting in a completely dense non­symmetric linear sys­
tem of equations to be solved, thus yielding the impractical large­scale simulation property
of O(N2) in work effort. By using multipole expansions and/or pre­corrected FFT meth­
ods, the system can be made much more sparse enabling O(N log(N)) scaling, however
with a quite large constant in front [6, 7]. A key feature of the BEM is the ability and ease
to resolve complex geometries. Commercially, it has been implemented into the widely
used frequency­domain­based software WAMIT [8]. Also, time­domain implementations
have also been made, e.g. for zero­speed problems [9] and for forward­speed problems
[10, 11]. An advantage of the time­domain formulation is that one ­ through impulsive
formulations ­ can solve all frequencies at once instead of individual problems for each
frequency as in the frequency domain.
Approaches for solving this problem have also been made using the Finite Difference
Method (FDM) both for pure nonlinear wave propagation [12, 13], linear wave­structure
interactions with zero speed [14, 15, 16, 17], and linear wave­structure interactions with
forward speed [18, 19]. With a FDM­based model, the entire fluid domain needs to be
discretized to solve the mass conserving Laplace BVP, hereby resulting in a larger linear
system of equations compared to the BEM discretization; however, the system will be
Efficient Added Mass Calculations for Floating Bodies using the Spectral Element Method

sparse due to the locally applied stencils. This can ­ e.g. by considerations of iterative
solvers ­ lead to the favorable O(N) scaling in work effort for an arbitrary accurate ordered
scheme [12, 13]. Complex geometries can be also be represented using this kind of model
basis; however, the inclusion of these are far from trivial.
The Finite Element Method (FEM) have also been used for nonlinear wave­structure mod­
eling as seen in e.g. [20, 21]. Due to the local support of the linear basis functions used
within the FEM, the resulting linear system of equations will be very sparse, hereby en­
abling optimal computational scaling and a small memory requirement. Also, the element
discretization approach makes it very suitable for complex geometries. A drawback for
the FEM is the restriction in spatial accuracy to second order due to the use of linear basis
functions.
This caveat naturally leads to another element based method, namely the higher order
version ­ or extension ­ of the FEM, i.e. the SEM. This method is essentially a multi domain
version of the classical single domain polynomial method [22, 23, 24], where higher order
basis functions are used to enable higher order spatial accuracy. The first time SEM was
used for fluid dynamic problems is in [25]. In the late 90s, the SEM was shown to be
inherently unstable for free surface flow using simplex elements due to [26]; however,
almost two decades later, the first stable SEM­based fully nonlinear potential flow model
for pure wave propagation were developed [27]. There has since then been developed
multiple extensions to this breakthrough research, e.g. [28, 29, 30, 31], for modeling of
wave­wave, wave­bed, and wave­structure interactions with optimal O(N) scaling through
p­multigrid methods. A recent review of state­of­the­art SEM model can be found in [32].
The clear advantage of the SEM is the huge geometrical flexibility (from the finite elements
that can be of many shapes and curvilinear as well), optimal scaling properties (O(N)),
and high spatial accuracy (from the spectral methods). In combination with the general
increase in computational resources, long­time and large­scale simulations will be very
much achievable using SEM discretizations.
No attempts are made in solving impulsive or pseudo­impulsive formulations using the
SEM before, which highlights the scientific gap that we seek to fill by proposing the mod­
els for this work. The developed 2D and 3D models will be validated numerically via
different verification techniques but also against known benchmark results from the liter­
ature and state­of­the­art solvers. This should ­ ultimately ­ confirm the legitimacy of the
new proposed models.

3.3.1
The impulsive method
As announced, the impulsive time domain formulation can be used for modeling the inter­
actions between ocean waves and floating structures. Following [4, 35, 36] the method
will be outlined in the following. The basic idea is to force the floating structure with an
impulse in velocity at initial time, t = 0, i.e. a pressure release, and hereafter study how
the system evolves in time. A practical analog to this mathematical approach is to imaging
a floating plastic duck in a bathtub, where everything is in a perfect steady­state equilib­
rium. If one ­ for scientific reasons of course ­ strikes the duck from above and straight
down (corresponding to the heave case) with a hammer, waves will form that then can be
observed and studied.

3.3.1.1
Response to a linear time­invariant dynamical system
Considering a linear time­invariant dynamical system, which is when the output of a linear
system due to some input is independent of time, like with the duck analogy, where it does
not matter whether you strike the duck with the hammer in the morning or in the evening.
The output ­ called the response ­ of such a system caused by an impulsive motion is
called the Impulse Response Function (IRF) [37]. The mathematical definition of this

3.3.2
The pseudo impulsive method
In the following, the pseudo impulsive method will be introduced based on [15, 18, 19,
35, 36]. The basic idea of this method is exactly the same as for the impulsive method;
however, the main difference is the definition of the impulsive motion. In Section 3.3.1.2
it became clear, that by exposing the system to an infinite velocity impulse in terms of
the generalized Dirac Delta function at an initial time, t = 0, unit amplitude velocities were
imposed for all frequencies. In a continuous setting, this approach is valid, however, in the
discrete one, where the spatial domain is discretized, there is a natural limit on how high
frequencies one can resolve. In Section 8.3.4 the consequences of introducing energy at
higher frequencies than allowed by the spatial resolution will be seen. This ultimately will
yields aliasing of unresolved higher frequencies causing spurious oscillations of the free
surface quantities. Also, the high­frequency content will in many cases be unnecessary,
as only the low frequency is needed to be contained within the system. The solution to
these two problems is to introduce an impulse, where the frequency content is tailored in
such a way that only specified frequencies are resolved.
The specific construction of such an impulse ­ the pseudo impulse ­ is heavily related to
the numerical discretization of the problem, thus first being introduced in Section 5.5. It
should be assumed in the following that a pseudo impulsive displacement, xk(t), exists.

4.2
Spectral Element Method
Now that the fundamental background is established for spectral polynomial methods in
one dimension, the way is partly paved for the SEM. However, before digging into the
theory of this numerical method, the main motives should be stated for doing so.
When using the polynomial methods, a couple of interesting features have been seen,
namely a global approach for polynomial representation using exact quadrature for inte­
gration on a set of nodes, that have been seen to provide great possibilities for numeri­
cal accuracy due to the spectral convergence rate. Nevertheless, there are some clear
limitations to this method: grid refinement strategies are non­trivial, the same goes for
complex geometries where traditional mappings is insufficient, and problems with singu­
larities. But also computational efficiency, as polynomial methods often result in solving
a dense system of equations due to the fact that every nodal point (or modal coefficient)
require information from every nodal point (or modal coefficient).
Introducing the concept of local approximations, or multi­domain methods, is the primary
entry for the SEM. All the nice spectral properties are retained on smaller parts of the
domain, which then are ”glued” back together to form a global solution while preserving
the local properties. Let this act as the starting motivation for implementing of the SEM.
In this section, the first part of a general multidimensional derivation of the SEM will be
given primarily based on [39, 40, 41]. With the phrase ”first part” it is meant to concern
everything except applying the SEM on a particular weak formulation, as will be done
in Chapter 5. As both two­ and three spatial dimensions have been implemented using
different elements (triangles, quadrilaterals, tetrahedrons, hexahedrons, and lines), there
will be some differences along the way that will be pointed out when present. Also, both
affine and curvilinear elements have been implemented for 2D and 3D domains for all
kinds of element types, though only affine elements will be considered in the following
until further notice.

Practical remarks on SEM discretization and implementation
A lot of code implementation in MATLAB has been done concerning the development of

where some of the routines originate, but it should be pointed out that the main portion of

NUDG­framework: An important piece in the developed SEM models is the NUDG­

included pseudo­algorithms, the global assembly procedures were implemented. Along­
side this, pseudo­algorithms for imposing boundary conditions were also utilized. For
large problems with many Degrees Of Freedom (DOF)s the assembly time become quite
dominant. A discussion of this is outlined in Section 10.4.

5.5
Construction of the pseudo impulse
In this section, a procedure for constructing the pseudo impulse will be outlined. This
procedure is primarily inspired by [18, 19, 15]. When constructing an impulse to be used
as the system input for the pseudo impulsive problem outlined in Section 3.3.2, one needs
to take a couple of things into account. The impulse should be designed in such a way
that:
• The frequency content is tailored to contain frequencies within the range that is
resolvable by the discrete mesh.
• The displacement, xk(t), at t = 0 should practically be zero, which in combination
with a zero­imposed velocity potential at the free surface will form the initial condi­
tions for the kinematic and dynamic free surface boundary conditions given by (3.88)
and (3.89) respectively.
• The radiation force response, Fjk(t), should reach a zero steady state solution at
the end of the displacement motion.
For this thesis a Gaussian type displacement function, has been used; however, some
brief experiments on using a hyperbolic tangent function were performed as well without
any significant improvements in the end result.

computational cost that is increased when having relaxation zones. But in practice, this
becomes insignificant as the target­domain­to­total­domain­ratio increases, especially if
the efficiency scaling of the numerical solver is optimal, e.i. O(n) or O(n log(n)).

5.6.3
Sommerfeld condition
If only the objective of getting rid of reflections is considered, one can turn to a Sommerfeld
condition which is a so­called radiation condition [51, 52]. The basic idea is to force an
outgoing flux, i.e. a inhomogeneous NBC, in terms of a velocity on the far­field boundary,
Γ∞, with the knowledge of the wave celerity, c. Say that the temporal dimension, t, is
discretized using the time step size ∆t, then one can compute the distance of ∆x = c∆t.
Hereby will the velocity at x = L for t = ti be the one at x = L −∆x for t = ti −∆t, if the
phase velocity is assumed constant.
On Figure 5.4 a conceptual illustration of the Sommerfeld condition can be seen in the
setting of the SEM. Notice how interpolated nodes are introduced on the elements at the
same height of the boundary nodes on the right far­field boundary.
Pros and cons:
The clear advantage and the beauty of the Sommerfeld condition is that no filtering or
forcing is used in terms of relaxation or embedded penalty techniques, i.e. no additional
absorption zone, as knowledge of the physics is incorporated into the numerical model.
However, there is no such thing as a free lunch; so this comes with a price as well. The
price to be paid is in terms of imposing inhomogeneous NBC on Γ∞at each time inte­
gration stage, and interpolation of the solution values as well. Another disadvantage is
regarding wave generation that is not included within the Sommerfeld conditions. This
will be further discussed in Section 5.6.5.

5.6.4
Grid stretching
The last technique that have been considered in relation with this thesis is grid stretching.
There is nothing theoretical about this approach, as it simply is stretching the grid so that
the wave solutions is unable to each the far­field boundary, Γ∞, within the time frame of the
simulation, and hereby not possibilities for reflections. Using the same spatial definition
of target and absorption zones as in Section 5.6.1, grid stretching is employed for x ∈
[L −La; L], by linearly stretching the grid by a stretching constant, Sc.
Pros and cons:
The obvious advantages is the simplicity, i.e. a single post­processing of the mesh; and
no additional work to do beside that. The caveat is that one should be carefully when
stretching the grid, as too rapid changes potentially could disturb the solution. Also, wave
generation is not included for this approach.
5.6.5
Combination of techniques
In the context of non­reflective domains, all of the techniques presented above (except
for the grid stretching approach) will ­ more or less ­ be limited to a single wave frequency
or at best a narrow band of frequencies. However, when considering impulsive methods,
like the ones outlined in Section 3.3.1 and 3.3.2, a wide range of wave frequencies will
be introduced. This calls for the combinations of the three former techniques (relaxation,
embedded penalty, and Sommerfeld), so that as much as possible of the frequency span
is covered.
For this thesis (when solving impulsive problems), a Sommerfeld condition will be used
to capture the low frequency waves in combination with either a short relaxation or em­
bedded penalty zone for capturing of the high frequencies. The physical interpretation ­
or analogy ­ is most easily understood thinking of short waves with a single long wave
component. If the relaxation or embedded penalty zone is tuned to the short waves, the
long wave will not feel the short absorption zone.

A Fourier transformation is essentially a spectral method [24], just as seen for the polyno­
mial based method in Section 4.1. Instead of using polynomial basis functions, an infinite
series of trigonometrical functions (complex exponential functions with cosine and sine
parts) are used as the basis. Note that restrictions on periodicity do apply for this kind
of function representation. We can again choose to truncate the infinite series approx­

coefficients as a discrete sum on a set of N abscissas instead of continuous integrals
(inner products). By resorting to these approximations, we arrive at the Discrete Fourier
Transform (DFT).
The evaluation of one single modal Fourier coefficient will then require an N­termed sum,
which should be done for each of the N basis functions; thus, resulting in a computational
scaling of the DFT operation of O(N2). A fast and very efficient way of computing the DFT
of a discrete time signal is using FFT. This operation is of O(N log(N)) in computational
work effort. For this thesis, Fourier transform will be performed using the built­in FFT
command in MATLAB.
5.7.1
Extending the time signal
The time signal, u(t), will be discretized uniformly by the time step size, ∆t, as outlined
in Section 4.3 and 5.3. For the pseudo­impulsive method, the displacement and force
signals will have the periodicity property of u(0) = u(T) = 0, with T being the sampling
period. The corresponding discrete cyclic frequency space is denoted by f and discretized
uniformly by ∆f that can be computed as ∆f = 1/T. The discrete frequency resolution
will decrease if the discrete time signal is extended, i.e. increasing T. Therefore, we now
have a way of refining the frequency resolution by practically extending the displacement
and force signal with zeros. An example of this can be seen on Figure 5.5 where two
discrete time signals, u1(t) and u2(t), are given. For t ∈[0; 10] we have that u1(t) = u2(t),
and for t ∈[10; 30] we have that u2(t) = 0. Thus, two different sampling periods, T = 10
and T = 30, respectively. The result of this extension is quite evident in the frequency
space, where the resolution gets increased by a factor of three. This particular trick will
be used extensively throughout the thesis.

5.8.1
Validation and debugging strategy for SEM solvers
Using the MMS for validation of a SEM solver should be done in incremental steps, show­
ing convergent results before moving on to the next one. Doing so should ease the im­
plementation, reduce potential hidden bugs, and ultimately make the process much more
efficient and robust. Considering the following steps:
• First step: If possible, construct a solution that is zero on all domain boundaries.
This can e.g. be done for square domain using combinations of cos(·) and/or sin(·)
functions. With this, it is unnecessary to impose any DBCs or NBCs as the weak
Galerkin formulation by definition assumes zero boundary values on the Dirichlet
boundaries, e.g. see Section 5.2.2.1. The convergence test can be seen as a
validation of the global assembly process for the system matrix and right hand side
vector.
• Second step: Construct a solution that is non­zero on all domain boundaries. Now,
impose DBCs on all boundaries. This step can be seen as a validation on how to
impose DBCs, but also a validation of the global assembly process for the system
matrix and right hand side vector, if the first step was unattainable.

• Third step: Consider the same solution as in the previous step, but change ­ one
at the time ­ the boundary conditions to NBC ones. This step hereby serves the
purpose of validating how to impose NBCs.
• Remark on convergence studies: Always remember to perform convergence
tests on all local and global operators, thus including: the solution to the PDE,
∥ϕSEM −ϕMMS∥, the differentiation matrices in all directions, ∥∂xϕSEM −∂xϕMMS∥,
and mass matrices, Mk.
A small note on the mass matrices, Mk: By integration (discretely: apply a double
sum) of the local mass matrices should yield the element area to machine precision
for affine elements, and the global version should give the entire domain area to
machine precision for affine elements. When considering curvilinear elements this
is not the case as these elements can not be approximated exactly by polynomials.
However, spectral convergence can be archived in the area approximation when
increasing the polynomial order. This property can be very useful in the implemen­
tation of curvilinear elements, e.g. see Section 5.2.4, 6.3.3, and 6.4.6.
If the convergence rate ­ for some reason ­ gets destroyed in the convergence study
consider the following debugging strategies:
• Check the mesh: As will be outlined and discussed in Chapter 6 is mesh genera­
tion a quite large and important task in relation to SEM solvers. The quality of the
meshes will dictate the quality of the output from the solver, even if the mesh is
correctly composed. However, some common mistake can occur when the mesh
is constructed, about which, potential pitfalls for 2D meshes are listed in Section
6.3.1. The list includes: check of counter­clockwise orientation, overall mesh qual­
ity, duplicate nodes, wrongly placed nodes, etc. For a 3D mesh everything becomes
more complex, but the same thoughts in general apply. The important thing to keep
in mind is always your expectations when/if the convergence test fails.
• Plot the absolute error: Visualized the absolute error, |ϕSEM −ϕMMS|, on the
mesh. If the error occurs on the boundary, there is a possibility that something is
wrong with the way boundary conditions are imposed, or a geometrically induced
error (see item from above).
• Reduce the complexity: Always consider if there are more simplified alternatives
or problems to solve; with this decreasing the potential source of errors, ultimately
enabling everything to be validated in incremental steps.
• Use of tabulated optimization parameters in [39]: If a 3D implementation is on­
going using simplex elements and the NUDG­framework, and you experience prob­
lems with the NBCs, please consider using the same tabulated optimization parame­
ters, α, for the 2D and the 3D code (Nodes2D and Nodes3D). If these are inconsistent,
a geometrical error is introduced as the 2D­based nodes are not aligned with the
3D­based ones.

6
Mesh Generation
The following chapter documents some of the experiences and learnings made concern­
ing mesh generation in 2D and 3D when doing this thesis. Even though the theoretical
content and level are less than the previous sections, the sections are of great importance,
as a large portion of the 2D and 3D solver developing time is spent on mesh generation.
Also, the following observations and leanings can ultimately enable future students to cut
corners with their implementations.
When using numerical solvers defined on a discretized spatial domain, meshes are crucial
for the overall simulation task. As the saying goes: garbage in equals garbage out. So
even if the solver is validated, efficient, accurate, robust, etc., the mesh should be of an
acceptable standard and quality to ensure that one gets the same level of quality in the
output.
Mesh generation often needs an in­depth understanding of the physical problem of inter­
est, i.e., what is expected and where different phenomena can occur. Take a uniform and
steady water flow past a cylindrical object: Upstream the cylinder, the streamlines will
be parallel, in front of the cylinder the streamlines contract, and behind the streamlines
diverge. This results in turbulent boundary layers and lee­wake vertices, which calls for
increased mesh resolution around and near the cylinder and in the downstream direction
of the structure, compared to the upstream and far­field regions. This thesis considers
free surface waves and interactions with floating structures in the scope of linear potential
flow theory. Optimally, this sets a need for the highest spatial resolution near the free
surface, ΓFS, and around the floating objects, Γbody, and less resolution at the seabed,
Γb, and in the far­field, Γ∞.
In the following, mesh generation is seen from a SEM discretized free surface potential
flow point of view, and all elements are assumed to be affine (straight­sided) until further
notice. In Section 6.3.3 and 6.4.6 mesh generation of curvilinear elements will be dis­
cussed for 2D and 3D, respectively. Section 6.3 highlights the different approaches for
2D mesh generation, and Section 6.4 highlights the approaches for 3D. To support the
3D section, there will in Appendix A be given a great number of examples. These seek
the objective of easing the mesh generation task for future students, whom are working
with the SEM.

• Always remember to check that the elements are oriented in a counter­clockwise
fashion. This can be achieved using a simple routine on an element basis, where
the orientation is reversed if the ordering is wrong.
• Always check the mesh output for elements with an area close to machine precision.
From experience, it has been observed that all of the three approaches tend to make
elements that essentially are straight lines.
• Always check for duplicate nodes and ensure that only one node is assigned to a
specific position. Also, check for unused nodes and remove if detected.
• Always check for nodes located outside the expected outer domain boundaries.
Also, check that the boundaries are shaped ­ in a discrete way ­ as expected.
• Always consider the quality of the mesh. A simply measure is adopted for triangular
element [56]. For the k’th element, compute the radius of the largest inscribed circle,

6.4.1
Advantage of Gmsh, terminology and more
In the following, the main advantage of Gmsh is discussed, and different terminology and
features of Gmsh are highlighted before getting into the examples of how to compute
meshes for different domains and geometries.
• Besides being a more well­developed program for mesh generation than e.g. DistMesh
or doing it by yourself, there are three main advantages when using Gmsh. That is:
1) the ability to generate curvilinear elements (more on this in Section 6.4.6), 2) the
self­identification of boundary information, boundary_info, and 3) the self­provided
connectivity matrix, c. A small comment on the latter advantage: Recall from the
2D mesh generation section that the connectivity matrix is determined by Algorithm
14 in [41]. Using Gmsh makes this algorithm irrelevant; however, one could strive
towards developing an equivalent algorithm for 3D problems.
• The higher­order node distribution is equidistant. In Section 6.4.5 a method for
mapping these onto the desired quadrature nodes is explained. The number of the
higher order nodes is: 1) vertices, 2) internal edge nodes, 3) internal face nodes,
and 4) internal volume nodes. The numbering of the internal nodes is recursive so
that the numbering follows that of the nodes of an embedded edge/face/volume of
lower­order [59]. Also, the elements and nodes are ordered such that the normal
vectors point outwards, i.e. counter­clockwise orientation. One should remember
to match the node ordering with the one from the NUDG­framework [39]. This can
be done effectively by comparing equidistant nodes from Gmsh and NUGD for each
order, P, ultimately resulting in tabulated values, that can be reused multiple times.
• The default for 3D elements in Gmsh is tetrahedrons, which makes it very easy to
generate meshes using these. When hexahedral elements are of interest, one has
to perform some quite complicated commands and tricks.
• Tetrahedral elements can be constructed up to 10th order and hexahedral elements
can be constructed up to 9th order. This knowledge is achieved when using Gmsh
and trying to generate the highest possible order element.
• Geometry kernel: In Gmsh one should specify whether the Built­in kernel (default),
or the OpenCASCADE kernel is used.
The latter kernel allows for fast construction of domains and geometry, as a lot of
pre­defined shapes (boxes, spheres, cylinders, etc.) can be used in various combi­
nations by subtracting and adding different shapes to form more complex geome­
tries. OpenCASCADE cannot be used for meshes with hexahedral elements.
Efficient Added Mass Calculations for Floating Bodies using the Spectral Element Method
63
==PAGE:80==
The former default kernel is a bit more tedious to use, as there are no pre­defined
shapes, so everything should be built from scratch. With this, setting all points,
connecting these points to form lines, connecting these lines to create surfaces,
and finally connecting these surfaces to define volumes. The kernel allows for all
kinds of elements, as hexahedral elements can be constructed via extrusion of a
recombined 2D surface to form a 3D volume, or using transfinite lines and surfaces.
• Recombine: The recombine feature in Gmsh is a 2D command that takes a 2D
mesh of triangles and merges these in pairs of two to form quadrilaterals.
• Extrusion: The extrusion feature in Gmsh is a 2D to 3D command that extrudes a
2D surface into the third dimension to form a volume. If the 2D surface is made of
quadrilaterals through recombination, the volume will consist of hexahedrals; if not,
the volume will consist of prismatic elements.
• Transfinite lines and surfaces: The transfinite lines and surfaces features in Gmsh
is a 2D and 3D command that forces a specified number of points on each line seg­
ment or surface. Using this, hybrid meshes can be constructed by constraining the
node location on interfaces of different volumes and surfaces. Note that transfinite
surfaces require four corner points, which sets a clear restriction on this command.
• Boolean operations: Boolean operations are 2D and 3D commands for combining
different geometrical objects. These can either be in terms of intersections, unions,
or differences. Boolean operations are only available with the OpenCASCADE ge­
ometry kernel.
• Characteristic length: The characteristic length is a parameter in Gmsh that is as­
sociated with the allowable approximate element size, typically denoted by lc. It is
set on a node­basis, hereby enabling unstructured graded meshes quite effectively.
• Physical quantities: Once the complete geometry is built, one has to identify physical
quantities. This can either be physical volumes or physical surfaces. The latter
is of key importance when boundaries are to be located. The following number
convention is used to group the surfaces into physical surfaces:
1. Free surface boundary, ΓFS.
2. Sea bed boundary, Γb.
3. Far­field boundary, Γ∞.
4. Body boundary, Γbody.
5. Symmetry boundary around the x­axis, Γsym,x.
6. Symmetry boundary around the y­axis, Γsym,y.
So when e.g. one seeks to impose a Dirichlet boundary condition on ΓFS, all the
nodes with the physical surface group number of 1, can easily be identified and
used.

tutorials (very useful) for understanding many of the items from above. Also, consider
reading Appendix A.3 in [60], or seeking Gmsh user experiences from Google or Youtube.
There is only one way to get familiar with the mesh generation, and that is to spend quite
some hours working with it.

Mesh generation of periodic domains
When pure wave propagation for longer time durations needs to be considered, it can be
beneficial to construct a periodic domain. On this domain, waves can exit on one side and
re­enter on the opposite side. The way to construct such a mesh is simply by modifying
the connectivity of the elements on a non­periodic mesh. Considering the following steps:
• The first step is to construct a non­periodic mesh for the domain of interest. The
two boundaries which are supposed to have a periodicity condition must contain
the same number of boundary elements and element configuration. For a 2D case,

==PAGE:86==
with a periodic requirement on the left and right boundary, the number of elements
must agree between the two. And for a 3D case, both the total number of elements
and the configuration most agree. What is meant by configuration is that if 4 × 5
elements constitute the right boundary, the same must apply for the left boundary.
• The second step is to locate the elements on the two boundaries that should have
enforced a periodicity condition. These should then be combined into pairs.
• The third step is to adjust the paired elements’ position, so their boundary nodes are
in the same positional placement, but mirrored.
• The fourth step is to modify the connectivity of the element on the left boundary to
include points on the right boundary.

for the entire discretization of Ω, or at least a single layer of these elements at ΓFS (a so­
called hybrid mesh) as done in [28]. Hybrid meshes in particular will be further discussed
in Section 10.2. The symmetry requirement is still needed for these elements. The main
reason these configurations work is that the temporal instabilities are associated with
accuracy in the approximations of the vertical gradients; thus, a lack of accuracy will lead
to an unstable scheme. By using perfect­squared quadrilateral elements, the nodal grid
distribution forms a perfectly straight vertical line, hence resulting in optimal approximation
without instability problems.

8.1.2
Diffusion and dispersion of the free surface elevation
When numerical solvers approximate waves, errors in the amplitude (diffusive errors) and
in the phase (dispersive errors) occur. These errors are both related to how the problem
is discretized in space, but also in time. In [12] a linear analysis of accuracy and stability
was carried out for their 2D FDM­based nonlinear potential flow model. With inspiration
from this study, we seek to investigate the diffusive and dispersive properties of the SEM
model; however, the analysis is not one­to­one transferable. The reason being, that the
FDM model can decouple the grid discretization from the order of accuracy, simply by
applying different FD stencils. This luxury is not possible for SEM models, as the accuracy
is linked to the nodal grid distribution of the elements. Therefore, we seek to perform a
much more primitive analysis of the diffusive and dispersive errors.

Now it has been established that the numerical solution has numerical diffusion and dis­

diffusive and dispersive errors are shown in Figure 8.5. What is meant by primitive is that
the analysis is purely based on error estimates after simulation. Consider the following
approach:
• Diffusive test: Perform one time step from t = 0 to t = ∆t and measure the diffusive
error as |ηmax(∆t)−A|. Note that this approach is slightly problematic, as the spatial
discretization not always align in the right grid point for the maximum to be located
in. That is also the explanation for the small wiggles in ηmax(t) in Figure 8.4, which
can be seen to decrease as P increases, thus introducing more nodal points, hence
yielding better spatial resolution.
• Dispersive test: Depending on the initial condition, run a simulation until the true free
surface elevation at x = L is zero, i.e. ηtrue(L, t) = 0. At this time, t1, compute the
free surface elevation at x = L, and measure the dispersive error as |η(L, t1)| t1
∆t.

errors than a coarser one. The main thing to notice is that diffusive errors are much more
dominant then the dispersive counterparts. These errors can also be reduced by using a

10.1
Spurious oscillations
It has become evident multiple times throughout the thesis, e.g. in Section 8.3.1.1 and
8.3.4, that one should pay special attention to the design and construction of the impulsive
displacement motion. Using the classical impulsive formulation, unit amplitude velocities
are imposed on the body boundary, Γbody, for the entire frequency spectrum. Hereby
introduces non­resolvable energy in the discrete grid that ultimately leads to non­physical
spurious oscillations of the free surface quantities.
Using the pseudo­impulsive formulation, we have the possibility to tailor a Gaussian dis­
placement signal to contain the desired frequencies. If the displacement is designed too
wide, hence a narrow frequency span, the final added mass and damping output will
be limited at the highest frequencies. On the other hand, if the displacement signal is
designed too narrow, hence a wide frequency span, the final output ­ in terms of free
surface quantities ­ will experience the spurious oscillations as for the classical impulsive
formulation. It was found that the optimum was around the smallest possible resolvable
wavelength of Lr = 3∆xmax, with ∆xmax being the largest distance between two grid
points.
In the case of a too wide frequency span two things were observed: 1) unresolved energy
was aliased towards the same particular mesh­related frequency in terms of oscillatory
motion of the free surface quantities and 2) an efficient way of computing added mass
and damping coefficients as one can design a very narrow Gaussian, thus limiting the
domain size and simulation time. This begs the question, whether one should priorities
non­physical efficient results over less efficient physical results?
Remark: The efficient computation approach was tried for some of the FDM­based pseudo
impulsive models that have been implemented at DTU Mechanical Engineering [14, 17,
15], and similar result was discovered.
10.2
Instability of the SEM for free surface flows
In Section 7.3 it became evident that temporal instabilities are introduced due to the spatial
discretization of the domain. From [26] and the analysis performed within this thesis, the
importance of symmetrical elements was shown and clarified. However, a discrepancy
was found in the study of the simple 4 × 4 structured 2D mesh using triangular elements
when comparing results from this thesis and the study in [28]. The former showed that
P = 5 and P = 6 were unstable orders, and the latter showed that P = 3 and P = 4
were unstable orders. This discrepancy leads to the conclusion that it should be possi­
ble to locate why and how these specific ­ yet different ­ polynomial orders gave rise to
instabilities by comparison of the two SEM models. Some very shallow comparison at­
tempts have been made; however, this instability problem deserves much more attention.

10.2.1
Optimal meshing for the SEM ­ at the moment
Suppose the instability issues of the 2D and 3D simplex elements are accepted. If so, one
should define approaches for accomplishing optimal meshing when considering SEM­
based models. These approaches will be made based on the experiences made while
developing the SEM solvers for this thesis. In general, hybrid meshed seems to be su­
perior as these can ensure stability in the free surface elements and allow for substantial
geometrical flexibility in terms of unstructured graded meshing. The following proposals
are completely based on learnings made by the authors of this thesis.
In the scope of square (2D) and cubic (3D) domains for pure linear wave propagation ­
that can be applied for varying bathymetry through a σ­transformation as done in [27, 12]
­ an optimal approach would be:
• For 2D: At least one layer of symmetrical quadrilateral elements is needed at ΓFS
to ensure stability. Triangular elements should be exploited under the layer(s) of
quadrilateral elements in an unstructured way to allow for the most efficient tessel­
lation, with this having the possibility for being very coarse at Γb. Gmsh should be
more than capable of this task by 1) defining two domains (one for quadrilaterals and
one for triangular elements), 2) merging the two domains using transfinite lines, and
3) recombining the quadrilateral domain. See Section 6.4 for the Gmsh terminology.
• For 3D: In the third dimension, a couple of suitable strategies can be exploited;
however, for the cubic domain and with the stability and flexibility restrictions in mind,
at least one layer of prismatic elements (extruded triangles) should be located at
ΓFS. The remainder of the domain tetrahedral element arranged in an unstructured
graded way should be exploited. It should be straight forward to employ Gmsh for
this meshing operation by 1) Defining two domains, 2) Extruding the 2D meshing
to form the prismatic elements for the top domain, and 3) completing the mesh by
using default meshing commands in Gmsh.
Why not use hexahedral elements at the top layer(s) and then tetrahedral for the rest
of the domain? Simply because one either should use non­conforming elements
under the hexahedrals or a layer of pyramid elements to connect to the tetrahedral
ones. This implies more work, especially as the nodal set and orthonormal basis for
the prismatic elements is a classical tensor product of a 2D triangle basis with a 1D
basis extruded into the third dimension.
And in the scope of linear wave­structure interactions an optimal approach would be:
• For 2D: To ensure stability at least one layer of symmetrical quadrilateral elements
is needed at ΓFS. Triangular elements should then be fitted in an unstructured
and graded fashion to allow for very fine resolution around Γbody and corresponding
coarse at Γb. The floating object can limit the thickness of the quadrilateral layer(s);
with this one should pay special attention to the region between the Γbody and ΓFS
boundaries. Gmsh could also be very suitable for this task. The steps are the same
as for the other 2D meshing strategy.

10.3
Scaling of computational work effort
In Chapter 7, the computational scaling of work effort was seen to be of order O(N log(N))
of the system for both the 2D and 3D solvers with N being the DOF. This is, of course,
much more efficient than O(N2) that is evident for dense system matrices, A; however,
one can always do better! Therefore, one should aspire towards order O(N) scaling
as done in [29, 30] to keep up with the need for efficient time­domain simulators that
ultimately can improve offshore engineering tasks by exploiting the growing improvement
in computer resources. The order O(N) achievement has been accomplished using a
geometric p­multigrid method that exploits the polynomial convergence features of the
SEM model to solve the computational bottleneck, i.e. the Laplace problem, efficiently.
Another way to decrease the computation work effort is by considering Reduced Order
Modeling (ROM), e.g. as done recently in [Guler2022reduced], for accelerating the com­
putations of the Laplace problem. The main idea behind ROM is to form a snapshot
matrix, S, of velocity potential solutions at different time instances. Then constructing a
Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) model using the most prominent features of S
via a singular value decomposition, hereby generating a low­dimensional model for the
interior points of the Laplace domain. Initial experiments have been performed and shows
great possibilities for accelerating the solver with a factor of 10­100 for the 2D solver. And
a further order of magnitude is believed to be gained for the 3D solver.

10.5
Curvilinear hexahedral elements
As became evident in Section 7.2 in Figure 7.10, the spectral convergence rate is ruined
when considering curvilinear hexahedral elements. The source of error has been traced
back to the meshing output from the mesh generator. Visualizations in the form of Gmsh
snapshots are shown in Appendix B to complement the following explanation: When the
meshing procedure outlined in Section 6.4.4 and Appendix A.6 is used to produce a curvi­
linear hexahedral mesh for a floating quarter of a sphere in a cubic domain, a geometrical
error is introduced. The geometrical error stems from non­curved elements under the
floating sphere getting their affine faces split into two. This problem is probably due to the
recombination algorithm used to generate hexahedral elements.
Whether Gmsh causes the problem or it is located 40­50 cm in front of the screen is
unclear. One thing to emphasizes is that the meshing approach for a floating sphere in a
cubic domain is quite complex as seen in Section 6.4.4 and Appendix A.6. This particular
issue is also another reason for using hybrid meshes as outlined in Section 10.2, where
tetrahedral elements take care of the potentially curved boundaries.

Conclusion
In this last chapter, we seek to conclude and summarize the main results and achieve­
ments of the entire thesis and aspire towards possible future work.
The overall and most significant result of this thesis has been the successful implementa­
tion and development of a new 2D and 3D time­domain impulsive formulated model using
a SEM discretization. With this, the mathematical formulation for linear free surface po­
tential flow theory was presented with and without floating structures. For the latter, two
different sets of governing equations were outlined, namely for the classical impulsive and
the pseudo­impulsive formulations. Fourier transformations showed how to conveniently
compute the added mass and damping coefficients for each of these formulations.
The theoretical background for the primary discretization method, the SEM, was estab­
lished by defining the 1D single domain polynomial methods, including describing con­
cepts of basis orthonormality, quadrature nodes, the duality between nodal and modal
space, and matrix­based operations. From this, different 2D and 3D spectral reference
elements were defined using tensor products, ultimately forming the building blocks for
the multi domain SEM via affine and curvilinear mappings. The weak Galerkin formula­
tion of the governing equations was derived, and the SEM was employed to discretize the
spatial problem into a sparse linear system of equations that was solved using a direct
numerical solver. Using a MOL approach in combination with a ERK4 time integration
scheme, the temporal governing free surface equations was discretized and evolved in
time.
As the mesh and the construction hereof constitutes an important part of a numerical
solver, a comprehensive chapter of experiences and learnings from mesh generation was
established. This was done with the sole purpose and desire to pass on useful insights to
future students, ultimately making mesh generation much more efficient and reduce inher­
ent potential risks of errors. Alongside this chapter a number of illustrations and examples
were included to ease the task of understanding, as well as strategies for debugging and
validation a SEM­based model.
The expected rates of convergence ­ spectral and algebraic ­ were confirmed via the MMS
for affine and curvilinear elements when increasing the polynomial order on a fixed mesh
and decreasing the mesh size with a fixed polynomial order, respectively. One exception
is curvilinear hexahedral elements, where the source of error was argued to be located in
the mesh generation. The computational scaling of work effort was demonstrated to be of
order O(N log(N)) for both the 2D and 3D solvers, and ways of decreasing the work effort
was discussed. Extensive stability analyses of the spatial discretizations were carried out
for different 2D and 3D mesh configurations, ultimately emphasizing the importance of
symmetrical elements on the free surface boundary. Ways of achieving stable and optimal
meshes for free surface flows with SEM discretizations were discussed as well.
Using the developed 2D and 3D models, different numerical experiments were carried
out and compared to exact analytical benchmark results from the literature and other nu­
merical solutions. For the 2D solver, long­time simulations were conducted on a periodic
domain, and different techniques for wave generation and construction of non­reflective
domains were validated in a numerical wave tank for pure wave propagation. Force sig­
nals and added mass and damping coefficient were computed for three types of float­
ing structures, where excellent agreement with the known solutions was achieved. A
Efficient Added Mass Calculations for Floating Bodies using the Spectral Element Method

==PAGE:128==
thorough investigation of non­resolved velocity imposed energy was performed using
the pseudo­impulsive formulation to survey the observed spurious oscillations of the free
surface quantities, concluding and emphasizing the importance of correctly tailoring the
Gaussian input signal. For the 3D solver, results for pure wave propagation on a periodic
domain were demonstrated, including an example of how crucial mesh­related instabili­
ties can be. Alongside this, added mass and damping computations for a floating sphere
and a box were performed, all showing great agreement with known solutions; however
finite depth effects was seen in the heave simulation for the latter structure. With the cur­

All things considered, the final output ­ but also the process ­ of the thesis has been a
great success, really highlighting the future possibilities using SEM for modeling of water
waves and their interactions with structures at sea. The solver’s immense geometrical
flexibility, efficient computation, and robustness ­ if designed and used correctly ­ looks
very promising.

Further work
When doing a thesis, there will always be that one extra thing you could have done differ­
ently or added. This is perhaps the never­ending story of working with scientific research­
related topics. In the following, we seek to outline some ideas for future work that either
seek to complete or expand the thesis:
• Algorithm for 3D connectivity matrix: As done in [41] for triangular and quadrilat­
eral elements, one should make an algorithm for generating connectivity matrices,
c, for all types of 3D elements. This will ease the (affine) mesh generation task as
one only needs to produce an EToV matrix, and from this, generate the c matrix.
• Comprehensive study of different non­reflective domains and conditions: One
should consider performing an in­depth study and comparison of techniques for
achieving non­reflective domains in relation to SEM, including even more approaches,
e.g. [66]. Hereby mapping out all pros and cons, including efficiency and robustness
considerations.
• Diffraction part: As stated in Chapter 3, are we solely solving for the radiation
potentials, ϕk, i.e. rigid body motions. One should extend the models to include the
diffraction potential, i.e. the incident wave potential and the scattered wave potential
due to interactions of a fixed structures and the incident waves.
• Stability for 2D and 3D simplexes: As discussed in Chapter 10, one should try to
find the cause of instability for 2D and 3D simplexes. This would be an outstand­
ing achievement, as these elements are the most versatile in terms of geometrical
flexibility.
• Varying bed: One could also consider doing added mass and damping simulations
in numerical wave tanks with varying bed slopes. Benchmark results exist for such
tank setups, hence an even further validation and verification of the models.
• Making a SEM specific mesh generator: To ease future SEM model develop­
ments for wave waters with and without structures, it would be beneficial to make a
SEM specific mesh generator. This could be through Gmsh e.g. using an interface
from MATLAB. See Section 6.4 for inspiration. By making such mesh generator, all
knowledge and experiences can be collected in one place.
