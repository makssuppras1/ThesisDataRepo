Abstract  
This thesis investigates how a consumer electronics company can translate high-level circular 
economy ambitions into concrete, measurable product requirements for recyclability. Drawing 
on a research collaboration with Jabra, it addresses that regulatory and strategic ambitions for 
Design for Recycling are not yet reflected in clear requirements and therefore have limited impact 
in product development. The research question is:  
How can Jabra translate their abstract circular economy ambitions into concrete, measurable product 
requirements for recyclability within their product development process? 
The study follows Design Research Methodology, combining empirical research at Jabra with 
literature reviews. Research Clarification and Descriptive Study 1 show that early requirement 
formulation is a key leverage point for circular product design and reveal barriers such as vague 
criteria, ad hoc processes, unclear ownership and traceability, and knowledge gaps on recyclability 
indicators. Practice on repairability illustrates that measurable requirements can anchor circular-
ity, while a systematic literature review highlights best practices for requirement elicitation but 
little operational support for formulating recyclability requirements. 
Prescriptive Study 1 develops a Jabra-tailored support concept: (1) an Excel preparation tool guid-
ing the Product Sustainability Manager in identifying and structuring recyclability ambition 
sources and drafting requirement inputs, and (2) a Miro-based workshop format enabling cross-
functional stakeholders to translate these inputs into candidate requirements with measurable 
indicators. Descriptive Study 2 evaluates the support on a real product, showing a more structured 
process, better overview of ambition sources, and more specific and traceable requirements, but 
also a need for clearer guidance in both tools. 
Prescriptive Study 2 refines the support into (1) a revised Excel tool, (2) an improved Miro tem-
plate, and (3) a facilitator guide. Together, these elements help Jabra translate evolving recyclabil-
ity ambitions into structured and increasingly measurable Design for Recycling requirements in 
early design stages. The thesis contributes by framing recyclability ambitions as a dynamic set of 
drivers, proposing a collect–structure–translate pattern for requirement formulation that explic-
itly involves relevant stakeholders and supports collaborative formulation of requirements, and 
offering an adaptable support tool for circular product design in practice. 
Future work includes additional PS2/DS3 iterations, integrating the support with company re-
quirement management systems, and exploring its adaptation for broader circular strategies be-
yond recyclability.

OpenAI) were used as support in the writing and development process to: 
-

-

-

entific results or conclusions.

wording of the thesis remain the full responsibility of the authors, and all AI-assisted content was

1 Introduction  
Climate change, resource depletion and biodiversity loss present just a few of the urgent and 
important topics within environmental sustainability. Seven out of nine planetary boundaries are 
already crossed, which heightens the risk of destabilizing Earth’s life-support systems (Richardson 
et al., 2023). The extraction of natural resources has tripled over the past 50 years, growing from 
about 30 billion tons in 1970 to over 106 billion tons annually in recent years (UNEP & IRP, 
n.d.). This intense resource use emphasizes modern economic activity but also leads to wide-
spread environmental impacts and health-related issues from e.g. air pollution and poor working 
conditions (UNEP & IRP, n.d.). If this trend continues, the overall resource consumption would 
be equivalent to the biocapacity of three planet Earths by 2050 (European Commission, 2025a). 
These trends highlight the need and urgency for systemic changes in how we use resources. This 
model is often described as a “take–make–use–dispose” paradigm, mostly referred to as linear 
economy, where natural resources are treated as infinite, products are no longer designed to last, 
which consequently produces large waste streams. The consumer electronics market provides an 
example of the linear economy’s drawbacks, as discussed next. 
 
1.1 Consumer electronics 
Consumer electronics include electronic devices such as smartphones, computers, televisions, and 
audio equipment. This sector has expanded rapidly with technological advances and consumer 
demand (Grand View Research, 2025). But it has also resulted in a growing amount of electronic 
waste (e-waste), where in 2022 alone, an estimated 62 million tons of e-waste was produced glob-
ally, and only about 22% of it was formally collected and recycled (Baldé et al., 2024), a trend 
driven by technological advancement, increased consumption, limited repair options, and short 
product lifecycles (Baldé et al., 2024). Apart from the environmental impacts from the production 
and use of electronic devices, the linear waste flow carries environmental and health conse-
quences. E-waste contains a mix of hazardous substances (such as lead, nickel, arsenic and bro-
minated flame retardants) which can leach into soil and groundwater or pollute the air if devices 
are improperly dismantled or burned (Alabi et al., 2021). At the same time, e-waste represents a 
big loss of valuable resources, such as precious metals and critical rare materials e.g. gold, silver, 
copper and palladium. Studies even have shown that a ton of e-waste PCBs contain around 100 
times more gold than a ton of mined ore (Tickner et al., 2016). When these materials are not 
recovered it thereby also represents a substantial value loss alongside the environmental harm.

1.2 The drivers for circular economy 
As an effort to tackle these negative trends, many governments, industries, and civil society actors 
are now pushing for a transition to a more circular economy (CE) especially in the electronics 
sector. Various policy initiatives and regulations have emerged to incentivize or enforce this tran-
sition. For example, the European Union has implemented the Waste Electrical and Electronic 
Equipment (WEEE) Directive, which requires member states to collect e-waste separately, sets 
targets for rates of recovery and recycling, and is responsible for the Extended Producer Respon-
sibility (EPR) that makes manufacturers financially and/or administratively responsible for the 
waste they place on the EU market (European Commission, 2019), incentivizing for take-back, 
proper disposal and design for recycling. Additionally, the EU’s CE Action Plan has launched a 
“Circular Electronics Initiative” that aimed at extending product lifetimes implementing 
Ecodesign regulations for smartphones and laptops, enhancing the collection and recycling of 
electronic waste and establishing a “Right to Repair” (R2R) for consumers (European Commis-
sion, 2025b).  
 
1.3 Circular economy 
CE challenges the traditional linear economic model. Instead of treating products and materials 
as waste at the end of their life, CE aims to keep products and resources in use for as long as 
possible, extracting maximum value and recirculating products and materials at end of life. By 
maintaining the value of products and materials, CE aims to decouple economic growth from 
resource depletion and environmental harm (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, (2019), by “maintain-
ing the value of products, materials, and resources for as long as possible” (Dreier et al., 2025; 
European Commission, 2020;Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019). Despite its benefits, only 4% 
of the Danish economy is circular (Circle Economy, 2023), which highlights both the challenge 
and the opportunity: there is vast room for improvement in designing more circular systems for 
products like electronics. 
To illustrate the main ideas behind CE, Bocken et al., (2016) present three CE strategies: “Slowing, 
closing and narrowing” resource loops. Slowing focuses on prolonging use of existing products 
through e.g. repairing and remanufacturing, or through intensified use e.g. by sharing. Closing is 
about recycling materials ensuring a closed material flow. Narrowing focuses on resource effi-
ciency, ensuring reduction of resources used for the product or production process. The latter 
strategy has subsequently been regarded as an “add on” because efficiency gains alone cannot jus-
tify a qualification as “circular” (Geissdoerfer et al., 2020).  
When aiming to reduce amounts of e-waste, slowing is relevant for extending the use of existing 
products, delaying replacement and thereby avoiding excess production and waste. Closing aims

at recycling the materials within electronic products. A CE strategy involves considerations of 
both the business model and the product design, as both are necessary to facilitate the transition 
towards CE (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2025). This thesis will mainly consider the product 
design aspect of CE.  
 
1.4 The case of Jabra 
Considering the above, companies in the consumer electronics industry are beginning to adapt 
and aim to align with CE strategies. The case company of this project is Jabra, a part of GN Store 
Nord, and is a global provider of enterprise audio and video solutions, such as headphones, office 
speakers, headsets and video bars, all presented in Figure 1. Previously, Jabra also targeted the B2C 
market but has now chosen to focus on B2B, with customer segments within offices, call centers 
and frontline workers.  
With headquarters in Ballerup and production there as well as Xiamen, China, Jabra produces 
the largest share of GN’s products and accounts for the most of GN’s corporate footprint and 
resource use (GSM, pers. Communication, August 6, 2025). Most processes of product develop-
ment including design, sourcing, quality assurance etc. happen in-house, as opposed to being 
sourced elsewhere, which makes a good opportunity for addressing challenges within circular 
product design (CPD). Jabra has recognized that integrating CE into its business is important for 
compliance with regulation as well as adhering to shifting customer demand. Nevertheless, 
achieving CE in practice poses challenges. This project will focus on developing a support for 
Jabra to fulfill CE ambitions and legislation, especially for recycling, and to integrate them into 
their product design.

2 Methodology 
2.1 Methodological framework: Design Research Method (DRM) 
The thesis follows the Design Research Methodology (DRM) proposed by Blessing & 
Chakrabarti, 2009. DRM provides a structured framework for conducting design research, con-
sisting of four stages: Research Clarification (RC), Descriptive Study 1 (DS1), Prescriptive Study 
(PS), and Descriptive Study 2 (DS2) (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). In this thesis there is an ad-
ditional iteration resulting in PS2. The stages are not strictly linear, and iterations and feedback 
loops are allowed as new insights emerge (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). This methodological 
approach ensures that the thesis is grounded in real-world understanding (through the DS stages) 
before and after developing the PS, thereby increasing both academic and practical relevance, as 
illustrated in Figure 2.

2.1.1 Research Clarification (RC) 
In the initial RC stage, the aim is to clarify the research problem and objectives. Researchers re-

3.1 Circular product design 
3.1.1 Product design’s connection to CE 
A central element of successfully integrating CE lies within product design and is fundamental 
in “early-stage in the transition towards the CE” (Aguiar et al., 2022). Choices made in the early 
design stage by engineers and designers, such as material selection and product architecture dic-
tate, for example, a product’s ability to last longer (slowing) or be recycled (closing) (Dreier et al., 
2025b). Decisions made in the design phase lock in approximately 80% of a product’s environ-
mental impact (McAloone & Pigosso, 2017; Shahbazi et al., n.d.). This can be explained by the 
concept of affordance, that argues that a product’s physical characteristics influence its function 
(Lidwell et al., 2010). In the case of CE, certain physical properties e.g. glueing components to-
gether, afford a more lightweight product but also make it difficult to disassemble. A design frame-
work that affords a CE strategy is hereby described as Circular Product Design (CPD) (den Hol-
lander et al., 2017a; Sassanelli et al., 2019).  
 
3.1.2 What is CPD? 
CPD is a design framework that consists of a range of principles with the commonality of all 
affording a certain CE strategy. This means that to fulfill e.g. slowing, a set of principles within a 
design framework must be followed. There are numerous design principles, but the main distin-
guishment in this project is between Design for X (DfX) and CPD. Essentially, both are design 
frameworks which include principles that create affordance for CE strategies. However, the main 
difference is that DfX is not solely focused on achieving CE: Historically DfX has been linked to 
(Kuo et al., 2001; Q. Huang, 1996). The “X” represents a chosen property, e.g. design for assembly 
or design for manufacturability (Kuo et al., 2001) – these are referred to as principles. However, 
with the emergence of CE, some DfX design principles focus on affording CE strategies, such as 
Design for Disassembly, Design for Standardization and Design for Recycling (Sassanelli, Rosa, et 
al., 2020; Sassanelli, Urbinati, et al., 2020), and these are therefore within the CPD design frame-
work, that use the same principles (Bocken et al., 2016; Sassanelli, Rosa, et al., 2020; Sassanelli, 
Urbinati, et al., 2020). Although there are numerous overlaps between the two design frameworks 
and their underlying principles, to narrow the scope, CPD is chosen in this project, as its aim for 
CE is more concise and focused compared to DfX. The terminology used to describe the hierarchy

3.1.3 Circular product design for consumer electronics 
When applying CPD to consumer electronics, and specifically to Jabra, certain design principles 
become central. Given the sector’s heavy resource use and high-value e-waste, understanding how 
CPD, and particularly Design for Recycling, can help address these impacts is essential. 
3.1.3.1  Design for Recycling 
While having many overlaps, Design for Recycling differentiates from Design for Disassembly by 
focusing more on the materials’ ability to be recycled, whereas Design for Disassembly is more 
interested in the disassembling of components to afford repair, upgrades, modularity and recy-
cling (Circit Norden, n.d.). Both are central for enabling recycling and therefore this project will 
include Design for Disassembly within the other and refer to all principles that afford recycling 
(including Design for Standardization and other overlapping principles) as Design for Recycling. 
There are numerous guidelines for designing consumer electronics for recycling, and some exam-
ples include: 
• Recyclable materials: Avoiding composites, choosing mono materials and avoiding 
manufacturing processes that combine materials e.g. 3K injection molding (Hultgren, 
2012), avoiding additives in metal alloys, and choosing fewer material types (Circit Nor-
den, n.d.).   
• Disassembly: Allowing disassembly by avoiding glueing different materials/components 
together, choosing joint types that can be disassembled non-destructively, e.g. screws, 
standardization of connectors and tools, modular architecture so materials or compo-
nents with similar recycling process are in one module, making critical parts, e.g. the bat-
tery or PCBs easily accessible (Circit Norden, n.d.; Formentini et al., 2025; Formentini & 
Ramanujan, 2023).  
• Guidance and information: Providing manuals for disassembly, material and product 
information, use existing recycling codes for plastics (Circit Norden, n.d.) 
When designing for recycling, it is important to note that the principles are not only ensuring 
feasibility of recycling, but also economic viability, as disassembly can be a time-consuming task. 
When aiming at implementing Design for Recycling it is therefore important to consider the 
above-mentioned guidelines, to afford enhanced recycling, minimize new resource consumption 
and reduce e-waste.

on how to implement in the PDP arises. A generic PDP is initiated by a planning phase where 
the market demand is explored, and the product is proposed. The common outcome of such step 
is a product requirement list, which will set the basis for the conceptualization, where the require-
ments are prioritized and the main architecture is defined. After this, the product is detailed, 
tested, validated and will be ready for manufacture and eventually launch (Mocko & Fenves, 2003;

As mentioned previously, the early design phase is where majority of the environmental perfor-

3.2 Product requirements 
Well-formulated product requirements can integrate circularity early, avoid downstream conflicts, 
and facilitate trade-offs (Byggeth & Hochschorner, 2006; Kravchenko et al., 2021). When CE cri-
teria are built into the requirements, they become non-negotiable design parameters, ensuring 
the product aligns with the company’s sustainability goals. At this stage, stakeholder needs, ex-
pectations, and constraints (from users, business, regulatory, environmental, and technical stake-
holders) are systematically gathered, analyzed, and translated into structured product require-
ments (Martin et al., 2002; Pacheco & Garcia, 2012). 
It has been argued in literature that while many organizations set broad CE targets, “existing cir-
cular design criteria lack specificity and measurability,” (Dreier et al., 2025) which makes them diffi-
cult to implement in product design. This leads on to what a “good” requirement is defined by. 
Following characteristics define a “good” requirement: 
• Unambiguous: Stated so it can only be interpreted one way, and easy to understand. Also 
relates to being singular, stating a single characteristic, to avoid confusion (Cross, 2021; 
DS/ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018, 2018) 
• Measurable: Expressed in quantified terms, e.g. specifying a maximum weight rather than 
using “lightweight” (Cross, 2021) 
• Feasible: Is realistic and can be realized technically (Cross, 2021; DS/ISO/IEC/IEEE 
29148:2018, 2018) 
• Necessary / relevant: Without the requirement a product characteristic cannot be ful-
filled by other requirements. The requirement is therefore essential to the product. Here 
it is also recommended to avoid loopholes such as “as much as possible” as this undermines 
the requirements necessity (Cross, 2021; DS/ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018, 2018) 
• Complete: The requirement describes the product’s characteristics to such an extent that 
no other information is needed to understand it (Cross, 2021; DS/ISO/IEC/IEEE 
29148:2018, 2018) 
• Verifiable: Relates to measurability and ensures that the requirement can be proven to be 
fulfilled (DS/ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018, 2018). 
It is generally also advised to use clear language and avoid subjective, vague or open-ended terms 
such as “user friendly”, “significant”, “as appropriate” or “cost effective” (DS/ISO/IEC/IEEE 
29148:2018, 2018).   
For the case of recycling, it is therefore not good enough to have a requirement like the example 
below:  
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
 
27 of 156 
 
 “The product should be as easy as possible to disassemble for repair and recycling, preferably 
without using too many different tools and without making the product look ugly or com-
promising on quality.” 
This requirement involves many fallacies including subjective language (“look ugly”, too many” 
and “quality”) suggesting vagueness, loopholes (“preferably”, “as easy as possible”, “should”) under-
mining its necessity, lack of measurable indicators that hinders verification and does not express 
what components or to what extent disassembly should happen. Instead, a better alternative could 
be: 
 “The product shall allow removal and replacement of the main battery within 5 minutes by 
a trained technician using only standard hand tools and with 0  
irreversible connectors.”  
This example enhances the requirements’ necessity and unambiguity by using specific language, 
giving measurable indicators, mandates by whom the process is done by, and allows for verifica-
tion by doing a test. Therefore, understanding how product requirements are elicited and formu-
lated is critical to enabling CPD, as without the proper requirement formulation, engineers and 
designers may misunderstand the requirement, and either make wrong design decisions or ignore 
the requirement completely (Shahbazi et al., n.d.; J. X. Wang et al., 2022).

3.3.1 GNs environmental strategy 
GN has formulated a corporate sustainability strategy called “Better for Planet”, in which circular-
ity is one of the key pillars. It outlines concrete initiatives such as increasing the amount of recy-
cled or bio-based materials, compliance with market specific legislation, e.g. WEEE in the EU, 
and preparing for upcoming R2R regulation (GN, n.d.). However, the strategy is still largely high-
level: Circularity initiatives such as “design all products for recyclability” exist as direction-setting 
statements, but they have not yet been broken down into measurable design targets. In practice, 
it is not yet defined which metrics are realistic to track, what kind of data is available internally, 
and what “recyclability” should mean for different product families (all interview transcriptions 
are presented in Appendix A: All Interview Transcriptions). This lack of specificity at the strategic 
level makes it challenging to integrate the ambition of this design for recycling, as no concrete 
targets or guidelines have been decided on and communicated to project leaders or R&D repre-
sentatives, who are responsible for prioritizing and integrating it into the PDP. This results in a 
situation described by the PSM and the GSM as a “chicken-and-egg” dilemma: Product develop-
ment teams find it difficult to formulate precise CPD requirements without defined targets, and 
GN Group Sustainability is hesitant to commit to specific circularity targets without understand-
ing what product development can realistically deliver. This knowledge barrier is also represented 
in literature where translation of knowledge between teams and management hierarchies hinders

3.3.1.1 Identified barriers to CPD in the corporate environmental strategy 
 Lack of measurable targets and guidelines inhibits the integration of CPD in Jabra’s PDP 
e.g. through requirements. Without clear metrics, ownership structures, or traceable KPIs,

3.3.2 Jabra’s product development process 
While a clear corporate strategy can enable CPD, it is also important to consider if and how Jabra 
is currently integrating CPD into their PDP, especially through requirements. Jabra’s PDP is a 
structured and often rapid development model, where new product concepts typically are based 
on existing designs rather than developed from scratch. Redesign projects often involve incre-
mental updates, such as cost-down material substitutions or interface changes (e.g., switching 
from USB-A to USB-C). These incremental redesigns do not normally involve revisiting or refor-
mulating product requirements (PSM, personal communication, September 8, 2025; the MTL, 
personal communication, September 8, 2025), and therefore this thesis focuses on new product 
development, as this is where CPD principles can be integrated in the early stages.  
3.3.2.1 Requirement formulation in the product development process 
Jabra’s PDP is structured into a sequence of gated phases, each representing a formal decision 
checkpoint with defined deliverables and cross-functional reviews (MTL, personal communica-
tion, September 8, 2025). Overall, their PDP is very similar to the generic PDP explained in Section 
3.1.4, starting with a planning/exploration phase, secondly a conceptualization phase, followed 
by detailing, launching and evaluating (PSM, personal communication, September 8, 2025). The 
following section will focus on the early stages of PDP, as this is where it was identified in Section 
3.1.4 that CPD needs to be included.  
Exploration phase: Like the generic PDP, the exploration phase’s aim is to explore market op-
portunities, define the business case and develop requirements, which will serve as the foundation 
for further development. The product architecture is still flexible but overall directions, especially 
ones affecting cost and market positioning, are locked before next phase. Therefore, the main 
delivery is a set of product requirements, that all relevant stakeholders are asked to upload to 
Jabra’s requirement software.  
The stakeholders include R&D, which consists of hardware, audio, software, mechanical, user 
experience (UX) among others, as well as AMS, commercial lead and project managers. The PSM 
is not formally included in the process, but experiences gradual inclusion due to colleagues being 
aware of her role and inviting her in (the PSM, personal communication, September 8, 2025).

This ad-hoc process hinders integrating CPD early on, as the PSM sits with valuable knowledge 
about design improvements for the environment, e.g. from LCAs that fails to be translated into 
requirements. 
The interviews with the PSM and the MTL indicated that the requirement software is organiza-
tional-wide accessible, and all stakeholders included in the PDP can access all current and previ-
ous projects, see requirements made from different departments, and follow the version history 
of the requirement process for a product, i.e. the different iterations of the specific requirement 
after rounds of validation and negotiation (which mostly happen in the following phase). Thus, 
the requirement software serves as a reference from previous projects, a dynamic tool for updating 
and negotiating requirements, and a traceable collection of all requirements from current and 
previous projects.  
The interviews also suggest that many CPD principles are not yet embedded in the PDP, and MTL 
even went to say that they are “unambitious” and “could be tougher”. Especially the design for 
recycling requirement is referred to as “fluffy”: “As far as possible, the materials should be separated 
into following categories: Metal, plastic, foam, electronics”. When asked about the consequences of 
this fluffy requirement, both argue that the ambiguity down prioritizes its implementation since 
project teams easily can argue against the requirement due to cost constraints. This barrier is 
underpinned by literature arguing that design challenges at the end of product life cycles are not 
integrated in the early design stages of product development, which can either be due to lack of 
knowledge or because the PDP happens rapidly (Shahbazi et al., n.d.; J. X. Wang et al., 2022), 
where the latter especially characterizes Jabra’s case, as mentioned by several interviewees. 
Conceptualization phase: During the conceptualization, the requirements are used to develop 
ideas and prototypes. This helps to highlight possible trade-offs between requirements, e.g. for 
feasibility or cost concerns. This facilitates negotiation and validation of the requirements, so the 
requirements are translated into engineering characteristics and architecture can be locked. As 
emphasized by MTL, “most decisions affecting repairability and sustainability happen during concep-
tualization,” since the later phases offer limited opportunity to alter fundamental design choices 
(the MTL, personal communication, September 8, 2025). This statement also reflects that CPD 
considerations are accounted for later than other product considerations. This phase ends with 
the overall architecture of the product, and the following phases of detailing and launching will 
begin.  
3.3.2.2 Identified barriers to integrating circularity in the PDP 
 Jabra struggles to integrate CPD and Design for Recycling, in the early stages of the PDP. 
Knowledge from the PSM rarely reaches decision-making and requirement-setting activi-
ties, and that the current recycling requirement is perceived as “fluffy” and is down-prior-
itized in negotiations. Taken together, these findings indicate that a central challenge in 
==PAGE:33==

integrating Design for Recycling early in the PDP concerns the formulation of measurable 
product requirements. 
 
3.3.3 End-of-Life initiatives 
Jabra has pursued downstream circularity initiatives focused on repair and take-back. Internally, 
these are driven mainly by AMS (the AOM, personal communication, August 25, 2025). 
Repair and service are described as a strategic focus area, driven by both regulatory developments, 
e.g. R2R and warranties. (PSM, personal communication, August 4, 2025; AOM, personal com-
munication, August 25, 2025). AMS has developed a repair strategy and decision guideline that 
classify high monetary value components and specify that selected parts must be removable and 
reusable. This guideline is linked to an internal repairability scoring system, derived from opera-
tional experience in Jabra’s repair centers. Each product is expected to achieve at least a B / “very 
repairable” rating on Jabra’s internal Repairability Index, which influences design decisions re-
garding, e.g., battery access, non-destructive disassembly of enclosures, and the accessibility of 
failure-prone components (the REP, personal communication, August 18, 2025).  This demon-
strates that repairability is being operationalized within Jabra through measurable criteria and 
concrete design expectations. So, an area where circularity considerations have begun to take prac-
tical form. 
In addition to repair, Jabra has implemented a recycling take-back program in collaboration with 
an external recycling partner. The partnership covers logistics, disassembly, material separation, 
and documentation of recycled material streams (e.g. aluminum, plastics, copper). The initiative 
is embedded in Jabra’s premium service offering (Jabra Care), which allows customers to return 
used products for responsible end-of-life handling (GN, 2023). However, despite these operational 
improvements, the initiative has been developed without formal product-level recyclability re-
quirements. As mentioned earlier, recyclability performance is not yet defined, tracked, or used as 
a design parameter, and there are knowledge gaps regarding how to evaluate or specify product 
recyclability during development.  
3.3.3.1 Identified barrier in End-of-Life initiatives 
 While Jabra has established structured approaches for repairability, it lacks equivalent 
frameworks and expertise for recyclability. The absence of measurable recyclability criteria 
and systematic methods for circular requirement formulation limits the company’s ability 
to link end-of-life outcomes with design-stage decisions.

4.1 Empirical study 
This section presents the empirical study conducted as part of DS1. Building on the finding from 
the previous RC chapter that Jabra’s recycling ambitions are not translated into clear product 
requirements, the purpose here is to understand why this happens. The overall aim of the empir-
ical study is to identify which specific barriers and enablers (SQ1) are embedded in Jabra’s current 
practice for formulating recycling related product requirements and where additional support is 
needed.  
The study explored three themes:  
1. Barriers: Which barriers are experienced when attempting to implement recycling re-
quirements?  
2. Competence (and awareness): What knowledge, methods and indicators are known or 
used by the different roles? 
3. Support needs: What kind of support (e.g. new knowledge, processes and approaches) 
would help Jabra formulate recycling requirements? 
These insights are used to define key barriers that support measures must address and to define 
the scope for the SLR. The empirical study employed semi-structured interviews with the PSM, 
GSM and MTL. For the summarized transcriptions, see Appendix A: All Interview Transcriptions, 
and for interview guides and methods see Appendix B: Empirical Interview Guide. 
4.1.1 Barriers for circular product design 
Lack of internal requirement formulation support: In RC it was mentioned by the PSM and 
MTL that the recyclability requirements are fluffy and not measurable. This raised questions 
about the existing support for requirement formulation within Jabra. Jabra has no formal guide-
line for what constitutes a “good” requirement, meaning that formulation is left to the responsible 
stakeholder. The internal requirement software does not assist in formulating new requirements. 
Instead, most requirements for new projects are based on, or inspired by, previous projects. This 
helps explain why current requirements often remain qualitative and why no specific design for 
recycling requirements have yet been defined. 
Lack of organizational anchor and traceability: The PSM repeatedly emphasized that sustain-
ability-related product requirements must have a clear organizational origin, rather than being 
perceived as the PSM’s personal agenda or ambitions. For the design team to accept and prioritize 
new requirements, it needs to be transparent who owns them and on what basis they are intro-
duced (the PSM, personal communication, September 8, 2025). Requirements rooted in legisla-
tion, customer demands and strategy are higher prioritized, especially when it is clearly commu-
nicated. This was reinforced by the MTL: “Requirements have to be anchored in the organization. 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
 
38 of 156 
 
They must be clear, justified, and balanced with other constraints. Otherwise, they won’t be taken 
seriously.” (the MTL, personal communication, September 8, 2025). 
Lack of external guidelines: Formulating CPD requirements is further challenged by limited 
external guidance. When legislation or certifications such as TCO certified (Finn, 2025), or the 
R2R (European Commission, n.d.-b) do not provide ready-made, quantitative criteria, it becomes 
difficult to translate ambitions into “hard” product requirements. As the GSM stated: “For recy-
clability (…) there is no regulation, no indices and it’s hard to translate into hard product re-
quirements” (the GSM, personal communication, September 9, 2025).  The MTL, similarly noted 
that the current requirement only states that “materials must be separable” without specifying 
how this should be achieved in practice (the MTL, personal communication, September 8, 2025).  
Because recyclability is not regulated for Jabra’s product categories and no external certification 
currently drives it, recyclability tends to be treated as a goodwill ambition rather than a require-
ment. In contrast, as explored in the RC, the repair-related requirements have been formulated in 
response to the R2R Directive (European Commission, n.d.-b) and TCO certified (Finn, 2025). 
However, both the GSM and the PSM pointed out that these requirements are “generic” and “not 
tailored to specific product categories” and are risking being “one size fits all” approach, where 
some products could (in principle) could have more potential for repairability than what is cur-
rently required (the PSM, personal communication, September 8, 2025; the GSM, personal com-
munication, September 9, 2025).  
Lack of recyclability and circularity knowledge: The interviewees expressed uncertainty about 
how to differentiate between circular strategies (e.g. repair, refurbish, recycle) and what each im-
plies for product design and business models. As the PSM expressed: “The difference is not clear, 
how does it change the product design or the system around it?”. All interviewees expressed a 
repeated call for knowledge about what circularity ambitions mean “hands on across the com-
pany”.  
The interviewees requested more training on circular design principles, including when to prior-
itize Design for Recycling versus Design for Repair and how to translate the targets into product 
development. The MTL mentioned there is an abstract knowledge of general CPD in R&D: 
“R&D understands circularity abstractly, but translating it into design actions, second-life strate-
gies, or quantifiable trade-offs remains unclear”. The ambiguous requirement is therefore hard to 
understand the implications of, which eventually undermines its importance. This all emphasizes 
the knowledge gaps on how to properly define measurable product requirements.  
Late or lack of involvement: The PSM explained that no formal step in the company’s PDP 
triggers implementation of circularity and instead the PSM is invited “by people who know that 
sustainability requirements come from me”. As a result, CPD considerations sometimes enter 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
 
 
39 of 156 
 
projects only when a team member proactively involves the PSM, and that sometimes is too late. 
This is a challenge since it is the early design phases that determine the architecture of the prod-
ucts, emphasizing the “need for requirements or guidelines going into the concepting phase in 
order to consider it from scratch” as MTL said (the MTL, personal communication, September 8, 
2025). It was further highlighted that results from LCAs reach the product development team too 
late in the PDP, causing an exclusion of insights and potential requirements to be built into the 
products, further emphasizing the lack of knowledge. Without a formal mechanism to involve 
the sustainability team, progressive insights from the PSM, e.g. LCA knowledge, don’t reach new 
development of products.  
Lack of structured formulation process: Apart from late involvement, the MTL and PSM men-
tioned an unstructured requirement formulation process in the PDP. The PSM says: “We don’t do 
workshops to elicit requirements. It mostly happens in 1:1 meetings or emails. It shouldn’t hap-
pen though email.” Here, it was identified that the requirement elicitation process is rarely sup-
ported by “common processes” or “collective approaches, methods or tools” but was rather “very 
much in the hands of the people” the MTL highlights. This led to two major challenges: The lack 
of a standardized process which can cause varying outcomes, and inconsistent cross-functional 
involvement which may exclude valuable information or interests.  
When assessing Jabra’s PDP with the MTL, project managers were found to play a central role in 
delegating tasks, involving the right teams at the right time, and submitting requirements within 
deadlines. As the repair strategy developed into concrete initiatives with dedicated project man-
agers, it was regarded as a successful initial approach due to clear governance, ownership, and 
defined requirements. However, the benefits have been difficult to replicate with other CPD strat-
egies, such as recycling, where project managers and ownership are lacking. The GSM noted: “We 
lack project managers and ownership spread. Due diligence is manual, and we don’t have a pro-
cess for gathering all the outcomes”. Without standardized structures or shared ownership, 
knowledge and experiences remain siloed, hindering the transfer of learnings across CPD pro-
jects. 
 
4.1.2 Prerequisites for circular product design and needed support 
The interviews highlighted several prerequisites and needs for support that could enable better 
integration of Design for Recycling: 
Product formulation: The MTL stated that when a product requirement is explicitly formulated, 
navigating tradeoffs and translating requirements into concrete design decisions is much easier 
and already has somewhat established processes and bodies of knowledge. Because the case of 
repair-related requirements was defined and measurable, the project team could estimate costs 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
 
40 of 156 
 
and assess technical feasibility early in concept development. This gave the team a common un-
derstanding of the design implications and thereby made it possible to continue the PDP into 
prioritizing and evaluating the requirements, integrating them into product design and eventu-
ally prototype and test. This was what the interviewees expressed the biggest need of support: they 
lacked guidance on defining measurable requirements and engaging necessary stakeholders to 
operationalize design integration.  
Include commercial voices: Although commercial stakeholders are less involved in the technical 
translation of sustainability ambitions into product requirements, they play a central role in pri-
oritizing and embedding circular product design. The Commercial Leads act as the voice of the 
customer and are key for Jabra to allocate resources to circular initiatives when these are not 
driven directly by regulation: “First thing… get this anchored within the commercial leads… 
R&D is mainly executing” the MTL expressed. Their influence is particularly strong in the nego-
tiation phase, where requirements are balanced against commercial constraints. Without their 
buy-in, circular requirements risk being deprioritized in favor of short-term cost or time-to-mar-
ket objectives, regardless of long-term value to the firm or the environment. Including commer-
cial stakeholders could even help translate repair or recycling requirements into business-relevant 
indicators and adjust KPIs to reward longer-term value capture (e.g. aftermarket revenue, cus-
tomer retention, lifecycle cost reductions), the AOM noted.  
Cross functional communication: The interviewees stressed the need to locate circular respon-
sibility across functions early in the lifecycle. As the MTL expressed: “You should have broad a 
team including Operations [where product sustainability resides, red.], AMS, Sustainability Man-
agers, and R&D should of course be there”. It was also expressed that sustainability related re-
quirements cannot just be written into their requirement software but needed approval and sup-
port from other departments: “I could integrate the requirements myself, but I could get push 
back and would have to remove it. The best way is to have it anchored in Group Sustainability 
and check with Mech” the PSM said. The lack of clear communication structure and collection 
of relevant stakeholders called for a formalization of cross-functional representation in the re-
quirement formulation process including, commercial leads, R&D, Operations, AMS and Com-
mercial functions.

4.2 Mapping recyclability ambitions for requirement formulation input 
A key finding from the empirical study is that Jabra lacks a systematic approach for translating 
recyclability ambitions into concrete product requirements. To enable this, the underlying “am-
bitions” (the internal and external sources that define what recyclability should entail for Jabra’s 
products) must first be identified, made explicit and understood. This is done using an explora-
tory literature review and collecting insights from interviews with Jabra to map the sources that 
can inform recyclability requirement formulation at Jabra and serves as an exploratory bridge 
between the empirical findings and the subsequent SLR. For each source type, its role in shaping 
Design for Recycling is described, along with its origin (e.g. regulator, certification body, internal 
strategy). The sources mirror the empirical finding that stakeholders often struggle to locate and 
synthesize relevant inputs for recyclability requirements, indicating a need for better overview 
and accessibility. 
 
4.2.1 Legislation 
Regulatory requirements emerged as the most influential external driver for recyclability, as em-
phasized by the interviews (the PSM, personal communication, September 8, 2025; the GSM, 
personal communication, September 9, 2025). Compliance is non-negotiable and therefore sets 
the minimum boundary conditions that products must meet. The following legislative instru-
ments are particularly relevant. 
The European Green Deal (EGD) sets the overarching ambition for climate neutrality and a 
resource-efficient, CE in the EU (European Commission, n.d.-c). Under this, the CE Action Plan 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
 
 
42 of 156 
 
(CEAP) outlies actions for a “cleaner and more competitive Europe,” including specific initiatives 
on sustainable products and circular business models (European Commission, n.d.-a). Within the 
CEAP, the Circular Electronics Initiative (CEI) targets electronics (e.g. smartphones, IT equip-
ment, audio and video devices) with measures aimed at extending product lifetimes through re-
pair, reuse and high-quality recycling.  
Within this, three instruments are particularly relevant for Design for Recycling: 
“Right to Repair” (R2R) Directive (Directive on repair of goods): This directive aims to make 
repair a more attractive and accessible option throughout a product’s life. It introduces obliga-
tions for manufacturers and sellers to facilitate repair (e.g. offering repair services, providing in-
formation on repair conditions, and making spare parts and repair information available for 
longer). While its primary focus is on repair, it indirectly influences product design by incentiv-
izing products that are more repairable and easier to disassemble, overlapping with design choices 
that also support recyclability (European Commission, n.d.). 
Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR): ESPR replaces and expands the earlier 
Ecodesign Directive 2009/125/EC, which mainly addressed energy efficiency. In In contrast, the 
ESPR extends the scope to almost all physical products and covers a wide range of CE aspects. It 
empowers the European Commission to set binding performance and information requirements 
on durability, repairability, recyclability, recycled content and substances of concern. The regula-
tion has yet to decide on specific CPD guidelines and instead focuses on giving the Commission 
a clear direction for rules that might come. Annex I, however, describes some overall CPD prin-
ciples and some guidelines that can serve as relevant inspiration for requirements (European Un-
ion, 2024).  
EU Batteries Regulation: Regulation (EU) 2023/1542 on batteries and waste batteries comple-
ments the ESPR with detailed sustainability, performance and information requirements across 
the battery life cycle. For battery-containing products such as Jabra’s products, it prescribes design 
obligations on durability, replaceability, recycled content and the introduction of a battery pass-
port, which must align with the broader ESPR framework (EU Battery Regulation 2023/1542: Ob-
ligations and Updates, 2025). 
WEEE Directive (2012/19/EU): The Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Di-
rective assigns producers responsibility for financing and organizing the collection, treatment 
and recycling of e-waste placed on the EU market. This extended producer responsibility is im-
plemented through registration in national WEEE schemes, reporting and financing of collection 
and treatment activities. However, the directive does not prescribe detailed product-level design 
requirements for easier disassembly or recyclability (Electronical Waste (WEEE) and Batteries (BAT) 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
 
43 of 156 
 
- The Danish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.; Waste from Electrical and Electronic Equipment 
(WEEE) - Environment, n.d.).  
Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): More broadly, EPR schemes assign manufacturers 
end-of-life responsibility in various jurisdictions. In some regions, specific EPR legislation for 
electronics requires companies to establish or join take-back, collection and recycling programs 
(Extended Producer Responsibility, n.d.).  
Since WEEE and general EPR schemes do not explicitly require products to be designed for re-
cycling, they are not considered further in the development of the support tool, but they remain 
important boundary conditions for Jabra’s overall circularity strategy. 
 
4.2.2 Partners 
Value-chain partners can provide practical input for Design for Recycling, particularly e-waste 
recyclers and component or material suppliers. These actors can highlight how specific design 
choices affect recycling feasibility in practice. As mentioned in Section 3.3.3 Jabra collaborates 
with a third-party recycling company that enables selected customers to return used products for 
recycling. The third-party partner could specify design-related expectations for them to maximize 
material recovery e.g. which components/materials must be easily removed from product. Align-
ing well with their partners may also serve as a business advantage, eventually strengthening the 
business model for extending end-of-life services.  
 
4.2.3 Certifications 
Voluntary certifications are another important external driver for designing products for recycling 
and circularity, as achieving certification can require adhering to specific design features. At GN, 
achieving the TCO Certified certification is a strategic decision that has been selected as the base-
line standard for relevant product categories (GN, n.d.; PSM, personal communication, Septem-
ber 8, 2025). 
TCO Certified is an independent, third-party sustainability certification for IT and electronics 
products, covering environmental and social criteria across the product life cycle. From a Design 
for Recycling perspective, it is particularly relevant because it includes requirements on ease of 
disassembly and material recovery at end-of-life (Comprehensive Sustainability Criteria with Direct 
Impact, n.d.). 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
 
44 of 156 
 
4.2.4 Industry standards 
Industry standards and guidelines provide additional references for defining recyclability require-
ments. These documents often supply common terminology, metrics and methods for assessing 
recyclability, enabling companies to align with recognized best practice. 
DS/EN 45560:2024 specifies a methodology for integrating circularity into the design and devel-
opment process of products by an organization, which includes CPD guidelines along with busi-
ness model and organizational considerations (Dansk Standard, 2024).  
DS/EN 45555:2019 specifies a methodology to quantify and assess the recyclability and recovera-
bility of products. For Jabra, aligning with DS/EN 45555 can support requirement formulation 
by offering standardized definitions (e.g. what recyclability percentage means in practice or how 
to measure ease of disassembly) (Bundgaard et al., 2017; Dansk Standard, 2019). 
Circular Electronics Partnership (CEP) provides non-mandatory but influential recommenda-
tions for circular product design in electronics, including design strategies that facilitate reuse, 
refurbishment and high-quality recycling. While not binding, such guidelines can help interpret 
legislative and market expectations and translate them into design-oriented principles (Circular 
Electronics Design Guide, 2024). 
 
4.2.5 Internal strategy 
Internal strategies also shape recyclability ambitions. GN’s new corporate sustainability strategy 
Better for Planet includes explicit “circularity ambitions”, for example, a high-level goal that prod-
ucts should be designed for recycling by 2030. Today these ambitions are still generic and not 
supported by measurable targets. As the strategy matures and more specific quantitative goals are 
defined it could serve as a direct input for product-level requirement setting. 
 
4.2.6 Internal data 
Existing internal data and knowledge provide further input to recyclability requirements. One 
key source is LCA results for Jabra’s products, which offer quantitative insights into environmen-
tal impacts across the life cycle, including end-of-life scenarios. 
In addition, internal knowledge from engineering teams can inform about design constraints, 
feasible material substitutions and technical trade-offs. When systematically linked to require-
ment formulation, such internal expertise can help ensure that recyclability requirements are 
both environmentally ambitious and technically achievable.

4.2.7 Competitors 
Competitor benchmarking helps position Jabra’s recyclability ambitions within the market con-
text and is a common practice to compare with competitors on various aspects (the PSM, personal 
communication, September 8, 2025). Benchmarking against other audio and electronics manu-
facturers can: 
- 
Reveal emerging industry norms and design features related to recyclability 
- 
Indicate what levels of ambition are realistic and competitive 
Insights from public sustainability reports, product teardowns or third-party assessments can 
therefore serve as inspiration for Jabra’s own recycling requirements. 
 
4.2.8 Customers 
Stakeholders at Jabra highlighted customer input (through the commercial lead) as another im-
portant driver. Demand for sustainable products has increased, and corporate and public-sector 
customers increasingly include environmental criteria in purchasing decisions and reporting 
frameworks. Jabra has experienced requests for end-of-life solutions such as take-back schemes 
and documentation of recycling options. These requirements can influence how recyclability is 
prioritized and may, over time, be translated into product-level design criteria (the AOM, personal 
communication, August 25, 2025). 
 
4.2.9 The dynamic nature of recyclability sources 
The landscape of sources informing recyclability requirements is dynamic. Regulations evolve, 
new directives are introduced, and existing ones are revised. Jabra already monitors emerging 
legislation such as R2R to adjust product design ahead of enforcement. Similarly, voluntary stand-
ards and certifications are updated periodically (e.g. new generations of TCO Certified) (the PSM, 
personal communication, September 8, 2025). Technological developments can change what is 
technically and economically recyclable, while increasing customer expectations can turn niche 
demands into mainstream requirements. In other words, sources cannot be treated as static, which 
requires regular revisits to keep the requirements updated. Therefore, a support for translating 
recycling ambitions into product requirements must be dynamic.

Within primary data collection two streams were evident: customer needs and product inputs. 
This data was gathered to inform requirements formulation, translating expressed customer and 
technical needs and grounding them in operational evidence of in-use behavior and context. Cus-

2021; Pai et al., 2021; Singh & Sarkar, 2021; Z. Wang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2019; Ying et al.,

In the studies, secondary data provided benchmarks and compliance constraints that shaped and 
validated product requirements. Methods were used like literature review (Ada et al., 2023; Pai et 
al., 2021; Vinodh et al., 2017) and sustainability standards, guidelines or regulation (Pai et al., 
2021; Quernheim & Schleich, 2024; Su et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2011) was used.  
Structuring & Categorization: Structuring and categorization approaches are mentioned 23 
times across 18 of the reviewed articles. These approaches focus on organizing, mapping, and 
clustering themes or collected data to make requirement formulation more systematic and trace-
able. The work is primarily carried out by R&D functions and data analysts/marketers. Examples 
range from general mapping and clustering techniques (Francis, 2012; Li et al., 2021; Pai et al., 
2021; Shimomura et al., 2018; Singh & Sarkar, 2021) to more specific representations such as 
requirement graphs (Li et al., 2020; Z. Wang et al., 2019).  
Collectively, these approaches reduce complexity in large and heterogeneous datasets and help 
reveal patterns, relationships, and gaps. As a result, they form an important foundation for trans-
lating dispersed qualitative and quantitative inputs into structured and comparable data. 
Stakeholder Engagement & Co-creation: Stakeholder engagement is explicitly mentioned 19 
times in 14 of the articles. These approaches focus on actively involving relevant actors in eliciting, 
clarifying and validating requirements, often through collaborative activities. Stakeholders are 
not limited to customers or end-users but consist also of internal experts from different functions, 
who contribute complementary perspectives and support each other’s reasoning. The analysis of 
actors involved shows that R&D teams participate in most of these activities, while decision mak-
ers and data analysts/marketers are included less frequently. 
Typical examples of approaches include workshops used to co-create (Braatz et al., 2025; Watz & 
Hallstedt, 2022) and stakeholder engagement activities aimed at confirming, validating, or im-
proving earlier work (Arkhurst & Fu, 2024; Badi, 2017; Nilsson & Lindahl, 2016; Sousa-Zomer & 
Miguel, 2017).  Overall, these studies indicate that structured stakeholder engagement (whether 
with internal experts, external stakeholders, or both) supports clearer and more robust 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
 
55 of 156 
 
requirement formulations by aligning perspectives, revealing conflicting needs and trade-offs, 
and decisions made in a shared understanding rather than isolated assumptions. 
Translation & Iteration: Translation and iteration approaches are mentioned 14 times across 9 
of the reviewed articles. These approaches focus on converting diverse inputs (such as values, sus-
tainability considerations, user needs, or technical data) into explicit requirement statements and 
iteratively refining them to improve clarity, completeness, or feasibility. The work is primarily 
carried out by R&D and Data analysts/marketers, with some studies also involving Decision Mak-
ers and Sustainability Experts. 
Examples of approaches within this category include the User Needs Pyramid used to prioritize 
parameters based on the level of user needs (Pai et al., 2021), Better-worse Analysis used to com-
pare attributes based on perceived improvement or worsening (Ying et al., 2022) and HoQ/QFD 
matrices used to translate/link customer or sustainability needs to technical characteristics (Falk 
& Schmitt, 2014; Kang & Zhu, 2022; Salari & Bhuiyan, 2018; Sousa-Zomer & Miguel, 2017; Trap-
pey et al., 2011; Watz & Hallstedt, 2018). Collectively, these methods exemplify how structured 
translation mechanisms support prioritization and make implicit needs more traceable as con-
crete requirement statements. These methods are helpful for translating and iterating using by 
linking or prioritizing inputs, but they do not provide operational support for turning those in-
puts into clear, specific and verifiable requirement statements. In other words, they bridge what 
matters but not how it should be written and checked as a formal requirement. 
In several articles, translation is discussed at a conceptual level, for example translating values, 
sustainability input, or user needs into requirements without providing detailed procedural guid-
ance (Arkhurst & Fu, 2024; Pai et al., 2021; Watz & Hallstedt, 2018). This may indicate a gap in 
the literature concerning operational support for moving from analyzed data to well-formulated 
requirements. Overall, translation and iteration approaches highlight the importance of not only 
collecting and structuring data, but also systematically transforming it into actionable require-
ments through repeated refinement cycles. This makes them a critical link between empirical 
insights and implementable product specifications. 
Sustainability & Indicator-based: Seven studies in the SLR employ sustainability and indicator-
based tools or frameworks to translate sustainability objectives into explicit, measurable product 
requirements, with a total of nine mentions. These approaches are primarily applied by R&D and 
sustainability experts. 
Across the studies, two recurrent method patterns are identified. First, integration into established 
requirements tools, where QFD variants are extended with environmental indicators to add a 
“Voice of Environment” and map customer and environmental requirements in a shared matrix, 
often using tools such as the LiDS Wheel, Eco-indicator 99, and EU ErP (Salari & Bhuiyan, 2018; 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
 
56 of 156 
 
Trappey et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2011). Second, the use of indicators derived from assessments 
and rules, including LCA-based indicators and hotspot analysis (Quernheim & Schleich, 2024), 
as well as information modelling approaches that convert regulations into concrete design con-
straints (Sree et al., 2013). Collectively, these studies demonstrate that conventional requirement 
elicitation practices can be systematically extended with sustainability indicators.

4.3.4 SLR take aways 
Across the 36 papers and 113 approaches, the SLR shows that the requirement process in PDP is 
typically organized as a structured, multi-step process rather than through isolated tools. Most 
contributions propose frameworks where specific tools are embedded in selected steps, while 
standalone tools for requirement formulation are relatively rare. A recurrent pattern appears in 
the literature: 
1. Collecting primary and secondary data 
2. Structuring and categorizing this input 
3. Translating and iterating explicit requirement statements 
Stakeholder engagement and sustainability or indicator-based approaches occur across several 
steps instead of only once. Most studies are generic in terms of sector and are driven by customer 
needs, while work that focuses on environmental sustainability and circularity is less common. 
The three-step pattern provides a useful backbone for requirement formulation, but it remains at 
a high level and only partly developed for sustainability and circularity. 
The SLR also reveals important differences in how data for requirement formulation is obtained. 
Customer driven approaches mainly rely on primary data from interviews, surveys or user studies. 
Environmental and circularity driven approaches are based largely on secondary sources such as 
regulations, eco design guidelines, life cycle assessments and sustainability reporting frameworks. 
Several papers on sustainability and circularity discuss translation of values or environmental in-
put into requirements at a conceptual level but provide limited procedural guidance on how 
teams should move from analyzed data to concrete requirement statements in practice. Many of 
these approaches are methodologically advanced, for example using complex matrices or indica-
tor systems, and assume that suitable sustainability requirements are already formulated. In many 
cases the requirement process is also facilitated by researchers or specialized sustainability staff, 
and the studies give little guidance on how such roles should be placed and maintained within 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
 
60 of 156 
 
an organization. This confirms that the individual steps need to be further specified and adapted 
before they can support day-to-day requirement work in companies. 
 
4.4 Insights and comparison between empirical and literature studies 
This section combines the findings from empirical study and ambitions mapping with the in-
sights from the SLR. This clarifies the commonalities and differences between the practical reality 
at Jabra with approaches from research. 
4.4.1 Commonalities 
4.4.1.1 Data collection 
Most of the frameworks presented in literature start with a data collection step to gather data 
from primary data collection or secondary sources. Current repair requirements in Jabra are taken 
from either TCO Certified or R2R because they issue concrete and standardized requirements. 
However, when experiencing the need for more progressive requirements than what TCO Certi-
fied and R2R provide, Jabra experiences a knowledge gap in how to find and define these require-
ments. This was the case with Design for Recycling and highlights the importance of sourcing 
relevant data and information before formulating requirements. 
4.4.1.2 Need for structured processes 
Most literature presented step by step frameworks with specific tools integrated within. This high-
lights a need for a structured process often support by tools or frameworks to avoid the process 
being too ad hoc, possibly ending up in “fluffy” requirements, as Jabra expressed. All interviews 
called for better structure, especially regarding the translation of circularity ambitions/targets to 
requirements, as there currently is no formalized process for this.  
4.4.2 Differences 
4.4.2.1 Collaboration of stakeholders 
The literature and empirical study identified stakeholder engagement and co-creation as essential 
for requirement elicitation to assess multiple interests. This would ensure feasibility from engi-
neers and designers, desirability from commercial voices, viability by including the decision mak-
ers and project managers, as well as sustainability concerns from the sustainability managers. 
However, the literature presented a greater deal of systematic integration of stakeholders. The 
frameworks included collaboration between R&D, marketing, sustainability experts and decision 
makers, failing to address possible limitations of this collaboration due to being out of scope for 
the researchers.  
In contrast, Jabra’s processes are shown to be fragmented and person-dependent, with sustaina-
bility actors involved informally and inconsistently, in contrast to the arguments from SLR 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
 
61 of 156 
 
emphasizing that stakeholder engagement and cocreation should be an on-going process 
throughout the whole requirement formulation process.   
4.4.2.2 Maturity level 
Literature tends to present well-structured and ambitious frameworks and tools, e.g. QFD or NLP-
based frameworks to formulate ambitions into measurable requirements and many also include 
the integration of requirements into the product design like QFD. The literature, however, did 
not address how ready the companies were to integrate these without the researchers’ facilitation 
or help. In Jabra an ad hoc approach was identified with limited methodological support, ques-
tioning if they would even be ready to integrate such an extensive tool. The reliance on secondary 
sustainability data, conceptual translation guidance and expert facilitation indicates that many 
approaches assume a higher maturity level than the one currently present at Jabra, where the core 
challenge is to express clear and actionable circularity requirements in the first place. This gap 
may be explained by the fact that research may focus more on developing state-of-the-art methods 
and models and demonstrating them in smaller, predefined cases, whereas the real-life case of 
Jabra reports more need for organizational anchoring, as mentioned in the empirical study.  
4.4.2.3 Requirement formulation vs. prioritization 
In the SLR, only the results related to requirement formulation were presented, as mandated by 
the filtering criteria, but this excludes the following steps in the requirement elicitation e.g. 
weighing and prioritization. The filtering ensured a focus on approaches relevant to Jabra’s need 
for formulating requirements, but it also removed many approaches that integrate circularity later 
in the process (see Figure 17). Many articles present approaches with the strengths of refining, 
prioritizing and propagating requirements, not in formulating circularity requirements from stra-
tegic ambitions and regulation. Firstly, this highlights a gap between literature’s suggested ap-
proaches and the actual need of Jabra, which lies a step before where literature suggests opportu-
nities. Secondly, it also proposes alternative ways to include CPD in the PDP, namely by adding 
weighing parameters related to environmental sustainability or circularity upon existing require-
ments rather than formulating them with CPD in mind from the beginning. Despite the preva-
lence of post-hoc weighting approaches in the literature, upfront CPD-focused requirement for-
mulation was prioritized, as Jabra’s context calls for circularity to be treated as a core design driver 
rather than a parameter added later. This is also an argument as to why no approach has been 
directly applicable to the context of this case.

4.4.2.4 Structure 
The analysis points to a suggested best practice that involves a three-step structure: 1. Data collec-
tion, 2. Structuring and categorizing, 3. Translating and iterating, where stakeholder and sustain-
ability considerations are included throughout the whole process. It is however important to note 
that this structure may be insufficient for Jabra. The most important argument is that the articles 
had the researchers facilitate and introduce their approach or methods, where a real-life case may 
need further introduction to fully be anchored within the PDP and organization, even when this 
project ends. SLR thereby presents a sufficient structure for formulating requirements but has not 
considered a structure for introducing a new way of doing this. Therefore, there might appear to 
be a gap between the empirical barriers, Jabra’s needs and the suggested approaches from SLR.  
4.5 Proposal for new structure for requirement formulation 
As discussed in the former section, the structure of requirement formulation as suggested by find-
ings from the SLR, seem insufficient for Jabra’s needs, as it does not address the introduction to 
the task or highlights a difference in finding relevant sources and finding relevant information 
from said sources. Therefore, a new structure for the requirement formulation process is pro-
posed, so it supports the needs expressed by Jabra, see Figure 18: 
 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
 
63 of 156 
 
 
Figure 18: Proposed structure for requirement formulation. 
 
This new structure has several changes: 
A new “step 0” that highlights the need for introducing a new support or process, where the user 
needs to understand the upcoming task. This step lacked in the structure from SLR mainly be-
cause the researchers in the articles acted as facilitators to introduce their suggested methods.  
Splitting up “data collection” into finding sources (step 1) and finding information (step 2). This 
step adheres to especially the PSM’s concern about where to find relevant sources and how to 
capture important information from these. Therefore, two separate steps for these tasks are pro-
posed. 
Splitting up “translation and iteration” into two separate steps, where a singular task is to for-
mulate the requirements (step 4), which then will be collected and maintained though iteration 
(step 5). As identified, Jabra has no formal process for requirement formulation, and therefore 
this would need its own separate step, before it is being iterated.  
This new structure points towards the development of a support that builds on the generic three 
step structure found in the SLR but develops the steps further by helping teams translate high 
level circularity ambitions into well formulated requirements in early PDP, giving concrete pro-
cedural guidance rather than only conceptual advice and working without external facilitators. 
This new structure therefore combines relevant information from both empirical and theoretical 
insights, increasing the success of by gathering state-of-the-art approaches and adjusting them to 
the needs of Jabra.

4.6.1 Purpose and scope 
This theme ensures that the support is clearly targeted at Jabra’s main need of translating recycla-
bility ambitions into actionable requirements and that users understand what the support is for 
and what it delivers. It also addresses the context of use: When the support should be used, and 
who should own it.  
SO1: Facilitate translating high-level recyclability ambitions into concrete product requirements and 
clearly show the link between them. 
SO1 addresses the central gap identified throughout the RC and empirical study: Jabra has high-
level circularity ambitions, but the existing recyclability requirement is “fluffy” and non-measur-
able. SO1 therefore captures the core function of the support: it must facilitate the translation 
process from diverse ambition sources (such as strategy, legislation, certifications, partners, cus-
tomers, etc.) to measurable requirements that can be negotiated and eventually integrated in their 
product designs enhancing CPD.  
SO2: Facilitate requirement formulation in the early stage of PDP for every new product development. 
SO2 anchors the support in the early PDP, where design freedom is highest and CPD decisions 
have the largest impact, as mentioned in Section 3.1.4. The empirical study revealed that recycla-
bility considerations currently enter too late, if at all at Jabra, see Section 4.1. SO2 reflects that the 
result of the support (a set of measurable recyclability requirements) must be available for every 
new product development at the early stages, even if the full support process is only run once or 
twice a year. This ensures that despite a rapid PDP, recyclability requirements can still be 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
 
67 of 156 
 
considered in the early stages. This was highlighted during the validation with the PSM and GSM 
(see Appendix F: Support Objective Validation Workshop).  
SO3: Be owned and facilitated by the PSM. 
The empirical study identified the PSM as the person currently responsible for sustainability-
related requirements and for translating insights from LCAs and certifications into the PDP. How-
ever, involvement is today ad hoc and dependent on individual awareness. SO6 formalizes this by 
assigning clear ownership of the support to the PSM. This creates a stable anchoring point for 
the process, inviting relevant stakeholders and ensuring that recyclability is not forgotten in the 
rapid PDP. The SLR similarly revealed that many research approaches implicitly rely on a “re-
searcher-facilitator”, a role that in a company context must be explicitly assigned. In the validation 
workshop, this was confirmed as a reasonable and realistic ownership structure. 
 
4.6.2 Guidance and introduction (step 0) 
Blessing & Chakrabarti (2009) recommends that a support has a clearly stated aim, defined out-
puts and defined application context, which will help the user(s) understand their upcoming 
task. This is especially relevant as Jabra has no current formalized requirement formulation pro-
cess indicating novelty of such task. 
SO4: State the aim of the support, expected outputs, the facilitated activity and the next steps. 
SO4 ensures that the support itself is transparently introduced to its users. In Jabra’s case, this 
means the support must clearly communicate that: 
- 
Its aim is to formulate recyclability-focused product requirements 
- 
It facilitates a structured, cross-functional requirement formulation activity, including who 
should be involved 
- 
Its main outputs are measurable requirements ready for uploading to the requirement 
software 
- 
These outputs feed into the exploration and conceptualization phases of the PDP 
A concrete criterion within this theme is that the support should define what is meant by a “product 
requirement” (C4). Both the RC and empirical study showed that current recyclability “criteria” 
are often vague and multi-interpretable. Clarifying this concept up front supports consistent for-
mulation and later verification. 
SO5: Include clear “how to use” guidance. 
In line with DRM’s recommendation that a support should include explicit instructions for use, 
SO5 requires a clear “how to use” section. This should describe the steps of the process, required 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
 
 
68 of 156 
 
inputs, expected outputs, involved roles and decision points. The SLR showed that many frame-
works function well when facilitated by researchers, but it is uncertain whether they can be ap-
plied without such support. SO5 addresses this by making the procedure explicit enough that 
Jabra can apply the support independently of external facilitation. 
SO6: Be intuitive for all relevant stakeholders without extensive training. 
Given Jabra’s rapid PDP and competing priorities, the support cannot require extensive training 
or specialized expertise. The empirical study showed that stakeholders already have basic 
knowledge of requirements and an established requirement software but lack specific guidance 
for recyclability. SO6 therefore requires that support builds on familiar concepts and tools, uses 
clear language, and provides a straightforward process that stakeholders can follow without rely-
ing on external experts. This was supported in the validation WS as important for adoption across 
teams and locations. 
 
4.6.3 Find sources (step 1) 
Step 1 should support the user in identifying relevant ambition sources as input for recyclability 
requirements. It directly responds to the empirical finding that Jabra lacks a systematic way of 
identifying recyclability inputs, and to SLR findings that sustainability-driven approaches often 
rely heavily on secondary data. Another central point was the need for preserving an explicit link 
from each requirement to its originating ambition. In the validation workshop, this clear linkage 
was stressed as crucial for getting recyclability requirements accepted in negotiation processes, 
especially when they compete with cost or time-to-market considerations. 
SO7: Include a systematic process or guideline for how to find CPD-related sources (e.g. which actors or 
databases to consult) and keep them updated. 
The mapping of ambition sources (see Section 4.2) revealed a broad and dynamic landscape span-
ning legislation, standards, certifications, partners, internal data, competitors and customers. The 
empirical study showed that beyond TCO and Right to Repair, the PSM has no structured way 
of finding additional, more progressive requirements. The SLR highlighted that many sustaina-
bility-driven approaches depend on secondary data such as regulations, standards, LCAs and 
guidelines. SO7 therefore requires that the support includes a systematic guideline for where to 
look (which actors, databases and documents), how to document findings, and how to keep them 
updated. 
SO8: Be able to trace the source of each requirement. 
In the empirical study and validation workshop, traceability was repeatedly highlighted as critical. 
Without a clear rationale (“who asks for this and why?”), recyclability requirements are vulnerable 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
 
69 of 156 
 
in trade-off discussions and are likely to lose against more established cost or performance re-
quirements. SO8 therefore requires that the support maintains a transparent link from each re-
quirement back to its source. This supports internal negotiation and documentation and mirrors 
the SLR finding that sustainability and indicator-based approaches often derive requirements 
from explicit rules, standards and assessments. In the workshop, this objective was ranked as es-
pecially important for supporting prioritization and negotiation. 
 
4.6.4 Find information (step 2) 
Step 2 should support the user in extracting relevant information from the found sources. This 
step was in the SLR categorized within “data collection”, but during the validation especially the 
PSM expressed concern on how to select and capture information that are relevant to translate 
into recyclability requirements. Therefore, a separate step for supporting this is necessary.   
SO9: Facilitate systematic capture of requirements. 
SO9 ensures that the support incorporates a structure for how to capture relevant information 
from the identified sources. 
 
4.6.5 Structure information (step 3) 
Once relevant inputs have been identified, they need to be organized in a way that supports anal-
ysis, prioritization and use. The SLR showed that structuring and categorization are widely used 
to turn large, heterogeneous datasets into manageable clusters and requirement sets. The empiri-
cal study revealed that requirements at Jabra are currently formulated in an unstructured way, 
and that requirements lacking a clear source are easily dismissed. 
SO10: Facilitate systematic organization of requirements. 
SO10 therefore ensures that the support includes a method for systematic capture and organiza-
tion, making each requirement explicit and traceable. In the workshop, this objective was ranked 
highly and closely linked to SO8 (traceability). 
 
4.6.6 Translate to requirements (step 4) 
Step 4 is arguably the most essential step, as this is where preparatory work from steps 0-3 will be 
compiled into measurable requirements, as mandated by SO1 and the overall RQ of the project. 
Both the empirical study and the SLR showed that requirement formulation benefits from struc-
tured co-creation activities. Therefore, this step requires that the support includes mechanisms 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
 
70 of 156 
 
(e.g. workshops, shared templates, structured discussions) that actively help stakeholders work 
together, rather than relying on unstructured 1:1 meetings or email exchanges. 
SO11: Facilitate internal collaboration with different stakeholders related to the PDP. 
SO11 evolved during validation. Initially it was phrased as “Enable simultaneous input from 
multiple stakeholders”, but the WS discussions pointed out that the real need was facilitated col-
laboration, not just parallel input.  
SO12: Define the roles and responsibilities of stakeholders including who should own which require-
ment types. 
SO12 extends to the wider stakeholder group. The empirical study showed that lack of structured 
ownership leads to siloed knowledge and inconsistent involvement. By clarifying roles and who 
owns which requirement types SO12 supports consistent responsibility for requirement content 
and reduces the risk of requirements being ignored or removed later in the process. The validation 
WS underlined the importance of including representatives from R&D, decision makers, legal 
and commercial voices, however, it will further be tested whether all stakeholders should be rep-
resented in step 4 solely or should be spread out during the whole process. 
 
4.6.7 Collect, maintain and iterate (step 5) 
Section 4.2.9 showed that the ambition landscape is dynamic: Regulations, certification criteria, 
standards, internal targets and customer expectations evolve over time. The empirical study also 
documented how Jabra already tries to anticipate upcoming regulations (e.g. Right to Repair), 
and the SLR highlighted indicator-based approaches as evolving with new data and rules. It is 
therefore necessary to support Jabra in this evolvement, so the requirements will continue to be 
relevant.  
SO13: Be dynamic so it can be updated and maintained as new relevant information or requirements 
occur. 
SO13 therefore ensures that the support is not static. It must include mechanisms to update both 
the ambition sources and the resulting requirement set. In the validation workshop, this led to 
the criterion that the support should be updated once or twice a year, which aligns with the 
periodic use of the support and ensures that “plug-and-play” requirements remain relevant. 
 
4.6.8 Format 
The format theme ensures that the support is realistically applicable in Jabra’s context. The vali-
dation WS confirmed that a digital format is essential, both for practical reasons (saving, sharing,

pants in the validation WS expressed a preference for widely known platforms such as Word, 
Excel, PowerPoint and Miro, which fit with existing IT infrastructure and working practices. 
SO14: Be available in digital form. 
SO14 therefore requires that the support can be implemented in commonly used digital tools, 
enabling collaborative use, integration with the requirement software and future adaptations.

5.2 Elaboration 
The following version presents the conceptualization and elaboration process of the support. It 
focuses on how the SO are designed into the support and thereby addressing the identified needs 
from DS1 and RC. Both the process and the outcome is presented. Each step in the support pro-

5.2.1 Excel creation 
The design of the Excel sheet was used following the 8 Golden Rules of Interface Design by 
Shneiderman et al., (2018). The rules were practiced for the support in following way: 
1. Consistency: Using same naming conventions throughout the sheets, applying uniform 
layout considering composition and placement of instructions, colors and fonts.   
2. Universal usability: Providing instructions and clear explanations of expected input as 
well as AI guidelines and GQ for new users of the support.  
3. Informative feedback: Conditional formatting for sources when typing in.  
4. Closure: Step 5 served as a “master sheet” collecting all information from the requirement 
formulation process, to present a clear output of the task and give a full overview. It also 
served as a step for collecting all requirements in a form directly applicable to the next 
steps in Jabra’s PDP.  
5. Prevent errors: Importing previous inputs would ensure consistency throughout the 
sheets and give the user fewer steps.  
6. Easy reversal: The feature of deleting and changing inputs, as well as navigating back and 
forth between sheets already exists in Excel, so no further action was taken here. 
7. Keep users in control: Allowing users to change inputs if wanted 
8. Reduce memory load: Using a different sheet for each step, placing instructions at con-
sistent places and avoiding distracting colors or overengineered functions.  
The specific design choices made in Excel are elaborated on for each step.

After the relevant sources have been selected, the user is expected to extract relevant information

When testing the process internally it occurred that the information likely to be captured in step 
2 would be of such character that it would be complex to directly translate it to requirements. 
This is because much relevant information may come in large and/or complex quantity of text, 
making the process more chaotic. It was therefore decided that there should be a “middle-step” 
before formulating the requirements, that would make the user create a simplified statement. The 
simplified statement would be reformulated from the original information entry, and in a sepa-
rate column, the user was instructed to “Reformulate each piece of information into a simplified 
statement that captures the core message in clear and comparable terms” in the guideline for Step 
3. Additionally, the task of simplifying the information would help the user identify multiple 
cases of the same statement from different information sources. This would allow the user to 
group identical simplified statements from different sources when formulating requirements. An 
example was given in the sheet, to inspire the user, where the original information entry was:  
 
“A portable battery shall be considered readily removable by the end-user where it can be removed 
from a product with the use of commercially available tools, without requiring the use of special-
ized tools, unless provided free of charge with the product, proprietary tools, thermal energy, or 
solvents to disassemble the product” (EU Regulation 2023/1542, 2023, article 11.1) 
 
The information was then simplified as: “The battery should be easy for the user to remove and replace 
without damaging the product with commercially available tools”. The example was meant to help the 
user understand what the expected output of the simplified statement is, and that it is not yet 
expected to formulate a requirement.  
It should be noted that simplifying information inevitably entails a loss of context and detail. 
This requires the user (in this case, the PSM) to have a solid understanding of the overall message 
and the intended outcome of the requirement formulation to preserve all necessary information 
during the process. In the initial support version, only one example was provided to illustrate a 
complete simplified statement.  Therefore, it would be necessary to test the user’s ability to for-

5.2.6 Step 4: Requirement formulation  
In the conceptualization it was decided that a WS should be conducted using Miro to facilitate 
requirement formulation in collaboration with stakeholders from R&D to ensure feasible, realis-
tic and measurable recycling requirements, see Section 5.1. A WS requires a predefined goal, a 
structured process and likely a tool or interactive format for the participants to engage in, and a 
facilitator (Playmakers, n.d.), which all should result in a finalized set of requirements. Therefore, 
elaborating step 4 requires 1) Finding a suitable process for going from simplified statement to 
measurable requirement, 2) designing the interactive space for collaboration, and 3) defining the 
facilitator’s role. 
5.2.6.1 From simplified statement to concrete requirements 
The SLR provided a structure for overall requirement formulation which was later revised to fit 
Jabra’s context, but no successful tool for translating them was found in the literature. However, 
the elaboration phase yielded a logic for formulating requirements based on discussions with 
supervisors.  
This logic brings necessary information into the requirement formulation that makes the require-
ment measurable and connected to CPD. The process along with an example is illustrated in

8.1 Implications the support   
The project has shown that Jabra experiences challenges in formulating product requirements 
related to recycling. These are expressed by the rapidness of the PDP, which fails to include re-
quirements originating from the PSM in time; the lack of organizational wide knowledge and 
current practices of understanding the implications of design for recycling and its connection to 
other CPD principles, and unformalized structures for requirement formulation that hinders ef-
fective knowledge sharing and a standardized process. The challenge is further highlighted by the 
very nature of the “ambitions” Jabra tries to translate into requirements: A corporate strategy 
without clear KPIs and legislation that is complex and lack concrete targets that can be replicated 
into the products. The findings from the SLR showed that while much research considers the 
latter stages of requirement elicitation, e.g. prioritization and integration in the product design, 
the formulation phase is often neglected and lacks an applicable structure, that can be facilitated 
without researchers’ involvement. Empirical and theoretical findings both suggest a need for a 
process for collecting relevant recycling information, structuring them and formulating require-
ments in collaboration based on these inputs.  
The support aids Jabra in this process, as its features facilitate an iterative requirement formula-
tion process, where multiple types of input can be structured and documented. This ensures not 
only that the requirements can be formulated, but also that they will have a stronger chance of 
being prioritized since they are measurable and feasible, ready to include in the early stages of 
development, and have stronger negotiation power due to the tracing of sources, expressing the 
requirements’ urgency and relevance for strategic and compliance considerations. The support 
was tested, and while it yielded positive results, further implementation and the impact of it de-
pends on certain factors:  
1) That the PSM continues to use, maintain and advocate for the support and the process it facil-
itates. If the sources are not updated e.g. when new legislation is announced, the requirements 
will remain static and will not follow the changing landscape of demands for CE. This was espe-
cially addressed in PS2, where the support engaged multiple iterations and increased guidance, 
but the support can only do that much, and therefore continuous use and improvement of the 
requirements and the information is crucial to stay ahead of the upcoming demands and inte-
grate CPD early. 
2) That there is support from colleagues and management to continue focusing on CPD, because 
without their input the requirements may become less relevant and will not be considered in the 
prioritization of requirements and integration in the product design. Fortunately, participants in 
the WS-test were positive about the purpose and process of the support, and acknowledged its 
role in the PDP, but it may also be because the PSM had invited them, filtering out more negative 
stakeholders. Therefore, it remains a task for the PSM to lobby for this purpose and create an 
==PAGE:140==
 
 
 
 
139 of 156 
 
alliance across R&D and management to get the agenda included. This was emphasized during 
the empirical barriers, that showed that without an organizational wide CE maturity increase, a 
new tool alone may not solely fix all barriers in Jabra. For this project, it was especially useful to 
have engaged participants from management, such as head of the mechanical team (ML) and a 
representative from Group Sustainability (HS). This way the support not only ensures feasibility 
of the requirements and integration in the PDP but has the potential to be used in the whole 
organization. The increased collaboration may prevent the same situation of siloed knowledge as 
the case of repair initiatives and instead ensure continuous learning throughout the whole PDP 
and organization.  
3) When the requirements are formulated, they are also prioritized and integrated into the design. 
The scope of the tool remains until the formulation is finalized and ready to be pushed to Jabra’s 
requirement software. From here, negotiations about priorities are conducted where considera-
tions for prize will probably appear as trade-offs to recycling concerns. While the support itself 
does not consider this stage, it does bring some negating power by including the sources for 
requirements, e.g. legislation, competitors or strategy. Hopefully, this will increase the chance of 
integrating the requirements in the final design, but it cannot be guaranteed. If Jabra wants to 
include CPD in their PDP and their final product designs, they need to prioritize it.  
4) That there is continuous feedback from the negotiation and integration phase to the formula-
tion phase, so the next iteration of requirements has embedded considerations from the first 
round. These may be factors for success and failure to ensure that the next round of requirements 
have a better chance of being included and have a large impact on the final design.  
This project has mainly focused on design for recycling as the main CPD principle, as this was 
the most relevant for Jabra. The support therefore includes examples of sources that are relevant 
for this. However, it can be argued that if other sources are chosen, other CPD principles can be 
represented in the process. This is both because of the dynamic nature of the “sources” table in 
step 1, but also because the WS has a CA related to each simplified statement, that thereby com-
municates the overall purpose to other participants, aiming the discussions and requirement for-
mulation in the proper direction. Lastly, the requirements in the master list are assigned to a CA, 
where other CPD principles can be reflected. 
The thesis has centered around a close collaboration with Jabra, and analyzed their barriers to 
requirement formulation, proposed a support that can be integrated into their PDP and considers 
their organizational structure and practices. It is therefore relevant to discuss how well the support 
can be transferred to another industry setting. The source examples from the support’s step 1 are 
tailored to consumer electronics, as the specific industry standards and legislation are mostly rel-
evant for this industry. However, the categories of sources and the guidance given are applicable 
and relevant to other industry settings, as it is generally recommended to seek information from 
==PAGE:141==
 
 
 
 
140 of 156 
 
external partners, benchmark with competitors, utilize internal knowledge and understand cus-
tomer demands. Moreover, some industry standards e.g. EN 45560 are not product specific and 
the CPD principles can be applied across many product types. The support’s flexible and dynamic 
nature allows for changing and updating sources, so it is argued that despite its immediate rele-
vance to Jabra and consumer electronic companies, it can be applied in other use-cases. 
Another consideration for implementation of the support, regards not the support itself, but its 
context of development and integration. The support was developed in close collaboration with 
the PSM and with support from representatives from strategy and management. Therefore, the 
successful acceptance of the support in this project may partly be due to the gradual introduction 
and tailoring to the needs and structure of Jabra. While this process may hardly be replicated, the 
above-mentioned implications still stand relevant when implementing such support in another 
company: Engagement, clear ownership and continuous use by the PSM, involvement with other 
stakeholders from different departments and management hierarchies, and clear output (require-
ments). The process of eliciting requirements may be different in another company, but as the 
support facilitates the production of a measurable requirement, it can be assessed, prioritized and 
integrated in however way another company does it.  
 
8.2 Reflection on methods and limitations 
The DRM provided the overall structure for this thesis by defining clear phases, goals, and mile-
stones. Using the stages RC, DS1, PS1, DS2 and PS2 helped us keep a line of sight from problem 
definition to an improved version of the proposed support tool based on evaluation. This sup-
ported structured progression from an initially broad interest in CPD to a more specific focus on 
recyclability requirements at Jabra. 
Initially, the RC focus was broad, but through ongoing empirical exploration the problem grad-
ually narrowed towards recyclability and, more specifically, recyclability requirements. This focus 
only crystallized after several interviews and scoping discussions, meaning that RC and DS1 were 
effectively intertwined rather than sequential. The same interviews often contributed both to un-
derstanding why CPD is difficult to integrate (RC) and to identifying barriers to formulating 
recyclability requirements (DS1). This non-linearity aligns with the agile character of DRM but 
made it challenging to maintain clear analytical boundaries between phases. It also means that 
parts of the problem definition were shaped relatively late in the process. 
A deliberate methodological choice throughout the thesis was to give strong weight to empirical 
data from Jabra, enabled by good access to stakeholders and multiple interview and WS opportu-
nities, which may influence the final impact of the support. The stakeholders were selected based 
on recommendations from the PSM, which ensured access to knowledgeable and motivated 
==PAGE:142==
 
 
 
 
141 of 156 
 
informants, but introduces a bias as perspectives from more skeptical or less sustainability-ori-
ented stakeholders are underrepresented. This may have influenced how barriers and needs were 
framed. Also, perspectives from stakeholders from AMS, who own the repair requirements, could 
have served the thesis well to understand their successes. However, due to barriers in contacting 
these stakeholders, the results from this aspect were limited. This presents opportunities for future 
work. Audio recording, note-taking, and subsequent transcription (partly supported by AI-based 
tools) enabled detailed analysis, but also introduced potential errors. Therefore, transcripts and 
summaries were reviewed against recordings where possible. 
The SLR mapped existing knowledge on formulation of requirements from fluffy knowledge. 
The screening of the 391 articles was split between the two authors using shared criteria, which 
was necessary for feasibility but introduced some subjectivity in inclusion and categorization. 
Because the SLR findings aligned well with the empirically identified barriers, the stepwise struc-
ture of the tool was implicitly decided before PS1, which created a strong line of argument from 
problem analysis to solution design. However, this early convergence also restricted the range of 
concept generation in PS1: more radical or alternative support formats were not explored system-
atically. In that sense, DS1 functioned not only as a descriptive phase but also as the beginning of 
a prescriptive step, shaped by the scope and boundaries of the SLR. 
In DS2, the support was evaluated through an application test, a UX test and a workshop. To-
gether, these gave informative insights into how the support is understood, used and experienced, 
but with some methodological limitations. For example, the Excel tool was only tested on the 
intended user at Jabra, the PSM, which limits insight into how other roles, and less experienced 
users, would work with the tool independently. The fact that each test was only conducted once 
also limits the robustness of claims about effectiveness and cross-functional adoption. From a 
methodological perspective, future work would benefit from additional DS2 iterations across dif-
ferent projects, roles and facilitators. 
Overall, the methodological choices within DRM and particularly the strong empirical orienta-
tion, early convergence on a specific solution type, and limited but deep evaluation, produced a 
contextually grounded and coherent support tool, while at the same time constraining the width 
of conceptual exploration and the general use of the findings.

8.3 Future research 
The aim of this thesis was to research how Jabra could formulate requirements for recycling based 
on abstract ambitions. These ambitions were defined, a process for requirement formulation was 
suggested and a support for facilitating collaborative requirement formulation was proposed. 
However, further research can be done to further ensure the support’s implementation both for 
Jabra and other companies. 
Firstly, it is suggested that the changes made during PS2 are tested and evaluated. It is especially 
critical to test the outcome of the WS and how the facilitation guide, and increased support for 
refinement of requirements, has increased their ability to formulate measurable recycling require-
ments. This can also be tested for other CPD requirements, and it is highly recommended to test 
this in other companies or even sectors, to adjust the supports scalability.  
Secondly, the project scope mainly considered formulating requirements, with considerations on 
securing a good foundation for prioritization through adding sources and importance, as well as 
including several stakeholders in the process. However, it is acknowledged that the PDP involves 
steps of prioritizing, negotiating and integrating requirements into the product design, which has 
not been considered in this project. Therefore, it is suggested to evaluate the impact of the support 
in these phases and understand the long-term implications of this. This can benefit from the many 
existing approaches, discovered in the SLR, which mainly concerned the negotiation and integra-
tion of requirements in the product design. Further research can therefore analyze how well the 
support interacts with other existing approaches, e.g. QFD or ranking requirements based on 
sustainability or circularity criteria.  
Lastly, it is acknowledged that ensuring CPD in the PDP alone may not fully integrate CE in a 
company context. As mentioned in the RC, the combination of CE business model innovation 
and CPD is crucial to integrate CE in a viable manner, that makes it attractive for companies to 
transition. This project is built upon the existing recycling initiative that Jabra has made with a 
third-party recycling partner.  For Jabra, it is recommended that once they have integrated recy-
cling product requirements and KPIs in their strategy, they explore CE business opportunities 
that may arise and leverage their existing design for recycling capabilities.

9  Conclusion 
The thesis takes its starting point in the global challenge of resource depletion and the need to 
decouple value creation from the linear “take-make-waste” model. Circular Economy offers a con-
crete pathway by keeping materials in use for longer, using fewer resources, and recovering value 
at end-of-life. Within this, recyclability is a key strategy, as more easily recyclable products can 
reduce the demand for virgin materials. 
Focusing on the consumer electronics company Jabra, the thesis investigated how recyclability 
ambitions can be translated into concrete, measurable product requirements. By analyzing barri-
ers, mapping ambition sources, and examining current practices, the research showed that recy-
clability is recognized at a strategic level but is not effectively integrated into products. Key barri-
ers include vague requirements, ad hoc processes, unclear ownership, and limited recyclability 
knowledge. A literature review explored relevant concrete sources behind recyclability ambitions. 
A systematic literature review identified a stepwise process for requirement elicitation but high-
lighted a lack of guidance in the beginning of adapting a new process.  
These insights led to the development of a structured support that helps product sustainability 
managers, and others responsible for circular product design, to formulate measurable product 
requirements. It does so through a preparatory process that gathers and structures relevant infor-
mation, enabling collaborative requirement formulation early in product development, when 
most environmental impacts are locked in. Additionally, by facilitating collaboration it increases 
companywide knowledge about circular economy, creates knowledge sharing across disciplines 
and thereby increases organization wide circular economy maturity by integrating circular prod-
uct design in the PDP. 
The support was developed and evaluated with Jabra and mainly targets design for recycling in 
consumer electronics but can be adapted to other circular design principles and industries. This 
way, the support provides a step closer to achieving circular product design for companies, and 
serves as a foundational step for minimizing e-waste, extracting value from recycled material and 
reducing environmental impact.
