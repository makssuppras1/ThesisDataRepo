Abstract
In the evolving landscape of urban development, cities are increasingly striving to be-
come ’smart cities’ characterized by advanced infrastructure and efficient public services.
However, alongside this digital transformation, it is crucial to ensure the protection and
judicious utilization of green spaces within urban environments. This thesis explores the
application of artificial intelligence (AI) and computer vision techniques to satellite imagery
for identifying and monitoring untouched biotopes in urban landscapes, with a focus on
the municipality of Høje-Taastrup in Denmark.
The study leverages high-resolution satellite imagery from Sentinel-2 and Landsat-8 mis-
sions to compute the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) time-series data,
which serves as a key indicator of vegetation health. A comprehensive data preprocess-
ing pipeline was developed, including cropping, reprojection, and alignment to ensure
data consistency. Two machine learning models, Random Forest and Gradient Boosting,
were trained using various feature extraction methods and labeling strategies to identify
unchanged areas. Five different training scenarios were explored with the derived fea-
tures and labels.
The performance of the models was evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1
score. The models trained on Landsat-8 dataset generally performed better than the mod-
els trained on Sentinel-2 dataset. It was observed that the Gradient Boosting model out-
performed the Random Forest model in cases where a 5x5 pixel window features were
used. The study demonstrated the effectiveness of using NDVI and machine learning
techniques to identify and validate untouched areas, providing valuable insights for envi-
ronmental monitoring and urban planning.
The findings have significant implications for environmental conservation, supporting sus-
tainable urban development by balancing the need for urban growth with the preserva-
tion of green spaces. The methodologies developed in this thesis offer a scalable and
cost-effective approach for continuous environmental monitoring, aiding municipalities in
making informed decisions about land use and conservation strategies.

Introduction
In the evolving landscape of urban development, cities are continuously progressing to-
wards becoming ’smart cities’, characterized by advanced infrastructure, digital connec-
tivity, and efficient public services [1].
However, alongside this digital transformation,
it is increasingly crucial to ensure the protection and judicial utilization of green spaces
within these urban environments. These green spaces not only contribute to the aesthetic
appeal of cities but also play a vital role in environmental sustainability and biodiversity
conservation.
In recent years, the rapid development of computer vision and artificial intelligence (AI)
technologies have revolutionized various sectors, spanning from healthcare to manufac-
turing. One particularly promising application lies in the domain of environmental con-
servation and the assessment of natural ecosystems. These technologies can aid in the
evaluation and monitoring of the environmental conditions, providing valuable insights to
inform decision-making processes aimed at preserving ecological integrity.
However, municipalities often face challenges in leveraging these technologies into their
operations and planning. Limited technical expertise and resource constraints can hinder
the effective utilization of these tools, despite their promise in enhancing environmental
management practices. There is a need for practical solutions to bridge this gap between
technological innovation and practical implementation.
In this context, the municipality of Høje-Taastrup [2] in Denmark embarks on an ambitious
initiative to protect and strengthen existing natural areas while expanding green spaces
within its jurisdiction. This initiative aligns with the municipality’s commitment to prioritize
nature conservation efforts as part of the Grønt Danmarkskort [3] strategy. The municipal-
ity’s vision includes the development of a robust tool capable of providing comprehensive
insights into areas outside existing nature reserves, guiding targeted conservation efforts
and nature initiatives.
1.1
Goals
This thesis aims to utilize Artificial Intelligence and Computer Vision to make informed
decisions regarding land cover changes and nature types, balancing the need for urban
development with the preservation of green spaces, in alignment with the municipality’s
objectives. Specifically, the thesis focuses on achieving the following objectives:
• Investigate the feasibility of using aerial/satellite imagery to gather detailed informa-
tion about habitat types.
• Assess methods to quantify the proportion of natural areas in an urban space, pro-
viding a comprehensive understanding of the current distribution of green spaces.
• Define the criteria for identifying and delineating the untouched nature within the mu-
nicipality, explaining how this determination could benefit the urban planning strate-
gies.
• Develop algorithms capable of accurately identifying areas of untouched nature
within the urban landscape, leveraging computer vision techniques and machine
learning methodologies to support informed decision-making by nature workers and
municipal authorities.

2.1
Untouched Biotopes
Untouched biotopes refer to areas within urban landscapes that have remained undis-
turbed and devoid of human intervention for extended periods. These areas typically
exhibit the following traits:
1. Long-term Preservation:
Untouched biotopes are characterized by their immaculate condition, having avoided
human activities such as development, construction, or agriculture over many years.
This preservation contributes to the maintenance of natural habitats and ecosystems
within urban environments.
2. Minimal Vegetation:
Untouched biotopes often lack significant vegetation cover, unlike cultivated or man-
aged green spaces. This may be due to a variety of environmental conditions such
as poor soil quality, limited access to sunlight, or natural ecological processes that
prevent the growth of dense vegetation. Additionally, some biotopes might be in-
herently dry, covered by sand or stones, or characterized as swampy areas. How-
ever, it is important to note that swamplands, which are also considered untouched
biotopes, often have significant vegetation cover. These conditions can explain why
such lands have not been developed into farmland or urban areas, particularly in re-
gions like Denmark where land use is carefully managed.
3. Environmental Conservation Opportunities:
Untouched biotopes present opportunities for environmental conservation and habi-
tat restoration efforts. Municipal authorities can use these areas to enhance biodi-
versity and ecosystem resilience within urban landscapes.
4. Habitat Preservation:
Untouched biotopes play a critical role as habitats for diverse flora and fauna, of-
fering shelter and nourishment for urban wildlife. These areas are essential for
supporting biodiversity and maintaining ecological balance, particularly in populated
urban settings. For example, untouched biotopes found around lakes or other wa-
ter reservoirs serve as important habitats for diverse species and contribute to the
overall ecological health of the region. Preserving these biotopes ensure the con-
servation of valuable ecosystems and the protection of vulnerable species within
urban landscapes.
5. Increasing Initiatives for Biodiversity Support:
Recently, there has been a noticeable increase in initiatives aimed at supporting
biodiversity through the protection and enhancement of untouched biotopes. Mu-
nicipalities and community organizations are increasingly recognizing the value of

2.2
Denmark’s Protected Nature Type/Para3 Areas
This project will utilize the para 3 areas/protected natural areas for ground truth. Accord-
ing to the Danish Agency for Data Supply and Efficiency, Para 3 areas refer to specific
geographical regions that are protected under the Danish Nature Protection Act, Para-
graph 3. These areas are designated to preserve the natural habitats and biodiversity
within Denmark, ensuring the conservation of various types of nature, including mead-
ows, bogs, heaths, and grasslands.
The purpose of protected nature type is to:
• Protected Biodiversity: By preserving these natural habitats, para3 areas play a
crucial role in maintaining the biodiversity of flora and fauna.
• Conserve Natural Landscapes: These areas help in maintaining the natural land-
scape, which is essential for ecological balance.
• Support Sustainable Land Use: The designation of para3 areas ensures that land
use within these regions is managed sustainably, balancing ecological preservation
with human activities.
The ”Protected Nature Type” designation is based on specific criteria that assess the
ecological value of the area. The data regarding these areas is maintained and updated
by the SDFE, and is publicly accessible through their geoportal [4].
By incorporating these Para 3 areas into the assessment framework, the thesis aims
to leverage this valuable data to enhance the accuracy and comprehensiveness of the
nature assessment within the Høje-Taastrup municipality. These areas provide a critical
reference point for identifying and preserving untouched natural habitats in urban and
rural settings.
2.3
Geographical Information System (GIS)
Geographic Information System is a technology used for capturing, storing, analyzing,
and visualizing spatial and geographic data. GIS integrates various types of data, like
geographic information - such as maps, satellite imagery, and spatial databases - with
powerful analytical tools to provide detailed insights into spatial relationships and pat-
terns, which are crucial for effective decision-making in environmental monitoring, urban
planning, disaster management, and more.
2.3.1
GIS Technologies and Real-world Applications
Spatial Analysis
Spatial Analysis employs GIS to examine the locations, attributes, and relationships of
features in spatial data through methods like overlay analysis, buffer analysis, and spatial
interpolation. The spatial analysis is useful for identifying patterns and trends, including
hotspots of biodiversity, regions of environmental degradation, or optimal locations for
new infrastructure. For instance, a city might utilize spatial analysis to pinpoint suitable
locations for new parks by considering factors such as population density, current green
spaces, and accessibility.

GIS plays a critical role in disaster management by offering tools to map hazards, assess

Urban Planning
Urban planners leverage GIS to scrutinize spatial data concerning land use, transporta-
tion networks, utilities, and demographics, enabling well-informed decisions about urban
development. GIS facilitates zoning, land-use planning, infrastructure development, and
the monitoring of urban growth. For instance, GIS can analyze traffic patterns and suggest
new road networks to mitigate congestion in rapidly expanding urban areas.
GIS technologies provide a robust framework for spatial analysis and decision-making
across various fields. By integrating with remote sensing, IoT, and AI, GIS enhances the
capability to monitor, analyze, and respond to environmental and urban challenges. The
GIS has its applications from conserving biodiversity to managing urban growth, and re-
sponding to disasters. As technology advances, the integration of GIS with other emerging
technologies will continue to expand its capabilities and applications.
2.4
Remote Sensing with Satellites
Remote sensing is a powerful technology used to observe and gather information about
the Earth’s surface from a distance, typically from sensors mounted on Earth-observing
satellites like Sentinel, Landsat, MODIS, or aircrafts and drones.
Remote sensing relies on electromagnetic radiation interacting with Earth’s surface and
atmosphere. It captures radiation across wavelengths such as visible light, infrared, and
microwave. Materials interact uniquely with this radiation, causing reflection, absorption,
or emission. Each material has a distinct spectral signature, showing its reflectance or
emission patterns across wavelengths. The main elements of remote sensing are de-
scribed in the figure below 2.3:
Figure 2.3: Elements of Remote Sensing [7]
==PAGE:17==
Remote sensing data is primarily categorized into two main types:
• Multispectral Data: These sensors capture imagery across multiple distinct spec-
tral bands, enabling analysis of features such as vegetation health, land cover, and
water quality.
• Hyperspectral Data: These sensors capture imagery across various narrow spec-
tral bands, offering detailed insights into the spectral signatures of various materials.
In this project, remote sensing with satellites is used. The utilization of satellite imagery
for remote sensing applications has made remarkable progress with the advent of mis-
sions like Sentinel, Landsat, and MODIS. These are some prominent Earth observation
programs offering multispectral imagery with global coverage.
2.4.1
Sentinel Satellite Program
The Sentinel satellite program, developed by the European Space Agency [8] under the
Copernicus initiative, is designed to provide a comprehensive suite of Earth observation
data critical for environmental monitoring, climate change studies, and disaster manage-
ment. The program consists of multiple satellites, each equipped with specific sensors
tailored for different observational needs. Some notable satellite missions are discussed
below.
Sentinel-1
Sentinel-1 [9], launched in 2014 (Sentinel-1A) and 2016 (Sentinel-1B), is equipped with
Synthetic Aperture Reader (SAR) technology. This satellite is designed to provide all-
weather, day-and-night imaging capabilities, making it ideal for continuous environmental
monitoring.
• Synthetic Aperture Reader: SAR enables high-resolution imaging regardless of
weather conditions or daylight. This is especially useful for monitoring soil moisture,
ice coverage, and land deformation. SAR can penetrate clouds and darkness, pro-
viding reliable data under adverse conditions.
Sensor Specifications: Sentinel-1 uses a C-band radar operating at a frequency
of 5.405 GHz. It provides a spatial resolution of 5m x 20m, allowing for detailed
imaging of the Earth’s surface.
Applications: SAR data is utilized for applications such as monitoring deforesta-
tion, mapping natural disasters like floods and earthquakes.
• Temporal resolution: Sentinel-1 revisits the same location every 6-12 days, de-
pending on the latitude and specific mission parameters.
Sentinel-2
Sentinel-2 [10], with Sentinel-2A launched in 2015 and Sentinel-2B in 2017, focuses on
high-resolution optical imaging for land monitoring. It is particularly useful for observing
vegetation, soil, and water cover.
• MultiSpectral Instrument (MSI): The MSI captures data in 13 spectral bands, rang-
ing from visible to short-wave infrared. This wide range of spectral bands allows for
detailed analysis of land cover types and conditions, from vegetation health to soil
properties.
Sensor Specifications: Sentinel-2’s MSI provides a spatial resolution of 10 meters
for its four visible and near-infrared bands, 20 meters for six red-edge and short-
wave infrared bands, and 60 meters for three atmospheric correction bands.
Applications: Sentinel-2 data supports applications in agriculture (crop monitor-
ing, soil health), forestry (deforestation tracking, forest health), water management
(water quality, reservoir monitoring).
==PAGE:18==
• Temporal resolution: Sentinel-2 provides a revisit time of every 5 days at the equa-
tor, ensuring frequent and detailed monitoring of land surface changes.
Sentinel-3
Sentinel-3 [11], launched with Sentinel-3A in 2016 and Sentinel-3B in 2018, is designed
for comprehensive ocean and land monitoring. It measures sea-surface topography, sea
and land surface temperature, and ocean and land color.
• Ocean and Land Colour Instrument (OLCI): The OLCI captures data in 21 spectral
bands, providing detailed information on ocean and land color. This is crucial for
studying biological activity in oceans and monitoring water bodies.
Sensor Specifications: OLCI offers a spatial resolution of 300 meters, which is
sufficient for global and regional environmental monitoring.
Applications: OLCI data is used for applications such as monitoring chlorophyll
concentrations in oceans, detecting algal blooms, and assessing water quality in
lakes and rivers.
• Sea and Land Surface Temperature Radiometer (SLSTR): The SLSTR mea-
sures thermal radiation to determine sea and land surface temperatures accurately.
This is essential for climate monitoring and studying heat fluxes between the ocean
and atmosphere.
Sensor Specifications: SLSTR provides a spatial resolution of 500 meters for ther-
mal infrared channels and 1 kilometer for the other channels.
Applications: SLSTR data supports applications like sea surface temperature mon-
itoring, land surface temperature analysis, and heatwave tracking.
• Temporal Resolution: Sentinel-3 provides daily coverage for some instruments,
ensuring high-frequency monitoring of oceanic and terrestrial parameters.

2.4.2
Landsat Satellite Program
Landsat [12], jointly operated by the United States Geological Survey (USGS) and NASA,
is one of the longest running Earth observation initiatives, dating back to 1972. It has
played a critical role in advancing the understanding of the planet by providing continu-
ous, high-quality satellite imagery for environmental monitoring, land use planning, and
resource management. Some notable Landsat missions are discussed below.
Landsat 8
Landsat 8 [13], launched in 2013, features two primary instruments: the Operational Land
Imager (OLI) and the Thermal Infrared Sensor (TIRS). These instruments have signifi-
cantly enhanced the data quality and spectral coverage compared to previous Landsat
missions.
• Operational Land Imager (OLI): The OLI captures images in the visible, near-
infrared, and short-wave infrared portions of the electromagnetic spectrum. It in-
cludes 9 spectral bands, which provide detailed information on land cover, vegeta-
tion health, and water bodies.
Sensor Specifications: The OLI offers a spatial resolution of 30 meters for most
bands, allowing it to capture fine details of the Earth’s surface. The panchromatic
band has a higher resolution of 15 meters, which is useful for sharpening the other
bands.
Applications: OLI data is used for various applications, including agricultural mon-
itoring, forest management, urban planning, and disaster response.
• Thermal Infrared Sensor (TIRS): The TIRS measures thermal radiation emitted by
Earth’s surface, providing insights into surface temperature. This information is cru-
cial for studying water resources, heat islands in urban areas, or volcanic activity.
Sensor Specifications: TIRS has two thermal bands with a spatial resolution of
100 meters. This allows for detailed thermal mapping, which is essential for hydro-
logical and climatological studies.
Applications: TIRS data supports applications such as evapotranspiration moni-
toring, thermal pollution assistant, and geological surveys.
• Temporal Resolution: Landsat 8 revisits the same location on Earth every 16 days.
Landsat 9
Landsat 9 [14], launched in 2021, continues the legacy of Landsat 8 by providing similar
capabilities with several improvements that enhance data quality and reliability.
• Enhanced Operational Land Imager-2 (OLI-2): The OLI-2 onboard Landsat 9
builds on the success of its predecessor with improved radiometric performance,
which enhances the accuracy of surface reflectant measurements. This improve-
ment is particularly beneficial for detecting subtle changes in land cover and vege-
tation health.
Sensor Specifications: Like OLI, OLI-2 captures data in 11 spectral bands with a
spatial resolution of 30 meters for most bands and 15 meters for the panchromatic
band.
• Enhanced Thermal Infrared Sensor-2 (TIRS-2): TIRS-2 provides improved cali-
bration and stability over its predecessor. This is critical for applications involving
surface temperature analysis and monitoring thermal anomalies.
Sensor Specifications: TIRS-2 maintains the 100-meter spatial resolution of ther-
mal bands, allowing for detailed thermal mapping and analysis.
• Temporal Resolution: Landsat 9 also revisits the same location every 16 days.

2.4.3
MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer)
The Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) instruments onboard NASA’s
Terra and Aqua satellites provide comprehensive global monitoring of the Earth’s atmo-
sphere, oceans, and land surface. Launched in 1999 (Terra) and 2002 (Aqua), MODIS
has been instrumental in providing consistent, long-term environmental data critical for
climate change studies, disaster management, and natural resource monitoring.
MODIS-Terra and MODIS-Aqua
Both Terra and Aqua satellites are equipped with MODIS sensors, which capture data
across a wide range of wavelengths, from visible to thermal infrared. This broad spectral
coverage allows for detailed observation and analysis of various environmental parame-
ters.
• Sensor Specifications: Spectral Bands: MODIS captures data in 36 spectral
bands, ranging from 0.4 to 14.4 micrometers. This includes bands specifically de-
signed for monitoring vegetation, ocean color, land surface temperature, and atmo-
spheric properties.
Spatial Resolution: MODIS provides data at three spatial resolutions: 250 meters
(for two bands), 500 meters (for five bands), and 1 kilometer (for 29 bands). This
range of resolutions enables both detailed local studies and broad global analyses.
• Temporal Resolution: The combination of Terra’s morning orbit and Aqua’s after-
noon orbit allows MODIS to achieve near-daily global coverage.
2.4.4
Limitations of Satellite Data
Despite their extensive applications, satellite data from Sentinel, Landsat, and MODIS
have certain limitations that need to be considered:
1. Resolution Limits
Spatial resolution determines the smallest object that can be detected. MODIS of-
fers coarse resolutions (250m, 500m, 1km), while Landsat (30m, 100m) and Sentinel-
2 (10m, 20m, 60m) provide higher resolutions. However, even these may not cap-
ture very small features or detailed urban structures.
This limits the ability to monitor fine-scale processes, such as small-scale deforesta-
tion, urban changes, or specific species habitats, necessitating the use of comple-
mentary high-resolution data from other sources.
2. Cloud Cover Issues
Optical sensors on satellites like Sentinel-2, Landsat, and MODIS can be obstructed
by clouds, affecting data quality and availability. It thus reduces the frequency of
usable images in cloud-prone regions, potentially delaying monitoring efforts and
requiring additional processing to mitigate cloud interference.
3. Data Availability
While satellite programs aim for regular coverage, data gaps can occur due to satel-
lite malfunctions, orbital adjustments, or other technical issues. Inconsistent data
availability can hinder continuous monitoring and long-term studies, making it chal-
lenging to maintain up-to-date environmental assessments.
4. Accuracy and Applicability
The accuracy of satellite data can be affected by atmospheric conditions (e.g., haze,
aerosols), sensor calibration, and data processing algorithms. It can cause errors
in environmental assessments, necessitating ground truthing for validation and im-
proving algorithmic corrections to enhance data reliability.
==PAGE:21==
5. Temporal Resolution
While MODIS offers near-daily coverage, the temporal resolution of Sentinel-2 (5
days) anmd Landsat (16 days) may not be sufficient for monitoring rapidly changing
phenomena. This limitation can affect the timely detection and analysis of fast-
evolving events, such as natural disasters or sudden environmental changes.
2.5
Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)
The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) [15] is a commonly used vegetation
index obtained from remote sensing data, particularly from satellite imagery. NDVI pro-
vides valuable insights into the condition, density, and spatial distribution of vegetation
across several landscapes.
NDVI is calculated using the following formula:
NDVI = (NIR −Red)
(NIR + Red)
where,
• NIR represents the near-infrared reflectance values of the surface, and
• Red represents the red reflectance values of the surface.
The wavelength ranges typically used for these bands are as follows:
• NIR (Near-Infrared): Approximately 700 to 1100 nanometers (nm)
• Red: Approximately 600to 700 nanometers (nm)

The image illustrates a map generated using the NDVI to assess the vegetation health and
density. Darker green areas represent regions with denser vegetation, while light green
or yellow areas indicate less dense vegetation or non-vegetated surfaces. NDVI values
typically range from -1 to 1, with higher values indicating denser and healthier vegetation.
Specifically,
• Values close to 1 indicate healthy and dense vegetation.
• Values around 0 indicate non-vegetated areas such as water bodies or urban areas.
• Negative values may indicate bare soil, clouds, or snow.

NDVI is widely used in various fields, including forestry, environmental science, agricul-
ture, and urban space planning.
2.6
Enhanced Vegetation Monitoring with Alternative Indices
While the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) remains one of the most widely
used vegetation indices due to its simplicity and effectiveness, several other indices have
been developed to address specific limitations of NDVI and to provide additional insights
under different environmental conditions. This section explores some of these alternative
indices, their advantages, and their applications.
2.6.1
Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI)
The Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) was developed to optimize the vegetation signal in
environments with high biomass and to improve sensitivity in areas with dense vegetation.
Unlike NDVI, EVI corrects for some atmospheric influences such as aerosol scattering and
absorption.
Formula:
EV I = G ×
(NIR −Red)
(NIR + C1 × Red −C2 × Blue + L)
where:
• G is the gain factor (usually set to 2.5)
• NIR is the near-infrared reflectance
• Red is the red reflectance
• Blue is the blue reflectance
• C1 and C2 are coefficients for atmospheric resistance (typically, C1 = 6 and C2 = 7.5)
• L is the canopy background adjustment (typically set to 1)
A study by Huete et al [16] demonstrated the effectiveness of Enhanced Vegetation Index
(EVI) in monitoring tropical forest dynamics, highlighting its ability to reduce atmospheric
and soil background noise.
2.6.2
Soil-Adjusted Vegetation Index (SAVI)
The Soil-Adjusted Vegetation Index (SAVI) was developed to minimize the influence of soil
brightness in areas with sparse vegetation. By incorporating a soil brightness correction
factor, SAVI can provide more accurate vegetation assessments in regions with exposed
soil.
Formula:
SAV I = (NIR −Red) × (1 + L)
(NIR + Red + L)
where:
• L is the soil brightness correction factor (typically set to 0.5 for standard SAVI)
A study by Huete [17] utilized SAVI to assess vegetation cover in arid regions, demon-
strating its effectiveness in reducing soil-induced variations in the vegetation signal.

Some other indices that are also crucial in environmental monitoring include:
Normalized Burn Ratio (NBR):
The NBR is used to identify burned areas and monitor post-fire recovery. It utilizes the
near-infrared (NIR) and short-wave infrared (SWIR) bands.
Formula:
NBR = (NIR −SWIR)
(NIR + SWIR)
Green Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (GNDVI)
The GNDVI is similar to NDVI but uses green reflectance instead of red, making it sen-
sitive to chlorophyll content in the vegetation. It is useful for assessing crop health and
chlorophyll concentration.
Formula:
GNDV I = (NIR −Green)
(NIR + Green)
2.7
Computer Vision and AI
In recent years, blending computer vision and artificial intelligence (AI) has completely
changed the way one interacts with visual data and has introduced new possibilities in
different fields, such as environmental science. Computer vision refers to the field of
study dedicated to enabling machines to interpret and understand visual information from
the surrounding environment, while AI algorithms empower these systems to learn from
data, adapt to new situations, and make smart decisions autonomously.
The evolution of computer vision and AI technologies can be traced back to the pioneering
work of researchers such as Rosenblatt(1958) [18] and Hubel and Wiesel(1962), who laid
the groundwork for neural-network-based approaches to pattern recognition and image
understanding [19]. Over the decades, rapid advancements in computational capabilities
and sensor technology have taken these fields forward leading to transformative applica-
tions across a wide range of industries.
The integration of computer vision and AI technologies presents significant potential for
the assessment and management of natural environments. These technologies allow
for the automated analysis of complex ecological data, offering significant improvements
in accuracy and efficiency. Some of their applications in environmental monitoring are
described below:
2.7.1

Change Detection Over Time
Change detection involves identifying and quantifying changes in an environment over

Change detection using AI typically involves analyzing time-series data from satellite im-

detect subtle changes in land cover, vegetation health, water bodies, and urban infras-

One of its applications can be seen to detect deforestation activities by comparing im-

chitectures (VGG16, ResNet50 and MobileNetv2) to classify forest and non-forest areas
accurately. The model was trained and validated on a new dataset of high-resolution
Landsat-8 satellite images collected from 2010 to 2020. The proposed semantic segmen-
tation model achieved an accuracy of 94.8 %. The results highlight the model’s ability to
detect deforestation and reforestation, offering a robust tool for environmental monitoring
and conservation efforts in Jordan. The figure 2.11 shows visual comparisons of forest
cover changes over time, highlighting areas of deforestation (loss) and reforestation (gain)
detected by the deep segmentation model in various regions of Jordan.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) and computer vision have revolutionized environmental moni-

Some key studies in this area are discussed below:
Study 1: Impact of Climate Factors and Human Activities on NDVI Change in China
The paper by Tuoku et al. [36] underscores the utility of NDVI combined with AI and com-
puter vision in monitoring land cover changes and highlights the significant roles of both
climatic factors and human activities in shaping vegetation cover in China from 1982 to
2020.
Utilizing data from MODIS, meteorological stations, and CO2 concentration records, the
study employed correlation and time-lag analysis to explore the relationship between
NDVI and climate factors such as temperature, precipitation, solar radiation, relative hu-
midity, and CO2 concentration.
A multiple regression with time-lag analysis was developed to simulate NDVI changes,
and the respective influences of climate factors and human activities on NDVI were quan-
tified by comparing simulated values with actual observations.
The results indicated a significant increase in NDVI across China (refer to Figure 3.9),
particularly after 2000, largely due to human activities. Precipitation and solar radiation
emerged as primary drivers of NDVI changes, while CO2 concentration and relative hu-
midity had lesser impacts. The regression model, enhanced by AI and computer vision
techniques, showed high simulation accuracy with a low error rate (0.96%), and post-
2000, human activities were found to positively influence vegetation recovery in most
areas, with an average impact exceeding 30%.

3.4.1
Summary of Key Findings
The literature review has highlighted significant advancements in the field of environmen-
tal monitoring using remote sensing technologies, NDVI applications, and AI and com-
puter vision techniques. Studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of high-resolution
satellite imagery from Sentinel, Landsat, and MODIS in monitoring vegetation health, de-
tecting land cover changes, and assessing urban green spaces. The use of NDVI has
been widely validated for its capability to monitor vegetation dynamics, identify deforesta-
tion, and detect agricultural land use changes. Additionally, AI and computer vision have
revolutionized environmental analysis by automating data processing tasks, enabling ac-
curate species identification, habitat mapping, and change detection over time.
3.4.2
Strengths and Limitations of Existing Research
The reviewed studies have showcased robust methodologies, including the integration
of high-resolution satellite imagery with advanced algorithms and machine learning tech-
niques. These approaches have provided valuable insights into environmental changes
and have significantly enhanced the accuracy of monitoring efforts.
However, certain limitations persist.
Many studies are constrained by the availability
and quality of satellite imagery, as well as the resolution and coverage limitations of the
datasets used. Additionally, while AI and computer vision techniques have shown great
promise, their application in urban environmental monitoring remain under-explored, par-
ticularly in terms of integrating these technologies with the urban planning and policies.
3.4.3
Identified Gaps
There are several critical gaps in the current body of research, particularly in the context
of utilizing Computer Vision and Artificial Intelligence technologies for informed decision-
making regarding land cover changes. The following gaps were identified based on the
objectives outlined in this thesis:
• Integration of AI and Computer Vision in Urban Monitoring:
Despite the growing interest in remote sensing technologies, there exists a notice-
able research gap concerning the integration of AI and Computer Vision specifically
tailored for urban space management. Existing studies focus more on agriculture
and forestry, often lacking a comprehensive approach that fully leverages the capa-
bilities of these advanced technologies in the urban planning and management.
• Inadequate Quantification of Natural Areas:
Current literature does not provide an in-depth assessment of the proportion and
spatial arrangements of natural areas within urban municipalities. Although some
studies are available, they often lack the granularity required to compute the biotopes
for effective conservation efforts.
• Criteria for Identifying Untouched Nature: Limited research has been conducted
on identifying untouched natural areas, leading to a lack of clarity and standard-
ization in criteria for delineating such areas within urban landscapes. Most of the
studies have focused on vegetation health and land cover changes over a period
of time. This inconsistency presents challenges for integrating findings into urban
planning strategies effectively.
==PAGE:43==
• Development of Accurate Algorithms Using Vegetation Indices:
While algorithms utilizing NDVI and other vegetation indices are available to detect
land cover changes are available, they may face limitations when assessing un-
touched nature, particularly in complex urban settings. Further research is required
to develop more reliable and robust algorithms capable of identifying untouched na-
ture.

5.2.1
Data Sources
The high-resolution satellite imagery data used in this project were sourced from the
Sentinel-2 and Landsat-8 satellite missions.
Sentinel-2:
Sentinel-2[8], operated by the European Space Agency under the Copernicus program,
offers high-resolution multispectral imagery that covers 13 spectral bands. This satellite
captures data in visible, near-infrared, and short-wave infrared spectrums with a resolution
as high as 10 metres for key bands - red and near-infrared that are utilized in computation
of vegetation monitoring.
The Sentinel-2 mission comprises two identical satellites, Sentinel-2A and Sentinel-2B.
This twin configuration offers a revisit period of 5 days at the equator with one satellite, and
2-3 days with both in operation under cloud-free conditions, which is crucial for tracking
environmental changes.
Landsat-8:
Landsat-8 [12], a part of the Landsat series jointly managed by NASA and US Geological
Survey, offers a comprehensive view of Earth’s surface. Landsat-8’s primary objective is
to provide consistent and reliable data contributing to climate change studies and envi-
ronmental monitoring.
Landsat-8 uses Operational Land Imager (OLI) and Thermal Infrared Sensor (TIRS) in-
struments to capture data in multiple spectral bands in the visible, near infrared, and
shortwave infrared, with a resolution of 30 metres for most bands and 15 metres for the
panchromatic band. TIRS captures data in two thermal bands, crucial for water manage-
ment and soil moisture studies, with a resolution of 100 metres.

bulk downloading of data in TIFF format, each Sentinel-2 image had to be individu-
ally selected and downloaded due to the bulk download option being available only
in JP2 format. This significantly increased the time required to accumulate a com-
prehensive dataset.
2. Limitations in Area of Interest (AOI) Specification: Unlike other platforms, the
Sentinel-2 data retrieval system did not support the use of a shape file or other
geographical boundary formats to precisely define the area of interest (AOI). This
limitation required manual selection of the AOI, which could lead to inconsistencies
and inaccuracies in the area being studied.
3. Tile Alignment Issues in Landsat-8: For Landsat-8 imagery, a notable challenge
was the alignment of area of interest in different tiles. Unlike Sentinel-2, where
the image alignment is generally consistent when the data is extracted, Landsat-8
images often showed variations in the images’ alignment in the tiles as can be seen
in the figure 5.10 . This discrepancy had to be resolved in the data pre-processing
module for later usage.

This function ensured that all images conformed to the same spatial configuration.
4. Data Consistency Checks and Corrections: If an image did not pass any of the
checks, it was subjected to cropping and reprojection to align it with the reference
file. This step was crucial for maintaining consistency in spatial resolution and align-
ment across the dataset.
Cropping Process:
• The image was first cropped to the AOI bounds.

4. Pad the Array: Pad the NDVI array to handle edge cases using a padding width of
2 and reflection mode.
5. Iterate over pixels to extract the window and features for the central pixel:
Loop through each pixel in the original array:
• Extract a 5x5 window centered on the current pixel.
• Compute the mean and standard deviation of the NDVI values within the win-
dow.
• Store the computed mean and standard deviation in the corresponding posi-
tions in the features_mean and features_std arrays.
6. Output: The function outputs two arrays: features_mean and features_std.
Explanation of Feature Choices
• Mean NDVI: The mean NDVI value provides an average measure of vegetation
health over a specific period (seasonal or annual). It helps in understanding the
general state of vegetation and identifying areas with consistently high or low vege-
tation density.
• Standard Deviation of NDVI: The standard deviation of NDVI values indicates the
variability in vegetation health. A low std deviation suggests uniform vegetation,
while a high std deviation indicates significant changes in vegetation density. This
feature is crucial for detecting areas with stable or fluctuating vegetation.
• Annual Mean NDVI: The annual mean NDVI captures the overall vegetation health
throughout the year, providing insights into long-term vegetation trends and stability.
• Annual Standard Deviation of NDVI: The annual std deviation measures the vari-
ability in vegetation health over the entire year. It helps in identifying areas with
stable vegetation patterns versus those with significant annual fluctuations.
• 5x5 Window Features: It provides local context, enhancing the model’s ability to
detect patterns influenced by neighboring pixels.

Implications of Using Highly Imbalanced Labels
Training models using these labels means dealing with significantly imbalanced datasets.
Typically, the number of unchanged areas is much smaller compared to the other classes.
This imbalance poses several challenges for model training:
• Bias Towards Majority Class: Traditional machine learning models tend to be bi-
ased towards the majority class, which can lead to poor performance in predicting
the minority class (unchanged areas).
• Evaluation Metrics: Standard accuracy metrics may not be representative of model
performance. Precision, recall, and F1 score become crucial metrics to evaluate
how well the model can predict the minority class.
• Synthetic Sampling Techniques: To mitigate the effects of imbalance, we em-
ployed the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE). SMOTE gener-
ates synthetic samples for the minority class by creating interpolations between ex-
isting minority samples. This technique helps in providing a more balanced training
set, allowing the models to better learn the characteristics of the minority class.
SMOTE Application in Training:
For each labeling method, SMOTE was applied to handle class imbalance. This involved
the following steps:
• Identifying Minority and Majority Classes: The minority class is the unchanged
areas, while the majority class includes all other areas.
• Generating Synthetic Samples: SMOTE was used to generate synthetic samples
for the minority class, balancing the training dataset.
• Training the Models: The balanced dataset was then used to train the Random
Forest and Gradient Boosting models.
By addressing the class imbalance through techniques like SMOTE, the models’ ability
to predict unchanged areas accurately was aimed to improve. The following sections
detail the training processes and results for each case, demonstrating how these labeling
strategies and imbalance handling techniques impacted model performance.
5.7.3
Model Training
In this subsection, the training process of multiple machine learning models to predict un-

employed, chosen for their robustness and effectiveness in handling complex datasets.
These models were trained using different cases, each involving unique feature extraction
methods and labeling strategies.
Random Forest Model:
The Random Forest classifier is an ensemble learning method that constructs multiple
decision trees during training and outputs the mode of the classes (classification) of the
individual trees. It is particularly suitable for this task due to its ability to handle large
datasets with high dimensionality, its robustness against overfitting, and its capability to
manage imbalanced datasets. Additionally, Random Forest provides feature importance
metrics, which are valuable for understanding the contributions of different features.
==PAGE:79==
Gradient Boosting Model:
The Gradient Boosting classifier is another powerful ensemble learning technique, which
builds models sequentially, each new model correcting errors made by the previous ones.
This method is highly effective for improving predictive accuracy and managing complex
relationships within the data. Gradient Boosting is known for its ability to handle both
classification and regression tasks, providing robust performance even with noisy or im-
balanced datasets. It also offers feature importance metrics, which help in interpreting
model decisions.
Training Process
For each model, various cases were explored using different feature extraction methods
and labeling strategies as already discussed in the previous sections. To summarize
1. Feature Extraction Methods:
• Individual Pixel Statistics: This method involves extracting features such as
the mean, standard deviation, annual mean, and annual standard deviation of
NDVI values for each pixel.
• 5x5 Pixel Window Features: This method involves extracting the mean and
standard deviation of NDVI values within a 5x5 pixel window, capturing local
spatial context around each pixel.
2. Labeling Strategies:
• Combination of Para 3 Areas and Unchanged Areas: Labels were gener-
ated by combining the protected Para 3 areas and the detected unchanged
areas using logical OR operations
• Combination of Para 3 Areas and Annually Stable Areas: Labels were cre-
ated by combining the protected Para 3 areas and areas identified as annually
stable.
• Prioritized Labels: A hierarchical labeling approach was used, prioritizing the
Para 3 areas (highest trust) and then incorporating annually stable areas (mod-
erate trust).
3. Imbalance Handling: To address the class imbalance in the datasets, we applied
the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) during the training pro-
cess. SMOTE generates synthetic samples for the minority class, ensuring the mod-
els receive balanced training data.
4. Model Evaluation: Both Random Forest and Gradient Boosting models were eval-
uated on their ability to predict unchanged areas. Their performance was compared
based on metrics such as overall accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score for the
true class (unchanged areas).

Summary
In this chapter, a solution was implemented for detecting the unchanged areas using satel-
lite imagery, focusing on data extraction , pre-processing, NDVI computation, machine
learning prediction, and validation. High-resolution imagery from Sentinel-2 and Landsat-
8 was acquired, with preprocessing steps ensuring data consistency through cropping,
reprojection, and alignment. NDVI values were calculated for each image, forming the
==PAGE:98==
basis for assessing unchanged areas via seasonal and annual NDVI statistics. Protected
nature type areas were extracted from Denmark’s miljøportal WFS API and integrated into
the analysis. Machine learning models, including Random Forest and Gradient Boosting
classifiers, were trained using features derived from individual pixel statistics and a 5x5
pixel window, with models validated against Para 3 areas provided by the municipality.
Validation results indicated varying success across different models and datasets, high-
lighting the effectiveness of the methodology in detecting unchanged areas using satellite
imagery.

mentation. The implications of the findings and potential improvements will be discussed.

6.2
Model Performance Analysis
The performance analysis of the models across different training cases and datasets pro-
vides valuable insights into the effectiveness of various feature extraction methods, label-
ing strategies, and the handling of class imbalance. This section summarizes key findings
and evaluates the strengths and weaknesses of the models used.
6.2.1
Accuracy Metrics
Overall accuracy was generally high across most training cases, particularly for the Sentinel-
2 dataset. However, as accuracy alone can be misleading due to class imbalance, it is
essential to consider other metrics such as precision, recall, and F1 score.
==PAGE:101==
6.2.2
Precision, Recall, and F1 Score
• Precision: Precision was notably higher for the Landsat-8 dataset compared to the
Sentinel-2 dataset, indicating better performance in correctly identifying true pos-
itives. This was particularly evident in Training Case 2, where precision reached
0.96 for the Landsat-8 dataset.
• Recall: Recall varied significantly across different training cases. Training Case 2
showed the highest recall for the true class, especially for the Sentinel-2 dataset
(0.86). This suggests that combining annually stable areas with Para 3 areas effec-
tively captures true positives.
• F1 Score: The F1 score, which balances precision and recall, was consistently
higher for the Landsat-8 dataset, indicating a more balanced performance. Gradient
Boosting models generally achieved higher F1 scores compared to Random Forest
models, demonstrating their superior capability in handling complex data structures.
6.2.3
Impact of Feature Extraction Methods
• Pixel-Level Features: Models using pixel-level features showed reasonable perfor-
mance, but their effectiveness varied depending on the labeling strategy. Training
Case 2, which combined annually stable and Para 3 areas, provided the best results
for pixel-level features.
• Spatial Context Features: Incorporating spatial context features (5x5 pixel win-
dow) improved model performance, particularly for the Gradient Boosting models.
This indicates that spatial context is crucial for capturing local variations and improv-
ing the detection of unchanged areas.
6.2.4
Labeling Strategies
• Combined Unchanged and Para 3 Areas: This strategy provided a good starting
point, but the results were not as strong compared to other methods. The precision
and recall were moderate, and further refinement was needed.
• Combined Annually Stable and Para 3 Areas: This labeling strategy was the most
effective, yielding high recall and F1 scores. It balanced the identification of true
positives and negatives, making it suitable for the detection of unchanged areas.
• Prioritized Labels: The prioritized labeling approach performed poorly, especially
with the Sentinel-2 dataset. The models struggled with low precision and F1 scores,
indicating difficulty in correctly identifying true positives. This suggests that the hi-
erarchical labeling approach needs further refinement.
6.2.5
Handling Class Imbalance
Despite the use of SMOTE to address class imbalance, the models still faced challenges,
particularly with the Sentinel-2 dataset. The severe imbalance skewed overall accuracy
and reduced the effectiveness of the models in detecting the minority class (unchanged
areas). Advanced imbalance handling techniques and more sophisticated models are
needed to improve performance.
6.2.6
Comparison of Models
• Random Forest: Random Forest models showed moderate performance but strug-
gled with class imbalance and complex data structures. They were outperformed
by Gradient Boosting models in most cases.
• Gradient Boosting: Gradient Boosting models consistently outperformed Random
Forest models when spatial context features were used. They showed higher over-
==PAGE:102==
all accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 scores, indicating their superior capability in
handling complex and imbalanced datasets.
6.2.7
Comparison Between Datasets
• Sentinel-2 Dataset: Generally exhibited higher overall accuracy but suffered from
severe class imbalance, particularly evident in Training Case 3. This imbalance re-
sulted in misleadingly high accuracy and low recall and F1 scores for the true class.
Sentinel-2 showed better recall in Training Case 2, indicating some effectiveness in
detecting true positives when combined with annually stable areas.
• Landsat-8 Dataset: Outperformed Sentinel-2 in terms of precision and F1 score
across most training cases. The Landsat-8 dataset provided more balanced detec-
tion of unchanged areas, as evidenced by higher precision, recall, and F1 scores.
This dataset appears to be more reliable for feature extraction and model training,
particularly when using Gradient Boosting models.
6.3
Discussion of Challenges and Limitations
The challenges and limitations faced during the development of this machine learning
framework are discussed in this section.
6.3.1
Class Imbalance
One of the significant challenges encountered in this study was the class imbalance,
with a smaller proportion of unchanged areas compared to the total dataset. This im-
balance can lead to biased model training, where the model becomes adept at predicting
the majority class but performs poorly on the minority class (unchanged areas). While
the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) was applied to generate syn-
thetic samples for the minority class, further techniques could be explored to improve the
model’s ability to predict minority classes. For instance, advanced resampling methods
like Adaptive Synthetic (ADASYN) sampling, ensemble methods like Balanced Random
Forest or EasyEnsemble, and cost-sensitive learning could provide additional improve-
ments. Moreover, experimenting with different class weighting strategies during model
training could help the model focus more on the minority class.
6.3.2
Data Preprocessing
The preprocessing steps were crucial for ensuring data consistency and quality, directly
impacting the accuracy of NDVI calculations and subsequent feature extraction. Key pre-
processing steps included filtering cloud-free data, cropping, reprojection, and alignment
of the satellite imagery. Any errors or inconsistencies in these steps could significantly
affect the results. For instance, inaccuracies in cloud masking could lead to erroneous
NDVI values, whereas improper reprojection could misalign the data, affecting the integrity
of spatial analyses. To mitigate these risks, rigorous quality control measures were im-
plemented, including manual inspection and automated checks for data integrity. Future
work could benefit from more sophisticated preprocessing algorithms, such as machine
learning-based cloud detection and advanced image registration techniques, to further
improve data quality.
6.3.3
Model Limitations
Both Random Forest and Gradient Boosting models have inherent limitations. The Ran-
dom Forest model, while robust against overfitting and capable of handling large datasets,
may struggle with noisy data and complex relationships within the data. Gradient Boost-
ing, on the other hand, can be sensitive to overfitting, particularly with imbalanced datasets,
and requires careful tuning of hyperparameters. Future work could explore more ad-
vanced algorithms or ensemble methods, such as XGBoost, LightGBM, or CatBoost,
==PAGE:103==
which might offer better performance and robustness. Additionally, investigating hybrid
models that combine the strengths of different algorithms, or employing deep learning
techniques like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for feature extraction, could fur-
ther enhance predictive accuracy and model stability.

6.5
Recommendations for Future Work
There is a lot of improvement required to make this framework robust and more accurate.
This section explores a few ways to enhance this research work.
6.5.1
Improving Model Performance
Future research should explore different machine learning algorithms, additional data
sources, and more sophisticated techniques for addressing class imbalance to improve
model performance. Techniques such as deep learning, hybrid models, or transfer learn-
ing from pre-trained models could be investigated. Incorporating more diverse data sources,
such as high-resolution aerial imagery, LiDAR data, or multi-spectral and hyper-spectral
imagery, could provide richer information for model training and improve predictive accu-
racy. Additionally, experimenting with advanced feature engineering techniques and data
augmentation methods could enhance model robustness and generalizability.
6.5.2
Addressing Limitations
Efforts should be made to refine data preprocessing methods and explore advanced mod-
eling techniques to overcome the identified limitations. For example, incorporating multi-
temporal data analysis to account for seasonal variations in NDVI could enhance model
accuracy. Developing more sophisticated cloud masking algorithms, improving image
registration processes, and employing advanced noise reduction techniques could further
improve data quality. Additionally, exploring the impact of different spatial and temporal
resolutions on model performance could provide insights into the optimal data configura-
tions for environmental monitoring tasks.

Further Research
Areas for further research include the impact of seasonal changes on model predictions

applicability of the models. Investigating the use of other vegetation indices, such as
the Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) or Soil-Adjusted Vegetation Index (SAVI), in combi-

land cover changes. Additionally, exploring the potential of integrating socio-economic
and environmental data, such as land use patterns, population density, and climate vari-
ables, could provide a more holistic understanding of urban ecosystems and support more
informed decision-making.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the application of artificial intelligence and computer vision tech-
niques to analyze satellite imagery and identify untouched biotopes in urban landscapes.
This project aimed to develop machine learning models for identifying and monitoring un-
changed natural areas using satellite imagery from Sentinel-2 and Landsat-8 datasets.
Various feature extraction methods and labeling strategies were explored to enhance the
accuracy and reliability of predictions. Key findings from the study indicate that Gradient
Boosting models outperformed Random Forest models, particularly when spatial context
features were utilized. The incorporation of spatial context features, such as a 5x5 pixel
window, significantly improved model performance, underscoring the importance of cap-
turing local variations in vegetation. Among the labeling strategies, combining annually
stable areas with Para 3 areas proved most effective, yielding high recall and F1 scores.
This balanced approach was crucial for accurate detection. Additionally, the Landsat-8
dataset generally provided better performance metrics than the Sentinel-2 dataset, sug-
gesting more reliable feature extraction and a more balanced detection of unchanged
areas.

indicative of minimal human impact, thereby providing insights into the distribution

• Define criteria for identifying and delineating untouched nature within the mu-

gies.
• Develop algorithms capable of accurately identifying areas of untouched na-
ture: The study developed and validated Random Forest and Gradient Boosting
algorithms, which effectively identified areas of untouched nature, leveraging so-
phisticated feature extraction and machine learning techniques.

municipality’s nature quality plan: The integration of satellite imagery and ma-
chine learning algorithms can provide a robust tool for the municipality, enhancing
their ability to monitor and manage natural areas, supporting the Grønt Danmark-
skort strategy.
