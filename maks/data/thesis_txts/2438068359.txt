ABSTRACT
Global warming and increasing emissions of greenhouse gases call for more eﬃcient and renewable
energy technologies. The energy use of fossil fueled heavy-duty vehicles (HDV) represents a large
fraction of the worldwide energy use and the demand is expected to increase in the years to come.
The Organic Rankine Cycle (ORC) is a technology that has shown great potential for waste
heat recovery from vehicles. However, the transient dynamics of the heat sources from internal
combustion engines (ICE) involve very large and fast ﬂuctuations in ﬂow rate and temperature,
and thus controlling the ORC safely and optimally is a challenging task.
This thesis investigates the transient behaviour of a mini-scale ORC system used for recov-
ering waste heat in the exhaust gas of a HDV and presents several PID-based controllers. A case
study of an optimized 10.9kW ORC implemented on a 13L Euro 6 diesel engine is used together
with real exhaust gas data from a transient driving cycle.
To design and test diﬀerent control strategies, a dynamic numerical ORC model has been
developed in the Modelica language using Dymola and existing libraries and validated against
transient experimental data of a 1kWe ORC. Using the validated model the system have been
simulated and the dynamic behaviour thoroughly analyzed. Furthermore a number of PID-based
controllers have been developed, tuned, optimized and tested under transient exhaust gas condi-
tions. Finally the performance of the controllers are evaluated and compared.
The validation shows an accuracy suﬃcient to support controller design.
All variables of
the dynamic model are reproduced with less than 8.2% deviation and a mean average percentage
deviation of less than 7% compared with experimental data for a transient event. Analysis of
the system dynamic behaviour show the system non-linearity, relationships between the system
variables and important knowledge for the controller design.
Simulation results show that a control scheme based on a PID-controller with anti-windup
and dynamic feed-forward (FF) shows superior performance compared to a PI-, PID- and
PID+static FF controller for minor disturbances. Simulations with real transient exhaust gas
data show a potential increase in the eﬃciency of the vehicle by 3.4 percentage points. However,
superheating could only be preserved at turbine inlet for 87% of the time. The results show that,
it is not possible to preserve superheating at all times with only a single PID-based controller
using the pump speed as control variable. This is due to: 1) the very ﬂuctuating exhaust gas
conditions, 2) changes in exhaust gas ﬂow rate and temperature are found to have a faster eﬀect
on the superheating temperature, than changes in pump rotational speed. The results show that
a single volatile disturbance is enough to result in 2-phase ﬂuid entering the turbine even for
aggressive proactive action of the pump rotational speed.
Based on the ﬁndings suggestions for improvements and future work are given. In particu-
lar it is recommended to focus on more advanced control strategies than PID-based controllers.

INTRODUCTION
Climate change and increasing emissions of greenhouse gases are currently some of the worlds
biggest challenges and have received increasing attention in the past decades. Global support of
the Paris agreement [1] and submission of national climate action plans from numerous countries
[2] testify the global need and wish to reduce emissions to avoid dangerous climate change.
Within road transportation, the energy use of Heavy-Duty Vehicles (HDVs) represents a large
fraction of the transportation energy demand. In the U.S. this share is the second largest and
fastest growing share of transportation energy demand [3, 4], and the energy consumption is
met almost entirely by diesel fuel and motor gasoline [3]. Predictions, made by the U.S Energy
Information Administration in their World Energy Outlook 2017, estimates that despite decreases
in the share of fossil fuels in the transportation sector in the decades to come, the total use of
fossil fuels will increase due to an increase in demand.
Modern HDV diesel engines cannot convert more than half of the fuel energy [5], and the
rest of the input energy is lost as waste heat. However, waste heat can be utilized by diﬀerent
means to increase energy eﬃciency of a HDV, but no commercially available option yet exists.
The Organic Rankine Cycle (ORC) is a thermodynamic cycle, which uses an organic ﬂuid to
recover waste or low grade heat and research have shown, that it has great potential for waste
heat recovery (WHR) from vehicles [6], [7].
An ORC implemented on a HDV converts some of the waste heat into usable energy, and
thereby reduce the engine fuel consumption. However, the waste heat from the Internal Combus-
tion Engine (ICE) of a HDV can involve very large and fast ﬂuctuations in temperature and
ﬂow rate depending on the driving cycle of the vehicle [8]. This dynamic heat source makes it a
diﬃcult task to control the ORC unit to ensure maximum net power output and safe operation
of the system. Without proper control of the cycle the transient operational conditions of the
ORC can result in (among others) component failure due to stalling or temperature shocks,
over-heating and ﬂuid decomposition of the working ﬂuid in the ORC [9]. Thus the development
of a suitable controller is of crucial importance to ensure eﬃcient and safe operation of the ORC.
1.1
Scope and objective
The primary objective of this project is to develop a control strategy for a mini-scale ORC unit,
which recovers waste heat from the internal combustion engine of a heavy-duty vehicle. The objec-
tive of the control strategy is ﬁrstly to ensure safe operation of the ORC and secondly to optimize
its power output under given realistic boundary conditions. To be able to understand plant-level
dynamic behaviour and interaction and to design and test the control approach a dynamic model
of the ORC unit is of vital importance. In this context the aim of the project is additionally to de-
velop and validate a dynamic model that satisfyingly describes the dynamic behaviour of the ORC.

1. Introduction
Methodology
Dynamic modelling and control is a demanding and diﬃcult task and to limit the scope of the
project, the design and optimization phase of the ORC unit is omitted. Instead an already
designed and optimized ORC system is used as a starting point for the project. This optimized
system is based upon previous research performed at the section of Thermal Energy at the Tech-
nical University of Denmark (DTU). Combined engine-ORC WHR systems are very complicated
and analysis of a complete system requires simultaneous simulation of both engine and ORC
side. Simulation of the engine signiﬁcantly increases the complexity and workload. Therefore
this is not part of the scope of the thesis.
Furthermore, the control part of the project is limited to design and optimization of Proportional-
Integral (PI) and Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) based controllers, which are found
to be interesting types of controllers for commercial operation, due to their widespread use,
low computational requirement and satisfactory performance in terms of control response and
robustness.
1.2
Methodology
To address the project objective, i.e. to develop a suitable controller, a numerical dynamic model
of an ORC unit based on ﬁrst principles has been developed. The model has been used to simulate
and analyze the dynamic behaviour of an ORC utilizing the exhaust gas from the engine of a HDV
to recover energy and increase the overall energy eﬃciency of the vehicle. The interaction between
the system components and its operational conditions are taking into account and real experimen-
tal data for a driving cycle of a diesel HDV is used as input to the model. The model is based on
a thermodynamic optimized mini-scale ORC system designed speciﬁcally for the purpose of WHR
on-board a HDV using the aforementioned experimental data, which is obtained from a truck
manufacturer. Details for the optimized ORC system are based on a recent study performed at the
Mechanical Department at DTU, section of Thermal Energy. Details are found in Imran et al. [10].
The developed model has been validated by comparing simulation results with experimen-
tal data for a 1kW ORC system. Knowledge and data for experiments with this system is based
on studies performed by Usman et al. [11, 12] and through contact with the authors.
Using the validated dynamical model, the dynamic behaviour of the ORC is analyzed by
performing a number of open-loop tests. With knowledge of the dynamic behaviour several PI
and PID-based controllers have been developed, optimized and used to control the ORC under
realistic exhaust gas conditions. Finally the performance of the diﬀerent controllers have been
analyzed and compared.
The dynamical model presented in this thesis were built on pre-existing foundations, which
allowed to reduce the model development time, and to exploit tested and validated modeling
code. The model have been developed in the Equation-based Object Oriented (EOO) modeling
language Modelica using the commercial software Dymola. EOO tools are optimal for dynamic
modelling and have already been successfully used for complex dynamic systems including
automotive applications [8]. The existing freely available Modelica libraries ThermoPower,
ThermoCycle and the ORC library have been utilized for model components. ExternalMedia,

Climate change and the transportation sector
Climate change and global warming have received increasing attention in the past two decades.
Numerous studies have documented anthropogenic emissions of greenhouse gases as the reason
for global warming and predicts further warming in the years to come [13, 14]. To ﬁght this
trend decreasing emissions of greenhouse gases including CO2 is of vital importance and in 2015
the Paris Agreement was adopted by 195 countries at the Paris Climate conference (COP21) [1].
The legally binding agreement aims at keeping the increase in global average temperature below
2°C. In order to achieve such a goal global greenhouse emissions must decrease and especially
CO2 emissions, which by far is the largest greenhouse gas emitted.
Data for the fuel mix and energy share of HDVs are, to the authors knowledge, scarce and diﬃcult
to come by. However data for the road transportation sector is to some extent representative for

road transportation [15]. This points to the fact that, the road transport sector accounts for a
major part of the worldwide emissions of CO2, but also that a large potential for reductions in
emissions can be found in this sector.
Alternative technologies are showing possible solutions to replace the ICE. Examples are electric
powered vehicles (EVs) and usage of liqueﬁed petroleum gas (LPG) or biofuels. According to the
U.S. Energy Information Administration’s (eia) World Energy Outlook 2017 [16], the usage of
reﬁned petroleum and other liquid fuels in the transportation sector are predicted to decrease
their fuel-share from 95% in 2017 to 88% in 2040. This is due to an increased usage of alternative
fuels. However the actual use of fossil fuels are expected to increase due to increasing demand

2.2
Potential for waste heat recovery on a HDV
In principle ﬁve diﬀerent sources of waste heat are available on a HDV depending on the complex-
ity of the engine system: the exhaust gas, the cooling ﬂuid from the Exhaust gas Recirculation
(EGR), the cooling ﬂuid from the Charge Air Cooler (CAC), the engine coolant and the lube oil
[19, 26]. All these ﬂuids are involved in diﬀerent processes in the vehicle engine system, which
heat them and thereby they potentially contain energy, which can be recovered to increase the
overall energy eﬃciency of the system. Exhaust gas and the engine cooling ﬂuid is always present,
but a CAC and EGR are not always implemented. However in most modern HD diesel engines
these technologies are utilized to improve the engine eﬃciency and reduce emissions. Except for
the lube oil, the mentioned energy sources are illustrated in Figure 2.3 for an ICE with EGR
and turbocharging implemented.
The intake air is compressed in the compressor side of the turbo-charger, then cooled and
ﬁnally injected into the engine combustion chambers together with the fuel which creates an
ignition process that drives the engine crankshaft and consequently drives the vehicle. After com-
bustion the exhaust gas leaves the cylinders and ﬂows trough the expander of the turbocharger,
which drives the compressor side. Finally the gas ﬂows through the after treatment system (ATS),
which cleans the gas before it is emitted to the ambient. Part of the exhaust gas is recirculated
back to the engine cylinders using EGR. WHR based on the exhaust gases can be placed both
upstream or downstream of the ATS.
Due to the combustion processes in the engine the exhaust gas is hot and contains a high
energy amount. This gives a potential for WHR. In addition, the combustion processes in
the ICE increases the temperature of the whole engine system. To keep the engine operating
temperature within speciﬁed limits, the cooling system is applied to remove heat from the engine.
As a result the cooling ﬂuid is a potential source for WHR.

EGR and turbocharging.

Brieﬂy explained EGR is a technique to reduce NOx emissions. Part of the exhaust gas is
recirculated back to the engine cylinders passing through a heat exchanger, which cools the
gas. This allows a greater mass of recirculated gas. As a result of the recirculated and cooled
gas O2 in the incoming air stream is diluted and provides gases inert to combustion to act as
absorbents of combustion heat to reduce in-cylinder peak temperatures. As NOx is produced in a
narrow band of high cylinder temperatures and pressures less NOx is produced. As a result of the
heat removal from the recirculated exhaust gas the EGR cooling ﬂuid increases its energy content.
The CAC is a heat exchanger, which is part of the turbo-charging process. The turbo-charger
(TC) increases the power output and eﬃciency of the ICE. The pressure of the intake air is
increased in the compressor of the turbo-charger, which allows more mass of air, and propor-
tionally more fuel, to be supplied to the combustion chambers [27]. As the pressure of the
charge air increases so does the temperature. Increasing temperature results in lower den-
sity of the air, and less oxygen available for the combustion process. To prevent excessive
temperature increases the CAC is implemented between the TC and the engine cylinders. As
a result of the heat removal from the charge air the CAC cooling ﬂuid increases its energy content.
All the mentioned energy streams can be classiﬁed as waste heat and can potentially be used
for WHR. However in practice not all these sources can be eﬀectively used. Table 2.2 presents
ranges for temperature and heat available for the diﬀerent WHR sources on a modern ICE.
All values are based on values from literature for HD diesel engines. The table is intended to
give an idea of the wasted energy ﬂows of an HDV with an ICE, rather than to supply directly
comparable values. The ranges include values that are not directly comparable. An example are
temperatures of the exhaust gas measured before and after the TC expander, respectively. Both
types contribute to Table 2.2. The exhaust gas and EGR coolant are by far showing the highest

Other potential sources are the EGR cooling ﬂuid and the engine coolant having a high tempera-
ture potential and a high quantity, respectively. The CAC cooling ﬂuid and the lube oil are both
considered to have low WHR potentials due to their low temperatures and ﬂow rates. The same
observations are seen in literature [6, 22, 33, 37]. Comparing numbers for the heat available in
Table 2.2, the coolant seems to have the second highest potential after the exhaust gas. However
Teng et al. [22] performed a second law analysis of an engine system and found, that despite
the heat rejection from the engine coolant forms around 20% of the fuel energy, this heat ﬂow
is of the least interest to WHR. This is because only a small portion of it can be converted to
use-able energy [22].
Thus the recovery of energy from heat sources such as the CAC and the coolant are not practical,
unless complex layouts are used (such as dual-loops, cascaded cycles or re-superheating). Such
layouts lead to increased weight, cost and space requirements and more complicated control
strategies [19]. Based on these initial ﬁndings the exhaust gas stream is identiﬁed as the most
promising waste heat source on a HDV.
Technologies to utilize waste heat
Conventional uses of waste heat on vehicles is limited to the exhaust gas and are usually for
turbo-charging or cabin air-heating [7]. However, only a small portion of the waste energy is
utilized in this way. In general three types of technologies exist which can be used to convert the
waste heat from ICE exhaust gas into useful energy [37]:
• Turbocompounding.
• Thermoelectric generators.
• Rankine Cycles.
Rankine Cycles for WHR are generally considered as the most promising solution in recent years
according Stanton [6], Colonna et al. [25], Lion et al. [19], Xu et al. [37], with the exhaust gas
and the EGR stream being regarded as the most viable economically energy sources. Wang et al.
[7] performed a review of researches and found that the Rankine cycle is the most favourable
working cycle for thermodynamic exhaust heat recovery. Compared to competing technologies
the main advantages of the Rankine Cycles are the cost [21], the simplicity and the availability
of its components [38, 39]. Turbocompounding and thermoelectric generators are not within the
scope of this thesis, but are interesting and competing technologies. For a short description of
these technologies refer to appendix A. Details on Rankine cycles and especially on the Organic
Rankine cycle are presented in the following section.
2.3
Organic Rankine Cycles & heavy-duty vehicles
Rankine cycles (RCs) are thermodynamic cycles utilizing a working ﬂuid to drive an expander. It
is commonly employed in power and Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants. Organic Rankine
Cycles (ORC) are Rankine cycles, with the main diﬀerence that an organic ﬂuid is used instead
of water/steam as working ﬂuid. A simple ORC is shown in Figure 2.4.

The two main advantages of an ORC compared to a traditional RC cycle are [25]:
• The selection of the working ﬂuid adds an additional degree of freedom to the designer
of the thermodynamic cycle. This means that the ﬂuid can be chosen such that, it is
optimal from a technical and thermodynamic point of view. The properties of the ﬂuid
(speciﬁc heat, saturation line etc.) can be chosen to ﬁt the speciﬁc heat source such that
the temperature diﬀerence between the source and the working ﬂuid in the evaporator
is minimized [22]. In addition ﬂuid and density levels in the system can, to a certain
extent, be chosen independently of the cycle temperatures. Water is not optimal for a low
temperature source, as the properties of water are not appropriate for such a heat source,
making it a less favourable choice of working ﬂuid compared to an organic ﬂuid [25].
• The expander design for systems with a low power output (few kW to few MW) is
diﬃcult to develop. Low output expanders using steam have diﬃculties with eﬃciency,
reliability and cost-eﬀectiveness. However, if the working ﬂuid is organic a much smaller
enthalpy decrease of the expanding vapor can be achieved (than for water/steam), which
allows for the design of an expander with a lower rotational speed and higher volumetric
ﬂow for a speciﬁc power output [25]. In addition: for many organic ﬂuids the expansion
process is completely dry, which avoids blade erosion and expansion ineﬃciency due to
condensation. For water a high moisture content is involved during expansion of steam.
This can make the use of reheat necessary, which adds cost and components [40, 41].
ORCS are in general relatively simple and consists of a limited number of components. The
evaporator, expander, condenser and pump are the minimum components needed. Systems
involving a more complex design often involves recuperators and liquid receivers. Due to the
properties of the organic ﬂuid, the evaporation process usually takes place at lower temperatures,
pressure and require less heat compared to RCs. The relatively low pressure ratio of the expander
enables the use of use of simple single stage turbines [41]. Additionally the freezing level of water
is too high for uses in automotive applications, where the system will be exposed to temperatures
below 0°C. However multiple organic ﬂuids have freezing levels much lower than water and do
not involve damaging volume increase.

Organic Rankine Cycles & heavy-duty vehicles
Another advantage of ORC systems is the applicability to almost any external thermal energy
source [25, 42]. Temperature diﬀerences between source and sink can range from as low as
30°C [25] and it has a high reliability and ﬂexibility, which makes it particularly attractive [42].
Therefore they are used in many applications. The more traditional ones include low-temperature
geothermal power production, power production from biomass or Concentrated Solar Power
(CSP), heat recovery from industrial processes and cogeneration for district heating [8, 25, 43].
More innovative applications are power generation in oﬀ-grid systems and heat recovery from
ICEs on board vehicles. The same principles of the ORC are governing no-matter the application.
Implementation of ORCs on heavy-duty vehicles
By adding an ORC as a bottoming cycle on a HDV the diﬀerent heat sources mentioned previously
can be utilized. These energy sources make up the heat source for the ORCs evaporator, which
ensures the evaporation of the working ﬂuid. Given the high potential for WHR as shown in
section 2.2, the exhaust gas is the most obvious choice of heat source. However the EGR is also
commonly utilized. Two examples of simple engine-ORC layouts are illustrated in Figure 2.5. In
Figure 2.5a only the exhaust gas is used as heat source. However, in Figure 2.5b two evaporators
in series are used. The ﬁrst one uses the exhaust gas to preheat and potentially evaporate the
working ﬂuid. The second one uses the recirculated exhaust gas in the EGR as a hot source to
evaporate and/or superheat the working ﬂuid.
(a) Single evaporator using exhaust gas.
(b) Series evaporators using EGR and exhaust gas.

The power generated by the ORC expander can be utilized either to power the electrical acces-
sories of the HDV (e.g. air conditioner, refrigerator, etc.), or to add mechanical power to the
vehicles crankshaft. Both methods of utilizing the generated power reduce the demand for engine
power (engine load) and lower the fuel consumption and emissions [37, 21]. This in turn aﬀects
the engine performance that subsequently aﬀects the heat sources of the ORC. For example: a
reduction of the demanded engine power results in changes in the exhaust gas conditions, which
alter the available heat for the ORC. This makes the two systems (engine & ORC) interdependent
[37].

State of the art
Traditional ORC applications are stationary or quasistationary. The heat input is constant or
varies slowly [8]. Thus the dynamic behaviour and the control system is not particular critical
except for startup and stop of the cycle. However, the use of ORCs for WHR of ICEs onboard
vehicles requires a good understanding of the dynamics and features critical control performance
to succeed [8, 25, 38]. The waste heat from the ICE of a heavy duty vehicle can involve very
large and fast ﬂuctuations in temperature and ﬂow rate depending on the driving cycle of the
vehicle [8]. This dynamic heat source makes it a very complicated task to control the ORC unit
[34]. Often the two main challenges are [44]:
1. Ensure safe operation of the system.
2. Maximize the net power generation or cycle eﬃciency.
Safe operation is important not only to keep passengers in the vehicle safe, but also to keep the
life expectancy of the components high [44]. Maximizing the net power generation is an obvious
control objective, but is often secondary to the safety of the system. The control objectives are
further elaborated in section 5.1.1. In addition to the control challenges, the transient nature of
the ICE heat sources mean that the ORC will often operate in oﬀ-design. This adds additional
challenges to the design and control aspect of ORCs implemented for WHR on HDVs. Finally
space limitations, weight and cost of the ORC components are among the most important aspects,
which directly aﬀect the practical implementation and commercial success of ORCs within the
ﬁeld of WHR from HDVs [10].

State of the art
A study by Teng & Park et al. [20, 29] experimentally demonstrated 3 to 5% diesel fuel savings by
utilizing an ORC to recover waste hear from a 10.8-liter HD diesel engine. Diﬀerent conﬁgurations
using ethanol as working ﬂuid were investigated to utilize waste heat from both the EGR and
the tailpipe exhaust gas. The best results were obtained by using only the tailpipe exhaust gas,
because the conﬁguration with both EGR and exhaust gas used a low EGR ﬂow rate, which
resulted in a low rate of evaporation. Special for this study, compared to other systems seen in
literature, is the separate use of a evaporator and a superheater.
Imran et al. [10] performed a multi-objective optimization of an ORC for WHR on a HDV
vehicle with a 450hp diesel engine. Using the genetic algorithm and real experimental data for
exhaust gas, the thermodynamic and economic performance were optimized with respect to the
ORC power output, the total weight, the total volume and the total cost. The decision variables
were: the evaporating, the superheat and the pinch point temperature of the evaporator. The
optimized ORC power output was found to be 10.9kW / 8.3kW in design/oﬀ-design (average)
conditions using pentane as working ﬂuid. This corresponds to an increase of net power output
of a hypothetical vehicle of 7.4% in design and 5.6% in oﬀ-design conditions, when comparing
with the ORC design point of the engine of 148kW.
In general implementation of an ORC unit is expected to reduce the fuel consumption of
a HDV by 3-10% [61] and gives an increase of the system thermal eﬃciency of a few percentage
points. Currently the relatively low ORC eﬃciency limits the power yield for implementations
on ICEs despite the amount of heat available [34].
Diﬀerent ORC designs are used in academia and tested commercially, but most systems are
relatively simple with only an evaporator, an expander, a condenser and a pump. This keeps the
cost, volume and the weight of the ORC system down. In addition a ﬂuid receiver is often placed
after the condenser to act as a buﬀer during operation [37]. This avoids surging of the pump and
compensates the liquid level ﬂuctuations in the evaporator [38]. In addition a bypass valve is
often used upstream of the evaporator (hot side) to avoid the ORC system from overheating
at engine loads exceeding the condensation capacity [37]. However this cannot be done on the
EGR recirculating gas, as low EGR outlet temperatures are needed to ensure the engine intake
volumetric eﬃciency [37].
Research challenges regarding ORCs on HDVs are the highly dynamic conditions and the
lack of commercially available high eﬃciency expanders. According to Ziviani et al. [46] this has
slowed down the development of small size ORCs. In general two types of expanders are used.
The turbo-machinery type and the volumetric type. A volumetric type is used more frequently,
because it is suitable for small-scale ORC systems. They are suitable for high-pressure ratios,
lower rotational speed and often involve a limited number of moving parts [46]. In several cases
in academia volumetric compressors have been modiﬁed to run in reverse as an expander [12, 62].

State of the art within dynamic modelling and control of ORC
systems
Due to the highly transient conditions of the heat sources on a HDV, dynamic modelling and
control are vital aspects of the successful implementation of an ORC. However, when taking all
ORC applications into account this is currently a research area that holds only a low share of
publications. Imran et al. [45] found that from 2000 to 2016 only 3.5% of publications dealing
with ORC systems involved the topic "dynamics and control". This is partly because most ORC
applications deals with relatively constant temperature and mass ﬂow rate proﬁles, but the
low share of publications can still be seen as an expression for the small amount of research
done within this speciﬁc ﬁeld. This corresponds with observations made by Petr et al. [30] and
Hernandez et al. [44]. However, even though they are scarce, studies on dynamic modelling and
control of ORCs are present in the literature.
Desideri [63] and Quoilin [64] both investigated and developed dynamic models of ORC systems,
validated their models and used them in case studies dealing with a trigeneration plant and a
CSP plant, respectively. Casella et al. [43] developed a library with components for the dynamic
simulation of ORC systems in the Modelica language, modelled an ORC system and thoroughly
validated the model using experimental data from a 150kW ORC system.
Wei et al. [42] presented a dynamic model, which has been used to develop a 250kW ORC
system using Modelica and Dymola. Two modelling approaches for heat exchangers are compared
in terms of complexity, accuracy and computational speed: the moving boundary and the
discretization technique. The discretization technique was found to be more accurate, but more
complex and more computationally demanding.
Xu et al. [65] developed a transient dynamic model of an ORC for WHR for heavy duty
diesel engine applications. Using experimental data they performed a model parameter identiﬁca-
tion and simulated an ORC system coupled to a 13L HD diesel engine. The model was validated
using experimental data for the system under transient operating conditions and the average
error was within 10% for all variables.
An overview of control strategies used for ORC systems are presented in a recent review
by Zhang et al. [66]. Diﬀerent controller types and strategies are highlighted: the traditional
Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller, the Linear Quadratic Integral (LQI) controller,
Minimum variance control (MVC), Optimal control, Model Predictive Control (MPC), diﬀerent
Compound controllers, Gain-scheduling control, Robust control (RC) and ﬁnally Neural control
(NC). Thus, a number of control strategies are available to the engineer working with control of
ORC systems, and according to Casella [8] there are no established and comprehensive tools
speciﬁcally for dynamic modeling of ORC systems, and there are no consolidated best practices
or ready-made recipes for developing the control strategies of ORC systems. Thus, it is a
challenging research area and to the best of the author’s knowledge, no single control strategy
shows unequivocal and superior results compared to others for WHR on ICEs.
Quoilin et al. [38, 64] modelled a simple ORC system using R245fa as working ﬂuid. They
used their model to simulate and compare the performance of three diﬀerent strategies based

controller. However, the used exhaust gas conditions have signiﬁcantly slower and smaller

for an ORC implemented in a diesel-electric railcar, which is denoted "Optimal control". The
control scheme uses dynamic real-time optimization using a dynamic model and show signiﬁcant
improvements compared to a classical control approach. But, the exhaust gas conditions used

the pump speed with the goal of keeping the vapor quality at the expander inlet under control

able to sustain superheated working ﬂuid at the expander inlet for a real driving cycle using the
very transient exhaust gas as heat source.

are considered. For all three strategies, the superheating could not be sustained at all times, but

CASE STUDY
This chapter presents the speciﬁc case studied in this project. The presented details form the
basis for development of the dynamic ORC model and the controllers.
When designing controllers for an ORC it should be done on a system with a realistic ORC
implemented. In practice an ORC would be designed and optimized speciﬁcally for the available
heat source. Multi-objective optimization and multi-parameter solutions are adviseable to use in
order to achieve the best overall performance of ORC systems, both in terms of thermodynamics
and economics [69]. Especially for WHR on vehicle applications it is important to study real-life
operating proﬁles in order to have a correct idea of the operating point, for which the WHR
must be designed and optimized. This operating point is often the point, where the engine is
operating most of the time during a working cycle [19].
However, performing such an analysis, design and optimization requires time and eﬀort and are
not in the scope of this project. Therefore it is omitted and instead an already optimized design
is adopted from the study performed by Imran et al. [10] and mentioned in section 2.4. Imran et
al. performed a multi-objective optimization study of an ORC unit for WHR on a HDV from
a techno-economic and sizing perspective for seven diﬀerent working ﬂuids. The design of the
ORC unit was based on 1D component models and empirical correlations were used to calculate
mass, volume and cost of the components in the ORC unit.
The optimization in Imran et al. [10] was performed for a static design point based on ex-
perience and transient data for a real heavy duty vehicle shared by a truck manufacturer. The
same transient data has been made available by the truck manufacturer for use in this project.
This makes the optimized design in the study by Imran et al. optimal for use in the present
study. Finally, the system proposed by Imran et al. [10] is simple and consists of a minimum of
components. In relation to both dynamic modelling, development of the controllers and realistic
applications in vehicles a simple system is considered to be an advantage. It simpliﬁes the
problem, and simple conﬁgurations are usually considered more suitable for vehicle applications,
because packaging and weight constraints are very important [19].
The optimized design for the system using pentane is used as case study in the present thesis.
Pentane was chosen among the seven working ﬂuids, because the optimized design showed
the highest net power output. The system, the optimized design and the available transient
experimental data are presented in the following sections.

3. Case study
System description
3.1
System description
The system consists of a HD diesel engine of a long-haul truck and an ORC unit which recovers
waste heat from the exhaust gas of the engine. The engine is a 13L euro 6 diesel engine with 450
hp with turbocharging and Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) as after treatment system. The
ORC unit consists of an evaporator, a radial inﬂow turbine, a condenser, a liquid storage tank
and a positive displacement pump. The system is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Illustration of the engine system and the ORC unit used for WHR of the exhaust gas.
Both evaporator and condenser are brazed plate heat exchangers of the chevron type, selected
because of their compact size. The principle of the whole system is as described in section 2.3.
The exhaust gas from the engine is ﬁrst used to drive the turbocharger, then treated in the
SCR and then used to evaporate the working ﬂuid in the ORC. Large scale ORCs usually use
diﬀerent HEXs for pre-heating, vaporization and superheating the ﬂuid. However, for small
scale ORCs it is more common to see a single HEX for the whole process [38]. This is also
the case for the current study. An alternative location of the ORC evaporator seen in some
studies is upstream of the SCR. However, this introduces an operational constraint on the outlet
temperature of the ORC as the temperature has to be above the minimum operating tempera-
ture of the emission-control system. This is not the case in the conﬁguration of the current system.

==PAGE:36==
3. Case study
System description
The cooling package of the ORC is encircled with dotted lines and consists of three radia-
tors and a fan. The usual two radiators seen in most ICE systems are the charge air cooler
(CAC) and the radiator used for the thermal management of the engine using cooling water.
This radiator is denoted R1 in Figure 3.1. In addition a 3rd radiator (R2) is implemented in
order to allow suﬃcient heat rejection from the ORC condenser. For most vehicle uses the ram
air speed is suﬃcient to supply cooling for the radiators, but during peak conditions such as up
hill, under warms conditions with a large load of the truck, the fan can be turned on to increase
the heat rejection of the cooling package. The valves in the ORC, located after the evaporator,
are mainly used for startup and stop of the system, but can potentially be used to control the
ORC during operation. However, this is not desire-able as it implies throttling losses and as the
heat source is waste heat, it is not desireable to bypass working ﬂuid past the expander, as the
power output decreases. The valves located after the SCR are safety measures to control the
exhaust gas ﬂow rate to the evaporator.
A generator is coupled to the ORC turbine in order to generate electricity.
As described
in chapter 2 the turbine shaft could also be connected to the engine shaft through a gear to add
mechanical power for propulsion. This would result in less power needed from the engine to drive
the engine forward, and would result in a fuel saving. However, this also aﬀects the exhaust
gas conditions of the engine resulting in diﬀerent exhaust gas conditions available for the ORC.
Thus, a combined engine and ORC model is needed to accurately simulate the system. As the
engine itself is not part of the scope of this study, and as the available experimental transient
data for the exhaust gas is based on an engine without WHR, a mechanical coupling between
the ORC turbine and the engine shaft is omitted. Instead a generator is used to convert the
mechanical energy of the ORC turbine into electricity. This energy is assumed to be used by
auxiliaries on the vehicle and to power the vehicle batteries. The electrical network on a long
haul truck consumes approximately 1 kW electrical power on average with peaks up to 4 kW.
This is potentially less than the net power of the ORC. Thus it is recommended to electrify some
vehicle and engine auxiliaries. Especially the big consumers such as the cooling fan and/or the
air-conditioning compressor [36]. In addition turbine expanders mate well with electric generators
[65] compared to displacement expanders, which further justiﬁes the system design incorporating
a radial inﬂow turbine.

DYNAMIC MODELLING AND ANALYSIS
In order to design suitable control strategies the dynamics of the ORC need to be well investigated.
This chapter presents the developed model of the ORC, the validation performed and a thorough
dynamic system analysis performed. Initially the applied methods and procedures are presented.
Following this the results of the validation and the dynamic system analysis are presented. It
is noted that, results and ﬁndings of the dynamic analysis are used for the following controller
design presented in chapter 5.
4.1
Method
4.1.1
Dynamic ORC model
This section presents the developed dynamic model of an ORC for WHR on a HDV and the
used method. The method is explained in detail, including the used software, the mathematical
formulation of the model components, the assumptions and the arguments for the used method
and models.
Introduction
As shown in section 3.2 the exhaust gas of a HDV involves large ﬂuctuations. In addition, small
capacity ORC systems are characterized by faster dynamics than most other thermal systems,
due to the lower volumes of the components [63]. Based on this, dynamic behaviour of the ORC
system is expected. But before a control system can be designed, the dynamic behaviour needs
to be well investigated [79, 80]. Three diﬀerent approaches have been identiﬁed in literature,
which can be used to investigate this dynamic behaviour: experiments, low-order models and
detailed physics-based models.
Experiments are expensive, time-consuming and potentially dangerous if not performed well.
Dynamic modelling oﬀers a good alternative and have several advantages over experiments [63]:
• A simulation is often cheaper and safer than conducting an experiment.
• A physical system can be characterized by a time constant that makes the experiment
in-feasible to conduct. Time constants too small or big for a human to experience or wait
for can make experiments inappropriate. Simulations using a model can overcome this.
• A simulation gives access to all inputs and outputs of the model, whereas not all state
variables or parameters are accessible for an experiment or are costly to obtain.
• A model allows manipulating the system out of the feasible range and to simulate unstable
working conditions.

==PAGE:42==
4. Dynamic modelling and analysis
Dynamic ORC model
Low order models are based on low order approximations and strong modelling assumptions,
which give them a high computational eﬃciency. They are less accurate than detailed physics-
based models and are usually used to simulate the gross dynamics of a system on a long time
scale, e.g. months or years [63].
Detailed dynamic models based on ﬁrst (conservation of energy, mass and momentum) can
accurately simulate the dynamic behaviour of components or plants. Operating points far from
the nominal one can potentially be investigated as the models oﬀer high accuracy, but has lower
computational eﬃciency than a low-order model and is often used for time frames ranging from
seconds to hours [63].
This type of model can be used to evaluate and optimize the time response of a system, to develop
and test control strategies and to tune the applied controllers. In literature the use of dynamic
models for evaluating the transient behaviour of systems and designing control strategies are
often mentioned as strictly necessary [30, 37] and are described by Casella et al. [43] as being
"the foundation of a design process which accounts for dynamic
requirements from the very beginning."
Complex system dynamics are expected from the ORC for WHR on a HDV under the transient
conditions. In order to capture and evaluate these dynamics an accurate and physics-based sys-
tem model is considered to be necessary [8, 37] and are chosen as the method for the current study.
The aim of the model is to be able to simulate and describe the system level behaviour of
the ORC system presented in section 3.1. The system behaviour is a result of the interaction of
the system components. The system model needs to be detailed enough to describe the dynamic
system behaviour, but should not focus on the detailed behaviour of single components in the
system. Such detailed analysis belongs to the realm of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) or
Finite-element models (FEM) [43].
Fundamentals of modelling and simulations
Detailed physics-based models are generally mathematically expressed by a large set of equations,
containing a set of coupled diﬀerential algebraic equations (DAE). Solving a system of DAEs is
challenging [63, 81] and requires a robust solver. Diﬀerent techniques, tools and software are
specialized to cope with these challenges and have been developed during the past years.
Multiple software are available for modelling of thermo-hydraulic systems. Examples used
commercially and academically are: EES® [74], Matlab® & Simulink® [82], OpenModelica® [83],
Dymola® [84], PythonTM [85] and Cycle-Tempo® [86]. Diﬀerences are found both in the type,
the abilities of the software and in the possibilities to implement ﬂuid libraries [46]. According
to Casella and Leva [87] three diﬀerent modelling and simulation approaches are in general
available:
• Specialized simulation packages.
• Generic equation-oriented software.
• Generic object-oriented software.

The ﬁrst type has not been considered as applicable for the current study, because it has no clear

The models are generally not transparent and it is diﬃcult to trace code back to the original
equations [87]. The two other types have a clear separation between the mathematical models
and the integration algorithms [87], making it easier for the user to work with the equations and

Equation-oriented software is, as the name implies, focused on the equations. This type generally
provides an environment for a mathematical description of the model, numerical integration and
optimization options [87]. However, there are often no use of standard connectors or reusable
component libraries.
Object-oriented software uses encapsulation and standard connectors to connect diﬀerent sub-
models. In general ’parent’ models are deﬁned ﬁrst, and then ’child’ models are based on the
’parent’ by adding equations to specify the behaviour of the model [87]. Often built-in libraries
with ﬂuid data and component models are available. As the project is limited in scope and time,
it is found optimal to make use of a user-friendly modeling tool with reusable models, built-in
libraries and ﬂuid data that is easy to access. In this context Modelica and Dymola have been
chosen as the modelling environment.
Modelica is a hybrid modeling language based on equations that support both continuous-
time and event-based aspects, but with an object-oriented approach. Hence it is classiﬁed as
an equation-based object-oriented (EOO) modelling language [87]. A vast majority of studies
in literature dealing with dynamic modelling and control of ORC systems use the Modelica
language as seen in Petr et al. [30], Quoilin et al. [38] and Wei et al. [42]. In addition Modelica
have already been applied successfully within automotive applications [8].
The main concepts of the Modelica language and the Dymola software are explained in the
following. For a comprehensive description of the abilities and advantages of the Modelica
language refer to Casella and Leva [87] and Casella [88].
Modelica and Dymola
The standard of EOO modeling and simulation is Modelica [89] and in recent years it has gained
increasing attention due to its abilities. It allows the description of continuous and discrete
components in a physical way by a set of causal or a-causal equations [63]. In addition several
libraries exist in the Modelica language with functional models of thermo-hydraulic components
and ﬂuid properties, which makes the modelling progress faster and more eﬃcient.
In Modelica the physical models can be written directly in terms of their equations includ-
ing diﬀerential-algebraic equations. The writing order is arbitrary and the form is independent
of the solution strategy [43]. Physical ports of component models are a-causal and connecting
two ports automatically add additional equations, which relate the corresponding variables. For
example these connection equations ensure that pressures are equal and that the mass balance of
ports sums to zero [43].

Fluid properties in Modelica
Thermodynamic and transport properties are essential for a thermodynamic model. Several
ﬂuid property libraries are available and compatible with Modelica and Dymola. Refprop® [91],

are open-source. In comparison both CoolProp and FluidProp are open-source. In addition the
ModelicaFluid library also provides thermodynamic properties for a few ﬂuids.

Numerical considerations and problems of dynamic modelling
When solving a dynamic system using a numerical model in Modelica the process can be divided
into two steps. First an initialization phase and following that a simulation phase. In the
initialization phase a set of consistent values are assigned or calculated for all model variables.
In the following simulation phase a solution trajectory is computed. The resulting set of values
from the initialization (t=0) is used for the simulation process [97].
Dynamic modelling is considered a challenging task as failures can occur [63, 87, 95]. These
failures often arise in the initialization phase, but can also occur during simulation, which is
also mentioned as integration in literature. Getting a meaningful initial state is important for a
successful simulation, but can be diﬃcult due to convergence sensitivity of the initial guesses
and can fail even with state of the art non-linear solvers [98]. Often dedicated steady-state
models are used to generate good guess values for the initialization of the dynamic model as
seen in Xu et al. [37]. Another approach is to set all derivatives to zero during the initialization.
In either case the non-linearities related to the dynamic part of the system are irrelevant, as
these values are set to zero (per deﬁnition of SS) [98]. However, non-linearities still exist for the

steady-state initialization, which can pose a problem for convergence of the model. Examples of
non-linearities can be temperature-enthalpy relationships, energy balance equations or controllers
inﬂuencing the system [98].
Additional challenges of dynamic modelling are chattering and ﬂow reversals [63]. Chatter-
ing can occur when discontinuities are found in the model variables, such as the discontinuity in
the ﬁrst derivative of the density (during the simulation phase) [64]. Chattering is unsteadiness
(oscillations) of the simulation and can lead to very slow simulations or simulation failures [63, 64].
Flow reversal, as the name implies, means that the ﬂow can reverse during the simulation. This
can result in a non-solveable set of equations. Approaches and tools to overcome these problems
exists for Modelica and examples implemented in Modelica can be found in Desideri [63], Quoilin
[64] and Casella et al. [98].
Finally other but minor challenges are: the abstract model structure of Object-Oriented modelling
and the many diﬀerent models with the same function seen in literature and the available libraries.
This means that the user of pre-deﬁned models has to choose among the available models without
in-depth knowledge of their diﬀerences and performance relative to one another.
A limitation of Modelica is its ability to deal with partial diﬀerential equations (PDEs). Modelica
does not support PDEs in their classic form, so before implementation in Modelica, they need
to be reduced to ordinary diﬀerential equations. A method to do so, is the ﬁnite element or
ﬁnite volume method [87]. Finally the combination of several components into a system model
often increase the simulation time considerably, and in some instances to unacceptable values. In
those cases Desideri [63] suggests to simplify parts of the model, for example to use constant
heat transfer coeﬃcients instead of correlation based ones.

Evaporator model
Small capacity ORC systems have lower volumes than large power plants and are therefore
characterized by faster dynamics. The time constants of the heat exchangers are the largest in the
ORC system and are determined by the thermal inertia of the system, the thermal capacitance
of the ﬂuids, the solid walls and the resistance to heat transfer [100]. Because of this, the HEXs
have the biggest impact on the transients of the system [64]. Therefore the heat exchangers in
an ORC model are crucial in order to accurately describe the dynamic behaviour [63].
Diﬀerent modelling methods for heat exchangers are found in literature. The two most commonly
found are the moving boundary (MB) and the ﬁnite volume (FV) methods both under the
assumption that the heat exchanger is described by a 1-dimensional ﬂow in a counter-ﬂow
straight pipe [34, 37, 42, 46, 63, 65]. Both methods are based on mass, energy and momentum
conservation laws over a deﬁned control volume and are able to deal with phase transitions [63].
Other more detailed model approaches are also found in literature, such as the 2D discretization
model adopted by Vaja [101], however they have not been considered relevant for the current
study due to their increased complexity.
In the MB method, the ﬂuid ﬂow is divided into control volumes depending on the amount of
states present in the heat exchanger (liquid, two-phase & vapour). The size of each control
volume varies in time during transient operation as the conditions of the HEX change. The
saturated liquid and saturated vapour boundaries form the boundaries between the control
volumes [42, 63]. Instead the ﬁnite volume method discretizes the heat exchanger in an amount
of equally sized control volumes. These volumes remain constant in size during the simulation
and energy, mass and momentum balances are applied for each control volume.
Both methods are found suitable for use in the current study, and have diﬀerent advantages.
Bendapudi et al. [102] performed a comparison of the two methods against experimental data of
a shell and tube HEX in a 300kW centrifugal chiller in transient conditions. The MB method was
found to be 3 times faster than the FV in terms of computation speed, but with an over-prediction
of the void fraction because of the assumption of homogeneous ﬂow in the two-phase region, thus
resulting in less accuracy. Similar results were found in studies by Wei et al. [42] and Desideri
[63]. Both studies conclude the FV method to be slower, but with higher accuracy and more
robustness. The simulation speed of the MB method makes it interesting for control purposes,
especially in real-time applications, where speed is a crucial element [65].
In this study the FV method has been preferred over the MB method, because robustness
and accuracy are valued higher and early stage simulations with a FV model showed acceptable
simulation speed. In addition the diﬀerent controllers developed and applied in this study are

• Adiabatic - No heat losses to the environment.
• The cross section area A is uniform along the tube.
• The ﬂuid velocity is uniform in the cross-section and turbulent friction is always assumed.
• Thermal diﬀusion eﬀects are neglected.
• Kinetic energy, gravitational forces and radiation heat transfer are neglected.
• Axial heat conduction is neglected in the ﬂow direction for both the wall and ﬂuid.
• The wall cross section area has a uniform temperature.

Assumptions

Miscellaneous
Other components exists in a physical ORC, which should be considered during the modelling
process, but have less importance for the overall model than the previously described components.
Junctions, piping & lubrication
Losses in junctions and piping can be estimated using correlations from literature [46], however
for simplicity this is neglected in the current study, unless for a heat loss between the evaporator
and expander implemented in the validation process described in chapter 4.1.2. In addition
compressible pipe volumes aﬀecting the transients of the systems are neglected.
Lubrication of the turbo-machinery is neglected and is not considered to have a signiﬁcant
impact on the energy conversion process.
Generator
The electrical generator includes mechanical rotating parts with inertia, that potentially could
aﬀect the transients of the turbine and thus the ORC. However, as a constant frequency of the
turbine is assumed, this has negligible eﬀect. Thus the generator is not included in the dynamic
model, but for the net power output a constant eﬃciency ηel is applied
˙Wel = ˙Wturb · ηel
(4.28)
Storage tank
As depicted in ﬁgure 3.1 the ORC system includes a storage tank also denoted as a liquid receiver.
This component prevents uncondensed vapour from entering the working ﬂuid pump [29] and
absorbs liquid level ﬂuctuations of the evaporator [64].
Assuming a homogeneous pressure in the entire volume of the receiver and with a suﬃciently
high volume, the liquid receiver will result in stable inlet conditions for the pump. However,
due to the simple condenser model with a constant and ﬁxed outlet state, no transients will
be present for the liquid receiver in the dynamic model and thus it is redundant to model it.
Therefore it is omitted from the model. Alternatively a mass and energy balance could be used
to model it as seen in Ziviani et al. [46], Desideri [63] or Quoilin [64].
Fluid sources and sinks
In Modelica ﬂuid ﬂow, which is not operating in a loop, need a source and a sink to generate
and remove ﬂuid. This is the case for the exhaust gas. To simulate this ﬂuid ﬂow, source and
sink models are implemented from the ThermoPower library. These models allow specifying
properties of a ﬂuid as pressure, temperature and mass ﬂow rate.

Validation of the dynamic model
4.1.2
Validation of the dynamic model
This section describes the validation of the dynamic ORC model. The validation is based on a
comparison with a set of experimental data acquired from a study performed by Usman et al.
[11]. General considerations about model validation, the experiments conducted, the used data,
adaptions to the dynamic model and the validation procedure are described in the following.
Introduction
The Validation or the "Veriﬁcation of the model" as it is called by Desideri [63] is of paramount
importance and a critical aspect in modelling and simulations [43, 46, 63]. Even though the
dynamic model is based on previously built and validated libraries, a validation is still necessary.
This is because the dynamic model developed in this project is using models from diﬀerent
libraries and simpliﬁcations such as the condenser model.
The model validation is done in order to build conﬁdence in using the developed dynamic
model and to ensure the models eﬀectiveness in predicting the physical phenomenon’s of the real
system being studied [63]. It can be performed in three ways according to Leva and Maﬀezzoni
[99]:
• Testing intrinsic correctness of the model.
• Model validation versus design data.
• Model validation by comparison with experimental tests. This can be for both steady state
and transient conditions.
Validation with experimental data is considered to be the best approach if possible. This approach
is taken in this thesis. However experiments often supply an incomplete set of data [110]. So
it can be necessary to combine information from experimental measurements with information
from design data of the system being studied [99]. This is also the case for this thesis, and is
further explained in subsection 4.1.2. However no systematic method is available to integrate all
available information, making the validation not so straight-forward [99].
The experimental data used for validation originates from a study performed by Usman et al.
[11]. The data is from an experimental test rig of a 1kW ORC system. The experiment used for
the validation involves the system being subject to a step increase in load, while being controlled
in a closed-loop by a PID controller. Step inputs are considered to be appropriate for validation,
because the plant dynamics are suﬃciently excited [99]. However, the shape of the system’s
transient response is determined by the dynamics of each component, which potentially could
have been corrupted by the response of the controller [63, 79, 99]. Thus the shape of the response
is in fact aﬀected by the controller action. For this reason open-loop validation is preferable to
closed-loop validation.

ically stable and tends to move away from its equilibrium point [99]. In addition most studies in
literature involving validation of dynamic ORCs have either incomplete sets of data1, or present
only normalized values due to conﬁdentiality reasons [34, 42, 43, 44, 65]. Thus making them

4. Dynamic modelling and analysis
Validation of the dynamic model
Validation procedure
Figure 4.8 illustrates the concept used for validation of the dynamic model. The idea is to
simulate the ORC system using the developed dynamic model, with the exact same boundary
conditions and control inputs as reported in the experiment. By comparing the simulation results,
with the experimental measurements the model can then be either validated or rejected in case
it does not simulate the real system satisfyingly. This is done for the whole system at once, and
thus a system level validation is performed.
As the ORC system is subjected to a step increase in load, it would be obvious to apply
the same step increase of 100W to the dynamic model as in the experiment, and then see if the
system behaves as observed in the experiment. However this approach cannot be used due to
the following reasons.
1. The experimental data is for a closed-loop.
2. The exact power output of the ORC is unknown.
Point 1 can be explained further: for closed loop the controller is aﬀecting the cycle dynamics
through its input. If a step input of 100W was given to the dynamic model, a similar controller
as in the experiment would be needed to control the cycle.
Point 2 can be explained further as: power output of the ORC is not 60W before the step increase
is applied. Based on the performed measurements the SS power output before the transient
event is circa 400kW. The diﬀerence between this output and the 60W applied to the light bulbs
is used to charge the battery illustrated in Figure 4.6. Thus the exact power output is unknown
and cannot be imposed on the dynamic model. Therefore, a diﬀerent approach is taken.
The dynamic model is simulated under the same conditions and inputs as in the experiment,
except from imposing the expander load. The following are given as inputs to the dynamic model:
• Component speciﬁcations
• Boundary conditions
• Expander rotational speed
• Pump mass ﬂow rate
The simulation results are then compared with the experimental results and evaluated. The
validation procedure is illustrated in Figure 4.8.

4. Dynamic modelling and analysis
Dynamic system analysis
4.1.3
Dynamic system analysis
The control system plays an important role for the operation of an ORC system. Minor diﬀerences
between two comparable systems can lead to completely diﬀerent control strategies [8]. Thus, in
order to develop an appropriate control strategy it is important to understand the conﬁguration
and dynamics of the speciﬁc system under study.
This section identiﬁes the available process and control variables and the disturbances of the
ORC. This is followed by an analysis, which investigates the dynamic behaviour of the system.
Findings and results are information and data used in the controller design.
System analysis
In order to develop a control strategy it is necessary to identify the possible control variables
(CVs), process variables (PVs) and disturbances of the system. A control variable is a manipulated
variable and a process variable is the variable, which is sought to be controlled. The identiﬁed
variables of the ORC system are presented and explained in the following.
Disturbances
The following disturb the ORC system and aﬀect its performance:
• Exhaust gas mass ﬂow rate and temperature.
• Cooling water mass ﬂow rate, pressure and temperature.
The exhaust gas conditions aﬀect the heat available for the ORC evaporator and ﬂuctuations
will result in transient behaviour of the ORC. In principle the cooling package of the engine is
subject to the driving conditions and does not oﬀer constant cooling for the ORC condenser.
However the cooling available is assumed to be adequate and therefore constant and disturbances
to the condenser are neglected.
Process variables
Based on the control objectives the possible process variables are identiﬁed:
• Superheating temperature.
• Evaporation pressure/temperature.
• Turbine inlet temperature.
The primary objective is to operate the system safely and therefore both the evaporation pressure,
the turbine inlet temperature and the superheating temperature are relevant variables. The
evaporating temperature is relevant in order to optimize the power output of the system. Based
on studies found in literature on control of ORCs the superheating temperature is found to be
the most common process variable [34, 38, 44]. This variable is more sensitive to disturbances
and is more easily excited to undesired values than the evaporation pressure and the turbine inlet
temperature. In addition: controlling the superheating temperature indirectly means controlling
the two other variables as given by eq. (4.29).

4. Dynamic modelling and analysis
Dynamic system analysis
Control variables
The available control variables of the ORC system are:
• Turbine inlet and by-pass valve openings.
• Exhaust gas by-pass valve opening.
• Pump speed.
In principle the turbine rotational speed can be also used as a control variable for systems with
a volumetric expander [38]. However, the rotational speed of a radial inﬂow turbine has a small
eﬀect on the operating conditions and is not a realistic control variable to use [67].
As depicted in Figure 3.1 a by-pass valve is present at the inlet of the hot side of the evaporator.
The valve allows to by-pass exhaust gas and therefore to reduce the heat input to the ORC,
which can help to limit the pressure in the ORC [67]. However in relation to control strategies it
is of little interest, as the purpose of the ORC is to recover as much heat as possible.
A by-pass valve is also present in the ORC loop, which allows to by-pass working ﬂuid past
the turbine. In nominal mode this valve is closed and all the working ﬂuid is expanded in the
turbine, in order to produce as much power as possible. Opening the by-pass valve will result in
a decrease of power output and lower the conversion eﬃciency of the ORC. In relation to optimal
control of the ORC this valve should always be kept closed. Nevertheless, it can be used as a
safety measure to by-pass working ﬂuid in case a positive superheating temperature cannot be
sustained. In addition it aﬀects the evaporating pressure in the ORC [37].
The pump rotational speed is the only realistic CV to use. This CV is most frequently used
in studies found in literature [34, 37, 38, 44, 67, 68, 72]. The pump speed imposes the mass
ﬂow rate of working ﬂuid in the ORC. Changes in mass ﬂow rate results in changes in the heat
transfer in the ORC and thereby the ﬂuid outlet conditions (superheating temperature). In
addition the evaporation pressure is aﬀected due to the change in heat transfer and the change
in mass ﬂow rate through the turbine.
Variables in the current study
Summarizing the described variables the following are considered applicable in the current study.
The disturbances of the ORC stem from the changes in exhaust gas conditions. The study is
focused on the pump speed as the control variable, as the remaining possibilities are sub-optimal
CVs. With one control variable a single process variable can be controlled. The superheating
temperature is chosen as the process variable, due to its more sensitive nature. In addition
controlling the superheating temperature indirectly controls the evaporation pressure and the
turbine inlet temperature.
Quoilin [64] found that two control variables are needed to take best proﬁt of a variable
heat source, as this allows both the superheating and the evaporation pressure to be controlled.
However, this is not possible in the current study unless the system is modiﬁed.

Dynamic system analysis
Dynamic system behaviour
In order to understand the system dynamic behaviour and the eﬀect of the control variable
and disturbances on the ORC system an analysis of the system transient behaviour have been
performed using the model described in section 4.1.1.
Initially the stability of the system have been theoretically analyzed. Following that a number of
open-loop step tests have been performed. The tests are performed by running simulations with
the ORC model without a controller applied. The open-loop step tests provide insight into the
dynamics of the system and give information for the control design phase. Outcomes of such
tests are, for example, the relationships between superheating, evaporating temperature and the
power output with the control variable and with the disturbances [44].
Open-loop step tests are preferred for such an analysis for two reasons: Open-loop tests give
system responses entirely determined by the dynamic storage phenomena of the system [43].
Secondly, a step excitation is the worst-case scenario for a system, because it applies the fastest
possible change to the system and covers the whole frequency spectrum [63]. Details and results
for the tests performed are presented in section 4.2.2.
In addition a parametric study of the eﬀect of the internal volume and mass of metal of
the evaporator have been performed. These two parameters are given to the dynamic model as
inputs and potentially have a large eﬀect of the transients.

– The evaporation temperature reacts faster and is less sensitive to disturbances and

– The time constants of the system, for changes in exhaust gas ﬂow rate and temperature,

result in a faster system response to both changes in the pump speed and in exhaust mass

The information gathered is valuable in relation to control design. The results of the step tests
are directly used to design a feed-forward controller, which is explained in section 5.1.2. However,
three characteristics of the system are found to be potential problems for the controllability of
the system:
1. First of all, the slower eﬀect on the superheating from changes in the pump speed than
from changes in exhaust conditions can result in the pump not being able to sustain the
superheat under rapid and large changes in the exhaust gas conditions.
2. Secondly, the initial superheating response to changes in the pump speed shows an opposite
trend than the long-term trend (i.e. the initial decrease followed by an increase). In
the case of rapid changes in exhaust gas conditions, which would drive the superheating
to its boundary limits (e.g. 0°C), a natural controller response would be to change the
pump speed (e.g. decrease it), which should make the superheating change in the opposite
direction (e.g. increase it). However, the small initial response of changing the pump speed
would enlarge the undesired eﬀect of the superheating (decrease it more), before the desired
eﬀect on the superheating would take over (increase it). This could result in undesired
superheating temperatures for limited time periods.
3. Finally the system is found to be more sensitive to changes, when the exhaust mass ﬂow
rate is low. This makes the control task more challenging.

5
CONTROL STRATEGIES
This chapter presents the developed control strategies. Initially the applied methods are explained,
which starts with identiﬁcation of the speciﬁc control objectives. Following this the control
strategies are presented and explained along with the parameter tuning and optimization
procedure applied. Finally results for simulation with the diﬀerent control strategies are presented,
explained and evaluated.
5.1
Method
5.1.1
Control objectives
In general the control objectives are the same for all ORC systems [8, 44]: to operate the system
safely and achieve maximum power output or conversion eﬃciency. These objectives also applies
to the this study. In particular the objectives are:
1. Run the system safely at all times.
a. Keep working ﬂuid superheated into the turbine.
b. Keep working ﬂuid pressure lower than the critical pressure.
c. Keep the working ﬂuid temperature lower than the auto-ignition temperature and/or
the decomposition temperature.
2. Achieve optimal WHR.
a. Maximize the net power output of the ORC.
Safe operation is required to ensure the safety of the vehicle, the driver and the ORC components.
Even though pentane is a dry ﬂuid, the formation of droplets in the turbine could occur, if a
positive superheating is not maintained at the turbine inlet. The formation of droplets can
damage the turbine and should be avoided [38, 44, 64, 112]. Therefore control objective 1a. is to
ensure a positive superheating into the turbine. Refer to equation (4.29) for the deﬁnition of the
superheating temperature.
The critical pressure sets an upper limit on the pressure in the evaporator of the ORC in
order to avoid a transcritical cycle. The goal of objective 2a. is to keep the system pressure lower
than 0.9Pcrit = 30.6 bar. Refer to Table 3.3 for Pcrit.
For ﬂuid overheating temperatures an organic ﬂuid will start to decompose or auto-ignite
[8]. For pentane the decomposition temperature is approximately 300°C, whereas the eﬀect of
pressure on thermal stability is found to be negligible [113]. Decomposition is the breakage of
chemical bonds between the ﬂuid molecules and the formation of smaller compounds, which can
react and create other hydrocarbons [41]. This changes the transport and physical properties
of the working ﬂuid and can have a signiﬁcant negative eﬀect on the system performance. In
76
==PAGE:91==

Controller design
addition it can result in safety hazards.
The auto-ignition temperature is approximately 260°C at atmospheric pressure. As this is lower
than the decomposition temperature, this value is chosen as an upper safety boundary for the
ﬂuid temperature. The maximum temperature naturally occurs at the evaporator outlet/turbine
inlet.
Assuming that the applied control strategy is able to operate the system safely the second
priority is to maximize the net energy output of the ORC for a driving cycle. This could imply a
control strategy aiming at keeping the superheating low [67] and following an optimal evaporating
temperature as seen in Quoilin [64].
Comparing with other studies, that deal with ORC WHR systems, it is found that the ob-
jectives are in line with literature dealing with WHR using an ORC on HDVs as Seitz et al. [34],
Hernandez et al. [44] and Peralez et al. [67].
5.1.2
Controller design
In order to control the superheating temperature using the pump speed a number of diﬀerent
controllers have been investigated, designed, tuned, optimized and ﬁnally implemented in the
ORC model to test their performance.
In general the control design consists of applying some design rules to determine the con-
trol parameters, such as the gains of a PI controller.
After this, the closed loop dynamic
performance of the system is evaluated by running a set tests with pre-determined inputs and
computing relevant characteristics of the transient response [108].
Control strategies
A number of diﬀerent control strategies have been proposed, analyzed and investigated commer-
cially and in academia.
Two commonly applied control strategies for Rankine cycles are the sliding pressure and constant
pressure operation strategies. The sliding pressure operation means that the pressure in the
evaporator is allowed to slide, if the heat input to the evaporator is changed [114]. The constant
pressure operation on the other hand implies that the evaporator pressure is kept constant by
controlling the inlet and the bypass valve of the turbine [114]. Compared to sliding pressure,
the constant pressure operation results in a decrease of eﬃciency at part loads, but gives more
controllability of the plant [114].
Two common control strategies for ORC systems are denoted FCL and FTE, which stands
for Following Connected Load and Following Thermal Energy, respectively [66]. In the FCL
strategy the mass ﬂow rate or the temperature of the heat source is a controlled variable and is
used to make the power output of the ORC match a certain load requirement. The FTE strategy
follows the variations of the heat source and seeks to maximize the energy conversion eﬃciency
of the ORC. In the FTE strategy the expander speed is used as a control variable according to
Zhang et al. [66].
77
==PAGE:92==
5. Control strategies
Controller design
To maximize the conversion eﬃciency two common approaches are usually taken [38]. The
ﬁrst is to deﬁne a constant evaporating and superheating temperature as control setpoints.
However, it is not possible to know a priori, which evaporating temperature is optimal for the
process. The second approach is to work with a varying evaporating setpoint temperature. The
setpoint is varied by some function, which relates the optimal evaporating temperature to the heat
source conditions and the current ORC condition [38]. Both of these approaches require two con-
trol variables to manipulate the two process variables: superheating and evaporating temperature.
In this study the general control strategy can best be described as sliding pressure.
The
inlet and by-pass valves are, as a starting point, always kept open, and therefore the pressure in
the evaporator slides, as the heat source varies. With only one control variable the safety of the
system is prioritized over the eﬃciency, however by aiming at a low superheating temperature,
the net power output is to some extent optimized [38, 44] and therefore the strategy can also be
classiﬁed as FTE.
Development of controllers
Development of the controllers are performed in the time-domain, where values of the state
variables are represented as functions of time, because the system is non-linear. Frequency
domain techniques are limited to linear systems and are therefore not applicable [115].
Several diﬀerent controllers have been developed with the purpose of controlling the pump
speed to ensure the superheating temperature into the turbine. These are described in the
following, and common for them all are the assumption of perfect actuators and sensors, which
means:
• Perfect control of the pump. No disturbance in the actuator process is present.
• No oﬀsets or noise are present in the measurements or signals related to the control system.

Feed forward control
Owing to the very transient behaviour of the exhaust gas and the dynamic behaviour of the
system under changes in exhaust gas conditions, feedback controllers such as PI- and PID could
potentially have problems rejecting the disturbances and keeping the superheating at setpoint.
PI- and PID controllers only take action when the PV have already been moved from the setpoint.
In that case the stable operation of the system is already disrupted. Thus, the feedback controller
starts its corrective action too late and can at best only minimize the disturbance. Early results
conﬁrmed this problem, and therefore a feed-forward (FF) controller have been investigated and
implemented.
FF improves disturbance rejection by measuring the disturbance D(t), use the information
to predict the impact on the PV and calculate a preemptive control action, which should coun-
teract the impact of the disturbance on the PV [118]. The control scheme is illustrated in Figure
5.4. Two diﬀerent FF controllers have been developed in the current study: a static FF controller
and a dynamic. Both controllers are based on a computational element FF, which is a function
of the disturbance and is used to calculate the FF control signal.

Tuning and optimization of controller parameters
The PI- and the PID controllers are initially tuned using the Nichols-Ziegler tuning rules and
afterwards their performance are improved by an optimization algorithm.
The Nichols-Ziegler tuning rules provide a straightforward and heuristic1 approach to tun-
ing the PI- and PID parameters. The tuning rules date all the way back to 1942 [119] and
results in a controller, which gives fast disturbance rejection [120]. The tuning rules cover two
approaches. The open-loop method and the closed-loop method [111, 117, 120, 121]. Both
approaches have been used, but the open-loop method gave too aggressive gains. Therefore only
results from the closed-loop have been used.
The closed loop approach consists of operating the system in closed-loop at SS with the PI/PID
controller applied and applying a step change in the setpoint of the PV. The integral and
derivative term of the controller are turned oﬀand the proportional gain is set to a low value.
Gradually the proportional gain is increased by trial-and-error until the response in the PV shows
true oscillations with a constant amplitude. The gain resulting in these oscillations is the critical
gain Ku, which is noted down along with the period of the oscillations Tu. Based on these two
numbers the PI- and PID parameters are calculated using the Ziegler-Nichols tuning rules. For
the system this procedure was performed at nominal conditions with a controller setpoint of 12°C

Rejection of a single volatile disturbance
The results of the simulations with the transient exhaust gas data do not point out one control
strategy to be superior to the others. However, this is found to be largely related to the con-
trollability of the system. The superheating is observed to decrease to 0°C in the same time
periods independent of the control strategy. This is highly related to the available heat to the
ORC. At times t=75s, 190s and 260s the superheating decreases to 0°C. This is exactly when the
mass ﬂow rate of exhaust gas decreases to low values. Despite the high decreases of pump speed
at these times (especially for the PID+FF static), the superheating still cannot be preserved.
Thus the eﬀect of the pump speed changes is not fast enough. This tendency has been further
investigated as explained in the following section.
5.2.2
Rejection of a single volatile disturbance
Owing to the unsatisfactory performance of the controllers it is interesting to investigate if
the lack of preservation of superheated working ﬂuid at evaporator outlet is due to control
strategies, poor tuning or the time constants of the system. The found time constants show that
changes in pump speed have a slower eﬀect on the PV, than changes in exhaust conditions. Thus
potentially, it is impossible for a control strategy with only the pump speed as CV to sustain the
superheating during the transient cycle. This is investigated by identifying a volatile disturbance
from the transient exhaust gas data and simulating the ORC with this disturbance as input.
This disturbance is shown in Figure 5.14a and is located at time t = 1800s in the data.
The ORC system is initially at SS and with a superheating temperature of 30°C. At time
t = 10s a steep decrease in the mass ﬂow rate and temperature of exhaust gas is experienced.
Simulations show that, if left unchanged, the superheating temperature will drop to 0°C in few
seconds. To see if the controller would be able to reject the disturbance and keep the superheating
above 0°C a change in the pump speed to a few RPM is additionally given as input. This very
aggressive pump speed change, should result in a signiﬁcant increase in superheating temperature
and thereby counteract the disturbance. The ORC response and pump speed change are shown
in Figure 5.14b.
As seen in Figure 5.14b the heat input to the ORC decreases signiﬁcantly from 105kW to
12kW within 9 seconds. This causes the evaporator outlet temperature Teva,o to decrease sharply
and faster than the evaporation temperature Tsat. This gives a sharp decrease in superheating
temperature, which levels oﬀfor a few seconds due to the pump speed change, but then continues
to decrease and reaches 0°C. After 80 seconds the superheating temperature becomes positive
again, due to the eﬀect of the decrease in pump speed, and continues to rise and gives an
overshoot as expected. This simulation shows that even for a very aggressive pump speed change,
it is not possible to maintain a positive superheating for some changes in the exhaust gas. The
disturbance is too great and the eﬀect of pump speed changes propagates too slowly through the
system and reaching a superheating temperature of 0°C is unavoidable.
Therefore, based on the simulation results, it is concluded that even with an optimal PID-
based controller implemented, it would be impossible to sustain the superheating at all times
due to the dynamic behaviour of the system. In particular due to the higher time constants of
pump speed changes than the time constants of changes in the exhaust gas conditions.

Summary of results
The results and ﬁndings are summarized to the following:
• Simulations show that, the main challenge in control design for the ORC comes from
the superheating temperature due to its non-linear characteristics and the large and fast
ﬂuctuations in exhaust gas conditions.
• The investigated controllers are all able to reject small changes in the exhaust gas conditions.
However, they perform diﬀerently and can be ranked in the following order of decreasing
performance: PID with dynamic FF, PID with static FF, PID and ﬁnally PI.
For a step decrease in exhaust mass ﬂow rate of 20%, the best disturbance rejection results
in a settling time of 60s and 5°C deviation from the setpoint.
• For simulations with real transient exhaust gas data the maximum allowable temperature
and pressure in the ORC system are not violated for all developed controllers. However,
none of the controllers are able to sustain the superheat at turbine inlet. This is despite
increasing the setpoint and tuning the gains to improve performance.
• The simulations with real transient data do not point out one control strategy to be
superior. The superheating decreases to 0°C in the same time periods for all strategies and
the overall trajectory of the superheating is the same for all responses, despite signiﬁcant
diﬀerences in the control signals from the controllers.
• Simulations suggest that the superheating temperature can be sustained for 87% of the
time and increasing the eﬃciency of the vehicle energy system by 3.4 percentage points.
• For a single volatile but realistic disturbance of the ORC, the pump is unable to sustain
the superheating even for a very aggressive change in speed. The eﬀect of changes in pump
speed simply propagates too slowly through the system compared to the eﬀect of changes
in exhaust gas conditions.
Based on the results and ﬁndings, the following are concluded
The control objectives deﬁned in section 5.1.1 cannot be fulﬁlled using the developed controllers.
The challenge lies in keeping the working ﬂuid superheated into the turbine. A FF element in
the controller signiﬁcantly improves the control performance for minor disturbances, whereas
for large disturbances, the diﬀerence in performance of the diﬀerent strategies are found to be
negligible. This is because the eﬀect on the superheating is slower for changes in the pump speed,
than for changes in the exhaust gas mass ﬂow rate and temperature. Thus the system dynamics
give very poor controllability, and using only the pump rotational speed as control variable is
inadequate to sustain the superheating.
Despite the unsatisfactory performance of the controllers, the outcomes of the study still provide
use-full information for future studies and research.

DISCUSSION
This chapter presents a discussion of the results obtained and put them into a larger perspective. In
addition recommendations for further work are given. The chapter is organized such that initially
the validation is treated, followed by the dynamic model and the dynamic system behaviour.
Finally the developed controllers and their performance of the controllers are discussed.
6.1
Validation
Validation of dynamic ORC models have been performed before in the academic world and with
high levels of success. The purpose of the validation in the current project is not to present
a dynamic ORC model with state of the art accuracy, but to ensure a model with acceptable
accuracy for the purpose of controller design and tuning. However, it is still interesting to
compare the validation results in the current study with results from literature. Papers have been
found which presents smaller deviations between experimental measurements and simulations for
transient events. However, compared with the results observed in literature, the results in the
current study are deemed good and acceptable. Table 6.1 presents an overview of the validation
process and results obtained from 4 diﬀerent studies including this project. These are brieﬂy
explained in the following.
In the study by Xu et al. [65] a physics based dynamic model of an ORC for WHR on a
13L HD diesel engine was validated using experimental data. For two diﬀerent transient events all
simulated variables were found to be within 10% of the experimental measurements and in most
cases below 5%. The two events was a step change of 20K of the turbine upstream temperature
and the engine power output from 1000Nm to 1250Nm, respectively. Both experiments were
conducted in closed-loop using a PID controller. The system utilized two evaporators. One
for EGR WHR and one for exhaust WHR. In addition the by-pass valves were modelled, thus
making their model a bit more complex than the one in the current study. However the model
development included identiﬁcation of empirical parameters such as isentropic eﬃciency of
expander, pump coeﬃcients from manufacturer data and so called eﬃciency multipliers for the
heat exchangers, thus allowing a precise tuning of the model to the speciﬁc experiment.
Wei et al. [42] validated their dynamic model using experimental data for a pilot 100 kW
ORC and reports their biggest relative error to be less than 4 % when comparing the simulations
using both the MB heat exchanger model and the FV model between the simulations and the
experimental data. Their system is similar in structure (components etc.) as in the current study
and based on the Modelica language and the Dymola software. As for the study by Xu et al. the
models for the pump and expander were based directly on performance data from the manufac-
turers. The transient event imposed on the system is not reported in the paper and evaluating
the reported results, the system seems to be operating at steady state. But measured changes in

6.2
Dynamic model and transient system behaviour
Based on the validation results the dynamic behaviour of the ORC model is found to be reliable.
In addition the results of the stair-case tests are found to be similar with a study by Hernandez
et al. [44]. Hernandez et al. experimentally performed similar tests for an 11kWe ORC with
R245fa as working ﬂuid and found similar trends for all investigated variables. Hence the general
dynamic behaviour of the system is deemed to be trustworthy.
However, the dynamic system behaviour should still be viewed critically. A very important
ﬁnding of the open-loop step tests, described in section 4.1.3, is the time constants of the
system. Changes in the pump speed are found to aﬀect the superheating temperature slower
than changes in the exhaust gas conditions. This results in a low controllability of the system.
Other studies report low control-ability [34, 68], but to the best of the author’s knowledge no
study investigates the relationship between time-constants for the CV and time-constants for the
transient disturbances. This is especially interesting for such transient conditions as presented in
this study, and it is a topic that could be further investigated. The investigation of the inﬂuence
of evaporator metal mass and internal volume presented in section 4.2.2 is a brief study of some
of the factors that inﬂuence this relationship.
The results of the investigation suggests that decreasing the evaporator volume could im-
prove controllability. However an assumption of the investigation were that, when changing the
evaporator internal volume all other parameters of the evaporator were kept constant. This is
obviously an assumption that does not hold true in real applications due to the geometry of a
heat exchanger. This investigation could be further improved by taking geometrical and heat
transfer relationships into consideration, i.e. when changing the evaporator volume, one should
modify the remaining input parameters of the evaporator accordingly.

Future work and suggested improvements
In general the dynamic ORC model developed is based on existing and tested library models and
literature have been widely consolidated when choosing a model or making an assumption. An
example of this is the assumption of the simple ﬁxed pressure condenser model being adequate
to describe the condenser in a real system. However, several simpliﬁcations and assumptions are
made for the model components and further development could made to improve these.
A natural addition to the ORC models is to implement a more detailed condenser and in-
clude it in the validation. By doing so, the liquid receiver located between the condenser and the
pump should also be modelled and implemented. Available models can be found in literature, but
it would possibly not aﬀect the results of the study signiﬁcantly as plenty of cooling is assumed
to be available. However, it would be a natural improvement and add credibility to the study.
Stodola’s law is originally intended for multi-stage turbines, which is normally eight or more
stages [8, 114]. Thus, using it to describe a single-stage radial inﬂow turbine is an approximation
and the eﬀect of this could be investigated. In addition, other turbine models from literature
could be implemented in the ORC and the system behaviour could be compared for diﬀerent
models.

6. Discussion
Control strategies
6.3
Control strategies
The simulation results showing the controller performance are the main results and also the most
interesting of this study. The study has focused on PI/PID-based controllers because of their
widespread use and low computational requirements, which would allow the controller to be
implemented on a standard ECU, which is not the case for more complex controllers. However,
the results of the study show that, the applied controllers were not able to sustain superheating
at the turbine inlet at all times under the transient exhaust gas conditions. This highlights the
challenge of controlling a mini-scale ORC for WHR from HDVs. In addition the results suggest,
that a control strategy using only the pump rotational speed as CV and a PID-based controller
is insuﬃcient to control the ORC safely, because of the very transient exhaust gas conditions
and high time constants. Thus, it has not been possible to fulﬁll the objective of developing a
PID-based control strategy, which could successfully operate the ORC safely and eﬃciently.
However, to the best of the authors knowledge, no study presents a simple PID-based con-
trol strategy, which is able to successfully sustain the superheating of an ORC under as transient
heat source conditions as the one used in this thesis. A possible reason for this is that, it is such a
challenging control problem, which is also an observation seen in literature Peralez et al. [67], Seitz
et al. [34], Feru et al. [68]. Comparing the results of the current study with results in litera-
ture (see section 2.4 for description of the the studies) the complexity of this challenge is conﬁrmed:
Quoilin [64] succesfully operated a mini-scale ORC under transient heat source conditions
using two PI-controllers and the pump and expander rotational speed as CVs and tuning parame-
ters manually. But the heat source conditions were much less transient, than the conditions used
in the present thesis. The results of the current study showed that, the developed controllers are
able to sustain the superheating under small disturbances in the exhaust gas. Thus these results
are comparable with the ones found by Quoilin [64]. However, the time constants of the system
in [64] are reportedly much lower. For a step decrease in the pump speed of 20% the system
reached SS in 120s. For the ORC in the current study the settling time is 820s. Thus the ORC
in Quoilin [64] reacts much faster to changes in the pump speed. This is highly related to the
size of evaporator used. The evaporator in Quoilin [64] had an internal volume of 6 liters and
a metal mass of 13kg. The evaporator in this study has an internal volume of 103 liters and a
mass of 32kg. The analysis of the eﬀect of evaporator internal volume on the system transients
showed that a lower volume results in a faster system. Taking the size of the evaporators into
consideration, these diﬀerences in settling time seems reasonable.
Another study which successfully maintains the vapour state at the expander inlet is Peralez et al.
[67]. However, like with Quoilin [64] the ﬂuctuations in heat source temperature and ﬂow rate
are much smaller than in the current study. Thus controlling the ORC is easier. Simulating the
ORC system from the current study under the transient conditions from Peralez et al. [67] this
is evident. Peralez et al. similarly attempted to control an ORC using only the pump rotational
speed as CV. Figure 6.1a shows the exhaust gas disturbances from Peralez et al. [67] normalized

Both Seitz et al. [34] and Feru et al. [68] presented studies with much more transient heat
source conditions than Quoilin [64] and Peralez et al. [67]. These conditions are more comparable
with the exhaust gas data used in this study. However, both studies show results where the
superheating could not be sustained due to the vary ﬂuctuating exhaust gas conditions. This is
in accordance with the ﬁndings of this study.
Seitz et al. [34] found the control task to be extra challenging at low engine loads. This observa-
tion is supported by one of the ﬁndings of this study. The stair-case test showed a more sensitive
system for low mass ﬂow rates, and in general the lack of superheating in the simulation results
appeared for low exhaust ﬂow rates.
For the PI, PID and PID+FF controllers constant gains of the feedback controller are used.
The system non-linearities and its higher sensitivity to changes calls for gain-scheduling to be
implemented. Gain-scheduling uses a scheduling variable to determine what operating range the
system is in and enable the appropriate controller (.e.g. set the PID parameters accordingly).
This addition is expected to improve the performance compared to the a PI and PID controller
without gain-scheduling. Possible future work could be to implement gain-scheduling with a PID
controller to test the improvement in performance such a controller could induce. Nevertheless,
adding gain-scheduling might not be suﬃcient. Initial results for a gain-scheduling PID controller
conﬁrms this. A simple gain-scheduling scheme has been developed, which uses the exhaust
mass ﬂow rate as scheduling variable to set the PID parameters. Initial results show a similar
performance as the PID with dynamic FF. In order to improve this gain-scheduling controller,
the exhaust gas temperature and the ORC conditions should be taken into account, but the
results show that the superheating reaches 0°C in the same periods as the other controllers.
Details can be found in appendix G.3. Seitz et al. [34] used gain-scheduling in combination with
FF and a linear quadratic regulator (LQR) and still could not sustain the superheating. Thus
gain-scheduling can potentially improve the performance, but not result in the superheating
being sustained at all times.
Comparing with results from relevant studies in literature it is conﬁrmed that safely oper-
ating an ORC for WHR on HDVs is a very challenging problem. It is concluded that a simple
PID-based strategy with only one CV is not adequate to safely operate the ORC, due to the
very transient exhaust conditions. Instead it is suggested to focus future research eﬀorts on more
advanced control strategies or investigating modiﬁcations to the system, which could improve
controllability. This is elaborated in the following.

6. Discussion
Control strategies
Future work and suggestions for improvements
Several factors inﬂuence the results of this study and improvements could made to the controllers
developed. In addition, other control schemes could be developed and implemented, which would
likely show better performance than for the presented controllers.
Improvements to control strategies
• The parameters of the PI- and PID controllers have been optimized for a step change in
exhaust mass ﬂow rate of 20% starting from nominal conditions. However for diﬀerent
operating points of the ORC and for disturbances with a diﬀerent amplitude and/or
changes in the exhaust temperature, these parameter settings are not optimal due to the
non-linearity of the system. This calls for gain-scheduling, which potentially could improve
the performance as the gains would be set accordingly to the exhaust conditions and the
ORC operating point. However, initial results show disappointing performance.
• The FF model is based on the settling times observed for the superheating temperature
for both disturbances and the CV. However, as seen in the analysis, the superheating
temperature reacts faster to the disturbances than to changes in the CV. And due to the
very transient data the superheating is never allowed to settle after one disturbance, before
a new one appears. Thus, the correcting action of the controller are not optimal. Therefore
the FF model could be improved. An improved model could be based on the superheating
temperature after a speciﬁed amount of time, i.e. 10 or 20s. This would allow the pump to
react more strongly and it could be beneﬁcial against some disturbances. Another idea is
to use ﬁrst order plus dead time (FOPDT) models to describe the transient behaviour of
the process and disturbance dynamics [118]. An FOPDT is a diﬀerential equation, that
describe how far, how fast and with how much time delay the response behaves. This could
potentially improve the accuracy of the FF control element, and take the diﬀerent time
constants into account.
• When the superheating reaches 0°C the error experienced by the PI and PID controllers
e(t) is constant, and therefore the proportional action becomes constant and the derivative
action zero. This is seen in the control signal as a constant decreasing trend. But in reality
the vapour quality might be decreasing below x = 1, which means, that the ﬂuid needs a
larger heat input to reach ﬁrst saturated vapour and then become superheated. Thus, the
error is in reality not constant as the controller experiences. An idea to overcome this is to
base the calculated error on the vapour fraction computed as

• As the superheating temperature reaches 0°C the PI- and PID controllers reach their lower
limit. This means that the control signal is saturated and wind-up would occur, if not for

• The by-pass valve in the ORC could be used as a safety measure when the superheating

and yield higher net output power. An idea is to relate the setpoint to the operating point

The suggested improvements are left for future work, however it is estimated that these im-
provements will at best give a better setpoint tracking and will not make the controllers able
to sustain the superheating at all times. This is due to the ﬁndings presented in section 5.2.2,
which showed, that some of the experienced transient events are simply too excessive to maintain
the superheating. The eﬀect of pump speed changes is too slow, and even a perfect controller
would not be able to do the job. Therefore more drastic changes are needed.
Suggested new strategies and modiﬁcations
• The dynamic analysis performed for the ORC in the current study shows a stable system,
but with poor controllability. An in-depth analysis of how to improve controllability would
provide insights on how to obtain more desirable system dynamics and thereby improve
controller performance, without using more advanced control strategies. Ideas to do so are:
– Increase the thermal mass of the evaporator, which would make the system more
resistant to disturbances. Drawbacks are increased system weight and potentially
reduced heat transfer.
– Change the turbine to a volumetric expander, and incorporate the expander speed as
an additional CV. The expander speed have a faster impact on the evaporator outlet
temperature [34] and could be used to control the evaporating pressure.
– Implementation of an accumulator at the outlet of the evaporator to store vapor,
which would act as a buﬀer in the system and dampen the impact of variations in the
exhaust gas conditions.
– Implementation of an intermediary loop between the exhaust gas and the ORC to
dampen the eﬀect of the transients. Drawbacks are increased volume and weight.
• More advanced control strategies seems like the most promising approach to operate the
ORC safely and eﬀectively without increasing the system complexity. Here model predictive
control (MPC) is suggested.
– MPC is an advanced control scheme, which rely on dynamic models of the process
variables. MPC uses measurements on the system to identify the current state of
the system and uses a dynamic model to predict the future state of the system. The

the control output. The objective of this optimization is to determine a sequence
of control moves, such that the predictions in the process variable move to the set
point in an optimal matter. This is done continuously at each sampling rate, and
the calculated control output is sent to the actuator [123]. This strategy allows the
controller to act before a disturbance aﬀects the system. Thus, the high time constants
of the pump speed changes are less critical, than for a PID-based controller.
Of all the suggestions it is recommended to focus future work on MPC. Modifying the system
increases its complexity, but the most interesting case for the industry is usually to keep the
system simple as it reduces costs. Systems involving more than one CV makes the control design
more complex, because it becomes a Multi-Input-Multi-Output (MIMO) control problem. On
the other hand MPC could be implemented in the system under study without modiﬁcations.
For a small-ORC Hernandez et al. [44] investigated PID- and MPC strategies and concluded,
that an adaptive MPC outperforms a PID strategy, as it is better to deal with the non-linearities
in the system. This control strategy is very interesting in relation to WHR on HDVs.

7
CONCLUSION
In the current work the transient behaviour of 10.9kW ORC system used to recover waste heat
from the exhaust gas of a heavy-duty vehicle has been analyzed and several PID-based controllers
have been developed, optimized and tested using a dynamic ORC model. The model have been
developed in the Modelica language using Dymola and validated against transient experimental
data of a 1kWe ORC. Exhaust gas data from a real transient driving cycle of a 13L Euro 6 diesel
engine has been used for simulations.
7.1
Results
The main results of the study are as follows.
Validation results
The performed validation shows satisfactory results and the developed dynamic model is
considered to be valid and suﬃciently accurate to support the design and tuning of controllers.
The main results of the validation are:
• All trends observed in the simulation are matching the experimental measurements, except
for the expander outlet temperature. This is due to uncertainties of the isentropic eﬃciency
used in the experiment, and the use of a constant eﬃciency in the simulations.
• All variables are reproduced with less than 8.2% deviation and a mean average percentage
deviation of less than 7%.
• The accuracy of the validation is comparable with similar studies found in literature.
Dynamic system analysis
By performing diﬀerent open-loop tests with the dynamic model, the following results have been
obtained and conclusions made regarding the system dynamic behaviour:
• The location of the poles of the linearized model suggest that the system is stable.
• The staircase tests performed conﬁrms the system non-linearity and reveals the relationship
between the main variables of the ORC and the pump speed.
– For constant heat source conditions increasing the pump rotational speed results in
increasing mass ﬂow rate of working ﬂuid, which results in increasing evaporation
pressure, decreasing superheating and increasing turbine power output.
106
==PAGE:121==
7. Conclusion
Results
– A step change in pump speed have a signiﬁcantly larger eﬀect on the superheating
temperature for low exhaust mass ﬂow rates than for high ﬂow rates. Thus, the system
is much more sensitive under low exhaust gas conditions.
• The open-loop step tests show the transient system response to changes in the exhaust gas
and the pump speed.
– The evaporation temperature reacts faster and is less sensitive to disturbances and
changes in the pump speed than the superheating temperature.
– The time constants of the system for changes in exhaust gas ﬂow rate and temperature
are found to be independent of the amplitude of the change, whereas this is not the
case for changes in the pump speed.
• Simulation results suggest that decreasing the internal volume gives faster system transients,
but could potentially increase controllability for the system under study.
• The system is found to have a low control-ability, which is related to:
– The superheating temperature reacts signiﬁcantly faster to changes in the exhaust
gas than to changes in the pump speed.
– For changes in the pump speed the initial response on the superheating in the ﬁrst
few seconds shows an opposite trend than what is desired.
– The system is non-linear and more sensitive to low exhaust ﬂow rates.
Control of the ORC
Simulations of the ORC under transient exhaust gas conditions using the developed control
strategies yielded the following results:
• The main challenge of the control task is to sustain a positive superheating temperature at
the turbine inlet, due to its non-linear characteristics and the large and fast ﬂuctuations in
exhaust gas temperature and mass ﬂow rate.
• The developed controllers are able to reject minor disturbances. However, they perform
diﬀerently and can be ranked in the following order of decreasing performance: PID with
dynamic FF, PID with static FF, PID and ﬁnally PI.
• A feed-forward element in the controller improves the controller performance for minor
disturbances, as it allows the controller to take action against changes in the exhaust
gas conditions, before the error between setpoint and process variable builds up and the
feed-back element takes action.
• Simulations with exhaust gas conditions for a transient driving cycle show an increase of
3.4 percentage points in the vehicle energy eﬃciency due to energy recovered by the ORC.
• The maximum allowable temperature and pressure in the ORC system are not violated for
all control strategies. However, a positive superheating temperature cannot be sustained.
For a 300s simulation with real transient exhaust gas data, the superheating is sustained
87% of the time.

==PAGE:122==
7. Conclusion
Perspectives & Future work
• A single volatile disturbance is suﬃcient to excite the superheating temperature to 0°C,
despite aggressive proactive action of the pump rotational speed.
Based on the results it is concluded that, it is not possible to operate the ORC safely with
only a single PID-based controller used to control the pump rotational speed. The superheating
cannot be kept positive, due to the very ﬂuctuating exhaust gas conditions. The eﬀect on the
superheating temperature for changes in the pump speed are found to be too slow compared
to the eﬀect of changes in the exhaust gas mass ﬂow rate and temperature. Thus, the system
dynamics give very poor control-ability, and using only the pump rotational speed as control
variable is inadequate to sustain the superheating.
7.2
Perspectives & Future work
The ﬁndings and conclusions made in the current study are in line with literature. To the best
of the author’s knowledge no study presents a simple PID-based controller strategy, which is
able to successfully sustain the superheating of an ORC under as transient conditions as used in
the present study.
Safely controlling the ORC under the transient heat source conditions of an HDV is found
to be a very challenging task, and the ﬁndings highlights important aspects of the dynamic
behaviour and control of ORC systems for WHR on HDVs. Multiple suggestions are given
for how to improve the developed controllers and controllability of the system, however it is
recommended to focus future work on implementing more advanced control strategies such as
model predictive control.

A ALTERNATIVE TECHNOLOGIES FOR WHR
Among the available and competing technologies for WHR on ICEs implemented on HDVs are
turbo-compounding, thermoelectric generators and Kalina cycles. These technologies are shortly
described in the following along with some other technologies available for WHR, but in general
not regarded as applicable for vehicles.
Turbo-compounding
Turbo-compounding can be done in two ways [124]. Mechanically by adding a power turbine
after the Turbocharger (T/C) to extract mechanical power from the exhaust gas. Electrically by
coupling an electrical generator to the turbocharger to extract excess power produced from the tur-
bine [124]. Hence the names mechanical and electrical turbo-compounding. Turbo-compounding
recovers only a portion of the available energy from the exhaust gas. After expansion in the
turbocharger or additional turbine, the remaining exhaust gas exists in the form of thermal
energy that cannot be eﬃciently recovered by additional turbo-compounding [37]. In addition
the technique does in general not utilize the waste heat in the EGR circuit [37]. Finally turbo-
compounding has fatal disadvantages such as engine back pressure and pumping losses [34, 124].
Weerasinghe et al. [125] compared turbocompounding with Rankine Cycle systems using nu-
merical simulations and found Rankine cycles to have several advantages over compounding
cycles. Their study showed that two percentage points of power or more can be produced by
RC and fuel savings of 20% compared to 2% can be achieved. Another advantage of RC is the
ﬂuid reservoir, which acts as a energy buﬀer. However turbo compounding has relatively lower
complexity, size, weight and cost [124].
Thermoelectric generators
A thermoelectric generator is made up of two semiconductors. Heat is supplied to the hot
junction and heat is removed from the cold junction. This results in the two semiconductors
being at two diﬀerent temperatures, which creates a diﬀerence in potential, known as the Seebeck
eﬀect [40]. The temperature diﬀerence between the hot and the cold junction thus generates
a Seeback voltage and a resultant current, and electrical power is generated [40]. For WHR
applications on ICEs, the temperature diﬀerence between the exhaust gas and the thermoelectric
material coolant is used to obtain the Seeback eﬀect [33]. The process involves only solid states
and no use of mechanical parts are needed which results in no generation of noise and vibrations.
The technology is reportedly very reliable [7] and the generator is compact and can be simply
structured [37].
However the technology has low eﬃciency due to the low ﬁgure of merit value of existing
thermoelectric materials and a relatively high cost of the thermoelectric semiconductor material
[7, 37].

A. Alternative technologies for WHR
The Kalina Cycle
Like ORC systems, the Kalina cycle is also a technology with a good potential for use with low
temperature energy sources [25]. The cycle is a further development of the Rankine cycle and
operates with a binary ﬂuid, such as ammonia-water. This gives an isobaric evaporation and
condensation process, that occur under variable temperature [126].
The Kalina cycle is able to reach higher overall eﬃciency than ORCs for the same temper-
ature range. For low temperature sources and low output power systems, the Kalina cycle is not
preferred over the ORC cycle [46]. The Kalina cycle is at a lower lower development stage than
ORC systems and have a higher level of complexity, which implies additional diﬃculties [25].
This is because the improved gain over the ORC cycle is limited and due to the more complicated
system layout of the Kalina cycle.

seen in Figure E.7f. The decrease in heat ﬂow rate is seen at time t = 10s and is coincident with
the step decrease in mass ﬂow rate at pump outlet. Thus less energy is transferred to the organic
ﬂuid, which is because the heat transfer coeﬃcient experiences a sharp decrease as shown in

Integral windup
Integral windup occurs, when large changes in setpoint or disturbances result in a large error
e(t), which causes the integral action to accumulate during the time-period needed (the windup)
to bring back the process value to the setpoint, and exceed beyond the saturation limits of the
control element. This means, that the controller keeps integrating the error even if the input or
the control variable have met its saturation limits. This results in overshooting and continuing
increase as the accumulated error is unwound. The result is excess overshooting. A method to
overcome this, is to use an anti-windup technique.

it is seen how the mass ﬂow rates becomes constant at the highest and lowest value. This show,
that the control signal saturates, and anti-windup is needed.

Gain-scheduling PID
G.3
Gain-scheduling PID
Initial investigations for a control strategy involving a gain-scheduling PID have been conducted.
The approach and results are explained in this section. Gain-scheduling is a control-strategy
used to control non-linear systems, by using linear controllers which each provide satisfactory
performance for diﬀerent operating points of the system.
The gain-scheduling mechanism implemented in this initial analysis is very simple. The scheduling
variable is the exhaust mass ﬂow rate and is used to enable the "appropriate controller". Figure
G.3 shows the principle behind the gain-scheduling mechanism. The setpoint and process variable
is fed to the PID controller. The gain-scheduling mechanism reads the mass ﬂow rate of exhaust
gas and change the PID tuning parameters Kp, Ti and Td to values, which is found to yield a
good control performance at the operating point. In Dymola a look-up table is implemented for
the PID parameters. These perform a linear interpolation that continuously adjusts the tuning
parameters as the exhaust mass ﬂow rate changes.
To identify reasonable tuning parameters for diﬀerent operating conditions of the system two
approaches have been taken. First the system was simulated for diﬀerent exhaust mass ﬂow
rates in the range [0.05kg/s ; 0.5kg/s] and was subject to a step change in mass ﬂow rate. the
SQP-algorithm was used to ﬁnd optimal parameters. However, these parameters were found to
result in too excessive gains and very poor performance when using the gain-scheduling PID to
control the ORC under the real transient exhaust gas conditions. Therefore a manual tuning
was used for the diﬀerent operating points. Trends was based on the trends found using the
SQP-algorithm, however with lower gains in general. The result is seen in Figure G.4. Here
diﬀerent values for Kp, Ti and Td are used depending on the mass ﬂow rate of exhaust gas.
The look-up table in Dymola makes linear interpolations between these values and sets the
parameters. Simulating the ORC system with the gain-scheduling PID controller and the above
relationships of the PID parameters and the exhaust ﬂow rate, the following results were obtained.
