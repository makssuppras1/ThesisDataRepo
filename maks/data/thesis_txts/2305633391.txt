Summary (English)
EEG is a tool for studying brain activity, prized for its low cost, non-invasiveness
and high temporal resolution. It is used by researchers and doctors who are try-
ing to understand what the brain does and how it relates to our daily functioning
and health. Arriving at these insights is made possible by techniques from signal
processing, machine learning and related ﬁelds. This thesis looks at one such
technique, called microstate analysis.
Microstate analysis segments an EEG recording into a sequence of topographies,
which remain somewhat stable for a short duration, before rapidly switching
to the next topography - the next microstate. Segmenting an EEG recording
consists of assigning each time-point in the recording to one of the microstates.
Several algorithms exist for performing microstate segmentation.
The most
prevalent of these are modiﬁcations of well-known clustering methods such as
agglomerative clustering and k-means clustering. Based on one of these, the N-
microstates algorithm [PMML95], I derive two new algorithms using variational
inference. One is a probabilistic version of N-microstates, while the other is
a factor model. Through experiments on simulated EEG data, I compare the
performance of N-microstates and its variational equivalent. Implementations
of a few of these algorithms form a toolbox for microstate analysis, written as
a plug-in for EEGLAB [DM04], a widely used environment for processing EEG
in MATLAB.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Electroencephalograms reveal aspects of brain activity. Noisy recordings of tiny
voltages from the scalp, can be translated by researchers and doctors, into in-
sights about cognition and health.
Can the methods they use to gain these
insights be improved using machine learning?
In this thesis, I review a group of methods called microstate analysis. I discuss
some shortcomings that these methods have, and reformulate one of them, the
N-microstates algorithm, in terms of Bayesian inference. Finally, I present a
further extension of this Bayesian model, which connects microstate analysis to
another group of methods, matrix factorization.
Brain imaging and EEG
EEG is a recording of the electric potential from electrodes placed on the scalp.
Despite being among the oldest imaging techniques, EEG is widely used in
medical and research applications, because it’s cheap, mobile, non-invasive and
silent. It has the highest time-resolution of all brain imaging techniques, on
par with MEG. The time-resolution only depends on the sampling rate, or how
many times the signal is sampled per second, which is often chosen to be around
250-500 Hz, but can go up to 20,000 Hz.
==PAGE:10==
2
Unfortunately, EEG suﬀers from poor spatial resolution. The number of elec-
trodes placed on the scalp, which can vary from a few to a few hundred, limits
the amount of detail in one frame of EEG. More importantly, the spatial res-
olution is constrained by the fact that the soft and hard tissues of the brain,
skull and scalp, separate the sources of current from the electrodes. When the
current passes through these barriers, the signal gets distorted and attenuated.
Compared with electrocardiography, where there is no skull to damp the signal,
EEG has a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). The SNR is lowered further by the
presence of artifacts consisting of induced or distorted electric activity from
non-brain sources which confound the signal from the brain. These artifacts
can stem from the subject, for example from muscles in the face and jaw, the
heartbeat, and eye and head movement; or they can stem from the equipment
and environment, like poor electrode contact, and noise from the power-line1.
Poor spatial resolution and a low SNR are the main reasons why researchers
must perform extensive preprocessing and data analysis if they are to infer the
location and dynamics of the brain activity hidden in their EEG.
EEG is one of the earliest inventions in brain imaging. It was introduced in
1924, when Hans Berger recorded the ﬁrst human electroencephalogram, or EEG
for short. Since EEG, additions to the ﬁeld’s toolbox have included positron
emission topography (PET), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), functional
MRI (fMRI), near infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) and magnetoencephalography
(MEG). The latter measures the same phenomenon as EEG, volume conduction
of electromagnetic ﬁelds.
The biophysics of EEG
The main sources of the EEG signal are neurons. There are an estimated 86
billion neurons in an average adult brain, which communicate with each other
by electrical and chemical signals. Each one consists of a body (soma), an axon,
which sends outgoing signals to connected neurons, and one or more dendrites,
which receive incoming signals. The contact points between the dendrites of
the receiving neurons and the axon terminal of the sending neuron, are called
synapses.
The dendrites and soma form a tree-like structure, enclosed by a
membrane that is spotted with hundreds to tens of thousands of synapses.
When a post-synaptic neuron receives an excitatory signal, ions carrying a pos-
itive charge (cations), ﬂow into the cell through the synapses, creating a sink.

Locations on the membrane where cations are pushed out are called sources. In
the case of inhibitory currents, with negative charge, the locations of sinks and
sources switch. Together, a source and a sink form a dipole.
When many ions are pushed out of many neurons at once, those ions will repel
close-by ions of similar charge, which in turn repel ions close to them, and so
on, until the electromagnetic ﬁeld due to the ion currents meets the electrons in
the metal of the electrodes on the scalp. This is called volume conduction.
Not all neuronal dipoles leave a mark on the signal, nor are dipoles resulting
from synaptic activity the only sources of current [BAK12]. Only ensembles of
synchronised neurons in suﬃcient number can generate enough power to reach
the scalp, and of those, only the ones that happen to reside in the superﬁcial
layers of the cortex reach the electrodes before they decay in power. Because
currents from synapses are relatively slow, compared to other currents that
contribute to the potential in the space around neurons, they are more likely
to overlap in time and therefore induce a measurable signal. Synchrony often
happens because networks of neurons oscillate in phase. Besides synchrony and
magnitude, it is the orientation of dipoles that determines to which electrodes
the currents will be conducted. Two nearby dipoles with diﬀerent orientations
can contribute to electrodes at distant locations.
The diﬀerence in voltage between any pair of electrodes is recorded by a volt-
meter. Doing this continously over time yields the analog EEG signal, which is
sampled to yield the discrete signal.
One of the electrodes must act as a reference to the others. The alternative
is to compute the average reference, where one exploits the fact that outward
positive and negative currents across an entire sphere sum to zero. In practice,
it is rare to have electrodes distributed evenly across the entire head, rather
than distributing them roughly on a grid that covers just the scalp.
The individual electrodes do not simply pick up whatever electrical activity is
in the tissue directly beneath them. Rather, each electrode captures a weighted
sum of the sources from a roughly 10cm2-sized patch underneath it. Equiva-
lently, each electrode captures a component of the source that was conducted
to it, while the rest of the signal from that source ends up in other electrodes.
This is called the forward model:

So each electrode records a mix of signals originating from temporally coherent

inferred without some additional assumptions about what it looks like.
Resting-state EEG and ERPs
Depending on the research question and available resources, an EEG study may
analyze recordings from many subjects, or just one.
Sometimes, the subjects may be asked to simply rest, in the absence of any
deliberate sensory stimuli; such recordings are called spontaneous, or ongoing,
or resting-state EEG. Resting-state EEG records activity that is responsible
for the bulk of the brain’s energy consumption.
Giving the subject a task
increases consumption by only 5% [RM06]. What the other 95% is spent on is
still unknown and as such, it’s being called the brain’s “dark energy”. However,
the gross features of spontaneous activity are apparent from fMRI recordings.
They reveal that a lot of brain activity is organized into large-scale functional
networks, or circuits, which in the context of resting-state activity are called
resting-state networks (RSNs). One of the original motivations for this thesis
is to develop tools that can detect the Default Mode Network, one of the most
studied RSNs, in EEG.
Alternatively, the subjects may be given a task to execute, such as responding to
a presented stimulus; a picture might be shown for the subject to describe. Other
examples of stimuli are repeated light ﬂashes, sound, touch, electric shocks, and,
notably, direct stimulation of the electric ﬁelds by a magnetized coil, called tran-
scranial magnetic stimulation (TMS). An EEG recording taken around the time
the stimulus is presented is called an event-related potential (ERP). Because
an ERP from a single recording reﬂects all brain activity that happened dur-
ing recording, rather than only the activity related to the event, researchers
average ERPs over many trials. A common experiment is to divide subjects
into groups, either based on some characteristic of the subjects, such as mental
health condition, or based on variations in the stimulus. The researchers form a
null-hypothesis, saying that there’s no diﬀerence between the average ERPs of
the groups, and one or more alternative hypotheses. A statistical test can reveal
==PAGE:13==
5
whether the null-hypothesis must be rejected. Continuing the example above,
one group might be asked to describe a grayscale picture, while the other group
describes colored pictures.
Hypotheses about ERPs or resting-state EEG are guided by prior knowledge
of the patterns that EEG exhibits.
EEG is a multivariate signal, with two
dimensions, space and time. The joint spatial distribution of potential depends
on time. In other words, EEG is non-stationary; which brain regions are likely
to be active can change from one moment to the next. Temporal patterns, also
called waves or rhythms, are classiﬁed according to their frequency. They range
from the slow delta rhythm, below 4 Hz, through theta [4;8[ Hz, alpha [8;14[,
beta [14;30[ and gamma [30;100[ Hz.
Overview of methods for analysing EEG
Approaches to EEG analysis can be ordered by how they treat the spatial and
temporal aspects of EEG[Mic09]2. This is done in Table 1.1. They all model
EEG as a product of spatial and temporal components, and all are unsupervised.
Representations of spatial and temporal dynamics vary in complexity.
The
simplest spatial component would be one electrode “on” and the others “oﬀ”.
Similarly, the simplest temporal component would be a delta function that is
1 when the EEG matches the spatial component, and 0 otherwise. A method
that used these two representations in tandem would simply pick one electrode
at every time-point, which would eﬃciently compress the signal, at the cost of
throwing virtually all information away.
At the other end, some variants of
source localization infer components that are local, or sparse, in time, space
and frequency, with many sources of diﬀering magnitudes and orientations con-
tributing to the observed signal.
In practice, the choice of representation depends on the research question.
For ongoing EEG, it is common to do a fast Fourier transform, on each chan-
nel. The resulting spectrum can be used to construct spatial maps of speciﬁc
frequency bands. In addition, the phase component of FFT is used to measure
coherence between signals at some frequency, which can indicate functional cou-
pling between two processes, or a single process reﬂected in both signals due to
volume conduction or reference eﬀects.

Spatially independent or uncorrelated components are constrained to be dis-
similar from each other, which is ill-suited for studying brain regions that are
nearby in the brain but are active at diﬀerent times. For example, a study of

to ﬁnd any diﬀerence between a subject seeing blue and the subject seeing red,

Microstate analysis
In the 70’s, Dietrich Lehmann observed that EEG tends to remain in a single
state for a short duration, before rapidly transitioning into another, and named
such states microstates. Although no strict deﬁnition is universally acknowl-
edged, a microstate always refers to a time-segment of EEG and a spatial map,
which is assigned to all frames in the segment. The microstates do not morph
gradually into each other, but remain dominant for a hundred miliseconds, give
or take twenty miliseconds, before rapidly switching to the next one.
Microstates have been used as biomarkers for Alzheimer’s-related dementia,
frontotemporal dementia, depression, Tourette’s syndrome and panic disorder
[KPLMF15]. They have been used in sleep-analysis for describing brain activity
in wakefulness, “in terms of duration, occurrence rate and transition sequences
of each map” [BKvW+12]. The same author describes microstates as “correlates
of information processing steps, in the sense of “atoms of thought””. They are
speculated to be related to dipole sources, and to large scale brain networks, as
stated in Gärtner et al. 2014: “Functionally, EEG microstates are understood
as spontaneous ﬂuctuations of activity in large scale brain networks”. At least
three studies, Britz et al., 2010; Musso et al., 2010; and Yuan et al., 2012; have
suggested a link between microstates from resting state EEG, and resting state
networks. Van De Ville et al., 2010, observe that “the microstate time series
has scale-free dynamics over six dyadic scales, over the 256 ms to 16 s range,
suggesting that resting-state brain activity at the EEG and fMRI timescales re-
ﬂect the same underlying neurophysiological processes”. An unpublished article
(at time of writing), looks at altered temporal dynamics of microstates during
gradual loss of consciousness during the step-wise induction of general anesthesia
with Propofol.
The 2014 article “Microstates in resting-state EEG: Current status and future
directions” reviews studies of how microstates reﬂect behaviour or indicate the
presence of neuropsychiatric disorders. It also reviews various ways of ﬁnding
microstates. The original method of segmenting EEG consists of comparing the
spatial map whose standard deviation (across electrodes) is temporarily max-
imal, to the spatial map at the preceding standard deviation maximum. The
standard deviation across electrodes is called the global ﬁeld potential (GFP). If
the distance between means of two GFP-peak spatial maps exceeds a predeter-
==PAGE:16==
8
mined threshold, the latter map is a new microstate. This method, called adap-
tive segmentation, doesn’t naturally lend itself towards grouping microstates
into classes.
On the contrary, clustering analysis ﬁnds classes in EEG in an unsupervised
manner. The two approaches implemented in the program CARTOOL are k-
means, where k is set using cross-validation, and a variant of agglomerative
clustering, in which clusters of short duration don’t necessarily merge with big-
ger clusters if they explain a lot of variance[MDLBM09].

Chapter 2
Theory
Segmenting EEG into microstates means clustering all scalp maps from a record-
ing into a smaller number of prototypical spatial maps - the microstates. After
onset, the microstates are stable for some duration, then rapidly change to a
diﬀerent microstate, which either has occured before, or not. For example, a
clustering algorithm might segment a minute of EEG into 4 microstates that
last an average of 100ms and occur 150 times each, in some order.
The goal of clustering is to learn a number of clusters whose points are similar
to each other within the clusters. Two complimentary ways of thinking about
microstate analysis methods, are that they estimate the density of the EEG
and the noise, and that they seek to compress the EEG, leaving out the noise.
All algorithms in this chapter assume certain properties about the structure
of EEG - for example, neither agglomerative clustering or k-means have time-
varying componenets: the distances from cluster mean to observation are the
same throughout a microstates’ duration. So they implicitly assume that any
variation in the signal during a microstate is noise. Assumptions dictate what
information gets preserved in the segmentation, and what gets left out. This
chapter is about how that choice is made in each method.

in a 2 × 2 ﬁgure, which shows how the algorithm segments real EEG into a
series of 4 microstates. The ﬁrst row shows 0.8 seconds of averaged EEG data,

which is implemented in the software KeyPy.

The N-microstate algorithm: A scale-invariant k-
means
Rather than assume that a microstate is stationary throghout its duration, the
N-microstate algorithm is scale-invariant [PMML95]. This means that given a
frame of EEG signal Yt, and two scalars α1, α2 ∈R, α1Yt and α2Yt are equally
likely to be assigned to a microstate Wk. Compare this to regular k-means,
which would assign α1Yt and α2Yt to separate microstates if the scaling pushed
the frames close to separate means.
Pascual-Marqui et al. illustrate their model by considering the extreme case of
just two electrodes. The electric potential measurements from any two electrodes
lie on a plane. A single point on this plane is a measurement from both electrodes
at some point in time; a trajectory on the plane corresponds to a sequence of
measurements during a time interval. The N-microstate model assumes that the
trajectory is made of straight lines originating in the same point, which project
radially outwards; a star. Microstates are vectors that point in the directions
of the arms, and so the points that make up a given arm are assigned to that

Experiments
Simulating data
The variational microstates model is generative. The microstates W, activations
X and assignments Z can be sampled from their distributions, to produce a
simulated signal Y = W(X ◦Z). Adding Gaussian noise results in a simulated
dataset, which allows for direct comparison between the simulated true factors,
and the ones estimated from the simulated data by the model.
The distribution of the activations xkt is not modelled explicitly in the N-
microstates model, but it’s given a normal distribution N(µkt, σ2
kt) here. There-
fore, I simulate EEG in one of two ways.
As Pascual Marqui et al. do in their experiment, the ﬁrst simulation draws the
microstates and activations from the uniform distribution.
The second one uses a forward model that accompanies the 128-channel face/scrambled
data, in the form of a 128×8196, where 8196 is the total number of ﬁxed sources.
Given a J ∈[1, 2, ..., 128], I select every 128/J’th row in the gain matrix (see Eq.
1.1), such that the total number of rows is J. I sample random sources (column
indices in the forward model) with probability 0.1 for each source, yielding 819.6
sources per microstate on average. The binary “source maps” are transformed
==PAGE:33==
25
to scalp maps by the forward model, and normalized by mean and standard
deviation. The activations are drawn from a Gaussian distribution, whose mean
is µ and covariance matrix is Σ2 = αI. The noiseless activations µ are sums
of sines and cosines with random frequency, random phase and random ampli-
tude. This way of simulating microstates and their activations makes for a much
harder inference problem, because simulating similar frames that correspond to
diﬀerent microstates and activations is much more likely. For both methods,
the larger K and the smaller T and J, the harder is it to ﬁnd a good clustering.
In both simulation procedures, the microstate assignments Z are a sequence of
labels [1, 2, ..., K], with a label repeating without variation for a period of time,
before switching to the next one.
The simulations are visualized in Appendix.
Validity, convergence and initialization
The mean ﬁeld update for each parameter minimizes the free energy as a function
of the other parameters. If the update is done based on the true parameters,
we can validate the resulting estimate by comparison with the true, simulated
parameter. If the estimates approximate the real parameters well, the updates
are probably correct. All the updates pass this test, except the noise precision
estimate ˆβ, whose error increases with the size of the true pecision β.
Convergence is deemed when the relative diﬀerence between the β-estimate in
the last two iterations falls below a preset tolerance. β is chosen for judging con-
vergence because it’s usually the last parameter to converge. The N-microstates
uses an equivalent criterion.
With each restart, the previous solution is discarded, hence the spikes. Each
restart is seeded by a new initialization.

minima, as is seen from the relative reconstruction error in the upper right corner

Performance and robustness to noise
If the performance of the algorithms is to be measured by direct comparison
between the estimated microstates and the simulated ones, the microstates must
be permuted such that they are aligned, otherwise the wrong microstates will
be compared. The order in which they appear as columns of W or rows in Z
or X is arbitrary and inconsistent. An alternative measure of performance is
the mutual information between the true and estimated segmentation Z, which
measures the amount of information one provides about the other, regardless of
the actual labels. Thus, the mutual information between the true segmentation
and the same segmentation with the labels changed is equal to the amount of
information in the true segmentation, which is the same as its entropy.
I ran Variational Microstates without smoothing (γ2 = 0), Variational Mi-
crostates with smoothing (γ2 = 4), and N-Microstates with smoothing (λ =
5, b = 3), all with 40 restarts, for 10 SNRs ranging from 0.9 to 12.4. I do this

Conclusion
The variational microstates algorithm is capable of ﬁnding more accurate seg-
mentations of simulated EEG than the N-microstates algorithm.
It is more
robust to low signal-to-noise ratios. When initialized with eigenvectors of the
sample covariance matrix, and projections of the time-points onto the eigenvec-
tors, it can reconstruct data slightly more accurately, on average.
==PAGE:42==
Chapter 5
Discussion
Finding good microstate segmentations using the variational microstates algo-
rithm is highly dependent on the learning rate, the smoothing parameter and
the noise variance of the time-components. Appropriate values for these hy-
perparameters can be found by Bayesian optimization, which must be done for
each separate recording. While the algorithm is unsupervised, it is still possible
to overﬁt - consider the case K = T, in which every time-point is its own mi-
crostate. The problem of model selection is notoriously hard. If it were possible
to adapt the modiﬁed cross validation criterion to this end, it would avoid the
the more computationally expensive technique of cross-validation on a test-set.
The polymicrostates algorithm is a more realistic approach to modelling EEG. It
relaxes the clustering constraint of traditional microstate analysis, but doesn’t
impose the assumption of independence, like ICA does. Whether this is an ad-
vantage or not is an open question, but one apparent advantage it has over ICA
is that it can produce intepretable decompositions of arbitrarily short record-
ings.
