The role of retinoic acid in 
driving skin inflammation 
Julie Mousing Bjerring, s134030 
 
 
Supervisors: 
Vasileios Bekiaris, Groupleader, Associate Professor 
Rasmus Agerholm, PhD student 
 
 
Department of Health Technology, Technical University of Denmark
ABSTRACT 
The vitamin A metabolite retinoic acid (RA) is used to treat skin diseases such as psoriasis, but in this project, we 
have provided evidence that topical application of RA on the ears of mice led to extensive inflammation 
accompanied by very high numbers of infiltrating monocytes and neutrophils in the skin. Herein we used the 
imiquimod (IMQ)-induced psoriasis model to show that RA exacerbates skin inflammation independently of γδ 
T cells. Furthermore, by using RAG-deficient mice lacking all mature T and B cells, we confirmed that RA-
induced skin inflammation is not mediated by T or B cells, and we further demonstrated that the mechanism is 
not ILC-driven either. Successful depletion of monocytes with anti-CCR2 revealed that the cell type may not 
actively contribute to the increase in epidermal thickening upon IMQ-induced skin inflammation stimulated with 
exogenous RA, even though psoriatic skin is characterized by having elevated levels of monocytes. Neutrophil-
depletion failed, leaving open the question whether RA acts directly on neutrophils to exacerbate skin 
inflammation. Since the cellular mechanisms of action are still unknown, and on the basis of the research 
conducted in this master thesis, we suggest investigating our hypothesis that RA-induced cell death in 
keratinocytes could act as a potent trigger for the observed skin inflammation and increase in monocytic and 
neutrophilic infiltration.

INTRODUCTION 
Section 1: Psoriasis 
1.1 Pathogenesis 
Psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory skin disorder. Globally, psoriasis affects 2-3 % of the population with a 
prevalence of 2.84 % in Denmark (Crow et al., 2012). At the moment, the exact genetic and environmental factors 
that contribute to the initiation of the disease is not fully understood, but we know that it is T cell mediated and 
most researchers conclude that Th17 cells are probably the major pathogenic cell type, hence some of the immune 
based therapies are directed against that cell type (IL-23, IL-17, TNF-α). Therapies that target IL-23 or IL-17 
have proved to be highly effective, highlighting the central role of these cytokines in the development of psoriatic 
disease.  
The disease starts with environmental stimuli damaging the epidermis (e.g. exogenous trauma, infection) and/or 
loss of immune tolerance leading to activation of plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs) and dermal DCs. Recognition of 
psoriasis autoantigens (e.g. LL37, ADAMTSL5, PLA2G4D-generated neolipid antigens) (Cheung et al., 2016; 
Arakawa et al., 2015; Lande et al., 2014) by dermal DCs results in IL-23 production. Proinflammatory DCs will 
then present autoantigens to CD4+ and CD8+ T cells and in that way trigger polarization and clonal expansion of 
IL-17- and IL-22-producing T cells, but other cell types like dermal γδ T cells, neutrophils and mast cells are also 
capable of producing IL-17 in response to IL-23 stimulation (Blauvelt and Chiricozzi 2018). Activation of Th17 
cells results in release of IL-17A, IL-17F, TNF-α, IL-26 and IL-29. IL-26 and IL-29 activate STAT1 to upregulate 
CXCL9/10/11 from keratinocytes and promote the influx of Th1 cells to the psoriatic skin (Stephen-Victor et al., 
2016; Wolk et al., 2013).  In response to IL17-A and IL-17F acting in synergy with TNF-α, keratinocytes start to 
produce IL-17C. This cytokine can stimulate Th17 cells to increase the production of IL-17A and IL-17F, induce 
antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) from keratinocytes (e.g. LL37, hBD2, S100s) (Wang et al., 2013), activate STAT3 
leading to keratinocyte hyperproliferation, and upregulate CXCL1/2/3/5/8 and CCL20 by keratinocytes to recruit 
neutrophils, macrophages, DCs and Th17 cells to the inflamed skin (Harper et al., 2009; Chiricozzi et al., 2011; 
Homey et al., 2000). IL-22, IL-19 and IL-36 produced by keratinocytes in response to IL-17 lead to epidermal 
hyperplasia and dysregulated differentiation of keratinocytes. Lastly, ILC3s are capable of producing IL-17 and 
IL-22 contributing to disease development.  
As shown in Figure 1, all these cytokine signals create a feedforward inflammatory response by activating 
STAT1, STAT3/NFκB and CCAAT-enhancer-binding protein (C/EBP) β and δ driving the development of 
psoriatic plaques by recruiting pathogenic immune cells and overproduce keratinocyte-derived psoriasis-related 
proteins (Hawkes et al., 2017). These positive feedback mechanisms sustain the IL-23/IL-17 signaling to 
accelerate the development of psoriasis (Hawkes et al., 2018).

1.2 The role of monocytes and neutrophils in psoriasis 
During early psoriasis, neutrophils are recruited to the epidermis where they form aggregates (i.e. Munro 
microabscesses) in the stratum corneum. It is well-known that neutrophils are able to secrete IL-17 and in fact, 
Lin et al. demonstrated that neutrophils and mast cells, not T cells, are the dominant cell type that contains IL-17 
in human psoriatic skin, and they actually claims that IL-17+ neutrophils and mast cells are more enriched than 
IL-17-producing T cells in psoriasis lesions (Lin et al., 2011). In contrast to these findings, a newer study has 
shown that monocytes and lymphocytes are the dominant source of IL-17 in psoriasis, not neutrophils (Yamanaka 
et al., 2017). The main features of neutrophils directly connected to psoriasis is the release of reactive oxygen 
species (ROS), release of granules and formation of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) that all together 
contribute to keratinocyte proliferation, T cell imbalance, angiogenesis and autoantigen formation (Chiang et al., 
2019). The review by Chiang et al. suggests that, besides T cells and keratinocytes, neutrophils are one of the 
major sources of IL-17 via NETs in psoriasis, and that NETs are able to induce Th17 cells (Chiang et al., 2019).

Regarding monocytes and macrophages, they have been identified as the major contributor for TNF-α production 
in IL-23-induced skin inflammation. It has also been reported that psoriasis patients have increased monocytes 
and macrophages in lesional skin compared to non-lesional skin (Wang et al., 2019). 
 
1.3 Genetic variants in psoriasis 
Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified over 40 psoriasis susceptibility loci, where the genes 
encode for proteins involved in autoantigens/antigen presentation, immune cell signaling pathways and skin 
barrier function. Among these, IL-23 and IL-17 are considered to be the key disease drivers (Gunter et al., 2019). 
 
Regarding autoantigens and antigen presentation, it is known that psoriasis patients may overexpress specific self-
antigens. For example, the autoantigen LL-37 produced by keratinocytes and APCs in the skin as a response to 
skin injury forms a complex with extracellular nucleic acids released during inflammation. This complex binds 
to human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-class I allele HLA-C* 06:02 which then activates myeloid dendritic cells 
(mDCs) and plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs). Activated APCs release IL-23 leading to production of IL-17A 
by Th17 cells. Along with this, recognition of the LL-37-HLA-Cw6*02 complex induces proliferation of Th17 
cells. HLA-C* 06:02 are also able to recognize the autoantigen ADAMTSL5 and present it to CD8+ cytotoxic T 
cells leading to production of IL-17A and IFN-γ. Another MHC class I gene includes HLA-B*27 presenting B27 
peptides to CD8+ T cells. Deficiency of the MGAT5 enzyme required for N-glycosylation in HLA molecules will 
lead to reduced threshold required for T cell activation and risk of losing immune tolerance, therefore triggering 
autoimmune diseases like psoriasis (Gunter et al., 2019). 
 
Psoriasis susceptibility loci have been associated with cytokine signaling pathways such as IL-20, IL-23/IL-17 
and NF-κB signaling. Psoriasis patients with IL-20 HT GGA haplotype had increased levels of IL-20 expressed 
by keratinocytes which can then bind and induce STAT3 activation that results in increased cell proliferation. The 
IL-12B haplotype has also been identified which can lead to increased expression of p40 subunit of IL-12 and IL-
23 causing an increase in the production of IFN-γ, formation of IL-12 and IL-23 and polarization of T cells to 
Th1 and Th17 cells respectively. The IL23A gene coding for the p19 subunit of IL-23 promotes survival and 
expansion of Th17 cells and therefore release of IL-17, IL-22 and TNF-α. Overexpression of the PTTG1 haplotype 
results in hyperproliferation of keratinocytes and overproduction of TNF-α. IL-17A and IL-17F interacts with IL-
17R and recruits TRAF3IP2 required for NFκB signaling. This leads to activation of the pathway and the resulting 
proinflammatory response. Other loci involved in the NFκB pathway in psoriasis patients includes TNIP1 gene 
haplotype. ABIN-1 coded by TNIPI controls NFκB cascades through linking of A20 to NEMO/IKKγ resulting in 
deubiquitination and inhibition of NFκB, so a defective ABIN-1 protein results in a dysregulated NFκB signaling.

Regarding skin barrier function, absence of LCE proteins involved in skin repair leads to keratinocyte

Increased expression of psoriasis susceptibility locus AIM2 in keratinocytes results in the formation of 
inflammasome triggering caspase-1 activation and release of IL-1β (Gunter et al., 2019).  
 
1.4 Treatments 
Although there currently exist no treatments capable of preventing disease onset, a number of drugs have shown 
great capacity to suppress the symptoms associated with the disease. Mild to moderate psoriasis can be treated 
topically with glucocorticoids, vitamin D analogues and phototherapy. Moderate to severe psoriasis requires 
systemic treatments like small-molecules taken as oral drugs (methotrexate, cyclosporin A, retinoids, fumaric 
acid esters, apremilast) or biologic drugs targeting inflammatory pathways like TNFα-signaling and the IL-23/IL-
17 axis. Biologics are administered as injections and include TNF-α inhibitors (etanercept, infliximab, 
adalimumab, certolizumab), monoclonal antibodies targeting IL-23 (guselkumab, tildrakizumab, risankizumab) 
and anti-IL-17 (secukinumab, ixekizumab, brodalumab) (Rendon et al., 2019). Anti-TNFα was among the first 
biologic used but the efficacy of this first-generation therapy appears to be much less than the benefits, so it is 
now outcompeted by drugs targeting the IL-23/IL-17 axis instead. Also, the mechanism by which anti-TNFα 
results in disease improvement is most likely because of its indirect effect on IL-17 via the regulation of IL-23 
produced from myeloid cells or DCs (Hawkes al. 2018).  
 
Section 2: Mouse models of induced psoriasis 
Although a number of human studies and genetic evidence have uncovered many potential mechanisms that can 
explain the pathogenesis of psoriasis, animal models, in particular mice models, have been instrumental in 
uncovering the detailed molecular pathways and cellular interactions associated with this disease.  
 
In 2004, Nestle et al. presented a new experimental animal model where psoriasis was spontaneously developed. 
They transplanted human symptomless prepsoriatic skin grafts into immunosuppressed AGR129 mice deficient 
in type I (A) and type II (G) IFN receptors in addition to being RAG-2-/- in order to avoid graft rejection, as the 
current transplantation model at that time, the SCID mice, were not ideal acceptors of human skin grafts. In this 
study, they observed spontaneous onset of bona fide psoriatic lesions that did not require any exogenous cells, 
and this was accompanied with proliferation of T cells dependent on the production of TNF-α to form psoriatic

lesions. Upon transplantation, epidermal keratinocytes, DCs, endothelial cells and resident T cells got activated 
to induce psoriasis (Nestle et al., 2004). 
 
Zheng et al. reported in 2007 that IL-23 injection into the ears of mice could induce the production of IL-22 and 
IL-17 from Th17 cells, hyperplasia of the epidermis (i.e. epidermal acanthosis) and dermal inflammation with 
increased numbers of eosinophils and neutrophils that does not model psoriasis, but many of the features in the 
IL-23 injection model closely resembles the features evident in psoriasis, e.g. upregulation of IL-22, epidermal 
hyperplasia and activation of phospho-STAT3 in epidermal keratinocytes (Zheng et al., 2007). 
 
Xenotransplants of human psoriatic skin into immunodeficient mice were the first approach to generate an animal 
model with psoriasis-like lesions, however, the imiquimod (IMQ)-induced psoriasis model is currently the most 
widely used animal model for studying the disease. IMQ is the active pharmaceutical ingredient present in the 
AldaraTM cream. Daily application will resemble plaque psoriasis by inducing inflamed scaly skin lesions, 
epidermal keratinocyte hyperproliferation, increased dermal vascularization and infiltration of inflammatory cells 
composed of T cells, APCs, pDCs and neutrophils (Van der Fits et al., 2009). Ear swelling and enlarged draining 
LNs will also be observed. In the study by Van der Fits, they demonstrated that the skin lesions induced by IMQ 
application closely resemble psoriasis lesions in human. 
 
According to the paper by Nestle and Nickoloff (Nestle et al., 2006) an ideal psoriasis model is defined by six 
criteria: 
1.) Hyperproliferation and altered differentiation of keratinocytes 
2.) Papillomatosis (skin surface elevation caused by hyperplasia) 
3.) Presence of inflammatory cells (i.e. T cells, DCs, monocytes/macrophages, neutrophils and mast cells) 
4.) A functional role for T cells 
5.) Altered vascularity 
6.) Response to antipsoriatic drugs 
 
Van der Fits et al. (2009) demonstrated that criteria 1-5 can be fulfilled with IMQ-induced psoriasis, which makes 
it the preferred in vivo model to study the disease. Besides being the current best model to resemble psoriasis-
like disease, it is easier to generate than xenotransplants or IL-23-injections and cheaper because it is normal 
wildtype mice that are being used. Regarding criterion 6, a study tested the effects of the topical vitamin D 
analogue CAL in the IMQ mouse model and showed that the antipsoriatic drug had suppressive effects on 
psoriasis-like inflammation (Kusuba et al., 2018).

Moreover, γδ T cells are required for IMQ-induced skin inflammation by being the major source of IL-17 (Cai et 
al, 2011). IMQ is an agonist of TLR7 and TLR8 expressed on monocytes, macrophages and DCs that promotes 
IL-23 production (Schön et al., 2007). IMQ-induced psoriasis is critically dependent on the IL-23/IL-17 axis, 
since deficiency of the IL-23 and IL-17 receptor in mice study resulted in no disease development even though 
IMQ was applied daily for 6 days (Van der Fits et al., 2009). Consistent with those findings, it has now been 
shown that IL-23 produced by Langerhans Cells (LCs) is required for the development of psoriasis in the IMQ 
model by inducing IL-17 production from CCR6+ γδ T cells (Yoshiki et al., 2014). However, studies with RORc-
ko mice, which lack the transcription factor RORγt and thus the IL-17 and IL-22 production was also impaired, 
revealed that IL-17 is not required for the early keratinocyte response in the Aldara™-induced psoriasis model. 
Nevertheless, inflammation was reduced in RORc-ko mice after seven days treatment, suggesting that IL-17 has 
a role in propagation of inflammation but not initiation (Walter et al., 2013).  
Even though Th17 cells are considered to be the major pathogenic cell type responsible for the development of 
psoriasis, IL-17 producing RORγt+ γδ T cells (γδT17 cells) have been connected to the pathogenesis of psoriasis 
because large numbers of dermal γδT17 cells have been found in both murine and human psoriatic skin (Cai et 
al., 2011). Before proceeding to discuss the role of γδT17 cells in the disease progression of psoriasis, it will be 
necessary to highlight the current knowledge about the γδ T cells in general. 
 
Section 3: γδ T cells 
3.1 Prevalence, phenotype, antigen recognition, tissue distribution and diversity of γδ T cells 
Unlike αβ T cells, γδ T cells only represent a small fraction (1-5 %) of all circulating T cells in mice and human, 
while αβ T cells are more prevalent (90 %). However, γδ T cells are primarily found in mucosal and epithelial 
sites, where they can comprise up to 50 % of T cells (Kalyan et al., 2012) (Carding et al., 2002). Unlike mice, γδ 
T cells do not exist in the human epidermis but they constitutes 5-10 % of total dermal leukocytes (Cai et al., 
2011). 
 
In the periphery, there exist two subsets of γδ T cells: The IFN-γ-producing and the IL-17-producing γδ T cells. 
The first population express the transcription factor Tbet and can be recognized based on the surface marker 
CD27. The IL-17-producing γδ T cells express the transcription factor RORγt and is CD27-CCR6+ (Serre et al., 
2013). 
 
What makes γδ T cells attractive to study is that they possess a combination of both the innate and the adaptive 
immune system. They function as a first line of defense due to their rapid response to bacteria, viruses and tumors.

They are able to generate a fast response in the case of infectious or inflammatory diseases and start to secrete 
large amounts of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Despite the production of cytokines, they can directly lyse infected 
or malignant cells and establish a memory response (Kabelitz 2011). Of all T cells, γδ T cells are the first lineage 
to be generated, since most of them are produced during fetal development in the thymus (Carding et al., 2002). 
 
What also distinguish γδ T cells from αβ T cells is that they are not restricted to the recognition of peptides 
presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) (Vantourout et al., 2013). γδ T cells lack expression of 
the CD4 and CD8 co-receptors. Instead, T cell receptor (TCR) γδ binds directly to an antigen’s superstructure 
independent of the MHC presenting peptides (Sato et al., 1993) (Kabelitz et al., 2013).  
γδ T lymphocytes are generated in the thymus from progenitor cells. Although γδ T cells and αβ T cells arise 
from the same progenitor, there is a difference in the anatomical distribution. Most of the αβ CD4+ T cells leave 
the thymus as naïve T cells and home to the lymph nodes (LNs) and the T cell zones in the spleen where they 
meet DCs presenting antigens, while γδ T cells can acquire their effector function already in the thymus where 
they are fully able to produce IFN-γ or IL-17. Some of them home to the LNs, others migrate to the tissues like 
the skin, intestine, lung and uterus (Papotto et al., 2017) (Vantourout et al, 2013).  
To sum up, the biggest differences between γδ and αβ T cells are their tissue distribution and the way they 
recognize antigens. 
 
The diversity of γδTCRs is more extensive than the diversity of αβ T cells and B cells combined (Carding et al., 
2002). γδ T cells have a T cell receptor that consist of one γ- and one δ-chain (TCRγ and TCRδ heterodimers) 
directing intracellular signaling through CD3 complexes (Deniger et al., 2014). The repertoire of human and 
mouse γδ T cells is based on TCRs made up of a variable γ- and δ-chain, so the different γδ T cell populations 
can be distinguished based on the differences in TCR Vγ- and Vδ-gene segment usage (Carding et al., 2002). In 
human, the two subsets Vδ1 and Vδ2 TCR predominate, and most of the tissue-associated γδ T cells are the Vδ1 
TCR (McVay et al., 1999) (Halary et al., 2005). Vγ5+ and Vγ6+ subsets are exclusively embryonic with Vγ5 
distributed in the epidermis and Vγ6 in the uterus, dermis and lung (Papotto et al., 2017). Peripheral γδ T cells are 
composed of Vγ1 and Vγ4 (Heilig et al., 1986) (Ito et al., 1988) (Pereira et al., 1995), but they are also present in 
the lymphoid tissue, liver and lung. Vγ4+ cells are also distributed in the dermis. The last subset to mention is the 
Vγ7-bearing cells in the intestinal epithelium. Vγ1+, Vγ4+ and Vγ7+ γδ T cells are found in both embryonic and 
postnatal life (Papotto et al., 2017). Relevant for this project to mention is that IL-17-production is mostly 
restricted to Vγ4+ or Vγ6+ T cells (Romani et al., 2008).

Having defined the γδ T cells, the next section will discuss a more specific subset, the IL-17-producing γδT cells

3.2 Activation of γδT17 cells and their effector mechanisms 
As shown in Figure 2, pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) like lipoproteins or lipopolysaccharides 
activate macrophages and DCs through the ligands TLR2 and TLR4. Then the APCs starts to secrete the cytokines 
IL-23 and IL-1β, stimulating the γδT17 cells to be activated (Sutton et al., 2009). γδT17 cells express the 
transcription factor RORγt and produce IL-17, IL-21 and IL-22 in response to IL-23 and IL-1β without the need 
for TCR activation (Sutton et al., 2009). Stimulation with IL-23 and IL-1β is sufficient for IL-17 production, 
while costimulatory receptors like CD28, BTLA and PD-1 on γδT17 cells are important for the expansion and 
regulation of γδT17 cells. CD28 has shown to be important for the expansion (Ribot et al., 2012), while both 
BTLA and PD-1 are able to negatively regulate the production of IL-17 by dermal γδ T cells and as such affecting 
psoriasis skin inflammation (Bekiaris et al., 2013) (Yasutomo et al., 2015). In the case of infection, IL-17 are able 
to attract inflammatory monocytes, promoting granuloma formation (Hamada et al., 2008). IL-17 production can 
also activate neutrophils which then participate in bacterial clearance and promote antigen presentation (Sumaria 
et al., 2011) as shown in Figure 2. Despite γδT17 cells being the first line of defense against infection, they can 
also do damage in the case of inflammatory and autoimmune diseases when infiltrating and accumulating in 
tissues of the skin and brain.

3.3 The role of γδT17 cells in diseases  
IL-17 is a very important target due to its immunotherapeutic potential. It has an important role in infection at 
epithelial barrier sites and to maintain the integrity of mucosal tissues (Veldhoen 2017). IL-17 has also shown to 
induce autoimmunity (Burkett et al., 2015) together with promoting or inhibiting tumor development and 
progression (Qian et al., 2017). 
 
After discovering that Th17 cells can secrete IL-17, it was found that also γδ T cells are capable of producing IL-
17. In fact, γδ T cells are able to make a faster response than Th17 cells because IL-17 mainly is provided by 
innate-like lymphocytes and cells of the innate immune system (Cua et al., 2010). Actually, γδ T cells is often 
considered to be the main producer of IL-17 in the case of inflammatory diseases, tissue homeostasis, infection 
autoimmune diseases and cancer (Papotto et al., 2017).  
 
3.4 The role of γδT17 cells in skin inflammation 
Murine dermal γδ T cells have received a lot of attention over the last years because they seem to be a key initiator 
in promoting the development of psoriasis. Likewise, the human dermis seems to contain γδ T cells equivalent to 
the dermal γδ T cells in mice (O’Brien et al., 2015). Nestle reported a novel pro-inflammatory γδ T cell, the 
Vγ9Vδ2 T subset, that is found in human blood and is rapidly recruited to the skin. They showed that in plaque 
psoriasis, numbers of circulating Vγ9Vδ2 T cells were decreased while they were increased in psoriatic skin. The 
decreased number of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells in the blood where normalized after treatment with psoriasis-targeted 
therapy, indicating clinical relevance for this subset of γδ T cells (Laggner et al., 2011).  
 
A previous study (Cai et al., 2011) has shown that dermal γδT17 cells in both mice and human play an important 
role in the pathogenesis of psoriasis. They discovered that innate dermal γδ T cells were the main producer of IL-
17 in the skin upon IL-23 stimulation and that the cells were present in high numbers in human psoriatic skin. 
Lack of γδ T cells or IL-17 receptor led to a significant decrease in epidermal thickening and infiltration of 
neutrophils. Another paper has also reported that the number of γδ T cells were significantly increased in plaque 
psoriasis (Seung et al., 2007). Skin samples from human psoriasis patients showed elevated numbers of dermal 
γδ T cells compared to normal skin. In fact, it was shown that in psoriatic skin there was an increase in γδT17 cell 
frequency to 15% compared to about 1% in normal skin (Cai et al., 2011).

Furthermore, the formation of plaques in the IMQ-induced psoriasis model in mice have shown to be dependent 
on RORγt+ Vγ4+ γδ T cells, and that these cells are the dominant source of the pathogenic cytokines IL-17A, IL-

other papers (Malhotra et al., 2013) (Gray et al., 2013). The two articles suggest that dermal Vγ4+ γδ T cells are 
the subset of γδ T cells that promote psoriasis. In the IMQ-induced psoriasis model, dermal Vγ6+ γδ T cells can 
induce some degree of skin inflammation, but dermal Vγ4+ γδ T cells are more pathogenic and are responsible 
for the development of psoriasis by being the primary source of IL-17 production (Cai et al., 2014). 
 
Regarding the pathogenicity of γδT17 cells during skin inflammation, Agerholm et al., recently highlighted the 
critical importance of STAT3. They showed for the first time, that STAT3 is necessary to induce the expansion 
of γδT17 and for the production of IL-22, IL-17A and IL-17F during IMQ-mediated skin inflammation. In the 
same paper, it was also demonstrated that STAT4 plays a role in maintaining optimal IL-17F levels during skin 
inflammation (Agerholm et al., 2019). In another paper, the same authors investigated the importance of Type I 
interferon (IFN-I) for the pro-inflammatory function of γδT3 cells. They demonstrated how γδT3 cells deficient 
in type I IFN receptor (IFNAR) signaling failed to induce skin inflammation and were unable to respond to 
cytokine stimuli (Agerholm et al., 2017). 
 
Section 4: Retinoic acid 
There are three main reasons to study the effect of the vitamin A metabolite retinoic acid (RA) in the context of 
psoriasis: Because of its known ability to promote Th17 cell differentiation during inflammatory conditions 
(Raverdeau et al., 2014); its suppressive effects on IL-17-production by γδ T cells (Raverdeau et al., 2016); and 
lastly because of the fact that topical application of retinoids is being used as therapeutic agents to suppress 
symptoms of psoriasis (Ding-Dar et al., 2009). The last reason has been the driving force of this project. 
 
Retinoic acid regulates a wide range of biological processes but RA signaling is dependent on cells that can 
metabolize vitamin A to RA.   
 
4.1 RA production and signaling  
The vitamin A metabolite is mainly produced by DCs in the gut, skin, lungs and draining LNs (Guilliams et al., 
2010). RA in the microenvironment can induce vitamin A metabolism in DCs and macrophages and as such, RA 
can be produced in DCs and macrophages to instruct T and B cells with RA (Molenaar et al., 2011).

Diet-derived vitamin A/retinol is absorbed by intestinal epithelial cells and DCs (Veldhoen et al., 2012). 
Afterwards it is metabolized into retinal and further RA, excreted and absorbed by other cells (McDonald et al., 
2012). Retinol can also be processed into retinyl ester, packed into chylomicrons and then transported via LNs to 
the liver for storage (Harrison et al., 2001). Subsequently, retinol is released into the circulation bound to retinol-
binding protein (RBP) and is then absorbed by cells via the retinoic-acid 6 receptor (STRA6) (Kawaguchi et al., 
2015). In the cytosol of these cells, retinol is metabolized to RA and transported to the nucleus. Here it binds to 
the retinoic acid receptors (RAR) α, β and γ and the retinoid X receptors (RXR) α, β and γ. This activates the 
RAR-RXR dimer and enables its binding to the RA response elements (RARE) in target genes and mediate its 
effector functions (Poon et al., 2008) (Kashyap et al., 2012). 
 
Besides RA playing an important role in the central nervous system (CNS) development by inducing neurogenesis 
(Yu et al., 2012), and its ability to induce cell differentiation and proliferation during spermatogenesis (Mezquita 
et al., 2019), RA plays an important role in the immune system ranging from development to homeostasis but 
also inflammation (Erkelens et al., 2017).  
 
4.2 The role of RA in immune development and homeostasis 
RA signaling is crucial for the regulation of normal development of innate lymphoid cell (ILC) progenitors and 
for the formation of SLOs by regulating the production of chemokines attracting lymphoid tissue inducer (LTi) 
cells and controlling the LTi cell maturation (Van de Pavert et al., 2014). 
 
The migration of DCs from the intestinal lamina propria towards the intestines requires the expression of gut-
homing molecules regulated by RA (Zeng et al., 2013).  
Also, in the gut associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), DCs and macrophages are able to produce RA for other 
immune cells by inducing the expression of the gut-homing molecules CCR9 and α4β7 on T and B cells (Iwata et 
al., 2004). RA are also able to upregulate CCR9 and α4β7 on type 1 and 3 ILCs (Kim et al., 2015).  
Unpublished data from Agerholm et al. suggests, that RA has an important role in the homeostasis of γδT17 cells 
in the LNs by regulating the balance between Vγ4+ and Vγ4- γδT17 cells (Agerholm et al., unpublished data). 
 
Despite RA’s role in development and homeostasis of immune cells, it has also shown to be important for T cell 
responses. 
 
4.3 The role of RA in T cell responses  
RA plays a role in balancing T helper cell responses and for proper T helper cell responses.

During steady-state, RA has shown to be crucial for the induction of Th1 and Th17 responses (Hall et al., 2011), 
and RA has also shown to be essential for regulating the transition from Th17 to Th1 (Brown et al., 2015). 
The role of regulating the balance between Th17 and regulatory T cell (Treg) responses has also been investigated. 
During steady-state, RA produced by DCs blocks the cytokines IL-6, IL-21 and IL-23 in naïve T cells, so that 
they cannot differentiate into Th17 cells (Bettelli et al. 2006) (Mucida et al., 2007) (Xiao et al., 2008), as well as 
blocking the production of IL-4, IL-21 and IFN-γ by CD4+CD44high cells that used to inhibit the conversion into 
Treg cells, allowing the naïve T cells to differentiate into Treg cells (Hill et al., 2008). Thus, Treg cells are induced 
at the cost of Th17 cells. 
To sum up, RA induce Treg cells and inhibits the differentiation of Th1 and Th17 cells, promoting a tolerogenic 
environment, since lack of Treg cells is associated with autoimmune diseases (Erkelens et al., 2017). 
However, during inflammatory conditions, RA are able to induce Th1 and Th17 and thereby activating pathogenic 
T cells and consequently enhancing inflammation (Raverdeau et al., 2014). 
 
In summary, during steady state, RA plays a role in maintaining tolerance, while it promotes effector T cell 
responses during inflammation (Mielke et al., 2013). This is an example of how RA can regulate the balance 
between pro- and anti-inflammatory immunity. 
 
4.4 The role of RA in inflammation 
During inflammatory stimuli, DCs has been reported to produce more RA (Yokota et al., 2009). Furthermore, the 
RA signaling has shown to occur concurrent with the development of inflammation and that the RA signaling 
was increased at the site of inflammation (Pino-Lagos et al., 2011). 
 
A previous paper (Raverdeau et al. 2016) investigated the role of RA on IL-17-producing γδ T cells in vitro and 
in vivo. When stimulating γδ T cells in vitro with IL-1β and IL-23, they showed that RA had a suppressive effect 
on IL-17A and IL-17F production. Upon in vivo studies in mice, treatment with RA showed to suppress IL-17 
production by γδ T cells, thereby inhibiting their pathogenic activity in autoimmune disease. This inhibitory effect 
on IL-17 production by γδT cells is mainly on the Vγ4+ subset. They also demonstrated the effect of RA on γδ T 
cells by showing that RA suppressed the expression of RORγt, IL-1R and IL-23R in γδ T cells. Lastly, they 
demonstrated that RA had a better effect on γδ17 cells than Th17 cells (Raverdeau et al. 2016). 
Overall, this demonstrates how RA can have suppressive properties on the pathogenic function of γδ T cells. 
 
It has been reported that RA induces the production of IL-22 by γδ T cells stimulated with IL-1β or IL-18 and IL-
23 in vitro, and that IL-22 production mediates protection in colon-induced inflammation (Mielke et al., 2013),.

IL-22 has shown to serve a protective role by reducing inflammation. Likewise, dysregulation of IL-22 has been 
linked with autoimmune diseases such as psoriasis (Perusina et al., 2016). Hence, these studies suggest an anti-
inflammatory function of RA. 
 
4.5 RA-induced cell death in keratinocytes as a potent trigger of inflammation  
RA has shown to be involved in both intrinsic and extrinsic apoptotic pathways. Regarding the intrinsic pathway, 
it has been reported that RA are able to upregulate the expression of caspase 3, 6, 7 and 9 in keratinocytes (73). 
Also, casp9 is a direct target gene for RAR (33). RA is involved in the extrinsic pathway by enhancing Fas-
mediated apoptosis (Peter et al., 2003; Engedal et al., 2008) and regulating the expression of casp8 (Altucci et al., 
2001). Multiple papers suggest that RA may target death ligands and their receptors in the extrinsic pathway 
(Altucci et al., 2001; Clarke et al., 2004).  
Studies based on mouse models have shown that keratinocytes are sensitive to apoptosis or necroptosis causing 
severe inflammatory skin lesions. Cell death in keratinocytes is a known prominent feature of many inflammatory 
skin diseases and it has been shown that genetic mouse models perturbing cell death pathways promote skin 
inflammation and immune cell recruitment due to necroptosis of keratinocytes (Kumari et al., 2017).  
 
The release of cytokines by dying keratinocytes is considered to be a mediating factor for inflammatory responses 
and recruitment of immune cells. It has been shown that Aldara™ is able to induce cell death and inflammasome 
activation in human and mouse keratinocytes, independent of TLR7 and IMQ. Aldara™-induced inflammasome 
activation caused death of human keratinocytes in a caspase-1-dependent lysis, i.e. pyroptosis. Activation of the 
inflammasome resulted in the release of pro-IL-1α and maturation of pro-IL-1β to the active IL-1β. Furthermore, 
Aldara™ induces G-CSF production by fibroblasts and IL-23 production by DCs and macrophages which induce 
secretion of IL-17 by γδ T cells. Both IL-17 and G-CSF have an impact on proliferation of keratinocytes. Aldara™ 
has also shown to induce S100A8/A9 and CXCL1, which are strong chemoattractants for neutrophils together 
with IL-1 (Walter et al., 2013). See Figure 3 below for a graphic representation of Aldara™-induced cell death 
and inflammasome activation in keratinocytes.

Section 5: Preliminary data 
Even though it has been shown before that RA are able to induce IL-22 production and suppress IL-17 production 
by γδ T cells (Raverdeau et al. 2016) (Mielke et al., 2013), our research group was not able to replicate this. We 
did not observe changes in IL-22 or IL-17 production at steady-state or after IMQ-application in RORγtCRE-
RARdnF/F mice. On the other hand, we have preliminary data showing that topical application of RA in the IMQ-
induced model of psoriasis exacerbates inflammation together with an increase in infiltrating monocytes and 
neutrophils. This suggests that RA can be very pathogenic during skin inflammation and that it works by 
regulating monocyte and neutrophil recruitment independent of γδ T cells (Agerholm et al., unpublished data).

Section 6: Aims and Objectives 
The overall aim of this thesis will be to test the importance of monocytes/macrophages and neutrophils in RA-
induced skin inflammation. The project can be divided into two parts: 
 
1.) Replication of preliminary data showing that topical application of RA in IMQ-induced psoriasis 
model leads to extensive inflammation accompanied by very high numbers of infiltrating 
monocytes/macrophages and neutrophils. 
2.) To test the importance of monocytes/macrophages and neutrophils in RA-induced skin inflammation. 
 
To achieve the abovementioned aims, the approach will be to induce inflammation in mice and then assess 
inflammation using standard techniques in the lab, i.e. lymphocyte preparations, flow cytometry, histological 
analysis and basic light microscopy. The depletion-antibodies anti-CCR2 and anti-Ly6G were used to deplete 
either monocytes or neutrophils to test the importance of these inflammatory lymphocytes in driving skin 
inflammation.  
 
We hypothesize that RA acts directly on monocytes/macrophages and/or neutrophils independent of T cells in 
order to exacerbate inflammation.

RESULTS 
RA-induced skin inflammation reveals a potential role for neutrophils and monocytes 
On the basis of the preliminary data showing that topical application of RA exacerbates skin inflammation in the 
IMQ-induced psoriasis model as well as very high numbers of infiltrating monocytes and neutrophils being 
observed in that context, we wanted to investigate which population arrives to the skin first during these 
inflammatory conditions. We hypothesized whether neutrophils recruit monocytes or vice versa. To do so, we 
started a time course where mice were sacrificed after two, four and seven days of IMQ application, respectively, 
such that the monocyte’s and neutrophil’s arrival to the skin in the context of RA-induced skin inflammation 
could be investigated.  
An increase in epidermal thickening between RA-treated mice and controls can be seen histologically on the H&E 
pictures in Figure 4A and in the graph. After two days of IMQ treatment, there was a significant albeit small 
increase in the length of the epidermis while after four and seven days, the epidermis was significantly more 
inflamed for the mice that were treated with RA than untreated mice and the mice treated with IMQ only. 
The infiltration of CD11b+Ly6G+ neutrophils in the skin was investigated, and a significant increase in both 
frequency and cell numbers was observed after two days of IMQ treatment, as depicted in Figure 4B. The effect 
became more pronounced after four days. The results from the experiment with seven days of IMQ application 
indicates insufficient incubation, as the control has higher amounts of neutrophils compared to the IMQ+DMSO 
samples and because the DMSO and RA-treated mice have more neutrophils after four days than after seven days. 
Because of the high infiltration of neutrophils after four days of IMQ+RA treatment, the frequency of 
CD11b+Ly6G- monocytes is shown to be reduced.  
Considering the monocyte infiltration shown in Figure 4C, a significant increase is observed in the frequency but 
not numbers of Ly6C++MHC-II- juvenile monocytes (i.e. P1) in RA-treated mice compared to DMSO mice after 
four days of IMQ application. Also, a small significant increase of Ly6C++MHC-II+ inflammatory monocytes (i.e. 
P2) occurred in mice treated with RA compared to mice treated with DMSO after seven days of IMQ treatment.  
Overall, measuring the epidermis revealed a dramatic increase in the length during RA-induced skin 
inflammation, most prominent after four and seven days. Upon analyzing, it was found that the frequency and 
numbers of neutrophils were high after already two days of IMQ+RA treatment. Frequency and cell numbers of 
monocytes were not remarkably changed. To conclude, the data suggest that neutrophils are recruited before 
monocytes during RA-induced skin inflammation.

RA exacerbates skin inflammation independent of T and B cells 
After excluding a potential role for γδ T cells, we wanted to investigate whether the IMQ+RA inflammation is T 
cell driven or purely innate. In order to test the role of RA in T cells, we used RAG-deficient mice lacking all 
mature T and B lymphocytes and induced psoriasis in the mice with use of IMQ in the presence of RA or DMSO 
as vehicle control.  
As shown in Figure 6A, representative pictures and graph indicate that the RA-treated RAG-deficient mice were 
significantly more inflamed compared to controls. Actually, RA-treated mice showed around the same epidermal 
thickening as we use to see for WT mice treated with IMQ+RA for seven days. 
Figure 6B shows the gating strategy for CD11b+Ly6G+ neutrophils and CD11b+Ly6G- monocytes within the 
CD45+ gate. Graphs confirmed a significant increase in both frequency and numbers of neutrophils and therefore 
a reduction in the frequency of total monocytes. 
In Figure 6C, it was observed that numbers of Ly6C++MHC-II- juvenile monocytes (i.e. P1) had a small but 
significant increase for RA-treated mice compared to controls.  
It is important to note that even though RAG-deficient mice lack all mature B and T cells, they still have ILCs 
and consequently, ILCs might compensate for the loss of IL-17-production by T cells. Hence, IL-7R and CCR6 
was included in the staining panel, and the results are shown in Figure 6D. After gating out CD11b, CD11c, 
MHC-II and Ly6G, a clear CCR6+IL-7R+ population could be detected. As shown in the graphs, there was a small 
but not significant decrease in frequency of CCR6+ ILC3s, while there was no difference in cell count in RA-
treated mice compared to controls, indicating that RA-induced skin inflammation in RAG-deficient mice is not 
ILC-driven. 
Collectively, this data has convinced us that even in RAG-deficient mice there is still a strong 
inflammation in RA-treated mice compared to controls, indicating a T cell independent mechanism and 
furthermore, there is no indications that ILC3s should compensate for the loss of T cells.

RA-induced skin inflammation is potentially independent of monocytes  
Having clarified that the RA-induced skin inflammation is a T and B cell independent mechanism, we 
hypothesized that RA acts directly on either monocytes or neutrophils.  
In order to test the role of monocytes, we depleted the monocytes with use of anti-CCR2 as described in Materials 
and Methods. As shown in Figure 7A, there was no observed difference in epidermal thickening between RA-
treated mice and the mice that received both RA and anti-CCR2, indicating that monocytes does not contribute 
to skin inflammation in the context of RA. However, there was hardly any difference in epidermal thickening for 
RA-treated mice compared to DMSO. In our experience, after seven days of IMQ application without RA, the 
epidermis is typically around 40-50 µm thick, while said experiment resulted in a 70 µm thick epidermis. We 
reasoned that too high dosages of IMQ was used (i.e. up to 10 mg) which enhanced the inflammation, while the 
RA samples were already strongly inflamed. Therefore, we went back to using a dosage of strictly 7 mg which 
solved the problem in all the other experiments carried out in this project.  
Figure 7B shows the gating for CD11b+Ly6G+ neutrophils and CD11b+Ly6G- monocytes within the CD3-CD45+ 
gate. Graphs confirmed a significant decrease in both frequency and numbers of monocytes during anti-CCR2 
injections which is in consistent with the higher frequency of neutrophils as monocytes were depleted. 
In Figure 7C, the depletion was further demonstrated by a significant decrease in frequency of Ly6C++MHC-II- 
(P1, juvenile monocytes), a decrease in both frequency and numbers of Ly6C++MHC-II+ (P2, inflammatory 
monocytes) and lastly a decrease in frequency and cell count of Ly6C+MHC-II+ (P3, resident monocytes). 
 
Collectively, this data indicates that RA plays a role in driving skin inflammation potentially independent 
of monocytes, however the histology data needs to be improved and replicated in order to arrive at a clearer 
conclusion. Since it was only the DMSO mice and not the RA-treated that were more inflamed than usual, we 
still believe that this experiment can indicate a monocyte independent mechanism.

RA exacerbates skin inflammation potentially dependent on neutrophils 
We attempted to deplete neutrophils in the skin and spleen with anti-Ly6G as described in Materials and Methods. 
As shown in Figure 8A, H&E pictures and graph indicate that the RA-treated mice were highly inflamed with a 
significant increase in epidermal thickening compared to controls. A small but not significant decrease was 
observed for the mice that received both RA and anti-Ly6G injections. 
Gating on CD11b+Ly6G+ skin cells in Figure 8B failed to detect any neutrophils with the Ly6G marker, 
misleading us to think that the depletion worked. However, the neutrophils were still present as CD11bhi in 
mentioned plot. In the spleen, no neutrophils were detected in the CD11b+Ly6G+ gate. It has been suggested 
elsewhere that identifying neutrophils based on Ly6G expression after depleting with anti-Ly6G is uncertain, 
because the depleting antibody is masking or internalizing the epitope of the Ly6G antigen, so that fluorescently 
labeled antibodies cannot bind (Faget et al., 2018). Therefore, other surface markers than Ly6G should be used 
in order to assess neutrophils.  
The alternative gating strategy is shown in Figure 8C where Ly6Chi and CD11c+ were gated out and neutrophils 
were found as CD11bhiMHC-II+ cells. Backgating with SSC-A/FSC-A plot (not shown) confirmed that the cell 
population had increased side scatter which indicates high granularity. Graphs in lower panel showed no decrease 
in neutrophils for mice receiving anti-Ly6G injections, neither in the skin nor in the spleen. On the other hand, 
the DMSO samples were again too high compared to RA-treated mice making it difficult to conclude anything 
reasonable in the context of RA’s role in driving skin inflammation. Also, the data in Fig. 9B does not reflect the 
high increase in neutrophils upon RA-treatment as we have seen in previous experiments.  
The last figure (Figure 8D) demonstrated a significant increase in frequency but not numbers of juvenile 
Ly6C++MHC-II- monocytes (i.e. P1) for the mice that received anti-Ly6G. However, the P1 population is from 
the CD11b+Ly6G- gate, where some of the neutrophils were also found, meaning that some of the neutrophils are 
to be found in the total CD11b+Ly6G- monocyte gate. 
The attempt to deplete neutrophils in the skin and spleen failed, so optimization with higher doses and 
more frequent injections are needed, leaving open the question whether RA acts directly on neutrophils to 
exacerbate skin inflammation.

DISCUSSION 
A time course study was conducted to understand the arrival of monocytes and neutrophils to the skin during RA-
induced skin inflammation. Since we have never done a time course in the context of RA before and given the 
fact that monocytes and neutrophils get activated much faster than T cells, it was very informative to do a time 
course. We found that already after two days, the epidermis of RA-treated mice was more inflamed, while both 
controls (i.e. the untreated and the IMQ+DMSO-treated mice) remained unchanged. Further, after these two days 
of IMQ-treatment, neutrophils began to arrive to the skin of the RA-treated mice, which did not occur to the same 
extent as for the two controls. In this early timepoint, IMQ did not seem to contribute to inflammation, and it 
followed that RA itself was sufficient to promote the disease symptoms. In the light of these findings, it would 
provide useful information to redo the experiment without IMQ to assess whether RA is driving initiation or 
solely progression of the disease. After four and seven days of IMQ, conclusive evidence revealed that the RA-
treated mice were more inflamed. While frequency of juvenile monocytes was increased in the RA-treated mice, 
the number of infiltrating monocytes in general was not remarkably changed during topical application of RA. 
Nevertheless, we cannot dismiss that monocytes could still be important, hence our data suggest a potential role 
for neutrophils in RA-induced skin inflammation, while further research should be conducted to understand the 
role of monocytes. 
In view of the importance of γδ T cells as a key initiator in driving plaque psoriasis (Cai et al., 2011) and the fact 
that RA has shown to suppress IL-17 production by γδ T cells (Raverdeau et al., 2016), an experiment was 
designed to investigate if the RA-induced skin inflammation is γδ T cell dependent. The results showed that RA 
drives skin inflammation independently of γδ T cells, suggesting that exogenous RA do not impact γδ T cells. 
On the basis of our results suggesting a γδ T cell independent mechanism, we raised the question: is the 
mechanism T cell driven at all or purely innate? In this project, RAG-deficient mice were used for the first time 
to investigate the role of RA in driving skin inflammation in the absence of all mature T and B lymphocytes, and 
it was found that RA regulated neutrophils and monocytes independently of T and B cells. Our data further 
demonstrated that the RA-induced skin inflammation is not ILC-driven in RAG-deficient mice. A previous study 
(Van der Fits et al., 2009) had shown that T cell deficiency in RAG2-/- mice resulted in reduced IMQ-induced 
skin inflammation, but in present project we still observed IMQ-induced skin inflammation in the RAG-deficient 
mice, suggesting that elevated numbers of ILCs present in RAG-deficient mice would compensate for the loss of 
IL-17-production by T cells.

Successful depletion of monocytes did not seem to affect the length of the epidermis in RA-induced skin 
inflammation, indicating a monocyte independent mechanism, but the experiment needs to be replicated with 
precisely 7 mg of IMQ instead of up to 10 mg IMQ to achieve a significant difference in the epidermal thickening 
between DMSO and RA-treated mice. 
Attempt to deplete neutrophils in the skin and spleen failed, so optimization with higher and more frequent doses 
of anti-Ly6G is needed. However, according to the paper by Faget et al. (2018), anti-Ly6G is not capable of 
depleting neutrophils in C57BL/6 mice due to rapid mobilization of immature neutrophils from the bone marrow 
to the periphery. But combined with a mouse IgG2a antibody recognizing the rat IgGκ light chain that will bind 
to anti-Ly6G, it is possible to obtain specific and efficient neutrophil depletion. Although anti-Ly6G failed to 
deplete neutrophils, data suggests that it still binds to Ly6G and impacted the neutrophil function, as a small 
reduction of epidermal thickening was observed. Due to lack of scientific articles describing successful neutrophil 
depletion in the skin, we reasoned that the failed depletion of neutrophils could be tissue specific.  
Our overall conclusion in this project, that RA exacerbates skin inflammation is particularly relevant because 
topical retinoids are being used in the clinic as antipsoriatic treatments (Van De Kerkhof, 2006). The reason is 
likely because they are known to inhibit keratinocyte differentiation, but the molecular effects in the epidermis 
has not been fully defined (Lee et al., 2009). In this project, we have provided evidence that topical application 
of RA led to extensive inflammation accompanied by very high numbers of infiltrating monocytes and neutrophils 
in the skin, but the cellular mechanisms of action are still unknown. In order to obtain better understanding of 
how RA acts on monocytes and neutrophils in order to drive skin inflammation, more research is needed, as 
suggested in below paragraphs. 
As RA has shown to regulate several genes associated with programmed cell death (Lee et al., 2009), we reason 
that RA could influence apoptosis in keratinocytes. The aforementioned paper also showed that RA was able to 
induce genes that promote apoptosis of keratinocytes including caspase 3, 6, 7, 8 and 9. We hypothesize that RA 
acts directly on keratinocytes to induce a caspase-independent form of programmed cell death (i.e. necroptosis) 
or inflammasome-induced pyroptosis through activation of a caspase cascade. This is likely to result in the release 
of proinflammatory cytokines like IL-1β and TNF-α, which are both able to induce IL-8 (Rascher-Eggstein et al., 
2004), most known for its role in chemoattraction of neutrophils, but can also recruit monocytes and lymphocytes 
to the site of inflammation (Turner et al., 2014). In order to test this hypothesis, it would be relevant to do a time 
course and measure cytokines and chemokines (i.e. IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-8) while also staining for death markers 
like caspase 3 and 8 to keep track of apoptotic keratinocytes.

We reasoned that since RA is applied topically, it likely works directly on keratinocytes and these have shown to 
respond very fast to RA (Lee et al., 2009). Hence, it could prove informative to use the transgenic mice K14-Cre 
that express Cre recombinase under the control of the human keratin 14 (K14) promoter providing a useful tool 
for creating keratinocyte-specific knockout mice (The Jackson Laboratory, K14-Cre). This would provide insight 
into the direct effects of RA on keratinocytes. 
Lastly, to really emphasize the role of monocytes and neutrophils in RA-induced skin inflammation, it is 
suggested to create knockout mice by crossing LySM-cre mice to a RAR dominant negative (RARdn) transgenic 
line that prevents active RARα signaling RARdnF/F (Rajaii et al., 2008) to target myeloid cells, including 
monocytes, macrohages and neutrophils (The Jackson Laboratory, LysMcre). Using Cxcr1-Cre mice could also 
be relevant to test the importance of monocytes, since the generated knockout mice do not express the chemokine 
CX3CR1 required for monocyte homeostasis (The Jackson Laboratory, Cx3cr1).

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Mice 
To investigate these hypotheses presented in this thesis, normal C57BL/6 mice and RAG-deficient mice were 
used. Animal breeding and experiments were performed in house at DTU Health Technology. Mice were only 
anesthetized and sacrificed by PhD students/PI provided with a license from the Danish Animal Experiments 
Inspectorate.  
 
IMQ-induced psoriasis model 
To induce psoriasis, 7-10 mg of 5 % AldaraTM cream (MEDA) was applied daily for seven days at the dorsal side 
of both ears. Before application, each cream sample was diluted in 5 µl DMSO. Some of the mice were treated 
with a mix of IMQ and RA. Stock solutions of 100 mM RA was diluted with DMSO to a concentration of 10 mM 
and aliquoted into Eppendorf tubes. Immediately before application, the aliquots were further diluted 1:5 with 
DMSO. During IMQ-application, the mice were anesthetized using isoflurane.  
Monocyte depletion 
Monocytes were depleted by administering anti-CCR2 (MC-21) as i.p. injections. Due to inflammatory 
conditions, a high amount of depleting antibody was used, i.e. 30 µg/mouse/day or 16.2 µl/mouse/day diluted in 
PBS to a total volume of 200 µl per injection. In order to achieve prolonged depletion of monocytes, daily 
injections for five days were performed. About five days after the first injection, neutralizing antibodies against 
MC-21 starts to develop and MC-21 is no longer active. Therefore, the five days of injection started after two 
days of IMQ-application.  
Neutrophil depletion 
To deplete neutrophils, mice were injected i.p. with 300 µg or 36.7 µl/mouse anti-Ly6G (clone 1A8, BioXCell) 
after two and five days of IMQ-application. Depleting antibody was diluted in PBS to a total volume of 200 µl 
per injection. 
Wash and culture media 
 
Dissected LNs and ears were kept in wash media containing RPMI 1640 (Invitrogen) with 10 % Fetal Bovine 
Serum (FBS) and 10,000 U/mL penicillin-streptomycin. Wash media was also used for washing cells. For 
resuspension and incubation, culture media was used containing RPMI 1640 (Invitrogen) with 10 % Fetal Bovine 
Serum (FBS) (Gibco), 10,000 U/mL penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco), 50 µM 2-mercaptoethanol (β-ME), 20 mM 
HEPES (Gibco), 2 mM L-Glutamine (Gibco).

Lymph node cell preparation 
Dissected LNs were cleaned for surrounding fat with forceps and paper, saved in 1 mL wash media and kept on 
ice. The LNs were crushed through a 70 µm strainer and the cells were washed by flushing the filters with 5 mL 
wash media. The remaining cell suspension was spun down at 1500 rpm for 5 min. at 4℃. After discarding the 
supernatant, the cells were resuspended in 2 mL culture media. After resuspension, the cell suspension was filtered 
through a 40 µm strainer. Then 80 µl of each LN sample was added to Eppendorf-tubes for cell count on Sysmex. 
Cells were then resuspended at a density of 107 cells/ml with culture media.  
Skin cell preparation and histology 
Ears were cut off, saved in wash media and stored on ice. For histology, a long and thin slice of the ear was cut 
and packed with Linsenpapier, put into yellow clamps and fixed with 10 % formalin overnight. Paraffin embedded 
sections were H&E stained. Skin lymphocytes were prepared by separating the ears into two layers and kept in 
wash media, while digestion enzymes were prepared. Culture media was mix with 0.25 mg/ml collagenase IV (1 
aliquot/2 mice), 0.166 mg/ml hyaluronidase (1 mg/6 ml culture media) and 0.1 mg/ml DNase I (25 µl/mice). Each 
ear sample was added to 5 ml of enzyme mix in a tube, after which the ears were cut into very small pieces. The 
ear tissues were left for stirring with a magnet at 800 rpm for 1 hour at 37℃. After digestion, skin lymphocytes 
were prepared in the same way as for the LNs.  
Spleen cell preparation 
Spleens were dissected and saved in 1 mL was media and kept on ice. The spleens were crushed through a  
70 µm strainer and the cells were washed by flushing the filters with 5 mL wash media. The remaining cell 
suspension was spun down at 1500 rpm for 5 min. at 4℃. After discarding the supernatant, 1 mL of Red Blood 
Cell (RBC) lysis buffer was added to each sample and stored on ice for 5 min. After lysing red blood cells, 5 mL 
culture media was added, and the cell suspension was spun down. After discarding the supernatant, the cells were 
resuspended in 4 mL culture media. After resuspension, the cell suspension was filtered through a 40 µm strainer. 
Then 80 µl of each spleen sample was added to Eppendorf-tubes for cell count on Sysmex. Cells were then 
resuspended at a density of 107 cells/ml with culture media. 
Fc block 
Before surface staining, skin samples were pre-incubated with mouse BD Fc BlockTM. This was done in order to 
avoid the non-specific binding of monocytes, macrophages and neutrophils to antibodies through their Fc 
receptor. BD Fc BlockTM (2.4G2 antibody) binds to mouse FcγI (CD64), FcγII (CD32) and FcγIII (CD64) 
receptors and as such blocks non-antigen-specific binding of antibodies (BD Biosciences 2019). BD Fc BlockTM

Surface staining
