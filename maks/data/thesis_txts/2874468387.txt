Abstract
With the increasing penetration of renewable energy connected to the grid, higher requirements
for the dynamic performance and fault ride-through capability of grid-connected converters are
proposed. Meanwhile, the grid tied converters have been seeing a continuously increase in both
capacity and voltage levels. This leads to the need for medium-voltage, high-power testing
platforms. However, most existing grid emulators operate only at low voltage levels. This
thesis carries out the design and verification of a power electronic building block (PEBB) for a
cell of a cascaded H-bridge (CHB) topology, aiming to provide a high-bandwidth, high-power-
density hardware foundation for a medium-voltage grid emulator (MVGE).
The thesis first completes the design of the PEBB power stage, including the selected power
semiconductor devices, the calculation of DC link capacitance, and the design of a voltage
limiting unit (VLU), and verifies its ability to limit transient overvoltage through simulation.
Subsequently, the design and thermal verification of key hardware such as current sensing
boards, DC link board, DC capacitor board, and heatsinks are completed to ensure their
thermal stability under rated operating conditions.
In terms of control and protection, gate driver boards integrating device overcurrent and over-
voltage protection for both the AFE and HB stages are also designed in this thesis. An RT-Box
expansion board is developed to realize the hardware implementation of rapid control prototyp-
ing (RCP) and protection logic. Based on the above hardware, the integration and open-loop
function verification of the PEBB is completed, including no-load/load switching tests and fast
response verification of DC link overvoltage and overcurrent protection.
In order to realize full-power, full-function testing, two back-to-back testing schemes, AC power
supply driven and DC power supply driven, are proposed and compared. Simulation analysis
show that the DC power supply driven scheme has more advantages in terms of hardware
requirements, construction complexity, and experimental feasibility, and is finally selected for
subsequent thermal stability evaluation and control algorithm development.
This thesis presents the design of a submodule for a CHB-based MVGE and the development
of dedicated testing methods for its individual components and functions, followed by the
integrated testing of a complete submodule. Furthermore, two full-power, full-function testing
schemes are proposed and comparatively evaluated, providing a robust hardware foundation
for the future implementation of a CHB-based grid emulator.

Motivation and Background
In recent years, the field of power electronics has seen significant advancements, including the
increasing penetration of renewable energy sources and their integration into the power grid
through power electronics-based converters. As the share of such converter-based generation
goes up, the performance requirements for grid-tied converters, particularly in terms of fault
ride-through (FRT) and voltage support capabilities, have become significantly more stringent
[1, 2, 3].
These evolving demands are reflected in national and regional grid codes, which
explicitly define the dynamic response criteria for converters during grid disturbances.
An
overview of such grid code requirements across different countries is provided in Figure 1 [4].
At the same time, the interaction between the grid and the converter-based generation has
become more complex, leading to new stability issues [5, 6, 7]. While offline simulation tools,
which rely on static and linear models, can provide initial insights, they are often insuﬀicient to
fully capture the complex and non-linear behaviours of grid-converter interactions, particularly
under weak grid or fault conditions. Therefore, dedicated testing platforms are required to verify
compliance with grid codes and to assess the dynamic performance of grid-tied converters under
realistic conditions.
Grid emulators (GEs) have thus emerged in testing systems. Fundamentally, a GE functions as a
programmable and controllable power source capable of replicating the electrical characteristics
of real-world power grids. These characteristics typically include voltage amplitude, frequency,
phase angle, grid impedance, and transient behavior. Most GEs operate as voltage sources
with configurable output voltages, enabling the emulation of steady-state and transient grid
conditions such as voltage sags and frequency deviations. They allow for safe, repeatable, and
comprehensive testing of grid-tied converters under various grid conditions. Moreover, they
support the development and tuning of control strategies to enhance system stability under
various equivalent grid impedance [7].
Meanwhile, at the device under test (DUT) side, namely the grid-tied converter, both capacity
and voltage levels are continuously increasing. As an example, the state-of-the-art capacity of
the wind turbines is 20 MW currently, but it is expected to reach 22 MW in the near future [8].
Simultaneously, the output voltage of the wind turbines is also rising. While the traditional
output voltage of wind turbines is 690 V, 3.3 kV wind turbine projects are ongoing [9, 10]. These
trends reflect an increasing demand for GEs designed for medium-voltage (MV) and high-power
operation.
However, existing GEs are limited to low-voltage levels [11, 12], and medium-voltage GEs are
still lacking. The absence of a suitable MVGE represents a significant challenge for testing grid-
connected converters with increasing capacity under real-world operating conditions. Therefore,
at the power electronics laboratory (PEL) at EPFL, a project of MVGE is under development
and aims to fill this gap by proposing a flexible, modular, and high-performance MVGE that
can serve as a general platform for testing any medium-voltage grid-tied devices, such as grid-
forming converters and DC distribution networks.
In medium-voltage applications, various modular power converter topologies are available, in-
cluding cascaded or interleaved neutral point clamped (NPC) converter [13, 14, 15, 16], the
modular multilevel converter (MMC) [17, 18, 19] and the cascaded H-bridge (CHB) converter
[20, 21, 22, 23]. In this thesis, the CHB topology stands out as a preferred choice for an MVGE

Since a GE is designed to reproduce the dynamic behavior of grid voltages and currents, it
enables the emulation of grid impedance at high bandwidths. This is made possible by the
series connection of submodules combined with carrier phase-shifted PWM, which effectively
increases the equivalent switching frequency and output bandwidth. Each submodule employs
2
==PAGE:15==
an H-bridge (HB) configuration to generate bipolar output voltages, and to further extend the
bandwidth and enhance the switching performance, silicon carbide (SiC) MOSFETs are used in
the HB stage. The use of SiC devices enables switching frequencies up to 80 kHz, far exceeding
the typical 10 kHz limit of Si-based IGBT switches. This higher switching capability enhances
the controller bandwidth and improves the fidelity of grid impedance emulation. Furthermore,
to accommodate the need for bidirectional power flow, required for emulating both power
absorption from and injection into the grid, an active front end (AFE) configuration, rather
than the diode front end (DFE) commonly used in conventional CHB converters, is adopted in
this project. Such AFE-based CHB converters feature bidirectional operation capability and
can realize four-quadrant operation attracting a lot of research interests in past years [24].
While most of the published literature has focused on control and modeling aspects, the ex-
perimental validation and power hardware testing of modular converters remain insuﬀiciently
documented. To bridge this gap, several test methodologies have been proposed in the litera-
ture, aiming to enable experimental validation at the submodule level. A resonant test scheme
for MMC submodules is proposed in reference [25]. In this method, the required sinusoidal cur-
rent for the submodule can be provided through resonance, allowing for rated current switching
test. A platform using two submodules in back-to-back connection is proposed in [26]. This
platform can accurately control the current flowing through the submodules, which allows for
electro-thermal behavior testing. Based on this, reference [27] proposes an improved mission
profile emulator for MMC submodules. It uses a half-bridge topology and a PIR controller,
further reducing costs and achieving more precise control of the arm current and capacitor
voltage. However, in these test methods, the controller needs to be adjusted specifically for
the test method, excluding the controller in the testing scheme. To solve the shortcoming, a
test method based on power amplifier is proposed to systematically verify all functions of the
submodule, including the controller and protection [28, 29]. However, it requires an expen-
sive power amplifier, and, the interaction between Hardware-in-the-loop (HIL) and the power
amplifier might introduce stability issues to the system [29]. In summary, these test methods
either cannot perform all functional tests or require expensive testing equipment. Therefore, a
new test method suitable for submodules needs to be proposed.
The objectives of this master thesis project can be summarized as follows:
• Develop and validate a modular power electronic building block (PEBB) for a CHB-based
MVGE.
• Ensure protection and reliable operation of the developed PEBB.
• Propose a cost-effective test platform for full-power evaluation of the PEBBs under real-
istic operating conditions.
1.2
System Overview
This MVGE aims to emulate an MV grid for research activities. The rated power of the GE
is 1 MVA, which is twice the capacity of the existing 500 kVA MMC [30] and ongoing 500 kVA
Matrix Modular Multilevel Converter (M3C) [31] at PEL, both serving as devices under test.
Such a high-power CHB-based GE will be connected with the PEL’s 1 MVA multi-winding
transformer as shown in Figure 3. The transformer features a 6 kV primary winding and five
secondary windings per phase, each rated at 710 V. Each secondary winding supplies one CHB
3
==PAGE:16==
cell, resulting in five cells per phase. The secondary windings are phase-shifted by 12◦relative to
one another (i.e., 60◦/5 = 12◦). This phase-shifting enabling harmonic cancellation and thereby
reduce the total harmonic distortion (THD) in the synthesized output voltage in DFE-based
CHB cells. In AFE-based configuration, this feature is less critical; nevertheless, it remains
inherent to the transformer. Based on this transformer winding arrangement, the ratings of
individual CHB cells can be then defined. Each cell is rated at 65 kVA. To achieve the desired
output voltage of 6 kV AC or 10 kV DC, the DC link voltage of each cell is set to 1200 V. A
summarized cell specification can be found in Table 1.

Power Stage Design
As introduced in Section 1.2, each CHB submodule in the MVGE is rated at 65 kVA, with a
rated input of 710 V three-phase line voltage at the AFE side and an output peak voltage of

The switching frequencies are 20 kHz for the HB stage and 5 kHz for the AFE stage. To meet
these specifications, a robust and eﬀicient power stage must be designed, including appropriate
power semiconductor devices, DC link capacitors, and protection circuits.

2.2.3
Issues of Overvoltage and Undervoltage
In addition to determining the amplitude of the second-harmonic ripple on the DC link voltage,
the DC link capacitor also plays a critical role in limiting over- and undervoltage during sudden
load changes on the HB side. These voltage deviations arise from the mismatch in transient
response between the AFE and HB stages.
Since the SiC-based HB responds significantly
faster than the AFE, the HB dynamics can be neglected and represented as an ideal current
source, while the AFE control is modeled for a simplified cell model for analyzing over- and
undervoltage behavior.
A PLECS simulation as shown in Figure 7 is conducted to find the maximum and minimum
voltage during a load step from ±Pn to ∓Pn. The AFE controller is shown in Figure 8 and
the simulation and control parameters can be found in Table 3. The HB current feed forward
is implemented as shown in Figure 8. The current steps are considered of ±54 A to ∓54 A
corresponding to ±Pn to ∓Pn for a DC link voltage of 1200 V.
The simulation results are presented in Figure 9. The three-phase AFE voltage and current
waveforms illustrate steady-state and transient operation at nominal power, showing balanced
three-phase operation. The DC link voltage is regulated at 1200 V, indicating that the AFE
controller maintains stable performance in various operating conditions.
When a load step transition from ±Pn to ∓Pn is applied, the DC current profile in Figure 9
shows an abrupt change in power flow.
In response, the DC-link voltage exhibits a peak
overvoltage of 1390 V and a minimum undervoltage of 1050 V. The overvoltage exceeds the
DC link capacitor’s rated voltage of 1320 V. Therefore, the overvoltage conditions must be
addressed.
The overvoltage can be mitigated through the implementation of the VLU, as
detailed in Section 2.3. Eliminating undervoltage is not necessary and can be done using the
control strategy described in Appendix A.

2.3
Voltage Limiting Unit (VLU)
To address the overvoltage issue discussed in Section 2.2.3, a VLU is introduced to protect the
DC link. The primary function of a VLU is to protect the DC link capacitors by dissipating
excess energy during transient power imbalances between the AFE and HB. Without such
protection, overvoltage events could lead to capacitor overstress or failure.
To further analyze the origin of this imbalance, both the AFE and HB are modeled as current
sources, as shown in Figure 10. Ideally, the currents from the AFE and HB should be equal.
However, during a load step on the HB side, the HB current changes rapidly due to its fast
control response, while the AFE current, governed by its DC link voltage controller, adjusts
more slowly. This mismatch leads to a temporary power imbalance across the DC link, which
manifests as either overvoltage or undervoltage. The following energy-based analysis quantifies
this energy imbalance and derives the corresponding design requirements for the VLU resistor.

2.5
Loss Estimation
A thermal management strategy must be defined to ensure reliable operation of the power stage.
To support heatsink selection, this section estimates the power losses in the semiconductor
devices under nominal operating conditions. These loss values provide a design basis for sizing
the cooling system.
The system under consideration includes a AFE connected to a 710 V transformer winding,
which regulates the DC link voltage to 1200 V. Each AFE leg conducts a nominal current of
approximately 54 A. On the output side, a SiC-based HB operates at a peak output voltage of
16
==PAGE:29==
690 V and supplies up to 94 A to the load. The switching frequency is set to 5 kHz for the AFE
stage and 20 kHz for the H-bridge stage.
The semiconductor losses were estimated using manufacturer tools tailored for its specific device
technologies. For the silicon IGBT based AFE, SemiSel V6 by Semikron Danfoss was used. For
the SiC MOSFET based HB, SpeedFit by Wolfspeed was employed. These tools consider both
conduction and switching losses based on input parameters such as voltage, current, switching
frequency, and thermal conditions.
The losses of AFE under nominal voltage and current with different load scenarios of power
factor (PF) are shown in Table 5 and losses for SiC HB side are shown in Table 6. For the
AFE, the highest loss occurs when the power factor is -1, with a power loss of 933 W. For the
HB side, the highest power loss occurs when the power factor is 0.7, with a power loss of 408 W.
The combined estimated loss of 1341 W provides the baseline for designing the cooling system.
Based on this, and considering the thermal resistance of typical heatsinks, forced-air cooling
is expected to be necessary. Detailed heatsink selection and thermal design are discussed in
Section 3.1.

Current Sensor
Although shunt resistors are widely used for measuring both DC and AC currents by detecting
the voltage drop across them, this method has certain limitations. For example, it usually
requires an isolated op-amp for isolation, but the isolation level of a typical isolated op-amp
is only 1-2 kV, which may be insuﬀicient for high-voltage applications. Additionally, shunt
resistors can result in significant power losses and heat dissipation challenges, especially at
higher current levels. Therefore, closed-loop fluxgate current sensors are selected for this project
due to its high accuracy, high linearity, and strong immunity to external noise.
Given the current levels in this design, up to 94 A, which are relatively high but still within
the range suitable for compact sensors and the use of thick copper layers on the PCB for

Normal Operation
During standard operation, PWM signals are sent from the controller
(AN/CA) to the ACPL-335JC. The gate driver turns on the semiconductor device (IGBT or
MOSFET) via the gate resistor RGP, and turns it off via RGN. These resistors are selected to
tailor the switching speed: lower values lead to faster transitions, while higher values reduce
switching stress. A Schottky diode DP is used in conjunction with the CLAMP pin to safely
handle parasitic currents during turn-off, especially for IGBT devices. The effectiveness of this
gate drive configuration is demonstrated through switching experiments, the results of which
are presented in Chapter 5.

The secondary-side power supplies (VCC2 and VEE2) are locally decoupled using appropriately

Integrated Protection Features
The ACPL-335JC has a protection mechanism for two
faults: namely a secondary side (semiconductor device side) power supply undervoltage lockout
(UVLO) and a power semiconductor device overcurrent (FAULT). These open drain FAULT
and UVLO outputs are connected to 10 kΩresistors and 330 pF filtering capacitors. Both of
these signals are low active logic, which means the fault will pull down the output.
The resistor RSS is used to turn off the devices slowly during an overcurrent event, thereby
reducing the risk of voltage overshoot which may damage the device.
Overcurrent Protection (DESAT)
The overcurrent protection circuitry is designed to
turn off the power semiconductor device safely when an overcurrent condition is detected.
The protection schematic is shown in Figure 33. When the PWM input is on, the OC pin
is enabled and its OC pin outputs a constant 1 mA current. This current flows through the
external resistor RBLOCK, the blocking diode DBLOCK, and the collector-emitter path of the
IGBT (or the drain-source path of the MOSFET). Therefore, the voltage on the OC pin is
determined by the sum of voltage drop across the resistor RBLOCK, the forward voltage of
DBLOCK, and the collector-emitter voltage VCE of the IGBT (VDS for MOSFET).
During normal operation, VCE remains low while the IGBT operates in the saturation region.
However, under overcurrent conditions, the IGBT exits saturation, and VCE rises significantly.
For a properly designed RBLOCK value, this increase causes the OC pin voltage to exceed a
predefined threshold of 9.2 V, thereby triggering the protection circuit. The gate driver then
immediately shuts down the IGBT to prevent damage.
To avoid false triggering during the IGBT’s turn on transient, when VCE drops from high to
low, the overcurrent detection must be momentarily disabled. This delay period, called the
total OC blanking time, is controlled by an external capacitor CBLANK. When the PWM is set
on, the current source will initially charge the blanking capacitor before conducting through
the RBLOCK path. Based on the datasheet, CBLANK can be calculated by the following equation:

Overvoltage Protection
While the ACPL-335JC does not provide built-in overvoltage pro-
tection, an external circuit is designed to protect the device from the voltage overshoot caused by
switching transients and circuit parasitic inductance. These overshoots may exceed the device’s
blocking voltage and lead to irreversible damage. To mitigate this risk, a fast-response over-
voltage protection is achieved by transient voltage suppression (TVS) diodes. The schematic
diagram of the protection is shown in Figure 35. To withstand high-voltage transients, three
SMAJ530HM3 TVS diodes (each with a reverse stand-off voltage of 477 V and breakdown volt-
age of 530 V) are connected in series, providing a combined clamping threshold of approximately
1590 V.
When the voltage across IGBT exceeds the threshold, the TVS diodes enter avalanche break-
down. The resulting current flows into the gate-emitter capacitance Cge, raising the gate voltage
and thereby turning on the IGBT. This allows the device to conduct and dissipate the excessive
voltage, effectively clamping the overvoltage and protecting the device from breakdown.
To prevent this protection path from interfering during normal operation, a reverse-connected
diode is placed in series with the TVS branch. This diode blocks current flow when the IGBT
is actively turned on via the gate driver, ensuring that the TVS path only functions during
fault conditions.

indicating that the VLU is working correctly to ensure device safety.

same behavior repeats since the two submodules are fully symmetrical.

lation test. In steady state, the HB of Submodule 1 delivers rated voltage and current while the
AFEs regulate the DC-link voltages. During power reversals, the HB stage maintains output
stability; the AFEs exhibit the expected slower adjustment to regulate the DC link voltages;
the VLUs act only transiently to enforce voltage limits; and the grid sees negligible net power
exchange. The results verify that the proposed test configuration enables sustained full-power
operation and provides a reliable platform to evaluate transient controller performance and
protections under demanding dynamic scenarios.

At t = 2 s, when the HB power changes from 65 kW to −65 kW, the HB stage reacts quickly.
The HB voltage stays stable, while the inductor current reversal with a short transient. The
AFE power follows with a delay. In this transient, the HB and AFE powers do not match
perfectly.
The DC links buffer this mismatch: vDC,1 rises and vDC,2 also deviates from its
average. On Submodule 1, a brief over-voltage is observed, which triggers the VLU and limits
the peak. The VLU action of limiting the overvoltage slows down the AFE recovery slightly
but keeps device stress safe. After the AFE catches up, both DC-link voltages return to about
1200 V and the system settles.

6.4
Comparison of Two Proposed Test Schemes
Both back-to-back test schemes present distinct advantages and limitations. In the AC source-
powered configuration, two PEBBs are used with the same software and control logic. The
setup requires a input transformer, two sets of three-phase inductors, and one single phase
inductor. A key advantage of this scheme is that both PEBBs can be tested simultaneously
under identical conditions, improving testing eﬀiciency. However, this configuration demands
a larger number of passive components and additional grid voltage sensor, thereby increasing
the system complexity and physical footprint.
In contrast, the DC source-powered configuration utilizes the same PEBB hardware, but one
submodule acts as part of the test environment, requiring a different software implementation.
This setup eliminates the need for an input transformer and reduces the number of external
passive components. Nonetheless, only one PEBB is actively tested at a time, and the two
submodules do not operate in identical roles. As a result, the testing workflow becomes more
time-consuming, and the system complexity increases in terms of software coordination.
A concise comparison of the hardware requirements and setup characteristics for the two con-
figurations is provided in Table 19, which summarizes the key differences in terms of component
needs, physical footprint, and testing coverage.
When comparing the two approaches, the choice of test configuration depends on the main
goal of the evaluation. The AC source-powered scheme is useful when two submodules need to
be tested at the same time under the same conditions, but this requires more hardware and a
larger setup. The DC source-powered scheme uses less hardware, because no input transformer
is needed and fewer passive components are required. However, it makes the software more
complex and only one submodule can be tested at a time. Which test bench should be preferred
therefore depends on whether the priority is hardware simplicity or testing coverage.
Moreover, since the primary purpose of the back-to-back test is to evaluate the thermal sta-
bility of the submodules under rated power conditions rather than to validate the final control
strategy, it is acceptable for the control implementation in the test setup to differ from that
of the final system. These reasons make the entire DC source-powered test configuration more
appealing.

7
Conclusion
This thesis addressed the design and testing of a PEBB for a CHB based MVGE. The main
objectives were to develop a complete submodule and to provide practical methods for testing
its individual components and overall performance.
The project started with the design of the power stage, including sizing of the DC link capacitor
and the design of the VLU for overvoltage protection. The design meets ripple and protection
requirements, as confirmed by simulation results.
All key hardware parts were developed, including current and voltage sensing boards, the DC
link and capacitor boards, and the cooling system. Thermal tests verified that the cooling
system can maintain safe operation at rated conditions. Gate driver boards were designed for
both the AFE and HB stages, integrating undervoltage, overcurrent, and overvoltage protection.
An RCP board was also created to connect the sensors and gate drivers to the RT-Box controller,
with fast fault-handling logic.
The complete submodule was assembled and tested. Open-loop switching tests confirmed cor-
rect operation of both AFE and HB drivers. The protection systems were verified to react
within 5 µs to overvoltage and overcurrent events.
Two back-to-back full-power test schemes were proposed and compared.
The DC source-
powered scheme was selected for its reduced hardware needs and simpler setup, while still
meeting the testing requirements.
7.1
Main Contributions
The main contributions of this thesis are:
• Designed and developed of complete PEBB hardware for CHB based MVGE, including
7 PCBs, DC link capacitor sizing, and VLU resistor selection.
• Tested the functionality and thermal stability of each submodule component to verify the
function under nominal conditions.
• Performed integrated testing of the PEBB, confirming correct switching, sensing, and
protection operation under various load and fault conditions.
• Proposed and evaluated of two back-to-back test schemes, the selection of the DC source-
powered scheme as the most suited scheme for future full-power validation.
7.2
Future Works
Future work can focus on the following directions:
• Use the DC source-powered back-to-back scheme to verify the functional correctness and
protection reliability of the submodule under various operating conditions.
