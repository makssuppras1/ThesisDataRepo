Abstract
Deep learning-based language models (LMs) have successfully pushed the state-of-the-art (SOTA)
for natural language processing (NLP) systems, however, we are starting to see their limits.
The
performance gains are a consequence of ramping up the size of the models with an exponentially
growth in the number of parameters. While the bigger models might improve the performance, they
are themselves not becoming smarter per se (Brown et al. 2020; Lacker 2020). The current approach
is to implicit encode knowledge into the models’ parameters, but as it turns out the models are bound
to make silly mistakes and their latent knowledge representation are not explainable to humans.
A cornerstone of modern NLP and artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) applications is the use of knowledge
graphs (KGs) in LMs. Combining the two have already reached SOTA in multiple NLP benchmarks
and because KGs in general empower a systems explainability (Lecue 2020) they could potentially be
the key in solving the fundamental issues seen for the current approaches.
The aim of the thesis is to investigate SOTA methods for enhancing LMs with external knowledge from
structured knowledge basis. In particular the thesis studies the entity-aware self-attention proposed
for embedding contextualized representations of words and entities. Thus, we immerse ourselves into
the details of LUKE (Language Understanding with Knowledge-based Embeddings) presented by
(Yamada et al. 2020).
In this process the thesis contributes by providing a comprehensive review of the LUKE model. We
evaluate LUKE on entity typing task, yielding results that enhance our knowledge of how the entity-
aware self-attention mechanism works. More so, we present a novel visualization tool for enhancing
explainability for attention mechanism, in which we are able to show gender-bias in LUKE’s latent
representations.

Dyb læring-baserede sprogmodeller (LMs) har med succes skubbet state-of-the-art (SOTA) for natur-
sprogsanalyse (NLP) systemer, men vi begynder dog at se deres begrænsninger. Præstationsgevin-
sterne er en konsekvens af at vi bygger større og større modeller med en eksponentiel vækst i antallet
af parametre. Mens de større sprogmodeller opnår bedre resultater, bliver de ikke i sig selv klogere
(Brown et al. 2020; Lacker 2020). Den nuværende tilgang er at implicit indkode viden i modelens
parametre, men det viser sig, at modellerne laver dumme fejl, og deres latente videnrepræsentation
ikke kan forklares for mennesker.
En grundsten for moderne NLP og kunstig intelligens (AI) applikationer er brugen af vidensgrafer
(KGs) i LMs.
Kombination af de to har allerede opnået SOTA i ﬂere NLP-benchmarks og fordi
KGs generelt giver en systemforklarbarhed (Lecue 2020), kan det potentielt være nøglen til at løse de
grundlæggende problemer, der ses for de nuværende tilgange.
Formålet med specialet er at undersøge SOTA-metoder til forbedring af LMs med ekstern viden fra
struktureret videngrundlag.
Især med fokus på at studere entitet-bevidst, der blev fremsat for at
indlejre kontekstualiserede repræsentationer af ord og entitet. Således fordyber vi os i detaljerne i

LUKE på entity typing opgaven, hvilket giver resultater, der giver indsigt i hvordan entitet-bevidst selv-

af forklaringen på selv-opmærksomheds-mekanismen, hvor vi er i stand til at vise køns-bias i LUKEs
latente repræsentationer.

CHAPTER 2
Background
Natural language processing (NLP), artiﬁcial intelligence (AI), and machine learning (ML) are some-
times used interchangeably, however, NLP is the ﬁeld of AI that makes human language intelligible
to machines. NLP enables machines to make sense of written or spoken text and perform tasks like
machine translation, question answering, voice assistance, and more. Intelligent systems that perform
such tasks use a vast text corpora to learn about words, and to automatically teach the system how
to model language without being explicitly programmed, NLP makes use of ML algorithms.
NLP systems are becoming increasingly prominent in our daily lives, with more and more people
adopting virtual assistants, like Google Assistant, Siri, and Alexa, but they are also relieving humans
of dull labor, such as keying in answers or document classiﬁcation. Ultimately, NLP may change our
world for the better.
The recent surge in development of NLP systems is largely driven by the technological advancement
both in hardware and in software, with the latter being the focus for this thesis.
Today’s most
sophisticated NLP systems trend towards pre-trained language models (LMs), such as OpenAI’s GPT-3
(Generative Pre-trained Transformer) (Brown et al. 2020) and Google’s BERT (Bidirectional Encoder
Representations from Transformers) (Devlin et al. 2018).
The backbone of these models is deep
learning, a subﬁeld of ML algorithms, that is build using neural networks. Although neural networks
have been around for a long time, with the initial idea dating back to 1943 (McCulloch et al. 1943), it
is only in recent years with available computing, i.e. supercomputing, and a suﬃcient amount of data
that deep learning has shown its true potential.
3
==PAGE:14==
Chapter 2. Background
Pre-trained LMs know a surprising amount about syntax and semantics, and NLP systems following
these models can interact with the user in a remarkably ﬂuent and natural fashion, with the systems
being able to engage in a conversation and answer basic question about the world while and sticking
to the topic. However, the performance gains obtained are also a consequence of ramping up the
size of the models with an exponential growth in the number of parameters used, i.e.
as BERT-
large 340M (Devlin et al. 2018), GPT-2 1.5B (Radford et al. 2019), Megatron (8.3B) (Shoeybi et al.
2019), DeepSpeed (17B) (Rajbhandari et al. 2019), and GPT-3 175B (Brown et al. 2020). In addition,
building and deploying these enormous models are expensive and some argue that deep learning has a
carbon emissions problem because of the tremendous energy expenditure (Strubell et al. 2019).
Human language is still the golden standard for communication systems, nonetheless, with the progress
seen in NLP systems it sometimes feels like the machines are catching up. NLP systems performance
can certainly be described as semantic processing, but they may not be the same semantic processing
that human speakers and listeners engage in. Thus, the question arises, as NLP systems become more
intelligent are; the systems also becoming human-like? Most NLP research is motivated by applications,
thus, whether their models are human-like may not concern the researchers and practitioners seeking
to optimize performance on particular tasks. For example, if an AI system is doing machine translation
with a high accuracy few would complain that the task was not solved the way human translators do.
Although NLP models keep getting more successful, there may be limits on their performance. As the
state-of-the-art (SOTA) keeps getting pushed the NLP models generalization is still limited outside the
training domain. Current SOTA models in natural language understanding (NLU) (Devlin et al. 2018)
and generation (NLG) (Brown et al. 2020) memorize factual knowledge needed to solve downstream
tasks implicitly in their parameters (Nematzadeh et al. 2020; Petroni et al. 2019). Even though this
implicit encoding of knowledge in model parameters tends to translate into better performance, it
also hinders model interpretability and is ultimately ineﬃcient since new models cannot re-use the
knowledge learned by previous models without expensive re-training.
The models are not incentivized to learn about the world per se, only to learn about how other words
in the context relate with the goal of predicting a target word.
Language models are looking at
statistical patterns in the data that is impossible for a human to understand, but it has now learned
a representation that is so sophisticated, that it can do things that seem intelligent for us. Thus, it
might be necessary to redeﬁne the conception of intelligence.
In other words, these models do not know what a plural is but they can learn that mouse and mice
occur in the same kinds of passages, and so they are assigned similar representations. The models
do not have knowledge about how the world actually work. They only have some sort of background
knowledge to the extent that the knowledge is present in the statistics of text, hence, the knowledge
is shallow and disconnected from the underlying reality.
The fact that people and machines do not share the same understanding about word meanings becomes
evident when GPT-2’s text generation capabilities are put to the test. In the paper by Radford et al.
GPT-2 is given a fanciful prompt concerning talking unicorns and is tasked to produce a reasonable
continuation (Radford et al. 2019):

to science.

In the example GPT-2 goes on to generate several more paragraphs of seemingly natural text, and
impressively it seems that the model understood enough about this bizarre scenario to write ﬂuently
about it. Nevertheless, a closer look at the paragraphs reveals GPT-2 contradicts itself almost im-
mediately: "these four-horned, silver-white unicorns". It is clear that the model does not understand
that unicorns must have only one horn.
OpenAI’s new model GPT-3 share the same architecture as GPT-2 but it is much bigger (more than
100 times) and better, crushing it predecessor in multiple NLP tasks. However, it does not appear to
have become much smarter. In a closed beta test the GPT-3 API for question answering was evaluated
(Lacker 2020):
Q:
How many eyes does my foot have?
A:
Your foot has two eyes.
Q:
How do you sporgle a morgle?
A:
You sporgle a morgle by using a sporgle.
Q:
How many rainbows does it take to jump from Hawaii to seventeen?
A:
It takes two rainbows to jump from Hawaii to seventeen.
Again, even though GPT-3 has trained on 500 billion tokens (25 times more data than GPT-2 (Brown
et al. 2020; Radford et al. 2019)) the model cannot be said to truly understand its inputs and outputs
in any meaningful way. The answers are syntactically correct, yet, they are absolute nonsense from a
human standpoint.
Showing these examples is not an attempt to undermine the models in any way. Language models
are extremely powerful and have many valuable applications, however, they do highlight that even the
most sophisticated models are unreliable and susceptible to basic errors that an average human would
never commit. Instead of admitting, "I don’t know" it will answer every question with conﬁdence with
no doubts in its "mind". Something that its creators are aware of:

2.1. Motivation
Chapter 2. Background
"The GPT-3 hype is way too much. It’s impressive (thanks for the nice compliments!) but
it still has serious weaknesses and sometimes makes very silly mistakes. AI is going to
change the world, but GPT-3 is just a very early glimpse. We have a lot still to ﬁgure out."
- Sam Altman (CEO of OpenAI) (Altman 2020)
But how do we correct the silly mistakes? Should we stick to the current trend and just build the
model even bigger and add more samples to the dataset in the hope that it will magically solve itself?
An obvious issue with this approach is, other than hardware requirements, what about the low-resource
languages that do not have an enormous dataset, are they destine in the NLP world?
Maybe we have to rethink and come up diﬀerent approaches. Instead of building bigger some research
show that it is possible to reduce the model size without compromising accuracy, such as lottery ticket
hypothesis (Frankle et al. 2019) and DistilBERT (Sanh et al. 2019). With less parameters it decreases
the storage requirements, and it takes less time to perform inference. Anyhow, this is an optimization
of the existing models in terms of computational requirements. Fundamentally the models are the
same, thus, smaller but still not smarter.

In the previous section the fundamental issues with the current trends regarding language models

3.2.1
n-gram models
One example of a count-based LM model is the n-gram models. The n-gram models uses word depen-
dencies, with n referring to the number of words that participate in the dependence relation (Hiemstra
2009b), i.e. a 2-gram (or bigram) is a two-word sequence of words (e.g. New York), and a 3-gram (or
trigram) is a three-word sequence of words (New York City).

In addition to the sparsity problem the curse of dimensionality is especially potent. The use of n-gram
models increases the number of parameters to be estimated exponentially with n (Hiemstra 2009b), i.e.
with a sequence of 10 words taken from a vocabulary of 100.000, there are 100.00010 = 1050 possible
sequences. Another issue with this method is that it does not capture the meaning of the text, or the
context in which words appear.
That said, the n-gram models do oﬀer explainability and scalability. However, its shortcomings led to
the idea of applying neural networks to solve the problem with the hope that neural networks could
teach a LM syntactic and semantic features, and to overcome the curse of dimensionality by generating
better generalizations.

3.3
Neural Language Models
The ﬁrst neural language model was proposed with the ambition to solve the curse of dimensionality
problem as seen for the n-gram models (Bengio et al. 2003).
The idea was to solve the problem using a distributed representation for words, i.e. continuous real-
vector for each word, nowadays known as word embedding. By representing a word using a distributed
representation it is possible to reduce dimensionality drastically compared to the sparse vector rep-
resentations generated from n-gram. Word embeddings in general and how to generate these will be
explained in greater details in section 3.5.4.
Bengio et al. proposed the model that combined the distributed representation of words with a neural
network using the following approach: 1) associate with each word in the vocabulary a word embedding,
2) express the joint probability function of word sequences in terms of the feature vectors of these
words in the sequence, and 3) learn simultaneously the word feature vectors and the parameters of
that probability function.
Even though their model architecture has gradually been improved by current approaches, the foun-
dations is still to be found in modern LM architectures:
• Embedding Layer that generates a word embedding by multiplying an index vector with a word
embedding matrix Yvocab
• Hidden Layers are fully-connected layers that applies a nonlinear transformation to the input
producing an intermediate representation, i.e. the ﬁnal output is an embedded vector represen-
tation ¯y
• Softmax Output Layer that computes the probability distribution over words in the vocabulary,
i.e. the probability of y given the context, c p(y|c) = softmax(Yvocab¯y),
y 2 Yvocab
The backbone of the proposed model was a feedforward neural network (FNN), which is arguably one
of the simplest neural network architectures.
Thus, let us ﬁrst take a step back and establish the fundamental understanding of deep learning, for
example how to train deep learning models, its core components, and then go through some of the
classic architectures and gradually increase the complexity until we reach Transformer which will then
lead us to LUKE.

In this thesis, we will be doing supervised learning which indicates that the problem we are trying

Unlike unsupervised learning, that is used for
clustering, supervised learning relies on learning from a dataset with target variables or labels for each
of the samples. In other words, unsupervised learning is the type of algorithms that looks for patterns
in a data where no pre-existing labels exists and with a minimum of human supervision, whereas
algorithms using supervised learning tries to estimate a target function that maps input data into
output variables.
When training a neural network samples from the dataset are propagated through the connected layers
until ﬁnally it generates a prediction for an input sample, a process referred to forward propagation.
Each layer consists of neurons or nodes whose outputs are computed using an activation function. In
the supervised setting the ﬁnal layer’s prediction can be inspected, i.e. comparing the true value and
the predicted value. The loss computation, the diﬀerence between the true value and the predicted,
is computed using a loss function. Hence, for every sample a corresponding loss value exist. The loss
value, sometimes referred to as error or cost, speciﬁes how wrong the network was for that speciﬁc
sample.
Through backpropagation the neural network computes the gradient of the loss function
with respect to the models’ parameters, i.e.
the network’s weights and bias.
The parameters are
then updated with the objective to minimize the loss value. To update the parameters optimization
methods, such as gradient descent, are utilized.
The optimization methods provide the directions
along which the parameters should be adjust in order to optimize the loss function and the step size
is determined by the learning rate.

3.4.6.2
Early Stopping
When training neural networks, a larger number of training epochs is often used than may be required.
This allows the network plenty of opportunity to ﬁt and then to overﬁt the training dataset. Just as too
little training will lead to the model to underﬁt the training data, too much training will lead the model
to overﬁt the training data, both of which will have poor performance on the test set. A compromise is
to train on the training dataset but to stop training at the point when performance on a development
dataset, also referred to as validation set, starts to degrade which is based on a performance measure
that is monitored, e.g. the loss or F1-score. This procedure is called early stopping and a widely used
form of neural network regularization.
For the training setup for LUKE, the model of interest for this thesis, early stopping has been imple-
mented. But instead of stopping the neural network training process when the generalization error
worsen we simply save the weights if the iteration performed was better than the prior iteration, while
discarding the others. Finally, the test set is used to evaluate the model with the set of weight that
performed best on the development set. For LUKE we use the F1-score on the development set as
performance measure.

3.5.1
Feed-forward Neural Network
The feed-forward neural network (FNN) is the prototypical deep learning model. The term feedforward
refers to ﬂow of information which takes place in the forward direction as x is used for intermediate
computations in the hidden layers, which ﬁnally is used to compute to the output y. Most importantly,
there are no feedback connections in this system in which outputs of the model are fed back into
itself. An example of FNN is visualised in Figure 3.1. When FNN are extended to include feedback
connections, the neural network becomes recurrent which is described in further detail in the next
Section.
Essentially all neural networks are networks typically composed by many neurons, i.e activation func-
tions, that are ordered into layers. The layers can be referred to as the ﬁrst layer of the network, the
second layer, and so on. The ﬁnal layer of a neural network is called the output layer. Because each
21
==PAGE:32==

Chapter 3. Theory
layer does not show the desired output, they are called hidden layers. The number of hidden layers
gives the depth of the model and the dimensionality of these hidden layers determines the width, i.e.
the number of neurons in each hidden layer (Goodfellow et al. 2016). In general deeper networks are
more powerful, capable and ﬂexible than shallow networks (Montufar et al. 2014).
Despite the ﬂexibility and power that come with FNNs architecture it can only be applied to problems
whose inputs and targets can be sensibly encoded with vectors of ﬁxed dimensionality (Sutskever et al.
2014). For example, many sequential problems are best expressed with sequences whose lengths are
not known a-priori, such as speech recognition and machine translation.
3.5.2
Recurrent Neural Networks
The recurrent neural network (RNN) is closely related to FNN, the only diﬀerence is, that the RNN
has feedback connections. The feedback connections allow the RNN to model the eﬀects of the earlier
parts of the sequence on the later part of the sequence, making it ideal for sequential problems.
To achieve recurrency the RNN encodes its input sequence into so called hidden states. The hidden
states are crucial, because when a RNN iterates through a sequence the previous hidden state become
input for the next hidden state. Thus, the new hidden state then also encodes some information about
our previous hidden states. A high-level abstraction of an RNN unit can be seen in Figure 3.3.

Although RNN models are capable of encoding and storing previous latent representation of its input,

3.5.2.1
Bi-directional RNN
The regular RNN process its input from left-to-right, which is required for solving auto-regressive tasks
(Graves 2013), e.g. language modelling, but is not required for tasks such as sentence classiﬁcation.
Thus, the RNN architecture can be extended to a bidirectional recurrent neural network (BRNN).
The principle of BRNN is to split the neurons of a regular RNN into two directions, one for positive
time direction, and another for negative time direction and then concatenate the hidden states of both
directions to form a new output (Schuster et al. 1997). A general structure of the BRNN is shown in
Figure 3.4. This structure allows the networks to have both backward and forward information about
the sequence at every time step which is especially useful when the context of the input is needed.

3.5.4
Word Embeddings
The simplest way to associate a word embedding to a word would be to pick it at random. How-
ever, with this approach the resulting embedding space has no structure in terms of the geometric
relationships, meaning that words’ embeddings are not reﬂecting the semantic relationships between
words. For instance, in a structured embedding space similar words are embedded into similar word
embeddings, i.e. any two word embeddings relate to the semantic distance of the associated words
with the notorious example vector(”King”)−vector(”Man”)+vector(”Woman”) ⇠vector(”Queen”)
(Tomas Mikolov, Yih, et al. 2013).

Most NLMs have an embedding layer that maps words from a vocabulary to the embedding matrix
and returns the embedding vector. In the embedding layer the embedding matrix essentially acts as a
dictionary lookup of all words and their corresponding embedded terms. The weights (the embedded
words in the embedding matrix) for the embedding layer are generally initialized randomly, just like
all the other layers. During training, the embedding layers’ weights are then gradually ﬁne-tuned and
adjusted through backpropagation optimized to solve a speciﬁc task. Meaning, when the model is fully
trained, the embedding space will have some structure but for a speciﬁc problem. As a results the
word embeddings become a by-product of the model, and as it turns out, the by-product embeddings
have limited use elsewhere.
Furthermore, a big issue is that generating word embeddings for large vocabularies in deep architectures
is computationally expensive. Thus, even though one of the ﬁrst NLM was proposed in the early 2000s
it was not before 2013 that NLM started to be adopted into NLP with the innovation of the pre-trained
embedding model, Word2Vec, whose explicit goal was to generate word embeddings (Tomas Mikolov,
K. Chen, et al. 2013).
The main diﬀerence between the traditional NLM and a method such as Word2Vec became the com-
putational complexity for generating word embeddings.
Word2Vec is geared towards generalizing
language, and creating word embeddings that capture some of the syntactic and semantic regularities
in language. Hence, the word embedding is the explicit product not just a by-product. Instead of
learning the word embeddings jointly while solving a problem the model solely focuses on creating the
word embeddings. Then, by utilizing transfer learning one can simple load the embeddings vectors
from a pre-trained embeddings space that already captures generic aspects of language structure on a
downstream task.
To train the model to generalize Tomas Mikolov, K. Chen, et al. proposed two novel architectures for
learning word embeddings: 1) Continuous bag-of-words (CBOW) that predicts the current word from
a window of surrounding context words, i.e. given the context window {wc−t, . . . , wt−2, wt−1, wt+1,
wt+2, . . . , wt+c} predict word wt and 2) continuous skip-gram that predict the surrounding window
of context words based on the current word, i.e. given word wt assume that its context is {wc−t, . . . ,
wt−2, wt−1, wt+1, wt+2, . . . , wt+c}. Essentially, we get CBOW by inverting the skip gram model and
vice versa. With these approached Tomas Mikolov, K. Chen, et al. was able to achieve state-of-the-art
performance for measuring syntactic and semantic word similarities (Tomas Mikolov, K. Chen, et al.
2013). In Figure 3.6 a high-level abstraction of the two methods is shown.
Word2Vec popularized word embedding models and soon followed competitive set of pre-trained word
embeddings, such as Global Vectors for Word Representation (GloVe) (Pennington et al. 2014) and
FastText (Joulin et al. 2016). Both of which pushed the SOTA even further. As technology improved it
became clear that what makes a good word embedding space depends heavily on the downstream task.
For instance, the embedding space for a movie sentiment analysis model may diﬀerentiate signiﬁcantly
for the embedding space for a legal document classiﬁcation model, because the importance of certain
semantic relationships varies from task to task.

3.5.5
Encoder-Decoder Models
Generally sequence learning in NLP refers to the problem of mapping from or to a sequence of obser-
vations using a neural network, also referred to as sequence-to-sequence problems. The idea is to ﬁnd
a mapping function f that deﬁnes the conditional distribution from an input sequence of n vectors to
a sequence of m target vectors:
f : X1:n ! Y1:m,
(3.30)
where X1:n and Y1:m are the notations we use for the n input vectors and m target vectors, respectively.
There are many complex tasks that requires such mapping, e.g.
summarizing tasks and machine
translation (MT) (Cho, Merrienboer, et al. 2014; Ji et al. 2017).
The sequence-to-sequence problem is not easily solved as the dimensionality of the input- and output
sequences is not ﬁxed, i.e. the number of target vectors m is not known a-priori and depends on the
input sequence. Luckily, as was seen in Section 3.5.2 the RNN model is suitable for mapping a sequence
to another sequence, however, the model still requires the input sequence and output sequence to align
(same number of outputs as inputs) (Sutskever et al. 2014). Thus, the challenge becomes how to solve
problems whose input and output sequences do not align.
A simple strategy for sequence learning could be to encode an input sequence to a ﬁxed-sized vector
using one RNN, and then to decode the vector to the target sequence with another RNN, which is
exactly the approach that was applied by Cho, Van Merriënboer, et al. in their novel architecture the
RNN encoder-decoder (Cho, Van Merriënboer, et al. 2014). The encoder-decoder framework seen in
Figure 3.7 consists of two parts: 1) an encoder network, and 2) a decoder network. When combined
they form the comprehensive sequence-to-sequence model (Seq2Seq), also referred to as the encoder-
decoder model.

3.5.7
The Transformer
In the paper "Attention Is All You Need" the authors introduced a novel neural network architecture,
the Transformer, which is an attention-based encoder-decoder model (Vaswani et al. 2017).
The
Transformer is similar to some of the existing RNN encoder-decoder models, however, it is without
recurrence and convolutions entirely and is instead relying solely on the attention mechanisms. Hence,
the overall Transformer architecture follows the same high-level abstraction as seen in Figure 3.7 but
instead of stacking recurrent units it is now a stack of encoder and decoder layers.
An important diﬀerence, is that the attention-based encoder allows the Transformer to process the
complete input sequence simultaneously. The biggest beneﬁt of processing the input sequence simul-
taneously is that the Transformer lends itself to parallelization, which unlike the RNNs’ sequential
nature precludes parallelization within training examples (this was discussed in Section 3.5.2). Hence,
the contextual encoding sequence, X1:n, in Equation (3.34) is only computed in the ﬁrst forward pass,
from then on it is simply reused by the decoder.
The architecture of the Transformer has proven superior compared to the RNN due to the high level
of parallelization enabling the creation of enormous models with billions of parameters, e.g. GPT-3
(Brown et al. 2020) and Megatron (Shoeybi et al. 2019). The Transformer model proposed by Vaswani
et al. has served as an inspiration for these models. For instance, BERT uses the bidirectional encoder
part of the Transformer (Devlin et al. 2018) and GPT-3 uses a modiﬁed version of the decoder part
(Brown et al. 2020) both models have pushed the boundaries of NLP capability in multiple NLP tasks
and through ﬁne-tuning in regards to downstream tasks. Note, that the Transformer is considered
bidirectional, however, this is a carry-over term from bi-directional RNN. The Transformer is much
more than that as the attention allows it to directly access all positions in the sequence during encoding
and decoding.
Before advancing, let us ﬁrst examine the Transformer’s complexity in an even greater detail and then
see how it is used and trained in a SOTA model, namely, LUKE.

3.5.8
LUKE
Yamada et al. propose a novel method for creating contextualized representations of words and entities
by developing LUKE (Language Understanding with Knowledge-based Embeddings) that achieves
SOTA in ﬁve entity related tasks. LUKE is a multi-layer bi-directional Transformer (Vaswani et al.
2017) that uses the pre-trained RoBERTa (A Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining Approach) model
as base. Furthermore, we will argue that by using direct entity linking from Wikipedia as its knowledge
base, LUKE’s embeddings can be treated as knowledge graph embeddings (KGEs), which we will discuss
further in Section 3.6.1. In this section we will describe the motivation for LUKE, its architecture and
the pre-training process.
In the paper Yamada et al. argue that contextualized word representations (CWRs) based on the

Transformer, such as BERT (Devlin et al. 2018) and RoBERTa (Y. Liu et al. 2019), provide eﬀec-

entity representations based on the rich entity-centric information encoded in the corresponding entity
embeddings.

3.6
Knowlegde Graphs
A modern development in NLP and AI applications concerns the use of knowledge graphs (KGs)
(Lerer et al. 2019; Yamada et al. 2020). Though the phrase knowledge graph (KG) has been used
in the literature since at least 1973 (Schneider 1973), the modern deﬁnition stems from the Google
Knowledge Graph from 2012 (Singhal 2012). The KG technology revolutionized Google’s search engine
which has led to an industry-scale knowledge graphs development, e.g. Airbnb (Chang 2018), Amazon
(Krishnan 2018), Microsoft (Shrivastava 2017), Facebook (Noy et al. 2019), among others. With the
growing industrial uptake of the concept it has also become of interest in academia where it has proven
useful in many ﬁelds, such as language models (Nayak et al. 2020; Peters et al. 2019).
The deﬁnition of a knowledge graph remains contentious (Hogan et al. 2020) but the core idea remains
the use of graphs to represent data from a knowledge base, often enhanced with some way to explicitly
represent knowledge. In general KG can be viewed as a way of encoding knowledge in a graph form
where nodes represent entities or literals (e.g. Copenhagen and Denmark) and labelled edges7 represent
speciﬁc types of relationships between nodes (e.g. country). Essentially, two nodes connected by a
relationship form a statement, also known as a fact, which often uses the notation (subject, predicate,
object) triples, e.g., (Copenhagen, country, Denmark). Modern knowledge graphs, particularly in
industrial applications, can contain billions of nodes. For instance, by 2020 Google Knowledge Graph
amassed over 500 billion facts and about ﬁve billion entities (Sullivan 2020).
In Figure 3.13 an illustration of a KG can be seen. An intuitive way of interpreting the KG is to think
of it as an accumulation of simple facts, e.g. Alec Guinness played Obi-Wan Kenobi, Obi-Wan Kenobi
is a character in Star Wars, etc. More so, if the knowledge graph intends to accumulate quantiﬁed

Traditionally the term knowledge graph embeddings (KGEs) refer to methods of generating unsuper-
vised vector representation of knowledge graphs features, e.g. vectors that encode nodes and labelled
edges, that can be used for a variety of machine learning tasks (F. Bianchi et al. 2020; Bordes et al.
2013). The KGEs should ideally capture the graph topology, node-to-node relationship, and other

entity linking during ﬁne-tuning and inference. For instance, KnowBERT (Peters et al. 2019) uses an
integrated entity linker to retrieve relevant entity embeddings from pre-trained graph embeddings and
then update the contextual word representations. Furthermore, (Lv et al. 2019) retrieve graph paths
from detected entities and encode them as heterogeneous representations for higher-level modules for
commonsense question answering. 2) Methods that use contextualized representations from pre-trained
LMs to enrich graph embeddings, e.g. KG-BERT (Yao et al. 2019) takes the head, relation and tail
of a triple as input and computes a scoring function of the triple, i.e., whether a triple is correct or
not. 3) Equipping and extending a language model, such as RoBERTa, with structured knowledge via
self-supervised pre-training on raw text using MLM, e.g. K-Adapter (Wang et al. 2020) and LUKE
(Yamada et al. 2020).
In this project we are focusing on the third class, namely, LUKE. In comparison with the ﬁrst class,
LUKE is exposed to the structured information only during pre-training. Thus, circumvent costly on
linking algorithms and knowledge retrieval in ﬁne-tuning and inference. Compared to the second class,
LUKE learns from raw text through MLM rather than triples, which fosters generalization on other
downstream tasks.

The integration of KGEs have pushed the SOTA in multiple NLP tasks (Yamada et al. 2020) and by
integrating semantic knowledge inside deep neural networks it may help in enriching the explainability
of pure black-box neural networks (Lecue 2020). That said, KGEs also come with some limitations.
First of all, KGEs are not explainable by default, they are sub-symbolic representations of entities
in which latent factors are encoded. There are some promising unsupervised end-to-end approaches
that tries to construct KGs from pre-trained language models (Wang et al. 2020).
Nevertheless,
the prediction from KGEs is the result of the combination of latent factors that are not directly
interpretable. Admittedly the ability to push an arbitrary benchmark is not necessarily what hinders
the application of AI in products at scale. Instead, you will need to formally demonstrate that your
approach is explainable, e.g. Article 22 of GDPR (Blackmer 2018). Furthermore, research suggest NLP
models’ latent representations show stereo-typical biases in the representation and may even amplify
the biases that are found in text corpora (Sun et al. 2019). Thus, explainability in KGEs is important
and there is literature that explores the usage of embeddings in the context of explainable and logical
inferences (F. Bianchi et al. 2020). In Section 5.1.3 we contribute to the research of bias within in
NLP by investigating gender-bias in LUKE.

Statistical Tools
During the evaluation of the models, a few statistical metrics are used. A short theoretic overview of
them will follow.
Confusion Matrix
A confusion matrix is a table that is often used to describe the performance of a classiﬁcation
model for which the true values are known. An example of such can be seen in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Example confusion matrix for a binary classiﬁer.
The confusion matrix itself is relatively simple to understand: True Positives (TP) are
the correctly predicted positive values, True Negatives (TN) are the correctly predicted
negative values, False Positives (FP) are the incorrectly predicted positive values (class is
predicted positive but is actually negative), and False Negatives (FN) are the incorrectly
predicted negative values (class is predicted negative but is actually positive).
Precision, Recall, and F1-score
Following Yamada et al. we will loosely report the precision, recall, and F1, and employ
the micro-F1 as the primary metric. These metrics are all related to the confusion matrix,
hence, we will brieﬂy introduce them individually.

predicted, i.e. out of those predicted positive, how many of them are actual positive. In
other words, how many selected items are relevant. The formula for it is:

Precision is a good metric if the objective is to minimize the number of FP, as a high

Recall is measure of how many of the positive cases the classiﬁer correctly predicted, over

Recall is a good metric if the objective is to minimize the number of FN, i.e. the chance of
missing positive cases, e.g. when the costs of FN is high.
Neither precision nor recall provide an overall picture of the model’s behavior alone. Instead one can
report the model’s accuracy or F1-score as the primary metric. Where accuracy is a more intuitive
metric, F1-score is better at addressing a more imbalanced dataset.
F1-score is harmonic mean of Precision and Recall and is given by
F1 = 2 · Precision · Recall
Precision + Recall
The idea is to provide a single metric that weights precision and recall in a balanced way.
Therefore, this score takes both false positives and false negatives into account. Intuitively
it is not as easy to understand as accuracy, but F1 is usually more useful than accuracy,
especially if you have an uneven class distribution, as it requires both precision and recall
having a reasonable value.

and after a treatment. The unpaired t-test is a statistical method to compare the averages
of two independent or unrelated groups to determine if there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
between the two.

Chapter Summary
In this chapter we introduced the ﬁve datasets, the NLP tasks, and the results that were published by
(Yamada et al. 2020). Due to the scope of the thesis, we decided to focus our attention toward one
of the ﬁve proposed NLP tasks, namely, entity typing. The reason for choosing entity typing is simply

Thus, a comprehensive assessment of the Open Entity dataset be conducted (the entity typing dataset).
Following (Yamada et al. 2020) we used a pre-processed version of Open Entity dataset which only
considers the nine general entity labels that was made available by (Z. Zhang et al. 2019). The main
ﬁndings when inspecting the dataset was
• The Open Entity dataset tries to solve the multi-labelled multinomial regression problem, i.e.
there are nine diﬀerent entity labels and a sample may refer to multiple entities.
• Although the annotators have been encouraged to provide at least one general entity label ap-
proximately 19% are without any labels.
• The entity frequency is balanced across the datasets, i.e. the ratio of an entity’s frequency in the
training, development, and test set are almost identical.
• The entity frequency is not as balanced for entity-to-entity, e.g. the entity label entity accounts
for 0.01% of the data samples, whereas the entity label person accounts for 36% of the entities.
Finally, we presented the statistical tools that will be used for evaluating the results and hardware we
had available for carrying out experiments.

As one can see, the entity to predict the label for is surrounded by the [ENTITY] token. Note, Yamada
et al. have manually added the token [ENTITY] to the RoBERTa vocabulary as these are present in
the default vocabulary, more so, the entity sequence [MASK]L and [PAD] comes from LUKEs entity
vocabulary. Thus, the attention between the tokens [[CLS], . . . , [SEP]] and the tokens [[MASK]L,
[PAD]] use the query matrix Qw2e in Equation (3.49). The special token [ENTITY] is learned only
during ﬁne-tuning but its embedding is initialized using the "@" word token (token ID: 787). The
entity sequence consisting of [MASK]L and [PAD] are initialized from their pre-trained representations.
Here [PAD] is the padding token that ensures that all the entity sequences are of same length.
For entity typing the label is predicted by placing a linear classiﬁer on top of the ﬁnal hidden represen-
tation of the corresponding entity representation of [MASK]L. The model is then trained using binary
cross-entropy loss averaged over all entities in the passage.
The ﬁnal layer in the neural network is a logits layer, which returns raw non-normalized values for our
predictions. For each sample the layer will generate a logit vector with a classiﬁcation score for each
entity. The logits take values between (−1, 1) and to map them back to probabilities we pass each
logit through the sigmoid function in Equation (3.17).
The model is performing multi-labelled multinomial regression, i.e. there are nine diﬀerent entity labels

5.1.3
What Are You Looking At?
Although large pre-trained language models can achieve very high accuracy when ﬁne-tuned on su-
pervised tasks it is not fully understood why (Clark et al. 2019) and as discussed in Section 3.6
explainability can be a key feature when implementing models in productions. In Section 5.1.2 we
investigated this question by examining the outputs of LUKE performing entity typing on carefully
chosen input sentences, similar to recent work done for language models (Linzen et al. 2016).
Complementary to this work, we wish to analyze the attention mechanisms used in Transformers and
the entity-aware self-attention mechanisms used in LUKE. An advantage of using attention, besides
improving the performance, is that it can help interpretability. For instance, Equation 3.48 outputs
z of the i-th attention head which is a weighted sum of the value vectors. The weights or attention
weights is then naturally interpretable because an attention weight has a clear meaning: how much
a particular token will be weighted when computing the next representation for the current token.
However, the multi-layer, multi-head attention mechanism in the Transformer model can be diﬃcult
to decipher. Since the attention heads do not share parameters, each head learns a unique attention
pattern, for example LUKE has 24 layers and 16 heads, which corresponds to 24 ⇥16 = 384 unique
attention structures for each input token3.
By studying the Transformer’s attention mechanism research shows that a substantial amount of
linguistic knowledge can be found not only in the hidden states, but also in the attention heads. For
instance, syntax-aware attention already exists in large pre-trained language models such as BERT,
which may be one of the reasons for its success (Clark et al. 2019). In addition (Vig and Belinkov 2019)
found that attention targets diﬀerent parts of speech at diﬀerent layer depths within the model, and
that the attention aligns with dependency relations most strongly in the middle layers. They also found
that the deepest layers of the model capture the most distant relationships and the results in (Clark
et al. 2019) suggest that attention heads in the same layer tend to behave similarly. Furthermore,
key attention patterns have been identiﬁed, such as; Attention to 1) next word, 2) previous word, 3)
identical/related words, 4) identical/related words in other sentence, and 5) delimiter tokens (Clark
et al. 2019; Vig 2018). Here (Clark et al. 2019) speculate that attention pattern 5) serves as no-op,
i.e. an attention head focuses on [SEP] in BERT when the attention head’s function is not applicable
in the input sentence.
The previous research has looked at Transformer models such as BERT and GPT-2, hence, they
have focused on the self-attention mechanism. However, we wish to further contribute by investigating
LUKE’s entity-aware self-attention mechanism. For this purpose, we became familiar with BertViz, an
open-source tool for visualizing attention in the Transformer at multiple scales4. BertViz is compatible
with most of the Transformer models in the Huggingface library, however, we had to conﬁgurate our
source code for LUKE to extract the attention scores. In doing so we have developed jupyter and
COLAB notebooks in which is is possible to visualize attention mechanism for BERT, RoBERTa, and
LUKE5.

5.1.4.3
Calibration Plot
Another assessment is to see if the models are calibrated, i.e. how well the predicted probabilities
match the expected distribution of probabilities for each class. For instance, a well calibrated (binary)
classiﬁer should classify the samples such that among the samples to which it gave a value to 0.8,
approximately 80% actually belong to the positive class. As mentioned in Section 5.1, LUKE’s output
for every sample is a logit vector, thus, an additional implementation is added to the existing pipeline,
namely, converting the logic scores to a probability scores. This is done using Equation (3.17) element-
wise on the logit vectors.
In Figure 5.15 the calibration plots are shown. Note, the better calibrated, the closer the predicted
probabilities will appear along the main diagonal from the bottom left to the top right of the plot.
Hence, if the predicted probabilities are above or below the main diagonal it indicates that the model
is underconﬁdent or overconﬁdent, respectively. Furthermore, each plot also contains a histogram that
shows the distribution of the predicted probabilities values.
These results show that for the stable solutions of the model’s predicted probabilities follow a somewhat
transposed sigmoid-shaped. In other words, the model trends toward being slightly underconﬁdent in
its prediction to begin with, however, at some point this changes and the model becomes overconﬁdent,
e.g. for all of the batch sizes in Figure 7.1b it starts by being a bit underconﬁdent but around a mean
predicted value of 0.3-0.4 it becomes overconﬁdent and at the mean predicted value 0.75 the fraction
of positive examples are closer to 0.5-0.6.
As seen previous in Figure 5.13 and 5.14 batch size and seed all have stable solutions for the diﬀerent
hyperparameter ranges which is also observed here. Because of the early stopping implementation all
of weight decay’s solutions seem stable, although, we know this is not true. As could be expected,
the smaller batch sizes make the model less calibrated. Hence, larger batch sizes make the model
convergence slower and it seems to generalize a bit better. As mentioned in Section 3.4.6.1, large
batches cancel out the noise that comes from small batches, e.g. by updating the weights more often
the curve will ﬂuctuate. Although, the small mini-batches provide some noise to the gradient this is
compensated by the fact that smaller batches convergence faster. Similar for the learning rate seen in
Figure 5.15a, where a learning rate of 1e−6 can be seen to provide the best calibrated model whereas
1e−5 was shown earlier to performs slightly better and convergence faster. It is also clear from the
histograms that for stable solutions the model seems very conﬁdent as a binary classiﬁer, i.e. either
the 0 or 1, as it is never somewhat in between.
For the other hyperparameters, learning rate, training size, and dropout it is possible to ﬁnd unstable
solutions. As expected, the range of which the model is unstable is in the same critical interval as
discussed earlier. The instabilities that can be observed have similar properties. All of the unstable
settings have a solution that is sigmoid-shaped, which is opposite of the others. The instability brings
uncertainty, as seen in the histogram. Small logits are equivalent to a probability of approximately
50%, see Equation (3.17). Furthermore, the model’s parameters are often initialized with small random
numbers. Hence, this could indicate that the model never gets far away for its initialization, e.g. the
small learning rate meant that loss function never found a proper minima.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis we set out to explore entity-aware representations for natural language processing based
on language models. In particular, we acknowledge that building bigger models have led to a gain in
performance, however, we argued that there are still two major issues with this current approach; 1)
although bigger models are better, they have not become smarter per se, thus, the models are still
prone to make silly mistakes, 2) pre-trained language models can achieve very high accuracy when ﬁne-
tuned on supervised tasks, but it is not fully understood why. These realizations were the catalysts
for this project.
Thus, as we embarked on that quest, we discovered the pre-trained language model LUKE which intro-
duced a novel approach for generating contextual word and entity representations. LUKE had recently
achieved SOTA in ﬁve entity related tasks, thus, this speciﬁc model became the focal point for the
thesis. We argued that LUKE’s approach of using direct entity linking from Wikipedia as its knowl-
edge base, could be considered an approach for generating knowledge graph embeddings. Therefore,
in relation to LUKE we became interested in knowledge graph technologies, especially the interplay
between the two of them and the possibility of enriching contextual word and entity representations
and enhancing explainability.
A detailed assessment of deep learning and state-of-the-art architectures, such as Transformer, was
provided. With this knowledge we were able to give a comprehensive review of LUKE both in terms
of its architecture and its pre-training process. In addition, we evaluated LUKE based on the NLP
task entity typing in a variety of experimental settings.
Our intention was to ﬁnd limits for the

the vector produced by the stack of decoders into a logits vector. The softmax layer
then turns those scores into probabilities The cell with the highest probability is chosen,
and the word associated with it is produced as the output for this time step.
The
output of each step is fed to the bottom decoder in the next time step. (Inspiration:
https://jalammar.github.io/illustrated-transformer/)

7.1.1
Extractive Question Answering on SQuAD Dataset
Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD) is a reading comprehension dataset for extractive
question answering (QA)(Rajpurkar et al. 2016).
Question answering and reading comprehension
systems are built to automatically answer questions posed by humans in a natural language.
In
general, there are two types approaches for achieving this, 1) extraction and 2) abstraction. Extractive
summarization picks up sentences directly from the document based on a scoring function to form
a coherent summary. Whereas, abstractive summarization tries to produce a bottom-up summary,
parts of which may not appear as part of the original document. The most successful summarization
systems so far utilize extractive approaches as abstractive methods have proven more challenging to
build requiring more complex language modeling (Rush et al. 2015; Sinha et al. 2018).
SQuAD 1.1 contains factoid questions, i.e. each question and corresponding answer is a segment of
text, or span, from the corresponding reading passage, or the question might be unanswerable. The
dataset consists of 107.702 question-answer pairs based on 536 articles posed by crowdworkers on a
set of Wikipedia articles. The latest version of SQuAD dataset can be downloaded from its website1,

7.1.2
Cloze-style Question Answering on ReCoRD Dataset
Reading Comprehension with Commonsense Reasoning Dataset (ReCoRD) is a large-scale machine
reading comprehension dataset which requires commonsense reasoning (S. Zhang et al. 2018). The
preprocessed training and development datasets are available from the oﬃcial website3.
ReCoRD contains 120,730 automatically generated queries created based on 80,121 unique CNN/Daily
Mail news articles with each query being validated by crowdworkers. The data split consists of 100.730
training, 10.000 development, and 10.000 test questions. ReCoRD is a cloze-style QA where the answer
to each query is a text span from a summarizing passage of the corresponding news. In Table 7.2 an
example from ReCoRD can be seen. The passage is a snippet from a news article where the named
entities are highlighted blue. The query is a statement that is factually supported by the passage and
[MASK] in the statement indicates a missing named entity. The goal is to ﬁnd the correct entity in the
passage that best ﬁts [MASK].
S. Zhang et al. proposed ReCoRD as a way to evaluate a machine’s ability of commonsense reasoning
in reading comprehension as it contains a large portion of queries requiring commonsense reasoning,
thus presenting a good challenge for research that tries to bridge the gap between human and machine
commonsense reading comprehension.
