Abstract 
 
As the climate change process intensifies, resilience thinking has attracted more and more 
attention from city planners and researchers to seek sustainable urban development, 
especially in coastal cities with fragile environments. The project reviewed and summarized 
the formulated urban resilience frameworks to investigate the dimensions that the 
assessment framework considered and widely adopted indicators grouped within the 
framework to evaluate the status of urban resilience.  
 
The urban green space (UGS) is of great importance in both urban resilience and sustainable 
urban development. The adequacy and accessibility are two aspects usually considered when 
evaluating the spatial features of urban green space. The project reviews the target set by 
governments and researchers. Hereafter, the formulated quantification method to measure 
both the adequacy and accessibility in GIS based manner is introduced. The urban green space 
coverage and urban green space per capita are applied in the adequacy aspect while in the 
accessibility aspect three methods as Euclidean distance, Service zone analysis and Catchment 
area method are applied. The catchment area method is attributed to urban green space while 
the two other methods as Euclidean distance and Service zone analysis are attributed to city’s 
residential area. 
 
The project applies the formulated quantification method in a comparative study of three 
Danish cities and investigates the relationship between indicators in adequacy and accessibility. 
It is found that when the city scale is similar, the UGS per capita has a positive correlation with 
UGS coverage. The result in three selected cities also shows that the shorter average distance 
to UGS is the spatial representation of higher UGS coverage, however, there is no absolute 
relationship between adequacy and accessibility of UGS. The result of Service zone analysis 
and Catchment area method indicates that the city with limited UGS resources could still 
achieve high accessibility if adopting an extensive spatial allocation. Also, it is found that large 
size of green space tract could not be effectively accessed by residents, which indicates that 
simply adopting a larger cluster of UGS is not a smart strategy. 
 
The project also observes the relationship between measurement of UGS indicators and urban 
form. The result also demonstrates that city with lower amount of urban green space has 
higher proportion of residential areas and built-up areas as exchange, which reflects a more 
compact urban structure. Apart from that, though the city with higher amount of UGS will 
have shorter average distance to UGS, the residents will have longer average distance to city 
center and commercial and industrial area as trade-off, which is considered negative to the 
city compactness.

1 Introduction 
The wave of urbanization is sweeping the globe. More than half the world’s population has 
been living in cities since 2007, and that proportion is predicted to rise to 60 per cent by 2030 
(United Nations, 2016). Cities drive productivity, innovation and investment, which contributes 
to the prosperity and sustainable economic growth (United Nations, 2018). The urban district 
around the world is appraised to contribute to 70 per cent of the globe’s gross domestic 
product (GDP) and has brought various benefits and convenience to many people (United 
Nations, 2021). 
 
Among all type of cities, the coastal city has its unique landscape resources and geographic 
advantages, which are conducive to economic development and human survival. The cities 
that are close to coastlines are the region where population, national economy and social 
development is highly concentrated. In Denmark, all ten of the largest cities are located on the 
coast or in an inlet and nearby coastlines are the intensive district of population and socio-
economic activities (Realdania, 2020). 
 
On the other hand, the accelerating process of urbanization brings many challenges such as 
deteriorating the natural environment and loss of green spaces, which has exacerbated the 
climate risks faced by cities and produces essential impacts on sustainable urban development 
(Ma et al., 2020). Climate change has caused a series of problems such as the sea-level rise, 
increased frequency of extreme weather events, and reduced biodiversity, which has been 
continued to produce huge influence on nature, human life and social-economic development 
of urban systems in a coastal region country like Denmark (Karl & Trenberth, 2003; Realdania, 
2020). 
 
With intensified climate change process, the resilience-thinking is getting increasing attention 
from city planners and researchers in the pursuit of sustainable urban development, especially 
in coastal cities with their fragile environment (Fu & Wang, 2018; Liang & Li, 2020). Urban 
resilience is gradually becoming an important concept in the climate change context and 
building urban resilience is suggested by Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) 
as a critical shift in addressing the risks of climate change in urban areas (IPCC, 2014). Urban 
resilience is also considered an important component of sustainable urban development. The 
United Nation has put the resilient city concept into the sustainable development goals (SDGs) 
11 that illustrated as “Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and 
sustainable” (United Nations, 2021). 
 
For the purpose of transforming the urban resilience from a theoretical concept into practical 
and measurable term, efforts have been made to formulate urban resilience framework and 
related indicators (Abdrabo & Hassaan, 2015). The establishment of urban resilience 
framework helps the city planners and decision makers to diagnose the pressure and evaluate 
the impact of interventions when emphasizing urban resilience in the urban planning scheme 
(Science for Environment Policy, 2018). It would be beneficial to review these urban resilience 
frameworks to obtain an overview on their focused dimensions and adopted indicators. 
==PAGE:20==
2 
 
 
In the context of climate change and enhance urban resilience, urban green space (UGS) is 
regarded as one critical component in the climate change adaption strategy that can be 
involved in the urban planning process (Alibašić, 2018). The urban green space is considered 
to be prominent in improving the quality of the urban environment and is regarded to 
contribute to the reduction of air pollution and alleviation of urban heat island (UHI) effect 
(Zupancic et al., 2015). The reduction of air pollution helps to improve the air quality of the 
city and meanwhile offering essential health benefit to the residents and communities, while 
the cooling effect of the urban heat island can help the city to adapt the climate change 
impacts and reduce the greenhouse gas (GHG) emission which results in severe climate change 
events (EPA.gov, 2019; Zupancic et al., 2015). 
 
In the context of sustainable urban development, urban green space is considered as an 
element of great importance in urban planning. In the SDGs 11 there is one specific target SDG 
11.7 orientated for UGS as “provide universal access to safe, inclusive and accessible, green 
and public spaces” (United Nations, 2021). This highlights the significance of UGS in the future 
sustainable urban planning and policy agenda (Pafi et al., 2016). The UGS such as parks and 
waterfront landscape architecture in the coastal cities provides recreation and aesthetic values 
and help to improve the quality of citizen’s life. It reflects the consideration of the public realm 
in urban planning and is an important manifestation of the attractiveness of the city. 
 
When evaluating the urban green space in sustainable urban development and practicing the 
planning of urban green space, the amount and allocation in the city could be the influencing 
factors. The adequacy of UGS such as UGS coverage and accessibility of UGS are indicators 
widely considered in various urban resilience framework (Baba et al., 2020; Esposito De Vita 
et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2016; Ma et al., 2020; Newell et al., 2020). In addition, the criteria by the 
government and organizations around the world are usually set based on the adequacy and 
accessibility factors (García Sánchez et al., 2018; Zupancic et al., 2015). For example, the world 
health organization (WHO) states that cities should provide minimum 9 m2 UGS per capita 
while the ideal amount is 50 m2. Meanwhile, the UGS should be available within 15 minutes’ 
walking from residents’ home (Russo & Cirella, 2018; Wüstemann, Henry; Kalisch, 2016). It 
would be beneficial to review these indicators with quantification method in Geographic 
Information System (GIS), which helps city planners to know how to measure these indicators 
with numbers to accurately and effectively translate theories into the actual construction of 
sustainable cities. 
 
Furthermore, a comparative study in different cities helps to present how would the 
performance of indicators vary in different cities and to investigate the if there is relation 
between different indicators and urban structure. Through the comparative study of the 
current status of urban green space in Danish coastal cities including the relationship between 
adequacy, accessibility and urban form could provide insights and experience for the future 
urban planning policies.

What type of frameworks and indicators are developed and adopted by researchers

 
What are the indicators that measure the spatial features of urban green space in

3 Literature review 
The literature review of this project consists of two parts. The part one of literature review 
would focus on the first objective of the thesis as reviewing the framework and indicators to 
evaluate general urban resilience of cities. The review will focus on what frameworks are 
developed and which dimensions do these frameworks considered when grouping indicators. 
The part two of the literature review would focus on the urban green space. The review of 
urban green space includes the benefits of urban green space in sustainable urban 
development and how do governments and researchers set targets when evaluating urban 
green space and practicing planning in both adequacy and accessibility aspects. 
 
The literature was found by using DTU online libraries as DTU Findit and literature search 
engines, for examples, the Science Direct and Google Scholar. The search of literature would 
focus on the tittle, abstract, introduction and the overall structure. In the review of urban 
resilience and related framework and indicators, the search focus more on the whether a 
systematic framework is developed in the literature. While the part two of the literature 
review is more about the review of the target set by governments and researchers in 
measuring the status of urban green space. Therefore, this part has searched the various goals 
of urban green space to see which amount of urban green space is considered ideal and how 
far the distance from urban green space is considered as high-level accessibility. The search is 
not only about the journal articles but also information from international organizations such 
as United Nations (UN) and World Health Organization (WHO). 
3.1 Urban resilience 
3.1.1 Urban resilience concept 
Building urban resilience is considered to play an important role in realizing the Sustainable 
Development Goals 11 illustrated as “Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, 
resilient and sustainable”. Indicated by the United Nations, a resilient city can effectively 
sustain the disaster and beneficial to the disaster risk reduction, which is an integral part of 
the city’s social and economic development and is an essential way towards the sustainable 
development in the future (United Nations, 2021). 
 
The concept of resilience first appeared in physics, which is used to express the ability of an 
object or system to deform under the action of external force while restoring its original shape 
(Klein et al., 2003). In the 1970s, Canadian biologist Holling introduced the concept of 
resilience into biology to express the ability of natural systems to maintain their stable state 
after being subjected to external disturbances (such as fires, floods, and human damage) 
(C.S.Holling, 1973). In the development of the resilience theory, the research field has 
continuously expanded, gradually extending from the field of ecology to the fields of 
engineering design, urban planning and social-economic development, which greatly enriched 
the connotation of resilience.

can be used as a goal of urban development (Norris et al., 2008).  
3.1.2 Urban resilience: framework and indicators 
Indicator is defined as “a parameter, or a value derived from parameters, which points to, 
provides information about, and/or describes the state of a phenomenon/environment/area, 
with a significance extending beyond that directly associated with a parameter value” while a 
framework is a basic structure grouping the indicators to evaluate certain major issues (Science 
for Environment Policy, 2018). 
 
Establishing urban resilience framework enables municipal planners and policy makers to 
measure socio-economic and environmental impacts, such as the current urban design, 
infrastructure, land-use policies, pollution, and citizens’ access to amenities and public 
services (Science for Environment Policy, 2018). How to evaluate the status and scientifically 
quantify urban resilience helps to accurately and effectively translate theories into the actual 
construction of resilient cities. 
 
Various researchers have explored ways to build a resilience assessment framework and some 
of them focus on the concept of resilience itself and the evaluation of the city’s current status 
which is static. Joerin et al. applies the Climate Disaster Resilience Index (CDRI) to assess the 
urban resilience in Chennai with five dimensions as physical, social, institutional, economic 
and natural (Joerin et al., 2014). Similarly, Mayunga assesses the resilience in a capital-based 
approaches, which elaborated as social capital, economic capital, human capital, physical 
capital and natural capital. (Mayunga, 2007). Cutter et al. proposed the Baseline Resilience 
==PAGE:27==

Indicator for Communities (BRIC), which compose of six major dimensions as ecological, social, 
economic, institutional, infrastructure and community competence (Cutter et al., 2008). In 
addition, Liang and Li have built a Resilience Evaluation Matrix (REM) that includes the stage 
module in the risk management (Liang & Li, 2020).  
 
With respect to the resilience evaluation in the urban development and planning stages, Fu 
and Wang developed the Integrated Urban Resilience Capacity Index (IURCI) to explore the 
possibility of involve resilience evaluation in scenario-based planning (Fu & Wang, 2018); Ma 
et al. developed the urban index system for resilience evaluation, which focuses on the urban 
resilience in the evolution of urban agglomeration and testify the framework in Guanzhong 
Plain, which is one of the major urban agglomerations in China (Ma et al., 2020). Hung et al 
developed the resilience metric named as the Climatic Hazard Resilience Indicators for 
Localities (CHRIL) addressing the urban resilience to climate hazards in land use management 
of metropolitan (Hung et al., 2016).

(1) Social dimension 
The social level is an essential part when considering implement the urban resilience to climate 
change as the society formed by human is the key component of urban system (Kim & Lim, 
2016). Social resilience is the result of the combined effects of population characteristics and 
social services (Cutter et al., 2010). The population characteristics of cities are important 
factors influencing the vulnerability of cities to risks brought by climate change effect while 
the adequacy of social preparation and social services are related to whether the city can 
respond to, resist and minimize the impact in time when a disaster occurs. 
 
(2) Economic dimension 
Reaching vigorous economic development in the city is critical in ensuring a resilient urban 
system with reliability and adaptability (Ma et al., 2020). The capital and employment sub-
dimension reflect the residents' ability to obtain income through employment and the extent 
to which the city can convert revenues into funds that can be used in disasters from the 
perspective of regional livelihood stability. The economic vitality is also recognized as a symbol 
of economic resilience through the diversification of economic development (Cutter et al., 
2010). The indicators which can reflect the vibrant economy such as mixed land use and 
innovation ability will be grouped under this sub-dimension. 
 
(3) Environmental dimension 
Environmental factors can reflect the vulnerability and sustainability of urban ecosystem and 
building environmental resilience can effectively narrowing down the adverse impact 
produced by human activities and urbanization, which corresponding to the mitigation of 
climate change risks. The ecological system sub-dimensions, for instance the adequacy of 
green space in the city, address the protection of urban environment. While the ecological 
environment quality (air quality, waste water discharge, greenhouse gas emissions etc.) 
directly reflect the impact posed to the urban environment. 
 
(4) Infrastructure dimension 
Resilient cities are also a sustainable development network composed of infrastructure 
systems and human communities. The Municipal infrastructure, for example, transportation 
(roads network, railways, etc.), emergency shelters and water supply and drainage facilities 
are the lifeline systems that sustain the life and production activities of urban residents. The 
operation of cities is inseparable from the support of infrastructure. The lack of a resilient

infrastructure system will increase the vulnerability of cities to disasters (Kim & Lim, 2016).

3.2.1 Urban green space in sustainable urban development 
The WHO defines urban green space (UGS) as “all urban land covered by vegetation of any 
kind irrespective of size and function” while the United States Environmental Protection 
Agency gives the similar definition as “land that is partly or completely covered with grass, 
trees, shrubs, or other vegetation” (United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2021; 
WHO Regional Office for Europe, 2017). Based on these two definitions, it can be seen that 
generally urban green space includes types as urban forests, parks, grassland and recreation 
grounds like community gardens. 
 
Urban green space could provide various benefits in all three aspects of sustainable urban 
development and therefore it is widely regarded that urban green space is of great importance 
in urban planning and reaching the overarching goals of sustainable urban development 
(Karade et al., 2017; Smaniotto Costa et al., 2008; Zupancic et al., 2015). A review of the

leisure opportunities and promoting the physical and psychological human health (Karade et 
al., 2017; Larsen, 2020). Neuvonen in their case of study in Helsinki stated that urban green

3.2.2 Review of target set in spatial features of UGS 
When introducing indicators to measure the status of urban green space on spatial features, 
the adequacy and the accessibility are two aspects always considered simultaneously. The 
adequacy of UGS such as UGS coverage and accessibility of UGS are both indicators widely 
considered in various urban resilience framework (Baba et al., 2020; Esposito De Vita et al., 
2019; Fu et al., 2016; Ma et al., 2020; Newell et al., 2020). Compared to adequacy, accessibility 
is more concerned as it demonstrates the spatial distribution pattern of the UGS (Du & Zhang, 
2020; La Rosa, 2014). Therefore, both aspects of UGS will be investigated and the accessibility 
will be emphasized in this project. Measuring performance requires the set of certain target 
against which the evaluation can be compared (Science for Environment Policy, 2018). It would 
be helpful to review the targets and criteria set by governments and researchers. 
Adequacy target 
(1) Urban green space coverage 
The size of urban green space is important as it effect the quantity and quality of ecosystem 
services provided to the communities. With the increasing urban population and more and 
more paved roads and buildings in the built-area, the remaining public open space and 
recreation area are becoming more and more precious as the space reserved with these 
spaces are reducing while the number of residents is gradually increasing (Gallus, 2018; 
Poudyal et al., 2009). From the perspective of adequacy, a widely adopted indicator is the 
green space coverage, which illustrates the percentage of green space located in the urban 
area. The UGS coverage in the city describe the overall richness of green space within the 
urban areas. 
 
The UGS coverage could vary greatly in different countries and cities. Fuller and Gaston in their 
research investigate the scaling of green space coverage in major European cities and they 
found that many Northern Europe cities have high UGS coverage with maximum value of 46% 
while many cities in Southeast Europe have lower UGS coverage and the minimum value 
among all the researched cities is only 2% (Fuller & Gaston, 2009). A summary of UGS coverage 
in several European cities together with the data source is provided by World City Forum and

potential users of one UGS component could be lower than 1. In this circumstance, it is 
assumed that this UGS component has no potential user in the 400m range. 
 
The higher average value of potential users in a city indicates a better state in accessibility to 
UGS. When the number of potential users is high, it indicates that the UGS component can 
provide ideal accessibility for many. On the contrary, the low value in potential users indicates 
that the UGS component could not be effectively achieved by residents in the high accessibility 
range. The minimum value of potential users will be 0, meaning that there is no potential user 
in the 400m catchment area of these UGS area. 
4.5 Measuring urban form 
The urban green space is part of the city’s land use pattern and urban spatial structure. As 
presented in the project objectives, it would be beneficial to explore and investigate how the 
urban green space allocated in relation to the urban form and how does this influence the 
performance of accessibility (Bahrini et al., 2017). Naturally it can be seen based on the shape 
of urban boundary that the Horsens urban area looks like more compact while the Vejle urban 
area is more disperse. However, the urban form needs to be measure with appropriate 
quantification method to be better interpreted. 
 
The urban form is applied to describe the spatial characteristic of a city as whether it is 
dispersed or compact (Dempsey et al., 2010; Kotharkar et al., 2014). The concept of compact 
city itself has been becoming part of the sustainable development (Kotharkar et al., 2014). The 
long travel distance and loss of nature resources which results from urban sprawl could lead 
to negative impacts such as environment degradation, congestion and greenhouse gas 
emission from fuel burning. On the contrary, the compact city emphasizes on mixed land use 
and high density, which shorten the residents’ commute time and provides a more diverse 
living environment (Kotharkar et al., 2014).

6 Discussion 
6.1 Implications for urban green space planning 
Adequacy and accessibility are two aspects that always adopted when practicing the planning 
of urban green space. The adequacy focuses on the enough quantity of UGS and an ideal status 
in adequacy is manifested by high value in UGS coverage and per capita UGS within the urban 
area. In the study of three selected cities in this project, the measure per capita UGS all 
approach the ideal target as 50 m2 suggested by WHO and for city Vejle and Kolding that have 
large proportions of urban forests the per capita UGS almost reached 100 m2. In the planning 
of the accessibility of urban green space, the thumb rule is to put urban green space close to 
people. Usually, the ideal distance threshold that demonstrates the high-level accessibility is 
400~500 meters, which correspond to approximately 5-minutes walking range. In addition, the 
WHO also suggests that the upper limits of accessibility of UGS should be less than 15-minutes 
walking range that is nearly 1500m. 
 
Adequacy and accessibility of UGS should always should be observed jointly when planning 
the urban green space. When emphasizing on improvement of adequacy of UGS, increasing 
the amount of UGS by placing vast green area is the most direct way. However, simply placing 
large size urban green space such as large urban park in the planning is not always a viable and 
smart solution. In the catchment area method analysis, the result suggests that the inner part 
of large area of UGS usually could not be effectively accessed by residents, which reduced the 
use efficiency of green space. Also, the city usually serves the purpose of fulfilling the 
population’s housing demand and creating sufficient economic value, which is also essential 
to meet the social and economic resilience. A land could bring more direct social-economic 
benefit to the government if it is developed for real estate or commercial and industrial areas 
which can create jobs opportunities and improve employment. The land resource in the city is 
gradually becoming more valuable and rarer and the debate on efficient land use could limit 
the land resources for the development of UGS in the urban planning process (Du & Zhang, 
2020). 
 
Secondly, when investigating the relationship between UGS indicators and urban form, the 
result suggests that simply adopting large area of urban green space could lead to the urban 
sprawl to compensate the land resources occupied by UGS for further urban development, 
which is opposite to the concept of compact city and sustainability. The increasing in the 
dispersion of the urban structure could bring various negative effect such as longer travel 
distance for residents.  
 
In this context when the adequacy aspect is restrained, the accessibility becomes more 
important to achieve better-offered benefits. Rather than placing single larger UGS, the 
planning could adopt the strategies of small patches of green space but with high accessibility 
to residential areas, for example, the allotment gardens and recreation grounds (La Rosa, 
2014). 
==PAGE:66==
48 
 
 
Nevertheless, the importance of adequacy aspect should not be overlooked in the planning 
process. In the result of Euclidean distance method, the high adequacy of UGS could lead to 
short average distance to UGS from residential area. Apart from that, the adequacy aspect is 
usually served as the base for evaluating the effect and benefit of UGS. For example, when 
assessing the benefit of saved energy and absorption of air pollutant by UGS, the evaluation is 
explained as a certain value per land area and a larger area of UGS represent higher benefit 
(Gallus, 2018). The excess pursuit of compact city structure and high density of built-up area 
could on the one hand bring short travel distance for the residents, but on the other hand 
could bring overcrowding in the residential area and difficulty in maintaining the provision of 
ample amount of urban green space per capita (Kotharkar et al., 2014).

Du and Zhang (Du & Zhang, 2020) 
 
To summarize, the city should take a holistic view on both adequacy and accessibility in the 
planning of UGS. Du and Zhang also summarize this point as the vast area of UGS contribute 
more to the adequacy while the small patch and extensive allocated UGS contribute more to 
the accessibility. To consider both the adequacy and accessibility the city could take a 
variegated sustainability UGS planning by adopting mix-sized UGS while ensuring the 
accessibility at the same time. 
 
Also in the coastal cities, the planning of urban green space could also serve the purpose as 
the planning the green infrastructure. The urban green space for example the artificial wetland 
could serve as the nature-based solution (NBS) for coastal protection and compared to 
concrete based grey infrastructure the NBS can be more easily adopted (CIRIA open space, 
2019; Davis et al., 2015). In this context, appropriately planning green space as coastal 
protection could not enhance the urban resilience but also improve the overall measurement 
of indicators of UGS in both adequacy and accessibility. For example in the catchment area 
analysis of three cities, it is notable that in all the three cities the maximum value of potential 
users appears near the city, which is usually up to approximately 3,000 population. This result 
==PAGE:67==
49 
 
from that population density near the city center is relatively high. On the one hand it 
demonstrated that the UGS near the city center can be more effectively accessed by many 
residents, on the other hand these UGS are shared by more population and have high service 
pressure. Meanwhile, it is also noticed that in all three cities, the city center with high 
population density is located closely to the coastlines. Placing green space as green 
infrastructure in these low-lying areas that demand higher adequacy of UGS while close to 
coastline could allow the residents to have access to more UGS meanwhile contribute to the 
improvement of the overall urban resilience. 
6.2 Urban resilience indicators and urban planning 
In the literature review section, the thesis summarized the dimensions that are widely adopted 
when formulating the urban resilience framework and indicators. While these indicators show 
different focus when incorporating them with urban planning. For example, the indicators in 
the sub-dimension population characteristics and capital and employment emphasized more 
on revealing the current social-economic status of the city while indicators related to land-use 
and accessibility are more straightforward to the implementation in urban planning. 
 
The general urban planning can be divided into three phases as Preparatory and exploration 
phase, Feasibility and planning phase, and Zoning phase(European Commission, 2017; World 
Bank, 2015). In the preparatory and exploration phase, the focus is on survey and data 
collection of all aspects including land use, social-economic characteristic, public services to 
prepare for the feasibility study and master planning in the next step. In these phases of urban 
planning, all the indicators summarized could serve the role as explanatory tool and to help 
evaluating the current status of urban resilience in the city and to formulate the problem 
identification and assist policymaking. 
 
While in the master planning and zoning phase, the focus goes on the spatial distribution of 
the city functions and identifying land use categories and corresponding zoning (Los Angeles 
City Planning, 2021). To integrate urban resilience thinking in the master planning scheme, the 
indicators should critically reflect the objectives and elements in order to support the decision-
making process and monitoring success (Science for Environment Policy, 2018). The elements 
in master planning could involve factors including land-use, open and green space, 
infrastructure, transportation and mobility, air quality and public services (Science for 
Environment Policy, 2018; World Bank, 2015). Therefore, the indicators that reflect these 
elements will be more relevant in the master planning and zoning phase and they could serve 
a role as performance assessment tool, for example the indicators related to adequacy and 
accessibility of urban green space which is detailly studied in this project and indicators related 
to transportation and mobility such as accessibility to public transport and road network 
connectivity, which are also an essential part of the infrastructure resilience of the urban 
context. Taking the instance of accessibility of UGS, city planners could compare the different 
measured result as the performance evaluation of the various spatial plans of UGS to 
investigate which strategy can achieve higher accessibility.

6.3 Limitations and assumptions 
6.3.1 Comparative study 
In this project three coastal cities as Vejle, Kolding and Horsens are selected in the comparative 
study to investigate the measurement of indicators. These three cities are selected because 
they have the similar scale and population level but with different urban structures, which 
forms the base for a comparative study. Nevertheless, it is also notable that all the three 
selected cities are middle-level city with respect to both size and population in Denmark. This 
is because in cities like Copenhagen or Aarhus which has the population over 200,000, the 
increase in city scale could lead to enormous increasement in complexity of analysis that is 
beyond the capability of a master thesis study.  
 
Besides, the number of selected cities is also limited with the same reason. This could limit the 
results discovered. For example in this project, when investigating the adequacy of UGS, the 
positive correlation between UGS coverage and per capita UGS is found. Also, the result of the 
comparative study of three cities indicates that there is no absolute relationship between 
adequacy and accessibility of UGS and there is a negative correlation between average 
distance to UGS and average distance to city center. However, these patterns are concluded 
only based on the result of three middle-level cities with similar scale and population state 
and the discovered pattern in this project could change when more cities with different scales 
and different land use consumption are studied. 
6.3.2 Quantification method 
When formulating the quantification method of different indicators regarding urban green 
space in this project, one significant issue is the definition of the selection range, as in which 
range the green space is selected for the analysis. As described in the beginning of the 
quantification method section, the urban green space is selected as the green space falls into 
the defined urban boundary from The Danish Agency for Data Supply and Efficiency. This is the 
most intuitive definition of urban green space, that is, green space within the urban fringe. 
However, in different research context one could have definition of urban green space in other 
ways, for example the green space within both the urban area and certain buffer range of the 
defined urban boundary. When the defined urban boundary changes, the selected green 
space for analysis would change in both amount and spatial distribution, which could further 
lead to dramatically variated results. 
 
In the quantification of the accessibility of the UGS, one assumption in the catchment area 
method is that the population density is equal in the 1km grid. This results from the lack of 
detailed demographic data of the Danish cities. The assumption is much better than assuming 
that population is average distributed in the residential zone if there is totally no population 
distribution data adopted. However, assuming that population is equal in 1km grid is still a 
rough estimation as the scale of selected Danish cites is quite small compared to the other 
metropolis in Europe, whose areas are around 36 m2 that is equivalent to a 6km × 6km square. 
==PAGE:69==
51 
 
If the detailed demographic data as population census tract is obtained, the result could be 
more accurate and better reflect the local variation. 
 
When choosing indicators attributed to the origin place as residential area, initially the project 
also plans to study the accessibility of UGS in different parts of the residential area within the 
urban boundary. When the subdivision of the city can be attained, the measurement of 
accessibility can also illustrate how much green space can be attribute to the population in 
different districts to reveal the status of equality of the UGS spatial distribution, which 
emphasizes on the aspect of equity. However, this method is finally abandoned as there is no 
specific authoritative subdivision of districts in Danish cities. Besides as discussed above the 
detailed population data is also missing to calculate the population in different parts of the 
city. This could result from that the scale of Danish cities is generally small. However, this 
method could be meaningful in the study of larger cities where the scale of urban area is large 
and the city can be divided into several sub-districts, for example, the famous 20 municipal 
districts named as their numbering in Paris. The study of local adequacy in large city could 
alleviate the complexity of the research and critically reveal the potential imbalance in the 
spatial allocation of urban green space.

7 Conclusion 
This project aims at answering the following problem statements, which corresponds to the 
set project objectives in the beginning: What frameworks and indicators are developed and 
adopted by researchers to evaluate urban resilience? What are the indicators that measure 
the spatial features of urban green space in the planning and how to quantify them in GIS-
based method? How would the measurement of indicators of urban green space differs in 
different cities and different urban forms? 
 
The main conclusions of this project are: 
 
 
The literature review of the urban resilience demonstrated that four dimensions as 
social, economic, environmental and infrastructure dimensions are usually adopted 
in formulating the urban resilience assessment framework and grouping various 
indicators. 
 
The urban green space is of great importance in urban planning and reaching the 
overarching goals of sustainability and has various benefits in all three pillars of 
sustainable urban development as social, economic and environmental 
 
The adequacy and accessibility are two main aspects considered when measuring 
the spatial features of urban green space in the planning. The 90 m2 of urban green 
space per capita is considered as ideal adequacy and 400m/500m is considered as 
ideal accessibility range. 
 
The urban green space coverage and per capita urban green space are two widely 
applied indicators for quantification in the adequacy aspect. With respect to the 
accessibility, the Euclidean distance and Service zone method are indicators 
attributed to residential area while the catchment area method are indicators 
attributed to urban green space. 
 
The results in comparative study illustrated that the adequacy and accessibility of 
urban green space have no absolute relationship. The high amount of urban green 
space could contribute to the shorter average distance for residents, but city could 
still take more extensive green space distribution strategy to achieve high 
accessibility when the amount is limited. 
 
The result in different cities shows that the city with shorter average distance to 
urban green space has a more dispersed urban form that reflected as long average 
distance for residents to travel to city center and commercial and industrial area.

G Sustainable urban development 
 
Sustainable development is an important agenda for the 21st century. Rapid urbanization 
brings many challenges such as deteriorating environment, which contributes to the climate 
change and threaten the sustainability. Facing this circumstance, it is important to promote 
economic development, maintain sustainable use of resources and improve the quality of life 
under the condition of not exceeding the carrying capacity of resources and environment. This 
is also the origin of the concept of sustainable development, which is proposed at Brundtland 
report by United Nations Commission on Environment and Development where sustainable 
development is defined as ‘development that meets the needs of the present without 
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs’ (United Nations, 
2013). 
 
In 2015, the United Nation released ‘The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development’, which 
provides a shared blueprint for peace and prosperity for people and the planet, now and into 
the future (United Nations, n.d.). The key of the agenda is the 17 Sustainable Development 
Goals (SDGs), which are an urgent call for action by all countries in a global partnership (United 
Nations, n.d.). 
 
 
Figure 31 UN Sustainable Development Goals (United Nations, n.d.) 
 
Sustainable urban development is a fundamental precondition for sustainable development 
and its importance shall not be overlooked. Among all the 17 goals, the SDGs 11 highlights the 
significance of the sustainable urban development. SDGs 11 is described as Sustainable Cities 
and Communities, which is the SDG committed to ‘make cities and human settlements 
inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable’ (United Nations, n.d.). Within the SDGs 11 there are 
targets and related indicators that aiming various topics for reaching sustainable urban 
development, which covers a wide range including housing, land consumption, transportation, 
==PAGE:90==
72 
 
urban green space and environmental quality, etc. 
 
 
Figure 32: Three pillars of sustainability (revised based on figures from Barile et al., 2018) (Barile et al., 
2018) 
 
The sustainability always comprised of three major pillars as economic, environmental and 
social, which are considered together as a holistic approach to achieve sustainable 
development. In the economic aspects, sustainable urban development should be at the lower 
level where resources are minimized, so that the urban economy can evolve in the direction 
of efficiency, stability and innovation. The environmental aspect focuses on the protection of 
environmental quality and develop and utilize resources within the scope of ecological 
carrying capacity. The social aspect focuses on the equality, the citizens’ well-being and their 
active participation in the city development (Barile et al., 2018; Þórhallsdóttir, 2018). Generally, 
the relationship between the three pillars is illustrated in the Figure 32.
