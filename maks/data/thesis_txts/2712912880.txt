Abstract
Snow depth on sea ice is an important control of sea ice growth and uncertainties in snow
depth are one of the largest sources of uncertainties in estimating sea ice thickness with
satellite based remote sensing methods. Currently the standard snow depth estimation
product is a modified Warren 1999 snow depth climatology. The warren 1999 climatol­
ogy was constructed from Soviet in situ snow depth measurements in the Arctic with no
measurements procured in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago; resulting in a need for im­
proved estimates of snow depth in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago. The Canadian Arctic
Archipelago is an area of great importance being projected to be part of the last area to
have year­round ice and is underrepresented in pan­Arctic data products.
A number of snow depth climatologies were created for this thesis from in situ snow depth
measurements taken on landfast sea ice along the coasts of Canada for the months of
October through April. These Climatologies showed promising results for the Canadian
Arctic Archipelago and the northeastern coast of mainland Canada, but does not show
as promising results beyond the edges of the Canadian Arctic Archipelago into the Arctic
Ocean.
When compared to the modified Warren 1999 snow depth climatology and the SnowModel­
LG, the interpolation methods created in this thesis produced lower snow depth estimates
in the autumn and early winter and greater snow depth later in the winter and in the spring
for the Canadian Arctic Archipelago. The snow depth accumulation rates were found by
these interpolation methods to be more evenly distributed through the months studied
than what was found by the w99m climatology and the SnowModel­LG.
Keywords: Canadian Arctic Archipelago, Snow on Sea Ice, Snow Depth Climatology,
Landfast Ice

1
Introduction
The Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA) is the northern most portion of Canada and con­
sists of a number of islands and narrow waterways. These waterways connect the Arctic
Ocean to the Atlantic Ocean through Baffin Bay. This route is an important point of ex­
change of water between the two oceans, with significant impacts on the global climate
[2] [3]. The harsh and remote nature of the CAA makes studies difficult and expensive [4].
These challenges are exacerbated by the narrowness of the waterways resulting in small
scale climatological patterns in the area with subsequent difficulty in creating satellite or
model­based sea ice products for the area [5].
The CAA is nearly entirely covered by landfast sea ice for most of the year with northern
areas of the CAA having high sea ice concentrations year­round [6][7]. This sea ice is
important to a number of arctic species and indigenous cultures that are living in the area
[8]. Like the rest of the Arctic, the CAA is experiencing significant sea ice retreat with
negative trends in sea ice extent [6], shorter periods of landfast sea ice [9], and model
predictions showing significant retreat in summer ice extent in the next quarter century
[6].
Depth of snow covering sea ice affects a number of physical properties of sea ice and
has been found to be one of the primary factors affecting the thickness of sea ice [10].
Uncertainty in snow depth is also the largest cause of uncertainty in most remote sens­
ing sea ice thickness calculations [11]. Currently, one of the most common methods for
determining snow depth for remote sensing ice thickness calculations has been the clima­
tology created by Warren et al. or modified versions of the Warren climatology [9] [12] [13]
[14] [15]. The Warren climatologies include no measurements collected in the CAA [16]
requiring extrapolations beyond the area of the interpolation, resulting in unreliable esti­

• Can a local snow depth climatology be constructed for the CAA from the Ice Thick­

• If so, how does this compare with current snow depth products available for the area
and the reference measurements available in and near the CAA?
• Are these created climatologies usable and reliable for use in satellite remote mea­
suring applications?

Theory
This chapter first discusses the current snow depth climatologies that are in use in this
thesis and discusses the principles behind the SnowModel­LG. Remote sensing principles
are then discussed with a focus on radar altimetry and the connections of radar altimetry to
the product of this thesis. The interpolation methods used in this thesis are then introduced
along with some mathematical principles used within the interpolation methods. General
information about sea ice and snow on sea ice is discussed; including sea ice type, how
sea ice forms, how sea ice affects snow and how snow affects sea ice. Finally, the specific
climate of the CAA is discussed including how the sea ice in the region behaves and
relevant weather trends in the region.
2.1
Snow Depth Climatology
A number of previous snow depth on sea ice climatologies have been created in the past.
The most commonly used snow depth climatology, referred to as w99, was created in
1999 by Warren et al. [16]. W99 climatology used data from the ”North Pole” program
of the Soviet Union. This program consisted of snow depth and density measurements
taken by hand at manned weather stations placed on multi­year sea ice in the Arctic
Ocean. Snow depth measurements from three stakes were recorded daily, and one to
three times a month snow depth and snow density measurements were recorded along an
approximately 500 m to 1000 m line; starting 500 m from the station and extending away
from the weather station [16]. Snow depth measurements were taken every 10 m while
snow density measurements were taken every 100 m [16]. Because only one to three
stations were active at a time, Warren et al. determined that there was not enough data
to create a yearly changing climatology. Instead, the data from every year was combined
to create a spatial interpolation for each month. The data was taken from the years 1954
to 1991, entirely on multi­year ice (MYI). The coverage also consists entirely of data from
the Arctic Basin which necessitated the need to extrapolate data for areas such as the
CAA [17]. Figure 2.1 shows the measurement locations used in the w99 climatology.
Because the measurements were taken during a time of greater MYI fraction and the ten­
dency for MYI to carry deeper snow than first­year ice (FYI), the climatology is considered
by some to no longer be representative of the state of sea ice in the Arctic Ocean [13].
Do to decrease in the MYI fraction and the subsequent rise in the FYI fraction, modified
versions of the Warren 1999 climatology, referred to in this paper as w99m, have been
created by decreasing the snow depth on areas of first­year ice (FYI) by a percentage,
often 50 %, to account for shallower snow depths associated with the now more prevalent
FYI [13]. A reconstructed W99 climatology was created as a reference for this paper by
doubling snow depth on FYI of a w99m climatology. Snow depths from the w99m clima­
tology can be seen in Figure 2.2 and snow depths for the reconstructed w99 climatology
are depicted in Figure A.8.
Kurz and Farwell have also shown that regional scale variability in snow depth is greater
than that shown in the w99 [18].

2.3.1
Altimetry
Altimetry is the process of measuring the height of a target by emitting an electromagnetic
pulse and recording the time delay and waveform of the return signal. As the electromag­
netic pulse travels at the speed of light, the range from the sensor to the target can be
expressed as half the total distance traveled by the pulse and can be found using Equation
2.1.
Rheight = c∆t
2
(2.1)
Where c is the speed of light, and ∆t is the elapsed time from the pulse emission to the
pulse return. Based on the location of the sensor and attitude of the sensor, the height of
the target can be determined from a base location, such as the earth geoid or the differ­
ence in the height of two targets; such as sea surface and the surface of sea ice or snow
on sea ice. The returned signal will not match the initial pulse, as the pulse encounters
a nonuniform area and will be scattered back from different points at different ranges en­
countered in the footprint. A waveform is constructed out of a number of antennae pulses,
based on the power received at different ranges. The time of signal return must then be
determined from the received waveform, in a process called retracking. A number of dif­
ferent methods can be used to determine this time of signal return; often it is determined
by a power threshold.
2.3.2
Freeboard Calculation
The freeboard is calculated by finding the difference between the range to the sea surface
and the range to the snow or ice surface. The sea surface elevation is taken from leads
(which are cracks in the ice where open water is visible). Heights of the sea surface can
be found in the data using a number of different lead identification algorithms discussed
by Yi et al, in [21].

2.6.3
Kriging Interpolation
As explained by Aalto et al., the Kriging methods are a set of geostatistical interpolation
methods based on the autocorrelation of a set of data. Autocorrelation is the assump­
tion that points located closer together will have more similar values than points located
further apart. Unlike the IDW and trend analysis, Kriging is not an exact interpreter; the
interpolation may have some difference between the predicted and measured points. The
Kriging methods are also very sensitive to choice in a semivariogram model, which will
be discussed in it’s own section [29].
Kriging Interpolation method combines aspects of both the trend analysis and inverse dis­
tance weighing. Kriging uses a semivariogram model and trend analysis to determine the
weights to apply to each observation, but uses Equation 2.11 similar to the IDW method.
This principle also accounts for the arrangement of the data, resulting in a weighting sys­
tem that compensates for potential issues, such as many clustered sample points that
would emphasize a location [28]. One of the major drawbacks of Kriging is the computa­
tional cost and the complexity of the interpolation method [28].

2.7
Sea Ice
This section will discuss general properties of sea ice that have the potential of affecting
snow accumulation or are affected by snow accumulation,
2.7.1
Sea Ice Growth
Sea ice initially forms as 3­4 mm diameter frazil ice crystals expelling their salt content.
These frazil ice crystals float on the sea surface and can follow one of two different pro­
cesses to develop into sea ice, depending on surface conditions [34].
Ice forming in calm water will develop into smooth thin ice, called grease ice, that consol­
idates into nilas sheets. Both the nilas ice sheets and the grease ice are easily identified
because of their dark color. The nilas ice sheets become lighter in color as they thicken
and transition into congelation ice. The congelation ice will then consolidate into an ice
sheet with a smooth bottom surface.
Frazil ice that forms in rough water will form into round disks, called pancake ice. The
pancakes will frequently collide forming ridges of raised ice and keels of lowered ice. The
pancakes consolidate into ice sheets with a rough upper and lower surfaces [34]. The ice
will then continue to grow from the bottom throughout the winter and early spring.
Sea ice will primarily grow in thickness from more congelation ice crystals forming on the
bottom of the sheet, but may also grow from the top in instances of rain on snow events
or in flooding events[34][35].
2.7.2
Polynyas and Leads
As explained by the NSIDC [34] Polynyas and leads are areas of open water in the ice
pack that are sources of new ice formation.
Coastal polynyas are formed when wind blows ice away from the shore exposing an area
of water to the atmosphere allowing heat transfer. During the freezing season this allows
for the formation of new ice that is then also blown away from the shore reopening the
polynya, resulting in a major source for new ice formation[34].
14

Leads are areas of open water caused by parallel or divergent motion in sea ice. During
the freezing season leads will freeze as a source of new ice, while in the melt season leads
will absorb more solar radiation than adjacent sea ice, accelerating nearby ice melt[34].
2.7.3
Landfast Ice
Landfast or fast ice is ice that is anchored to the coast or sea floor and is characterised
by the fact that it does not move with the wind and current [36]. Landfast ice can grow out
from the shore, be formed from drift ice that fastens to the shore, or a combination of both
resulting in landfast ice that may vary in age. Landfast ice may be pushed out of hydro­
static equilibrium by interactions between the shore and bottom surface [36]. Shalina and
Sandven found landfast ice has higher snow depths on average in the Kara and Laptev
seas than the total average for the respective seas; while the East Siberian Sea was found
to have deeper overall average snow depth than the average snow depth for landfast ice.
Shalina and Sandven attributed this finding to the prevalence of MYI in the East Siberian
Sea [10].
2.8
Snow on Sea Ice
Snow coverage and sea ice interact in a number of ways including insulating the ice and
delaying snow accumulation.
2.8.1
Insulation
Snow cover insulates sea ice from the air slowing the rate of sea ice growth when air
temperatures are below freezing[15]. Snow also has a higher albedo (the fraction of the
total solar radiation that is reflected by an object) than ice, resulting in less solar radiation
absorption when the ice is covered in snow[10]. Snow cover has been found to be a
greater factor in the controlling of sea ice thickness than the temperature [7].
Once air temperatures rise above the freezing point, the snow will melt before the sea
ice, delaying the onset of sea ice melt. Once the snow starts to melt, it will pool in low
points called melt ponds; smoother ice will result in wider shallower melt pods than rough
ice[10]. These melt ponds have a lower albedo than ice or snow resulting in a greater
percentage of solar radiation being absorbed in these areas than the surrounding snow
and ice[10].
Many remote sensing methods do not have the capability of distinguishing between melt
ponds and open water resulting in potential underestimates of sea ice concentration.
2.8.2
Accumulation
As snow fall may start before the formation of sea ice in an area, snow accumulation on
land will often start before snow accumulation on sea ice, as a result, snow fall over open
water will be lost. After sea ice has formed though, the sea ice accumulates snow at a
similar rate as on land [9]. Deep snow accumulation on thin ice can result in flooding
events where the surface of the sea ice is pushed below the water level resulting in water
intrusion into the snow forming slush. This slush is then able to freeze resulting in ice
growth from the top of the ice at the cost of a decrease in snow depth [35]. Both of these
factors lead to snow depth on sea ice being lower than that of snow depth on land and
resulting in a relationship between precipitation and the snow depth on sea ice that is
more complicated than simply increased precipitation leading to higher snow depths on
sea ice.

The Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA) consists of a chain of 36,563 islands north of
Canada and west of Greenland. The channels in between the islands, often referred to
as the northwest passage, connect the Arctic Ocean to Baffin Bay. Sea ice is present in
the CAA year­round with a minimum extent in September and maximum extent in March,
making ship passage difficult year­round [3]. The area is also dominated by landfast ice
most of the year with some locations maintaining multi­year landfast ice connecting is­
lands and blocking the water ways [6].
2.9.1
Climate Change
Like the rest of the Arctic, the CAA is experiencing the effects of anthropogenic climate
change, although at a lesser rate than the rest of the Arctic [6]. The CAA is likely experi­
encing smaller loss of sea ice concentration, sea ice age and sea ice thickness because,
it has a continental climate and sea ice influx from the Arctic are replenishing ice losses.
The MYI and landfast ice common to the CAA also respond more slowly to changes in
climate than other forms of sea ice [6]. The northern most portion of the CAA is a part
of the last ice area, the area of the Arctic that will be the last to lose summer sea ice
concentration [37] [3].
CAA is experiencing greater summer ice retreat, earlier break up of landfast ice, delayed
freezing onset, and a decrease in the age and thickness of sea ice [9].
2.9.2
Sea Ice
This section will discuss the behavior of sea ice in the CAA which directly affect the process
of snow accumulation on sea ice.
Ice Extent
Sea ice coverage in the CAA reaches its maximum extent in March when consolidated
landfast ice extends from the CAA/Arctic Ocean boarder to Barrow Strait [2] and over 90%
coverage reaches beyond the CAA covering Houston Bay, the northern Quebec coast and
the coast of Labrador as far south as the Strait of Belle Isle (between Newfoundland and
Labrador)[6].
The yearly summer sea ice concentration is expected to decrease 46% by the year 2050,
which is more rapid than the change in yearly winter sea ice concentration that may not
have a statistically significant decrease in this time frame [6].
The northern portion of the CAA is covered in sea ice year­round with 10 months of land­
fast ice [3] while the southern portions of the CAA have areas of open water at the time
of minimum ice extent; this can be seen in Figure 2.5 B [6].
Ice Age and Thickness
Much of the Canadian Arctic Archipelago in Figure 2.5 are the areas down stream of
Barrow Strait and Lancaster Sound consisting of an area of of polynyas and is the main
source of new ice that is exported into Baffin Bay throughout the winter [2]. This exported
ice is characterized by low thicknesses and shorter periods of time for snow accumulation
than ice formed early in the season.

stationary and mobile polynyas in the CAA grow and export ice into both the Arctic Ocean

Methodology
This chapter discusses the gridding method used for compatibility, the implementation of
the interpolation methods and design choices, and relevant additional functions.
4.1
Grid and comparability
The data was fitted to the National Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC) Ease­Grid 2.0
which is a 25 km by 25 km Polar Stereographic that uses the EPSG: 6931 projection
and the WGS­84 ellipsoid [55]. This grid was chosen as it is commonly used for data
products in the arctic region and was used in the Cryosat 2 data product. Using this
grid simplifies the comparisons in this thesis and will increase the compatibility of the
climatologies created in this thesis with other products. This is then fitted to the area of
interest by removing all cells outside the range 45°N to 85°N latitude and ­145°E to ­ 40°E
longitude. The grid cells are defined by latitude and longitude coordinates of the center
point of the grid cell.
A second more restrictive area of interest is also defined connecting the points [68N, ­
132E], [82N ­50E], [60N ­100E] to [72N ­50E] using the above projection. This second
area of interest can be seen in Figure 4.1. This region was selected to have a northern
boundary that passed through the waterway passage between the CAA and the Arctic
Basin removing areas outside the narrow waterways of the Canadian Arctic Archipelago,
allowing for analysis of the interpolation methods in this unique area.
Figure 4.1: Map depicting the area selected for the CAA subregion. Blue indicates the
total area of interest while red represents the grid cell in the CAA.
Data from OIB, CRREL, and Assist were separately fitted to the grid cells using a function
that assigns data points to the closest grid cell center point using great circle distances
as described in Equation 2.8; the function is included in the appendix as A.2.9. The value
of the grid cell is then determined by the mean of all snow depth measurements assigned
to the cell. The data from the British Antarctic Survey was already presented in this grid
and required no changes; this included the SnowModel­LG snow depth values, the w99m
snow depths values, the sea ice concentration data, and the sea ice type data. The

4.2
Input Selection
A number of input criteria were employed to select or modify the data used to construct
the climatologies. Sea ice concentration for each grid cell is used in two ways in the
interpolation methods. Stations are only incorporated as an input to the interpolation
if they are within 500 km of a grid cell that has a sea ice concentration of at least 70
%; this was done because a large number of stations would be reporting snow depth
measurements far removed from a significant area of ice. 500 km was selected to ensure
that there would be measurements on the far side of the interpolation points whenever
possibles so that extrapolating for areas on the ice edge would not be required. This
large margin also makes the climatologies more reliable if used for a year with greater
ice extent than seen in the years 2010 to 2018 without needing to use cells that were
extrapolated. The interpolation methods also only calculate snow depth estimates for
grid cells that have sea ice concentrations at 70 % or greater; this was done to give a
more realistic distribution for analysis and comparability to the SnowModel­LG and w99m
climatology; the final climatology products included in the supplement will cover the entire
area of interest to be compatible with any sea ice extent.
For all climatologies, the range of years can be selected basing the interpolation entirely
on measurements from within this time interval. The final climatologies presented in this
thesis are based on data from the year 1980 up to the most recent data collected in the
year 2021. This time interval was selected as 1980 appeared to be the most recent year
that would have a reasonable spatial coverage with the majority of the stations having
multiple years of data; as there is a significant drop in stations reporting in the late 1980s
and 1990s. By the year 2000 there are only seven stations still reporting. Because of the
effects of climate change, the most recent data was desirable as the older data does not
reflect the current state of the climate.
Because the interpolation methods require a single snow depth value for each station,
climatologies were created using both the median and the mean of the snow depth data
at each station. The interpolations using the mean snow depth of the stations had overall
deeper snow depth estimates, though the difference between the two climatologies did
not exceeded 10 cm for any grid cell of any of the implementations. The greatest differ­
ences occurred around Mould Bay and in Baffin Bay in the Kriging methods and at the
northeastern and southwestern edges for the Trend Analysis. This can be seen in Fig­
ure 4.3 which shows the anomaly between the median and mean for all interpolations for
the month of March; March is chosen as it experienced the greatest differences between
the two averaging methods. The median value of snow depth for a station are used to
construct the interpolations presented in this thesis to minimize the effects of years with
outlying snow depth measurements.
The IDW and Kriging methods also had unique inputs discussed in Section 4.3.2 and
Section 4.3.3 respectively. The results of these modifications are discussed in the sections
on the implementation of each individual interpolation method.

4.3.1
Trend Analysis
The Trend Analysis starts with the preprocessing steps restricting data to the relevant
years and month, removing stations that are not within 500 km of sea ice and calculating
a median snow depth for each station. A number of Trend Analysis interpolations were
created in this project. All Trend Analysis interpolations were created using least squares
to solve Equation 2.6 with the in situ snow depth measurements as the observations and
the latitude and longitude locations projected into the EPSG:6931 projection for the x and
y values to ensure that the distances in the analysis stay consistent.
4.3.2
Inverse Distance Weighting
The Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) starts with the preprocessing steps restricting data
to the relevant years and month, removing stations that are not within 500 km of sea ice
and calculating a median snow depth for each station. A distance matrix is then created
storing the distance parameters from each unknown point to all stations of interest. A
weight vector is then calculated by inputting the distance matrix into Equation 2.12. The
estimates are then created by multiplying the weights of each station by the station’s
measurement value for each unknown point. The p value from Equation 2.11 was selected
by comparing the interpolations for the p values one through ten with reference data; the
results showed two local minimums for combined RMSDs at p=3 and p=10. Using a p
value of 10 resulted in what was essentially a nearest neighbour approximation, while p
value of 3 showed a more reasonable approximation. A p value of 3 was used for all IDW
interpolations in all subsequent sections.
4.3.3
Kriging
This section discusses the creation of the Kriging interpolations and the decision­making
processes that accompanied them.
Semivariogram Models
The semivariogram models were created separately for each month as the nature of the
autocorrelation differed as the sea ice extended further southeast incorporating more sta­
tions at greater distances into the interpolation. The linear model, spherical model, and
exponential model were fitted to the data using the Matlab nonlinear least squares func­
tion to find the values for range and sill that resulted in the smallest root mean squared
difference. The nugget was manually set to zero because no semivariogram showed an
off set in the data, and when the function was used to determine the nugget it would al­
ways result in an unrealistically high value, resulting in semivariogram models that did
not represent the behavior of the data; a minimum range was also set to 2000 km to for
similar reasons. Table 4.1 shows the results of this process for each month along with the
chosen values for sill and range. Figure 4.2 shows the semivariograms for each month
plotted with the three types of semivariogram models.

Time Comparison
Kriging Interpolations and Trend Analyses were constructed using both the 1947 to 1980
data set and from the 1980 to 2021 data set. The differences between the two time
separated climatologies can be seen in Figure 5.5. It can be seen that the main areas
of difference between the two time periods are a decrease in snow depth in Baffin Bay
and an increase in snow depth in the Arctic Ocean; in both of these areas the interpo­
lation is less reliable. The CAA region also displayed decreases in the snow depth for
many of the months investigated. Months with deeper snow depths showed larger dif­
ferences than months with overall shallower snow depth. The more recent climatology
also showed deeper snow depths in the eastern Hudson Bay area and along the east­
ern coast of Labrador. The areas of greatest change in the Trend Analysis followed the
edges of the interpolation, but with a similar pattern of increased snow depths in the Arctic
Ocean and decreases in Baffin Bay and the CAA; which can be seen in Figure 5.6. The
increases in snow depth in the Arctic Ocean are likely an anomaly precipitated by the lack
of measurements on either side of the unknown points, potentially caused by a greater
decrease in snow depth at the stations further southwest than the areas closer to the pas­
sages between the two regions. The magnitude of the change in the Trend Analysis is
significantly less in Baffin Bay and less localized to the area; this is likely caused by the
greater amount of smoothing in the Trend Analysis leading to the snow depth decrease
shown in the Baffin Bay being spread across the entire eastern edge of the interpolation
region.

6
Conclusion
In this paper a number of local snow depth climatologies were created for the Canadian
Arctic Archipelago and examined in an attempt to improve the knowledge base of the
region and create a more refined product for use with satellite sea ice thickness retrieval
methods. In situ snow depth measurements on landfast sea ice at multiple locations in the
CAA that had been collected by various stations in Canada was interpolated using trend
analysis, Kriging interpolation methods and inverse distance weighing. The climatologies
only use measurements recorded after 1980 to minimize the effects on data of changing
climate. The greatest limitations restricting these climatologies is the decrease in reporting
stations in the 1990s and the potential bias caused by proximity to the coasts.
Operation Ice Bridge, ASSIST Data Network and CRREL ice mass buoys were used as
reference data to compare to the interpolation methods created in this thesis and two ex­
isting snow depth products; the SnowModel­LG and the w99m climatology. The Kriging
Interpolation and Trend Analysis climatologies created for this thesis compared favorably
to the existing climatologies and have merit for use in satellite investigations; while the
source data appeared to be too sparsely distributed for a reliable product from the in­
verse distance weighting interpolation method. The comparisons were complicated by
prohibitively limited snow depth measurement data in the CAA with most of the reference
data located in the Arctic Ocean near the CAA. The geophysical and climatological differ­
ences between the CAA and the nearby Arctic Ocean limit the reliability when employing
this data to draw conclusions for the CAA. The OIB flights showed the most reliable geo­
graphical pattern as it covered the widest area and indicated that the Kriging interpolation
and Trend Analysis did not represent areas in the Arctic Ocean well in comparison to the
preexisting snow depth estimation products. The data from OIB flights in the CAA in­
dicated that the SnowModel­LG and the w99m climatology may underestimate the snow
depths in the CAA during March and April, which could potentially be caused by the preva­
lence of landfast sea ice and the continental climate of the CAA potentially causing higher
snow depth values on FYI in the CAA than would be expected by FYI in other regions.
The climatologies created in the paper have produced higher snow depth values and po­
tentially more accurate results for the CAA. The climatologies created in this thesis are
comparable or exceed current products for the CAA and may be reliable for use in satellite
remote measuring applications.
6.1
Future Work
The interpolation methods included in this thesis do not predict snow environments for
the areas of the Arctic basin accurately and would be best used in tandem with preexist­
ing models or climatologies that better represent these regions. The example presented
in this thesis is insufficient for general use and as such a more involved and realistic
method to incorporate the climatologies created in this thesis into the preexisting snow
depth products would be required.
The snow density is now recorded by the Canadian Ice Thickness Program and was not
estimated in this thesis despite the importance of snow density when using snow depth
estimates to calculate sea ice thickness from freeboard measurements. As such, the
w99m snow density measurements or snow density from another product would need to
be used when using any products from this thesis to calculate sea ice thickness.

Codes
Main ­ Calls other functions creating the climetologies.
Assist ­ Loads and preprocesses ASSIST data.
bridge ­ Loads gridded and processed OIB data from saved file.
bridgeraw ­ Loads and preprocesses OIB data.
Buoy ­ Loads and preprocesses CRREL data.
comparison ­ Creates difference maps and histograms of two inputted climatologies or
data sets.
Cryo ­ Loads and preprocesses British Antarctic Survey CryoSat­2 data including loading
the SnowModel­LG, the w99m climatology, and the w99 climatology.
distfun ­ Calculates great circle distance called by other functions.
Grid ­ Replaces latitude and longitude coordinates of inputted data to Ease Grid 2.0.
IDW ­ Creates Inverse Distance Weighting Interpolations.
krig ­ Creates Kriging Interpolations.
load_insitu ­ Loads and preprocesses Canadian Ice Thickness Program data.
month_lkup ­ Returns abbreviation of month for inputted number.
plot_c ­ Creates a map figure of the CAA.
plot_canada ­ Creates a map figure of Canada.
plot_north ­ Creates a map figure of the North Pole.
plot_w99 ­ Plots SnowModel­LG, w99m climatology, or w99 climatology for Canada.
Poly_C ­ Creates a mask distinguishing if points are within 100 km of selected center
point.
red ­ Creates a white to red colormap. Based on redblue by Adam Auton.
redblue ­ Creates a blue to white to red color map, written by Adam Auton [56] from MAT­
LAB file exchange.
SemiModel ­ Creates semivariogram Models.
semivar ­ Calculates the semivariance of inputted data.
station_ice ­ Determines which in situ stations are within 500 km of sea ice.
trend_a ­ Creates Trend Analysis interpolations.
The MATLAB codes were added to the thesis using a package created by Florian Knorn
available on the MATLAB file exchange [57].
