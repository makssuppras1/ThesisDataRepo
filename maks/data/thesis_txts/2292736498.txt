Preface
This thesis is submitted in partial fulﬁlment of the requirements for the degree of Master
of Science in Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The project is
completed in the spring of 2015 and accredited to 30 ECTS points.
The project is part of a larger investigation of diﬀerent aspects of a novel connection in
tempered glass. This thesis mainly focuses on the eﬀect of multiple pins and how multiple
pins and holes inﬂuence each other in tempered glass. The connection itself is incredibly
versatile with respect to placement, pin size, glass thickness, materials, tempering stress,
and group geometries, however to limit the investigation this is reduced signiﬁcantly. As
such, this thesis only manages to scratch the surface, in search of an optimal design.
Reading this thesis in conjunction with the other investigations made this spring will give
a better overview, and help future projects in the search of an optimal dowel connection.

Abstract
This thesis investigates, by means of numerical modelling, how multiple holes in tem-
pered glass, drilled after tempering, inﬂuences the residual stress distribution around
the holes, as well as the bearing capacity of a dowel connection. The investigation is
carried out as a parameter study, in which dowel depth, dowel radius, dowel conﬁgu-
ration geometry, and center hole distance is varied to see how these eﬀect the overall
bearing capacity of the connection.
It is found that larger pins generally leads to larger bearing capacities, at the cost
of a more brittle failure mechanism. More ductile connections may be made if pins are
suﬃciently small, to allow for yielding before the glass breaks. Also, signiﬁcant diﬀerence
is seen, depending on how multiple pins are placed with respect to each other and the
load direction.
Lastly, a proposal for the optimal conﬁguration of dowels - with respect to the parameters
investigated in this thesis - is given, based on bearing capacity and overall reliability.

1.2
Glass as a construction material
The term glass covers a large variety of materials with approximately the same chemical
composition. Diﬀering production methods leads to an even larger number of variants,
and because macroscopic material properties are highly dependent on local ﬂaws and
embedded impurities, the precision with which the glass can be made has large impacts
on the ﬁnal strength of the glass. See [Haldimann et al., 2008] for a good description
of diﬀerent material compositions, production methods and how the ﬁnal product is
aﬀected by these factors.
In the present thesis, the term glass will simply refer to the chemical variant called soda-
lime-silica glass, summarized in Table 1.1, produced by the ﬂoat method, i.e. melting
the glass and pouring it onto a bath of molten tin where the glass ﬂows out and creates
a very uniform plate. The plate is drawn by rollers to an annealing lehr where the glass
is cooled slowly without creating internal stresses.
The glass has a lot of nice structural properties, including a relatively high stiﬀness
(70GPa), relatively low density (2500 kg
m3 ), linear elastic behaviour with extremely high
compressive strength, a low degradation rate and high resistance to environmental inﬂu-
ences. The most severe drawback of glass, from a structural point of view, comes with
the tensile brittleness and imperfect surface. Small defects and surface cracks from the
production process and general handling of the glass create stress raisers at the crack

1.2.1
Tempered glass
Tempered glass (also called strengthened glass or toughened glass) is the name of glass
that has undergone a thermal or chemical treatment to increase the apparent strength, by
creating a stress state in the glass plate with compressive stresses at the free surfaces, and
tensile stresses (for equilibrium) at the center. These compressive surface stresses help
keep the cracks closed and thereby eﬀectively add a prestress that has to be overcome
by stresses from external loads before the surface can be in tension and thus crack
propagation becomes an issue. This utilizes the fact that the compressive strength of

A sign convention for compressive prestress
As it will always be the compressive stresses that are of interest in the prestress con-
ditions, and as compressive stresses are traditionally denoted as negative, a literary
inconvenience occurs, namely that large (in magnitude) compressive prestresses are ac-
tually the lowest stresses, and vice versa. So, to make things clear: in this thesis, ”large”
or ”high” or ”the maximum prestress” will always refer to the lowest (most negative)
compressive prestress, and conversely, ”small” or ”low” compressive prestresses denote
the largest (closest to zero) stress. Compressive prestresses will still be shown with the
appropriate sign (−) in all graphs and tables.
1.3
Connections in glass
A variety of diﬀerent connections exists for tempered glass. Some general connection
types will be shortly presented below, with a few pros and cons. Last, the novel connec-
tion, investigated further in this thesis, will be presented.
1.3.1
Mechanical connections
Mechanical connections typically consist of linear supports, friction clamps, or bolted
connections.
• Linear supports are most easily constructed when the self weight of the glass
ensures that contact is ensured between glass and support. No special preparation
of the glass is needed before tempering.
• Friction clamps are used near the edges and carry loads in any direction. Friction
clamps usually include some type of rubber spacer between steel and glass parts.
The main issue is that the rubber spacers are not as resilient to external inﬂuences
as the glass, so it may be diﬃcult to keep the friction intact after years of service.
==PAGE:16==
6
1. NOVEL CONNECTION IN TEMPERED GLASS
• Bolted connections are also able to carry loads in any direction and can be placed
freely on the glass plate as long as a minimum distance to edges are adhered
to. Bolted connections require holes in the glass, that must be drilled before the
tempering, and as such pose an extra challenge since holes and bolts needs to align
when the glass is mounted on site. Further, tempering a glass plate with a hole
might distort the hole, making it impossible to make an accurate prediction of how
large stresses will be near the hole edge.
1.3.2
Glued connections
The standard glued connection is made with structural silicone glazing. The silicone is
more or less transparent, and therefore does not obstruct the line-of-sight, but for long
term loads it has very low bearing capacities. [Haldimann et al., 2008] lists, as a rule of
thumb, a long term tensile bearing capacity of 0.014Mpa and a long term shear capacity
of 0.007 −0.011MPa. Even for moderate loads, the low bearing capacities means that a
large glue areas are required, and the connection becomes visually less attractive.
1.3.3
Post tempering drilled novel dowel connection
The proposed novel connection, (see also [Nielsen, 2013]) is made in a single tempered
glass sheet where drilling of partial holes (up to approximately 20% through the glass
plate, [Nielsen, 2012, Thorup, 2014]) is possible without shattering the glass. Inside the
partial holes are placed inserts/bushings/glues of a relatively soft material to prevent
local ﬂaws or impurities from creating high stress-concentrations and thereby shatter the
glass. In this thesis, high density polyethylene (HDPE) is chosen as the insert material
and the inserts are assumed to be made in a fashion that prevent tensile stresses from
being transferred between materials. HDPE is chosen because it is easily molded to any
shape, even in the relatively small sizes used in the novel connection. Also, HDPE is
translucent, minimizing the visibility of the connection. The dowels, or pins, are made
of structural steel which in this thesis is considered as a linear elastic perfect plastic
material. The material properties used in this thesis can be seen in Table 1.2.
One of the drawbacks of the novel connection is that there exists a conservative attitude
towards post tempering treatment of glass among glass producers, as these cannot guar-
antee the end product quality of the glass when post tempering treatments are applied.
As such, many glass producers generally does not warrant post tempering treatments,
see e.g. [PPG, 2002].

Strength of tempered glass
A method for calculating the allowable tensile stresses in tempered glass is given in the
norm draft [prEN-13474-3], but it assumes that maximum tensile stresses always occur
at the surface of the glass panes. However, in the case of partial holes the largest stress
may occur elsewhere, and hence the largest allowable stress is - in this thesis - found by
a similar procedure as in the above mentioned norm draft.

to 1.0), the norm draft calculates the allowable tensile stresses as a (load duration depen-
dent) inherent glass strength and a (load-duration independent) contribution from the
prestress. Since the prestress is implemented in the numerical models, this contribution
is not considered as a strength in this thesis, but as a (beneﬁcial) load1. Neglecting
the load duration dependency, the allowable tensile stress, fg;k, anywhere in the glass

possible to make some estimates based on simpliﬁcations. These estimates will serve
throughout the numerical calculations, as a guide for whether the numerical results are
in a plausible order of magnitude.

2.1.1
Approaching the uniform stress condition
In the case of deep holes in very thick tempered glass plates, however, the residual
stress distribution has practically no stress gradient in the z-direction, and hence the
top ﬁbre ”feels” a uniform far ﬁeld stress and approaches the analytical solution. The
deﬁnition of ”very thick” is deliberately kept vague, as glass plates are traditionally
very thin compared to the other dimensions of the glass. In this context ”very thick”
refers to a comparison with the local sizes, i.e. the hole radius, as the global sizes are of
no importance since it is assumed that the stress concentration is located far from any
edges. As such, it is expected that smaller holes will lead to larger stress concentration
factors, converging towards the analytical value of 2.

3.1
Prestress conditions in fully tempered glass
The tempering of glass leaves the glass in a pre-stressed state, called the residual stress
state.
The residual stresses are compressive at the surface of the glass, helping to
keep the ﬂaws and cracks closed, and tensile at the center of the glass, retaining total
equilibrium of the glass plate. Near edges and corners of a tempered glass plate, the

used, where the stress distribution across thickness of a plate is modelled as a parabola,
which is in good corresponding with the ﬁnding in the above mentioned articles.

3.4
3D continuum model of the dowel connection
As discussed in Section 1.3.3, the novel connection consists of a glass plate (with a hole),
a steel pin (slightly smaller that the hole) and a HDPE insert, ﬁlling the gap between
the hole and the pin.
Even though the shear connection is a contact problem by nature, a (3D) continuum
model is used to calculate the stress distribution in the glass plate, and in this way
determine how the connection behaves and how large the bearing capacity of the con-
nection is expected to be. This is done because continuum models are easier to use for
parameter studies, as they are more robust with respect to meshing. Because of the
sudden changes in stiﬀness in a contact model, meshes may have to be tweaked for each
model, to behave as expected.
To ensure that the model represents reality as good as possible, a few key features have
been used:
• The materials are governed by their respective constitutive relations, according to
Table 1.2.
• The geometry of the hole has a ﬁllet at the bottom, as a drill would not be able
to make a perfect cylindrical hole. Fillet-radius, rf, is assumed to be 15% of hole
radius, rf = 0.15rh.
• Seam cracks are placed at a fraction of the pin-to-insert interface and the insert-to-
glass interface. Further, a seam crack is placed below the pin and insert, ensuring
no shear stresses to be transferred through the end of the pin. In the 3D analyses in
Abaqus, seam cracks are simply an internal face where all nodes are double-nodes

on the other side of the crack. In tension, this produces the eﬀect of a crack (that

• How large the fraction of the interfaces that should be given a seam crack is, is

3.4.3
Tempering, load and performing analysis
Tempering is implemented as described for the axi-symmetric model (Section 3.2) and
the subsequent load step is made as a non-linear, displacement controlled static step.
The displacement controlled loading is chosen, in order to ensure that multiple pins
are behaving as if they were connected to an (inﬁnitely rigid) splice plate. The non-
linearity is included to investigate what happens if and when the pins are yielding, and
to accommodate for geometrical non-linearities that may locally occur due to the loads.
Models are constructed using a parametrized Python-script, and a second Python-script
extracts the total reaction forces per pin in the load direction (U1/x), as well as the
max. principal stress near each hole.
Appendix F is dedicated to further checks of the model, including a convergence analysis
on a single (representative) model. The model is found to be converged for stresses near
the hole, away from crack-tips.
3.4.4
History of the model
The model has been under continuous improvement throughout the modelling process.
A list of key changes made are listed below.
• Initial model: Inserts have the shape of a perfect cylinder (no ﬁllet) and insert
material is divided in a HDPE-material in front of the pin (in load direction) and
a much less stiﬀmaterial behind and under the pin to prevent tensile stresses in
the glass. Some of the problems with this initial model are very large stresses at
bottom-front-edge of the hole, and a problem with continuity of the insert-material
(i.e. the insert cannot use the inherent stiﬀness of the annulus shape).
• The load step is changed to a displacement controlled load. Reaction forces are
summed for each pin in a Python script, since Abaqus has no automated way of
summing a large number of nodal reaction-forces.
• Tensile stresses are avoided by use of seam cracks instead of soft insert material,
and insert material is changed to HDPE all over.
• Bottom-hole-edges are rounded to depict reality better.
The tricky geometry,
means that a good mesh of the region is diﬃcult. Tetrahedron-elements are shortly
considered for the entire model, but rejected, because element sizes are diﬃcult to
control in Abaqus without excessive partitioning and seeding.

• Poisson-ratio of the insert material is changed to 0, to prevent (un-physical) large

4.1.4
Necessary distance to free edge, to have undisturbed residual
stresses
Near the free edge, the tangential stresses deviate from the assumed parabolic distribu-
tion, and since this thesis only deals with holes drilled far from free edges, the question of
how far ”far from free edges” really is, arises. This is investigated by measuring the com-
pressive surface stress (tangential stress in the model) along a path starting at the free
edge and moving inwards, towards the centric holes. The result is seen in Figure 4.4.
The ﬁgure shows that the compressive surface stress has reached, and stabilized, at the
undisturbed residual surface stress, according to Section 3.1, at a distance of 10mm from
the free edge. This applies to a glass plate with a thickness of 10mm. To be on the
safe side, all models from this point on will assume that a distance of 15mm from center
hole, ensures that the condition of being far from free edges has been met.

Chapter 5
Discussion and conclusions
The drilling models are operating in the linear elastic regime of the glass, and drill
depths are kept low enough that failure of glass should not be a problem. As such,
the only limitation of the model, is the incapacity of modelling behaviour close to an
edge, where residual stresses deviate from the parabolic shape assumed in this thesis.
Also, the shear models are only valid until initial fracture of the glass, after which the
behaviour is governed by fracture mechanical eﬀects.
When investigating the redistribution of stresses, it was found that the increase in com-
pressive stresses around the hole has local minima, reducing the eﬀective strength of the
hole edge at certain angles. The lowering of stresses are being increasingly dominant for
smaller center hole distances. The eﬀect was seen to be ampliﬁed, when holes get near
to multiple holes in perpendicular directions. This means that - from the perspective of
increasing the apparent strength around the hole - holes should be restricted to a single
row of holes. Also, adhering to a minimum center hole distance of 2 hole diameters lim-
its the reduction in apparent strength of the glass to approximately 10% (for 2 holes).
Considering the small radii of the holes in play, this is still quite closely placed holes.
From the shear connection investigation, it is known that smaller center hole distances
generally decrease the overall bearing capacity.
For a single row of pins transverse to the load direction, it is seen that stresses develop
almost independently of center hole distance until initial yield of the pin, after which,
the bearing capacity is generally governed by the strength of the pin. The relatively
small reduction of bearing capacities, for reduced center hole distance, is mainly due to
an overall reduction of stiﬀness of the connection.
For two pins in the load direction, the eﬀect is almost opposite; no reduction of overall
stiﬀness is seen, but stress development is highly dependent on center hole distance, until
47
==PAGE:58==
48
5. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
and after initial yielding of the pin. For this reason, the bearing capacity is diminished
much faster (for reduced center hole distance), than what was seen for pins transverse to
the load direction. This also means that predictions of bearing capacities will be more
unreliable, since even small deviations in center hole distance could lead to a signiﬁcant
decrease in bearing capacity.
For both pin conﬁgurations, pin yielding was seen for pins with a relatively small di-
ameter, and glass breakage was found for thicker pins. Only in a single coincidence
was the center hole distance able to change the behaviour of the failure of the connec-
tion. Since pin yielding gives better possibility of an overall deformation capacity of
the joint, as well as a better redistribution of forces, when perfect alignment of pins
and holes are not possible, it is advantageous to use pins with a relatively small radius,
i.e. around ø1.5mm pins. Considering the depth of the pin, a shallower pin results in a
very slight reduction of bearing capacity in models where the glass is predicted to break,
while models where pin yielding is the dominant failure mechanism is unaﬀected by pin
depth, since the pure shear capacity only depends on cross sectional area. Therefore,
no clear conclusion is drawn on which hole depth is more advantageous. Lastly, from
the bearing capacity point of view, a single row of pins transverse to the load direction
generally performed best. Even for very closely spaced holes, the reduction in individual
bearing capacity is weighed up by the fact that closer spaced pins allows for more pins
on a given length.
Future projects
This thesis has only scratched the surface of groups of dowels and many further investi-
gations and improvements are required before attempts should be made to use them in
reality. Topics that has been limiting to this thesis includes investigations for diﬀerent
insert materials, thicker glass plates, and more pin conﬁgurations. Further, it is known
from continuum mechanics that stress concentrations in a ﬁllet depends on the curvature
of the ﬁllet, and as such, an investigation on how this parameter changes the predicted
bearing capacity, would be interesting. Also, a measurement of the imperfections in a
drilled hole, may help assessing what an appropriate curvature of the bend may be.
It is also desirable to determine the direction of the max. principal stress at the ﬁllet,
compared to the direction of the residual stresses, which are in-plane with the glass. This
could give an idea of the extend to which the residual stresses are actually beneﬁcial to
the connection. The max. principal stress is not found to be in the same direction as
the tangential residual stress, so the actual increase in apparent strength may not be
described accurately simply by the increase in in-plane compressive stresses.
==PAGE:59==
49
Lastly, a much needed investigation is a sensitivity analysis of the connection, in the
sense that bearing capacity of the connection may change drastically if pins are not be
placed perfectly in the center of the holes due to construction tolerances. This sensitivity
analysis could be couples with a signiﬁcant amount of uniform tests (preferably for
multiple, closely spaced pins), for which the mean bearing capacity and the standard
deviation would give an impression of the overall sensitivity of the connection.
Given all this, there may still be a good 5-10 years of development before this connection
can be a reality, but so far no real discouraging results have been produced that should
discourage the continued development of this type of connection.

Appendix E
Crack angle investigation
This appendix is dedicated to the problem of ﬁnding a reasonable angle in front of the
pin, where forces can be transferred from pin to insert, and insert to glass. The angle is
assumed the same for both interfaces, because the insert simply acts as a buﬀer-layer, to
reduce high stress concentrations, as found in Section 2.2. The crack angle, β, deﬁned in
Figure E.1, is assumed to be somewhat less than 90 degrees, since higher angles would
certainly ensure that tensile and shear forces were carried from the back and side of the
pin to the glass. Since the target is to make the continuum model work as much like a
contact-model as possible, it is desirable to have a crack angle that minimizes transfer of
shear and tensile forces, while still distributing the compressive stresses on a signiﬁcant
area in front of the pin.
