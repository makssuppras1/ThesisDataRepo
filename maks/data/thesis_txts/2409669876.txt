Abstract
With the increased focus on reducing green house gas emissions and the aim to drive
sustainable and climate friendly growth, the new F-gas directive was adopted in the
EU in 2015.
With this, CO2 seems be the preferred refrigerant of the future es-
pecially within mobile applications due to its low global warming potential, good
thermo-physical characteristics and good safety classification.
For the industry and especially within marine applications ultra-low evaporation tem-
peratures with CO2 has been of great interest.
These improvements increase the
freezing capacity on-board by lowering the freezing time, contrary to buying larger
systems, which might not be possible due to limited space. Furthermore, the use of
ejectors might also improve the performance of these systems by reducing expansion
losses and hereby increase the refrigeration capacity. Alternatively, they can be used
for circulating the refrigerant and replace expensive CO2 pumps.
With this motivation a CO2 ejector jet pump system and CO2 liquid ejector overfeed
system was analyzed and the main components such as compressor, ejector, pipes and
condenser was sized for designing a test rig for future research. This was done using a
quasi-static model of the entire refrigeration system. The systems were designed to be
used with five plate freezer sections with a refrigeration load between approximately
45 kW and 5 kW, where the high initial peak value decreases fast and also depending
on the evaporation temperature. It was analyzed with two compressor sizes using a
variable speed drive for capacity control.
Based on this, it was found that the jet pump systems theoretical evaporation tem-
perature limit was at the triple point temperature of -56°C. The freezing time of this
system with fixed evaporation temperature was found to be 84 minutes at -55°C. With
the compressor size of 7.5 m3/hr the freezing time was prolonged by 9.4 minutes or
22.2 minutes with a compressor size of 5 m3/hr. The coefficient of performance of the
system was found to be 2.2 at -55°C evaporation temperature, which corresponds to an
increase of 8.9% relative to direct expansion system at the same boundary conditions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Backgroud
The constant burning of fossil fuels to power our way of life releases large amount
of green house gasses (GHG) into our atmosphere. A green house gas is defined as
any gas that absorbs infrared radiation and hereby contributes to the GHG effect.
GHGs are naturally occurring and are a vital part of keeping a habitable temper-
ature on earths surface. Since the industrial revolution the burning of fossil fuels
and the releasing of GHGs has accelerated drastically. Due to this, GHGs are accu-
mulating in our atmosphere which strengthens the green house effect on earth and
creates what is called global warming i.e the average temperature on earth is increas-
ing. The most direct threat due this, is rising oceans and changing weather patterns.
By now the global average temperature has increased 0.85°C since start of 1900s and
the concentration of CO2 is 40% higher than before the industrial revolution. The
common accepted threshold for the temperature increase is 2°C. Passing this thresh-
old increases the risk of dangerous and catastrophic changes for human life and the
global environment on earth. The most common GHGs are Carbon dioxide (CO2),
methane (CH4), nitrous oxide and fluorinated gases (F-gas). F-gases are emitted in
much lower quantities than CO2 and methane but has a much higher global warming
potential, some up 23.000 times more than CO2 [9].
In 2015 the new F-gas directive was adopted in the EU, which replaced the F-gas
directive from 2006.
The aim of the directive is to drive sustainable and climate
friendly growth within the development of appliances which uses these gasses[10].
These aims are realized by the following directives:
• Restricting the sales of F-gasses from 2015 to 2030. The goal is to reduce the
sales of F gasses to one fifth of 2014 levels.
• Prohibit the use of F-gasses in new equipment where more climate friendly
alternatives are possible.
• Require proper service and check up on existing equipment as to reduce emis-
sions.
These restrictions accelerates change especially within refrigeration and heat pump
technology since this industry generally use F-gasses in their products and appliances.
==PAGE:14==
2
1 Introduction
If producers start to operate with more climate friendly refrigerants they will have an
obvious advantage, especially in regards to cost effectiveness due to taxes imposed
on the high GWP refrigerants. This means that the producers and the industry must
develop and implement new technologies to stay competitive on the market.
R22 (CHClF2) has been the most used refrigerant in Europe until the ratification
of EC Ozone Regulation (EC/2037/2000). This regulation prohibited the use since
2001 due to its ozone depletion properties and to meet the goals under the Montreal
Protocol. The term “use” involves servicing and maintenance and that it is still legal
to operate an existing system provided it does not need to be recharged.
R22 is
still chosen in new systems and retrofitting projects on a global scale outside the
EU. In the future as fluorinated gasses are restricted under the EU F-gas directive
suitable substitutes must be found, which are practically feasible and within the EU
F-gas directive restrictions. The preferred refrigerant to substitute R22 should have
a long term prospective and potential to enable a high-energy efficiency and high
cooling performance within the limited space available on mobile applications. The
safety classification is also of critical importance when the usage is intended in mobile
applications.
CO2 (R-744) is characterized as a natural refrigerant since it is naturally occurring
in the earths atmosphere. It has GWP of 0 or 1 depending on definition and can
also be considered a form of carbon capture. CO2 is a non-flammable, non-toxic and
non-explosive gas. Due to these reasons CO2 is a good possible substitute to replace
the established high GWP F-gasses, which are to be phased out in the near future.
The use of CO2 as a refrigerant is not a new concept and has been studied since 1860s
in refrigeration applications, largely due to its natural occurrence and easy availabil-
ity. Though, by the 1940s CO2 popularity declined when the synthetic refrigerants
entered the market and by 1960 it was almost non-existent both in marine applica-
tion and in land based units.[5]. CO2 poses some technical difficulties mostly related
to the high pressures necessary. At the saturation point of -1°C the corresponding
pressure is approximately 34 baro and 64 baro at 25°C. On the other hand it has
excellent thermo-physical properties such as low viscosity, high thermal conductivity
and high vapor density. This means that more compact systems can be built. With
the advancements in materials science CO2 systems today are competitive against
synthetic based refrigerant systems from both an efficiency and cost effective perspec-
tive in some application areas. Especially in large commercial refrigeration systems
the trend in northern Europe is to switch to CO2 or ammonia systems.
Already back in 1797 fishing vessels began using natural ice refrigeration in order
to preserve the quality of the caught fish. By the end of 1800s refrigerated spaces
on marine vessels became much more prevalent due to advancement in technology
[12]. This made the trade of fruit, meat and other kinds of produce more feasible.
Norway has the largest fishing fleet in Europe based on total capacity of engine power
in kW [11]. On these vessels refrigeration either via transported ice or mechanically
driven systems is critical to preserve the quality of the fish. Companies have started

Motivation
Kuldeteknisk AS is an entrepreneur in Tromsø and partner in FME HighEFF deliver-
ing refrigeration systems to most types of users such as industrial, supermarket and
office buildings. The company has distinguished itself in the market as innovative
and operates continuously with small and larger R&D activities for its customers. In

of decrease heat transfer resistance and thereby decrease the freezing time. Due to

increase refrigeration capacity. Alternatively they might work as a jet pump to circu-
late CO2 to the plate freezers and thereby eliminate the need for the very expensive
low temperature CO2 circulation pumps.

1.3
The refrigeration cycle
The classic refrigeration cycle is called the vapor compression cycle. A principle di-
agram for this refrigeration cycle can be seen in figure 1.1 with the corresponding
log(P)-h and T-s diagram is figure 1.4. As can be seen, this cycle consist of a com-
pressor, condenser, expansion valve and an evaporator. The four components has the
following functions:
• Compressor: circulates the refrigerant through the system with the desired flow
rate and pressure. This is done together with the expansion valve.
• Condenser: condensation of the refrigerant by rejecting heat to the ambient via
a heat exchanger. This heat exchange is established by a temperature difference
between to two sides of the heat exchanger.
• Expansion valve: controls the flow rate to the evaporator and hence the evapo-
rator pressure and temperature.
• Evaporator: evaporation of the refrigerant by absorption of heat via a heat ex-
changer. This heat exchange is established by a temperature difference between
to two sides of the heat exchanger.
==PAGE:17==
1.3 The refrigeration cycle
5
Figure 1.1: Basic system diagram. The numbers indicated are state point referred
to in the text.
The cycle has a high and a low pressure side, which is the condenser side and evapo-
rator side respectively. This pressure difference is established by the compressor and
expansion valve in conjunction. The cycle has the following steps:
1-2: The cycle starts at the compressor where the refrigerant enters as saturated
or super heated vapor on the low pressure side and is compressed and discharged
to the high pressure side as a super heated vapor. This is done by the compressor
utilizing a certain work input.
2-3: From here it enters the condenser and exits as a condensed saturated liquid
by rejection of heat.
3-4: The next step is the receiver making sure only liquid enters the expansion
valve.
Here the refrigerant flow rate in controlled in order to maintain the
desired pressure in the evaporator.
4-1: In the evaporator the refrigerant is evaporated by boiling due to a heat
input. This heat input is the refrigeration capacity of the system and comes
from what the system cools down, may it be in a plate freezer or a certain room
temperature.

Overall 7 regions can be recognized. At very low enthalpy the state of matter is solid
and at high enthalpy, below the critical line, the state is a gas phase. In between

1.3.2
The isentropic process
The isentropic process is an important concept since both compression and expansion
is assumed to approximate this behavior within an assumed isentropic efficiency. The
process requires that it is adiabatic i.e.
no heat or mass transfer on the system
boundary and reversible i.e. no increase in entropy. Due to these requirements it
is an idealized model but useful as a tool to model the behavior of compressors and
turbines etc. The mathematical model of a compressor and expansion based on the
isentropic process can be seen in the methodology chapter under section 2.1.1.6.

The vapor compression cycle is associated with 4 main losses compared to the ideal
Carnot process. These losses are illustrated in figure 1.4 and are denoted L1, L2, L3
and L4 and are the following:
• L1: This loss is due to the compression process not being an ideal isentropic

refrigerant in the compressor which is due to the internal friction of the gas.

components for this replacement could be rotary type expanders or turbine expanders.
Though these types of components have not had commercial success mostly due to
the difficult operating conditions, mechanically complex design and therefore high
cost. Another possible component with a simple and robust design is the ejector,
which will be introduced in the next section.
1.3.4
Ejector technology
The working principle of ejectors follows the Bernoulli’s principle which states that
when the velocity of a fluid increases the pressure of the fluid will decease and vice
versa. The basic configuration of an ejector can be seen in figure 1.5 and consist of
a primary nozzle with a high pressure fluid. A secondary nozzle with a low pressure
fluid. A mixing chamber where the two fluids meet and a diffuser section. It works by
discharging the high pressure fluid through the primary nozzle and hereby transform it
to kinetic energy i.e high velocity and low pressure. This high kinetic energy primary
fluid will then mix with the entrained fluid from the secondary nozzle and interchange
momentum and hereby accelerate the entrained fluid. If the fluid is supersonic a shock
wave will occur and with it a sudden pressure increase. The pressure will increase
again in the diffuser section as the velocity is decreased by the expanding nozzle
geometry.
The use of ejector systems can improve the cycle coefficient of performance (COP)
by reducing the throttling losses associated with the expansion valves in classic vapor
compression cycle. However, designing the ejector for a specific system is challenging
and in order to obtain these improvements the ejector must have the correct geometry
for the specific application. In literature a number of different mathematical models
have been developed in order to calculate the optimal geometry and estimate the
performance.
The model by A. Kornhauser [1] is a pseudo 1D model. It does not take into account
choking effects of the nozzles and the corresponding limits of this, especially on the

the effects of the changed downstream pressure due to the Venturi effect. The mass
flow can be increased by rising the pressure of the primary fluid due to the increased

The subsonic ejector can operate in a critical mode and a sub-critical mode. In the
critical mode the primary flow is choked and the secondary mass flow remains con-

flow pressure it can be seen that the secondary mass flow rate is constant during crit-

choking and the mass flow rate of both secondary and primary flow will be dependent
on the back pressure. With the back pressure fixed and increasing the primary pres-
sure it can be seen that the primary and secondary mass flow rate increases during
sub-critical operation. At some increase in pressure it will shift to critical mode where
the primary fluid will start choking. A further increase will reduce the mass flow rate
of the secondary flow.
The supersonic ejector is double choking in the critical mode and the primary flow
is choking during sub-critical mode. It can be seen by having fixed primary pressure
during critical mode the entrainment ratio is constant since both fluids are choking
and both their mass flows are constant. At some increase in back pressure the sec-
ondary flow will stop choking and be dependent on the back pressure. In this regime a
linear relationship exists. Further increase will result in ejector fault where back flow
will occur. Increasing the primary flow pressure with fixed back pressure will however

1.3.5
Plate freezers
Plate freezers are most commonly used for bulk freezing of food products. They freeze
the food in rectangular blocks with a small width, which insures rapid freezing. This
is important for both the quality of the products and the freezing capacity which can
be installed within a limited space.
Figure 1.7: Principle sketch of a vertical plate freezer.
They are usually constructed in a row configuration with parallel vertical plates where
the refrigerant is evaporated, see figure 1.7. The plates have typical sizes of 0.8m and
0.6m for the width and height respectively. The space between the vertical evaporate
plates can be 0.1m, but can usually be adjusted by a hydraulic press mechanism, which
also insures good contact between the product and the plates. The refrigerant enters
the plates from the bottom and is usually constructed in a single pass configuration
depending on size and manufacturer.
The operational behaviour of the plate freezer versus a traditional evaporator is dif-
ferent. The heat transfer from the product to the plate freezer is decreasing as the
products is freezing due to the fusion process, which increases the thermal resistance
and the temperature between refrigerant and products is also decreasing. This is
demanding operation-wise and usually a setup has multiple plate freezer racks, which
are in use at different times. This reduces the peak load and levels out the demand
on the refrigeration system.

2.2.2
System 2 - Jet pump ejector cycle
This system is a jet pump ejector refrigeration cycle where the ejector circulates
the refrigerant using the liquid from the condenser as the motive force to accelerate
the gas from from the evaporator.
This system has the benefit of unloading the
compressor by increasing the suction pressure. This in turn reduces compression work
and therefore increases the system COP. In figure 2.5 the pressure-enthalpy diagram
and the corresponding P&ID can be seen for this system. The system consist of a
compressor, condenser, ejector, liquid receiver, expansion valve and an evaporator
(plate freezer). It should be noted that due to the ejector, the fluid is expanded in
an isentropic process rather than than in an isenthalpic process as system 1. This
reduces the enthalpy of the fluid slightly during expansion and is illustrated by the
green line in the pressure-enthalpy diagram. The cycle has the following steps:
1a-1b: The cycle starts at state point (1a) with saturated gas leaving the liquid
receiver. From here an amount of the gas is directed to the SG-HEX such that
the overheating at (1b) is 5 K. This is controlled by the three-way valve.
1b-2: The superheated gas (1b) enters the compressor and from here the gas
is compressed isentropically with an isentropic efficiency and leaves as a high
pressure superheated gas at (2).
2-3a: The superheated gas (2) is condensed and exits the condenser as a satu-
rated liquid (3a).
3a-3b: The saturated liquid (3a) enters the SG-HEX and exits as a subcooled
liquid (3b). The degree of subcooling matches the rejected heat between (1a)
and (1b).
3b-4: The subcooled liquid from the condenser (3b) is expanded, accelerated
and mixed with the gas from the evaporator (7) and then slowed down to raise
the static pressure and exits as a two phase refrigerant (4).
4-5: The two phase refrigerant enters the liquid receiver that separates the flow.
The gas leaves at the top of the liquid receiver to (1a). The liquid exits in the
bottom as a saturated liquid (5).
5-6: The liquid (5) is expanded to the evaporator pressure and enters the two
phase region (6).The expansion is assumed an isenthalpic process.
6-7: The flow (6) enters the evaporator and exits as a saturated gas (7)

2.2.3
System 3 - Liquid overfeed cycle
This system is a liquid overfeed system where the ejector recirculates the refrigerant
and overfeeds the evaporator such that dry-out of the refrigerant is eliminated. This
increases the heat transfer over the evaporator. The refrigerant is assumed to exit the
evaporator as a two phase flow and not as a saturated gas due to this overfeeding. In
figure 2.5 the pressure-enthalpy diagram and the corresponding P&ID can be seen for
this system. The system consists of a compressor, condenser, ejector, liquid receiver,
expansion valve and an evaporator (plate freezer). It can be seen that the quality of
the secondary flow is lower in this cycle which also results in a lower outlet quality
of the ejector.
This also results in steeper isentropic lines during compression as
mentioned in section 1.3.2. The cycle has the following steps:
1a-1b: The cycle starts at state point (1a) with saturated gas leaving the liquid
receiver. From here an amount of the gas is directed to the SG-HEX such that
the overheating at (1b) is 5 K. This is controlled by the three-way valve.
1b-2: The superheated gas (1b) enters the compressor and from here the gas
is compressed isentropically with an isentropic efficiency and leaves as a high
pressure superheated gas at (2).
2-3a: The superheated gas (2) is condensed and exits the condenser as a satu-
rated liquid (3a).
3a-3b: The saturated liquid (3a) enters the SG-HEX and exits as a subcooled
liquid (3b). The degree of subcooling matches the rejected heat between (1a)
and (1b).
3b-4: The subcooled liquid from the condenser (3b) is expanded, accelerated
and mixed with the two-phase flow from (7) and then slowed down to raise the
static pressure and exits as a two phase flow (4).
4-5: The two phase refrigerant (4) enters the evaporator and exits with an
assumed quality (5).
5-6: The two phase refrigerant (5) enters the liquid receiver and separates the
two phases. The gas leaves at the top to the SG-HEX (1a). The liquid exits in
the bottom as a saturated liquid (6).
6-7: The liquid (6) is expanded to the desired secondary nozzle pressure and
enters the two phase region (7). The expansion is assumed an isenthalpic pro-
cess.

2.3 Design of experimental test rig
33
The experimental test rig is to be build in conjunction with an existing refrigeration
system. Due to this the entire system consists of a high temperature cycle and low
temperature cycle. The high temperature cycle will be a 2-stage CO2 refrigeration
system located at NTNU in Trondheim. This system is delivered by the company
Advansor from Aarhus, Denmark and has 3 different evaporation temperature levels,
at -8°C and -38°C and -48°C. The experimental test rig designed is to condense against
the -8°C evaporation level as indicated on the P&ID.
2.3.1
Dimensioning parameters
The rig to be designed is sized based on the following decisions:
• 5 plate freezer sections with dimensions of 0.8 m and 0.6 m for the width and
height respectively. This makes it possible to freeze down 4 blocks at a time and
gives a total of 8 active freezing surfaces with total area of 3.84 m2. The space
between the plates are assumed to 0.1m. These are the same sizes as indicated
the boundary conditions table for the three evaluated system layouts in table
2.2, 2.3 and 2.4.
• Condense against the the high temperature stage with a maximum of 5 K tem-
perature difference during the freezing process.
• The pipes in the experimental test rig are sized such that the refrigerant ve-
locities are within the below mentioned interval. These velocities are based on
recommendations from the company Advansor [3].
– Compressor discharge pipes 5-12 m/s
– Medium temperature suction pipe 3-12 m/s.
– Low temperature suction pipe 5-15 m/s
– Return liquid from condenser pipe 2-4 m/s
– Liquid line to evaporator pipe 1-2 m/s

In this model the heat transfer decreases fast because the ice formation introduces a
large thermal resistance. As the heat flux decreases so does the VFR and the neces-

not feasible and the flow rate would be restricted by the compressor. In this case the
evaporation temperature would increase in order to obtain equilibrium between the
evaporation of the refrigerant and the heat flux. This is due to the pressure increase
in the evaporator since the compressor can not remove the vapour fast enough.

the thermal resistance of the growing ice boundary has become constant.

temperature of -55°C and -15°C respectively. The compressor pressure ratio is increas-

ratio is the main component in the decreasing trend in COP at lower evaporation
temperatures.

This is because the lower evaporation temperature means a lower saturated evaporator
pressure. The pressure difference between the outlet of the ejector to the evaporator
varies between 0.8 bar to 0.4 bar decreasing with higher evaporation temperature.
This is due to the expansion valve that ensures the correct evaporation temperature.
The compressor pressure ratio is relatively low and it should not be a problem for to
find reciprocating type compressor for this interval.
The ejector entrainment ratio is increasing in a linear trend with decreasing temper-
atures from around 0.95 at -15°C to 0.72 at -55°C. The entrainment ratio is fixed by
the quality out of the ejector. A similar trend can be seen for the ejector pressure
ratio which also increases from 1.01 to 1.13. Both of these parameters are coupled.
With decreasing temperatures the enthalpy of the saturated vapor out of the evap-
orator i.e. secondary flow increases slightly. This means that the enthalpy of the
mixed fluid in the mixing section will also increase which corresponds to a higher
quality. With increasing enthalpy of the saturated vapor the slope of the isentropic
lines decrease. This means that the expansion of the fluid in the ejector nozzle has
a larger enthalpy difference and therefore increases the fluid velocity. This increased
velocity will in turn give a larger static pressure increase in the diffuser. This pressure
increase follows the isentropic lines which also corresponds to a higher quality and
enthalpy.
Fixed max volume flow rate
In figure 3.8 the results of the simulation with a restricted max volume flow of 9 and
13.5 m3/h is shown for system 2. The argument for these max flow rates is explained
in section 3.2.1.
This plot is done with a minimum evaporation temperature of -
55°C. As can be seen, after a certain time the volume flow rate decreases which
coincides with a constant evaporation temperature (TCO2). This is necessary in order
to keep the evaporation temperature constant at the minimum of -55°C. If the volume
flow rate was not reduced the evaporation temperature would keep decreasing. This
behaviour is because the change in energy of the refrigerant has to equal the heat
flux. So when the heat flux decreases due to increasing thermal resistance so must
the volume flow rate i.e mass flow in order to keep the balance.

4.1
Results
Two main system layouts have been evaluated. An ejector jet pump system desig-
nated system 2 and a liquid overfeed ejector system designated system 3. This has
been done using the theoretical framework introduced in section 2.1. This work re-
sulted in a quasi-static model of the individual systems. The two systems are hard to
compare in the sense that they serve very different purposes. The jet pump system
(system 2) uses all the recovered work to increase the suction pressure to the com-
pressor and hereby reduces the pressure ratio and thus also the compression work.
This improves the COP of the cycle. On other hand, in the liquid overfeed system
(system 3) the recovered expansion work is used to circulate refrigerant and increase
the performance of the evaporator.
The largest difference between system 2 and 3 is the possible minimum evaporation
temperature.
System 2 can theoretically go all the way down to the triple point
assuming this is the minimum allowable temperature. However, System 3 can only
reach approximately -40°C due to the secondary nozzle pipe line is at a lower pressure
than the evaporator pressure, which is necessary to operate at a constant stable pres-
sure out of the ejector. By decreasing the quality out of the evaporator i.e. increasing
the entrainment ratio for system 3, the pressure in the secondary pipe-line can be
increased and in this way the possible minimum evaporation temperate decreased.
This can be seen in the sensitivity analysis in figure 3.17 on the lower left plot. In
addition the ejector efficiencies also has a large impact on the pressure. Though, due
to time constraints this has not been investigated but should be addressed due to its
significant importance on the freezing time.
It was found that system 2 had a COP of 2.2, 3.0 and 4.2 at the evaporation temper-
atures of -55°C, -45°C and -35°C, which corresponds to an increase of 8.9 7.6 and 6%
relative the traditional vapor compression cycle. System 3 had a COP of 3.3 and 4.0
at the temperatures of -40°C and -35°C with no significant increase relative to the
vapor compression cycle. Generally COP of system 2 was slightly higher than for sys-
tem 3. The compressor pressure ratio was also found to be lower for system 2 than for
system 3. The freezing times at the same temperatures were very similar, but system
3 was slightly faster for freezing the water to -20°C. Though, this is dependent on

The optimum ejector AR for the systems were very different.
System 2 had an
optimum AR between 9-10, where 9.5 was chosen for the later analysis. For system
3 the optimum AR was between 2.2-2.3 where 2.25 was chosen for further analysis.
Both of these AR’s are calculated based on the chosen mixing pressure. The mixing
pressure was found by iterating the ejector model for each system with different mixing
pressures and different evaporation temperatures and then the conditions with the
highest ejector efficiency based on equation 2.26 was chosen. This optimal ejector
efficiency might not coincide with highest cycle COP.
The very large pressure lift by system 3, which was 1.2-1.8 is likely due to the lower
quality fluid. The amount of KE from the primary fluid is larger for system 3 than
for system 2 due to the lower mixing pressure. At the same time the average quality
of the fluid in system 3 is lower than system 2. This means that the slope of the
isentropic lines for the compression are much steeper for system 3, which will result
in a larger pressure lift. The pressure lift for system 2 was between 1-1.12.
In the study [15] by N. Lawrence they studied the same two system layouts as in
this paper with the refrigerant 410A on an experimental air-conditioning unit. In
their study it was found that the liquid overfeed system had a larger COP increase
compared to the jet pump system. This is in opposition to the results found in this
thesis. In their air-conditioning system the evaporation temperature is not fixed but
the outlet air temperature is. This means that increasing the performance of the
evaporator reduces the temperature difference necessary for the heat transfer. This
reduces the compression ratio and hence the compression work. This gives an increase
in the cycle COP. In the systems evaluated in this study, the evaporation temperature
is fixed and therefore the reduced compression work cannot be obtained. However,
the freezing time can be reduced with the increased heat transfer. In N. Lawrence’s
study it is also mentioned that for the ejector jet pump system it might be better to
increase the entrainment ratio and reduce the pressure lift and in this way recirculate
more fluid through the evaporator and increase its performance. Operation in this
manner has not been investigated in the paper and it is assumed to be saturated
vapor exiting the evaporator for the jet pump cycle, system 2. Another important
note is that they used the same ejector for both systems and they postulate that the
ejector geometry of the overfeed system is not of high importance as long as it can
provide sufficient pressure to overfeed the evaporator and overcome the pressure loss.

ejector efficiency and the VFR, PR compressor, PR ejector and entrainment value was
fairly robust to changes in the ejector efficiencies, compressor efficiency, superheating
before the compressor and condensation temperature. The sensitivity for system 3
was in general most sensitive to changes compared to system 2. Especially the ejector

4.2
Ejector model
The ejector solutions found for system 2 and 3 are in the subsonic regime. The veloc-
ity of the primary flow in system 2 is between 140 and 115 m/s in the temperature
range of -55°C and -45°C assuming ideal size ejector. The velocity for system 3 is
between 170-140 m/s in the range -45°C and -35°C. These velocities are calculated
based on the KE of the streams in figures 3.3 and 3.11. The Mach speed of CO2 can
be seen in appendix A.4 and the lowest speed is 223 m/s for a saturated gas state. The
benefit of subsonic ejectors is the lower pressure loss. Though supersonic ejectors are
generally able to attain higher pressure ratios due to the higher velocities. Another
complication for supersonic ejectors at very low temperature might be the possibility
of solid particles forming in the mixing section, which could block or disturb the flow.

The solutions found are based on a guessed ejector efficiency for the primary, sec-
ondary and diffuser sections. Having used other values other solutions might have
been the optimal choice e.g. lowering the diffuser efficiency parameter. The values
were chosen in order to stay below 0.3 overall ejector efficiency. The actual efficiency
values have to be calibrated based on empirical data so that the model predicts the
behaviour as close to the real behaviour as possible.
Some studies have been done on this topic e.g. [8] for the refrigerants R134a, R1234yf
and R600a.
In this analysis the ejector efficiencies were found to be ηp = 0.75,
ηs = 0.70 and ϕm = 0.8 and ϕp = 0.9 for two phase flow. Here the ϕ are frictional
loss coefficients for the primary and mixing sections. The ejector model is generally
similar except that it is assumed that the ejector is supersonic and double choking.
Instead of assuming a mixing pressure it is assumed both streams reach sonic condi-
tions. This study, which estimated the frictional and isentropic efficiencies based on
data, found good agreement between the model and experiment. For the two-phase
flow of this study the error was between 0.6-4.4% for the different parameters of the
system based on experimental data from Ersoy & Sag (2014). No studies has been
found which estimates these isentropic efficiencies for CO2 under similar operating
conditions as in this thesis.
This means that the found ejector sizes might not be reliable due to the very complex
behaviour of ejectors and the lack of empirical data. The next step in this work is
therefore an empirical approach and the found solution in this thesis might work as
a starting point. However, as previously mentioned the study by N. Lawrence [15]
used the same geometry for both a jet pump cycle and liquid overfeed.
4.3
Heat transfer model
The time of freezing was found to change 14% when changing the convective heat
transfer coefficient between the refrigerant and the plate over the range of 500 to
6000 W/m2-K. The reference values used for system 2 and 3 is 2800 and 3500 W/m2-
K, respectively. If the increased convective heat transfer coefficient of system 3 is
underestimated this would decrease the calculated freezing time. The COP would
be mostly unchanged in this model due to the fixed evaporation temperature. In
opposition, if the heat transfer coefficient of system 2 is overestimated the freezing
time would increase.
The chosen convective heat transfer coefficients are average values over the entire
evaporation process. In [17] by. J. Pettersen the heat convective heat transfer coeffi-
cient for CO2 in micro channel (0.81mm diameter) flows were found to range between
10 and 20 kW/m2-K with mass fluxes between 190-570 kg/m2-s for nucleate boiling.
The heat transfer post dry-out had significant lower convective heat transfer ranging

900-2000 W/m2 −K with a mass flux of 360-720 kg/m2-s based on data from Groen-
eveld and Delorme (1966) [16]. The dry-out happens faster at higher mass fluxes

the benefit of an oil free system could benefit the system a lot in regards to freezing
time.
4.4
Temperature limitations
Literature on refrigeration systems operating below the triple point is very limited.
The paper by H. Yamaguchi [22] explores the behaviour of dry ice blockages. This
was done for a two-stage CO2 refrigeration system reaching -62°C. The operation of
this system was found to exhibit a self cleaning behavior where the accumulation of
dry ice would reach a certain size and then fall apart. This resulted in small pressure
variations, but no blockages occurred during the 180 minute experiment. It was found
that a low flow velocity resulted in larger dry ice particles and accumulation due the
the particles colliding easier and forming larger particles.
Another important aspect is the heat transfer coefficients that can be achieved with
a solid-gas flow. If the heat transfer cannot reach sufficient large values the size of
the sublimator will be to large and expensive and make such systems unfeasible. The
paper by XR. Zhang [23] reported the average convective heat transfer coefficient of
the same system as by H. Yamaguchi to be 310 W/m2-K, but acknowledges that more
studies need to be conducted on this topic. This might be larger than CO2 gas under
some conditions, but it is still much less than that of liquid CO2 and the average
convective heat transfer during flow boiling in a traditional evaporator.

4.5
Additional considerations
These results are based on a quasi-static model that does not take into account the
dynamic behaviour. The dynamic is mostly present at the beginning of the freezing
process where the model changes a lot with time. The freezing process of large slabs
with small width does have unsteady state heat transfer characteristics. Though, it
is to the authors understanding that the chosen approach in this paper estimates the
true behaviour reasonable well, in order to use it for analysis and design of the systems.
The systems are simulated with one rack consisting of five plate freezer sections that
freezes four blocks of water. Because of this the heat transfer variation from start
until the end is very large. This demanding operational behavior will be true for the
experimental test rig. In the industry and on fishing vessels multiple plate freezer
racks would be used at different times. In this way the heat transfer demand would
be leveled out.
The systems should likely be designed with a possible by-pass of the ejector with an
expansion valve during the initial start up of the system.
4.6
Future work
The best way forward is possibly an empirical approach to find the boundaries and
operational characteristics of ejectors in this setup and at this temperature level.
Though some design considerations still needs to be made. The two main subjects
lacking, that still needs to be approached, are control system design and oil return
system for the systems.
In regards to theoretical considerations, that should be addressed, is increasing the
entrainment value for the system 3 and hereby decreasing the possible evaporation
temperature. This issue is by far the most restricting factor in regards to lowering
the freezing time for this system. Increasing the entrainment value would likely also
benefit system 2, as a way to increase the evaporator performance and lower the
freezing time. Though, it would decrease the cycle COP due to the reduced suction
pressure.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
For the industry and especially within marine applications ultra-low evaporation tem-
peratures has been of great interest. These improvements increase the freezing capac-
ity on-board by lowering the freezing time, contrary to buying larger systems, which
might not be possible due to limited space. Furthermore, the use of ejectors might
also improve the performance and cost of these systems by reducing expansion losses
and hereby increase the refrigeration capacity. Alternatively, they can be used for
circulating the refrigerant and replace expensive CO2 pumps.
With this motivation a CO2 ejector jet pump system (system 2) and CO2 liquid ejec-
tor overfeed system (system 3) was analyzed and the main components were sized
for designing a test rig for future research. This was done using a quasi-static model
of the entire refrigeration system. The systems were designed for being used with 5
plate freezer sections corresponding to a refrigeration load between approximately 45
kW and 5 kW, where the high initial peak value decreases fast and also depending
on the evaporation temperature. The designed test rig was to be used together with
an existing refrigeration system and therefore condense against the evaporator of this
system at -8°C with a maximum of 5 K temperature difference. This corresponded
to a condenser size between 17 and 3 m2 depending on the overall heat transfer co-
efficient which was 500 and 3000 W/m2-K respectively for the mentioned sizes. The
compressor size was analyzed for the two sizes og 5 and 7.5 m3/hr at 50 Hz, which
with the Danfoss FC 103 drive, gives a gliding capacity range between 2.5 and 9.0
m3/hr or 3.75 and 13.5 m3/hr, respectively for the two compressors.
Based on this, it was found that the jet pump systems theoretical evaporation tem-
perature limit was at the triple point temperature of -56°C. The optimal ejector area
ratio was found to be 9.5 and an ejector setup was proposed consisting of three differ-
ently sized ejector with sizes of 1, 1.5, 2.5 mm2 for the compressor size of 13.5 m3/hr.
This gives a total of 6 ejector capacity. The freezing time of this system with fixed
evaporation temperature was found to be 84 minutes at -55°C. With the compressor
size of 7.5 m3/hr the freezing time was prolonged by 9.4 minutes or 22.2 minutes
with a compressor size of 5 m3/hr. The COP of the system was found to have been
increased by 6% relative to direct expansion system (system 3) at the same boundary
conditions.
==PAGE:86==
74
5 Conclusion
For the liquid overfeed system the theoretical evaporation temperature limit was at
the temperature of -40°C. This higher limit is due to the pipeline going to the sec-
ondary ejector nozzle chamber reaching pressures of 5.1 bar i.e the pressure at the
triple point. Though this behavior can be reduced by increasing the entrainment
value of the ejector. The optimal ejector area ratio was found to be 2.25 and an
ejector setup was proposed consisting of three differently sized ejector with sizes of
1.5, 2, 2.5 mm2 for the compressor size of 13.5 m3/hr. This again gives a total of 6
ejector capacity. The freezing time of this system with fixed evaporation temperature
was found to be 117.4 minutes at -40°C. With the compressor size of 7.5 m3/hr the
freezing time was prolonged by 4.0 minutes or 8.8 minutes with a compressor size of 5
m3/hr. The COP of this system was unchanged relative to the direct expansion cycle.
