Abstract
Fairness in biometric systems requires accurate and unbiased skin tone classification.
This study evaluates face image quality assessment algorithms to identify biases in skin
tone estimation.
Classification methods were employed based on predefined RGB color classes, utilizing
the Google SkinTone guide with ten distinct skin tone categories. The datasets consisted
of FFHQ and LFW images, supplemented by 10,000 synthetic images generated using
StyleGAN and 50,000 hyperparameter­controlled images. Additionally, a subset of 1,500
manually labeled images was incorporated to establish ground truth for evaluation.
To improve accuracy, the TRUST model (Toward a Racially Unbiased Skin Tone Estima­
tion) was utilized. This model reconstructs facial geometry using the FLAME 3D model
and estimates albedo to separate intrinsic skin properties from lighting effects.
Four classification models were assessed: Weighted Grayscale, HSV, YCbCr, and Aver­
age Grayscale. Each model provides a unique perspective on skin tone representation:
• Weighted Grayscale improves accuracy by considering human visual sensitivity to
RGB channels.
• HSV separates hue, saturation, and value components for perceptual analysis.
• YCbCr isolates luminance from chrominance for better skin tone differentiation.
Key Findings
• Bias Score analysis revealed variations in classification accuracy across different
datasets and color spaces. The highest bias was observed in lfw Hyperparameter
­ Weighted Grayscale (1.658), while the lowest bias was found in ffhq StyleGAN
­ YCbCr (0.801).
• Models trained on lfw Hyperparameter datasets exhibited higher bias scores,
with YCbCr, HSV, and Grayscale models consistently ranking among the most
biased.
• ffhq StyleGAN models showed significantly lower bias scores, suggesting more
balanced skin tone classification across all categories.
• The ideal distribution assumption (10% per class) was not met across any dataset,
with systematic deviations in classification results.
• Weighted Grayscale models generally exhibited higher bias compared to YCbCr
and HSV models, indicating that incorporating chrominance information helps re­
duce classification disparities.
• The findings highlight the need for improved bias mitigation strategies in skin tone
classification models, particularly for datasets with higher variance in illumination
and demographic diversity.

Introduction
The introduction of this work is structured into five main sections. It begins with Mo­
tivation, highlighting the significance of the research and its practical implications. The
Initial Problem Statement (IPS) then defines the core challenges addressed in the study.
Following this, the Research Questions section outlines the key inquiries guiding the in­
vestigation. The Overview of the Project provides a concise summary of the objectives,
scope, and methodology.
The last section focuses on Face Image Quality Assessment, with a special emphasis
on the role of skin tone in this evaluation. This section highlights the importance of
fairness in face image quality assessment algorithms concerning skin tone and
explores how existing algorithms perform in this regard. Within this context, the Open
Source Face Image Quality (OFIQ) tool is introduced as a key method for analyzing face
image quality. This tool provides a practical means to evaluate and enhance image quality,
ultimately improving the reliability and fairness of biometric systems, particularly in
relation to skin tone.
1.1
Motivation
Biometric systems are increasingly integrated into our daily lives, serving critical applica­
tions in security, healthcare, and technology. However, despite their widespread adop­
tion, biases [1] related to skin tone remain a significant challenge, for quality algorithms in
face image quality assessment algorithms. Research indicates that such biases often re­
sult in reduced performance for individuals with darker skin tones, raising concerns about
fairness, inclusivity, and ethical implications. Addressing these disparities is essential for
creating equitable and reliable biometric systems.
The ability to accurately identify and classify skin tones plays a crucial role in understand­
ing and mitigating these biases. Current approaches often rely on simplistic models that
fail to capture the diversity of human skin tones, leading to inconsistent and biased out­
comes. With advancements in machine learning techniques such as Generative Adver­
sarial Networks (GANs) [2][3][4][5], Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) [6], and the TRUST
model [7] for albedo estimation[8], it is now possible to simulate and analyze a wide range
of skin tones more effectively. This study focuses on evaluating multiple approaches to
skin tone classification, with the Weighted Grayscale Method as the primary approach,
alongside three parallel models: Average Grayscale, HSV, and YCbCr. By employing ad­
vanced tools for albedo estimation, robust skin tone classification pipelines, and diverse
datasets, this research aims to comprehensively evaluate face image quality assessment
algorithms. Ultimately, the findings will contribute to the development of more inclusive
biometric technologies that accurately represent individuals across all skin tones. This
study also aims to examine the impact of lighting conditions on the accuracy of facial skin
tone recognition. Changes in light intensity and type can alter the perceived skin tone,
which may lead to mismatches and inconsistencies in the classification process. Inappro­
priate lighting, such as overexposure or heavy shadows, can result in misclassification,
particularly for individuals with darker skin tones. The evaluation and computation of these
quality components are defined in the ISO/IEC DIS 29794­5 standard [9] . This standard
establishes guidelines for measuring face image quality to enhance fairness and reliability
in biometric applications.

1.2
Initial Problem Statement (IPS)
Biometric systems play a key role in security, healthcare, and technology, yet biases
related to skin tone remain a major challenge in this field. Face Image Quality As­
sessment (FIQA) algorithms often perform less effectively for individuals with darker
skin tones, raising concerns about fairness and inclusivity in biometric systems. Accu­
rate and unbiased skin tone classification is essential to improve fairness and mitigate
these biases.
Traditional classification models often fail to capture the full diversity of human skin tones,
leading to inconsistent and biased results. In addition, lighting conditions signif­
icantly affect skin tone perception, causing errors in classification and potential
misidentification in face recognition systems. Addressing these challenges requires
more sophisticated approaches that integrate advanced machine learning techniques and
robust dataset processing methods.
To tackle these issues, this research investigates and enhances skin tone classification
methods in FIQA, integrating advanced tools and methodologies:
TRUST Model [7]: Utilizes albedo estimation techniques to separate intrinsic skin
properties from lighting effects, ensuring accurate skin tone detection across dif­
ferent illumination conditions.
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs): Expands the dataset by simulating diverse
skin tones, enabling better evaluation of classification models.
Hyperparameter Utilization: Incorporates skin tone categories (MST 1 to 10) [10] into
2D albedo representations to create a color­normalized dataset, improving fairness
and consistency in classification.
Dataset Annotation: Ensures precise ground truth labeling of skin tones to enhance
dataset reliability and contribute to more accurate classification outcomes.
Skin Tone Classification Pipelines: Compares Weighted Grayscale as the primary
method, alongside HSV, YCbCr, and Average Grayscale, to identify the most effective
skin tone classification technique.
Distance Metrics: Applies Euclidean Distance to match extracted skin tone values to
predefined classes using the Google SkinTone guide.
By systematically analyzing and evaluating different classification approaches, this re­
search aims to bridge the gaps in current methodologies and contribute to the develop­
ment of more inclusive, fair, and reliable biometric systems.

1.3
Research Questions and Overview
The research questions define the framework of this study for assessing and evaluating
skin tone in face images. These questions not only establish the study’s objective but
also guide the analysis and investigation of challenges related to accuracy and fairness
in face image quality assessment algorithms and biometric systems.
1.3.1
Research Questions
The primary research questions addressed in this thesis are as follows:
1. How can deep generative models be used to generate a fully synthetic face image
dataset that is balanced in terms of skin tones?
2. Can the fully synthetic and balanced dataset be used to identify biases within face
image quality assessment algorithms?
3. Which visualization methods can be used to demonstrate that a quality assessment
algorithm is biased toward skin tones?
1.3.2
Overview of the Project
• Selection and Optimization: Choosing and optimizing the FLAME 3D face model
and TRUST framework for extracting and analyzing skin tone features.
• Dataset Generation and Analysis: Creating synthetic datasets using Genera­
tive Adversarial Networks (GANs) and integrating them with real datasets for
comprehensive analysis.
• Algorithm Evaluation: Assessing the fairness and accuracy of OFIQ algorithms
in classifying different Monk skin tones.
• Result Analysis: Identifying biases and proposing improvements to enhance fair­
ness in biometric systems.

Background
2.1
Face Image Quality Assessment (FIQA)
In operational face recognition systems, the quality of biometric samples is crucial to en­
suring accurate and reliable recognition. The quality of face images is represented by
either an overall quality score or specific quality components, such as uniform lighting,
sharpness, and proper pose. Assessing these components provides valuable feedback
for improving the capture process, ultimately enhancing system performance.
2.2
Open Source Face Image Quality (OFIQ)
The Open Source Face Image Quality (OFIQ) framework, developed by Germany’s BSI
organization [11], is a groundbreaking tool for assessing face image quality in biometric
systems. This framework aims to enhance the performance and usability of biometric data
by providing a standardized, transparent method for evaluating face images. Highquality
data is critical for the efficiency of large scale systems like the European Union’s Entry
Exit System (EES), which handles hundreds of millions of records.
Unlike quality assessment algorithms NFIQ since 2004, facial images lacked a compa­
rable standard until OFIQ’s development. This framework evaluates face image quality
using over 20 distinct components, such as lighting, head positioning, and sharpness,
directly impacting recognition accuracy. OFIQ identifies specific areas needing improve­
ment, enhancing image capture processes and overall system performance.
As the reference implementation for ISO/IEC 29794­5, OFIQ supports both national and
international biometric systems. Its open source nature ensures widespread adoption
and interoperability. The tool evaluates face image quality based on divided into capture
related and subject related categories, offering detailed feedback on a 0 – 100. By ad­
dressing critical gaps in quality assessment, OFIQ fosters unbiased, reliable, and efficient
biometric systems while promoting global standardization and fairness [12][13]. From the
28 quality components defined in the ISO/IEC 29794 standard, this section will focus on
six quality components for Facial Image Assessment from OFIQ that are related to skin
tone and facial image quality.

2.3.1
Fairness Considerations in Bias and Variance
Achieving fairness in machine learning requires balancing bias and variance, as both sig­
nificantly impact model accuracy and generalization across diverse user groups [15]. High
bias leads to oversimplified models that may ignore essential features of certain groups,
causing unfair predictions. For instance, a facial recognition model trained primarily on
lighter skin tones may perform poorly on darker skin tones [16][17]. Conversely, a model
trained predominantly on darker skin tones under low­light conditions might struggle to
recognize the same individuals in well­lit environments due to high variance.
On the other hand, high variance results in models that overfit to training data, reduc­
ing fairness when applied to new data. A model trained predominantly on images with
lighter skin tones might fail to generalize well to darker­skinned individuals under varying
lighting conditions [18]. Similarly, a model overfitting to specific skin tones may produce
inconsistent results when tested on diverse populations.

Generative Adversarial Networks
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [4] [20] are transformative tools in skin tone
simulation, enabling realistic manipulation of skin tones in digital images. GANs gener­
ate synthetic data by employing an adversarial framework, addressing challenges like
fairness and diversity in machine learning datasets.
Structure and Functionality of GANs: GANs consist of two competing neural networks:
Generator (G): Generates synthetic images or skin tone adjustments from random noise
or latent spaces. Discriminator (D): Distinguishes real images from generated ones, pro­
viding feedback that improves the generator. This iterative process enhances the realism
of generated skin tones, making them indistinguishable from real images [21] [22].
GANs are pivotal in ethnicity alteration, modifying skin tones and facial features to rep­
resent diverse ethnicities, thus addressing demographic imbalances in facial recognition
datasets. Advanced models like StyleGAN and Pix2Pix create synthetic datasets with
varied skin tones, fostering fairness in machine learning models [21] [23].
Realism is enhanced through techniques like total variation loss and color correction.
These methods minimize noise, ensure smooth transitions, and align synthetic images
with real­world skin tones. Such refinements are essential in domains like entertainment
and forensic investigations, where realism matters [24] [22].
Despite their effectiveness, GAN­based skin tone simulations face challenges like dataset
bias and lighting variations. GANs generate synthetic data to balance skin tone represen­
tation and mitigate distortions caused by lighting, using advanced training techniques and
optimizations in color spaces like YCbCr [25] [23].
Technical hurdles, such as artifacts in generated images, are overcome with loss functions
like perceptual loss and truncation tricks, ensuring smooth transitions and reducing visual
distortions. Additionally, lightweight GANs have been developed for real­time applications
in AR and VR environments [22] [24] [25] [23].

This chapter explores the latest advancements and challenges in skin tone fairness eval­

3.1
Advances in Skin Tone Fairness and 3D­Aware Face
Image Quality Assessment
In this research, advancements in face image quality assessment (FIQA) have been ex­
plored to enhance fairness and reliability in biometric systems. A notable contribution
is the work of Grimmer et al. [34], which introduces innovative approaches using 3D­
aware synthetic data. Their study aligns with ISO/IEC CD3 29794­5 standards, ensuring
comprehensive quality evaluation and actionable feedback for improving image capture
processes.
One key advancement is the Syn­YawPitch dataset, which contains 144,000 synthetic
images with controlled yaw and pitch variations. This dataset enables precise evaluation
of pose impact on face recognition accuracy, supporting the development of pose qual­
ity estimators in compliance with ISO/IEC standards. The use of synthetic data allows
controlled analysis of pose, lighting, and geometry on FIQA.
Another major innovation is 3D­aware face image synthesis. Traditional models like
StyleGAN excel in generating realistic images but struggle with preserving 3D facial
structure. In contrast, EG3D integrates explicit and implicit 3D representations, ensuring
structural consistency and improving pose impact analysis. These advancements con­
tribute to more robust and reliable FIQA algorithms.
Demographic fairness remains a crucial concern, as studies reveal biases in face qual­
ity metrics, disproportionately affecting individuals with darker skin tones. Component­
based quality measures, such as pose­specific estimators, help evaluate fairness by
isolating individual variability factors. Additionally, 3D Morphable Face Models (3DMMs)
allow the separation of skin tone from lighting effects, improving the accuracy and im­
partiality of quality assessments.
Furthermore, SYP­Lasso, a lightweight regression model proposed by Grimmer et al.,
enhances pose­dependent quality estimation while adhering to ISO/IEC standards. These
advancements collectively establish a foundation for equitable FIQA frameworks, ad­
dressing both technical and ethical challenges in biometric fairness.

evaluate the impact of demographic factors, such as race and gender, on biometric sam­

Evaluating fairness in Face Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) algorithms is crucial for

features [37, 38]. Furthermore, fairness metrics such as the Gini Aggregation Rate for
Biometric Equitability (GARBE) and Fairness Discrepancy Rates (FDR) have become
essential in quantifying and addressing biases in FIQA systems [36].
Despite these advancements, significant challenges remain, including occlusion effects,
illumination variations, and the underrepresentation of darker skin tones in training
datasets. These issues amplify systemic biases and hinder the fairness and reliability of
FIQA assessments [38]. Thus, continuous research and methodological improvements
are necessary to develop FIQA algorithms capable of providing equitable evaluations
across diverse demographic groups [13, 38].

bias. Experimental results confirm that the proposed method significantly enhances skin

d =

5.3.2
Color Conversion and Matching with the Monk Skin Tone Scale
The Monk skin tone classes are analyzed across multiple color spaces, including RGB,
HSV, Grayscale (Weighted and Average), and YCbCr. Each color space provides unique
advantages for the representation and analysis of skin tones, allowing for a more detailed
understanding of their characteristics.
The RGB color space is widely used for image representation, as it directly encodes colors
in terms of Red, Green, and Blue components. However, alternative color spaces such as
HSV, Grayscale, and YCbCr offer better perceptual and analytical advantages in certain
applications:
Skin Tone Fairness Evaluation of FIQA Algorithms based on 3D Morphable Face Models
31
==PAGE:44==
• HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value): This color space separates the chromatic compo­
nents (Hue and Saturation) from the intensity (Value), enabling perceptually uniform
analysis and facilitating tasks such as color classification.
• YCbCr (Luminance­Chrominance): Commonly used in image and video process­
ing, this color space separates brightness (Y) from chromatic components (Cb and
Cr), making it effective for tasks like skin tone detection and compression.
• Grayscale: Grayscale reduces the color representation to a single channel of in­
tensity, simplifying analysis:
– Average Grayscale: This method calculates the mean of the Red, Green, and
Blue channels, providing a basic representation of brightness.
– Weighted Grayscale: In this approach, weights are assigned to the Red,
Green, and Blue channels (0.299 × R + 0.587 × G + 0.114 × B), with more
emphasis on the Green channel to align with human visual perception.
Converting the Monk skin tone classes to these color spaces allows for a more compre­
hensive analysis, as each space highlights different aspects of the data. The inclusion
of both Weighted and Average Grayscale ensures a detailed examination of brightness
characteristics, while HSV and YCbCr provide chromatic and luminance insights.

5.4
Bias Score Analysis
In this analysis, the Bias Score is calculated to evaluate the deviation of the Monk Skin
Tone class distribution from an ideal distribution. The calculation process consists of
several key steps. First, data is read from files corresponding to each dataset, and the
Monk class value (ranging from 1 to 10) is extracted for each sample. Next, the data is
categorized based on these classes, and the relative distribution of each class is com­
puted, resulting in a probability distribution that represents the share of each class in the
dataset. Subsequently, an ideal distribution is created, assuming that each class should
constitute 10% of the total data. Finally, the Bias Score is determined by computing the
sum of the absolute differences between the observed distribution and the ideal distribu­
tion. A higher value of this index indicates a greater deviation from a balanced distribution
and, consequently, a higher bias in the data.

Answers to Research Questions
Q1: How can deep generative models be used to generate a fully synthetic
face image dataset that is balanced in terms of skin tones?
Deep generative models, such as StyleGAN, can generate synthetic face images by con­
ditioning on predefined skin tone classes. By integrating balanced training data and en­
forcing class constraints, a uniform distribution of skin tones can be ensured.
Q2: Can the fully synthetic and balanced dataset be used to identify biases
within face image quality assessment algorithms?
Yes, by applying the synthetic dataset to existing face image quality assessment (FIQA)
models and analyzing variations in quality scores across skin tones, systematic biases
can be detected.
Q3: Which visualization methods can be used to demonstrate that a
quality assessment algorithm is biased toward skin tones?
Distribution plots (e.g., KDE plots) can highlight score disparities among different skin
tones. PCA plots can also illustrate clustering patterns that indicate bias.

6
Limitations and Future Works
This study examines the impact of dataset imbalances on bias in skin tone classifica­
tion. Collecting real­world data is time­consuming, and while synthetic data gener­
ated by GANs, particularly StyleGAN, helps reduce reliance on real data, it still lacks
realism and full skin tone diversity. The limited diversity in datasets has led to bi­
ases in lighter (1,2) and darker (9,10) skin tones, and image quality issues have
further affected accuracy. Factors such as lighting conditions, resolution, blur, and
head angles have also influenced the results. Additionally, GANs require optimized
training to mitigate bias, a challenge that this study has not fully addressed.
To improve results, future research should focus on expanding datasets with a broader
range of skin tones and ensuring balanced representation. Better control of light­
ing conditions in real­world data can further enhance accuracy. Leveraging advanced
models such as StyleGAN3 and Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) could generate more
realistic synthetic data and reduce bias. Additionally, optimizing GAN training tech­
niques can significantly enhance model performance.
Strategies for Fair Model Development
Ensuring fairness in skin tone classification requires integrating various techniques to
minimize bias at different stages of model development. One key approach involves pre­
processing techniques, where dataset adjustments are made before training to mitigate
bias. Another important method is in­processing techniques, which modify the learning
algorithm itself to reduce disparities during training . Additionally, post­processing tech­
niques adjust model predictions after training to ensure fairness in outcomes. These
strategies collectively improve fairness and should be considered in future research to
enhance model generalization across diverse skin tones[48].
Another promising approach involves utilizing 3D dynamic models to study lighting
and head angle variations, making the models more robust. Future studies should
also compare other models alongside TRUST to identify strengths and weaknesses in
mitigating bias. Furthermore, applications in surveillance and security should be im­
proved by optimizing computational efficiency and leveraging parallel processing.

Conclusion
This study examines the challenges associated with Facial Image Quality Assessment
(FIQA) while considering skin tone diversity and its impact on deep learning models. By
leveraging deep generative models, a synthetic and balanced dataset covering a wide
spectrum of skin tones was generated, enabling the identification of biases in FIQA al­
gorithms. The results demonstrated that traditional FIQA methods exhibit significant bias
against certain groups within the skin tone spectrum.
The study also analyzed the impact of lighting conditions and image quality on the distribu­
tion of quality scores. Findings indicated that utilizing synthetic data and optimizing deep
learning models could help mitigate biases. Furthermore, advanced generative models
such as StyleGAN3 and Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) were proposed to produce more
realistic synthetic data, thereby enhancing analysis accuracy.
However, challenges such as limited data diversity and the need for further optimization
in GAN training processes remain. Future research should explore dynamic 3D models to
study variations in lighting and head poses. Additionally, evaluating alternative models us­
ing the TRUST metric may help identify strengths and weaknesses in current approaches
to bias reduction.
Ultimately, this study highlights that while synthetic data is an effective approach for
improving FIQA accuracy, further improvements in the diversity and quality of gener­
ated data are necessary to ensure that FIQA algorithms operate fairly across all skin
tone groups.
