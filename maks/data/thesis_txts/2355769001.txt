Abstract
In this thesis, an investigation of shafts connected by diďŹerent couplings were performed.
A focus on torsional oscillations and the energy eďŹciency of couplings aďŹected by a
misalignment angle was successful. Experimental results show that the torsional vibrations
are a property of the shaft system dynamics and that the most energy eďŹcient coupling
type is a universal joint. A mathematical model describing a universal joint was compared
to the experimental results showing the model was too simple for accurately predicting the
dynamic behavior.

Introduction 1
Coupling misalignment is the second most common fault in rotating machines, only second
to unbalance[1]. The angular misalignment can create torsional vibration and therefore
increased stresses in the machine. The functional principles of a universal joint is the same
as a ďŹexible shaft coupling; both couplings can connect two shafts that have an angular
misalignment.
There are two main focus areas in this thesis. The ďŹrst area is to study how the energy
dissipates from a rotating armature with diďŹerent coupling types and misalignment angles.
The second area is on the dynamic behavior of shafts that are connected with either a
universal coupling or a ďŹexible coupling. The goal of this investigations is to be able to
determine what period that the torsional vibrations occur and if it is possible to calculate
this with a mathematical model.
In order to address these areas, two diďŹerent types of experiments are performed for a range
of couplings and misalignment angles. The dynamic shaft behavior from the experiments is
compared to the mathematical model described in the article: "Dynamic stability analysis
of torsional vibrations of a shaft system connected by a Hookeâs joint through a continuous
system model"[2].

The two experiments, Spindown and Motionshape are performed almost in the same way,
having only two diďŹerences. The ďŹrst is that the total number of samples for the Spindown
experiment is larger. This is because the sample size needs to be large enough for the

The second diďŹerence is that the power to the electromagnetic clutch is powered oďŹduring

1. Set the driver side assembly to desired coupling misalignment and tighten the bolts
until the assembly is secure on the test bed.
2. Plug in the power cables of the inverter, vibrometer, DAQ, external power supply
and the computer.
3. On the power supply make sure that it is powering the clutch and the encoder.
4. Power on the inverter and set the direction to forward.
5. The laser in front of the retro reďŹective tape and slide the laser shutter down.
6. Position the laser so that it is level and point perpendicular onto the middle of the

laser is needed[4].
7. While the shaft is rotating press the "Laser on/oďŹ" button and adjust the laser
position until the green LED labeled "Lock" is lit.
8. On the vibrometer control, push the "speed" button, such that the display outputs
the speed[RPM] of the shaft.

Discussion 9
The experimental investigations in this thesis were successful at showing the eďŹciency
of diďŹerent couplings. All the Spindown results show that when an increase in coupling
misalignment occurs, the coupling use more power and thus spin down faster than. This
makes sense because of the increased material strain that occur when a coupling is
misaligned. Larger material strain can also be the contributing factor that causes the
power consumed by the couplings to increase at higher angular velocities. The extra power
that is needed to drive a coupling with a misalignment angles is linear for small angles.
This can be seen for the Klonex yellow, the Klonex red and the HRC110 tested at 0.5âŚ
in ďŹgure 4.12, 4.17 and 4.22 respectively. The extra power needed become linear with
increasing angular velocity, this is best demonstrated for the universal coupling at 0.5âŚseen
in ďŹgure 4.7. As the misalignment angle increases and the coupling is operation at smaller
velocities the power needed to drive it spikes. This is seen in ďŹgure 4.6 with the universal
coupling at angles 10âŚand 15âŚuse over 100% power compared to the system without a
misalignment angle. It can be seen in ďŹgure 4.27 that the FFX60 tire coupling operating at
its maximum rated angle of 4âŚ, uses over 200% of the power that the system would without
a misalignment angle. Choosing the right coupling for an application can have signiďŹcant
energy eďŹciency gains. This is especially clear in ďŹgure 4.28 where a comparison between
couplings at the same misalignment is shown. The hardness of the polymer element in
a ďŹexible coupling does aďŹect the amount of power dissipated in the coupling. Figure
4.29 show that a softer polymer in the Klonex coupling uses more power than the harder
polymer. The damping ratio in the softer polymer must therefore be higher.
The Motionshape experiment was successful in showing that the Klonex, and HRC110
couplings had torsional oscillation periods of 2 every rotation, which can be seen in ďŹgure
5.11, 5.17 and 5.23 respectively. The FFX60 coupling only had one torsional oscillation
every rotation as seen in ďŹgure 5.27. The Klonex and HRC couplings have substantially
diďŹerent looking polymer elements than the FFX60 tire coupling. This indicates that the
torsional vibration period is determined by geometrical and material properties rather than
a positional dependent velocity ratio. Throughout all of the experiment for the ďŹexible
couplings, the magnitude of the torsional vibration between experiments with and without
a misalignment angle are vary similar. This also indicates that the torsional dynamics are a
property of the shaft system dynamics rather than the positional dependent velocity ratio.
The experimental results of the universal couplings has a positional dependent velocity
ratio that matches the theoretical ever other rotation. This can be seen in ďŹgure 5.7, 8.6
and 8.10.

9. Discussion
9.1
Limitations of experiment
It cannot be said with certainty that the same torsional vibration or energy dissipation
rates will happen if the system parameters such as shaft stiďŹness and inertia disk size
change. The tendencies such as; increase in misalignment angle causes an increase power
consumption and higher velocities causes higher power usage should still hold true as this
was seen across all test. The experimental setup did not allow measurement of bearing
forces. This made it impossible to determine whether all the energy dissipated purely into
material hysteresis like it was assumed in the report, or if some of this energy also went
into the bearings. It is likely that there are increased transverse forces in the bearings
since the ďŹexible couplings where elastically deformed when a misalignment was introduced
because for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.[8]
9.2
Limitations of mathematical model
The dynamic behavior of the experimental results for the universal coupling at misalignment
angles 0âŚ, 5âŚ, 10âŚand 15âŚcould not be reproduced with the mathematical model. The
reason for this that the mathematical model is too simple for modeling the rotor system
in the experiment. The article used for describing the torsional dynamics of the shaft
system was chosen after a literature study at the beginning of this thesis. The article is
from 2014 and at the start of this thesis was the most advanced ďŹnite element model that
include a positional dependent velocity ratio. Comparing the experimental results to the
model it can be seen that it is too simple to correctly predict the dynamic behavior of two
shafts connected with a universal joint. The ďŹrst problem is that the coupling is modeled
perfectly rigid. The other modeling problem is that the system stiďŹness and damping
parameters are the same for all the elements on the driver side and driven side respectably.
Since the coupling is geometrically complex with contact areas that include thin oil ďŹlms it
is very diďŹcult to take these axially varying material parameters and combine them evenly
across either of the shafts. This is also the reason why the ďŹexible shaft couplings where
not analyzed with the model. The mathematical model uses a constant angular velocity of
the driver disk. Because this constant velocity is needed, the motor is connected to the
rest of the armature. Although the motor is set to a constant velocity, an electric motor
maintains this by applying many electromagnetic pulses to the rotor. These excitation of
the input shaft is not taken into account by the model, but could have an eďŹect on the
shaft system dynamics.
A solution to these problems would be build a ďŹnite element model with individual elements
that can have diďŹerent material parameters. This would make it easier to analyze how
diďŹerent materials in a coupling element will aďŹect the dynamic properties of a shaft system.
Having a model that does not need a constant input velocity but rather a start velocity
would make it possible to compare the spin down times from the Spindown experiment to
the solution. Another way of changing the model would be addition of the motor excitation
in the force contribution.

9.3
Proposal for further study
An expanded study where incrementally larger disk are tested could determine if the
experimental results found in this thesis hold true for all types of loads. An improved
experimental setup where an adjustment screw was powered by a stepper motor would
improve the accuracy and repeatably of the experiment. A program could be written to
test the couplings at smaller incremental misalignment intervals. Test results that are more
comprehensive could thereby be used as a lookup tables for choosing the best coupling for
a given misaligned shafts application. The analysis was performed at an angular velocity
of around 2Ď rad/s. This was close to the lower angular velocity sensing limit of the laser
vibrometer. To summarize this problem, this sensor was not ďŹt for the experiment. A
better choice of sensor would be to use another rotary encoder like the one already used in
the experiment. Unfortunately this was not possible because of the way power transfer to
the setup was designed. Redesigning the experimental setup so that a rotary encoder is
place at either end of the shaft also increases the number of places that the experimental
setup can be compared to the mathematical model.

Conclusion 10
In conclusion the experimental results showed that couplings use an increasing amount
power usage when the angular velocity increase. likewise the power use increases with
an increase in misalignment angle. The most eďŹcient coupling tested was the universal
joint which should be picked for applications where power eďŹciency is a concern. Likewise
a harder polymer element should be picked for power eďŹcient applications. It was seen
in the Motionshape experiment that the period of shaft oscillations for the Klonex red,
Klonex yellow and HRC110 couplings all exhibit a Ď periodic oscillations like the universal
joint. The tire coupling exhibited a 2Ď periodic oscillation.
A mathematical model can be used to to predict the dynamic behavior of a universal
coupling, the accuracy of such model cannot be determined without comparing it to an
experiment. The model used in this thesis had too many simpliďŹcations to predict of the
behavior accurately. An empirical study is found to be a better choice for analyzing the
dynamic behavior of shafts connected with rigid and ďŹexible couplings.
