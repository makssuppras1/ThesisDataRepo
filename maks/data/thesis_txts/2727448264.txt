Abstract
In today’s rapidly evolving digital landscape, ensuring the security and integrity
of computer networks has become paramount. One crucial tool which plays a piv-
otal role in this endeavor is Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDSs). With
the ever-increasing diversity and sophistication of cyber threats, organizations
and individuals must recognize the importance of intrusion detection deployment
as a proactive defense mechanism. An NIDS automates the process of monitoring
and analyzing network traﬀic with the objective of facilitating detection and sub-
sequent reporting of potential intrusions. The research community is persistently
striving to devise enhanced detection algorithms and systems. To accurately eval-
uate and compare various initiatives in this field, the availability of appropriate
datasets is indispensable. However, suﬀicient datasets are limited and diﬀicult to
replicate. For this reason, the Intrusion Detection Dataset Toolkit (ID2T) was
created.
ID2T aims to generate synthetic, yet realistic attacks, which can be
injected into benign background traﬀic. In this thesis, a series of enrichments to
the attack arsenal of ID2T is showcased and evaluated. This includes the intro-
duction of a Multi-stage attack category. Moreover, the thesis entails a number
of initiatives, based upon identified limitations, which facilitate the injection of
realistic, synthetic attacks. Such initiatives include the integration of background
traﬀic manipulation, generation of realistic timestamps and much more.

Preface
This Master’s thesis was prepared at the department of Applied Mathematics and
Computer Science at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the re-
quirements for acquiring a Master’s degree in Computer Science and Engineering.
This thesis is concerned with the continual development of realistic, yet syn-
thetic datasets for Intrusion Detection Systems. This has been achieved via the
introduction of a number of enrichments as well as adjustments of the Intrusion
Detection Dataset Toolkit.
This work is of great importance as evaluation of Intrusion Detection Systems
relies heavily on datasets, which are generally limited, insuﬀicient and lack repli-
cable characteristics.
Abdirisaq Farah
Martin Daniel Nielsen
Kongens Lyngby, 1st of July, 2023

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the ever-changing digital environment of today, guaranteeing the security and
integrity of computer networks has emerged as a top priority. With the increasing
dependence on technology, both individuals and organizations risk encountering
sophisticated threats such as phishing attacks and malware.
Consequently, the deployment of Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDSs)
have become an imperative necessity in the modern digital landscape. An NIDS
automates the process of monitoring and analyzing network traﬀic with the ob-
jective of facilitating detection and subsequent reporting of potential intrusions
to administrators. Thus, it aids in a proactive defense and functions as an im-
portant utility for incident response teams.
With this in mind, the research
community continually strives towards the development of novel methodologies
and algorithms to augment the capabilities of NIDSs.
The need to develop NIDSs, capable of keeping up with motivated adversaries
and their evolving attacks have lead to extensive research. This research mainly
focuses the identification of established and emerging cyber-security threats [31].
It remains a crucial challenge to evaluate the effectiveness and detection accuracy
of a given NIDS. In order to evaluate and compare these devices, in a reliable
manner, researchers need adequate datasets with labeled malicious network traf-
fic. Regrettably, such datasets are generally insuﬀicient, outdated and lacking
ground truth [55]. Moreover, it is preferable for these datasets to be publicly
available, so they can be shared among the research community for replication of
other experiments. However, this is generally not the case as of today. In numer-
ous instances, researchers attempt to perform adaptation that compensates for a
given datasets shortcomings before using them [90]. To overcome said obstacles,
the Intrusion Detection Dataset Toolkit (ID2T) was created.
ID2T facilitates the creation of labeled datasets by injecting synthetic attacks
into user-specified background traﬀic, in the form of the interoperable Packet
Capture file. This allows for comparability and evaluation of scientific results
as well as enabling an accurate replication. As opposed to simply merging the
==PAGE:18==

background traﬀic and synthetic traﬀic together, ID2T performs analysis on the
properties of the background traﬀic and replicates particular packet fields accord-

In general, the concept of

an entirely synthetic dataset, purposefully minimizes the presence of shortcom-

the research community – for evaluation of novel detection algorithms [31]. More-
over, the tools reach also extends to network security specialists and educational
purposes.
ID2T is still being developed and it remains important to gather more require-
ments and use cases for the community to expand and improve the capabilities
for ID2T.
1.2
Problem Statement
The primary focus of this thesis is to enhance ID2T with modern attack imple-
mentations and overall functionality to facilitate reliable creation of datasets, as
well as addressing a number of identified limitations. With these aims in mind,
the thesis will attempt to provide answers for the following research questions:
– What considerations should be made to facilitate the injection of synthetic,
yet realistic network traﬀic?
– What approaches can be used to assess the level of artifacts present in a
given dataset?
– Which datasets are current available for evaluation of IDSs and how does
ID2T’s datasets compare to them?
– Which options are currently available for synthetic data generation and how
does ID2T compare to them?
– How can one dynamically replace packet and payload contents of malicious
packets, while complying with the requirements of synthetic attack injec-
tion?
– Which novelties should be incorporated for ID2T to manipulate the back-
ground traﬀic based upon a given attack implementation?
– Which measures should be taken for ID2T to generate datasets that are
conducive for evaluation of intrusion detection systems.

Requirements for Datasets and Synthetic Attack
Injection
This section presents general requirements for datasets which include syntheti-
cally injected attacks – as well as the corresponding data generation tool. The
following list of requirements is derived from research in Chapter 3 concerning
the State of the Art as well as the research from Vasilomanolakis et al. [31, 123],
Cordero [29] and Bhuyan et al.[6]. In addition, all requirements are classified
depending on their functional or non-functional nature.
In this particular setting, a functional requirement outlines a precise capabil-
ity.
Therefore, the emphasis of these requirements concerns the measures to
create datasets that enable an effective and meaningful comparison among vari-
ous NIDSs. Moreover, the non-functional requirements describe a constraint or
characteristic that must be meet with regard to performance, usability, et cetera.
It should be noted that, throughout this thesis, the terms ’IDS’ and ’NIDS’ are
used interchangeably.
In general, considering the implementation intricacies of the data generation tool
in question, it is not anticipated that each dataset and its associated data gener-
ation tool will completely adhere to every individual requirement. Nonetheless,
it remains the case that the more of these requirements are met or at least fully
considered, the more value the resulting dataset will bring to the research com-
munity.
1.3.1
Requirements for Datasets
The functional requirements for datasets are listed in the following:
• Functional Requirements
– Data Labeling: some variation of data labeling must accompany the
dataset such that individual attack packets, representing the malicious
network traﬀic, are associated with a corresponding label.
– Renewability: datasets should refrain from any temporal defects. In
addition, the dataset should be continually updated to be congruent
with modern intrusions and network patterns.
– Ground Truth: the aforementioned Data Labeling must be consistent
and accurate. This implies that all normal and benign background
traﬀic should not contain any labeling.
Moreover, all labels associ-
ated with the packets should be correct as it is paramount for IDS
evaluation, that researchers can compare with ground truth.

– Flexibility: datasets must be applicable for evaluation of distinct types

Requirements for the Creation of Synthetic Attacks
The functional requirements are listed below:
• Functional Requirements
– Modelling at Packet-level: modelling of synthetic attacks need to be
performed at the packet-level, as opposed to the flow-level. This is the

attack packets, therefore leading to a concise dataset.

reduce the number of artifacts within the resulting dataset.
– Attack Parameters: users should have the option of supplying simple
parameters which are relevant to the attack – such as the IP address
of a particular server, or simply specifying the IP address of the victim
in question.

2 Background
2.1
Intrusion Detection Systems
An Intrusion Detection System (IDS) is a passive hardware or software product
which automatizes [96] the process of monitoring and analyzing events [31] oc-
curring in a host system or local network. The aim of this process is to enable
detection and subsequent reporting of potential intrusions [96] – which indicate a
violation of stated security policies [123] to security administrators. An IDS has
the capability to draw conclusions upon a number of threats including malware,
unauthorized access, Dos-DDoS attack, probe attacks, escalation of privileges
[96], et cetera. Overall, an IDS improves the security of a host system or an
entire local network, and attempts to discourage adversaries [96].
Thus, the goal of an IDS is to detect any attempt of bypassing the perimeter
of a local network or host system, which could potentially look to compromise
its integrity, confidentiality or availability [123] – and to do so with the highest
possible degree of accuracy. As a result of the increasing complexity in modern
security attacks, the introduction of IDSs have become a staple for institutions,
organizations and general enterprise networks. This is incorporated in conjunc-
tion with the pre-existing firewall [96] and provides an added layer of security
to a network infrastructure [124]. Moreover, IDSs are extensively used to detect
known as well as unknown intrusions on networks from any internal and external
adversarial [96]. In conclusion, it is imperative for modern devices to be moni-
tored for any signs of intrusion on a continual basis to ensure correct functioning.
With regards to the positioning, an IDS can generally be classified as a Network-
based IDS (NIDS) or a Host-based IDS (HIDS). An NIDS is applied as a singular
addition to a given local network, which monitors and analyzes the inbound and
outbound network traﬀic [31].
On the contrary, an HIDS inspects intrusion on the granularity of a single de-
vice [95]. Via an HIDS, more detailed information is monitored and analyzed for
the host system in question, including system log files, utility activity as well as
inbound and outbound network traﬀic [31]. Upon detecting any form of unau-
thorized modifications or network traﬀic, the appropriate people will be alerted
[95]. Thus, for this application to prove meaningful for an entire enterprise, it
would be necessary to deploy HIDSs for all its devices – however, such instances
of IDSs are known for adding a relatively high level of computational overhead,
thus reducing the performance of the host system [123].
As this thesis is centered around the monitoring of inbound and outbound traf-
fic, NIDSs will remain the sole focus. The typical location of an NIDS within
a network infrastructure can be viewed in Figure 2.1. As seen, it is located at
the network perimeter just behind the firewall, which conceals the local network.
This placement facilitates the detection of inbound malicious traﬀic, however it

Moreover, an IDS can also be categorized depending on the particular intrusion
detection methodology. There are two main types to consider in this context,
namely signature-based (or misuse-based) and anomaly-based intrusion detection

A signature-based IDS performs simple pattern matching when searching for so-
called ’signatures’ of known attacks. These signatures form the ruleset which the
network flow is analyzed against [103]. Once a particular signature is identified
==PAGE:28==

– the security administrators are alerted. Common examples of such signature-
based IDSs include Suricata and Snort [123], and are often applied as an added
line of defense for small to medium sized operations networks due to their high ac-
curacy. However, such types of IDSs are incapable of detecting novel adversarial
behaviours – due to the requirement of such signatures being present [103]. For
this reason, it is vital that such types of IDSs contain the most recently identified
intrusions to achieve and maintain a high degree of effectivity [31].
Anomaly-based IDSs function under the premise that intrusions in general exhibit
certain characteristics which are distinguishable from regular network traﬀic [31].
Consequentially, an anomaly-based IDS initially attempts to understand the nor-
mal system state by defining a number of ’normal’ network traﬀic characteristics
[95]. From this point, any behavior which deviates from the established norm is
considered to be an anomaly and by implication, a potential intrusion. In con-
trast to a signature-based detection, the anomaly-based detection methodology
also has the capacity to detect unknown intrusions, commonly referred to as Zero
Day exploits. However, this detection methodology typically suffers from a high
rate of false positives, while a signature-based detection methodology typically
results in a high rate of false negatives [123].
A subset of anomaly-based IDSs are enhanced through machine learning mod-
els [71]. Such initiatives have arised in the past decade to improve cyber-security
measures [84]. One significant advantage to the utilization of a machine learning
model is that the model and its algorithms can continually evolve over time from
past experiences [71]. Key phases residing in an IDS build upon these principles
is a training phase which relies on feature extraction for a number of relevant
parameters such as packet header [72], payload contents, timestamp et cetera.
2.1.1
Suricata’s Signature-based Intrusion Detection System
A well-known example of a signature-based IDS is Suricata, which is an open
source network analysis and threat detection software used by a large number of
private and public organizations [48]. Suricata’s IDS utilizes open-source rulesets
[87] and provides a signature language that enables users to define and prevent
specific threats based on their unique security requirements [48]. Suricata pro-
cesses network traﬀic by employing its detection module, which interprets the
established rules. It inspects each packet and the associated metadata derived
from the packet’s payload.
If a packet aligns with any defined rule, the IDS
triggers an appropriate response as dictated by the actions specified within the
matched rule [88].

2.2
Intrusion Detection Evasion Techniques
Adversaries have implemented various strategies to evade the detection capabili-
ties of particular IDSs for delivery of any illicit traﬀic activities. The continual
development of intrusion detection evasion is congruent with the continual im-
provement in IDSs capabilities. Consequently, many evasion techniques are, at
present, capable of bypassing a number of deployed IDSs [69]. Widely utilized
evasion techniques include polymorphism, metamorphism, encryption, obfusca-
tion, anti-reversing protection [119] et cetera. Moving on, a few notable intrusion
detection evasion techniques are introduced in the following. The presented tech-
niques all have some relevance to the overall contributions of the thesis and are
expanded upon in Chapter 6 concerning Attack Implementations.
2.2.1
Payload Obfuscation
Obfuscation of payloads, is a form of concealment at which packet contents are
encoded, while remaining functionally identical [68]. This technique is primarily
used with a malicious motive [69].
Utilization of this technique is a common
occurrence for the Log4Shell exploit, which is presented in Section 6.5.1. Specifi-
cally, base64 obfuscation is utilized.
This intrusion detection evasion technique capitalizes on the fact that many
signature-based IDSs primarily focus on detecting known signatures or patterns
associated with prevalent attacks. By employing base64 obfuscation, adversaries
can effectively mask identifiable patterns of their malicious payload, making it
diﬀicult for a given IDS to detect the malicious activity. In general, a substan-
tial part of signature-based IDSs carries some limitations regarding the arsenal
of prevalent signatures and encoded variants of such [69]. Adversaries may even
apply two layers of encoding onto certain packets with the aim of escalating the
number of signatures required to detect nefarious network traﬀic [68].
Conse-
quently, an effective IDS should support the hexadecimal encoding format or
having these hexadecimal strings in its set of attack signatures [12].
2.2.2
Time-based Evasion
Adversaries may introduce time delays or momentary sleep within a given attack
with the intention of prolonging the execution time [119]. This is often times
intended for evasion of particular anomaly-based IDSs. A simple version of this
is implemented in all attack implementations, belonging to a newly introduced
attack category, which is presented in Section 6.7. The time-based evasion tech-
nique is often times used in the initial phases of complex attacks, referred to as
scanning or reconnaissance [69] – at which information about the victim is being
gathered. This technique is effective from an adversaries point of view as the

2.3
Content Management Systems
A Content Management System (CMS) tool is an interactive application that
aids in the creation of websites in a timely manner [10]. The first CMS tools
were introduced in the latter part of the 1990s [134] – moreover, at present, CMS
tools are utilized for 64% of all web sites [128]. Subsequent chapters will cover a
number of vulnerabilities related to such types of applications.
A CMS consists of a number of interconnected components which enable system
users in creating, publishing, editing and managing their content without the ne-
cessity of understanding a series of programming languages, such as the HyperText
Markup Language (HTML), which is the standard language for web page creation.
Essentially, a CMS facilitates knowledge delivery within a website. Moreover, it
allows for the creation of an infrastructure that supports multi-user collaboration
and contribution to various knowledge management activities, making it a valu-
able tool for eﬀicient and simultaneous content creation and management [134].
A plethora of different CMS tools are made available on the market. Among,
the long list of options, three CMSs stand out in terms of notoriety: Wordpress,
Joomla and Drupal. A significant part of their popularity stems from these CMSs
being open source and thus freely available for any user [34]. In addition, through-
out the past 12 years, the popularity of Wordpress, Joomla and Drupal has been
increasing in a steep, linear fashion [34], making them highly relevant in modern
times. More specifically, Wordpress is the most popular CMS tool with a current
CMS market share of 63.3%. Meanwhile, Joomla and Drupal, respectively make
up 2.7% and 1.8% of the CMS market share [127].
From the prism of cyber security, open source software in general present a dou-
ble edged sword. On one hand, open source software provides people with the
option to adapt it to their specific needs and preferences, which in many cases
include addressing potential vulnerabilities.
However, on the other hand, any
person with nefarious intentions also have the ability to study as well as probe
the publicly-available source code with the aim of identifying a vulnerability.
As a result, CMS tools have in recent years presented one of the most critical
entry points for adversaries [13]. These attacks are preceeded by probing and
active scanning for adversaries to detect the type of CMS as well as its version,
theme and plugin. This thesis aims to explore this aspect of CMS attacks. The
subsequent section, shortly introduces the Droopescan tool utilised for the scan-
ning attacks.

2.4
Botnets
A botnet is defined as a coordinated group of malware instances, known as bots
which are controlled remotely via covert Command and Control (C&C) channels
[62] by a so-called botmaster. Through such C&C channels – established via C&C
servers – a botmaster can issue commands to their network of bots [20]. The role
of the C&C servers can be viewed for Figure 2.4. Upon a given host being infected
with a bot virus, a small program is installed for the future activation of the bot-
master – thereby making the device a part of the botnet [62]. This program is
typically referred as the initial binary [27]. The current implementation of ID2T
includes attack scripts that inject packets from hypothetical or real botnets [31].
One key distinction between a botnet and other types of malware is the ability
of the botnet to enable a large number of simultaneous actions [19]. Overall, one
side effect of this aspect – from the perspective of the network security personnel,
whom is specialized in network traﬀic analysis – is that the bots within a botnet
share similar characteristics [62] unless particular measures have been taken to
circumvent or disguise it to a certain degree. These measures include encrypted
channels, non-standard port use and protocol tunneling [120], as detailed in Sec-
tion 2.2.3.
Botnets are considered to be moving targets, meaning that all aspects of botnets
are mutating over time – for this reason, there does not exist a single mitigation
or detection technique to provide a permanent solution [11]. For instance, the
first iterations of botnets spread predominantly via Internet Relay Chat (IRC)
[51], which is a communication protocol with a client-server architecture, that was
developed in the late 1980’s. However, at present, the attack vectors of botnets
are significantly more widespread. These attack vectors include infected email
attachments, vulnerability attacks, et cetera. [126].

modern botnets present a great challenge for network security and personal pri-

In addition, botnets have become increasingly intelligent throughout the iter-

Current iterations are even capable of identifying a

2.4.1
Botnet Architectures
Botnets are commonly divided into three basic architectures: centralized, decen-
tralized and hybrid [51]. An illustration of their respective architectures can be
seen in Figure 2.5. These structures determine how commands are disseminated
throughout the covert C&C channel [126].
Figure 2.5: Centralized, Decentralized and Hybrid Botnet Architectures [50]
Within a centralized botnet, a central C&C server is responsible for sending com-
mands to its bots [126]. The protocols adopted by these botnets are predomi-
nantly based on HTTP and IRC [62]. It is known for being relatively eﬀicient,
and has been the standard practice for a number of years. In addition, botnets
==PAGE:35==
2.5 Cyber Kill Chain
19
following this architectural setup is typically highly concealed [62]. However, this
architecture is less resilient to countermeasures, as the centralised C&C server
acts as a Single Point of Failure (SPoF) for the entire botnet [126].
Due to the risk of centralized botnets being disabled by shutting down the SPoF,
alternative architectures have emerged in recent years. The decentralized archi-
tecture is based on the peer-to-peer (P2P) network protocol. Examples of this
type of implementation include GameOver Zeus, Sality, among others [20]. A
decentralized architecture is characterized by all the bots within the botnet con-
tributing to the coordination process via dissemination of commands to their peers
[51]. Since these P2P-based botnets circumvent the the issue of the SPoF, this
architectural setup greatly enhances the durability of the botnet [62].
Lastly, in a hybrid architecture, the dissemination mechanism is a combination
of the two aforementioned architectures. This would typically include a multi-
tude of C&C servers, tasked with handling a specific number of bots, which are
communicating through P2P [51].
2.5
Cyber Kill Chain
The term ”kill chain” refers to a military concept which describe an attacks key
phases. These key phases correspond to locating, fixing and dispatching the tar-
get, et cetera. Moreover, a kill chain can be utilized for defensive purposes as well.
More specifically, a kill chain can help ones understanding of how to terminate or
obstruct the flow of the opposing attack in a pre-emptive manner through com-
partmentalization of various junctures.
A similar concept was applied for the cyber kill chain framework, proposed by
Lockheed Martin in 2011 [105]. This framework compartmentalizes the various
phases of attacks. In turn, the framework provides general guidelines and clar-
ity to interfere with and foresee attacks and potential vulnerabilities in different
phases.
The cyber kill chain framework is commonly applied in attack modelling for inci-
dent response teams, digital forensic investigators as well as malware analysts to
work in a chained manner [132]. In addition, the cyber kill chain is intended to
defend against sophisticated attacks, such as Botnet and Multi-stage attacks. In
continuation, we present a detailed walkthrough of the the seven phases of the
cyber kill chain framework:
[1]
Reconnaissance: Identification, Selection and Profiling of Target
Information is being gathered about the target, in anticipation of discovering
==PAGE:36==
20
2 Background
vulnerabilities and potential entry points in systems or infrastructure [105].
In addition, reconnaissance exists in an active and a passive form. Active
reconnaissance includes activities such as port scanning, which involve di-
rect interaction and probing of the target system or network. While target
identification is a passive reconnaissance method, meaning that the target is
unaware of the proceedings, target profiling can be done on a social as well
as system level. This is most likely achieved through gathering of public
email addresses, automated scanners as well as deployment of spying tools.
In this process, the adversary will typically also harvest information con-
cerning the targets deployed software applications and/or operating system
details. All of the aforementioned information may be useful in phishing
or spoofing attacks.[120]. In practice, a substantial part of reconnaissance
includes crawling the World Wide Web and network tracing tools to get
information about the target [132].
[2]
Weaponization: Coupling of Remote Access Trojan (RAT) with
an Exploit into a Deliverable Payload
An attack vector is developed along with a backdoor [132]. In the event of
the original point of entry being identified by network security personnel,
the backdoor enables access to the system. Moreover, the RAT, which is the
software that executes on the target’s system and provides remote, hidden
and undetected access for the adversary, is typically sent. To put it concisely,
a general exploit often acts as a carrier for the RAT and utilizes software
vulnerabilities to drop and execute the RAT. The types of access provided
by a RAT include general system exploration, file upload or download and
most importantly execution of remote files [132].
[3]
Delivery: Transmission of the Cyber Weapon to the Targeted En-
vironment
The attack is launched toward the target. This is most often done via phish-
ing emails, but can also be achieved by USB stick, or some other means of
malware transportation excluding the network [105]. More specific details
solely rely on the selected type of attack. At the time of launching the attack,
social engineering techniques may be utilized to increase the effectiveness of
the campaign.
[4]
Exploit: Triggering the Adversaries Payload on the Target System
The adversary manages to silently execute/install the given payload [132].
During this phase, the malware often perform lateral movement move across
the network, in the quest of identifying additional potential entry points
while avoiding host and network-based security measures [132]. To trigger
the exploit, certain conditions must be met:
i The software/operating system of the targeted system should not be
updated to versions wherein the exploit is patched.
==PAGE:37==
2.5 Cyber Kill Chain
21
ii Security mechanisms and or anti-viruses should not detect the exploit or
payload neither statically nor dynamically scan during run time [132].
[5]
Installation: Installation of Backdoor and Maintaining Persistence
This phase is also referred to as the privilege escalation phase. The malware
arrives on the system of the victim and further attempts to install malware
among other potential cyber-weapons within the target network with the
aim of maintaining persistence within the network [105] and fulfilling its
overall objectives.
Strategies for this phase include installing malware via trojan horses, ac-
cess token manipulation as well as command-line interfaces. Moreover, this
phase often involves installing a persistent backdoor to maintain access to
the server for an extended period of time [120].
[6]
Command and Control (C&C): Outbound Internet Controller Servers
to Communicate with Compromised Host
The installed backdoor is utilized to gain control over the network, which
can be used for several malicious operations [105]. At this point, the ad-
versary can monitor and guide the deployed cyber-weapons and tool stacks
in a remote manner. The covert C&C communication channel can be es-
tablished in many different ways to mask it as standard traﬀic. This can
for instance be achieved using protocol tunneling, encrypted channels and
non-standard port usage [120]. Once an adversary reaches the C&C phase
of an attack campaign, subsequent remediation, mitigation and assessment
typically become very extensive.
[7]
Action: Data Exfiltration, Network Spreading, System Disruption
The malicious objectives of the adversary is being realised through suﬀicient
control of over the network [105].
Throughout the past few years, the inclusion of an eighth phase in the cyber kill
chain framework has been proposed by several security experts. This phase would
be referred to as monetization. In this phase, the adversary focuses on deriving
income from the successfully executed attack. This could primarily be achieved
either through putting valuable information for sale on the dark web or through
some ransom to be paid from the victim of the operation.
In spite of the popularity and added value of the cyber kill chain framework,
from which organizations can begin to develop a cybersecurity strategy - a sub-
stantial minority consider the framework – originating from 2011 – to be outdated
and lacking innovation. One part of the reasoning for this perspective is that the
framework does not account for the increasing usage of interconnected devices,
==PAGE:38==
22
2 Background
which has exponentially increased the attack surface for many enterprise organi-
zations [120]. In addition, the cyber kill chain is limited regarding detection of
certain types of attacks. An example of this is the frameworks inability to detect
insider threats which are currently acknowledged as one of the most dangerous
cyber threats to an organization’s network and data security [131]. The original
framework revolves around malware and payloads, and therefore does not con-
sider other types of attacks such as Zero Day exploits, SQL injections and Cross
Site Scripting.
Overall, attack models such as the cyber kill chain framework provides a bet-
ter insight into the network, enable better security strategies or find potential
vulnerabilities. Furthermore they can provide a better understanding of the be-
havior and the goals of the adversary [111].
2.6
Multi-stage Attacks
The term Multi-stage attack, also called a Multi-step attack, describe a sophisti-
cated intrusion technique for penetrating well protected network infrastructures.
Specifically, the term describes an attack which is executed in a series of stages.
This attack type is among serious cyber security threats [76] for organizations
and governments [15].
Multi-stage attacks are referred to as the ensemble of
steps taken by one or several adversaries [91] in the quest of exploiting the target
system [60].
Modern techniques for evading security, stealing intellectual property as well as
other sensitive information often include the introduction of a number of attack
vectors – with the intention of obtaining initial access to the target system [15].
The majority of these attacks fall within the category of Multi-stage attacks. Ex-
amples of such attacks include botnet attacks as well as Advanced Persistent
Threats.
The primary challenge concerning the prevention of Multi-stage attacks is the
fact that individual stages may not be perceived nearly as malicious as the sum
of stages (for instance via utilizing Zero Day exploits as part of the penetration
strategy), when combined [76]. One of the key characteristics or Multi-stage at-
tacks is that they can have one or several intended targets, depending on the
degree of complication for the given attack [60]. In addition, certain multi-stage
attacks can utilize the concepts of social engineering and deception to give insider
access for adversaries to the target system [61].

State of the Art
This chapter delves into the current landscape of IDS research, specifically focus-
ing on static datasets and employed dataset generation tools within the field. The
chapter provides a comprehensive study of the latest developments and insights
that are shaping the direction of research and technology in this field of study.
Datasets serve as the foundation upon which IDSs are developed, trained and
evaluated. Thus, it is of utmost relevance for these datasets to accurately rep-
resent the diverse and complex traﬀic patterns of real-world networks, include a
wide range of both normal and malicious activities and be adequately labeled to
facilitate factual evaluation.
Unfortunately, the state of the art for datasets falls short of meeting such ideals
– which have often been acknowledged as a significant obstacle for the research
community [2]. The inadequacy of datasets impedes advancements in the field,
presenting hurdles with respect to replication of prior studies as well as assess-
ment of novel detection methods. As a result, the continuous improvement of
datasets and dataset generation tools have become a fundamental area of focus
in IDS research.
The chapter starts with an exploration of static datasets, which is widely used in
the research community. A comprehensive review will be uncovered, delving into
a number of extensively utilized static datasets. The subsequent section will focus
on dynamically generated datasets, an emerging trend which offers enhanced flex-
ibility and customization. While these tools provide innovative capabilities, they
also introduce a new set of challenges that need to be addressed. The chapter
will be concluded with a summary, drawing a line from the state of the art in the
field to the contributions of this thesis.
In the grander scheme, the presented analysis serves to frame the contributions
of this thesis within the broader context of the field, highlighting its relevance
and potential impact. The end goal for the ensuing chapters is to build upon the
existing ID2T application, based upon the conducted research.

3.1
Static Datasets
Within this context, a Static dataset is defined as a dataset which is generated or
collected from real-world or synthetic network traﬀic. Such datasets are charac-
terised by their inherent immutability, rendering them challenging to replicate or
adjust to the dynamic terrain of network security. Furthermore, several studies
found them to be plagued with artifacts and defects – which can be considered
a by-product of their synthetic generation process [55, 67]. The presence of such
artifacts is notorious for reducing their reliability in relation to IDS evaluation.
These artifacts categorised in Chapter 4 will be briefly detailed for each of the
datasets presented in this section.
The following presents some of the widely used static datasets, briefly describing
their respective strengths and limitations. This is followed by a summary and
comparison table of all the presented datasets.
3.1.1 DARPA-98 & 99
The MIT Lincoln Laboratory’s creation of the DARPA datasets in 1998 and 1999
set a precedent for the generation of datasets in relation to IDS evaluation. The
idea was to emulate a network interaction between a U.S. Air Force base and the
Internet, inclusive of both benign and malicious traﬀic [77]. A specially designed
test bed environment served as the foundation for generating these datasets. The
benign traﬀic was simulated to reflect the typical network traﬀic observed be-
tween the Air Force base and the Internet. This simulated environment included
various user roles such as developers, secretaries and managers operating common
UNIX applications.
==PAGE:41==
3.1 Static Datasets
25
Moreover, the DARPA 1999 edition datasets incorporated a wide variety of attack
types, intended to test the ability of IDSs to detect various intrusions. For this
edition, the focus shifted to creating datasets consisting of three weeks of training
data, including two weeks of background traﬀic devoid of any attacks and one
week with a few labeled attacks. Subsequently, two weeks of unlabeled test data
were also provided for further analysis.
Despite the breakthroughs these datasets represented at the time, it remains im-
portant to note that they have been found to contain several artifacts which may
influence the detection capabilities of anomaly detectors [78]. For instance, one
of the notable artifacts is the fixed TTL value for all network packets associated
with an attack [85]. Additionally, there are issues related to the usage of certain
packet header fields. For instance, in DARPA-99, the use of the Type of Service
(ToS) field in the IPv4 header followed the outdated RFC 791, which does not
align with the interpretations according to the more recent RFCs, such as RFC
3168, RFC 3260 and RFC 8311 [45, 53, 21].
Moreover, their age makes them less applicable as new protocols such as Quick
UDP Internet Connections (QUIC) and Multipath TCP (MPTCP) were published
in the last few years. Consequently, artifacts of a temporal nature are present in
the datasets. While they were revolutionary, at the time of their creation, these
limitations hinder their eﬀicacy for the evaluation of modern IDSs.
3.1.2 KDD-99
The KDD Cup 99 competition was instigated in 1999 with the goal of accelerating
progress in IDS research [70]. The main challenge was to build a predictive model
with the capacity to discern benign connections from malicious ones. In this con-
text, a network connection is defined as TCP streams which traverse between two
IP addresses, under a specific protocol within a defined time period. The compe-
tition utilized a version of the DARPA-98 dataset to extract relevant connection
data. The initial DARPA-98 dataset, encompassing roughly four gigabytes of raw
traﬀic data gathered over seven weeks, was transformed into about five million
connection records. In a similar vein, two weeks of test data were compacted into
two million records where each of these records held 31 unique features.

3.1.3 NSL-KDD
As mentioned, Tavallaee et al. [117] identified artifacts for the KDD-99 dataset
which negatively impacts the performance and evaluation of anomaly detection
mechanisms. In relation to this, the author attempted to solve said issues with a
new dataset proposal which consisted of specifically chosen records of the KDD-
99 dataset. Firstly, this was done by first removing the duplicated records – as
this lowers bias by methods that have better detection rates on frequent records.
Secondly, increasing the diﬀiculty level by ensuring that the number of selected
records from each diﬀicult level is inversely proportional to the percentage of
records in the original KDD-99 dataset. The author concludes by showing a more
accurate evaluation of the tested IDS model for the dataset.

3.1.4 MAWI
The Measurement and Analysis on the WIDE Internet (MAWI) working group
have been curating a traﬀic data repository since 1999 with the dual intent of
advancing research in traﬀic analysis and facilitating the creation of related tools.
The datasets in this repository are derived from the WIDE backbone networks, a
practical experimental network that links Japanese academic institutions and re-
search organizations to various global networks. The datasets contain PCAP files

which document backbone network traﬀic between Japan and the United States,
with each PCAP file encapsulating 15 minutes of network traﬀic. The datasets
retain header information for detailed analysis, but payloads and IP addresses
are excluded to maintain anonymity. The traﬀic was captured at six different
sampling points, among which only samplepoint-F remains active and continues
to receive daily updates.
Despite its renewability and recentness, the MAWI dataset was found to have
certain limitations that hinder its usage. These limitations include the absence of
payload data and lack of ground truth, as it constitutes unlabeled network traﬀic.
Fontugne et al. [46] attempts to address these issues by proposing an advanced
graph-based approach.
This approach combines several independent anomaly
detectors and labels malicious traﬀic when a certain threshold is reached, thus
offering some degree of labeling for the traﬀic data.
3.1.5 UNSW-NB15
The UNSW-NB15 dataset was released in 2015 by Moustafa [89]. This compre-
hensive dataset consists of 2,540,044 records capturing realistic modern network
activities, including both normal and abnormal traﬀic, which are also referred to
as attacks. The dataset was gathered through the use of an IXIA traﬀic generator
running on three virtual servers. Two of these servers were configured to simulate
regular network traﬀic, while the third server was tasked with generating abnor-
mal network traﬀic.
The dataset consists of 42 features, which include packet-based and flow-based
features, and are extracted from raw network packets using the Argus and Bro-
IDS tools. Packet-based features are derived from both the packet header and
its payload. In contrast, flow-based features are calculated using the sequenc-
ing of packets as they travel from a source to a destination within the network.
The UNSW-NB15 dataset classifies network activities into ten distinct classes:
Normal, Fuzzers, Analysis, Backdoors, DoS, Exploits, Generic, Reconnaissance,
Shellcode, and Worms. This classification of activities allows for exploration of
different types of network behaviors detection by IDSs [39].
However, two issues were identified in the dataset as pointed out by Zeinab et al.
in their analysis [135]. These issues fall into the class imbalance and class overlap
categories. Zeinab et al. conclude that these issues must be addressed before
leveraging the dataset for classifier model development. This was supported by
Mulyanto [90] who pointed out that class imbalance can lead to degraded perfor-
mance and poses a severe challenge to machine learning algorithms. Mulyanto
proposes an improved classification model to work against the imbalance and
show a higher detection accuracy can be achieved in the majority classes as well

3.1.6
Other Datasets
This section briefly describes other relevant datasets in the IDS research field.
CDX dataset — Given the lack of labeled datasets for intrusion detection studies
in 2009, Sangster et al. [102] proposed a method using network warfare competi-
tions to generate modern, labeled datasets. These competitions typically involve
both benign and malicious traﬀic. By placing network sensors close to data gen-
erators and using knowledge of adversary and victim locations, traﬀic could be
accurately labeled. This approach was successfully tested during the 2009 Inter-
Service Academy Cyber Defense Exercise (CDX). Despite, the labeled traﬀic, the
dataset suffers from lack of diversity and low volume. The lack of diversity stems
from the ratio of malicious to benign traﬀic being unrealistically close [43].
LBNL dataset — The dataset produced by the Lawrence Berkeley National Lab-
oratory (LBNL) and Berkeley’s International Computer Science Institute (ICSI)
constitutes network traﬀic data spanning over 100 hours. Originating from an
enterprise network, this dataset amounts to approximately 11 gigabytes of data.
The primary objective of the LBNL project was to characterize the activity of
internal hosts within an enterprise network. As such, its relevance for IDS eval-
uation is limited due to the absence of labeling and its specific scope. Further,
given that the dataset was collected in January 2005, it carries the drawback of
being relatively outdated [75].
CAIDA dataset — The Center for Applied Internet Data Analysis is an organiza-
tion dedicated to collecting and publishing network traﬀic captures for research
purposes.
They offer various tailored datasets, each one focusing on different
types of attacks or anomalies [25]. Although these datasets bring value to the
research community, they do not provide labels and are therefore limited in scope
with regards to IDS research.
SimpleWeb dataset — This dataset is developed by the University of Twente,
composed of eight separate traces of network traﬀic, which were collected from a
university network. The collected network traces have been anonymized for pri-
vacy, with all payload data removed where applicable. Furthermore, the dataset
includes a trace that was collected from a honeypot, where all captured traﬀic
was labeled as malicious [18]. Aside from the labeled trace, the dataset does not
provide ground truth and is somewhat antiquated with regards to contemporary
network traﬀic.

Kyoto dataset — The Kyoto dataset is a unique collection of feature records
extracted from network traﬀic captured through advanced honeypots at the Uni-
versity of Kyoto. These honeypots were designed with disguise mechanisms to
enhance the validity of the captured data [65]. The dataset utilizes the same 14
features identified in the KDD-99 dataset but also incorporates an additional 10
features that relate to outputs from various IDSs. The dataset has been updated
up until December 2015. Nevertheless, the dataset is limited is scope as raw cap-

UMass Trace dataset — The University of Massachusetts’s Laboratory for Ad-

3.3
Dynamic Datasets
Within this context, Dynamic datasets are defined as datasets which are gener-
ated by tools that leverage background traﬀic or provided properties to create
synthetic network traﬀic. Researchers have devised such tools in an attempt to
tackle the inherent deficiencies of static datasets. The necessity for such synthetic
datasets springs from the scarcity of modern datasets that encompass a diverse
range of attack traﬀic. Moreover, the invention of such data generation tools,
enables researchers to replicate and modify specific scenarios relevant to their re-
search in a flexible manner.
This section aims to present existing tools for the generation of dynamic datasets.
This presentation will be followed by an evaluation of these tools to better under-
stand their capabilities and limitations.
3.3.1 FLAME
FLAME, developed by Brauckhoff et al. [24] serves to inject handcrafted anoma-
lies into network flows. In this context, a flow is defined as a collection of packets
from a source to a destination IP during a specified time interval, as observed at a
certain point in a network. FLAME offers an engine which empowers researchers
to manually select anomalies to be inserted into the background traﬀic.
This
facilitates the generation of dynamic and customized datasets that can cater to
specific anomaly detection scenarios.
The authors present three modes for injecting anomalies into the background
traﬀic. Additive anomalies, involves the addition of synthetic flows that neither
interact with nor affect the existing flows within the dataset. Subtractive anoma-
lies, involves the selection and removal of certain flows from the background traﬀic.
Lastly, Interactive anomolies, involves both the addition of synthetic flows to the
background traﬀic and the removal of selected flows. Three case studies were con-
ducted as part of their research, namely TCP SYN network scan, ingress shifting,
and TCP SYN DoS attack.
However, it is important to highlight that its reliance on network flows limits
its application to flow-based IDS evaluations. This is because flow-based intru-
sion detection only inspects the packet header without analyzing the packet pay-
load, rendering it incapable of detecting attacks that leave no flow footprint [122].
Lastly, it should be noted that the FLAME tool is currently discontinued.

3.3.2
ISCX-UNB
Shiravi et al. [106] proposed a solution to the observed issues surrounding the
public availability of diverse and authentic datasets. This proposal involved the
creation of dynamic datasets which are modifiable, extendable and reproducible.
This work was carried out at the Information Security Centre of Excellence, Uni-
versity of New Brunswick. The authors suggested generating dynamic sets based
on specific representations of network traﬀic features.
These representations,
termed as profiles, were divided into two categories: α and β.
The α profiles were used to describe the behaviors of attacks. Where each α pro-
file represented an attack scenario, designed and implemented using conventional
tools like Nmap, Metasploit and BruteSSH. The implemented attacks included
HTTP DoS attacks, IRC botnet DDoS attacks, and brute force Secure Shell
(SSH).
The β profiles represented models of legitimate user activities, thereby generating
benign traﬀic. The creation of these profiles involved analyzing benign traﬀic to
extract mathematical distributions from packet sizes, payload sizes and packets
per flow.
Several β profiles were created as part of the study, simulating the
legitimate use of various protocols like HTTP, Internet Message Access Protocol
(IMAP), Simple Mail Transfer Protocol (SMTP), File Transfer Protocol (FTP),
and SSH.
Unfortunately, the approach has several limitations. The manual creation of α
profiles could introduce variations, thus affecting the replicability of the results.
Moreover, constructing the β profiles on unlabeled traﬀic might lead to a degra-
dation in quality. The tool requires a test bed deployment and manual profile
creation, thus compromising the Usability requirement, as the user’s knowledge
significantly influences the generated profiles. Finally, while the authors made
the dataset public and opposed to the generation tool itself, which is not publicly
available.

ID2T
ID2T, the toolkit under discussion, was initially introduced through a prototype
released in 2015 by Vasilomanolakis et al [30].
It underwent further improve-
ments leading up to its first public release in 2016 [3]. ID2T enables users to
inject synthetic attacks into user-specified background traﬀic. ID2T has a robust
statistical collection mechanism, capable of analyzing and replicating the unique
network characteristics of the background traﬀic. This ensures that the injected
attacks align well with the intrinsic characteristics of the original traﬀic. This
quality allows for the creation of realistic, context-aware attack scenarios for IDS

Artifacts
When generating synthetic datasets, one should be wary of artifacts, which of-
tentimes affect the reliability of the network traﬀic in the resulting dataset. An
artifact is defined as some form of defect in a dataset – introduced as a ramification
from the creation of synthetic traﬀic – which does not conform to the expected
properties of network traﬀic. When generating synthetic datasets, the end result
must mimic real-world network traﬀic as closely as possible. For this reason, it is
paramount that ID2T reduces potential artifacts to its minimum. The remainder
of this section is derived from research presented in Chapter 3 concerning the
state of the art as well as the research conducted by Vasilomanolakis et al. [31]
and Bhuyan et al. [6].
Examples of artifacts include TCP communication with a windows size always
equalling zero [31], usage of the port number zero, a particular set of TTL values
that are repeated in an uncommon fashion or a large concentration of incorrect
checksums. Many other instances of identified defects of a similar nature was
pointed out in Chapter 3 – leading the reader to the subsequent classification of
artifacts in the context of synthetic network traﬀic.
Upon observing Figure 4.1, an artifact is firstly divided into two discernible cate-
gories, namely Invalid Network Traﬀic and Network Traﬀic Abnormalities. Invalid
Network Traﬀic refers to any deviations or irregularities that do not conform to
the anticipated attributes of real-world network traﬀic. Most often, this type of
artifact is expressed from an incorrect usage of a given network protocol or net-
work specification. In other words, Invalid Network Traﬀic refers to a potential
collection of network packets that are somehow malformed in relation to specified
standards and thus of suspicion to anyone analysing the network traﬀic.
On the contrary, an artifact classified as a Network Traﬀic Abnormalitiy describes
any type of suspicious inconsistency that conforms to the prescribed ruleset for
network specification and protocols.
An example of this could be a series of
significantly deviating TTL values between two IP addresses, which would be

and Network Specific Context. It remains important to make the distinction be-
tween the two contexts as it is a key factor in differentiating certain defects.
The Network Agnostic Context denotes a domain for artifacts which are inde-
pendent of the specific characteristics and behavior of the network. Generally,
such artifacts occur as a result of some infringement for general networking prin-
ciples. This could for instance be the presence of an invalid IP address [31].

The Network Specific Context denotes a domain for artifacts which does not
comply with the characteristics of a particular network setup. Such artifacts are
directly related to the protocols, configurations, or peculiarities of the specific
network environment. An example of an artifact within this context could be the
occurrence of a public IP address inside a local network.
The category of Invalid Data spans across both stated contexts, as it can be
seen in Figure 4.1. Firstly, network traﬀic can be incorrect with respect to the
Network Specific Context when certain network characteristics are left unfulfilled.
An example of this could be that certain packets are not being fragmented even
though the Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU) [31] is exceeded. Moreover, In-
valid Data can also occur as a result of incorrect network protocol usage – for
instance the transmission of incorrect checksums, thus belonging to the Network
Agnostic Context.
A significant subset of Invalid Data resides in the Temporal Invalid Data cate-
gory, which refers to network traﬀic that is no longer valid due to modifications
of modern protocol standards. This classifies under Network Agnostic Context as
these modern standards are updated at a global level. In a few years, an example
of such an artifact could be utilisation of HTTP 1.2 as opposed to the newer
HTTP 2.0 [114] or usage of prior versioning of TLS 1.3 [7].
An artifact categorized as a Predictable Pattern represents some series of network
packets which appear in a repeated fashion, with little to no variance, against
the expected behavior of the network. Such patterns are typically absent in real-
world traﬀic – thus we consider these artifacts in the category of Network Agnostic
Context (e.g. a predictable pattern of generated timestamps for injected packets).
Such an artifact could for instance occur as a result of a number of TTL values
which follow some general trend. Another example of this could be an unusual
relation between IP addresses in a trace file.
The category of Synthetic Abnormality entails artifacts which contains some
packet fields that does not coincide with a given network. For the most part,
such artifacts are tied to the Network Specific Context as they are perceived ab-
normal when compared to the remainder of the network traﬀic, which is the key
detail that separates it from Invalid Data.
The term Network Traﬀic Sterility refers to a state in which network traﬀic is
devoid of any extraneous or irrelevant network packets. In other words, the net-
work traﬀic is too clean. A certain fraction of legitimate benign network traﬀic
contains malformed packets as well as other slight defects. Common factors lead-
ing to an increased number of such defects include a poor network implementation
[31]. This category of artifacts is exists, in some capacity for all networks, regard-

5.3
Attack Generation
The current iteration of ID2T possess the ability to generate synthetic attacks for
later injection in two distinct manners: Template-based and Script-based attack
generation. As the name suggests, Template-based attack generation involves a
Template PCAP file, residing in a given attack implementation, which is manip-
ulated, based upon statistical distributions of the Input PCAP file. On the other
hand, Script-based attack generation generates attack packets on-the-fly, whilst
establishing characteristics for the attacks packets.
Both techniques are heavily utilized for the current iteration of ID2T. In the
following sections, details for both attack generation types are provided. Finally,
the attack generation performance of ID2T’s is assessed.
5.3.1
Template-based Attack Generation
Utilization of Template-based attack generation is generally suitable for relatively
complex cyber-attacks. This complexity may for instance stem from a varied us-
age of network protocols [123] as well as attacks which have a distinctly bursty
nature with respect to their timestamps.
The main prerequisite for this attack generation technique is the ’Template PCAP
file’. This file contains the network packets for a given attack implementation –
and is typically acquired either through a lab or a dataset which is publicly ac-
cessible – for instance from Unit 42 by Palo Alto Networks [94] or VirusTotal [125].
From this point, the packets within the Template PCAP file is modelled based
upon statistical distributions from the Input PCAP file. Thus, a given attack
implementation will entail replacement of a series of packet fields (such as the
preferred malicious IP addresses) as well as ensuring that port numbers, TTL,
etc. are generated as intended.
Finally, ID2T utilizes key functionality from the library, Scapy [104] – which
is a powerful packet manipulation library with the capability of decoding and
manipulating packets for a plethora of network protocols. This library is utilized
in part for adjusting packet fields with respect to the statistical distributions and
user specified input parameters. This is primarily done to conceal the attack in
question, whilst reducing any artifacts from the Network Specific Context category
as specified in Chapter 4 on Classification of Artifacts.

Script-based Attack Generation
Script-based attack generation is a technique which is suitable for cyber-attacks
with a relatively low complexity. For these reasons, such implementations typ-

Exhaustion. This is the case as such attacks generally does not vary much in
protocol usage and other packet field by comparison to other attack types. Thus,
a good example where this technique is applicable is a generic DDoS attack, at
which a plethora of source IPs target a single IP address [123].
Moreover, the Scapy library remain heavily utilized in such attack implementa-
tions, to create the attack packets for subsequent injection. The main prerequisite
for this attack generation technique is to follow the class-template style [3] of ID2T
– at which the attack implementation will be added automatically.

based on the contents of the packet’s data – any modification to a given packet will
lead to incorrect checksums. If this consideration was not fulfilled, an anomaly-
based IDS could relatively easily identify a potential spike in the concentration
of packets that are missing a verification [123].
5.5
Limitations of ID2T
ID2T does not have the ability to modify the background traﬀic based upon the
synthetically injected packets. This limits the types of attacks that ID2T is ca-
pable of injecting to ones that do not alter the state of the particular network.
For this reason, ID2T would benefit from having an additional module which has
the capability to modify the benign background traﬀic based upon the activity
in the injected attack packets. At the present moment, ID2T contains attack im-
plementations for DDoS attacks, at which the targeted IP addresses can proceed
with its normal traﬀic after the perceived DDoS attack has been executed. The
proposed solution to this problem can be viewed in Section 6.3.3.
Another key limitation for ID2T is that the user caries the responsibility of en-
suring that the specified Input PCAP file remain free from any malicious traﬀic.
If the user fails to provide such a file, the resulting data labeling will not take
the malicious traﬀic from the background traﬀic into account. A dataset which
only contains partial data labeling of malicious traﬀic, is not suitable for IDS
evaluation. In the future, ID2T should aim to reduce this possibility.
Lastly, the generation of timestamps for injected attack packets using the Template-
based attack generation technique is inaccurate and leads to a significant number
of artifacts. This problem occurs as a result of the frequent mixing of the injected
packets’ order – leading to retransmissions amongst other shortcomings. More-
over, this could pose challenges for the evaluation of machine learning-based IDSs
using ID2T. This limitation has been eradicated and its implementation details
can be viewed in Section 6.2.3.

HTTP Header Manipulation
The first of these developed functionalities is a function for manipulating HTTP
headers within TCP packets. This function shown in Listing 6.8 allows for a higher
degree of variability and customization in the HTTP headers of synthetic attacks.
def update_http_header(self, ip_pkt, http_header, header_injection):
http_pkt = ip_pkt.payload.payload
load = http_pkt[Raw].load.decode()
header_pattern = http_header + r":.*\r\n"
new_load = re.sub(header_pattern, header_injection, load)

# Recalculate the IP len as well as IP and TCP checksums if the payload length
,→has changed
if tcp_payload_len_diff != 0:
self.update_ip_packet_len_and_checksums(ip_pkt)
return tcp_payload_len_diff
Listing 6.8: HTTP Header Update Function
The purpose of this function is to locate and replace a specified HTTP header
within a packet. It takes as inputs an IP packet, the name of the HTTP header to
be replaced, and the new header value to be injected. The function then executes
the replacement and, if the length of the payload changes as a result, recalculates
the IP length accordingly. The function then returns the difference in payload
length after the update. It should be noted that although the Scapy version in
use automatically recalculates checksums, it does not detect the change in length
of the IP packet, thus the values are updated manually.
A prime example of the function’s utility is demonstrated when generating Log4Shell
attack traﬀic. In this scenario, the function update_http_header() can be used
to inject a user-provided or a random LDAP payload in any of the headers. This
allows for the simulation of numerous Log4Shell attack variations based on the
same Template PCAP file. Thus, amplifying the diversity of the test scenarios.
Lastly, it is worth noting that many exploits are characterized by their variable
payloads, which this function aids in replicating.
Beyond the given example and its implementation for the Confluence server at-
tack, this function offers future attack implementation the possibility of using
diverse payloads. Thus, it contributes towards enhancing Attack Diversity - a
non-functional requirement set out in Section 1.3.

Generation of Ephemeral Ports
An important facet of generating synthetic attack traﬀic which closely resembles
real-world patterns is the generation of ephemeral ports. The functionality, de-
veloped through two main functions, generate_ephemeral_ports() and get_
unique_random_ephemeral_port(), contributes to the generation of diverse at-
tack traﬀic.
An instance of the functions utility is seen in the scanning attacks, which in-
volve multiple TCP streams. In real-world scenarios, ephemeral ports are pseudo-
randomly assigned by the connecting connecting machine based on available ports.
Consequently, replicating an attack traﬀic with unchanged source ports fails to
accurately emulate the dynamic nature of real-world traﬀic.
The generate_ephemeral_ports() and get_unique_random_ephemeral_port()
function address this issue in tandem. The former function, highlighted in List-
ing 6.9 presides over the conversation, identifying whether a unique random
ephemeral port for the original port already exists. If it does, this previously
generated ephemeral port is returned. Otherwise, the function invokes the get_
unique_random_ephemeral_port() which generates a unique ephemeral port

number within a specified range. This function ensures the uniqueness of the
port in the attack traﬀic and defaults to the Linux ephemeral port range.

if ep in reserved_ports:
ep = tcp_pkt.getfieldval("dport")

The functions are found in the BaseAttack class and are utilised in various at-

port source distribution, particularly relevant when a single attack is executed
multiple times on a dataset.
The realism of ephemeral ports reduces the artifacts generated by repeated runs,
thereby enhancing the Quality requirement of ID2T datasets. The importance of
this realism extends to machine learning-based IDSs, which rely on pattern recog-
nition within network traﬀic. Katsura’s IDS, for instance, depends on a variety
of entropy features, including ephemeral ports [66]. Moreover, Blaise’s botnet

integration of a functionality that emulates real-world ephemeral port behavior
can provide a more realistic training dataset for such IDSs.

6.4
Probe and Surveillance Attacks
In recent years, probing and surveillance network attacks have emerged as signifi-
cant threats to the security of various systems. These attacks gather information
about a target network, its systems, or its applications. The goal being to iden-
tify potential vulnerabilities, entry points, or valuable data that can be exploited.
Such attacks can be categorized within the reconnaissance phase of the cyber kill
chain model, presented in Section 2.5 and more precisely, within the active recon-
naissance phase, as an adversary unavoidably leaves traces during these probing
attacks.
Continuing from the active reconnaissance phase, probing attacks may escalate
to discovery of vulnerabilities and exploitation. This is particularly concerning
as these vulnerabilities could pave the way for various malicious activities such
as credit card skimming attacks, redirects to scam websites, SEO spam, and de-
facement as detailed in Sucuri’s Website Threat report [112].
This section introduces three new scanning attacks of the CMS category into the
ID2T framework. This is achieved via the introduction of Joomla Scan, WordPress
Scan and Drupal Scan. The integration of these CMS-specific scanning attacks
into ID2T expands its scope and offers a more diverse dataset for evaluating IDSs.

6.5
Exploitation Attacks
Exploit attacks constitute a category of cyber-attacks that specifically target
known vulnerabilities within a victim’s device, facilitating the execution of unau-
thorized or malicious code. These attacks demonstrate high eﬀicacy as they cap-
italize on software weaknesses that have not yet been patched.
In the context of the cyber kill chain model, exploit attacks transpire during the
weaponization and delivery phases. As for weaponization, the adversary typically
employs a custom software tool or crafts a script designed to exploit the partic-
ular vulnerability. This is followed by the delivery phase, where the exploit is
transmitted to the target system in the form of a payload.
This section extends the exploit attack injection capabilities of ID2T. This is
achieved via the introduction of Log4Shell, Confluence Server Webwork OGNL
Injection and WinHTTPSys.
6.5.1
Log4Shell
The first of these exploit attacks is Log4Shell, which is a critical vulnerability
discovered in the Apache Log4j library, a popular Java-based logging utility used
in many applications and systems. The exploit originates in the Java Naming
and Directory Interface (JNDI) lookup performed by Log4J without proper user-
input sanitation. This vulnerability in the Log4j library allows adversaries to
achieve RCE by exploiting the feature that enables developers to perform JNDI
==PAGE:95==
6.5 Exploitation Attacks
79
lookups while logging events.
An adversary can control what is logged by the application and insert a spe-
cially crafted string that tells Log4j to perform a JNDI lookup of an arbitrary
URL using certain protocols. The adversary can then host malicious code at that
URL, which will be executed when the vulnerable application processes the log

The Win HTTP stack also known as, http.sys, is a kernel driver integral to
the Windows operating system. It provides foundational network-related func-
tionalities for processing HTTP requests in the Windows operating system and
is often used in communication for HTTP-based Windows services, such as In-
ternet Information Services (IIS). The Win HTTP stack vulnerability lies in the
incorrect handling of HTTP headers within requests sent to the kernel driver. As
per the analysis by researchers at Trellix, the vulnerability lies in the two functions
UlpAlllocateFactTracker() and UlAllocateFastTrackerToLookaside(). The
issue arises when the functions fail to zero out the allocated memory, thereby pro-
viding an avenue for exploitation [26].
Adding to the severity, Microsoft has indicated that the exploit can be lever-
aged to create a network worm that can affect connected hosts where the HTTP
trailer support feature is activated. The vulnerability has been identified in mul-
tiple versions of the Windows operating system, including Windows 10, Windows
11, Windows Server 2019, and Windows Server 2022 [28].
Besides the widespread reach of the exploit, another compelling reason for its
inclusion is its impact on the background traﬀic. Specifically, the kernel crash
halts the emission of any further traﬀic by the affected device.
This scenario
underscores the necessity for adding background traﬀic manipulation capabilities

As introduced in Section 2.4, botnets constitute interconnected devices seized by a

6.6.1
Mirai Botnet
The Mirai botnet, first discovered in 2016, marked a significant milestone in the
evolution of botnets due to its far-reaching impact and ability to target IoT de-
vices. Its primary function was to conduct large-scale DDoS attacks. One such
attack targeted Dyn over the DNS port, quickly becoming the largest DDoS at-
tack in history, with a magnitude of 1.2 Tbps. This resulted in massive internet
outages across the globes [82].
In October 2016, Mirai’s source code was leaked on a public forum, leading to
an escalation in the development of mutations. These mutations possess similar
characteristics and maintain a code structure akin to the original, typically split
into three components: the bot, the C&C server, and the loader. While most of
these mutations lack distinct names, they often incorporate new exploits, expand-
ing their threat landscape [58]. The original Mirai code, written in C, showcased
an effective method for compromising vulnerable devices using a predefined list
of default login credentials. Upon successfully infecting a device, the malware
fortifies itself by blocking additional incoming connections, effectively hindering

further exploitation [8].

Multi-stage Attacks
This section introduces a new category of cyber-attacks to ID2T, namely multi-
stage attacks. As detailed in Section 2.6, multi-stage attacks encompass diverse
actions, each aimed at achieving specific objectives, such as breaching security
defenses, escalating privileges, establishing persistence, and finally executing the
desired malicious operation. Given their complex nature, multi-stage attacks are
==PAGE:116==
100
6 Attack Implementations
often more challenging to detect and prevent compared to single-stage attacks,
making them a significant threat [9].
This section presents a combination of the previously introduced attack imple-
mentations as a multi-stage attack. Firstly, the Joomla Scan x Joomla Exploit
combination, consisting of the stages: active reconnaissance and exploitation.
While multi-stage attacks often involve more than just these two stages, this sce-
nario offers a fitting introduction of the Multi-stage attack category into ID2T.
The introduction is broadened further with a second, more complex attack se-
quence, combining the Mirai botnet, a Log4Shell Scan and a Log4Shell Exploit.
This attack introduces a new vector for the Mirai botnet to obtain initial access.
Thereafter, escalating privileges and establishing foothold. The attack culminates
with C&C communication and execution of desired malicious operation.
These attacks introduce inter-dependencies between different attack stages, high-
lighting the importance of time intervals and the success of each stage. Moreover,
these Multi-stage attacks empower ID2T to simulate advanced attacks, thereby en-
abling researchers and security professionals to test the resilience of IDSs against
sophisticated threats.

It is paramount for the synthetic packets to respect the properties of the back-

The central module in ID2T involved in meeting the specified requirements, is
the Statistics module, which, as specified in Section 5.2, extracts statistical in-
formation from the background traﬀic. Subsequently, the responsibility lies with
the individual attack implementations to employ the accessible information to its
utmost extent. In this context, ID2T Gen 2 includes efforts to ensure constant
TTL values between the adversary and the victim for all newly introduced attack
implementations. Furthermore, all newly introduced attack implementations in-

number of attack implementations residing in ID2T Gen 1, which was lacking
such considerations, for instance the Sality botnet presented in Section 6.2.1.
Considerations have been made to ensure that window sizes remains positive
in all cases for certain attack implementations, which injects a small number
of packets. Generally speaking, the random and dynamic mapping of window
sizes from the background traﬀic, as depicted in Figure 5.4, remains the utilized
approach. However, in cases where the used background traﬀic frequently has
window sizes with values of zero, indicating that the receiver buffer is empty, it
is ensured that the injected packets only have window sizes that are positive, as
the order of magnitude for such attacks is too small to carry general mishaps of
the background traﬀic.

communication for synthetic attack traﬀic by considering subnet masks. This
remains of particular relevance for all attack implementations presented in Sec-

7.4.4
Summary of Resolved Artifacts
Upon the discovered and resolved defects, the following section presents a recap
the identified limitations. This is followed by a brief conclusion concerning the
degree of resolvement of the identified artifacts. Lastly, the introduction of the
realistic timestamps and their improvement in relation to ID2T’s non-functional
Quality requirement is assessed.
In broad terms, two categories of artifacts were identified and eradicated. These
artifacts relate to the following problems:
[1] Assigned timestamps for datasets which are incompatible with the bursty
nature of real-world network traﬀic – as they typically increase at an un-
changing pace, indicating a pattern for sequence of network traﬀic, which
can be anticipated and interpreted on.
[2] Assigned timestamps that result an invalid ordering of injected packets,
which lead to a substantial concentration of general mishaps and identified
warnings.
In continuation of the first point, the timestamp computation for ID2T Gen 1
was missing any form of differentiation based upon the difference in estimated
elapsed time for various types of network packets. For instance, an HTTP GET
request will be rather time consuming, relatively speaking. This kind of relation-
ship between inter arrival times of packets can be classified under Predictable
Patterns due to its foreseeable nature in an Network Agnostic Context. Thus, any
anomaly-based IDS, which takes timestamps into consideration, would be able to
draw conclusions based upon the generated timestamps.
Furthermore, this classification of the artifact is valid, as it is classified under
Network Traﬀic Abnormalities, which refers to suspicious activity leading to syn-
thetic network traﬀic that is incongruent with the characteristics of real-world
traﬀic.
In regards to the second point, exemplified by the figures residing in Figure 7.7,
one can classify this artifact as a Synthetic Abnormality, as the interchanging net-
work packets often lead to unrealistic network traﬀic flows. A notable instance
of this matter is highlighted and commented on for Figure 6.3. With the imple-
mented adjustments, the network traﬀic flow is guaranteed to remain identical
to the Template PCAP file, from which the attacks are generated, resolving both
aforementioned artifacts.

8.1
Conclusion
This thesis has presented a compelling case for the necessity of establishing dy-
namic and high-quality datasets to enable a reliable assessment of IDSs. In light
of this objective, a comprehensive set of requirements for synthetic dataset cre-
ation have been discerned. The emergence of ID2T has provided an improved
alternative in the research field and aims to continually widen its plethora of syn-
thetic attacks along with their quality. In addition, it remains evident that ID2T
is still a work in progress in relation to its overall capabilities to inject realistic
network traﬀic, thereby minimizing artifacts in the process.
As a result, this thesis has provided a multitude of attack implementations resid-
ing in the attack categories of Probe and Surveillance, Exploitation, Botnet and
the newly introduced Multi-stage.
Such contributions have widespread attack
vectors as well as characteristics and are all topical for the modern digital land-
scape. A series of significant contributions have been applied for the introduced
attack implementations as well as particular pre-existing attack implementations
to enhance its realism of their results.
The introduced efforts include a Background Manipulation module, implementa-
tion of realistic timestamp generation as well as inclusion of general functionality
that can be utilized for future attack implementations, such as dynamic gener-
ation of payload contents, whilst remaining fully complaint with given protocol
requirements.
The contributions have improved ID2T’s capability to fulfil its
Quality requirement along with a wide array of other requirements for datasets
and their respective data generation tools.
This was predominantly achieved
through improvements which adhere to or advance the Modelling af Packet Level
& Analysis of Background Traﬀic. Finally, a comprehensive evaluation has been
conducted to assess all the aforementioned enhancements.
In summary, this thesis has successfully progressed ID2T in a number of direc-

8.2
Future Work
In guiding the future development of ID2T, two distinct paths of enhancement
emerge.
The first path focuses on technical rectifications and advancements. This includes
addressing the current non-functional TIDED module. It also involves adding sup-
port for IPv6, HTTP/2.0 and updating the Scapy Library. These enhancements
not only improve performance but also provide support for more contemporary
protocols.
Additionally, consideration should be given to re-implementing the
Background Manipulation module in C++ to enhance its eﬀiciency. A final point
in this technical path is to extend the newly implemented module to ID2T’s at-
tack implementations residing in the Resource Exhaustion category. This would
equip ID2T with the ability to realistically mimic the changes in network flow
patterns caused by these attacks, such as network congestion, increased latency,
and high packet loss rates.
The second path focuses on the capabilities of ID2T’s to more accurately repli-
cate and represent real-world network traﬀic conditions. In it current form, ID2T
only supports a single continuous trace as input. The accommodation of multiple
trace inputs would be a beneficial advancement, that enhances its Scalable inputs
and Flexiblity criteria. This could include varying samples from different times
of the day or different points within the same network. Next, widening the scope
of IDS placements for which ID2T can generate traﬀic is another promising ad-
vancement. This would advance ID2T from only focuses on generating traﬀic for
network edge placements to also simulate traﬀic for IDSs that monitor both edge
and inter-host communication. This enhancement would increase applicability
across diverse network configurations.
An essential step in this second path is expanding the arsenal of attacks in ID2T,
particularly incorporating those attacks that are emerging in the threat landscape.
The Multi-stage attack architecture established in this thesis provides a founda-
tion for incorporating Advanced Persistent Threats (APT) attacks into ID2T.
Notably, the introduction of the multiple traces capability significantly aids in
the integration of APT attacks. This proposed enchantment allows for the gen-
eration of slower, more complex attacks unfolding over multiple days, without
necessitating a continuous time interval. Thus, enhancing the Renewability and
Attack Diversity of ID2T.
