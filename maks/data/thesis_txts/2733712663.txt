Abstract
The need for fast electronic components with low energy consumption has let to the field of fab-
ricating electronic devices based on two-dimensional (2D) materials and modulating the devices
by optical signals with strong electric fields. The edge of 2D materials highly concentrate the
external electric field, and these materials are therefore expected to display high performance of
electric field induced electron emission, which can be exploited in nanoelectronic devices. The
understanding of field emission (FE) from the basal plane versus the edge of graphene is not well
understood, and a greater understanding of the behaviour is beneficial for developing graphene
based electronic components relying on FE. This thesis search to decoupling of FE from the
basal plane and the edge of graphene, and use a THz field to induce FE from graphene. The aim
is furthermore to fabricate a graphene based floating gate field effect transistor (FGFET) where
the channel conductivity can be modulated by using a THz beam to move carriers between
graphene sheets via FE.
The investigation of basal plane field emission was conducted on both exfoliated graphene
as well as graphene grown by Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD), using a THz spectroscopy
setup, where the electron emission was measured using a time-of-flight spectrometer. A signifi-
cantly larger amount of electrons was collected for measurements where the CVD graphene was
grounded than when comparing with the ungrounded measurements. The ungrounded sample
furthermore displayed a decrease in emission over time, while the grounded sample maintained
a stable emission over time. This seem to suggest that the resupply of electrons from electrodes
increase the emission and that ungrounded graphene can to some extend be depleted for elec-
trons, which is a valuable realisation for using graphene in non-volatile memory devices.
Isolating and exposing the edge of graphene sheets was done by encapsulating graphene in
hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) and etching trenches into these stacks. A static localised elec-
tric field was imposed at the trench location by a biased tungsten tip inside a Scanning Electron
Microscope (SEM) chamber. The measurements are inconclusive, but could indicate that the
edge emission is suppressed do to contaminants, and that the emitted electrons clean the edge
which enable a greater amount of emission.
Assembling micro-mechanical exfoliated graphene and hBN and patterning the stacks using
Electron Beam Lithography, resulted in the realisation of 2D material based FGFET’s, in-
tended to be modulated by asymmetric single-cycle THz pulses.
Subsequent testing of the
devices lead to the breakdown of the source and drain electrodes, incapacitated the devices for
measuring any effects the THz pulses might have.
The results obtained in this thesis suggests that field emission from graphene induced by asym-
metric single-cycle THz pulses, does not follow the general understanding of field emission
described by the Fowler-Nordheim model. The crystallinity of the sample, as well as the po-
larisation of the incident THz pulses could affect the electron emission. Further investigation
of these topics is therefore expected to lead to increased understanding of the physics of field
emission in graphene.

Introduction
Over the past five decades, the electronic industry has experienced unprecedented growth,
marked by a rapid advancement in computational power. A consistent trend of miniaturisation
in electronic components has been a testament to Moore’s Law, with a consistent doubling of the
number of transistors on a chip approximately every 24 months[1]. Central to this progression
has been the silicon based-processing technology, which has served as the foundation for these
rapid advancements in integrated circuits. This technology, however, is confronting the physical
limitations of further scaling, as transistor gate dimensions approach the realm of single-digit
nanometers[2]. Furthermore the silicon-based technology faces challenges related to the sub-
stantial power consumption incurred when increasing the speed of charge movement in silicon.
Alternative materials and approaches to keep up with the continued demand of improved speed
and capability of electrical components are therefore sought after.
One promising candidate for non-volatile memory applications is memristive systems. A mem-
ristor is a two-terminal electrical component that can alter and retain its internal resistance
based on applied electric stimuli[3]. Several forms of memristive systems have been suggested,
but an example of a structure that can be used for this purpose is a floating gate field effect
transistor (FGFET), a schematic of which is shown in figure 1. This design utilises that the
conductance of a semiconductor can be varied by changing the carrier concentration by applying
an external voltage[3]. A process known as the electric field effect. Incorporating a gate mate-
rial into the device that is not electrically connected, a so called floating gate, which induce an
electric field, will thereby affect the electrical conductance of the channel, and thereby modulate
the current flow in the device.
The materials used for these components are meanwhile equally important, as especially the
scalability and electrical properties are dictating the performance.
Figure 1: Floating gate field effect transistor (FGFET). The main components include a source, drain,
channel layer, insulating dielectric, the embedded floating gate, and the gate.
Since it was first isolated in 2004 by Konstantin Novoselov and André Geim[4], graphene has
been considered a promising material for high performance nanoelectronics. This atomically
thin material displays incredibly favorable properties such as record breaking electrical con-
ductivity, carrier mobility, and mechanical strength[2]. Its linear energy dispersion around its
zero-bandgap structure additionally allow for continuous tuning of the Fermi level through the
field effect[4], which makes it a very attractive material to use for field effect transistors (FETs).
Integrating graphene with other low dimensional materials can both enhance the properties of
graphene[5], as well as proving useful for compact solutions for high complexity components[6].
Despite the absence of a bandgap, graphene is thus an interesting material to use both for the
conducting channel as well as for the gate material for a FGFET[6][7].
Building on this premise, this thesis focuses on exploring and developing graphene-based FGFET
Page 1 of 64
==PAGE:8==
devices.
The transport of charge carriers to and from the floating gate, is central to these devices, and
can be achieved through field emission. A process where a large electrical field enables a mate-
rial to emit electrons through tunneling.
Inducing the charge transfer needed for operation of electrical components is traditionally per-
formed by applying DC electric fields. Light assisted optoelectronic applications can meanwhile
prove to enhance the operation speed as well as reduce the energy consumption. In this con-
text, optical signals in the terahertz (THz) frequencies present a compelling avenue. Ultrafast
non-destructive THz pulses with high peak field strength can be achieved[8], making this an
ideal frequency range to use for achieving field emission.
Utilising the field effect of graphene was the intent of the paper detonating the graphene research,
and is thereby by no means a new thought. Field emission from graphene has also been studied
for nearly as long, although the understanding of the underlying physics is much more limited.
Field emission from the basal plane of graphene has been demonstrated repeatedly[9][10] in
spite of predictions suggesting otherwise[11], and field emission from the edge of graphene
also shows deviations from the Fowler-Nordheim model[12][13], which is classically used to
describe field emission. Past investigations of field emission from graphene has primarily been
studied through static electric fields[11], and expanding the knowledge and data for optically
induced field emission is therefore key to progress with the development of graphene based
optoelectronics.
1.1
Scope
The goal of this thesis is to advance the understanding of field emission from graphene, partic-
ularly on how the basal plane and edge emission can be studied separately. Furthermore the
objective is to fabricate a graphene based memristive device leveraging graphene’s field emission
properties modulated through ultrashort single-cycle THz pulses.
Chapter 2 is intended to provide the reader with the necessary background knowledge to com-
prehend and evaluate the findings throughout this thesis. It begins by introducing the electronic
properties of graphene as well as its integration with other materials into a device. The chapter
also includes a discussion of the Fowler-Nordheim model describing field emission, and how
this model might be adjusted to graphene. A description of THz spectroscopy is additionally
included, accounting for the THz generation and detection methods used in this work. The rel-
evant frequencies and field strengths are also accounted for through a theoretical investigation
of the Keldysh parameter. An in-depth account of the device envisioned is established, together
with considerations of the design and its operation as a light controlled FGFET. The chapter is
concluded with a model exploring the theoretically expected shift in Fermi level of the graphene
gate as a function of peak power of the incident THz pulses.
Chapter 3 accounts for the experimental methods used in this thesis.
It covers the main
methods used to characterise the materials and devices including optical microscopy, Raman
spectroscopy and Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM). A description of the main equipment used
for the fabrication process is described, as well as relevant settings used for realising the struc-
tures presented. The characterisation methodology of the field emitting properties of the final
devices is additionally accounted for. This include a probing station inside a Scanning Electron
Microscope (SEM) used for characterising the field emission through static electric fields, as
well as the two different THz spectroscopy setups used.
The main results achieved through this thesis is presented in chapter 4. The results include

2
Theory
2.1
2D materials
The isolation of the atomically thin carbon crystal, graphene, was first achieved by André
Geim and Konstantin Novoselov in 2004[4], and led to a rapid assignment of a Nobel prize
merely 6 years later in 2010.
Since then, graphene has been extensively researched for its
many remarkable properties not found elsewhere. With the realisation of single-layer graphene
followed a realm of other layered crystal structures, each with individual properties. To this
date, still more two-dimensional (2D) materials are being discovered, both theoretically as well
as experimentally. Since this thesis is structured around graphene devices, the following section
will give a brief introduction to some of the properties of graphene, as well as how integration
with other 2D materials can enhance certain qualities.
2.1.1
Graphene
Graphite is an allotrope of carbon, and is a three-dimensional (3D) crystal consisting of sheets
of graphene. Each graphene sheet, is a 2D material in its most natural form, meaning that it
has a spatial thickness of a single atom. The carbon atoms are arranged in a honeycome lattice,
comprised of two triangular sublattices, often denoted A and B. The chemical bonds between
neighboring atoms is remarkable strong, with a short interatomic distance of 1.42 Å, making
graphene the mechanically strongest material known (in-plane). This large strength arise from
the s-orbital and two of the three p-orbitals in the outer shell, each contributing one electrons
to form sp2-hybridised orbitals, that are shared with three neighboring carbon atoms, forming
σ-bonds. The created σ-bonds between each atoms are separated by 120◦, while the π-bonds,
formed by the pz-orbitals, are out of plane[2], see figure 2c. While the electrons in the σ-bonds
are localised, the π-bonds consists of delocalised electrons, creating an excellent environment
for electron transport in graphene[14].
The dispersion relation of the π-bonds can be determined analytically, for instance using the
tight binding model. The 2pz bonding (π) and anti-bonding (π∗) bands meet each other in a
singular point in the K and K’ points in the Brillouin zone. In its intrinsic form, graphene is
thereby a zero-bandgap semiconductor, also referred to as a semi-metal. A visualisation of the
electronic dispersion near the singularity points is provided in figure 2a.
Near the points where the Fermi surface reduces to discrete points, called the Dirac points, the
dispersion relation behaves linearly[14], following
E±(k) ≈±ℏvF k
(1)
where the crystal wave vector Q ≫k for the linearity to hold. The quantity vF is the Fermi
velocity, i.e. the group velocity of the electrons, which near the Dirac points is momentum inde-
pendent. In strong contrast to conventional semiconductors with parabolic dispersion relations.
This implies that the electrons acts as massless particles, which allow for the relativistic velocity
of vF ≈c/300[15] near the Dirac point. The consequence of this bandstructure is extraordinarily
high carrier mobility leading to micrometer-scale ballistic transport at room temperature[15].

In undoped graphene the point of minimal conductivity, called the charge neutrality point, is
located exactly at the Fermi level[14]. Electrically doping graphene, i.e. changing the carrier
concentration, can shift the charge neutrality point, and this can be done through the field
effect. The conductivity of graphene can thereby be changed linearly with an applied gate po-
tential to graphene.
Carbon atoms can form stable bonds with most other atoms, including abundant elements that
occupy the atmosphere. Hydrocarbons are therefore commonly formed and contaminates the
surface of materials. Due to the strong interatomic bonds in graphene however, chemical binding
from ambient molecules is difficult. While chemisorption is not impossible for all molecules,
the primary adsorbants are physisorbed, and contamination therefore still play a role, even for
chemically inert 2D materials. Several studies have shown, that within minutes after exfoliation,
graphene starts to show sign of being contaminated by airborne hydrocarbons[16].
It is known that the work function of the material can change drastically dependent on the
surface characteristics[16], and it has been shown theoretically that different hydrocarbons will
induce different surface potentials for graphene[17]. This strongly suggests that inconsistencies
in surface potential measurements reported in literature of graphene and graphite, arise from
airborne hydrocarbons. Since the heterogeneity of the surface potential will increase with the
time graphene is kept in an ambient environment[16], it can be beneficial to shield graphene
from surface contaminants by some method of encapsulation.
Graphene’s interaction with substrates exhibiting surface roughness can lead to deviations from
its ideal planar geometry.
These deviations, manifested as out-of-plane bending or ripples,
facilitate better surface conformity[18], enhancing graphene’s adherence to the substrate due to
increased contact area. This adaptability however also introduces complexities, particularly in
transferring graphene between substrates, see section 3.3.2. Moreover, the presence of ripples
in graphene can change the hopping parameters and thereby alter the electronic properties
of graphene[19]. These out-of-plane deformation are also seen for suspended graphene, since
thermal fluctuations at room temperature are sufficient to overcome the elastic energy associated
with off-plane pyramidalisation of the three σ-bonds and the torsional misalignment of the π-
bonds[20]. This is visualised in figure 3b.
In order to straighten out the graphene, as well as simply isolating it from ambient condi-
tions, graphene is commonly encapsulated by hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), creating a hBN-
graphene-hBN heterostructure[5], as illustrated in figure 3c.

Encapsulating graphene by hBN elevates certain desired properties in graphene.
Although several methods can be used to achieve graphene crystals, two methods are predomi-
nantly applied. Mechanically exfoliating graphene from graphite flakes using scotch tape, and
transferring the flakes onto a substrate, is the method known to produce the highest quality of
graphene crystals[21], but the throughput is often very low. This method is useful for high qual-
ity crystals, but it will be difficult to mass produce samples with this method due to scalability
challenges. Amongst several synthesis methods, Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD) has shown
itself to be a promising method for producing large scale graphene of high quality. This method
exposes a catalytic substrate to one or more precursors which form a uniform graphene film on
the surface of the substrate through decomposition[8][21]. The growth is heavily dependent on
precursors, temperature, pressure, substrate, and growth time. Although advancements have
been made in the synthesis of graphene, CVD grown graphene typically display lower quality
crystals than exfoliated flakes[8][21].
2.1.2
Hexagonal Boron Nitride
As graphene on SiO2 substrates display inferior electronic properties relative to the theoretically
estimated ones, there is a need for improving the supporting substrate for graphene based de-
vices. Surface states and scattering sites are easily modified by roughness in the substrate, and
while suspending graphene improves its properties[22], it is a challenging geometry to work with.
Hexagonal Boron Nitride (hBN) is a dielectric with an isomorphic lattice geometry of graphene.
As depicted in figure 3a which shows graphene on top of a layer of hBN, hBN consists of boron
and nitrogen atoms positioned in the A and B sublattices respectively, resulting in a bandgap of
≈5.97eV[5]. Due to the strong in-plane ionic bonds, hBN, like graphene, is expected to be free
of dangling out-of-plane bonds, and is therefore also chemically inert[5]. Furthermore, the lattice
mismatch with respect to graphene is small compared with many other crystals, (≈1.7%)[5]. All
of the above mentioned properties suggests that hBN is a high quality substrate for graphene,
that can support the realisation of graphene devices with properties closer to the theorised ones.

2.3
Electron emission
Electrons are bound to atoms by attractive potentials, but under certain circumstances the elec-
trons can overcome the resulting work function and be emitted into the surroundings. Electron
emission from conductors and semiconductors often occur by on of three different mechanisms;
thermionic emission, photo emission, and field emission.
As the name suggests, thermionic emission is governed by thermal excitations of electrons to
higher energy states, allowing the electrons to overcome the potential barrier from the vacuum,
which otherwise keeps them from escaping into the surrounding.
A similar process can be
achieved by photo-induced excitations through absorption, hence the name photo emission.
Unlike photo- and thermionic electron emission, field emission does not function by excitations
of the electrons inside the material, but by a quantum tunneling effect, enabled by a strong
external electrical field. For any functional application of this phenomena, it is essential to have
an idea of the amount of carriers leaving the material, and what parameters can be altered to
change this current. This was first described by Ralph H. Fowler and Lothar W. Nordheim in
1928[24].

2.3.2
Fowler-Nordheim equation for graphene
For an optimal field emitter, the current density should be as high as possible, while the field
required to notice this current should be as low as possible. It is therefore of interest to utilise
materials with high field enhancement factors, and low work functions. Traditionally, single
crystal tungsten is a very good field emitter, primarily attributed to its quite low work function
of Φ = 4.5 eV[28]. 2D materials have however been a growing field of research in regards to
electron emission due to their high field enhancement factors arising from sub-nanometre scale
emission sites at the edges[28]. A material like graphene is a good candidate for realising field
emission devices, due to its extraordinary transport ability, its narrow edge, and its high com-
patibility with thin film fabrication [28][12].
2D matarials such as graphene diverges from most of the assumptions made in the FN model
and it is therefore expected that alternative models are needed for nanomaterials such as
graphene[26][27].
First off, the low dimensionality in the out-of-plane direction mean, that
the periodicity of the lattice structure only persists in the plane. The band-structure is there-
fore only well dispersed along two spatial dimensions. Along the third spatial dimension the
electrons experience quantum confinement which could leading to quantised energy levels in
the out-of-plane direction[29]. Secondly, all free electrons in 2D structures are located at the
surface, i.e. at a boundary to vacuum. In 3D crystals however, the electrons near a vacuum
boundary only make up a a marginal proportion of the electrons available in the crystal. The
escape of electrons into vacuum is therefore expected to be significantly different for atomically
thin crystals[27][30]. Additionally, the non-parabolic dispersion near the K-point and K’-point
is in strong contrast with the assumed parabolic dispersion in the FN model. Considering the
entirety of the dispersion, there are regions where graphene has a parabolic shape, especially
around the Γ-point, and certain models suggests, that it is also wise to consider these regions [31].
As a comprehensive model taking everything into account is not possible at present, researches
use the approach they have found most suitable, which has resulted in a long list of different
models describing the field emission in low dimensional materials such as graphene. This list
includes, but is not limited to e.g. [25][26][13][32]. As the original FN equation was constructed
on account of Sommerfeld’s theory where fermions are non-interacting[27], the more realistic
treatment of the many-body system at low dimensionality have been investigated using first
principle calculations such as DFT[27] or using the tight binding model[31]. To be of even
greater value for the experimental science however, an analytical expression at which the ac-
quired data can swiftly be compared to is desired. Equation 3 based on the FN model has
been wildly successful, even a century after its publication, due to its relatively high accuracy
despite the simple expression, which allow for a fast comparison between the model and the data.
For device applications, most groups therefore focus on enhancing the morphology of graphene,
either by inducing features on the surface through strain, defects, or patterning, or by simply
taking advantage of the exposed edge to achieve a high field enhancement factor.
Some experiments meanwhile find a remarkably large tunneling current when probing the flat
surface, which suggests that field emission from graphene is not as well understood as could be
assumed. S. Santandra et. al[9] already showed in 2011 that a measurable current could be

graphene surface provides a medium from which a significant current can be drawn.
Emission from the basal plane and from the edge of 2D materials is expected to be funda-

2.4
Terahertz spectroscopy
Most literature have investigated field emission from graphene by applying a DC bias over the
material[11][28][13][34][10], thereby producing electronically enabled field emission. A strong
electric field can meanwhile also be applied by optical means. This both comes with other
prospects, as well as different challenges relative to electronic measurement methods.
Since field emission only occur under a very large field, typically on the order of 100 V/µm
[11][12][9], these types of experiments needs to be conducted in high vacuum (HV) or ultra
high vacuum (UHV) conditions, as such field strengths are orders of magnitudes larger than
the dielectric breakdown of air. Alternatively, the electric field needs to have a large enough
frequency for the dielectric breakdown not to occur. Not all frequencies can be used to achieve
field emission however. Illuminating the sample with high frequency light from the visible fre-
quencies and upwards, will result in a high amount of absorption, and because the energy of
single photons at these high frequencies are on the order of the work function or larger, the main
electron emission effect observed is photo emission[35]. Lower frequencies are thereby needed
in order to reduce the probability of photoemission. Too high photon energy can additionally
lead to sample damage.
In the regime of too low frequencies such as in the microwave regime, most light sources have
challenges in reaching intensities sufficient to produce electron emission, at least if we disregard
large-scale facilities[35]. In the infrared and terahertz (THz) regime, the energy is so low that
photoemission is rarely a problem, while the peak intensity is sufficient to achieve field emission.
The time-averaged intensity however can be large enough to induce thermal effects. This is best
resolved by illuminating the sample with a pulsed setup. The pulsed laser width is significantly
shorter than what is possible using AC bias supplies, which allow for exploring field emission
on a very different time scale. When a THz pulse interacts with graphene, the carriers act
on a much shorter timescale than the temporal width of the pulse due to the linear dispersion
around the K and K’ points. The peak field strength is therefore more dominating for the the
electronic motion in graphene than the interaction time given by the pulse duration.

THz time domain spectroscopy (TDS) have already been shown to be a reliable method of

The conventional THz pulse contains multiple oscillations of the electric field within each enve-

is zero. While the time integrated electric field is still zero when using single-cycle THz pulses
with highly asymmetric electric fields, the peak field strength is larger along one direction. This
is an essential feature for manipulation of electrical carriers in integrated circuit devices and
for fundamental studying of charge carrier on very short time scales, because the field induced
tunneling is non-linear with respect to the field strength.
2.4.1
THz generation
The THz generation methods used for this thesis are based on non-linear processes driven by
tabletop laser sources at near-infrared frequencies. The main pump laser used for these pro-
cesses is the Ti:Sapphire regenerative amplifier, which is a mode-locked femtosecond (fs) laser
with a central frequency of λc = 800 nm[36]. Since two different setups are used for this work,
both generation methods will be described briefly:
Optical rectification
Optical rectification belongs to the group of processes called difference frequency generation
(DFG). The process is a second order non-linear effect, which takes place in a non-centrosymmetric
crystal like that of zinc telluride (ZnTe) or lithium niobate (LiNbO3)[37], the latter of which is
used in this work. Upon imposing an electric field onto the non-linear crystal, a polarisation
will be induced in the material, and the charges disposed by the induced polarisation will begin
to oscillate, somewhat according to the envelope of the incident field. Due to the contribution
from lattice vibration, these charges will not completely follow the incident field, and the result
is that the phase velocity of the electrons is lowered relative to the incident field[35]. Oscillat-
ing, and thereby accelerating, charges will result in emission of electromagnetic radiation, in
this case in the THz regime. As it can be imagined, this effect is not limited to single frequencies.
Two-color air-plasma
THz generation in non-linear crystals have a limited bandwidth due to absorption of optical
phonons[38].
Using air plasma as the the mediating medium can generate broadband THz
spectra. A way of doing this is by focusing two frequencies co-propagating onto a gas, which will
Page 13 of 64
==PAGE:20==
generate a plasma filament. The fundamental of the fs laser and its second harmonic generated
through a frequency-doubling non-linear crystal, together form an asymmetric electric field,
which accelerates the free electrons in the plasma, acting as a radiation source[36][38]. The
emitted THz spectra is very broadband, typically spanning >20 THz, and frequency ranges as
large as 75 THz has been reported[38].
2.4.2
THz detection
Although other methods exist, e.g. using the pyroelectric effect, most THz detectors are based
on a coherent detection scheme, i.e. probing the THz signal with an optical signal inside a de-
tection medium. Although Photoconductive (PC) switching is a very established and broadly
used method, it was not used in this thesis, and the focus will therefore be on the two coherent
detection schemes that was used; Electro optical (EO) sampling, and Air Based Cohered De-
tection (ABCD).
ABCD
Air biased coherent detection (ABCD) utilises a gas as the detection medium. Unlike solid
state detectors, such as electro-optical crystals, gases like air do not suffer from phonon reso-
nances. Absorption and dispersion of the THz pulse is therefore almost not existing [35][39].
This means that there will not be any gaps in the detection spectrum, and gasses are there-
fore ideal for very broadband spectroscopy. The detection technique uses air/nitrogen gas as
medium for a non-linear four-wave-mixing process, where the coherent THz waveform detection
is enabled by introducing a strong AC bias field via high voltage (HV) electrodes. The THz
beam and the optical signal are spatiotemporally overlapping in the region of the high bias,
and through a four-wave mixing process an electrical field induced second harmonic (EFISH) is
generated. The EFISH is recorded, since it is containing information about the THz field[38][37]
Electro-optical sampling
EO detection is based on the stroboscopic effect, where the effect of the THz field strength is
probed by a short optical pulse. By scanning over a range of delay times, the temporal waveform
of the THz pulse can be mapped. A few percent of the power from the laser pulse is used for
the optical pulse, which is directed into a non-centrosymmetric crystal via a delay stage. In the
absence of the THz pulse, the optical pulse will pass through the generation crystal effectively
unaltered, beside from the phase delay due to the refractive index of the crystal. After passing
through the crystal, the pulse is collimated, and then encounters a quarter-waveplate, meaning
that the incident linearly polarised light will be perfectly circularly polarised. Subsequently, a
Wollaston prism is used to split the beam into the orthogonal components, which each has 50%
of the intensity. The signals are read by a balanced photodetector, producing an output of 0.
When the THz transient propagates through the EO crystal, a birefringence proportional to the
field strength is induced in the crystal[37]. This changes the phase delay of the of the optical
probe pulse, which thereby yields an elliptically polarised beam after the quarter-waveplate,
resulting in a non-zero readout of the photodetectors. The measured intensity read of the pho-
todetectors is modulated by the phase change, which in turn is dependent on the field strength.
Since the induced birefringence and the phase change of the optical pulse is linear, it is possible
to determine the instantaneous field strength of the THz transient using this method[35]. By
changing the delay time, ∆τ, between the THz and probe pulse, the temporal waveform of the
THz field can be mapped.
Some crystals experience the Kerr effect (a quadratic change in the refractive index as function
of the field strength), while others response linearly, referred to as Pockels effect[35]. In the
setup used for this thesis, a ZnTe crystal was used, which means that the field induces a linear
electro-optic effect.
A thicker crystal increases the optical path length, but due to phonon
Page 14 of 64
==PAGE:21==
coupling which narrows the bandwidth, the thickness of the crystal puts a spectral constrain
on the method[35][39]. The ZnTe crystal used was 958 µm. In most cases, the read signal is
relatively small, and it is therefore very susceptible to noise. Possible sources of background
noise could be ambient light in the laboratory, or electrical noise in the diodes themselves. In
order to achieve high signal to noise ratio, a mechanical chopper wheel was therefore utilised, set

The electrons emitted from the sample due to the THz beam will have a spread in emission
angle. Therefore only a portion of the electrons will be directed into the ToF tube. Those elec-

in signal is proportional to the energy of the electrons, one can measure the energy distribution
of the emitted electrons. The number of electrons cannot however be predicted with certainty
with this method.
The actual mechanics of a ToF system comes down to a start/stop counter that enables mea-

photodetectors. Practically, it means that the incidence beam is split in two, where one is used
to illuminate the sample, while the other is used to trigger the timer. For optimal precision in
the energy measurements, the optical path length of the two beams should be the same. As
the setup is done in free space, this means that the two beams geometrical paths should be
equidistant. There is however a difference in the two paths of ≈105 cm in the setup used in
this thesis. This might seem like a significant distance, as the ToF tube it self is only 1 m, but
since the THz beam travels much faster than the electrons, the time difference is only a few
ns. The impact of the offset will be larger the higher energetic the electrons are. Most of the
electrons are expected to arrive around a time corresponding to the energy of the work function,
which for undoped graphene can be expected to be on the order of 4-5 eV[28]. The majority of
the electrons are therefore expected to move through the ToF tube on the order of several 100

of tunneling. In order to distinguish between whether the experimental parameters result in
the majority of the carriers undergoing photoemission or field emission, the Keldysh parameter
is commonly used.

2.6
Memristor device
In this section I will briefly describe the general concept of gated field effect transistors and how
graphene is affected by the field effect. Subsequently I will describe the device imagined for this
thesis, and what design parameters I consider most important to be aware about.
The design of floating gate field effect transistors (FGFET) are similar to that of a metal-oxide-
semiconductor FET (MOSFET) or a graphene field effect transistor (GFET). Both consists of
a normal transistor junction, where carriers flow between source and drain. Additionally, a
Page 17 of 64
==PAGE:24==
gate is separated from the channel by a dielectric material, ensuring electrical insulation. In a
MOSFET, a voltage applied to the gate will modify the carrier concentration in the channel,
and modify charge flow between the source and drain[41]. In a FGFET, an additional layer of
conducting material is incorporated between the control gate and the channel, still surrounded
by insulating material. This new gate layer is however not electrically contacted, meaning that
it is floating, and thereby acts as a storage for any charges that makes its way to this layer. The
effect of accumulated charge in the floating gate layer will, as illustrated with the example of a
MOSFET, result in a modulation of the current flowing between the source and drain. Logic
states can thereby provided by the read current in the channel[41], although the device can also
exist in a continuum of states.
One popular use of FGFET is for memeory devices, as the retention of charges change the con-
ductivity/resistance of the device. In other words, it can be used as a memeristor. Memristor
devices are circuit elements that can alter their resistance by the applied electrical stimuli, and
can retain their resistive state dependent on the history of such stimuli[3]. The use of memristors
is especially interesting for flash memory technology, as they retain information without a power
supply[42], while also having a higher density as well as a lower cost compared to traditional
RAM[42]. The capability of simultaneously storing and computing data also make memristors
suitable for biologically inspired neural networks. The control over the resistance via the applied
signal emulate synaptic functions, which makes them very promising candidates in the field of
neuromorphic computing[3][42]. Being able to concurrently store and modulate the transmitted
data, is also seen as a possibility to overcome the Von Neumann bottleneck associated with
present days computational architecture, where energy and speed are compromised by the data
movement between memory and processor[3][42].
When a gate electrode is brought in the vicinity of a graphene sheet, the carrier density of
graphene can be modulated. In a case where the gate and graphene sheet is positioned in a par-
allel plate capacitor configuration, the applied voltage between the gate and the graphene sheet,
will electrostatically dope the graphene sheet, thereby changing its carrier density, n = k2

The design of the device for this thesis is building on the principle described above. The

the floating gate is vertically displaced from the channel, the "control gate" is positioned outside

several advantages over a vertically stacked floating gate:
(i) First off, lateral displacement of the control gate and the floating gate exploit the enhanced
field emission expected from the graphene edges.
(ii) Secondly, the charging and discharging of the floating gate is expected to be controllable by

One intriguing aspect of floating graphene gates, is the discussion of whether a graphene sheet
can be depleted of electrons. Since the floating gate have no electrical connection, if electrons
are emitted due to a large external field, there is no re-resupply. In the case where both the
control gate and the floating gate are not connected, an interesting situation is expected to
occur: When electrons are passed from e.g. the control gate to the floating gate, both graphene
gates will experience a shift in charge neutrality point. If the two sheets are of similar geome-
try, the change will happen symmetrically around the Dirac point, with one sheet having a net
positive charge, and the other having a net negative charge. Assuming it is possible to deplete
a graphene sheet from carriers, it will become harder and harder to extract the charges from a
graphene sheet, since the potential barrier to be field emitted will increase, due to a net opposite
charge in the sheet. Assuming that the carriers stay in the designated graphene sheet, and that
the transfer of charges lead to the expected shift in charge neutrality point, this concept could
be further developed for highly efficient electrical circuit components.

2.6.1
Device parameters
The electrostatic field between the neighbouring graphene sheets due to transport of charge
carriers, could be a critical factor for the retention time of a memory device. How the charges
distribute in graphene will of cause affect this field, but the distance between the two adjacent
graphene sheets will likewise be of great importance. The gap size can also be controlled during
the fabrication process, making it relevant to consider.
Field emission between adjacent graphene sheets is not something novel, and typical experimen-
tal values for the gap size reported in literature are in the ballpark of ≈100 nm[12][10]. This is
a large distance for a tunneling current, and one would expect that the electron emission from
one sheet to another, is practically unaffected by the presence of the other graphene layer. The
reason for the relatively large gap size observed in literature, is likely due to a practical reason,
as a 100 nm gap can be fabricated with large reliability and reproducible. As the electrons
tunnel through the surface barrier, but does not undergo a tunneling directly from one flake to
another, it is difficult to predict if the gap size will affect the field emission induced current. It
could be speculated however, that a larger gap will decrease the probability of emitted electrons
reaching the other flake, as even a small emission angle will make the electrons miss the other
flake.
Assuming the transferred charges are all evenly distributed in the graphene sheet, one can
estimate the static strength of the field between two sheets of graphene after having moved
charges from one graphene sheet to another.

3.2
Characterization tools
Despite being atomically thin, the electronic structure of graphene ensures that it absorbs
around 2.3% of visible light[43]. On its own, the optical opacity is meanwhile insufficient of
detection with an optical microscope. A significant enhancement in optical contrast between
graphene and a SiO2/Si substrate can be achieved by using an appropriate thickness of oxide
layer to achieve optimal interference of the incident light[44]. Because of this, the most com-
mon method of detecting graphene as well as few layer graphite today, is using bright field
optical microscopy (OM). The resolution as well as the information provided by OM is however
restricted to inform about the contrast. Consequently, other characterisation tools are often
needed to verify the flake thickness and quality.
The thickness of the oxide layer has a large impact on the contrast of the graphene, which is
used for a qualitative assessment of the thickness. The original discovery of exfoliated monolayer
graphene by Novoselov and Geim was performed on a SiO2/Si wafer with the SiO2 thickness
Page 24 of 64
==PAGE:31==
being 300 nm. Although this was coincidental, this is still the preferred oxide thickness for high
contrast graphene imaging. 300 nm of SiO2 is additionally favorable for electrical devices as
it provides suitable isolation between the device and the silicon substrate, which is why this
thickness was used in this project.
Using an optical microscope for characterising the number of graphene layers fast, is in the
laboratories used for this thesis, a task that require human skill. Intuition can only come from
being exposed to the pattern needed to be recognised multiple times.
Using an unfamiliar
microscope or being new to the field of 2D materials, therefore makes it difficult to not only
detect single/few layer flakes, but especially to determine the number of layers. In order to
being able to recognise monolayer graphene with a more quantitative certainty in this thesis,
the contrast of different layers of graphene, as well as their characteristics determined by Raman
spectroscopy was investigated. This type of investigation would preferably have been done on
a larger amount of samples, and should optimally be done by an automatic system. For the
sake of making a quick study that allowed faster and more confident evaluation of the exfoliated
graphene flakes for this project, this study was nonetheless helpful. For further information,
please see appendix E.
3.2.1
Raman spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a fast and non-destructive method that is commonly used for character-
ising the number of layers as well as the quality of 2D materials[44][45]. Raman spectroscopy
utilise inelastic scattering of incident photons, which occur due to phonons and molecular vi-
brations in the material. Using a monochromatic laser source, the shift in the incident and
scattered light can be used to determine the structural characteristics of the material.
Raman spectroscopy reveals some prominent peaks of which the position, intensity, and line
shape provide information about the material properties. Three distinct peaks are commonly
used to characterise graphene. The G band is a first order Raman scattering process, and is
a result of in-plane vibrations from the in-plane transverse optical (iTO) and the longitudinal
optical (LO) phonon modes. It is often located around 1582 cm−1 and is more prominent for
graphite, and less for few layer graphene[45][46]. In case of defects being present in the lattice,
the D band is observable at around 1350 cm−1[46]. The D peak origins from a second order
Raman scattering process involving the iTO phonon and a defect in the lattice. The intensity
ratio of the G and D peaks can be used to characterise the number of defects present. The 2D
peak is an overtone of the D peak (therefore called the 2D peak), and arise from a second or
third order scattering process of the iTO phonons. The characteristic Lorentzian line profile
for monolayer graphene becomes broader and less symmetric for multilayer graphene/graphite,
while the peak intensity also drops for more layers. The intensity ratio of the 2D and G peak,
together with the line shape of the 2D peak is therefore commonly used to determine the the
number of graphene layers.
In this thesis Raman spectroscopy was therefore used for quality assessment and identification
of the number of layers for graphene samples.
It is worth being aware however, that the
frequency of the D and 2D bands are dependent on the energy of the incident laser. For the
Raman measurements done in this report, a Thermo Fisher DXR3 Raman Microscope with an
excitation wavelength of 455 nm (2.72 eV) was used, which might produce slightly displaced
peaks when comparing to literature where a 532 nm (2.33 eV) laser is commonly used[46].

3.2.2
AFM
Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) is a well established surface characterisation tool, which is
commonly utilised in material science, due to its relatively high spatial resolution as well as the
compatibility with a large variety of samples[47]. It is thereby ideal to use for samples where
topography information is desired with nanometer precision. The scanning probe technique
uses a thin probe positioned on a cantilever. The interatomic adhesion forces described by the
Lennard-Jones potential, result in a deflection of the cantilever when the tip of the probe is in
proximity of the materials surface. The deflection is monitored by the reflection of a laser on top
of the cantilever, which is captured by a quadruple-photodiode. The differential signal obtained
by the shift in laser position on the balanced photodiodes, is used as feedback to change the
height of the AFM tip[47].
In this project, AFM was to determine critical dimensions of the devices fabricated, especially
the width of the trenches made. As the trenches width was below the diffraction limit, AFM was
also used to identify the homogeneity of the trenches, as well as to characterise if the desired
etch depth was reached. As mono-atomic thickness of layers can be resolved with AFM[47],
AFM was furthermore used in this project to characterise the height of few layer graphene as
well as hBN flakes.
The AFM measurements were performed on a AFM Icon-PT, using Peak force tapping mode,
and using a Tap150A tip[48].

3.3.2
Stacking
In order to fabricate heterostructures such as encapsulated graphene, a setup based on temper-
ature controlled adhesion was used. The general approach relies on a transfer method with a
transparent stamp that has a larger adhesion to the flakes than the substrate used for exfolia-
tion. Using this stamp, the stack of flakes can be assembled on the stamp, by picking up each
flake in succession. In this way, only the top crystal is in direct contact with the polymer, which
reduces bubbles and other interlayer impurities.
The stamp is constructed in accordance with the procedure in Ref. [51]. A schematic of the
stacking stamp can be seen in figure 12. For further information on the stamp creation, see
appendix D. It consist of a glass slide, polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), poly carbonate (PC), and
scotch tape. The PC ensures a large adhesion, while the PDMS underneath is used for its high
elasticity, and thereby allow the PC to conform well to the flake on the substrate. The PC is
applied to glass slides from a solution of 7% PC dissolved in Chloroform.
The stacking stage is comprised of two mechanical stages, both of which have individual control
of the lateral and vertical position, as well as rotation. These are additionally placed on top of
a vibrational-damping stage. One of the translation stages is used to steady the stamp, which
is held in position by a mechanical clamp with screws. The other stage is used for placing the
sample, which maintains its position due to vacuum suction from underneath. The sample stage
is positioned under an optical microscope for the purpose of locating the sample and for visual
monitoring of the stacking procedure.
The transparency of the stamp is a critical point, as this allow for visual overview of the
process during the entire procedure, which is a necessity for the alignment procedure as well as
visualising when the stamp and flake is in contact. The position of the stamp as well as the
substrate is controlled by micro-manipulators.

have low adhesion as this eases the transfer process. At room temperature, the adhesion be-
tween graphene or few layer graphene and the substrate, which for most purposes are SiO2/Si,
is comparable to that between graphene and PC. As for most polymers, the closer one ap-
proaches the glass transition temperature, the less stiff the polymer becomes, and it thereby
starts to deform. At elevated temperature, the adhesion between graphene and PC therefore
exceeds that of graphene and SiO2. For most graphene transfers, the temperature of the stage
is therefore elevated to ≈80 ◦C when picking up few layered graphene flakes, or a bit higher,
i.e. ≈90 −100 ◦C for single-layer graphene. Since hBN has a slightly lower adhesion to the
substrate, and is also generally thicker than the single- to few layered graphene, the tempera-
tures needed for transferring hBN are rarely as high. Temperatures in the range of 60-90 ◦C
were used for transferring hBN flakes, depending on the thickness of each individual flake.

graphene can be seen in figure 13

Due to surface roughness and misalignment between the stamp and the sample, not all of the
stamp will reach the point of contact with the sample concurrently. I found that the position
of the contact point relative to the flake of interest was critical for the success of a transferring
process. If the contact point is directly on top of the flake of interest, the chance of breaking the
flake is increased, since the flake will experience an inhomogeneously distributed force across the
Page 29 of 64
==PAGE:36==
surface, as it tries to adhere to both the stamp and the substrate. Additionally, the stamp will
have an increased adhesion length in the outer most point, due to the elasticity of the materials
used for the stamp. Most flakes approached by this method was therefore fractured, and was
very difficult to get off the substrate at all. If the point of contact is too far from the flake of
interest however the force needed to ensure that the stamp was covering the flake sufficiently,
i.e. ≈300 µm past the flake, also increases the chance of destroying the flake. My experience
was furthermore that it also made it more challenging to pick up the flake of interested, and
additionally unnecessary flakes in the vicinity was often picked up. The optimal pick-up proce-
dure found was therefore somewhere in between these two extremes: Ensuring that the contact
point is far enough from the flake, that the covering of the flake can be controlled easily by the
micro-manipulators. This distance used was typically < 0.5 mm. This value can be ensured
by lowering the stamp in close proximity to the substrate surface, in an area away from the
flake of interest, to ensure no interaction with the flake of interest. As the stamp approaches
the surface, interference wrinkles from the polymer start to appear in the region of the contact
point. With the position of the most protruding part of the stamp known, the sample stage can
be manipulated to position the flake in a safe distance from this point.
When withdrawing the stamp, the speed should be as constant as possible, and was in this
project done at < 0.5 µm
s , as this, besides maintaining the entirety of the flake, allow for revers-
ing the process if the flake does not adhere to the stamp. In case the flake does not adhere to the
stamp, the temperature can be increased, and the withdrawing of the stamp can be attempted
again.
For ease of application, the final stack is placed on a substrate with already defined metal
electrode pads, see appendix F The deposition of the stack on the chip was done by elevating
the temperature to 190 ◦C to make the PC melt. To remove the PC covering the assembled
stack, the chip was submerged in chloroform (CHCl3) for 2 minutes, followed by a brief dip in
isopropanol (IPA), and a careful blow dry by nitrogen. The blow dry can be powerful enough
to rip off the stack from the substrate, so this is a step where the process needs to be handled
with extra care.
3.4
Lithography
Nanofabrication processes can often be (crudely) separated into two categories: 1) Defining
lateral (x,y) patterns on the surface, 2) transferring a vertical structure (z) onto the defined
pattern.
The first of these steps is referred to as lithography, where the second, known as
pattern transfer, can be a number of processes, for instance etching or deposition.
In this section, I will briefly introduce the lithographic tools used for this project.
3.4.1
Electron beam lithography
Although several lithographic tools exists, Electron Beam Lithography (EBL) is arguably the
most useful for high resolution patterns, as this maskless writing tool have resolution about an
order of magnitude smaller than photolithographic tools[52].
A beam of electrons is used to directly write on a thin electron-sensitive resist, meaning that no
mask is needed to draw the pattern. The interaction between the electrons and the resist, breaks
chemical bonds in the polymer, altering the resist’ solubility. The patterning is performed in
single-point exposures in a raster scan, and since the beam has a Gaussian profile in the lateral
directions, the exposed resist therefore do not experience a homogeneous dose. Careful optimi-
sation is therefore needed to avoid over- or under-exposure of any regions. The resist used for
EBL is often an organic compound like poly(methyl methacrylate) PMMA (C5H8O2), and this
Page 30 of 64
==PAGE:37==
was also used for EBL processes in this project. PMMA is a very well established non-toxic
resist which has relatively short molecular chains making it ideal for high resolution patterns.
The high resolution in EBL is primarily contributed to the small de Broglie wavelength of the
electrons, which for non-relativistic energies is given as;
λ =

2mE
Since typical electron energies are on the order of 30 keV to 100 keV[52], this corresponds to
approximately 4-7 pm (0.004-0.007 nm). In practice, the beam diameter is increased by orders
of magnitude, due to aberrations and scattering mechanisms.
In this thesis an acceleration voltage of 120 keV was used to achieve a small beam cross section
to define the trenches. Such a large acceleration voltage increases the writing time, which is
not a concern for this project as the patters made are relatively small, and only a few samples
were processed. If a design similar to the one used in this thesis should one day be realised for
commercial applications however, the writing time is of great importance. For the geometries
less sensitive to high resolution, such as the general device shape, faster lithography methods
could therefore be used, for instance photo-lithography.
3.4.2
Thermal scanning probe lithography
Thermal scanning probe lithography (t-SPL) is a nanolithographic method that uses a sharp
heated tip to create surface modifications in the resist when being scanned over the sample.
Like EBL it is a mask-less technique, but unlike EBL, one does not have to deal with computa-
tional intensive proximity effect correction that arise due to electron scattering mechanisms. An
intriguing feature of the t-SPL system is that it allow for 3D control of the patterning, with sub
1 nm resolution along the vertical axis[53]. This feature can be exploited for many things such
as inducing localised strain, which can be a key ingredient in nanoelectronics, nanophotonics,
and material science. The compact system furthermore does not require more space than a lab
workbench, and can function under ambient conditions, making it ideal for rapid prototyping
at the nanoscale[54][55]. The system however also function in other in environments, making it
possible to work with materials sensitive to ambient air.
t-SPL working principle is based upon removing resist by pressing a heated silicon tip into
the polymer. This results in local removal of a part of the polymer, leaving behind a tip-shaped
cavity in the resist[55]. The resolution of the pattern is therefore limited by the shape of the tip
which, due to fabrication, can be expected to show some variance in geometry[54]. The pattern
is however highly controllable by other means, in that a mathematical design of the pattern
can be loaded directly into the software. The pattern drawn into the resist, is then dictated by
the set temperature as well as the actuation force between the cantilever and the sample. The
cantilever is ohmicly heated, and the temperature of the tip itself is therefore not equivalent
to the set temperature due to cooling. A bias is also set between the probe and the sample
stage to control the actuation. As the tip is driven into the resist, the tip is cooled down much
faster than when simply hovering in the air. The actuation of the tip is thereby measured by
the change in temperature relative to when the tip is in air (how much the current needs to be
changed to maintain the same temperature). This allow for the t-SPL system to concurrently
pattern the sample and measure the topography, meaning it can both write and read. The
scanning probe microscopy (SPM) feature can be used independent of the patterning, and is
commonly used prior to the lithographic step for optimal aligning[54].
Page 31 of 64
==PAGE:38==
During the patterning, a part of the removed volume of resist is sublimated, but it also oc-
cur that the tip either mechanically moves part of the resist, or that the tip is contaminated
due to interaction with the resist. This tip contamination is one of the greatest limitations of the
t-SPL, as the change in tip shape and size will affect the process from the moment the deposition
of resist occur[54]. The choice of resist can heavily determine the severity and the rate of tip
contamination. Although both poly-phthalaldehyde (PPA) and poly-methyl methacrylate-co-
methacrylic acid (PMMA/MA) are used as resists for t-SPL, PPA is typically preferred due to
its reliability to decompose at elevated temperatures[53][54]. It can meanwhile be troublesome
to spin coat PPA in an even layer onto samples containing 2D materials. To increase adhesion,
one can either spin coat the sample with PMMA/MA prior to PPA spin coating, or simply use
PMMA/MA on its own as it is often a more stable polymer. The stability however also results
in a more difficult decomposition process during the lithography step, which can lead to more
sever tip contamination than if using PPA[54].
The resist used throughout this thesis was PPA dissolved in anisole in a 5% wt. ratio. The
PPA polymer solution was stored in a refridgerator at 4 ◦C. Prior to spin coating the PPA
was left in room temperature for 15 min, in order to avoid water condensing and contaminating
the solution when opening the lid[56]. The spin coating was performed for 40 seconds at 2000
rpm with an acceleration of 1000 rpm/s2 and baked for 2 minutes at 110 ◦C on a hot plate to
remove residual solvent. For better conformation of the resist to the surface, it was found that
30 seconds of O2 plasma etch prior to the spin coating was beneficial. The parameters used for
this pre-coating etch, was: flow rate: 10 SCCM. Power: 15 W. This resulted in a pressure of
around 23 µbar, with a DC bias of 150 V. As PPA decompose above 120 ◦C[56], the resist was
removed by placing the sample on a 250 ◦C hot plate for about 30 seconds. The amount of
contamination quantified by Raman spectroscopy was found to be very small, see appendix J
Together with another student, Philip Holm, it was found that annealing samples before coating
the wafer with resist, yielded successful and homogeneous spin coated layer of resist for a variety
of samples, including: exfoliated graphene and hBN, and transferred graphene and hBN. The
annealing recipe used was: Annealing in a 3% H2/Ar atmosphere, with a base pressure of 3.9
mTorr, by a 30 minutes ramp up to 380 ◦C, and then kept at 380 ◦C for 3 hours, with 200
SCCM resulting in a process pressure of 55 Torr.
Annealing has the advantage over O2 plasma ashing that it does not etch the sample nor the
substrate.
The t-SPL tool used during this project was the commercial NanoFrazor developed by Heidel-
berg Instruments.
3.5
Pattern transfer
Using either lithography tool is a mean of defining a pattern in the resist. In order to transfer
this pattern into the sample, reactive ion etching (RIE) was utilised. Graphene as a carbon
allotrope, is readily etched using O2, while hBN does not respond significantly to O2 plasma[57].
hBN can therefore be used as a mask for graphene, which is practical when working with
encapsulated graphene stacks. hBN can meanwhile be etched using fluorine gasses such as SF6,
which conveniently does not edge graphene. Fluorine can meanwhile functionalise graphene
edges, which is known to alter properties such as the work function[17].
A short O2 etch
was therefore performed at the end of each etching session, to remove any remaining fluorine
molecules from the graphene edge. hBN has furthermore been shown to have a vertical etch
angle of ≈66◦, which is independent on the crystal orientation[57]. This remarkable property
of SF6 etch on hBN makes it possible to use etched hBN as a mask for increased resolution of
the underlying layer.

3.7.2
THz measurements
Two different setups were used for conducting THz induced field emission measurements, both
of them relying on tabletop Ti:Sapphire laser amplifiers, but with different THz generation
and detection mechanisms. The one setup used for measuring the memristive device used a
two-color air plasma generation scheme and a ABCD detection scheme. This setup is further
elaborated in 3.7.4. The other setup utilised optical rectification from a LiNO3 crystal for gen-
eration of the THz spectra of ≈0.1-1.2 THz. EOS is used for measuring the THz beam, and
a vacuum chamber with a ToF tube is used to detect emitted electrons from the samples. The
THz generation takes place over a some distance inside the LiNO3 crystal, but the crystal also
absorbs THz frequencies. For this reason, the pump beam is close to the corner of the LiNbO3
crystal. In order to improve the output-power, a layer of Kapton tape, which has a refractive
index between air and LiNbO3[35], was applied to the output facet.
This setup was used for measuring field emission from simple graphene samples not fabricated
into devices.
All measurements using THz illumination was done in transmission mode.
This imposed a
requirement on the substrate to be transparent to the relevant frequencies. SiO2/Si is the most
common substrate for 2D materials, which is convenient for electronic devices as Silicon can be
used as a global backgate for the device. Too many carriers in the substrate will however make
the substrate opaque to THz radiation. For the measurements done with THz illumination,
high resistivity silicon (HR-Si) were therefore used as substrate, as it has an almost constant
transmission coefficient of T ≈0.7 through most of the THz frequencies[58].
The CVD grown graphene samples were cleaned by: Submerging the samples in acetone at 60
◦C for 1 hour, followed by 10 minutes submersion in IPA and a gently blow dry by N2. The
samples were then annealed in a 3% H2/Ar atmosphere, by a 30 minutes ramp up to 350 ◦C,
and then kept at 350 ◦C for 3 hours.
3.7.3
Micro-mechanically exfoliated graphene probed by THz
The hypothesis of producing a FGFET with the design in this thesis relies on being able to in-
duce field emission from exfoliated graphene with a THz beam. A natural milestone is therefore
to characterise, or at least verify that this is possible. In order to verify that the field emission
origins from a graphene flake, it is beneficial to isolate such a flake, so signal from other flakes
on the substrate surface is not mistaken for signal from the graphene flake of interest. The THz
beam size is meanwhile ≈500 µm in diameter, so any other electrically conducting substance
within a distance of 500 µm of the graphene flake, will possibly emit electrons when illuminated
by the THz beam. This creates a challenge, as the exfoliation process is not exactly determin-

its vicinity, and the incident THz beam will thereby cover more than the flake of interest, as
shown in figure 15a. Several solutions could be used to isolate a single flake such as:
- transferring the flake to another substrate, illustrated in figure 15b.
- covering the surface with a polymer/resist, and only removing the polymer in the flake region,

3.7.4
Two color air plasma terahertz
Figure 17 shows a schematic of the air based THz-TDS setup. The incident beam originates
from the Ti:Sapphire amplified fs laser, which has a central wavelength of 800 nm, a pulse
width of 40 fs, and a repetition rate of 1 kHz. It is directed through a beam-splitter, where a
few percent of the beam is guided towards the delay stage and is used for probing the beam
Page 36 of 64
==PAGE:43==
profile later on. The majority of the fs laser pulse is guided through a β-barium-borate (BBO)
crystal which facilitates the frequency doubling. Due to the birefringence in the BBO crystal,
the generated second harmonic (400 nm) will have its polarisation rotated with respect to the
800 nm photons. The dual wave plate (DWP) after the BBO crystal ensures that both beams
have horizontal polarisation[36]. The lens before the BBO crystal (L1) focuses both colors of
light onto a nitrogen-purged atmosphere (to reduce the water content, as water absorbs THz in
certain frequencies[38]), where the gas is being ionised by the optical pulses, thereby holding a
large number of free electrons. Due to the asymmetric electric field provided by the fundamental
and second harmonic (SH), the free carriers oscillate, leading to a photocurrent which radiates
a broadband THz transient in the range ≈0.5-15 THz, with a center frequency at about 4 THz.
Due to the large bandwidth, off-axis parabolic mirrors (OPM) are used to collimate the THz
beam. The insertion of a high resistivity (HR) silicon wafer acts as a low pass filter, blocking
both the fundamental and SH of the pump beam while transmitting the THz beam. The HR-Si
does attenuate the THz field by about 50%[38], but this enables a simple way of reducing the
THz power, by simply adding more HR-Si wafers.
The focal point of the THz beam is used as the sample plane, where a mechanical stage with
three axis controls is used for loading the sample. The beam is afterwards steered and focused
onto a spot between two high voltage (HV) electrodes, where the field between the electrodes
let the nitrogen-purged air act as a non-linear medium for the THz pulse, which will create an
electric field induced second harmonic (EFISH). The optical pulse is meanwhile also focused
between the two HV electrodes, through a small hole in the center of the fourth OPM. This
does not affect the THz beam, which is toroidal at this mirror. The mixing of the optical pulse
with the THz pulse result in a third-order effect, where the intensity of the EFISH is modulated
by the THz beam, and probed by the optical pulse[36]. Since the intensity of the EFISH will be
proportional to the strength of the transient THz field (and the bias of the HV electrodes)[37],
the signal can be recorded by an avalanche photo diode (APD) after the THz and fundamental
frequency has been filtered by a band pass filter (BPF). An avalanche photo-diode is physically
smaller then a PMT, making it less susceptible to noise from other optical signals in the labo-
ratory.
The spatial profile of the THz beam can be probed by blocking a part of the beam with a
knife-edge, stationed on a motorised linear stage.
By collecting the full wave form, before
translating the knife-edge to block more/less of the beam, the THz power can be mapped as a
function of the knife position, which yield information the spatial distribution of the frequencies
in the THz beam.

4.1.1
Sub-discussion
Doing THz transmission measurements means that the propagation direction of the THz pulse
is perpendicular to the sample, and thereby that the polarisation is in-plane with the sample. It
is assumed that the electric charges respond to the electric field, meaning that if the polarisation
is in-plane, then the electrons will be accelerated in-plane. This would explain why the basal
plane does not emit any electrons. The edge of graphene is meanwhile not covered, meaning
that emission is expected from these. The size of the aperture into the ToF tube is on the order
of mm’s, and are additionally some mm’s away from the sample surface. Furthermore, the ToF
tube is around 1 m long. Electrons emitted from the edge of the graphene flake is therefore not
expected to have an emission angle that would allow them to hit the PMT at the end of the
ToF tube. That no electrons were detected is therefore not immediately surprising. A follow
up on this result is however included in section 5.

Sub-discussion
Remember that the samples presented in this section were measured using the same setup as

Comparing to the results of the exfoliated flake, the results presented in this section is signifi-
cantly different. The edge of the CVD was not exposed, meaning that the emission is expected
to origin from the basal plane. This first of all challenge the view that field emission from the
basal plane of graphene is not expected to occur[11]. The results furthermore suggest that the
theoretical model and preconceived picture of field emission from graphene is incomplete. Pro-
posed reasons for measuring emitted electrons perpendicular to the graphene plane are further
discussed in section 5. In the following, I will dive into interpreting the results obtained in this
section.
Can graphene be depleted for electrons?
The obvious observation made from figure 20 is that that the electron emission for the un-
grounded graphene decays as more measurements are performed. This is in contrast to the
instance where the graphene is grounded, where the magnitude of the electron emission is a
factor of 20 larger, and additionally is constant over consecutive measurements. This seems to
suggest that the graphene is depleted for carriers when irradiated with a sufficiently strong THz
beam. Notice that this effect is not limited to a local depletion, as the center of the beam is
translated 1 mm laterally between measurements. The effect can also be observed in figure 21.
This is important for device fabrication, as some devices might need large electron emission,
which according to the data presented in figure 20 and 21 is best achieved if the graphene has
a charge supply. If ungrounded graphene meanwhile can be exhausted for electrons to some
extent, it is of great interest to determine and control the time scale of which the charge neu-
trality point maintain shifted. This feature could lead to graphene based memory devices.
Does the results of the simulation fit the observed behaviour?
Based on the model presented in section 2.7, the theoretically expected amount of electrons
emitted at a field strength of 15

What is the field enhancement factor from the basal plane of CVD graphene?
The field enhancement factor of the grounded CVD graphene from Graphenea found by equation

be expected to have a field enhancement factor of 1. A field enhancement factor on the order of

with a coefficient of determination of R2 = 0.96. However, the data also seems to be converging

and surface chemistry, it would be surprising if the actual field enhancement is changed as a
function of the incident field strength.
What this result is more likely to indicate, is that

4.4.3
Discussion
Why do the trenches display different depth in the AFM measurements?
The trenches fabricated using EBL do not all show the same depth from the AFM measure-
ments. This is meanwhile believed to be a tip limitation from the AFM rather than an actual
physical lack of depth in the stack, which is supported by the limited AFM data points inside
the trenches. As can be seen from the measurements in figure 27, the more narrow the trench,
the less deep they appear. The AFM tip diameter is on the order of 6-10 nm[48], which coincides
very well with the most narrow part of the trenches.
It is meanwhile important to be aware, that due to the different aspect ratios of the trenches,
different etch rates are expected. The difference in depth observed in the trenches from the
AFM measurements therefore might actually be the physical appearance, and not merely an
artefact as a result of the imaging technique. Using a high aspect ratio tip, this question could
possibly be resolved.
What is the reason for the spikes in topography measurements
From the line profile of the device shown in figure 27d spikes in the topography measurements
can be observed. These are also visible in the AFM image in figure 27b. OM records of the
devices show that the patterns first show up after stacking. The features are believed to be
folds in the stack, which has most likely occurred during staking, due to a difference in thermal
expansion rate of the different materials[59]. Since the wrinkles were not observed until after
the stacking procedure was completed, it is not possible to determine which layer produces these
folds with certainty. A qualified guess is meanwhile that they appear in the top hBN layer. The
Page 47 of 64
==PAGE:54==
reasoning behind this is, that as the stack is heated up, so is the polymer on the stamp, which
makes the polymer wrinkle. As it conforms to the 2D flake, that flake also starts to wrinkle.
The highest temperature used is at the last step, where the top hBN flake is in contact with the
polymer, meaning that this could be where the wrinkling happened. It is puzzling meanwhile
that these folds occur in all of the stacks prepared in this batch, as they were not observed for
any other devices made during this thesis.
How can the current variation as a function of probe position be understood?
Turning the attention to the field emission experiments, a clear change in current is observed
when the probe is moved in the ± y-direction, (see figure 14). This could be contributed to
several possible factors:
- The probe moves laterally away from the exposed graphene edge, and the top hBN layer is
blocking electron emission. This would verify the expectations, that strong field emission is
expected from the graphene edge, but that hBN block the field emission due to too wide of a
tunneling barrier.
In case the sample is not perfectly aligned in the y-plane, moving the probe along the y-axis
will also move the probe closer/further from the sample surface. This could lead to several
situations:
• The tunneling current is exponentially dependent of the distance between the probe and
the conducting layer. Moving the probe closer/further from the surface is therefore ex-
pected to vary the tunneling current.
• The field strength is linearly dependent on the anode-cathode distance, and changing this
distance would therefore change the field emission.
• The probe could have crashed into the sample surface. Since the top hBN layer is ≈14
nm thick, this means that the probe could be contacting the graphene.
It is not possible to say which of these three suggested scenarios have occurred during the
measurements. A visual change in the tip can normally be observed if it contacts any sample,
as it will bend, break, or otherwise deform. This was not observed during the experiments,
suggesting that tip/hBN contact was less likely, although it is still possible that the interaction
was not observable.
Another observation made is that when the probe is positioned on top of the expected slit
position, the current is not constant but appear to be accumulating over time. This could be
an effect of electron emission assisted edge-cleaning. As the electrons are emitted, this could
remove contaminants and functionalised molecules on the graphene edge. Several graphene field
emission studies have reported similar findings[9][13], where the current becomes more stable
after several voltammetric cycles. To investigate this hypothesis and possibly get a better in-
sight in the intrinsic field emission from a graphene edge, measurements with constant probe
position over a prolonged period of time could be conducted.
The field emission measurements done in this section were only performed on a single slit on a
single device, although at different positions along that slit. The data and the discussion here,
is therefore not guaranteed to be representative, and a larger amount of data sampling is needed
before any conclusions can be drawn. Based on the measurements performed using the local
tungsten tip for probing the field emission, it is not possible to confirm nor deny whether the
measured current origin from the isolated graphene edge.

4.6
Thermal scanning probe lithography
A big limitation of t-SPL is that its patterning properties are not necessarily deterministic, and
depend on both the tip condition as well as the sample. For optimal patterning, i.e. a high
aspect ratio pattern in the resist, the settings of the t-SPL potentially needs to be adjusted
according to the thermal properties of the sample in question. In order to understand the effect
of patterning on different samples, this project conducted a structured study, guided by the
following hypotheses:
• A material with better thermal transport properties, i.e. thermal conductance and heat
capacity, will cool down the tip faster. Since cooling of the tip results in a smaller pattern-
ing depth into the resist, different materials with same geometry, as well as materials with
similar properties but different geometry, are expected to result in different patterning
depths.
• The polarisability of the sample might affect the patterning, since a more polarisable
material will respond more to the potential applied between the tip and the substrate,
and the electric field will thereby be screened to a greater extend for a more polarisable
sample.
In order to investigate these hypotheses, the two phenomena have to be decoupled. Since the
patterning is a convolution between the actuation and the heating however, simply changing
parameters will not be sufficient.
Philip Holm and I therefore decided to investigate if the
patterning depth changed as a function of thickness of the sample, when keeping some set pa-
rameters constant. Since this study was conducted in close collaboration with another master
student, Philip Holm, ideas were frequently shared between us, and the exact origin of each
thought is therefore difficult to account for. The general knowledge of how the operational
principles of t-SPL and the idea that there might be a dependency on the thermal heat ca-
pacity of the material meanwhile stems from Philip. Philip also did the patterning with the
t-SPL system. I had the idea that the pattern might be understood from the electrostatic force
between the sample and the tip and a possible polarisability dependency. I did the sample
preparation (exfoliation, stacking, cleaning, optical characterisation, and spin coating) and the
AFM measurements.

good tool for detecting hBN flakes, even when the height is on the few nm scale. This is rather
impressive considering that about 100 nm of resist is present between the probe and the sample.

Discussion
Despite having already reflected upon the results presented in the previous section, certain
topics can further be discussed, especially in terms of the implications of the results.
This
section therefore attempts to address the analysis of the results a bit deeper as well as present
an outlook on possible changes and optimisations for the devices made. The section ends with a
discussion of experiments that were not performed during this project, but which could advance
the understanding of topics studied in this thesis.
5.1
THz induced field emission of graphene
The attempt on achieving a measurable electron emission signal from the exfoliated graphene
flake in figure 16 was unsuccessful, while a clear electron emission was detected for the CVD
grown samples using the same setup. The reasons for the lack of emission could have multiple
origins. One explanation could be that the exfoliated flake is too small to emit a measurable
signal. Considering that the flake has an area of ≈100x100 µm, and assuming graphene to have
a charge density of 1012 cm−2, the flake is expected to carry about 108 electrons. That the flake
should not have enough electrons available to emit a measurable signal is therefore not expected
to be the cause. Another explanation could be that the electrons are emitted prior to the data
acquisition. Irradiation from the THz beam prior to the collection of data, could have resulted
in the flake emitting electrons, thereby shifting the Fermi level to an amount that resulted in a
saturated state, where a higher field strength was needed to extract any more electrons. This
hypothesis is supported by the electron emission count as function of position and time from the
grounded/ungrounded CVD graphene in figure 20. Had electrodes been contacted to the flake,
this hypothesis could have been investigated by measuring the charge neutrality point of the
flake before, immediately after, and some time after exposure to the THz beam. Notice however
that the electrodes would have changed the total amount of carriers available, even if they were
not grounded. Moreover, since the main motivation for this experiment was to investigate the
field emission of a high quality graphene flake that had not been compromised in quality by for
instance coating with a polymer that would have left residues, covering the electrodes with a
polymer would also have altered the experiment. This would therefore not have been an optimal
way of investigating the effect of doping pristine graphene by THz irradiation.
The data displayed of the cleaned CVD graphene in figure 19, shows that field emission can be
re-gained after cleaning. The emission regained is meanwhile not to the extend of a freshly made
sample that has not been involved in device fabrication processes, as shown in figure 22. This
could suggest that contamination from ambient storage, and/or from the fabrication process
suppress the field emission to an extend that cannot be recovered by the cleaning process inves-
tigated. The quality of the samples could meanwhile also be different, and it would especially be
interesting to investigate if the a difference in crystallinity between the two samples is observable.
The energy band found from the ToF measurements of CVD graphene is lower than the es-
timated work function.
This suggests that the field emission do not follow the general FN
equation assuming that the field enhancement factor is β = 1. As the CVD grown graphene
was not single crystalline, grain boundaries or similar defects present in the sample can possibly
have affected the field emission, effectively changing the field enhancement. Such considerations
that could affect the field enhancement factor could likewise be used as possible explanations
for the observed emission perpendicular to the polarisation direction of the THz beam. While
we do not fully understand the reason for the observed electron emission from the basal plane,
nor find any concluding reasons in literature, I include some of my possible explanations:
• It is possible that the electrons emitted are not electrons tunneling through the surface
Page 56 of 64
==PAGE:63==
barrier, but are actually thermionic emission of electrons. The electric field could poten-
tially lower the barrier height, while accelerating the electrons to higher energy states.
This could allow the electrons to obtain enough energy to overcome the barrier height.
This suggestion does not explain the directional change of the electrons however.
• Misalignment in the setup would result in the ˆz component of the polarisation being non-
zero. Since the THz beam is focused onto the sample, the field is squeezed, thereby also
creating a non-zero ˆz component of the polarisation. This is meanwhile an effect that
would be expected to affect both the exfoliated and CVD grown graphene.
• Any impurities in the graphene will act as scattering sites, and these events might not
conserve the in-plane momentum, which would allow for out-of plane-emission. These
scattering sites could for instance arise from impurities, protrusions, or domain boundaries.
The crystallinity of the sample could therefore have an implication on the field emission.

5.3
Fabrication methodology
t-SPL has prospect to be utilised in device fabrication when in need of super high resolution.
Indeterministic patterning however makes it less inviting to use when the goal is to realise de-
vices where the absolute resolution of the pattern is of less concern. The inconsistencies mainly
originates from tip dependent patterning[53][54], which is hard to mitigate. They are further-
more difficult to anticipate, as changes to the tip can occur during patterning.
The comparison of the smallest possible slit size produced by EBL and using single-pixel patterns
with t-SPL, shows that a more narrow slit can consistently be produced using t-SPL. The
comparison does not however account for the many effects that other parts of the process could
have on the FWHM. The resist, the development, and the pattern transfer, is expected to affect
Page 58 of 64
==PAGE:65==
the final resolution of the pattern, and these procedures were not the same for the two samples
compared. Due to vertical etch anisotropy of hBN[57], the FWHM is expected to decrease
with increased depth during the etching process, unless the sample is over-etched. As the two
pattern types compared were on different flakes of different height, with different final depth,
the FWHM is expected to differ, even if the process of producing these patters had been exactly
the same. The comparison could be performed in another way that would reduce variables that
are not directly related to the lithography step. Patterning on the same flake using the two
different lithography tools would mean that the subsequent etching is expected to affect the two
different types of patterns similarly. A single resist should therefore be used for both lithography
processes, and PMMA/MA should therefore be utilised.
5.4
Contaminants and device outlook
How do the electrons distribute inside the graphene in the presence of an external
electric field?
The current between the tungsten tip and the encapsulated graphene sample, shown in figure
28, in the time interval [109 min : 112 min] could be interpreted as an accumulation of electrons
at the edge of graphene as a function of the external electric field. The distribution of electrons
inside a graphene sheet in the presence of an external electric field or in the situation where
the Fermi level has been shifted, is not known for certainty. If the carriers do accumulate at
the edge, the effect will be different dependent on the method of applying the external voltage.
If the external field is a DC bias, with the anode close to the edge of the graphene, the accu-
mulated charges can be expected to screen the field inside the graphene sheet, and the barrier
in the vicinity of the edge will increase. If the graphene sheet is meanwhile exposed by a THz
pulse, which will travels orthogonal to the basal plane but posses a polarisation parallel to the
basal plane, the field will act more homogeneously on the charge carriers. If illuminated by a
THz beam, the edge barrier is therefore not expected to change drastically whether the charges
accumulate at the edges, or distribute themselves evenly over the entire layer.
How does edge contamination affect field emission, and what can be done about
it?
The free electrons in graphene are existing in the π-orbitals. That also mean that their spacial
distribution is following the geometry of this out-of-plane 2pz orbital. How the emission depends
on these local states is not simple to predict, but P. Poncharal et al. [34] has shown that the
emission pattern can follow the spatial distribution of these orbitals if the first principle emitters
are removed through a cleaning process. This is an important result, as this suggests that many
emission sites are not utilised if lack of awareness and/or actions are not taken to eliminate
dominating emission sites. The elimination of unwanted emission sites from contaminants can
be temporarily resolved through a thorough cleaning procedure. This could for instance be
chemical cleaning, but as discussed here, it could possibly also be by field emission. The limi-
tation of this is that after the cleaning step, the device will still be exposed to an atmosphere
that will build up contaminants through time.
In order to implement field emitting graphene devices into integrated circuits, a method of
maintaining clean edges needs to be deployed. The devices fabricated in this thesis has the
limitation that the edge is exposed because the graphene is encapsulated prior to the pattern
transfer. If the pattern was transferred to the graphene before it was encapsulated, this would
create a concealed layer of patterned graphene, where the graphene slit would not be directly
exposed to the ambient atmosphere. A conceptualisation of such a device is illustrated in figure
35. The geometry suggested is meanwhile not easy to fabricate, and it would require a few more
steps than the method used for the devices made in this thesis. In order to realise an idea like
the one in figure 35, the graphene must be placed on an underlying substrate such as hBN prior
Page 59 of 64
==PAGE:66==
to the pattern transfer. Otherwise mechanical transfer of the separated graphene sheets would
be nearly impossible to do in practice, as a small shift in one of the sheets position might short
circuit the device. Additionally, the top as well as the bottom hBN flake must be larger than
the graphene sheet, as airborne molecules could otherwise enter the slit from the side openings.
Another important and unknown part of this process, is the discussion of whether the top hBN
layer is rigid enough to span the slit gap without collapsing and thereby blocking the slit. It
is anticipated that two primary parameters will dictate this condition: 1) The thickness if the
top hBN layer, and 2) the width of the slit. The thicker the hBN layer, the more rigid it will
be, which will be beneficial for this purpose. The wider the graphene slit is, the more likely the
hBN layer is to fill out the gap. It is therefore of interest to make as narrow slits as possible
in the graphene. It is unknown how the field emission behaves as a function of the distance
between the adjacent graphene sheets, but one can speculate that a smaller gap size will result
in a similar or lower threshold field strength for electrons to be field emitted and travel to the
adjacent graphene sheet.
Figure 35: Conceptual idea of encapsulating a graphene slit within hBN to seal it from ambient conditions.
For realisation of patterning such a device, I suggest to perform the lithography step using
t-SPL over other lithography tools. The results presented in section 4.5 suggests that high
resolution slits can be made using single-pixel lines. These results are obtained from hBN flakes
with thicknesses of ≈20 nm. Etching directly in mono/few-layer graphene meanwhile has the
downside that one cannot exploit the masking properties of hBN. This first of all means that
the smallest possible slit size is dictated by the lithography and etching process, and cannot
be enhanced by using hBN as a narrowing mask.
Secondly, it means that annealing must
be done prior to spin coating PPA on, instead of doing a O2 plasma etch. This might also
prove useful, as this step might clean away unwanted contaminants accumulated during the
transfer process. Although it was found that PMMA residues can be effectively cleaned away
well, see appendix J, experience shows that PPA decomposes more consistently than PMMA.
Consequently, using PPA for the lithography step instead of PMMA or PMMA/MA is expected
to contaminate the graphene less. Furthermore, the process of writing an arbitrary line in a
graphene sheet is generally faster using t-SPL than using EBL. This is beneficial as stacking,
annealing, spin-coating, patterning, etching, and the subsequent encapsulation can be done in
rapid succession. Especially because all of the equipment needed for this is available in the
Nanomade laboratories.
For patterning small features directly on graphene, it is suggested to investigate the control of
the feature size as a function of resist thickness, as it has been reported that the very small
patterns can be achieved using a thin layer of resist[55].

5.5
Unfinished experiments
Several planned experiments were not accomplished during this project. This was not due to a
lack of relevance, but rather to practical matters such as equipment unavailability, uncontrollable
processes etc. The experiences gained during this project furthermore brought my awareness
to additional topics that would be helpful to explore, in the journey to achieve an operational
THz induced graphene based FGFET. In this section I delve into some considerations regarding
these experiments. It hopefully provide a comprehensive reference that can serve as a source of
inspiration for anyone looking to build upon the concepts explored in this thesis.
5.5.1
THz induced field emission
It would be interesting to investigate the theory of whether the crystallinity of the sample is
influential on the emission properties. For poly-crystalline graphene, grain boundaries could
both be believed to act as scattering sites, but could also be imagined to concentrate the field,
thereby increasing the field enhancement factor locally. This could be one reason for observing
large amounts of out-of-plane emission from the CVD grown graphene which is expected to
have a relatively poorer crystal quality than the exfoliated graphene. This type of investigation
could be performed both by using DC electric fields as well as AC fields, such as a single-cycle
THz source, providing that a signal can be obtained.
Another experiment that could shed more light on the electron emission from graphene, would be
to investigate the emission dependency on the polarisation angle relative to the plane. Despite
not expecting it, a large amount of emission from the basal plane of graphene when the THz
field was polarised parallel to the in-plane direction of graphene was observed. It could therefore
be beneficial to measure the electron emission as a function of the degree of sample tilt, relative
to horizontal. No holder of that nature is currently available for the setup used, but future
experiments could produce one, making such measurements possible.
An observation that
might be important for such experiments, dependent on the sample size, is that when changing
the angle between the sample and the incident THz beam, the spot size will become ellipsoidal,
and thereby the field strength will be more spread out. For a sample considerably smaller than
the spot size, this effect will be negligible due to the shape of the beam. For large thin films
however, such as larger CVD grown graphene samples, this effect might be relevant to be taken
into account when performing the data analysis, since the intensity and thereby the peak power
will be lowered for large angles.
5.5.2
DC measurements
The samples produced for in-situ SEM measurements were additionally designed to accommo-
date measuring the (I,V) curves as a function of the graphene slit size. This could be accom-
plished by applying a bias over two adjacent but separated graphene sheets and measuring the
current, for a number of different slit widths. As discussed in section 2, a probing station in a
vacuum chamber is preferred for these type of experiments, which is provided in the SEM. This
information is key to have for anyone hoping to optimise the performance of a component based
on field emission from a graphene edge. Since no systematic study has been performed on how
the gap size affects the field emission, one cannot chose the design parameters for the device
with confidence. The measurements anticipated were meanwhile not possible to perform, due
to the imprecision of the connection to the electrodes. For future work on devices of similar
structure or working principle as the ones described in this project, measurements that yield
information about the effect of the gap size is suggested to be highly prioritized.

Fabrication process optimisation
As is evident from section 4.5, the width of the fabricated slits deviates significantly from

5.5.4
t-SPL
The initial interest in performing a structured study of the sample dependent patterning using
t-SPL, was revolving around an attempt to somewhat decouple the thermal and dielectric ef-
fects. After sharing experiences and discussing the operational principle, Philip and I came up
with the following four experiments to test our hypotheses:
Experiment 1: Heat transport of hBN (control experiment)
Three hBN flakes of similar thickness, characterised by OM and AFM if needed. Investigate
the topographic read measurements, and compare patterning characteristics for constant set
temperature and actuation voltage without any feedback.
Experiment 2: Heat transport of hBN.
Three hBN flakes of different thickness, characterised by OM and AFM if needed. Investigate
the topographic read measurements, and compare patterning characteristics for constant set
temperature and actuation voltage without any feedback.
Experiment 3: Heat transport/polarisability of hBN and graphene.
Using the same parameters as for hBN, read and pattern over a monolayer graphene flake.
Compare the depth for hBN vs graphene. Also compare the topographic reading of monolayer
graphene with multi-nm thick hBN.
Experiment 4: Heat transport/polarisability of hBN and graphene.
Do a topographic scan and a pattern over a hBN/graphene/hBN stack. The stack also needs
to be measured with AFM. Compare the height measurements from t-SPL and AFM.
As disclaimed by section 4 the investigation was terminated during Experiment 2. The decision
was guided by the realisation that our control of the instrument was eclipsed by unmanageable
and, to our capabilities, unpredictable variables. The main component of any scanning probe
instrument is the probe itself. In the case of the t-SPL, the probe is in direct contact with a
polymer, which alters the tip during operation. Similar effects are known from AFM, although
the effect is typically observed to a smaller extend. The main take-away from this is, that
when changing the tip, all parameters change to some degree, which increases the complexity
of controlling the operation.
The anecdote narrated in section 4.6 brought us awareness of the resist’s altering nature. Be-
ing deliberate about the storage requirements of PPA, it was not expected to discover issues
dictated by the resist in the patterning process. The complications of patterning on, as well as
off, hBN flakes despite changing tip, leads to the conclusion however, that the resist was the
limiting factor.

6
Conclusion
The primary objective of the work in this thesis has been to utilise terahertz time-domain spec-
troscopy setups for inducing field emission in graphene. Specifically the endeavors have been
directed towards two goals regarding field emission from graphene: (i) To decouple the basal
plane and edge emission, and (ii) to design and fabricate a graphene based floating gate field
effect transistor operated by single-cycle terahertz pulses.
THz induced field emission from the basal plane of CVD grown graphene was successfully
achieved. Observations revealed that, compared to ungrounded samples where field emission
decreased over time, grounded graphene samples maintained a consistently higher and constant
level of emission. Analysis using the Fowler-Nordheim model revealed a significantly higher elec-
tron emission from the basal plane of graphene than would be expected from a bulk structure
with similar properties. Furthermore, a comparative study with the Fowler-Nordheim model
and three alternative models suggested that models tailored for graphene’s basal plane emission
more accurately fit our data. Regression analysis confirmed that each of these models provided
a better fit to the data than the standard Fowler-Nordheim model.
The field emission from the edges of graphene was investigated using encapsulated graphene
samples. For this purpose, a conducting tip within a Scanning Electron Microscope was em-
ployed to measure the tunneling current as a function of its position. Due to the coarse control
of the tip’s position relative to the sample, our results were inconclusive. The measurements
suggests the possibility of graphene edge being cleaned during field emission, although further
studies are needed to confirm this.
Floating gate field effect transistors based on exfoliated graphene and hexagonal boron nitride
were fabricated with the intend of being measured in a two-color air plasma terahertz setup.
Breakdown of the electrical contacts during device testing meanwhile rendered the devices in a
state where no measurements could be performed.
The findings of this thesis indicate that graphene is capable of exhibiting strong field emis-
sion. Notably, the emission from the basal plane of graphene was observed at lower peak field
strengths than traditionally expected for a flat surface, highlighting its potential effectiveness
in field emission applications.
The primary limitation of this project was the inaccessibility to an electrical probing station
under high vacuum conditions. Had such facilities been available, the samples produced might
have enabled a more comprehensive characterisation of field emission, potentially enriching our
understanding of field emission from both the basal plane and the edges of graphene. Such
measurements could have also provided valuable insights into how various design parameters
influence devices that utilise field emission in graphene layers.Future research could investigate
elements that are relevant for utilising the device as a functional electrical component. Investi-
gations could explore, for instance, how field emission depends on the spacing between adjacent
graphene sheets, or how the gating strength varies with the geometry of the gates. Additionally,
studying the retention time of carriers in the floating gate could yield important information.
Another intriguing concept to pursue, is to isolate the field emitting region of graphene from
ambient conditions, to examine field emission under conditions distinct from those in this the-
sis. Such studies could reveal new dynamics of field emission, opening avenues for innovative
applications.
