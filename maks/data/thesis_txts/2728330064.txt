Abstract
Plastic usage has seen a significant increase in everyday life since the 1950s, when it was
first introduced. Thanks to its chemical and mechanical properties, which guarantee a lasting
and versatile use, plastic is employed in many sectors, among which the fishing industry. Dolly
ropes (DR) are polyethylene threads placed underneath the net of beam trawlers, to protect it
from wear and tear, once they are dragged on the seabed.
Plastic pollution in water environments is becoming a more and more pressing subject, given its
negative effects on aquatic flora and fauna, among which entanglement, impacts to the species
fecundity and internal organs functionality, which can also be transferred across the food web.
This project aims to assess whether the influx of DR fragments coming from the North Sea
into Limfjord is able to explain the abundance of MPs in the inlet, assuming no other influxes or
sources are involved. The North Sea is an area where beam and bottom trawling are extensively
performed and Limfjord is chosen as the study area for this project because of its proximity to
the North Sea.
The transport of DRs will be simulated with an Agent Based Model (ABM) in MIKE, the soft-
ware released by DHI (Danish Hydrological Institute), in Limfjord. The model robustness will be
tested in four simulation years (2002, 2008, 2013 and 2017), which differ for wind conditions.
The model will include beaching of fragments, with a stochastic approach, and sedimentation.
The DR will also be subjected to biofouling, which will influence their density and affect their
buoyancy in the water column.
The model validation will be assessed by comparing the model results to the observational data
gathered during a field work campaign performed on the coasts of Limfjord in December 2022.

Introduction
Since the 1950s, the use of plastic materials in everyday life increased exponentially, leading
to a plastic-dependent society. There are many reasons that can explain such massive use,
mainly due to the material’s properties that allow it to be used in a variety of contexts. As a mat-
ter of fact, not only can plastic assume different shapes but also, depending on the additives
that characterize it, plastic can respond differently to thermal or mechanical stress [10].
The same properties that make plastic such a versatile and extensively used material - resis-
tance and durability among others - are also responsible for plastic’s long residence time when
introduced in a natural environment [11].
The fishing industry is not making less use of plastic, compared to other areas.
Plastic fragments from nets or fishing gears, or the microplastics (MPs) that are generated from
their degradation, can be ingested by marine organisms, causing negative effects on their motil-
ity, fecundity, feeding rates and also on internal organs functionality. It has also been proven
that these impacts can be transferred across the food web, both vertically to a lower trophic
level and horizontally through predation[12]. Underwater species are not the only ones to be
affected: birds are affected as well, especially when plastic litter comes in the shape of threads.
Seagulls, gannets and fulmars can get entangled in them or even eat them, mistaking them for
food [13].
The goal of this project is to assess whether the influx of dolly ropes (DR), polyethylene threads
used in beam trawling and transported from the North Sea into Limfjord, is able to explain the
abundance of MPs in the inlet. The assumption is that no other influxes or sources are involved.
In order to prove this hypothesis, the transport of DRs is simulated with an Agent Based Model
(ABM) in MIKE, the software released by DHI (Danish Hydrological Institute).
Limfjord, located in the northern part of the Jutlandic peninsula in Denamrk, was selected for two
reasons. The first reason relates to its location, as it is an inlet of water connected to the North
Sea, known for extensive beam trawling activities [14], unlike the Baltic Sea. The second rea-
son regards this project’s intention to build upon a previous study conducted in partnership with
the Department of Ecoscience in Aarhus Univerisity. This study involved fieldwork conducted
specifically in the Limfjord region and from interviews conducted among fishermen and stake-
holders working in the sound, it became evident that although no beam trawling takes place in
the area, fragments of (DR) can be found in various points of the coastline. This explains also
why the modelled plastic fragments are dolly ropes specifically.
According to the International Bottom Trawls Survey (IBTS) [15], in 2017 115 DRs were found
to be floating in the North Sea (Figure A.1 in Appendix A). Most of the fragments were 20 cm
long, but the extreme values ranged between 0.5 and 117.7 cm [16]).
The topic is discussed in this project with the following structure: Chapter 2 presents a com-
prehensive review of the literature, examining the key theories, empirical studies, and giving
an overview of the current state of the art. Chapter 3 elucidates the research methodology,
describing what dolly ropes are, detailing the study area, the model type and the data collection
methods employed. In Chapter 4, the theory behind the model and a more extensive descrip-
tion of its implementation in MIKE are given. The results of the simulations are presented in
Chapter 5, coupled by a discussion of their implications in Chapter 6, where suggestions for

future research are also given. Lastly, in Chapter 7, the conclusions are drawn and the project
is put into perspective to enlighten its relevance.

2
Literature review
The attention to plastic pollution in the marine environment is growing as fast as the problem
itself. The risks for the ecosystem are multiple and therefore the description of plastic transport in
the marine environment, through mathematical models and simulations, becomes very relevant
and necessary.
Some studies have been carried out in freshwater [17], most of them in seawater [18] [19] [16]
[1] and some in coastal [20] or topographically complex areas [21], just like Limfjord. Many
are focusing on plastic transport in the surface waters of those areas which dynamics can be
associated with Ekman transport: the 5 subtropical gyres [22], [23]. Some models have studied
whether a permanent accumulation of debris like the one in the garbage patches [24] can also
be present in smaller and more enclosed areas than the open ocean: Mansui et al. [25] and
Liubartseva et al. [19] tested it in the Mediterranean basin and concluded that the retention time
is not enough to define the accumulation permanent.
The fate of plastic debris can be classified with one of the following possible options [20]:
• Beaching
• Settling on the sea floor
• Degeneration/fragmentation
• Resuspension
• Wind-driven transport on the surface
Many models have also included beaching and sedimentation, as factors influencing the fate.
Liubartseva et al. [19] introduced beaching with a stochastic approach in a 2D Lagrangian
model of the Mediterranean Sea, whereas Yoon et al. [26] did it in a simulation for the transport
of macroplastics in the Japan Sea and van der Mheen et al. [27] in the Indian Ocean.
When describing transport, the following factors can influence the movement of plastic debris
in the water [21]:
• Current speed and direction
• Wind speed and direction
• Seeding locations
• Wind drift coefficient
Wind drift is a source of uncertainty because it can only be identified experimentally, therefore
it is sometimes omitted. According to Critchell et al. [20], if the debris is submerged for most of
its life, wind drift can be neglected. Neumann et al. [18] proposes a 2D Lagrangian model with
testing of multiple wind drift values, instead.
Size, density and shape can represent a challenge when it comes to describe the transport
of different plastic pieces, since they can all influence their movement in the water column.
Critchell et al. [20] suggests to use only one type of debris, to overcome the problem. For
this project, only dolly ropes are considered, but different fragment sizes are tested to evaluate
a possible correlation with their fate pattern. Also, their density is set to non-constant as it is
considered that biofilm can grow on them.
4
==PAGE:13==
Because of the impact it has on the density variation, biofouling is assumed to affect the sinking
speed of plastic fragments. Many models, some more complex [16], [17] than others [1], have
extensively studied the process of biofilm growth and the effects it has on plastic transport: even
though the pristine plastic fragment has a density lower than that of water, which would allow it
to float, biofilm could still make it sink by increasing its density.

3
Materials, methods and study area
This chapter starts with a characterization of the materials modelled in this project: dolly ropes
(DR). After, an overview of the methods is given, explaining how an Agent Based Model (ABM) is
a good way to simulate DR transport. The last portion of this chapter provides both a description
of the study area, Limfjord, and of the used hydrodynamic (HD) setup for the model domain. A
paragraph containing information on the field work performed in Limfjord is also included.
3.1
Dolly Ropes (DR)
DR are plastic threads, typically 1.5 – 2 m long, braided to create bundles and then attached to
the cod end of trawling nets of beam trawlers. As soon as the net is released in the water and
dragged, these threads spread out underneath it, protecting it from wear and tear.
They are typically made of polyethylene (PE) and the choice for this specific crafting material
can be justified with multiple reasons:
• it is cheaper than more eco-friedly and sustainable materials [14].
• it is strong and resistant to tearing [28]
• it is light. Weight influences the power needed from the boat to drag the net, which in turns
influences the fuel consumption. [14]
While being a protective layer for the trawling net, DR are easily ripped off as soon as the net is
dragged over a rock or any sharp object. The OSPAR (Oslo and Paris) Action Plan [28] states
that in the first few weeks after their installation, 10 – 25% of these newly attached threads are
ripped off. Once they are released in the water, the risks for the environment are various.
In 2021 the National Center for the Environment and Energy carried out a monitoring of the
marine waste on Danish beaches [29] and found out that plastic made up on average 81% of
the registered litter. Strings with a diameter smaller than 1 cm, which include also DR, rank
among the Top15 list of the most commonly occurring types of litter on all the beaches under
examination. For beaches situated along the North Sea, where extensive bottom trawling oc-
curs, strings hold the highest placement in the list, with a median value of 63 fragments per 100
m of coast. However, for beaches on the East side, they rank below the 10th position, with only
2.75 fragments on the beaches along the Kattegat Strait and 2.5 along the Øresund Strait. In
this last location bottom trawling has been prohibited since 1932 [30].
3.2
Agent Based Models (ABMs)
ABMs focus on the behavior of individual ”agents”, which responds to inputs from the surround-
ing environment [31]. Typically, the models generate outputs able to track changes in the agent
positions over time and space. ABMs are extensively applied in biological and ecological con-
texts [32] and given the objectives of this project, an ABM was considered the most suitable
approach. Agents can be categorized based on specific characteristics that determine their
behavior. In this study, a single class of agents has been defined: the ”passive drifter” agent.
These agents do not move on their own and instead, their motion is uniquely influenced by the
current and wind speed and direction. The ABM therefore enables the tracking of their trajec-
tories within Limfjord under the influence of hydrological inputs from the HD model.
A more extensive description of the ABM used in this project is given in Appendix B, following
the protocol defined by Grimm [31].

3.3
Study site: Limfjord
Limfjord is a sound of brackish water in the northern part of the Jutland peninsula, in Denmark.
It stretches for 180 km, connecting the North Sea to the Kattegat channel and creating the North
Jutlandic Island. The water temperature ranges from an average of -1°C in winter to an average
of 22 °C in summer. The water is characterized by a salinity gradient that drives the flow from
west, where the salinity is higher (32-34 psu), to east (19-25 psu). The main driver of the flow,
however, is the wind, which blows for most of the year from west to east and only in summer
in the opposite direction, thus moving water back into the North Sea at the Thyborøn Channel.
The last contribution to the flow is the tidal system, which has only a minor effect since the
average amplitude is 0.1 – 0.2 m. All these factors result in a total yearly flow of about 8.7

4.1
Theory
The following paragraphs explain in details the theory behind the definition of the ABM for DR
transport. With the goal to describe their vertical movement in the water column and the factors
influencing it, an overview of the biofouling model is given first. After, both the settling and the
rising speeds are defined, with a description of each term composing the chosen formulas.
4.1.1
Biofouling
Biofouling is a natural phenomenon occurring underwater, during which colonies of microor-
ganisms form a biofilm around the tackled object. Based on its thickness, this external layer,
can switch the buoyancy of the object from positive to negative, thus making it sink in the water
column. [2] The biofilm growth model used in this project is taken from Murawski et al. [1] and
it is a simpler and empirical version, compared to more complicated models used for instance
in Kooi et al. [2] and Cuttat [16]. The reasoning behind this choice is that biofouling, though ac-
knowledged as a factor influencing the settling velocity of the DR fragments, is not the primary
focus of this project.
The biofilm growth is described as a saturation process (Equation 4.1), where the DR fragment
gets covered constantly as time goes by. It is however based on the assumption that at their
release, no biofilm is present on the fragments’ surface.

MP2 and MP3), based on their density and on their diameter. MP3, with a density of 965 kg

a) concentration in the water was above a threshold value. chl-a concentration is considered
a proxy for primary production, which is linked to detritus production, on which the bacteria
composing the biofilm feed on.

Murawski et al., with higher chl-a (primary production) in those months where the light availability
is higher as well.

The only particle class defined is DR_as_passive_drifter, which describes a class of particles

transport of this kind of particle class is described with the Equations defined in the Theory sec-

for the Horizontal movement and the other one for the Downward movement which is the
vertical one. They are explained in details below.
4.2.1
Horizontal movement
The horizontal vector requires to be described with a direction and a speed. The direction has
been set equal to the current direction HDir whereas the speed has been defined as follows:
IF(TESTHITSTATE([HITSTATE], 8) == 1, bottom_motion, HSpeed)
The IF-clause tests whether the particle hits the seabed or not. If affirmative, its motion on the
seabed happens only if τ at that specific location and time step exceeds τc. Otherwise, the DR
fragment keeps moving with the same speed as the current horizontal speed HSpeed.
4.2.2
Downward movement
The vertical movement has to be described only with the speed, since its direction is determined
by the sign of the expression. A condition is imposed on the particle’s buoyancy: if the right-
hand side of Equation 4.10 is greater than the right-hand side of Equation 4.9, then the particle
moves with a speed determined by Equation 4.9, taking into account the impact of VSpeed, the

current vertical speed. VSpeed can either amplify or reduce the buoyancy of the particle. If the
aforementioned condition is not satisfied, the particle moves with a settling speed as described
by Equation 4.2, once again accounting for the influence of VSpeed.
The condition described above is implemented in MIKE as follows:
IF(g*water_density*pi()/6*POW(2*radius_average,3) > DR_rise_BALANCE,
VSpeed-DR_rise_BALANCE, DR_set-VSpeed)
In MIKE a positive speed is assumed to be a sinking speed, whereas a negative speed makes
the particle rise. This explains the signs in 4.2.2, where DR_rise is negative and DR_set is
positive. The input of the vertical current speed is added up with opposite signs, to account for
its effect on the particle’s motion.

4.2.3
DR sedimenting
The DR fragment is considered sedimented and removed from the system whenever it touches
the bottom and τ is not strong enough to win over τc. Since no bedload transport is included in
the model, a DR fragment is also removed anytime θ is smaller than θs, which means that the
DR is not able to resuspend.
At each time-step, the accumulated number of sedimented fragments is passed to the Eulerian
framework, where a state variable is updated to create a map of the sedimented DR fragments.
4.2.4
DR beaching
When the DR fragment touches the land boundary around the model domain (Figure 3.4) at any
location, the test below returns 1.
IF(TESTHITSTATE([HITSTATE], 16) == 1, 1, 0)
If this happens, the DR can get beached, meaning stuck on land, or not. To determine whether
this occurs, a stochastic method similar to the one employed in van der Mheen et al. [27] is
used: a constant value representing the beaching probability is set between 0 (no DR get stuck
on land) and 1 (all of them get stuck on land). At each time-step, this value is compared to
a random number, generated with the function RAND() in MIKE, which returns a uniform dis-
tributed random number in the interval [0,1[. The random seed is kept fixed, so that in case a
simulation has to be re-run, the random numbers are always the same.
If the random number is greater than the constant value, the fragment is considered beached.
Unlike van der Mheen et al., no time condition on its presence on land is taken into account,
and the fragment is simply removed from the system if classified as beached.
Also, the possibility that the fragment could be reintroduced into the water column by the un-
dertow or the wind is not considered, although this occurs in reality.
At each time-step, the accumulated number of beached fragments is passed to the Eulerian
framework, where a state variable is updated to create a map of the beached DR fragments.
4.2.5

The DR fragments can leave the domain thorough 2 open boundaries: one at Thyborøn channel
into the North Sea and one at Hals into the Kattegat Strait (see Figure 3.4). Once they are
outside, they are lost and removed. The simulation is not able to account for the possibility of
them re-entering the system.
The way a fragment is defined as ”leaving the domain” is different for the east and for the west
boundary. The exit condition at Hals (Figure 4.6a) is defined as follows:
IF([XPOS] >= 579000, 1, 0)

5
Results
This chapter presents the simulation results in a step-by-step approach. It starts with a con-
vergence analysis to find the time-step that allows to better appreciate the changes in teh sim-
ulation, followed by tests on the effects of releasing particles from different individual sources
and varying fragment lengths. Once the optimal source and length are identified, the simula-
tions are run. The analysis focuses then on what happens to the fragments after their release,
detailing results for suspended particles, beached, sedimented, and exited ones. The chapter
also explores the impact of releasing particles from multiple sources at the same time. Finally,
it concludes with a sensitivity analysis of the constant probability of beaching, enhancing result
reliability.

Discussion
6.1
Model validation
The goal of this project was to assess whether the influx of DR fragments coming from the North
Sea into Limfjord could be able to explain the abundance of MP in the inlet, assuming no other
influxes or sources were involved.
Being impossible to have the simulation setup for 2022 (the year of the field work) ready within
the deadline of this project, 2008 was chosen as the year with the most similar wind conditions,
when compared to 2022. The validation of the model would be confirmed if the beaching pattern
obtained in 2008 closely resembled the one observed in 2022. With a p-value < 2.26 10−16, 2008
shows to have a positive correlation with 2022 and therefore the initial hypothesis on the North
Sea influx as the only source of MP in Limfjord can be validated.
The model results generally describe beaching as the most recurring fate for DR plastic, when
compared to sedimentation and the possibility of fragments leaving the system through one of
the two open boundaries, on the eastern and western side of the fjord. This finding can be
partially explained with the choice of a probability of beaching of 90%, however it can also be
supported by several papers that studied the frequency of this phenomenon over others. Among
them, Harris [42], which reviewed over 80 other papers to study the occurrence of MP particles
in bottom and beach marine sediment, and Graca et al. [43], which conducted an experimental
study with the same purpose in the Southern Baltic Sea.
Graca et al. states that the concentration of MP in bottom sediments can reach values up to 27
particles kg−1 of dry weight, whereas in beach sediments the value increases up to 53 particles
per kg−1 d.w. Generally, the concentration gradient decreases from the coast towards the open
deep sea area.
Harris concluded that, even though MP eventually end up sinking in the seabed as a result of
biofouling or flocculation, the highest concentration of MP fragments can be found on coastal
environments, rather than shelf and deep sea.
When it comes to fragments leaving the domain via the open boundaries, the absence of frag-
ments exiting at the eastern open boundary is curious, particularly given that the fragments
reach locations very close to Hals, as Figures E.2 and E.3 in Appendix E show.
However the current speed in that area drops to very low values, as Figure 6.1 illustrates, which
could explain the absence of DR released in the Kattegat Strait.

6.2
Model assumptions and limitations
The model was developed based on certain assumptions, which in turn resulted in limitations
in the obtained results.
The chosen biofouling model was implemented with a simple saturation process based only on
two parameters. The lack of an encounter rate of the DR fragments with the algae, as well as
the absence of a biofilm growth rate based on either physical parameters (temperature, light
availability, etc.) or biological ones (seasonality in primary production), has probably introduced
limitations to the model’s precision. Even though the biofouling model was not the core of the
project, a different approach, beyond the simple activation with the onset of spring and the
constant growth of the biofilm, could have increased the accuracy of the results.
It is also worth mentioning that the initial assumption that DR fragments have no biofilm at time
t = 0 might not be realistic. If fragments are released back into the fjord from their beaching
location on the coast or by currents from the North Sea, they could already have a layer of
microorganisms on their surface, influencing the time needed for them to reach the saturation
level of biofouling necessary for sinking.
Furthermore, it needs to be mentioned that diffusivity has been treated homogeneously over
the model domain and defined in the same way both vertically and horizontally, which does not
match the real variability in the two directions.
Another factor worth considering is the utilization of a time-step in the final setup, that differs
from the one used in the HD model and therefore results in the necessity for interpolation. The
estimation of the particle position and conditions introduces some level of approximation, which
can affect the accuracy and precision of the results.
On top of the limitations listed so far, related to specific choices in the model setup, the methodol-
43
==PAGE:52==
ogy employed in this study, combining agent-based simulations with circulation models, presents
fundamental limitations in itself.
Agent-based simulations, while effective in capturing the individual behavior and interactions of
the agents, are based on simplifications and assumptions that might not fully reflect the com-
plexity of real-world processes.
Circulation models are based on simplifications of physical phenomena, which necessitate ap-
proximations that might not accurately represent the complex dynamics of the actual environ-
ment.
6.3
Potential sources of error
In the specification and set up of the beaching criterion, no time condition has been defined. The
model is built so that once the DR fragments reach the coast, they are immediately classified
and removed ”beached”. Consequently, the simulation does not account for the potential of
fragments being returned to the water column due to undertow or wind. This fails to replicate
reality, where beaches can become sources of DR fragments over time.
Regarding the open boundary at Thyborøn, it needs to be considered, when evaluating the
total number of DR fragments exiting the domain, that MIKE does not account for the possibility
of them being pushed back inside the fjord. Again, as it was in the definition of beaching, this
behavior does not replicate reality. As a result, the number of exited fragments is overestimated
and this miscalculation is further enhanced by the fact that the particles missing from the mass
balance are also counted as particles leaving the system.
The most probable explanation for this difference in the mass balance lies in the rectangular
box’s inability to record all the leaving particles. This most likely happens because some parti-
cles have a position check just before entering the box and in the following time-step, they are
already positioned outside of it. As these particles are never inside the box, at any time-step,
the Eulerian state variable keeping track of the exited particles is not updated with them, which
are consequently not counted for.
The imprecision in the definition of the sediment transport, specifically in how the resuspension
condition operates, has also led to miscalculations in the total number of sedimented fragments.
The absence of bottom transport further contributes to this discrepancy.
6.4
Relevance
The significance of this project lies in its relevance to the subject of plastic transport model-
ing in water domains. By modeling the movement of plastic fragments, it is possible to track
their trajectories and eventually predict potential sink locations. This predictive potential can be
crucial in the organization of targeted cleanup campaigns, enabling resources to be efficiently
allocated.
The relevance extends further to the ability to validate the sources of plastic influx. This valida-
tion not only allows to identify them but facilitates targeted interventions to directly address the
issue, by removing or inhibiting the input of plastic contaminants into the water. By combining
modeling results with actions on the field and with national/international environmental plans,
there is the potential to better preserve aquatic environments.
In this sense, this project aligns with the current state of the art on the subject of plastic transport
models and plastic pollution management. Among some other relevant studies, the project of
Florence Cuttat [16] has to be mentioned, as it focuses on DR transport and beaching loca-
tions in the North Sea, Christensen et al. [44], focused on macro-plastics in the Baltic Sea, or
Liubartseva et al. [19] in the Mediterranean Sea, or Yoon et al. [26] in the Japan Sea.

6.5
Further research
As it concerns the model setup, more effort needs to be spent in the definition of the sediment
transport, in order to define both the bed load transport and the resuspension in a way that
better fits the HD of Limfjord.
It is also essential to consider the potential for fragments that have beached to re-enter the
domain, potentially turning the coast into a source of DR fragments itself. In this perspective, a
follow up on the results obtained from this project could be done: further simulations could be
run using the same setup, but focusing on the year immediately following the one utilized in this
study and considering multiple releasing sources, placed in the identified beaching locations
from the current study.
To overcome the miscalculation of the particles leaving the system in Thyborøn, drawing a line
instead of using a box could be a solution. This would probably involve setting up multiple
conditions based on the particle’s position, with checks along both the x and y axes. It is also
possible that a single straight line might not be enough. Instead, it could be necessary to split
the line into two segments that follow the channel’s layout.
Performing a reverse simulation could also give some interesting insights, especially if there
is any information on the source data, like location and density of the released fragments. By
executing a reverse simulation, the trajectories of plastic fragments can be traced back to their
initial release locations. If the reverse simulation successfully converges with the releasing
points, it increased the model’s reliability and enhances confidence in its predictive capabilities.
Finally, a second round of field work could be done, to support the model results and further
verify its validity. Samples could be collected from various different places in the fjord, corre-
sponding to the locations shown in the beaching maps, and especially on the northern coast
of Nissum Bredning, where the concentration appeared to be at the highest. A count of the
collected fragments in each location could also be done and compared to the modelled results.

7
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to assess whether the influx of DR fragments coming from the North
Sea into Limfjord could be able to explain the abundance of MPs in the inlet, assuming no other
influxes or sources were involved. In order to do so, the transport of DRs has been simulated
with an ABM in MIKE, the software released by DHI, in Limfjord, a sound in the north of the
Jutlandic peninsula of Denmark.
The development of the ABM successfully validated the observational data obtained from the
field work. Consequently, the initial hypothesis that the influx of DR fragments from the North
Sea could be the main contributing factor to MP abundance Limfjord, fails to be rejected. Among
the four simulation years, the one showing the best correlation with 2022 - the year of the field
work - was 2008, chosen as the one with the most similar wind conditions to 2022.
The simplifications and assumptions in the model’s design, including the definition of biofouling,
diffusivity, and other factors, can potentially reduce the accuracy of the results. Yet, the model
has the potential to identify the locations, quantities, and trajectories of beached, sedimented,
and exiting particles. This capability offers valuable insights for the identification of sources and
the formulation of targeted solutions to mitigate the problem.
With some improvements, including a more detailed representation of sediment transport and
the incorporation of additional observational data from various different locations, the model’s
reliability can be increased. To enhance its validity even more, a reverse simulation could be
run, which in case of a positive outcome could help locating sources of plastic pollution with
greater precision.
In conclusion, this study’s findings suggest that the influx of DR fragments is indeed a factor
contributing to the MP abundance in Limfjord. The ABM, while showing some limitations, proves
to be a valuable tool to describe particle transport and fate within the model domain. Future
refinements of the model will contribute in identifying sources and guiding strategies to address
the MP pollution issue in water environments.

B
ODD protocol
This section of the appendix describes the ABM following the ODD (Overview, Design concepts,
and Details) protocol by Grimm et al. [45]
B.1
Model description
The model used is a 3D model run in MIKE, the software released by DHI. Running the HD of
Limfjorden was not part of this project and it had previously been computed in MIKE 3FM and
provided by DHI. The transport of dolly ropes was described with an ABM model, which was
implemented in MIKE ECOLab module and then coupled with the hydrodynamical results. The
ECOLab module has 2 frameworks: Eulerian and Lagrangian.
B.2
Purpose
This project aims to model the transport of dolly ropes (DR) inside Limfjord. The null hypothesis
is that the abundance of dolly ropes fragments found on the coasts of the fjord can be explained
by the influx coming from the North Sea and the model aims to validate this by comparing the
simulation results with the real data of the sink locations.
B.3
Entities, state variables and scales
B.3.1
Agents/Individuals
The model is based on only one class, representing DRs, which transport is exclusively con-
trolled by external factors and not by their own movement, therefore making them passive
drifters. The state variables and the equations describing their transport are therefore written
accordingly to this definition, considering that only the hydrodynamic flow conditions influence
their movement.
B.3.2
Spatial units and temporal scales
The horizontal domain of the HD part is described with a triangular/rectangular mesh, which
was given, as part of the HD module. There are 2 open boundaries: one on the North Sea side,
at Thyborøn harbor, and one on the Kattegat side, at the reef outside of Hals. The rest of the
Vertically, the space is described with a combination of 5 sigma layers, weighting each 20%,
and 15 z-layers, equidistantly spaced by a fixed distance of 1 m.
In the model, DR are allowed to move freely vertically and horizontally, but whenever they hit
either the bottom or the coastline, they are removed from the simulation. This allows to limit the
number of entities to only the active ones, so that the comprehension of the results becomes
easier and smoother.
The HD was run from 1st January 2017 to 1st January 2018, with discrete time-step of 3600 s.
As a result of this, 4 decoupled files were given, to which the ECOLab template, containing the
ABM, was appended. This final setup was run for the years 2002, 2008, 2013 and 2017 with a
discrete time-step of 900 s.
B.3.3
Environment
Seasonality, in terms of temperature and light availability which affect the biofilm formation,
current and wind direction and speed are the forces driving the behavior of the passive drifter
agent.
51
==PAGE:60==
B.4
Process overview and scheduling
In each time-step, the Eulerian framework is updated before the Lagrangian. This means that
the equations are updated first for all the grid elements/position, following the order of the grid
cells and after that, each variable of the dolly rope class is updated, following the order of grid
element. Two vectors, describing respectively the horizontal and the vertical movement of the
particle, are updated as last, so that the particle is moved to its new position in the following
time-step, before the state variables are also updated. If any particle leaves the domain through
an open boundary or hits the land and it gets beached, it is removed. The information on the
particle position is carried from the previous time-step to the following one and the results are
written in the 2D and 3D maps at each one of them, which represent the Eulerian variables.
B.5
Design concepts
B.5.1
Basic principles
The flowchart in Figure 4.7 describes the logic behind the model.
The DR fragments are subjected to biofouling in the spring and summer months. Biofilm growth
is implemented with a saturation process. Particles are released January 1st for each one of the
simulation year, with a continuous release of 5 particles per time-step for the whole simulation.
They are all released at the surface. The particles move with the current speed and direction
and when they touch the bottom, they can either get stuck on the sediment or get resuspend. No
bedload transport is included in the model. If it gets sedimented, it is removed. A stochastical
approach to beaching is defined in case the particle touches land. If it gets beached, it is
removed. If the DR leaves the domain, it gets removed. In any other case, the particle floats in
the water column.
B.5.2
Emergence
As a consequence of gradients in the seawater temperature and salinity, which in turns affect
its density, the position of DR fragments in the water column might change. This effect can be
further enhanced by the presence of biofilm on the fragment, which instead acts on the fragment
density. The presence of tides and the effect of major changes in wind patterns and HD forcings
(like the current speed and direction) can also affect the transport of the fragments inside the
fjord and consequently the likelihood of them getting washed up on the shore or buried in the
sediment.
B.5.3
Interaction
DR fragments can vary in length from a few millimeters to more than 50 cm. In case the longest
threads meet, during their transport in the fjord, the result can be a bundle of entangled threads,
which density is then different than the one of a single thread and therefore which transport is
different. This hypothesis has not been tested in the model.
B.5.4
Stochasticity
Beaching is described with a stochastic method: a randomly generated number is compared to
a constant beaching probability. The result determines the beaching (and following removal) of
the particle or its reintroduction into the water column.
B.5.5
Observation
The simulation keeps track of the accumulated number of beached and sedimented particles,
together with those that leave the system through an open boundary. A state variable in the
Eulerian framework is created for each of those and updated for every time-step. Their distri-
bution is shown in a 2D map. The accumulated density of floating particles is instead shown in
a 3D map. Particle tracks are also outputted and overlapped onto the 2D map.
52
==PAGE:61==
B.6
Initialization
At time t = 0 the release of 5 particles happens at a specific location in Thyborøn Channel.
Regardless of the considered year, each simulation starts January 1st, but biofouling does not
start before March 21st, so at time = 0, each released particle has a constant density.
B.7
Input data
The model uses HD information carried by the decoupled file generated by the HD setup itself.
The decoupled file is provided by DHI. Besides that, a current and wave induced shear stress
is also provided by DHI.
The biofouling model is taken from Murawski et al. [1]. The critical shear stress for incipient
motion is calculated based on the sediment type, as explained in Goral et al. [7].
