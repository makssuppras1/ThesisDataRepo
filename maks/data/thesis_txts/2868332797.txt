Abstract
Effective human­robot collaboration requires more than task execution; it demands an un­
derstanding of human actions, intentions, and needs in dynamic environments. This dis­
sertation explores the integration of attention modeling into Theory of Mind (ToM) frame­
works to enhance implicit coordination in human­robot interaction. By modeling agents’
attentional states and incorporating mechanisms for attention shifts, the proposed frame­
work enables robots to better infer and adapt to human collaborators’ beliefs and focus.
The research contributes to three key areas: (1) integrating attention representation and
dynamics into a Dynamic Epistemic Logic (DEL)­based ToM framework, addressing phe­
nomena such as inattentional blindness and joint attention; (2) designing and implement­
ing a method for human attention inference based on environmental affordances; and
(3) developing collaborative mechanisms, such as pointing, to facilitate implicit coordina­
tion by directing human attention to relevant information. The framework was validated
through experiments in dynamic environments, demonstrating its ability to resolve false
beliefs, anticipate human needs, and improve coordination efficiency.
By advancing attention­based Theory of Mind (ToM) frameworks, this work enhances the
precision of ToM implementations in robotic systems. It improves their ability to represent
human agents’ beliefs and facilitates seamless, implicit collaboration in dynamic environ­
ments.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, advancements in artificial intelligence and robotics have brought us closer
to a world where humans and robots collaborate smoothly across a variety of daily tasks.
From healthcare and customer service to rescue missions, robots can boost productivity,
improve safety, and assist in tasks that are beyond human capabilities, whether physically
or cognitively. For this kind of collaboration to be truly effective, robots must go beyond
merely performing tasks; they need to understand, anticipate and adapt to human actions,
intentions and needs in real­time dynamic environments, promoting implicit coordination.
Achieving implicit coordination requires Theory of Mind (ToM), which is the ability to infer
what others are thinking and perceiving. In human­robot interactions, ToM allows robots
not only to recognize human intentions and beliefs but also to understand what their hu­
man collaborators are paying attention to at any given moment. Attention is essential
for effective teamwork, especially in situations that demand quick and coordinated re­
sponses.
Consider, for example, a rescue operation in a disaster zone where both humans and
robots are working together to locate survivors. If a robot simply assumes that its human
collaborators are aware of everything it perceives, it may miss critical cues or duplicate
efforts. However, by inferring the human’s attentional focus, the robot can prioritize pro­
viding crucial information the human may have overlooked. Additionally, if the humans’
perception of the situation leads them to believe that certain actions are unfeasible, the
robot can decide to take on those tasks itself, complementing the human’s understanding
and expanding the team’s overall effectiveness. Building systems that incorporate at­
tentional inference holds the potential to create truly effective, collaborative human­robot
partnerships, enhancing teamwork in challenging and dynamic settings.
1.2
Challenges and Statement of Purpose
This dissertation aims to demonstrate how attention modeling can be a foundational as­
pect of ToM to enhance implicit coordination in human­robot interaction. Achieving this
requires addressing several key challenges.
One of the primary challenges lies in constructing an effective attention model within a
multi­agent system, where each agent’s unique perception, focus, and resulting beliefs
can be accurately represented and tracked. Current methods for ToM often focus on
agents’ intentions and beliefs but tend to overlook the crucial role of attention in shaping
what each agent believes. By developing an attention model that allows a robot to track
where each agent’s focus lies, it becomes possible to better understand and represent the
true beliefs of each agent, laying the groundwork for smoother and effective teamwork in
dynamic environments.
Another critical challenge is inferring human attention dynamically and contextually. Cur­
rent research on inferring attention often relies on gaze estimation but lacks the capacity
to consider environment and possible agents’ context. By taking into account the spe­
cific surroundings and objectives of the agents, a robot can better predict what a human
collaborator might or might not be focused on at any given moment, enabling it to build
a more accurate mental model of the human’s beliefs and attention priorities. Besides

this, inferring human attention can also be crucial for the robot to recognize the goal the
human is pursuing and adapt his actions taking into account the pursued goal.
The ultimate purpose of this research is to implement a system in which robots can use

human agent’s focus, the system can selectively provide relevant information, guide the
human’s attention to relevant details at a given moment, or take on tasks that align with the

team goals more efficiently. This approach has the potential to transform human­robot
partnerships, enabling robots to become proactive and contextually aware collaborators
in complex and dynamic settings.
1.3
Research Objectives and Document Organization
The primary objective of this dissertation is to enhance implicit coordination in human­
robot interaction by advancing ToM through the integration of attention modeling. This
work builds on the framework developed by Bolander and Dissing [1], which incorporates
ToM within a Dynamic Epistemic Logic (DEL) formalization and the corresponding imple­
mentation in a robotic system. The aim is to integrate the attention modeling proposed by
Belardinelli and Bolander [2, 3], introducing essential attentional principles and dynamics
into the foundational framework [1] to significantly enhance the accuracy of representing
each agent’s beliefs. It is important to highlight that this integration requires adaptations
and simplifications to address compatibility and complexity constraints.
From the integration of attention representation and mechanisms into ToM, another impor­
tant goal emerges: developing a method that enables the robotic system to dynamically
infer human attention and update the integrated attention model accordingly. This ap­
proach aims to go beyond gaze estimation by exploring how contextual and environmental
cues influence human focus. Achieving this capability significantly enhances robotic sys­
tems’ potential for goal recognition, allowing a better anticipation regarding human needs
and coordinate more effectively in collaborative tasks.
The final objective of this dissertation is to implement implicit collaborative mechanisms
within the robotic system, or robot, exploring the advancements achieved from attention
representation, dynamics and inference. This would enhance the robot’s ability to act
as a proactive collaborator, providing relevant information to human collaborators and
facilitating effective coordination in implicit environments.
To validate the work developed in this dissertation, experiments were designed in which
human agents form inaccurate beliefs due to unperceived events arising from their limited
capacity to attend to all aspects of the environment. The developed framework is eval­
uated for its ability to represent agents’ attention states and, consequently, the resulting
belief representation, as well as its capability to provide relevant information that assists
human agents in pursuing their goals.

2.1
Collaborative Robots and Theory of Mind
Collaborative robots, or cobots, are designed to work alongside humans in shared en­
vironments without physical barriers, unlike traditional industrial robots [6]. Therefore,
focusing on the interaction between humans and robots is crucial for the success of this
new technological transition in the human­machine collaboration, ensuring safety and ef­
ficiency.
Throughout human history, working in groups has led to the establishment and develop­
ment of explicit and implicit communication conventions, contributing to a more intuitive
and effective collaboration. These conventions allow humans to have a better coordina­
tion, often resulting in a better overall outcome [7].
It is important to emphasize that communication alone does not define whether coordi­
nation is implicit or explicit. Explicit coordination involves the deliberate assignment of
responsibilities, schedules and predefined actions to enhance collaborative performance
[8]. In contrast, implicit coordination is characterized as the agents’ ability to adapt to one
another by anticipating actions and needs [8, 9].
Since explicit coordination relies on well defined protocols, it often reduces collaborative
performance, due to delays in request­response communication, increased overhead or
difficulties handling unexpected events and uncertainty. Implicit coordination aims to re­
duce these constraints by encouraging flexibility and reducing communication dependen­
cies [10, 11].
Example 2.1.
A common real­life example that illustrates implicit coordination contri­
butions is setting the table for family dinner. The family is gathered in the kitchen shortly
before dinner, while the father is cooking. As the father approaches the cabinet where the
dinner towels are stored, the rest of the family interprets this action as a cue that dinner is
almost ready and that the father needs help setting the table. In response, one child takes
the cutlery, while the other fetches the plates. With everyone cooperating to set the table,
including the cook, the mother checks the cooking pots and pans to see if something else
needs to be done for dinner to be ready.
In contrast to this described behavior, the father could have explicitly announced that din­
ner was almost ready and assign tasks for each family member. However, this explicit
coordination approach would be less efficient, wasting time on announcing and planning,
while in implicit coordination behavior father’s simple action not only accelerates the pro­
cess, but also triggers high­performance collaboration and efficiency.

While this real­life example describes coordination among humans, in the future the same
scenario could involve a collaborative robot assisting in household tasks. This evolution
outlines a critical objective in Human­Robot Interaction: minimizing the need for humans
to adapt their behavior when cooperating with robots. An essential way to achieve this
is maintaining similar communication standards between humans and robots. Achiev­
ing these shared communication standards, requires advanced cognitive capabilities, so
the robot is able to understand humans’ intentions and needs in different environments
and phases of collaborative tasks [7]. Cognitive capabilities are essential for collabora­
tive robots to operate safely and efficiently alongside humans, minimizing hazards and
enhancing overall system performance [6].
A shared understanding and representation of collaborators’ cognitive states is a cru­
cial factor in enhancing team’s performance, facilitating implicit coordination mechanisms.
This mutual awareness allows agents to integrate others’ conditions into their own action
planning and supports anticipatory mechanisms to detect emerging needs [10]. The abil­
ity to attribute beliefs, goals and intentions to other agents is crucial for successful social
interaction; this is called Theory of Mind (ToM) [12].
This concept was first introduced by Premack and Woodruff [13], trying to determine if
chimpanzees are able to infer mental states on others like ”purpose or intention, as well
as knowledge, belief, thinking, doubt, guessing, pretending, liking, and so forth” [13]. This
study was conducted showing a set of videotapes to an adult chimpanzee where a human
is faced with some problem. The videotape is stopped before the problem is solved, some
photographs with different solutions are shown to the chimpanzee and he has to pick the
correct solution. The distinction between ToM and empathy is emphasized by explaining
that empathy involves understanding another’s purpose but does not require the ability to
attribute knowledge or belief to the other. ToM requires a more complete understanding,
incorporating both the other’s motivation and cognitive states. The study [13] also ex­
plores higher­order mental state inference, examining whether the chimpanzee has the
ability to attribute cognitive states to a human observing another human struggling with
specific tasks. The authors argue that assigning cognitive states to others is a primitive
human capacity that emerges naturally, likely shared to some extent with other species,
such as chimpanzees, though less developed. They highlight the important role of ex­
perience to refine and expand this ability while its formal pedagogy might even suppress
this natural capability, by focusing more on logical explanations of behavior.
Following Premack and Woodruff’s pioneering study [13], many different fields have ex­
tensively studied and contributed for the research in ToM. Philosophers have aimed to un­
derstand humans’ mental mechanisms and knowledge representation, ethologists have
tried to understand which other animals besides humans have this ability and psycholo­
gists have focused on children’s social skills development studies and its implications in
developmental disorders such as autism. Through the years, many different ToM models
were presented as a result of the extensive and deep research on it.
The integration of ToM into robots was first introduced by Brian Scassellati [12]. He em­
phasizes the importance of robots’ capability to understand not only the physical proper­
ties of the environment, but also the behaviors and actions of animate agents on it. This
extends beyond the understanding of others’ mental states to include their attention and
emotional state, as a crucial part to comprehend the others’ perspective from the world.
From this, the robot learns to anticipate humans’ reactions enabling the ability to adapt in
implicit collaboration.
Scassellati reviews two of the most influential ToM models, the one from Baron­Cohen

[14] and the other from Leslie [15] to implement these approaches in a real robotic set­
ting. Both models are grounded in studies of infants’ development and cognitive experi­
ences, providing insights into the acquisition and mechanisms of ToM. This first framework
integrating ToM into robots [12] prioritize the ability to distinguish between animate and

and similar evaluation process can be applied. Unlike human studies, robots allow for
controlled testing on various conditions, experiences and approaches, which would not
be ethically acceptable or would be practically unfeasible with humans. This demonstrates
a promising future for research in this area.
2.2
Affordances, Attention and Intention
Joint attention and intention recognition are crucial for the development of ToM, as joint
attention enables a shared perception of specific attended properties in the environment
and intention recognition supports prediction of others’ interaction with that same envi­
ronment perceived [14]. These two mechanisms are deeply connected to affordances,
which structure how individuals interpret and engage with the possibilities offered by their
surroundings.
The concept of affordances was first introduced by James J. Gibson [16], who suggested
that ”the affordance of anything is a specific combination of the properties of its substance
and its surfaces taken with reference to an animal” [16]. From an ecological perspective,
this combination of the properties represents the possibilities for action that an environ­
ment offers, specifying benefits or dangers to an animal. As a result, affordances are
more easily detected in the environment, requiring less learning to be recognized, and
more relevant than isolated properties that lack intrinsic meaning and provide no valuable
information for the animal´s interaction with its surroundings.
To illustrate this concept, Gibson [16] explored examples from daily life, how individuals
perceive affordances such as supportability, stand­on­ability, walk­on­ability, or seat­on­
ability. For all these affordances to exist, a combination of physical properties have to
be fulfilled. For an object to afford support, it has to follow certain conditions regarding
horizontality, flatness, extension or rigidity. Then, if it affords support, it is also stand­on­
able, walk­on­able and run­over­able. Before detecting such affordances, humans do not
consciously reason about the underlying properties like horizontality, flatness, extension
or rigidity.
It is also important to note that these physical properties must be evaluated relative to
the perceiver. For instance, a large and heavy animal requires more constrained condi­

tions for properties such as extension and rigidity mentioned above than a small and light
animal. Similarly, for an object to afford sitting on, it typically must be knee­high above
the ground. This measure varies significantly between an infant and an adult [16]. This
relativity makes it evident how affordance perception is inherently tied to the observer
characteristics.
Although affordances are usually directly perceived, they can also be misperceived. Mis­
perception occurs when the information collected from an object or the observer’s per­
ception about his own characteristics is inaccurate leading to false beliefs acquisition.
Experiments show that babies misperceive the supportability of transparent objects, indi­
cating that such errors can be mitigated through experience [16]. Many other factors like
sensory limitation, self awareness or context can consequence mistaken perceptions.
False beliefs can significantly influence how individuals interact with the environment, of­
ten resulting in unexpected or undesired outcomes, specially when these beliefs are criti­
cal to their decision­making. Mistaking a closed glass door for an open one is a common
example of affordance misperception among humans. In this case, the collision affor­
dance is incorrectly not perceived, leading to the false belief that the door is open. As a
result, the perceiver attempts to walk through it, colliding with the door. This demonstrates
how misperceived affordances lead individuals to inappropriate actions, which sometimes
can have disastrous outcomes as exemplified by Gibson [16]:
”If quicksand is mistaken for sand, the perceiver is in deep trouble. If a covered
pitfall is taken for solid ground, the animal is trapped. A danger is sometimes
hidden­environment, poison ivy is frequently mistaken for ivy. In an artificial
environment, acid can be mistaken for water.”
Detecting misperceived affordances and resulting false beliefs is crucial for effective col­
laborative robots. For instance, if a warehouse robot knows the recently received inven­
tory fits in the current room but observes a worker attempting to move it elsewhere, the
robot should infer the worker’s false belief and inform them that action is unnecessary.
The ability to detect and address false beliefs is further enhanced in collaborative contexts,
where studies have shown that individuals adjust their action planning and perception of
affordances based on what they and their collaborators can achieve together, highlighting
the integration of joint attention, intention recognition, and group­based affordance per­
ception in collaborative contexts [17]. The way individuals perceive the world profoundly
influences how they believe they can interact with it, and consequently, how they do in­
teract.
Gibson defends that humans learn to focus only on the critical information from the envi­
ronment through the education of attention, less noticing irrelevant stimulus [16]. Atten­
tional mechanisms prioritize specific stimuli and focus cognitive resources where they are
most needed at a given moment [18]. By bridging the gap between the actual available
affordances in the environment and their detection and interpretation by the observer,
attention plays a crucial role in shaping how actions are planned.
Human attention system is grouped in three different components: alertness, selectivity
and processing capacity [19]. Alertness is responsible to keep the individual optimally
sensitive and ready to external stimuli requiring priority. This readiness reduces the re­
action time to environment changes requiring immediate response [18]. Selectivity is the
ability to filter the most useful information from the environment based on the current sit­
uation. Studies show that individuals directing focus attention on relevant sensory events
enhance neural responses and diminishes responses to the unattended ones [19]. Pro­

cessing capacity is related to the finite amount of cognitive resources available to process
information. While selectivity manages which stimuli should be handled, the processing
capacity manages how much and how efficiently these stimuli or tasks must be handled
[19].
The three components work dynamically to balance readiness, prioritization, and effi­
cient resource allocation. For instance, in a highly alert state, such as escaping from
a lion, selectivity narrows focus on specific stimuli, such as identifying relevant proper­
ties in the environment that facilitate escape, and processing capacity adjusts to ensure
that these stimuli are effectively handled. The interaction between these components
highlight their functions and differences. When a target is detected in the environment, it
influences other cognitive processes prejudicing the ability to handle other tasks or stimuli
[18]. This demonstrates the limited capacity of the human attention system and underlines
the importance of the selection ability to focal attention in processing capacity allocation.
Interestingly, monitoring multiple events minimally interferes with other cognitive compo­
nents, unless some stimuli captures human’s focus [18]. This clarifies some distinctions
regarding two attention states: one where the selectivity influences and the attention is
clearly oriented to some event; another where alertness maintains without focusing on any
specific object or event. These states illustrate how the attention system adapts to the
environment demands and profoundly influences how an individual perceives the world
around. In multi­agent environments, attention plays a crucial role in shared activities.
Joint attention is crucial to guarantee effective collaboration between humans as it is able
to provide a kind of a perceptual common ground, enabling the involved agents to share
representations from the attended objects and events [20]. Shared representation from
relevant objects enhances the group adaptation to the sudden environment changes [20].
In addition, studies also suggest that coordinated mental representation is able to trigger
predictive mechanisms regarding other agents’ actions and improve assigning cognitive
states to others [21]. These capabilities can profoundly impact the cooperation between
agents contributing to achieve better results on their common task. Besides this, inferring
other agents’ attention focus provides significant hints about the others’ goals improving
the observer’s capability to effectively contribute [20]. Understanding goals and intentions
is foundational to reason about what others are actually doing because the same physical
action can be interpreted in many different ways from the observer perspective [22]. In
crowded parking lots this phenomenon occurs frequently. While searching for a parking
space, finding a car with the engine turned on at a parking space with someone in the
driver’s seat creates uncertainty. The key question is whether the driver intends to leave,
cooperating with the current pursued goal, or whether he just parked the car, meaning the
search must continue. In this scenario, the ability to discern about other driver’s intentions
and goals would resolve the uncertainty contributing to a better cooperation in this shared
environment.
It is important to clarify the differences between goals and intentions. A goal usually refers
to a desired state of the environment in the future or to the internal representation of that
same state [22]. In contrast, Bratman’s [23] notion of intention is not merely the desired
outcome, but the mental state representing a commitment to a specific course of action
aimed at achieving that outcome. Intentions are described as a partially hierarchized and
future­directed reasoning process where beliefs play a crucial role in shaping this hierar­
chy. This mechanism guides which actions or considerations should take precedence and
which can be postponed, based on the agent’s current understanding of the environment
[23].

In the context of artificial intelligence, intentions are particularly significant as they embody
the agent’s commitment to action plannings, bridging the gap between high­level goals
and what is required to achieve them. This commitment serves as a guiding principle for
decision­making and a planning tool, ensuring coherence and coordination over time [23].
Understanding someone’s goal provides the observer with clarity about their desired out­
come, while understanding their intentions gives insights about how they plan and what
they are willing to do to achieve it.
Example 2.2. Suppose a scenario where 2 individuals, an experienced chef and a novice
cook, share the same goal: preparing the best dinner possible for their loved ones. In this
case, their intentions will diverge completely. The expert trusts in his experience and culi­
nary knowledge to execute his action plan, while the novice will strictly follow the recipes
and instructions avoiding necessity to adapt and unexpected outcomes. For a collabora­
tive robot aiming to cooperate on this, understanding these differences is essential due
to the necessity to adjust to each individual’s strategy.
In cooperative environments, recognizing that other agents are also guided by their own
intentions, leads to shared intentionality which emerges as the most efficient approach
to achieve a common goal. Multiple agents are considered to share intentionality when,
beyond pursuing the same goal, they also coordinate their plans assigning complemen­
tary action roles to achieve the desired common result [22]. For instance, consider the
novice cook and the experienced chef as brothers, with the same shared goal, preparing
a Christmas dinner for their loved ones. To achieve their shared goal, they must align their
intentions and ensure shared intentionality regarding how the cooking tasks will be man­
aged. If they maintain their initial intentions, the chef improvising and the novice rigidly
following the recipes, the result unlikely will reach their shared expectations. By coordi­
nating their tasks and assigning complimentary roles, they can combine their strengths
and ensure the desired outcome is achieved.
The attribution of complementary functions is a complex process, and even more com­
plicated in implicit coordination scenarios. It requires a mutual understanding of the dif­
ferences between agents to enable consensus on role distribution for the shared task
[22]. In a series of experiments, Richardson and colleagues [24] explored how pairs of
participants collaborated to lift planks of varying lengths from a conveyor belt. Figure
2.1 illustrates this experimental setup. The task required participants to switch from lift­
ing individually to jointly handling the planks when the length exceeded their ability to lift
alone. The findings revealed that individuals adapt their perception of affordances based
not only on their own capabilities but also on those of their collaborators. For example,
pairs of participants lifting planks from a conveyor belt transitioned to joint lifting based on
the length of the planks and their combined arm spans [24]. This highlights how group
resources and awareness of others’ capabilities influence the perception of affordances
and collaborative decision­making.
In addition, joint attention is fundamental to achieve the common desired outcome, as it
guarantees common representation from the shared attended events. Without this com­
ponent, collaboration is profoundly harmed. From about 12 to 14 months, humans infant’s
shared intentionality and joint attention emerge, having a crucial contribution to the ability
of establishing complementary roles in the cooperation between the child and adult. This
interaction between shared intentionality and perception is essential for the infant’s de­
velopment, facilitating learning and integration into the social and culture world they grow
into [22].

2.3
Dynamic Epistemic Logic
Epistemic Logic provides a formal framework to symbolize agents’ knowledge and beliefs,
whose work is often attributed to the Finnish philosopher and logician Jaakko Hintikka [25].
However, knowledge and beliefs are dynamic because agents acquire new information.
Thus, an extension from Epistemic Logic was developed to model how knowledge and
beliefs change over time. This extension is called Dynamic Epistemic Logic (DEL). There
are many different notations for this combination of dynamic logic with epistemic logic, but
the system adopted in this dissertation is the one proposed by Hans van Ditmarsch [26].
Example 2.3 illustrates the complexity of reasoning not only about agents’ knowledge
of the domain, but also about their knowledge of other agents’ knowledge, referred to
as second­order knowledge. This multi­layered reasoning may be crucial in multi­agent
environments, which makes it essential to represent it.
Example 2.3.
Imagine three kids ­ Anne, Alice and Bob ­ decide to play hide and
seek while waiting for their parents. In the first round, Anne is the seeker, while Alice and
Bob must hide. Before hiding, Alice and Bob inform each other about their hiding spots,
planning to collaborate to prevent Anne from finding them. Alice is hiding on top of the
tree and Bob is hiding in the garage.
In this scenario, we can conclude the following:
• Anne doesn’t know where Alice and Bob are hiding.
• Alice knows both where she and Bob are hiding.
• Bob knows both where he and Alice are hiding.
• Alice knows that Bob knows where they are both hiding.
• Bob knows that Alice knows where they are both hiding.
• Anne doesn’t know Bob and Alice know where each other is hiding.
Second­order knowledge is sufficient to illustrate the relevance of epistemic reasoning
in this practical example. The introduction of DEL allows for the representation of the

future events.

2.3.1
Language
The language adopted in this dissertation does not incorporate a knowledge operator,
instead a belief operator is employed. This distinction is essential to represent agents’
mental states accurately. It requires not only accounting for their knowledge, facts that
the agents are certain of, but also their beliefs, which may or not align with reality. If a
belief aligns with reality it constitutes knowledge. However, agents may hold false beliefs
which will also influence on their actions justifying the relevance to represent them.
Prior to the presentation of the formal definitions of the basic concepts adopted in the
DEL, it is relevant to clarify the distinction between atoms, literals and propositions.
An atom (or atomic proposition) is a basic, indivisible unit of meaning. It represents a
simple statement that cannot be broken down into smaller components. Atoms might
represent the smallest building blocks of thought or belief that individuals can hold .
A literal is a specific instance of an atom that includes either the atom itself (positive literal)
or its negation (negative literal). Literals allow for the representation of both beliefs and
disbeliefs, enabling reasoning about what is true or false.
A proposition is a declarative statement that can be either true or false. Propositions can
range from single atomic statements to complex expressions involving multiple atoms and
logical connectives and quantifiers. Propositions are statements that can be evaluated as
true or false.
In ToM, these distinctions are important for understanding how humans and artificial sys­
tems represent and manipulate information, beliefs, and knowledge. Atoms and literals
serve as the foundational elements, while propositions enable the articulation of more
complex reasoning and mental states.
The following definitions are relative to a given finite set of agents Ag and a set of propo­
sitional atoms At.
In Example 2.3: Ag = {Anne, Alice, Bob} and At = {a, b}, where a represents whether
Alice is hiding on top of the tree and b represents whether Bob is hiding in the garage.
a and b are both atoms and positive literals, while ¬a and ¬b are negative literals. The
formula a ∧b is an example of a proposition.

3.1
Theory of Mind using Dynamic Epistemic Logic
As technology advances, the integration of robots into dynamic multi­agents systems en­
vironments is becoming a priority. This shift has led to a growing demand for robots
capable of improving efficiency, reducing human workers risk and enhancing processes
flexibility [28]. To operate effectively in these environments, robots must not only exe­
cute tasks efficiently but also behave in a socially accepted manner that does not require
other agents to adapt their natural behavior. Achieving this requires robots to take into
account other agents’ perspectives and reason about them. This capability is known as
possessing a Theory of Mind (ToM) [1].
To implement ToM on robots, certain fundamental properties are essential regardless from
the specific approach that researchers choose to explore. These aspects are critical for
enhancing the robot’s perception of the environment, as perception is a key point in the
success of any ToM framework. A visual system, typically employed by multiple cameras
to allow 3D visual perception, is vital and usually complemented with feature detectors
adapted to suit the intended application domain. In addition to visual perception, audi­
tory systems utilizing microphones are commonly used to improve human­robot interac­
tion, enabling explicit communication with other agents. Relevant works such as Cynthia
Breazeal et al. [29] demonstrate how advanced vision systems amplified their robot’s,
Kismet, social ability to interact with humans. Similarly, Rachid Alami et al. [30] illustrates
the relevance of perception systems in human­aware navigation planners, highlighting
their role in enhancing human­robot coordination.
Considering perceptual systems as fundamental components, many different approaches
have been explored to build a ToM framework. Each approach aims to create a system
that closely imitates human ToM exploiting robot’s sensory and computational resources.
As referred in Section 2.1, Scassalleti [12] was a pioneer in exploring ToM in robotics, and
his implementation is heavily influenced by developmental psychology theories. His work
integrates some modules identified by Baron­Cohen [14] and Leslie [15] as essential for
human psychological development, focusing on identifying gaze direction through visual
attention and distinguishing between animate and inanimate agents. However, not all
approaches rely on psychology findings as the foundation of the system.
Another category of frameworks relies on probabilistic reasoning to predict agents’ be­
havior. For instance, bayesian inverse planning aims to understand agents’ goals and
beliefs from observed actions by inverting Markov decision problems [31].

A different perspective comes from Bolander and Dissing [1] who implemented ToM through

3.2
Attention in Dynamic Epistemic Logic
As explained so far, ToM is the ability to consider others’ perspectives of the environment
through reasoning about their beliefs, intentions and goals. Attention plays a crucial role
in this process, as it is the cognitive mechanism responsible to filter and prioritize the
information humans perceive and interpret. Attention’s selectivity, capacity limitation and
sense of effort are evident in our daily lives as they directly impact our perception [2]. Being
aware of these limitations is important to avoid underestimating how far our perception
deviates from reality. Nevertheless, humans often overlook the fact these same factors
also affect others. This misconception can lead to significant challenges when modeling
the shared and unshared knowledge in dynamic domains involving interactions with other
agents [2].
Bolander et al. [35] take the first step toward by integrating attention into a DEL frame­
work through attention atoms ha which represent whether agent a is paying attention. If
ha is true, agent a is attentive, which means aware of everything happening, including
any event and change in the environment. In case ha is false, agent a pays attention to
nothing, considered an inattentive agent. Belardinelli and Bolander [2] introduced Basic
Attentiveness and Inertia principles, based on attention atoms ha.
In their work [2], relevant events concerning a proposition are modeled as announce­
ments, assuming that all announcements are trustful and reliable. Following this, Basic
Attentiveness states that ”attentive agents believe what is announced” [2] , while Inertia
expresses that ”inattentive agents believe nothing happened, namely they maintain the
beliefs they had before the announcement was made” [2].
Their work [2] advances the modeling of attention into DEL, generalizing attention atoms
to simple propositional atoms p ∈At in the domain. hap is interpreted as ”agent a is paying
attention to whether p”, extending the earlier reasoning to individual atomic formulas.
To incorporate attention into a DEL framework, the definitions presented in Section 2.3
(Definition 1, Definition 2, Definition 3, Definition 26) have been modified to include the set
H = {hap : p ∈At, a ∈Ag} as part of the atomic formulas in the domain. This integration
enables the representation agent­specific, proposition­oriented attention states within the
epistemic logic framework. This provides a more realistic representation of attention,

To formalize partial learning, Belardinelli and Bolander [2] adapt the previously introduced
Inertia principle and introduce a key principle: Attentiveness.
Attentiveness expresses that if an agent a is paying attention to some proposition p, hap,
then when some announcement on p, a learns both the announced literal, l(p), and also
the attention atom regarding p, hap. In other words, agent a’s beliefs are updated both
about p and their own attention about p when an event that changes p occurs.
In contrast, Inertia now specifies that if an agent a is not paying attention to p, ¬hap, then
a learns nothing about p. This means their beliefs regarding p and his awareness about
p remain the same when some event relevant to p occurs [2].

The integration of attention shifts into attention modeling is fundamental for representing
human’s perception in dynamic environments. Without incorporating attention shifts into
a DEL framework, the analysis of perception would remain static. This would result in
the same as capturing a snapshot and analyzing the agents’ awareness at that exact
instant, rather than accounting for the continuous changes and updates on the agents’
attentional states. Belardinelli and Bolander [3] divide attention shifts into two different
kinds, top­down attention and bottom­up attention changes.
Top­down attention change refers to the goal­oriented or voluntary shifts in attention.
These changes are triggered by an agent’s decision, motivated by his current goals as
they are considered a conscious act. Such shifts are assumed to be private attention
changes, meaning that only the agent performing the attention shift is aware of that
change, while others do not perceive it. This is modeled through an event model con­
taining two events: e1, the designated event representing the learning of +hap and e2
representing no changes. Due to the privacy of this attention shift, agent a is the only
one accessing e1 and not accessing e2. Depending from the circumstances, implicit co­
ordination mechanisms or gaze direction may allow other observers to correctly predict
voluntary attention shift. However such cases are not considered by the authors.
Bottom­up attention change addresses stimulus­driven or involuntary shifts in attention.
An unexpected loud sound, a sudden bright light or movements in peripheral field of vision
are examples of events that involuntary capture humans’ attention. Bottom­up attention
is modeled based on the assumption these shifts are public: every agent’s attention is
drawn to the stimulus and all agents are also aware that others’ attention shifts have
occurred. This shared awareness extends beyond the attention shift itself, as ”attention­
grabbing literals” [3] are also learned, ensuring all agents acquire knowledge about the
newly attended literals. The authors do not describe how this event model should be
modeled.

basic approach by defining a upper boundary m on the amount of atoms an agent can

3.3
Human Cognition and implicit coordination
Understanding how humans perceive, plan and execute actions is essential for improving
collaboration among agents in complex environments. As described in Sections 2.1 and
2.2, implicit coordination relies on mechanisms such as anticipation and joint attention,
both requiring sophisticated cognitive processes. Exploring the relation between human
cognition and action is fundamental to enhance collaborative robots’ interaction with hu­
mans, improving their adaptability and collaboration skills.
Donald Norman has contributed to significant advancements in human­machine interac­
tion bridging the gap between psychology and design through his work on cognition and
action. In his work [40], Norman introduces two cognitive processes behind action:
• The three levels of processing to represent different layers of cognition, each playing
a crucial role in action and decision­making.
• The seven stages of action which is described as a ”simplified action cycle” [40] that
provides valuable insights to understand human action.
The cognitive processing is composed of three distinct layers: visceral, behavioral and
reflective. These layers operate at different levels of awareness and interaction with the
environment, collectively shaping how individuals perceived and engage with that same
environment.
The visceral level governs immediate perception and rapid, unconscious reactions driven
by the visceral system, often referred as ”the lizard brain”. Learning at this level is minimal
occurring primarily through increased or reduced sensitivity. Adapting to clutch control in
combustion cars is a clear example of the visceral layer’s role in learning. Its strong con­
nection to the motor system, closely tied to the muscular system, is crucial to guarantee
synchronization between the mind and body [40].
The behavioral level is more conscious, involving actions and learned skills triggered by
the recognition of familiar patterns [40]. At this level, while actions are executed and
perceived mostly without conscious effort, we remain aware of the actions and their goals,
though not the finer details. Driving on a highway illustrates this, as actions like passing
a car or slowing for a toll are learned by pattern recognition from the environment.
The reflective level represents the cognitive processing period where analysis of the per­
ceived environment and decision making occur. This level operates deeply and slowly,
typically following or anticipating events, as a reflection on their causes or regarding the
prediction of the future outcomes [40]. Planning a drive from Copenhagen to Oslo exem­
plifies this, as it requires considering ferry schedules and traffic conditions in advance.
Norman divides the seven stages of action into three phases [40]:
1. Goal Formation ­ The first phase includes forming the goal.
2. Execution ­ The second phase consists of three stages focused on action planning
and execution.

In the first phase ­ forming the goal ­ there are no relevant distinctions between the novice
and the skilled driver. However, during the execution phase, the effects of learned expe­

During the planning stage, the driver analyzes the environment and make decisions ac­

driver becomes aware of the necessary action sequence and its purpose, activating the
behavioral level. At this point, pattern recognition and prior knowledge play a crucial role,
underscoring the distinction between the novice and the skilled driver during the execution

is activated, triggering the muscular response and enabling to execute the turn. Details
such as the clutch pedal control referred before, can be crucial for the novice driver at this

3.4
Methods to infer human attention
In Section 2.2, the tight relationship between attention, intention and affordances was
thoroughly explored. These connections motivated the investigation into methods for in­
ferring human attention to enhance the representation of attention states within the DEL
framework. Initially, it was inspected how the relationship with intention could contribute
to this effort. However, recognizing intention proved challenging as it represents a men­
tal commitment to an action or plan. Consequently, the research was turned to classical
planning approaches, specifically studying how goal and plan recognition could support
human attention inference.
Mirsky, along with Keren and Geib [44] provide precise definitions for goal recognition:
”Given a sequence of (usually noise free) symbolic observations of actions
to produce a (usually unstructured) label from a fixed set that identifies the
high­level goal of the observed agent for given time windows”
and plan recognition:
”Given an ordered set of observed actions execute by an agent and a set of
plans to be recognized (including the goal of the plan), infer which plans the
agent is executing, where they are in the execution of those plans, and the
goal the plan is being executed in furtherance of”
They denote goal recognition as a sub­problem of plan recognition. Figure 3.8 highlights
the distinctions between goal and plan recognition. While, goal recognition focuses on
identifying which goal (G1, G2, G3, G4) the agent is pursuing based on observed actions
starting from their initial position, plan recognition aims to identify the specific path the
agent is taking to reach the pursued goal. The diagram contains multiple possible paths
leading to the same goal, showing how successful plan recognition provides more detailed
information about the observed agent compared to successful goal recognition.

The most recent and complex algorithms [46, 47] result in a probability distribution over
the possible goals. However, this introduces challenges regarding domain adaptability as
assigning probability values to each possible goal requires precise data for every envi­
ronment the algorithm is applied. Small changes in certain environments can significantly
impact probability values, requiring the algorithm to have a detailed description of the
environment to ensure accurate data assignment.
Given these limitations of probability­based approaches, the investigation was directed to
alternative methods for inferring human attention that do not heavily depend from data.
One promising direction arose from exploring landmark­based methods. In the planning
literature, landmarks are actions or facts that must be executed at some point in every
possible plan to achieve a particular goal. Figure 3.8 identifies landmarks with green
dots, representing indispensable positions to reach certain goals.For instance, if an agent
aims to achieve G1 or G2, he must pass through position L1. Applying this concept to
attention inference, if an agent is setting the dinner table and the cutlery is only stored
in a specific cabinet, that cabinet acts as a landmark. The agent will inevitably turn his
attention to that cabinet at some point, providing cues about his subsequent actions.
A primary challenge of a landmark­based approach is to assign landmarks to each goal.
The algorithm introduced by Hoffman [48] can accomplish this, but scalibility issues arise
when ensuring soundness. For each candidate landmark, the goal is tested for solvabil­
ity without including the evaluated candidate landmark into the action plan. If the goal
is unsolvable without that candidate landmark, it is considered a landmark. However,
this process does not scale well in complex domains, as ensuring soundness requires
extensive computational effort. This highlights the trade­off between accuracy and effi­
ciency in landmark­based goal recognition, underscoring the need for scalable solutions

The investigation explored alternative approaches to infer human attention, leading to
a deeper research on the relationship between affordances and attention shifts. This
connection is strongly supported by Tom McClelland [17], who introduces the concept of
attentional affordances suggesting that as affordances represent possibilities for action:
”attentional affordances are possibilities for acts of attention” [17].
McClelland’s work [17] highlights the importance of affordances and attention in action
selection due to the phenomenology of availability. For instance, if a heavy cement brick is
placed on top of other, the one on top is perceived as ’grippable’, while the one underneath
is not (until the top brick is removed). This reasoning about action availability precedes
the physical relationship indicating that one brick is on top of other, explaining why the
one in the bottom cannot be gripped. Attention shapes action selection by addressing
the limitations of human attention capacity. Since humans cannot focus on everything at
once, attention filters the perceived possibilities for action, directing focus toward what
appears most promising for achieving the desired goal [17].
McClelland connects affordances to attention shifts, citing studies showing that the motor
system can be triggered when an afforded action is perceived; this phenomenon is known
as potentiation. Potentiation varies across perceived affordances and is influenced by
many factors. When faced with a tea pot and biscuits and intending to consume both, the
distance of each item impacts significantly the action sequence. If the tea pot is closer,
it may be reached first, followed by the biscuits. Applying this to attentional affordances,
potentiation plays a crucial role in attention shifts. The relevance assigned to each object,
property or region in the environment is shaped by various factors such as long­term
habits, relevance to achieve desired goals or context [17]. Recognizing how these factors
influence potentiation is crucial for predicting attention shifts.
At this point, it is important to clarify why evaluating affordances in the environment ap­
pears to be a more comprehensive approach than traditional methods in artificial intelli­
gence, such as classical planning, for inferring human attention shifts. As described by
Norvig and Russel’s [41], in classical planning, actions are represented through action
schemas, which define actions in terms of explicit preconditions and effects. While this
is effective for planning, as it is focused on feasibility and consequences of actions, it
does not account many external factors that influence human attention. These factors
include saliences in the environment, misperceived properties in the environment or mis­
judgments about the effects of actions. Unlike action schemas, which rely on rigorous
preconditions and effects, humans evaluate their actions and the surroundings relevance
through affordances.
Example 3.4.
Consider a scenario where a man, who is a novice cook, is cooking and
needs a knife to cut a chicken. He has two knives equally close to him: knife 1 and knife
2, illustrated in Figure 3.9. Knife 1 is large, shiny and has an ergonomic handle, but it is
completely dull. Knife 2 is smaller, with a slightly damaged handle and faded appearance,
but it is sharp enough to cut. From the perspective of classical planning, knife 2 satisfies
the precondition of sharpness to execute the action cut. But this is not necessarily how
humans perceive the world and operate. In this case, the man’s attention might be shifted
to knife 1. Despite knife 1’s inability to execute the intended action, its visual may create
a stronger potentiation effect, particularly for a novice cook.
This example demonstrates that human attention and action evaluation are not solely
driven by the explicit preconditions of actions, as in classical planning, but are shaped by

affordances and their perceived relevance in the environment.
The study of affordances has become crucial for understanding the various factors that
contribute to attention shifts. The concept of affordances, introduced by James J. Gibson
[16] led to adaptations and expansions by numerous researchers. The definitions sup­
ported by Tony Chemero [49] and Donald Norman [40], which describe affordances as
relations between agent’s ability and environment properties, were vital for understand­
ing how affordances can help inferring attentional focus.
Chemero [49] describes events as changes in the affordances setup that define the animal­
environment relationship. These changes can occur due to modifications in the properties
of the environment or changes in an animal’s abilities. For example, human infants ini­
tially cannot afford walking during the early months of their life, but around 18 months,
this affordance emerges as their abilities develop. Similarly, if a chair breaks, it no longer
affords sitting, illustrating how changes in the environment can alter affordances.
This perspective on events as changes in the affordances layout is vital for inferring at­
tention. One of attention’s primary functions is to shift toward relevant regions in the
environment, ensuring that an agent’s internal representation of the environment remains
closely aligned with the environment’s current state.
Norman, whose approach focuses on design­engineering and usability, presents the con­
cept signifier [40]:
”For me, the term signifier refers to any mark or sound, any perceivable indi­
cator that communicates appropriate behavior to a person.”
Signifiers indicate which actions are possible and how to interact correctly. When com­
bined with attention, eye gaze and pointing gestures can function as signifiers, directing
others’ attention to the relevant stimuli, essentially communicating where attention should
be focused. Just as the arrows on a keyboard inform users about buttons’ function, human
infants capture their caretakers’ attention through pointing gestures to relevant objects or
regions [42]. However, pointing does not guarantee the observer attends the intended
aspect. If I point to a sharp red knife, will the observer focus on its redness, sharpness,
both or neither? As previously discussed, such details depend on various factors which
will influence the attention­grabbing stimuli of each object or region in the environment.

Methodology
This chapter outlines the work developed and the contributions made in this dissertation.
Since the foundational Theory of Mind (ToM) framework implemented by Bolander and
Dissing [1], described in Section 3.1, serves as the starting point for this dissertation,
throughout this chapter it will be referred to as Foundational Framework. The contribu­
tions detailed in this dissertation, particularly in this chapter, extend and build upon the
Foundational Framework [1], culminating in what will be referred to as the Attentional
Framework. When the term ’DEL framework’ is used, it specifically refers to the DEL
component of the Attentional Framework, distinct from the robotic system component,
which is also included in the framework.
Several implementation decisions from the Foundational Framework [1] were retained to
facilitate the integration of new advancements. To ensure compatibility with these retained
decisions, some of the newly introduced extended mechanisms required adaptations and
simplifications.
This methodology chapter begins by detailing the integration of attention mechanisms,
as introduced by Belardinelli and Bolander [2, 3] into the Foundational Framework. The
integration of attention dynamics incorporates attention selectivity, enabling agents to fo­
cus on subsets of atomic formulas and dynamically adjust their attentional states through
attention shifts, as outlined in Section 3.2. It is important to note that implemented adap­
tations and extensions to the attention shift mechanisms introduced by Belardinelli and
Bolander [3] are presented in 4.2, following the Dynamic Epistemic Logic (DEL) definitions
introduced in Section 2.3. Meanwhile, these same extensions, when based on the event
model Definition 6 from their work [2], are discussed in Section 4.6.
Following the implementation of the attention model within the Foundational Framework
[1], this chapter proceeds to describe the implementation of pointing actions into the
robotic system, detailing how these actions are modeled within the attention dynamics.
It is followed by the necessary adaptations to the generalized version of Sally­Anne task
experiment, providing a comparison to the Foundational Framework [1]. For simplicity,
the generalized version of the Sally­Anne task will be just referred as Sally­Anne task
throughout this work.
Building upon the advancements achieved through attention integration, a strategy to
infer human attention based on affordances is proposed, aiming to predict attention shifts
at specific moments. This approach draws on the literature and concepts explored in
Sections 3.3 and 3.4. The chapter concludes by describing additional work that were not
integrated into the main contributions, either due to its lack of relevant to the implemented
mechanisms or to the experiments presented in Section 5.
This project was developed in C++, aligning with the Foundational Framework [1], which
was primarily implemented in the same language. The complete codebase, including
detailed documentation, is publicly available on GitHub at R2DTU­Attention Repository.

Initial epistemic state
When the domain within the DEL framework is defined, it is necessary to create the initial
state of the model, to have the initial epistemic representation of the intended scenario.
Following the implementation decision from Foundational Framework [1], the creation of
the initial state is determined by specifying the set of positive literals that hold in the this
state. A set of atoms add ⊆At ∪H is specified, where for any p ∈add, ℓ(p) = p holds,
and for any p /∈add, ℓ(p) = ¬p in the designated world of the DEL framework. This results
in an epistemic model where there is no uncertainty regarding any atom. Furthermore,
it is assumed that inattentive agents always believe that for any unattended atom, the
corresponding negative literals hold. Representing the epistemic representation of the
DEL framework as the pointed Kripke model (M, w): (M, w) |= ¬hap →(M, w) |= Ba¬p.
The integration of attention atoms, along with the modeling of joint attention and inatten­
tional blindness, is fundamental to understand how the initial epistemic state of a specified
domain or scenario is constructed, as demonstrated through Example 4.1.
Incorporating these properties into the DEL framework required additional processing to
handle the creation of the initial state of the DEL domain. This processing involves map­
ping propositional atoms to the agents who do not pay attention to those atoms. This
mapping is called False Attention Map. The False Attention Map represents the primary
extension of this dissertation to the method used for constructing the initial state of the
DEL framework, in comparison to the Foundational Framework.

Attentiveness and Inertia
So far, I have described fundamental properties and details necessary to establish the ini­
tial state within the DEL framework. However, Attentiveness and Inertia are the principles
that shape the most relevant contribution of integrating attention into ToM: the selectivity
of the attention mechanism and its impact on each agents’ perception of the surrounding
environment. The Propositional Attention Event Model F(φ) (Definition 13) models these
principles, as detailed in Section 3.2.

It is important to recognize that the DEL version adopted and implemented in the Founda­
tional Framework [1] is not directly compatible with the event model Definition 6 adopted
in [2]. Due to the complexity of F(φ) and the divergence between these two contributions,
the decision was made not to implement F(φ). Instead, Attentiveness and Inertia were
implemented using alternative approaches that are more aligned with the Foundational

significant changes are addressed to implement Attentiveness and Inertia within the DEL
framework.
Considering this, the first significant change refers to the Attentiveness principle. Atten­
tiveness states that attentive agents learn both the announced literal and and their own
attention directed to that literal. Following Definition 3, events are defined by postcondi­
tions. This introduces a challenge: each agent would require a unique event with distinct
postconditions, given that learned attention literals are agent­specific. This proliferation
of events within an event model increases model complexity and introduces scalability
challenges. To address these issues, I opted to substitute Attentiveness with the Basic
Propositional Attentiveness principle.
Basic Propositional Attentiveness functions similarly to Basic Attentiveness, described
in Section 3.2, but accounts for proposition­oriented attention atoms, like Attentiveness.
Basic Propositional Attentiveness expresses that agents attentive to a specific proposition
believe the announced literal from that proposition but do not learn their own attentiveness.

the integration of attention dynamics within the DEL framework, Propositional Attention

Attention shifts
As explained in Section 3.2, attention shifts are crucial for representing the progression
of agents’ mental states in dynamic environments, where various stimuli capture agents’
attention at different moments.
While Belardinelli and Bolander’s work [3] captures key properties necessary to incorpo­
rate attention shifts into the DEL framework, it simplifies certain complexities. This sim­
plification motivated to expand upon their work, aiming to integrate additional essential
properties that enhance the attentional representation of the agents involved.
It is important to note that the representation of top­down attention and bottom­up attention
event models were presented orally as referenced in the ”Additional contributions” section
of [3]. However, these formalizations have not been published in an academic paper,
leaving the slides from Belardinelli and Bolander’s talk as the only available source for
these event models representation. To ensure clarity and accessibility, the respective
slides are included in Figures 4.1 and 4.2, presenting the formalization of these two event
models. The information provided in these slides, combined with details from [3], serves
as the foundation for developing the proposed implementation of attention shifts within
the DEL framework.
Bottom­Up attention shift
The bottom­up attention shift is denoted by the event model F(φ, G), as illustrated and
described in Figure 4.1. It is important to clarify that F(φ) is the Propositional Attention
Event Model, whose definition was provided in Definition 13.
As previously mentioned, the implementation includes modifications compared to the for­
malization of the bottom­up attention.
These changes result in two different types of
bottom­up attention shifts: minimal bottom­up attention and expanded bottom­up atten­

I consider it relevant to model attention shifts where newly attended literals are not learned,

The following example does not illustrate public attention shifts, but highlight the necessity

Example 4.5.
Consider two propositions: t, representing whether a ball is on top of
a table, and u, representing whether a ball is inside a box. When attention is shifted to
t, the literal is immediately learned: either t, if the ball is on top of the table, or ¬t if it is
not. In contrast, if attention is shifted to u, the literal regarding whether the ball is inside
or not remains unknown. However, if an event occurs, such as someone opening the box
and removing or placing the ball, the resulting literal from this change is learned by the
attentive agent.
This comparison demonstrates that paying attention to a proposition does not necessarily
mean the newly attended literal is learned. With the distinction between the two imple­
mented bottom­up attention shifts clarified, the key distinction between these and the
original bottom up attention will now be explored.
The primary change from the original bottom­up attention to the implemented versions
concerns the universality imposed by the original formalization. The original bottom­up
attention shift is not formalized only as a public attention shift, where all agents are aware
of the attention shift, but also as an universal attention shift, all agents turn their attention
to the same external stimulus. However, public attention shifts do not inherently require
to be universal attention shifts. The following example is relevant to illustrate why a public
attention shift is not necessarily universally applied to a given proposition.

Pointing
At this point the attention modeling implemented in Attentional Framework represents
agents a ∈Ag focus on propositional atoms p ∈At within the domain through attention
atoms hap ∈H, and models attention shifts that naturally occur in dynamic environments,
through diverse top­down and bottom­up attention event models. In collaborative tasks,
understanding other agents’ awareness is essential for accommodating and adapting to
their actions, as previously mentioned. However, at times, other agents demonstrate
lacking relevant information, requiring clarifications. In implicit coordination, such clarifi­
cations can be achieved by directing others’ attention to relevant important aspects of the
environment.
The pointing gesture functions as the most direct action to effectively guide others’ atten­

the robotic system within the Attentional Framework. However, to reinforce the attention
target, body pose and gaze were also implemented, ensuring that the robot’s entire body
language aligns with the intended goal. This combination creates a clearer and more
natural interaction.

4.5
Affordances to infer Human Attention
The integration of principles for attention dynamics (Section 4.1) and attention shifts (Sec­
tion 4.2) into the DEL framework enables the representation of agents’ attention states in
dynamic environments. Additionally, pointing (Section 4.3) provides a mechanism for the
robot to ensure other agents’ attention is directed to relevant information in crucial situ­
ations, representing it in the attention model. But how can the robot infer each agents’
attention target without direct intervention? How can agents’ attention be inferred to up­
date their mental states representations effectively? These questions are essential for
enabling the robot to anticipate other agents’ actions and enhance its adaptive skills.
Affordances and events
As described in Section 3.3, each stage of action is associated with a cognitive process­
ing level, reflecting how an agent is influenced by primitive impulses (visceral), recog­
nized learned patterns (behavioral), or deeper reasoning (reflective) at different moments
during interaction with the environment. Since the aim is to infer human attention at spe­
cific moments, classical planning is well­suited when the agent is guided by the reflective
processing level. However, it becomes less valuable in stages of action dominated by
behavioral and visceral processing levels, as illustrated in Example 3.4.
As described in Section 3.4, affordances, through the potentiation effect and phenomenol­
ogy of availability, provide valuable cues to anticipate human behavior and to infer human
attention, when behavioral or visceral levels are guiding human’s action.
I consider affordances to be decisive to infer attention shifts in response to events in the
environment. While it is evident that an agent does not necessarily shift attention solely
when events occur, attention shifts during static periods are not considered in this ap­
proach. Following Chemero [49], events are understood as changes in the affordance
layout. Consequently, attention shifts are evaluated whenever the landscape of affor­
dances perceived by the human is modified.
Affordances relations
Relations between affordances can be established to anticipate and infer attention shifts.
For an agent operating with high rationality, most actions aim to create new possibilities
for interacting with the environment to achieve the desired goal. For example, if an agent
Attention for Implicit Coordination in Human­Robot Interaction
57
==PAGE:66==
opens a cabinet, it is likely that his attention will shift to one of the items stored and sub­
sequently interact with it. Similarly, if the agent picks up a glass, his attention will likely
shift towards a drink to fill the glass.
If a single new affordance becomes available after an agent’s action, his attention may
shift towards the object associated with that affordance. However, if the shift does not
occur it could be due to one of the following reasons:
• Unexpected outcome from the action execution: The agent expected different new
possibilities for action, but false beliefs may lead to unnecessary actions. For exam­
ple, if a dish is needed, the agent may open the cabinet where the dishes are usually
stored, only to find that all dishes are in the dishwasher. In this case, opening the
cabinet door fails to produce the expected outcome and the following attention shift.
• External factors: External events can redirect the agent’s focus. For instance, while
opening the dishwasher to grab a dish, the agent notices the rice pan is overflowing
and rushes to turn it off.
• Long­term habits and unconscious decisions: Habits and unconscious, visceral­
level decisions heavily influence how an agent executes actions and shifts attention.
For instance, if the agent needs both a dish and cutlery, opening the dishwasher
results in agent affording to grab both objects. However, after picking up the dish,
the agent might choose to pour the rice from the pan into the dish, as it is another
new possibility for action, instead of also retrieving the cutlery. Such decisions may
even be unpredictable to the agent themselves.
Establishing relations between affordances is challenging due to these factors, as illus­
trated by the examples above. However, as a starting point for developing methods to
infer human attention shifts, simpler scenarios can be considered. In such scenarios, it
is assumed that when new affordances become available, at least one of them will be
required for subsequent actions, requiring the human’s focus in the near future.
The first relation between affordances emphasizes the phenomenology of availability of
new affordances in the environment. This is referred to as the causality relation between
affordances, defined as follows:
1. The agent performs an action that utilizes affordance f1.
2. As a result of this action, a new affordance f2 becomes available in the environment.
3. f1 →f2: The availability of f2 is causally linked to the action associated with f1.
The causality relation is a key assumption to predict that, following an event, the agent’s
attention is directed towards the items associated with newly available affordances, nar­
rowing the uncertainty about the agent’s attentional focus. While this is a simplification, it
is evident not all such items will necessarily capture the agent’s attention, only a subset
of them may do so.

hmp. As they assign different default values to the propositional atom l, epistemic state
s1 reflects this divergence.
Example 4.12 highlights the challenge of implementing the Defaulting principle into the
DEL framework. In the Propositional Attention implementation, event accessibility condi­
tions were dictated by agents’ attention propositions. However, with the introduction of
Defaulting, default values must also be considered. This is necessary because agents
who do not attend to the same proposition, but hold different default values cannot have
their beliefs update handled by the same event, as they learn different literals, as demon­
strated by the father and mother in Example 4.12.
To address these challenges, I opted for a simpler implementation of default values. In­
stead of each agent a ∈Ag assigning a unique default value to each proposition p (da(p)),
a shared default value d(p) for each proposition p was implemented. This adjustment re­
solves the challenge described as all inattentive agents experience the same belief update
for a given proposition. Consequently, the accessibility conditions need only to consider
attention propositions. With this simplification, the scenario presented in Example 4.12
cannot be modeled, representing a regression relative to the adopted formalisms.
Regarding its implementation, due to this simplification, it is a simple modification into
the DEL framework. Integrating simplified Defaulting, replaces inertia and when an agent
does not attend some event regarding atom p, it is learned d(p), which can have the same
result as Inertia, if d(p) = ⊤.

Evaluation
Throughout the experiment, the Attentional Framework accurately represented each agents’
beliefs, based on the assumption that agents are attentive to all propositional atoms when
present and inattentive when absent. However, due to this assumption, it was not possi­
ble to evaluate the framework’s ability to model partial learning; one of the primary con­
tributions of integrating attention mechanisms into Theory of Mind (ToM) models. This
assumption was necessary because the implemented methods to infer human attention
could not be applied in this situation. In spite of that, it is important to highlight that with
more versatile attention inference methods, such as gaze estimation, the integration of
attention mechanisms marks a significant step toward enabling partial learning.
From an experimental perspective, the most significant contribution of this dissertation,
compared to the Foundational Framework [1], lies in its approach to resolve false be­
liefs. The use of pointing to direct attention better aligns with implicit coordination envi­
ronments, as it focuses human attention on relevant information, reducing the need for
explicit communication and minimizing communication overhead. This approach acceler­
ates the clarification process. Additionally, the DEL modeling in the Attentional Framework
eliminates the need to reapply ontic actions to update the epistemic model after the false
belief is resolved, as described in 3.1. Instead, this update is achieved by applying the
conscious top­down attention (V({¬ln(r, 1)}, t, {ln(r, 1)})) to the agent holding the false
belief, specifically towards the propositional atom associated with that false belief, and
the expanded bottom­up attention shift (H({ln(r, 1)}, Ag, {ln(r, 1)})), which is applied to
all agents and triggered during the pointing action sequence. This contribution is sig­
nificant, as it allows the Attentional Framework to model the sequence of actions in the
environment more realistically and efficiently than before.

True belief scenario
The True Belief scenario is straightforward, without any false beliefs or unperceived phys­
ical events affecting any of the agents. Despite its simplicity, this scenario provides a
valuable opportunity to evaluate the attention inference method and the attention mecha­
nisms implemented in this dissertation, as the agents present are not always attentive to
all propositional atoms.
The detection of the attention shift in this scenario arises from the complementary affor­
dances between the apple (”cuttable”) and the knife (”cutting”). The Attentional Frame­
work, assumes that when Tiago holds the knife, his focus is likely to shift towards the
apple, as it is the only object in the environment with complementary affordance to the
knife. While some objects, such as the apple, can be used independently of other objects,
a knife typically requires a complementary affordance, such as ”cuttable”, to fulfill its pur­
pose, its functional affordance. This assumption demonstrates the framework’s ability to
infer attention shifts in particular contexts.
This scenario demonstrates the Attentional Framework’s ability to model Tiago’s con­
scious attention shift towards the propositional atom associated with the actual apple loca­
tion when he holds the knife. The robot, R2DTU, remains attentive to every propositional
atom as it has not left the scenario. Due to the joint attention mechanism employed in the
conscious top­down attention implementation, R2DTU is aware of Tiago’s attention shift.
In contrast, André remains inattentive to all propositional atoms since he reappeared, so
he does not notice Tiago’s attention shift. While the Attentional Framework does not pro­
vide a graphic representation of each agent’s attention states, the attentional states at the
moment of Tiago’s counscious attention shift (s4) can be verified through the figures in
Appendix A.5. These observations confirm the success of the joint attention mechanism
introduced in the conscious attention shift.
This scenario is also relevant to notice that when agents leave the scenario, modeled
through minimal bottom­up attention, the inattentional blindness phenomena, described
in Section 4.1 does not persist. This is because the implemented attention shifts only
account for conscious attention shifters, the agents who shift their attention are always
aware of that shift. Consequently, inattentional blindness phenomena, modeled in the
initial state for atoms that an agent is not attending, cannot re­emerge, once left the initial
inattention blindness. This is also demonstrated in Appendix A.5. Although inattentional
blindness was not represented throughout this experiment due to agents being initially
attentive to all propositional atoms, analyzing the effects of agents leaving the scenario
and reflecting on the implemented attention shifts supports this conclusion.
False belief scenario
Similar to the True belief scenario involving the knife, Tiago’s approach to the cup is inter­
preted as a conscious attention shift towards the propositional atom associated with the
bottle’s actual location. This interpretation arises from the complementary affordances
between a cup (’fillable’) and bottle (’pouring’), implying that Tiago is likely to approach
the bottle in the near future. The same justification presented in the True belief scenario
is applied to this case, a cup typically requires a complementary affordance, such as
”pouring” to fulfill its purpose, its functional affordance.
As R2DTU is fully attentive to the environment, due to the joint attention mechanism,
verified also in the True belief scenario, it detects Tiago’s intention when he approaches
the cup, modeled by a conscious top­down attention shift regarding the object of false
belief. Recognizing that Tiago mistakenly believes the bottle is inside box 3, R2DTU

6
Discussion and Future Work
The goal of this dissertation encompassed several interrelated objectives that were de­
veloped progressively.
The first objective was to integrate attention modeling, as proposed in [2, 3], into the
Theory of Mind (ToM) framework established in [1].
This integration began with the representation of attention atoms within the Dynamic Epis­
temic (DEL) model of the framework, enabling the modeling of relevant attention phenom­
ena, such as inattentional blindness and joint attention, from the initial state. To accom­
modate this, the concept of observability, previously modeled by observability atoms in
the DEL model, was removed due to incompatibility with the concept of attention.
Subsequently, attention dynamics were implemented into the framework, enabling the
modeling of partial learning. This extension was fundamental for capturing agents’ se­
lectivity, which is crucial for modeling and predicting agents’ beliefs and intentions, en­
hancing implicit coordination. However, discrepancies between the DEL versions used
in [2, 3] and [1] led to the decision of not implementing relevant mechanisms, such as
Propositional Attention Event F(φ). This decision resulted in regression compared to for­
malized principles, with the Attentiveness principle being replaced by Basic Propositional
Attentiveness. Consequently, agents do not learn about their own attentiveness when
attending to events and acquiring new information.
The integration of attention was concluded with the implementation and formalization of
attention shifts, which introduced significant advancements over the initially formalized
attention shifts in [3]. These contributions are essential to model the agents’ selectivity
in dynamic environments where changes in focus provide essential cues about agents’
beliefs, intentions, and needs. However, this implementation also revealed several chal­
lenges, particularly in formalizing the implemented mechanisms due to scalability issues.
Compared to the adapted formalizations, the implemented attention shifts demonstrated
limited versatility, compromising the integration of future attention mechanisms. This limi­
tation is evident in the need to formalize and implement similar attention shifts as separate,
unconnected mechanisms, while the adapted formalizations allowed for a more cohesive
and interconnected approach.
To address the limitations related to the first established goal, future work should focus
on the adaptation and implementation of the Propositional Attention Event F(φ). This
is crucial to resolve the scalability issues, enabling the implementation of fundamental
principles such as Attentiveness, and enhancing the versatility among the developed at­
tention shifts. Additionally, it will facilitate the integration of future relevant extensions into
the framework.
Another relevant direction for future work is the re­integration of observability atoms, with
a redefined concept. Instead of associating observability atoms between agents, they
could be linked between agents and propositional atoms within the epistemic model. This
approach would enable the framework to distinguish between merely shifting attention
and actively learning the current state versus learning only when relevant events occur
for the attended atom. This enhancement would result in a more robust and precise model
design, greatly improving the scalability and versatility of the DEL framework.

The second objective of this dissertation was to design and implement a method for human
attention inference based on potential cues in the environment.
This dissertation presented the design and implementation of an attention inference method
based on affordances perceived in the environment. The experiments demonstrated its
relevance in specific contexts and how the fundamental idea could offer valuable insights
into understanding human collaborators’ intentions and necessities. However, the cur­
rent implementation within the framework remains rudimentary, relying on hard­coded
approaches, that make it constrained and challenging to adapt. Moreover, this approach
did not enable to design experiments that heavily explore and showcase the partial learn­
ing ability of the DEL model implemented. This is crucial as this capability is one of the
primary contributions of integrating attention into Theory of Mind frameworks.
Concerning these constraints, integrating gaze estimation into the framework should be
prioritized in future work. This enhancement would enable to demonstrate and explore the
DEL modeling of partial learning, considering gaze as a key determinant of agents’ focus.
This contribution would significantly advance the framework’s ability to represent human
agents’ beliefs, intentions, and concerns in dynamic, implicit coordination environments.
Finally, the third goal was to design and implement collaborative mechanisms adapted to
implicit coordination environments within the robotic system , utilizing the advancements
resulted from attention modeling.
In this regard, the act of pointing was a relevant contribution to enhance implicit collabora­
tive mechanisms into the robotic system. By directing human agents’ attention to relevant
information, pointing facilitates to smoother and more effective implicit collaboration, re­
ducing communication overhead and consequently, minimizing communication delays.
For future work,an interesting direction to explore would be the integration of eye gaze
into the robotic system to signal shared attention during critical moments. This mecha­
nism would enhance joint attention awareness among collaborative agents, providing a
clearer understanding of what is considered perceptual common ground during their col­
laboration. Such integration could lead to significant improvements in the effectiveness
and fluidity of implicit coordination.

Let a state (M, w) and an edge­conditioned action (E, e0) be given with M = (W, R, V ),

defined as the state:
(M, w) ⊗(E, e0) = ((W ′, R′, V ′), (w, e0)),
where:
• W ′ = {(w, e) ∈W × E | (M, w) |= pre(e)}.
• R′
a = {((w, e), (v, f)) ∈W ′ × W ′ : (w, v) ∈Ra and (M, w) |= Qa(e, f)} for each
a ∈Ag.
• V ′((w, e)) = {p ∈{At ∪H} | post(e) |= p or ((M, w) |= p and post(e) ̸|= ¬p)}.

(M, w) |= Baφ iff (M, v) |= φ for all (w, v) ∈Ra
(M, w) |= Eφ iff (M, w) |= Baφ for every a ∈Ag
(M, w) |= Cφ iff (M, w) |= Ekφ for every k ≥1

E2φ implies all agents know that all agents know φ, and so on for k ≥1.

Overview of Euler Angles
Euler angles are a widely used method for representing the orientation of a rigid body
in three­dimensional space. They describe the rotation of an object with respect to a
fixed coordinate system (x, y, z) using three consecutive angular rotations about specified
axes. Euler angles are particularly useful in robotics, computer graphics, and aerospace
for calculating and representing an object’s orientation relative to a reference frame.
Definition of Euler Angles
The orientation of a rigid body is defined by three angles:
1. Yaw (ψ): Rotation about the z­axis (vertical axis). Yaw represents the horizontal
rotation or the direction the object is facing in the x­y plane.
2. Pitch (θ): Rotation about the y­axis. Pitch represents the tilt of the body upward or
downward.
3. Roll (ϕ): Rotation about the x­axis. Roll represents the side­to­side tilt of the body.

These rotations are applied in a specific order, depending on the chosen convention (e.g.,
ZYX or XYZ). In the ZYX convention, which is common in robotics:
• The first rotation is about the z­axis (yaw).
• The second rotation is about the y­axis of the rotated frame (pitch).
• The third rotation is about the x­axis of the rotated frame (roll).
Relationship to the Standard Coordinate System
Euler angles are used to transform a body from its initial orientation to a desired orientation
in the 3D coordinate space:
• The x­axis represents the forward direction of the body.
• The y­axis represents the side direction (perpendicular to x).
• The z­axis represents the vertical direction.
Using the ZYX convention:
1. Yaw (ψ): Rotates the body around the vertical z­axis, changing its direction in the
horizontal plane (x­y).
2. Pitch (θ): Tilts the body around the y­axis, changing its elevation (upward or down­
ward).
3. Roll (ϕ): Rotates the body around the x­axis, adjusting its side­to­side tilt.

Overview of YOLO (You Only Look Once) for Object
Detection
What is YOLO?
YOLO (You Only Look Once) is a state­of­the­art object detection algorithm that offers
high­speed and high­accuracy detection of objects in images and videos. Unlike tradi­
tional object detection methods that apply classification to multiple regions of interest in
an image, YOLO uses a single neural network to predict bounding boxes and class prob­
abilities directly from the entire image in one evaluation. This unified architecture makes
YOLO significantly faster than many other object detection methods.
Key Features of YOLO
• Speed: YOLO processes images in real­time, making it suitable for applications re­
quiring high­speed detection, such as robotics, video surveillance, and autonomous
driving.
• Accuracy: YOLO achieves high accuracy by using a single neural network that
predicts multiple bounding boxes and associated class probabilities simultaneously.
• Unified Architecture: The algorithm divides the input image into a grid and predicts
bounding boxes, confidence scores, and class probabilities for each grid cell in one
pass.
Pre­trained Models in YOLO
YOLO provides several pre­trained models, which are trained on large­scale datasets
such as COCO (Common Objects in Context) and Pascal VOC (Visual Object Classes).
These pre­trained models enable developers to use YOLO for object detection tasks with­
out the need for extensive training from scratch. The models differ in size and performance
trade­offs:
• YOLOv4 and YOLOv5: These models offer a balance between speed and accu­
racy, making them widely used for real­time applications.
• YOLOv7 and YOLOv8: These are the latest versions, providing improved accuracy
and faster inference times compared to earlier versions.
• Tiny YOLO: A lightweight version designed for edge devices with limited computa­
tional resources, such as embedded systems and IoT devices.
Applications of YOLO in Experimental Scenarios
In the context of this dissertation, YOLO’s pre­trained models were utilized to detect rele­
vant objects within the experimental scenarios described in Chapter 5. By leveraging pre­
trained weights, the implementation was able to achieve accurate object detection without
requiring additional training, saving time and resources. Furthermore, object affordances
were hard­coded based on the detected objects to align with the designed experimental
tasks.
