Abstract
Biodiversity supports human survival in many ways. The first is to support human existence not
only as a direct source of nutrients and resources, from food to raw materials for pharmaceutical
ingredients but also for other recreational purposes and as a source of income for many. It is
described as the variety of organisms for both terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems and includes
biodiversity, within species, between species and of the ecosystems these species live in. Despite
its importance, human civilization keeps marginalizing it at an extinction rate up to hundreds of
times higher than the natural rate over the past ten million years. This makes biodiversity loss
one of the three global extreme environmental threats for the coming decade concerning life, along
with ”climate action failure” and ”extreme weather”. Yet, biodiversity impacts are not as inves-
tigated as other environmental problems and definitely not quantified into environmental impact
assessment methods. There are several environmental impact assessment tools both qualitative
and quantitative for this purpose. LCA (Life Cycle Assessment) is a standardized quantitative
tool which is increasingly used and helps to evaluate the effects of a product or a system. The
aim of this study is to determine to what extent biodiversity can be assessed in the context of LCA.
There are five basic drivers of biodiversity loss: habitat loss and degradation, climate change,
pollution, overexploitation and unsustainable use land and invasive species and all of them are
divided into multiple pressures. It was found that out of the total 17 biodiversity stressors, 10 of
them are already covered in LCA meaning that only for these 10 there are fate models incorporated
in LCIA methods . In other word land use and water use of the loss of habitat, climate change,
and acidification, eutrophication, ecotoxicity, thermal pollution, ionizing radiation and lastly pho-
tochemical ozone depletion of the pollution driver are already covered up to some extend in some
LCA method like ReCiPe H and PEF (Product Environmental Footprint). For stressors such
as noise pollution, light pollution, vibrations, electromagnetic radiation, monoculture, overuse of
land and invasion of species there are either missing models or the existing LCIA methods are not
yet incorporated in software tools such as EASETECH.
Besides which stressors are covered in the assessment there are other aspects concerning biodiver-
sity like which ecosystems are included in the method for which taxonomic groups are the fate
models results on and for which biodiversity level. Taxa or taxonomic groups are ecological groups
under which species of the same category are grouped together. Depending on the scope of the
study, each time another method is to be chosen.
Despite the fact that biodiversity indicators were identified for the entire system, just the wastewa-
ter treatment section of the urban water system was chosen as the case study of the tested method
because waste and residue management in general is one of the most influential human activities.
It is crucial for the proper functioning of a society, hence it is critical to understand how it affects
biodiversity. The examination of Aarhus’ Marselisborg plant operation, which handles wastewater
produced by 220000 persons, was chosen as the case study.The plant’s performance is specifically
assessed from 2014 to 2019.
Stressors linked with wastewater treatment plants include acidification, ecotoxicity, eutrophica-
tion, thermal pollution from treated water outflow, electromagnetic radiation, odor, noise, and
light pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions from plant operation. The use of sludge on land is
also linked to ecotoxicity, eutrophication, and acidification. The aforementioned stressors are asso-
ciated with certain symptoms. Increased dichlorobenzene is linked to ecotoxicity, while increased
SO2 and phosphorus or nitrogen are linked to eutrophication. Animal stress can be caused by
odor pollution, while noise and light pollution have all been linked to behavioral changes, bodily
injury, and lower reproduction. Thermal pollution and electromagnetic radiation also cause phys-
iological injury and diminished reproduction. Finally, fluctuations in maximum, minimum, and
average precipitation and temperature, as well as extreme weather events and ocean acidification,
are markers of greenhouse gas emissions.
==PAGE:3==
A new method called Biodiversity Impact Assessment + (BIA+) was devised and evaluated on
the case study. This technique can be used to cover all three levels of biodiversity for as many
stressors as desired. In the presented case study, inventory data are taken from LCIA methods
and then converted to concentrations and plotted on biodiversity status graphs.
The BIA + axis biodiversity status scale ranges from 0 to 1, with 0 indicating no biodiversity
and 1 indicating ideal biodiversity relative to a baseline year’s biodiversity status. Despite the
fact that the plant originally discharged treated effluent into Aarhus Bay, sufficient data could not
be obtained to generate the biodiversity graphs from scratch, so the existing river graphs provided
by the technique inventors were utilized to test the method’s applicability. Only the two most sig-
nificant stressors are brought into the aforementioned technique, as they emerge from the overall
LCA midpoint results: ecotoxicity and eutrophication for both local emissions to the Aarhus River
and global emissions from the entire system. Regardless of the weights of the stressors, it appears
that the performance of Marselisborg WWTP in terms of local emissions improved substantially
after 2014. Since 2018, the addition of chemicals has had a minor impact on the biodiversity
status. Between 2015 and 2019, the status fluctuates from 0.6 to 0.55, which is higher than the
norm.
Background emissions cannot be assessed by using BIA+ method because they are assumed to be
created globally and BIA+ takes into consideration habitat types, the further it can go is country
level, by calculating the average habitat type of every country. To assess the whole LCA model it
was assumed that everything needed for the wastewater treatment is produced in Denmark and
all the emissions affect only Denmark. The results for both the local and national levels show the
variation of eutrophication potential as a function of wastewater discharge, river flow and phos-
phorus concentration and they indicate the need to optimize the use of chemicals in wastewater
treatment to reduce the eutrophication potential keeping in mind that the technology used from
Marselisborg plant concerns compliance with the legal limits for discharge directly into the sea
and not into a river. The weights used were equal for both stressors and when a slightly bigger
weight of 0.57 was used for eutrophication, the biodiversity status was improved by almost one
point in the case of national level. For the local emissions the results were still improved but less
than a point with the differentiated weights. Overall, eutrophication and ecotoxicity that were
tested are inversely proportional.
It seems that if all the necessary data are available for the creation of the biodiversity status
graphs, the method is applicable. However, the inclusion of as many taxonomic groups as possible
for all ecosystem types makes it difficult to have results in a relatively small-time frame. Moreover,
marine ecosystems are not included in this method and the freshwater ecosystems do not have an
eco-region of their own but are included in the surrounding environment. The need to cover the
remaining midpoint categories/stressors remain even though the method can be chosen if all data
are available. Including biodiversity in LCA methodologies, even if that means as a supplementary
tool can have positive impacts. It gives the stakeholders and the decision makers guidelines on
how to improve their performance even to comply with regulations or just to use it to report how
its activity influence this aspect of sustainability by enhancing the completeness of LCA tools as
it focuses on biodiversity related issues.
This work is novel since it is the first to identify urban water system stressors in relation to
biodiversity and link stressors to indicators. Additionally, BIA+’s applicability is being evaluated.
On that note, it should be mentioned that the results are the outcome of a brainstorming session
combined with searching; they have not been officially evaluated and as a result, there is some
uncertainty.
Key words: Life cycle assessment, biodiversity impact assessment, urban water systems, wastew-
ater treatment

Introduction

Introduction
According to CBD (Convention of Biological Diversity), biodiversity is described as the variety
of organisms for both terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems and includes biodiversity, within species,
between species and of the ecosystems these species live in [14]. This term is inclusive all three lev-
els of biodiversity: Genetic, Species and Ecosystem. Genetic diversity refers to the variety of the
genes within the same species. Species diversity refers to the diversity of species within a given
ecosystem and Ecosystem diversity refers to the diversity of ecosystems in a well-defined territory.
Biodiversity supports human survival in numerous ways.
Firstly, by supporting human exis-
tence with nutrients and resources for direct use, from food to raw materials for pharmaceutical
purposes. There are also other indirect benefits, biodiversity serves as bequest that could ensure
the well-being of generations to come, but also provides a source of income for indigenous people
or people who make a living out of recreational activities [15]. Around 40% of the global economy
is supported and provided by nature[16]. Despite its importance in sustaining life, human civiliza-
tion keeps marginalizing it at an extinction rate up to hundreds of times higher than the natural
rate over the past ten million years [16].
The phenomenon of glacial and inter-glacial periods forced the earth for millions of years to
dramatically change the conditions on its surface. During the ice ages, the average temperature
approached 1 degree Celsius and large parts of the earth were covered by glaciers. Contrarily,
during the inter-glacial periods the sea level rises even 66 meters, accompanied with an increase in
the temperature. These cycles are affected by the orbital changes of the earth with the so-called
Milankovitch cycles. This occurs naturally and biodiversity adapts up to a certain point. A rate
of extinction is observed of species that cannot adapt to the new conditions. This transition from
glaciers to inter-glaciers and vice versa developed over thousands of years, and the extinctions
recorded during this transition are defined as natural.
Human activity brings changes to the environment in a much shorter period, leaving a much
narrower margin for species to adapt to it, thus resulting in a much higher extinction rate [14].
The subsequent environmental issues involve, among other problems, the loss of biodiversity.
”Biodiversity loss” is one of the three global extreme environmental threats for the coming decade
concerning life along with ”climate action failure” and ”extreme weather”. Species level diversity
has decreased by 68% in the period from 1970 to 2016, genetic diversity has dropped by 6% and
1.9 million km2 of ecosystems has been lost, regarding ecosystem diversity [7]. Yet the necessary
attention has not been given to understanding the complexity of the issue so that preservation
measures could be applied [17]. In this direction, at the COP15 (15th Conference of the Parties
to the United Nations Convention on Biological Diversity) it was decided by the member states
for a new framework to enter into force. The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework
consists of 4 goals and 23 targets regarding biodiversity halting to be achieved by 2050 [18].
• The empowerment of ecosystem robustness by preserving the abundance restored when di-
minished
• The ecosystem services need to be restored when diminished, like the support function of
nutrients in soils
• Equal share of traditional and digital knowledge regarding biodiversity compartments
• Enough resources for all member states to meet the framework’s goals
• How is the performance of Marselisborg WWTP between 2014 and 2019 with relevance to
biodiversity status
There is a biodiversity strategy for 2030 of the European Commission, according to which there is
a need for better and more efficient inclusion of biodiversity into decision policy making. There-
fore, the environmental footprint of organizations, systems and products value chains is of great

The European Union established another directive aiming to promote sustainable investments over

and stakeholders to promote sustainability investments under the EU taxonomy [21].
All anthropogenic activities are affecting the environment, but some of them are more degrading.
Waste and residue management is one of the most impactful activities, while it is also particularly

in finding hotspots in systems and provides a framework for comparing alternative otions and
solutions. It consists of four phases [25]:

1.1
Objective of the study and Research Questions
The aim of this thesis is to highlight the gap in LCA methods with regards to biodiversity and
investigate if biodiversity loss can be assessed using an LCA tool with regards to Urban Water
System. This will be investigated with a case study that will evaluate the performance of Marselis-
borg wastewater treatment plant in Aarhus, Denmark by using data provided by the supply or
found in previous studies. All the applicable indicators will be compared in EASETECH software
and the monitoring of the performance of the plant from 2014 to 2019 will be sought. Considering
the progress of waste and energy technologies, any improvements will be observed. Additionally,
any missing data for a better representation of the topic will be noted, so in a future application
of the method there will be better depiction. All the above will be studied under the prism, that
uncertainty does not completely spoil the results and therefore it should be studied if it is possible
to draw conclusions given the uncertainties. It is known that biodiversity as a subject has some
gaps, and it is unknown if these gaps will affect the conclusions of this study and the applicability
of any proposed method. Secondarily, the association with other impact categories will be pur-
sued. It is worth noting that the project’s aim is not to cover any existing methodological gaps,
rather than examine to what extent biodiversity can be assessed using LCA.
The questions this study aims to answer are presented below:
• How does the urban water system affect biodiversity with focus on WWTPs (Waste Water
Treatment Plant)?
• Which are the most appropriate biodiversity indicators to quantify biodiversity loss?
• Can biodiversity be assessed within LCA, and which LCA model is the most representative?
• Which other environmental impact assessment tools are the most suitable for the assessment
of biodiversity?

2.1
Biodiversity Stressors
According to SCBD (Secretary of the Convention on Biological Diversity) there are five basic
drivers of biodiversity loss: Habitat loss and degradation, climate Change, pollution, overexploita-
tion and unsustainable use land and invasive species [26]. All of them are divided into multiple
pressures. As can be seen in Figure 1 the pressures are grouped based on if they are included in
LCA studies or if not yet.
Figure 1: Drivers of biodiversity loss and LCA coverage. The green boxes indicate that these stressors
are already included in LCA, those in yellow boxes are not yet, and the blank and doted boxes are drives
categories that are not identified yet. Ionizing radiation is not assessed yet in regards to biodiversity
2.1.1
Covered stressors within LCA
Habitat loss driver is divided into land use and water use pressures which are both included in
LCA [4]. Land use is supposed to be the biggest stressor for biodiversity loss especially for ter-
restrial ecosystems [27]. On the contrary, water use has been given less attention which makes it
less applicable in LCA [28].
Climate change is another major driver, only involvng the pressure of greenhouse gas emissions
(included in LCA). It should be noted though, that not all aspects of climate change are included
such as changes in temperature, precipitation, and the frequency of extreme weather events. These
characteristics indicate changes in the geochemical cycle [4]. This means that these effects of the
changes on biodiversity have not been assessed yet.
Pollution is a complex category and some of its most important aspects are acidification, eu-
trophication and ecotoxicity. Acidification refers to how the acidic substances effect terrestrial
and aquatic ecosystem and consequently how species are affected by the lower pH of their envi-
ronment. Ecotoxicity refers to the direct effects of substances on biodiversity. There are several
models such as ReCiRe considering the effects of soil acidification on the surrounding plant species
and of marine acidification on marine species. There is also a model for the effects to freshwater
ecosystems, agricultural land, urban and non-urban atmosphere. Eutrophication deals with how
nitrogen and phosphorus affect aquatic ecosystems. More algae are produced leading to reduced
water quality [4].

2.1.2
Stressors not covered by LCA
There are many other pollution stressors that are not covered by LCA, as seen in Figure 1. Start-
ing with noise pollution, every synthetic noise that can affect biodiversity from instant death to
physiological, behavioral and reproduction changes with permanent or transitory effect, is consid-
ered pollution [29]. These could lead to animals not being able to mate or leaving their territory.
In addition, some sounds can mask signals of birds or harm their receptive ability. This has also
detrimental effects as reproduction opportunities might be reduced and the migration route could
be lost leading to inadequacy to find shelter [30]. The exposure levels are lowering as distance from
the source increases. There is no developed pathway of the noise pollution impact on biodiver-
sity due to lack of data and therefore the modelling of this midpoint category is not feasible now [31]
Another type that has not been adequately examined is odor pollution. There is a proposed
method which implies that odor produces stress on biodiversity but not in a physiological way,
rather than by influencing the temperament of species individuals [4]. It is assumed that odor
can affect biodiversity through nausea and headaches to humans. In that direction, a model was
produced, called odor footprint, creating 33 characterization factors for 33 odorous chemicals mea-
sured in hydrogen sulfide equivalents similarly to CO2 equivalents for carbon footprint [1]. In this
method, the species populations or the exposure pathway of the LCIA part are not considered.
The focus is on measuring the resilience of those 33 chemicals, providing a basis for their compar-
ison in terms of intensity . The list of chemicals is available in Appendix A.
The effects of ionizing radiation are also not covered but it is known that it its effect on animals
is similar to the effects on humans. This type includes the impacts of radioactive emissions and
it gets measured by the decrease in life expectancy due to cancer or other mutations [31], [32].
Some sources of this type of radiation are energy related such as nuclear fuel processes, phosphate
extraction, operation of coal power plants, and oil and gas extraction [33]. These emissions are
either air or water transmitted and therefore this radiation can be ingested by sea animals or
inhaled by terrestrial animals, while after the deposition on soils, the vegetation could be also
affected. Practically, the energy transferred to the body tissues interfere with its structure, which
may lead to further to cellular distraction or DNA alteration [33]. The other form of radiation is
the electromagnetic radiation which also has detrimental effects, similar to the ionizing. Apart
from species’ loss it can also cause behavioral changes [4]. This is usually emitted by telecommuni-
cation nets and implies the weakening of the immune system, thus leading to further deterioration
of their health. It could also imply reduced reproduction abilities as long as changes in their
habitat as a conequence of the vegetational changes, which leads to further species reduction [29].
Another heavy pressure is artificial light pollution. LED (Light Emitting Diodes) and halo-
gen emissions are assessed in a study on aquatic environment showing that the existence of light
controls the fish immigration roots meaning that it can also lead to invasion of species [34]. Like
noise it can influence physiology, reproduction, and the behavior of species [29]. The eyesight of
nocturnal species is especially bothered, even for a few hours. Moreover, the high light concentra-
tion can change the way animals recognize and identify objects in their environment. On top of
this, the day and night cycles of the animals’ change, thereby altering the hormones of the species
and in particular the melatonin levels. Behavioral changes include the obstacles of hunting and
gathering of food and the consequences of the shift of day-night cycles. Animals have deteriorated
activity and travel shorter distances than they used to, so they have unfavorable conditions to seek
food. The above conditions lead to reduced reproducing capacity which leads to further species
reduction [29].
The last pollution type is the thermal one. It is well known that most aquatic species have
restricted thermal endurance. The discharge can have either cooling or heating effect. A charac-
terization factor is created about the effects of cooling water in aquatic environments using the fate
and the effect parameters of the temperature alteration based on a model and literature review.

The unit of the characterization factor is 1.510 −2PDF ∗day ∗m3/◦C ∗m3 [35]. It could safely
be assumed that heating discharge could also have serious effects.
Moving to the unsustainable use, illegal hunting, overfishing, and monoculture are not included in
any method [4]. The impact of illegal hunting on wild fauna is not limited to species biodiversity
but also affects other areas. In research conducted for the effects of mammal hunting on tropical

The last biodiversity loss driver is the invasion of alien species [4]. It is a complex concept
and has not been yet well assessed yet. Apart from the ecological ones, the consequences of the
invasion are also economic as many indigenous people rely on those ecosystems for their economic
stability [39]. All ecological functions as well as the food chain relations are hindered, and indige-
nous species are forced to either migrate or extinct.

2.3
Biodiversity Metrics
The units of the above methods turn out to be footprint indicators, meaning that they measure
the impact of a certain activity. There are also metrics measuring the state of the biodiversity
as it will be explained below. There is an attempt from Pre-sustainability to bridge these two
different metrics to better depict the condition of biodiversity.
2.3.1
Biodiversity state indicators
The Living Planet Index is the foremost utilized metric to declare the loss of species. It is based
on yearly reported data. Due to the simplicity of the method, it is not as detailed as, for example,
the Red List Index, and the complexity is disregarded. It is equal to geometric mean of the global
abundance of all species within a taxonomic group and specifically of vertebrate from all three
types of ecosystems, normalized to a baseline year (1970) [40]. For many species that population
data are not available, time series extracted from online data archives are used [41]. Those time
series are regional data for a specific population. As a metric it focuses on species extinction.
2.3.2
Biodiversity footprint indicators
LCA tools use the following metrics:
The Potentially Disappeared Fraction was initially created for LCA to point out the loss of
species being under pressure, implemented in LCA methodologies such as eutrophication. This
7
==PAGE:18==
Background
does not depict the actual global extinction rates as the metric’s concluded extinction is smaller
than the global one [41]. In other words, this metric method calculates characterization factors
of 18 different midpoint categories and for different inventory data for ecosystem quality endpoint
category. The idea behind the model is that one species, which is arbitrarily selected, is the basis
for the calculation of the disappeared fraction. A value of 0 means that species are lost and in
the case that the value of 1 is assigned, 0 species are lost. This metric is used in ReCiPe model
which is also adopted in the produced model in the case study which is examined in this report [41].
The Species Threat Abatement and Recovery (STAR) is a footprint method for local
impacts for local projects. This constitutes it unsuitable to assess global impacts. The populations
losses are weighted by using the Red List Index. It also helps in decision making by pointing out
the contribution of investments to species extinction. A total of 5362 threatened species as defined
by the Red List Index are included in the method. It is stated that around one fourth of the global
extinction is related to crop production and could be reduced by decision making, while 56% could
be restored by conserving habitats and ecosystems [42]. Biodiversity Impact Credit is another
method for assessing the local impact of global extinction. It sums up the abundance of changes
that are caused by an organization on a global scale. It regulates the changes to cover the species
that are close to extinction but not yet extinct. This metric can be used under the Science based
targets framework of the studied organizations [43].
2.3.3
Biodiversity state and footprint indicators
As a method Mean Species Abundance focuses on local ecosystem intactness and services. It
also compares current population data of species under a certain pressure with baseline data of
a year that the species were not under that pressure, but it also evaluates how different stressors
will affect the mean species abundance. If a species was introduced after the baseline year, it is
not included in the metric. With this method, the arithmetic mean is used and cannot be used to
measure endangerment status. The values it can take is between 0 and 1 (0 meaning not intact
at all and 1 meaning fully intact populations of the original species) [44]. When used as footprint
metric, the abundance is measured in MSA per m2 per year. This method was originally created
by GlOBio using the same stressors models as in PDF (Potential Biodiversity Loss) of ReCiPe,
although having inverse results.
2.3.4
Bridging state and footprint metrics
To produce a new metric that could serve as a link to state and footprint metric PDF and LPI
can be utilized. PDF, as previously explained, is used to measure the businesses’ impact on LPI.
This can be translated to LPI ≈−PDF ∗LPI [41]. LPI indicates the situation of biodiversity
before the change and LPI expresses the size of the change.
2.4
Case study - Marselisborg WWTP
The case study of the report is located in Aarhus, the second largest city of Denmark.
The
Marselisborg WWTP is the largest plant of Aarhus and is able to treat wastewater generated
from 220000 adults in average. The wastewater gets through six different cleansing processes.
Firstly, it gets mechanically cleaned by getting through a grated frame so that the materials of
non-biological origin such as personal care products are removed and sent to incinerator. hen the
material is transferred to an aeration tank to be biologically cleaned. The material goes through a
coarse aggregate of mineral origin with living microorganisms such as bacteria for the organic sub-
stances to be broken down through respiration. Thereupon, ammonium is oxidized to nitrate with
a process named Nitrification and then the nitrate is converted to free nitrogen by bacteria, which
is then released in the atmosphere [45]. Since 2013, phosphorus is recovered from wastewater and
helps in production of recycled fertilizer, meaning that the previous step is of high importance for
8
==PAGE:19==
Background
the plant [46].
Afterwards, the second phase of the cleaning begins, which is the chemical cleaning. In this phase,
a chemical mixture is released in the water, including iron and/or aluminum compounds, so that
a precipitation process is initiated. The remaining substances are bound to the sludge, settled on
the bottom of the tank, which is then removed prior to the last filtration of the water by sand
before it gets released to the final recipient. The effluent water is pumped to Kattegat, a strait
between western Sweden and eastern Jutland. Kattegat bay has a depth of 100 meters and extends
over an area of 20000 km2. This means that the cleaned wastewater is moved 2.08 km by a piping
system from the plant to the bay. For simplicity purposes the discharge is modelled to evolve in
the Aarhus River as no available data were found for marine biodiversity status alike freshwater
that was provided by the method creators.
The removed sludge is then categorized as sludge A and sludge B. Sludge A is moved to the anaer-
obic digestion before it can be applied on land as a fertilizer. Sludge B undergoes a supplementary
compost after the anaerobic digestion. After the anaerobic digestion, the sludge is dewatered by
the liquor treatment technology, based on which, the sludge is placed in a batch reactor. It is
estimated that the plant can cover 150% of its energy needs, apart from the phosphorus fertilizer
production, biogas and heat production without any external input, thus being considered as one
of the most sustainable plants. Figure 4 shows the plant with the real discharge along with the
assumed discharge as it will be explained later.

affect biodiversity.

Kattegat Ecosystem characteristics
After the water being discharged to Aarhus River, it ends up in Aarhus Bay of Kattegat sea
affecting further this ecosystem. Kattegat sea is located between Sweden and Denmark. The
area is also called the inner Danish waters along with south-western Baltic Sea, as can be seen in

Figure 5: Map showing the inner Danish waters [5]
2.5.1
Water stratification
As part of the Danish inner water, Kattegat area blocks the exchange of the oceanic brine of Baltic
sea and the briny waters of North Sea. This zone of mixed water is called an estuary. The two
water bodies with different salinity also differ in their density, creating a flow where the saltwater
flows below the brackish water as it has a higher density while mixing. This mixture of water
quantities with different salinity level makes it habitable for both saltwater and freshwater species
[5]. This mixture also creates a flow of water from the bottom to the surface with a nutrient
dense load which interacts with algae and boosts their increase. The average remaining time of
these nutrients, also called residence time, aggravates the sensitivity of the area along with oxygen
depletion since more oxygen is required from the enhanced algae production [6]. While this process
is happening, there is also a slow and steady mixing of the water quantities with different density.
10
==PAGE:21==
Background
This entire process is called estuarine circulation with the inflow coming from the North Sea and
the outflow leading to the Baltic Sea.
Figure 6: Annual salinity means for the inner Danish waters cross section [6]
Oxygen depletion occurs when the oxygen demand is higher than the one provided in the system.
In that term, the enhanced nutrient provision results in increasing algae population overly in the
surface layer, thus leaving very small quantities of oxygen for benthic organisms. Oxygen depletion
occurs when there is a content below 4 mg per liter.
Even though there are many areas in the Kattegat Sea that suffer from oxygen depletion in the
Aarhus Bay, it has been observed a periodic moderate depletion concentrating in September and
August according to measurements of 2021 [6].
2.5.2
Benthic vegetation
The sandy seafloors are covered with eelgrass, unlike rocky seafloors of deeper compartments cov-
ered with macroalgae. This means that Aarhus Bay is covered with eelgrass which provides a
productive habitat in which many species can thrive. The functions of vegetation do not stop
there but expand.
It acts as a natural barrier for the waves in the case of coastal areas and
the rooting system, it consolidates the seafloor as well as it filters out nutrients and storing car-
bon. All these functions are making the vegetation an important parameter regarding climate
adaptation and marine biodiversity conservation. Despite that they are facing a decay which can
also be evaluated as ecosystem quality decay as macroalgae, and eelgrass are quality indicators [6].
The biggest threat of these benthic vegetation types is eutrophication. The nutrients are causing
the overproduction of phytoplankton near the surface leaving no oxygen for benthic vegetation or
leaving no spaces for the solar radiation to reach this deep in the ocean bed. Other deterioration
factors include the seabed erosion caused by anthropogenic activities like ( e.g. ship passing),
temperature increase, and the massive accumulation of species, such as the green sea urchin,
combined with the constrained preying of the green sea urchin [47], [48].

2.5.3
Benthic Fauna
The benthic fauna species form a wide-raging community of which the functionalities differ. One
common function is managing most of the organic matter. The fauna species can be categorized in
three subgroups. The species that consume phytoplankton are called filterators. Filterators are in
shallow waters where phytoplankton are in abundance, and they can control their population but
also suspended solids of other origin. Another invertebrate species is the grasses, made up of snails
and crustaceans. This species relies on seabed vegetation for its sustenance. In pelagic zones there
is a third type named sediment eaters which consume the organic material that reached that deep.
The species is subdivided into soft and hard fauna. The soft-bottom kind lives on the sediment
and the hard-bottom is attached to the rocky bed. These are the species that can be found in
Danish waters but from place to place the species vary depending on the physical features of the
environment. Such parameters include geophysical structure of the area, the oxygen content, flora
type, salinity etc. it is worth noting that salinity is a key factor to species richness for an aquatic
environment that explains why there is a reduction of species from the salty North Sea to the
briny Baltic Sea [6].
As previously stated, one of these crustaceans’ functions is to consume organic material. If there is
more organic material due to eutrophication, the biomass of the crustaceans increases but oxygen
depletion, which is a product of eutrophication, prevents them from growing in size and population.
Another function is the bioturbation of the sediment, by which oxygen is realized in the ocean
opposing oxygen depletion. Because of the role they play in oceanic environments, benthic species
are representative of the quality of the environment while remaining at the same place for a big
part of their lives [6].
2.5.4
Marine mammals
The three marine mammals with the biggest populations are harbor seals, gray seals, and porpoises
and all of them are protected according to EU directive. There are protected habitat areas for all
of them.
The gray seal is increasingly impacting the spotted seal as they have the same nesting areas and
the same food targets, while they are more corpulent. The spotted seal, which is the most common
seal in the inner Danish waters, prefers coastal areas for reproduction and retreat purposes. In the
Kattegat Sea, an increase in the harbor seal population has been observed, evident by the 11%
growth since 2002[6].
The gray seal has a wider swimming range than the harbor and spotted seals despite using the
same areas for resting and nesting. Gray seals have also increased in numbers despite being extinct
for almost 100 years. Lastly, porpoises extend even further beyond the Danish borders[6].
2.5.5
Kattegat sea biodiversity stressors
According to the Marine Strategy Framework Directive of 2019, anthropogenic nutrient loads,
species invasion and noise are the biggest stressors of biodiversity [49]. The picture of offshore
waters regarding eutrophication is better compared to the Bay’s. There have been measures to
reduce nutrient input, but the ecosystem needs some decades to response to the reduction. Marine
activities, like passing ships, and extraction activities can harm the marine mammals by blocking
hunting activities and communication, especially when the manufactured noise pollution has low
frequencies. It has been found that the ships’ frequent passing roots overlap with the nesting areas
of the porpoises. There are not threshold noise values established yet. There are a small number
of new species registrations in all marine areas of Denmark including Kattegat which might be
due to the not optimal ecosystem conditions [49].

4.1.1
Extraction of drinking water and treatment
All clean water is of groundwater origin and the need for drinking water is covered entirely by
groundwater in the case of Denmark [51]. The need for drinking water constitutes one fifth of the
total need and is constantly increasing according to the growing demands of society. Water is also
used by various industries as a means of cooling, but also for irrigation purposes [52]. When water
is not pumped in a viable manner, the drop in the groundwater table causes phreatophytic stress,
meaning that the reliant vegetation species have less available water leading to several changes. By
an unviable drop of the water table, mean- ing decreased groundwater level beyond what expected
according to the hydrogeological cycle, phreatohytic stress is caused to the related vegetation. All
plants can intake water with their roots, if that is within their reach. This is a function which
works in parallel with the precipitation. In the arid and semiarid regions, the vegetation can
only intake water from the water table. These changes are regarding plant anatomy, genetics and
group dynamics which are intensified in arid areas [53]. The intensity of the stress depends on
factors such as the root system size and the water scarcity tolerance of the vegetation. This can
subsequently affect the rest of the biomass.
There are three diverse types of groundwater dependent ecosystems: 1) the species of those ecosys-
tems that inhabit within the aquifers named stygofauna; 2) the species that need the groundwater
seeping areas like springs, wetlands, and lakes and lastly, 3) species that are dependent on the
water above the water table in the unsaturated zone, around the root system of the vegetation
like subterranean ecosystems and phreatophytes. [54]. All the above species are dependent on
groundwater based on their structure and function and these can be alternatively divided into
those that depend on the aquifer regardless of location like type 2 and those that are dependent
on the groundwater due to their hydrographic position like type 1 and 3 [55]. There are around
384 groundwater dependent species.

After the extraction, the water is moved to the drinking water treatment plant for remediation
based on human health standards. There are several common steps which might differ from plant
to plant and country to country. The emissions of these types of plants are quite like those for
wastewater treatment, and differentiation focuses on the quantity of emissions apart from the
quality of the input water. In general, the drinking water treatment consists of pretreatment,
coagulation and flocculation, sedimentation and disinfection[56].
• Pre-treatment removes all the organic debris like brunches leaves or litter of other origin
• Coagulation and flocculation are part of a process under which chemicals are added to
the water and bind with smaller debris to create bigger particles. The water is mixed to
help in that direction
• Sedimentation is the process taking place after the big particles are formed. In this stage
those impurities are sinking at the bottom of the tank before moving to the next stage
• Filtration is the process in which the water passes through different layers and more im-
purities are filtered out
• Disinfection is the process in which chemicals are added to remove viruses and bacteria
There is a high energy demand in such plants, which leads to a substantial carbon footprint. The
energy is required to carry out all the remediating activities, from pumping water to mixing it [57].
Along with climate change, other potential harmful processes include eutrophication, ecotoxicity
and acidification. These effects arise from the chemical that are used in the system, if not properly
handled before the residues of the remediation are released back in the nature after the treatment.
Secondary impacts could include the noise, light and electromagnetic radiation arising from the
plant’s operation.
4.1.2
Combined Sewer Overflow
The CSO (combined sewer overflow) structures, collect wastewater and stormwater and transports
it within a piping system to the WWTP before it gets released to a water body. Under normal
conditions the water gets first to the WWTP be fore it is discharged, but under heavy rainfall
events the capacity of the system is exceeded and hydraulic shock occurs [58]. This extra volume is
directly released in the recipient. The substance of the untreated water can then be a threat to the
surrounding biodiversity as the water causes ecotoxicity, acidification, eutrophication and oxygen
depletion. In occasions that there is industrial wastewater used for cooling, thermal pollution is
also caused, as the discharge alternates the recipient’s temperature with detrimental effects on the
non-thermally tolerant species. All these organic compounds, chemicals, viruses, and bacteria can
kill the fish of the water bodies if the heavy rainfall events are frequent [59].
Organic matter reserves the oxygen of the water body causing depletion. Subsequently, the pho-
tosynthetic production decreases, and the turbidity increases, which leads to further metal and
organic compounds to be increased and the water body further polluted. Then, the nutrient levels
increase leading to eutrophication [60]. Same amounts of pollutants are measured to be emitted
in water bodies as from WWTP, although it should be noted that the heavy rainfall events occur
a couple times per year , unlike WWTP which occurs constantly [61].

4.1.3
Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems
A way to slow down the filling of CSO is the implementation of SuDS (Sustainable Urban Drainage
Systems). These structures could be infiltration trenches, permeable pavements, swales and basins
and constructed wetlands [62]. The infiltration trenches are stonework storage pods in which
the stormwater can be stored so that it can penetrate and infiltrate the groundwater slowly. The
large debris particles can be screened by the incorporated filters and parts of the pollutants can
be removed by the stonework.
The permeable pavements are a mixture of penetrable asphalt, stone and concrete blocks
that can cover parking lots and pavements. The water can be directed to the subsoil, or it can
be led to infiltration trenches. Swales and basins imitate grassland channels and grass is used
to direct water towards a reservoir or a constructed wetland. The constructed wetlands are
categorized as surface and subsurface ones. The surface CWs (Constructed Wetlands) have a
free water surface, which is not deep and is planted with aquatic or floating vegetation with a
long rooting system called macrophytes. This whole constructed system imitates natural systems
like pods or shallow lakes. Apart from supplying some storage capacity for the water before it
can get to CSO, it also filters out primarily TSS (Total Suspended Solids) and COD (Chemical
Oxygen Demand) among other pollutants. The subsurface CWs are a permeable structure made
of sand and gravel, which provides ample hydraulic conductivity. Again here, there is a layer of
macrophytes which associates the rooting system, the water that flows within the system and the
gravel filter system [61].
When SuDs are not properly implemented and are functioning incorrectly, they can have a destruc-
tive effect on the surrounding biodiversity. The issue of microplastics arises, which can interact
with the soil quality with further ecotoxicological effect except from the rest of the chemical pollu-
tants [63]. These microplastics can interface with other pollutants in the water, such as pesticides
or organic compounds or heavy metals, which will transfer the microplastics further down the
subsoil. These can aggregate through several trophic levels of the groundwater bound ecosystems
causing physiological changes. It also affects the structure and function of soil, which also infuences
biodiversity as a habitat. Specifically, pH, water retention and density are changed [64].

4.2.2
Climate Change
The only stressor in this category is GHG (Green House Gas) emissions. There are several
indicators of GHG: changes in temperature and precipitation minima, maxima and average values.
The precipitation minima can lead to extreme droughts and wildfires and the precipitation maxima
can lead to floodings.The maxima and minima temperatures along with the previous precipitation
indicators can also result in extreme weather, another indicator. These changes of environmental
characteristics can lead to shifting the species’ ranges or widening the range which can also lead to
the invasion of species and increased risk for other species. Furthermore, there is a risk of habitat
loss, for example coral reefs by warm waters [68]. Lastly, carbon emissions can also cause ocean
acidification which can affect some species and the habitat types [4].
4.2.3
Pollution
Acidification’s indicator is increase of SO2. When the pH value of a habitat is decreased, there
is an increase of sulfur oxide (SO2). In terrestrial environments the soil’s pH is decreased by
the deposition of emitted pollutants which subsequently increases the risk for plants and as a
matter-of-fact species [69]. In the case of freshwater, the deposition of emitted pollutants is also
responsible for acidification. The lakes and rivers ecosystems’ populations reduce, due to habitat
loss to species extinction as they cannot move to other areas [70]. Ocean acidification accelerates
climate change as it is caused by GHG emissions.
Ecotoxicity is measured by the content of Dichlorobenzene eq, with higher values highlighting
greater ecotoxicity. Similarly to acidification, in the conditions of the environment makes it unliv-
able for several species. In the case of freshwater ecosystems, there is increased risk of extinction
for the species, while in the marine and terrestrial ecosystems it forces the species to invade to
other areas or ecosystems. This get applied to all trophic levels.
Odor pollution is causing stress on species, which can in turn cause physical abnormalities. If
irritation, respiratory implications and nausea equal to human stress, the same can be assumed
for animal stress. Humans perceive chemicals as parts per billion to trillion which are some orders
of magnitude lower than animals [1] meaning that effects could be more intense on animals. This
stress can affect the reproduction as well as behavioral and physiological habits of exposed animals
leading indirectly to species’ loss.
Noise pollution indicators highlight the fact that in areas with high noise record, there is ab-
sence of biodiversity due to physiological and behavioral changes. This can also lead to reduced
reproduction. Light pollution, similar to noise pollution, affects physiology and behavior, while
also implying reduced reproduction as explained in 2.1. Thermal pollution affects the processes
and functions of organisms of aquatic environments as most species have a narrow temperature
range tolerance [35]. This leads to area habitat loss or habitat degradation and species range
shifts towards environmental with pre-stressor levels [68]. Another stressor is the vibration that
affects the physiology and behavior of exposed marine species such as shore crabs [71]. Increased
activity has been noticed when animals are exposed to this type of pollution and specifically in
male individuals.

4.2.4
Unsustainable use
Monoculture, as an agricultural practice, promotes the cultivation of a single species which apart
from interfering with the soil’s functions, excluding the existence of other species. Both effects
are interfering further to the hydrogeochemical cycles especially after specific pesticides are used
so specific nutrients are enriching the soil. The indicators of this stressor are not specific as they
fall under several categories. The leaks of the pesticides to the groundwaters or to other water
bodies, such as rivers and lakes, trigger other pressures, including eutrophication and ecotoxicity.
Furthermore, the way the land is used leaves less natural habitat area for species to cover their
needs. However, the same term can be used for forests with a single plant species. In that way the
area is supposed to cover the needs of wildlife, but it ends up having more negative effects than
the intention was, i.e. to increase biodiversity by reducing part of the land stress [4].
Overuse is the other pressure of this driver. Overfishing is an indication in the aquatic ecosys-
tems as there is species loss occurs both directly and indirectly through the food web. In terrestrial
ecosystems illegal hunting is the equivalent. In this case, apart from indirectly stimulating species
loss of the related food web species, there is also disruption of the ecosystems as mammals are
dispensing seeds and plant sperms. Furthermore, the unsustainable use of insecticides and herbi-
cides in agricultural areas also indirectly impacts biodiversity through the leaks to water bodies
as was argued above. Lastly, the disruption of predator-prey interface, by several reasons that are
described above, has also effects on biodiversity, as the greater the population of prey is, the larger
the population of predators becomes. If the prey population decreases, the predator population
will also decrease [4].
4.2.5
Invasion of species
Even though the invasion of species is one of the most crucial drivers of biodiversity loss, there
are many reasons underlying it, and are mostly indirect. Lately, macro-plastics’ existence in the
marine environments help many species to travel to other ecosystems and interrupt the equilibrium
of the invaded ecosystem. Secondly, the enlarged habitat range, due to climate change and land
use, may force many species to invade other areas [4].

Impact assessment checklist is a method according to which, the specialists’ team is
creating a list of links of the activity with the environment and all the relevant components

comprehend which part of the system affects which compartment. All biodiversity stressors

Environmental risk assessment also identifies which parts of an activity impact the
environment and which substances. There is a category of the method named ecological
risk assessment which focuses on the risk that the chemicals and GMOs (Genetically Mod-
ified Organisms) cause to the ecosystems and the individuals of the species. Less often this
assessment can focus on physical characteristics like temperature rise of a stream [82]. The
focus of the tool is primarily on acidification, eutrophication, ecotoxicity and thermal pollu-

harmfulness of the chemical compounds based on their inherent properties.Hazard assess-
ment takes a step further the identification and pairs effects of the chemicals and exposure
to the chemicals and lastly, risk characterization, determine how possible it is for the effect
to happen.
• Quantitative screening tools
The Leopold method was one of the earliest quantitative EIA tools and is still used today
because to its simplicity. All environmental components are arranged in the first column,
and all activity processes are arranged in the first row, to form a table. Then, for each
environmental component and process, there is a cell separated into two for two different
inputs: the impact’s magnitude (M) and its severity or importance (I). F.2 of Appendix F
has a graphic demonstration. Both inputs take values from 1 to 10. For magnitude 1 means
healthy environment and 10 indicates the most polluted state. For the importance factor
1 indicates that this process is not relevant to the environment component being studied
and 10 means that it is highly dependent. All biodiversity stressors can be assessed by this
method as it is customized each time to each project [13].

four thematic categories of assessed components: ecology, pollution, aesthetics and human
interest. All the parameters of these four categories as well as their weighting values are

4.8
Discussion of methods
Regarding the LCA part of the case study, among the available methods in EASETECH, ReCiPe
H is the most inclusive as the most stressors and ecosystems are covered and that is the method
chosen for the case study. But still, biodiversity aspects such as acidification and ionizing radiation
are left aside even with this method option.
No matter which LCA method is chosen, they are some shortcomings to be able to evaluate
biodiversity without the use of additional methods. Apart from the fact that not all stressors
are included in LCIA methods, even for the covered stressors, the focus is primarily on species’
richness, while disregarding species evenness and the other two biodiversity categories which are
essential to the wider biodiversity [84]. That is, only one surrogate species or a few taxonomic
groups were used in the processes of characterization modelling [85]. This means that the bio-
diversity which assessed species richness on a surrogate species, or some taxonomic groups could
not be representative for the whole ecosystem, all species within the ecosystem and not even for
the same surrogate species.
The third limitation is that while the results reflect country-wise stressors, a higher resolution
is required as a country is an obscure area reference. A country as a unit contains more than
one ecosystem and the results could not be meaningful for all of them [86]. The last constraint
describes the different point of view between biology and LCA. Biology focuses on high resolution
and deals with ecoregions. As for LCA, the focus is on the greater picture, which requires some
generalization area wise, since, additionally, there is a comparative tool of many midpoint cate-
gories.
Overall, due to lack of data, it is not clear how to measure biodiversity loss and how to map
the human interaction with this loss. However, BIA+ is a method proposed that can assess all
three levels of biodiversity despite its uncertainty and therefore it will be chosen to get tested for
the case study of this report.

the ecotoxicity was 0.039 and has been normalized to 0.43. It seems that the correct and region-

6.2.4
Uncertainty Analysis
The uncertainty of this study is high as there are many assumptions on at least 3 distinct levels.
Starting with the Easetech model, year specific data were used only for the wastewater treatment
unit. The rest are similar for all the years. Secondly, anaerobic digestion and composting data
were derived from another plant but assumed to be the same as in Marselisborg. The heavy metals
fractions of the wastewater treatment were derived from Avedore plant and assumed to be the
same in Marseliborg. Annually updated values are the energy and district heating mixes, P, N,
COD, BOD, water quantities and the external processes needed for the treatment.
The second level takes into account the fact that there was insufficient data to create biodiversity
status graphs of aquatic eutrophication and ecotoxicity via fate modeling, and the findings were
presented on river graphs that were available. This may yield some comparative results, but it
should be apparent that the Marselisborg plant’s technology aims to meet the legal restrictions
for discharge into the sea rather than a river. This suggests that, for less ambiguous results, either
the plots should have been designed for marine ecosystems rather than fresh water habitats, or
technology related to river discharge should have been modeled in Easetech.
Third, because they do not belong to the same habitat type, the chemicals employed in the
system that are produced elsewhere cannot be successfully examined by this method. For a better

Round-up Discussion
7
Round-up Discussion
Overall, including biodiversity in LCA methodologies, even if that means as a supplementary tool
can have positive impacts. It enhances the completeness of LCA tools as it focuses on biodiversity
related issues. As biodiversity is one of the pillars of sustainability, it makes the LCA tool a better
sustainability assessment technique. The analysis of biodiversity impacts from systems or prod-
ucts, give a better picture of how sustainable is influenced by it and subsequently in what extend
are ecosystem operations affected with final acceptance of humans. Furthermore, it provides more
specialized ecosystem and species related hot-spots which provide room for improvement. Compa-
nies can also be benefited with this addition to LCA tools. It helps them to mitigate according to
several sustainability schemes and frameworks like Eu taxonomy in order to either have a positive
effect on biodiversity or to not have a negative effects on it. This gives the stakeholders and the
decision makers guidelines on how to improve their performance even to comply with regulations
or just to use it to report how its activity influence this aspect of sustainability.
The societal implications of the inclusion of biodiversity in LCA are also substantial. The fo-
cus that biodiversity gets through the LCA tools, can help the prevention of the ecosystems and
species and prevent the extinction of some endangered species. This supports human survival by
resources that are provided as well as by ensuring a source of income for many societies and lastly
serving as a bequest for future generations. Furthermore, more stakeholders can be included in
the process of conducting an LCA such as NGOs and other communities, which forms o sense of
inclusivity for all the concerned groups. Lastly, the consumers can be more aware of their decisions
for the biodiversity related aspects of their purchased goods and services. The more attention the
inclusion of biodiversity in LCA gets the more funding will be available for such projects and the
stricter the regulation will get for a more successful application of the regulations. Additionally,
more focus will be paid in the LC sector with a better inclusion of biodiversity stressors compared
to now.

Conclusions
8
Conclusions
Human related biodiversity loss is one of the biggest threats of humankind and according to plan-
etary boundaries, in which biodiversity is part of the biosphere integrity boundary, changes in
biodiversity integrity have been intensified in the last 50 years. That is, human activity might
have driven biodiversity to a point of irreversible damage, which subsequently will affect ecosystem
services and humans’ lifestyle. Further research is needed seeking available data for the control
variables of this boundary, in the wider efforts to understand whether our planet is within or
beyond the uncertainty zone. In that direction, there are several operational and non-operational
LCA methods and depending on the scope of the method the focus could be on the inclusion of
the maximum number of taxa, of all the ecosystem types, of all three biodiversity levels and of
the maximum number of stressors.The purpose of this study is to ascertain the extent to which
biodiversity can be evaluated in relation to LCA.
BIA+ as a method, seeks to fill the gaps and address weaknesses of other previously created meth-
ods , such as the inclusion of only 3 out of the 5 biodiversity drivers. BIA+ takes into consideration
not only species richness, but also species evenness, which is the other component of species bio-
diversity, but also ecosystem and genetic biodiversity. The method states that biodiversity can be
assessed within an ecoregion or at most at national level as 14 major habitat types have been set
by WWF.
Based on this method, firstly some graphs need to be made for each midpoint category which
will depict the relation of biodiversity quality and an equivalent concentration of the midpoint
category for a specific habitat type. This can be achieved with the help of biology researchers
and scholars, in an interdisciplinary approach. Afterwards, the LCIA midpoint results need to be
interpreted to a concentration to be plotted in the biodiversity status graphs. The last step is
to weigh all the considered midpoint categories to create a final biodiversity status for the given
habitat type, which at most could be at national level.
The case study focuses on the urban water system’s WWTP. Acidification, ecotoxicity, eutrophi-
cation, thermal pollution from treated water outflow, electromagnetic radiation, odor, noise, and
light pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions from plant operation are all stressors associated with
wastewater treatment plants. Sludge use on land has also been linked to ecotoxicity, eutrophi-
cation, and acidification.
Certain indicators are associated with the aforementioned stressors.
Increased dichlorobenzene is associated with ecotoxicity, whereas increased SO2, phosphorus, or
nitrogen is associated with eutrophication. Odor pollution can cause animal stress, while noise and
light pollution have all been linked to behavioral changes, bodily injury, and decreased reproduc-
tion. Thermal pollution and electromagnetic radiation both cause physiological harm and lower
reproduction rates. Finally, extreme weather events and ocean acidification, as well as fluctuations
in maximum, minimum, and average precipitation and temperature, are indicators of greenhouse
gas emissions.
In the Marselisborg WWTP case study, only the two biggest stressors are assessed: ecotoxicity
and eutrophication. The habitat type of Aarhus was assumed to be the same as in the whole
country and the WWTP under study was supposed to discharge the treated water in Aarhus
River instead of the bay. This was assumed since there was no available data for the creation
of biodiversity status graphs specifically for the bay, while no group of biologists was assigned to
the project. Moreover, the weighting of the two midpoint categories, eutrophication and ecotox-
icity, that are being tested, has been equal, meaning 0.5 for each. With this weighting, it seems
that when eutrophication is low, ecotoxicity is very high. It should be noted that the technology
used from the plant concerns discharge into the sea and the results might have been different if
the technology was concerning freshwater discharge. Overall, it seems that the performance of
Marselisborg WWTP regarding the local emissions, has improved after 2014 sharply, regardless of

the weights of the stressors. After 2017 there is a very small reduction of the biodiversity status
which could be assigned to the addition of chemicals in the process. The status ranges from 0.6
to 0.55 between 2015 and 2019 which is above the average status.
Besides LCA there are other tools that are chosen to assess biodiversity net gains or losses which
again depends on the scope of the study:
• If the focus is on ecosystem biodiversity level, another tool is the biodiversity net gain,
a QGIS based tool. It helps in the evaluation of habitats.
• If the focus is on species biodiversity level, another useful tool is the species surveys. In
this case, the collection of species data can be assessed by using other tools such as Marine
Biotic Index for soft-bottom, marine species.
• If the focus is on genetic biodiversity level, a good option is the eDNA test, which
provides information about the gene pool of several species.
Finally, it should be noted that there are other screening tools for assessing biodiversity as part
of environmental assessment studies as seen in 4.6.
This study is novel since it is the first to identify urban water system stressors in connection
to biodiversity and to link stressors to indicators but also, the applicability of BIA+ is being as-
sessed. On that note, it should be noted that the results are the result of a brainstorming session
paired with some searching; they have not been officially evaluated, so there is some ambiguity.
8.1
Limitations
A general limitation of the usage of LCA for the biodiversity assessment, is that biology scholars
generally prefer high resolution. In other words, they study the biodiversity of a specific location,
unlike LCA experts that prefer the global scale of the effects. Other impact assessment methods
use specific taxonomic groups to estimate how biodiversity will be affected. If the same is to be
applied in LCA techniques, more time and money are required to collect the inventory data with
higher resolution.
Regarding the individual stressors as seen in 4, while each of them pertains to a specific indi-
cator, not all of them are included in LCA methods either because the methods are not updated
or because the fate modelling of these midpoint categories are not finalized yet. The stressors that
are not included so far include noise pollution, light pollution and vibration and electromagnetic
radiation of the pollution driver, and the unsustainable use and invasion of species drivers.
Moving to the applicability of BIA+ method, it seems that if all the necessary data are available for
the creation of the biodiversity status graphs, the method is applicable to LCA studies. However,
the inclusion of as many taxonomic groups as possible for all ecosystem types makes it difficult to
generate results in a relatively small timeframe. Moreover, marine ecosystems are not included in
this method and the freshwater ecosystems do not have their own eco-region but are included in
the surrounding environment.
8.2
Recommendations and Future Work
Based on the results of this study, it is suggested that biodiversity stressors that have not yet been
employed be incorporated in LCIA methods as midpoint categories and even further the stressor
range. Micro and macroplastics, for example, might be added as a new midway category, which
is a work in progress. Many of the other factors and stressors are linked to species invasion, thus
it might be studied further. Aquatic organisms, for example, use macroplastics to travel to other

environments. Furthermore, more work could be spent acquiring more regionalized inventory data
with higher precision in order to overcome the gap with biologists.
To be able to use the BIA+ approach, the biodiversity data of the study region must first be
studied. Genetic diversity data can be discovered by eDNA tests, ecosystem data can be generated
through the use of ecosystem technologies such as Net Gain, and species biodiversity data can be
discovered through species surveys. These can then be used to form a generic biodiversity status
graph by linking them to chemical equivalents concentrations and plotting the LCIA results.
Finally, despite the high uncertainty of the results, it would be beneficial to include the building
of the WWTP yearly fraction per year wastewater inflow to be more inclusive.
