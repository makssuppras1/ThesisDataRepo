ABSTRACT 
 
The main goal of this Master Thesis was the analysis, investigation and development of a new heating and 
cooling system based on the current heating and cooling ceiling systems. Some other investigations were 
carried out (for example integration with a acoustic metal ceiling).  
 
In order to simplify the current systems, facilitate the assembly process and reduce the operational and 
maintenance cost; proposals were designed in accordance with “Product development method”. To gain 
the goals, an assessment of the elements and their material composition was done through “House of 
quality”. 
 
In order to fulfill the requirements from Nearly-Zero Energy Buildings, input data has been compromised by 
the thermal performance for cooling and heating in those building models. Also the passive houses and BR 
codes requirements has been considering. Two different simulations have been performed.  
 
The first simulation was performed by HEAT2 software. This simulation consisted of two new designs of 
heating and cooling systems which a metal acoustic ceiling was implemented in. Once those systems were 
designed and their feasibility of assembly is evaluated, two models were implemented on this software. 
Once the heat flows was obtained, the thermal transmittance was calculated thanks to the temperature 
difference between water and air temperature.  
 
The second simulation consisted of one non-residential and one residential model. Both buildings were 
located in Copenhagen. They were simulated on IDA ICE software to calculate the heating and cooling 
loads. In accordance with energy requirements from standards and considering the thermal transmittance 
for cooling and heating from the new systems, an evaluation of heating and cooling load was performed.  
 
After evaluating results and their engineering characteristics, an evaluation was done to analyze the 
viability of this new product.

Background 
 
1.1. Heating and cooling ceiling 
Both heating and cooling plays an important role in terms of habitability and indoor comfort levels. In 
accordance with the following sub-chapter, energy consumption has to be reduced. Chasing this goal, new 
methods have been developed in the building sector for heating and cooling. The most remarkable 
methods are: 
x 
Low temperature heating and high temperature cooling.  
x 
Insertion of energy sources such as solar heating, heat pumps, geothermal energy, wind, ground 
floor heat pumps.  
On one hand the most efficient way to develop low temperature heating is in floors, but on the other hand, 
the best place to set cooling system is in ceilings. However, there is a main problem. Two convoluted and 
big systems are working individually. That means extra material use, more installation time, higher 
reparation cost and consequently more expenses for heating and cooling system.  
In order to lower the total cost, a solution came up. Both systems must be simplified. To get it, both system 
must be integrated and share same elements. Although energy efficiency is different, total cost will 
decrease. This was called hydronic and it is gaining popularity because of a main reason. Thermal comfort 
has reached optimum level of thermal comfort and energy consumption has been reduced. Radiant cooling 
system run into same water loop as warmer water does during the heating season. Therefore this network 
of pipes is able to turn the ceiling into cooled surfaces which absorb heat energy.  
According to “Construction specifier being radiant” article, combination of radiant heating and cooling is 
pretty new in some developed countries such as United States and Canada. To take a chance of success on 
the market, this system needs more advanced control systems. In accordance with a study from “New 
Building Institute in the United States” to gain the energy goals, this kind of system is implemented in at 
least one half of zero-energy buildings. 
1.2. Sustainable development 
Energy consumption in buildings has to be reduced due to the two main reasons:  
x 
First, greenhouse gas emission. This is emitted by using fossil fuels like coal. See Figure 1. 
x 
Second, security of supply. Worldwide energy supply and energy supply to Europe. Europe consists 
of developed and developing states where there is a high dependence on the outside world. In the 
way of being self-sufficient, energy consumption must be reduced.

Other factors such as natural population growth trend, resources limitations, increase of energy price and 
European environmental policies must not be neglected. 
According to United Nations, in 2050 population rate would reach 7.4 billion people (low projection) and 
10.6 billion people (high projection). Even in the best scenario, there would be an increase of 21%. 
Furthermore, less fossil fuel resources in the upcoming years will cause an unviable energetic system. This

cooling systems. That means a higher efficiency and consequently lower energy consumption.

3.1.3. Developing product.  Aluminum pipes embedded in aluminum foam panel 
New concepts have been developed in order to lower energy consumption. A new heating and cooling 
panel is been developed and tested in a 260 m2 office room. The panel is mainly composed by aluminum 
foam that changes extremely fast its temperature. Thanks to an excellent thermal conductance of 
aluminum cells, the temperature of the entire panel is uniform. The heat is transferred into or from the 
foam through cooper tubes, which are embedded in the foam, keeping perfect contact to cell aluminum 
foam. Good thermal conductance of the foam resulted in short length of embedded tubes, what is 
beneficial for low flow resistance. The following two pictures show distribution of temperature of this panel 
by heating and cooling (Figure 8).

To fulfill indoor climate requirements and other standards, indoor environments must be under

Thermal comfort depends on the satisfaction of tenants. Requirements for thermal conform (EN

temperature difference, draft, mean radiant temperature, operative temperature, and 
temperature asymmetry must be considered.  
5.1. Operative temperature 
The operative temperature is essential factor. It has a strong connection with the mean radiant 
temperature. This consist of the angle factor and the room surface temperature. Each angle factor 
is multiplied by its room surface temperature value. Those results are collected and summed in 
order to obtain the mean radiant temperature value. The operative temperature is a combination 
of mean radiant temperature and air temperature. Air temperature is easily measured. 
 
In some cases, the operative temperature is an average of mean radiant and air temperature. The 
following conditions are given as an example of that. If the low air velocities is <0.2 m/s, the 
difference between those two temperatures is <4 °C, the air temperature 25 °C and the mean 
radiant temperature is 21 °C, the operative temperature will be 23 °C. See Figure 15.  
 
 
Figure 15: Schematic drawing about kind of temperatures (Indoor Climate lectures) 
This means that both factors contribute equally to determine the operative temperature and, 
consequently, the level of thermal comfort in this space.  
 
In comparison with a convective heating and cooling system, a radiant heating and cooling system 
(hydronic water systems in our case) achieves the same level of operative temperature (same level 
of thermal comfort) at a lower air temperature and a surface cooling system at a higher air 
temperature. In the convective heat exchange transmission, the heat losses depend on room air 
and external surfaces. On the other hand, in the radiant heat exchange transmission, the heat 
transfer depends on the external surfaces and the rest of surface in this space.

6.1. Heat transfer 
Actually, three physical mechanisms influence the heat transfer. The interaction between them defines 
heat transfer. They are conduction, convection and radiation. In our case, the thermal transference is 
mainly radiation and slightly conduction.  
6.1.1. Conduction 
Heat and cool are two types of energy which are transmitted through one solid and one fluid media by 
conduction. Conducting mechanism takes place especially in solid bodies and is influenced by their material 
properties. Heat is transferred through the wall, ceiling and floor of a standing person (See Figure 27).

13. Generating alternatives 
 
The main purpose of design is to make something new, innovative and special. Customers pay more 
attention to new products than the new version of current products since they prefer improvements rather 
than unsuccessful innovations. 
To achieve the goal, all three systems must be modified and a single system will work as a whole. To 
integrate all systems some components will be neglected and a new combination of the other components 
will be set. In this case two different systems have been designed to fulfill both engineering requirements 
(NZEB requirements) and customers preferences and likings (easy to install and maintain and lower 
operational and maintenance cost) throughout the following Morphological chart.   
Although both systems have different designs, some other characteristics such as material components and 
size components are very similar or equal. The following points show some evaluations made to design the 
best possible systems.  Factors such as the heat transfer performance, handiness, ease of assembly, 
integration with the rest of ceiling components and the operational and maintenance cost are considered 
in this evaluation.  
x 
Pipe composition. Analysis and evaluation of some pipe material such as copper, PEX, PVC and PE 
(HDPE & LDPE) pipes were made. Although the thermal conductivity of copper is higher than PVC 
and PEX, some other factors have determined that pipes made of copper are not a feasible 
proposal for pipe material election. Among plastic pipes (PVC, PEX and PE) its thermal conductivity 
values and other factors such as its flexibility and handiness make a remarkable difference. 
According to Thermal Conductivity Material standard, the thermal conductivities of PVC, PEX and 
PE are 0.19 [W/m*K], 0.36 [W/m*K], and 0.38 [W/m*K], respectively. Furthermore, due to a 
number of features such as higher flexibility, lower weight and increased longevity, PE (HDPE) is 
selected among the three options for the pipes material.  
 
o Charter Plastic Company offers several PE products to transfer heat though water. One of these

housing. Its diameter ranges from 16 mm to 600 mm. According to the catalog, it is used in 
many types of geothermal installations including vertical well, slinky, pond and horizontal 
loops.

14.1. Thermal conductivity of the elements 
This sub-chapter has been split in two. 
14.1.1. Calculation of thermal conductivity in junctions 
To find out total the thermal resistance from those models, thermal contact resistance between elements 
has to be measured and included in the total thermal resistance. In both cases, there is a junction between 
HDPE pipe and aluminum. Furthermore, in the first model (see Figure 34), another junction (aluminum-
aluminum) is present.  
The thermal contact resistances calculation is based upon articles in which thermal surface resistance was 
calculated. The description of how to determine the thermal contact resistances of the junction is explained 
below.   
x 
Aluminum-aluminum junction: According to the article “Fundamentals of Heat Transfer” by Frank P. 
Incropera, David P. DeWitt, thermal surface resistance for this junction ranges from 1.5*10-4 
[m2*K/W] to 5*10-4  [m2*K/W] if the pressure between elements is 100 [KN/m2] or 0.1 [MPa] which 
is feasible. This pressure value is reachable due to the structural design.  Final value obtained is 
3.5*10-4 [m2*K/W]. 
x 
Aluminum–PE junction: For this case, calculations have been made in accordance with the same 
article. PE material is not illustrated in the graph. The junction between glycerin and aluminum has 
been chosen. Its value is 0.525*10-4 [m2*K/W] at 0.1 [MPa] surface pressure, which is feasible. 
However, to ensure correct results, further calculations are shown in next sub-chapter.  
14.1.2. Calculation of final thermal conductivity of the elements 
To explain how contact resistance is modelled in HEAT 2, all thermal resistances are compiled and final 
thermal resistance of all the elements is obtained in this sub-chapter. Knowing this resistance and the 
thickness of each material, the final thermal conductivity is obtained and its value is inserted as input data 
on the HEAT2 model. As an illustrative example, the final thermal conductivity of HDPE calculation is 
described step by step and can be found in table 15.  
The final thermal surface resistance value for aluminum and PE junction has been set to 0.525*10-4 
[m2*K/W]. However, more precautions may be taken. Table 15 illustrates how an increase of 400% in 
thermal surface resistance affects to the final thermal conductivity. The result shows that even though the 
increase in thermal surface resistance is rather large (400%), the final thermal conductivity drops only 5%.

22. Discussion 
 
22.1. Dew point 
As mentioned in sub-chapter 5.8., the dew point depends on relative humidity (RH) and air temperature. 
For example, if operative temperature is around 24 °C and RH is not higher than 50%, the dew point will be 
around 12.5 °C. One critical day is selected to evaluate and analyze its dew point.  
August, the 25th is the hottest day of the year. Thus, water supply reaches its lowest temperature level (17 
°C). This day, room air temperature is as hot as 25 °C. From Figure 23 (on Dew point chapter), dew point 
can be easily calculated (13.5 °C). Thus, there is a temperature gap of 3.5 °C between the water 
temperature and dew point. Since cold water must remain at least 1.6 °C above the dew point to mitigate 
the potential condensation, it is demonstrated that even for the worst case scenario condensation is 
minimized. 
22.2. State of art and new product.  
In accordance with Current products sub-chapter, an evaluation of the current systems is performed at 3.5 
°C and 6 °C temperature difference for heating and cooling. As the following two Figures (95 and 96) show, 
the capacity of heating and cooling is measured within this temperature range. As can be seen, current 
products provide higher heating and cooling than new products. The cause is simple; limitation of current 
energy consumption is not as severe as the requirements from NZEB due to many factors (such as better 
insulation and internal gains reduction). Hence, those current systems are not needed thus far and they will

22.3. Application and use  
Investment and investigation in new systems are needed and therefore desirable.  
It has been already explained that low energy consumption systems can provide enough heating and 
cooling at temperature differences not higher than 3.5 °C for heating and 5.7 °C cooling NZEB requirements 
respectively. 
One remarkable system function is the capacity to transfer heat from one zone to another zone. Due to 
climate conditions, Copenhagen and surrounding areas do not present the best location to produce 
overheating since solar gain effect in this area does not influence in a great extent the heat production. 
Thus, in this area, there is no little overheating to transfer to zones with need for it. However, this is not the 
case in countries where solar gain outweighs the cold temperatures during spring and fall, producing 
overheating. More precisely, room spaces oriented to the south will receive solar heat from the windows. If 
overheating is produced, it could be transferred to spaces oriented to the north (cooler). By doing so it is 
possible to avoid the shortage of heating, increasing slightly the operative temperatures and thus, the 
thermal comfort.

23. Conclusion 
 
23. 1. Heating and cooling consumption 
In accordance with the first simulation, two different systems are designed, developed and investigated in 
several versions. After obtaining their thermal transmittance, one version is chosen to investigate further. 
In terms of performance, the most important factor is the spacing pipe. All versions range from 30 cm to 50 
cm. In order to guarantee that all versions fulfill the mentioned requirements, the most unfavorable case is 
chosen.   
“50 mm spacing pipe and 20 mm ø” from the first model is integrated into a residential and non-residential 
IDA ICE models. In accordance with the results, this version has fulfilled all heating and cooling 
requirements (20-25 W/m2) for cooling and (10 W/m2) for heating. However in relation with 2020 
standards, heating consumption has not complied with the requirements for residential buildings (≤ 12 
KWh/m2). This is because only some of input data fulfill 2020 requirements. For instance, U_value (0.1 
(W/m2*K) and internal gains have been reduced in residential model.  
Consequently, if this new system fulfills heating and cooling requirements for a passive house, which are 
more dependent of energy resources than 2020 buildings, will also fulfill heating requirements for 2020. 
Hint: Due to the lack of time, ventilation was not accounted for in this investigations and could be 
implemented in further investigations.   
23.2. Assembly and maintenance  
Due to the integration of one heating-cooling system with one acoustic system, the assembly process is 
much simpler. Moreover, due to this simplification, failures and reparations will not occur so often. Apart 
from this fact, lifestyle of tenants will not be disturbed as much as before. Also saving in operational and 
maintenance costs must be pointed out. In accordance with evaluation costs, saving money can reach 
around 40%.  
23.3. Environment 
Apart from what is written above about high thermal performance and due to this simplification, material 
saving must be taken into account. If the market entry is as favorable as expected, material saving potential 
is remarkable. Furthermore, taking into consideration its whole life cycle: from extraction of materials, 
production of product and transportation, the reductions in pollutant emissions would be significant.

25.2. History of this product from the start until now 
When water-based central heating systems started being developed and along many years, circulators 
were powered by non-electric sources. In order to move water through the loop it was the differential 
pressure between the coexistent hot and cool water that move the water through the loop. The density of 
heated water density is slightly lower than the density of cooled water. Heated water therefore rose up and 
cooled water remained at the bottom thus creating a water circulation through the loop. The majority of 
these systems contained an expansion tank on the top floor.  
Although water heating systems were safer than steam systems, a careful maintenance was needed. As a 
consequence of evaporation, the water level could slowly drop.  
At the very end of 19th century, a German scientist named Hans Von Pechmann discovered a polyethylene 
based pipes. However, those pipes were not sold at that time for there was no commercial use for them.  
25.2.1. Emergence of radiant ceilings 
In 1907, Arthur H. Barker experimented with small hot water pipes embedded in concrete or platter 
composed, proving it as an efficient heating system. A product called “Panel heating” arrived on the market 
and it started being used in conventional buildings in Europe.  
Since 1905, Frank Lloyd Wright incorporated several forms of radiant heating systems, emulating those he 
has seen during his travels to Japan. Three decades later, he installed a radiant panel heating in the Johnson 
Was Building. A few more systems were installed in the following years. He implemented the hydronic in-
slab radiant system in the heating system. More systems were developed later increasing its performance 
and saving energy.  
25.2.2. Radiant panel history 
In 1929 R.F. Norris of C.F. Burguess Frenger, designed a system for acoustical radiant heating ceiling.  
In 1953, Frenger designed a suspended panel type air conditioner “US 2662743 A”. Two years later, Rapp 
designed a combined radiant heat and acoustic tile unit structure “US 2721731 A”. Finally, a panel for 
radiant heating or cooling system “US 2782006 A” was designed by Frenger in 1957. Sketch in Figure 108.  
 
Figure 108: Sketch of "US 2662743 A" product (Frenger)

application of emerging technologies, improvement of thermal interface and increase surface area, 
implementation of graphite due to its unique properties, etc. See Figure 109.

Apart from the improvements, pipe from hydronic radiant systems have been developed along the years. In 
the beginning, pipes consisted of steel or iron piping were embedded in concrete slabs. After World War II, 
copper became the preferred material choice due to its malleability and ductility. However, by the 1970,
