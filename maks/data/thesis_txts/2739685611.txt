Abstract
Corrosion in tanker ships poses significant safety and economic risks to the maritime
industry. Traditional inspection methods are often time­consuming, labor­intensive, and
subject to human error. This thesis presents an innovative approach to corrosion detection
and mapping in tanker ships using autonomous drones equipped with RGB cameras and
LiDAR sensors.
The primary objective of this research is to develop a robust, automated system for de­
tecting and persistently mapping corrosion in complex 3D environments. A deep learning­
based image analysis is integrated with 3D point cloud processing, along with the intro­
duction of a novel persistent mapping algorithm.
The methodology employs a UNet architecture with various backbones (MobileNetV3­
Small, MobileNetV3­Large, ResNet18, and ResNet34) for pixel­wise corrosion detection
in RGB images. These 2D detections are then projected onto a 3D point cloud generated
from LiDAR data. To maintain consistent corrosion mapping as the drone moves, a voxel­
based representation is implemented using OctoMap, which accumulates evidence over
time.
Results demonstrate that the system effectively detects and maps corrosion, achieving
mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) scores of approximately 40% across all models on
the test set. The MobileNetV3­Large model achieved the highest Corrosion Detection
Rate (CDR) of 0.79 and showed the best balance between accuracy and computational
efficiency, making it ideal for drone­based applications. However, qualitative analysis on
drone­captured images revealed challenges in domain adaptation, with ResNet18 show­
ing better generalization to new environments.
The system’s ability to create persistent 3D corrosion maps was demonstrated through a
complete drone inspection, visualized using RViz. A video demonstration of the system in
action is available at ’https://youtu.be/CAsVO3fOcgg’, showcasing the real­time mapping
capabilities.
This research advances the field of automated corrosion inspection by bridging the gap
between 2D image analysis and 3D spatial mapping. While the current implementation
shows promising results, future work will focus on improving domain generalization, ex­
panding the dataset, and enhancing the stability of real­time 3D mapping.

Introduction
Corrosion is a pervasive and costly issue that affects a wide range of industries, lead­
ing to significant safety hazards, operational inefficiencies, and financial burdens. The
global cost of corrosion is estimated to be around 3.4% of the world’s GDP [1], highlight­
ing its economic significance. In the maritime sector, which is particularly vulnerable due
to the harsh environments faced by ships and offshore structures, corrosion poses a se­
vere threat to the structural integrity of vessels, especially tanker ships. Over time, the
corrosive effects of the marine environment can lead to the gradual degradation of metal
surfaces, compromising the safety and operational efficiency of these vessels. Figure 1.1
illustrates a typical ballast tank within a marine vessel, showcasing an environment where
corrosion commonly occurs.
Figure 1.1: Ballast tank in a marine vessel (left) and it’s location inside the tanker ship
(right).
Corrosion detection in marine vessels has traditionally relied on manual inspection tech­
niques, which, while effective to a degree, present significant limitations in the context of
large­scale, complex structures like tanker ships. These methods typically involve visual
inspections carried out by trained personnel, often supplemented by basic tools and non­
destructive testing (NDT) techniques [2]. Trained inspectors visually examine surfaces
for signs of corrosion such as rust, pitting, or discoloration. While this method can be
effective for detecting surface­level corrosion, it has several drawbacks:
1. Limited Access: Many areas of a ship, particularly in cargo tanks or ballast spaces,
are difficult or dangerous for human inspectors to access.
2. Subjectivity: The accuracy of visual inspections can vary significantly based on the
inspector’s experience and judgment.
3. Time and Labor Intensity: Thoroughly inspecting large vessels is a time­consuming
process, often requiring days or weeks to complete.
4. Intermittent Nature: Visual inspections are typically performed at set intervals, which
can allow corrosion to progress significantly between inspections.
The limitations of traditional methods underscore the need for more advanced, automated
corrosion detection systems. These systems have the potential to revolutionize the main­
tenance of the maritime industry by offering increased inspection speed, reduced labor

1.1
Objectives
This thesis explores the development of a drone­based automated system for corrosion
detection and persistent mapping in tanker ships. The primary objectives of this research
are to:
• Develop a deep learning model capable of accurately detecting and classifying mul­
tiple types of corrosion in diverse environments.
• Create a system that can process images in real­time on hardware suitable for drone
deployment.
• Implement a 3D mapping system that can create a persistent, high­resolution rep­
resentation of corrosion distribution.
• Achieve a high corrosion detection rate while maintaining a balance with computa­
tional efficiency.
• Develop a visualization system that clearly distinguishes between different types of
corrosion in the 3D map.
To achieve these objectives, I propose a novel approach that combines computer vision
with 3D spatial mapping. At the core of the system is a UNet­based deep learning model
designed to detect and segment corrosion in 2D images. This model builds upon re­
cent advancements in convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image segmentation,
adapting these techniques to the specific challenges of corrosion detection in maritime
environments.
While 2D image analysis provides valuable information about surface corrosion, under­
standing the spatial extent and severity of corrosion requires a three­dimensional perspec­
tive. To achieve this, the research utilizes LiDAR to obtain depth data, which is combined
with the pinhole camera model to project 2D image pixel coordinates into 3D space. This
enables the creation of a 3D model of the tanker ship, onto which detected corrosion areas
are mapped, resulting in a comprehensive spatial representation of the ship’s condition.
One of the significant challenges in this process is the efficient organization and manipu­
lation of 3D spatial data. This approach not only allows for a more intuitive visualization
of corrosion patterns but also enables quantitative analysis of affected areas. By calcu­
lating the surface area of corroded sections and comparing it to the total surface area, the
system can categorize the ship’s condition, providing ship owners and regulatory bodies
with a more reliable tool for maintaining vessel safety and integrity.

Related Work
The detection and monitoring of corrosion in marine vessels and steel structures have
seen significant advancements with the integration of deep learning and drone technolo­
gies. This section explores recent developments in automated corrosion detection, focus­
ing on deep learning approaches, drone­based inspection systems, and the challenges
in data collection and model training.
2.1
Automated Corrosion Detection Using Deep Learning
Recent advancements in corrosion detection have increasingly relied on deep learning
models due to their ability to learn complex features from raw data. The study by Nash et
al. (2022) introduced a deep learning­based corrosion detection system with confidence
estimation [3], which incorporated uncertainty modeling to improve decision­making in
automated corrosion assessments. This system helps mitigate errors by identifying re­
gions of low confidence, thereby improving the reliability of corrosion detection in critical
infrastructures.
Another study by Das et al. (2024) explored the use of DenseNet121 and EfficientNetB7
backbones for pixel­wise corrosion segmentation in steel structures, achieving impressive
accuracy results, although their method required manual annotation of corrosion images,
which can be labor­intensive. [4]
Building on the importance of reliable datasets, Nash et al. (2019) explored the use of
crowdsourced data for training deep learning models in corrosion detection [5]. Their
approach addresses the challenge of acquiring large, diverse datasets for model train­
ing, a common hurdle in developing robust corrosion detection systems. By leveraging
crowdsourced annotations, they demonstrated the potential for creating more generaliz­
able models capable of detecting corrosion across various environments and conditions.
2.2
Corrosion Detection via Drone­based Systems
The integration of deep learning with drone technology has opened new possibilities for
structural inspections. Forkan et al. (2022) introduced CorrDetector, a framework that
utilizes drone­captured images and ensemble deep learning for structural corrosion de­
tection [6]. Their system employs multiple convolutional neural network (CNN) models
to enhance detection accuracy and robustness. CorrDetector’s ability to process drone­
captured images highlights the potential for autonomous, large­scale inspections of com­
plex structures.
Drones have been increasingly used for autonomous inspections, particularly in difficult­
to­reach environments such as ship interiors and industrial facilities. For example, Ameli
et al. (2024) demonstrated the use of UAVs equipped with deep learning models to per­
form corrosion segmentation and condition rating in steel bridge inspections, highlighting
the versatility of drones for both 2D and 3D inspection tasks [7].
2.3
3D Mapping and Spatial Representation in Corrosion
Detection
While 2D image­based corrosion detection has seen significant progress, the integra­
tion of these techniques with 3D mapping for comprehensive structural assessment is an

understanding its distribution and severity within complex structures like ship interiors.
Hornung et al. (2013) introduced OctoMap, an efficient probabilistic 3D mapping frame­
work based on octrees [8]. Although not specifically designed for corrosion mapping,
OctoMap’s approach to creating memory­efficient 3D representations has been widely
adopted in robotics and could be adapted for spatial corrosion tracking.
Recent advancements have started addressing this gap in the context of industrial envi­
ronments. For example, Figueiredo et al. (2024) proposed a system integrating LiDAR­

the potential for combining vision­based corrosion detection with SLAM to create persis­
tent and spatially accurate representations of corroded areas.
In the context of structural inspection, Khaloo et al. (2018) demonstrated the use of un­
manned aerial systems for 3D reconstruction and damage quantification of civil infras­
tructure [10]. Their work showcases the potential of combining drone technology with
advanced 3D reconstruction techniques for comprehensive structural health monitoring,
which could be extended to corrosion mapping in marine vessels.
The integration of 3D mapping with deep learning­based corrosion detection presents
unique challenges. These include the need for real­time processing of high­dimensional
data, accurate registration of 2D detections onto 3D surfaces, and handling of occlu­
sions and incomplete data. Addressing these challenges is crucial for developing robust
systems capable of creating accurate and persistent 3D representations of corrosion in
complex environments.
2.4
Challenges and Future Directions
Despite these advancements, several challenges remain in the field of automated cor­
rosion detection. The work by Nash et al. (2019) [5] highlights the ongoing challenge
of acquiring large, diverse datasets for training robust models. Their approach of using
crowdsourced data offers a potential solution, but also raises questions about data quality
and consistency.
The studies by Forkan et al. [6] and Das et al. [4] both underscore the complexity of devel­
oping systems that can operate reliably in varied environmental conditions. Factors such
as lighting, surface texture, and the presence of other structural features can significantly
impact detection accuracy.
While the work by Figueiredo et al. (2024) shares similarities with this project, such as
the use of LiDAR­based SLAM for corrosion detection, there are key distinctions. Their
system focuses on handheld devices for portable inspections, whereas this work lever­
ages a drone­based approach, providing the added advantage of inspecting inaccessible
or hazardous areas, such as the interiors of ship tanks. Additionally, this work uses Oc­
toMap for voxel­based corrosion mapping, which offers a unique approach for creating
persistent and memory­efficient 3D representations.
Moreover, while significant strides have been made in 2D image­based corrosion detec­
tion, integrating these techniques with 3D mapping for comprehensive structural assess­
ment remains an area for further research. This project aims to address this gap by com­
bining deep learning models for corrosion detection with 3D voxel mapping techniques,

Image Dataset
The Dataset chapter provides an overview of the data used to train the deep learning mod­
els in this study and outlines the preprocessing techniques applied to ensure the dataset
was compatible with the chosen architectures. This chapter discusses the challenges of
working with real­world corrosion data, such as class imbalance and image standardiza­
tion, and explains the steps taken to prepare the dataset for training.
3.1
Data Preprocessing
The dataset utilized in this work consists of 600 images and their corresponding annotated
masks, which were provided by supervisors. These images were captured in various
marine vessel environments under different conditions, leading to a wide variety of image
resolutions:
• 4160x3120
• 4032x3024
• 640x480
• 3120x4160
• 1024x768
Since deep learning models like UNet [11] require consistent input sizes, it was necessary
to standardize these images. The UNet architecture, chosen for its suitability in pixel­wise
segmentation tasks, operates best with images that are resized to dimensions divisible
by powers of two. To accommodate this, the images were resized while maintaining their
aspect ratios to either 384x512 or 512x384 pixels based on their original dimensions,
followed by padding to a uniform size of 512x512 pixels. This preprocessing step was
important to ensure consistent input across the dataset.
In addition to resizing, a notable class imbalance in the dataset presented a significant
challenge. One of the corrosion classes was severely underrepresented, appearing in
only 47 images. Since this imbalance could result in unreliable model performance (over­
fitting to the dominant classes), it was decided to remove this class from the dataset. As
a result the final dataset was reduced to 556 images, with three corrosion classes and a
background class remaining.

CNN Architecture
4.1
UNet Architecture for Corrosion Detection
The goal of the deep learning models developed for this thesis is to accurately segment
areas of corrosion from high­resolution images captured inside tanker ships. The archi­
tecture chosen for this task is the UNet, a well­established model for image segmentation
due to its ability to capture both global and local context through its encoder­decoder struc­
ture. This section describes the UNet model, including the adaptations made to enhance
its performance for corrosion detection.
4.1.1
Overview of UNet
UNet [11] was chosen for this study due to its proven effectiveness in image segmenta­
tion tasks, particularly in scenarios requiring pixel­level precision, such as corrosion de­
tection. Its encoder­decoder structure, which captures both high­level features and fine
details, makes it ideal for identifying corrosion patterns on complex surfaces. Previous re­
search, such as the study by Das et al. (2024), demonstrated UNet’s strong performance
in detecting corrosion on metallic surfaces, showcasing its ability to generalize well across
varying corrosion types and environmental conditions [4]. This makes it a suitable choice
for maritime applications, where accurate segmentation is crucial for maintenance. Fig­
ure 4.1 illustrates the overall structure of the UNet architecture.
Figure 4.1: The UNet architecture from the original paper: ”Each blue box corresponds
to a multi­channel feature map. The number of channels is denoted on top of the box.
The x­y­size is provided at the lower left edge of the box. White boxes represent copied
feature maps. The arrows denote the different operations.”[11]
The encoder path of UNet progressively reduces the spatial dimensions of the input image
From Pixels to Persistent Voxels: Drone­based Corrosion Detection in Marine Vessels
using Deep Learning and 3D Voxel Mapping
13
==PAGE:22==
while increasing the depth of the feature maps. This process captures high­level semantic
features, allowing the network to understand the context of the image. In the original UNet
architecture, the encoder consists of repeated applications of 3x3 convolutions, each fol­
lowed by a rectified linear unit (ReLU) and a 2x2 max pooling operation with stride 2 for
downsampling.
The decoder in UNet reconstructs the image resolution by progressively upsampling the
feature maps produced by the encoder. Each upsampling layer is followed by convolution
operations to refine the feature maps, eventually restoring the original input size. This
process allows the network to generate a full­resolution segmentation output.
A key feature of the UNet architecture is its use of skip connections between correspond­
ing layers of the encoder and decoder paths. These connections ensure that fine spatial
details are preserved, which are essential for accurately delineating corrosion areas. By
concatenating feature maps from the encoder path with the upsampled features in the
decoder path, the model can integrate both high­level semantic information and low­level
spatial information, leading to more precise segmentation outputs.
The final layer of the UNet architecture consists of a 1x1 convolution that converts the
feature maps into a pixel­wise classification map. In the context of corrosion detection,
this layer assigns each pixel to one of the predefined classes: background or one of the
corrosion classes.
4.2
Encoder Adaptations
To enhance the feature extraction capabilities of our UNet model while maintaining compu­
tational efficiency, we replaced the standard encoder with pre­trained backbones. Specif­
ically, we experimented with two types of architectures: ResNet (18 and 34) and Mo­
bileNetV3 (Large and Small). This section details the key features of these architectures
and how they were integrated into our UNet model.
4.2.1
ResNet Encoder
ResNet (Residual Network) architectures, introduced by He et al. [12], are designed to
facilitate the training of very deep networks. We utilized ResNet18 and ResNet34 as
encoder backbones in our UNet model. The key innovation in ResNet is the introduction
of residual connections, which allow the network to learn residual functions with reference
to the layer inputs.
The core idea behind residual connections is to create shortcut connections that skip one
or more layers. These connections perform identity mapping, and their outputs are added
to the outputs of the stacked layers. This can be represented as:
y = F(x, {Wi}) + x
(4.1)
where x and y are the input and output vectors of the layers considered, and F(x, {Wi})
represents the residual mapping to be learned.

Introduction

5.2
Data Preparation
The dataset used in this study was divided into training, validation, and test sets following
a 70­20­10 split. To ensure reproducibility, a manual seed was set during the data splitting
process. Given the limited number of corrosion images, data augmentation was employed
to introduce variability and mitigate overfitting. The following augmentation techniques
were applied:
• Horizontal and vertical flips
• Random cropping with padding back to target size
• Random 90 degree rotations.
These augmentations ensure that the model is exposed to a variety of transformations
of the same image, simulating real­world conditions where corrosion may appear from
different perspectives. This not only improves the model’s generalization ability but also
artificially increases the size of the training dataset, which is particularly beneficial given
the limited number of original images.

limited dataset.

5.5.1
Iterative Model Saving
During the training process, iterative model saving was implemented to retain the best­
performing model based on the validation Intersection over Union (IoU) score. After each
epoch, the model’s performance on the validation set was evaluated, and the model with
the highest validation IoU was saved. This strategy ensures that the best version of the
model is preserved, preventing overfitting and providing the most generalizable solution
for corrosion detection.
5.5.2
Gradient Clipping
To further stabilize the training process, gradient clipping was applied with a norm of 2.
Gradient clipping is a technique used to prevent exploding gradients, a common issue
in deep learning when training with high learning rates or very deep architectures. By
limiting the magnitude of the gradients, this method ensures that updates to the model
weights remain stable, allowing for smoother convergence during training.

• Sensitivity to pixel­level misalignments: The mIoU metric is highly sensitive to small
pixel­level differences. In corrosion detection, slight variations in the predicted bound­

model accurately identifies the general location and extent of corrosion. A predic­
tion that correctly identifies a corroded area but is offset by just a few pixels from the
ground truth will be penalized heavily by the mIoU metric, despite being practically
useful for corrosion assessment.
• Imperfections in ground truth labels: It’s crucial to recognize that the ground truth la­
bels themselves, being human­annotated, are not 100% perfect or consistent. Cor­
rosion patterns can be subtle and complex, leading to variations in how different
annotators might label the same image. These imperfections in the ground truth
can have a significant ”domino” effect on the metric scores of the models. When

sitions and irregular patterns, make precise pixel­level annotation challenging even
for human experts. This ambiguity in defining exact boundaries of corroded areas
contributes to both ground truth labeling inconsistencies and difficulties in model
predictions.
These factors underscore why the models may perform better in the qualitative analysis
than the quantitative metrics suggest. The models are learning to detect corrosion ef­

7.2
Fundamentals of 3D Mapping in Corrosion Detection
7.2.1
Point Cloud Generation
Point clouds are three­dimensional representations of an environment, consisting of nu­
merous points in space. In the context of this corrosion detection system, point clouds
are primarily generated using Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) technology mounted
on the drone.
LiDAR operates by emitting laser pulses and measuring the time it takes for these pulses
to reflect off surfaces and return to the sensor. This time­of­flight principle allows the
system to calculate the distance to each point in the environment. By rapidly emitting
thousands of laser pulses in different directions, the LiDAR sensor creates a detailed
3D map of its surroundings. Each point in the resulting point cloud includes X, Y, and Z
coordinates representing its position in 3D space.
As the drone navigates through the ship’s interior, it continuously generates and updates
the point cloud. This real­time data acquisition allows for dynamic mapping of the envi­
ronment, capturing the geometric structure of the ship’s compartments, including walls,
floors, ceilings, and various structural elements.

7.2.2
OctoMap and Octree Structure
To efficiently manage and utilize the point cloud data, the system employs OctoMap, a
probabilistic 3D mapping framework based on octrees. An octree is a hierarchical data
structure that partitions 3D space by recursively subdividing it into eight octants, offering
an effective balance between spatial resolution and computational efficiency.
Figure 7.1: Illustration of an octree structure: (a) Spatial partitioning of a bounding cube,
(b) Corresponding octree representation, (c) Voxel with point cloud data [18]
As illustrated in Figure 7.1, the octree structure begins with a root node representing the
entire 3D space of interest. This root node can be subdivided into eight child nodes,
each representing an octant of the parent space. This subdivision process continues
recursively, creating finer resolutions in areas of interest. The smallest units of the octree,
known as leaf nodes, represent voxels at the finest resolution. These voxels contain the
actual point cloud data, depicted as black dots in the figure.
The octree structure offers several advantages for 3D mapping in corrosion detection.
Its memory efficiency allows for sparse representation of space, meaning areas with no
information, such as empty spaces in the ship, do not need to be stored explicitly. The
multi­resolution nature of octrees enables varying levels of detail, where areas with com­
plex structures or detected corrosion can be represented at a finer resolution, while empty
or uniform areas use a coarser resolution.
In the context of the corrosion detection system, the octree structure facilitates efficient
integration of point cloud data from the LiDAR sensor. As the drone moves and collects
new data, the octree can be dynamically updated, refining the map in real­time. This

1. Confidence Accumulation: When corrosion is detected in a voxel, its confidence

map.
4. Area Tracking: The system keeps track of the total inspected area and the corroded
area, updating these values when a voxel’s state changes. This allows for real­time
calculation of the overall corrosion percentage.

2. It then projects this point onto the 2D image plane to check for corrosion.
3. Based on the corrosion detection result, it updates the corresponding voxel in the
CorrosionTracker.

7.4.2
OctoMap Implementation and Challenges
OctoMap is utilized for efficient 3D spatial mapping. OctoMap creates a voxel grid from
the LiDAR point cloud data, providing a discretized representation of the ship’s interior.
This approach offers several advantages:
1. Memory efficiency through its octree data structure
2. Probabilistic occupancy estimation for handling sensor noise
3. Multi­resolution capabilities, allowing for different levels of detail
However, the use of OctoMap also presents challenges, particularly in maintaining stabil­
ity in real­time updates. A significant challenge encountered during implementation is the
instability of the voxel grid during real­time scanning. As the drone moves and scans its
surroundings, the grid continuously updates, causing voxels to intermittently appear and
disappear. This ”flickering” effect is mainly caused by slight inaccuracies in the drone’s
position estimation, sensor noise, and minor imperfections in measurements. These fac­
tors affect the probabilistic occupancy estimation in OctoMap, leading to voxel locations
being recomputed frequently, which in turn results in the grid being overwritten with new
voxel data.
Several methods were explored to mitigate this issue, including implementing Exponen­
tial Moving Average (EMA) for voxel state updates and using probabilistic approaches to
represent voxel data. However, these techniques proved ineffective and were ultimately
discarded. While the final result after a complete scan offers a reasonably accurate cor­
rosion map, the real­time visualization remains less stable than desired. This instability
affects the system’s ability to deliver consistent, real­time feedback during the inspection
process, limiting its utility in scenarios where continuous, reliable updates are critical.

Several factors could contribute to this performance shift:
• Domain Shift: The visual characteristics of corrosion in the drone­captured images

• Image Quality: Drone­captured images might have different lighting conditions, res­

• Overfitting vs. Generalization: The reversal in performance rankings indicates that

A crucial factor contributing to this performance shift is the varying number of training

main.
This analysis underscores the critical need for diverse training data and robust valida­
tion across multiple domains in developing practical corrosion detection systems. It also
highlights the potential benefits of ensemble methods or domain adaptation techniques
to bridge the gap between controlled training environments and real­world applications.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to develop an innovative drone­based system for automated corrosion
detection and mapping in marine vessels, with the aim of addressing the limitations of
traditional inspection methods. Through the integration of deep learning, 3D mapping
techniques, and drone technology, significant strides towards achieving this goal has been
made.
Revisiting the initial objectives:
• Deep learning models capable of detecting multiple classes of corrosion has been
successfully developed. The best­performing model, MobileNetV3­Large, achieved
a mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) of 0.41 and a Corrosion Detection Rate
(CDR) of 0.79 on the test set.
• The system demonstrated real­time processing capabilities, with the MobileNetV3­
Large model achieving 153 FPS, well above our goal of real­time performance on
drone hardware. Althoug
• A 3D mapping system using OctoMap has been implemented, creating a persistent
voxel­based representation of corrosion distribution.
• The visualization system, implemented in RViz, successfully distinguished between
different types of corrosion in the 3D map using a color­coded scheme, enhancing
the interpretability of the results.
• We demonstrated the system’s ability to perform a complete inspection of a simu­
lated ship interior, as evidenced by the full drone inspection run visualized in RViz

the stability of the voxel grid during real­time scanning, resulting in a ”flickering” effect as

further refinement and improvement, the system developed in this thesis provides a solid

8
Future Work
The development and implementation of the drone­based corrosion detection system
have revealed several areas for potential improvement and further research.
Stabilizing 3D Voxel Mapping
A significant challenge in the current system is the instability of the voxel grid during real­
time scanning. As the drone moves, imperfect measurements and sensor noise cause
voxels to flicker, appearing and disappearing as new information is processed. While this
doesn’t severely impact the final result, it affects the robustness and visual quality of the
real­time mapping. Future work should focus on:
1. Implementing Exponential Moving Average (EMA) to smooth out voxel updates.
2. Exploring advanced probabilistic approaches for voxel data representation and up­
dating.
3. Investigating Kalman filtering or other state estimation techniques to reduce noise
in sensor measurements.
Improving Domain Generalization and Dataset Expansion
The current models show signs of overfitting to the training domain, resulting in reduced
performance on drone­captured images. A key factor in this limitation is the relatively small
dataset of 500 images. While this sample size is not uncommon in corrosion detection
research, it presents clear limitations for robust, generalizable models. Future work should
prioritize:
1. Aim to increase the dataset by an order of magnitude or more, targeting a minimum
of 5,000 to 10,000 diverse corrosion images.
2. Developing a more diverse training dataset that includes a wider range of corrosion
types, lighting conditions, and camera angles.
3. Incorporate a substantial number of non­corroded surfaces and potential false pos­
itives (e.g., rust stains, paint deterioration) to improve the model’s discrimination
capabilities.
4. Implementing domain adaptation techniques, such as gradient reversal [20], to en­
hance model generalization across different environments.
5. Exploring adversarial training methods to make the model more robust to domain
shifts.
By significantly expanding and diversifying the dataset, future iterations of the corrosion
detection system could achieve much higher accuracy and generalization capabilities.
This expanded dataset would not only improve the model’s ability to detect various cor­
rosion types accurately but also enhance its robustness across different inspection sce­
narios and vessel types. Moreover, a larger dataset would provide a stronger foundation
for implementing advanced techniques such as transfer learning and domain adaptation,
further improving the system’s overall performance and reliability.
