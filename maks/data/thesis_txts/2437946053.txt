Abstract 
The next generation mobile network, 5G, is just a few years away from becoming commercially available. 
Apart from bringing higher bitrates and lower latency than previous generation networks, the power 
consumption is expected to increase substantially. This is primarily due to the increase in the number of base 
stations needed to form a sufficient 5G HetNet structure. Mobile networks have been designed as “always-
on” networks resulting in a nearly constant power consumption regardless of the traffic load. 
In this report it is investigated how implementation of a SON sleep-algorithm can affect the power 
consumption in LTE and 5G RAN like topology. Further, how such a power saving strategy affect the user 
QoE in terms of retransmissions and throughput. The investigation is based on simulation through a MatLab 
model known as MONSTeR. 
The system analysis showed that power savings up to 48% is achievable without significant reductions of the 
user QoE. An additional and more aggressive sleep-algorithm has been investigated on a 5G RAN topology 
where additional power savings where achieved compared to the one used in MONSTeR. 
The results indicate that it is possible to change from the classic “always-on” to a “on-when-needed” 
strategy, without QoE compromises.

1 Introduction 
Since the introduction of the first commercial mobile communication systems in the 1970’s, followed by 
GSM, UMTS, LTE and all versions in between, the focus have mainly been on coverage and capacity of the 
networks. The better coverage, the more customers can access the service and the higher capacity the better 
the service. However, the rapid growth and demand for information and communication technology (ICT) 
systems have led to a massive increase in power consumption. It is expected that by 2030 the global energy 
consumed by LTE and 5G wireless access networks alone will exceed 2.500 TWh annually, and rising [1]. 
The trend in telecommunication is heading towards heterogeneous network structures, an increased number 
of Small, Macro, Micro and Pico cells. This has led to an increased interest in energy efficiency (EE) and 
how this can be a part of the future mobile communication systems, especially as part of the upcoming 5G 
standard in 2020. The telecommunication community are currently proposing a variety of initiatives on how 
to improve EE in LTE and 5G, [2], [3] and [4] just to mention a few, covering both elements in the radio 
access network (RAN) as well as in the mobile backhaul. 
Apart from the positive environmental impact of a reduced energy consumption in telecommunication 
networks, the potential reduction of operational expenditures (OPEX) from the operator’s perspective seems 
attractive. According to [5], 50-60% of the power consumption in cellular networks are related to the RAN. 
This have led to the introduction of so-called “Green radio” initiatives aimed at reducing power consumption 
in RAN components. 
The first part of this thesis presents a comprehensive literature study on the transition from LTE to 5G, the 
current state of the 5G standardization process, the architecture and the EE initiatives mentioned. Further, the 
state of the art within EE and energy optimization in wireless mobile communication systems is presented as 
well as the challenges and limitations. 
The second part of this thesis is based on network simulations in order to optimize EE strategies. The 
simulations are based on the MatLab model “MONSTeR”. The model is constructed around the LTE system 
toolbox available in MatLab. The model is public available at Github1.

2 From LTE to 5G 
The 3GPP standardization of the 5G system architecture is currently in stage 2, as specified in [6] as of 
December 2017. To explain the architectural differences between LTE and 5G, both architectures are 
examined the following sections, starting by the latest iteration of LTE. 
 
2.1 LTE architecture 
The latest available radio access network “Long Term Evolution - Advanced” (LTE-A), currently specified 
in 3GPP release 10 [7] define the benchmark within commercial available mobile networks. LTE-A is built 
around the evolved packet system (EPS), evolved universal terrestrial radio access network (E-UTRAN) and 
the packet data network (PDN). An overview of the architecture is shown in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: LTE-A network architecture. 
The LTE components and interfaces in the E-UTRAN, EPS and PDN are briefly described in the following 
subsections. 
 
2.2 LTE-A RAN and EPC 
The RAN/E-UTRAN for LTE consists of mainly two components. These being the eNodeBs (eNB) and the 
LTE Relay Nodes (RN). Both are base stations and in charge of providing coverage and capacity in the radio 
related part of network. An eNB is directly connected to the backhaul through access technology like a fiber, 
xDSL, native Ethernet or wireless in form of a point-to-point microwave radio. On the other hand, a Relay 
Node is using the LTE-A air-interface to perform self-backhauling through an eNB nearby. The eNB 
providing capacity for the RN then become a “donor eNB”, and the resources towards the backhaul are then 
shared between the donor eNB and the attached RN(s). 
==PAGE:14==
S123317 
 
 
 
DTU - Department of Photonics Engineering 
 
Page 3 
By applying RN(s) at strategic places in the network, the coverage of the donor eNB can be extended at a 
relatively low cost. The same is true for indoor Femto-cells. Femto-cells rely on Ethernet to access the 
backhaul. 
The eNB is responsible for a series of control plane functions. One important function, in terms of energy 
optimization, is the radio resource management (RRM) that is used for controlling the radio interface, 
including resource allocation and monitoring, prioritization and traffic scheduling. Further, functions within 
mobility management (MM) are of interest, such as radio signal level measurements of both user equipment 
(UE) and eNB, in relation to handover functionalities. 
The Mobility Management Entity (MME) is the main control element in the EPC. It provides authentication 
of UEs and is responsible for selecting the Serving Gateway (S-GW) and PDN Gateway for the eNB to use 
when handling UE traffic. 
The function of the Serving Gateway (S-GW) is data tunneling based on MME instructions. While the MME 
changes based on the location of the UE, the S-GW remains the same. The S-GW reroutes traffic in case of 
handover or MME update. 
The Packet Data Network Gateway (PDN-GW) is the edge router between the EPC and external networks. 
The Policy and Charing Resource Function (PCRF) is a part of the policy and charging control (PCC) in the 
network and handles policies related to customer billing. 
A more detailed description of the LTE RAN, EPC, interfaces and protocols can be found in [8]. 
 
2.3 LTE backhaul interfaces 
Communication between network components in LTE is performed through varies interfaces, some divided 
into user and control planes, for transport through the backhaul. As illustrated in Figure 1, all eNBs 
communicate with an edge router/EPC over the S1 plane, using the general packet radio service tunneling 
protocol user (GTP-U) interface. The S1 plane consists of a user plane (U-plane) for transport of user traffic 
between the eNB and the mobile management entity (MME). The S1 control plane (C-plane) consists of the 
signaling information traffic between the eNB and the MME. 
The X2 U-plane and C-plane are used for transferring user data and signaling information, such as inter-cell 
radio resource management information, between eNBs in cases where an inter eNB handover is performed. 
Further information on the S1 and X2 interfaces can be found in [9]. 
The S11 interface is used between the MME and the serving gateway (S-GW). A new bearer must be 
established, since the user and control plane from the S1 interface is separated ([10] s.10.3.3). The S11 
interface uses the GTPv2-C protocol as specified by 3GPP, see ([11] s.5.1) for the general format. 
The S5/S8 interface is used between the S-GW and the public data network gateway (PDN-GW). The 
interface is split between non-roaming (S5) and roaming (S8) scenarios. The protocol for the S5/S8 interface 
is the GTPv2-C, as for the S11 interface. 
The S6a interface is used between the MME and the home subscriber server (HSS). The HSS is used for user 
location, authentication etc. The interface relies on the Diameter protocol and the interface functions can be 
found in ([10] s.10.6.2.2). 
The Gx interface is used between the PDN-GW and the Policy and Charging Rules Function (PCRF). The 
Gx interface is based on the Diameter protocol and is used for policy and charging control. 
The interface from the S-GW to external networks uses the SGi interface. The SGi interface is IP based. 
Connections can vary based on the external parties and the bearer type.

2.4 5G architecture (3GPP TS 23.501) 
The standardization of the 5G architecture is not completed by the spring of 2018. The current 3GPP 
technical specification on the subject, TS 23.501, introduces the overall idea of the architecture. However, 
while the core network has been renamed, the functionalities remain more or less the same as in LTE-A. The 
most interesting change in the EPC compared to LTE is the structure in which the varies components are 
interconnected. Figure 2 shows the 5G core network system architecture (without roaming functionalities), 
where: 
UE: User equipment, (R)AN: (Radio) access network, AMF: Access and mobility management function, 
SMF: Session management function, UPF: User plane function, DN: Data network, PCF: Policy control 
function, AF: Application function, NSSF: Network slice selection function, AUSF: Authentication server 
function, UDM: Unified data management. 
 
Figure 2: 5G core network system architecture (non-roaming). 
The main difference in the RAN part of the 5G network, compared to LTE, is that the new N2 interface 
allows both 5G New Radio (NR), LTE and non-3GPP access technologies, such as Wi-Fi etc. 
The AMF in Figure 2 corresponds to the MME in LTE. As described in [12], the AMF provides 
authentication, authorization, mobility management etc. in relation to the UE. The SMF is responsible for 
session management and allocation of IP addresses, much like the S-GW and PDN-GW in LTE. The PCF is 
in charge of policy control for providing Quality of Service (QoS), based on packet flow information from 
AF, as well as mobility and session management in relation to the AMF and SMF.  
The AUSF and UDM store authentication data and subscription data, much like HSS in LTE. For further 
information see 3GPP TS 23.501 [6]. 
 
2.5 5G RAN interworking with EPC 
The rollout of the 5G RAN, and especially the New Radio (NR) concept, will likely be the first step towards 
a partial implementation of 5G, as conducted by the industry at the moment [13]. The backward 
compatibility of 5G allows for interworking between the 5G RAN and the existing EPC of LTE. This allows 
for a dynamic RAT (multi-RAT) and a streamlined transition between LTE and NR ([14] s.3.4). Figure 3 
shows an example of a combined 5G RAN and LTE EPC.

2.5.1 LTE Layers data flow 
Both LTE and 5G are all-IP based networks. This requires that IP packets can be adapted from the IP layer to 
the physical layer, for radio transmission. The E-UTRAN consists of five protocol layers. The logical 
connection between these are shown in Figure 4.  
 
Figure 4: LTE protocol layers and data flow 
==PAGE:17==
S123317 
 
 
 
DTU - Department of Photonics Engineering 
 
Page 6 
Packets that are received at a layer is defined as a Service Data Unit (SDU), while packets leaving a layer is 
defined as a Protocol Data Unit (PDU). 
The PDCP (Packet Data Convergence Control) layer receives SDU packets (IP) from the IP layer and 
perform header compression and adds a PDCP header before parsing them as PDU packets for the RLC 
layer. The PDCP header compression is performed due to air resource savings and works by replacing the IP 
20 bytes header with a 1-4 byte(s) token [15]. 
The Radio Link Control (RLC) layer receives the SDU packets (PDCP PDUs) and perform packet 
segmentation. Depending on the radio conditions and size of the RLC SDUs the packets can be combined or 
split into several RLC PDUs. 
The MAC layer adds the MAC header and padding to the RLC PDUs (MAC SDUs), such that the length of 
the MAC PDU corresponds with the transmission time interval. The MAC PDU is then parsed to the 
physical layer and processed as described in section 2.5.2. 
2.5.2 Physical layer 
Both LTE-A and 5G NR is based on Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM) in the downlink 
direction and on the Single Carrier Frequency Division Multiple Access (SC-FDMA) in the uplink direction. 
The use of OFDM in the downlink direction is due to its interference resilience. The carriers are closely 
spaced, and the signals are orthogonal to each other. This makes OFDM very resilient and spectrum 
efficient. On the other hand, this results in a high peak to average power ratio that further results in increased 
power consumption in the radio frequency amplifier in order to accommodate linearity[16]. For the same 
reason OFDM is not used in the uplink direction.  
In the uplink direction SC-FDMA is used. SC-FDMA use only one carrier instead of multiple as in ODFM. 
This results in a lower peak to average power ratio, while obtaining the interference resilience from OFDM.  
In OFDM/SC-FDMA, the time domain is divided into frames. Each frame is 10ms and consists of 10 sub-
frames. In the frequency domain, the spectrum is divided into slots of 7 symbols/resource blocks. The 
channel bandwidth and the number of resource blocks in LTE can vary. The options are shown in Table 1.

A sub-frame consists of 2 slots, i.e. 14 resource blocks. When assigning resources in LTE, the smallest entity 
consists of one sub-frame times 12 sub-carriers, also known as a physical resource block (PRB). The 
structure can be seen in Figure 5.

To achieve the best possible spectral efficiency each symbol is modulated. In LTE three modulation formats 
are available, based on Quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM). The three QAM modulations are: 
 
4QAM: 2 bits per symbol 
 
16QAM: 4 bits per symbol 
 
64QAM: 6 bits per symbol 
The modulation is chosen based on the channel quality indicator (CQI), and indirectly the signal-to-noise 
ratio (SNR). The higher SNR level, the higher modulation is possible. 
2.5.2.1 
Downlink reference signals 
As seen from Figure 5, some symbols are dedicated reference signals/pilot tones. The main function of the 
reference signals is to support the demodulation and equalization of the information transmitted. 
Additionally, the reference signals also provide channel quality feedback, used for control of down- and 
uplink power [17] for instance. 
The downlink reference signals can be divided into three groups. These are the cell-specific reference signals 
(CRSs), the UE-specific reference signals (DM-RSs) and channel state information reference signals (CSI-
RSs) [18]. 
The CRSs are transmitted in every sub-frame, according to the distribution shown in Figure 5. The channel 
estimation and demodulation probabilities are exclusive to the higher transmission modes (7 and up). For 
more information on transmission modes see [19]. The CRSs are used for channel state information (CSI) as

2.6 Network planning 
The two fundamental topics in mobile network planning are coverage and capacity. When it comes to 
planning of mobile networks, both coverage and capacity need to be adjusted to match the environment in 
question. 
Traditional network planning is based on “always-on” base stations that are ready to cope with peak load at 
all times. The annual Cisco Global Mobile Data Traffic Forecast [20] predicts that the global mobile data 
traffic will increase almost 300% between 2018 and 2021, around the time 5G implementations are getting 
momentum. To avoid a factor three increase, or worse, in power consumption, alternative network and 
topology planning measures will need to be investigated. 
2.6.1 Coverage 
Coverage planning depends on the geographical area, the user profiles in that area and the existing network 
topology. In a typical LTE network, the goal is to have just enough overlap in coverage between eNBs to 
perform seamless UE handover. This provides the maximal coverage with the minimal number of eNBs and 
reduced interference. 
New RAN designs, as suggested in 5G NR, will change the way coverage is deployed. Instead of only eNBs 
providing coverage, several alternatives will emerge, resulting in increased coverage overlap. The question 
of how this impacts things like user experience, power consumption and functionality is then to be 
considered.  
2.6.2 Capacity 
When coverage is in place, the next task is to provide the covered area with sufficient capacity. A mobile 
network consists of two parts in terms of capacity, as shown in 2.2. These are the radio interface, from the 
eNB to the UE and the backhaul interface, from the eNB to the backhaul. Both interfaces should be 
dimensioned such that bottlenecks are avoided. 
This project is related to the RAN part of the mobile network, therefore the radio resources will be the main 
focus. The changes in network planning will change the design and demands of capacity in future mobile 
networks. 
The alternatives in both coverage and capacity, the impact of future energy optimization solutions and state 
of the art is described in the literature study in section 3.

than previous. A description is given of the current transmission methods on the physical layer, including

highlighted.

3 Energy optimization in mobile networks 
As stated in the introduction, the energy consumption of mobile networks is rising. A mobile system consists 
of three main components: the radio access network (RAN), the aggregation part and the core. The energy 
consumed in the core of the network is already in the process of optimization, for instance by projects like 
M-CORD [21]. The aggregation part of the network is transitioning towards a fully optical access network, 
with a much lower energy consumption compared to traditional xDSL access networks or radio link 
connections [22].  The RAN part of the network, i.e. the base stations, as stated in [5] and [23], consumes 60-
70% of the energy within a mobile network, while being responsible for a large part of the carbon footprint 
as well [24]. The RAN is therefore a good starting point in the investigation of energy optimization in mobile 
networks. 
The number of base stations will increase in the upcoming 5G network. The focus in previous generations of 
mobile networks have been on coverage and capacity of the varies systems, and not so much on the energy 
efficiency of the networks. The result is a near “always-on” network topology that is ready to cope with peak 
hour traffic at all times.

3.2.1 Mobile backhaul 
3.2.1.1 
Topology adjustments 
The mobile backhaul, as described in section 2.2, can be optimized by changes or adjustments in the 
topology. This can be done for instance by introducing new topologies like Cloud RAN (C-RAN), as shown 
in [28] where the authors find that a cooperative Baseband Unit (BBU) pool can be used for load balancing 
and thereby reduce the energy consumption compared to cases where the BBUs are decentralized. This also 
results in a reduced need for cooling, since Remote Radio Heads (RRH) are air-cooled. 
3.2.1.2 
Component virtualization 
A second technique for improving the energy consumption is by introducing component virtualization. One 
such approach is demonstrated by The Open Networking Foundation (ONF) that are working on a Software 
Defined Network (SDN) solution called M-CORD (Mobile Central Office Re-architected as a Datacenter) 
platform [21]. M-CORD virtualize all components in the core and access network towards the RAN, in what 
they call the virtualized mobile packet core (vEPC), creating a C-RAN structure. This can potentially save 
energy due to the centralized architecture. 
3.2.1.3 
Cooling management 
The two techniques mentioned above both contributes to a more centralized network structure. This allows 
for more efficient cooling of network components. Implementation of Heterogeneous Networks HetNets, as 
described in section 3.2.2, further contributes to energy savings by cooling reduction. From the RAN 
perspective, this saving is primarily related to the fact that smaller base stations (Micro, Pico, Femto) often 
do not need additional cooling while in operation compared to Macro base stations [29]. 
3.2.2 Base stations 
One of the popular ways to increase the coverage and capacity in mobile networks is by deploying a so-
called hierarchical cell structure (HCS) [30]. The HCS uses a Macro-, Micro-, Picocell style structure as seen 
in Figure 6. While the HCS approach is great in operator’s pursuit of increased coverage and capacity of 
their network, the energy consumption, on the other hand, might increase as well. The basic cell structure is 
constructed based on peak traffic loads and is therefore often over dimensioned outside peak hours.

3.2.2.1 
Traffic adaption 
From an energy optimization point of view, one of the more explored solutions is to turn off/put to sleep 
cells that are not necessary at times where the cell capacity is under-utilized. In both [31] and [32], UE traffic

patterns are used to construct a traffic forecast used in the respective cell areas. Both papers find that energy 
savings can be obtained based on forecast methods and that the energy saving is closely linked to the base 
station density. However, both methods solely rely on the traffic forecast when turning base stations back on 
again. This static approach is very inefficient and can result in situations where the network become 
congested. Both solutions are very sensitive to modifications of the coverage area, making scaling and real-
world implementations difficult due to changing attenuation conditions. 
In [33], the authors introduce a performance metric based on the area spectral efficiency (ASE). The ASE is 
then used as an indicator for how utilized a given area is and based on that turn on/off given base stations. 
Their approach is to use Signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio (SINR) from attached UEs to estimate the 
ASE. A more simplified version based on the SNR measurements in the network were purposed as well. 
When the number of active UEs drop below a threshold, the base stations where turned off. When the ASE 
in the area decreases, i.e. more UEs are active, the base stations are then turned on again. Based on 
simulations the authors found that the energy consumption of their test network could be reduced by 60-
70%. Despite the good results in [33], the solution lack the functionality of UE handovers in cells that are to 
be turned off. The QoE can therefore not be guaranteed. Further, there are no indications of how scalable the 
solution is. 
3.2.2.2 
Coverage and power adaption 
Another method that can be incorporated in an energy optimization strategy is cell coverage manipulation, 
also known as cell zooming. This can be done by adjusting the transmission power level and/or adjusting the 
tilt of the base station antennas. The idea is that in situations where the cell capacity is under-utilized, it can 
reduce its transmission power or be placed in a sleep-mode. Surrounding base stations can then 
amplify/adjust their coverage in areas where base stations are in a power saving state. Figure 7 shows 
examples of cell zooming by coverage reduction and cell sleep-mode.

The cell zooming algorithms introduced in [34] is based on a so-called zooming period that consists of three 
stages: a coordination state where base stations in a given area collects cell information, a transition state 
where the base stations adjust based on the coordination findings, and a serving state where the base stations 
operate in the new configuration. A centralized and distributed version of the algorithm were compared, with 
the result that the centralized algorithm outperformed the distributed algorithm in terms of energy 
consumption in relation to outage blocking probability. In the centralized version, a cell zooming server is 
used for collection and coordination of base station settings. This can result in a scalability issue.

most likely not hold up in a real-world implementation.

The coordination of cells can be used for determining when to power on/off base stations. The 3GPP

3.2.3 Femtocells 
Femtocells are designed to provide low-power cellular coverage indoors, in licensed spectrum, especially in 
residential and office buildings [38]. As seen in Figure 1 the traffic served by a femtocell is backhauled 
through the internet and can thereby offload traffic from the mobile network. The implementation of 
femtocells can be used in combination with the energy optimization strategies, discussed in the previous 
sections. The use of femtocells can bring down the number of stationary residential UEs, i.e. users that are at 
home or work, that otherwise would be attached to a base station. However, it can be a challenge to 
implement femtocells in such ways that pilot power from the femtocell does not interfere with passing users. 
The authors in [39] propose a method for auto-configuration and self-optimization in femtocell deployments. 
Their simulation results demonstrate that it is possible to adapt the transmission scheme and power in such a 
way that only the targeted areas/users are covered. 
The results in [39] are supported by the findings in [40], where the same principle of self-optimization is 
tried on varies femtocell scenarios. The optimization process is done by use of self-organizing network 
(SON) mechanisms, where cells learn their environment in order to adjust their transmission strategy 
towards optimal performance. In [40] the SON-algorithm uses the UE(s) SINR measurements to determine a 
virtual cell size for optimal SINR. The study did not investigate energy savings from a network point-of-
view but found that the average energy consumption could be reduced by nearly 60% from the UE 
perspective. The subject of SON will be discussed further in section 3.2.5.1. 
3.2.4 UEs 
3.2.4.1 
Load profiling 
The UEs can be used in load profiling. The traffic and load patterns can be used to estimate/predict future 
load in cells. The amount of details and information that can be extracted by the operators is demonstrated in 
[41], where mobile traffic is categorized into five areas; resident, transport, office, entertainment and 
comprehensive. This information can be used to categorize base stations and thereby optimizing the 
coverage and capacity of certain geographical areas. It is likely that such categorizations can be used to differ 
between varies energy optimization strategies. However, from an energy efficiency standpoint the amount 
and type of traffic in reference to the time of request is the most relevant parameters. 
3.2.4.2 
Device to Device relaying 
Device to Device (D2D) communication was first introduced in mobile networks in the LTE-A standard. The 
goal of D2D is to let UEs act as small LTE relays between the base station and the UE requesting coverage. 
This is a cheap way of boosting coverage, reducing Tx-power and interference, from a mobile operator point 
of view. The concept is shown in Figure 11. 
==PAGE:28==
S123317 
 
 
 
DTU - Department of Photonics Engineering 
 
Page 17 
 
Figure 11: D2D relaying. 
Figure 11 shows how four UEs (red) act as relay for a UE (blue) that are outside coverage of the base station. 
This means that the operator does not have to increase Tx-power to a level beyond that required to 
communicate to the (red) UE relays. From a UE point of view, acting as relay raises two main concerns. 
1. Acting as relay will increase the power consumption, i.e. drain the battery of the mobile phone. 
2. UEs are relaying unknown, and potential illegal, traffic to/from other UEs. 
The first concern related to increased battery usage can lead to unsatisfied customers, if they experience their 
phones need charging several times a day. To get customers to willingly use battery power to serve others 
requires some sort of motivator. It could be in form of a subscription discount, where the user gets a financial 
benefit, or it could be token based where users “earn” tokens when they provide relay services and “spend” 
tokens when they receive relayed services, as explained in [42]. 
The second concern is related to security and privacy of the customers. It is up to the individual operator to 
ensure that all necessary precautions are taken before UE relaying is implemented in their networks. 
3.2.5 Technology 
The following section concentrates on a selection of technologies that can be used in energy optimization 
strategies. Each technology can be applied in several aspects of mobile networks and is therefore listed in 
this separate section. 
3.2.5.1 
Self-organizing networks 
The concept of self-organization in networks have existed for quite some time. The idea of having self-
organizing networks (SON) in mobile networks have emerged during the past few years. The first time SON 
is mentioned in a standardization process for mobile networks is in 3GPP release 8 from 2014 [43]. The 
main motivation for introducing SON in mobile networks are that: 
 
Mobile networks are becoming larger and more complex 
 
Parallel operations are performed across multiple wireless network elements 
 
The amount of configuration of new/existing base stations is rising 
The 3GPP definition of SON in LTE networks is described as functionalities that enable a network to 
become self-configuring, self-optimized, self-healing and to handle neighbor base station relations. 
While these requirements are relatively weak in the definition, several research papers have investigated the 
topic of SON in mobile networks. One such paper is [44] that presents a comprehensive survey on self-
organization in future cellular networks. The authors identify the primary functions that are desirable in a 
mobile SON as adaptive functionality: the network should be able to detect changes and adapt in a way that 
minimize/maximize the effect of the change. Emergent behavior: as emphasized by the fundamental concept 
of SON in the form of monitoring, detecting, diagnosis and compensation processes. Distributed control: 
==PAGE:29==
S123317 
 
 
 
DTU - Department of Photonics Engineering 
 
Page 18 
even though distributed control is not a requirement in the self-organization, it will be an advantage in 
mobile network implementations. This is due to the size and scalability of the networks. Local interactions: 
as an extension to the distributed control. The network should be able to obtain a global network behavior, 
based on local decisions from local interactions.  
The above functionalities comprise to three main SON characteristics. 
 
Scalability: In order for a SON solution to be affective in a mobile network, the complexity of the 
solution must not exceed with the scale of the implementation. This is closely related to the desired 
decentralized structure, since algorithms that can operate in local entities can scale rather well. 
 
Stability: The SON operations must be able to reach a stable state within a reasonable timeframe. 
Further, there should be an upper limit to the number of changes an algorithm can perform in a given 
time interval. This is important since improper stability precautions can lead to oscillation between 
states, with the risk of infinite state looping.   
 
Agility: The agility in SON implementations are related to both the scalability and the stability. The 
implementation should be able to adapt in such a way that is not too fast, resulting in over 
reaction/compensation, or neither too slow. 
It should be noted that algorithms that perform on the characteristics mentioned above can be engineered in 
many ways. Some of the more complex solutions are wrapped around machine learning disciplines like 
cognitive networks [45]. 
The use cases for SON in mobile networks are many, as highlighted in [44]. From an energy optimization 
point of view, SON can be applied in areas of coverage and capacity optimization, interference reduction and 
automatic neighbor relation functions. The combination of these provides the tools needed for 
implementation of SON driven energy optimization. 
Coverage and capacity optimization via SON management builds on measurements reports from the 
individual cells. These are then used as triggers for cell adjustments, much like cell zooming described in 
section 3.2.2. These adjustments are not exclusive to the RAN, as described in [46] and demonstrated in [47], 
SON can be used for load balancing across the mobile backhaul and core network as well. 
3.2.5.2 
Coordinated multi-point 
Interference have always been a challenge in mobile networks. In LTE(-A) the frequency reuse is 1, which 
means that every cell uses the exact same frequency spectrum. This can lead to situations where, especially 
at the cell edges, the SNR drastically decreases. To accommodate this problem, 3GPP incorporated an 
enhancement in release 11 called coordinated multi-point (CoMP). CoMP is categorized via two methods, 
being joint processing and coordinated beamforming. In this section, only the joint processing aspect is 
considered, as this potentially brings value to the energy efficiency of mobile networks. The idea behind 
beamforming is discussed in section 3.2.5.3. 
CoMP Joint Transmission (CoMP-JP) is built around a HetNet structure, where larger coverage cells (CC) 
provide a basic coverage for a group of smaller booster cells (BC). Each CC base station is interconnected 
with one another via the X2 interface. All BC base stations are interconnected as well and connected to their 
respective CC base station. 
The goal of CoMP-JP is to improve coverage and cell edge throughput, which then lead to an increase in the 
overall system throughput. Figure 12 shows three CC base stations and their border cells, each containing a 
number of BCs.

From an energy efficiency point of view, the dual layer strategy is the most interesting. In this case, the UEs 
are connected to a number of BCs, that are relaying back to the coordinating CC for final data processing. 
In the uplink direction, the UE will send the same data packets to multiple BCs, the packets are not decoded 
at the BC but forwarded to the CC. At the CC, all “copies” of the packet is decoded and any errors corrected. 
This type of transmission allows for a lower SNR than usual. The same procedure is used in the downlink 
direction, where multiple BCs send the same packets to the UE. 
From an energy efficiency standpoint, a CoMP-JP supported network can be beneficial by lowering the 
required transmission powers from base station equipment (on the UE side). This requires strict control and 
management of the BCs involved, as described in section 3.2.2.2. Examples of energy efficient algorithms 
for optimized CoMP-JP is often build on the network blocking probability and traffic thresholds, as the 
simulations in [49] suggest. 
The downside to CoMP is that it generates a lot of traffic on the X2 interface between the CCs and BCs. 
Every element transmits the same data. This can lead to backhaul congestion in some heavy loaded 
networks. 
3.2.5.3 
MIMO beamforming 
The RAN environment in 5G is often considered being a user dense urban area. Such areas are prone to 
intercell interference, due to the number of macro base stations and the large number of users in a small area. 
To improve the spectral efficiency and limit interference in such situations, multiple-input multiple-output 
(MIMO) beamforming was introduced in 3GPP release 9. 
MIMO is known from previous versions of LTE and is the ability to use the same antenna array and 
frequency resources to serve several UEs. Massive MIMO is an enhanced version that refers to the large 
==PAGE:31==
S123317 
 
 
 
DTU - Department of Photonics Engineering 
 
Page 20 
number of antennas in the base station compared to traditional MIMO. Another improvement in massive 
MIMO is the ability to utilize multipath propagation. To perform transmissions in multipath environments 
the channel state information (CSI) is needed [50], i.e. the spatial transfer functions between each antenna 
and each UE. The CSI is then used for encoding and decoding of the respective transmissions from each 
antenna array. The basic concept of beamforming is built on precise CSI that enables the antenna array to 
adapt the radiation pattern, such that any UE can be reached with the strongest signal while creating as little 
interference as possible. 
The benefits in MIMO beamforming are much alike those in CoMP. By reducing interference and improving 
the spectral efficiency the transmission power can be reduced, thereby saving energy. Varies algorithm 
optimizations have been proposed in the literature such as [51] and [52] where simulations show energy 
efficiency improvements under challenging SINR conditions. 
3.2.6 Other technologies 
This section resembles technologies that are not currently a part of the existing mobile networks. The 
technologies are suggested as an alternative solution to some of the more classic implementations. 
3.2.6.1 
Common Public Radio Interface 
The Common Public Radio Interface (CPRI) is an industry cooperation, between Ericsson, Huawei, NEC 
Corporation and Nokia. CPRI is aimed at defining publicly available specifications for the key internal 
interface of radio base stations [53]. 
The latest specification is the eCPRI, introduced in August of 2017, that includes transport, connectivity and 
control functionalities supporting 5G NR. eCPRI supports a functional split in the physical layer, making 
implementation at base stations ideal. 
According to the documentation in [53], the scope of eCPRI to establish a connection between eCPRI Radio 
Equipment Control (eREC) and eCPRI Radio Equipment (eRE). The eREC and eRE are the only two 
components in eCPRI. In an LTE mobile network, the eREC would be implemented in the eNB, and the eRE 
would be equal to the eNB antenna(s). eCPRI supports three transport interfaces: 
 
eCPRI User Plane data plane: Data flow between radio base station and UEs. 
 
eCPRI Control and Management data plane: Control and management of data flows for operation, 
administration and maintenance of nodes. 
 
eCPRI Synchronization data plane: Data flow for synchronization and timing. 
The three interfaces are defined above the transport layer and supports existing standards. The modified 
eCPRI protocol stack can be seen in Figure 13 against the 5-layer reference model.

From an energy efficiency point of view, this can result in a potential energy saving at the base stations. 
Traditionally, the base station and base station antenna is connected by a coaxial cable. This results in base

base station) is connect via fiber to the eRE (antennas), allowing for a much greater distance between the 
two. If the base stations are virtualized, as mentioned in section 3.2.1.2, a great number of base stations can 
be aggregated and serve the eRE, thereby replacing the RRHs and relay nodes in LTE.

4 Performance Metrics 
The following sections describe the performance metrics relevant in this project. These are then evaluated in 
terms of how well fitted they can be used for energy optimization. The literature study shared a common 
strategy regarding energy savings in mobile networks. The idea of placing base stations in an energy saving 
mode/sleep mode/turn off when the number of active users/utilization reaches a certain threshold. The 
evaluation methods have primarily been based on metrics related to the operator perspective of the 
communication, especially how much energy can be saved in the RAN. However, for the most part the 
subject of user QoE seem to be less of a priority or just ignored. The QoE is one of the most important 
measures for the customer satisfaction. When implementing energy saving mechanisms that result in users 
being forced to handover to potentially less ideal cells, the QoE must be considered.

described below.

4.8 Block Error Rate (BLER) 
The block error rate (BLER) is defined as the ratio between erroneous packets received and the total number

the true loss in the communication stream.

5.1 MONSTeR model data flow 
The MONSTeR simulation model is developed on the LTE system toolbox that provides the low-layer signal 
processing and channel modelling. The higher levels, system and networking components have been 
developed by the MONSTeR team at DTU Photonics. The functionalities follow the LTE protocol layers as 
described in section 2.5.1. and can be viewed in Figure 15. 
At the eNB side, the application/IP layer handles the traffic models for packet generation. At the PDCP 
layer, packets are segmented/reassembled. The RLC layer performs queuing and ARQ tasks. At the MAC 
layer HARQ procedures, multi-user scheduling (weighted Round-Robin), codeword processing and 
handover coordination are performed. Finally, the MAC layer maps the data into transport blocks for 
scheduled UEs. At the physical layer the transport blocks are mapped into an OFDM grid, according to the 
Down Link Shared Channel (DL-SCH) and Physical Downlink Shared Channel (PDSCH) for transmission. 
The generated OFDM grid includes the following signaling: The Primary synchronization signal (PSS) and 
Secondary synchronization signal (SSS), used by UEs to obtain the cell identity and frame timing [60], the 
Physical Downlink Control Channel (PDCCH) to inform UEs of the resource allocation and modulation and 
the Physical Broadcast Channel (PBCH) for broadcast of Master Information Blocks. 
When the data and signaling are mapped, the OFDM grid is then modulated and filtered through the channel 
model. The channel model used for simulations in this project is ITU1546 [61].

6 Simulation process 
The purpose of simulations is to imitate real world scenarios without having access to these. A simulation 
model strives to become as close to the real system as possible but remain simple enough to understand and 
operate. The benefit of using the MONSTeR model to conduct LTE performance simulations is the broad 
range of metrics implemented in the model. MONSTeR enables simulations based on basic LTE network 
parameters and thereby gives insight to complex areas of mobile communication that otherwise would be 
difficult to investigated. The downside of using MONSTeR mainly consist of the limitations in 
understanding all details of the simulation model. This have led to the project workflow shown in Figure 18. 
 
Figure 18: Project workflow for MONSTeR simulations. 
The simulation approach shown in Figure 18 is focused around five steps. The initial step is the system 
analysis, where each simulation is planned, and the expected outcome is considered. Next is the simulation 
step where the simulation parameters are configured, and the simulation is started. The simulation results are 
then analyzed and compared to the initial expectations. Based on this analysis one of two options are chosen. 
If the results deviate or an error/bug is identified, the next state is then improving or optimizing of the 
simulation model and re-run the simulation. This step was performed in close collaboration with the 
MONSTeR team. The final step is validation of the obtained results. 
 
6.1 MONSTeR versions 
At the time of this project the MONSTeR framework is in a developing phase and still considered to be in 
alpha stage. This results in frequent updates and bugfixes from the developing team. The results obtained 
through this project has therefore been conducted on various versions of the MONSTeR framework. The 
process of identifying bugs according to step 4 in the procedure shown in Figure 18 resulted in numerous 
minor updates to the model. From an energy efficiency standpoint two major versions of the MONSTeR 
framework has been released. These versions are related to the traffic arrival models available. 
The MONSTeR version used for producing the results in section 7 was performed on a locked version at 
MONSTeR pull request #61 from the 9th of May 2018. The exact version is available at the Github page4.

6.2 Assumptions 
Every simulation model is based on assumptions in order to reflect world scenarios. This section presents the 
assumptions made in the MONSTeR model and how they impact the results and the interpretation of these. 
The assumptions are made based on the simulation parameters as well as the results obtained throughout the 
entire simulation process. 
6.2.1 Scalability of the model 
To perform a validation of the results obtained with the MONSTeR model the scalability of the model is 
considered. The Achilles heel of the MONSTeR model is the amount of computations performed every 
scheduling round. This lead to relatively slow simulation times, even for short simulation intervals. The 
simulation times increases further as the network topology and number of UEs expands. For this reason, the 
majority of the simulations performed in this project does not exceed a simulated time above 1000ms.

In reality, power management and energy optimization in mobile networks would be performed over a lager 
timescale of several minutes/hours and not in seconds as is the case of the MONSTeR simulations. This raise 
the question of how scalable the model would be compared to an actual implementation in a RAN setup. 
Regarding the scalability, a few simulations were run for 10.000ms (10s) to verify that the results are stable. 
This proved to be the case. It is therefore believed that the result from the MONSTeR framework can be 
scaled without significant loss of integrity. 
The simulations for this project is only focused on DL traffic from the eNBs to the UEs. As described in 
6.2.3 the movement of the UEs are at a minimum and no handovers are observed. The network behavior in 
the UL direction is therefore unknown. 
6.2.2 Complexity 
Every simulation model is balanced between increased complexity and compromises in relation to the real 
system. The MONSTeR framework is a complex model and it has not been possible to fully comprehend all 
aspects of each module in the span of five months. For this project the MONSTeR framework is tested and 
validated against known references to determine the model and results legitimacy. The simulation results are 
thereby accepted as representative for the original system. 
The alternative would be to construct a new simulation model from the ground. This would limit the 
investigation into the subject drastically. 
6.2.3 UE movement 
The short simulation intervals of 1000ms result in very limited movement of the UEs. The mobility scenario 
chosen for all simulations are “pedestrian”, resulting in UEs moving with a constant speed of 1 m/s. Because 
of this rather limited distance during simulations, UEs are considered stationary when analyzing heatmaps 
and similar data visualization tools. Another consequence of the limited UE movement is the lack of UE 
handovers. While the variable traffic model partially compensates of this, a scenario that take into account 
handovers is preferable. 
6.2.4 Hardware switch times 
The default eNB switch on/off times in the MONSTeR framework is 1ms. From a hardware perspective this 
switch time seem very aggressive and will most likely not resemble hardware found in eNBs today. The 
switch times are adjustable, and several tests are conducted in section 7 related to the impact of the switch 
times. However, the actual switch times that would be possible in today’s state of the art eNBs remain 
unknown and it has therefore not been possible to validate the switch times up against real components. 
6.2.5 Traffic model issues 
With the introduction of the variable traffic arrival model described in section 6.1.2, the option of running 
simulations with two different traffic types became available. However, in some cases the traffic model 
showed to be unreliable. When using web browsing as either primary or secondary traffic type the traffic 
model produced wrong results. The issue is related to the number of UEs and the network topology. So far, 
the following number of UEs should be avoided: 15, 25 and everything above 84. It has not been possible to 
debug the model to identify the exact cause of the problem. To identify if a simulation is affected the 
throughput metric can be used. When plotting the average throughput of all UEs per simulation round, a 
noticeable cut in the data stream can be observed after around 500ms. An example can be found in Graph 4.

6.4 Summery 
The simulation process is built around five steps. 1: System analysis, 2: Simulation, 3: Results/analysis, 4: 
Improvements/optimization and 5: Validation. The process has proven to be effective in the contentious 
validation of the MONSTeR framework and the scenarios of interest. 
The simulation process has resulted in improvements and new functionalities in the MONSTeR framework. 
This is primarily the introduction of the variable traffic arrival model and the ability to use web browsing as 
a traffic type. 
As part of the process several assumptions related to the limitations of the MONSTeR framework were 
uncovered and documented. These are related to the scalability, complexity, movement, SON parameters, 
traffic model issues and backhaul. 
Finally, a data set were obtained from the TT-Network containing RAN measurements. These data are used 
for comparison and validation of the simulation scenarios and results.

7.3 Impact of SON parameters 
The simulation results in both 7.1 and 7.2 indicate that power savings are achievable. However, there are still 
a few parameters that need to be investigated in order to determine how valid these savings would be. These 
parameters are the hysteresis time and switch times in the SON part of the simulation mode. 
As default both the hysteresis and switch time is set to 1ms. To investigate how these time values effect the 
result a series of simulations was performed at 5ms and 10ms respectively. All four simulations are 
performed only on seed 5, due to time constraints of this project. According to the standard deviation, the 
accuracy of the result is within +/- 4%.

7.5 Summery 
Two RAN topologies were simulated. The first topology consisted of one Macro eNB and four Micro eNBs 
and revealed EE improvements up to 600% with only minor worsening of the user QoE. The average HARQ 
retransmissions improved by less than 1% and the throughput were reduced 1.1%. These are changes that 
most likely will not be noticed by the users and the QoE therefore remain unaffected. The maximum power 
saving was 48%. 
The second topology were constructed as close to a 5G RAN structure as possible in the MONSTeR 
framework. The traffic load was increased as well. The setup consists of 1 Micro eNB and 3 Pico eNBs. The 
results showed that EE could be improved 340%, resulting in a 4% reduction in HARQ retransmissions and a 
minor reduction in throughout of 0.5%. The maximal power saving in this topology reached 26%. 
A more aggressive sleep-algorithm was introduced, with the ability of turning base stations off completely 
when inactive for a certain period. This introduced an additional power saving of 9% at low PRB utilization 
(below 37%). 
The results indicate that power savings are obtainable, based on the DL traffic analysis.

8 Outlook - Network planning and requirements 
The findings in sections 7.1 to 7.4 indicate that power savings are achievable as a result of implementation of 
the sleep-algorithm. The results can be used in future network planning where the demands for coverage and 
capacity is expected to increase, as described in section 2.6. 
A full implementation of the SON sleep-algorithm will result in a split where the old “always-on” network 
topology is replaced by a “on-when-needed” topology. The new topology supports the same peak capacity 
and coverage as the traditional network, at a lower operational cost due to reduced power consumption and 
provides the same QoE for the end user.  
The results obtained in this project shows that it is possible to use a SON sleep-algorithm, such as the one in 
Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 to obtain power savings in 5G style LTE networks. The result from the traffic 
model show that no significant reduction in user QoE is registered, based on the EE, throughput and HARQ 
metrics. 
The traffic model only simulates DL traffic and the subject of how a SON sleep-algorithm behaves in UL 
still need to be investigated. Some of the areas in which the network requirements and planning procedures 
will be affected in case of implementation are discussed in the following sub-sections. 
 
8.1 Coverage 
The coverage planning requirements for a network that are to benefit from the SON sleep-algorithm is 
different from traditional planning in the sense that coverage overlap is required. In order not to lose 
coverage when powering down a base station, at least one other base station should be ready to take over. A 
loss in coverage can result in users not being able to attach to the network, thereby receiving the worst 
possible QoE. On the other hand, the requirements to where base stations are placed at how many should be 
installed can be loosened. This is due to the reduction in power consumption when base stations are not in 
use they do not consume energy. In this way, the network planning can be much more detailed than previous 
and provides much more flexible coverage depending on the transmission conditions and traffic demand. 
Another aspect related to coverage is the impact of beamforming in networks where the SON sleep-
algorithm is operating. In beamforming, eNBs can cover UEs that are in coverage of a neighboring eNB. In 
cases where the beamforming coverage ends, the UE will perform handover to the nearest eNB. This add to 
the complexity of coverage planning. 
 
8.2 Topology awareness 
Not all base stations are identical. There will be variations in terrain, interference, backhaul capacity, 
network type, spectrum available, number of active users etc. This is different from the simulation where all 
the eNBs of the same type performs identical. One example of a special topology can be found in Figure 22 
and shows an arena that are equipped with 10 Pico eNBs that are able to provide coverage and capacity to 
users during event, such as a football match etc. The arena is also covered by two Macro eNBs that provide 
background coverage in the entire area. In a scenario like this each Pico eNB will associate two neighboring 
Pico eNBs and one/two Macro eNB(s) as their network neighbors. Areas like these can experience quick and 
significant changes in traffic demand in both DL and UL depending on what is happening at the arena. To let 
the Pico eNBs enter sleep-mode before such an event can likely result in a congestion of the Macro eNB, due 
to the user density. The users will get a worse QoE than if the Pico eNB had remained active.

S123317

Figure 22: Arena eNB topology awareness. 
Other examples of topologies that requires special planning is tunnels, buildings/indoor, highways etc. 
Basically, every topology is its own case and should be handled individually. 
8.2.1 Topology and load measures 
Additionally, to avoid a situation where base stations are unintentionally overloaded, implementation of a 
base station classification system would be useful. 
Each base station obtains a classification based on radio and backhaul conditions and capacities. This 
information is then shared with neighboring base stations, such that a base station is aware of how much can 
be offloaded to each site during sleep/shutdown. In that way, the optimal conditions can be predicted and 
situations where traffic is handed over to an almost congested base station avoided. The classification can 
also be used for marking base stations that are not to be placed in sleep-mode. 
 
8.3 5G multi-RAT 
The 5G NR concept as described briefly in section 2.5 introduces a multi-RAT structure in mobile networks. 
This means that the same base station, in this context often referred to as a hybrid-eNB, can serve UEs via 
multiple RATs, such as WLAN, 5G millimeter waves (mmW), 5G centimeter waves (cmW), 5G wide area 
(WA) and LTE-A simultaneously. The topic is well outlined in [64] where an architecture is purposed as 
well. 
This adds an additional layer of complexity to the network planning compared to the topology used in the 
MONSTeR simulations. It can be difficult to predict how placing a hybrid-eNB in sleep-mode will affect a 
UE that is served by multiple RATs. Even a partial sleep-mode where only selected antennas at the hybrid-
eNB are sleeping can change the radio conditions. 
It can be argued that the 5G Access and mobility management function (AMF), seen in Figure 2, should be 
able to coordinate such dual connectivity tasks, but the exact interaction with the SON sleep-algorithm 
remain unknown. 
 
8.4 Cellular IoT 
One of the popular topics in mobile communication at the moment is the world of Internet of Things (IoT). 
While regular users (UEs) are fairly predictable in their behavior, in terms of movement and service, IoT 
devices are still unknown territory. IoT is a broad label used for equipment that are connected to the internet, 
that previously was not known to have an internet connection. 
==PAGE:64==
S123317 
 
 
 
DTU - Department of Photonics Engineering 
 
Page 53 
A special branch of IoT is Cellular IoT where devices are connected to a mobile network, in this case 
NarrowBand Internet of Things (NB-IoT). NB-IoT is a part of 3GPP Release 13 and uses a bandwidth of 
200kHz in the frequency spectrums 700MHz, 800MHz and 900MHz. The spectrum allocation can be either 
in-band, out-band or standalone. In-band spectrum utilizes regular PRBs in LTE carriers, out-band spectrum 
utilizes the guard bands in LTE while standalone spectrum is separate from the regular utilized spectrum. 
By implementing a SON sleep-algorithm, the impact on NB-IoT transmissions must be considered. NB-IoT 
devices must authenticate, like any other device, but unnecessary authentications are consuming power in 
devices, that are supposed to use as little battery power as possible. This can result in potential NB-IoT 
devices run out of battery prematurely. 
The second aspect in NB-IoT devices are the nature of the communication. Often packets are small, send 
sporadic and not acknowledged. This means that in situations where eNBs often change between active and 
sleep mode, packets can get lost and/or require the device to search for available eNBs. 
   
8.5 Coverage/capacity measuring 
Coverage and capacity measurements are used by MNOs as well as third-parties and customers to analyze 
and diagnose mobile networks. The traditional way of measuring coverage and capacity is by field tests 
where user equipment is used to log the respective (quality) parameters. However, in a mobile network were 
a SON sleep-algorithm is implemented, this way of measuring performance will not be sufficient. The results 
will be inaccurate if base stations are “sleeping” during measurements or active “unnecessarily” thereby 
showing better performance that what the average user will experience. It might even be necessary to 
develop entirely new ways of defining RAN performance. 
 
8.6 Security 
The security measures in a RAN with a SON sleep-algorithm implementation might need a new way of 
authentication between eNB and UE. This is primarily related to the hardware switching times. The security 
between the UE and eNB is handled by “Access Stratum” (AS) protection protocol, as described in [65] 
section 6. The process of establishing a secure connection and exchanging keys might exceed the hysteresis 
time, if the hysteresis time is a few milliseconds. This means that a new authentication method might need to 
be implemented to support the fast network changes in the sleep-algorithm or that the hysteresis time cannot 
be less than the authentication time. To prevent stingray-style surveillance attacks an additional 
authentication method may be needed. This method must allow UEs to authenticate its serving eNBs based 
on a security mechanism that might not be a part of the AS. The purpose of such a new authentication 
method would be to allow a faster hysteresis time, i.e. significant faster than AS authentication.

results obtained in section 7 indicate that power management can be beneficial, but that in order to use the 
results in future applications, new areas need to be investigated. These include coverage planning, topology 
awareness, multi-RAT configurations, Cellular IoT, performance measuring and security. An additional 
estimate of how much the energy consumption can be reduced in a given area were presented and compared 
to the current LTE deployment of the same area. It was found that a new and finer meshed HetNet structure 
would be needed in order to benefit from the power saving algorithm.

9 Conclusion 
The current LTE network is about to be accompanied by 5G in the years to come. This will increase the 
number of base stations in the RAN as well as the power consumption dramatically. The current RAN 
topology is built on a concept of “always-on” structure, where eNBs are, at least in theory, never powered 
down. The RAN is thereby often over dimensioned outside peak hour load, resulting in waste of energy - 
Energy that potentially could be saved by placing underloaded base stations in a “sleep-mode” when their 
services are not required. 
The core functions in mobile networks are coverage and capacity. With this in mind, the subject of energy 
savings in mobile networks were investigated. It was observed that an overlapping coverage structure could 
support implementation of a SON sleep-algorithm. The MONSTeR framework built on the MatLab LTE 
toolbox were selected to perform simulations into the subject. The primary goal was to investigate how the 
user QoE would be affected by a SON sleep-algorithm and secondary to estimate how much power can be 
saved in operating the eNB structure. 
Two RAN topologies were simulated. The simulations ran for 1000ms and were based on DL traffic models 
with a Poisson arrival process, to obtain a DL environment as close to a real network as possible. The traffic 
models used, consisted of pure video streaming and a blend of web browsing and video streaming. 
The first simulation consisted of one Macro eNB and four Micro eNBs. The results showed that the use of 
the SON sleep-algorithm in Algorithm 1 had an insignificant impact on the HARQ transmissions and 
throughput experienced by the users. Additionally, the simulation showed that the power saving reached 
above 48% at low PRB utilization and remained above 30% even at moderate PRB utilization. 
The second simulation topology were designed to model a 5G RAN where one Micro eNB and 3 Pico eNBs 
provided the resources. Additionally, this setup allowed for simulations with higher loads. The results 
showed that at 16% PRB utilization power savings of 26% were achievable and that savings around 5% were 
measurable even at 70% utilization. 
By use of a more aggressive sleep-algorithm, that are able to turn base stations off completely after a long 
period of sleep, further improvements in power consumption were achieved. Algorithm 2 raised the savings 
to nearly 36% at a utilization as high as 16% PRB. 
These savings were achieved without affecting the user QoE. 
Additional simulations were performed to analyze the role of hardware switching and hysteresis times. The 
results indicated, that the hysteresis time should be as low as possible in order to obtain the best energy 
efficiency and QoE. The results further showed that longer switching time improved the EE, but the QoE 
metrics degraded. 
Substantial longer simulations are needed to invest the SON parameters affect. This is due to the ratio 
between the SON timers and the simulation runtime.  
In section 8, the relation between the expected 5G RAN architecture is held against the insights from the 
simulation results. It is clear that implementation of the SON sleep-algorithm in 5G as well as LTE is 
associated with a high level of complexity. Both the topology and application of the mobile network must be 
considered in order to prevent unwanted reductions in network performance and user QoE. 
In section 8.7, an urban environment was used as a reference for implementation. The selected area showed 
that the necessary HetNet structure of the current LTE network most likely would not be sufficient enough to 
benefit fully from the SON sleep-algorithm. 
Based on the system analysis in this thesis, the main conclusion is that changing the “always-on” RAN 
approach to a “on-when-needed” approach has a profound positive impact on power consumption with 
insignificant impact of the user experience.

QoE aware energy optimization                        
in future mobile networks 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Abstract— The traffic demand for mobile networks are 
constantly raising and with the introduction of 5G within the next 
few years the power consumption in mobile networks seem to 
reach new heights [1]. In this paper the user of Self-Organizing 
Network (SON) sleep-algorithm in a mobile network is 
investigated. Throughout a series of simulation, it is determined 
how much power can be saved by implementing a SON sleep-
algorithm and which impact such a measure have one the users 
QoE, based on a series of network metrics, including HARQ 
retransmissions, throughput, eNB power consumption, energy 
efficiency and utilization. A reduction of up to 25% of the total 
power consumption were achieved, with little if no impact of the 
user QoE.Keywords – SON, eNB Sleep-algorithm, energy 
efficiency  
I. Introduction 
Since the introduction of the first commercial mobile 
communication systems in the 1970’s, followed by GSM, 
UMTS, LTE and all versions in between, the focus have always 
been on coverage and capacity of the networks. The better 
coverage, the more customers can access the service and the 
higher capacity the better the service. However, the rapid 
growth and demand for information and communication 
technology (ICT) systems have led to a massive increase in 
power consumption. It is expected that by 2030 the global 
energy consumed by LTE and 5G wireless access networks 
alone will exceed 2.500 TWh annually, and rising [1]. The trend 
in telecommunication is heading towards heterogeneous 
network structures, with an increased number of small, Macro, 
Micro and Pico cells. This has led to an interest in energy 
efficiency (EE) and how this can be a part of the future mobile 
communication systems, especially as part of the upcoming 5G 
standard in 2020 [2]. The telecommunication community are 
currently proposing a variety of initiatives on how to improve 
EE in LTE and 5G, [3], [4] and [5] just to mention a few, 
covering both elements in the radio access network (RAN) as 
well as in the mobile backhaul. 
 
Contributions. This paper makes the following contributions:

II. Background 
As stated in the introduction, the energy consumption of mobile 
networks is rising. A mobile system consists of three 
components: the radio access network (RAN), the aggregation 
part and the core. The energy consumed in the core of the 
network is already in a optimization process, for instance by 
projects like M-CORD [6]. The aggregation part of the network 
is transitioning towards a fully optical access network, with a 
much lower energy consumption compared to traditional xDSL 
access networks or radio link connections [7].  The RAN part 
of the network, i.e. the base stations, as stated in [8] and [9], 
consumes 60-70% of the energy within a mobile network, while 
being responsible for a large part of the carbon footprint as well 
[10]. The RAN is therefore a good starting point in the 
investigation of energy optimization in mobile networks. 
The number of base stations will increase in the upcoming 5G 
network. The focus in previous generations of mobile networks 
have been on coverage and capacity of the varies systems, and 
not so much on the energy efficiency of the networks. The result 
is a “always-on” network that is ready to cope with peak hour 
traffic at all times.

from the telecommunication community. One area of particular

and optimization are traffic adaption, coverage/power 
adjustments and cell coordination. 
From an energy optimization point of view, one of the more 
explored solutions is to turn off/put to sleep cells that are not 
necessary at times where the cell capacity is under-utilized. In 
both [11] and [12], UE traffic patterns are used to construct a 
traffic forecast used in the respective cell areas. Both papers 
find that energy savings can be obtained based on forecast 
methods and that the energy saving is closely linked to the base 
station density. However, both methods solely rely on the traffic 
forecast when turning base stations back on again. This static 
approach is very inefficient and can result in situations where 
the network is congested. 
In [13], the authors introduce a performance metric based on 
the area spectral efficiency (ASE). The ASE is then used as an 
indicator for how utilized a given area is and based on that turn 
on/off given base stations. Their approach is to use Signal-to-
interference-plus-noise ratio (SINR) from attached UEs to 
estimate the ASE. When the number of active UEs drop below 
a threshold, the base stations are turned off. When the ASE in 
the area decreases, the base stations are then turned on again. 
Based on simulations the authors found that the energy 
consumption of their test network could be reduced by 60-70%. 
Despite the good results, the solution lack the functionality of 
UE handovers in cells that are to be turned off. The QoE can 
therefore not be guaranteed. Further, there are no indications of 
how scalable the solution is. 
Another technique is cell zooming. The cell zooming 
algorithms introduced in [14] are based on a so-called zooming 
period that consists of three stages: a coordination state where 
base stations in a given area collect cell information, a transition 
state where the base stations adjust based on the coordination 
findings, and a serving state where the base stations operate in 
the new configuration. A centralized and distributed version of 
the algorithm were compared, with the result that the 
centralized algorithm outperformed the distributed algorithm in 
terms of energy consumption in relation to outage blocking 
probability. In the centralized version, a cell zooming server is 
used for collection and coordination of base station settings. 
This can result in a scalability issue. 
Some of the challenges in cell zooming is highlighted in [14] as 
well. The largest being related to UE movements which can 
lead to traffic fluctuations that can result in compensation loops. 
The second challenge is related to compatibility, since cell 
zooming will require a massive base station upgrade to be 
supported (tilt function, new management channels etc.). The 
last challenge is related to unintended network behavior, such 
as inter-cell interference or coverage holes. 
B. Cell coordination 
The coordination of cells can be used for determining when to 
power on/off base stations. The 3GPP technical report in [15] 
describes three feasible energy optimization scenarios based 
on cell coordination in LTE(-A). In all three scenarios, the 
base stations only enter sleep mode when all remining UEs are 
handed over to a neighboring base station. The technical 
report concludes that an inter-eNB structure can be used for 
eNB power control. Based on this aspect, the structure 
proposed in this paper should be possible. The eNBs provide 
background coverage for one another when needed. Combined 
with SON features this provides a solid tradeoff between 
network performance and power consumption. 
The use cases for SON in mobile networks are many, as 
highlighted in [16]. From an energy optimization point of view, 
SON can be applied in areas of coverage and capacity 
optimization, interference reduction and automatic neighbor 
relation functions. The combination of these provides the tools 
needed for implementation of SON driven energy optimization. 
This paper investigates how the above mentioned techniques 
can be used for power saving in an 5G style RAN with an LTE 
backhaul. This is performed by simulating a realistic network 
based on real protocol behavior. 
 
III. The monster framework 
The MONSTeR simulation model builds on the LTE system 
toolbox that provides the low-layer signal processing and 
channel modelling. The higher levels, system and networking 
components have been developed by the MONSTeR team at the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). 
At the eNB side, the application/IP layer handles the traffic 
models for packet generation. At the PDCP layer, packets are 
segmented/reassembled. The RLC layer performs queuing and 
ARQ tasks. At the MAC layer HARQ procedures, multi-user 
scheduling (weighted Round-Robin), codeword processing and 
handover coordination are performed. Finally, the MAC layer 
maps the data into transport blocks for scheduled UEs. At the 
physical layer the transport blocks are mapped into an OFDM 
grid, according to the Down Link Shared Channel (DL-SCH) 
and Physical Downlink Shared Channel (PDSCH) for 
transmission. 
The generated OFDM grid includes the following signaling: 
The Primary synchronization signal (PSS) and Secondary 
synchronization signal (SSS), used by UEs to obtain the cell 
identity and frame timing, the Physical Downlink Control 
Channel (PDCCH) to inform UEs of the resource allocation and 
modulation and the Physical Broadcast Channel (PBCH) for 
broadcast of Master Information Blocks. 
When the data and signaling are mapped, the OFDM grid is then 
modulated and filtered through the channel model. The cannel 
model used for simulations in this project is ITU1546.

are introduced. This means that active Micro cells will 
contribute to the interference levels, whereas the contribution

IV. Results 
The simulation parameters can be found in table 1The lower 
threshold levels are chosen as 1%, 20%, 40%, 60% and 80% of 
the PRB utilization. At 1% the eNB never initiate the shutdown 
procedure and is therefore used as a baseline across the 
simulation. 
The results are summarized in graph 1, where the baseline 
corresponds to a network where the eNBs are never placed in 
sleep-mode and the optimized measure is the average 
performance with the sleep-algorithm implemented. From the 
results, it is clear that average power savings of 11% are 
achievable. At lower utilization periods, as much as 25% power 
can be saved by placing unused eNBs in sleep mode. The results 
show a minor reduction in HARQ retransmissions, due to 
reduced interference and that the throughput remains 
unchanged. The average PRB utilization increases from 40% to 
55% and indicate better use of resources, while the average 
increase in EE is 40%. 
Based on these results it can be concluded with high confidence 
that implementation of the sleep-algorithm does not have a 
negative impact on the user QoE. Further, MNO power savings 
between 11 and 25% are achievable. 
 
Further, the results show that the lower threshold is less 
significant compared to both performance and power savings,  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
with an average standard deviation between the best and worst 
performing threshold of just 1%. 
 
V. Conclusion 
The findings in this paper indicate that power savings are 
achievable as a result of implementation of the SON sleep-
algorithm. Power savings up to 25% can be achieved, without 
affecting the QoE of the users, by implementation of power 
management at the base stations. The results can be used in 
future network planning where the demands for coverage and 
capacity are expected to increase. 
A full implementation of the SON sleep-algorithm will result 
in a split where the old “always-on” network is replaced by a 
“only-on-when-needed” network. The new network structure 
supports the same peak capacity and coverage as the 
traditional network, at a lower operational cost due to reduced 
power consumption and provides the same QoE, based on 
retransmissions and throughput, for the end user. Some of the 
areas in which the network planning procedure will be 
effected is coverage.

at least one other base station should be ready to take over. A 
loss in coverage can result in users not able to attach to the

Green Challenge 2018 
Green Challenge Abstract 
- Energy efficiency improvements in 5G mobile networks 
Since the introduction of the first commercial mobile communication systems in the 1970’s, followed 
by GSM, UMTS, LTE and all versions in between, the focus have always been on coverage and 
capacity of the networks. The better coverage, the more customers can receive the service and the 
higher capacity the better the service. However, the rapid growth and demand for information and 
communication technology (ICT) systems have led to a massive increase in power consumption. It is 
expected that by 2030 the global energy consumed by LTE and 5G wireless access networks alone 
will exceed 2.500 TWh annually, and rising1. The trend in telecommunication is heading towards 
heterogeneous network structures, an increased number of small, macro, and micro cells. This has led 
to an increased interest in energy efficiency (EE2) and how this can be a part of the future mobile 
communication systems, especially as part of the upcoming 5G standard in 2020. 
The number of base stations will increase in the upcoming 5G network. The focus in previous 
generations of mobile networks have been on coverage and capacity of the varies systems, and not 
so much on the energy efficiency of the networks. The result is a near “always on” network topology 
that is ready to cope with peak hour traffic at all times. 
This project investigates and analyze how EE in future Radio Access Networks (RANs), like 5G, can 
be optimized without impacting the users Quality of Experience (QoE). This is done by testing how 
Self-Organizing Networks (SON) behaves when a “sleep-mode” is introduced, which allows base 
stations to partially power down when a certain lower utilization threshold is reached. The project is 
built on network simulations performed by use of the sophisticated MONSTeR3 (MObile Networks 
SimulaToR) through Matlab. 
So far the simulation results have provided great insight into the world of energy optimization in mobile 
RANs. The results show that EE improvements between 100 and 500% is obtainable without 
significant reduction of the user QoE. At the same time energy savings between 30 and 45% seems 
likely based on introduction of the MONSTeR eNB4 sleep algorithm. 
The results are shared with one of the largest mobile network operators in Denmark (TT-Network) to 
provide insight, inspiration and guidance in their energy optimization strategy.
