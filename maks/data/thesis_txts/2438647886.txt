Summary
For some hydrodynamic thrust bearing applications it is desirable to reduce
friction in order to optimize the bearing’s eﬃciency. By changing the lining on
the bearing pads from a white metal lining, which is the most common lining
material in bearings, to a polymer lining, the start up friction in the bearing
and its fatigue strength may be improved. This change in the lining material
will aﬀect the bearing’s operational conditions such as: pressure distribution,
load carrying capacity, deformation and power loss. In this master thesis, the
eﬀects of having a lining made from softer materials, such as a polymer, instead
of a more rigid one, on the operational conditions of the bearing were examined.
Analytical calculations for both a ﬁxed incline bearing pad and a pivot point
bearing pad were performed in order to obtain the ﬁlm thickness function, pres-
sure distribution and forces acting on the pad according to diﬀerent opera-
tional conditions. Further investigations into the bearing’s operational condi-
tions, and change there of, when implementing a softer lining material were
conducted through numerical calculations by the use of the centered ﬁnite dif-
ference method. The ﬁlm thickness function, pressure distribution, deformation
of the liner and the forces acting on the bearing pad were calculated for a poly-
mer liner and compared to those of a rigid liner. The eﬀect from the thickness
of the liner on the deformation and power loss in the bearing were evaluated.
The principal results indicate that a higher thickness of the lining causes a sig-
niﬁcant increase in the deformation and a slight decrease in the bearing’s power
loss. They also indicate that a pivot point thrust bearing has about twice as
high of a power loss than a ﬁxed incline thrust bearing while having signiﬁcantly
lower deformation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A hydrodynamic thrust bearing is a bearing whose purpose is to accommodate
axial loads. The bearing develops that load carrying capacity due to the relative
motion of either one or both surfaces that are separated by a thin oil ﬁlm and the
diﬀerence in their geometry. They are often used in heavy equipment, such as
ships or hydro electric power stations, to take the horizontal thrust component
along the propeller shaft or rotor. [1, p.123] [2, p.165]
The bearing pads are most commonly lined with a white metal of tin-lead-
copper alloys, such as Babbitt metal, which is beneﬁcial due to its low coeﬃcient
of friction in comparison to other metals.
Other beneﬁts of Babbitt are its
resistance to galling and how well it can withstand cyclic loading [3].
It is desirable to reduce friction even more in hydrodynamic thrust bearings after
a standstill in order to optimize their eﬃciency. That is even more beneﬁcial in
cases where the rotational speed of the bearing often changes, for example in
windmills. By changing the lining on the thrust pad from a white metal lining
to a polymer lining, the start up friction and fatigue strength may be improved.
The polymer is more ﬂexible than white metal and will therefore inﬂuence the
pressure distribution in the bearing diﬀerently than a rigid surface. However,
some problems are likely to occur when using a polymer lining. It might be
diﬃcult to ﬁnd a polymer that can handle the high temperatures that tend to be
generated in thrust bearings without aﬀecting how it adheres to the underlying
steel backing, as well as one that can withstand cyclic loading as well as white
==PAGE:12==

metal. In order to examine this, a test-rig has been set up at DTU where the
rotating disk is equipped with a polymer lining. That test-rig will be the base
for this thesis, as the calculations performed here will be used in cohesion with
the test-rig experiments. [4, 5, 6]
In this thesis the eﬀects of having a polymer lining instead of one made from
much more rigid material, on the bearings load carrying capacity and power loss
are estimated. Is it feasible to switch to a polymer lining or will the change have
less than desirable eﬀects? That is done by looking into the pressure distribution
in the bearing [4].
To begin with, a solution to a much simpliﬁed problem
is achieved, which is then built upon, step by step, making it more realistic.
Ending with an adequate solution to a numerical problem that demonstrates a
more pragmatic example of a hydrodynamic thrust bearing.
As a start, analytical calculations for both a ﬁxed incline thrust bearing and
a pivot point thrust bearing are carried out. For which the function for the
ﬁlm thickness, the pressure distribution and the forces acting on the bearing
pad are calculated for diﬀerent operational conditions. The results from these
calculations are then presented and discussed. These simpler calculations gave
a much deeper understanding of the thrust bearing theory and the correlation
between diﬀerent parameters of the bearing. From there it is easier to move on
to numerical calculations for both bearing types since deeper understanding of
the problem has already been achieved. A numerical model is set up using the
centered ﬁnite diﬀerence method, which is used to calculate the function for the
ﬁlm thickness, the pressure distribution, the deformation of the lining and the
forces acting on the bearing pad are calculated. From that a more pragmatic
solution to the bearing’s friction force and power loss as well as an understanding
of the eﬀects from diﬀerent operational conditions, has been achieved. From
there the fatigue strength of the polymer lining can be studied and experiments
carried out.
Following this introduction is Chapter 2, where the general thrust bearing theory
is explained and both ﬁxed incline bearing pads and pivot point bearing pads
are introduced. In Chapter 3, the experimental set up at DTU, the Disc Fatigue
Test Rig, is described. In Chapter 4, the analytical calculation procedures are
explained and results presented and discussed. In Chapter 5, the method for the
numerical calculations is described and results presented and discussed. This is
then followed up with a discussion of principal results in Chapter 6 and in the
ﬁnal chapter, Chapter 7, a conclusion is made along with some suggestions for
future work.

Chapter 2
General thrust bearing
theory
As previously mentioned, the hydrodynamic thrust bearing is a bearing that
sustains axial loads. Its load carrying capacity arises due to the relative motion
of either one or both surfaces separated by a thin oil ﬁlm and the diﬀerence in
their geometry. If the two surfaces are completely parallel, the velocity gradients
between them will be equal and no pressure will build up in the oil ﬁlm, causing
the bearing not to be able to support any load. Therefore a diﬀerence in the
geometry of the two surfaces is needed, however small, in order for pressure to
generate and the bearing to be able to support a load. Diﬀerent types of thrust
pads are used in diﬀerent thrust bearings, depending on the design parameters.
[1, p.123] [2, p.165]
A hydrodynamic thrust bearing, as seen in Fig. 2.1, consist of a rotating runner
pressing on a set of bearing pads, where a thin oil ﬁlm separates them. The
thrust bearing can include diﬀerent number of bearing pads, in the example
shown in Fig. 2.1 there are 6 pads, each with cross section AA′.

2.2
Pivot point bearing pad
A pivot point thrust bearing, as seen in Fig. 2.5, is designed to take up optimum
inclination according to the operational conditions [1, p.123]. It is therefore a
good ﬁt where operational conditions would vary, as for example in windmills.

The pivot point thrust bearing operates so there is a balance of moments and
forces around the pivot point. Normally the pivot point location is close to the
center of the pad. The geometry of a pivot point bearing pad is the same as that
of a ﬁxed incline bearing pad and therefore, the function for the ﬁlm thickness
is the same.

Analytical calculations
Analytical solutions are solutions that can be obtained by the use of previously
deﬁned equations. Those can be convenient and easy to obtain but give how-
ever often quite limited solutions since some simpliﬁcations of the problem most
often need to be made in order to use them. For this particular problem it was
a good start to begin with an analytical model in order to get better acquainted
with the problem and its parameters before increasing the complexity.

4.2
Pivot point bearing pad
As previously mentioned, the geometry for pivot point and ﬁxed incline bear-
ing pads is the same and therefore also the function for the ﬁlm thickness.
The derivation of the function for the pressure distribution is therefore also the
same.
What diﬀers from a ﬁxed incline bearing pad is that the pivot point
bearing pad takes up optimum inclination according to its operational condi-

Its value becomes so that there exists an equilibrium of forces and moments

Chapter 7
Conclusion
There is a lot to consider when choosing a thrust bearing or the bearing pad’s
lining material. If the bearing needs to operate under constantly changing run-
ning conditions and power loss is not of great importance, a pivot point thrust
bearing would be an appropriate choice. However, if the running conditions
required are quite stable and a relatively high deformation is a not a concern, a
ﬁxed incline thrust bearing would have less power loss and therefore be a good
choice, that is, from a theoretical point of view. However, a bearing pad will
never have a complete ﬁxed incline due to machining accuracy.
The inﬂuence from the thickness of the liner on the deformation and power loss
is that the deformation increases considerably with increasing thickness while
the power loss only decreases slightly. The criteria for the bearing is therefore,
again, of great importance when choosing the softness and thickness of the ma-
terial. For further information on general bearing design limits, see [12].
Future work could be, for example, to investigate the thermal eﬀects from the
temperature rise in the bearing, the spherical inclination of the pivot point pad
and a more pragmatic calculations of the deformation. The accuracy of the
results could also be improved by increasing the number of grid points and in-
cluding half-points on the grid. It would also be of interest to see and compare
experimental data to these ﬁndings.
In this thesis it has been demonstrated:
