Summary
The study provides a method of analyzing the behaviour of a 3D printed continuous ο§¹ber
composite with complex microstructure and its a ο§¹nite element model generation. The
eο§Äects of the porosity on the mechanical properties of 3D printed structure fabricated by
the FDM process are investigated through cross-section analysis. A part of the cross-
section is considered for the analysis because of the simulation of the whole cross-section
is computationally challenging. Simulations are conducted in BECAS, a cross-section
analysis software to estimate its stiο§Äness matrix and the stress concentration factor
under diο§Äerent load cases. Stress analysis results obtained from BECAS are compared
with analytical solutions which show good agreement. The eο§Äect of porosity in the
cross-section is discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
3D printed composites
3D printing of composite structures is a relatively new manufacturing process, thus there
is a need to investigate the mechanical performance of the 3D printed composites. This
technology was ο§¹rst reported from Charles Hull in 1986 [Tianxi Liu et al., 2020]. The
growing demand of the production of customized products together with the desire of
avoiding the manual eο§Äort has led to the evolution of 3D printing or additive manufac-
turing (AM). 3D printing provides solutions while eliminating the production costs and
enhancing the eο§Éciency in manufacturing sector. This manufacturing technique has also
a smaller ecological footprint and could be also a path towards to a more sustainable
production.
3D printed technology is a very promising manufacturing process. The biggest advan-
tage is the ability to directly manufacture objects with absolute precision and arbitrary
geometry in the most cost eο§Äective way. In comparison to the conventional fabrication
process of composites, 3D printed technology allows complex geometry formation, con-
versely at the same time it lacks the ability to pressurize the 3D model which allows
structural βÄ€voidsβÄù or porosity. As a result, the porosity of the 3D printed structures could
be higher than conventionally manufactured structures, which in turn could aο§Äect the
mechanical performance of the structure in industrial applications. Porosity can increase
the stress at vicinity of the pore area or in other words increase the stress concentration
factor in the structure.
1.2
Summary of Previous Work
The mechanical properties of composite materials have been extensively studied in
the literature. X-ray Computed Tomography (XCT) imaging and image processing
techniques are recognized tools for characterization of the microstructures. Especially
when combined with Finite element analysis (FEA) analysis which oο§Äers further quality
inspection with structural analysis. Mechanical performance of 3D printed Polylactic
acid (PLA) material in relation to internal pores was analyzed in Wang et al., 2019
using experimental tensile tests and ο§¹nite element micromechanical models. The actual
microscopic details of the pores were captured using X-ray computed tomography. Three
diο§Äerent specimens were experimentally analyzed and modelled using FEA. The pore
sizes were determined from the XCT, which then used to create model of the RVE

(Representative Volume Element) in Matlab. The analysis was ο§¹nalized in Abaqus. The
results verify that low porosity contributes to better mechanical performance. The study
claimed that XCT information about porosity volume can be a valuable tool in the
observation of porosity and also in an eο§Äort to avoid destructive tests.
Soete et al., 2019 used X-ray Computed Tomography to analyze 3D printed composites

modeled. Diο§Äerent models of the RVEs having single ο§¹bre, 12 ο§¹bres, 20 ο§¹bres and 50

the single ο§¹ber RVE, on the contrary the models with more ο§¹bers showed discrepancy.
However, out of plane properties were better approached with the RVE of multiple ο§¹bers.
The results demonstrate that randomness in the RVE show qualitative agreement of
experimental observations. The authors mention the importance of selecting the RVE, as
a larger area would entail a bigger ο§¹ber volume fraction, which would mean an immediate
change of the eο§Äective properties.
A ο§¹bre bundle structure was scanned and further analyzed from Jespersen et al., 2018.
The objective of the study is to present accurate automatic approach converting X-ray
CT data into a discrete number of elements and creating a mesh. The obtained data was
ο§¹rst segmented in Avizo and then the geometry was smoothed in Geomagic Wrap. The
modelling process was ο§¹nalized in Abaqus. The stresses of the model were obtained and
compared with previous data from the literature and good agreement was observed.
The material microstructure of a glass-matrix composite was investigated from Mon-
torsi et al., 2004. Object Oriented Finite Elements (OOF), a ο§¹nite element code, specially
used for microstructure analysis was implemented. The code is able to transfer 2D images
into ο§¹nite element meshes. Indeed, electron microscopy micrographs of a reinforced glass
matrix composite was meshed and further analyzed in order to compute the residual
stresses. The resulting residual stresses were found to be tensile in the reinforcement
material, though compressive in the matrix. ItβÄôs worth mentioning that the residual
stresses are crucial for the behaviour and the properties of the composite. Understanding
the residual stresses in the composite structure can predict microcracking or general
failure. The authors consider the 2D plannar analysis restriction in their results, as it is
not possible to capture with the applied method the 3D nature of the composite.

1.3
Project Deο§¹nition and objectives
In this project, the mechanical performance of the 3D printed continuous ο§¹ber composites
(CFCs) is investigated using microstructure characterization and Finite Element (FE)
modelling. The objectives of this work are basically to: (1) Use an image segmentation
technique to perform cross-section material characterization of a 3D printed composite
specimen (2) Convert the segmented image to an FE mesh and determine the ο§¹ber
volume content and porosity of the specimen cross section (3) Use an FE cross sectional
analysis tool (i.e. BECAS) to evaluate the cross-section stiο§Äness and stresses in the 3D
printed specimen under diο§Äerent loading cases.
Many structures whose one dimension is signiο§¹cantly larger than the other two di-
mensions can be usually considered as beams [Nayfeh and Pai, 2004]. This assumption
is of great importance in engineering problems, leading to simpler the mathematical
formulations.
Despite the fact that ο§¹nite element simulations are deemed to be very accurate, it is
highly computational consuming in regard to a micromechanical model. Cross-sectional
analysis is very convenient in many engineering ο§¹elds and allows for the minimization
of computational cost. Additionally, the analysis of cross-sectional micrographs can
give a signiο§¹cant insight about the behaviour of the composite. In view of the diο§Écult
part to obtain the exact microstructure mesh from a cross section image, a method is
presented to solve this issue. Simulating anisotropic or non linear elastic behaviour of
the microstructure can be a very diο§Écult task, especially when the process demands a
detailed mesh with high number of elements. In view of these issues, BECAS (beam
cross-section analysis software) will be used for the cross-section analysis of the 3D
printed microstructure. The great advantage of this approach is that BECAS can manage
to obtain the cross-section stiο§Äness matrix, while accounting for any anisotropic material
properties. Furthermore, as a cross-sectional analysis using BECAS requires a 2D FE
model of the composite structure, it will reduce the computational time signiο§¹cantly
compared to 3D FE models usually used for the structural analysis.
The attention is focused on the assumption of a building block, explained in section 2.1,
part from a real microstructure to be used for mechanical performance. This micrograph
of the cross-section of a 3D printed CFC specimen is analyzed using Avizo, an image

Background Knowledge and
Theory
A composite material is formed by combining at least two materials with diο§Äerent
properties, so that together they could give unique material characteristics. Composite
materials are used extensively in many ο§¹elds, aeronautics, automotive and transportation,
wind energy and medicine. What does make the composites be the ideal solution in
many applications is their strength and stiο§Äness combined with their low weight. Their
lightness allows for energy savings and advanced performance. Some of the most familiar
reinforcements in composites are: carbon ο§¹ber, glass ο§¹ber, aramid, and vectran. Based
on the product requirements, size and desired properties, reinforcements are available in
roving, milled ο§¹ber, chopped strands, continuous or chopped. Among the these forms of
ο§¹bers, unidirectional ο§¹bers combine general advances as increased stiο§Äness and strength
[Eksi and Genel, 2017].

produced from a diο§Äerent technology can present unique properties. As it can be seen

Material Extrusion is a widely known technology because of its simplicity. The
material is extruded through a nozzle over a build platform and can be either in form
of ο§¹lament or paste. Depending on the form of material used the process is charac-
terized as Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) or Liquid Deposition Modeling (LDM).
Vat Photopolymerization is another technology for 3D printing that selectively solidiο§¹es
layers of liquid resin via radiation. Stereolithography, is the ο§¹rst polymerization method

Additive manufacturing techniques are prone to produce components that contain
pores. In fact the additive manufacturing (AM) process is basically responsible for the

seen in diο§Äerent parts of the 3D printed specimen, when the void content is increased.

Image to mesh review
The analysis of continuous ο§¹ber composites (CFCs) has been a challenging problem
in engineering. A typical approach to study the behaviour of the CFCs and calculate
their eο§Äective properties is Finite element analysis. Diο§Äerent modelling techniques for
CFCs include Representative Volume Element (RVE) models or micromecanical studies

Based on the second technique, the micro-structure model can be either extracted

a detailed model of the microstructure of a specimen can be computationally signiο§¹cantly
expensive. An advancing method relies in mesh generation based on the images. The
way of analysis changes based on the data and the number of materials of each case.
Despite the advancements in general volume mesh generation, creating a mesh directly
from an image remains a challenge. A common technique starts with the segmentation
process, specially in case of heterogeneous materials. On the other hand if the material
of analysis is homogeneous, mesh generation can be completed without the ο§¹rst step of
image segmentation.
The process of segmentation is based on the fact that the pixels of each image have
diο§Äerent characteristics, such as color, brightness, contrast and gray level. In this way,
the image can be divided into various segments, giving the possibility to separate material

2.4
The Finite Element Method
The Finite Element Method (FEA) and the Finite Element Analysis (FEA) are mainly
used by engineers for structural analyses. Finite element modelling, which developed
by engineers in the mid-1950s [Felippa, 1999] is commonly used in order to acquire
numerical solutions for problems which can be described by diο§Äerential equations. It
is an approximate method, however this analysis can give reliable results in problems
regardless of the level of complexity. Following this analysis a structure is divided into
parts, which are the ο§¹nite elements. The individual equations for each ο§¹nite element
can be combined into a system of equations, giving the solutions for the entire problem.
Meshing elements can be used in combination of types depending on the modeling
problem. A ο§¹ner meshing could bring more accurate results, but also increases the
complexity and the computational time. The basic steps of the analysis after the building
of the initial geometry are [Nikishkov., 2004]:
1. Discretization of the structure into ο§¹nite elements. Meshing the geometry with the
appropriate number and type of elements. For 2D models, the elements chosen for
discretization are basically triangular or quadrilateral elements. For 3D models,
the type of the ο§¹nite elements can be tetrahedrons, hexahedrons or pentahedrons.
2. Obtain the element stiο§Äness matrix.
3. Apply Boundary conditions to the model.
4. Assemble local element K to global stiο§Äness matrix.
5. Solution for the unknown displacement.
6. Calculation of the desired results.
Although Finite Element Analysis and the results are approximate, this method
oο§Äers many beneο§¹ts if used in a right way. The basic advantages relate to the design
and behaviour of a structure when it has to be tested for its safety and utility. FEA
techniqueβÄôs key characteristic is the cost reduction for the testing process. It allows
engineers to make fast decisions while having many data to process. The accuracy of
the results are based on the general understanding of the simulated system, the right
deο§¹nition of the assumptions and the accurate constraints set by the user.
Meshing
A mesh is a set of points which based on its appearance can be also called a grid.
Increasing the number of elements for the mesh, the accuracy may be enhanced, but the
computational costs will be increased too. A convergence study can be done in order a
compromise between accurate results and solving time is found. The process involves
gradually increasing the number of elements until the mesh dependency results becomes
stable.

Stress Concentration Factor
Avizo segmentation process has given the ability to model and separate the porosity
boundaries in the building block. The next step is to determine the stress concentration
factor which develops at boundaries of the pores and can have a signiο§¹cant impact on
the mechanical performance of a 3D printed structure. In general, the change in material
property and geometrical irregularity trigger stress concentrations.
In this phenomenon it is observed to occur stress increment above the average normal
stress. Stress concentration factor usually depends on the geometry of the pore, the
position of the pore in relation to the load direction and of course the material properties
in each case [R. et al., 1996].
The stress concentration factor, K is approximated with the fraction of the peak stress
œÉmax to the nominal stress œÉaverage and is dimensionless ratio showing how concentrated

Discussion
In this project, a microstructural ο§¹nite element based approach was used to determine the
the cross-section stiο§Äness and stresses of an engineering structure made by 3D printing.
The objective was to underline the eο§Äect of porosity in the developing stresses by using
a modelling approach which takes into consideration the exact morphology and spatial
distribution of the microstructure. Two cross-sections with simple geometry have been
presented, considering one single material case and one bimaterial case. Diο§Äerent loading
conditions were applied and the results of each case were validated analytically, supported
by the comparison with BECAS results. The stiο§Äness matrix for both the cross-sections
have been calculated.
Regarding the ο§¹rst isotropic single material cross-section, equations proposed by
Navier and Jourawski are considered. The normal stresses are following linear variation
with respect to the bending moment. The assumption βÄùplane sections remain planeβÄù is
no longer valid when the rectangular cross-section is loaded with a shear force. Warping
describes the deviation of out-of-plane displacements. For solving this discrepancy a
shear correction factor should be multiplied in the stiο§Äness parameters regarding shear.
The shear stresses distribution follows Jourawski equation. The shear stresses are in-
duced by a shear force, validating the parabolic distribution of stresses that assumed in
prismatic beams. BECAS results could prove that the formulas are signiο§¹cantly eο§Äective
for an isotropic, homogeneous cross-section composed of a single material. Very good
approximation between analytical and the BECAS output is obtained.
For the second bimaterial case with one ο§¹ber, established models from the literature
such as the rule of mixtures were used for the estimation of cross section stiο§Änesses of
composites, because based on the cross-section characteristics, the Navier and Jourawski
formulas are violated. The stiο§Äness parameters are found by the same procedure as in
the single material cross-section. The BECAS results for the stiο§Äness tensors were in
agreement with analytical calculations. The value of the computed stresses are highly
depended on the mechanical properties and the volume fraction of the materials. The
stress results in the diο§Äerent parts of the cross-section relate to the YoungβÄôs modulus.
This thesis aims to develop an analysis process of a 2D cross-section image in order
to identify its mechanical performance. A basic challenge of the process was basically the
segmentation process. The image segmentation method allows for an automated, fast
way to convert the image data into mesh topology. The mesh generated in Avizo is a 3D
mesh, so as there is no immediate way to directly use it in BECAS, some modiο§¹cation
steps should be done in ANSA. The accuracy of the mesh has a signiο§¹cant eο§Äect on

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis contributes to the cross-section analysis of a 3D printed Continuous Fiber
Reinforced Composites (CFRC) specimen. A modelling process of a cross-section image
into a ο§¹nite element model is explained. The cross-sectional properties are obtained
based on BECAS (Beam Cross-section Analysis Software) and results for stresses and
the stress concentration factor based on diο§Äerent porosity percentages are predicted. The
basic conclusions of the thesis are the following:
βÄΔ It was established that in an isotropic, symmetric cross-sections the formulas of
Navier and Jourawski give satisfactory results in relation to BECAS.
βÄΔ A good approximation between the analytical results and BECAS calculations is
also obtained for the second bimaterial square cross-section.
βÄΔ The building blockβÄôs percentage of porosity is found 2%. The modelling process of
the building block (image to mesh) can be a very useful tool in order to estimate
the porosity percentage of 3D printed components. The application can be done for
multiple cross-sections which gives the opportunity to evaluate an entire structure.
βÄΔ The stiο§Äness matrix tensors are well predicted to be lower in the case of the 2%
porosity, verifying that porous materials are responsible for stiο§Äness changes and
on the mechanical behaviour of the materials.
βÄΔ The analytical rule of mixture approach predicts well the extensional stiο§Äness
K33. Indeed a detailed analysis is needed to capture the stiο§Äness matrix with the
analytical approach. On the other hand BECAS is a computational eο§Écient tool
which provide all the anisotropy terms for arbitrary-shaped cross sections.
βÄΔ The stress concentration factor depends on the porosity and on the distribution
of the pores. The value of the stress concentration factor depends on the type of
loading. In case of extensional loading the stress concentration factor is low, which
is a contradiction from the theoretical approach, while in case of shear the stress
concentration factor is as expected higher.
βÄΔ It was demonstrated that cross-section analysis in BECAS is capable of estimating
coupling eο§Äects which is challenging by a theoretical approach.
βÄΔ Transferring cross-section images to ο§¹nite element models provides a great advantage
in terms of computational time. The modelling process can be applied for diο§Äerent
materials and for diο§Äerent components which can give great motivation for future
work.
