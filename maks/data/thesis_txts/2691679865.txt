Abstract
Human skin’s reflection coeﬀicient has been shown to approach unity. In acoustics, the
human body is thus generally assumed to be rigid. Knowledge on the acoustic impedance of
human skin could relax this hypothesis, and could for instance help improving the models
behind Head Related Transfer Function simulations. In this master thesis, the feasibility
of the impedance estimation of nearly acoustically rigid bodies via the equivalent source
method is assessed. Preliminary, boundary element simulations are performed to test the
method. A semi-analytical solution to the problem of scattering of sound by an elastic
cylinder with a rigid core is derived. This particular shape was initially chosen to model
the behaviour of the skin around an human arm. The solution of this problem is used
as a benchmark for further tests of the proposed impedance estimation method, and the
results of the simulations are compared with experimental data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Scattering of sound by the human head and torso leads to time and amplitude sound
pressure differences between the left ear and the right ear. These inter-aural differences
are fundamental in the human sound localization process [1].
Thus, a broad range of
applications, including the testing of hearing impairment devices [2] and the development
of virtual audio methods [3], demand a precise knowledge of the sound field close to the
human ears. When simulating numerically the sound field scattered around an object, via
e.g. the boundary element method, knowledge of the surface impedance of the object is
needed in order to model the boundary conditions in the Helmoltz Integral Equation [4]. In
acoustic simulations of Head Related Transfer Functions (HRTF), human skin is generally
assumed to be rigid. Yet, it has been shown that using diffent materials for crafting the
pinnae of an acoustic measurement dummy head leads to changes in the measured HRTF
[5]. These changes are particularly significant at mid to high frequencies, at which the
complex details of the head, and particularly of the pinna, induce a substantial amount
of reflexions. The knowledge of the impedance of human skin could thus help improving
HRTF simulations, allowing to relax the rigid scattering hypothesis.
There has been only few research on the acoustic properties of skin. In [6] and [5], mea-
surements of the impedance of skin are performed with two microphone impedance tubes,
on the respective frequency ranges [1,6] kHz and [1,18] kHz. Both references agree on the
fact that skin’s reflection coeﬀicient approaches unity. In [5], the author points out the
limitations of the impedance tube method, mainly due to clamping boundary conditions
on the ring where the impedance tube touches the skin.
This justifies the need for a
contactless method for measuring the surface impedance of in-vivo human skin.
In this thesis, the feasibility of estimating the impedance of human skin with near-field
acoustic holography on a broad frequency range (from 1 to 20 kHz) is assessed.
The
equivalent source method (ESM) is used to recover the incident and scattered fields on
the surface of a scattering object as a superposition of fields coming from equivalent
sources placed respectively inside and outside of the object [7, 8, 9]. This method presents
the advantage to be adaptable to the different surface geometries that the human body
presents.
Moreover, the equivalent source method has been shown to be an eﬀicient
tool to estimate the impedance of surfaces [10]. In chapter 4, boundary element method
simulations are performed to compare the performance of the equivalent source method
when estimating the impedance of a porous and a nearly rigid material.
The nearly
rigid material is described by an impedance model of the human skin proposed in [5]. In
chapter 5, two study cases of scattering of sound by elastic objects of different geometries
are solved analytically. The two geometries are a plane and a cylinder. The cylindrical

analytical results are used as benchmark cases to test the method proposed in the thesis. In

2.1.1
The acoustic scattering problem
Let Ωbe a topologically open volume of R3, whose boundary ∂Ωdelimits an object of
arbitrary shape, as shown Figure 2.1. The geometrical center of the object is chosen as
the origin of the coordinate system. A time-harmonic ambient sound field of pulsation ω
(time dependency ejωt, which will be omitted in what follows) is assumed to exist in R3.
The associated sound pressure will be denoted pinc. The presence of the object gives rise
to a scattered pressure ps outside the object. The total pressure in R3 −Ω(i.e. outside
the object, including the object’s boundary) is thus p = pinc +ps. It satisfies the Helmoltz
equation:
∆p + k2p = 0,
(2.1)
where k = ω
c0
is the acoustic wave number and c0 is the speed of sound in air (which is
the fluid that is assumed to surround the object in this thesis).
The unknown of a scattering problem is the scattered field, with pressure ps, which satisfies
Equation 2.1 because of the principle of superposition. The scattering effect of a finite-size
object must vanish far away from the object, which forces ps to satisfy the Sommerfeld’s
radiation condition ([11, 12]), which will be introduced later in subsection 2.2.1. In order
to close the problem, a boundary condition has to be defined on the surface ∂Ωof the
scattering object. The most general formulation of a lossless boundary condition is the
Robin (or impedance) boundary condition and links the normal particle velocity u to the
sound pressure p via the specific impedance Z of the boundary ∂Ω(which is not necessarily
homogeneous over the boundary):
(p −Zu)|∂Ω= 0.
(2.2)
Note: The equivalent source and boundary element methods are analysis and simulation
tools that will be described chapter 3. They are more easily introduced in the context of
sound radiation by an object. In radiation problems, no ambient sound is assumed to exist
in R3 and the sound field originates from the vibrations of the object of interest. However,
the radiated sound field still satisfies Equation 2.1 in R3 −Ωand will be denoted p.

2.1.2
Scattering of sound by the human body
In subsection 2.1.1, it was showed that the knowledge of the impedance (or impedance
map) of a boundary allows to formulate a scattering problem.
In Head and Torso Related Transfer Function (HRTF) simulations, the human head and
torso are approximated as acoustically rigid (|Z| tends to +∞). This is is modeled via
a Neumann boundary condition where the normal velocity is null over the boundary.
The Neumann boundary condition corresponds to the Robin’s one (Equation 2.2) with
a specific impedance tending to infinity in absolute value. A more complete formulation
of the problem demands knowledge of the specific impedance of the different parts of a
dressed human body (clothing, hair, skin).
The characterization of the impedance of hair can be performed via a two microphone
impedance tube as in [13] or [6]. Human hair has been shown to be slightly absorptive
(absorption coeﬀicient α > 0.1 on the audible range). Due to this specificity, the two-
microphone impedance tube appears to be an adapted tool to measure the hair’s specific
impedance. As they are generally made of fabric, clothes can be expected to be absorptive
as well, and the impedance tube seems an adapted tool to measure their impedance as
well.
Several authors propose to use a two microphone impedance tube in order to measure the
acoustic characteristics of human skin. [6] shows the resulting reflection coeﬀicient on the
frequency range [1,6] kHz, whose frequency average is of 0.97. [5] shows the measured
impedance curves on the frequency range [1,18] kHz. The corresponding reflection coeﬀi-
cient’s absolute value exceeds 0.9 on the whole frequency range. In both references, skin is
shown to be highly reflective, which is in agreement with former mechanical ([14], via mea-
surements of mechanical impedance) and acoustic ([15], via a two-microphone impedance
tube). This allows to predict high absolute values of impedance compared to the specific
impedance of air (Zair = ρ0c0, ρ0 being the density of air and c0 the speed of sound in
air).
This thesis aims to propose a method dedicated to acoustically characterize the acoustic
impedance of skin.
The method consists in reconstructing the pressure p and normal
particle velocity u of a sound field at the surface of a scattering object (ideally a part
of the human body) plunged in an ambient sound field. According to Equation 2.2, the
specific impedance of the object’s surface can then be reconstructed as the ratio of these

2.3
Sound scattering by elastic objects: analytical cases
In this section, two cases of sound scattering by elastic objects are studied analytically.
The first object is an infinite elastic layer attached to a still support on its back and
illuminated by a normal incidence plane wave. The second object is an infinite cylinder
compounded of a rigid core surrounded by an elastic layer, hit by a plane wave. Both
of these cases will be used later on as benchmarks for the numerical tests of the method
proposed in this thesis.

3.1.1
Near field acoustic holography and the ESM
Near-field Acoustic Holography (NAH) was originally applied to analyze the sound field
radiated by vibratory objects. In NAH, the sound field is measured on a surface close to a
radiator. Projecting the measurements onto the span of a well-chosen family of functions
allows to either backward propagate the measurements and reconstruct the vibratory
fields on the surface of the radiator, or forward propagate the measurements in order
to reconstruct the sound field further away from the radiator. At high frequencies, the
radiation of sound by vibrating objects induce evanescent wave components, that decay
exponentially away from the source and significantly contribute to the sound pressure
only close to the source. These components can be captured by acoustic sensors only close
enough to a radiator, hence the need for measuring in its near-field.
The Equivalent Source Method (ESM) is a NAH technique proposed by [7] which lies
on the assumption that the sound field radiated (or scattered) by a vibrating object can
be expanded as the superposition of a continuous distribution of point sources inside the
object. This method applies to radiators (or scatterers) of arbitrary shape, which makes
it a powerful tool in the context of this thesis. Note that the Inverse Boundary Element
Method (IBEM, [20], [21] )also applies to arbitrary shaped objects. The main drawback
of this method is that it requires the numerical computation of singular integrals, which
makes it computationally heavy and leads to ill-posed problems. [7] shows that the integral
formulation of the ESM is equivalent to the Helmoltz integral equation (HIE), which is
the basis of IBEM and will be introduced section 3.2. [20] shows that ESM and IBEM
yield to similar reconstruction errors when the assumptions required by both methods are
satisfied. These elements led the author of this thesis to choose the ESM as an appropriate
NAH tool.
In this chapter, the ESM framework will be derived and explained. In subsection 3.1.2 and
3.1.3, the fundamental equations of ESM are set in its original context: reconstructing the
sound field on the surface of a radiating source. subsection 3.1.4 adapts the framework to
the problem of sound scattering.

3.2.3
Practical matters
3.2.3.1
CHIEF points
The theoretical acoustic resonances of the bounded domain Ωinduce that Equation 3.18 is
ill-posed. Indeed, a vector obtained by adding natural modes to a solution of Equation 3.18
will still be a solution. Some nodes can be chosen in the domain Ωin order for the internal
modes to not be solutions of the homogeneous Equation 3.18. These nodes are the so-
called CHIEF points. More insight on this regularization method can be found in [4], [16]
or [29].
3.2.3.2
Computational cost
For numerical methods such as BEM, a suﬀicient number of elements per minimum wave-
length is needed in order to guarantee a valid simulation. A rule of thumb prescribes
8 elements per wavelength. Thus, at higher frequencies, a higher number of elements is
needed, which increases the numerical complexity of the method. 8 elements per minimum
wavelength will be used for the BEM simulations in this thesis.

4.4
Discussion
In section 4.2, a primary investigation of the sources of error inherent to the proposed
impedance estimation method was proposed.
It was shown that the particle velocity
reconstruction at the surface of a nearly rigid material is more challenging than at the
surface of an absorbing material.
This is due to the surface particle velocity almost
vanishing in the case of a nearly rigid material. The use of a finite measurement aperture
was shown to have a significant negative influence on the accuracy of the estimation of
high impedances at low frequencies.
In section 4.3, the results of the ESM reconstruction of the fields at the surface of a nearly
rigid cylinder obtained with a closed and a patch measurement surfaces were compared. In
absence of measurement noise, both the closed and the patch measurement setups led to
consistent results. The presence of measurement noise was shown to lead to inconsistent
results at low frequencies and for the patch setup, where the truncation error due to the
finite extent of the measurement surface highly emphasizes the reconstruction error. If the
use of a closed measurement surface is not feasible experimentally, a frequency range has to
be set to delimit the validity of the results. For the geometries studied in this chapter, this
lower frequency seemed to correspond to the frequency for which the acoustic wavelength
is about the extent of the aperture. As mentioned earlier, averaging allows to decrease
the lower recoverable frequency. The used of a closed measurement surface was shown to
lead to consistent results even in presence of noise.
However, the results from the impedance model proposed in [5] has not been validated,
and their limits are discussed in the corresponding thesis. Thus, the skin impedance model
used in the simulations from this chapter might not be representative of real skin. In order
to go further towards the validation of the ESM impedance reconstruction framework in
the context of the acoustic characterization of skin, another model will be introduced in

showed compliance-like impedances with a non-null resitive part. However, the surface
impedances of the objects of interest appeared to be very high compared to the charac-
teristic impedance of air. This gave the nearly rigid behaviour that was expected, but the
results from [5] for the impedance of skin were significantly less high.
In both cases, the impedance estimation results in absence of noise were satisfying on
the whole frequency range, except at a localized frequency in the cylindrical case. Sev-
eral analysis were performed to determine the source of this peak and did not lead to
conclusions.
The planar case led to similar conclusions as in section 4.2. In light of section 4.2, the use
of a patch surface of finite extent induces a lower frequency bound of 4-5 kHz below which
the impedance estimation gives poor results. This lower frequency bound is twice higher,
and the errors involved in the new study case are significantly higher, than in section 4.2,
while the holographic and equivalent source surfaces have the same size in both cases and
the number of measurement points and equivalent sources has been increased in this new
section. From this comparison, it can be concluded that the very high impedance of the
sample is the main source of error in presence of noise, compared to the error due to the

6.3
Discussion
Figure 6.4 showed that the proposed experimental setup can be expected to allow the
equivalent source method reconstruction of the pressure and velocity fields on the surface
of the cylinder between 6 and 13 kHz according to the simulations performed in section 5.2.
An analysis of the measured pressure showed that sound scattering is likely to bias the
experimental results from about 10 kHz.
However, the magnitude of the impedance estimated experimentally, shown Figure 6.6, is
not comparable to the one of the impedance predicted by the analytical model of the sound
field scattered by an elastic cylinder, whose results were introduced section 5.2. The ESM
based impedance estimation method captured a significantly stronger compliant effect,
which could indicate that the cylinder and its support were moving back and forth under
the action of the sound field, acting as a mechanical spring. The losses captured by the
impedance estimation would in this case be mechanical losses.
Several attempts were made to try to stiffen the support of the cylinder and prevent its
vibration. However, the support had to be fixed to the ground of the anechoic chamber via
a long pole, as seen Figure 6.2a. Another supporting system seems necessary in order to
avoid vibrations of the support. Due to time restrictions, other options could unfortunately
not be envisaged.
This last observation highlights the intrinsic diﬀiculty of high impedance estimation, which
can be explained with a simple electrical analogy. If the system under study has a signif-
icantly higher impedance than its support, then the support acts as a short-circuit: the
energy tends to make the support vibrate instead of exciting the system of interest. In
this case, only the impedance of the support is recoverable.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In the numerical tests with BEM simulations and analytical benchmarks, respectively
chapter 4 and chapter 5, the estimation of high acoustic impedances via the equivalent
source method was shown to be feasible, even if subject to some limitations in presence
of noise. The main limitation appeared to be coming from the velocity reconstruction.
This is due to two combined reasons : amplification of the noise in the pressure-to-velocity
ESM reconstruction, and low values of velocity on the surface of high impedance objects.
Thus, the noise amplified by the velocity reconstruction process is comparable to the
velocity values on the surface of the objects. Future work could include a formulation
of the equivalent source reconstruction with particle velocity measurements. This could
allow to get rid of the noise amplification issue and would be a way to improve the velocity
reconstruction. Another idea would be to directly measure the velocity on the surface of
the sample with a non invasive method, e.g. with a laser Doppler vibrometer.
The silicone rubber used to craft the coated cylinder appeared to be significantly harder
mechanically than skin. It can thus also be expected to be significantly harder acoustically
than skin. The results from the models describing the scattering of sound by silicone-
coated objects in chapter 5 showed very high impedances compared to the one of air.
The computed sound fields around an elastic cylinder showed only little difference when
compared with the ones around a rigid one. A more detailed model of the vibrations of skin
would include a lower Young’s modulus, non homogeneity, non linearities, non isotropy
and visco elasticity. Such a model would have been out of the scope of this thesis. Other
models and test materials will thus have to be built and chosen in order to mimic the
acoustic behaviour of skin. First, this could lead to more interesting acoustic properties.
The impedance of such a material could be expected to be lower than the ones obtained
with the model of this thesis, which would also ease the impedance estimation process.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this thesis, the equivalent source method was used as a tool to estimate the impedance
of acoustically hard materials. Numerical and analytical sound scattering models were
used to test the method on different objects. First, in chapter 4, the method was tested
on sound fields simulated via the boundary element method. Simulations were first per-
formed on a porous material and were successful. Estimation of the impedance of porous
materials already existed in the literature, but these simulations allowed the author to get
familiar with both the boundary element method and the equivalent source method. More
simulations were run with a scattering object covered with a material of impedance given
by the model for skin derived in [5]. This model presents high impedance values compared
to the specific impedance of air. The first limitation of the method was showed to lie in the
reconstruction of the velocity on the surface of a nearly rigid material. The reconstruction
of a velocity field based on pressure measurements leads to an amplification of noise. This
induces the level of the noise to reach the velocity level on the object’s surface. Using
patch measurement surfaces emphasized this issue at low frequencies. In spite of these
limitations, the estimation of the impedance of this material lead to satisfying results on
the frequency range [1,20] kHz.
These first numerical simulations were thus successful, but the materials under study suf-
fer from a lack of realism. In chapter 5, two analytical cases were built up to numerically
construct the sound field around rigid objects coated with silicone. In the first model, the
problem of the reflection of a normal indidence plane wave by an infinite elastic layer was
solved. The impedance of this plane showed a compliant behaviour until a mechanical res-
onance appear due to the finite thickness of the layer. In the second model, the scattering
of a plane wave by a rigid cylinder coated with a layer of silicone was analysed. The choice
of a cylinder was made in order to build a first approximation of a human arm. The elastic
coating was shown to induce a compliant behaviour similar to the one observed with the
infinite layer, with a more complex pattern due to shear waves allowed to propagate in
the coating. As in the infinite layer case, the cylinder’s behaviour was shown to be close
to the one of a rigid cylinder.
The reconstruction of the objects’ impedance led to satisfying results in absence of noise.
However, the presence of noise affected the impedance estimation more critically than in
chapter 4. This is due to the fact that the silicone rubber used in these simulations was
significantly more rigid than what the impedance model from [5] predicts for human skin.
Eventually, in chapter 6, an attempt to experimentally reproduce the results of chapter 5
was performed. Inconclusive results were obtained, which can be explained by the need
for a perfectly still support for the object of interest and unwanted scattering from the

cylinder’s support, from the microphone and the microphone’s rod, and from the robotic
arm.
To improve the results presented in this thesis, it is thus necessary to design a still support
to which a cylinder or a human arm can be perfectly fixed. A focus has to be made on
reducing the scattering from the tools used in the setup.
The silicone-coated objects
studied in chapter 5 showed very high impedances compared to the impedance of skin as
measured in [5], which made the impedance estimation task unnecessarily more complex.
The choice of a softer material that would better mimic the behaviour of skin and the
crafting of more realistic samples thus seem necessary. Additional mechanical properties
can be included in a numerical or analytical model to set up a new testing benchmark for
the impedance estimation method. If the velocity reconstruction still brings issues on a
softer material, other ways of measuring the sample’s surface velocity can be assessed. It
could for instance be reconstructed via the equivalent source method using particle velocity
measurements, which does not induce the noise amplification which was problematic in
this thesis. Direct particle velocity measurements could also be performed with a laser
Doppler vibrometer.
