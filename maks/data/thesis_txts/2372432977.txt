Abstract
Whole-genome sequencing has an expanding role in public health with the technological advance-
ment in high-throughput sequencing. Public health authorities sequence thousands of pathogenic
isolates each month for microbial diagnostics and surveillance of pathogenic bacteria. Whole-
genome sequencing data could be used to infer the phylogeny of the isolates, thus spot outbreaks
and connect cases with potential sources. Moreover, uploading the data to public repositories
facilitates the surveillance of pathogenic bacteria on an international scale.
Our aim is to build a platform for the surveillance of pathogenic bacteria, that incorporates
publicly available sequencing data. The Batch Evergreen pipeline downloads the newly available
whole-genome sequencing data daily from the public repositories. To decrease the computa-
tional burden, the data is divided into sets by matching the isolates to a closely related reference
genome. Then the reads are mapped to the reference to gain a consensus sequence and SNP
based genetic distance is calculated between all the sequences that were mapped to the same
reference. Sequences are clustered together with a threshold of 10 SNPs to reduce the redun-
dancy in each set. Finally, phylogenetic trees are inferred from the non-redundant sequences and
the clustered sequences are placed on a clade with the cluster representative sequence.
We employed two methods to calculate the genetic distance (traditional and pairwise) and two
methods for inferring a phylogeny (neighbor-joining and maximum likelihood). The four types of
trees were compared for a benchmarking data set and for the downloaded isolates. Based on the
accuracy of the phylogenies and the wall-time required, the traditional distance calculation and
neighbor-joining proved to be the most suﬃcient for our purposes.

Introduction and Background
High-throughput sequencing technologies have reached a development level, where they are avail-
able to a wider audience as benchtop machines. In the ﬁeld of microbiology, the use of whole-
genome sequencing (WGS) is spreading, thanks to the continuous decrease of the costs and
improvement of accuracy. It is no longer used only for research purposes, as it has reached public
health laboratories, and it is expected to gain more ground in the next years [1].
WGS data yields more information beyond what is necessary for the typing of the microorganism.
Amongst other things, WGS can be used to infer a phylogenetic tree for a set of isolates. Close
phylogenetic relationship of the isolates could imply shared origin, and given other epidemiolog-
ical information, the presence of an outbreak. Microbial diagnostic WGS pipelines seldom oﬀer
phylogenetic context for the processed isolates, although it could be an important tool to spot
potential outbreaks.
The aim of this project was to build a platform for this purpose. It would continuously download
and process new WGS data from the public repositories, in order to build and update phylogenetic
trees for bacterial pathogens.
1.1
High-throughput sequencing (HTS)
The ﬁrst generation of DNA sequencing was Sanger sequencing, which had high costs and low
throughput. After the Human Genome Project was concluded in 2003, a new initiative was started
with the aim of bringing down the cost of sequencing so the human genome could be sequenced for
$1,000. This prompted the development of the next-generation (or high-throughput) sequencing
technologies [2].
The second generation technologies, such as the Illumina and IonTorrent platforms, are similar
in one aspect: they still require a clonal ampliﬁcation step, in order to have the necessary
amount of DNA for the systems. Meanwhile, single-molecule sequencing platforms, from Paciﬁc
Bioscience and Oxford Nanopore Technologies, can directly sequence the DNA without the need
for ampliﬁcation. These third generation sequencing technologies produce sequencing reads with
longer lengths, which make them suitable for de novo assembly of bacterial and viral genomes
and study of structural variation. However, as of now, they still have higher error rates and costs
than the short-sequence read technologies, therefore their user-base is smaller [2,3].
The work presented here currently only supports data acquired from Illumina paired-end sequenc-
ing, as that gives the bulk of the available WGS data. Therefore that method will be presented
in more detail.
1.1.1
Illumina sequencing technology: sequencing by synthesis
Solexa, which was bougth later by Illumina, released its ﬁrst sequencing platform in 2006, that
relied on the sequencing by synthesis approach [2].
As ﬁrst step, DNA is extracted from the sample. For whole-genome sequencing, it is fragmented
into smaller pieces by a suitable method, which is usually enzymatic. Then adapters are ligated
to the ends of the fragments. Solid-state bridge ampliﬁcation follows, where the fragments are
bound to the surface of a ﬂow-cell, via a primer speciﬁc for the adapters, and then complemented
using unlabeled nucleotides. At the end of the ampliﬁcation step, each template has up to a
thousand copies in a close proximity, forming the clonal clusters [4].
Primers are added to start the ﬁrst sequencing cycle. Both single and paired end sequencing is
possible on the Illumina sequencers. With paired-end sequencing, the sequencing is done from
6
==PAGE:8==
both ends of the DNA template. Primers are hybridised to adapters on one end for the forward
read cycle, then to the other end after a bridge ampliﬁcation step, for the reverse read sequenc-
ing [3]. The diﬀerent Illumina platforms are capable of diﬀerent sequencing read lengths, up to
300 basepairs [5]. Depending on the fragment length, the forward and the reverse reads may
overlap, creating ”pseudo long reads”. However, usually they do not, but the paired reads still
help elucidating the placement of the reads during de novo assembly [3].
The sequencing steps are the following: four type of reversible-terminated, labeled deoxynu-
cleotides (dNTPs) are added at once, together with DNA polymerase. One dNTP is incorpo-
rated in each synthesizing strand, and then the polymerization stalls, because the 3’-end of the
dNTP is blocked. The ﬂow-cell is washed to remove the free dNTPs, and the signals from the
incorporated nucleotides are read by the optical system. Afterwards the chemical group blocking
the 3’-OH is removed and the synthesizing step is repeated [4,5].
Newer Illumina sequencers have a two channel laser system, that increases the speed of the
sequencing, yet retains the same accuracy, as with the previous machines [2, 6]. The Illumina
platforms have reportedly a higher than 99.5% accuracy rate. However, they are still prone to
substitution errors, and not representing AT-rich and GC-rich regions correctly [5].
Illumina carries out the basecalling, translating the visual signals into nucleotides for each read,
and provides the data in FASTQ format [7].
This format contains a quality score for each
nucleotide which is given as a PHRED score (Q), expressing the estimated probability of error
(Pe) for given nucleotide [8]:
Q = −10 × log10(Pe),
1.1.2
Utilization of technology
HTS has versatile applications in the study of gene expression and regulation (RNA-Seq), RNA
and protein interactions (ChIP-seq), genome organization (long read sequencing for the study of
structural variation), etc. With the decreasing costs and maturing technology of single-molecule
direct sequencing, the range of these applications is expected to increase further [2,5].
WGS and whole exome sequencing have currently the most wide-spread use [5]. In microbiology,
WGS has both research and clinical applications. With bacterial genomes, the repeating elements
hinder de novo assembly, and genomes can not be fully assembled and closed using short-read
technology alone.
However, comparative genomic studies and ecological studies have largely
beneﬁted from the new technology [1].
In clinical use, the aim is usually not to squence novel species, but to identify an isolate based
on a sequence that is closely related to an already known sequence. The taxonomy of the isolate
could be determined by sequencing and then mapping the short reads to reference genomes. In
addition, an SNP based phylogenetic study could be carried out to determine the transmission of
the pathogen [1].
1.2
Surveillance of outbreaks of foodborne bacterial infections
1.2.1
Notable foodborne bacterial pathogens
Foodborne pathogens are those, that are transmissible by contaminated food. They have a serious
social and economical eﬀect on the global population. There were 582 million cases worldwide
due to foodborne diseases in 2010, with nearly 40% of them occurring in children below the age of
5, according to the World Health Organization [9]. The following foodborne bacterial pathogens
cause the majority of the illnesses.
7
==PAGE:9==
Campylobacter jejuni is a Gram-negative, microaerobic, curved rod shaped, motile bacterium,
that has a single ﬂagella on one or both ends. It is the most frequent cause of diarrhoea in
developed countries. Chicken and other domesticated animals can carry the pathogen in their
intestine, while remaining asymptomatic. However, C. jejuni is sensitive to the environmental
conditions [10]. Contaminated food or water transmits the bacteria into the host, where it ﬁrst
infects the small intestine, then the large intestine and rectum. The pathogens can cross the
mucus layers and invade the epithelial cells of the host. The disease is usually self-limiting, with
symptoms of nausea, diarrhoea and abdominal pain, sometimes accompanied by fever and general
aches at the beginning. In rare occasions, complications can arise after the clearance of the initial
infection [11].
Escherichia coli and Shigella spp. are Gram-negative, rod shaped bacteria of the Enterobacte-
riaceae family, that could be motile and could have ﬂagella [11]. E. coli is found both in humans
and animals, while humans are the only host for Shigella spp. Shigella spp. are largely indistin-
guishable on a species level from E. coli, but as of yet, they are still considered to be a separate
taxonomical group [12].
E. coli is commensal bacteria of the human gastrointestinal track.
However, several strains
are pathogenic. A subset of them cause gastrointestinal infections world-wide, with high mor-
tality rates, especially in young children.
These strains belong to the following pathotypes:
enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC), Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC), enteroinvasive E. coli
(EIEC) and Shigella spp., enteroaggregative E. coli (EAEC), diﬀusely adherent E. coli (DAEC),
enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC), and adherent invasive E. coli (AIEC). EIEC/Shigella and AIEC
strains are capable of invading the cells of the host, meanwhile STEC and ETEC produce toxins.
Common symptom is diarrhoea, that in case of STEC and EIEC/Shigella can be bloody. Fur-
thermore, abdominal pain, fever and vomiting can occur. The diseases are usually self-limiting,
with no treatment besides oral rehydration therapy needed, except in cases of severe or persistent
infections, when antibiotics are given [13].
Salmonella enterica are Gram-negative, facultative anaerobic bacilli of the Enterobacteriaceae
family [11]. S. enterica has six subspecies, and more than 2000 serovars, but the strains that
infect humans and domestic animals mainly belong to the subspecies enterica [14]. S. Typhi, S.
Paratyphi A and C, and S. Sendai cause enteric fever, that has a mortality rate close to 20%,
and requires antibiotic treatment. Non-typhoidal salmonellosis manifests as gastroenteritis, with
sudden onset of diarrhoea, fever and abdominal pain after 6-72 hours of the infection. It is
usually self-limiting and clears up in 5-7 days. However, severe causes can lead to dehydration,
renal failure and death, therefore oral or intravenous rehydration therapy and antibiotic treatment
could be needed [11,15].
Listeria monocytogenes is a Gram-positive, non-sporing bacillus, that predominantly causes lis-
teriosis. The bacteria are present in the environment and they can survive certain food-preparation
techniques, leading to infection. The disease has two types: non-invasive gastrointestinal liste-
riosis and invasive listeriosis. It mainly aﬀects immunocompromised individuals, the elderly and
pregnant women, and it can infect the fetus as well. Perinatal invasive listeriosis results in abortion
or stillbirth; or in case of a live-birth, generalized infection, sepsis or meningitis. In immunocom-
promised adults, it mainly presents as meningoencephalitis or septicaemia. Usually penicillin or
ampicillin, in combination with gentamicin, is given as a treatment [16]. The mortality rate
depends on the manifestation, but it can be as high as 60% [11].
1.2.2
Epidemiology
Epidemiology is the ”study of the nature, distribution, causation, mode of transfer, prevention and
control of disease” [11]. In conjunction with epidemiology, surveillance is a method for infection
8
==PAGE:10==
control, and consists of three main elements: collection of epidemiological data, integration and
evaluation of the data, and dissemination of the data to relevant parties that could take actions.
Surveillance covers the infectious agents, the hosts, the reservoirs, the vectors, and any additional
factors that could contribute to the spread of the disease [11]. For enteric diseases, four types
of surveillance exists: occasional, syndromic, laboratory-based and integrated laboratory-based
surveillance. The latter means that the testing covers isolates from humans, animals, food, and
sometimes environmental samples and animal-feed [17].
In epidemiology, a case is an instance of illness, and if no connections are found to other cases,
then it’s sporadic. If there are more than two cases, that are connected in time or location, then
there is an outbreak. If large percentage of the cases happen in a short time period, then the
outbreak is explosive. This can indicate that there is a common source, for example contaminated
water or food. Person-to-person spread is more gradual, with the cases spread out in time. There
could be a mixture of the two types, where the outbreak has a common source, but afterwards
it also spreads person-to-person [11].
An epidemic is when cases happen on a larger scale than it is expected normally, but the phe-
nomenon is only temporary. If it spreads beyond the borders of a country, then it is called a
pandemic. A disease also can be endemic in a region or a country, in which case it is not limited
in time [11].
Public health authorities and hospital employees are deeply invested in recognizing and stopping
outbreaks. During outbreak investigation, epidemiological data is collected in order to shed light
on the infectious disease.
The infectious agent is determined by clinical microbial diagnostics. Traditionally, that includes
culturing and isolating the pathogen, usually using selective media. The isolate is then typed
by various methods, for example Gram staining, morphological characterization, and biochemical
tests, or more recently, matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-ﬂight (MALDI-TOF)
mass spectrometry [18].
Molecular typing methods are also applied. In case of C. jejuni, culturing the bacteria is cumber-
some and traditional biochemical tests are not informative, therefore polymerase chain reaction
(PCR) is used instead to conﬁrm the presence of the pathogen [10].
Susceptibility testing is frequently done as well, to characterize the isolates’ resistance towards
antibiotics used in clinical practice [18].
It is necessary to place the infectious agent into a more discriminatory category than species, to
establish links between cases and sources in an outbreak investigation. Such subtyping is phage
typing, serotyping, or antibiogram typing. For example, O:K:H serotyping E. coli, is based on
lipopolysaccharide ’O’ antigens, capsular ’K’ antigens and ﬂagellar ’H’ antigens [19]. However,
cross-reaction between the antigens can occur, therefore other, molecular subtyping methods are
preferred [13]. One such method is the pulsed-ﬁeld gel-electrophoresis (PFGE) based method,
which is considered to be the ”gold standard” of typing not just E. coli [13], but also S. enterica
and L. monocytogenes isolates. First, bacterial DNA is digested with restriction enzymes, then
fragments are separated by PFGE, which results in a ”ﬁngerprint”. This pattern is unique for
each subtype. The Unites States Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) established
PulseNet in 1996 for the surveillance of foodborne pathogens based on PFGE patterns [20].
However, the most frequently used molecular method to subtype is multi-locus sequence typing
(MLST). MLST schemes, consisting of allele fragments of 6-10 house-keeping genes, have been
set up for selected species, that are continuously maintained and updated with new alleles. The
selected genes are ampliﬁed by PCR, then Sanger-sequenced to determine the loci. Based on the
combinations of alleles, sequence-types are assigned to the isolates [21].
Isolation and subtyping could take days, or even weeks for organisms with slow growth rate,
such as Mycobacteria [18]. Therefore, the speed of an outbreak investigation using traditional
9
==PAGE:11==
methods couldn’t keep up with an explosive outbreak, and provide necessary information in real
time. Moreover, some traditional subtyping methods, such as serotyping, don’t have enough
power to discriminate between cases [17].
1.2.3
Genomic epidemiology
With next-generation sequencing and WGS, a new chapter opened up in microbial diagnostics.
DNA is extracted after culturing or directly from the sample, prepared and sequenced [18],
enabling rapid diagnostics, since the whole-genome sequence encompasses all the information
that could be used to predict the characteristics of an isolate. Additionally, the methods that rely
on WGS have the promise to have suﬃcient discriminatory power, reproducibility and repeatability,
while also being compatible with previous typing methods, such as MLST [17].
Joensen et al. (2014) provided proof of this concept, when they concurrently analysed and se-
quenced verocyto-toxin-producing E. coli (STEC) in 2012, during a 7-weeks-period in Denmark.
More than 40 isolates were sequenced on an IonTorrent platform, then processed with web-based
tools provided by the Center for Genomic Epidemiology (http://www.genomicepidemiology.
org). They found, that WGS based typing, subtyping and virulence factor prediction were faster
and less expensive, meanwhile provided more accurate results, than traditional methods. Fur-
thermore, the phylogenetic relationship between the isolates could be inferred directly from the
WGS data, and the phylogenetic tree constructed with the nucleotide diﬀerence tree (NDTree)
method [22] was in concordance with the epidemiological data. [23]
Methods for surveillance usually employ two approaches to discover clusters. One approach is
to compare allelic diﬀerences in a set of genes common to the strains, for example by using
whole-genome MLST schemes [24,25]. Another approach is to identify genomic variants (single
nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP), insertions and deletions) compared to a reference sequence by
mapping the reads to it. The drawbacks of using a reference are that SNPs can only be identiﬁed
in regions, that are present in the reference, and highly variable regions could be excluded from
analysis, because the mapping algorithms would not be able to map the reads to it [17]. In
addition, the methods are sensitive to the systematic errors that the various sequencing platforms
have, therefore quality control steps are necessary [26]. First, the raw sequencing reads could be
trimmed before mapping, i.e. the bases at the ends, that fall below a certain PHRED score are
removed [27]. In addition, the SNP calling algorithm could also have criteria for accepting the
SNP, e.g. whether it has suﬃcient amount of mapped reads that support it [26].
WGS based typing for outbreak detection in S. enterica was tested by Leekitcharoenphon et
al.
(2014) in a retrospective study.
They compared the performance of several methods to
correctly cluster isolates according to being in an outbreak or not. They found, that the SNP
Tree and NDTree methods, using ﬁltered SNPs and maximum parsimony and Unweighted Pair-
Group Method with Arithmetic Mean (UPGMA) algorithms, respectively, produced equally good
phylogenetic trees. However, SNP tree proved to be sensitive to the chosen reference genome,
while NDTree did not. Meanwhile, a reference free approach, K-mer tree, and a pan-genome
tree, that compared the presence and abscence of genes present in each genome, couldn’t cluster
the isolates in concordance with the epidemiological data [22].
The United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) established a network of WGS labora-
tories, starting from 2012. These laboratories sequence foodborne bacterial isolates from clinical
and environmental samples and upload the data to the GenomeTrackr database at NCBI. The
network has been expanded and became international, with the hopes that it will aid investigators.
(https://www.fda.gov/Food/FoodScienceResearch/WholeGenomeSequencingProgramWGS)
In September of 2013, several US agencies started a collborative pilot project to sequence all
10
==PAGE:12==
L. monocytogenes isolates in real-time for surveillance. WGS data provided better discrimination
between samples, than PFGE. In some cases it revealed, that the samples were more closely
related, than PFGE suggested, in others, that they did not cluster together, despite common
PFGE patterns. Overall, more clusters were detected, and sooner, than with PFGE, thus the
average cluster size was smaller.
In addition, more outbreaks were resolved and linked to a
source.
For rapid detection of outbreaks, phylogenetic trees were built using whole-genome
MLST, based on the allelic diﬀerences [25]. Moreover, the isolates were also analyzed using
SNP-based approaches with a diﬀerent pipeline [27].
The eﬀectiveness of WGS in C. jejuni surveillance was also studied, however as the pathogen’s
epidemiology is not completely understood and many of the cases go unreported, there are
hindrances to employing the method. Deﬁning the cutoﬀvalue for identifying clusters of related
cases is the most pressing problem. Genomic diversity was found to be heterogeneous in C. jejuni
sequence types, with identiﬁed long term clonal populations.
Therefore deﬁning a generally
applicable threshold for the species might not be possible [24]. This subject was also explored in
other pathogens. For S. Typhimurium, Octavia et al. (2015) concluded that such threshold would
be dependent on the diﬀerent mutation rates in vivo and ex vivo, and the sampling times, therefore
could not be exactly deﬁned [28]. Moreover, whole-genome MLST based phylogenetics approach
is favored for C. jejuni, as there are genomic regions with high diversity that are associated with
host adaptation. Thus, these need to be disregarded in order to link sources and cases, and a
gene-by-gene comparison appears to be a better solution, than SNPs. Still, the WGS approach
was capable of linking cases in diﬀuse outbreaks and pinpoint sources [24].
NCBI runs the Pathogen Detection project (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pathogens) for
selected species. They analyze assembled genomes and WGS data, and provide SNP cluster and
kmer trees, that could be browsed through their Isolates Browser.
1.3
Phylogeny
Phylogeny describes ”the evolution of genetically related organisms” [29]. Traditionally, phylo-
genetic studies were done to discern the evolution of species, but genotype based phylogenetic
methods can be applied to bacterial populations as well. However, events such as homologous
recombination or horizontal gene-transfer can not be captured by a simple bifurcating tree [30].
But the isolates in an outbreak are supposedly clonal, which means that their evolutionary re-
lationship can be inferred. Several approaches could be used, two is employed in this project:
distance and maximum likelihood based methods [17,31].
Distance based phylogeny requires a matrix as the input, that contains the observed distances
between the samples in the set. The resulting tree is the one that ﬁts the data and has the
lowest sum of branch lengths. We chose to employ the neighbor-joining algorithm [32, 33] and
the number of SNPs between the sequences as the measure of distance. The algorithm constructs
a tree by clustering the data points. It computes the following for all nodes:

(v) are calculated as

and

The distances from the two nodes in the matrix are replaced with newly calculated distances to

Dvk = (Dik + Djk −Dij)/2,
where k is any other node in the tree. These steps are repeated until all the nodes are linked. The
method does not assume a molecular clock, therefore it can be applied to nodes with diﬀerent
sampling times [31].
Meanwhile, a maximum likelihood based method seeks to maximalize the likelihood of the tree,
which is the probability of the data given the hypothesis. It employs discrete-character models,
so for example it takes a multiple DNA sequence alignment. It also needs a starting phylogeny
with branch lengths and an evolutionary model, that describes the probabilities of changes in
the states over the branches (e.g. nucleotide substitutions and transversions). Computing the
likelihoods require the assumptions that the evolution is independent in diﬀerent sites and in
diﬀerent lineages. There are various algorithms for ﬁnding the optimum tree. They search in the
space of tree topologies for the one, that has the branch lengths that give the highest likelihood
for the input sequences [31].
1.4
Homology reduction
It is not always desirable to have too similar sequences in a data set. If that is the case, then
homology reduction is preformed on the data set, to ensure that the sequences are not more
similar to each other than a certain threshold. This can be done in several ways. We chose
the Hobohm 1 algorithm [34].
It takes an ordered set of sequences and clusters them by a
given threshold. It goes through the input list and the sequences are either added to the list of
non-redundant sequences, or discarded, depending on whether the similarity between the given
sequence and the sequences in the list is above the threshold, or below, respectively.

3
Methods
3.1
Batch Evergreen pipeline
Inferring phylogeny for all the publicly available WGS data at once is unfeasible, as the computa-
tional time that would require is enormous. Therefore it is advisable to divide the data in some
manner, and then infer one phylogenetic tree for each set of sequences. This partitioning could
be done by subtypes, for example. Such solution would suﬃce, either we choose to follow a SNP
based or a whole-genome MLST based approach. Moreover, the similarity between sets should
be such, that it allows diversity in the phylogenetic trees. Therefore, we chose to use a homology
reduced database of reference sequences, ensuring, that none of the reference sequences are more
than 98% similar. Furthermore, to also avoid having too much redundancy in the sequence sets,
we decided to cluster the new sequences in each set to the previously downloaded ones and to
each other, based on genetic distance, with a Hobohm 1 algorithm.

As for the basis for the genetic distance, we opted to go with SNPs, as it allows for better
discrimination between closely related strains and it requires less computational power.

Consequently, our aim was to build a pipeline (Figure 2), that can:
• Download raw sequencing reads from the public repositories
• Find the closest matching reference genomes (templates)
• Map the reads to the reference genome to create consensus sequences
• Calculate the genetic diﬀerences between the consensus sequences for a given reference
• Perform homology reduction on the new sequences
• And infer a phylogenetic tree for each group of non-redundant sequences
Additional design considerations:
• Accuracy of the inferred phylogenetic tree should be suﬃcient
• Suﬃcient speed and scalability, as it needs to ﬁnish within a day
• Allocation of computational resources should be dependent on the amount of data
• Ability to run on high performance computing cluster
• Restartable, using check-points

the pipeline stops, as that indicates the presence of a non-temporary network problem.

prediction results are saved in a work-directory. When all runs are done, the results are parsed

to have a higher than 94% total template coverage, which is the ratio of the number of unique
16-mers that are common to the query and the template, and the total number of unique 16-mers

is done to ensure, that the reads suﬃciently cover the template to get a consensus sequence and
that the bases can be called with conﬁdence.

would contain all the runs that have the same template.
batch evergreen pipeline checks if everything ran correctly and whether there are template
ﬁles. If there are, then it constructs a command suitable for the TORQUE Resource Manager
based on the arguments it received. Namely, the wrapper for the second part of the pipeline is
launched for each template ﬁle via a job array. The processes for each template are self-contained,
so they can run in parallel as individual jobs on the system.

The genetic distances between the new and the old non-redundant sequences are calculated in a
parallel manner, and the new sequences are clustered to the non-redundant ones with a threshold
of 10 SNPs. Afterwards, the remaining new sequences are clustered together with the Hobohm
1 algorithm. The new clusters are added to the previously found ones in a hash, and dumped to
disk with the python pickle package. The genetic distances between all non-redundant sequences
are the input for the PHYLIP Neighbor program. Consequently, the genetic distances between
the old non-redundant sequences need to be also calculated, if we are considering all sequences
for the unknown bases, as the eliminated columns would aﬀect the distances. But if we are just
considering them pairwise, then the values will not change from batch to batch, and therefore
the distance matrix for the non-redundant sequences can be saved to disk, and reloaded, when
needed.
It is an option to save the redundant sequences in VCF format, that only contains the positions
that diﬀer between the given sequence and its cluster representative sequence.
To reduce the computational burden of tree inference only the non-redundant sequences are used
in it. If there are at least three non-redundant sequences, then a distance based phylogenetic tree
could be made with the Neighbor program from PHYLIP [38]. If there are more than three, then
a maximum likelihood based tree can be inferred with a multi-threading version of IQ-tree [39] as
well. For less than 200 sequences, bootstrapping is performed for 1000 rounds. Both are executed
from the tree cobbler wrapper. Running it with the distance option requires the path to the
PHYLIP distance matrix, while the likelihood option takes the list of non-redundant sequences,
that is used to prepare a multiple-sequence alignment in fasta format.
After the non-redundant trees are constructed, the clusters are placed onto the trees as clades
with the same distance from their ancestral node as the cluster representative. This is done using
the ETE-toolkit [40].
The ﬁnal phylogenetic trees are saved in newick tree format, with a ﬁlename constructed from
the distance calculation method (all or pw), the tree inference method (dist, ml) and the time at
the start of the tree cobbler wrapper.
3.2
Comparison of phylogenetic trees
To compare the tree topologies, the Robinson-Foulds symmetric diﬀerences [41] were calculated
between all four types of phylogenetic trees that were made using the same sequences. The
symmetric diﬀerence between two trees are the number of partitions made by the inner branches
20
==PAGE:22==
in one tree, that are not in the other tree, counted for both trees. The maximum possible value
is the sum of the number of inner branches in the trees. For bifurcating trees, that have the
same number of leaves and inner branches, this is 2 × n −6, where n is the number of leaves
on the tree. To allow comparison between trees for diﬀerent templates, the value of symmetric
diﬀerence was normalized by the maximum value of symmetric diﬀerence for the given trees.
The ETE-toolkit was employed to compute the normalized symmetric diﬀerence. The program
allows comparison between trees with diﬀerent nodes, by pruning both trees until only the common
nodes remain.
3.2.1
Escherichia coli phylogeny benchmarking dataset
The in vitro evolution dataset was used as a model for an outbreak.
To test the eﬀect of
homology reduction on the accuracy of the inferred trees, the clustering was carried out with
thresholds 1 - 10 SNPs and trees were also inferred without homology reduction. Afterwards, we
processed the inferred phylogenetic trees with the epidemictrester.py script [35]. As 51 from the
101 nodes are supposed to be inner nodes, the branches were collapsed to have only 50 leaves
on the phylogenetic tree. The collapsed trees were compared to the ideal phylogeny in two ways.
The parent-child method counts the parent-child relationships that are the same in the inferred
tree as in the ideal tree. The clade method compares the nodes in the clades with the ideal
clades.
The normalized symmetric diﬀerences of the collapsed trees with the same clustering threshold
was also computed, after the collapsed node names were shortened to only keep the name of the
sample that was taken at the latest time-point among them.

In the second part of the pipeline, the new sequences that were matched to the same reference
genome are processed together.
First, the consensus sequences are created. The time needed for the reads to be mapped to the
reference genome is dependent on the amount of runs, the amount of reads and the length of the
reference genome. It took 3.5 - 16 minutes for one consensus sequence to be done, but 28 can
be mapped simultaneously, therefore the time required does not follow a strict linear relationship
with the number of runs (Figure 11).
The distances between the consensus sequences were calculated in two manner, which aﬀects
the wall-time of the process. The pairwise manner has O(N × k) time complexity, because the
distances between the old sequences (N) do not need to be recalculated every time. Meanwhile,
when we consider all sequences for the unknown bases, the time complexity is O(N2). Figure 12
shows, that the pairwise distance calculation requires less time, than the other method. There is
one batch, that exceeded 4000 seconds in wall-time, and the reason for this is that the distance
calculation between the old sequences is not yet done in parallel.
The distance based inference of the phylogenetic trees requires less than 1 sec every time (data
not shown). In contrast, the maximum likelihood based tree construction can take signiﬁcant
time, despite the multi-threading. Figure 13 displays the wall-times needed for the diﬀerent set of
sequences to ﬁnish. IQ-tree determines the optimum number of threads for each dataset, which
has a further confounding eﬀect on the wall-time, besides the number of sequences in each set.

Phylogenetic trees and metadata
The metadata associated with the runs are provided by the people who deposited the WGS data.
The pipeline only takes runs, that have accompanying information about their collection date
and location, but many other parameters can be ’missing’ or ’unknown’. Examples can be found
in Appendix D.
The metadata could be added to the phylogenetic trees as tip labels by utilizing the nexus tree
ﬁle format [43]. Looking at the latest phylogenetic trees created for L. monocytogenes J1 220
(Figures 22 and 23), the clusters made during the homology reduction are not concordant in
all cases between the ’all’ and the ’pairwise’ method. The clusters made by the ’all’ method
sometimes contain more sequences, for example the orange colored sequences belong to one
cluster on the ’all’ trees, while on the ’pairwise’ trees they form two clusters and one sequence
stands alone.
Meanwhile, the sequences that belong to the same cluster are from the same geographical region,
but not always from the same time period. The clustered red sequences are from 2014 and 2016,
by both type of distance calculation. However, the sequences that were collected on the same
day are clustered together. So, if the isolates collected from 2001.04.24 had been from humans,
instead of environmental sources, they would have been part of an outbreak.
If we also examine the clusters on the latest ’all’ maximum likelihood phylogenetic tree for C. jejuni
(Figure 24), we yet again see, that the sequences in the clusters are not necessarily from the same
time period. Furthermore, sometimes they are not even from the same geographical region, like
in the case of the brown cluster on the top of the tree.
All the sequences that are on these trees had a publication date that fell between May and January
of 2017, yet the actual collection dates are from diﬀerent times.

5
Discussion
Our ultimate aim with the Batch Evergreen pipeline is to provide background information with
regards to the phylogenetic relationship of the pathogenic bacterial isolates, that are uploaded
to the Compare Bacterial Analysis Pipeline. To fulﬁll this aim, it needs to process the relevant
WGS data for given time period in a relatively short time, i.e. add the runs that were published
the previous day to the phylogenetic trees within 24 hours.
The amount of WGS data that could be used is relevant for the usability of the pipeline, but the
more data we have, the more computational time it requires to run the pipeline, threatening the
feasibility of the setup. While testing the pipeline with the food-borne bacterial pathogens, we
saw that approximately 70% of the runs returned by the query were not added to the phylogenetic
trees. There are two reasons for this. First, the lack of metadata renders the WGS data useless
for our purposes. We have no means to circumvent this, as we already require only the minimum:
sample collection time and location. Outbreak surveillance needs this information. Adding these
’unknown’ runs to the phylogeny would just increase the computational time in the pipeline,
without conferring any beneﬁt.
Therefore the runs without the necessary metadata are not
downloaded, and unfortunately just the community can improve on this situation, by ﬁlling out
the metadata ﬁelds when uploading the runs.
The second reason for the reduced amount of runs the pipeline can use, is the necessity to have
a close reference genome. Since we map the reads to the reference genome to gain a consensus
sequence for the distance calculation, it is desirable, to have a high template coverage, in order to
minimize the number of unknown bases. We could increase the amount of runs with templates
by recurring updates to the homology reduced reference database, allowing not just complete
genomes as references, but assemblies as well, and lowering the threshold for accepting a match
to a template. The ﬁrst two solutions would increase the amount of references in the database,
potentially allowing more runs to be matched closely with one. As for the last solution, if the
threshold for the total template coverage is set to for example 90%, we would gain new runs:
all those on Figure 9, that fall between 90% and 94%. The consensus sequences of these runs
could have more unknown bases, if there are no reads that can be mapped to the reference and
not just mismatches, therefore potentially less positions could be used to calculate the genetic
distances. However, the 94% and 90% is calculated for the 16-mers, and these values correspond
to 99.6% and 99.3% in the sequence-space. Thus lowering the threshold by 4% only results in
0.3% change in the probability of matching bases.
In addition, the requirement for the multiple matching templates could be removed, allowing the
run to be placed onto multiple trees, minimizing the possibility of overlooking a connection.
The feasibility of our setup is mainly determined by the time the pipeline needs to ﬁnish running.
The wall-time requirement of the two parts of the pipeline is diﬀerent. The ﬁrst part is independent
of the sequences that were previously processed, it just depends on the number of new runs.
Therefore the wall-time is not increasing inﬁnitely. It can be kept in hand by increasing the
number of parallel processes and the computational resources, as needed.
The second part of the pipeline requires more and more wall-time as the number of non-redundant
sequences increases by each time the pipeline is run. One exception from this is the mapping,
which is only needed for the new sequences. However, mapping can still take considerable time
and increasing the number of cores for all the jobs in the array is not feasible. Therefore the use
of a faster mapping algorithm would be desirable.
The wall-time of distance calculation either grows exponentially with the number of non-redundant
sequences, if we employ the ’all’ calculation method, or linearly, if the ’pairwise’ is used. The
code for the ’all’ calculation method can be further modiﬁed to parallelize the distance calculation
between the old sequences, but this would only reduce the wall-time, the complexity would remain
37
==PAGE:39==
O(N2).
However, N is the number of non-redundant sequences, which does not necessarily
grow at the same pace as new sequences are coming in. Table A.1 shows, that the number
of non-redundant sequences can diﬀer considerably from the number of total sequences. The
homology reduction during the distance calculation does reduce the number of sequences and
thus the computational time. We can also see, that the ’all’ distance calculation method leads
to less non-redundant sequences and bigger clusters, than the ’pairwise’ method, due to the
loss of information with the removed positions. This is positive for the feasibility, but might be
detrimental to the usability.
The last module of the pipeline creates the phylogenetic tree. With regards to the speed, there
is no doubt, that the neighbor-joining method outperforms the maximum likelihood method, as
the former took less than a second in every case, while the latter required more than 18 hours
for just around 300 sequences.
In conclusion, it seems feasible to run the pipeline daily and ﬁnish inferring a phylogenetic tree,
but only if the tree inference is done by a distance based method, such as neighbor-joining.
Speed, however, is less important than accuracy, when it comes to the phylogenies. Therefore
we tested the accuracy of the phylogenetic trees produced by the pipeline. Trees were inferred
for the in vitro E. coli evolution dataset, then the four diﬀerent phylogenies were compared to
the ideal phylogeny and to each other. In order to test the eﬀect of the clustering, diﬀerent
clustering thresholds were applied to the dataset before the tree inference. When the clustering
was set to a few SNPs, both the parent-child relationship and the clade test showed, that the ’all’
distance calculation method and the neighbor-joining tree produced the tree that best resembled
the ideal phylogeny. However, at clustering at 10 SNPs, the diﬀerence in performance between
the neighbor-joining and the maximum likelihood method was negligible with the ’all’ method.
Meanwhile, the ’all’ distance calculation method still outperformed the ’pairwise’ method. These
results suggest, that the ’all’ distance calculation method and neighbor-joining could produce
phylogenies that have an acceptable accuracy.
The tests were carried out on ”collapsed” trees, where the nodes closer than a certain threshold
were treated as one node. This ideally would result in the ancestor samples becoming inner
nodes. However, by clustering the sequences before the tree inference and then simply adding
them back, the distances below the threshold were just taken as zero, essentially removing the
ﬁner details from those clades. Thus all the clustered sequences were just collapsed into one
node, which reduced the accuracy of the trees. This is the reason for the decreasing fraction of
accurate parent-child relationships and clades as the threshold is increasing. Consequently, the
accuracy of the trees could be perhaps improved by lowering the threshold for the clustering.
Another improvement could be made by inferring phylogenies for the clusters and grafting the
sub-trees onto the non-redundant tree.
The normalized Robinson-Foulds symmetric diﬀerences were also calculated between the collapsed
trees. At lower level of clustering, the trees made with the same inference method were more
similar to each other, suggesting that the diﬀerence between the trees is derived from the tree
inference method, and not the distance calculation and clustering. However, at 10 SNPs, the trees
made with the same distance calculation method were more similar to each other, as the clustering
had a more stronger eﬀect, than the tree inference. In addition, the ’pairwise’ neighbor-joining
tree was more dissimilar compared with the ’all’ trees, than the ’pairwise’ maximum likelihood
tree.
The normalized symmetric diﬀerences were determined between the latest phylogenetic trees for
each template to compare the topologies. The ’all’ and ’pairwise’ maximum likelihood inferred
trees (Figure 18) were more similar to each other than the ’all’ and ’pairwise’ neighbor-joining
trees (Figure C.13). As the maximum likelihood method takes a multiple sequence alignment, it
works independently from the calculated distances. Therefore it is not sensitive to the distance
38
==PAGE:40==
calculation method. Rather, the diﬀerences between the maximum likelihood trees are mainly
due to the diﬀerences in the clustering of sequences. Meanwhile, the neighbor-joining trees are so
dissimilar, that can not be explained just by the diﬀerences in the clustering. It seems, that the
diﬀerent distance calculation methods result in distance matrices that yield diﬀerent relationships
between the sequences.
Comparing the trees made with the same distance calculation method, but diﬀerent tree inference
method, we see, that the ’all’ neighbor-joining and maximum likelihood trees (Figure 19) are more
similar, than the ’pairwise’ ones (Figure 20). So, while the two tree inference methods produce
diﬀerent trees, the dissimilarities are not necessarily as big, as between the ’pairwise’ trees.
In summary, the ’pairwise’ neighbor-joining trees are dissimilar compared to any other type of
trees, indicating a problem with this particular tree type. This notion is supported by the frequent
presence of negative branch lengths in the ’pairwise’ neighbor-joining trees (see Appendix E).
However, as previously mentioned, the ’all’ distance calculation method results in less non-
redundant sequences, than the ’pairwise’. As the number of sequences increases, the matri-
ces that are used to calculate the genetic distances become smaller, since more columns are
removed due to having an unknown base. Thus the genetic distances between the sequences
are reduced, and sequences are clustered together, even though they are not less than 10 SNPs
apart, when considered pairwise. Therefore there could be bigger clusters on the ’all’ trees, with
sequences clustered together, that are not necessarily similar, leading to the prediction of non-
existing outbreaks. Consequently, although the ’all’ distance calculation method is preferred for
the neighbor-joining method, as it creates more accurate trees, perhaps the ’pairwise’ method is
better for the clustering of the sequences. Moreover, the increasing number of unknown bases
(and non-counted SNPs) could reduce the accuracy of the tree inference method, as the tree
grows in size.
Finally, looking at the trees with metadata added to the nodes, it became apparent, that al-
though sequences from samples taken on the same day and at the same location do end up on
the same cluster, clusters frequently have sequences that were collected on diﬀerent dates and
locations. However, clonal populations that are preserved for longer time periods exist, both
for L. monocytogenes and C. jejuni. Extended outbreaks could also be behind these clusters.
Therefore, clusters that contain clinical samples, where there is agreement in the collection dates
and locations, suggest strong possibility for an outbreak. If there are also environmental samples
in the cluster, it could possibly be a source or could be connected in another way to the outbreak.
In conclusion, the Batch Evergreen Pipeline has been tested with more than 16000 whole-genome
sequencing runs for the most prominent bacterial foodborne pathogens. Based on the accuracy
of the resulting phylogenetic trees and the wall-time constraints for the pipeline, neighbor-joining
with the traditional SNPs based genetic distance calculation method is the preferred approach
for large-scale, whole-genome phylogenetic tree inference.
6
Future perspectives
It was demonstrated that the Batch Evergreen pipeline can handle the amount of WGS data that
is expected to be generated daily. However, in order to improve the usability, more development
steps are needed.
1. The query or the download should be modiﬁed to return more relevant runs, i.e. runs
from more than 15 years ago should not be on the trees, as they are less relevant for the
surveillance in the present.
2. The number of runs that are placed on the trees should increase. More runs should have

mize the number of processed runs, the run that doesn’t have a template in the database,
but has suﬃcient quality could be assembled and added as a reference assembly to the
database. In addition, the possibility of lowering the acceptance threshold should be con-

3. A faster mapping script should be implemented in the second part of the pipeline. This
algorithm should also ensure, that the called SNPs have the necessary support from the
mapped reads.
4. The distance calculation module should be rewritten, to calculate the distances between
the old sequences in parallel. Moreover, a mixed solution should be investigated, where the
clustering is done based on ’pairwise’ distances and afterwards the distances between the
non-redundant sequences is determined by the ’all’ method. In theory, this would result in
better discrimination between the closely related sequences, yet ensure correct tree inference
with neighbor-joining. Furthermore, the threshold for clustering should be reviewed.
5. The possibility of inferring sub-trees from the clusters and then grafting these sub-trees
onto the non-redundant trees should be investigated.
Ultimately, the pipeline should be connected to the Compare Bacterial Analysis Pipeline and made
compatible with the Evergreen service. Therefore it needs to be switched to an SQL database
based system for storing the information about the processed runs. Finally, the phylogenetic trees
labeled with the metadata should be displayed on a web based platform for the users.
