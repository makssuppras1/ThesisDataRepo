Abstract
Piezoelectric materials, known for their ability to convert mechanical stress into elec­
trical energy, have found diverse applications across a wide range of industries. Their
potential is currently limited by the constraints of the possible manufacturing processes
due to cost, dimension and time related issues. In recent years additive manufacturing
has emerged as a promising alternative for fabricating complex structures with improved
precision and design flexibility.
This master’s thesis focuses specifically on the extrusion­based material deposition
of piezoceramic slurries. The primary objective is to design and construct a machine
capable of depositing ceramic slurries with varying properties onto a rotating platform to
create ring­shaped objects.
To accomplish this goal, the characteristics of these slurries have been thoroughly
studied, and the key variables in the process have been identified. To construct a machine
capable of fulfilling this task, the working principles and construction components of var­
ious additive manufacturing machines have been extensively researched and analyzed.
During the design phase, a systematic approach was employed to ensure a suitable out­
come. This involved conducting research, analyzing data, generating concepts, selecting
the most suitable options to develop and optimize a virtual CAD model, and ultimately
construct a physical machine.
The resulting machine was built at DTU AM Lab, utilizing their tools and extrusion
printers. Necessary parts were sourced from various suppliers or manufactured on­site.
At the conclusion of the project, the machine was successfully constructed, and a test
using a simulated slurry was conducted to evaluate its capabilities and identify any draw­
backs.
To further optimize and develop the printer, it is recommended to perform tests using
actual piezoceramic slurries. This will provide a comprehensive understanding of the
machine’s capabilities and limitations.
Despite the limitation of conducting tests solely with a simulated slurry, this thesis
successfully demonstrates the design and construction of an extrusion­based system for
creating circular parts. While meeting the specified requirements within the scope of the
simulated tests, further improvements can be made by incorporating actual ceramic ma­
terials. This thesis lays a foundation for future advancements in extrusion­based manu­
facturing of piezoceramics and showcases the potential for realizing functional designs
with attention and dedication.

Introduction
This master’s thesis in the field of mechanical engineering focuses on the extrusion
Additive Manufacturing of piezoelectric ceramic slurries. The main objective of the project
is to design and build a 3D printer which can extrude piezoelectric ceramic material on a
rotating platform hence enabling the precise and efficient creation of ring­shaped object
in varying dimensions. The slurry includes template particles whose precise orientation
is crucial for the successful integration of this technology into the industry. As a result,
ensuring optimal particle orientation becomes a prominent focus within the printer design.
1.1
Problem Statement
Piezoelectric materials have gained significant prominence due to their widespread
applications across diverse industries worldwide. These remarkable materials are em­
ployed in an array of devices, ranging from watches and ultrasound equipment to micro­
phones, inkjet printers, speakers, and an extensive range of sensors and motors (Fig­
ure 1.1). Their unique ability to convert mechanical energy into electrical energy and
vice versa makes them indispensable in modern technology. In order to maximize the
performance and functionality of these materials, researchers have explored various ap­
proaches. While the growth of single crystals has demonstrated exceptional properties,
this method is often impractical due to its time­consuming and resource­intensive nature.
Another promising approach involves aligning the grains during the sintering phase of
the material, commonly utilized in tape casting. Tape casting involves the deposition of
thin layers of piezoceramic slurry onto a carrier surface, followed by shaping and lam­
ination. However, this process has inherent limitations in terms of size, cost and time
constraints. Consequently, there is a growing need for an alternative process that can
overcome these limitations. In recent years, extrusion printing of ceramic slurries has
emerged as an innovative and promising technology. By utilizing suitable nozzles, op­
timizing printing parameters, and employing appropriate slurries, this advanced printing
method holds immense potential for the future advancement of piezoelectric materials.

1.2
Objective
The main objective of the thesis is to design and build a machine capable of the
deposition of ceramic slurry onto a rotating platform while orienting the template particles
included in the slurry, ensuring that their longer sides are facing the platform.
To achieve the project objectives, it is crucial to incorporate robustness and adaptabil­
ity into the machine’s design. It will ensure that the machine will overcome the challenges
and variations in the printing process, resulting in consistent and reliable performance.
Adaptability is equally important, allowing the machine to adopt to a wide range of vari­
ables, such as material properties and printing parameter.
The project’s scope includes research on piezoceramic materials and their proper­
ties, by gaining a comprehensive understanding of the underlying principles, the designer
will be able to design and optimize the printer’s extrusion system for precise material de­
position. The research is being conducted in close partnership between CTS Ferroperm
Piezoceramics, a leading company in the field of piezoceramics, and the Additive Manu­
facturing group of DTU. The collaboration allows for the exchange of knowledge, exper­
tise, and resources, ensuring that the project benefits from both academic knowledge and
industrial relevance. Throughout the research, valuable guidance, support, and advice
are being provided by experts from CTS Ferroperm and the DTU AM Group, contributing
to the project’s advancement and ensuring its alignment with real­world applications.
The following are the intended learning and research objectives of this thesis:
Research objectives:
• Researching existing solutions for extrusion based material deposition.
• Investigating the different variables that could effect the quality of extrusion printing
piezoelectric ceramic slurries.
• Based on the previous researches creating concepts and evaluating them.
• Evaluating the printer and providing feedback on improvement opportunities.
Learning objectives:
• Developing understanding of piezoelectric materials including their properties and
manufacturing processes.
• Developing understanding on the importance of template orientation in piezoelectric
slurries.
• Developing understanding on the used electronics and coding.
• Understanding the different components of extrusion AM.
• Designing and creating virtual model of the 3D printer.
• Assembling the physical printer.

Design Approach
There are multiple approaches used for dividing the design phases. For this thesis,
the four­phase diamond structure model was selected based on previous experience (Fig­
ure 2.1). The initial two phases prioritize divergent thinking to gather extensive knowledge
and to generate multiple solutions, while the latter half focuses on convergent thinking to
identify the and develop an optimal solution.
Figure 2.1: Diamond structure of the four design phase.
Problem Definition: During the problem definition phase, it is necessary to clearly
define the design problem or challenge at hand. This phase consists of understanding the
requirements, constraints, and objectives of the project. Conducting research and gath­
ering relevant data and information about the problems field, including existing solutions,
industry standards, and user needs. By accomplishing a comprehensive understanding
of the problem, one can create a solid foundation for the following design phases.
Design Exploration: The design exploration is a creative and iterative phase where
the focus is on to generate design concepts and alternatives to found different solutions.
Sketches, rough designs, and virtual prototypes are created during the phase. Evaluation
of each design concept should also take place based on factors such as feasibility, per­
formance, cost, manufacturability, and the many previously identified criteria relevant to
the project such as the intended application, user requirements, aesthetics, ergonomics,
and sustainability aspects. Collaboration and input from the different team members will
further enrich the exploration phase.
Design Optimization: In the design optimization phase,the focus is to refine and
improve the selected design concept. Creating computer­aided design (CAD) to have de­
tailed models to visualize and analyze the design more accurately. Providing calculations,
simulations and running test are necessary to identify areas for improvement. Making the
necessary adjustments and optimizations to enhance functionality, efficiency, reliability,
and overall quality. This phase usually involves working with domain experts, conduct­
ing experiments, and conducting physical or virtual testing to validate and fine­tune the
design.

Design Communication: This final phase focuses on effectively communicating the
final design to the relevant stakeholders. The designer must create clear and comprehen­
sive documentation of the design, including detailed drawings, diagrams, and specifica­
tions. These documents serve as a blueprint for manufacturing, assembly, and implemen­
tation. It is crucial to use appropriate technical language, standards, and conventions to
ensure effective communication and avoid misunderstandings. Feedbacks and sugges­
tions from the relevant parties should be implemented into the design.
This process is usually iterative, meaning that the phases may overlap or the revisit­
ing of previous stages as new insights and information emerge might be necessary. On
Figure 2.2 a more detailed overview of each phase is visible.

In the initial phase of this project the main objective involved gathering information
from company representatives and supervisors from DTU. A visit to CTS’s Danish fa­
cility provided valuable insights into the manufacturing processes and the fundamental
requirements of piezoceramics, including their properties and characteristics. Weekly
meetings served as a recurring platform for discussing the progress and current state
of the project, fostering valuable discussions, insights, and the gathering of valuable in­
formation. Throughout research was conducted to identify factors that could potentially
impact the extrusion process and understand the behavior of piezoceramics. The study
also involved studying different types of extrusion machines and additive manufacturing
machines. A visit to the AM Lab at DTU proved beneficial for understanding working
principles and identifying essential components. As the research phase concluded, a
comprehensive list of machine requirements was obtained, providing a solid foundation
for the subsequent design phases.
Afterward, a comprehensive analysis of various machines and solutions was under­
taken, systematically exploring each element such as the frame, extruder, motion trans­
mission, and movements, in order to understand their respective advantages and disad­
vantages. Although commercially available ceramic printers are rare, there are numer­
ous hobbyist and custom­built projects that held promise as potential design alternatives.

3.1.1
Ceramics
Ceramics, derived from the ancient Greek word ”keramos” meaning ”burnt stuff,”
have a history tracing back to 24000 BC, the early stages of human society. During
this time, ceramics were primarily crafted using a blend of animal bone, fat, ashes, and
clay­like materials. These ingredients were combined to create miniature figures, which
were subsequently fired in primitive kilns. Around 9000 BC, the first practical pottery ves­
sels emerged, serving as containers for grains and other food items. As such, ceramics
can be regarded as the oldest man­made materials, originating from the dawn of human
civilization [3–6].
By the latter half of the 20th century material scientists discovered and synthesized
many different types of ceramics with widely ranging properties and values. The manufac­
turing process now not always needs high temperatures, so it become relatively difficult

Dielectric materials:
Ceramic materials that are good electrical insulators are referred to as dielectric ma­
terials. While these materials do not conduct electric current when subjected to an electric
field, they are not completely unaffected by it. The electric field induces a redistribution
of charges inside the material, leading to the formation of an electrical dipole. This phe­
nomenon is the origin of the term ”dielectric” [3, 8].
Polarization:
Polarization refers to the process of creating an electrical dipole. Various factors con­
tribute to polarization, including: electronic polarization, orientation (dipolar) polarization,
space charge polarization and atomic or ionic polarization.
.
Figure 3.3: Different types of polarization [3]
Each of the four types of polarization holds significance, but when it comes to piezo­
electricity, atomic or ionic polarization is the most important. This polarization mechanism
involves the displacement of atoms or ions within a crystal structure when subjected to
an electric field. A wide range of polarization effects is possible through this mechanism,
depending on the crystal structure, the presence of solid solution, and other factors [3, 9].
Piezoelectricity:
Having established the fundamental terms, we can now delve into a comprehensive
description of piezoelectricity.
Piezoelectric materials exhibit electric polarization when subjected to mechanical
stress within a single crystal structure. This phenomenon results in the accumulation of a
net positive charge on one side of the crystal and a net negative charge on the other side.
Conversely, the reverse effect is also true: applying an electrical voltage to the material
induces mechanical stresses.The effect is represented in Figure 3.4 .[3, 10].

The piezoelectric effect was discovered by Pierre and Jacques Curie and first re­
ported in 1880. Piezoelectricity was identified by the Curies in a number of naturally oc­
curring and laboratory­grown single crystals like quartz, zinc blende (sphalerite), boracite,
tourmaline, topaz etc. [3, 11].
The piezoelectric effect arises due to the absence of central symmetry in the a ma­
terials crystal structure. Hence, only crystals that are lacking center of symmetry, known
as anisotropic crystals, exhibit piezoelectric properties. However, even within these crys­
tals, piezoelectricity is not observed in all directions. This is illustrated in Figure 3.5 .
In the unstressed state, the positive and negative charges are balanced, resulting in no
polarization. When stress is applied in direction b, polarization does not occur because
the charges still balance each other. However, when stress is applied in direction c, the
charges become unbalanced, leading to the occurrence of piezoelectricity and polariza­
tion[3, 12].
Figure 3.5: Directionality of polarization in a piezoelectric material [3].
Pyroelectricity is a property observed in specific crystals that possess natural elec­
trical polarization, resulting in the presence of electric fields. It can be defined as the
capability of certain materials to produce a temporary voltage when subjected to heating
or cooling. The change in temperature causes slight shifts in the positions of the atoms
within the crystal structure, leading to a modification in the material’s polarization. [3].
Ferroelectric materials share a similar property to pyroelectric materials, however,

The orientation mechanism in ferroelectric crystals due to an applied electric field is

electric field is reduced to zero, most of the domains will remain aligned resulting in the
remanent polarization value. [3, 14] If now the direction of the electric field is reversed
the domains are forced to change their direction. At a certain point the domains in one
direction balance with the domains to the other direction resulting in zero net polarization.
Continued increase in the negative electric field will lead to net polarization in the other
direction [3, 12, 14].

3.2.1
Bulk processes
A bulk component only contains a single layer of piezoelectric ceramic with electrically
conductive metal applied on two opposing side. These electrodes are used during the
poling of the material. Bulk piezoelectric components are manufactured by compaction of
piezoceramic powder by conventional processes like uniaxial pressing, isostatic pressing,
and extrusion.
Figure 3.9: Flow chart for bulk manufacturing process.
1. Raw materials: high purity raw materials are used mainly lead oxide, titanium oxide,
zirconium oxide and a range of dopants.
2. During the mixing phase the raw materials are mixed with additives and solvents.
3. After that they are calcinated at 900­1000°C to remove organics and in order to
create a more homogenous material mix.
4. The purpose of the milling phase is to further improve the homogeneity and to reach
the required particle size.
5. Binders are added to the slurry so it would maintain its shape after the pressing.
Following this, the mixture is subjected to a spray­drying process to obtain powder.
6. In the pressing phase the slurry is pushed into a cavity to acquire its form.
7. Following the pressing the unintended materials like binders, organic compounds
and additives are removed by a very slow burn out usually at a temperate around
500­700°C.
8. During the sintering phase the grains grow in size and diffuse to achieve a dense
part. It happens at a higher temperature and require a lot of optimizations of the
process.
9. To create a surface applicable for electroding some amount of machining, grinding
and polishing must take place. This process is also required to attain the exact
dimensions of the part.
10. Electroding usually performed by screen printing and firing of an electrode paste
(typically silver). This will provide the external electrodes on the part.

3.2.2
Multi­layer process
Bulk piezoelectric components are suitable for sensor applications and resonant sys­
tems. However, when it comes to actuator applications, they are not the optimal solu­
tion. This is because achieving a significant quasi­static displacement with bulk compo­
nents necessitates the use of very high operating voltages, typically ranging from 500V
to 1000V.
To address this challenge, the multi­layer technology was developed. A multi­layer
component consists of multiple layers of piezoelectric material, arranged in an alternating
fashion with internal electrodes. These internal electrodes are arranged in a successive
pattern of positive and negative electrodes. On one side of the component, all the positive
electrodes are connected to an external electrode, while on the other side, the negative
electrodes are connected to another external electrode. This electrode configuration en­
ables the desired electrical behavior and functionality of the multi­layer component.
The most common process for creating multi­layer components is with tape casting
with the doctor blade process. A schematic of the tape casting process and the doctor
blade apparatus is illustrated in the Figure 3.10. The technique involves the application
of a slurry onto a continuously moving carrier surface. The slurry is then spread and
leveled to achieve a controlled thickness using a long, smooth blade at the edge (the
doctor blade). This process ensures a uniform and consistent coating of the slurry on the
carrier surface.
The slurry used in the tape­casting technique consists of a binder system dissolved
in a solvent. The inclusion of an adequate amount of binder ensures that, upon solvent
removal, a flexible tape is obtained. The solvent is removed through the process of evapo­
ration. However, it is crucial to carry out this evaporation slowly to prevent issues such as
cracking, bubbles, or distortion in the tape. To achieve controlled evaporation, the tape­
casting apparatus includes a long section between the doctor blade and the take­up reel.
This extended portion allows for controlled heating or the passage of airflow, facilitating
the gradual and uniform removal of the solvent from the slurry.

multi layer manufacturing process [3].

3.3
Particle orientation
Piezoelectric materials includes a wide range of substances, like ceramics, crystals,
thin/thick films, textured ceramics, composites, polymers, polar glasses, and more. Ta­
ble 3.1 demonstrates that modern single crystals offer the highest values for piezoelec­
tric and ferroelectric properties. They possess much higher properties when compared
to their polycrystalline counterparts [17, 18]. However, the fabrication of single crystals
poses problems such as high cost and exceeding growth time. These challenges makes
their widespread engineering application unfavorable [19, 20]. For these reasons, there
has been growing interest in the development of self aligned piezoceramic systems.
Texturing is a notable technique that offers a compromise between polycrystalline
and single crystal structures in ceramics. It involves orienting all grains within the ceramic
in a specific polar direction. The Templated Grain Growth (TGG) method is employed to
achieve texturing.
In this method, a seed particle with a platelet­like anisotropic shape, which is signifi­
cantly larger than the matrix particle size, is introduced into the tape casting slurry. As the
slurry flows under the doctor blade, the shape of the seed particle causes its wide surface
to align parallel to the carrier film’s surface. This alignment of the seed particle is crucial
for generating uniformly oriented grains during the sintering process. The composition
of the seed is also important, as it must remain stable without dissolving during sintering
while maintaining a similar crystal lattice spacing to facilitate epitaxial growth of the matrix.
During sintering, the ceramic matrix particles surrounding the template seed expe­
rience grain growth. Consequently, these particles align themselves in the same polar
direction as the template crystal, resulting in a material with texture.The purpose of this
alignment is to maximize the efficiency of the ceramic material [21, 22].
Figure 3.11: Schematics of TGG via different texturing mechanisms.

or have the template surface with the lowest energy, serving as the site for grain growth

Additive Manufacturing
The following section will briefly introduce the history of additive manufacturing with
a focus on extrusion printing and ceramic AM.
History of AM technologies
In the past, industries commonly used subtractive manufacturing to build products by
cutting away material from a solid block. In contrast, additive manufacturing employs a
layer­by­layer approach, enabling more intricate designs and interior modeling. One of
the initial applications of additive manufacturing was rapid prototyping (RP), which greatly
expedited the creation of prototypes. This allowed manufacturers to evaluate and test
designs before moving on to the production of final products [26].
In the rapid prototyping process, computer­aided design software is used to create
3D models. Machines then construct physical objects based on these models. In 1984,
Charles Hull introduced stereolithography, a groundbreaking technique. He obtained a
patent for stereolithography in 1986, describing a process in which UV light solidifies liquid
polymers into cross­sections of a 3D model.
In the mid­1990s, the AM industry bifurcated into two areas of focus: high­end appli­
cations for complex and engineered parts, particularly in fields like medicine, and print­
ers geared towards concept development and cost­effective functional prototyping. Scott
Crump invented Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) in 1988 by using a glue gun to extrude
a mixture of polyethylene and candle wax, creating a toy frog for his daughter. Crump went
on to patent the technology in 1989, developing an automated system for its implemen­
tation.
Currently, additive manufacturing technologies are extensively utilized across various
industries, including medical, construction, and jewelry and fashion [27–29].

Ceramic Addiditive Manufacturing
The incorporation of AM in ceramic component manufacturing has opened up new
avenues for creating unique shapes and properties. The pioneering work on AM of ce­
ramics was reported by Marcus and Sachs during the 1990s. Subsequently, with ad­
vancements in materials science and computer technology, a diverse range of AM tech­
niques tailored specifically for ceramic production have emerged. These technologies
can generally be classified into three categories based on the form of the pre­processed
feedstock: slurry­based, powder­based, and bulk solid­based methods. It is worth not­
ing that slurry­based methods employ ceramic/polymer mixtures with viscosities varying
from low­viscosity (mPa∙s) inks containing a lower ceramic concentration to high­viscosity
(Pa∙s) pastes with a significantly higher ceramic loading.
Distinct from the plastic or metal printing processes, ceramic extrusion printing follows
a different approach. Unlike plastic or metal, many ceramic slurries retain flowability at
room temperature, rendering the heating of the nozzle and bed unnecessary. Although
the material may still be in filament form, it is typically contained within a container or a
similar vessel. It is important to note hat while these printers have the capability to handle
various types of ceramics, they are primarily intended for design and creative purposes
rather than industrial or manufacturing applications [32–35].

In tape casting systems, drag flow predominates, leading to torque being applied to

In contrast, during extrusion printing, the flow is pressure­driven, resulting in a central core
of constant velocity known as plug flow. As there is no torque acting on the anisotropic
particles, the core consists of unaligned material [37–39].

5.1.3
Motion transmission
In the context of AM, the primary means of generating motion is achieved through
the use of electrical motors, usually stepper motors, that convert electrical energy into
rotational movement. However, this rotational motion, with the exception of the base
30
Development of Extrusion Based Additive Manufacturing System for Piezoelectric Ceramics
==PAGE:43==
plate, does not serve the purpose of moving the extruder head or the various axes in a
desired way. To overcome this challenge, it becomes necessary to convert the rotational
motion into linear motion. This conversion is typically accomplished through two common
methods: belt­driven and lead screw­driven transmissions.
Belt­driven transmission: In belt­driven transmission, a synthetic rubber belt rein­
forced with high­tensile fibers is connected to both ends of a carriage typically in a closed
loop. A specialized pulley, known as a timing pulley is inserted into this loop (Figure 5.4).
The timing pulley has pockets cut into it that match the profile of teeth present on the belt.
When a motor turns the timing pulley the belt moves. The timing pulley and belt come in
a variety of widths (w), teeth profiles, and pitches (P) (the distance between consecutive
teeth). For 3D printing, the GT tooth profile is optimized to prevent backlash or loss of
motion when motor reverses the direction of the pulley. The timing pulley comes in a va­
riety of different diameters. Intuitively, the larger the diameter of the pulley the greater the
circumference, which means that more belt will be moved per rotation of this pulley. To
calculate the distance travelled by one rotation of the pulley and to calculate the distance
travelled in one step of a 200 step per rotation stepper motor:
d1rot = P · N
(5.1)
d1step = d1rot
200
(5.2)
In typical pulley­belt systems, a commonly observed pitch size is either 2 or 3 mm,
often accompanied by 20 teeth (N). By applying these values, we can easily deduce that
the distance covered per step amounts to 0.2 or 0.3 mm, respectively. However, this level
of precision may not be sufficient for certain applications. Although microstepping can
help to refine the movement, it may result in a compromise regarding overall accuracy.
Moreover, ensuring an appropriate tension in the belt is crucial for effective torque transfer,
necessitating periodic re­tensioning during maintenance intervals [50, 51].
Figure 5.4: Typical pulley­belt system in 3D printer [52].
Lead screw­driven transmission: Lead screw­driven transmission operates simi­
larly to the motion of a nut sliding along a bolt while the bolt rotates. By preventing the nut
from rotating as the bolt spins, the resulting motion is purely linear. It is crucial to maintain
a stable carriage without wobbling during movement along the rail, as any wobble would

introduce unaccounted for lost motion. In linear screw­driven transmission, the compo­
nents analogous to the ”nuts” and ”bolts” are more intricate. The lead screw possesses
specialized threading that facilitates smooth load transfer from the nut, which is attached
to the carriage, without jamming(Figure 5.5).
Figure 5.5: Typical lead screw and screw nut system [53].
Compared to the belt­driven systems, lead screw­based transmissions offer improved
accuracy but tend to be slower and more costly. However, the enhanced accuracy is not
the primary reason why lead screws are commonly utilized for the Z­axis of 3D printers.
One key advantage of lead screws is their resistance to self­rotation. Consequently, when
power to the printer is cut, the carriage remains stable and does not drop unexpectedly. In
lead screws, pitch and lead are two important parameters that describe the characteristics
of the screw’s threading:
Pitch: The pitch of a lead screw refers to the distance between adjacent threads or the
distance the nut advances axially with one complete revolution of the screw. It is typically
measured in millimeters and represents the linear displacement achieved per revolution.
Lead: The lead of a lead screw is similar to pitch but also takes into account the
thread’s helix angle. It represents the linear displacement of the nut along the screw’s
axis for one complete revolution, considering both the axial and rotational motion. In
other words, the lead includes the axial movement due to pitch as well as the helical
advancement caused by the helix angle.

Stepper drive
Stepper drives serve as the driver circuits responsible for controlling the operation
of stepper motors. These drives operate by delivering current in pulses through different
phases to the stepper motor. An important factor in achieving precise motion resolutions
48
Development of Extrusion Based Additive Manufacturing System for Piezoelectric Ceramics
==PAGE:61==
is microstepping. In microstepping, the drive utilizes current regulation techniques to mit­
igate torque oscillations, enabling the operation of stepper motors in a wider range of
applications.
A microstepping drive modulates the current along a sine wave, ensuring that no pole
of the motor is fully turned on or off. While microstepping does not necessarily improves
the accuracy, it offers higher resolution compared to other driving modes. This is partic­
ularly advantageous in scenarios where the motor encounters no­load scenario. During
motor operation, instances of missed steps may arise. However, by distributing energy
across the motor rather than delivering it all at once, microstepping minimizes issues such
as ringing and overshoot.
Overall, stepper drives and microstepping play integral roles in enabling precise con­
trol and improved motion resolutions for stepper motors.

Recommendation for Future Work
Recommendations for improving the design:
Despite the successful fulfillment of the requirements, there are notable drawbacks
and shortages in the current state of the printer that need to be addressed.
Firstly, there is an issue with material leakage at the lower connection point of the tube
and the nozzle. This may be due to the lack of precision in the manufacturing process
(polymer extrusion printing) for these types of connections.
Additionally, an effective way to allow trapped air in the container to escape poses
a challenge. This problem will persist as long as the tube is not completely filled with
material up to the plunger’s level.
The upper section of the extruder, which houses the moving middle part, the stepper
motor and its holder, is excessively large. It would be beneficial to optimize or redesign
this section for a more compact solution.
The process of refilling the container is time­consuming and complicated due to the
requirement of removing the three connecting rods between the top and bottom caps each
time.
To improve the extruder design, the following modifications are recommended. Firstly,
implementing a through­shaft stepper motor (refer to Figure 8.1) or a gearbox that allows
the lead screw to pass through it would eliminate the need for the bulky upper section of
the extruder. This would result in a more compact and balanced extruder assembly, with
the majority of the weight situated at the container section.

Another important design change is to address the connection points between differ­
ent sections of the extruder. Instead of relying on rods to secure the upper and lower caps,
utilizing geometric locking mechanisms or threaded designs is suggested. This would not
only help mitigate the leakage issue but also simplify the refilling process.
It is essential to replace the polymer extrusion­made parts. While they currently func­
tion sufficiently, they were not intended as long­lasting solutions. The poor surface quality
of these parts can accumulate slurry, making cleaning and maintenance challenging. Ma­
chined parts with superior material characteristics, such as stiffness and strength, would
be more suitable in the long term operations.
Additionally, the electrical parts are currently permanently fixed on a plastic sheet.
To enhance the overall organization and safety, it is recommended to create an electri­
cal cabinet and apply it to the back section of the printer, where space has intentionally
been reserved for this purpose. Implementing effective cable management would also be
beneficial.
Finally, fixing the printer to a sheet metal or wooden sheet would allow for the posi­
tioning and fixing of the manual linear actuator that positions and moves the rotary stage.
By implementing these design improvements, the functionality and efficiency of the
machine can be significantly enhanced. This would result in a more balanced and user­
friendly system, addressing the current limitations of the printer.
Recommendations for testing:
As stated in section 4.1 the quality of the print and the particle orientation is highly
dependent on the process parameters. To achieve satisfactory results, it is highly rec­
ommended to conduct thorough testing and optimization of the printing process. The
optimization steps should follow a systematic approach, as outlined below:
Extrusion Speed Optimization:
• Begin by setting the extrusion height at a sufficiently high point (10­15 cm)
• Set the extrusion speed to a very low value (1­2 steps/second)
• Gradually increase the extrusion speed from a the very low value.
• Eventually, droplets of the material will appear.
• Continue increasing the extrusion speed until a continuous jet of 20­30 mm is con­
sistently maintained.
• This establishes the lower range of extrusion speed suitable for the specific material
and nozzle used.
Printing Height and Rotational Speed Testing:
• Creating a matrix to test different printing height and rotational speed combinations
for one extrusion speed.
• Starting to print with the lowest height value established for the used nozzle.
• Increasing rotational speed by previously established steps.
• Repeating the process until the ring is continuous and its width does not fluctuates .
• Changing print height and repeating the process.

• When the process window for the extrusion speed has been established it can be
increased and process can be repeated.
• Analyzing and testing of the printed rings to acquire optimal printing conditions
In the following these optimization steps, it becomes possible to fine­tune the printing
process and identify the optimal combination of extrusion speed, printing height, and ro­
tational speed. This iterative testing approach will help achieve the best possible results
for a given nozzle size and material combination. Optimization of other factors like the
nozzle length, shape and ascpect ratio should also take place to yield better results.

Conclusion
In chapter 1, the learning and research objectives were outlined. Here a conclusion
and examination on how these objectives were fulfilled will be given in the same order as
previously stated.
The designer conducted extensive research to gain understanding of the properties
and manufacturing processes of piezoceramic materials. This knowledge is presented
and discussed in detail in section 3.1and chapter 4. The underlying physical phenomena
and the basic calculations relevant to these materials are detailed and described in sub­
section 3.1.2 and subsection 3.1.3. Furthermore,section 3.2 explores the manufacturing
processes of bulk and multilayer piezoceramics. These chapters provide a comprehen­
sive understanding of the significance of this project and the need for the development of
such a machine.
The particle orientation holding significant importance in the project was detailed even
further. Table 3.1 in subsection 3.1.3 illustrates that different piezoceramics exhibit varying
properties, and section 3.3 delves into the consequences and basics of particle orienta­
tion. This analysis highlights the importance of addressing particle orientation during the
extrusion process.
In order to find a suitable solution for material extrusion, the designer extensively
researched similar machines, as detailed in section 5.1. Thoroughly studying these ma­
chines was crucial to gain an understanding of the necessary requirements and to discern
the advantages and disadvantages associated with specific design layouts.
To effectively control the machine, the designer acquired knowledge about basic elec­
tronic devices, cabling, and coding, as outlined in section 6.5. Components such as the
stepper drive, Arduino, and power supply were carefully selected following research and
learning. The code used for controlling the machine can be found in the Appendix B. By
fulfilling these objectives and conducting comprehensive research, the designer gained
the necessary knowledge and understanding to develop and control the machine effec­
tively.
An in­depth study was conducted to explore the variables that could affect print quality
and particle orientation, as described in section 4.1. These variables were categorized
based on their dependencies, with a particular focus on the process variables. Factors
such as printing height, printing speed, and nozzle shape or length were found to have
a significant impact on the orientation and quality of the final print. Therefore, special
attention was given to designing the machine with the capability to adjust these aspects
on a large scale.
The different components of AM machines were introduced in section 5.1. It was
essential to understand the fundamental principles and elements used in such machines
before the design phase. The solutions were evaluated, and their strengths and weak­
nesses were discussed to gain a better understanding of the challenges ahead.
With this knowledge, various solutions for different aspects were listed and assessed,
leading to the creation of two possible concepts. The detailed descriptions of these con­
cepts can be found in section 5.2, and the selection matrix shown in Table 5.1 was used
in the decision­making process, as explained in section 5.3. Ultimately, Concept B was
chosen as the final concept to pursue.

Subsequently, an extensive design phase was undertaken, during which the different
parts of the machine were initially developed as CAD models. The main CAD model

vertical motion systems, and rotary stage. Detailed discussions of these parts, along with
the electronics and code used to operate the machine, can be found in chapter 6.

was assembled, with many components manufactured at DTU’s AM Lab and the Work­

the machine fulfills the requirements stated in section 4.2, are presented in chapter 7.

sary, as discussed in more detail in chapter 8.

nozzle design options, different extrusion speeds, and other factors affecting print quality.

system capable of creating circular parts. The machine has successfully met its specified

evaluate the machine’s performance under real conditions. This would enable a more
accurate assessment of the system’s capabilities and potential areas for improvement.
In conclusion, this master thesis has successfully achieved its learning and research
objectives, demonstrating significant progress in documenting the design process and
providing detailed insights. Despite the limitation of conducting tests solely with a simu­
lated slurry, this master thesis serves as evidence of how, with attention and dedication,
it is still possible to realize a working design and machine using mostly state of the art
litterature and tools. Lastly, It lays a solid foundation for future advancements in the field
of extrusion­based systems and sets the stage for continued research and development
in such field.
