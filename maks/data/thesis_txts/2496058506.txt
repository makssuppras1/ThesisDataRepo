Abstract
Using satellite radar interferometry (InSAR) it is possible to measure the deformation
of the Earth’s surface. One of the techniques for measuring deformation from space is
called Persistent Scatterer Interferometry (PSI). In this thesis I have implemented a PSI
algorithm using data from the Sentinel-1 satellites. The output of the algorithm is a map
of the linear surface deformation and a displacement time series for individual points on
the ground. The performance of the algorithm is assessed with three methods.
First, the algorithm is applied to a simulated scenario.
From the simulation the
accuracy is found for various noise levels both for a linear and non-linear simulated
displacement. For a ﬁve-year time, series of a linear deformation with phase noise of
σ0 = 0.1 the Root Mean Squared (RMS) residual is 0.53 mm/year.
The second validation method is based on an experiment performed by SDFE, The
Danish Agency for Data Supply and Eﬃciency. The experiment involved applying a
vertical displacement to two corner reﬂectors. It was then tested if the displacement
was visible in the satellite data. The algorithm implemented showed the correct timing
and magnitude of displacement that was applied to the corner reﬂectors. Displacement
accuracy had a residual RMS of ≈0.6 mm compared to the known displacement.
In the last chapter, I applied the algorithm to the town Thyborøn, Denmark. Thy-
borøn is known to be sinking based on historic height measurements. Calculating the
residuals between the PSI results and the height measurements gives an RMS of 1.5
mm/year and a high correlation (0.83). Similar results are obtained when comparing
against SqueeSAR R
⃝, a commercial PSI algorithm.
Additionally, I have investigated the accuracy of the deformation time series. Although
they are somewhat unreliable, they agree well with SqueeSAR R
⃝in some cases.
This report has found that the implemented Persistent Scatterer method can map the
displacement of the Earth surface with a precision of a couple of mm.

Report Introduction
1.1 Measuring Earth Surface Displacements
Earth’s surface is constantly moving. The ground can move for natural reasons depend-
ing on the ground geology or it can be human caused, due to for example excess water
pumping. Sinking, or subsidence, is an increasing problem for coastal cites. A world
bank report from 2013 predicts that by 2050 subsidence and sea level rise combined will
cost the major coastal cities $1trn a year[1].
The current most accurate method for measuring surface displacement is called lev-
eling. Although, leveling is accurate, it is a highly manual process with only one point
being measured at a time. Leveling reference points are therefore rarely updated and
sparsely distributed.
Developments within the ﬁeld of radar interferometry allow satellites orbiting at al-
titudes of 600-800 km to measure the displacement of the Earth’s surface with mm
precision. In contrast to leveling, space-based measurements are frequently repeated
and cover large areas.
One space-based technique for measuring the deformation, and the topic of this re-
port, is known as Persistent Scatterer Interferometry (PSI). The PSI technique is based
on InSAR which exploits diﬀerences between two radar images acquired from diﬀerent
vantage points. With PSI, the displacement history of individual points such as build-
ing corners and railway tracks can be determined. Ferretti et al. [5, 6] formulated the
original PSI method resulting in the spin-oﬀcompany TRE and the patented algorithm
SqueeSAR R
⃝.
In my project, I have implemented a PSI algorithm inspired by the original formulation
by Ferretti et al. [5, 6].

simulated displacement.

Radar Interferometry Theory
This chapter describes the theory behind how it is possible to measure mm displace-
ments from space. The chapter starts with a brief description of the basics of Synthetic
Aperture Radar (SAR). Interferometric SAR (InSAR) is then described followed by an
introduction to Persistent Scatterer Interferometry (PSI).
2.1 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)
Synthetic Aperture Imaging Radars (SAR) capture images of the earth to use for remote
sensing. SAR images are unlike a camera, in that the SAR sends out a signal and waits
for the signal to return. A SAR measures the time it takes for the signal to travel from
the satellite (600-800 km height) down to the ground and back up again. Assuming the
speed of light the time delay is converted to a distance.

2.3 Persistent Scatterer Interferometry
2.2.4 Atmospheric Phase
The atmospheric delay φatm is due to the radar signal not traveling through a vacuum.
φatm is in the order of mm-cm and is spatially correlated [18]. In Section 2.3.3 we will
see how neighboring pixels are used to mitigate the atmospheric delay.
2.2.5 Scattering Phase and Noise
The scattering phase φsct is a type of noise that results from changes between the two
acquisitions. If the point P shows a stable phase between the two acquisitions, φsct = 0
and P is said to be coherent. When φsct is high P is said to be decorrelated.
In vegetated areas there is a high risk of signal decorrelation because the scattering
phase changes with the seasons.
Other sources of decorrelation are from changes in
viewing geometry (i.e. large changes in −→
B) and thermal processor noise.
Coherent points are better for resolving the additional phase terms [18]. If φsct be-
comes too dominant it is diﬃcult to separate the diﬀerent phase contribution terms.
The degree of (de)correlation is expressed with |γ| ∈[0; 1] where one is highly corre-
lated.
In Persistent Scatter Interferometry we choose points speciﬁcally to have low scattering
phase.
2.2.6 Phase Unwrapping
The phase values measured in the interferogram are restricted to the numeric range
[−π : π]. InSAR only measures the location on a circle but not how many times, k, we
have wrapped around the circle. In Equation 2.10, the 2πk is called the phase ambiguity,
where k is the integer number phase cycles. One of the main obstacles in InSAR is to
resolve the phase ambiguity or unwrap the phase
Figure 2.2 shows that when the actual phase received by the radar is larger than π it
is adjusted by a multiple of 2π to be conﬁned to the interval −π and π.
2.3 Persistent Scatterer Interferometry
Diﬀerential InSAR uses two SAR interferograms to map the deformation from one im-
age to the next. Multi-temporal InSAR, on the other hand, uses several diﬀerential
interferograms to map changes in deformation over time (φdefo(ti)). Persistent Scatterer
Interferometry (PSI) is a variation of multi-temporal InSAR.
The ﬁrst formulation of PSI was by Ferretti et al. [5, 6] who spun-oﬀthe company TRE
from Politecnico di Milano [3]. The original formulation was followed by the development
of the Small BAseline Subset (SBAS) technique by Berardino et al. [2]. The SBAS is a
widely used approach for deformation modeling but will not be addressed in this thesis.
Many variations of the original PSI technique exist today such as the Spatio-Temporal
Unwrapping Network (STUN) method developed by Kampes [12] and the open source

2.3.2 Deformation Model
The goal of PSI is to estimate the deformation time series, φdefo(x, ti). To estimate the
deformation the additional terms in 2.10 must be eliminated. PS pixels/targets (x) are
selected to have high coherence/stable phase so, φsc = 0. The topographic and ﬂat earth
phases are removed using a reference ellipse and a DEM. The remaining phase terms
are:
φ(x, ti) = φdefo(x, ti) + φDEMerror(x, ti) + φAtm(x, ti) + φnoise(x, ti) + 2πk
(2.22)
Where (x, ti) shows that the phase is for a speciﬁc target with coordinate, x, and is a
function of the time, ti. The DEM Error phase, φDEMerror(x, ti) is due to inaccuracies
in the DEM model used to eliminate φtopo. Like in Equation 2.15 the DEM error can be
written as a linear function of the baseline
φDEMerror(x, ti) = Cq · B⊥,i
(2.23)
Where Cq is proportional to the residual DEM error, ∆h. Inserting Equation 2.19 and

φ(x, ti) = CqB⊥,i + Cvti + wi + 2πk
(2.24)

the phase we use phase arcs between neighboring pixels [5].

2.3.3 Phase Arcs
The phase diﬀerence between the two neighboring PSs r and s is deﬁned as the phase

∆φ(xr, xs, ti) = φ(xr, ti) −φ(xs, ti)
(2.25)
Where xr and xs are the image coordinates of the PSs. Because the phase ambiguity

∆φi = ∆CqB⊥,i + ∆Cvti + ∆wi
(2.26)

Ferretti et al. [5] |∆wi| < π while Kampes [12] states that a more reasonable criteria is
σ∆w ≤0.6. In general it is assumed that the criteria is met, since [12]:
• The linear displacement is modeled and the residual non-linear motion is small
• The atmospheric residual is small because we are taking the diﬀerence between
neighboring pixels.
• The PSs are selected on the basis that they have low noise

3.1.3 Co-registration and Interferogram Formation
Co-registering involves aligning the pixels in two images taken from diﬀerent vantage
points. For my PSI Pre-processing I co-register and form the interferograms using the
remote sensing software called GAMMA. GAMMA takes a DEM and orbital model as
an input.
Digital Elevation Model (DEM)
The DEM model is used for correcting for topographic variation in the interferogram
(Equation 2.10). The DEM model used in this case is the SRTM (v. 4.1) model. The
spatial resolution of the SRTM data is 30 by 30 meters and the vertical resolution is 1
meter[11]. A high accuracy DEM is not essential for PSI. Although, an accurate DEM

Orbital Model
In combination with the DEM, an orbital model is used to correct for the topography.
The orbital ﬁles used are the Non-Time Critical Precise Orbit Ephemerides available
from Copernicus Precise Orbit Determination Service. The orbit trajectories have an
accuracy of 5 cm [4].
3.2 Persistent Scatterer Selection
3.2.1 Initial PS selection
In contrast to traditional leveling, we cannot decide what points to measure the height
of with PSI. Instead, a pixel in a SAR image needs to fulﬁll a certain criterion to qualify
as a Persistent Scatterer (PS) i.e. a target where we can measure the height.
The criteria for initially selecting the PSs is by setting a threshold for the Dispersion
index, DA. The Dispersion index, DA is a proxy for the phase noise and is deﬁned in
Section 2.3.1.
Before calculating DA, the SAR images need to be radiometrically calibrated. A basic
radiometric calibration is implemented by subtracting the mean amplitude value of all
pixels from each image. DA is then calculated for each pixel from the stack of images.
The pixels below the threshold are accepted as PSs.
The original PS method by Ferretti et al. [6] uses a threshold of DA < 0.25 while more
recent methods such as Hooper et al. [10] uses DA < 0.4. A higher threshold results in
more PSs being selected. Too high a threshold will give a poor result due to high noise.

3.3 Networks
3.3 Networks
To calculate the phase arcs requires forming a network connecting all the PSs. The PSs
are the nodes; between each node there is an edge. The phase arc between one PS, r,
and another PS, s, is deﬁne as the edge (r-s).
In this report I have implemented two networks: Delaunay Triangulation and a Star
Network.
A Dealunay triangulation results in a network that is ’well shaped’ [14]. The shape is
due to the Delaunay algorithm favoring large internal angles compared to sharp angles.
Figure 3.5a shows an example of a Delaunay triangulation with 500 randomly distributed
PSs. The MATLAB function delaunayTriangulation() is used to form the network.
The Star network forms a similar network to Delaunay but with diﬀerent criteria. In
the Star Network, each PS is connected to the 5 nearest PSs. Figure 3.5b shows an
example for three selected PSs (squares). The network connects the squares to its 5
nearest points (circles). Note, in the implementation the circles have a similar network
where they also are connected to their 5 nearest points.
The Star network ensures that the distance between phase arcs is short. In contrast,
with the Delaunay Network there is no guarantee that the phase arcs will be short.
With shorter phase arcs it is more likely that the criteria ∆wi < π is met, meaning that
unwrapping errors are less likely.

6.3.5 Potential Improvements
Simulation 2 showed that jumps arise when the noise is increased. I speculate that the
jumps are likely due to unwrapping errors during the spatial integration.
In the STaMPS PSI algorithm, developed by Hooper et al. [10], the arcs with a high
residual error are discarded iteratively for each spatial inversion. An iterative approach
where poorly ﬁtted phase arcs are discarded may result in a less jumpy time series.

Conclusion and Future Work
7.1 Conclusion
In this thesis, I have implemented a Persistent Scatterer Algorithm for calculating earth
surface displacements. The algorithm is based on the original Persistent Scatterer inter-
ferometry (PSI) technique ﬁrst formulated in Ferretti et al. [5, 6].
The algorithm, as described in Chapter 3, takes a stack of Sentinel-1 interferograms
as input. The output is a map showing the average linear deformation velocity of points
called Persistent Scatterers. For each Persistent Scatterer (PS) there is a corresponding
time series of the deformation history. The PSs are selected as points with low phase
noise to better resolve the nuisance terms in the interferometric phase (Equation 2.10).
I have implemented two types of networks; a star network and a Delaunay network.
The networks are used for calculating diﬀerences between the phases of neighboring PSs
i.e. phase arcs. Using the phase arcs, the implemented solution ﬁrst uses a search of the
parameter space, followed by a network integration to ﬁnd the preliminary velocity and
DEM Error. After the preliminary estimation step, a spatial inversion is performed to
calculate the deformation time series.
Chapters 4-6 apply the algorithm, discuss choices and assess its performance.
In
Chapter 4, the algorithm is tested under controlled conditions using a simulation. A
radar image is simulated with a known deformation at various noise levels. The results
showed that at low noise levels a linear deformation was estimated with an accuracy
of half a mm/year. For a non-linear time series, the deformation was predicted with a
residual root mean squared error (RMS) of less than one mm.
Following the simulation, I used data from Sentinel-1 to measure the deformation
of two corner reﬂectors (Chapter 5). In 2016 SDFE performed an experiment where
they applied manual deformations to two corner reﬂectors and used precision leveling
to determine the height changes. Selecting the corner reﬂectors as PSs, the deformation
time series is found and compared to the leveling. The time series found using PSI, gives
the correct timing and magnitude of deformation with sub-mm accuracy. Additionally,

the Persistent Scatterer (PS) selection procedure is validated.
Chapter 6 applies the PSI algorithm to the town Thyborøn, Denmark. From historic

In conclusion, the implemented algorithm measures earth surface displacement accu-
rately to within a couple of mm.
7.2 Future Work
Although, the linear velocity estimates are highly accurate the displacement time series
contains large deviations.
From the simulation I found that the deviations arise at high phase noise levels.
I implemented two networks of phase arcs in this thesis, where the Star network
performed slightly better than the Delaunay network. The diﬀerence in performance
between the two networks indicates that large deviations can be reduced by changing
the network or the phase arcs.
To better understand the problem, the noise between phase arcs should be investigated
further. One solution, implemented in the STaMPS algorithm by Hooper et al. [10], is
iteratively removing poorly ﬁtted phase arcs during the spatial inversion.
