Abstract
Nowadays, clinicians use a lot of their time to annotate therapeutic sessions and come up
with a diagnosis. This is a tedious process as the videos can be more than two hours long.
This thesis is a collaboration with Gentofte Hospital and aims to reduce the amount of time
the clinicians have to spend on the videos and help them come to a faster conclusion
regarding the diagnosis of OCD child patients.
The project will focus on understanding the relationship between patient and therapist
and how this might differ between the OCD and control groups. In order to do so, some
key features in defining the dyadic interaction were measured: the nonverbal head/body
synchrony, and the childвҖҷs gaze (such as how often the patientвҖҷs gaze rests on the theraВӯ
pist). Some other independent features such as the patientвҖҷs head/hand movements and
emotions were also extracted.
The clinical data is confidential, so the feature extraction pipeline was built on a mockВӯup
dataset. Therefore, the project uses a methodology applied to the mockВӯup data, followed
by a methodology applied to the clinical data. Moreover, an analysis was performed on the
final OCD dataset, consisting of seven control videos and 10 OCD videos. The purpose
of the analysis was to see if there is any difference in head/body synchrony between the
two groups, as well as what features contribute to this synchrony.
The conclusion was that there is a synchrony difference when it comes to the two groups,
but as the dataset is quite small there was no clear cluster differentiation noted. A clasВӯ
sification of the two classes was performed using the extracted features, followed by a
chapter of conclusions where future work possibilities were explored.

4.1.2
Therapeutic Alliance
Therapeutic alliance (or working alliance) [32] is the description of the interaction between
the therapist and patient. The purpose of this concept is to improve patient outcomes by
focusing on the patientвҖҷs own efforts to heal, aided by the therapist.
A strong therapeutic alliance is characterized by the patientвҖҷs willingness for selfВӯimprovement
supported by the therapist. Empathy is an important skill for both parties to have and is
required to build rapport.
Rapport can be built by matching body language, matching emotions, matching commuВӯ
nication energy levels, sharing common interests and thoughts, and so forth. This is part
of the reason why as more time a dyad spends with one another, their rapport grows, and
so does their Therapeutic Alliance.
The three key components of the therapeutic alliance are described as:
1. Agreement on goals (Collaborative Goal Setting)
2. Agreement on interventions (Shared Decision Making)
3. Effective bond between patient and therapist (The Therapeutic Relationship)
The third key component has an element of nonverbal communication and nonverbal
synchrony embedded into it. This is because the effective bond of the dyad impacts the
psychological affect (apparent emotions), which can be displayed in the form of movement
[33][34].
4.1.3
Nonverbal Synchrony
Nonverbal synchrony [29] is an effect that occurs between people communicating with
each other when nonverbal methods of communication start to synchronize. As an exВӯ
ample between two individuals, the movement of each other starts to become more corВӯ
related the more they interact. Movements such as head movement, gesticulation, gaze,
and facial affect are coordinated with each other.

4.1.4
Gaze
Gaze and eye contact are an important part of nonverbal communication between people
[35]. Some of the nonverbal cues that gaze communicates include displaying affection,
distaste, confidence, curiosity, etc. In conversation, individuals would look at each other
between 30Вӯ60% of the time. Effective rapport is estimated to be present at roughly 60Вӯ
70% [36]. Understanding the amount of gaze of a person towards another serves as a
useful feature to collect.

4.2
Feature Extraction Theory
4.2.1
Feature Extraction Intro
By measuring body movement, a type of nonverbal synchrony can be determined. TypiВӯ
cally when people are communicating, body language is apparent in most circumstances.
Examples like gestures, gaze, and posture emerge during communication. Nonverbal
synchrony happens when one individual responds to another individualвҖҷs body language
by either through mimicry or through some other body movement. The type of body moveВӯ
ment does not necessarily matter, but more so the timing of the responding body moveВӯ
ment, if it happens at all. Neurotypical dyadic interactions tend to display more nonverbal
synchrony, and familiarity between participants also increases the frequency of nonverbal
synchrony [29].
Two methods were used to detect body movement: MediaPipe [25] and Motion Energy
Analysis (MEA) [26].
4.2.2
Motion Energy Analysis (MEA)
While it was understood that the therapeutic relationship between the patient and theraВӯ
pist was a defining characteristic in patient outcomes [42], the methods to achieve a good
therapeutic relationship/alliance were less cut and dry. Nonverbal synchrony was also
understood as both an indicator and method to develop therapeutic alliance [43]. HowВӯ
ever, nonverbal synchrony was just a concept in psychology. There was no way for it to
be measured in a meaningful and quantifiable method. This made it difficult to empirically
compare the nonverbal relationship between one dyad to another, or even the same dyad
but during different sessions.
To break it down, the two main parts of nonverbal synchrony are the dynamic aspect (or
movement), and the temporal aspect which involves how simultaneous or timeВӯlagged
the dynamic aspect is. Motion Energy Analysis (MEA) [26] was developed to quantify the
level of nonverbal synchrony between a set of patientВӯtherapist interactions. MEA relies
on tracking the dynamic aspect of the dyad by the amount of pixel change from one frame

Random Forest
It is an extension of the decision tree classifierНҫ the Random Forest (RF) [71] uses ranВӯ

Randomness is applied to the selected parts within a dataset and the choice of features
to train on, also known as bootstrapping. The number of features used for training is
typically the square root of the total number of features. The bootstrapped dataset could
have repetitions of the same datapoint within a dataset. This is then performed several
times on the dataset and creates a set of decision trees. These were then applied to a
testing dataset that was partitioned from an original dataset. Typically, each tree is trained
on twoВӯthirds of an original dataset.

5
Preprocessing and Feature Extraction
Pipeline
The clinical data videos have very large file sizes. Because of the large file sizes, this
can slow down the computationally heavy feature extraction. To add to this, the different
feature extraction systems perform at different speeds, so it becomes beneficial to apply
some kind of preprocessing on these videos to improve extraction speed. The FFmpeg
program [89] was used to serve as the video editing software to help with preprocessing.
To illustrate the time taken for feature extraction, based on this thesis, Motion Energy
Analysis (MEA) [90] would be able to complete a 30Вӯminute, 25 FPS video (45000 frames)
in roughly 10 minutes. MEA runs at about 75 FPS. In contrast, FER [67] runs at roughly 1
FPS, almost two orders of magnitude slower than MEA. It would take over half a day for
FER to perform on the same video MEA had done in 10 minutes. This is also assuming
that the rate of FER stays constant, which has been observed to not be the case, as the
rate slowly lowers over time for an uninterrupted Spyder [91] or Jupyter Notebook [92]
kernel. Due to the speed of how quickly MEA can process frames, MEA can run on raw
videos.
For these systems to complete their processes faster, giving them less work to do is
the most effective solution.
Resolution downscaling and downsampling were the two
main ideas that were considered. Resolution downscaling was considered too risky to
be used, as it could affect the systems that relied on image recognition (FER, OpenFace
[59], MediaPipe [25]) so downsampling was used instead. Macroexpressions, which are
the вҖңnormalвҖқ facial expressions, typically last between 0.5 вҖ“ 4 seconds [93]. This means
the downsampling must not be so aggressive that the sampling rate goes below 2 Hz (2
FPS) so that it would be capable of catching the shortest macroexpressions. The mockВӯ
up videos were all recorded at 30 FPS, so a downsampling of 1/10, or a decimation was
used to reach 3 FPS.
A cropping operation is then performed on each downsampled video twice. One crop
for the patient, and the other for the therapist. This is so that the feature extraction is
performed on a single participant at a time. It was ensured that the cropping operation
did not crop out frames where the therapist or patient stuck their hands out of frame. With
these downsampled and cropped videos, MediaPipe and OpenFace feature extraction
can take place.
The summarized preprocessing and feature extraction pipeline is as follows:
1. Run MEA on raw video.
2. Reduce framerate to no fewer than 2 FPS.
3. Crop out the child and therapist, with two separate videos for each.
4. Run OpenFace and MediaPipe on the separated videos.
5. Split the segmented videos into frame images.
6. Sharpen frames using Python code.
7. Split the frame images into separated folders of at most 1000 frames each.
Intelligent Computer Vision Systems Applied in Psychiatry
31
==PAGE:46==
8. Run FER on the folders of 1000 frames. Restart the Spyder/Jupyter Notebook kernel
after every folder.
MediaPipe [25] was run on a Python [70] code using Spyder [91]. Python is an openВӯ
source programming language, and Spyder is an openВӯsource environment written in
Python. MediaPipeвҖҷs landmark locations were recorded for each frame along the xВӯaxis
and yВӯaxis and are then saved in a .txt file. OpenFace [59] records many different action
units on the face, but eye gaze angle is the one of particular interest, especially when the
patient makes eye contact with the therapist.

8.1
Discussion
As mentioned before the motivation of this thesis is to offer support in faster addressing
the mental health issues of children. Analyzing the OCD dataset was not an easy job, as
there was no baseline reference. It was difficult to predict the outcome of the analysis, as
visually looking at the videos, there was no obvious delimiter between the OCD/control
groups.
This work aimed to answer the following questions:
1) How can computer vision systems be used to identify neurodivergent sympВӯ
toms?
This work shows a promising start, but of course, it would be beneficial to scale it up as this
study uses a limited number of samples. Using extracted features from the clinical data
like: вҖқOpenface_gaze_childвҖқ, вҖқFer_emotions_childвҖқ, вҖқhand_left(right)_x(y)_child(adult)вҖқ,
вҖқhead_x(y)_child(adult)вҖқ, вҖқTotal_video_red_body(head)вҖқ referring to the total synchrony of
the head/body related to the patientВӯtherapist interaction, a classification with an accuracy
of 76% between the OCD/control classes was attained. Better feature extraction might
lead to an improvement in the accuracy of the classification, as well as a multitude of new
features, maybe related to the therapist, like the therapistвҖҷs emotions, but in that case, a
better setup of the camera would be needed, in such way to better capture the therapistвҖҷs
face.
2) Are there visual (body languageВӯwise) differences between the two particular
groups, precisely OCD/control ones?
As mentioned before, visually it is hard to tell if there is an obvious difference between
the two groups. The OCD group seems to have a tendency of moving more. However,
that difference is not big enough to see a clear tendency of clustering between the two
groups when applying PCA. However, by applying advanced methods of visualization like
UMAP, one can see that the control data has a predisposition of being more centered and
that the OCD group tends to be placed on the outer parts of the control one. Moreover,
applying this analysis to a more robust dataset will provide detailed information on the
data distribution tendency.
3) Is there any significant difference between the synchrony percentage of the
OCD/control group?
As demonstrated by the boxplots, when it comes to the synchrony percentage between
patient and therapist, the OCD group has a higher mean and wider distribution compared
to the control group, in both head and body synchrony. Moreover, when talking about
head synchrony, the difference is way notable, meaning that the OCD group has a higher
tendency to synchronize with the therapist. This was unexpected, as the original hypotheВӯ
sis was that OCD patients would have less synchrony. Moreover, the Wilcoxon statistical
test was performed, which confirmed that there is a median difference between the synВӯ
chrony percentage of the two groups.
4) Which are the features that mostly explain synchrony? Are they related to headВӯ
/body movement?
Intelligent Computer Vision Systems Applied in Psychiatry
67
==PAGE:82==
The feature extraction was done independently of the synchrony percentage. Then RF
regression and SHAP were used for a better understanding of the synchrony. It is known
that the synchrony percentage is determined by correlated head/body movements, but
which exactly are the features that have more weight on the outcome? As the results
from Figure 7.17 show the most important feature related to head synchrony seems to be
вҖқFer_emotions_childвҖқ. Being a headВӯrelated feature it makes sense to have a high impact
on the head synchrony outcome. However, the SHAP model had as a second importance
feature the вҖқhand_right_y_childвҖқ which seems inconsistent, compared to the RFвҖҷs second
feature, namely head_y_adult. When talking about body synchrony, it seems like hand
movement has an important weight for both models. However, RF is again more reliable
than SHAP, by having the second important feature вҖқhand_right_y_childвҖқ as opposed to
вҖқhead_y_adultвҖқ. Overall, RF results seem more plausible, but SHAP can be used with a
lot more models, therefore similar results might be achieved.
8.2
Constraints and Future Work
Because of patient confidentiality, there were heavy restrictions on how the interview
videos and the data extracted from them were handled.
Associated staff status was
needed, and a confidentiality agreement was signed. Keycard access was required to
enter the premises, and an official hospital computer account was required as well. A
few programs that were used on the mockВӯup data on personal computers, MATLAB and
FFmpeg, could not be used without administrative permission. This came as a surprise.
The estimated time to expect a fix was about working ten days, which was considered
too long, and other alternatives were used instead. It took roughly 100 hours to complete
every attempted feature extraction of the original 21 videos of the clinical data. The overВӯ
all time at the hospital took 19 days, from first gaining access to the clinical data to the
final extracted feature. The videos themselves were forbidden to leave hospital hardware,
and only the interpreted data was allowed to leave to ensure confidentiality compliance.
The original pipeline for the feature extraction was made to accommodate this situation.
Because FFmpeg had been restricted on the hospital computer, Python and the OpenCV
library [98] were used to perform the preprocessing steps instead. Future work would and
ought to have much more time allocated to deal with any slowВӯmoving bureaucracies.
As stated previously, the formats of the video interviews changed from video to video.
Within these videos have differing obstructions either due to obtuse angles, hair covering
the faces, tables, or face masks. Because of obtuse angles, having a participant look
away from the camera would completely occlude the face. Puffer jackets with homogeВӯ
neous colors make pose estimation difficult. All these contribute to the reduction of the
quality of the feature extraction.
In future work, it would be beneficial to have a more ideal format. An ideal video format
would be to have the patient and therapist equidistant to each other and the camera so
that they would take up the same amount of space in the video. The whole upper torso
would be unobstructed, including the face especially if the participants have long hair. The
participants ought to be seated roughly 45 degrees offset from each other so that both
hands are unobstructed, or to have a type of fishВӯeye lens that can capture the frontal view
of both patient and therapist facing each other. Having it this way would also reduce the
need for adjusting hairstyles that would block the sides of the face since the camera would
be in front of the dyad simultaneously. There could be a recommendation for participants
to not wear puffer jackets if possible.
Another constraining issue was the simple lack of data. There were fewer than 20 samples
which would not encompass even 1% of all the children in Denmark who have received
68
Intelligent Computer Vision Systems Applied in Psychiatry
==PAGE:83==
a diagnosis for OCD in 20 years [100].
In future work, it would be a tremendous undertaking if much larger datasets are used
when dealing with this much patient confidentiality. As a hypothetical example, one of the
participants simply decided to pull out of the study. This would force the analysis of the
thesis to be redone, which would take more time and effort. In fact, a similar scenario
did happen during the course of the thesis, but on the basis of a control patient being
reclassified as another nonВӯOCD classification instead. With this number of moving parts,
it would probably take considerably more effort than what the scope of this thesis allows.
Other possibilities for future work could also include extracting other features. Completely
new features such as audio, to analyze for any audio synchrony between a dyad. Other
features such as gaze could also have more interesting gaze angles extracted, such as
more specific parts of the frame like the therapistвҖҷs hands. An example could be that if
the therapist starts gesticulating, the patient could focus on the therapistвҖҷs hands, hold
eye contact, or look at something else entirely. This would be a more difficult feature
to track due to the angle of the camera, and the moving hands that traverse the frame.
This would require heavier use of pose estimation. Any вҖқlowВӯlevelвҖқ synchrony in the MEA
data was ignored in this thesis as there was a focus on strong displays of synchrony, and
the lowВӯlevel synchrony could simply be noise. However, this could be a feature worth
studying. There indeed were stark differences between the OCD and the control patients
in this area, and warrants further study in future work.
FER was very slow to use despite having a powerful desktop computer. However, it
is unsure if a more powerful computer would help speed, as the specifications for the
hospital computer are already a very powerful one, but still only managed roughly one
frame processed per second. It would probably be more beneficial to have several weaker
computers performing FER extraction, but unfortunately, the hospital laptops are much too
weak to perform and tedious to handle the task. For future work, would be beneficial to
use other emotion extraction software that has the realВӯtime capability. Action units from
OpenFace [59] have the possibility to have emotions derived from them.
A method that could be used in future works is to utilize imputation methods on missing
data. As there were times when features become obstructed, it could serve as a useful
tool to ensure a more consistent feature extraction.
