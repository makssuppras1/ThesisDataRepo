Abstract
Obesity is the main cause of the pathological condition, metabolic syndrome (MetS).
MetS increases the risk of non-communicable diseases, like type 2 diabetes (T2D) and
cardiovascular diseases (CVD), and with a rise in the number of obese individuals,
prevention and treatment of this is needed.
Understanding obesity and MetS is
crucial for effective treatments, thus, animal models have been pursued.
In this
project, diet-induced obesity experiments have been performed on Ossabaw minipigs.
The aim of these experiments were to mimic the MetS seen in humans and further
elucidate the inflammatory profile seen under obesity. Inflammatory markers, as well
as, blood parameters were investigated to determine if the obesity state and MetS were
obtained. Furthermore, experiments with differenct lenghts and diets were performed.
The inflammatory marker α1-acid glycoprotein (AGP) showed no significant change
to a short-term diet-induced obesity, indicating that this protein might not be a
good marker for inflammation.
However, C-reactive protein (CRP) was found to
significantly decrease while haptoglobin (Hp) showed a significant increase to this
diet-induced obesity. Lean pigs of this experiment were found to have an increased
AGP concentration, suggesting that the proteins might be effected by other factors
like age. Regarding experiments performed for at longer period of time, no significant
change were seen in the inflammatory markers. However, results did suggest a trend
towards an elevated AGP and Hp concentration for the diet-induced obese pigs.

Resumé
Fedme er hovedårsagen til den patologiske tilstand, metabolsk syndrome (MetS).
MetS øger riskoen for sygedomme, der ikke er smitsommen, som type 2 diabetes
(T2D) og cardiovaskulære sygedomme (CVD). Med en stigning i antallet af individer
med fedme, er der brug for forbyggelse og behandling af dette. At forstå fedme og
MetS er afgørende for effektive behandlinger, og derfor er dyremodeller brugt.
I
dette projekt er der blevet udført diæt-induceret eksperimenter i Ossabaw minigrise.
Målet med disse eksperiment var at efterligne MetS set in mennesker og derudover
kigge på den inflammatoriske profil under fedme. Inflammatoriske markøre samt blod
parametre blev undersøgt for at se om fedme stadiet samt MetS var opnået. Desuden
var eksperimenterne forskellige længde med forskellige diæter. Den inflammatoriske
markør orosomukoid viste ingen signifikant ændring efter en kortsigtet diæt-induced
fedme, hvilket indikerede at dette protein måske ikke var en god markør for betæn-
delse. Derimod var C-reaktivt protein (CRP) signifikant aftaget, mens haptoglobin
(Hp) var forøget efter denne diæt-induceret fedme. De slanke grise fra dette eksper-
iment have en forøget AGP koncentration, hvilket viser at disse proteiner måske er
påvirket af andre faktorer som alder. I forhold til eksperimenterne der blev udført
i en længere periode.
Here blev der ikke set nogen ændring i de inflammatoriske
markøre. Derimod viste resultaterne en tendens mod en forøget orosomukoid og Hp
koncentration for de diæt-inducerede fede grise.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The global obesity prevalence has almost tripled in adults over the past 40 years [1, 2].
In an environment where there is easy availability of inexpensive and energy-dense
foods, along with a reduced need for physical activity, obesity has the ideal parameters
to emerge [1]. The World Health Organisation (WHO) defines obesity as ”abnormal
or excessive fat accumulation that presents a risk to health” [3]. Furthermore, WHO
has declared obesity as a chronic relapsing progressive disease [3]. Obesity can lead
to non-communicable diseases like type 2 diabetes (T2D) and cardiovascular diseases
(CVD) [3, 4]. These non-communicable diseases are, by causing around two-thirds
of the global deaths, the leading cause of global death [5]. Moreover, obesity plays a
major part in the pathological condition termed metabolic syndrome (MetS). MetS is
defined as a cluster of metabolic abnormalities that, besides obesity, include insulin
resistance, hypertension and dyslipidemia, which are all risk factors that can increase
the development of T2D and CVD [6].
To study MetS and obesity, rodent models have been used, as they are inexpen-
sive to maintain and easily modified through genetic engineering.
However, clear
metabolic and physiological differences are seen between rodents and humans, thus,
complicating the translation to humans [7]. Accordingly, the pig has been pursued
as an attractive biomedical model for obesity in humans. The pig has metabolic fea-
tures and a cardiovascular system which are similar to humans [7]. In this project,
the Ossabaw minipig will be the main focus.
The mechanism by which adipose tissue expands is one determinant of metabolic
health.
It is thought that some individuals have a bigger adipose tissue storage
and still remain metabolically healthy [8].
Thus, investigation of this phenotype
might help in obtaining a better understanding of obesity and its related metabolic
dysfunctions.
To treat obesity, many different invasive and non-invasive therapeutic strategies
exist. Surgical-based strategies are quite eﬀicient, however, there are post-operative
adverse effects which limit the access for many patients to these procedures [9]. The
recommended treatment for obesity is lifestyle modification, however, this typically
only results in modest weight loss that is often regained. Pharmacological treatments
can also be used for lifestyle interventions, but are only offering moderate weight
loss [10]. One such pharmacological treatment is semaglutide. This is a long-acting
glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) analogue that is quite potent and only requires ad-
ministration once every week [10]. The Semaglutide Treatment Effect in People with

1.1
Objectives and outline
The aim of this project is to phenotypically characterise diet-induced obesity in a
different range of experiments using Ossabaw pigs. Obesity is associated with a low-
grade systemic inflammation, which produces a number of immune factors in the
adipose tissue. From long-term low-grade systemic inflammation, a change in the
levels of inflammation markers like acute phase proteins (APP) can be observed [12].
Thus, it is hypothesised that the levels of the APP in the diet-induced Ossabaw pig
will change. In this project, the interplay between obesity and the immune system
will be investigated by analysing the response of acute phase proteins, both locally,
in subcutaneous adipose tissue (SAT) and visceral adipose tissue (VAT), and system-
ically, in serum samples, as well as, liver and heart tissue. Furthermore, the immune
factors produce active kinases that interfere with insulin signalling, leading to insulin
resistance, which is the cause of many metabolic diseases like T2D and CVD [13].
Thus, it is also hypothesised a dysregulated metabolic profile will be seen in the
diet-induced obese Osaabaw minipigs. This will be investigated with fasting insulin,
fasting glucose and weight measurements.
In human obesity, elevated levels of glucose, insulin, cholesterol, triglycerides and
glycated haemoglobin (HbA1c) will be found. The elevated levels of these biomarkers
lead to MetS [14–18]. It is hypothesised that these blood chemistry parameters will
also be elevated in the diet-induced obese Ossabaw minipigs, as they will develop
MetS. However, for pigs, the fructosamine levels will be investigated instead of HbA1c
[17]. Thus, the blood chemistry parameters cholesterol, triglycerides, fructosamine,
glucose and insulin along with low-density lipoprotein (LDL), high-density lipoprotein
(HDL) and three liver enzymes will be investigated to identify if MetS has been
induced in the obese Ossabaw pigs.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Obesity pathogenesis
One of the evolutionary physiologies in humans is the ability to conserve body fat as a
survival mechanism. Thus, it is seen that a large segment of humanity is biologically
predisposed to excessive weight gain [19]. All the energy that humans obtain comes
from food and drink. This energy is stored as high-energy molecules and is expended
during activity and basal metabolic functions. When energy consumption exceeds
energy expenditure, most of the excess energy is stored as fat.
When an excess
amount of body fat is obtained it is characterised as obesity [20].
When an individual develops obesity, the ”set point” of the body weight is reset
to a higher value [19]. This might also be the reason why many who lose weight
through dieting also regain most of their weight within 1-5 years [9, 19]. Studies have
shown that a regain of the pre-dieting weight reverses the metabolically beneficial
effects of weight loss. This goes to show that the memory of past obesity remains. In
a study performed in mice by Fischer et al. [9], it was found that a history of obesity
leaves an inflammatory imprint in the liver and perigonadal fat, which are two very
important tissues in the development of glucose intolerance and insulin resistance in
mice. In this study, six-week-old male mice were randomly split into groups given
either a high-fat diet or a control diet. These diets were given for 20 weeks. After 20
weeks, a group from the high-fat diet group was changed to a low-fat diet for 5 weeks.
A significant reduction in weight was seen within the first week, however, at the end
of the experiment the perigonadal adipose tissue inflammation along with a specific
inflammatory imprint in the liver were found in the formerly obese mice [9].
Adipose tissue secretes adipokines, which are molecules that regulate inflamma-
tion and metabolism. One of these molecules is leptin [21]. Leptin is very important
in the relationship between obesity and energy homeostasis. If there is a deficiency of
leptin it can cause severe hyperphagia and obesity in humans, as well as, in animals.
Thus, an intact leptin-regulated neurocircuit is a requirement to maintain a normal
body weight in humans. Conversely, elevated plasma leptin levels are seen in most
individuals with obesity, which might raise the possibility of obesity being associated
with leptin resistance [19]. Moreover, gut microbiota dysbiosis influences metabolic
health, host energy metabolism and immune functions, and thus is a contributing fac-
tor in the development of obesity. Additionally, increased amounts of branched-chain
amino acids, which have been associated with T2D, might be produced from obesity-

associated gut microbiota. Thus, understanding the interplay between the host and
the gut microbiota might help increase the understanding of obesity pathogenesis and
insulin resistance [22].
Many different factors are contributing to the development of obesity.
From
pathological overeating and physical inactivity to impaired brain circuits. Many ad-
vances in knowledge on the development of obesity have been obtained, however, the
understanding of the pathophysiology and etiology of obesity is still incomplete.
For adults, obesity is defined as a BMI of 30 or greater [1].
The BMI is an
easy way to define obesity, as it is a simple indicator that is defined as the mass in
kilograms divided by the square of the height in meters [23]. Even though BMI is
used to determine obesity, it has been found to be a poor indicator of the body fat
percentage [24]. In addition, the BMI index of an individual does not show where
the fat mass is placed, which is crucial information when it comes to MetS [24, 25].
Thus, better markers of obesity are needed.
2.2
Adiposity and inflammation
There are two kinds of adipose tissue. The white adipose tissue (WAT), which is
essential for storage of energy, and the brown adipose tissue (BAT), which through
mitochondrial respiration is responsible for thermogenesis. In WAT deposits, ”beige”
adipocytes are also found, and these have similar thermogenic properties to brown
adipocytes upon stimulation [26]. WAT was considered to be an inactive tissue with
the purpose of thermal insulation. However, with its ability to utilise glucose and its
ability to store and release energy-rich fatty acids, it was found to be very important
for energy homeostasis [27].
The adipose tissue morphology is also influenced by the anatomical location of the
fat depot. Subcutaneous WAT, also referred to as SAT, is found in more than 80% of
the total body fat, whereas only 10% and 20% of the total body fat is comprised of
visceral fat, also referred to as VAT, in women and men, respectively [27]. An excess
amount of SAT is suggested to lead to MetS [28]. However, VAT is more strongly
associated with metabolic risk [29]. It secretes adipokines in an unregulated manner
which leads to an inflammatory state and it releases fatty acids into the circulation.
The combination of these factors leads to pathophysiology of cardiometabolic diseases
[29]. VAT is associated with an impaired glucose metabolism [28]. This was seen in a
study performed by Storz et al. [28]. Here, 400 subjects who had no T2D diagnosis,
but had qualification to be either prediabetic or diabetic, were given an oral glucose
tolerance test. Furthermore, a control group were also given the oral glucose tolerance
test. From the study, it was found, that elevated VAT was associated with prediabetes
and diabetes [28].
The adipose tissue can increase its capacity of storing lipids by either enlarging
the fat cell volume, which is termed hypertrophic expansion, or by increasing fat cell
number, which is termed hyperplastic expansion. In the latter form of expansion,
new small-sized adipocytes are generated. Hyperplastic expansion is considered to

be a healthy form of adipose tissue expansion. On the contrary, when an extended
hypertrophic expansion is seen it has been found to lead to adipocyte dysfunction [30].
The adipose tissue expandability hypothesis posits that a failure in the adipose tissue
expansion capacity is the key factor linking positive energy balance and metabolic
dysfunction, rather than obesity [31]. Thus, one individual might have a larger ex-
pansion capacity than another.
However, when the capacity of the adipocytes is
exceeded, it leads to the enlargement of the cells and increased distance between
the cells and the surrounding blood vessels. This leads to hypoxia which facilitates
the infiltration of macrophages, by adipokines [30, 32]. Adipokines are cell-signalling
proteins, that play an important role in regulating metabolism, inflammation, cardio-
vascular function, immunity and cancer [21]. They are thought to be important links
between obesity and the immune system [33]. However, when an uncontrolled accu-
mulation of adipocytes occurs, it can lead to a dysregulated production of adipokines,
which can then facilitate infiltration of immune cells, like macrophages [31, 34]. These
macrophages are considered to have the M1 phenotype, that triggers a local inflam-
matory response by secreting pro-inflammatory mediators, like interleukin 6 (IL-6),
interleukin 1 beta (IL-1β) and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFα), as seen in Figure
2.1 [30–32]. This local inflammatory response quickly spreads systemically to become
a chronic low-grade inflammation [35].

The pro-inflammatory cytokines that are secreted activate many kinases that in-
terfere with the insulin receptor substrate (IRS), phosphoinositide-3-kinase (PI-3K)
and protein kinase B (PKB) causing insulin resistance [13]. Insulin resistance leads
to elevated plasma glucose levels, which in response leads to the insulin levels increas-
ing, also called hyperinsulinemia [36].
Furthermore, insulin resistance might also
have an impact on the metabolic process of uptake, transport and storage of lipids.
In post-prandial states, triglycerides are lipolysed into free fatty acids (FFA). The
FFA are taken up by enterocytes, where they are assembled into triglycerides again.
Along with the triglycerides, the enterocytes also take up cholesterol, which, together
with phospholipids and apolipoproteins are packed together into chylomicrons. The
chylomicrons, then enter the circulation to deliver FFA to the adipose tissue, for stor-
age and energy expenditure. In the liver, very low density lipoproteins (VLDL) are
synthesised. These are triglyceride-rich lipoproteins that increase when food-derived
triglycerides and FFA reaches the liver. These proteins also deliver FFA to the adi-
pose tissue for storage and energy expenditure [37]. The hydrolysis of chylomicrons
and VLDL into FFA is catalysed by lipoprotein lipase (LPL) [37, 38]. While the
hydrolysis of intracellular lipids is catalysed by hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL). Both
of these lipases are stimulated by insulin [19, 37]. The HSL is inhibited by insulin
and thus stops the hydrolysis of triglycerides in adipocytes. However, when there
is insulin resistance the HSL is not inhibited, which leads to hydrolysis of triglyc-
erides and release of FFA into the circulation. The FFA is taken up by the liver, and
VLDL is once again hydrolysed by LPL, releasing FFA that is taken up by the liver.
Continuous hydrolysis of VLDL forms LDL [37].
LDL can cross the arterial wall
and accumulate there. An accumulation of LDL in the arterial wall can induce an
inflammatory response and ultimately lead to atherosclerotic plaques forming inside
the arterial wall [14]. An elevated level of triglycerides and LDL along with decreased
level of HDL is also what is defined as dyslipidemia [37].
The systemic chronic low-grade inflammation also induces inflammation in the
blood vessels. When this happens, it leads to endothelial dysfunction and impaired
renal sodium handling, which are both characteristics of hypertension [39].
The combination of the metabolic dysfunctions mentioned, insulin resistance, dys-
lipidemia, hypertension, and obesity as the main source, can give rise to MetS [25].
The development of MetS increases the risk of CVD and T2D [40, 41]. Thus, identi-
fying and understanding every part of MetS is crucial for finding treatment, and thus
preventing the development of these diseases [40].

2.3.1
Metabolically healthy obesity
The relationship between obesity and T2D, also termed diabesity, is still not com-
pletely understood [43, 44]. It is known that more than 80% of people with T2D
also have obesity. However, it is also seen that 10-30% of people with obesity are
metabolically healthy and that around 30% of individuals with normal-weight ex-
perience metabolic abnormalities. Furthermore, mortality rates are observed to be
higher for normal-weight people with T2D than overweight or obese people with T2D
[43]. Not everyone develops obesity under obesity-promoting conditions [1]. This
difference can be caused by genetic variations or epigenetic events that can have an
influence on individual fat content [1, 45]. This is also why a more personalised ap-
proach to health should be embraced [46]. Most of the obesity-associated alleles also
have an association with poor metabolic health. However, an association between
BMI-increasing alleles and a more favourable metabolic profile has also been made
[45]. A subgroup of individuals with obesity has been described to have a lower risk
of getting obesity-related comorbidities [3]. Instead, they have favourable lipid and
inflammatory profiles along with high insulin sensitivity [47]. This subphenotype is
referred to as metabolically healthy obesity (MHO) [47, 48].
MHO and the concept of this might help better understand the mechanisms linking
obesity to cardiometabolic diseases. The concept was developed in the 1950s when
observations led to the finding of obese individuals with different predispositions to
diabetes and atherosclerosis. MHO has frequently been defined by the absence of
any metabolic disorder and CVD in a person with obesity. However, there is a large
variation from investigator to investigator. The uncertainties in defining MHO can
imply that MHO does not represent a well-defined biologically determined subgroup of
obesity. A more standardised MHO criterion is needed, thus a harmonised definition
of MHO has been proposed recently with a list of criteria. Besides a BMI greater than
30, these criteria include a fasted serum triglyceride level below 1.7 mmol/l, a fasted
blood glucose level below 6.1 mmol/l, a fasted HDL cholesterol serum concentration
above 1.0 mmol/L in men or 1.3 mmol/l in women, diastolic blood pressure below
85 mmHg, systolic blood pressure below 130 mmHg, no CVD manifestation and no
drug treatment for diabetes, hypertension and dyslipidemia [3].

2.4
Biomarkers
To detect MetS, cholesterol, lipoproteins, glucose, insulin, fructosamine, triglycerides
and liver enzymes will be used as biomarkers in this study. Furthermore, the main
focus will be on acute-phase proteins.
2.4.1
Triglycerides, cholesterol and lipoprotein
Triglycerides are the main dietary lipid and around 90% - 95% of the energy that is
produced by adipocytes is derived from triglycerides. An increased intake of triglyc-
erides can break the metabolic balance and lead to the accumulation of VLDL. Thus,
elevated triglycerides are often associated with obesity [18].
Cholesterol plays an important role in regulating the fluidity, permeability and
micro-structures in the cell membranes. If an excess amount of cholesterol is obtained,
membrane fluidity could decrease, membrane micro-domains could be disrupted, and
overall lead to cell dysfunction [14]. Obesity can lead to an increased level of rem-
nant cholesterol [37]. As mentioned in section 2.2, cholesterol is distributed to the
peripheral tissue with the help of VLDL, which is secreted from the liver. VLDL
and LDL differ in the percentage of cholesterol and triglycerides that make them
up. While VLDL contains more triglycerides, LDL contains more cholesterol [14]. In
addition, excess cholesterol from peripheral cells is transported by HDL to the liver
for excretion and thus promotes reverse cholesterol transport. HDL is also secreted
by the liver and is commonly decreased in human obesity [15].
2.4.2
Glucose, fructosamine and insulin
As mentioned in section 2.2, insulin resistance has a crucial impact on the MetS.
Insulin is secreted by pancreatic β-cells. The secretion of this hormone is based on
the availability of circulating blood glucose and fatty acids, as its metabolic function
is to maintain the homeostasis of lipids and glucose. When insulin resistance occurs,
the cells do not respond to insulin, which then leads to the blood glucose levels going
up. The β-cells will start to produce more insulin to compensate, however, due to
continuous overload, the β-cells become worn out and an increase in the blood glucose
levels is seen again. When the β-cells are completely worn out and dysfunctional it
can lead to T2D [16]. To assess insulin resistance, the homeostatic model assessment
(HOMA) can be used [51]. This model shows the relationship between fasting glucose
and fasting insulin dynamics [52]. The formula to calculate is as follows:
HOMA-IR = fasting plasma glucose [mmol] · fasting plasma insulin [mU/L]

2.4.3
Hepatic enzymes indicating hepatic dysfuntion
Alanine transaminase (ALTL) and aspartate transaminase (ASTL) are the gold stan-
dard biomarkers when it comes to the diagnosis of hepatocellular injury [55–57]. These
enzymes are in the cytoplasm of the hepatocytes and are released into the circulation
if the hepatocellular membrane is disrupted [55]. In addition, glutamate dehydroge-
nase (GLDH) is also a liver-specific biomarker for hepatocellular injury [55, 58]. This
enzyme is a mitochondrial enzyme and has been shown in studies to be similar to
ALTL or even outperform ALTL in terms of specificity and sensitivity [55].
2.4.4
Acute phase proteins
As a consequence of the low-grade systemic inflammation associated with obesity,
a number of immune factors are produced in the adipose tissues.
It is seen that
this serious and long-term complication is associated with changes in the levels of
inflammation markers like APP [12].
The acute-phase response (APR) is the systemic response to inflammation. Along
with the innate immune response, APR contributes to inflammation resolution and
the healing process; it also prevents infections and clears pathogens [59]. The APR
is regulated by cytokines like IL-6, IL-1 and TNFα, and is synthesised primarily by
the liver, as seen in Figure 2.3 [59, 60]. When APR is activated, which among others,
can happen by trauma, infections and inflammatory stimuli, it results in significant
changes in the circulating concentration of APP. Depending on whether their con-
centration increases or decreases, APP can be divided into positive or negative. If
the concentration in plasma increases, the APP are considered positive and if the
concentration decreases, the APP are considered negative. Furthermore, APP can
also be divided into major and minor APP. If the concentration of the APP has a
change of high magnitude it is considered major and if the APP has a change of low
magnitude it is considered minor. The serum or plasma levels of APP can be used as
an unspecific biomarker of disease’s severity, due to the changes in APP concentra-
tion as a result of a given stimulus. Different kinetics from the APP are seen after a
stimulus [59].
The APR in the pig is very similar to the one seen in humans. In humans, as
well as, in pigs, CRP, haptoglobin (Hp) and serum amyloid A (SAA) are major

positive APP, while albumin is a negative APP. One difference seen in the APR
between humans and pigs is with α1-acid glycoprotein (AGP), which is a positive
APP in humans, but in pigs has been reported to either increase, remain unchanged
or decrease depending on which stimulus is investigated [59]. The focus in this project
is on AGP, CRP and Hp.

The main function of AGP is to modulate the immune system, through drug
transport and modulation of cell proliferation and differentiation. It is defined as a
transport protein in plasma. The liver is the main contributor of AGP, but during
inflammation, AGP is also expressed in adipose tissue, endothelial cells, the nervous
system and immune cells. In many types of pathological conditions, a higher level
of AGP in plasma is seen. These pathological conditions include obesity, CVD and
some types of cancers. Human AGP is found in two main forms, the AGP1, which
is encoded by the Orosomucoid 1 (ORM1) gene, and the AGP2, which is encoded by
the ORM2 gene. The two forms have identical structures and have sequences that
only differ in 22 amino acids [61]. Furthermore, ORM1 gene has three variants, F1,
F2 and S. In pigs, AGP is only found in one isoform [12]. In a study performed
by Christoffersen et al. [59], 30 Göttingen minipigs (15 male and 15 female) were
divided into 4 age groups (6, 16, 24 and 40 to 48 weeks old) and fed a restricted
standard minipigs chow. Here, it was seen that the female pigs overall had a higher
AGP concentration than the males and that AGP concentration for both males and
females at 6 weeks and 16 weeks was higher than at 24 weeks and 40-48 weeks. The
concentration levels for all the pigs were between 222.8 µg/mL to 771.8 µg/mL, where
the lower concentrations were seen in the older pigs and the higher levels were seen
in the younger pigs [59]. Furthermore, in a study conducted in Ossabaw minipigs
by Rødgaard et al.
[12], an AGP increase in obese pigs compared to their lean
counterpart was observed [12]. However, in another study conducted by Heegaard et
al. [62], AGP was found to be a negative APP in response to aseptic inflammation.
This study was performed in Landrace pigs [62].
CRP is a marker related to T2D and CVD [63, 64]. It has been shown to be the
strongest factor associated with obesity in humans [65]. This marker belongs to the
highly conserved protein family, that has great effects on the regulation of the immune
system, by binding to foreign cells and thus enhancing phagocytosis by macrophages
[64, 65]. The human CRP is composed of five identical polypeptide units that are
==PAGE:34==
12

noncovalently arranged around a Ca-binding cavity. An increased plasma level of
CRP is associated with ischemic cerebrovascular disease and ischemic heart disease
[64]. In the same study performed by Christoffersen et al. [59], the 16-week-old female
pigs had significantly lower CRP concentrations than the other age groups, however,
the male pigs had no significant difference across age, and no significant difference
between genders and corresponding age was seen. The CRP concentration for both
females and males was between 2.27 µg/mL and 21.35 µg/mL [59]. In another study
performed by Ludvigsen et al. [66], castrated male Göttingen minipigs were divided
into three groups and fed either a standard chow, an atherogenic diet (HFD) or an
atherogenic diet with superimposed mild streptozotocin-induced diabetes (HFD-D).
One part of the total pigs was fed for 22 weeks (standard chow group and HFD), while
the other part was fed for 43 weeks (standard chow group, HFD group and HFD-D
group). Here, it was found that the groups fed HFD and HFD-D, for both 22 weeks
and 43 weeks, had higher levels of CRP than the standard chow group. The increase
observed was more than threefold [66].
Hp is a glycoprotein that is, as mentioned, secreted from the liver but also the
lungs [67, 68]. It is composed of 2 α and 2 β subunits [69]. Binding free haemoglobin
is one of the most important functions of Hp, as this prevents iron-utilising bacteria
from benefitting from hemolysis [64, 67]. Furthermore, Hp has been reported to also
have chemotactic activity, thus it can by interacting with the C-C chemokine receptor
2, attract monocytes [67]. For Hp, the study performed by Christoffersen et al. [59]
found a higher concentration in 6-week-old male pigs than the male pigs in the other
age groups. In addition, the female pigs from the 16-, 24- and 40-48 week-old groups
had higher Hp concentrations than for the corresponding male groups. As for the
concentration levels, both the female and male pigs had concentrations between 13
µg/mL and 8783.8 µg/mL, where the lower values were seen for the older male pigs,
and the higher values were seen for the younger male and female pigs [59]. In the
study, described in section 2.5, that was performed by Rødgaard et al. [70], the Hp
concentration was found to have no changes in obese Göttingen pigs, which is contrary
to what has been reported in pigs, as well as, what is seen in human obesity [59, 70].
It is seen that both age and gender have an impact on the concentration of the
different acute-phase proteins. Thus, it is very important to keep this in mind when
performing studies on animal models, like diet-induced experiments.
2.5
Animal models
With the help of high-fidelity animal models, it would be possible to further under-
stand and thus provide therapeutic options for the secondary pathologies, like T2D
and CVD, related to MetS [25, 71]. Pigs show a great degree of similarity to human
anatomy, physiology, biochemistry and metabolism [25, 70]. Like humans, pigs are
omnivores, and thus also flexible with regards to different dietary programs [25, 72].
Porcine models are separated into two categories: live-stock breeds and miniature
breeds. Miniature breeds are small and thus more suited to biomedical research [25].
==PAGE:35==
2.5 Animal models
13
They are great for facilitating long-term experiments and can develop MetS and its
secondary comorbidities [25, 73]. The Göttingen minipig has been used as a human
obesity and diabetes model. In a study performed by Rødgaard et. al [70], the ef-
fect of diet-induced obesity on the gene expression of innate immune factors in the
liver and in three types of adipose tissues was elucidated. Fourteen female Göttingen
minipigs (seven obese and seven lean) were included in the study. To avoid hormonal
effects on the feed intake, the pigs were ovariectomised at 9-15 months of age. The
lean pigs were fed restricted standard chow twice a day, while the obese pigs were fed
ad libitum minipig standard chow. The results from this study showed that the body
weights of the obese Göttingen minipigs were significantly higher than the lean pigs
[70]. Additionally, studies have shown that Göttingen minipigs that are given a MetS
diet develop an increased level of plasma triglycerides, impaired glucose tolerance and
insulin resistance, which are all risk factors of MetS [74].
2.5.1
Ossabaw minipig
In this report the focus is on Ossabaw minipigs. The Ossabaw minipigs reach 40 kg
at sexual maturity. Their phenotype has been described as thrifty, as a consequence
of their ability to extract high proportions of energy from their diet and accumulate it
as fat eﬀiciently, like humans. Genetic investigation of the Ossabaw’s phenotype has
shown a single amino acid missense mutation, from Val199 to Ile, in the regulatory
γ3 subunit of the AMP-activated kinase gene. It is thought that this mutation is
the key to the metabolic dysfunction in Ossabaws when given a MetS diet, as this
mutation is associated with an increased level of intramuscular adiposity and reduced
muscle glycogen [25]. As this phenotype resembles human obesity physiology closely,
the Ossabaw model has become very relevant for translational studies [25, 73]. When
Ossabaw pigs are fed a high-fat and high-calorie diet they develop MetS [41, 75]. This
was seen in a study performed by Lee et al. [41], Ossabaw pigs were subjected to
different diets for 24 weeks to induce MetS and liver injury that mimics human MetS
associated steatohepatitis (MASH) [41]. MASH is associated with fibrosis, necrosis
and inflammation [76]. In the study by Lee et al. [41], male and female Ossabaw pigs
aged 5 to 10 months were divided into a control group, with 15 pigs and standard chow,
a fructose group, with 9 pigs and high-fructose diet, an atherogenic diet group, with
13 pigs and an atherogenic diet with high-fructose, and a modified atherogenic diet
group, with 7 pigs and fructose-based atherogenic diet, that was modified in terms of
source of fat, protein content and choline concentration. The control groups consumed
2500 kcal every day, while the other groups consumed 6000 kcal daily [41]. A high
fructose diet can increase triglyceride levels, while a high cholesterol and cholate diet
increases the cholesterol levels and induces steatohepatitis [77, 78].
Results from
this study show that the pigs from the three non-control groups all had a significant
weight gain compared to the control. The fructose group had the most weight gain,
however, they only developed obesity and insulin resistance. Furthermore, in this
group, no liver injury was found. The pigs from the atherogenic group and modified
==PAGE:36==
14

atherogenic group also had a significant weight gain and, contrary to the fructose
group, replicated MetS. Moreover, the modified atherogenic group resulted in a high
level of serum leptin and TNF-α and developed all the criteria needed for diagnosis
of MASH. Thus, proving that Ossabaw minipigs do develop MetS and associated
comorbidities when given a high-fat and high-calorie diet [41].
Porcine models cannot recapitulate all aspects of human metabolic and vascular
diseases, however, the advantages seen for this model are far better than for small-
animal models [79]. Thus, the pig is an excellent biomedical model for obesity [70].

an in-house sandwich ELISA. To coat the plate the capture antibody, a monoclonal
antibody (3.8/D7, [80]), was diluted 1/8000 in a 0.1M carbonate buffer with a pH
of 9.6.
Of this dilution, 100 µL was added to each well and then, the plate was
incubated overnight at 4◦C. The next day, the plate was washed 4 times in washing
buffer consisting of 0.05% Tween20 in PBS. Subsequently, the plate was blocked using
a blocking buffer that consisted of 5% skimmed milk and 1% BSA in PBS. This was
also used as a dilution buffer. The plate was incubated with the blocking buffer for 30
minutes at room temperature in a box to block air from the ventilation. During the
30 minutes, the standard (KVL) and samples were prepared by dilution. Following
the incubation, the plate was washed 4 times in wash buffer and the standard was
added to two columns of the plate in a 2-fold dilution series. Subsequently, 100 µL
of the diluted samples were added to the plate in double determinations and then
incubated at room temperature for 1 hour in a box. After the incubation, the plate
was washed 4 times in washing buffer and 100 µL of polyclonal rabbit anti-human
haptoglobin (DAKO A030) diluted 1/1000 in the dilution buffer was added to the
wells.
The plate was then incubated for 1 hour at room temperature in the box.
After 1 hour, the plate was washed 4 times in washing buffer and HRP conjugated
streptavidin (DAKO P397) was diluted 1/5000 in a buffer consisting of 1% BSA in
wash buffer. The plate was then incubated at room temperature for 1 hour in the
humidity box. After 1 hour the plate was washed 4 times in washing buffer and then
developed. To develop the plate, 100 µL of TMB reagent (TMB PLUS2, KEM EN
TEC no. 4390A) was added to each well. The plate was developed in the dark for
approximately 15 minutes. The plate was stopped with 0.5M of sulfuric acid and the
OD values were measured at 450nm and 650nm, as the background absorbance. The
samples were repeated until an appropriate dilution and result was obtained. The
DL for serum samples was 13 µg/mL and for the tissue extracts it was 0.65 µg/mL.
3.4
Glucose measurements
To quantify the fasting glucose levels in pig serum samples, a blood glucose meter
(Accu-check, No. 225702) was used and the measuring was performed according to
the manufacturer’s instructions. This device can measure the blood glucose levels
between 0.6-33.3 mmol/L through an electrochemical reaction [81]. Of the serum
samples 3 µL was pipetted onto a disposal bag cleaned with ethanol. The electrode
end of the strip needed for the glucose meter was inserted into the machine and then
used to pick up the serum drop from the disposal bag. The glucose meter would
subsequently measure the glucose level, which was written down.

3.5
Insulin assay
The fasting insulin levels were quantified using a Porcine Insulin ELISA kit (Merco-
dia, No. 10-1200-01) and the assay was performed according to the manufacturer’s
instructions. First, suﬀicient microplate well strips, coated with mouse monoclonal
anti-insulin were prepared. Furthermore, the wash buffer was prepared by diluting 1
bottle of the wash buffer provided with 1000 mL mili-Q water. Subsequently, 25 µL
of both the porcine insulin calibrator and the undiluted samples were added into the
wells. Everything was performed with double determinations. The enzyme conjugate
used for this ELISA was peroxidase-conjugated mouse monoclonal anti-insulin. This
was prepared by combining 1 vial of the enzyme conjugate provided with 1 vial of
the enzyme conjugate buffer provided. 100 µL of the diluted enzyme conjugate was
then added into each well and incubated on a plate shaker at room temperature for
2 hours. Following the incubation, the plate was washed 5 times with the washing
buffer and developed by adding 200 µL of TMB into each well. The plate was incu-
bated in the dark at room temperature for 20 minutes. To stop the plate reaction
50 µL of the stop solution was added to each well. The absorbance was measured
at 450 nm. The samples that were out of range were assigned the DL of 2.3 mU/L.
A lot-to-lot variation was seen from the manufacturer thus a conversion factor was
added to the results.

Pad Prism 10 (graphpad.com). The Shapiro-Wilk and Kolmogrov-Smirnov tests were
used to evaluate the normality of the dataset. For Gaussian distributed datasets with
one time point, the unpaired Welch t-test was used to compare the distinct groups.
For Gaussian-distributed datasets with two or more time points, an unpaired Two-
Way ANOVA or a Welch ANOVA test was performed. For non-Gaussian distributed
data, an unpaired Mann-Whitney and an unpaired Kruskal-Wallis test were per-
formed for datasets with one time point and datasets with two or more time points,
respectively. To calculate the HOMA-IR, the equation from section 2.4.2 was used.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
5.1
Short-term experiment
Through the use of anthropometric measurements, such as weight BMI and BAI,
and blood biomarkers, like LDL, HDL, cholesterol, triglyceride, fructosamine, insulin
and glucose concentration, the diet-induced obesity state of the pigs was determined.
From the short-term, low-cholesterol diet the weight, BMI and BAI showed striking
differences between the groups, which by the end of the experiments became more
evident. The weight increases as the pigs become obese. However, as the weight also
accounts for the muscle mass, this can also cause the weight to increase [82]. Thus,
BMI was calculated to determine if the pigs became obese. BMI is not commonly
used in pigs to define obesity, however, it is a common parameter that is used to
define overweight and obesity in humans [4]. From the BMI results, it was seen that
a significant difference was obtained between the obese and lean pigs from the start
of the 100% HFHF diet until the end of the experiment. However, as the weight
is still needed to calculate this parameter, an accurate body fat percentage is still
not determined in the obese pigs [82]. Consequently, the BAI is calculated, as this
parameter estimates the adiposity percentage, without the weight for calculation [82].
To calculate the BAI, the hip circumference and height (length for pigs), is used.
These measurements strongly correlated with body fat percentage [82].
The BAI
estimates the fat distribution around the abdomen. From section 2.3.1, it is known
that higher abdominal adiposity is related to metabolic diseases [45]. From a study
performed by Kogelman et al. [4], BMI and BAI were evaluated in comparison to
each other. Here, it was found that BAI has a stronger indication for adiposity than
BMI, as the BAI showed a stronger negative correlation for backfat from pigs than
BMI [4]. From the BAI results, it is seen that right before the end of the experiment
significant difference was seen between the lean and obese pigs, indicating the obese
pigs became obese during the experiment. However, by the end of the experiment,
there is no significant difference between the obese and lean pigs. Indicating that the
body fat percentage for the obese pigs was not different compared to the lean pigs.
This could have been caused by an increase in the fat around the abdomen for the
lean pigs, making the difference between the obese and lean pigs not significant.
Regarding the glucose levels, in both groups, these seem to remain quite stable
between the start and end of the experiment, with only week 13 showing a clear
difference between the groups. This trend does not continue indicating the obese pigs

concentration for the lean pigs were around 5 mU/L, which is similar to what is seen
for the lean pigs in this project [83]. However, the elevated fasting blood glucose
and elevated fasting insulin concentrations are contradicting to what was seen in this
project. The pigs in this project did not indicate any insulin resistance, which is also
confirmed by the HOMA-IR index at the end of the experiment. The glucose and
insulin levels that did not show any response to the diet might be explained by how
the pigs were handled during the experiment. In week 15, the serum samples were
taken later in the day, instead of the first thing in the morning, which could have an
effect on the results.
The HDL, LDL and total cholesterol concentrations all increase with a similar
trend for the obese group. The total cholesterol concentration in week 13, shows a
significant difference between the obese and lean pigs. An increase is also observed
in week 15 for the obese pigs compared to the lean pigs, however, this increase is
not significant.
Although this diet is low in cholesterol, it is seen that the short
period of time is enough to induce cholesterol levels that are higher for the obese pigs
compared to the lean pigs. For the HDL concentration, a significant increase is seen
in the obese pigs compared to the lean pigs in week 15. For the LDL an increase
is also seen, however, this increase is not significant. These findings are contrary to
what is seen in human obesity, as lower HDL and higher LDL concentrations are seen
in response to obesity [14, 15]. From the study performed by Lützhøft et al. [22] an
increase in the LDL and HDL concentrations was seen for the obese pigs [22]. This is
in agreement with what is seen in this experiment. However, in the study performed
by Lützhøft et al. [22], a decrease in the HDL/non-HDL ratio was seen [22]. This
contradicts what was seen in this project as the LDL/HDL ratio was decreased (or
the HDL/LDL ratio was increased) for the obese pigs compared to the lean pigs. In
human obesity, this ratio is increased [15, 37], thus, the findings from this project are
contradicting, indicating that this risk factor of MetS might not have been induced
in the pigs.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
From the short-term obesity study, it was found that a low-cholesterol diet with
high-fat and high-fructose content can in a short period of time develop obesity in
the Ossabaw minipig. From this experiment, a 2.35-fold increase compared to the
beginning was obtained for the obese pigs. Glucose measurements from serum samples
showed a significant increase in the obese pigs compared to the lean pigs 2 weeks before
the end of the experiment, however, no significant increase was seen at the end of
the experiment. In addition, from the low LDL/HDL ratio and low fructosamine, it
is seen that the risk factors associated with MetS in humans did not develop in the
pigs. The impact of age on the concentration of AGP was seen in independent studies.
Here it was seen that the AGP concentration stabilise around the age where the pigs
become sexually matured. However from the AGP results seen from the short-term
experiment, an elevated AGP concentration was obtained for the lean pigs at the end
of the experiment compared to the beginning, indicating some inflammation might
have occured in the lean pigs. Furthermore, the CRP results from the short-term
experiment decreased significantly for the obese pigs in the end compared to the
beginning. This might have occurred due to inflammation in the obese pigs in the
beginning of the experiment. For the Hp results an increase was obtained for both the
obese and lean pigs in the end compared to the beginning, indicating other factors like
the age of the pigs might have an influence. Furthermore, no systemic inflammation
was seen in the protein extracts. However, from the AGP VAT and SAT results, a
local inflammation was obtained in the lean pigs.
From the Wisconsin obesity experiment, a 2.03-fold weight increase was obtained
in obese pigs by the end of the experiment. None of the APP serum results from this
experiment were significantly different. However the results suggest a trend towards
an increased AGP and Hp concentration. As this experiment was performed over 75
weeks it indicates that these proteins might be good markers for an experiment that
is performed for a longer amount of time. Furthermore, a significant increase was seen
in the CRP concentration in the SAT extracts of the obese pigs compared to the lean
pigs. Thus, a local inflammation might have occurred. A non-significant, however,
elevated glucose concentration was found in the obese pigs compared to the lean.
This shows that some of the risk factors associated with metabolic syndrome were
induced in these pigs. This was also confirmed by the HOMA, where a clear difference
between the obese and lean pigs was seen. However, not all the risks associated with
metabolic syndrome in humans were induced in the pigs, as no significant increase

From both studies, it can be concluded that the Ossabaw minipig can develop

6.1
Future perspective
To optimise these studies a diet with more cholesterol for a longer duration of time
is needed. Furthermore, the amount of biological replicates should be high enough
to obtain a high statistical power to be able to see a significant effect. Lastly, the
age of the pigs needs to be considered more in further studies of the inflammatory
profile. Analysis of CRP and Hp should be performed in young Ossabaw minipigs to
obtain baseline measurements. These proteins might, like what is seen for AGP, also
be influenced by the age of the pigs. Thus, having baseline results for these pigs is
crucial for diet-induced experiments, as it is important to know, what the change in
APP concentration is induced by.
