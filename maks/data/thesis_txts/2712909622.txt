Abstract 
The thesis explores different maintenance scenarios that are linked to both preventive and 
predictive purposes. The target is to propose a new maintenance policy, since traditional 
maintenance approaches, reactive maintenance, are implemented under certain limitations. 
Thus, there has been a quite extended research and effort to recommend alternative 
maintenance approaches, preventive and predictive, as it is critical to develop an efficient 
maintenance strategy to prevent unexpected breakdowns, improve overall performance and 
reduce operational costs.  
The literature review indicates that there are various ways to implement predictive 
maintenance, but the main focus of the study is to investigate machine learning and statistical 
models that predict the remaining useful life, RUL. More specifically, three machine learning 
algorithms are compared to each other, aiming to select the best, based on their overall 
performance. The results indicate that the random forest algorithm is the selected machine 
learning approach, that is used for predictive maintenance. On the other hand, survival analysis 
is the statistical method that is selected as an alternative way of applying predictive 
maintenance. Three proposed scenarios, preventive and predictive, together with the current 
approach, reactive, are compared at their best possible performance, by defining the optimal 
maintenance conditions using stochastic optimization. The comparison is conducted by 
introducing key performance indicators, KPIs, that assess the proposed policies. The results of 
the optimization show that the predictive maintenance based on machine learning achieves the 
best results and manages to increase the utilization of the machine by 8% and reduce both the 
maintenance cost and the downtime, by 18% and 31%, respectively. Furthermore, sensitivity 
analysis is implemented for the stochasticity factors of the optimization algorithm, in order to 
investigate their impact.  
In addition, the maintenance scenarios are evaluated by using decision support models, aiming 
to include additional factors, such as human resources and investment, that need to be taken 
into consideration. The decision-making process is based on extracted decision criteria, aiming 
to indicate the best choice for the maintenance policy. The results of the decision support 
methods are aligned with the optimization results. Thus, it is verified that implementing 
predictive maintenance using machine learning models is the best maintenance policy, for the 
purpose of this study, and can achieve promising results.

Introduction 
1. Introduction 
The thesis investigates different approaches of preventive/predictive maintenance. The existing 
traditional maintenance strategies, reactive maintenance, focus on acting when an actual 
breakdown occurs, leading to increased downtimes. However, the integration and 
implementation of mathematical predictive and statistical models can introduce a new 
maintenance strategy, that forecasts a breakdown, fault prognosis, so as to do maintenance in 
advance, aiming to optimize the production/machine performance and reduce the operational 
costs. So, the first part of the thesis implements different predictive models, that exist in the 
literature, using published data (Matzka, 2020), assesses the accuracy results of the models, 
which indicate to what extent these models can be implemented in reality, and constitute a 
maintenance policy. Followingly, the best maintenance conditions are defined using stochastic 
optimization for each maintenance scenario, aiming to compare their best possible 
performance, though comparison of selected key performance indicators, KPIs. The next step 
is to evaluate the different predictive/preventive maintenance policies by using decision 
support modelling, that analyzes the impact of the maintenance policies, including additional 
factors that need to be taken into account. The decision-making process is based on selected 
decision criteria, aiming to indicate the best choice for the maintenance policy. Specifically, 
implementing a predictive/preventive maintenance strategy may require or bring consequential 
changes that need to be addressed and assessed. To continue with, the result of the maintenance 
policy selection from the decision support models is discussed and compared to the respective 
outcome of the KPIs’ comparison, after implementing the stochastic optimization. Lastly, 
recommendations and suggestions for future research are mentioned prior to the conclusions 
of the thesis.   
1.1 Aim and objectives 
The thesis explores preventive/predictive maintenance approaches by implementing various 
machine learning and statistical models to published data (Matzka, 2020). The aim is to propose 
an optimal maintenance policy that not only considers to increase the targeted KPIs, but also 
takes into consideration the business perspective, by using decision support modelling for the 
proposed maintenance policies, and provide a complete and comprehensive overview of a 
maintenance strategy that could potentially be implemented in an industry. 
The research questions can be structured as:  
✓ How can various predictive models be implemented to forecast a breakdown/failure and 
propose a novel maintenance policy?  
 
✓ How can the maintenance conditions for each proposed policy be optimally defined using 
stochastic optimization, aiming to compare their best possible performance?

1.2 Scope, limitations and assumptions 
The scope of the thesis is to propose a novel maintenance policy that will be based on 
preventive/predictive approaches and could be applied in an industry, instead of the traditional 
maintenance methods, and establish a new maintenance strategy. The selection of the new 
policy will be based on both predictive/preventive and decision support models, so as to take 
into consideration the business perspective as well.  
The thesis is conducted by accepting the limitations that are described below: 
• The data quantity is not suitable for all the literature methods.   
• There is no information for the machine, the type of industry, the current maintenance 
policy and the maintenance cost. 
• There is no financial information, setup cost, for each maintenance policy, as they 
highly depend on the type of industry.  
In order to deal with the aforementioned limitations, the following assumptions are introduced: 
• The maintenance cost is calculated by some initial cost values that reflect the failure 
frequency. 
• The downtime duration of an actual breakdown is twice as much as the duration of 
preventive maintenance. The duration derives from a triangular distribution, where the 
parameters are set in a way to reflect the failure frequency. 
• The current maintenance policy is a run-to-failure approach.   
• The machine operates 8 hours per day, which corresponds to a total number of 60 
production days.

2. Literature Review 
This chapter describes the literature review that has been conducted and used as input for the 
thesis. Different maintenance approaches are analyzed, and it is described how these concepts 
are implemented in the industry. A special reference to the predictive maintenance takes place, 
where various applied models are mentioned and all the benefits, challenges and applications 
of each approach are stated, as a final comparison. To continue with, the decision support 
process is analyzed together with relevant methods and techniques, which support the decision-
making process, which in this study is the selection of the suitable maintenance policy.   
2.1 Maintenance approaches  
Maintenance is considered to be a process of utmost importance for an industry and can play a 
significant role in achieving a combination of high quality and performance with reduced costs. 
Industries face a continuous challenge of high unforeseen downtime of machines that affect 
their performance and can potentially result in important penalties and irreparable reputation 
impact. Traditional maintenance approaches, reactive maintenance, are implemented under 
certain limitations, such as high repair costs, insufficient or inaccurate mathematical 
deterioration processes and equipment mistreatment. This explains why there has been a quite 
extended research and effort to recommend alternative maintenance approaches 
(preventive/predictive maintenance), as it is critical for companies to develop an efficient 
maintenance strategy to prevent unexpected breakdowns, improve overall performance and 
reduce operational costs (Yongyi, et al., 2019).  
The concept of implementing preventive/predictive maintenance, PM and PdM, strategies 
instead of reactive maintenance, RM, is reflected in the emerging growth of modern 
technologies (e.g., internet of things (IoT), sensor technology, artificial intelligence). RM is 
only used to restore the equipment's working state after it has failed, which leads in significant 
delays and high reactive repair costs. To eliminate the occurrence of an actual breakdown, PM 
is carried out according to a schedule that is usually based on time (time-based). This may 
result in excessive maintenance activities and expensive related costs. PdM is based on an 
online estimation of the equipment state and can enable effective pre-failure actions in order to 
ensure the best trade-off between the two afore-mentioned maintenance approaches (Yongyi, 
et al., 2019). 
2.2 Reactive maintenance (RM) 
Reactive maintenance, RM, is a run-to-failure maintenance strategy (Mobley, 2002; Swanson, 
2001). A maintenance action takes place, only when the equipment has broken down or has 
run to the failure point, Although RM sounds tempting, few companies implement a run-to-
failure management. By pushing the equipment to its limits, RM ensures maximum utilization 
and, as a result, maximum manufacturing output. When an industry uses run-to-failure concept, 
maintenance does not happen, until an equipment or system breaks down. On the other hand, 
repairing or replacing a component could cost more than the production value gained by 
running it to failure (see Figure 10).

2.3 Preventive maintenance (PM) 
Preventive maintenance, PM, often identified as planned maintenance, is a method of 
scheduling regular maintenance actions on particular equipment/machine to reduce the risk of 
failure (Gertsbakh, 2013; Mobley, 2002; Wan, et al., 2017). The maintenance is carried out 
while the machine is still running, aiming to minimize the corresponding downtime, 
maintenance cost and unforeseen failures. 
Almost all PM management programs (Mobley, 2002; Ahmad & Kamaruddin, 2012) are time-
based. In other words, processes depend on the amount of time that has passed. The failure 
pattern of the equipment is considered to be predictable or known, as depicted in Figure 4 by 
bathtub curves (Cook, 2018; Alvarez-Alvarado & Jayaweera, 2018). The bathtub curve 
suggests that new equipment has a high level of risk during the first few weeks of operation 
due to installation issues. After this break-in phase, the failure rate drops to a manageable level 
for a long time. With the passage of time after this typical life period, the likelihood of failure 
rises considerably. The PM approach can be divided into two phases: 
• The first phase is to statistically evaluate the failure features of the machine, based on 
the time series data that have been collected.  
• The next phase is to determine the best maintenance strategies for maximizing 
manufacturing KPIs (reliability, availability), while minimizing maintenance costs. 
 
Figure 4: Statistical bathtub curve of a piece of equipment (Cook, 2018; Alvarez-Alvarado & Jayaweera, 2018)  
PM may lower repair costs and unscheduled downtime, but it could also lead to unneeded 
repairs or catastrophic failures. The theoretical rate of failure, rather than actual information on 
the condition of the specific equipment, is used to determine when a piece of equipment will 
enter the "wear out" phase. This frequently leads to costly and utterly unneeded maintenance 
being performed before the occurrence of a problem or after possibly catastrophic harm has 
occurred. This will also result in a lot more planned downtime and complicated inventory 
management. If the equipment fails before the projected "wear out" time, RM must be used to 
fix it. According to previous research, the maintenance cost of repairs performed in a reactive

maintenance case (i.e., after failure) is typically three times higher than that of repairs 
performed on a scheduled basis (Mobley, 2002). 
2.4 Predictive maintenance (PdM) 
The approach of predictive maintenance has been investigated for quite some time, but it has 
recently become a topic of increasing interest, as technological improvements facilitate the 
implementation of it (Nguyen, et al., 2015). Condition monitoring, fault diagnosis, fault 
prognosis, and maintenance planning are considered as crucial aspects of PdM (Saltelli, et al., 
2008). The advanced technologies have the ability to detect, isolate, and identify early failures 
in industrial equipment and components, to monitor and predict fault occurrence, and to include 
decision-support or automation to create optimized maintenance schedules.  
Predictive maintenance, PdM, also referred as condition-based maintenance (CBM) (Williams, 
et al., 1994), tries to forecast when the machine equipment is likely to fail and choose when a 
maintenance activity should be conducted, in order to maintain a reasonable balance between 
maintenance frequency and cost (Carvalho, et al., 2019). The goal of PdM is to optimize the 
machine equipment performance by using the current operating state (Mobley, 2002). Installed 
sensors in machines and tools acquire different kind of data, such as vibration data, thermal 
pictures, ultrasonic data, operation availability etc., which are used as input in the predictive 
modeling (Yongyi, et al., 2019). The predictive model uses algorithms to analyze data, identify 
trends, and forecasts when equipment needs to be fixed or removed. 
Instead of pushing equipment or components to operating until failure occurs or replacing them 
at an early stage when they are still functional, PdM assists organizations in improving their 
strategy by doing maintenance tasks only when it is truly required. Therefore, downtime, high 
maintenance costs, unneeded inventory, and unnecessary maintenance tasks can be reduced 
with PdM. However, the cost of the condition monitoring devices (e.g., sensors) that are 
required for PdM is frequently higher than for RM and PM. Additionally, the operation of 
the PdM system is expected to be more complicated as a result of data collecting, analysis, and 
decision-making activities that add complexity. Table 1 summarizes the reductions that can be 
achieved in a machine equipment with the implementation of a functional PdM process.

2.4.1 Machine learning (ML) methods 
The industrial environment is characterized by increasing competitiveness, accelerated 
response times, cost reduction, and production reliability to align with customer needs. This 
results in rising of the new industrial paradigm of Industry 4.0, aiming to shift the strategy of 
the companies to integrate the digital transformation. Digital technologies have enabled a novel 
data-driven approach based on analyzing large amounts of data from sensors, that are installed 
in the industrial equipment. Predictive maintenance based on machine learning is one of the 
most popular data-driven and analytical methods for monitoring industrial equipment with the 
goal of increasing performance (Arena, et al., 2022).  
Machine learning is an area of computer science that allows to predict, diagnose, plan, and 
recognize behavioral patterns by learning from historical data, rather than knowing them 
beforehand. Learning algorithms can be divided to the following categories (Arena, et al., 
2022): 
• Supervised: the algorithm takes as input a set of labelled training data. 
• Unsupervised: the algorithm takes as input an unlabeled training data. 
• Semi-supervised: the algorithm takes as input both labelled and unlabeled training data 
• By reinforcement: in which the learning algorithm interacts with the environment, takes 
some actions, and receives rewards (positive or negative) based on the chosen action. 
The most common ML techniques that can be used for fault diagnosis, prognosis, and 
prediction of remaining useful life (RUL) in predictive maintenance applications are supervised 
and unsupervised. The selection of suitable metrics to provide a reliable measure of the 
effectiveness of a particular classifier or regressor, is part of the evaluation of the ML model 
performance. Several metrics, such as the confusion matrix, accuracy, recall, and ROC curve, 
are used to evaluate the reliability and the consistency of the algorithm. These metrics are 
measured as the outcome of correct or incorrect prediction of healthy equipment status. As a 
result, the implementation of a machine learning method, as a tool for predictive maintenance, 
needs to take into consideration two key points (Arena, et al., 2022): 
• The amount and quality of the available data, which affect the model performance. 
• The performance metrics that provide useful information on the output of the model 
and can be used as a criterion in the decision-making process.

2.4.1.1 Random forest (RF) 
Random forest is a common machine learning algorithm that is considered to be a supervised 
technique that can be applied for both classification and regression tasks. It is highly correlated 
to the process of ensemble learning, a process that combines various classifiers or regressors 
to deal with a complicated problem and boost the model performance (Carvalho, et al., 2019). 
It has been initially proposed by (Leo, 2001). As it is indicated by the naming, random forest 
creates a "forest" (ensemble) with several random decision trees and sums the predictions by 
simply calculating the average. Random forest improves its performance when the number of 
variables is higher than the number of observations (Biau & Scornet, 2016). It needs to be 
mentioned that despite the fact that random forest collects many different decision trees (see 
Figure 5), there are quite a few differences between the behavior of these two, that are listed 
below (Leo, 2001; Prytz, et al., 2015; Biau & Scornet, 2016): 
• Decision trees often need to deal with the problem of overfitting in case they are 
allowed to expand without any limitations, whereas random forest is built by random 
subsets of features which create smaller trees. Thus, overfitting is not an issue. 
• The computation time for a decision tree is smaller compared to random forest, which 
is a slower algorithm. 
• Decision trees predict by formulating rules and nodes from the given data, while 
random forest does not use any formulas, but randomly chooses observations and builds 
decision trees.

2.4.1.2 Support vector machine (SVM)  
Support vector machine is another frequently applied and well-known machine learning 
approach for performing classification and regression, due to its high accuracy score (Sexton, 
et al., 2017; Chang & Lin, 2011). The major feature of this algorithm is its great precision in 
separating several classes of data, as well as its ability to identify the ideal point for the afore-
mentioned classes (Susto, et al., 2013). It is considered to be a supervised machine learning 
method for regression analysis and pattern identification.  
Support vector machines started out as non-probabilistic binary classifiers. In this case, the data 
points are supposed to have two classes and the training algorithm structures a model that 
assigns new examples to one of the two classes. Thus, it is formed as a non-probabilistic binary 
linear classifier (Yongyi, et al., 2019). However, they are now used in multi-class situations as 
well. The algorithm creates n-dimension hyperplanes in this scenario, which classify data into 
n groups/classes. The high classification of the algorithm for both linear and non-linear 
problems, explains the fact that is has been widely implemented for different purposes, such as 
face detection, recognition and verification, as well as fault diagnosis for machine equipment. 
(Santos, et al., 2015; Soualhi, et al., 2015; Sun, et al., 2016; Han, et al., 2019).

2.4.1.3 Artificial neural networks (ANNs) 
An artificial neural network consists of various nodes or neurons (hidden layers), that are 
defined as processing units and can perform in a simplified way. They are linked using 
connection channels and they only process their data, that have been indicated, through the

other is the key point to the high performance of an ANN. It is considered to be a widely applied 
ML algorithm and has been used for soft sensor development in industrial processes (Soares &

In order to create an ANN, there is no need for extremely high-level knowledge to support 
decisions, since only historical data are needed. It is a robust system and even if the data present 
inconsistency, the ANN is not affected. So, an ANN can be structured and implemented for a

2.4.1.4 K-nearest neighbors (k-NN)  
K-nearest neighbors is a non-parametric machine learning algorithm, meaning that there are no 
assumptions for distribution for the data, that is based on the supervised learning technique. It 
applies for both classification and regression, but mostly is used for classification tasks 
(Yongyi, et al., 2019).  
As an initial step in this algorithm, the value of k needs to be determined. The value selection 
is often data-driven and higher values of k may decrease the noise in the 
classification/regression, but make decision margins between classes less discrete. After that, 
k-NN algorithm investigates the resemblance between a new data point (test instance) and 
already available data (training instances), and assigns the new data point to the class that is 
most comparable to the available ones. This is done by calculating the Euclidean distance 
between the test and the training instances, indicating the level of similarity. The output of the 
algorithm depends on whether it has to do with classification or regression. The outcome for 
the classification problems is the proper class for each data record, whereas in the regression 
problems, the outcome is the predicted value of each data point, that is calculated from the 
average of k nearest neighbors’ values (Yongyi, et al., 2019). 
A useful method that can be applied in the k-NN algorithm is to quantify the contribution of 
each neighbor, by extracting weight factors so as to indicate that a nearer neighbor affects the 
average value at a higher extent, compared to the ones being at a longer distance. Particularly,

2.6 Decision support  
Decision-making is a process where decision-makers (DMs) try to find the ideal solution to a 
problem. However, a truly perfect solution can be found only if you take into consideration one 
criterion. In reality, building a decision strategy exclusively on one criterion is inadequate. It 
is very likely that several contradictory and often non-comparable criteria contribute to the 
==PAGE:28==
 
Page | 18  
 
Literature Review 
decision-making process and should be taken into account. As a result, it is unfeasible to find 
a solution that is ideal for all DMs, based on all the established criteria (Løken, 2007).  
Multi-criteria decision making (MCDM) is a basic term for approaches that support individuals 
in taking decisions using their opinion in cases where various contradictory criteria exist. It is 
reasonable to say that using MCDM is a way of solving complicated problems by 
disassembling them into smaller sub-problems. After resolving them, they can be combined to 
provide a better outline to the decision-makers (Barfod, 2012). One of the most critical aspects 
of an analysis in the field of operations research (OR), is the structure of a decision problem. 
Specifically, it is of utmost importance to convert a primarily unclear and not well-defined 
problem to a situation where the problem is structured in a way that can be solved using the 
appropriate mathematical models (Winterfeldt & Fasolo, 2009). The decision analysis 
emphasizes on the afore-mentioned structure by receiving input from the DMs about their 
targets, worries, challenges and translate these into a transparent description of the decision 
problem, which can be configured by combining knowledge of decision theory and applying 
modelling (Belton & Stewart, 2002). 
2.6.1 Multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) 
Multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) is a term very similar to MCDM, but different in the 
way that the technique should support the stakeholders in taking decisions, as the method 
cannot decide on its own. Thus, the goal of MCDA is to assist the DMs by ranking different 
scenarios according to a number of criteria, so that they feel confident with the outcome of the 
final decision (Chen, et al., 2008). By using this multi-criteria method, the decision-makers feel 
secure that the vital factors have been defined correctly, which decreases the likelihood of being 
regretful for the taken decision, at a later time (Belton & Stewart, 2002). Lastly, the MCDA 
method ensures that the DMs recognize and comprehend the essential criteria in the decision 
process and avoid taking decisions spontaneously or without assessing all the including factors. 
MCDA consists of a variety of methods with multiple steps that have been developed over the 
years, aiming to define and design decision-making processes in a way that provide consistency 
and transparency (Langemeyer, et al., 2016). The effective implementation of these methods 
requires to describe the problem, set different alternative solutions, and extract criteria that 
could be related to financial indicators, environmental and social aspects, quality performance 
or other appropriate criteria to the respective case. After that, the MCDA method ranks the 
selected alternatives and suggest the best approach, according to the given criteria. However, 
the MCDA method contains a high level of subjectivity, since the criteria of assessment are 
selected based on the decision makers’ preference and feelings (Barfod, 2018). 
According to the literature, there are many methods that are used for MCDA and differ due to 
the calculation steps each technique follows. For each method, there is a different way that the 
selected scenarios (alternatives) and criteria are weighted and ranked. Thus, it is quite often to 
get different final results for methods that use the same data (preferences) as input. The decision 
analysis, in this case, uses methods and techniques that are based on pair-wise comparisons 
between the different alternatives and the selected decision criteria.

2.6.1.2 Analytic network process (ANP) 
The analytic network process (ANP) method denotes a more generic approach of the analytic 
hierarchy process (AHP) and has been implemented in different cases, according to the 
literature (Jinyuan, et al., 2012; Ning & Xue-wei, 2006; Xiao-bo & Ting-ting, 2009). The main 
target of the ANP technique is to rank a defined group of alternatives using a specific number 
of decision criteria. This concept relies on making pairwise comparisons and quantifying 
preferences between the alternatives and the decision criteria. The most appropriate scenario is 
the one with the highest ranking. 
An example of ANP is depicted in Figure 12. As can be seen, there are nine decision nodes, 
one goal, five criteria and three alternatives, and three clusters. The nodes are structured in a 
way that different links exist among them, either in one direction or in both ways. The first 
cluster includes the goal, the second one is composed of criteria 1,2 and the third cluster 
consists of criteria 3, 4 and 5. Finally, the three alternatives belong to cluster 4.

based on all the comparisons (Brestovac & Grgurina, 2013).

(𝑘−1)/2 pairwise comparisons, there is a limitation in the number of criteria and alternatives 
for each cluster. It is suggested to use an upper limit of five criteria and alternatives in each 
cluster. Finally, rank reversal is a potential challenge for this technique, as the ranking can 
change with the addition of an alternative. In other words, this model reflects the decision-
making process in circumstances that the significance of the decision criteria may change with 
respect to the existing alternatives (Brestovac & Grgurina, 2013). 
2.6.1.3 Rembrandt 
AHP is a basic and widely applied technique in the MCDA family. However, due to mainly 
three reasons, this method has been criticized over the years, leading to extended efforts from 
the research community to improve the performance of the model. Thus, a new system has 
been introduced, which uses Ratio Estimation in Magnitudes or deci-Bells to Rate Alternatives 
which are Non-DominaTed, REMBRANDT (Barfod, 2012). The main differences between 
these two techniques lie in the calculation of the scores, the extraction of value functions and 
the scale that the two methods use. Specifically, the rating in Rembrandt uses a logarithmic 
scale that takes input from a scale with a range from zero to eight, including only even numbers 
as well as their negatives. Secondly, the calculation of scores uses the geometric mean, defined 
as the average value of a group of numbers that is calculated by using the product of their 
values, rather than the sum. This leads to avoid potential rank reversal, whereas in the AHP the 
scores are estimated by the Perron-Frobenius eigenvector method. The detailed scale of 
Rembrandt is presented in Table 5. Furthermore, the measurement of performance of all 
alternatives against individual criteria, namely the value functions (score aggregation), differs 
in those two techniques, since AHP uses the additive model (aggregate scores by arithmetic 
mean), while Rembrandt make use of the multiplicative model (the product of alternative 
relative scores, weighted by the power of weights that are obtained from analysis of hierarchical 
elements above the alternatives (Barfod, 2012).

3. Diagnostic Analytics 
The diagnostic analytics method is used to describe the basic features of the data. It is an 
advanced analysis that investigates potential reasons of why and what has happened. Its main 
characteristics are analytical techniques such as, data visualization, data discovery and data 
correlation. It is considered to be one of the essential steps for conducting statistical and 
predictive data analysis. It also enables to identify similarities among defined variables, thus 
creating a baseline for conducting further analyses and predictive modelling (Lawton, 2022). 
As a first step, the data of the technical features are being analyzed, so as to visualize the 
respective distributions and to check for potential anomaly periods during the operation of the 
machine. Secondly, the different product types are investigated to get insights for the produced 
quantity of each product type and the variation they have on the process specifications, that is 
based on the technical features. To continue with, the failures and the remaining useful life 
(RUL) are examined, in order to get clarification on how the RUL metric is defined, calculated 
and distributed. Moreover, the reasons behind each failure type and how the failure types are 
linked to the technical features, are extracted from the analysis. Finally, after conducting this 
sequential data analysis, the correlation of the data is visualized by using a correlation matrix, 
which is an important assessment tool, that verifies the dependencies between the different 
features that have been highlighted during the data analysis, and gives input to the machine 
learning models that are used for predictive purposes.

3.1 Data description 
Before analyzing the data, it is worth mentioning the dataset structure and the kind of 
information each data feature provides. The dataset consists of 10000 records that indicate the 
product type, technical features and when/why a failure occurs. More specifically, a detailed 
explanation for each feature is given below (Matzka, 2020): 
• Product type (-): consists of a letter L, M, or H for low, medium and high as product 
quality variants. 
• Process time (min): The process time of each product taking discrete values of 2, 3, 5 
minutes for product type L, M, H, respectively. 
• Technical features: 
o Temperature (K) of the air in time each product is processed. 
o Temperature (K) of the machine for each product. 
o Rotational speed (rpm) which is calculated from a power of 2860 W. 
o Torque (Nm) of the machine for each product. 
• Tool wear (min): the accumulated process time between failures 
• Product sequence (-): the accumulated number of processed products between two 
failures 
• Failure type: The failure category, which can be defined as: 
o Heat dissipation failure: occurs when the difference between air and process 
temperature is below 8.6 K and the tool’s rotational speed is below 1380 rpm. 
o Power failure: occurs when the power is below 3500 W or above 9000 W (the 
required power is calculated from the torque and rotational speed measured in 
rad/s). 
o Overstrain failure: occurs when the tool wear and torque of each product 
exceeds 11000, 12000 and 13000 𝑚𝑖𝑛· 𝑁𝑚, for each product type. 
o Tool wear failure: occurs when the machine fails, due to high accumulated 
process time. 
o Random failure: each product (process) has a probability 0.1% to fail, regardless 
of the aforementioned process parameters. 
The machine breaks down, if at least one of the failure modes is true, and the failure label gets 
the value of 1, otherwise it takes the value of 0.

3.5 Remaining useful life (RUL) 
The remaining useful life, RUL, is an estimation of the remaining time that an industrial system 
can operate successfully before a critical failure occurs or a certain degradation level has been 
reached, and there is urgent need of the equipment to stop functioning (Yongyi, et al., 2019). 
The RUL can be calculated based on observations, or relevant features and it is a subjective 
estimation that heavily relies on the available data (Okoh, et al., 2014).  
For this study, RUL is calculated using the provided dataset and more specifically the 
estimation is based on the time each failure occurs, that is known in advance. Therefore, the 
RUL is calculated in the time interval between two failures and it is a descending function that 
takes as an initial value, the subtraction of the process time of the first failure from the process 
time of the second failure. To continue with, the RUL value at each time decreases by 
subtracting the process time of each product from the initial value, until it reaches the value of 
0. Figure 22 illustrates an example, so as to better explain the calculation process.

5. Stochastic Optimization 
In chapter 4, the ML models are analyzed and implemented in order to find the one which can 
predict the RUL, as accurately as possible. The main purpose of this chapter is to find the 
optimal maintenance conditions for each policy. Therefore, four scenarios are investigated (see 
Table 9) and evaluated by comparing manufacturing KPIs.

The first scenario is assumed to be the current approach, due to the limited provided 
information, and it is the scenario where maintenance is applied only after a failure occurs (run-
to-failure). The second scenario is the policy, where maintenance is applied on discrete and 
fixed time intervals, regardless of a recent breakdown occurrence. This means, that the 
maintenance is scheduled every X number of processed products. The third and fourth scenario 
(PdM-ML and PdM-SA) are the policies, where the failure is predicted by different approaches 
(RUL and survival probability), and the maintenance is applied before a failure occurs. In other 
words, maintenance is scheduled in discrete but not fixed time intervals, whenever the value 
of predicted variable is lower than a certain threshold.    
In order to compare these scenarios, it is easily understood that the optimal threshold value for 
each scenario needs to be identified. For instance, in the third scenario, the question that needs 
to be answered is: should preventive maintenance be implemented whenever the RUL falls 
below 5%, 20% or 50%? 
It is important to compare the scenarios at their best performance. To achieve this, the threshold 
variables and the corresponding ranges are introduced for each scenario. In the second scenario, 
the product sequence is set as the threshold, with a range between 20 and 240, whereas in the 
third and fourth scenario, the predicted RUL and survival probability are set as threshold 
variables with a range between 5-75 % and 5-80%, respectively. Table 10 contains the

5.1 Policy function 
As aforementioned, the optimization is separated to two main functions and two sub-functions. 
The two main functions are the policy and replicant function, while the two sub-functions are 
the randomness and cost function. The policy function is considered to be the most important, 
as it creates the maintenance policies, according to the threshold. Figure 39 depicts the pseudo 
code and flowchart of the policy function. As can be seen, this function takes as input the type 
of maintenance policy, the data, the threshold and it iterates over the products to find the time 
and number of preventive and reactive maintenances, that occur for the specific threshold.

values of the KPIs, for the last replicant, are the outputs of the function. In Figure 45, the rolling 
average results for the KPIs of availability and cost, that derive from the replicant function, are 
displayed for the second scenario and a specific threshold. It seems reasonable to say that as 
the number of replicants increase, the rolling averages values tend to converge to a steady state.

5.4 Sensitivity analysis 
The optimization algorithm is created in a way to be able to compare the various maintenance 
scenarios at their best possible performance. The results indicate that scenario 3 is the one with 
the best KPIs, among the four alternatives.  
However, the optimization has been built by making three critical assumptions. The first relates 
to the initial values of the maintenance costs (see Table 12), where they are set in a way to 
reflect the significantly high failure frequency, so as to provide a comparable baseline for the 
different scenarios. The second assumption has to do with the calculation of the downtime for 
preventive and reactive maintenance, respectively. The downtime is calculated using a random 
value from a triangular distribution that depends on the type of maintenance and is set to be 
within a smaller range for the preventive compared to the reactive maintenance (15, 30, 45 for 
preventive and 30, 60, 90 for reactive). This random value is then multiplied by the number of 
preventive or reactive maintenances (see equation 27). The final assumption refers to the 
reactive condition (see Table 11), where the reactive condition is set to be the same for all the 
scenarios, due to limited amount of data. Table 15 summarizes the assumptions and their 
potential impacts.

The second and third assumption are selected to be investigated, as they are considered to be

complexity of the required sensitivity analysis. To address the first assumption, a variance-
based sensitivity analysis (Saltelli, et al., 2008) should be implemented, in order to investigate 
how the uncertainty of each individual cost value affects the objective function, based on factor 
prioritization. However, due to the complexity of this method, it has been decided to be out of 
scope and it is recommended for future outlook. 
The stochasticity factors are investigated, by checking how the optimization algorithm would 
perform for two different sensitivity analysis cases. The failure stochasticity is increased by 
50% and then decreased by the same percentage (50%), aiming to compare the new results 
with the original optimization results. The same applies for the downtime. By doing so, it is 
possible to explore to what extent the stochasticity factors affect the optimal values.

6. Decision Support  
This chapter introduces a different way to evaluate the maintenance policies that have been 
thoroughly investigated previously, by comparing the defined KPIs. In order to select and 
establish a new maintenance policy, additional criteria need to be taken into consideration, that 
have to do with the business perspective and the sequential changes that the policy selection 
brings. The decision-making process can be supported by assessment tools and methods that 
are structured in way that can provide an integrated overview of each maintenance scenario. 
This is succeeded by introducing further criteria, except the defined KPIs, that could potentially 
impact the selection of the maintenance approach. 
6.1 Criteria description 
The selected decision criteria that affect the maintenance policy selection are described and the 
reasons why they are chosen are explained, in order to justify the concept of this choice.  
Criterion 1 (C1): Cost 
This criterion is linked to the maintenance cost that has been previously analyzed for the 
different maintenance policies (scenarios), as one of the main targets of the stochastic 
optimization is to minimize the maintenance cost compared to the current state (see Table 14). 
As has been mentioned, there is a reduction of 1.2% in the maintenance cost for the second 
scenario, and a decrease of 17.6% and 12.9% for the third and fourth scenario, respectively. 
Thus, it is obvious that this criterion favors the predictive maintenance policy with the machine 
learning approach (third scenario). The sequence of preference for the selected scenarios for 
this criterion is: 
𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 3 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 4 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 2 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 1 
(39) 
Criterion 2 (C2): Machine utilization 
This criterion relates to the performance of the machine (equipment) that has been also 
investigated in the stochastic optimization, by calculating the availability of the machine for 
the different scenarios. It is important to achieve a better performance of the machine 
equipment with the new maintenance policy, since it aims to reduce the number of actual 
breakdowns and thus increase the amount of time the machine functions. Table 14 lists the 
values of availability for each case. It can be observed that the third scenario achieves the 
highest percentage of availability (81.4%), followed by the survival analysis (fourth scenario) 
with 80.9% and lastly the preventive maintenance (second scenario) with 78.7%. The first 
scenario achieves an availability of 75.2%. Thus, it is reasonable to say that a moderate increase 
is succeeded with the predictive maintenance policy based on machine learning model. The 
sequence of preference for the selected scenarios for this criterion is: 
𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 3 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 4 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 2 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 1 
(40)

Decision Support 
Criterion 3 (C3): Initial investment 
This criterion relates to the actions that need to be taken, in case a preventive or predictive 
maintenance policy is selected. There is a financial aspect that needs to be taken into 
consideration if a relevant strategy is to be implemented. The predictive maintenance concept, 
especially for the machine learning approach (third scenario), requires specific training to the 
employees from consultants or experts, so as to familiarize themselves with this new way of 
working and develop the necessary skills in order to be able to deal with potential issues that 
may arise. An effective change management is of vital importance in order to secure smooth 
transition to this new maintenance policy. Also, there is a setup cost to install the sensor 
equipment. On the other hand, the second scenario (preventive maintenance) requires changes 
to the maintenance scheduling, but with a low investment to be required (Thomas, 2018). Thus, 
this criterion favors the existing maintenance policy (run-to failure) instead of the proposed 
approaches. The sequence of preference for the selected scenarios for this criterion is: 
𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 1,2 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 3 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 4 
(41) 
Criterion 4 (C4): Production scheduling complexity 
This decision criterion introduces the degree of complexity for the production scheduling that 
relies on the total number of reactive and preventive maintenances. As mentioned in Table 14, 
the lowest number of total maintenances, 42 in total, is achieved by scenario 3. It is considered 
that the production scheduling becomes more complex as the total number of maintenances 
rises. This is attributed to the increased downtime that makes the production planning more 
difficult, especially due to the fact that the number of reactive maintenances is not known in 
advance. The respective total numbers of maintenances for the second and fourth scenario is 
59 and 49 respectively, which is quite different from the third scenario. The production 
scheduling for the first scenario is not that complex compared to the other cases, as the total 
number of reactive maintenances is 46. Thus, this criterion favors mainly the machine learning 
approach for predictive maintenance (scenario 3). The sequence of preference for the selected 
scenarios for this criterion is: 
𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 3 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 1 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 4 > 𝑆𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑜 2 
(42) 
Criterion 5 (C5): Human resources 
This indicator describes the needs for human resources for each maintenance approach. The 
second scenario is not expected to require additional resources compared to the first scenario. 
However, this is not the case for the predictive maintenance policies. The establishment of a 
predictive policy indicates that extra resources are necessary in order to implement this 
automated process. Indicatively, IT employees are responsible to supervise the operation of the 
sensors and the data extraction to the cloud services, as well as the maintenance of the whole 
IT system. It is assumed that the fourth scenario is not cloud-based, as it is not depended on the 
sensor data. Data analysts are needed, in both cases, to use the new databases and create 
dashboards that provide the necessary information for the occurrence of a maintenance activity.

Criterion 6 (C6): Sustainability 
The criterion of sustainability combines the number of products that are discarded, because 
they are processed when reactive maintenance occurs and the number of purchased tools that 
both lead to a non-sustainable approach. The reduction of these values indicates a more 
sustainable strategy. The highest number of reactive maintenances (46 in total) is achieved by 
the first scenario. The fourth scenario achieves the lowest number of reactive maintenances, 
while the predictive maintenance based on machine learning (third scenario) and preventive 
maintenance (fourth scenario) have slightly higher values (20 for the second scenario and 16 
for the third). It is observed that in case of preventive maintenance, more tools are purchased, 
since the maintenance frequency is higher. Thus, by combining the two aspects, this criterion

(44) 
The decision-making models prioritize not only the different alternatives based on each 
decision criterion, but also conduct a comparison between the decision criteria, as to which are 
considered to be more or less important in the decision support process. The target for this 
study is to combine better performance of the machine in a more sustainable and less complex 
way. Thus, the criteria that contribute more to achieve this goal are the machine utilization 
(C2), the complexity of production scheduling (C4) and the sustainability (C6). The criterion of 
cost (C1) is considered to be also significant, as it is included in the KPIs that have been 
investigated in the stochastic optimization process. Lastly, the criteria of initial investment (C3) 
and human resources (C5) follow in the priority ranking. Due to absence of data that could 
support further investigation of these factors, they are not among the first ranking places. They 
are described as they are perspectives that surely need to be taken into consideration for the 
selection of the maintenance policy, but they are not highly ranked. A qualitative prioritization

Discussion and Future Outlook 
7. Discussion and Future Outlook 
This chapter aims to discuss the findings of the thesis from a holistic point of view, clarify the 
selection of the different methods and assess their relevance. Furthermore, future 
recommendations are mentioned, that are linked to the assumptions and limitations of the study.  
Τhe conducted literature review indicates that there are various ways to implement a predictive 
maintenance strategy. The focus is to investigate the predominant machine learning regression 
models and the survival analysis. The regression approach has been selected due to the fact that 
the predicted variable is RUL, which has been structured and defined in a way, that it is a 
continuous variable. In case that the RUL was structured as a binary variable, classification 
ML models, which have been previously explored (Matzka, 2020), could be potentially 
implemented. For this study, the regression models are considered to be a different approach. 
Furthermore, the RUL could be defined in a different way compared to the selected one, 
resulting in potential differences to the dependencies of the data features, and consequently to 
the input to the predictive modelling process. An alternative proposal could be to model the 
RUL by using a specific distribution type (e.g., exponential), as has been described by (Li, et 
al., 2020). The survival analysis is also a recommended method for predictive maintenance and 
it is desirable, as it is considered to be a less complex method, compared to the machine 
learning approach (Hrnjica & Softic, 2021). Lastly, it is recommended to investigate deep 
learning models, since it is likely to achieve a higher predictive accuracy, due to the complexity 
of these methods and their detailed architecture. However, the limitation of available amount 
of data would still be an issue in this case.  
Generally, the major challenge of this study is the limited amount of data and information about 
the different features of the dataset. The fact that the data records are limited, introduce a 
constraint to build the predictive models and implement the stochastic optimization. As 
illustrated in Figure 29, approximately 60% of the given data has been used to build the 
regression and survival analysis models, 33% is being utilized in the stochastic optimization, 
where the different maintenance policies are applied and the performance of the ML and SA 
models is tested. Finally, 7% is excluded to reduce the noise level during the training and testing 
of the machine learning models. In case more data were available, an increased number of 
records would be allocated for predictive modelling, leading to potentially higher accuracy. 
Moreover, more available data would result in investigating each maintenance policy to an 
extended production period, leading to more realistic results, since the optimization algorithm 
would run for an increased number of products. Furthermore, in case more information about 
the different features or the process conditions of the machine were available, the diagnostic 
analytics process would further investigate this information, and would potentially contribute 
to a more efficient and detailed structure of the machine learning models. This could lead to 
more accurate results and improved model performance. 
The stochastic optimization is conducted by assuming some initial values for the maintenance 
costs, due to limited information. These values are set in a way to reflect the significantly high 
==PAGE:77==
 
Page | 67  
 
Discussion and Future Outlook 
failure frequency, so as to provide a comparable baseline for the different scenarios. However, 
the assumption of the initial cost values should be further explored, and it is recommended for 
future research. To address the aforementioned assumption, a variance-based sensitivity 
analysis (Saltelli, et al., 2008) should be implemented, in order to investigate how the 
uncertainty of each individual cost value affects the objective function, based on factor 
prioritization. Thus, the necessity to have more information about the maintenance cost is 
highlighted, since it is a fundamental aspect of selecting a maintenance approach. 
Τhe stochastic optimization is conducted by integrating the randomness function, which 
calculates the number of breakdowns in each scenario (reactive condition), and indicates that 
a breakdown is likely to occur, apart from when the proposed maintenance policy is applied. 
As mentioned in Table 11, the reactive condition is assumed to be based on the product 
sequence, which is not directly linked to the threshold variables of the third and fourth scenario, 
RUL and survival probability. Theoretically, the variable that is being optimized should be the 
same that evaluates the failure occurrence. Ideally, the right approach would require to use the 
ML models to an extensive amount of data and investigate the failure probability per either 
RUL or survival probability values. However, this is not feasible, as the available data are not 
enough for training/testing the ML models, investigating the failure probability and applying 
stochastic optimization. Thus, this is an assumption that needs to be taken, due to the limited 
available data. The failure probability, reactive condition, is considered to be one of the two 
stochasticity factors of the optimization algorithm. Also, the downtime is calculated using a 
random value from a triangular distribution that depends on the type of maintenance. It is set 
in such a way, that the reactive maintenances contribute twice to the downtime, compared to 
the contribution of preventive maintenances. The calculation of downtime is the second 
stochasticity factor of the optimization algorithm. The aforementioned factors are further 
investigated, by applying sensitivity analysis, 50% increase and 50% decrease, in order to 
check, to what extent, the optimization algorithm is impacted.  
The decision support models are structured using typical MCDA methods, that have been 
selected according to the respective literature review. The target is to assess the alternative 
scenarios by introducing decision criteria related to critical factors that could impact the 
maintenance policy selection and need to be taken into consideration. Thus, the criteria of 
human resources and initial investment are included in the models. It is reasonable to say that 
in order to implement a predictive maintenance strategy, additional human and financial 
resources (e.g., setup costs) are needed, so as to ensure a proper implementation and transition 
to this novel maintenance approach. However, as can be seen from the pairwise comparisons 
and the results of each model, the aforementioned criteria are prioritized at a lower level, 
compared to the rest (see Figure 52). Due to data limitations, it has been decided that the criteria 
prioritization should be based on given information and results that derive from the existing 
data.  
Normally, the MCDA methods are conducted after a cost-benefit analysis, CBA, as a 
supplementary tool to assess the different alternatives. In this study, an investigation of the 
==PAGE:78==
 
Page | 68  
 
Discussion and Future Outlook 
maintenance cost has been implemented, but there is no further information regarding 
investment amounts or setup costs for each scenario, so as to be able to have a better overview 
of the different alternatives. It is suggested for future research to implement a decision support 
model that combines CBA and MCDA, assuming that relevant information is provided and a 
cost-benefit analysis can be applied. According to the literature, COSIMA is a decision support 
model that could investigate the selected scenarios, by combining CBA and MCDA and 
providing a composite outcome, that relates to both financial and strategic impacts for the 
decision-making process (Barfod, et al., 2011).  
Additionally, a feasibility risk analysis, FRA, for the different maintenance scenarios is 
suggested to be implemented for future research. This analysis utilizes information from the 
conducted CBA, and by using Monte Carlo simulation to selected features from the CBA, it 
provides information regarding the probability of occurrence for each scenario and highlights 
potential deviations from the initial evaluation. SIMDEC is a decision support tool that takes 
input from both the FRA and CBA and could be used as a future decision-making method. This 
model could indicate the best maintenance scenario by combining both feasibility risks and 
non-monetary or strategic challenges (Leleur, et al., 2010).

Conclusion 
8. Conclusion 
The thesis extensively investigates different maintenance policies and proposes three 
alternative approaches, that are linked to both preventive and predictive purposes. There are 
four scenarios that have been analyzed, including the current approach. This chapter 
summarizes the conclusions of the study, by reflecting on the defined research questions. 
- How can various predictive models be implemented to forecast a breakdown/failure and 
propose a novel maintenance policy?  
It is proved that it is feasible to use machine learning models for predictive maintenance 
purposes, although their implementation highly relies on the type of available data. The 
selected predictive ML models are structured by taking input from the diagnostic analytics. An 
important indicator of the analysis, for this study, is the key dependencies of the RUL. It can 
be seen that the RUL is highly correlated to the accumulated process time and the number of 
processed products, which is attributed to the approach the RUL is structured. It is worth 
mentioning that there is a higher correlation to the air temperature, compared to the rest 
technical features, which means that RUL is affected at a greater extent by the particular 
feature. Thus, the features that need to be part of the predictive modelling are indicated.  
Three regression models have been trained and tested on a small amount of data, due to the 
limited data availability. By comparing their performance, it is found out that the random forest 
algorithm is able to predict more accurately, especially when the RUL converges to zero. 
However, this links to a drawback that relates to the computational performance, as the random 
forest algorithm requires more time, in average, to make a prediction, compared to the rest 
models. Nevertheless, it is the selected model for the predictive maintenance policy based on 
machine learning. On the other hand, the survival analysis is selected as a representative 
statistical method, due to the fact that it is less complex compared to the machine learning 
approach, and it can be used for predictive maintenance purposes as well.  
- How can the maintenance conditions for each proposed policy be optimally defined using 
stochastic optimization, aiming to compare their best possible performance?  
Stochastic optimization is implemented to find the optimal maintenance conditions for each 
proposed policy, by defining different threshold variables to be optimized for each scenario, so 
as to compare them at their best possible performance. The optimization algorithm is structured 
based on the available data and consists of four functions, the policy, the replicant, the cost and 
the randomness function. It sequentially iterates over the products, evaluates whenever 
preventive and reactive maintenance takes place, and calculates the KPIs, according to the 
defined threshold and the type of maintenance. Furthermore, the stochasticity is introduced by 
the calculation of downtime, which depends on both preventive and reactive maintenances, and 
the failure probability, which interprets the likelihood of the machine to break down, regardless 
of when maintenance takes place. The comparison of the scenarios shows that the predictive 
maintenance policy using machine learning (scenario 3) provides the best results. To be more 
==PAGE:80==
 
Page | 70  
 
Conclusion 
specific, scenario 3 reduces the total number of breakdowns by 55% and it achieves a decrease 
in the maintenance cost by approximately 18%. Finally, the downtime is lowered by roughly 
31% and the availability is increased by 8%. It is worth mentioning that the achieved results 
are slightly below the indicated reductions, according to the literature. The fourth scenario 
provides results that are slightly worse than the third, followed by the second scenario.   
Furthermore, sensitivity analysis is implemented for the stochasticity factors, in order to 
investigate their impact to the optimization algorithm. It is conducted by increasing and 
decreasing the respective values by 50%, and compare these two cases with the original 
optimization results. The results of the downtime stochasticity show that the values of the 
objective function are affected, but the threshold values that correspond to the local optimum, 
remain the same. However, the results of the sensitivity analysis for the failure stochasticity 
indicate that both the objective function and the threshold values are affected. More 
specifically, the optimization results differentiate and depend on the increase or decrease of the 
failure probability, meaning that the optimal threshold value slightly changes to higher or lower 
values. This is a clear indication that among all the assumptions that are taken into 
consideration, the failure stochasticity has the greatest impact to all the maintenance policies.   
- How can the different maintenance policies be evaluated, by taking into consideration the 
business perspective/impact using decision support modelling? 
The decision support models are used in order to evaluate the proposed maintenance scenarios 
by introducing decision criteria that are related to critical factors, which could impact the 
maintenance policy selection and need to be taken into consideration, apart from the KPIs that 
have been used in the stochastic optimization.  Thus, the criteria of human resources and initial 
investment are included in the models. The results of the decision support methods are aligned 
with the optimization results and the predictive maintenance using machine learning seems to 
be the best alternative. The fourth scenario follows in the ranking, showing that predictive 
maintenance using survival analysis could be also an alternative solution. The small deviations 
among the criteria weights and final results for Rembrandt and AHP are attributed to the fact 
that these two methods use different calculation methods. However, the input for these two 
models is the same and thus, the final ranking results do not differentiate. It is worth noting 
that the MCDA methods are characterized by a high subjectivity level, since the evaluation 
criteria and the individual scores are selected based on the decision maker's preference and 
feelings (Barfod, 2018).  
ANP model introduces a different way to assess the scenarios, as the criteria are sorted in 
clusters, that are given different priorities, and pairwise comparisons are conducted among all 
the different scenarios and the criteria in the clusters. The final result is impacted by the cluster 
prioritization and all the pairwise comparisons and reflects the concept that the decision-
making process is affected if the significance of the decision criteria change with respect to the 
established alternatives. For this study, the different way that the criteria are clustered do not 
change the final result, indicating that the predictive maintenance using machine learning is the 
best maintenance policy.
