Abstract 
In this study, four synthetic peptides and one hydrolysate were tested for their effectiveness as 
metal-chelating antioxidants in mayonnaise. This project aimed to gain results that could contribute 
to the overall goal of identifying natural antioxidants with activity comparable to synthetic 
antioxidants used in foods today. Results from bioinformatic tools were used in the selection 
process of the peptides and one hydrolysate was included to compare the isolated peptides to the 
hydrolysate with unknown peptides and peptide concentration. The method surface plasmon 
resonance (SPR) was tested to see if it could screen peptides and hydrolysates for metal chelating 
activity. The results from SPR were to be correlated with results from a storage experiment in 
mayonnaise with peptides and hydrolysate as added antioxidants. The storage experiment included 
the analyses of peroxide value (PV), tocopherol consumption, radical formation by electron spin 
resonance (ESR), and secondary oxidation products/volatiles. SPR was performed in a pH 4 buffer 
to mimic the pH in mayonnaise and with a nitrilotriacetic (NTA) chip with Ni2+ as a representative 
of Fe2+. This study did not find a successful way to use SPR as a screening method for metal 
chelating activity at pH 4. Too much non-specific binding was observed for all tested peptides 
rendering it impossible to determine a dissociation constant as a measure for metal binding activity. 
Several attempts to lower the non-specific binding, like increasing NaCl concentration in buffer 
did not sufficiently lower the non-specific binding. More research is needed to establish if the 
method can be modified to successfully measure metal binding activity at pH 4. A 28-day storage 
experiment was performed with the four peptides and one hydrolysate as antioxidants. After 28 
days all mayonnaise samples remained physically stable, but lipid oxidation was observed in all 
mayonnaises on day 28. None of the peptides or hydrolysate showed an inhibitory effect on lipid 
oxidation throughout storage. The measurements of peroxide value (PV) and tocopherols did not 
show any significant difference for any of the peptides compared to the control. Results from ESR 
and secondary oxidation products indicated that the peptide DNHETYE and the hydrolysate <0.8 
kDa could have a prooxidant effect since radical and volatile concentrations were significantly 
higher on day 28 compared to the control.

1. Introduction/background 
The attention and concern to the amounts of waste and how the world manages it has grown in 
recent years. In 2015, the United Nations constructed 17 new sustainable development goals where 
goal number 12 is “Responsible consumption and production”. This includes objectives about 
reducing global food waste at the consumer and retail level and reduce loss in production and 
supply chains. The food industry is a big contributor to waste but initiatives to reduce it are 
growing, for example at the production level by utilizing valuable side streams, or at the retail and 
consumer level where shelf-life is optimized (UN, 2015). 
Further pressure is put on the food industry because a demand from consumers for more natural 
products is growing. This can be seen as a rise in demand for plant-based foods and the so-called 
clean-label foods. The definition of clean label can vary but is normally considered as food with 
no synthetic ingredients. This removal of synthetic ingredients from food is complicated because 
they are so effective, and few natural alternatives have so far been able to compete so far (Cassiday, 
2017).  
The PROVIDE project (Protein valorization through informatics, hydrolysis, and separation) was 
one initiative to find successful alternatives to synthetic food ingredients. The project ran from 
2017 to 2022 and the aim of the project was to find protein sources from productions where they 
were currently not fully utilized, and to develop new high-value food and feed ingredients. With 
the use of bioinformatics, the project intended to identify and predict the functional peptides found 
in proteins from various natural sources like potatoes and seaweed. These peptides could have 
several beneficial functions like emulsifying or antioxidant properties (Hansen et al., 2017). 
The antioxidant activity is especially interesting since lipid oxidation is a big problem in foods with 
high amounts of unsaturated fat and the oxidation will affect both the safety and sensory quality 
negatively. The rate of lipid oxidation can be affected by several factors like the degree of 
unsaturation of the fatty acids, temperature, and amount and type of oxygen available, but also the 
presence of transition metals like iron which can have a prooxidant effect. The use of antioxidants 
can slow down lipid oxidation and especially the antioxidants with metal chelating activity are 
important when it comes to food that is rich in iron and unsaturated fat (Frankel, 2005).

Mayonnaise is an oil-in-water emulsion with low pH, rich in unsaturated fat, and has high amounts 
of transition metals and is therefore very prone to lipid oxidation. To prolong shelf-life, mayonnaise 
is often stabilized with synthetic metal chelators like ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), 
which is highly effective, but due to the demand for clean label foods, the identification of effective 
natural metal chelators is valuable (Velur Balasubramaniam et al., 2022). 
One of the studies performed under the PROVIDE project analyzed peptides derived from potatoes 
and how their antioxidant activity affected the oxidative stability of emulsions (García-Moreno et 
al., 2020). Potatoes have a relatively low protein content (1-2%), but due to the large global 
production of starch, with protein currently considered as waste, it could have big potential if 
functional proteins could be identified and introduced in the food industry.  
Methods to narrow the search for functional peptides and faster analysis of their activity are 
therefore sought after. Bioinformatics is a fast tool that aims to predict the activity of peptides. The 
models can be theoretical or empirical where the model is based on deep learning from previously 
reported results on amino acid sequence and activity. Bioinformatic predictions can reduce time 
and costs compared to other screening methods but are still face some limitations (García-Moreno 
et al., 2021). Another interesting method that could prove to be useful in determining metal 
chelating antioxidant activity of peptides, is Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR). SPR has been 
successfully used to measure peptides’ metal binding activity, but so far only at neutral pH 
(Canabady-Rochelle et al., 2018; El Hajj et al., 2023). Since many foods, mayonnaise included, 
have lower pH (≈ 4), it is relevant to know how the peptide binding changes at lower pH and if 
SPR can still be used as a screening method.

2. Aim 
The overall objective of the project is to test selected synthetic peptides and one hydrolysate for 
their effectiveness as metal chelating antioxidants in mayonnaise to find a successful alternative to 
EDTA in the food industry.  
SPR will be used to evaluate the metal chelating activity of the selected peptides and hydrolysate. 
This activity will be correlated with results from storage experiments in mayonnaise using peptides 
and hydrolysates as antioxidants. This will include measurements of peroxide value (PV), 
tocopherol consumption, volatiles (secondary oxidation products), and ESR (a measure of free 
radicals).  
2.1. Hypotheses 
- Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) can be used to screen synthetic peptides and hydrolysates for 
their metal chelating activity.  
- There will be a correlation between the dissociation constant from SPR and the formation of 
primary and secondary oxidation products.  
- The selected synthetic peptides and hydrolysate will have an inhibitory effect on lipid oxidation 
in mayonnaise without affecting the physical stability negatively.  
- The best performing peptides or hydrolysate have metal chelating activity comparable with EDTA 
in mayonnaise.

3. Theory 
The following section provides the theoretical background for the project. 
3.1. Emulsions 
An emulsion can be described as two immiscible liquids, most often water and oil, where one of 
the liquids is dispersed as droplets in the other liquid. Two overall classifications are used for 
emulsions, the first being an oil-in-water (O/W) emulsion which can be described as oil droplets 
dispersed in the aqueous phase (e.g., milk, mayonnaise, and sauce) and the second where water 
droplets are dispersed in the oil phase which is called an water-in-oil (W/O) emulsion (e.g. butter). 
The liquid that makes up the droplets in the emulsion is referred to as the dispersed phase and the 
surrounding liquid is the continuous phase. To convert the two immiscible liquids into an emulsion, 
homogenization is needed. Homogenization can be obtained mechanically by subjecting the phases 
to high-energy disruptive forces, but under certain circumstances, emulsions can also form using 
low-energy methods that rely on spontaneous formation of droplets when phases are mixed. After 
homogenization, emulsions will often tend to separate back into the two phases after some time 
because they are thermodynamically unstable systems which leads to droplets merging with their 
neighbors when colliding. To form kinetically stable emulsions which will not separate during shelf 
life, stabilizers can be used. A stabilizer can be classified under different names depending on its 
mode of action, but common for all is that it is any ingredient that can enhance kinetic stability in 
emulsions. Emulsifiers are amphiphilic surface-active molecules that can absorb to the surface of 
droplets formed during homogenization and as a result, form a protective layer that prevents 
droplets from merging when getting close to each other. Other stabilizers can be texture modifiers, 
weighting agents, or ripening inhibitors. Choosing the correct stabilizer for the emulsion is very 
important, not only for the kinetic stability but also for texture and other sensory properties which 
affects the quality of food (McClements, 2016). 
3.1.1. Mayonnaise 
Mayonnaise can be described as an oil-in-water emulsion usually produced using 70-80% edible 
oils as a dispersed phase, vinegar (and in some cases also water) as the continuous phase, and egg 
yolk as an emulsifier at the interface. Because of vinegar and lemon juice, the pH is low (≈ 4).

Mayonnaise is most often produced with only vegetable oil like rapeseed oil, but it could be 
beneficial to replace some of the traditional oil with other health-promoting oils such as fish oil 
which is high in omega-3 fatty acids. Other alternatives to traditional mayonnaise on the market 
can be low-fat and vegan mayo. Mayonnaise will most often have added antioxidants like EDTA. 
Sometimes thickening agents like alginate, pectins, and seed gums can also be seen added to 
mayonnaises. Many mayonnaises will often also have preservatives like potassium sorbate added 
to protect against microbial growth even though the low pH has an inhibitory effect against 
microorganisms (McClements, 2016).  
3.2. Lipid oxidation 
Lipids in food can influence several quality parameters like flavor, texture, and nutritional value.  
The major component of lipids is fatty acids and they can be described as carboxylic acids with an 
aliphatic chain. The chain can be without double bonds (saturated) or have one or several double 
bonds (unsaturated). Most vegetable oils are highly unsaturated while animal fat is mostly 
saturated. 
Unsaturated fat is often considered healthier than saturated fat, but the major issue with unsaturated 
fat is that it is prone to lipid oxidation (McClements & Decker, 2007).  
Producers of foods containing lipids need to ensure that the safety and quality of the food remains 
stable after processing and for the duration of the shelf-life. However, this can be problematic due 
to lipid oxidation which will affect the sensory quality of the food but also the nutritional quality 
and safety (Frankel, 2005).  
3.2.1. Autooxidation 
Lipid oxidation is initiated at the double bond in unsaturated fatty acids and the more double bonds 
there are, the faster the fatty acids can oxidize.  
The degradation of unsaturated fat via lipid oxidation is often referred to as autooxidation and can 
be viewed as a chain reaction in three steps: initiation, propagation, and termination. Figure 1 
illustrates a simplified overview of the three steps in autooxidation.

Figure 1: Simplified overview of autooxidation of lipids showing initiation, propagation, termination, and decomposition to

In the initiation phase there is an initiating effect (I) that causes the abstraction of hydrogen from 
the unsaturated fatty acid (LH) and a fatty acid radical/alkyl radical is formed (L●). The initiating 
effect can be radicals formed the from dissociation of hydroperoxides already present as impurities 
in the food, the decomposition of hydroperoxides by transition metals, or exposure to light with a 
presence of a sensitizer.  
In the propagation phase, the addition of oxygen to the radical leads to a peroxyl radical (LOO●). 
Peroxyl radicals have high energy and can promote the abstraction of a hydrogen atom from 
another molecule like a new unsaturated fatty acid. Unsaturated fatty acids are especially 
susceptible to reacting with peroxyl radicals because the carbon-hydrogen covalent bond is weak. 
When hydrogen is added to the peroxyl radical, a fatty acid hydroperoxide (LOOH) and a new 
alkyl radical on a fatty acid are formed. So, the reaction is propagated from one fatty acid to another. 
The termination phase is basically the combination of two radicals to form a non-radical species. 
If oxygen is present, the predominant fatty acid is the peroxyl radical. This means that under 
atmospheric conditions, the termination reaction will happen between two peroxyl radicals, and in

environments with low oxygen, the termination reaction can happen between alkyl radicals to form 
fatty acid dimers (Frankel, 2005; McClements & Decker, 2007). 
Oxidation rates will be lower at the beginning of lipid oxidation and can often exhibit a lag-phase, 
but as radicals build up, it will eventually lead to increased amounts of primary oxidation products. 
The rate of lipid oxidation is affected by several factors like the degree of unsaturated fat, the 
amount of free fatty acids from the beginning, transition metals, and environmental factors like 
temperature and light exposure (Matthäus, 2010).  
The most common transition metals related to lipid oxidation in food are copper and iron. In foods, 
they are often part of protein complexes. Transition metals can be considered prooxidants because 
they can produce radicals via electron transfer or break down lipid hydroperoxides and create 
radicals. The reaction shown in Figure 1 is one prooxidant way the transition metals can work 
where an alkoxyl radical (LO●) is formed, but it can also react as seen below in Figure 2 where a 
peroxyl radical (LOO●) is formed, although this second reaction happening at a much slower rate 
(Frankel, 2005).

3.2.2. Decomposition into volatiles 
The primary oxidation products do not directly influence the sensory properties of the food but can 
mean a decrease in nutritional value or generate precursors to potentially toxic compounds. 
However, when the lipid hydroperoxides break down into secondary oxidation products, it can 
greatly affect the sensory properties. The formation begins with the decomposition of lipid 
hydroperoxides into alkoxyl radicals or peroxyl radicals. These can undergo β-scission where the 
alkoxyl radical or peroxyl radical can abstract an electron from neighboring carbon-carbon bonds 
and cleave the fatty acid chain on either side of the alkoxyl radical. Depending on the original fatty 
acid, the location of the hydroperoxide on the fatty acid, and on what side the β-scission happens, 
different low molecular weight volatile compounds can be formed like aldehydes, ketones, acids,

and alcohols. Their volatility can lead to rancidity perceived as off-flavors, odor,s or color changes 
(McClements & Decker, 2007). 
From Figure 1 it can be seen that transition metals also affect the formation of secondary oxidation 
products because of the decomposition of lipid hydroperoxides. 
When looking at emulsions containing rapeseed oil, fish oil, and peptides, some volatiles are more 
relevant than others. Hexanal is the main product from linoleic acid which is a dominant fatty acid 
in rape seed oil, (E,E)-2,4-heptadienal and 1-penten-3-ol are often the products from breakdown of 
omega-3 fatty acids present in fish oil. 2-ethylfuran can be formed from the degradation of amino 
acids present in the peptides and has a bitterness that is not favorable in food products like 
mayonnaise (Limacher et al., 2008). 
3.2.3. Lipid oxidation in mayonnaise 
Because of the high amount of edible oils, mayonnaise contains a high amount of unsaturated fats 
which makes it prone to autooxidation. Vinegar and lemon juice will result in a low pH in 
mayonnaise which can increase lipid oxidation, and especially the formation of secondary 
oxidation products. This is because low pH can induce the release of iron from the egg yolk from 
the oil/water interface to the aqueous phase (Jacobsen, Timm, et al., 2001). Producing mayonnaise 
with fish oil will further increase the risk of lipid oxidation because of the omega-3 fatty acids 
(McClements, 2016).  
3.3. Antioxidants 
To delay or prevent lipid oxidation in lipid-rich food, and thereby increase shelf-life, antioxidants 
are essential. Depending on the food matrix, the activity of antioxidants can vary greatly. 
Lipophilic/non-polar antioxidants are more effective in O/W emulsions like mayonnaise because 
they will orient themselves at the oil-water interface as a result of their surface activity. 
Antioxidants are often classified as either primary (direct) or secondary (indirect) (Decker et al., 
2010).

3.3.1. Primary antioxidants 
Primary antioxidants are also called free radical scavengers (FRS). FRS can react directly with a 
lipid radical species either by hydrogen transfer or electron transfer and form an antioxidant radical 
as seen in Figure 3 below. 
 
Figure 3: Antioxidative reaction from free radical scavenger (FRS) (Decker et al., 2010, Understanding antioxidant mechanisms in 
preventing oxidation in foods) 
The antioxidant radical is far more stable than the lipid radical because it can use its structure to 
move the radical via resonance delocalization. The effectiveness of FRS relies on its ability to 
donate its hydrogen. An example of a natural FRS is tocopherol (most dominantly α-tocopherol) 
but many effective synthetic FRS are also available (Decker et al., 2010).  
3.3.2. Secondary antioxidants 
Secondary antioxidants can be described as compounds that neutralize prooxidant catalysts and 
slow down the rate of lipid oxidation indirectly. The focus in this study will be on metal chelators 
since this is the main antioxidant mechanism studied here.  
Transition metals are prooxidants and essentially present in all food and therefore an important 
parameter for food producers to control. Food producers can remove small amounts of metals from 
raw material by refining or avoid metal contamination from e.g., process equipment, but the most 
effective way of controlling metals in lipid oxidation is by use of metal chelators (Frankel, 2005). 
Metal chelators can have different modes of action. It can happen by occupation of all metal 
coordination sites, formation of insoluble complexes with the metal, prevention of metal redox 
cycling, and steric hindrance between metals and lipids or intermediates from oxidation like 
peroxides. Citric acid is often used as a natural metal chelator in oils and can have an increased 
effect with tocopherols because of their synergistic effect. However natural metal chelators can at 
this point not compete with synthetic chelators and are often used in combination. 
Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) is a common synthetic metal chelator used in food 
emulsion systems like mayonnaise (Decker et al., 2010). EDTA can retard lipid oxidation by

forming a complex with iron at a molar ratio of 1:1. EDTA is cost-effective and reactive due to its 
high iron stability constant and the broad range of pKa’s making it capable of binding iron in most 
foods. EDTA is better at chelating Fe3+ than Fe2+ which means it can have a prooxidant effect if 
not used in a concentration ratio higher than 1 because Fe2+ is a more active catalyst in lipid 
oxidation (Decker et al., 2010). Concerns have been raised about many synthetic antioxidants 
including EDTA. There is however little evidence suggesting that EDTA should be harmful to 
humans in the amounts consumed from food and other sources (Acceptable daily intake 1.9 mg/kg 
bw/day) (Berkheiser, 2023).  
3.3.3. Peptides as antioxidants 
Proteins are long chains of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. Most often, chains above 100 
amino acids are considered proteins and peptides if they have 2-100 amino acids. A protein’s 
primary structure is the sequence of the amino acids and secondary, tertiary, and quaternary are 
terms to describe the spatial structure of the protein (Explainer: Peptides vs Proteins - What’s the 
Difference?, 2017). 
Proteins are not only essential macronutrients; they can also contain bioactive peptides with several 
beneficial effects on health and food quality. Plant proteins can for instance contain antioxidative 
peptides. Bioactive peptides can be seen as protein fragments that are inactive when embedded in 
the parent protein but can be released with enzymatic hydrolysis.  
Antioxidant peptides can act as both free radical scavengers and metal chelators. The composition 
of amino acids, the sequence, the charge, and the hydrophobicity affect the effectiveness of the 
peptides as antioxidants. Aromatic amino acids like tyrosine (Y) and histidine (H) can show 
antioxidative effect because they can donate an electron to radicals and stabilize via resonance 
structure. Hydrophobic amino acids like leucine (L) and alanine (A) can enhance the solubility of 
the peptide in the lipid and thus accessibility to hydrophobic radicals and polyunsaturated fatty 
acids. Acidic and basic amino acids like aspartic acid (D) and glutamic acid (E) contain amino and 
carboxyl groups in the side chain and can work as hydrogen donors and metal chelators, however, 
this is very dependent on the pH in the food matrix in question (Sarmadi & Ismail, 2010). The location 
of the amino acid is also important, like studies indicating that histidine at the N-termini can have

a metal chelating effect because of the imidazole group (Sarmadi & Ismail, 2010). The length of the 
peptide can also affect the activity, where some studies indicate that shorter peptides perform better. 
This is most likely due to the structure being more open and easily reacting with iron (Yesiltas et 
al., 2022). Peptides from food are generally considered safe and easily absorbed, but when using 
them as an additive it is important to keep in mind that they can have other properties that can 
affect taste (such as bitterness), texture, and color (Agyei et al., 2015). 
3.4. Methods for detecting oxidative stability and antioxidant activity 
Many different measuring techniques are present to measure the degree of oxidization and 
antioxidant activity. In complex food systems, one assay is often not enough to accurately reflect 
how lipid oxidation is inhibited and how effective the antioxidant is. A combination of 
measurements determining primary oxidation products and secondary oxidation products is 
beneficial. The physical stability of the emulsion is also important to consider since this can also 
have an impact on lipid oxidation and the quality of the food for the duration of shelf-life (Agyei 
et al., 2015).  
3.4.1. Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) 
Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) is a relatively new method able to determine the kinetics of a 
binding process, for example between antigen and antibody in an immunoassay or between a metal 
chelator and a transition metal. It is an optical method to measure the refractive index near a sensor 
surface. The three most essential parts of the instrument as the optical unit, the liquid handling unit, 
and the sensor surface. In Figure 4 the principle is illustrated.

Figure 4: Polarized light shine on a sensor chip with a metal coating (here gold film). Analyte pass through the flow channel and 
bind to the immobilized ligand causing a change in the light observed in the optical detection unit (Schasfort, 2017, Handbook of 
Surface Plasmon Resonance). 
As shown in Figure 4, SPR is an analysis where polarized light is shone through a prism onto a 
sensor chip with a thin metal film coating. At a certain angle of incidence, light will excite surface 
plasmons and then induce surface plasmon resonance. This will cause a dip in the intensity of 
reflected light. The resonance angle/SPR-dip is the angle at which the maximum loss of reflected 
light occurs and is dependent on the optical characteristics of the system e.g. the refractive index. 
The refractive index does not change at the prism but will change in the vicinity of the metal surface 
when accumulated mass adsorbs on the metal. SPR can in that matter give real-time information 
on the kinetics of e.g. protein and its adsorption on the surface. SPR sensors lack intrinsic 
selectivity which means that a change in the signal can be due to several things like difference in 
the refractive index, change in concentration, or non-specific and specific adsorption of material. 
To have selective detection at an SPR sensor, the surface needs to be modified with ligands suited 
for selective capturing of the target analyte, but not prone to adsorb any other unwanted 
components present in the buffer or sample.  
In simpler terms, SPR is a measurement of the target analyte captured on a ligand. The ligand can 
either be permanently or temporarily immobilized on the sensor (Schasfoort, 2017). The surface of 
most sensor chips is covered with a matrix of carboxymethylated dextran which improves the 
binding environment on the surface for the ligand or the capturing molecule holding the ligand.

The analyte has two flow channels: a blank without ligand and one with ligand. The phases in an 
analysis cycle can be seen in Figure 5 below.  
 
Figure 5: Direct SPR analysis cycle. (1) Buffer is in contact with the sensor giving baseline. (2) Sample is injected continuously 
(association phase) and a refractive index occurs when the system buffer refractive index is different from the analyte buffer 
refractive index. (3) Buffer is injected (dissociation phase) and ΔR is an indication of the measured response due to target compound 
bound. (4) Bound species from the surface are removed with a regeneration solution (regeneration phase). Following this, a new 
analysis cycle can run (Schasfort, 2017, Handbook of Surface Plasmon Resonance).  
From Figure 5 it can be seen that the ligand is already present on the surface. During the association 
phase, other components in the sample, besides the analyte, can also adhere to the surface as non-
specific. In the dissociation phase, these non-specifically bound components are flushed off. To 
regenerate a regeneration solution breaks the specific binding between ligand and analyte. SPR can 
be used to determine kinetic parameters such as the reaction rates ka and kd and the equilibrium 
constants KD and KA. ka is the association constant and represents the rate of the complex between 
analyte and ligand forming per unit of time. kd is the dissociation constant and expresses the number 
of complexes between analyte and ligand dissociating per unit of time. In the beginning, the 
association reaction is highest, but as the process continues, more complexes are produced and the 
dissociation reaction is increasing. Equilibrium is reached when ka and kd are equal. KD and KA are 
mathematically expressed the same way, but it is recommended to use KD to explain the affinity of 
the analyte to the ligand.

The system buffer used in SPR is usually near neutral pH with sufficient salt and a surfactant like

3.4.2. Physical stability 
The physical stability of mayonnaise can be measured by droplet size. 
The size of droplets in an emulsion can influence the stability and quality parameters like 
appearance, texture, and taste. During homogenization, the size of droplets decreases. Reduced size 
of droplets can enhance stability (reflected in Stokes law), but there must be enough emulsifier 
since the surface area increases as droplets decrease. Measurements of droplet size over time can 
indicate whether the emulsion is stable. Droplet size is normally measured with laser diffraction 
and is based on the principle that a light beam directed through an emulsion is scattered by droplets 
in a well-defined manner – this being that smaller droplets will scatter the light at large angles and 
larger droplets at smaller angles (McClements, 2016). Droplet size is often given as the volume-
weighted mean D[4,3] and the surface-weighted mean D[3,2] of droplets.  
3.4.3. Storage experiment 
A storage experiment can show the oxidative stability in a food and indirectly the antioxidant 
activity. The storage experiment will often consist of measurement of primary oxidation products 
and secondary oxidation products. 
Primary oxidation products can be determined by peroxide value (PV). The PV gives a good 
indication of lipid oxidation in the propagation phase. Measuring PV is based on the principle that 
peroxides oxidize ferro iron (Fe2+) to ferric iron (Fe3+) and it reacts to form a colored complex that 
can be measured photometrically. When following PV over time, a lag-phase might be observed 
before a significant increase because naturally occurring antioxidants scavenge free radicals. 
Furthermore, PV will often reach a point where it stops increasing and starts to decrease. This is 
because the decomposition of hydroperoxides to secondary oxidation products becomes dominant. 
The consumption of naturally occurring antioxidants is also measured. In oils, naturally occurring 
tocopherols (α-, β-, γ- and δ-tocopherol) are present and the consumption can be followed with 
high-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) detection. Usually, the amounts of tocopherols will 
decrease over time, but in some cases, a slight increase can be seen due to regeneration of them.

Secondary oxidation products can be followed with GC-MS where volatiles are captured and 
measured. When analyzing oil and emulsions, the most dominant and significant volatiles are those 
originating from omega-3 fatty acids and omega-6 fatty acids (Frankel, 2005). 
A relatively new method for analyzing oxidative stability is electron spin resonance (ESR) which 
can measure radical formation. It is a sensitive technique for detecting free radicals and can be used 
directly on some foods. Radicals are formed after initiation, in the propagation phase, and when 
hydroperoxides are decomposed. ESR is based on the principle that radicals display different 
paramagnetic properties under a varying magnetic field. During analysis, samples are irradiated 
and the absorption of electromagnetic radiation at different energy levels is measured as a function 
of the applied magnetic field. ESR has limitations because it can only measure directly in foods 
with very low water activity and not in oils and foods like mayonnaise because the radicals are 
very short lived. However, indirect measurement is possible with the use of a lipophilic spin trap. 
Spin traps are diamagnetic compounds capable of forming more stable radicals or also called spin 
adducts. Spin traps are usually in the form of a nitrone like α-phenyl-N-tert butyl nitrone (PBN) 
where nitroxide is formed (Andersen et al., 2005). The amount of spin adducts is proportional to 
the free radicals formed (Boerekamp et al., 2019). The reaction can be described as PBN reacting 
with LOO● radicals forming LOO-PBN which then decompose to LO● radicals, benzaldehyde, 
and 2-methyl-2-nitrosopropane (MNP). MNP can act as a secondary spin-trap with L● radicals to 
form the MNP-L adducts that are measured with ESR. The observed signal in ESR predominantly 
originates from the adducts of MNP and alkyl radicals. When PBN is used as a spin-trap, ESR is 
often used in combination with Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) which determines 
benzaldehyde concentration and as a result of this, the cumulative amount of LOO● that has been 
trapped by PBN can be determined (Merkx et al., 2021). In Merkx et al., 2021, they confirmed that 
ESR was compatible with measuring radicals in mayonnaise and not just limited to oils when using 
a PBN spin trap. Furthermore, the effect of EDTA as an antioxidant in mayonnaise was also 
successfully measured with ESR.

4.1. Selection of peptides used in the project 
The peptides selected for this project are peptides known to be embedded in potato protein. Two 
bioinformatics models provided information on potential activity: a theoretical model for metal 
chelation based on Pearson’s Hard and Soft Acids and Bases (HSAB) theory developed by the 
University of Lorraine and an empirical-based bioinformatics approach AnOxPePred (Olsen et al., 
2020a). In the HSAB model, the ten most abundant proteins in potatoes were subjected to 
theoretical enzymatic hydrolysis. The peptides identified were then ranked (moderate, good, or 
excellent) for their potential to chelate Ni2+, Fe2+, and Fe3+ based on their amino acid sequence. 
Seven peptides were selected for Immobilized Metal Affinity Chromatography (IMAC) analysis 
(Unpublished results). 
Affinity chromatography is used to isolate and detect desired compounds and is based on specific 
and reversible binding of the target compound to a stationary phase. IMAC can retain proteins and 
peptides based on their affinity for metal ions and a retention time can be used as a measure of how 
well the peptides bind iron (Harris & Lucy, 2020).

5.2. Methods 
5.2.1. SPR 
The affinity of peptides/hydrolysate and immobilized nickel was analyzed with SPR. SPR was 
performed on a Biacore X100 Instrument (Cytiva, Marlborough, MA, US) with a nitrilotriacetic 
acid (NTA) sensor chip at room temperature. The method was adapted from Knecht et al. 2009 and 
Canabady-Rochelle et al. 2018. The buffer was changed from a PBS pH 7.4 to 10 mM acetate 
buffer pH 4.0 (2.27 mM sodium acetate, 7.7 mM acetic acid, 15 mM NaCl, 0.005% Tween 20).

A mother solution of 10000 µM was prepared for each peptide/hydrolysate by dissolving in buffer 
and afterward filtered. Seven concentrations (250, 500, 1000, 2500, 5000, 7000, and 10000 µM) 
were made from the mother solution by diluting in buffer. Duplicates were run for concentration 1 
(250 µM), concentration 4 (2500 µM), and concentration 7 (10000 µM). For the hydrolysate, it 
should be noted that the concentration was in glycine equivalents determined with o-Phtalaldehyde 
(OPA). The results from OPA can be seen in Appendix A.3.  
A NiCl2 solution was used to load Ni2+ onto the chip. All measurements were performed with a 
flow rate of 20 µL*min-1. As a reference cell/blank, an NTA flow path without Ni2+ was used. Each 
concentration of peptide/hydrolysate was injected in both flow paths for 270 seconds followed by 
270 seconds of undisturbed dissociation time. Between each concentration, the surface of the chip 
was regenerated for 1 minute with an injection of 500 mM imidazole solution followed by washing 
with a regeneration solution made from 10 mL acetate buffer added 87 µL 350 µM EDTA followed 
by another wash with an SDS solution (0.5% v/v) for 1 minute at 40 µL * min-1 and finally a wash 
with running buffer. Each of the regeneration and washing steps was run twice. Blanks with buffer 
before and after each sample series were used for double referencing during data processing.  
The sensorgrams were processed in the BIAevaluate software. The isotherms were expressed in 
Resonance Unit (RU) as a function of the peptide/hydrolysate concentration. The dissociation 
constant (KD, M) could be determined at equilibrium by fitting the experimental data with the 1:1 
binding model.

hooked up to another nitrogen supply where it was ventilated for 20 minutes at 50 mL/min. Every 
time the method was run, duplicates of Tenax tubes with 1 µL of standard (1.5 mg 4-methyl-1-
pentanol/g ethanol (96%)) was also measured to validate the method. The standard was directly 
added in the Tenax tube with a syringe and ventilated with nitrogen for 5 minutes at 50 mL/min. 
Sample-Tenax tubes, standard-Tenax tubes, and two empty regenerated Tenax tubes were mounted 
on the auto-sampler Turbomatrix 650 (PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) and analyzed with GC-
MS (Hewlett Packard 5890 gas chromatograph; Agilent 5973 Mass Selective Detector) using a 
J&W Scientific DB-1701 column (l: 30m, D: 0.25 mm, 1.0 µm film) with a working temperature 
of 300°C.  
A calibration curve was made with 18 volatile standards of interest with concentrations 0.5, 1, 5, 
10, 25, 50, 100, and 250 µg/g. The selection of volatiles was based on the identified oxidation 
products from day 21 and expected oxidation products from literature (Yesiltas et al., 2022). The 
stock solution of volatile standards (12.5 mg/g in rapeseed oil) was made by weighing 
approximately 40 mg of each standard (double for (E,E)-2,4-heptadienal) into the same container 
and adding rapeseed oil to a total weight of 3.2 g. The stock solution was diluted with rapeseed oil 
to the above concentrations. Fresh mayonnaise was used as a matrix and added 30 mg of each 
standard solution. The calibration curve was also done in triplicates.

6. Results and discussion 
6.1. SPR 
With measurements in SPR, we are presented with results in the form of a sensorgram and a 
sorption isotherm which represents the fitting. The sorption isotherms show response (response in 
ligand flow cell subtracted response in blank flow cell) as a function of the antioxidant 
concentration. From this, a KD is obtained. The lower the KD the higher the binding is between the 
peptide and Ni2+. In this analysis, the maximum number of data points that could be obtained was 
seven because this was how many rack positions were left after necessary reagents were included. 
For reliable results, all seven data points should be part of the isotherm and equally distributed 
around KD. The fitted curve should show a hyperbolic profile indicating saturation at high 
concentrations. If a plateau is not seen, this could indicate that the concentrations chosen are not 
high enough and the results are less reliable. An example of sensorgram and sorption isotherm is 
seen below in Figure 8 for DLH in PBS buffer at pH 7.

6.2.2.5. Oxidative stability discussion 
The storage experiments did not indicate that any of the selected peptides could have antioxidant 
activity in mayonnaise. The results from ESR and volatile secondary oxidation products could 
however indicate that DNH and HYD could have a prooxidant effect. If the peptide contained 
amino acids capable of reducing Fe3+ to Fe2+ this would accelerate lipid oxidation and formation 
of secondary oxidation products. A study (Ka et al., 2016) aimed to evaluate the oxidative 
properties of selected amino acids in both in vitro assays and O/W emulsions. The results showed 
that cysteine (C), tryptophan (W) and tyrosine (Y) had the highest antioxidant properties in in vitro 
assays like 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH), 2,2’-azino-bis(3-ethylbenz-thiazoline-6-
sulfonic acid) (ABTS) and ferric reducing antioxidative power (FRAP) assays. Tryptophan (W) 
did however show prooxidant effect in O/W emulsions by accelerating loss of headspace oxygen, 
having levels of peroxides similar to the control and having the highest concentration of conjugated 
dienes. None of the peptides selected for this study contained tryptophan (W), but it could be 
present in the hydrolysate and explain why this showed a prooxidant effect. It should be noted that 
the study by Ka et al., 2016 was performed in emulsions at neutral pH and with iron concentrations 
far from what is seen in mayonnaise. Also, in Ka et al., 2016 the main effect studied was the free 
radical scavenger activity of the amino acids which further complicates comparison with 
mayonnaise where the ability to chelate metals is the most noteworthy effect. Ka et al., 2016 did

however prove how difficult it can be to predict antioxidant activity in real food systems since high 
antioxidant power in in vitro assays did not necessarily show high antioxidant power in emulsions. 
Tryptophan (W) is considered a redox-active amino acid together with tyrosine (Y) (Prabhulkar et 
al., 2012). Redox-active amino acids have the ability to reduce Fe3+ to Fe2+ and thereby accelerate 
lipid oxidation and formation of secondary oxidation products. DNH does have tyrosine (Y) in the 
sequence, but further studies should be performed to confirm this being the cause for prooxidant 
activity since tyrosine is normally considered an amino acid with antioxidant properties (Varona et 
al., 2023; Yesiltas et al., 2022). 
Peptides derived from plants have been identified to have antioxidant activity in emulsions and 
mayonnaise previously. In Varona et al., 2023, a peptide from potato, used in the same 
concentration, significantly decreased lipid oxidation in mayonnaise presumably because of its 
metal chelating activity. The peptide was longer (length = 14) than any of the peptides included in 
this study but was still considered a short peptide. The pI (0.52) was lower than the peptides 
included in this study. The low pI tells us that the peptide is negatively charged at pH 4 and means 
that there might be an electrostatic attraction between negatively charged peptide and positive 
metal ions making it easier to react with iron (Varona et al., 2023). MDD included in this study 
also had a low pI (0.64) but did not show antioxidant activity in any analysis. The best performing 
peptide from Varona et al. 2023 was rich in aspartic acid (D) and glutamic acid (E). Several of the 
peptides included in this study were also rich in aspartic acid and glutamic acid but did not show 
antioxidant activity which could indicate that the location of the amino acids and the length can 
influence the activity. In Varona et al. 2023 another peptide derived from spinach showed good 
potential as an antioxidant in pH 4 emulsions but did not have the same effect when tested in 
mayonnaise. This again shows how the characteristics of more complex food systems affect the 
antioxidant property of the peptide. A concentration range of the antioxidative peptide from potato 
was also tested in mayonnaise in Varona et al., 2023. The results showed that the potato peptide 
had an optimum concentration of 330 mg/kg. Since the concentration used in this study was lower 
(75 mg/kg), it cannot be excluded that a higher concentration of the peptides/hydrolysate would 
have resulted in some antioxidant activity.

Based on bioinformatics and previous studies, the selected peptides and hydrolysate should have 
good potential as antioxidants, but bioinformatic tools like AnOxPePred are not pH specific and it 
can be difficult to predict how the peptides will react in a complex food matrix like mayonnaise 
compared to the reactivity in an emulsion. Complex foods contain many molecules that can react 
with peptides and modify their structure and affect their activity. Furthermore, viscous systems like 
mayonnaise can make it harder for the antioxidant to react with other molecules (Kamdem & Tsopmo, 
2019).  
7. Conclusion 
It was not possible to use SPR as a method for detecting antioxidant activity of peptides at pH 4 in 
this study. Non-specific binding was too high and specific binding too low and therefore no KD 
was established for any of the tested peptides/hydrolysate. Several attempts were made to lower 
non-specific binding like increased NaCl concentration and increased Tween 20 concentration in 
buffer, but the non-specific binding remained too high. Increasing pH to 5 was also tried even 
though correlation to activity at pH 4 would be more difficult, but this still did not give a result 
from which a KD could be determined. To summarize, this meant that no correlation could be made 
between the dissociation constant from SPR and primary and secondary oxidation products.  
All mayonnaise samples remained physically stable after 28 days of storage. Primary and 
secondary oxidation products were successfully measured but not one of the results indicated that 
the peptides/hydrolysate should have an antioxidant activity in mayonnaise since no significant 
difference from the control was seen in PV and tocopherol experiments. Results from ESR and 
secondary oxidation products did sometimes show DNH and HYD to be significantly different 
from the control but with higher concentrations compared to the control and therefore indicating a 
prooxidant effect. The mechanism behind the prooxidant effect is difficult to establish since 
previous studies found tryptophan could have a prooxidant effect. This could potentially explain 
why HYD had a prooxidant effect but would not explain why DNH shows a prooxidant effect since 
tryptophan is not in the sequence.  
This study underlined the difficulty of predicting antioxidant activity for peptides in complex food 
systems and at low pH. The issue of predicting antioxidant activity has also been observed in

several other studies where antioxidant activity found for peptides and hydrolysates in 
bioinformatic tools, in vitro assays and emulsions did not show the same activity in real food 
systems like mayonnaise. Even though less efficient than EDTA, some studies have been 
successful at measuring antioxidant activity in mayonnaise from peptides present in potato protein 
(Varona et al., 2023), and the need to keep exploring the field remains. However, a deeper 
understanding of how the peptides react in real food systems is necessary to more accurately predict 
antioxidant activity.  
8. Future research 
Several opportunities for future research have arisen from the background of this study. Since SPR 
has shown potential at predicting metal chelating activity for peptides at pH 7, it should be 
examined further if the method could be modified to work at pH 4. A possible solution could be to 
change the chip. Since it is presumed that the problem in this study is from non-specific binding 
happening between the peptide and dextran, it could be beneficial to have a chip without dextran. 
The polyethylene glycol (PEG) sensor chip has a PEG immobilization matrix instead of dextran 
(Cytiva, 2022). If a suitable capturing molecule/linker like NTA can be found and used to bind 
nickel to the surface, this could be a way to measure metal chelating activity without interaction 
between peptide/hydrolysate and the immobilization matrix.  
The bioinformatics tool AnOxPePred is based on large sets of data from experimentally tested 
antioxidant peptides and non-antioxidant peptides, but the model is not pH specific (Olsen et al., 
2020b). It would be useful if the model could be adapted to include data on pH since this and many 
other studies have shown the substantial effect pH can have on lipid oxidation and reactivity of 
peptides.   
Finally, the length and concentration of the peptides could be studied further since some studies 
have found antioxidant activity from peptides longer than the ones studied here and shown a higher 
antioxidant activity when higher concentrations of peptides were added to mayonnaise.

A.3. OPA assay for determining glycine equivalents in hydrolysate 
 
Method description: The concentration of the hydrolysate to be used in SPR was determined as 
glycine equivalents (mM) using an ortho-phthaldialdehyde test (OPA assay). The OPA solution 
was made with 40 mg of OPA dissolved in 1 mL pure methanol. A Borax buffer was prepared 
(sodium tetraborate 100 mM, 1% w/v sodium dodecyl sulfate, pH 9.3), and 100 mg of N,N-
dimethyl-2-mercaptoethyl ammonium was dissolved in a few mL buffer. The two solutions were 
mixed in a 50 mL flask and buffer was added up to 50 mL. A glycine calibration curve was made 
with a 5 mM glycine in water stock solution diluted to 4, 3, 2, 1.5, 1, 0.75, 0.5, and 0.25 mM. The 
hydrolysate was dissolved in water at 1 mg/mL and 20 µL of this solution was mixed with 200 
µL of OPA solution in the wells of 96-well plate. Both calibration curve solutions and 
hydrolysate solutions were done in triplicates and a blank with 20 µL water was also included. 
The plate was shaken for 20 seconds at 500 rpm in an Eppendorf ThermoMixer C (Eppendorf 
SE, Hamburg, Germany) and incubated for 3 minutes away from light at room temperature. 
Absorbance was measured with a BioTek EON Microplate Spectrophotometer (Winooski, VT, 
USA) at 340 nm. The absorbance from the blank was subtracted from the sample absorbance. 
The glycine equivalent for the hydrolysate was found using the calibration curve and expressed 
as mM glycine eq. ± SD.
