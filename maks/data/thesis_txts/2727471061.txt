Abstract 
Alginate lyases are enzymes that can catalyse the degradation of alginate by a β-elimination mechanism. 
Alginate lyases are categorized into different polysaccharide lyase families (PL families). Alginate lyases can 
be divided according to four folds: the β-sandwich jelly roll, the (α/α)n barrel, the α/α toroid + β-jelly roll and 
the β-helix and six types of β-elimination mechanism, including the metal-assisted- β-elimination, the H2O-
assisted β-elimination, the Tyr/Tyr β-elimination, the Tyr’/Tyr β-elimination, the His/Tyr β-elimination and 
the Lys-dependent β-elimination.  
Six new alginate lyase targets from the PL6 family were chosen for production, enzymatic characterization, 
and crystallisation experiments to further investigate the characteristics of the PL6 alginate lyase and the 
determinant of specificity, unknown to this day. The PL6 family members are characterized the β-helix fold 
and the metal-assisted- β-elimination mechanism. The metal-assisted- β-elimination reaction involves a 
positive divalent ion which neutralize the C5 carboxyl group, while the catalytic acid is speculated to be an 
Arg and the catalytic base a Lys.  
Five of the target PL6s were produced and enzymatically characterised. Their specificity was tested on a 
library of different substrates, revealing specificities towards polyG (a polysaccharide consisting of only one 
of the building blocks of alginate: α-L-guluronic acid) or polyMG (a polysaccharide consisting of both α-L-
guluronic acid and the other constituent of alginate: β-D-mannuronic acid, in consecutive blocks of MG).  
The extensive enzymatic characterisation of the new PL6s is a significant contribution to the limited 
knowledge of the PL6s and the catalytic differences between them. The Michaelis Menten parameters 
expanded the range of the determined parameters of other PL6s. This information was combined with 
structural data obtained from both AlphaFold models of the targets PL6s and the four crystal structures 
modelled for SbPL6 (Sphingobacteriaceae bacterium PL6). Along with a substrate free structure, substrate 
complexes with GGGG, GMGG and the first PL6 complex with MGMGMG were obtained.  
Through thorough analysis of the crystal structures of SbPL6, and through comparisons to the existing crystal 
structures of other PL6s and the AlphaFold models of the target PL6s, insights into structural motifs which 
may have effect on the catalysis reaction was gained. The C-terminal loop in SbPL6 was revealed to be 
flexible. Additionally, a comparison of the endolytic and exolytic PL6 models and structures revealed a 
conserved loop in the exolytic two-domain PL6s, which presence may be the determinant of the exolytic 
activity. Likewise, a loop found in the exolytic, one-domain SdPL6 may serve the same purpose.  
Furthermore, the analyses revealed two positions of interest in relation to both the catalytic mechanism of 
the PL6s and determinants of specificity. An Arg in the –1 subsite of G/GM specific PL6s was substituted by 
an His in the polyM specific PL6s and an Asn in the polyG specific PL6s. Moreover, a conserved Tyr in the 
same subsite, was replaced by a His in SbPL6 and was found in a position enabling interacting with the 
substrate. This finding was of interest due to widespread involvement of Tyr as a catalytic residue in the 
alginate lyases.

Resumé 
Alginat lyaser er enzyme, som kan katalysere nedbrydningen af alginat ved en β-eliminations mekanisme. 
Alginat lyaser kategoriseres efter polysakkerid lyase familie (PL). Alginat lyaser kan inddeles efter fire fold: β-
sandwich jelly roll, (α/α)n barrel α/α toroid + β-jelly rol og β-helix og efter typen af β-eliminations mekanisme, 
som inkluderer metal-assisteret- β-elimination, H2O-assisteret β-elimination, Tyr/Tyr β-elimination, Tyr’/Tyr 
β-elimination, His/Tyr β-elimination og Lys-afhængig β-elimination. 
Seks nye alginate lyase mål fra PL6 familien blev udvalgt til produktion, enzymatisk karakterisering, og 
krystallisering, for at kunne studere PL6 alginat lyasernes egenskaber og hvad der er bestemmende for deres 
specificitet, som stadig er uvisse. Familiemedlemmer fra PL6 er karakteriseret af en β-helix fold og en metal-
assisteret β-eliminations mekanisme, som involverer en positiv divalent ion, som neutraliserer C5 
carboxylsyregruppen, imens den katalytiske syre og base menes at være henholdsvist en Arg og en Lys. 
Fem af de udvalgte PL6 blev produceret og enzymatisk karakteriseret. Deres specificitet blev testet på et 
udvalg af substrater, som afdækkede specificitet for polyG (et polysakkerid bestående af en af byggestenene 
i alginat: α-L-guluronsyre) og polyMG (et polysakkerid bestående af både α-L-guluronsyre og den anden 
bestanddel af alginat: β-D-mannuronsyre, i blokke af MG). 
Den udstrakte enzymatiske karakterisering af de nye PL6’ere er et signifikant bidrag til den begrænsede viden 
om PL6’erne og de katalytiske forskelle imellem dem.  Michaelis menten parametrene er en udvidelse af 
spændet for PL6’ernes karakteristik. Denne information blev kombineret med strukturelle data fra både 
AlphaFold modeller af de nye PL6 mål og fire krystal strukturer modelleret af SbPL6 (Sphingobacteriaceae 
bacterium PL6). Sammen med en substrat-fri struktur, blev der opnået substrat-komplekser med GGGG, 
GMGG og det første PL6 kompleks med MGMGMG.  
Igennem grundige analyser af krystalstrukturerne af SbPL6 og igennem sammenligning med eksisterende 
strukturer af andre PL6’ere og AlphaFold modellerne af de PL6 mål, blev der opnået indsigt i strukturelle 
motiver, som kunne have betydning for katalysen. Det blev påvist at det C-terminale loop i SbPL6 var 
fleksibelt. Ydermere, en sammenligning af de exolytiske og endolytiske PL6 modeller og strukturer 
åbenbarede et bevaret loop i de exolytiske to-domæne PL6’ere, som måske er bestemmende for den 
exolytiske aktivitet. På samme måde blev der fundet et loop i den exolytiske, et-domæne SdPL6, som kunne 
have samme funktion. 
Ydermere påviste analyserne to positioner af interesse med hensyn til den katalytiske mekanisme hos PL6’ere 
og rester med betydning for specificiteten. En Arg i –1 subsitet af de G/GM specifikke PL6’ere blev erstattet 
af en His i de polyM specifikke PL6’ere og af en Asn i de polyG specifikke PL6’ere. I øvrigt var en bevaret Tyr i 
samme subsite erstattet med en His i SbPL6, og sad i en position som gjorde det muligt for resten at 
interagere med substratet. Dette fund var af interesse for den vidt spredte involvering af Tyr som en 
katalytisk rest i alginat lyaser.

Aim of the study 
This study has been a part of the DSMADE project (Discovery Specificity Mechanism of Alginate Degrading 
Enzymes), which aims to discover new alginate lyases and to reveal the determinants of specificity, which 
remains to be unknown for several of the alginate lyase families. This is valid for the alginate lyases belonging 
to the PL6 (polysaccharide lyase family 6), where the catalytic mechanism is elusive, and the determinants of 
specificity are unknown. The aim of this study is to investigate the structure/function relationship in PL6 
family, though biochemical characterization, and structural analyses of both crystal structures and AlphaFold 
models, connecting specificity to structural motifs in relation to existing knowledge. To that end, six alginate 
lyase sequences were chosen from a PL6 phylogenetic tree (unpublished, Tandrup et al.) as targets.

1. Introduction  
1.1. Alginate 
Alginate is a linear, unbranched, anionic biopolymer consisting of 1,4 linked β-D-mannuronic acid (M) and 1,4 
α-L-guluronic acid (G). These residues are arranged in patterns of either polyM, polyG or polyMG blocks, with 
the content of either residue varying with the alginate source (1–3).  G and M are C5 epimers, resulting in 
different distinct conformations of polyG and polyM blocks, with the β-(1→4)-linked polyM residues being 
more aligned than the α-(1→4) linked polyG residues (Fig. 1) (4).  
 
Figure 1 – Alginate moieties and blocks. Two or more 1,4 α-L-guluronic acid (G) are linked to form a G-block Likewise, 
two or more 1,4 linked β-D-mannuronic acid (M) are linked to form an M-block. The GM block consist of a G linked to 
an M in one or more repeats. From (5). 
 
Alginate is the fundamental carbohydrate constituent in brown algae and is mainly found in the cell walls (6). 
Alginate is also produced by the bacteria Azotobacter and Pseudomonas (2,3), however bacterial alginate is 
acetylated as opposed to algae alginate (7). Alginate provides strength and flexibility to the algae structure 
(3), while some mucus Pseudomonas aeruginosa strains produce alginate for biofilm formation and 
Azotobacter produce a more rigid alginate staying in close contact with the cell, used in the formation of 
desiccation resistant cells (8).  
Commercially produced alginates are currently only derived from algae sources, where it is extracted in large 
scale primarily from the genera Macrocystis and Laminaria for its wide uses in food, pharma, and medical 
industries (7,9).  
Alginate is biocompatible, non-toxic, non-immunogenic, and biodegradable (1,6), and due to its 
physicochemical properties, a wide range of alginate salts are commonly used as viscosity increasing agents, 
thickeners, and suspension and emulsion stabilizers in the food and pharmaceutical industries.  
Alginates can be formulated into soft elastic gels, fibres, foams, nanoparticles, and multilayers at 
physiological conditions (3). Both alginate fibres and foam are utilized as wet wound-dressings. One type is

polymers, such as collagen, chitosan or polylactide (1,2,9,13).

Alginate is further considered as a promising candidate for future treatment of obesity and type 2 diabetes, 
as is has demonstrated to be able to attenuate the glycaemic response, possibly by modulating gastric 
emptying, inhibiting glucose transporters and intestinal absorption or reducing the activity of 𝛼-amylase, 
minimizing the hydrolysis of dietary starch (2).  
 
1.2. Alginate oligosaccharides (AOs) 
Not only alginate, but also AOs may be used in the future treatments of obesity and type 2 diabetes, as both 
are able to modulate the glycaemic response. Although the precise mechanism is unknown, AOs proved 
effective in lowering body weight, serum lipid, and total cholesterol (2,15).  
Furthermore, AOs prompted growth of Akkermansia muciniphila, Lactobacillus reuteri and Lactobacillus 
gasseri in the gut correlated with improvement of high fat diet induced metabolic disorders. AOs have been 
shown to increase the production of short chain fatty acids in both pigs and mice and may find a future use 
as a prebiotic. Particularly the increase in butyric acid is known to downregulate proinflammatory effects in 
the colon, and thereby protect the colon against colon cancer (15,16).  
The antioxidant abilities of AOs may find future applications in the food industry, as AOs prepared by 
enzymatic depolymerization of alginate can prevent lipid oxidation by scavenging hydroxyl and superoxide 
radicals. This is suggested to stem from the conjugated alkene acid structure formed during enzymatic 
depolymerization (Fig. 4) (17,18).  
OligoG revealed potential for application in treatment of chronic respiratory diseases involving infections 
with Pseudomonas aeruginosa, such as in cystic fibrosis. The AOs induced dose-dependent biofilm disruption, 
while enhancing the efficacy of the antibiotic colistin in a microcolony model. Rather than a direct interaction 
with the biofilm, the oligoG was shown to decrease the production of quorum sensing signals promoting the 
switch to the mucoid P. aeruginosa phenotype (19). Direct use of alginate lyase Alg2A from Flavobacterium 
in combination with the antibiotic ceftazidime has also proven to be effective on P. aeruginosa from infected 
lungs, and were able to penetrate the P. aeruginosa biofilm, delivering the antibiotic and killing the bacteria 
(Fig. 4) (20).

The preparation of AOs is mainly achieved by acid hydrolysis that rapidly depolymerizes alginate (21), but 
this is both expensive and problematic for the environment. Chemical depolymerization may also be achieved 
by the oxidative method, using hydrogen peroxide, producing only water as a biproduct. While the acid 
hydrolysis mechanism is known (22), the oxidative method is yet to be described, but it has been suggested 
that hydroxyl radicals formed by hydrogen peroxide extract protons from M or G residues, leading to 
structural rearrangement of the residue and breakage of glycosidic bond. Other methods such as hydrolysis 
by subcritical water or gamma radiation may also be used to produce AOs (7,23,24).  
A more economical and environmentally friendly and specific method of modulating AOs length and 
constituents is through enzymatic processes. Different mechanisms and specificities of alginate lyases can be 
utilized in production of unsaturated AOs of a specific structure and length (Fig. 5) (25).  
1.3. Alginate lyases 
Alginate lyases are enzymes that catalyse the degradation of alginate by a β-elimination mechanism. The 
glycosidic 1-4 O-linkages between the sugar monomers are broken, producing an unsaturated moiety at the 
new nonreducing end, denoted as Δ (Fig. 5) (26,27). The double bond absorbs light at 235 nm, and the 
product formation can be followed in a spectrophotometric assay (28). The production of unsaturated 
monosaccharides will, however, cause a loss of signal over time, as the saccharide ring is able to open, and 
the double bond is lost (25).

1.3.1. Specificities and polysaccharide lyase families (PL) 
Alginate lyases are classified according to their specificity towards the building blocks of alginate or their 
mode of action. Alginate lyases with the highest specificity towards polyG lyases are classified into EC 4.2.2.11 
and polyM specific lyases are classified into EC 4.2.2.3. PolyMG specific lyases are not classified beyond 
belonging to the polysaccharide lyases families (EC 4.2.2.-). Some alginate lyases can degrade both polyM 
and polyG blocks and are referred to as bifunctional. All known polyMG specific alginate lyases can degrade 
either one or both types of homogenous substrate.  
According to their modes of action, alginate lyases can be further classified into two types: endo- or exolytic. 
Endolytic lyases degrade the alginate chains releasing oligosaccharides as the main products, while exolytic 
alginate lyases act on the ends of the alginate chains, producing monosaccharides or disaccharides (26,27).  
Alginate lyases belongs to several PL families based on their sequence similarity: PL5, PL6, PL7, PL8, PL14, 
PL15, PL17, PL18, PL31, PL32, PL34, PL36, PL39, and PL41.  PolyG specificity has been found in PL5, PL6, PL14, 
PL18 and PL31, while polyM specificity is found in PL7, PL8, PL14, PL31, PL32 and PL36. The two PL-families, 
PL18 and PL6 containing polyG specificity also contain examples of polyMG specificity, while the PL7 family 
with polyM specificity, also has examples of polyMG specificity (29,30).

1.3.2. Mechanisms of catalysis 
The mechanism behind the general β-elimination reaction is theorized to follow 3 steps: 1) the C5 carboxyl 
group is neutralized by interaction with a metal ion or a positively charged residue, reducing the pKa of the 
C5 proton. 2) The C5 proton is abstracted by the catalytic base, creating an enolate intermediate. 3) Electron 
transfer from the carboxyl group results in the formation of a double bond between C4 and C5 and cleavage 
of the O-glycosidic bond. The glycosidic oxygen is protonated by the catalytic acid group with formation of a 
new reducing end (Fig. 6), (20,26).  
 
Figure 6 – The general β-elimination reaction. The positively charged residue or ion (+) reduces the pKa of the C5 proton. 
The catalytic base abstracts proton, leading to electron transfer from the carboxyl group resulting in double bond 
formation and cleavage of the glycosidic bond. The catalytic acid protonates the glycosidic oxygen, with formation of a 
new reducing end. Adapted from (31). 
The β-elimination reaction of alginate lyases can be classified into six types, following the classification of Li 
et al., 2021: Metal-assisted β-elimination, His/Tyr β-elimination, Tyr’/Tyr β-elimination, Tyr/Tyr β-
elimination, Lys-dependent β-elimination, and H2O-assisted β-elimination (20).   
Metal-assisted- β-elimination and H2O-assisted β-elimination 
Metal-assisted- β-elimination utilizes a positive divalent ion to neutralize the C5 carboxyl group, likewise an 
H2O is used to help orientate the substrate in the H2O-assisted β-elimination (20).  
The PL6 family is thought to catalyse the cleavage of alginate by a metal-assisted mechanism, using the metal 
ion to neutralize the carboxylate group. A Lys act as the catalytic base, while the catalytic base has been 
determined to be an Arg residue.  
The addition of 0.2 mM EDTA to a solution of AlyGC, from Glaciocola chatamensis S18K6T, completely 
abolished activity by chelating the Ca2+ in the solution (32). In BcelPL6, from Bacteroides cellulosilyticus 
CRE21, sodium-citrate presumably had the same affect, chelating the Ca2+ and abolishing activity. Both 
BcelPL6 and AlyGC where crystallized with Ca2+ in a conserved Ca2+ binding site (32,33).  
Examples of Ca2+independent catalysis have likewise been found in the PL6 family, where Patl3640, isolated 
from Pseudoalteromonas atlantica T6c, though more active on polyG in the presence of Ca2+, remains active 
when treated with EDTA. Furthermore, Pedsa0632, from Pseudobacter saltans, is not activated by Ca2+, for 
AlyF from Vibrio splendidus OU02, activity is not lost in the presence of EDTA. In both crystallized enzymes, a 
water replaced Ca2+ in the Ca2+ binding site, and the crystallized Pedsa0632 remained active (31,34).   
Tyr/Tyr β-elimination, Tyr’/Tyr β-elimination, and His/Tyr β-elimination  
A β-elimination reaction where a conserved Tyr acts as the general acid is widespread throughout the alginate 
lyase families, except the PL6 family. In these mechanisms, positively charged residues are utilized to 
neutralize and position the C5 carboxyl group. All PL5 family alginate lyases employ a Tyr/Tyr mechanism, 
where the same conserved Tyr acts as the general acid and the general base. This mechanism is also seen in 
Atu3025 from Agrobacterium fabrum, belonging to PL15. A slightly different mechanism (Tyr/Tyr’), where 
==PAGE:18==
7 
 
another conserved Tyr acts as the general base, is seen for Alg17c from Saccharophagus degradans belonging 
to PL17 family. The His/Tyr β-elimination, where a His acts as the general base, is widely found in the PL7 
family, but also seen in alginate lyases from the PL15 and PL39 families (20,26).   
Lys-dependent  
In the alginate lyase Aly36B from Chitinophaga sp. MD30 belonging to PL36, the only hydrophilic residue 
placed in the right vicinity of the O4 in the +1 and -1 subsite accommodating the co-crystallized M4/M3, is 
Lys143. Likewise, a mutation of this lysine abolishes activity, indicating that Aly36B likely possesses a unique 
Lys-dependent mechanism (35).  
1.3.3. Alginate lyase folds 
Based on the structural classification coined by Li et al., 2021, alginate lyases have four different fold types: 
(α/α) toroid fold (PL5), β-sandwich jelly roll fold (PL7, PL14, PL18 and PL36), β-helix fold (PL6 and PL31), and 
α/α toroid + β-jelly roll, (PL8, PL15, PL17 and PL39). Structural representatives from the families PL32, PL34, 
and PL41 have yet to be reported(20,29). 
The (α/α)n barrel fold 
Several antiparallel α-helices positioned counter clockwise makes up the (α/α)n barrel. Only members of the 
PL5 family have been reported to contain the (α/α)n toroid, with four representatives having solved crystal 
structures (20,29). The A1-III alginate lyase from Sphingomonas sp. A1 consists of 12 α-helices making up the 
(α6/α5) barrel (36), while the AlgL from Pseudomonas aeruginosa PAO1 is comprised of an (α5/α5) barrel 
(37).

The β-sandwich jelly roll fold 
The β-sandwich jelly roll fold is represented by 21 crystal structures of alginate lyases, 16 structures belonging 
to PL7, two to PL14, two to PL18 and one to PL36 (29). The β-sandwich jelly roll fold is made up from two 
curved antiparallel β-sheets, placed on top of each other. The outer sheet (SB) is concave and forms the 
catalytic cleft and substrate binding site (Fig. 7), (20).   
The PL7 family contains two examples of dimeric enzymes, representing both the endolytic (AlyC3) and the 
exolytic mechanism AlyA5 (38), and several of the PL7 alginate lyases contain, besides the catalytic domain, 
an additional carbohydrate-binding module (CBM). CBMs have no catalytic activity but have effect on the 
catalysis by facilitating disruption of insoluble substrates, regulating activity, or bringing the enzyme in 
proximity of the substrate. An example is the PL7 family member VxAly7B from Vibrio xiamenesis QY14 that

α/α toroid + β-jelly roll fold 
The structures of the alginate lyases from PL8, PL15, PL17 and PL39 are more complex than other families. 
So far, the least complicated fold is found in the PL17 family, where the structure of Alg17c from 
Saccharophagus degradans sp. 2-40, consists of an (α6/α6) toroid fold N-terminal domain and a β-sandwich 
fold C-terminal domain. Atu3025 from Agrobacterium fabrum, belonging to the PL15 family, consists of one 
(α6/α6) toroid barrel N-terminal domain, but two β-sheet folds, while the multidomain alginate lyase DP0100 
from Defluviitalea phaphyphila belonging to PL39 is yet more intricate, consisting of 8 conserved domains. 
The two catalytic domains consist of an (α6/α6) toroid barrel domain in combination with a β-sandwich 
domain (Fig. 7), (20).  
The β-helix fold  
The β-helix fold is found in two other types of polysaccharide lyases besides the alginate lyases belonging to 
PL31 and PL6, the pectate lyases and the chondroitinases. The main structure of the fold characterized by 
three β-sheets (PB1-3, connected by three turns, in the arrangement PB1-T1-PB2-T2-PB3-T3 (20).  
PsAly from Paenibacillus sp. strain FPU-7 is the only crystal structure representative from PL31 (PDB ID: 6KFN) 
(29). The active site cleft in PsAly is open, and is formed by residues from PB1, T1 and T3. Compared to PsAly, 
the PL6 N-terminal domain (NTD) barrel structure is longer, made up from twelve β-strands, rather the ten 
found in PsAly. Furthermore, the PL6 structures indicate only one cation binding site, opposed to the two 
found in PsAly (Fig. 7 and 8) (40).

1.3.4. The PL6 family 
To date nine PL6 enzymes have crystal structures, two of them being chondroitinases and seven being 
alginate lyases. Out of the seven alginate lyases, two are polyMG specific, while four are polyG specific and 
one is polyM specific. Further 23 PL6s have been characterized, 21 of them being alginate lyases. The 889 
sequences distribute into three subfamilies, with 532 sequences in subfamily 1, 146 sequences in subfamily 
2, and 56 are in subfamily 3 (29). Subfamily 1 consists of both alginate lyases and chondroitinases, most being 
alginate lyases with different substrate specificities and modes of action, while subfamilies 2 and 3 contain 
alginate lyases with only polyMG specificity and an endolytic mode of action (41).  
Active site and the catalytic site ion 
Out of the seven alginate lyase PL6 member crystal structures, two substrate-complexes obtained with native 
enzyme has been achieved, including Pedsa0632 from Pseudobacter saltans in complex with ΔGG and AlyF

atlantica T6c has been crystallized with linear Δ (Table 1). Other substrate-complexes have only been 
achieved with mutations to either the catalytic lysine or arginine, or by complexing with substrate the enzyme 
is not active on (29,30).

Biochemical and catalytic characteristics 
All categorized PL6 sequences belong to the Bacteria domain, with almost half belonging to the class of 
Gammaproteobacteria, and several are found in the Alphaproteobacteria, Bacteriodota, Actinomycetes, 
Planctomycetota, Verrucomibrobiota and Myxococcota (29). Not surprisingly, many of the bacteria have been 
isolated from a marine environment, including both Thalassomonas sp. LD5 (TsAlyPL6) and Shewanella sp. 
Kz7 (OalS6), (43,44), while Pedobacter haimanesis (FsAlyPL6) was isolated from seaside soil (45) and 
Glaciocola chatamensis (AlyGC) from marine sediment (46). These four PL6s have been shown to achieve the 
highest activities spanning a pH range of 7-10 and temperatures between 30-45oC. The optimal of BcelPL6 of 
pH 7.5 from the human gut bacterium Bacteroides cellulyticus falls within this span (47). Both the PL6s 
originating from a human gut bacterium, BcelPL6 and BcAlyPL6 (48), have close resembling catalytic 
parameters, with kcat of 43.4 ± 1.6 s-1 and 54.75 ± 0.83 s-1, respectively, and KM of 1.96 ± 0.18 mg mL-1 and 
1.28 ± 0.06 mg mL-1, respectively. The kcat of FsAlyPL6 is slightly lower being 38.45 s-1. A few alginate lyases 
are neither found in gut or the ocean, for example AlyMG from the soil and water bacterium 
Stenotrophomonas maltophilia. However, the apparent highest activity of AlyMG is reached at pH 8 and 40oC 
and does not stand out from the rest of the characterized PL6s (Table 2).

2. Materials and methods 
2.1. Bioinformatics  
2.1.1. SignalP (6.0) 
SignalP 6.0 was used to predict signal peptide sequences in the protein sequence of the five enzymes, in 
order to learn the sequence of the mature enzyme and calculate its theoretical molecular weight (Appendix 
1, Table 1). SignalP 6.0 is based on protein language models, using information from unannotated protein 
sequences from all domains of life and was trained on 25,393 proteins (53,54).  
2.1.2. Clustal omega MSA (Multiple Sequence Alignment) 
The Clustal omega MSA tool avaible from EMBL-EBI (European Molecular Biology Laboratory’s European 
Bioinformatics Institute) was used to produce an MSA of both the target PL6 sequences and the sequences 
of the PL6’s with crystal structures (55,56). 
2.1.3. InterProScan  
The InterProScan tool avaible from EMBL-EBI (European Molecular Biology Laboratory’s European 
Bioinformatics Institute) was used to make sequence-based predictions on the target PL6s sequences (57,58). 
2.1.4. The CAZy database 
The CAZy database is a freely available curated database of classified carbohydrate-active enzymes. The CAZy 
database was used procure information and statistics about the PL families and to identify available PL6 
sequences, solved crystal structures and statistics (29,30). 
2.1.5. AlphaFold (ColabFold) 
ColabFold was used to predict the modeles of the five target enzymes and one ColabFold model was used as 
search model in molecular replacement to solve the first crystal dataset. ColabFold is a freely accessible 
platform for protein structure prediction, which uses a homology search by MMseqs2, to build a multiple 
sequence alignment (MSA) as input for AlphaFold2. The platform run on Google Colaboratory (59,60).

Transformation of PL6 containing plasmids using BL21 cells

Enzyme production

kanamycin and incubated for 16 h at 37oC under constant shaking at 180 rpm. The 10 mL cultures were added

density at 600 nm) between 0.6 and 1. The cultures were then incubated overnight at 16oC, under constant 
shaking at 180 rpm. The cells were harvested by centrifugation at 5000 x g for 10 min, after which the 
supernatant was removed.

General conditions of the enzymatic assays 
The enzymatic reactions of alginate lyases are followed by measuring the absorbance of the newly formed 
double bond in the new non-reducing end at 235 nm (69). The absorbance measurements were performed 
on either a PowerwaveXS Epoch plate reader (at 22oC) or a PowerwaveXS BIO-TEK Plate reader (at 22-50o).

residue of 6150 M-1 cm-1 (70) and a light path of 0.557 cm.  
In all enzymatic assays a reaction volume of 200 µL was used, and the reactions were initiated by adding 50 
µL substrate to 150 µL enzyme mix. Negative controls of each experiment were made by adding 50 µL 
substrate to 150 µL MiliQ water.

Data processing 
The XDS program package (77) was used for the data processing of the recorded datasets. Overall data 
processing consists of two steps: integration, and scaling and merging. The integration process can be further 
separated into four steps: 1) spot finding, 2) indexing, 3) parameter refinement, and 4) integration. XDS finds 
the diffraction spots in all the diffraction images, (other programs may only find a selection) to be indexed in 
the next step.  
Indexing means that each reflection is given a 3D identifier, referred to as Miller indices, h, k, and l. Indexing 
is based on the obtained unit cell parameters and parameters such as crystal to detector distance and the 
wavelength of the incident beam.  
In the parameter refinement step these parameters are refined, and the differences between calculated and 
observed spots are minimized, while the intensities of partial reflections are used to determine the accurate 
center of the reflection. When the position of the spots is known, the intensities of the spots are measured 
in the integration step. 
The final process of scaling and merging puts the measured intensities into a common scale and merges 
symmetry-related measurements making it easier for structure solution programs to deal with the data (78).  
==PAGE:34==
23 
 
Molecular replacement 
In the data processing step, information such as unit cell dimensions, resolution and the approximate space 
group is obtained. The unit cell is the smallest part of the crystal, which can generate the complete crystal 
lattice by translation. The asymmetric unit related to the unit cell by symmetry operations, which defines the 
space group of crystal. The precise space group of the crystals were obtained from the space group search, 
automatically performed when importing the datasets to CCP4i2, the program suite used to solve the crystal 
structures (79).  
In order to solve the crystal structure both the amplitude (|Fhkl|) and the phase (eiαhkl) of the scattered x-rays, 
making up the structure factor, must be known: 
𝐹(ℎ𝑘𝑙) = |𝐹ℎ𝑘𝑙| ∗𝑒𝑖𝛼ℎ𝑘𝑙 
The recorded intensities of the reflections are proportional to the amplitude of waves; however, the phase 
information is lost in the diffraction experiment. Different techniques exist to solve this so called “phase 
problem”, in this project the missing phases were obtained by molecular replacement using the program 
MOLREP. This method obtains the phase information from a previously solved homologous protein, using 
the Patterson function to translate and rotate solved crystal structure into a position simulating the structure 
of the protein of interest best.  
Together with the calculated number of molecules in the asymmetric unit, an AlphaFold model of SbPL6 was 
used as the search model of the first SbPL6 structure, SbPL6.1. The SbPL6.1 structure was then used as search 
model for the second model SbPL6.2, and finally SbPL6.2 was used as the search model of the third solved 
structure, SbPL6.3 (76,80).  
Refinement 
From the experimentalle determined amplitudes and the phases obtained by molecular replacement, a 
three-dimensional density map of the average unit cell is calculated using the inverse Fourier transform of 
the structure formula:

ℎ𝑘𝑙
𝑖(ℎ𝑥+ 𝑘𝑦+ 𝑙𝑧)] 
ρ(x, y, z) is the density in real space with the coordinates x, y, z, while hkl is the reciprocal space coordinates. 
V is the unit cell volume, while the term exp[-2πi(hx+ky+lz)] is the three-dimensional phase (76).   
Two density maps are calculated, the 2Fo-Fc density map and the Fo-Fc difference density map. The 2Fo-Fc 
density map uses twice the observed structures (Fo) and subtracts the search model derived structure factors 
(Fc), which results in a map where the observed structure factors are weighted more, resulting in missing 
density where atoms in the search model is missing or placed different in the experimental map and empty 
density, where atoms are found in the experimental map, but not in the search model. In the difference 
density map good agreement between the data and the search model result in no density. Atoms in the 
search model which do not exist in the data, result in a negative red coloured density peak in the map, and 
atoms found in data, but not in the search model, result in a positive density peak, coloured green (81).

maps is improved in an iterative process both manually and automatically. Manual refinement was 
performed in Coot (82) and the automatic refinement were

3.1.3. AlphaFold models 
The quality of the produced models highly depends on the input MSAs, however, high quality predictions 
require a little above 30 diverse sequences to produce. By representing the AlphaFold models coloured by B-
factor, the lDDT (predicted local distance difference test) score is visualized, providing the confidence in the 
local placement of each residue. An lDDT score above 90 results in a model of high accuracy (red colour), 
while a score between 70-90 (orange-yellow) results in a generally correct protein backbone prediction. An 
lDDT score below 50 (blue-green) should not be interpreted but is usually a good predictor of disorder. A plot 
of the PAE score, the distance error for every pair of residues, provides a measure for the confidence in the 
long-distance placement of pairs residues. Further, an MSA plot is provided, visualizing MSA sequence 
coverage (59,60,86).   
The MSA sequence coverage was above 1000 sequences for AaPL6, SbPL6 and WhPL6, though for both SbPL6 
and WhPL6, the sequence coverage decreased towards zero in the N-terminal (Fig. 16 and Appendix 3, Fig. 
1). The predicted lDDT score for both AaPL6, SbPL6 and WhPL6 was generally above 80, however both the C-
terminals score decreased to around 40. The decrease in lDDT score of the C-terminal were likewise seen in 
the rest of the models. In both AaPL6 and SbPL6 a substantial decline in the lDDT score is also found in the 
N-terminal, corresponding to the N-terminal loop (Fig. 17 and Appendix 3, Fig. 1). The placement of the C- 
and N-terminal of all models in relation to the rest of the model is likewise low, signified by a PAE score of 30 
(Fig. 17 and Appendix 3, Fig. 1) (86) The SbPL6 AlphaFold model was used as the search structure for 
molecular replacement for SbPL6/FREE..

for the rest of sequences, and were particular low beyond residue 400, which results in a low lDDT score and

4. Discussion 
4.1. Production and purification 
Crystals were achieved for SbPL6, while the issues with crystallisation of the rest of the target PL6s, might 
have been because of impure or too low concentration enzyme samples used in the screening. The SDS-PAGE 
analysis of the fraction pooled for the protein samples, did not reveal issues with impurity. However, 
“shoulders” on the peaks of the AaPL6 and SdPL6 in the chromatogram could indicate that these samples 
contained at least two different populations AaPL6 and SdPL6 (Fig. 18, pg. 28 and Fig. 21 pg. 31, Results), 
respectively, possible due to dimerization. Still, the yield of each production was rather low ranging from 5.7-
13.2 mg L-1 culture, at least compared to the production of BcelPL6 averaging 30 mg L-1 culture (33). An 
optimization of the production and purification, though time consuming, could be valuable for future studies. 
Furthermore, a supernatant sample was not taken from the production of FsPL6, SbPL6, SdPL6 and WhPL6, 
though these would have been helpful in confirming the solubility of the target protein and should be 
included in further productions.  
4.2. Biochemistry and kinetics 
While enzyme purity is of importance, the enzymatic assays are less sensitive to smaller impurities, and not 
as dependent on high-concentration enzyme stocks as crystallisation. The initial test of activity indicated 
differences between the specificity of the target PL6s, either having highest activity towards polyG (AaPL6, 
SdPL6 and WhPL6, or towards polyMG (FsPL6 and SbPL6). All the enzymes showed activity on at least one 
other substrate, mainly polyG or polyMG, notably, significant activity on algG was seen for both SdPL6 and 
WhPL6. These evaluations were based on different concentrations of polyG and polyMG, using only half the 
concentration of polyMG, making it uncertain it uncertain whether the highest activity was towards polyMG 
or polyG. The assays should have been repeated on equal concentrations of each substrate and the kinetic 
assay should have been performed on both substrates, particular in the case of WhPL6 and FsPL6, where the 
activity for WhPL6 on polyG is only slightly higher than polyMG (Fig. 23, pg. 33, Results). 
The pH optimum was for all targets, except FsPL6, found around to be either 7 or 8. FsPL6 had a pH optimum 
of ≥ 9, and should had been tested at pH above 9, however an optimal buffer for testing at higher pH were 
not found. Compared to other characterized PL6 family members, the results are not surprising. Only 
FsAlyPL6 has a pH optimum outside the 7-8 range, with a pH optimum of 10.0 (49) (Fig. 24, pg. 34, Results). 
The activity of both AaPL6 and SbPL6 decrease with increasing concentration of NaCl, and the optimum is 0 
mM NaCl and 50 mM NaCl, respectively. While SbPL6 was isolated from a freshwater bacterium, AaPL6 was 
isolated from a marine bacterium. On the other hand, both SdPL6 and WhPL6 which are isolated from marine 
bacteria too, have higher salt tolerance, with NaCl optima of 100 and 200 mM (Fig. 24, pg. 34, Results). All 
the enzymes showed increased activity with the addition of Ca2+. The optima were found at a Ca2+ 
concentration of 1-4 mM, and while the activity of WhPL6 and FsPL6 remained > 70% without Ca2+ present, 
both AaPL6, SbPL6 and SdPL6 only retained < 10% activity, indicating calcium dependency (Fig. 24, pg. 34, 
Results). The activity of AaPL6, SdPL6, FsPL6 and WhPL6 did increase with increasing temperature, yet SbPL6 
showed no significant change in activity with the increasing temperature. Both SbPL6, SdPL6 and WhPL6 
showed maximum activity at 50oC, while AaPL6 had temperature optimum at 40oC, and FsPL6 at 22oC. Other 
characterized PL6 have a temperature optimum in the range 30-45oC, a span only including (Fig. 24, pg. 34, 
Results). Small differences in the applied procedures could affect the perceived temperature optima, leading 
to the differences seen between the observations. Furthermore, only five other observations exist using the 
same method, therefore there is a possibility of the span expanding, when more observation from other PL6s 
are included.

The Kms ranging from 0.113 to 1.24 mg mL-1 does not stand out from the Kms determined for the PL6 family 
members, BcelPL6, BcAlyPL6 and OalS6. They were determined to have a Km of 1.96, 1.28 and 0.37 mg mL-1, 
respectively. The more extreme Km value of FsPL6 (on polyMG) could be explained by a poor fit of the data 
and the Michaelis Menten model, with the R2-value of 0.929, and also by the assay not reaching high enough 
substrate concentration, to achieve saturation, however the same could not explain the low Km of 0.0048 mg 
mL-1 found for SbPL6. Not reaching substrate saturation may also be the reason for the size of the Vmax of 
FsPL6 of 17.0 µM s-1 (polyG) and 8.47 µM s-1 (polyMG), leading to kcat values of 27.4 s-1 and 30.9 s-1, 
respectively. However, these two kcat values are the one closest matching the reported kcat values of BcelPL6, 
BcAlyPL6 and OalS6, of respectively 43.4 s-1, 54.75 s-1 and 38.45 s-1 (33,42,52). Substrate saturation was 
neither reached for WhPL6, which again could have influenced the Vmax of 3.29 µM s-1, resulting in the kcat of 
14.1 s-1. In the lower end the Vmax values of AaPL6, SbPL6 and SdPL6 were determined to be 0.155 µM s-1, 
0.0783 µM s-1, and 0.640 µM s-1, respectively, which resulted in kcat values 0.439, 0.783, and 1.11 s-1, 
respectively. The fit to the Michaelis Menten model for SbPL6 was the worst fit of all the Michaelis Menten 
plots, with an R2 value of 0.885, while substrate saturation for AaPL6 which could have influenced the 
obtained kinetic parameters (Fig. 25, pg. 37 and Table 10, pg. 36, Results). High substrate concentrations 
could not be achieved on polyMG due to the lack of availability, and the kinetic assays therefore did not 
achieve substrate saturation. This could have affected the Michaelis Menten parameters for these two 
enzymes, nonetheless, the results overall indicate wide variations in the substrate affinity and rates of 
depolymerization for the PL6 family.  
4.3. X-ray crystal structures 
The substrate density in the models SbPL6/GGGG and SbPL6/GMGG indicated either the content of an M 
residue or a distorted G in the second moiety from the non-reducing end. Though the substrates used for 
soaking was pure, there is a possibility of contamination by a tiny amount of GGMGGG and GMGG oligomers, 
caught by the enzyme with high affinity to the GM or MG in this position. The activity of the crystals could 
have been completely abolished, perhaps due to the lack of Ca2+ as the activity was seen to drop <10% 
without Ca2+. Yet again, an interesting explanation for the difference in activity on polyG and polyMG could 
be that the second G must be distorted into an M-like conformation to perform the glycosidic bond cleavage, 
and the additional energy needed to perform the distortion leads to a decreased rate of polyG 
depolymerization compared to polyMG (Fig. 29, pg. 45, Results). 
4.4. Structure function relationships 
The flexibility of the five SbPL6 loops surrounding the active site groove, indicated by increased B-factors of 
the models, could not be distinguished from the background of B-factor plot, which could indicate less 
noteworthy flexibility of the loops (Fig. 30, pg. 46 and Fig. 31, pg. 47, Results). Still, the interesting position 
of the loops introduce the question of whether these loops play a part in either the catalysis or the substrate-
binding. However, low RMSD differences were found between the SbPL6 without substrate bound 
(SbPL6/FREE) and the SbPL6 complexes (SbPL6/GGGG, SbPL6/GMGG and SbPL6/MGMGMG), indicating that 
no considerable structural change is seen between the SbPL6 with substrate bound and the substrate free 
SbPL6. The rigidity observed in the SbPL6 structure could, on the other hand, also be affected by crystal 
contacts, preventing movement.  
The highest Cα RMSD difference was seen between SbPL6/FREE and SbPL6/GGGG and might explained by the 
difference in the search models used for molecular replacement (Fig. 35, pg. 52, Results). For SbPL6/ FREE 
the search model was the AlphaFold model, which had some quality issues (Fig. 35, pg. 52, Results). The 
search model used for SbPL6/GGGG was SbPL6/FREE, likewise for SbPL6/GMGG, SbPL6/GGGG was used as 
search model, and so SbPL6/GMGG was used as search model for SbPL6/MGMGMG. Using the higher quality, 
==PAGE:68==
57 
 
solved crystal structures as search models might be one of the reasons to produce higher quality and more 
similar structures.          
High B-factors were additionally localised to the C-terminal loop, correlated with large RMSD differences 
between the structures (Fig. 31, pg. 47, Results). The flexibility of the C-terminal loop correlated with 
decreased density of the residues in the loop, leading to differences in the modelling of these residues 
between the model, most likely resulting in the large RMSD differences. The high flexibility of the C-terminal 
loop introduces the question of what the function of this loop is, whether it has a stabilizing effect on 
structure or it could be a remaining part of an evolutionary lost C-terminal domain.  
Both AaPL6 and VbPL6 did not contain the positively charged patch on the surface, characteristic to the 
binding site of PL6s (Fig. 33, pg. 49, Results). An explanation could be that the active was not placed in the 
area corresponding to where the projected substrate from SbPL6 bound. However, both in AaPL6 and VbPL6, 
the catalytic Arg and Lys is placed within 4 Å of the projected GGGG (from SbPL6/GGGG). It is unlikely that 
the results are caused by a medium or poor quality AlphaFold model, while the catalytic Arg and Lys are 
placed so precisely. Therefore, this might indicate that alginate is not preferred substrate of the enzymes, 
and only a side-activity, while mainly depolymerizing less polyanionic substrate.  
A correlation between the number of domains and exolytic activity was found for the PL6s with crystal 
structures, where the three exolytic enzymes contained two domains and the four endolytic enzymes 
contained one domain. This correlation could not be demonstrated for SdPL6, where the initial enzyme assay 
indicated exolytic activity, though the AlphaFold prediction only contained one domain. In the analysis of the 
exolytic active site, a conserved loop from the C-terminal domain of the exolytic PL6s was found to protrude 
into the active site, possibly blocking further access of the substrate leading to exolytic cleavage. This could 
of cause not be demonstrated for the single-domain SdPL6, however in the same manner, a loop in a similar 
position was protruding from the β-helix into the active site and might have the same function as the 
conserved loop in the two-domain exolytic PL6s (Fig. 34, pg. 50, Results). The progression curve for SdPL6 
indicated the loss of signal was smaller than compared to AaPL6 (Fig. 23, pg. 33, Results). This might be due 
to a smaller production of monosaccharides in combination with a higher production of oligosaccharides, 
which could mean that the loop is less efficient in blocking the access, however a product analysis is needed 
to further substantiate this theory.  
4.5. The PL6 active site 
The substrate-interacting residues of SbPL6 were demonstrated to consists of three Arg, one in subsite –2 
and two in subsite –1, the two Arg in subsite –1 matching the two Arg in the active site platform common to 
PL6s, deduced by Violot et al. (2021) (31). Furthermore, both the position of the Ser and the His in the SbPL6 
active site matches the PL6 active site platform. The only difference is the Tyr located in subsite -1, which is 
replaced by a His in SdPL6. Interestingly, the Tyr substitution is not replaced by another residue containing a 
hydroxyl group, but rather with a His, able to hydrogen bond to the carboxyl group in either substrate. The 
low Km, could be explained by a higher affinity and stronger binding of the substrate, which could have 
connectiont to the His substitution.  
The 1 Å difference between the position of Arg346 in SbPL6/FREE and the SbPL6 complexes, moving it into 
hydrogen bonding reach of the C3 hydroxyl group of the sugar moiety in subsite –2, indicate a small 
movement due to substrate binding (Fig. 35, Results). However, not much other movement was indicated 
for the SbPL6 complexes, which point more in the direction of a relative rigid active site.  
Comparing the non-conserved active site residues between the PL6s with crystal structures grouped by 
specificity, indicated three positions of interest (Fig. 36, pg. 53, Results). In subsite -1 the G/MG specific PL6s 
==PAGE:69==
58 
 
have an Arg, while the polyG specific Patl3640 has a His and the polyM specific BcelPL6 has an Asn. However, 
both the Asn and His are placed too far from the substrate to be able to have an interaction, while the Arg in 
the G/MG specific PL6s is able to interact with the carboxyl group in both M and G moieties accomodated in 
subsite –1. The Arg could be involved in either proton abstraction or in distorting the G into an M like 
conformation, making the enzyme able to catalyse the cleavage of both polyMG and polyG. However, the 
Arg is not found in the polyG specific Patl3640, which for this theory to be right, G specificity should have 
occurred twice by different mutations in the evolution of PL6s. 
In a third active site alignment (Fig. 37, pg. 54, Results), the non-conserved residues between the target PL6 
AlphaFold models and SbPL6/GMGG was analysed, based on grouping of the residues according to specificity. 
In the +1 subsite the polyMG specific FsPL6 contain a His, while the rest of the target PL6s contain an Asn, 
and while the Asn points towards the carboxyl group of the moiety in subsite +1, the His is able to interact 
with the C3 hydroxyl group at the subsite +2. This FsPL6 His is not rediscovered in the polyMG specific PL6s 
with crystal structures, which all contain an Asn like the rest of the target PL6s. This indicate that this His is 
not related to polyMG specificity. No other clear differences in active site residues which could influence the 
specificity are revealed from the alignment of the target PL6 AlphaFold model. This is probably due to the 
inexact placement of side chains in the AlphaFold models, which is supported by the finding of several single 
positions without counterparts in the rest of the target PL6s.  
The conserved Tyr replaced by a His in SbPL6, in subsite -1 of the target PL6 active site alignment, is 
interesting. This is because of the placement, with the Tyr pointing the hydroxyl group towards the substrate 
moiety in subsite –1, but also because of the widespread involvement of Tyr in the β-elimination reaction. In 
alginate lyases from other PL families, different β-elimination reactions have a Tyr indicated to both as a 
catalytic base and catalytic acid, while His has been found to take part in the reaction as a base. Though Lys 
has been established as the catalytic base, further investigation in the function of this Tyr/His would be 
interesting. 
The addition of the crystal structures and complexes of SbPL6 to the limited number of PL6 crystal structures 
and especially complexes, has both substantiated former established theories on the structural features 
characteristics to PL6, while new theories on substrate interaction and catalytic mechanism have been 
proposed. Furthermore, the enzymatic data is a significant extension to the limited knowledge on the 
enzymatic characteristics of the PL6 alginate lyases. Furthermore, the new data points on the PL6 active site, 
together with the enzymatic data, constitute a substantial contribution to the DSMADE project and the 
pursuit to expose the determinants of specificity in PL6 alginate lyases.

5. Conclusion 
Five priorly uncharacterized target alginate lyases belonging to the PL6 family were produced and 
biochemically characterised. Their specificity was tested on a library of substrates and indicated substrate 
specificities towards polyG and polyMG, commonly found in the PL6 family. The observed Michaelis Menten 
parameters comparable to other PL6s included the Kms of SdPL6, with 0.113 mg mL-1, of AaPL6 with 0.991 
mg mL-1, and WhPL6 with 1.24 mg mL-1, and the kcat of FsPL6 on polyG and polyMG of 27.4 s-1 and 30.9 s-1, 
respectively. Michaelis Menten parameters were found to be outliers in relation to the established 
parameters of other PL6s included Kms of SbPL6 and FsPL6 (on polyMG) determined to be 0.00482 and 11.2 
mg mL-1, and the kcats of AaPL6, SbPL6 and SdPL6, which were derived to be 0.439, 0.783, and 1.11 s-1. 
The target PL6s were modelled using AlphaFold, which in agreement with InterPro predictions, indicated that 
both one- and two-domain alginate lyases were found among the targets. A correlation between number of 
domains and a either exolytic or endolytic mechanism could not be substantiated, however a correlation 
between exolytic activity and a conserved loop in the two-domain PL6s were shown. Furthermore, a similar 
loop was shown in the single domain SdPL6.  
Crystallisation of SbPL6 was achieved and from the datasets collected, four datasets with the highest quality 
were selected. The four datasets chosen included SbPL6 without the presence of substrate in the active site, 
and three complexes modelled with GGGG, GMGG and MGMGMG. The datasets were collected at 
resolutions of respectively 2.08, 1.49, 1.16 and 1.41 Å, for SbPL6/GGGG, SbPL6/GMGG, SbPL6/MGMGMG, 
and the refinement process led to Rfree and Rwork values of 0.29, 0.27; 0.20, 0.19; 0.19, 0.17 and 0.22, 0.20.  
The SbPL6/GGGG and SbPL6/GMGG complexes indicated either a distortion of the second G moiety in GGGG, 
or that the second G was in fact an M moiety, originating from impurities in the soaking substrate. The SbPL6 
models further indicated a flexible C-terminal loop, with an unknown function, but otherwise a rather rigid 
structure and active site. From the alignments of the active sites of the SbPL6 models, and comparison to 
both the other existing PL6 crystal structures and target PL6 AlphaFold models, several residues which might 
have an influence on the specificity of the PL6s were indicated. The two most interesting positions with 
differences, were the –1 subsite Arg of the G/GM specific PL6s, substituted by an His in the polyM specific 
BcelPL6 and an Asn in the polyG specific Patl2640, and the conserved Tyr in the same subsite, replaced by 
His in SbPL6.

6. Further studies  
 
From the crystal structures of SbPL6 several structural elements and residues suspected of having an effect 
of the catalysis of alginate were identified: the C-terminal loop, Arg277 and His316, both in subsite –1. It 
would be of interest to perform activity, stability, and crystallisation studies of an SbPL6 mutant missing the 
C-terminal loop to study its importance in relation to stability and catalysis. Likewise, activity, stability, and 
crystallisation studies of SbPL6 mutants with either a substituted Arg or His could indicate if they are of 
importance to the catalytic mechanism. A product analyses could help substantiate the indicated exolytic 
activities, and further indicate exolytic or endolytic activity of the target PL6s.  
From a broader perspective, more data points on the specificity, biochemical characteristics and structure 
will further help to discover the determinants of specificity in the PL6 family.
