Preface 
For the purpose of this thesis, it is important to acknowledge the difference between biological sex 
and gender. These terms are often used interchangeably but are two distinct things.  
Whilst there are many definitions from different sources; sex, as defined by the UK Government and 
in-line with other European conventions, refers to “the biological aspects of an individual as 
determined by their anatomy, which is produced by their chromosomes, hormones, and other 
interactions. Usually assigned at birth.” (Office for National Statistics, 2019) They define gender as “a 
social construction relating to behaviours and attributes based on labels of masculinity and 
femininity; gender identity is a personal, internal perception of oneself and so the gender category 
someone identifies with may not match the sex they were assigned at birth.” (Office for National 
Statistics, 2019)  
 
However, in discourse related to inequality between the biological sexes, it is often gender that is 
referred to. For example, the World Health Organisation (WHO) have published the following 
relevant terms and definitions, most of which relate to sex rather than gender (World Health 
Organisation Europe, 2022): 
 
• 
“Gender analysis identifies, analyses and informs action to address inequalities that arise 
from the different roles of women and men, or the unequal power relations between them, 
and the consequences of these inequalities on their lives, their health and well-being. The 
way power is distributed in most societies means that women have less access to and 
control over resources to protect their health, and are less likely to take part in decision-
making. Gender analysis in health often highlights how inequalities disadvantage women's 
health, the constraints women face to attain health and ways to address and overcome 
these. It also reveals health risks and problems which men face as a result of the social 
construction of their roles. 
 
• 
Gender equality is the absence of discrimination on the basis of a person's sex in 
opportunities, the allocation of resources and benefits, or access to services. 
==PAGE:4==
 
3 
 
• 
Gender equity refers to the fairness and justice in the distribution of benefits and 
responsibilities between women and men. The concept recognizes that women and men 
have different needs and power, and that these differences should be identified and 
addressed in a manner that rectifies the imbalance between the sexes.” 
 
Confusion can arise from terminology used. For example; I was assigned female at birth and I self-
identify as a woman. This means that I am a cisgender (cis) woman. Had I decided that I did not 
identify with social characteristics typically assigned to the female sex, I may have decided to self-
identify as male, leading me to be a transgender (trans) man. Although this was not the case, I 
believe that I possess many characteristics that are typically perceived as masculine, despite having 
no terminology associated with my personal description that indicates anything other than female. 
 
Throughout this paper the terms ‘gender gap’, ‘gender equity’, and ‘gender equality’ will be used to 
describe disparity between sexes in line with WHO descriptions above. Little to no consideration will 
be made to those identifying as transgender since they equate to only 1.4% - 3.5% of the general 
population dependent on geographical region and age (Williams Institute, UCLA, 2022) (Harvard 
School of Public Health, 2016). We can assume however that those people identifying as male or 
female, regardless of whether this was assigned at birth, are included in the following definitions. 
 
For ease of reading and unless otherwise stated, the terms “woman, women, female(s)” and “man, 
men, male(s)” will be used when referring to a person’s sex and the terms “feminine/femininity” and 
“masculine/masculinity” will be used when referring to gendered behaviours respectively associated 
with those binary sexes. Throughout the discussion, I will use sex and gender interchangeably with 
regards to the volunteers that facilitated the experiment, and the participants included in the 
results, since all of them identified with the same gender as matched their biological sex. 
Introduction 
Motivation 
There is now an awareness within society that there is a gender gap in the workplace generally, and 
within entrepreneurial ecosystems, and many now outwardly signal that they wish to see this gap 
close. For example, in Denmark, Boston Consulting Group (BCG) and UN Women Nordic Office have 
brought together 24 Danish leaders to a Gender Diversity Roundtable (GDR). This initiative has been 
ongoing since 2017 and aims to allow these leaders to share experiences and best practices on how 
to promote and advance gender diversity. The 24 leaders include many prominent Danish 
universities as well as some of the largest commercial organisations such as Lego, Novozymes and 
Ørsted. They have published a manifesto which promotes unconscious bias training in companies 
==PAGE:5==
 
4 
and organisations among other recommendations to increase the number of women in positions of 
leadership within the Danish labour market (Gender Diversity Roundtable Denmark, 2022). This is in 
addition to the Diversity Commitment initiative by FoundDiverse, which includes prominent 
investment groups such as The Danish Innovation Fund, Vækstfonden and the Danish Business 
Angels (DanBan) (FoundDiverse, 2022).  
But why is this important? And what else can be done to close this gender gap? 
Increasing number of women in entrepreneurship is of course important for gender equity given 
that 51% of the global population is made of women. However, if what we aim to achieve is to reap 
the benefits of having greater diversity in experience, characteristics, ways of thinking and approach, 
then perhaps focussing solely on equality between the sexes alone is not enough. It requires creating 
entrepreneurs of people who may not usually either be associated with, or tend towards, 
entrepreneurship, and whom gatekeepers of success do not ordinarily favour. This means also 
focusing on specific personality traits.   
This study aims to investigate stereotypically gendered behaviours with regards to entrepreneurial 
endeavors, and to explore ways that feminine females could raise their chances of being supported 
by the gatekeepers of success. Specifically, I aim to explore whether masculine traits can be faked by 
the founder to overcome any typical gender bias held by the evaluator. The focus of this research is 
particularly on women but could have further research implications for feminine males also. 
The intention is to help identify useful findings that can be used to move towards both greater 
gender equity and a greater diversity of personality traits in entrepreneurship, and more specifically, 
within health technology industries. 
Background to the Gender Gap in Entrepreneurship 
It is starting to become a widely accepted truth that the physical, political, social, economic, and 
professional worlds that we currently live in, have not been created equal for men and women. As 
recently as 2019, a book entitled “Invisible Women: Data Bias in a World Designed for Men” was 
published. The author, (Criado-Perez, 2022), took considerable time to research instances 
throughout society globally where a lack of data about women, and specific behaviours, needs and 
preferences of women, has led to “perpetual, systemic discrimination against women, and has 
created a pervasive but invisible bias with a profound effect on women’s lives” (Criado-Perez, 2022).  
The examples she illustrated ranged from design of everyday physical items, such as seatbelts being 
designed based only on male data; to widely used medical protocols, such as cardiac arrest 
response, being designed around male presentation of symptoms even though females present 
different symptoms. 
==PAGE:6==
 
5 
The inequality between the sexes is widespread, and so it is of little surprise that we also see gender 
gaps within the realm of entrepreneurship. In almost all societies it is observed that women’s 
participation in entrepreneurship is lower than men’s (Gupta, Goktan, & Gunay, 2014). Reasons for 
this have been researched extensively, with focus on differences between both entrepreneurial 
drive and intention (Gupta, Turban, & Wasti, 2009) (Brush, Abdul, Kelley, & Greene, 2017), and with 
regards to difference in behaviours of investors, and other such gatekeepers to entrepreneurial 
success, towards male and female entrepreneurs   (Gupta, Goktan, & Gunay, 2014) (Hecht, 2016) 
(Alsos & Ljunggren, 2017) (Ewens & Townsend, 2020). 
One might think that the development of entrepreneurship as a distinct study discipline in many 
universities and business schools over the past twenty years would be helping to close this gap. 
However, that does not seem to be the case (De Carolis & Litzky, 2019). Although it has been 
reported by multiple scholars that entrepreneurial education typically has a greater positive impact 
on women than men (Moberg, 2020) there are several barriers to entry to this type of education, 
and to entrepreneurship in general, for them. Self-efficacy, or confidence in own ability and belief in 
possessing skills required to be successful in entrepreneurship, is one such factor (Wilson, Kickull, & 
Marlino, 2007). Furthermore, competition is often a primary component within many specific 
educational entrepreneurship programs (Brentnall, Rodrigues, & Culkin, 2018) if not the main 
element (Jones & Iredale, 2010). Removing all other factors that may deter women from 
entrepreneurial activities, scientific evidence still supports that female students are less likely to 
engage in competitive situations than male students (Niederle & Vesterlund , 2007) (Markowsky & 
Beblo, 2022) and so it seems these programs are not inherently designed for women. Yet another 
example of a data gap leading to ill-considered solutions for women. 
Of course, it is not only students of entrepreneurship that have the possibility to start their own 
businesses. Graduates of other disciplines can, and one might argue should, also choose to embark 
on a startup journey using their knowledge and skills. With the large number of critical, global, 
societal challenges that humanity currently faces, from climate change to the threat of antibiotic 
resistance, it is probably now more important than ever to find innovative solutions and new 
business models. However, despite there now being more female owned businesses than reported 
in previous years (Elam, 2021), there is still a large disparity between the number of male and female 
graduate entrepreneurs, with women being consistently underrepresented (Piva & Rovelli, 2021). 
This gender divide is compounded further when assessing health technology industries. A report 
published in 2019 by Oliver Wyman cited that only 9% of HealthTech startup founders are women. In 
established HealthTech companies only 13% of CEOs are women, and of all C-suite positions in these 
firms just 30% are held by women (Vault, 2022). Furthermore, researchers have found that in the 
United States (US) biomedical inventions patented by women are 35% more likely to benefit 
==PAGE:7==
 
6 
women’s health than inventions patented by men (Rembrand, Samila, & Ferguson, 2021). Yet, in 
2020, only 12.8% of all patented inventions in the US were filed by individual women or women-led 
teams (United States Patent and Trademark Office, 2020). This is one of the factors contributing to 
the gender divide in the MedTech industry where only 15% of all startups, working to combine 
medical research with technology to create innovative health care solutions, are led by women 
(MedTech Pulse, 2021). 
This may not be totally shocking given that there is a normalised perception that the ideal, industry 
agnostic, entrepreneur is male (Ahl & Marlow, 2012) (Henry, Foss, & Ahl, 2015). Furthermore, 
participation in entrepreneurial activities where high growth is pursued, particularly endeavors that 
require substantial funding often from venture capital firms, is typically considered a masculine 
behaviour (Gupta, Turban, & Wasti, 2009) . Health and medical technologies fall perfectly into these 
categories. 
It is not only within numbers of founders that a gender gap is evident. It is well documented that 
male founders have historically received more venture capital (VC) investment than female 
founders. A trend that does not seem to have changed over the past decade despite more women 
embarking in entrepreneurial activities (Pitchbook Data, Inc., 2020). It was reported by the World 
Economic Forum in 2017, that only 2% of VC funding in the US and Europe went to female-only 
founded teams, where 10% was given to mixed-sex teams, and the remainder to all-male teams 
(Novorol, 2019). "In 2020, all-women-founded startups raised just 0.7% of the investments in the 
Nordics. 7.3% went to mixed-gender founding teams, while all-men founding teams raised 92%" 
(Unconventional Ventures, 2021). As recently as 2021, a new initiative, the ‘Diversity Commitment’, 
was launched in Denmark by a company called FoundDiverse with commitment from eight of the 
top investment firms within Copenhagen. At the time of launching the initiative they reported that 
95% of the Diversity Commitment partners investments went to male-only founded teams. They 
collectively agreed to reduce this number to 80%, and within one year most, but not all, of the 
partners had achieved the goal (FoundDiverse, 2022). However, not only does 80% still represent an 
exceeding disparity, but this number also does not present the entire picture. It does not 
demonstrate the number of men vs. women seeking funding, nor does it show the percentage of 
money split between male vs. female founded startups. If the deal size for female teams is much 
smaller than to male teams, then there is still a problem with equality.  
Data about VC funding in the HealthTech sector specifically is hard to come across, and there is some 
difference between digital health startups and deep tech health startups. Though in 2019, Rock 
Health reported that 14% of VC deals in digital health startups in the US were closed by female CEOs 
(Landi, 2019). This seems slightly more optimistic than the overall VC picture of equity split and is 
likely due to more women working in the health sector overall. Though again, this does not reflect

the entire picture since it considers only a small geography, digital tech only, and female CEOs rather 
than full-team composition. 14% is also still a low figure when about 51% of the general population 
is made of women.  
The Importance of Equality 
So why is this inequality an issue? Firstly, it has been found that when assessing entrepreneurial 
characteristics using a 13-point entrepreneurial task and relationship (ETR) scale, there is no 
difference between male and female entrepreneurs (Laguía, García-Ael, Wach, & Moriano, 2019).  
This would suggest that the staggering disparity in numbers of men and women in entrepreneurship 
is simply unfounded.  
Furthermore, as stated by Jakob Lindmark Frier, Head of Communications as Digital Hub Denmark, 
"Diversity is not only a nice-to-have, it is an economic imperative that can be of competitive 
advantage. Companies that embrace diversity and drive inclusion in leadership achieve better results 
when it comes to innovation and creativity” (Digital Hub Denmark, 2022). This is supported in the 
literature, with many studies reporting that diversity of gender leads to better financial performance 
(McKinsey & Company, 2018), drives greater innovation (Mayer, Warr, & Zhao, 2018), and increases 
“collective intelligence” within organisations (Woolley, Chabris, Pentland, Hashmi, & Malone, 2010).  
Though, in a world where society now realises that biological sex and gender are not the same thing, 
and the lines between them are ever more blurred, what does diversity of gender really mean?  
Gender Stereotypes and Bias 
There has been an abundance of research on gender stereotyping that has consistently corroborated 
the notion that certain traits are perceived as more characteristically masculine or feminine (Eagly, 
1987) (Canary, Emmers-Sommer, & Faulkner, 1997) (Heilman M. , 2008). Whilst many things have 
changed over recent decades with regards to the roles that men and women now hold in society, 
these gender stereotypes continue to persist (Hentschel, Heilman, & Peus, 2019). Attributes more 
often applied to men include assertiveness, greater rationality, tendency for risk-taking, autonomy, 
and a social style characterized by decisiveness, deliberateness, and control. Stereotypical traits of 
women include warmth, gentleness, understanding, sensitivity, a lack of logic, emotionality, and an 
absence of authority (Buttner & Rosen, 1988) (Bachen, McLoughlin, & Garcia, 2009).  
Whilst research continues to demonstrate generalized differences between men and women across 
a variety of competencies, in every study there are always many outliers to the normalized 
categorisations. Simply put, there are many instances where individuals do not conform to typical 
gender stereotypes (Kessler, Cortland, & Kinias, 2018). Considering this, it must now be 
contemplated that creating gender diversity is perhaps far more complex than previously 
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
understood. One might argue that simply including an equal number of men and women within 
startup teams and companies is an oversimplification of a solution.  
Gender stereotyping is often problematic and can give rise to discrimination for many reasons. 
Commonly accepted theories of sex role orientation likely play a big part. Simply put, the 
perpetuated belief that men and women should have different roles based on both perceived and 
actual trait differences between the sexes. This has been found to remain true regardless of whether 
the owner of that belief is themselves male or female (Sexton & Bowman, 1990). It has been well 
documented in the literature with regards to the view that specific work-based roles should be 
assigned to either men or women, where men are seen as more suited to professions within realms 
such as engineering, surgery and judiciary, and women best aligned to care based domains such as 
nursing or service roles (Heilman M. , 1983). Since senior management in business is typically 
considered men’s work (Heilman M. , 2001) there is an implication that both men and women in 
positions of authority, such as investors, could be more likely to favour male founders based on the 
assumption that men have more of the required skills to successfully lead, grow, and scale a new 
business. 
These assumptions frequently lead to male and female startup founders being treated differently, 
based on their perceived gender, throughout the startup evaluation process. In one study published 
in 2018, the researchers investigated the dynamics in the question and answer (Q&A) section of an 
investor pitch meeting. They found that investors irrespective of their own sex or gender, typically 
ask male entrepreneurs’ promotion-focused questions with emphasis on attaining growth, things 
such as ‘How do you intend to acquire customers?’ and ‘What does your revenue forecast look 
like?’. In contrast, they typically pose prevention-focused questions to female entrepreneurs. 
Questions like ‘What does customer retention look like?’ and ‘Are you operating at break-even?’. It 
was found that the type of questioning then influences the response type given, leading to male and 
female entrepreneurs responding with promotion and prevention responses respectively. Promotion 
responses induce a more positive outlook on the scaling potential of the business, whereas 
prevention responses illicit thoughts of risk and failure (Kanze, Huang, Conley, & Higgins, 2018). This 
has subsequently been corroborated by researchers who found that women who signal an 
entrepreneurial attitude to investors, elicit prevention considerations, whereas men who signal the 
same are met with promotion considerations (Malmström, Voitkane, Johansson, & Wincent, 2020). 
This built on these authors’ earlier work where they had previously posited that governmental 
venture capitalists, when making funding decisions as a group, used language and rhetoric 
consistent with the gender stereotypes ascribed to the biological sex of the founders that they were 
evaluating, and that this heavily influenced the funding decisions reached (Malmström, Johansson, & 
Wincent, 2017). 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
Furthermore, there are still a greater number of men than women in entrepreneurship and 
innovation ecosystems that act as the gatekeepers to entrepreneurial success; investors, board 
members, advisors, mentors, etc. (Alsos & Ljunggren, 2017). This is likely a further reason that the 
gender gap in entrepreneurship is so stark. Firstly, homophily theory predicts that people display a 
tendency to interact with other individuals who are like themselves with regards to qualities, age, 
sex, and race, among other things (McPherson, Smith-Lovin, & Cook, 2001). Meaning that male 
gatekeepers are more likely to show preference for male founders who they assume to have 
masculine characteristics, and therefore perceive them to be more like themselves, and thus provide 
more support to them. Evidence as to whether this holds true for female investors and female 
founders is however conflicting. Some scholars suggest that both men and women VC’s have a 
critical approach to female founders and a favorable attitude towards male founders (Voitkane, 
Johansson, Malmström, & Wincent, 2019), which corroborates the social role theories. Though 
others have suggested that gender homophily within the investor-founder dyad gives rise to female 
investors expressing greater interest in female founders (Ewens & Townsend, 2020) and that VC 
firms containing women partners often invest in female-run startups at a higher rate (Hecht, 2016). 
This is not to say that the gatekeepers are consistently, deliberately, and consciously favouring male 
founders over female founders. Much of the bias that exists is unconscious, or implicit, and much of 
this is based on gender stereotypes rather than merit of an individual’s characteristics and 
achievements (Gupta, Turban, & Wasti, 2009). Everybody has unconscious biases. They influence 
one’s behaviours and are shaped by previous personal experiences, background, cultural context, 
and societal stereotypes (Banaji & Greenwald, 2013). Bias can be about anything, not just gender, 
and many people have unconscious biases that can be related to age, body weight, race, verbal cues, 
and regional accents among other things (Imperial College London, 2022).  
 
Overcoming Gender Bias in Entrepreneurship 
Evidence suggests that whilst many organisations that operate within the field of innovation openly 
state that they are aiming to reduce the gender gap by putting practices in place to highlight bias 
and attempt to train this away; and some may be acting upon recommendations from places such as 
the Danish Chamber of Commerce (Dansk Erhverv, Danish Chamber of Commerce, 2021), and from 
legislation such as the Act on Equal Treatment of Men and Women (Danish Law, 1998), that aim to 
prevent discrimination based on gender. Unfortunately, actions speak louder than words and the 
literature points towards female founders still being treated differently to male founders when 
being evaluated. Therefore, the question remains as to how gender bias can be overcome to both 
promote equality between the sexes and increase gender diversity within startups.  
Recommendations from leading experts in this field, such as Candida Brush and her colleagues at the 
Diana Project (Babson College, 2022) propose that, in line with homophily theory, adding more 
==PAGE:11==
 
10
female partners into venture capital firms increases the success of female entrepreneurs seeking 
capital (Brush, Greene, Balachandra, & Davis, 2014). However, it has also been found that female 
founders initially backed by female investors are less likely to be successful at raising future funding 
rounds as they are outwardly assumed, by others, to have achieved early financial backing due to 
gender homophily only, rather than to skill or competence. Even when there is no evidence that 
anything other than aptitude was considered (Snellman & Solal, 2022).  
Whilst all evidence points to the answer to closing the gender gap in entrepreneurship lying at an 
organisational, systemic level, rather than at an individualistic founder level (Bohnet, van Geen, & 
Bazerman, 2015), many recommendations are offered in the literature to attempt to offer help to 
female founders that may increase their likelihood of receiving VC funding. Things such as adding 
males into the founding team (Godwin, Stevens, & Brenner, 2006), and offering promotion focused 
answers to questions in a pitch setting regardless of the framing of the question (Kanze, Huang, 
Conley, & Higgins, 2017), have been demonstrated to increase access to funds and social network at 
the very least.  
Furthermore, some may argue that all the above advice is somewhat irrelevant since there is a 
specific personality type that is more attracted to entrepreneurship. It is speculated that this 
personality type generally possesses more characteristics more commonly associated with 
masculinity, and as such is found in a greater number of men than women (Sexton & Bowman, 
1990). Therefore, one could theorise that by putting measures in place to inhibit or overcome bias it 
would only go so far in addressing the gender inequality issue since more males are inherently 
attracted to entrepreneurship. Again though, this claim is based on stereotypes alone, and is not 
supported by the literature. In-fact, it has been found that when entrepreneurship is presented as 
gender neutral there are no differences in intention between men and women (Gupta, Turban, & 
Bhawe, 2008). 
Traits of the Entrepreneur 
The literature in gender in entrepreneurship has focused on mainly 3 pathways: comparison 
between male and female; experiences and contributions from a female perspective; and the social 
construction of gender and its implications for entrepreneurship identity and discourse. However, 
the bulk of published literature still focusses on differences between men and women as biological 
binaries assimilated with masculinity and femininity respectively (Henry, Foss, & Ahl, 2016). To date, 
only a small handful of researchers have started to explore whether the gender bias observed in 
entrepreneurship is a result of bias against women and in favour of men, or whether it is in-fact bias 
towards societally constructed gendered characteristics.  
Building upon earlier research, one such study found that people who view themselves as similar to 
men, with regards to stereotypically masculine behaviour, display greater entrepreneurial intentions 
==PAGE:12==
 
11
(Gupta, Turban, & Wasti, 2009). However, it is a large leap to make to suggest that those that see 
themselves as similar to women and thus perhaps self-identify as having more feminine traits, do 
not possess entrepreneurial intention and could not make excellent entrepreneurs if given the 
opportunity.  
Another study concluded that within elevator pitch competitions, the finalists are more likely to 
have displayed stereotypically masculine behaviours, regardless of their actual biological sex or 
gender. Leading to an overall conclusion that pitching “like a girl” decreases chances of being 
selected as a finalist (Balanchandra, Briggs, Eddleston, & Brush, 2017). The same researcher also 
found that there is a general preference towards masculinity within VC funding decisions 
(Balachandra, 2019).  
To the best of my knowledge, no research has yet been conducted to investigate whether gendered 
traits can somehow be fabricated to reduce the number of masculine personality types in 
entrepreneurship.  
Overview of the Research 
Building upon the research already outlined in the introduction, the main aim of this study was to 
offer further insights into gender stereotypes and their role within entrepreneurship, with the aim of 
adding information that could help to close the gender gap in entrepreneurship. It was designed to 
look at authenticity with regards to gender stereotypes, and how genuine the person is perceived to 
be when attempting to impersonate gendered traits that they do not usually possess.  
As this is an exploratory piece of research in this field, no firm hypotheses will be tested. Instead, a 
series of research questions have been posed. 
Research Questions 
The main research questions that are being investigated are as follows: 
1. If the same idea is pitched by people of different gender, will an evaluator favour the idea 
more when pitched by a person that display more masculine traits regardless of their 
gender? 
2. Can evaluators determine authentic gendered behavior vs. inauthentic (fake) gendered 
behavior in a one-minute elevator pitch video? 
3. Does authenticity of the pitcher influence the overall perception of the idea being pitched? 
4. Can authenticity of the founder override any implicit gender bias held by the evaluator? 
5. Does the sex/gender (assuming they are the same) of the evaluator influence their behavior 
towards certain business ideas, or towards founders of certain genders (again, assuming this 
is the same as biological sex)? 
==PAGE:13==
 
12
Aims 
1. Evaluate whether authenticity of the HealthTech entrepreneur overrides any implicit bias 
held by the evaluator with regards to gender stereotypes. 
2. Examine whether gender impacts perceived authenticity of a HealthTech entrepreneur. 
3. Determine whether there is any correlation between authenticity of the HealthTech 
entrepreneur and positive perception of the evaluator. 
4. Find out whether gender relatedness of the evaluator to the idea effects their decision. 
Method 
Design 
An experimental design was chosen for this research since the main aim was to investigate whether 
a cause-and-effect relationship existed between authenticity of a founder’s stereotypically gendered 
characteristics, and positive assessment from an evaluator of that founder. It would simply not be 
possible to thoroughly evaluate the reasons for the evaluators’ responses in a real-world setting to 
do this since insufficient variables could be controlled.  
Four volunteers whose biological sex was declared as male or female and who each identify as the 
gender associated with their biological sex, had their personalities categorized as authentically 
feminine or authentically masculine; one each of male authentically masculine, male authentically 
feminine, female authentically masculine, and female authentically feminine; were selected. 
The volunteers were recruited into the experiment based on their self-reported masculinity and 
femininity from an online gender role personality test, the CT-GRI© (IDR Labs International, 2022) 
which is derived from the Bem Sex Role Inventory (BSRI) (Britannica, 2022). The BSRI, though derived 
in 1974, has still been found adequate for purpose and is still one of the most widely used tools for 
evaluating masculinity and femininity in psychological research studies (Brems & Johnson, 2011).  
More volunteers were asked to complete the Gender Role Test than were needed in the experiment. 
This was so that suitable volunteers could be found. Suitability was determined by limiting other 
variables besides stereotypical gender traits. 
Specific variables attempted to be controlled were: 
• 
Age 
• 
Race 
• 
Language / Accent 
• 
Level of Education 
• 
Clothing / Style 
==PAGE:14==
 
13
The four selected volunteers were each asked to record a roughly one-minute  "elevator pitch" video 
for three separate HealthTech startup ideas, for which scripts were provided (See Appendices 1 - 3), 
in their authentic pitch style. They were then asked to record the same pitches again, but falsifying 
stereotypical traits associated with the opposite gender to that which they had most affinity to as 
per the gender test. They were asked to consider that traits typically considered feminine include 
being happier, milder, less confident, and less aggressive. Whereas traits usually considered as 
masculine included being assertive, self-assured, and serious.  
The ideas being pitched were kept within the same overall health category of cancer treatment, to 
ensure the same level of severity would be assumed for each. The solutions described were vaguely 
similar to limit the bias towards specific technologies or personal technology preferences. All cases 
described were deliberately at the pre-seed startup stage to make sure that evaluators were 
predominantly evaluating the founder and not the business case, technological development, 
intellectual property, traction, or any other variable usually considered by investors when 
conducting due diligence of new ventures.  
The recorded pitches were included in an online platform where participants could view and 
evaluate them. Additional questions about the pitch and pitcher were included in the online 
platform as described below. 
 
Participants 
The study was shared within entrepreneurship and innovation expert networks predominantly in 
Denmark. It was stated that ideal participants should be accustomed to evaluating startup pitches. 
Targeted participants included different types of investors, advisors, coaches, advisors, board 
members and mentors. A demographic section was included in the study to evaluate what type of 
participants took part. To ensure that each video pitch was viewed by at least 3 separate people, a 
minimum of 72 participants were sought. 
Procedure 
Participants evaluated one pitch from each of the HealthTech categories. The pitch that they viewed 
would be displayed at random so that it was not known whether the pitcher was displaying 
authentic or unauthentic gendered traits, but logic was included to ensure that they would not view 
the same individual more than once. After each pitch they were asked a series of questions with the 
aim of evaluating the quality of the idea, business feasibility, and the quality of the pitch. They were 
also asked to use three of their own words to describe the presenter. 
To assess implicit bias of the participants, a pre-established implicit association test (IAT) developed 
for Project Implicit at Harvard University and described in the book ‘Blindspot’ (Banaji & Greenwald, 
2013) was embedded into the research platform (Bar-Anan, 2020) prior to the randomized pitch 
==PAGE:15==
 
14
videos. This was designed to measure the strength of association between gender and career, and 
often reveals a relative link between family and females and career and males (Project Implicit, 
2011). 
The online study platform was available to participants for a total of three weeks. This was a very 
short time but was limited due to the availability and setup of the software and the timeline 
available for the master’s thesis. 
Tools  
The online research platform chosen to conduct the study was Qualtrics DesignXM (Qualtrics, 2022). 
This was selected so that videos could be embedded, and so that the videos in each of the three 
HealthTech categories (men’s, women’s, gender neutral) were randomized for each participant. It 
also allowed for creating logic within the study that meant that each participant saw only one pitch 
per category, and that the same volunteer was not viewed more than once by each participant. 
Finally, the advanced data analysis and reporting features were a benefit to the project. 
Results 
Data 
82 responses were collected in the online platform. 41 of these had conducted the study through to 
100% completion. Only these 41 were included in the analysis and discussion. 
More than twice the number of males who identified as men completed the study, compared to the 
number of females who identified as women.  
There was a good spread of participants of different ages, most falling between age 26 and 56, which 
is to be expected of working professionals.  
Only 2 out of 41 of the participants considered themselves to be a Person of Colour (POC) and so this 
was not considered a variable that could be analysed. All other participants did not consider 
themselves a POC. 
The participant group was made up predominantly of professionals that work within 
entrepreneurship and innovation ecosystems, typically in a role that could be considered as a 
gatekeeper to success for founders and startup teams. Whilst the larger male group did have slightly 
more experience in evaluating HealthTech startups specifically, this was negligible. The entire group 
had a collective, self-reported, average experience level of 6.05 out of 10 in evaluating HealthTech 
startups, where 0 indicated no experience and 10 vast experience.

Interpretation 
On average all three business pitches were equally evaluated by both male and female evaluators 
with regards to the quality of concept and business feasibility. Though not too much can be inferred 
from the summary data alone; given the lack of respondents, the uneven proportion of male and 
female evaluators, and the distribution of the video randomization leading to certain pitches being 
viewed more than others; it does provide a good indication that any interpretations made about the 
results was not biased by the opinion of the idea being pitched, which means that the evaluator 
could properly assess the characteristics of the pitcher as had been planned. Furthermore, as the 
evaluators did not know that authenticity of the pitcher was being experimentally manipulated, it 
can be assumed that the evaluators’ impressions of the pitchers was not biased by this knowledge.  
The first step made to analyse the data, was to investigate whether perceived authenticity of the 
pitcher made any difference to the evaluators assessment. There were two elements to this. Firstly, 
the scores given to the quality of the concept, the business feasibility, and the pitch itself.  
For this a statistical analysis could be conducted using the sum of all these measures to determine 
the overall pitch ‘score’. What was sought here was to determine whether any halo effect (Perera,

regression equation suggests that for every additional point of genuineness the total score of the 
pitch goes up by 0.47. This indicates that about 25% of the quality of a pitch is determined by the 
perceived genuineness of the presenter.

Discussion 
Important Findings 
This study was designed to start to assess the role of authenticity within entrepreneurship in health 
technologies and related industries with regards to gender stereotypes. To date, the literature 
regarding stereotypical gender traits, as opposed to gender or biological sex, and how this relates to 
entrepreneurial activity, is scarce. This study I believe is the first where an attempt has been made to 
examine whether behaviours usually considered masculine or feminine can be acted, or faked, in 
order to impact the perception of the person evaluating a startup pitch, often investors.  
Firstly, no observable differences were found between male and female evaluators and the scoring 
they gave or the words they used to describe the video pitches. Thus, all findings have been 
generalized across men and women evaluators.  
Secondly, there was no observable gender bias found among participants. There are several reasons 
this could be the case, which are discussed more extensively within the Limitations. However, these

The key finding taken from this study, that answers one of the proposed research questions, is that 
perceived authenticity does impact the evaluator’s overall perception of the concept and the pitch. 
Evaluators typically rank video pitches 25% higher when they believe the pitcher to be authentic 
than when they don’t. I may be able to infer from this that this could impact the chance of that 
evaluator supporting the founder beyond the pitch, whether that be financially, with advice, 
mentorship or in another way, though this cannot be determined from these results. However, 
another finding was that evaluators score both authentic and inauthentic pitches the same in 
genuineness. Returning to the original research question then, of whether evaluators can determine 
authentic gendered behavior vs. inauthentic (fake) gendered behavior in a one-minute elevator pitch 
video, the answer is that they cannot.  
This implies that, founders may be able to “fake it ‘til they make it” by attempting to enact 
characteristics that they do not naturally tend toward, or by exaggerating specific traits. However, in 
doing so this is not guaranteed to advance their entrepreneurial endeavors since most evaluators 
cannot distinguish between authentic and inauthentic pitches from a video alone, and as such, being 
authentic is equally as likely to not guarantee additional favour with evaluators. 
This raises the question of whether founders need to attempt to fake it. When assessing whether 
the same business pitches were scored more favorably if pitched in a masculine way, the limited 
data captured in this experiment suggests that they are not. In fact, the masculine male volunteer 
scored the lowest on average across all pitch categories. However, this data is too limited to make 
any conclusions. It is also contrary to the findings published by (Balachandra, Briggs, & Eddleston, 
2017) who found that investor decisions were biased against a display of stereotypically feminine 
behaviours.  
What was found though, is that men perceived to be authentic did score slightly higher than women 
who are found to be equally as authentic. Therefore, of the group of people most likely to be

preferentially by investors, regardless of the sex/gender of the investor. This was not a statistically

With regards to the words used to describe the pitchers, the most noticeable observation was that 
authentic pitches were described as ‘confident’ far more frequently than were inauthentic pitches. 
Conversely, the word ‘shy’ was used more frequently for inauthentic pitches. These words have 
previously been included in inventories of gendered traits, with confidence, or assertiveness, usually 
being ascribed as masculine, and lack of self-esteem, nervousness or shyness being associated with 
femininity (Bem, 1974) (Feingold, 1994) (Gruber, Distlberger, Scherndl, Ortner, & Pletzer, 2020). This 
could have pointed to an underlying gender bias towards masculinity had the numerical data shown 
that this impacted the overall scores of the pitches. However, this was not the case. This is discussed 
further in the limitations of the study. 
Limitations 
This work suffers from several limitations that have contributed to the unexpected results and lack 
of data. Some of which may form the basis for future research. 
In this study overall, there was no significant difference found for male or females, or masculine or 
feminine traits, with regards to scoring by evaluators or with regards to words used to describe 
them. Nor were there any differences in scoring or descriptive behaviours observed between female 
or male evaluators. Therefore, the research question related to whether authenticity of the founder 
can override gender bias held by the evaluator could not be sufficiently answered. 
It was surprising to see that no significant gender bias could be observed. This is counter to all the 
background literature and to the typical results obtained from the IAT that “often reveals a relative 
link between family and females and between career and males” (Project Implicit, 2022). 
There could be several reasons for a lack of observed gender bias in this study. Firstly, the 
participants were recruited through my own network. This meant that they had an existing 
knowledge that I was investigating factors that influenced gender bias, and therefore had been 
primed with an awareness of the focus on this. It could therefore reasonably be conceived that they 
were deliberately attempting to not display bias and so were consciously ranking the pitchers more 
evenly and using similar words to describe them all.  
Moreover, due to the recruitment process and participation being entirely voluntary, one could also 
imagine that the participants were already more aware of implicit bias than the generalized 
population and be motivated to changing current practices. In fact, one of the only incentives 
==PAGE:28==
 
27
offered for people to take part was the prospect that this research could contribute towards better 
gender equality in startups in the future. Therefore, this would suggest that participants already had 
an inherent interest in achieving this goal and thus were more likely to already be more aware of 
their own biases and be attempting to navigate these to optimise their own portfolio of mentees, 
investments, startup board positions etc.  
Thirdly, all data captured for each variable was based on a single volunteer pitcher only, and on a 
video rather than a live pitch. This means that other factors were invariably included in the 
evaluators’ perceptions and thus the overall data. Physical appearance, whilst trying to be controlled 
to some degree by choosing volunteers of same race, similar age, with no discerning characteristics, 
and asking them to wear neutral clothing; could be, and likely was, still a factor that could not be 
removed from influencing participant responses. This includes so many things that can affect an 
evaluators perception of the person pitching, things such as tone of voice, body language, level of 
eye contact with the camera, and, not least, the visible appearance of biological sex. Researchers 
have previously found that venture capitalists perceive traits in entrepreneurs that they attribute to 
people of the visible sex observed, even if there is no evidence for the entrepreneurs expressly 
demonstrating those traits during the pitch (Carter, Shaw, Lam, & Wilson, 2007).  
I suggest that future research includes a larger group of pitchers so that there is a greater proportion 
of data produced for each of the variations of masculine male, feminine male, masculine female and 
feminine female, and that greater controls are introduced of the specific masculine and feminine 
traits being displayed by each. This should balance out results being swayed by variables such as 
physical appearance and other forementioned attributes. Furthermore, it would be interesting to 
see the results when using live pitches as opposed to videos since a more full impression of a person 
is usually obtained from an in-person meeting. 
Finally, the pre-formulated implicit association test (IAT) to measure association between gender 
and career that was embedded in to the Qualtrics platform comes with some documented caveats 
from the Project Implicit Team, who state: “The IAT (like all the other “implicit measures”) is not 
accurate enough to provide a reliable estimate of attitudes for each participant” (Bar-Anan, 2020). 
As this is a deep and personal element of one’s personality, implicit association is not easy to 
accurately predict. In fact, one may suggest that without longitudinal studies of real-world 
behaviours across a variety of contexts it is nigh impossible to determine individuals’ levels of 
implicit bias’. Moreover, the standard output presented from using this IAT was given in the form of 
‘Your result suggests no/slight/moderate/strong automatic preference for Family/Career’. 
Therefore, without further analysis it is difficult to determine exactly what this output indicates with 
regards to gender association.

Further limitations were found in the pitch setup. As this was a master’s thesis project that had no 
funding, the volunteers who recorded the pitches were just that, volunteers. They were willing to 
donate some time and energy into helping me, simply as a favour. They were friends, colleagues, 
and tutors from the Danish Technical University (DTU) department of entrepreneurship, most of 
whom already knew me. Firstly, finding volunteers in an already quite homogenous pool such as 
DTU, that until recently only accepted engineers into master’s programs, limited the variety in types 
of characteristics that one might find in the wider community. Secondly, they were not incentivized 
in any way to take part and so the level of effort varied between each, making for some minor 
inconsistencies with regards to passion in pitch delivery.  
Additionally, they were not trained actors, and so their personal interpretations of the ‘opposite 
gender traits’ also varied, both in understanding of the different traits, and in quality of their 
interpretation. This being especially more challenging as it was not simply a case of asking men to 
act like women and vice versa. All the volunteers identified as the gender assigned to their biological 
sex, but for two of them the gender test revealed a closer affinity to behaviours typically associated 
with the opposite gender. Asking these volunteers to ‘fake’ stereotypical traits of the gender and sex 
that they already identify with, is somewhat challenging. 
With regards to the pitches themselves, effort was made to ensure that each case contained the 
same pertinent information, was based on a similar health issue, and the level of progress that the 
fabricated startup was stated to have achieved was the same for each. However, this meant that 
each case was fully scripted. Therefore, the language used in the pitch was never natural to the 
volunteers, which invariably meant that there was always an element of it feeling less authentic than 
it would have if they had developed the pitch themselves. In many cases, as the volunteers were 
doing this in spare time, they had not learned or rehearsed the pitches ahead of recording either, so 
their reading was apparent in the videos. This may also have tarnished how authentic and 
compelling that they are perceived to be, since in a real pitch setting the presenter usually knows 
their content by heart, has created it themselves, and it is related to a real business that they feel 
passionately about pursuing.  
If this study were to be replicated, I would strongly advise to pay actors to conduct the pitches and 
have them rehearse and memorise both styles before recording, after first receiving consistent 
coaching about which specific traits to include in each of the masculine and feminine pitches. Ideally, 
the people pitching would look and sound more alike to begin with also. Not an easy feat when 
recruiting both men and women into the project to be compared based on behavioral traits alone. 
However, I would also recommend adding additional scope to include impact of physical 
appearance. Perhaps with some pitches being audio only for a subset of participants. I would

recommend that the setting for, and the quality of, the video and audio recordings was consistent 
throughout also to limit any effect this could have on overall impressions.  
Within the study design further critique can be made. Firstly, within the questions included in the 
study. The aim was to try and assess how likely the evaluator would be to support each of the 
volunteer founders in the business venture that was pitched moving forward. However, they were 
not directly asked this. Instead, this was inferred from the responses to questions about quality of 
the idea, the overall business feasibility, and the pitch, and in the words used to describe the pitcher. 
In hindsight, I would have included a direct question that stated, “Would you be likely to support this 
founder in this startup moving forward?”, or a scale rating asking how likely they would be to 
continue to support this case. This would have made the results much more explicit in terms of 
answering the research questions, and possibly could have added further weight to the finding that 
confidence, a trait typically associated with masculinity, was assigned more to pitches deemed to be 
authentic; and shyness, a trait often associated with femininity was assigned more to pitches 
thought to be inauthentic. Perhaps had this question been included, a numerical gender bias would 
have been observed. 
Furthermore, the data itself was a limitation within the interpretation.  Almost half of the 
participants that began the study failed to complete the research. A certain level of attrition is 
expected of participants, but this seemed unusually high. My assumption is that this was due to the 
design of the flow in the online platform. An information screen was included between the IAT and 
the start of the pitch evaluations which thanked the participant for their participation in the IAT and 
went on to explain that the next phase of the experiment would be to observe 3 short video pitches 
and then answer a few questions about each. However, many participants ended the session at that 
stage, indicating that they had not read past the ‘thank you’ messaging or noticed the ‘Next’ button.  
Finally, due to a variety of factors that were out of my control, the timeline for conducting the 
research element of this project was rather short. This meant that the online study platform was 
only accessible for a short time before results needed to be assessed. Due to this, and the time of 
year, meaning that many people had already begun taking extended vacations, the participant 
engagement was a disadvantage to this project. 
If this research were to be expanded or replicated, I would advise recruiting participants much 
earlier, and incentivizing them to take part. Vetting the participants to some degree also to ensure a 
more even spread of men and women could be an advantage. I would also recommend embedding 
logic and rules into the online system that meant that more of the questions were mandatory and 
could not be skipped over. I would also change the information screen to ensure that it was clear 
that the research did not only comprise the demographic questions and IAT.

Conclusion 
Unfortunately, the data obtained in this experiment was not sufficient to be able to fully answer all 
the research questions or address all aims. The key finding taken from this research is that 
evaluators typically rank pitches 25% higher when they believe the pitcher to be authentic than 
when they don’t. However, evaluators are unable to accurately assess authenticity and so are 
equally as likely to rate an authentic pitch and an inauthentic pitch as the same. Further research is 
required to determine any correlation between gendered characteristics and authenticity as results 
of this study were inconclusive. 
Future Work 
There is currently very little literature published related to authenticity of stereotypically gendered 
behaviours in the research. In recent years, and in addition to the plethora of research that has been 
conducted investigating gender equity in entrepreneurship, investigators have examined impact of 
exaggerating information in pitches (Cottle & Anderson, 2020), the impact of displaying emotions 
whilst pitching (Warnick, Davis, Allison, & Anglin, 2021), and impact of general pitch presentation 
style (Clark, 2008). (Balachandra, Briggs, & Eddleston, 2017) have begun the work to examine the 
impact of gender stereotypes in investor pitches, but yet, nobody has published anything related to 
authenticity of gendered behaviours. Therefore, this research should pave the way for further 
investigators to start examining this in greater detail to explore further whether there is any cause 
and effect existing between gender stereotyped behaviours and positive recognition of evaluators. 
As a point of departure, it would be interesting to see this study replicated on a larger scale, across 
different geographies, with more volunteers trained in enacting gendered traits and behaviours, 
conducting live pitches instead of prerecorded videos, and with incentivised participants that were 
not aware that gender bias was being investigated.  
Implications for Uvisa 
I chose this thesis project topic as I am a cis female identifying as a woman and am also a health 
technology entrepreneur. My company, Uvisa Health, is developing a non-pharmacological medical 
device to treat vaginal infections. As such, I not only fall into the category of ‘female founder’ but am 
also working in women’s health, which some might still regard as a niche despite these solutions 
addressing problems experienced by almost half of the world’s population. Therefore, I am often 
exposed to discussions around the topic of gender equality and have a keen interest in this.  
Even in my own personal entrepreneurial experiences, I have encountered gender inequality 
frequently. In my educational master’s program at the Danish Technical University (DTU), it was 
evident to me that I, a biological female identifying as a woman, was part of the minority. Of the 42 
students that began on the MSc. Technology Entrepreneurship program in September 2019, I was 
one of only ten women that were accepted on to the program. Of the six students that then left the 
==PAGE:32==

program within the first three months, four of these were women, leaving only six women out of 36 
remaining students.  
Of the six of us remaining, I am one of the three that have now launched their own startup, which 
made me wonder, why? What is special about us? It isn’t only within my own master’s program. I 
noticed that in general, in the competitions that I enter, in the incubator and accelerator programs 
that I am part of, in the literature, I am in the minority.  
When I embarked on this project, myself and the two other female technology entrepreneurs from 
my mater’s program took the gender test, as did many other female entrepreneurs within our 
network in and around DTU. Interestingly, all the women that took it had higher masculinity scores 
than femininity, despite identifying as female. This raises the question of whether we were attracted 
to entrepreneurship because we embody behaviours widely viewed as masculine, or whether we 
were attracted regardless of the stereotypical gender of our personality traits but have only received 
sufficient backing due to our masculine behaviours.  
I still do not have the answers to these questions based on this data. However, this study does point 
towards a reduction in implicit gender bias among the gatekeepers to startup success within the 
Copenhagen entrepreneurship and innovation environment. It also suggests that authenticity pays-
off, in that pitches perceived to be authentic are more likely to be positively evaluated. Whilst I 
cannot ignore the staggering amount of literature that tells me that I am disadvantaged as a female 
founder, I also cannot ignore the changes in the entrepreneurial zeitgeist within Denmark. There are 
movements and organisations specifically trying to close the gender gap. There is a lens on it. With 
luck, this means that perhaps my business, and I, will be evaluated on merit more than on biological 
sex of myself and the other founder, our gender, or our perceived traits. Providing I stay genuine to 
myself and express this outwardly, my chances of seeking favour with those that I ask to evaluate 
Uvisa, and myself, will remain equally as high as for me as for anybody else that I am competing with 
for funding and recognition.

Appendix 1: Men’s Health Script 
Problem: 
Prostate cancer is the most common form of cancer among men, and is the second leading cause of 
cancer deaths in the United States.  
  
It can often be treated and managed successfully, but this requires early detection. However, there 
are often no early symptoms and so the cancer can progress without being diagnosed. 
  
Furthermore, many men are afraid and embarrased to get regular check-ups since this usually 
involves an invasive examination which many men find uncomfortable. 
  
Solution:  
Based on novel research conducted in partnership between DTU and KU, we are developing a test kit 
for early detection and prevention of prostate cancer.  
  
Biomarkers for prostate cancer are not a novel finding, but the technology used to identify these 
markers has, up to now, been large, expensive and difficult to operate.  
  
We have found a way to detect the markers in urine using a chip the size of a thumb nail and are on 
track to commercialise this within 2 years.  
  
We will create home test kits to allow men to test themselves, without the need for regular and 
potentially humiliating doctors appointments.  
 
Appendix 2: Women’s Health Script 
 
Problem:  
Everyone knows about breast cancer. Women talk about it. There are huge campaigns run to raise 
awareness about it.  
  
Cervical cancer gets much less attention and yet it is killing >300.000 people Worldwide every year.   
  
When diagnosed, cervical cancer is one of the most successfully treatable forms of cancer, as long as 
it is detected early and managed effectively.  
  
But to catch it early requires regular screening, which, though offered every 5 years by many 
countries, lots of women forget about or choose to ignore for fear of pain, discomfort and 
humiliation.  
  
Solution:  
Therefore, we are developing a device that can be used at home that uses state of the art optics, 
microscopy and imaging technology, to allow women to see their own cervix.  
  
The device will link to a mobile application so that images captured are transmitted and stored, it 
will automatically compare the image history, and then alert the user to any detected changes in the 
cervical tissue.  
  
Furthermore, the app will send notifications to the user to prompt regular usage of the device. 
  
We have developed the first prototypes and are on track to begin clinical trials within the next 12 
months.

Appendix 3: Gender Neutral Script 
 
Problem:  
One in every 3 cancers diagnosed is a type of skin cancer. 
  
The rate of this has been increasing in past years, likely linked to the decrease in protective ozone in 
the atmosphere, meaning that people are being exposed to more harmful UV radiation than 
previously.  
  
The main problem in detecting skin cancer, is that it often looks like harmless moles or freckles. 
  
As we age, it is normal to develop new darkened spots all over our bodies, and so the formation of 
new moles and freckles often doesn't cause alarm. Furthermore, many people don't understand how 
serious skin cancer truly is, with many believing that treatment is easy. However, over 50.000 people 
die each year from the disease. 
  
Solution:  
Using state of the art technology, we are developing a scanner that can detect even minute changes 
in the skin.  The scanner will capture images and transmit these to a mobile phone application, 
where any tiny changes are detected based on historical data. An alert is then given if medical 
consultation should be sought. 
  
In order to reach maturity and scale, we will initially sell the tool to health clinics and hospitals to 
trial the scanner and to start collating the data needed to optimise the solution. Though, we plan to 
market the scanner to the general public as an early detection and prevention tool.  
  
We have established our first research partnership with a hospital in Australia, and will begin the 
first trials in late 2023.
