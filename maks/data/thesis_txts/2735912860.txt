Abstract
This report investigates the greenhouse gas and air pollutants emissions footprint during
the operation and maintenance (O&M) phases over the lifetime of floating wind farms, in
the context of Europe’s ambitious target to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050. The study
highlights the innovative application of floating wind farms, which, by providing access
to deep water sites, offer environmental benefits through less invasive seabed activities.
Crucially, the work aims to improve the understanding of total greenhouse gas (GHG)
emissions from O&M activities, addressing a significant gap in current methodologies.
These methodologies often generalise O&M emissions by comparing them to bottom­
fixed offshore wind turbine installations, leading to an underestimation of Global Warming
Potential (GWP) by up to 20.4%. By analysing the influence and variation of GHG and
air pollutant emissions over the lifetime of the farm, this research aims to provide a more
accurate assessment of the environmental impact of floating wind farms.
The research aims to quantify and analyze total GHG and air pollutant emissions from
O&M activities of floating wind farms, focusing on variables that affect these emissions
over the farm’s lifetime. It explores the emission impact of different vessel trip phases,
the relationship between the distance to shore and emissions, and the optimal criteria
for a maintenance fleet to minimize emissions. Employing a combination of WOMBAT
software and a custom Python­based model, the study processes operational event data
for precise emission calculation.
A case study of the Morro Bay Wind Farm in California is utilized for practical application
of the methodology. The findings indicate a significant contribution of the floating offshore
idling phase of 49% to total emissions in onsite major component replacement strategy
with a GWP of 15.0 gCO2eq/kWh. This emission is of the same order of magnitude as
the entire GHG emission from a bottom mounted offshore wind farm. The research also
discovers that reducing the load factor for auxiliary engine in offshore idling and limiting
Heavy Lift Vessel (HLV) operations can significantly decrease GWP to 6.6 gCO2eq/kWh,
a 56% reduction compared to the base case. The study also demonstrates that every 20
km increase in distance from shore results in a GWP increase of about 0.5 gCO2eq/kWh.
Crucially, the research shows that deploying a ”green fleet” using Liquid Natural Gas
(LNG) fuel can reduce the GWP by 30%, from 15.0 gCO2eq/kWh in the base case to
10.3 gCO2eq/kWh, and completely eliminate SO2, PM2.5 and PM10 emissions. These
findings are essential for the development of strategies to reduce the environmental im­
pact of floating wind farms, thus contributing significantly to Europe’s sustainability and
environmental goals.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In alignment with the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development adopted by the United
Nations [1], Europe has established an ambitious objective of attaining carbon neutrality
by 2050, aiming to address climate change and transition to a more sustainable future.
Driving this mission is the innovative integration of floating wind farms, presenting a pi­
oneering solution in the pursuit of a cleaner, more environmentally sustainable energy
sector [2].
Floating wind farms represent a revolutionary leap in ocean energy technology, offer­
ing the offshore wind industry two key advantages. Firstly, they allow access to sites
in waters deeper than 50 metres, while simplifying the installation of turbines even in
mid­depth conditions of 30 to 50 metres. This innovation has the potential to become a
cost­effective alternative to bottom­fixed foundations, according to International Renew­
able Energy Agency (IRENA) [3]. Furthermore, the environmental advantages of floating
foundations, characterised by less invasive activity on the seabed during installation, dis­
tinguish them from fixed­bottom designs [4].
The projections for expansion in the offshore wind farm sector are remarkable. Float­
ing offshore wind technology unlocks approximately 80% of the global offshore wind re­
sources, situated in waters deeper than 50 meters. As highlighted in the IRENA 2019
report on the future of wind energy [5], it is estimated that floating wind farms could rep­
resent roughly 5­15% of the overall installed offshore wind capacity worldwide by 2050,
amounting to nearly 1,000 GW, equivalent to approximately 150 GW.
1.2
Research objectives
The assessment and mitigation of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from floating wind
farms is critical to ensure their environmental sustainability and effectiveness in reduc­
ing their carbon footprint. Detailed assessments are essential for modelling and thus
reducing these emissions.
To this end, comprehensive life cycle assessments (LCA)
have been conducted over the years, taking into account emission from all phases of the
wind farm’s life, including production, installation, operation, maintenance and decommis­
sioning. These assessments quantify emissions such as those from material production,
transport, construction and continuous operation [6].
An important aspect of the LCAs assessment of greenhouse gas emissions from floating
wind farms is the analysis of the operation and maintenance (O&M) phases. Several
reports and studies have emphasised the need to examine the O&M phase for an accurate
assessment of emissions [7]. However, existing methodologies often generalise these
emissions by assuming the equivalence of O&M to fixed installations due to a lack of data
from existing floating wind farms, which can lead to a significant underestimation of the
Global Warming Potential (GWP)1 of up to 20.4% [9]. Addressing this gap is crucial to

1.3
Research questions
1.3.1
Main research question
What is the cumulative carbon emission footprint during the Operational and Maintenance
phases throughout the lifetime of a floating wind farm?
This report aims to investigate the cumulative carbon footprint during the operation and
maintenance (O&M) phases over the lifetime of a floating wind farm. It seeks to ascer­
tain the total GHG emissions from O&M activities, focusing on the key parameters that
influence emissions and their frequency over the lifetime of the floating wind farm. By
examining the stages of O&M in a comprehensive manner, the report aims to provide de­
tailed insights into environmental impacts, enabling a better understanding of the drivers
of emissions and their variations over the lifetime of floating wind farms.
1.3.2
Sub­questions
• Among the different phases of a vessel trip (Cruise, Hotel, Maneuvering), which one
has the most significant impact on overall emissions, and why?
• What is the correlation between the distance to the shore and GHG emissions? Why
does proximity to the shore matter in emission levels?
• What criteria should be considered in determining the ideal fleet of maintenance
vessels to support O&M activities and minimize emissions?
1.4
Report Outline
The thesis is structured into several key sections, starting with the Introduction, which pro­
vides a comprehensive background, research objectives, questions, and an outline of the
report’s contents. Following this, the Literature Review explores the current landscape of
floating offshore wind energy, operation and maintenance practices, and the environmen­
tal impact in terms of greenhouse gas and air pollutant emissions. The Data Collection
section focuses on gathering and analyzing marine vessel properties, including engine
and fuel types, as well as emission factors related to air pollutants and greenhouse gases.
Methodology is then discussed, detailing the use of WOMBAT and a Python­based emis­
sion model, encompassing activities such as wind farm energy production and availability.
The Case Study delves into the vessel fleet under consideration, offering an examination
of the Morro Bay Wind Farm as a reference point, along with a detailed discussion of
the chosen fleet’s specifics. Results and Discussion follow, addressing the impact of trip
phases, distance to shore, and fleet composition on emissions. Finally, the Conclusion
reflects on the findings and suggests areas for future research.

2
Literature Review
2.1
Floating offshore wind energy
Offshore wind farms, which harness the powerful winds of the oceans, have emerged as
a key frontier. The global importance of wind energy is underlined by its integration into
the renewable energy portfolios of many countries. In recent years, an innovative chapter
has been added to the wind energy story with the development of floating wind turbines,
which have the potential to unlock new offshore locations in deep waters.
Floating wind turbines (FOW) present distinct advantages and challenges when compared
to their bottom­fixed (BFOW) counterparts. On the positive side, floating turbines offer
the advantage of being installed in deep water, taking advantage of stronger and more
constant winds, that results in higher energy yields. The current estimated cost is 180
€/MWh and is expected to drop significantly to 60 €/MWh by 2030. The projected cost is
equal to the current expenses associated with BFOW. [10].
These floating structures also contribute to smaller visual and acoustic impacts, offering
more flexibility in terms of installation, maintenance, and decommissioning. They also
avoid generating powerful high­frequency vibrations. In the process of installing fixed
turbines on the seabed, the seabed is drilled for long periods, potentially damaging ma­
rine fauna. Although it is possible to mitigate this impact by creating a noise curtain with
air bubbles, implementing such a solution entails significant costs. Furthermore, a wind
turbine anchored to the seabed experiences more frequent vibrations than a floating coun­
terpart, because it vibrates at a higher frequency when fixed. The wind turbine generator
is particularly sensitive to high­frequency vibrations and is the most expensive component
of the entire turbine system. In contrast, the low­frequency vibrations experienced by a
floating wind turbine help to prolong its operational life [11].
However, floating wind energy involves more physics and more interaction than bottom­
fixed wind turbines [10]. The floating movement of the turbine consists of six degrees of
freedom (DOF), three of translation and three of rotation, as shown in Figure 2.1. Surge
and pitching are generally the most dominant DOFs. Challenges include the complex dy­
namics due to coupled systems, where the aerodynamic loads on the rotor are influenced
by the hydrodynamic motion of the floater. The design of a floating wind turbine must
avoid the same load frequencies produced by wind turbulence, wave impact, unbalanced
rotor mass and aerodynamics, and tower shadow effects, in order to avoid resonances
and fatigue failures.
In addition, other challenges focus on developing accurate and fully coupled aero­hydro­
elastic simulation tools for FOW turbines, defining operational and control strategies for
turbines and wind farms, and enhancing new logistical strategies for turbine installation
and maintenance.
Floating turbines are equipped with a platform enabling them to hover on the water sur­
face.

from oil and gas experience. Currently, there is no pre­established or favored design; in­

parameters including the wind resource, wave spectrum and accessibility of the harbour.

• Spar buoy This solution achieves stability through the strategic positioning of the bal­
last under the main buoyancy tank, resulting in rigidity in translational movements.
Assembly can be carried out either at sea or offshore, with the possibility of towing
the turbine to the installation site. However, this design faces challenges related to
sensitivity to rotational motions and deep draughts, which may limit access to ports
[14].
• Semi­submersible Its design can reach static stability by widely dispersing buoyancy
on the water surface, allowing assembly in a dry dock before towing the turbine to
the installation site. In particular, semi­submersible floats prove to be independent
of water depth, making them versatile for different installation sites. Unlike spar
buoys, semi­submersibles are not limited by shallow water and can operate effec­
tively at depths greater than 100 metres. However, this design presents challenges,
including the experience of significant wave­induced movements, in particular large
rotational movements induced by wave loading, leading to substantial movement
at the top of the tower. Furthermore, the semi­submersible structure tends to have
more components above the waterline, increasing structural complexity and poten­
tially affecting its overall resilience in harsh marine conditions. The cost of mooring
lines, crucial for stability, is affected by their length and semi­submersibles often
require long catenary mooring lines attached to horizontally loaded anchors [14].
• Tension­leg platform (TLP) It provides static stability by tensioning the mooring lines,
using a submerged buoyancy tank to minimise wave­induced motions. This design
features rigidity against rotational motions, contributing to its stability. TLPs are
suitable for intermediate water depths, generally requiring depths between 50 and
150 metres. Although TLPs have shorter mooring lines that extend vertically, they
must bear a significantly higher load to maintain constant tension between the an­
chors and the buoyancy tank. The anchors of the suction piles, which are vertically
loaded, play a crucial role in compensating for the buoyancy force. However, as­
sembly difficulties arise due to the fact that the TLPs are not self­stabilizing, limiting
the feasibility of assembly in dry dock. Furthermore, the design involves high verti­
cal loads on moorings and anchors, presenting engineering complexities that need
to be addressed for the effective use of TLPs in offshore wind projects [14].
One of the pioneering projects in this field is the 2MW Hywind demonstration project off
the coast of Norway, initiated by Equinor in 2009 [15] and still in operation today. In 2015,
the company received permission to install a wind farm in Scotland. Hywind Scotland be­
came the world’s first floating wind farm, consisting of five turbines, this pilot park covers
approximately 4 square kilometres and has an impressive installed capacity of 30 MW. As
a sign of technological innovation, each turbine has a diameter of 154 metres and reaches
a maximum height from the base to the turbine of 253 metres. The project is located in
water depths between 95 and 120 metres and uses a spar substructure. Using standard
offshore wind turbines, this innovative initiative is set to power around 35,000 homes in
the UK, representing a milestone in the advancement of sustainable energy solutions.
Commissioned in 2017, the project’s remarkable success is underlined by its exceptional
availability, which exceeded initial forecasts. Beate Myking, senior vice president of Sta­
toil’s offshore wind operations, notes that the volume of electricity generated significantly
exceeds expectations, with the capacity factor reaching an impressive 65%. This result is
particularly noteworthy during the winter, when winds are strongest, as Hywind Scotland’s
capacity factor exceeds the typical 45­60% range observed in bottom­fixed offshore wind
farms [16]. The history of floating wind turbines is intertwined with ongoing research and
development efforts to optimise design, improve stability and reduce costs[10].

2.2
Operation and maintenance
Due to the complex dynamics of floating wind turbines explained in the former section, the
operation and maintenance encounter many different challenges, even if some strategies
took advantage of the lessons learnt from the growth of fixed bottom wind technology.
Logistical issues, such as greater distance from the coast and exposure to harsher envi­
ronments, pose fundamental problems from an O&M perspective [17]. Moreover, wave
susceptibility, maintainability, anchoring and mooring complexity and turbine movement,
all of which have a direct impact on O&M. Environmental conditions such as wind speed,
turbulence, wave height and sea conditions also significantly affect the reliability and main­
tainability of FOW installations, as well as in BFOW. The greater distance from the coast
results in more adverse weather conditions, reducing site accessibility. Literature high­
lights the importance of adequate port and network infrastructure for FOW adoption, as
most European ports are currently not equipped for the scale of operations required [18].
Maintenance activities in the wind energy sector are broadly classified into two types: pre­
ventive maintenance, which is planned and typically performed during periods of low wind
speeds, often in the summer months, to minimise the impact on production; and correc­
tive repair and replacement, an unplanned response to outages that is considered critical
due to the accumulation of downtime until the fault is resolved. Routine maintenance
activities include, for example, the planned inspection for the export cables and offshore
substations, control of bolted junctions, the replacement of consumed parts with less than
design life or the lubrication of mechanical parts. Failures are generally categorized in
minor repair, major repair, and major replacement (MCR) [19]. The O&M strategies for
floating wind turbines can be broadly categorized into off­site and on­site approaches.
The off­site tow­to­port maintenance scenario involves disconnecting floaters from cables
and moorings for the replacement of major components. This approach allows the float­
ing turbine to be towed to shore for repairs by crane, but is complex, time­consuming
and expensive. Although emerging solutions are being sought for the maritime industry,
tow­to­port remains the main option for commercial floating wind projects, given current
technology. Maritime operational complexities, including hazards during disconnection,
field movement and heavy lifting operations, pose safety, equipment and environmental
issues, affecting the cost of operations. Power outages during disconnection raise down­

3
Data Collection
In this chapter, the focus is on examining the driving forces behind greenhouse gas emis­
sions and air pollutants. The aim is to select a dataset that categorizes vessels based on
their various engine types, sizes, and the fuel they utilize. Special consideration is given
to the specific trip phase undertaken by these vessels.
3.1
Marine vessel properties
The emissions generated by maritime navigation arise from engines used as main propul­
sion engines and auxiliary engines used to provide power and services within the vessels.
Main propulsion engines, which are larger and designed for continuous operation, tend
to emit different quantities and types of pollutants compared to auxiliary engines, which
are used for power generation onboard ships when at sea. Auxiliary engines are typically
smaller and may operate intermittently, which can affect their emission profiles. If possi­
ble, it is important to assess emissions for both the different engine categories.
Marine engines used in O&M in offshore wind emit a range of pollutants as a byproduct of
fuel combustion. The trend of emissions from marine engines is complex and influenced
by factors such as engine technology, operation, and fuel type.
3.1.1
Engine type
A comprehensive analysis reported by the EMEP/EEA Air Pollutant Emission Inventory
Guidebook 2023 [25] conducted in 2010 showed that around 99% of the world’s maritime
fleet is powered by diesel engines, while steam turbines account for less than 1% of
power generation. Therefore, the emission analysis is carried out by considering and
categorising emissions from diesel engines exclusively.
The categorisation of diesel engines is generally based on their operating speed Table 3.1.
Slow­speed diesel engines (SSD) are the giants of marine propulsion, with low­speed
engines feature long stroke lengths and low rpm. These engines account for around
18% of the marine diesel engine population. They favour torque over speed, ensuring
consistent performance in the maritime sector. Typically operating at less than 300 rpm,
and often between 80 and 140 revolutions per minute, these engines are mainly two­
stroke, cross­headed and have 4­12 cylinders. Modern variants boast impressive power
outputs in excess of 4,000 kW per cylinder, despite their measured speed. Primarily used
as main propulsion systems, they are an integral part of the maritime energy infrastructure.
They make a significant contribution to both energy production and fuel consumption [25].
Medium­speed diesel engines (MSD) account for the largest share of the marine diesel
engine market at around 55%, and medium­speed engines are synonymous with versa­
tility and efficiency. Operating in the 300­900 rpm range, these engines offer a balance

3.1.2
Fuel type
The type of fuel used by these engines is another crucial factor in the types and amounts
of emissions produced. Marine engines primarily burn heavy fuel oil also know as bunker
fuel oil (HFO/BFO),marine diesel or gas oil (MDO/MGO), and, increasingly, liquefied nat­
ural gas (LNG).
Heavy fuel oil, the traditional choice for large vessels due to its cost effectiveness, is a
high viscosity residual fuel that requires preheating for effective combustion and is known
for its high sulphur content [26]. On the other hand, marine gas oil, a distillate fuel, is
generally cleaner, has a lower sulphur content [27]. Marine Fuel Oil, often a blend of
heavy fuel oil and distillate fuels, balances cost and environmental impact and offers a
more versatile option for ships [28].
LNG, which is emerging as an alternative, has environmental benefits, including lower
carbon dioxide and almost negligible sulphur oxide emissions. Primarily composed of
methane, LNG is stored at cryogenic temperatures and offers a cleaner combustion pro­
cess compared to traditional marine fuels [29]. Its adoption is part of the maritime in­
dustry’s ongoing efforts to reduce its environmental footprint, in line with the International
Maritime Organisation’s (IMO) targets for reducing greenhouse gas emissions [30]. How­
ever, the use of LNG requires significant changes to a ship’s fuel storage and handling
systems and is dependent on the development of a global LNG infrastructure [31].

the port of departure and head for their destination, which may be in the same country or

Specific fuel consumption (SFC) for marine diesel engines is a critical parameter that
indicates the efficiency of fuel use by an engine and it is a function of the load factor (LF)
as a percentage of the Maximum Continuous Rating (MCR) 2. When it refers to a specific
driving mode, for example transit or manoeuvring, it is called Specific Fuel Operating
Consumption (SFOC). It is measured in grams of fuel per kilowatt­hour of energy produced
by the engine (g/kWh) and typically shows a curve where the minimum SFC is found at
around 80% [32]. As the engine load deviates from this optimal point, the SFC tends
to increase, indicating less efficient fuel use. At low percentages of MCR, such as idle
or low power settings, the SFC can increase dramatically. Similarly, at very high engine
loads close to 100% MCR, the SFC also tends to increase, reflecting that the engine is
not operating as efficiently as it could at its optimum point. This is particularly relevant for
marine engines which often operate for extended periods at a consistent load. A higher
SFC value indicates a less efficient use of fuel and a higher fuel consumption rate, which
can increase the fuel costs and emissions of the engine and shorten the service life of
the engine components. A lower SFC value indicates a more efficient use of fuel and a
lower fuel consumption rate, which can reduce the fuel costs and emissions of the engine.
Figure 3.2 illustrates the relationship between the SFC and the MCR of the MAK 32 C
slow­speed diesel engine. It should be noted that various factors, such as the engine’s
age, operational speed, and type of fuel used, can significantly influence the shape and
position of this curve, causing it to shift either upwards or downwards.
According to the European Environment Agency [25], different load factors are identified
for hotelling, manoeuvring and cruising (transit) modes, as shown in Table 3.2. These
factors are used as the basis for assuming the SFC for main and auxiliary engines with
slow, medium and fast diesel engines, depending on the fuel type, as shown in Figure 3.3.
As can be seen, bunker fuel or heavy fuel oil has the greatest impact on SFC, which
reaches the highest values, peaking at 318 g/kWh for the high­speed diesel engine during
manoeuvring for the main engine. Auxiliary engines, on the other hand, have a higher fuel
consumption during cruising due to the lower load factor compared to manoeuvring and
hotelling. In each category, the lowest values are obtained with LNG. Furthermore, due
to the most efficient use of the engine at 80% of the MCR, cruising on the main engine is
the mode that consumes the least fuel.
In this report, these values are used to accurately calculate the emissions associated with
offshore wind operations.

can be reduced through changes in fuel sulphur contents or the use of sea water

made in this report. Exposure to sulphur dioxide can adversely affect the human
respiratory system and also leads to the formation of other sulphur oxides, which
can react with atmospheric compounds to form small particles that contribute to
PM pollution. Environmentally, high concentrations of gaseous SOx can damage
plant foliage and growth, contribute to acid rain that damages sensitive ecosystems,
reduce visibility through haze formation, and cause material and cultural damage.
• PM10 & PM2.5: Particulate Matter in the air is a mixture of solid particles and liq­
uid droplets. It includes visible substances like dust and soot, as well as particles
so small that they require an electron microscope to be seen. PM is categorized
based on size. PM10 includes inhalable particles with diameters of 10 micrometers
or smaller, while PM2.5 consists of finer particles less than 2.5 micrometers in di­
ameter. PM2.5 particles pose significant health risks as they can penetrate deep
into the lungs and even enter the bloodstream. Additionally, PM2.5 is a major en­
vironmental concern as it contributes to visibility reduction and haze. In maritime
emissions, the levels of PM produced depend on factors such as fuel type, engine
age and condition, and combustion efficiency. Heavier fuels typically lead to higher
PM emissions, especially of PM2.5.

4
Methodology
The model framework for calculating vessel emissions during floating offshore wind O&M
integrates two distinct components. Firstly, the WOMBAT software, developed by NREL
[40], is utilized to generate an extensive list of operational and maintenance events over
a given timeframe for a selected wind farm simulation. This part of the process lever­
ages WOMBAT’s capability to accurately document every operational detail, providing a
comprehensive dataset of activities.
Subsequently, a custom Python­based code is employed to process the event list gener­
ated by WOMBAT. This code is designed to read, organize, and categorize these oper­
ations based on the type of vessel involved and the respective travel phase. It then pro­
ceeds to calculate the greenhouse gas emissions and air pollutants for each categorized
operation. The calculation methodology integrates emission factors and vessel proper­
ties, aligning with the detailed operations event logs from WOMBAT. This combination of
WOMBAT’s discrete event simulation output with the Python code’s analytical capabilities
results in a robust and accurate assessment of emissions associated with floating off­
shore wind O&M activities. This model framework not only quantifies emissions but also
provides insights that can aid in developing strategies to minimize environmental impacts
in offshore wind projects.
4.1
WOMBAT
The activity dataset and the wind farm energy production and availability extracted by the
WOMBAT simulation are described in the corresponding sections below.
4.1.1
Activity dataset
WOMBAT uses a discrete event simulation model to evaluate O&M costs. This method
offers several advantages, including detailed documentation and understanding of a sys­
tem’s processes, as well as a prescriptive approach to examining specific impacts. It
is characterized by a sequence of events in a specific order without changes occurring
between them, contrasting with optimization methods that focus on identifying the best
choice. In this project, the recorded events are utilized not for cost calculation purposes
but rather to quantify emissions.
Figure 4.1 illustrates the software architecture of WOMBAT. The key points of the wombat
are outlined below in the interest of the project.
User Defined Input: To initiate a simulation in WOMBAT, user­defined inputs are neces­
sary. These include specifying the weather profile, defining the start and end years of
the project, setting the working hours, and providing a data directory for loading and sav­
ing files. This user­defined input capability ensures that the simulation can be tailored to
specific project requirements and scenarios, providing a flexible and customizable tool for
analyzing wind farm operations and maintenance strategies. Table 4.1 shows the base­
line inputs. In particular, scheduled maintenance follows a fixed frequency model, setting
maintenance intervals based on a specified number of days to ensure regular servicing.
Unscheduled maintenance, which accounts for failures, employs a Weibull distribution to
model the variability of such events—defaulting to an exponential distribution when the
shape parameter is set to 1. The scale parameter is inversely proportional to the annual
rate of system failures, effectively representing the Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF)
in years. The model then uses random sampling to predict the timing of the next failure.

Event Logs as Output: WOMBAT generates detailed logs as part of its output, docu­

which requires some repairs to be carried out off­site at a port facility. This configuration
scheme allows for customised use of service equipment, balancing year­round availabil­
ity for routine activities with cost­effective, threshold­based mobilisation for more sporadic
and intensive repairs.

4.2.3
Combining vessel activities with phase trip
Marine vessels operating offshore follow a different voyage protocol than transport ves­
sels such as cargo ships. Unlike traditional voyages that involve continuous sailing from
port to port, offshore vessels often sail from port but then stop at specific offshore loca­
tions, such as wind farm sites or substations. At these offshore locations, they carry out
various activities in varying weather conditions. In order to accurately calculate emis­
sions, it is crucial to align the classification outlined in Table 3.2—cruise, manoeuvring,
and hotelling phases—with the specific behavior of vessels involved in floating wind farm
maintenance. This ensures a more precise estimation of emissions associated with their
operations. Figure 4.3 provides an overview of how the different categories of ship ac­
tivities are associated with the corresponding phase of the trip. A detailed description of
these, together with the assumptions, is given below.
Figure 4.3: Schematic diagram of the different combinations of vessel activities to the
corresponding phase trips in the model.
• Idling at port While in port, most engines run at low load, but still consume fuel
and therefore pollute. However, due to the difficulties in accurately measuring fuel
consumption during waiting times due to weather delays in port, idling is not taken
into account. Instead, it is assumed that all engines are switched off and power is
supplied directly from the dock [41].
• Travelling Voyages from port to site (i.e windfarm, turbine, substation) and between
different wind turbines are included in the cruise category.
• Towing Determining the exact percentage of rated engine power used by tugs during
towing operations, such as transporting floating wind turbines, is a challenge due to
several factors. These include the specific nature of the task, the size of the tug and

the prevailing environmental conditions. In the analysis provide by Garcia­Teruel
et al. [9], a 30% increase in fuel consumption during towing compared to cruising
was identified. However, these data come from small tugs and provide only a rough
estimate, lacking specific details on more demanding scenarios, such as towing a
loaded barge (e.g. a 15 MW turbine and the floating platform) in oceanic conditions.
Consequently, towing is assumed to operate at 80% load factor at economic speed,
equating towing to cruising.
• In­field operations The various maintenance and repair tasks carried out by different
vessels in the wind farm area, such as maintenance, repair, unmooring/mooring
reconnection, and crew transfer, are categorized under the manoeuvring phase.
• Offshore idling It occurs when vessels have to stop operations offshore due to
weather delays, preventing them from carrying out maintenance. Once again, it
is difficult to estimate the engine load in this case for a floating wind farm. For
example, the model for O&M emissions analysed by Offshore Renewable Energy
Catapult [42] does not distinguish between operation in the field and idling offshore,
as it focuses primarily on fixed­bottom turbines. In this context, vessels have the
option of either mooring directly to the turbine or anchoring to the seabed, given the
shallower water depth compared to floating turbines. However, this report uses the
hotelling mode described by the EEA Guidebook to identify offshore idling due to
weather delays, assuming that the vessels cannot anchor anywhere. It is important
to note that during this phase, both the main and auxiliary engines operate at the
same load factors as during the manoeuvring phase. Nevertheless, assessing the
differences between idling and active operation is crucial for a more comprehensive
analysis of the overall emissions impact.

5.1
Vessel fleet
Various vessels from different companies within the marine industry have been chosen
to form the base vessel fleet. Table 5.3 classifies the vessels into WOMBAT categories
and lists the parameters required to run the simulation. These parameters include speed,
fuel type, rated power and diesel engine type for both main and auxiliary engines, as
required by the emission calculation model. A brief summary of each vessel category
is given below, together with the corresponding data sources for the vessels used in the
base fleet in square brackets.
• Crew trasfer vessel (CTV) These vessels are generally accessible throughout the
year and are primarily used to transport crews to and from offshore installations.
[Iceni Conquest, [46]]
• Small Crane or Field Support Vessel (FSV): This category includes vessels fitted
with small cranes or similar equipment. They are highly deployable, offer lower
operating costs compared to larger cranes and play an important role in improving
operational efficiency and cost effectiveness. [NCT OFFSHORE Coco, [47]]
• Large cranes or heavy lift vessels (LCN/HLV): Despite their higher costs and longer
delivery times, these vessels play a crucial role in the management and installa­
tion of large and bulky components in offshore wind projects. Their primary func­
tion revolves around maintenance tasks, which are a fundamental aspect of on­site
strategies. It is important to note that there is currently no floating crane vessel on
the market with the capacity to handle a floating 15 MW offshore wind turbine, taking
into account both the lifting height and weight requirements [44]. [Seaway Yudin,
[48]]
• Cable Equipment (CAB): These specialised offshore wind vessels are designed to
lift cables from the seabed. They play a key role in the installation and maintenance
of the subsea cables that connect wind turbines to the onshore grid. [Van Oord
Nexus, [49]]
• Tugboat (TOW): Tugboats in floating wind projects play a crucial role in towing and
positioning floating platforms, supporting installation activities, facilitating mainte­
nance and repair operations, and providing emergency response capabilities. [JDC
Assister, [50]]
• Diving Support Vessel (DSV): Tailored for offshore operations, diving support ves­
sels are specialized vessels equipped with the necessary gear for inspecting sub­
structures and cables underwater. They hold a pivotal position in preserving the
integrity and functionality of underwater components through regular inspections
and maintenance. [Learsk Marine C­FORCE, [51]]
• Anchor Handling Vessel/Tug (AHV/AHT): Designed for offshore operations, anchor
handling vessels are specialised tugs equipped to repair mooring lines and anchors.
[BB Ocean, [52]]
In addition to their service speed, TOW and AHV vessels have an additional parameter
relating to towing. This parameter is different from the transit speed, as indicated in the
Table 5.3. Specifically, the towing speed for TOW is set at 18.5 [km/h]. Similarly, for AHV,
a towing speed of 10.44 [km/h] is used, maintaining the same ratio between transit and
towing speed as in the case of TOW.

6
Results and discussion
This section presents and discusses the results of the study. The structure will mirror that
of the research, beginning with an investigation into the impact of various trip phases,
followed by an examination of distance to the shore, and concluding with an analysis of
how the fleet characteristics influence emissions. The on­site strategy will be the initial
focus of analysis, with the tow­to­port strategy presented where feasible. It is noteworthy
to mention that in WOMBAT, several challenges arise when executing the tow­to­port
strategy, thus the related results are presented with a significant level of uncertainty.
In all scenarios, several unique simulations were conducted, each with a different seed.
This approach was adopted to mitigate the stochastic influence of failure distribution on
the results, thereby enabling a consistent basis for comparison. The results are presented
in terms of mean values, accompanied by the standard deviation. However, the standard
deviation is only reported when it signifies meaningful differences. In instances where
the standard deviation does not convey significant variation, solely the mean values are
provided.
6.1
Impact of trip phase
This section first provides an overview of the different normalised emissions compared
to total energy production for the base case, illustrated in the former chapter 5. This
approach allows the results to be discussed and compared with various studies found in
the literature. Histograms are then used to show the duration of maintenance operations
for each vessel in the fleet, distinguishing between cruising, manoeuvring and offshore
idling. This method helps to identify potentially significant aspects. At the same time, the
emissions in relation to the duration are plotted, with a distinction between main engines
and auxiliary engines. Following these analyses, further simulations are performed to
explore the impact of varying inputs on the results.

allocated for basic operational needs.

emission reduction possibilities and avoid prolonged idling of large vessels. Specifi­

drive generators, as well as replacements for power converters and electrical sys­

considered impractical for CTVs to handle major generator replacements. A new
Table A.7 has been created with updated service vessel details for this scenario.

2, where service tasks are shifted from HLVs to CTVs where possible to minimize
emissions.

6.2
Impact of distance to shore
To accurately assess the influence of varying the distance of the wind farm from the shore
while ensuring the consistency of simulations multiple simulations are conducted at each
iteration of distance with different random seeds. This approach is employed due to the
observed tendency of WOMBAT to generate a specific set of maintenance and failure
events for each input configuration. The analysis considers the range from 20 to 200 km
from the coast. This represents the distance each vessel must travel to reach the wind
farm, while the distances between turbines remain unchanged. Additionally, WOMBAT
does not assess wind and wave data individually for each location when adjusting the dis­
tance from shore. Consequently, the total energy production, represented by the capacity
factor, remains constant throughout the iterations of varying distances. The only variation
occurs due to downtime caused by repair tasks, known as availability. The analysis was
performed using the base case for the onsite strategy.
The tow­to­port strategy is not presented due to the issues caused by the selection of
random seeds. For each distance to the port, it is necessary to search for the seeds
that allow the simulation to succeed by running a loop from 1 to 100. This practice is
considered extraordinary, as it is assumed that the final version of WOMBAT will resolve
this problem.

7
Conclusion
For the on­site strategy, the analysis concludes that the base case is the worst scenario,
as numerous hours are spent by the Heavy Lift Vessel (HLV), particularly in offshore idling
due to weather delays, resulting in a Global Warming Potential (GWP) equivalent to 15.0
gCO2eq/kWh, close to the GWP of an entire LCA for an onshore wind farm. Indeed, off­
shore idling accounts for 49% of the total emissions, while maneuvering contributes 33%,
and the cruise phase represents only 18%. Additionally, the various scenarios explored
show that reductions in the model’s load factor (LF) for offshore idling and reductions in
operations conducted by the HLV can decrease the GWP to 6.6 gCO2eq/kWh, reducing
emissions by 56% compared to the base case. In this context, the contribution of each
phase trip is balanced, with cruise, maneuvering, and offshore idling now accounting for
37%, 36%, and 27%, respectively.
Despite uncertainty in the results due to the software used, the tow­to­port strategy instead
presents excellent results with a GWP around 4.2 gCO2eq/kWh for the Low LF scenario.
Here, the presence of tugboats significantly reduces emissions because maintenance op­
erations are conducted onshore, and the time spent offshore idling is considerably lower
compared to the on­site strategy. However, availability of the windfarm only reached 85%,
compared to 87.9% in the on­site strategy base case.
Distance from the shore has a significant impact on total emissions. Specifically, car­
bon emissions between 20 and 200 km were analysed using the base case with on­
site strategy, revealing that for every 20 km, the GWP increases by approximately 0.5
gCO2eq/kWh. In contrast, the windfarm availability remains unaffected by distance, with
a consistent value of around 88% identified.
In conclusion, the investigation extends to the ”green” fleet employing Liquefied Natu­
ral Gas (LNG) fuel for the on­site strategy, offering a comparison with the base case
scenario. Emissions experience a notable decline of 30% in GWP, transitioning from
15.0 gCO2eq/kWh in the base case scenario to 10.3 gCO2eq/kWh. Furthermore, SO2
emissions are entirely eliminated, and PM2.5 and PM10 witness a substantial reduction of
almost 100%.
7.1
Future work
First and foremost, it is essential to complete the development of WOMBAT to ensure
consistent and accurate simulations. There are still issues with the software that prevent
the simulations from running smoothly. For example, it was necessary to modify the core
code to assign the distance from shore to all vessels, each of which had different pa­
rameter configuration methods. In addition, the tow­to­port strategy results in significantly
longer simulation times than the on­site approach, which significantly delayed the analy­
sis in this report. Finally, it is essential for developers to carry out future validations with
real data to validate the O&M simulations with real cases. Therefore, the data presented
in this report should be considered as a first step, but it is recommended to revisit the
analysis once the software is fully developed.
In addition, it is recommended to provide more detailed descriptions of vessel movements
for floating wind farms. Collaborating with vessel and marine engine manufacturers could
improve the accuracy of fuel consumption data for specific phases. This involves con­
ducting thorough investigations into vessel trips in reality. This effort could result in model
