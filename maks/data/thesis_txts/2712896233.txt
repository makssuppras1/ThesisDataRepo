Abstract 
To ensure future needs, circularity is crucial to the building industry. This study examines the need to 
assess multiple life cycles of building materials in Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and tests allocation 
approaches, to support the industry in a circular and sustainable transition. Questions arise about how 
benefits and burdens should be allocated between life cycles of building materials. The potential of 
circular construction is evaluated by End of Life scenarios of recycling and reusing wood. An 
attributional LCA is conducted to compare the impacts of a glue laminated timber (GLT), concrete, and 
steel column relative to Global Warming Potential (GWP). To examine a bio-based material, the analysis 
includes assessing biogenic carbon accounting by the 0/0, -1/+1 and the dynamic approach of GWPbio 
factors. To assess the scenarios of recycling and reuse for one- and three life cycles of 50 years, the 
Circular Footprint Formula (CFF), the 100:0 cut-off approach, the 50:50, and the Linear Digressive (LD) 
have been tested and evaluated. Comparing the three structural materials for GWP, wood was 
favourable, as the material needs the least energy in production and has the potential to store carbon 
for decades. Comparing the biogenic carbon approaches, the GWPbio method showed a more realistic 
result, crediting relative to the rotation- and storage periods. GWPbio is motivating for life cycle 
extension and additional life cycles. From evaluating the allocation approaches, the CFF formula is the 
only approach considering the market situation relative to supply and demand and the quality relative 
to downcycling or direct reuse. Furthermore, the CFF formula aims to support all scenarios, including 
reuse, recycling, incineration with or without energy recovery and final disposal. For increased future 
demand of wood, consequences may arise regarding biodiversity and land use change. Thus, 
sustainable forest management must include a perspective on the rotation period to avoid 
deforestation and burden shifting.

1 Introduction  
Seen from the recent decades, urbanisation has exponentially increased simultaneously with the 
human population growth (Ghaffar et al., 2020). Urbanisation is generating more buildings, 
consequently increasing resource consumption. Currently, the global construction industry is 
responsible for consuming 32 % of the natural resources (Ghaffar et al., 2020). Additionally, it is 
accountable for 39 % of all human-induced emissions, of which 11 % comes from manufacturing of 
building materials and products (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021). In developed countries, construction 
and building maintenance generates 40 % of solid waste streams (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021). In 
addition to these numbers, the building industry is experiencing a scarcity of resources. In Denmark  
raw materials such as lime, chalk, stone, sand, and gravel will only last a few more decades (Regioner, 
2017).  
By the current material consumption and waste management, most materials are either downcycled 
or end up as waste after demolition. If the consumption continues scarcity of resources will increase if 
not transition to circular construction. In relation to the impact from consumption of materials, the 
global warming potential is an impact category of great importance. Global warming and climate 
change is considered being crucial, according to the climate tipping points presented by the 
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC, 2022). To meet these challenges, Denmark target a 
reduction of greenhouse gas emissions by 70 % in 2030, compared to 1990, and aims to reach climate 
neutrality in 2050 (Secretariat, 2022).   
The aim of lowering the environmental impact of buildings creates awareness of the embodied energy, 
which is the amount of energy used to produce building materials. As production of most building 
materials requires large amounts of energy, focusing on building materials is essential for a sustainable 
transformation of the building industry. By circular construction, the industry can manage building 
materials and utilise the economic value and durability for multiple life cycles. However, the current 
practice is to downcycling materials, even though the economic and environmental benefits of reusing 
building materials are much higher (Eberhardt et al., 2019).  
To analyse the environmental performance of building materials, there is established a consensus that 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) can assess individual products for a single life cycle (Malabi Eberhardt et 
al., 2020). When considering circularity, a multiple life cycle perspective is essential. Therefore, 
questions arise about how benefits and burdens should be allocated between multiple life cycles.  
Corresponding to reducing CO2 emissions and saving scarce resources, bio-based materials have gained 
interest in the building industry. Bio-based materials have potential to store carbon, needs a minimum 
energy in production and is a renewable resource. These parameters can contribute to a more 
sustainable industry. As buildings have a long use stage, the building structures have a definite potential 
to store biogenic carbon. Furthermore, gaining knowledge of the environmental benefits associated 
with temporary storage of biogenic carbon in bio-based building materials is needed. The most 
consistent and realistic accounting method is not yet agreed upon, causing irregularity in assessing bio-
based materials. (Andersen et al., 2021).  
To examine these issues further, this study evaluates the need to assess multiple life cycles of building 
materials in LCA. End of Life allocation approaches are tested to support the industry in a circular and 
sustainable transition. As bio-based materials reveals potential to lower environmental impacts, the 
material selection is investigated, considering how biogenic carbon storage will impact the LCA results. 
This study is based on an examination of three different building materials: wood, concrete, and steel, 
in the case of a loadbearing column. Hence, the study aims to support and develop the built 
environment towards a circular construction and delink the environmental impact from economic 
growth. The research questions are stated in the following section.

3 The LCA Methodology and Quantifying Emissions  
To evaluate the potential for reuse and recycling of building materials, it is necessary to collect 
information and map processes and emissions concerning the life cycle of a product. When knowing 
the processing, handling, and transportation of the given product, it is possible to quantify and analyse 
the impact of these processes. A glue laminated timber column is examined and compared to a 
loadbearing column of steel and concrete. To calculate the emissions and the potential for reuse, it is 
essential to use a standardised method to quantify the emissions. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is an 
established scientific approach developed to analyse the environmental impact categories and quantify 
the burdens and potential benefits associated with the life cycle stages of a building or building material. 
LCA is comparative and shows if one product has a lower environmental impact than another. This 
section introduces the LCA methodology and the life cycle perspective to quantify emissions. 
Additionally, the strengths and challenges found in the LCA methodology are stated, which leads to the 
research questions stated in the above section 2.   
 
3.1 LCA Methodology  
LCA is a standardised method to assess the environmental impacts throughout the life cycle of a 
product (Andersen et al., 2021). The quantitative methodology of LCA allows to compare the 
environmental impacts of different processes and product systems. Thereby LCA can compare 
processes and point out the processes that contribute the most to the overall environmental impact, 
which should receive attention and be optimised. (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018).  
The LCA framework operates with four separate phases: The goal and scope definition, Inventory 
analysis, Impact assessment and Interpretation.  
The goal and scope have the intention to state why the study is conducted and what question is 
intended to answer by performing the LCA. It also scopes the product system by deciding which 
activities and processes belong to the product’s life cycle and selecting the geographical and temporal 
boundaries. The next step of the LCA is to collect information about the physical flows and study all the 
processes identified belonging to the product system. The Inventory states the input of resources, 
materials, and products and finds emissions and waste output. The flows must be scaled by the 
reference flow of the product determined from the functional unit. The outcome of the Inventory 
analysis is a list of elementary flows for the product system. The inventory analysis often relies on 
generic data from databases. The Impact assessment is performed based on the Inventory flows. It 
transforms the flows into impacts on the environment using models from environmental science. It 
includes the selection of impact categories, e.g., Global Warming Potential (GWP), and the 
characterisation to quantify each of the assigned elementary flows to impact the indicator of the 
category. The Impact Assessment is automated mainly by LCA software. Normalisation and weighting 
can support comparison across the impact categories. Finally, the results of the LCA study are 
interpreted to answer the questions in the goal definition. Sensitivity analysis and uncertainty analysis 
are applied to ensure the robustness of the conclusions made from the LCA study. (Michael Z. 
Hauschild, 2018).  
Often, the LCA of a building or building material takes the basis of the attributional approach to LCA, to 
find what environmental impact the product is responsible for. The approach considers the impacts 
which can be attributed to the product under study. On the other hand, consequential LCA finds the 
environmental consequences of consuming the product. Therefore, the consequential LCA considers 
the future impacts estimated due to a change in demand. (Andersen et al., 2021).

3.1.1 Life Cycle Perspective  
When collecting the information used to state the elementary flows for the Inventory, a lifecycle 
perspective is used in LCA. The life cycle perspective reflects materials being extracted from the 
environment and followed until they are finally returned to the environment at their End of Life (EoL). 
Throughout the life cycle, the influence done to extract and produce a product will impact other 
environmental parameters.  
The environmental impact is analysed to quantify the burdens and potential benefits associated with 
the life cycle stages of raw material extraction, production, manufacturing, use and disposal of a 
product. The different life cycle stages are divided into modules, where A1–A3 refers to the production 
stage (cradle to gate), A4–A5 is the construction processes, B1–B7 is the use stage and C1–C4 indicates 
the EoL stage. Beyond the life cycle is module D, which counts for using recycled or reused material in 
a new product for a new life cycle. (Hill and Dibdiakova, 2016). These stages and modules are seen in 
Figure 1 below.

(BIRGISDÓTTIR, 2020). 
The EoL stage of a product is always uncertain, and future emissions and processes must be predicted. 
Especially the use stage for buildings, which often is decades, introduces high uncertainty regarding the 
following stages including the EoL stage (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018). The uncertainty includes 
potentially changing electrical mixes throughout the use stage, waste treatment procedures and 
efficiencies. Also, the actual lifetime can vary compared to the technical service life stated. 
Furthermore, it is difficult to know if a product actually will be recycled even if stated in the year of 
production.  
 
3.1.2 Handling of Multifunctional Processes and Co-products 
In addition to the primary functions of a product system, secondary functions can also appear in the 
life cycle. Secondary functions interact with other systems than the studied primary product system. A 
process is multifunctional when it provides more than one function or delivers several outputs, where 
not all are used by the primary product’s reference flow. This is e.g. the case for waste incineration, 
which provides more than one service. The process offers the multiple services of getting rid of wastes 
as the primary function and delivering both electricity and heat simultaneously in a secondary function.  
As soon as a co-product arises from a multifunctional process, it will be used in another system. The 
environmental impacts from the process can no longer be entirely ascribed to the primary product 
system studied in the LCA.  
==PAGE:11==
Page 5 of 82 
 
This issue of multifunctionality is handled in the ISO 14044 standard (ISO.org, 2006). It presents a 
hierarchy of solutions. It states that the solution is a subdivision of the concerned process, and if this is 
not possible, system expansion and, as the last option, allocation should be applied. Subdivision is 
performed by introducing additional detail in the modelling of the process to subdivide into two or 
more processes that each contribute to producing only one of the products (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018) 
(European-Commission-JRC, 2010). In the case of sawing wooden logs into columns, it is impossible to 
divide the sawing process. When sawing, the primary product is the column, but the process will also 
generate sawdust and small wood residues. The sawdust and residues cannot be avoided but is used in 
other product systems. Thus, the sawing process is multifunctional and generates co-products.  
Next preferred in the ISO hierarchy is system expansion, also called substitution. This means expanding 
the second process (e.g. incineration of sawdust) with the most likely alternative way of providing the 
secondary function of the first process. This is equivalent to subtracting the alternative way from system 
1, also called credit system 1, with the avoided inputs and outputs. The system expansion approach is 
thus mathematically equivalent to crediting for avoided production of the secondary products (Michael 
Z. Hauschild, 2018). E.g., if the sawdust from the sawing process is going to incineration, the secondary 
function can provide electricity from sawdust as a co-product. This avoided electricity production from 
the average mix, including both renewable and non-renewable sources, is then subtracted from the 
primary process of sawing wood into columns. The system is credited for making more products, and 
hence not all emissions is given to the primary product.    
If neither subdivision nor system expansion is possible, the ISO standard recommends physically 
allocation. This means dividing the inputs and outputs of the multifunctional process between the 
different products, by a physical relationship between the products or functions. This could be in terms 
of mass or weight. When no physical parameter can be identified, allocation can be performed from an 
economic relationship of the products’ respective economic values. (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018).  
 
3.1.3 Allocation at End of life – Open-loop and closed-loop systems 
Several allocation procedures are applicable for reuse and recycling. Reuse can be defined as products 
or components used again for the same purpose as initially designed for. Recycling involves processing 
and treating waste materials to produce new materials in new product systems. This often requires 
more energy as materials are reshaped or cut to enter a new system.  
The most crucial difference between reuse and recycling is the perspective of closed-loop and open-
loop, seen in Figure 2. A closed-loop system is where the material is kept in the same system. At the 
End of Life (EoL), the material for recycling or reuse is shunted back to an earlier process in the same 
system. It directly replaces the input from primary production of the same material for the product and 
less virgin material is needed for the next life cycle (European-Commission-JRC, 2010). This is direct 
reuse or internal recycling of production waste, for a closed-loop system.  
Open-loop recycling is a more complex but a more common way of recycling. At the EoL, the material 
for recycling is used in different system(s). Open-loop recycling is typical for materials that often are 
recycled to the same type of material but can be used for different products (European-Commission-
JRC, 2010). This is often seen as a material pool in the market, e.g. for glass and metals being remelted 
into other products in another system.

3.2 Strengths of LCA   
LCA is a recommended tool for quantifying emissions and environmental impacts. It is a quantitative 
and objective method of assessing a product’s environmental impact throughout its life cycle. It has the 
strength as a scientifically based and ISO-standardised method for evaluating the environmental 
performance of a product (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021). LCA is chosen as a  method in the context of 
several international and European standards, including ISO-14040 (2006), ISO-14044 (2006) and EN-
15804 (2019), which provides consistency to the methods used to conduct LCA. (Hoxha et al., 2020).  
The life cycle perspective in the LCA methodology allows for identifying and preventing the burden-
shifting between life cycle stages. Burden shifting can happen when efforts to lower environmental 
impacts in one process or life cycle stage unintentionally create even more significant environmental 
impacts in other processes or life cycle stages. This risk will be reduced by considering all stages and 
including as many life cycle stages as possible. (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018).   
An LCA can assist in identifying prospects to improve the environmental performance of a product in 
one or more stages of the life cycle. It can inform decision-makers in the industry or government to 
make better environmental choices about strategic planning and design (ISO.org, 2006).  
The most considerable strength of LCA is the comprehensiveness as it allows for the comparison of 
environmental impacts of product systems including many processes, accounting for resource uses and 
emissions taking place in different places at different times (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018). Therefore, 
LCA is a method that can predict the impact of products and compare associated emissions and 
environmental impact.

3.3 Challenges of LCA  
The comprehensiveness is also a limitation, as it involves simplifications and generalisations when 
modelling a product system and the environmental impacts. When considering the uncertainties of 
mapping flows and resources, it is seen that LCA cannot calculate the actual environmental impacts, 
only the potential (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018). The environmental impacts are based on assumptions, 
scenarios, and future predictions.  
Another significant methodology limitation is that LCA is only comparative and shows if one product 
has a lower environmental impact than another. Therefore, it is impossible to conclude if a product is 
environmentally sustainable, in absolute terms, from an LCA study. (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018).   
Furthermore, the following sections describe the challenges of biogenic carbon accounting and multiple 
life cycles in LCA, leading to the research questions found in section 2.  
 
3.3.1 Biogenic Carbon Accounting  
One of the main advantages of using wood as a building material is the capability to store large amounts 
of atmospheric carbon for long periods and thereby have an opportunity to lower CO2 emissions and 
reduce climate change. There is an ongoing discussion on how, and even if, temporary carbon storage 
contributes to reducing GWP. (Hill and Dibdiakova, 2016), (Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018).  
The growth of trees includes the uptake of carbon by photosynthesis and carbon storage appear 
through biomass accumulation within the trees, thus called biogenic carbon. This means that as the 
trees are growing, they take up carbon. Carbon storage will have the most effect if the wood is used in 
long life cycle products after the tree is harvested. Moreover, the biogenic carbon is released 
immediately if the wood is incinerated as biofuel. This presents a potential for sustainable harvested 
wood to be used in construction, and store even more carbon in the building than achieved if the forest 
was matured naturally and decomposed (Ramage et al., 2017). If keeping the carbon out of the 
atmosphere until the carbon is released at EoL, it is argued that it may positively affect the environment 
(Michael Z. Hauschild, 2018). Some of the arguments for temporary storage of carbon are to buy time 
for technological progress and adaptation in the society and to delay or temporarily avoid radiative 
forcing, which can reduce the risk of exceeding possible climate tipping points (Brandao et al., 2013).   
The question is how the biogenic carbon stored in bio-based products should be accounted for in LCA 
and if it should be included in the environmental impact evaluation. LCA of bio-based products is 
especially challenging due to the uncertainties in modelling the natural biomass. There is no clear 
consensus on modelling the biogenic carbon released or absorbed during the life cycle (Hoxha et al., 
2020). Fundamental aspects still lack consensus in methodologies, such as a static or dynamic account 
of carbon and the choice of climate indicator and time horizon applied. This causes complexity in 
quantifying the embodied CO2 emissions of a building using wood. Therefore, scepticism regarding the 
environmental potential of wood in construction remains. (De Rosa et al., 2018), (Andersen et al., 2021).  
One simplifying assumption is biogenic carbon neutrality. This aspect assumes the amount of carbon 
harvested is balanced out by a similar amount of carbon regrown in the forest. This results in an net-
zero impact on the carbon balance (Head et al., 2021). The most used biogenic carbon accounting 
methods are the 0/0 and the -1/+ 1 approach. These are both static methods (De Rosa et al., 2018).  
The 0/0 approach excludes biogenic carbon completely from the assessment, simply stating the 
perspective and assumption that wood is biogenic carbon neutral when the CO2 uptake during tree 
growth is equivalent to the CO2 released at the EoL. Thus, the 0/0 approach does not consider any 
benefits of carbon uptake and has no impact on carbon release (Andersen et al., 2021).   
==PAGE:14==
Page 8 of 82 
 
The -1/+1 approach, seen in Figure 3, includes biogenic carbon in the assessment, but as the 0/0 
approach, accounts for biogenic carbon as neutral when considering the whole life cycle (Andersen et 
al., 2021). The approach includes the uptake of biogenic carbon in the product stage as an 
environmental benefit and the release at the EoL stage as an environmental impact. Therefore, it is 
seen that the product and construction process stages present lower environmental impacts, because 
they are shifted to the EoL stage. (Hoxha et al., 2020).

The two approaches should provide the same results if applied correctly, as both assume bio-based 
materials to be biogenic carbon neutral. Nevertheless, the -1/+1 approach causes a risk of false results 
if only some life cycle stages are considered. Several LCA studies only include the product 
manufacturing stages (A1-A3), justifying between 70 % and 80 % of the GWP is caused here (Hoxha et 
al., 2020). But at these stages, only the uptake of biogenic carbon is included in the assessment when 
using the -1/+1 approach. The release of carbon is excluded because the EoL stage is not considered. 
This will lead to negative results for the GWP and failing to provide accurate data to inform decision-
making. Therefore, and in general, it is not suggested to limit the system boundary to specific life cycle 
stages (Hoxha et al., 2020). However, the -1/+1 approach can increase the transparency of the biogenic 
carbon flows throughout the life cycle, even though the interpretation of the biogenic carbon flow is 
simplified. 
The main criticism of the two simplified biogenic carbon approaches is to not consider the impact of 
the carbon emissions timing, nor the influence of the rotation periods related to the biomass growth. 
The rotation period in the forest is defined as the time it takes for the tree to grow until harvest. It is a 
crucial parameter that is slightly considered in the static approaches. The severity of some climate 
change impacts is linked to maximum temperature and CO2 concentration in the atmosphere; hence 
the timing of carbon emissions will have a climate effect. To better capture the impact of time, dynamic 
approaches have been developed. (Hoxha et al., 2020), (De Rosa et al., 2018).    
A dynamic time-dependent approach will instead assess emissions by a time-specific characterisation 
factor, as seen in Figure 4. Two different aspects of carbon uptake in the forest for the dynamic 
approach are noticed. The first aspect assumes that trees grow before using the harvested biomass 
product, following the natural carbon cycle. The other aspect is accounting for the regrowth of biomass 
after harvesting, and hence assuming an equal amount of the harvested biomass would start growing 
right after the production process. Thereby the benefits of carbon uptake are based on the situation in 
the forest. If the forest is regrown at the EoL, then carbon neutrality or benefit can be considered. This 
dynamic approach is deemed to be more transparent and reliable. (Hoxha et al., 2020).

The dynamic approach of the GWPbio method considers the time relation and combines the rotation 
period and storage period from the year after the wood is felled in the forest. The GWPbio factor is based 
on the method by Guest et al (Guest G, 2013). The factors are created from a function including the 
storage period of the wooden materials, the rotation period in the forest, and the time horizon where 
global warming potential (GWP) is measured. This study considers a 100-year time horizon.

all cases. Moreover, the GWPbio value never reaches -1, even though only carbon storage occurs. 
(Skullestad et al., 2016). Hence, the factor will never credit the system with the optimistic -1 factor and

By accounting for the time relation of carbon stored and regrowth of forests, the GWPbio states if a net 
sequestration of carbon will occur (Andersen et al., 2022). This means that if the storage period is long 
and rotation period is short, a negative value (benefit) is obtained for biogenic carbon. Hence, the 
carbon balance becomes net-negative and not zero. In contrast, if the storage period is short, i.e. if the 
bio-based materials are incinerated immediately, and the rotation period is long, then the GWPbio factor 
becomes positive. This indicates an overall increase in carbon emissions and thereby a burden 
(Andersen et al., 2022). From this approach, the rotation period of biomass products is essential since 
biomass regrowth will also require a rotation period to balance the global carbon cycle. The GWPbio 
matrix helps benefit long-lived bio-based products as a beneficial climate change mitigation (Guest G,

2013). The GWPbio method and the study by Guest et al. (Guest G, 2013) shows that biogenic carbon is 
not necessarily neutral, and the climate impact depends on the forest life cycle and on how the material 
is handled after the raw material extraction.  
The majority of current standards recommend the –1/+1 approach, while environmental product 
declarations (EPDs) are mainly following the 0/0 approach (Hoxha et al., 2020). Even though the 
dynamic approach to biogenic carbon accounting is the most reliable, it is primarily recommended in 
scientific papers. The different methodological approaches to dynamic LCA highlight the need to 
investigate the biogenic carbon accounting in LCA and develop standardised methods to assess biogenic 
carbon.  
 
3.3.2 Circularity for Multiple Life Cycles in LCA 
Circularity can be defined as seeking to narrowing by efficient resource use, slowing by extending the 
use stage, and closing resource and material loops for recycling instead of disposal (Eberhardt et al., 
2020). The effect of this methodology should be to keep materials and products at their highest value 
for as long as possible. As a result, waste and emissions can be minimised, and a renewable flow of 
products and materials is ensured (Eberhardt et al., 2020). When closing the loops, building materials 
can be reused for multiple life cycles.  
The focus of conventional LCA methods is analysing individual products and single life cycles. This does 
not cooperate with the idea of circular construction as described above. Assessing the environmental 
benefits of circularity in the building industry requires multi-systems and multi-life-cycle perspectives. 
This raises the question: How should the benefits and burdens of materials used for multiple life cycles 
be allocated between the cycles?  
The ISO 14044 standard (ISO.org, 2006) describes the issue of multifunctionality in general terms and 
provides a conceptual basis for modelling the EoL processes. The standard is made for the guidance of 
all product types, not only building material specific, and includes the ISO hierarchy, described in section 
3.1.2. Each of the allocation methods has limitations, especially concerning the circularity. The cut off 
approach excludes the secondary function, which is central to the concept of circularity. Regarding 
system expansion, it is difficult to predict future resources avoided at recycling, reuse and recovery and 
must therefore be assumptions and forecasting. Allocation can be based on a physical relationship or 
based on the economic value of the multiple products. Although economic distribution may be too 
arbitrary, it can be preferred if it is the only feasible procedure (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020). The 
guidance on modelling the allocation at EoL is general and can be interpreted in different ways.  
Questions arise regarding the interpretation of allocation approaches. Is the recycling of product A still 
to be included in the system boundary of product A, or does it belong to product B in the next life cycle 
as recycled content? On the other hand, it could be included in both product systems, but is leading to 
double counting? Or should the recycling process of product A be partly allocated to product A and partly 
to product B and should this be done on an equal basis of the half to each life cycle. Or is one product 
more responsible for the recycling process than the other? (Allacker et al., 2017). 
Interpretation of the allocation procedure has resulted in a range of different allocation approaches for 
the EoL modelling, all trying to allocate burdens fairly between cycles. However, it is causing no 
consistency nor comparability of different product LCAs. No single widely accepted approach among 
the various allocation approaches exists. (Eberhardt et al., 2020), (Garcia et al., 2020). Hence, more 
research and test of methods is necessary to implement circularity to LCA. This study will test different 
approaches arising from these questions for multiply life cycle products.

4 Life Cycle of a Glue Laminated Timber Column 
To perform an LCA of a glue laminated timber column (GLT column) for one- and multiple life cycles, 
the processes of each life cycle stages is studied and calculated relative to the environmental impact of 
GWP. The Inventory flows includes raw material extraction, manufacturing, further treatment, 
transport, use stage, and End of Life (EoL) scenarios, as visualised in Figure 5. To view the figure in large 
scale, see Appendix A.1. Moreover, this figure is based on a pre-thesis, seen in Appendix B.1.  
Bio-based products such as wood include two connected life cycles. The first cycle consists of the forest 
life cycle, where the raw material is extracted and processed into logs. The forest process has the 
unique quality of being renewable, symbolised by a green circle. The forest life cycle is connected to 
the building material life cycle, symbolised by raw material being extracted from the forest.  The second 
cycle, connected by the transportation of the logs to the manufacturer, is the building material’s life 
cycle. Depending on the choice of EoL-scenario there is a possibility of keeping the material in the loop, 
seen as an orange cycle. This second life cycle includes manufacturing logs into GLT columns, transport, 
use stage, and EoL scenarios: reuse, recycle, incineration or disposal. For least processing, the reuse 1:1 
avoids stages, and thereby closes and narrow the loop. The specific stages of the life cycles are 
described in the following sections. Furthermore, co-products arise in both cycles and are leaving the 
primary system for another function. These outputs are seen in the bottom green bar as the harvest 
residues, sawmill residues and construction waste going to either incineration, pulp and paper or 
production of fibre products.

The forest life cycle is included for bio-based building materials, as the wooden material is extract from 
the forest. This is shown as the light green circle in Figure 5. The Forest life cycle starts with seeding, 
including preparing the land used for the purpose. The tree is growing, and in this process, it takes up 
CO2 from the atmosphere, symbolised by the blue arrow. During the growing period, thinning and 
pruning optimise forest growth. When time goes by, fully grown trees are matured and stop the uptake 
of CO2. Without human interaction, the trees will decompose over time and release CO2 back into the 
atmosphere. If the fully grown trees are harvested instead, the CO2 keeps embodied. At harvest, some 
residues are produced when removing smaller branches, used for energy recovery when incinerated. 
The next step is to debark logs and prepare the logs for the following processes. The raw material 
extraction is then completed, and the round wood logs are transported out of the forest.  
The round logs are transported to the Sawmill manufacturing process from the raw material extraction. 
Here the logs are cut into wood panels at the factory, and the remaining parts are demolished into 
more refined pieces used in fibre products and for energy recovery when incinerated. The left-over 
sawdust is incinerated and used for energy recovery. When the panels are finished, they are 
transported to the Production of building material for the treatment process. Here the panels are 
shaped into building materials such as glued laminated timber, where glue is added and may be treated 
with durability extending chemicals. The final wood product is transported to the construction site for 
further construction of the building on site. Here some minor part becomes construction waste due to 
customisation. The building life span depends on the type of construction. Nevertheless, the building 
life span consideration period is 50 years regardless of individual material properties. Included in the 
use stage, replacement and recovery of damaged materials are incorporated.  
At the End of life of a building, the building materials are taken apart and collated for different purposes. 
The building material will decompose and release CO2 and other emissions into the ground by putting 
it into a landfill. Therefore, today a minimal amount of waste is going into landfills in Denmark. Another 
option at End of life is for the building material to go to incineration to have energy recovery as an 
output. When incinerated, CO2 is released.  The most preferred option is to reuse or recycle the building 
material to extend its service life of the building material. Recycling includes processing the building 
material to procedure another material and often as downcycling. e.g., a wooden column is transported 
to the sawmill to be cut into smaller parts for façade cladding or fibres for particle boards. This requires 
energy for the processes, but no or little new raw material is put into these products. This is indicated 
by following the orange lifecycle and not taking the material out of the circle, i.e., closing the loop. 
Reuse does not require new processing, as characterised by direct reuse. This means that the building 
material is designed to disassemble the product from the building and move it to another building 
where it can be used for the same purpose – i.e., not downcycled or processed but used as a column 
again in a new building's life cycle.

5 Prerequisites  
The study carries out a comparative analysis of a wood-, concrete- and steel column with comparable 
strength properties. The wooden column is a Glue Laminated Timber Column (GLT Column), the 
Concrete Column is a prefabricated C30 Column (Concrete Column), and the Steel Column is a H-profile

from structural calculations, and the representativeness of time, technology, and place. Moreover, this 
section visualises the product system of each included material. 
The professional software, SimaPro version 9.3, is used for the product system modelling. This program 
helps to allocate complex system- and product emissions in a transparent and detailed way. To conduct 
the analyse, ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint (H) is selected as the methodology used in SimaPro. This 
methodology assigns 18 impact categories and contains the desired indicator, Global Warming 
Potential [kg CO2-eq]. This choice leads to some limitations due to the neglect of the remaining 
indicators; on the other hand, it eases the comparisons. The chosen database in SimaPro is Ecoinvent 
3, providing the necessary products and systems. All processes are modelled from the Ecoinvent 
database, and thus only generic data is used for this LCA.

inventory consists of the main processes for making GLT columns for the first life cycle. From the study 
of the life cycle described in section 4, the relevant processes are found. The included processes in the 
Inventory used in SimaPro are listed in Table 8 on the next page. This includes forest management, 
sawmill processes, and GLT manufacturing. These processes are found in SimaPro’s database Ecoinvent

The forest process and management are included in the sawmill process. The dataset of the forest 
process includes sawlog and veneer logs from the market, where softwood is from Europe without 
Switzerland. Moreover, the transport distances are included. The activity starts in the forest, with the 
delivery of the sawlogs and veneer logs and ends with the delivery of the product to the consumers.  
Within this data, forest management is included. The management data covers the production and 
harvesting of 1 m3 of stem wood from spruce without bark from sustainable forest management in 
Sweden. The activity starts with site preparation assuming natural regeneration and planting, including 
seedling production, site preparation, planting, tending, young growth tending, clearing, thinning, and 
harvesting. Moreover, the operations include processing wood fuel to chips and chopped wood over 
one rotation period. In addition to this, it covers the maintenance and construction of forest roads. The 
activity ends with assortments at the forest road.  
The sawmill process includes the sawing process of sawlog and veneers log in the sawmill, where slab 
and siding are co-products. These co-products are further chipped into chips at the sawmill. The sawing 
process starts with sorting the log at the sawmill and ends with sawn softwood at the sawmill. The 
service of sawing 1 m3 softwood at the sawmill contains interlinked processes, such as the process 
structure of the sawmill. Additionally, the construction of the sawmill is also included. Manufacturing 
of the GLT column occurs at a wooden board factory. The data covers German production, which is 
assumed to be representative of Europe. The data for GLT are estimated based on data for three-
layered laminated boards. The activities start with the reception of sawn softwood and glue at the 
factory gate. The activity ends with GLT at the factory gate. In the forest and sawmill processes, there 
are created residuals. The residues are handled as co-products credited by substitution, see Section 
3.1.2. The co-products are going to the pulp and paper industry, to fibre products or are credited as 
energy recovery from incineration.

6.4 Impact Assessment and Results of One Life Cycle  
From the above sections 5.1 and 5.4, describing the functional unit of a loadbearing column and the 
Inventory flows from the processing of wood, concrete and steel, an impact assessment is made for the 
impact category Global Warming Potential (GWP). The GWP midpoint characterisation factor describes 
the radiative forcing impact of one mass-based unit of a given greenhouse gas relative to carbon 
dioxide. The unit is kilo carbon dioxide equivalent [Kg CO2-eq]. The direct consequence of global 
warming is an increase in the atmosphere and oceans’ temperature, leading to critical impacts such as 
the sea-level rise and extreme meteorological events (IPCC, 2022). The LCA methodology in SimaPro 
and other software uses GWPs developed by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). 
Endpoint characterisation factors such as Climate Change can also be considered but are uncertain due 
to the complexity of the impact pathway (Levasseur, 2015). Consequently, this study and impact 
assessment only consider the midpoint GWP.  
From the conducted impact assessment in SimaPro of the GLT-, concrete and steel column the total 
emissions for each column’s life cycle stage are found and presented graphically. The result of the glue 
laminated timber (GLT) column can be seen in section 6.4.1 and 6.4.2, concrete in 6.4.3 and, steel in 
6.4.4. The CO2 emissions are estimated relative to the life cycle stages where they appeared.    
Relative to the GLT column, the initial results are found without considering biogenic carbon, 
symbolised with yellow bars in Figure 18. This resulted in high negative impacts benefiting the system. 
Further three different biogenic carbon methodologies are examined and discussed.

6.4.2 Biogenic Carbon Accounting   
In most LCA studies performed today, the biogenic carbon in bio-based products is treated as climate 
neutral. This is due to the assumption that the carbon sequestrated during tree growth is released after 
the final use of the bio-based material, either through incineration or by natural decomposing 
(Andersen et al., 2022). However, this approach can be questioned, as factors such as forestry rotation 
and time of temporary sequestration in the building are not considered in these approaches. See 
section 3.3.1 for details in biogenic carbon accounting. Furter in this section the 0/0 approach, 1/+1 
approach, and the GWPbio factor approach are evaluated, based on the amount of biogenic [kg CO2- eq] 
stored and released during one life cycle.  
 
Calculation of Biogenic Carbon 
To compare biogenic carbon stored in wood and the associated GWP [kg Co2-eq], there is a need to 
convert biogenic carbon to kilogram carbon dioxide equivalent [kg CO2-eq]. This is done by finding the 
wood density of dry matter and multiplying it by a converting factor. The dry matter of wood contains 
50 % carbon, and the molecular weight of carbon is 12. The molecular weight of carbon dioxide is 44. 
By calculating this correlation, the converting factor of 1,83 is found. (Treindustrien, 2009).

Comparison of Biogenic Carbon Methods 
The main characteristics of each method are described in this section, for more details, see section 
3.3.1.  A comparison of the three methods is conducted and displayed graphically in Figure 19.  
The 0/0 approach implies that the amount of biogenic carbon is being balanced out by the carbon 
uptake in the forest and carbon release at End of Life (EoL). This method will produce identical results 
as if not considering biogenic carbon (Andersen et al., 2021). The results are therefore expressed by 
yellow bars in Figure 19.     
The -1/+1 approach accounts for biogenic carbon as neutral when considering the whole life cycle. It 
includes the uptake of biogenic carbon in the product stage as an environmental benefit and the release 
at the EoL stage as an environmental impact (Andersen et al., 2021).  
In this study, the total amount of biogenic [kg CO2-eq] stored in the GLT column are found. This quantity 
is multiplied by -1 and applied in life cycle stage A1, the forest process. Furthermore, at life cycle stage 
D, the amount of stored biogenic carbon [kg CO2-eq] is multiplied by a factor 1. This creates a negative 
value of 97 [kg CO2-eq] in A1 and a positive value, 97 [kg CO2-eq] at life cycle stage D. By this approach 
the biogenic carbon is assumed neutral for the complete life cycle. Even if the wood mass is not 
incinerated or decomposed at EoL, the approach does release biogenic carbon [kg CO2-eq] before 
entering a new life cycle. The results are expressed in dark green bars in Figure 19.    
The dynamic approach used in this study, accounts for the biogenic carbon released during the 
column’s life cycle at the time a wood mass is discarded from the system. This amount is multiplied by 
a GWPbio factor relative to the rotation period in the forest and the storage time i.e. the time where the 
wood residues leaves the primary system. The GWPbio factor is based on the method by Guest et al. 
(Guest G, 2013).  
Harvest and sawmill residues are discarded from the primary system at year 0 and have a storage time 
of 0. This results in a positive GWPbio factor, indicating burdens to the environment. The remaining 
biomass in the column is stored for 50 years. After 50 years only 22 % of the mass are incinerated at 
EoL. When stored for 50 years assuming a 60-year rotation period, the GWPbio factor is negative, 
indicating a credit for storage of the carbon and thereby an environmental benefit. When considering 
a 100-year rotation period, the GWPbio factor is positive, indicating a burden. The GWPbio factors are 
seen in Table 1, section 3.3.1.

From the analysis of one life cycle, the most impact (both burdens and benefits) is related to the life

is characterised by co-products arising from the forest process and sawmill process, which credits the

and reveals negative values of [kg CO2-eq] (benefits) if not considering biogenic carbon. This is by the 
assumption that the substitution for incineration is based on the production of electricity from a generic 
dataset of Ecoinvent 3 in SimaPro considering a grid mix for Denmark.  
From the results presented in section 6.4.5, the advantage of GLT as a load-bearing structural material 
is clear. The results only concern Global Warming Potential (GWP). However, other impact parameters 
should be investigated before making any decisions. This analysis finds the potential to use wood 
instead of concrete and steel for a load-bearing column to lower the GWP. Even if considering the 
biogenic carbon accounting or not, the GLT column reveals the lowest GWP.  
The analysis of one life cycle of the wood column concludes that biogenic carbon accounting is crucial 
for the total LCA results. If considering biogenic carbon by the dynamic GWPbio method, burdens are 
associated with the manufacturing stages. This is due to the GWPbio factors, considering the forest’s 
rotation period and the storage period of biomass products. As the manufacturing of a wood column 
creates a lot of residues from the forest and sawmill, a large amount of biomass is leaving the primary 
system at year 0. This makes a storage period of 0 years and results in a positive GWPbio factor, assigning 
burdens to the system at the manufacturing stage. This entails that the total impact of the wood column 
is found as a burden to the environment. This perspective shifts the conclusion of a net negative result 
(benefit) when not considering biogenic carbon to a conclusion, which assigns the life cycle of a wood 
column with burdens. When comparing this burden to the concrete- and steel column, the impact is, 
however, relatively lower. The first life cycle when incl. biogenic carbon accounting results in 24.64 [kg 
CO2-eq] for wood, 62.74 [kg CO2-eq] for concrete and 300.25 [kg CO2-eq] for steel’s life cycle. The 
dynamic biogenic carbon approach using GWPbio factors with a rotation period of 60 years, will be 
applied for further analysis.  
Based on the functional unit, the volume of the steel column is the smallest compared to the GLT- and 
concrete column. Nevertheless, the steel column provides the highest impact for one life cycle. This is 
due to the energy consumed when heating and melting the steel and from the raw material of pig iron. 
Comparing these energy-requiring processes, the GLT column is not treated by the same sort of energy 
intensive manufacturing processes.

7 Allocation for Three Life Cycles 
Different sustainability strategies are considered in the building industry to lower the Global Warming 
Potential (GWP) and meet the goals of reducing CO2 emissions. The strategies include circular economy, 
design for disassembly, renovating rather than building new, and using more bio-based materials in 
construction (Andersen et al., 2021). Circularity concerns the resource and energy inputs, and waste 
and emission outputs. Material resource consumption is minimised by extending the use stage, adding 
life cycles, and closing material loops by reusing. These approaches have the potential for significant 
savings of environmental impact (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020), (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021). Section 
10 examine the potential for circular construction and design principles. 
From the previous sections 6.4 and 6.5 and the literature reviewed (Helene Frederiksen, 2022), wood 
has a lower GWP than concrete and steel as a loadbearing building material. To examine the potential 
for circular construction, the following sections consider the modelling of reuse and recycling scenarios 
at EoL. The potentials are assessed by different allocation approaches by the scenarios of recycling and 
reusing a GLT column for three life cycles. This section examinates four allocation approaches and 
describes each characteristics. The results of the approaches are evaluated to find the most reliable 
result for allocating benefits and burdens. For the GLT column, biogenic carbon accounting is included 
in the results. At the end of this analysis, a sub-conclusion upon the allocation approaches is found.

7.2 Allocation Approaches 
Allocation issues arise about LCA when the End of Life includes reuse, recycling, or energy recovery for 
following life cycles. This is due to systems including multiple material outputs or when the primary 
product involves inputs from other products’ life cycles. Conventional LCA methods focus on assessing 
individual products and separate life cycles. This does not cooperate with the circular construction 
principles and evaluate the material’s potential for multiple life cycles. The building industry uses 
materials with complex and long life cycles (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020), which should be possible to 
assess in an LCA.  
This section examinates how to model building materials for multiple life cycles in LCA. This includes 
fairly dividing burdens and benefits relative to the manufacturing of the product and the product’s End 
of Life (EoL) scenario. EoL involves the possibility of reuse, recycling, energy recovery, and disposal. To 
be able to accomplish this, an allocation approach is needed. Four different allocation approaches are 
evaluated and tested in the following sections.  
 
7.2.1 Four Tested Allocation Approaches  
Interpretation of the allocation method has resulted in a range of different approaches for the EoL 
modelling, all trying to make a fair division of burdens and benefits for multiple cycles. It is causing no 
consistency nor comparability of different product LCAs regarding multiple life cycles. The allocation 
approaches can generally be sorted into three commonly acknowledged approaches, known as the 
0:100, 100:0 and 50:50 approaches. Even though various EoL allocation approaches have been 
proposed and discussed, there is no agreement on which procedure is preferred. (Malabi Eberhardt et 
al., 2020). 
By the 0:100 approach, the full impact of the recycling process is allocated to the product producing 
the recycled material. That means no burdens are allocated to earlier products, using the input of 
recycled materials. All burdens and benefits of recycling are then distributed to the last cycle. The 
approach assumes that the recycled materials produced at the EoL of the first life cycles will retain the 
properties of the original material input. And thus credits the product for displacing virgin material 
production in proportion to the recyclability rate.  
Oppositely, the 100:0 approach allocates the virgin material impacts and the recycling impacts to the 
first cycle, where the impact is emitted.  
The 50:50 approach allocates the recycling impacts in equal proportions between the two cycles, which 
is producing the virgin material and the one which is using the recycled material.  
To make comparative assessments of complex long life cycle products, a single prescriptive EoL 
allocation approach and formula is preferred (Allacker et al., 2017). Therefore, four of the most used 
approaches are exanimated and evaluated. The approaches and corresponding formulas differ in the 
allocation approach (0:100, 100:0 and 50:50 approach or a mix of these) and vary in the allocation of 
credits for avoided production, recycling, and disposal of the product.   
Approaches examined:  
• 
The Circular Footprint Formula (CFF) uses a mix of allocation approaches  
• 
The 100:0 cut-off approach  
• 
The 50:50 approach  
• 
The linearly degressive (LD) approach  
The approaches are described in more detail in the following sections.

7.2.2 The Circular Footprint Formula (CFF) 
The European Commission has created a guidance on how to allocate burdens at EoL. The approach is 
named the Circular Footprint Formula (CFF) (European-Commission, 2012). The CFF formula builds on 
existing approaches and uses all three allocation principles (100:0, 0:100 and 50:50). The allocation 
depends on the market situation of the material, whether there is a high or low supply and demand. 
Other allocation approaches favour either ingoing or outgoing secondary materials, while CFF covers 
both recycled content at the input side and recyclability at EoL in the output. The CFF formula aims to 
support all scenarios at EoL, including reuse, recycling, incineration with or without energy recovery 
and final disposal. Both open and closed-loop systems can be examined. The formula includes a 
material-, energy-, and disposal clause, seen in Figure 24.    
In relation to the material clause a quality correction ratio is included. This ratio reflects a difference in 
quality between the secondary material and the primary material, i.e. if downcycling or not. The CFF 
approach does not include how to define the qualities, but they can be estimated from a physical 
relationship or economic value. (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020).  
The CFF formula contains a clause concerning the material input and output of both primary (virgin) 
and recycled material, the energy used and recovered, and a part for disposal, as seen in  Figure 24. 
These three clauses added up, allocate the emissions to the one life cycle studied. If assessing more life 
cycles, the formula must be used for each life cycle, resulting in emissions allocated by the same formula 
with different parameters included for each life cycle relative to the scenario. The CFF formula and 
content are described in Figure 24, and all parameters are shown in Table 14. To view the figure in large 
scale, see Appendix A.5.

7.2.3 The 100:0 Cut-off Approach 
European LCA practice for construction products and buildings is based on the European standards EN 
15978 and EN 15804. In these standards, multifunctionality is handled through system expansion. Here 
the impacts of reuse, recycling and energy recovery are integrated into the system by crediting for the 
avoided processes from the most likely corresponding process. Impacts at EoL are calculated using the 
cut-off allocation approach. (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020).  
The 100:0 cut-off approach allocates the environmental impacts of recycling entirely to the product’s 
life cycle, providing the recycled materials. No burdens from recycling operations are allocated to the 
following life cycles.   
The environmental impacts of virgin material production are entirely allocated to the life cycle in which 
the material is first used, typically the first life cycle. Disposal is allocated to the last life cycle.  
From equation (1), the ratios of virgin and recycled content are found in parameters R1, ‘the proportion 
of material in the input to the production that has been recycled from a previous system’ and R2, ‘the 
proportion of the material in the product that will be recycled (or reused) in a subsequent system’. If all 
material will be reused for the following cycle, R2 equals 1. This results in the disposal parameter (ED) 
balancing out as it will be multiplied with (1-1) = 0. If the input is all reused material from an earlier 
cycle, R1 will be equal to 1; consequently, Ev will balance out as there is no virgin input and no emissions 
related.

The 50:50 approach is unconventional, assigning burdens from the recycling processes equal to the first

recycled material.

7.2.5 The Linear Degressive Approach 
Another unconventional approach is the Linear Degressive (LD) approach. However, it has been 
discussed and gained attention by researchers (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020), (Allacker et al., 2017).   
The LD approach allocates the impacts from virgin material production linearly digressively to all life 
cycles by assigning the highest share of impact to the first cycle where the production happens. 
Similarly, the disposal impact is allocated linearly digressively to all cycles, allocating the highest share 
of impacts to the last cycle where the disposal happens (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020). Reuse and

recycling impacts are dealt with by the 50:50 approach between the life cycles, and hence the impacts 
for recycling are equally allocated between the cycles.

e.g. the number of life cycles. The higher the number (n) of cycles, the lower the impact of the products, 
as it is allocated to (n) sections. Accounting for the number of times a material is reused or recycled 
aligns with the circular construction principles. (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2020).

7.6 Evaluation of End of Life Allocation Approaches  
Currently, no widely accepted allocation modelling approach is used to assign emissions to multiple life 
cycles (2022). Noteworthy differences in impact distributions between the allocation approaches were 
found for the four different allocation approaches for both scenario 1 and scenario 2, considering 
recycling and reuse, respectively. The results are found in sections 7.4 and 7.5 above.   
From the circular construction perspective, it is needed to motivate the building industry to design for 
multiple cycles and adopt a system perspective. Therefore, locating the most reliable and motivating 
allocation approach is essential. For all approaches, the most considerable share of allocated impacts 
arises from the production process in the first cycle. When allocating the most significant share to the 
first cycle, an incentive for reuse and recycling is achieved in the second and third cycles. This is due to 
a significantly less impact found in recycling or reusing instead of producing virgin material. The second 
and third cycle are assigned substantial fewer emissions when reusing or recycling material.  
The 50:50 approach allocates half of the reuse or recycling impact to the next cycle. Consequently, 
some motivation for reuse in the future is created, as not all are assigned the first cycle. Contrary, 
uncertainty is made when allocating impacts to future processes, as the future is based on predictions. 
In the future, essential factors such as increasing production efficiency, transportation and recycling 
technologies may be realised. The future will also include a more considerable share of renewable 
energy in the grid mix, as accounted for – see section 5.5.3. (Eberhardt et al., 2020).   
The 100:0 cut-off approach creates an irrational motivation to use secondary materials because the 
high impacts from production in the first life cycle are not considered in the second cycle. Hence, the 
cut-off approach only considers recycling or reuse but does not include the overall system perspective.  
The LD approach accounts for the number of cycles and thereby the times a material will be reused or 
recycled before EoL. This agrees with the terms of circularity even though the number of cycles is 
uncertain and challenging to predict. The approach can be misleading by adding cycles that may not 
exist, as the number of cycles lowers the impact per cycle. It will be unreliable and cause greenwashing 
when adding too many cycles if not designing for it in practice. (Eberhardt et al., 2020), (Malabi 
Eberhardt et al., 2020). The LD approach allocates emissions to recycled material, assuming virgin 
material must be produced to have recycled material. Therefore, regardless of virgin or recycled 
material, each life cycle is allocated a part of the emissions from the production. Nevertheless, when 
allocating the emissions linear degressive, one could argue that each cycle's burdens and benefits are 
distributed fairly. LD assumes all cycles are responsible for the environmental impacts emitted in the 
production of the material in the first life cycle. In that way, the approach drives the stakeholder’s 
ambition to contribute to the system of multiple life cycles.  
The CFF differs from 100:0, 50:50, and LD as it credits reuse, recycling, and energy recovery. This is not 
included in the other approaches. Furthermore, the CFF approach considers the market situation of the 
material relative to the supply and demand and thereby deals with the uncertainty of multiple cycles in 
the future. The quality parameters consider the treatment for a new cycle, and the system is credited 
if not downcycling. The CFF approach includes various parameters, making it comprehensive and time-
consuming due to the estimation and prediction of multiple parameters. Nevertheless, the approach 
provides transparency, reliability and completeness when examining the parameters carefully. 
(Eberhardt et al., 2020).  
The following section presents the pros and cons of the four approaches and concludes upon the 
approach used in further analysis.

7.7 Sub-conclusion for Allocation Approaches  
No consistency nor standardisation is found when examining End of Life (EoL) allocation approaches 
assessing multiple life cycles of building materials. This makes comparing LCAs difficult, as the different 
allocation approaches achieve different results. As no standard method is agreed upon, and reuse, 
recycling and recovery at EoL are not thoroughly investigated in the industry, it is complicated to decide 
which approach is the most reliable, consistent, and trustworthy. This study identified and tested four 
allocation approaches; the Circular Footprint Formula (CFF), the 100:0 cut-off approach, the 50:50 
approach and the linearly degressive (LD) approach. These are applied for a reuse- and a recycled 
scenario of a glue laminated timber (GLT) column.

the results of the recycling scenario showed a significant benefit allocated by the CFF, as energy 
recovery is considered. The other approaches do not include this. From the results stated in the prior 
sections and elaborated in section 7.6, there are several pros- and cons relative to each allocation

8.1  Recycling and Reuse of GLT Column  
As seen in Figure 28, the reuse scenario has been assigned fewer emissions than the recycling scenario, 
in the first life cycle. The recycling process creates 22 % waste, leaving the system for incineration. This 
is accounted for in the parameter Erecycling. Furthermore, the quality of the partitional column, estimated 
in an economic value, is 14 % less than for the GLT column. This is accounted for in quality output, Qout. 
Thus, less mass is transferred to the second cycle and the quality is reduced, compared to the reuse 
scenario.   
For the second life cycle, less energy and processing are applied to reuse the GL column than to make 
a partitional wall column. Therefore, more emissions are allocated in the recycling scenario. 
Additionally, the quality parameters influence the system, as the recycling scenario does include 
downcycling for each cycle, e.g., when converting from a partition wall column to a fibreboard.

recycling, as some material volume is leaving the system after both one and two cycles, i.e. after 50 and 
100 years of storage, respectively. This influences the impacts of the third cycle.

to biogenic carbon and the EoL scenario. For recycling in scenario 1, the fibreboard is incinerated at 
EoL, creating energy recovery. This causes the total mass being discarded from the system, which 
credits the system for storage at this point in time. The considerable negative impact (benefit) is seen 
in life cycle 3 in Figure 28.

8.3 Sub-conclusion for Reuse of a Wood, Concrete and Steel Column 
Discovered from the analysis and results, the three materials, i.e., wood, concrete, and steel, are 
different related to how they are modelled and the total impact. From the modelling, wood and 
concrete are made from virgin material in the first life cycle; in contrast, steel is made from 80 % 
recycled scrap. Furthermore, the last cycle is unique for the three materials, as recycling requires 
individual processes, which distinguishes the materials from each other. E.g. at EoL, the glue laminated 
timber (GLT) column is cut into fibreboard, concrete is made into roadfill, and steel is remelted, causing 
various impacts.  
When comparing the impact of global warming potential (GWP) of the three materials, wood reveals a 
significant minor impact for both one- and three life cycles. This is due to less energy-consuming 
processes of manufacturing the GLT column and credits arising from co-products. However, the credit 
from energy recovery is considered sensitive for the results. Considering the reuse scenario for the GLT 
column manufacturing in 2072, in section 7.5.1, future predictions include a considerable amount of 
renewable energy sources, resulting in less credit for avoided production. This will influence the total 
impact of the GLT column.   
In the modelling of the GLT column, biogenic carbon accounting by the GWPbio factor is found to be a 
crucial parameter. If not accounting for biogenic carbon, a considerable negative impact is given, which 
benefits the first life cycle. This results in no motivation to recycle and reuse the column for multiple 
life cycles, as this will lead to no benefit for the stakeholder of the second of the third life cycle. Hence, 
it is essential to consider the unique properties of the individual materials.  
From the analysis of the three materials, the drivers for reuse and recycling are evident in saving 
resources and reducing emissions. A significant reduction in the use of virgin material and a reduction 
in waste disposal are seen in both scenarios. Moreover, it has been found a great potential to decrease 
emissions for multiple cycles. The reuse scenario of the various materials is similar in the second cycle, 
as reuse causes little impact, and minimal processing is needed for all materials. This is because the 
reuse scenario concerns direct reuse for both input and output in the second cycle.   
In conclusion, all materials significantly save emissions when reused. Thereby, it is clear that reusing 
loadbearing building materials creates considerable potential for reduction of GWP. If it is possible to 
extend the lifetime or add cycles, it will save emissions and resources.

the energy mix found in SimaPro’s Econivent 3 database for Denmark with The Danish Energy Agency’s

In the book Life Cycle Impact Assessment (Rosenbaum et al., 2018), examining the sensitivity of a model 
is to which the variation of an input parameter or a choice leads to an interpretation of the model 
results. If a changing parameter substantially changes the results, the model is sensitive to the 
parameter.  
The results show a significant amount of credit is given in the life cycle stages A1-A3 using The Danish 
Energy Agency’s numbers from 2018. Based on the number of fossil fuels and coal used for energy

glulam production are incinerated. This provides energy substitution and has additionally been 
examined for the projected numbers for 2025.  
This sensitivity analysis shows that the energy parameter makes a considerable difference in the results, 
and the energy parameter in the modelling should be considered sensitive to such changes. 
Additionally, using Ecoinvent 3’s numbers, as done in this study, can be considered to be on the safe

10 Circular Economy in the Building Industry   
The findings from the LCA analysis indicate that the reuse of building materials significantly saves 
emissions and reduces Global warming potential (GWP). This potential is evident but not yet commonly 
practised in the building industry due to several challenges of introducing a new practice. Furthermore, 
a long tradition of downcycling with low risk and -responsibility characterises the process of handling 
materials and waste in the building industry. To reduce waste generation, resource consumption and 
emission to the environment, this section tries to find how circular construction can be implemented 
and which challenges the industry meets for scaling reuse and more circular material flows.  
This is examined by retrieving information from industry organisations, researchers, and interviews of 
key actors in the building industry. The primary data is found through the Information Centre for Circular 
Economy in Construction (VCØB), which develops and publishes knowledge about the circular economy 
to construction actors, and the Danish Technological Institute (DTI), which publishes handbooks, 
guidelines, and manuals and contributes to developing the industry. In addition, a sector development 
report published by the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 
was used. As a supplement, practical challenges and possible solutions were discussed by conducting 
interviews of Anders Strange Sørensen from the contractor firm, Enemærke & Petersen and Katja Udbye 
Christensen, the business and project manager for circular use of concrete elements at the department 
of concrete at DTI. Furthermore, to evaluate the state of the art regarding sustainability, 
interdisciplinarity and circularity in the building industry, the authors of this study participated in 
‘Vinterakademi’ 2022. Here conversations with several stakeholders and experts on these subjects were 
organised. The knowledge and information gathered are presented in the following sections.   
 
10.1 
Circular Construction 
Circularity can be defined as extending the life cycle of materials, products and components by recycling 
or reusing them into new life cycles. This involves reducing waste when a material reaches the end of 
its service life, keeping it within the loop and creating added value. The circular economy is a business 
model considering materials in a constant flow through practical and innovative reuse, minimising 
waste production and reducing the use of virgin materials (Ghaffar et al., 2020).  
To ensure sustainability, it is necessary to find an approach to overcome the contradiction between 
economic growth and environmental sustainability by decoupling growth from resource consumption. 
This cannot be done by the current linear business model, which extracts too many resources and 
generates tonnes of waste (Eberhardt et al., 2019). The circular economy will create an incentive to 
manage resources in a regenerative way by providing more economic stability through resource 
security. (Eberhardt et al., 2019). The circular strategy replaces the End of Life-concept with circularity 
and future cycles. The aim is to reduce environmental burdens, and secure resources in the future.  
There is no commonly accepted definition of circular economy in the building industry as the concept 
involves various design and construction strategies. These strategies could be Design for Disassembly, 
prefabrication and modularity, standardisation, material selection, adaptability, material optimisation, 
durability, reuse and recycling (Purchase et al., 2022). However, circular construction is a term within 
the circular economy, used for the building industry.

Figure 32 Illustrates the different pathway choices of a material and how this relates to creating a longer life span. The 
difference between linear-, recycling-, and circular economy, is how materials are treated relative to life span and EoL. The 
linear economy shows the straight and relatively short pathway towards the EoL waste scenario. The pathway of the recycling 
economy has several loops before being treated as waste in EoL. This is done by narrowing and slowing the material loop.

Circularity can be described as imperatives to reduce, reuse, repair, refurbish, recycle, and recover, 
which will keep materials at the highest value for as long as possible (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021). 
(Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021) states the following practices and remedies to circular construction:  
• 
Narrowing loops: Efficient use of materials and resources. 
• 
Slowing loops: Extending the service lifetime.  
• 
Closing loops: Cycling materials in a flow.  
Integrating these circular principles into the building industry will call for fundamental changes in the 
activities, supply chains, and market structures. Conversely, adapting to circular construction will 
increase competitiveness by securing resources in the future and stable prices in the market, creating 
innovative corporate opportunities and more efficient manufacturing and consumption (Ghaffar et al., 
2020).

10.2 
 State of the Art 
To implement circularity of building materials, three strategies have been focused on in this study: 
waste prevention, reuse and recycling and design for disassembly. These enable the reuse of columns 
for multiple life cycles. However, practical challenges in implementing these strategies today appear in 
the building industry. To get an insight into the building industry's state of the art regarding circular 
construction, this section includes two conducted interviews and a review of the participation at 
“Vinterakademi” 2022. These initiatives are conducted to gather first-hand information directly from 
stakeholders of the building industry.  
 
10.2.1 Interviews of Actors  
Two interviews have been conducted to examine why circular construction is challenging to practice. 
The interviewed actors are the Head of Sustainability at Enemærke & Petersen, Anders Strange 
Sørensen and The Concrete department’s Business manager at the Danish Technological Institute (DTI), 
Katja Udbye Christensen.  
Enemærke & Petersen is one of Denmark's most prominent contractors for renovation. Due to a 
significant market share of renovation projects, the firm gets a lot of building materials from each case. 
Hence, they started developing resource mapping tools and considering circularity in their business.  
Anders states that: “it is great to be able to map resources, but there is a need to gain more 
competencies and knowledge for people working in the industry, thereby developing a market for reused 
materials. Otherwise, we have a lot of materials but nowhere to place or use them.”  
He indicates that the market is not ready yet, as there are still challenges regarding construction

10.3 
Creating Circular Construction 
To create a circular construction, the building industry must decouple growth from using natural 
resources by reusing structures and building materials more than todays practice (Ghaffar et al., 2020). 
To minimise resource consumption and waste from a building process, the stakeholders must consider 
waste prevention, preparation for recycling, and reuse of materials and components (VCØB-kvikguider, 
2022). In new projects, the possibility of recycling or reuse needs to be included from the very beginning 
to secure a high level of material utilisation and value, whether it is a new building, renovation, or a 
demolish project.  
To implement the necessary transition to circular construction, waste cannot be considered zero value 
materials but rather a resource representing economic, environmental, and ecstatic value. Therefore, 
the change to the circular construction is based on multiple strategies, including resource flows through 
reuse and recycling, extending the use phases of materials, repairing buildings by renovation, and 
reducing the use of natural resources. This will require stakeholders in the building industry to 
collaborate and agree on the quality requirements for recycled materials, new test methods for 
loadbearing structures when reusing, standardisation for how to reuse, and design considering reuse, 
even before the materials are used for the first time. (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022), (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 
2021).   
This study will further investigate the strategies of waste prevention, reuse and recycling, and design 
for disassembly, shown in Figure 33. These strategies are required and essential for the reuse of 
loadbearing structures when implementing the circularity of building materials. For instance, to reuse 
a loadbearing column, it is crucial to think through the element’s design and the connection to other 
building elements. The three strategies of waste prevention, recycling and reuse of building materials, 
and design for disassembly are presented in the following sections.

10.3.1 Waste Preventions  
To reduce waste from construction processes, there is a need to take action on today’s practice of 
handling waste. The waste hierarchy (VCØB., 2022), seen in Figure 34, advocates an order of preference 
in the effort to lower- and manage waste. In Denmark (Miljøministeriet, 2021) and in the EU, the waste 
hierarchy functions as a prioritisation tool.  
 
Figure 34 Waste Hierarchy, Adopted from (VCØB., 2022). The vital action is to minimalize waste production and avoids treating 
quality materials with potential as waste. Used material can be reused or recycled before incinerated or sent to disposal at 
landfills. 
The most preferred and essential action is to avoid treating materials like waste and prevent materials 
from being damaged and ending up as waste. This can be done by direct reuse or choosing high-quality 
and durable materials, where toxic and hazardous substances have been excluded. (VCØB., 2022).    
Reuse can be defined as products or components used for the same purpose that they were initially 
designed for. To prepare the used material for reuse, it is cleaned, repaired, and the quality is 
controlled. Conversely, recycling involves processing and treating waste materials to produce new 
materials to new product systems. This often requires more energy as materials are reshaped to enter 
a new system. (VCØB., 2022).  
Furthermore, Recovery can be done when the material is at its final stage. In this case, the material 
cannot be reused or recycled but could instead be used in a different product system, serving the 
function of replacing virgin raw materials. For instance, crushed construction waste could be utilised as 
a material to build roads or as energy recovery for heat and electricity production. By doing so, the 
disposal stage is excluded, and the consequence of materials going to landfills is avoided (VCØB., 2022).

10.3.2 Reuse and Recycling of Building Materials 
Both economically and environmentally benefits are found in the direct reuse of building materials and 
components (Eberhardt et al., 2019). Direct reuse requires less energy consumption than the energy 
and resources needed for recycling (Eberhardt et al., 2019). Handling and treating recycled materials 
can have a more significant environmental impact on Global Warming Potential (GWP) than 
manufacturing virgin materials. Therefore, it could be a burden to the climate when using materials 
with recycled content. Nevertheless, GWP is not the only relevant environmental parameter; avoiding 
the use of scarce resources is also essential (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022).   
For example, concrete elements at EoL are usually downcycled into road fillings or crushed to be 
recycled as aggregates in a new concrete mix. However, recycled aggregates only contribute to a 
climate saving of 0.3 % compared to a conventional concrete mix (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022). The savings 
of recycling is minimal and almost negligible since concrete production requires cement, which has a 
significant climate impact. On the other hand, reusing entire concrete elements such as slabs, columns, 
and beams could save up to 96 % CO2 emissions compared to manufacturing virgin concrete (VCØB-
kvikguider, 2022). This is due to avoided production of new virgin cement and less manufacturing 
processes. Likewise, reusing steel elements as loadbearing structures saves 78 % compared to making 
new steel from 20 % virgin and 80 % recycled steel (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022). Furthermore, reusing wood 
for loadbearing structures instead of virgin production provides a 77 % saving (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022).  
For all materials, it can be concluded that there is a significant climate saving by using reused materials, 
where the treatment for the next cycle is minimal. However, reuse requires other strategies such as 
Design for Disassembly. Consequently, choosing suitable materials which are durable for multiple life 
cycles should be a top priority in the early design phase.  
The calculated numbers presented in this section outline is the CO2-eq savings when using reused 
materials compared to virgin (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022). These numbers are based on generic data, and 
actors should conduct specific LCA’s on the actual project to calculate the accurate emissions. However, 
these numbers are comparable to the findings in the LCA conducted in this study – See section 8.2.

10.3.3 Design for Disassembly  
Design for disassembly (DfD) can be defined as facilitating a connection design intended to enable the 
disconnection of individual elements. The design focuses on material separation at any stage in the life 
cycle, e.g., replacing components, renovation, or at EoL of a building. The disassembly design can be 
used for components such as wall cladding and flooring or for the structural elements to be separated 
at the EoL. (O'Grady et al., 2021). DfD considers durable and lasting materials, robust connections, 
standard-sized prefabricated elements and an easy separation (Malabi Eberhardt et al., 2021). 
DfD can reduce waste and support reuse in multiple life cycles. By sufficiently designing components 
and buildings, the concept of disassembly can reduce resource consumption by keeping the materials 
in the loop. To apply DfD, the client and consultant must decide to state this at the beginning of a 
project for the designer to make suitable connection- and design choices. (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022).   
A fundamental principle of DfD is designing accessible connections. This improves disassembly and 
avoids using heavy equipment or too many non-standard tools. The focus should be on mechanical 
joints such as bolts, screws, or nails for connecting parts. Therefore, non-removable and chemical joints 
such as binders, sealers, glues, or welding should be avoided, making it easier to separate and reuse 
the elements.  
The principles of DfD must be translated into practical instructions. Therefore Guidelines for Design for 
Disassembly (Abelone Køster, 2019) has been made, which ensures that the principles are followed and 
implemented in practice. Some of the guidelines are stated below: 
• 
Use recycled or recyclable materials 
• 
Design joints and components to withstand repeated use 
• 
Minimise the number of different types of materials 
• 
Parts that cannot be separated should be made of the same material 
• 
Avoid toxic and hazardous materials 
• 
Use mechanical and non-chemical joints 
• 
Identify material types and save the information

10.4.1 Documentation of Quality for Loadbearing Structures  
By focusing on reusing elements, some fundamental complications arise. Especially for loadbearing 
elements, the quality and bearing capacity may not be equivalent when first produced. The elements 
have been exposed to different environments with varying degrees of protection and subjected to 
various loads. This complicates reusing loadbearing structures. Therefore, documenting the quality is 
crucial.   
DTU and actors from the building industry are developing methods to test the quality of the 
construction materials such as wood, concrete, and steel. The methods are examined in a new research 
project called Grand Solutions BusinessReuse (DTU, 2022). The test and documentation of quality are 
done by non-destructive test methods, where the scientists measure the properties of construction 
materials without changing or damaging them. Non-destructive test methods differ from traditional 
methods such as a compression test, i.e. measuring how much the material can withstand being 
compressed before breaking. If conventional destructive test methods are applied, several samples will 
be damaged and cannot be reused (DTU, 2022). Through the DTU project, the non-destructive test 
methods will be examined and support changes in legislation and standardisation about loadbearing 
structures, which can motivate for more reuse.   
Additionally, the (P)RECAST project by the Danish Technological Institute (Christensen, 2022) is running, 
which aims to develop the technological and documentation basis for prefabricated concrete elements 
to be reused as load-bearing structures in new construction. The project underlines that if it should be 
possible to reuse concrete elements, it is essential that the stakeholders of the entire value chain are 
included in the development process, as it requires new knowledge and innovative ways of working for 
all actors. The project involves Danish Technological Institute, Aarhus University, GXN Innovation/3XN, 
Per Aarsleff, Søndergaard, G. Tscherning, COWI, Peikko Denmark, Hi-Con, AP Ejendomme, Brabrand 
Boligforening, Fonden Dansk Standard and Branchefælleskabet Dansk Beton. Additionally, the project 
is supported by MUDP under the Ministry of Environment of Denmark (Christensen, 2022).  
 
10.4.2 Selective Demolition and Hazardous Substances  
One of the challenges when recycling and reusing building materials is the risk of containing toxic and 
hazardous substances, complicating waste management and reuse processes. By selective demolition, 
the materials are correctly sorted and subsequently reused when the building is demolished. The 
materials containing pollutants or hazardous substances are sorted for disposal and handled separately 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2017). The harmful substances and materials could be PCB (Polychlorinated Biphenyls), 
lead, cadmium, chlorinated paraffin, and asbestos (Miljøstyrelsen, 2017). If these substances are 
dispersed into the atmosphere, e.g. through crushed construction waste, the toxicity can contaminate 
the soil and water. Furthermore, if reused materials, including toxic substances, are used in new 
buildings, it can cause a damaging indoor climate or infuse other materials. Therefore, problematic 
substances must be identified and sorted out before handling the waste. Hence, preliminary screening 
and mapping of materials are necessary. Here, the materials are evaluated relative to reused, recycled, 
recovered, or handled as toxic or hazardous substances for disposal. For the building industry to sort 
and generate more reused material, it is needed to establish guidelines for a ‘gentle’ selective 
demolition performance. A preliminary screening of problematic substances may be an additional task 
complicating the demolition process. Therefore, requirements should be formulated and incorporated 
as early as possible in the project to improve the chances for reuse. (VCØB-kvikguider, 2022).

10.4.3 Risk and Responsibility  
The building industry is complex, causing a challenge in implementing circularity in the overall value 
chain. This is due to stakeholders focusing on short term goals, substantial supply chains, and an overall 
lack of collaboration between stakeholders. The marked mechanisms of today have a strong tradition 
for project-based practice, where the facilitation of a circular construction and the long term goals do 
not fit (Eberhardt et al., 2019). Building projects requires inputs from stakeholders within a complex 
and long-ranging supply chain. Therefore, circularity cannot be accomplished without the collaboration 
and engagement of all supply chain stakeholders. Having varying goals and motivations causes the 
different stakeholders to work against each other to achieve the most significant profit for the 
respective building projects. Instead of collaboration, some partnerships lead to insufficient 
cooperation, scepticism, and disagreement about responsibility. This adds up to the risks in the 
projects, resulting in the refusal to support change in the industry. Sustainability initiatives are 
considered an additional risk as no experience is established and because the potential profits are not 
visible to the stakeholders (Eberhardt et al., 2019). Moreover, when the building industry stakeholders 
focus on short-term profits, it does not benefit long-term goals such as sustainability (Eberhardt et al., 
2019). 
Reusing loadbearing structures is a new practice where the responsibility is not assigned to the 
stakeholders by a standard procedure. To reuse loadbearing structures, innovative and committed 
partners willing to take risks and responsibility is required. Thus, creating experience in the field to 
improve reuse of materials in the future. The challenge is to establish an infrastructure for materials, 
create a flow from demolished materials into new buildings, and create a plan to improve the economic 
aspects of reuse while minimising the risk.

10.5.1 Collaborations and Partnerships  
A sector-development report from DTU (DTU, 2021) recommends the actors in the construction 
industry to collaborate on large pilot projects when scaling circular construction. The overall 
recommendation of the report is that the actors and stakeholders collaborate on shared strategic 
investments. The report recommends the stakeholders to: 
• 
Renovate existing buildings as their primary focus point 
• 
Reuse building materials at the highest level  
• 
Focus on lasting access to resources, reduction of construction waste and reduction of 
emissions.  
A standard system for all stakeholders is needed to deal with risk-sharing and included at the beginning 
of the project. The risk and responsibility-sharing should be done by collaborating with suppliers and 
customers to mitigate uncertainties in the following stages of construction. This process requires new 
standard procedures and pilot projects to learn from.  
Furthermore, an economic benefit could engage partnerships. If long-term investment and 
collaboration between partners are prioritised this could result in long-term savings. This will maximise 
the combined profit for all stakeholders, and resulting in even more profit than achieved from acting 
alone (Eberhardt et al., 2019).

Experience, practices, and partnerships are needed to gather information on how to perform circular

10.5.3 Early Design Stage 
The design stage of a building represents a crucial role to ensure resource efficiency and reducing 
environmental impacts, as most environmental, social and economic cost factors have already been 
affected and defined during the design of the building (Eberhardt et al., 2019). A key concept within 
the early design stage is 'integrated design'. This could be incorporated by engineering knowledge and 
consultancy (DTU, 2021). Some examples of this are by using digital tools in collaboration with 
architects, or by applying Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) and documenting the building impact at the 
beginning of a project.  By applying LCA, the amount of GWP and resource consumption would be more 
evident and be the basis for decision making. Furthermore, Design for disassembly can be incorporated 
into the project, securing future reuse.

10.5.4 Standardising Procedures and Processes 
More experience is needed to standardise procedures and processes to implement full-scale circularity 
in the building industry. By standards, the industry must agree on how to build with circular material 
flows. Standards about circular construction should be developed, e.g. for a ‘gentle’ selective 
demolition process, documentation of building materials, test methods for loadbearing structures, LCA 
allocation for multiple lifecycles, and how to prevent waste in all stages.  
Common European standards are developed in a new European committee, the Comité Européen de 
Normalization (CEN), where the research project Grand Solutions BusinessReuse (DTU, 2022) 
contributes by investigating test methods. Denmark's standardisation organisation, Dansk Standard, 
has been appointed by CEN to lead, develop and operate the committee. The committee’s work aims 
to promote the transition to a circular economy in construction through standard approaches, 
languages and definitions (DTU, 2022).

Sub-conclusion and Recommendations for Circular Construction   
From the sections above, evaluating the circular construction and the potential for reuse of building 
materials in the building industry, some general and crucial parameters are stated. This results in the 
following recommendations upon what to consider when implementing circularity:     
• 
Reuse materials and elements at the highest level and secure the technical and economic value.  
• 
Use fewer resources. Renovate the existing building mass instead of building new.  
• 
Design for disassembly is a crucial tool. It facilitates the replacement of the materials and allows 
for better separation when the building needs to be renovated or demolished. 
• 
By choosing robust and durable materials, it is possible to reduce resource consumption over the 
life of the building.  
• 
Minimise the waste from the construction process and demolition at EoL, according to the waste 
hierarchy.    
• 
Conduct a preliminary screening of problematic substances in buildings to map which materials 
can be reused, recycled, recovered, or handled as toxic or hazardous substances for disposal. 
• 
Demolition should be carried out as selective demolition to reuse and recycle at the highest value.  
• 
Collaborate with the project’s different stakeholders to secure optimal design and execution. 
Interdisciplinary has high value.  
Further research and tests are needed to scale circular construction:  
• 
A new framework within legislation and the technical ownership to form a common technical

• 
Pilot projects securing more experience and skills of the actors.  
• 
Common standards for recycling and reuse of building materials. 
• 
Documentation approaches for reuse of loadbearing structural elements. 
• 
Innovation of new building materials and new building processes. 
The examination of the challenges experienced in the industry reveals that reusing loadbearing

11 Discussion  
The conducted LCA study shows prospects of lowering the Global Warming Potential (GWP) by applying 
structural building material of wood, compared to concrete and steel. However, this study only assesses 
effects related to the GWP. Subsequently, the importance of other potential environmental impacts 
has not been considered and may lead to overlooking environmental trade-offs causing unintended 
burden shifting. On this basis, decision-making cannot be completed by only considering GWP. 
However, GWP and climate change are essential impact categories, as climate tipping points can cause 
significant and irreversible changes in the state of the climate system and thereby lead to severe 
impacts on human society (IPCC, 2022).  
Furthermore, the inventory analysis is based on an attributional approach. A consequential approach, 
which seeks to find the environmental implications of a decision, must be applied to evaluate the 
consequences of future impacts of structural material selection.   
According to (K. Haugbølle, 2021), the service lifetime of loadbearing structures is 120 years. When 
assessing multiple life cycles, each cycle is assumed to be 50 years (K.Kanafani, 2021), accordingly the 
maximal lifetime of the columns is challenged to be 150 years. If the study included an evaluation of 
each materials service lifetime, it could be argued that the materials have different durability. 
Moreover, the column connections are not included in the analysis. A steel connection relative to the 
GLT column may contribute to a larger share of the total impact compared to the concrete and steel 
column. However, the individual connections are assumed to have an equivalent impact and, therefore, 
not considered in the study.  
One of the characteristics of conducting an LCA on bio-based materials such as wood is the amount of 
credit given for energy recovery. Based on the included amount of fossil fuels in the energy mix, the 
equivalent credit is given when wood residues from forest, sawmill and glulam production are 
incinerated. This provides energy substitution and net negative emissions; consequently, stakeholders 
are being credited when using wood products for a short time period. This will not delay or reduce the 
total GWP due to biogenic carbon not being considered. Relative to future forecasts, various 
parameters influence the future energy mix, such as political initiatives. Moreover, this study shows 
that a more considerable share of renewable energy affects the credits and provides less credit for 
avoided production.  
An additional character of bio-based materials is the embodied biogenic carbon. Without accounting 
for biogenic carbon for one life cycle, the emissions in life cycle stage A1-A3 (cradle to gate) are -18,31 
[kg CO2-eq], and -2,47 [kg CO2-eq] in life cycle stage D (beyond the system). This distribution is primarily 
due to energy recovery. This study assessed three different biogenic carbon modelling approaches: the 
0/0, -1/+1 and the dynamic approach using GWPbio factors by Guest et al. (2013). Applying the 0/0 
approach did not change the quantity of emissions. Similarly, considering the total net emissions for 
one life cycle, the -1/+1 was found carbon neutral. When evaluating the life cycle stages individually, 
the results from the -1/+1 approach created -115,35 [kg CO2-eq] in the cradle to gate and 94,56 [kg 
CO2-eq] in the life cycle stage D. By applying the -1/+1, all burdens are shifted to the columns EoL, 
creating a disadvantage in treating the column at EoL and an advantage in virgin production of the 
column, if considering the life cycle stages individually. Applying the GWPbio factors, the results for one 
life cycle were found to be 26,86 [kg CO2-eq] from cradle to gate, and -5,89 [kg CO2-eq] for recovery in 
D. Consequently, the total emissions during one life cycle are positive due to considering storage- and 
rotation period. This provides a more realistic representation of the GWP, motivates life cycle 
extension, and may create a positive mindset towards less material consumption by circulating 
materials. This result was also found when allocating relative to three life cycles.

Evaluating the potential of circular construction, different allocation approaches for various EoL-
scenarios were assessed. For each allocation approach, the two scenarios of reuse and recycling of a 
GLT column were examined. The four approaches reveal a considerable difference in the number of 
parameters used when allocating between the three life cycles. Two main characteristics of the Circular 
Footprint Formula (CFF), the 100:0 cut-off approach, the 50:50 approach, and the linearly degressive 
(LD) approach are how they allocate virgin- and recycling material impact. The approaches also 
differentiate between the recycle- and the reuse scenario.    
According to the allocation of virgin material, the 50:50 and 100:0 cut-off approaches assign the impact 
of virgin material entirely to the first life cycle. In contrast, the LD approach distributes the number of 
emissions from virgin production linear digressive. This results in minor emissions in the first life cycle 
and more emissions in cycle two and three when compared to the other approaches. The CFF approach 
allocates the impact of virgin production by considering the amount of virgin material in each life cycle. 
Relative to the recycling scenario, this amount will decrease for each cycle.  
Regarding recycling impacts, the 50:50 approach allocates the reuse/recycle process between two 
cycles. The LD allocates by the same approach as the 50:50 relative to the reuse/recycle process. The 
100:0 cut-off approach allocates the total impact of the recycling process to the cycle consuming the 
recycled material. CFF is the only approach considering marked trends and the quality of the material. 
Therefore, not only the amount of material being reused/recycled but how this product is reflected in 
the market and the quality. By considering these parameters, the CFF approach makes it less desirable 
to downcycle.  
The LD approach is the only method considering the number of life cycles regarding how emissions 
from virgin material production should be allocated. Even though this parameter considers circularity, 
this could lead to overestimating the number of cycles. Due to the long service life of building materials, 
concerns arise regarding uncertainty and greenwashing resulting from the allocation of impacts 
between cycles.  
Finally, the 100:0 and 50:50 approach is simple and easy for the practitioner to apply in the assessment. 
Conversely, the CFF approach stands out from the various approaches as being the most 
comprehensive, including 17 parameters. This may complicate the process of allocating for multiple 
cycles. However, this results in more details and more transparency to the results. The CFF formula 
depends on the market situation of the material, whether there is a high or low supply and demand. 
Other allocation approaches favour either ingoing or outgoing secondary materials, while CFF covers 
recycled content at the input side and recyclability at EoL as output. The CFF formula aims to support 
all scenarios at EoL, including reuse, recycling, incineration with or without energy recovery and final 
disposal.  
Two motivating conditions of allocating burdens relative to virgin production are observed. The first is 
linear digressive, considering the number of cycles and allocating the impact linear digressive from most 
impact in the first life cycle to minor in the final cycle. Fewer impacts are allocated each time the 
material is reused. The approach considers a system perspective, encouraging to involve design and 
demolition of the product in advance of the first life cycle. Therefore, this approach motivates the reuse 
of materials and circular design. The second condition is relative to CFF, where the most impacts are 
allocated to the first life cycle and less to the following. In difference from LD, the impact allocated to 
the final cycle is negative for wood. When the product is credited with a negative impact (environmental 
benefits), this motivates a circular material flow. This creates an even bigger incitement to reuse the 
material for multiple cycles by storing the biogenic carbon for as long as possible.  
Nevertheless, the difference in allocation approaches makes it difficult to conclude which approach 
provides the most reasonable impact to each cycle. LCA allocation at EoL for multiple cycles is 
challenging due to the multiple products and recycling steps involved in the assessment.

12 Future Perspective  
This section aims to include a broader perspective of the consequences of using wood materials. 
Thereby, evaluate the opportunities and challenges of applying more wooden products in the building 
industry. From the consequence of an increasing wood demand, challenges brought to attention are 
forest management, biodiversity, and land use change. The consequence of increased wood demand is 
included in the perspective of this study and is elaborated on in this section. Furthermore, the new 
aspect of absolute sustainability is considered, where systems and products are assessed relative to the 
planetary boundaries, not only to the number of emissions. To accommodate sustainability, there is a 
need to consider the reuse of wood in terms of absolute sustainability. An introduction to absolute 
sustainability can be found in Appendix J.1.  
The construction of buildings is expected to grow by more than 230 billion m2 in 2060 due to increased 
global wealth and a greater need for improved housing (Andersen et al., 2022). Suppose new buildings 
will be constructed using more bio-based products and loadbearing structures of wood. In that case, it 
will generate additional demand for wood extraction to satisfy future demand for biomass energy 
production and construction. Therefore, the forests must be managed sustainably to avoid 
deforestation where materials production exceeds the growth rate of new trees. Hence, the rotation 
length in the forests is essential to account for.  
With an increasing demand for wooden products, it is relevant to consider if the current size of global 
forests is sufficient to satisfy the potential future demands for wood in buildings (Andersen et al., 2022). 
With increased use now, it is essential to plan how to sustain the future demand. The study by 
(Andersen et al., 2022) investigates the consequences of increasing wood demand. Their results 
indicate that the main driver of deforestation is likely to be the increased use of bioenergy and less by 
producing building materials. 
However, when extracting these materials from nature, it is essential to consider what an increasing 
demand can cause to biodiversity and land use parameters. Biodiversity refers to the variety of different 
ecosystems and the organisms living there and considers genetic variation or species variation within 
an area (Nolan et al., 2009). Using natural resources to make products that meet human needs can 
affect biodiversity. With the increasing demand for building materials, biodiversity is receiving 
increasing attention from society to protect and preserve nature. Land use refers to the intention of 
land being dedicated to a purpose such as wood production, ore extraction, food production, the 
housing of humans, or biodiversity protection and includes a function and practice to produce a product 
of this land (Nolan et al., 2009). Land use managed by human activities can threaten biodiversity, 
including removing native species and introducing alien species or soil degradation, which will course 
the permanent loss of the environmental attributes on the site (Howard et al., 2021). Thus, the 
parameter of land use change is relevant to an increased demand for building materials, energy, and 
food. (Helene Frederiksen, 2022). 
Due to biodiversity and land use parameters a greater demand for wood products creates doubts about 
the impact on forest carbon stocks, forest condition, and the provision of other critical social and 
environmental benefits that come with healthy forests (Pasternack et al., 2022). Adopting 
unsustainable forest management or shifting harvest intensities or rotation length away from 
recommended levels will negatively impact the climate (Pasternack et al., 2022). The prospect of 
increased use of wood as a building material and part of a climate solution for the construction industry 
can potentially cause negative and positive consequences. To ensure positive outcomes and a reduction 
of CO2 emissions, it is relevant to investigate and assess the case of material substitution and increasing 
demand from a consequential perspective. (Helene Frederiksen, 2022).

13 Conclusions  
From this study, a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) have been conducted of a GLT-, concrete-, and steel 
column for one- and three life cycles. Based on the results, it can be concluded that the glue laminated 
timber (GLT) column performs best according to Global Warming Potential (GWP). However, it is not 
possible to explicitly conclude that the GLT column performs environmentally better than the concrete 
and steel, as only GWP is evaluated. Nevertheless, increased use of wood in construction appears to be 
a valuable initiative for reducing greenhouse gas emissions.   
The analysis of one life cycle reveals that biogenic carbon accounting is crucial for the total LCA results 
of the GLT column. This is seen when applying the GWPbio factors considering the forest’s rotation- and  
storage period of biomass products. As residues leave the system in the manufacturing stage in year 0, 
the GWPbio factor results in positive emissions, assigning burdens to the system. This perspective turns 
the negative emissions (benefits) when not considering biogenic carbon into positive emissions 
assigning environmental burdens to the life cycle of the GLT column. However, the impact is relatively 
lower when comparing this burden to the concrete- and steel column. Assuming carbon neutrality for 
the entire life cycle is found to be a simplification of the system, as the forest system and carbon balance 
in the atmosphere are neglected. By the 0/0 and -1/+1 method, the potential for carbon storage is not 
considered, and no benefit is obtained for long life cycle products. Furthermore, the risk of not assigning 
any burdens from biogenic carbon is found in these methods. If the biomass is incinerated or 
decomposed a short time after harvest, the carbon amount in the atmosphere increases. This is due to 
the biogenic carbon being released before new trees take up the carbon in the forest. Therefore, it is 
concluded that a dynamic method considering the time perspective is more reliable and transparent 
when considering wood materials in buildings. The result of this study indicates that if the storage 
period is sufficiently long, the biogenic carbon accounting results in net negative emissions, thereby 
benefitting the system.   
To evaluate building materials for circular construction, it is found crucial how to allocate impacts 
between multiple cycles. This study identified and tested four allocation approaches; the Circular 
Footprint Formula (CFF), the 100:0 cut-off approach, the 50:50 approach and the linearly degressive 
(LD) approach. The CFF approach considers the market situation of the material relative to the supply 
and demand. By doing so, the approach deals with the uncertainty of multiple cycles in the future. 
Moreover, the quality parameters are found essential for the results, considering the quality of both 
input and output. Thus, CFF considers value reduction from downcycling. The CFF approach stands out 
from the other approaches as the most comprehensive and may complicate the process of allocating 
for multiple cycles. However, this study considers the CFF approach being the most transparent, 
consistent, and reliable approach. 
From the EoL scenarios, the drivers for reuse and recycling are evident in saving resources. A significant 
reduction of virgin material and waste disposal is seen in both scenarios. Additionally, to reduce GWP, 
a clear advantage of recycling and reusing is found. Comparing the reuse and recycle scenario of a 
loadbearing GLT column, reuse is found favourable. This is due to a larger share of biogenic carbon 
stored and less energy consumed. When comparing the GLT-, concrete- and steel column for three life 
cycles by the CFF allocation approach, the reuse scenario reveals equivalent impacts for the second 
cycle. Reusing the columns directly causes a minor impact due to minimal processing needed. In 
conclusion, if reusing either a GLT-, concrete- or steel column, a substantial amount of resources and 
emissions can be saved. Therefore, if implementing additional cycles concerning direct reuse of 
loadbearing columns, there is a considerable potential to save environmental impact of buildings.     
This study finds the EoL scenario crucial to future wood demand and GWP. Consequently, it should be 
pursued to develop innovative reuse practices in the building industry. This will require collaboration 
and risk sharing between the stakeholders in the industry, standardisation for reuse, new test methods 
for loadbearing structures, and early design considering design for disassembly.
