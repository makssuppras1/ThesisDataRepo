Title: Structural topology optimization techniques for 3D printing
of Wind turbine blades
Author: Miguel Fernandez Perez
Department: VIM
Summary:
In this master thesis project the use of topology optimization methods
on beam cross-sections is investigated for the purpose of designing
wind turbine blades created by additive manufacturing. The workﬂow
presented for the generation of structural blade designs is based on
the formulation of optimization problems that provide the shape and
amount of material needed inside the airfoil of a blade.
The blade is modeled as a beam with its properties deﬁned by the
characteristics of a set of sections along its length.
These cross-
sections are represented by a ﬁnite element discretization from which
a set of optimization problems are formulated, such as the minimiza-
tion of compliance or the minimization of mass of the blade section.
A numerical tool has been implemented in Matlab and tested for
a range of parameters and formulations to evaluate its suitability to
provide cross-section topologies in the context of wind turbine blades.
The consideration of additive manufacturing techniques and materials
has been assessed by using the topology optimization tool. Numerical
experiments performed have proven the usefulness of the optimization
tool for the selection of candidate materials (attending to their me-
chanical properties) for additive manufactured blades.
The cross-section topology optimization tool has been applied to pro-
pose two novel structural designs for the blades of the 10MW reference
wind turbine developed at DTU Wind Energy. A design made from
aluminum and a design made from glass ﬁber reinforced composite
are provided through the topologies on a set of sections along the
blade. These topologies match the stiﬀness properties of the existing
design; however, lower overall mass has not been achieved. These
design examples are considered to prove the potential of the meth-
ods and formulations developed to generate blade designs exploiting
additive manufacturing.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
This Master Thesis project explores the possibilities of using structural topology
optimization techniques as a tool to generate novel wind turbine blade designs made
possible by additive manufacturing techniques.
1.1
Rationale
In the search for sustainable energy sources to accommodate present and future needs
from society, the extraction of power from the wind has shifted from being a promising
solution to cover signiﬁcant amounts of electricity demand in some regions. In order
for wind energy systems to become truly sustainable, steep innovation is perceived
by the author to be required to reduce both econommic and environmental costs.
The trends in the wind energy sector towards more eﬃcient horizontal axis wind
turbines, by using longer blades, lighter structures, and higher structural performance
can be grasped by citing from a selection of studies on cost reduction analysis, the
following remarks:
• Crown Estate on Improved blade design and manufacture:
To improve manufacturing by further automation. Manufacturing costs con-
tribute around 30% of the costs of blades and, based on experience over the last
30 years, can be reduced by a further 25% in the foreseeable future. Improve-
ments in blade design standards and process, including greater design optimisa-
tion. This builds on the increasing sophistication of design tool software.
• KIC Innoenergy on Optimisation of rotor size with improved materials:
Many novel materials and manufacturing processes are in development to give a
mix of stiﬀer, lighter, lower cost blades. In some cases aerospace innovaions are
now starting to be incorporated. This will allow larger rotors (a 10% increase is
modelled) to be used with lower cost penalties than those associated with existing
technologies, leading to an increase in optimum (lowest LCOE) rotor diameter
for a given wind speed site.

Further progress via the use of more advanced tools and modelling techniques
will continue to provide beneﬁts in terms of increased aerodynamic performance,
decreased CAPEX (of the blades and also the rest of the turbine) and OPEX
(due to increased reliability).
• NREL on Advanced materials:
The availability of new materials and new material forms allows more ﬂexibil-
ity in design concepts and fosters the development of enlarged rotors by using
lighter, stronger blades that reduce or more eﬃciently resist loads. Carbon ﬁbers,
although more expensive than glass ﬁbers, can reduce weight and tip deﬂections
when used in select load bearing portions of the blade. Griﬃn (2002) estimates
a 10% to 20% reduction in blade costs just going to carbon-hybrid materials.
Reduced rotor mass results in reductions in the rest of the support structure,
especially in the tower, on the order of 2%. With blades accounting for 10% to
15% of turbine costs, the net result is a 2% to 6% decrease in turbine capital
cost.
The innovations envisioned in these studies are considered to be in line with the
investigations involved in this project.
1.2
Research objectives
The investigation consists in exploring methods to unlock novel blade designs with
potential to reduce the cost of wind energy systems.
The main goals set to conduct this investigation are the following:
• The development of a concept design workﬂow for wind turbine blades.
• Identiﬁcation of the potential of this design approach in conjunction with addi-
tive manufacturing techniques.
• Identiﬁcation of the challenges and limitations for its practical application.
1.3
Manufacturing of wind turbine blades
In this section an overview of current manufacturing techniques for large wind turbine
blades and of novel additive manufacturing procceses is given.

1
INTRODUCTION
Current materials & manufacturing
Current large-scale wind turbine blades are built from ﬁber reinforced composites.
Composites consist of deformable material which acts as a matrix bonding ﬁlaments
of a reinforcing stiﬀmaterial. When combined, it results in a material with mechan-
ical properties suitable for large lightweight structures such as blades [10]. Materials
used as matrix are thermoset polymers (eg. epoxy resins) or thermoplastic polymers
(eg. polyethylene). Materials being employed as reinforcing ﬁlaments are glass ﬁbers
and carbon ﬁbers.
The manufacturing processes using composites for wind turbine blades, in general,
involve stacking layers of reinforcing ﬁbers along a mold of the desired blade geometry,
which are bonded with matrix material. This bonding is produced from the chemical
processes that start when heat is applied, leading the resin impregnating the ﬁbers
to cure.
In short, the manufacturing of composite materials on blades in an industrial scale is
done by vacuum infusion or by using ”prepregs”. The ﬁrst process consists in placing
ﬁbers on a mold surface, sealing the volume for the composite layer, and by creating
a vacuum. Then, ﬂuid resin ﬂows through the ﬁbers ﬁlling the voids while curing. By
using pre-impregnated ﬁbers ”prepregs” the layers of ﬁbers already contain the resin,
which has to be cured by heating the composite. The choice between the manufac-
turing technique involves considering, between others, the cost of the materials, the
level of automation or the resultant mechanical properties [19].
From the perspective of the constraints that the manufacturing process adds to the
design of blades, in [27] the following aspects are identiﬁed for general laminate struc-
tures:
• Blending: Need of continuity between the plies of adjacent composite panels.
The composition of these laminates cannot be chosen freely as it can induce
stress concentrations at the interface between panels.
• Ply-drop: Abrupt changes in the thickness of a laminate can induce delami-
nation and stress concentration. Therefore, the amount of material allocated
along a structure cannot be chosen freely in a design process.
• Residual stresses from cool-down: When the panel cures it can deform and
induce residual stresses depending on the layout of plies through the thickness.
• Draping: It is the ability of a prepreg to adapt to the mold surface and depends
on the composite ﬁber orientation and resin employed, therefore, their choice
in the design might be restricted.
Additive Manifacturing
Instead of creating models bounded by these constraints, the consideration of using

additive manufacturing as a technique to build wind turbine blades is explored. A
description of these processes and the manufacturing constraints are presented.
Additive manufacturing is deﬁned by the ASTM as the process of joining materials
to make objects from three-dimensional (3D) model data, usually layer upon layer,
as opposed to subtractive manufacturing methodologies. Although current manufac-
turing techniques of wind turbine blades are not subtractive, they are far from the
level of automation provided by additive manufacturing. A short reveiw on additive
manufacturing (comonly named 3D printing) is given ahead in order to have an un-
derstanding of its current status, potential and constraints of its own for the design
of structures.
Additive manufacturing (AM) technologies were developed already several decades
ago and applied in the ﬁelds of product design and architecture for rapid prototyping.
It has been in the last years when these technologies are being used to manufacture
end-use products [18]. To this date, examples of the use of AM in the wind energy
sector have not been found. However, the aerospace and automotive industries are
already designing components speciﬁcally to be built by AM (multiple examples are
shown in [18]). Advancements in AM technologies are expected to continue as inter-
est grows in multiple disciplines, with the possibility to transcend to the wind energy
industry in the years to come.
The available materials to be used in AM are restricted by the manufacturing tech-
nologies developed to date. The range of techniques found in the literature are the
grouped by the following:
• SLA (Stereolithography) and MJM (Multi-Jet Modeling): A liquid photosen-
sitive resin is converted to solid state by the selective exposure to ultraviolet
(UV) light. The Multi-Jet process allows to selectively deposit multiple ma-
terials simultaneously. The photo-sensitive resins required are not considered
suitable as a structural material.
• SLM (Selective Laser Melting), EBM (Electron Beam Melting) and LMD (Laser
Metal Deposition): A high-power laser/electron beam acts as a heating source
to locally melt the feedstock of metal powder, and allows it to bound with the
object already built (see Figure 1.1). Materials available to be used comprise
titanium, aluminum or stainless steel, with its properties as good as the metal
itself or better [15].
The SLS (Selective Laser Sintering) process requires less intensity from the laser
and is used to melt polymers such as Nylon, which has an elastic modulus below
3GPa.

• FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling): A plastic ﬁlament is heated to a tempera-

ABS which is below 2GPa.
• CLIP (Continuous Liquid Interface Production): This process by [36] uses a
pool of liquid feedstock and by a controlled reaction to UV light it is cured
continuously as the object is extruded (see Figure 1.2(b)). It has the advantage
with respect to SLA or SLS of much higher depositions speeds, of about 1 meter
per hour.
• CFF (Continuous Filament Fabrication): This process developed by [26] can be
understood as a FDM process with an attached nozzle that lays a continuous
ﬁber ﬁlament together with the fused polymer, creating a ﬁber reinforced com-
posite. It is indicated to use Nylon as matrix and carbon, glass or Kevlar ﬁbers
as reinforcement, with mechanical properties around half of those materials
when employed in standard laminates.

The particularities of these manufacturing techniques can pose restrictions to the

1.4
Design of wind turbine blades
In this section an introduction to the design methods that can be employed in the
design of structures, such as wind turbine blades, is given. This is followed by an
overview of the numerical models that are considered relevant in order to formulate
and implement a structural optimization tool.
1.4.1
Design workﬂow
In order to design a structure numerous processes are involved. In [21] these are
generalized to the following:
1. Deﬁnition of the needed functionalities of the structure
2. Generation of ideas and conceptual designs to fulﬁll those needs
3. Optimization of the design to its best performance achievable
4. Adapt the design attending to social, market and aesthetic factors

INTRODUCTION
It is indicated in [12] that the step between generating conceptual designs and their
optimization is traditionally done using the intuition of engineers and designers to
generate designs and then by evaluating them with numerical simulations or tests.
For this, the design is modiﬁed and veriﬁed in several iterations until it reaches a
desired performance and is compliant with every requirement.
Alternatively, a methodology involving mathematical design optimization has the
potential to automatize the procedure to obtain complex new designs with high per-
formance, ideally demanding less time and resources along the design. This approach
requires the mathematical description of the problem addressed in order to numer-
ically quantify the optimized designs. With the deﬁnition of design objective and
constraint functions, optimization algorithms can be applied to reach optimal de-
signs.
In this project, a mathematical structural optimization workﬂow is proposed, which
is limited to the structural behavior of a wind turbine blade. The models are bounded
to asses the mechanical performance of the material employed inside the blade, leav-
ing the aerodynamic performance and other design drivers out of the framework.
In general, a structural optimization problem takes the form in Figure 1.4. The
Figure 1.4: General structural optimization problem, from [12]
objective function f(x,y) evaluates the performance of the design, which can be the
structure’s mass, cost or a metric of its structural response. The constraints have
to reﬂect the mechanical problem (i.e static equilibrium) together with other design
restrictions. The design variables (x,y) correspond to properties of the structure that
inﬂuence the design and which are the outcome of the optimization procedure.
According to [12], structural optimization can be grouped into three branches: sizing
optimization, shape optimization and topology optimization. Their main diﬀerence
is in the consideration of the design variables and how they aﬀect the structural be-
havior of the structure. For instance, in size optimization, a truss structure (with a
ﬁxed bar conﬁguration) can be optimized in terms of the needed cross-sectional area
of each of its bars, so as to minimize the overall deformation.
For structural blade design the category selected is topology optimization, which al-
lows the highest design freedom inside a mathematical design model. In [5], these
techniques are described as:
”The purpose of topology optimization is to ﬁnd the optimal lay-out of a structure
within a speciﬁed region. The only known quantities in the problem are the applied
loads, the possible support conditions, the volume of the structure to be constructed and

possibly some additional design restrictions such as the location and size of prescribed
holes or solid areas. In this problem the physical size and the shape and connectivity
of the structure are unknown. The topology, shape, and size of the structure are not
represented by standard parametric functions but by a set of distributed functions de-
ﬁned on a ﬁxed design domain.”
A set of topology optimization models are formulated in this thesis with the intention
to become a tool to be used in the process of designing new wind turbine blades.
A proposal of a design workﬂow for a new wind turbine blade is shown as a ﬂowchart
in Figure 1.5 . The proposed algorithm indicates a possible use of the numerical tools
developed inside the design process of a blade.
It can be observed in Figure 1.5 how the topology optimization framework would
need as inputs some a priori information about the desired blade, such as its length,
airfoil distributions, available materials and estimation of design loads.
Once the adequate optimization problem is set for the speciﬁc design objectives and
requirements, a topology is found.
Then, the design loads (which are independent of the topology optimization scheme)
can be re-computed for that design, by the use of external tools as aeroelastic codes.
The optimization process can be solved iteratively using updated design loads, until
convergence is considered (eg. when the structural properties of the blade converge).
The following step could be the evaluation of other structural requirements which
are not accounted in the topology optimization scheme, such as stress concentrations,
fatigue or local/global buckling. If those requirements are not met, there should be
the possibility of imposing new constraints to the model or increasing the existing
restrictions. The optimization could be performed again in order to meet the new
requirements.
Finally the design obtained should be evaluated in terms of the beneﬁts it was in-
tended for, such as lowering the material or manufacturing costs, increased reliability
and lifetime, etc.

1.4.2
Numerical modeling
From prior work found in the literature, of relevance are the early attempts in [11]
and [20] on topology optimization of beam cross-sections, applied to optimize sections
against bending moments and torsion. In [24] the minimum compliance problem on
cross-sections that account for warping displacements is formulated and evaluated.
The work in [6] presents a detailed formulation for cross-section analysis and multi-
material topology optimization of beams, applied for optimal beam sections using
ﬁber reinforced composites.
Formulations on topology optimization for laminated composite layouts can be found
in [34] and in [33] where manufacturing constraints are accounted. For the case of
blade structures, in [14] a wind turbine blade is designed using topology optimization
based on a very large 3D ﬁnite element model.
The core challenge is to develop a numerical tool which can generate conceptual de-
signs for wind turbine blades with the highest structural performance which could
lead to more cost-eﬃcient designs.
The tool is based on structural topology optimization, for which a set of problem for-
mulations (objective and constraints functions) reﬂect the structural requirements of
the wind turbine blade to be designed. These are deﬁned mathematically in Section
2.2.
The optimization problems are based on a structural model, this is, a formulation
describing how the loads and boundary conditions interact with the material inside
the structure. For the particular case of wind turbine blades as continuous slender
structures, beam theory is considered. A beam is modeled by setting the equilib-
rium of forces and moments along its length, with its mechanical properties deﬁned
through the cross-sections. The framework for the cross-sectional analysis needed in
the model is described in Section 2.1 and relies on the work by [6].
The main reason to use of a beam model versus a full 3-D model (understood as
the discretization of the complete blade by solid or shell ﬁnite elements) is that the
reduced order beam model is computationally much less demanding, which is of crit-
ical importance in an optimization scheme involves hundreds of iterations. Another
important driver to use a beam model is that it can handle output from aero-elastic
codes such as Hawc2 [22] which are able to model the complexity of the phenomena
involved for a wind turbine blade in operation. The predicted loads from these soft-
ware can then be used straightforwardly as an input in a topology optimization model.

The manufacturing constraints described in Section 1.3 from current production of
blades pose limitations to the applicability of topology optimization techniques for

wind turbine blade designs.
The consideration of additive manufacturing is estimated to provide larger design
freedom, which can enable topology optimization as a valid tool to consider in the
process of wind turbine blade designs.
However, these nascent manufacturing techniques still pose some design constraints
which should be accounted in order to formulate useful structural optimization mod-

In [9] the lack of speciﬁc research in the literature on topology optimization
considering AM constraints is indicated. Then, the identiﬁed constraints in Section
1.3 are assessed by the following considerations:
• Maximum manufacturing size: It is assumed to be unlimited, by considering
no limitation on the area available for the nozzle to travel. It is also assumed
that the deposition rates are high enough to build a full-scale blade and that
the time to manufacture does not pose any restriction.
• Minimum feature size: It is assumed that the size at which the nozzle can ”print”
is much lower than the ﬁnest discretization possible of the design space. If not,
the ﬁltering used in model could be used in order to reﬂect this constraint.
• Support material and self-supporting angle: These can limit the ﬂexibility to
adapt to changing topologies along the blade needed when depositing material,
since the continuity of the structure has to be ensured.
By considering the
building direction along the blade length, these issues should not restrict the
design, as the structure should be self-supporting as it is extruded.

2.1
Structural model
The formulation of a topology optimization problem is based on a structural model,
from which the design parameters are related to the response of the structure. The
structural model should accurately predict the behavior of the mechanical system
designed, while being simple enough to be applied in hundreds of iterations. For the
design of long and slender wind turbine blades, beam theory is perceived to be a
good compromise between accuracy and computational performance. Therefore, the
structural models implemented in this project are based on beam theory generalized
for anisotropic and inhomogeneous cross sections.
2.1.1

For beam theory to hold, the structural model should be long and slender with-
out abrupt changes in geometry, material distribution or external loading along its
length.This is considered to be the case for a typical wind turbine blade. The ﬁnite
element approach of a blade as a beam structure can be visualized in Figure 2.1.

element in [23] which is freely available in Matlab code.

2.2
Topology optimization model
This section covers the mathematical description of the optimization problems formu-
lated for beam cross-sections. It comprises the parametrization of the design variables,
the required regularization, the deﬁnition of objective and constraint functions and
their sensitivities.
2.2.1
Parametrization
The main purpose of the optimization problem is to obtain a topology of the cross-
section, understanding it as the optimal distribution of structural mass on a ﬁnite
design space which is the cross-section area. Then, the design space is deﬁned by the
set of elements from the ﬁnite element discretization of a cross-section slice (such as
in Figure 2.3).
SIMP
The parametrization of the design space for a single material topology has been done
using SIMP (Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization) . This is a so called ”den-
sity based” parametrization, where each design variable is referred to an element of
the ﬁnite element mesh, and is continuous in the domain [0, 1]. It corresponds to a
relaxation of the discrete binary problem by a continuous variable, commonly named
”density”. The consideration of continuous design variables is crucial to formulating
problems which can be solved numerically by gradient based algorithms. The magni-
tude of the design variables represent the amount of material given to the elements,
from being empty when 0 to full of material when 1. The amount of material on each
element inﬂuences the structure through its stiﬀness.
The drawback of a continuous parametrization are the possible intermediate ”densi-
ties”, which prevent obtaining topologies identiﬁed by elements being full/empty of
material. This is addressed in the SIMP formulation by penalizing the intermediate
values of each design variable ρe using the power law in
Ee(ρe) = Emin + ρp
e(E0 −Emin), ρe ∈[0, 1]
(24)
where E0 is the Young modulus of the material considered for the structure. The
parameter Emin is set to value six orders of magnitude below E0, with the purpose
of avoiding elements without any stiﬀness when ρe is 0, which can induce numerical
instabilities when solving the assembled system of equations in Eq.(27). The penal-
izing factor p does not hold a physical meaning, and is restricted to p ≥1. The
penalizing eﬀect of the SIMP on intermediate values of the design variable ρ is shown
in Figure 2.6. By using p above 1, the design variables have a non-linear eﬀect on the

2.2.3
Problem formulation
This section presents the mathematical formulations used to describe the structural
optimization problems addressed in this project.
Minimization of cross-section compliance
This topology optimization problem is referred to as the optimal allocation of a ﬁxed
total mass in a design domain in terms of its structural performance. It is evaluated
by the ability of a structure to be the stiﬀest possible, understanding it as its capa-
bility to deform as less as possible under external loads.
A physical metric of how a structure is aﬀected by a speciﬁc load is the external
work it transfers. By equilibrium of energy, this is equivalent to the strain energy in
the structure. The lowest this energy, the less the structure deforms, thus is stiﬀer
against its loads.
For the beam structural problems of this project, the compliance could be computed
as the product of the external load vectors (forces and moments) along the beam
by the induced displacements (translations and rotations) on the those nodal posi-
tions of the beam. Another possibility could be to compute the known internal loads
(forces and moments) induced on the cantilever beam and multiply them by the in-
duced strains and curvatures along each of the cross-sections. The latter option is
considered, as it avoids the formulation of a beam element on top of the cross-section
analysis, and as it will be shown in further analysis it is more adequate in the frame-
work of wind turbine blade optimization.
Then, the compliance Cs(ρ) is deﬁned as the product between the internal loads on
the cross-section θ and their induced deformations ψ. Thus from Eq. (2), the cross-
section compliance Cs(ρ) = θTψ is obtained as Cs(ρ) = θT(Ks(ρ)−1θ).

3.1.3
Optimization solver
The optimization problems are solved using the IPOPT solver [37]. This numerical
tool is based on interior point algorithms for the solution of large non-linear pro-
graming problems. IPOPT can be interfaced to the Matlab environment, in which
the structural optimization tool has been developed.
Therefore, in each iteration
involved the objective and constraint functions and its sensitivities are computed in
Matlab, providing the input for the IPOPT solver to generate a new solution which
minimizes the objective function while fulﬁlling the constraints. This updated solu-
tion of the design variables is evaluated again in the Matlab environment to be sent
to the solver. This iterative procedure is ﬁnished when convergence of the solution is
achieved (the solution is feasible and its rate of change below a predeﬁned tolerance).
3.1.4
Passive/active elements
The discretization of the cross-section slice is done by a rectangular mesh, from which
the 3D ﬁnite element matrix is assembled quickly irrespective of the geometry of the
section considered, without the need for a speciﬁc mesh as input for the problem.
This level of generality, however, has to account for the speciﬁc shape of the section
to be designed. In order to restrict the design space to be bounded inside a geometry
given by the designer, and inspired from [3], passive elements are introduced.
Passive elements are referred to as those elements in the cross-section mesh which
are outside the design space. These are design variables which should be set to zero
and not included in the optimization. It is implemented by carefully selecting those
elements and setting their boundaries as design variables as 0 ≤ρ < 0. By this,
the IPOPT solver automatically disregards them as variables. The drawback is that
these elements are still part of the 3D stiﬀness matrix, keeping the same number of
d.o.f’s from the rectangular mesh of the section, although they do not contribute to
the problem by adding stiﬀness.
The use of passive elements in this project is done to disregard those elements outside
the airfoil shell of a blade section (which logically cannot play a role in the design).
The airfoil shape at each section is known by a vector of points enclosing the airfoil,
deﬁned by a pair of coordinates. By comparing the location of each element in the
mesh to the area enclosed by the airfoil set of points, the elements which are outside
are identiﬁed (and the correspondent boundary condition applied).
This procedure is also used to identify all those elements which are on the border of

only that component which is its maximum or minimum is used (l∗), this is, each

envisioned as a reality for the reinforced ABS polymer (assuming that the properties

similar to traditional ﬁber reinforced laminates, which is studied in the section ahead.

4.3
Multimaterial additive manufacturing
The particular mechanical properties between orthotropic materials on a cross-section
beam is seen to aﬀect the topologies for which the structure optimized reaches its high-
est stiﬀness. By combining several materials with a changing degree of anisotropy,
stiﬀness and density, it is intuitive to think that the overall performance of the struc-
ture can be improved.
Given the assumption that additive manufacturing techniques allow the use of anisotropic
materials such as ﬁber reinforced composites, the next step of complexity is to assume
that these techniques are suitable for manufacturing a range of anisotropic materi-
als inside the same structure.
Novel designs made possible by this consideration
are investigated ahead, by solving the optimization problem of minimizing the mass
of multiple materials on the cross-section. The case study is the blade section at
21m of the 10MW RWT with deformation constraints set by those of the reference
cross-section. The multimaterial optimiization generates its topologies by choosing
the amount of material on each design variable ρe,m from the set of candidate trans-
versely isotropic materials in Table 4.2. From the parametrization of this problem in
Section 2.2.1 note that the number of design variables is now ne × nm, i.e., number of
elements times the number of candidate materials. A constraint is enforced on each
element of the section so that the sum of the amount of each material on the element
is between 0 and 1.

5
Structural optimization of the
DTU 10MW reference blade
In this chapter two structural designs are proposed for the 10 MW reference wind
turbine blade generated using the topology optimization tool developed. From the
ﬁndings in Chapter 3 the problem formulation considered is the minimization of the
cross-section mass with deformation constraints (see problem 3.1 in Section 2.2.3).
The constraints correspond to enforce that the strains and curvatures of the design
do not exceed those strains and curvatures expected on the reference 10MW-RWT
sections for the extreme load cases. The full load vector of each load case is considered
(see Section 3.1.1).
The topology of the complete blade is obtained through the topologies of a set of cross-
sections along the blade. Ten sections of the blade are considered to be representative
of the behavior of the blade along its length, with its respective geometries and load-
cases. Of the 89 meter blade span, its discretization by ten sections is done at the ra-
dial positions of 5.8m, 9.8m, 15m, 21m, 27.4m, 33.8m, 43m, 55.8m, 68.6m, 81.8m. Each
of the cross-sections representing the a blade slice is optimized independently so that
it fulﬁlls its own deformation restrictions. The outcome of this optimization is the
topology of the beam at ten positions along the blade with its respective mass per
length.
5.1
Design proposal using aluminum
A structural design of the 10MW-RWT blade to be built in aluminum with additive
manufacturing techniques is proposed. Aluminum is selected for this design as it is
one of the available materials used in additive manufacturing where high mechanical
properties can be achieved. Other high performing metals such as titanium are used
in AM by the aerospace industry; however, from a cost perspective, aluminum is
considered the most realistic of them to be used in large wind turbine blades. More-
over, the consideration of a metal is done in order to generate a design from a single
isotropic candidate material. Since polymers currently employed in AM suﬀer from
anisotropy due to the manufacturing process, their use is not considered realistic to
generate topologies from isotropic material.

The topologies of the sections near the root (a), (b) and (c) mainly follow the contour
of the airfoil, while the topologies with thin airfoils avoid allocating material at the
nose and trailing panels. The explanation to this trend might come from the distri-
bution of torsion moments. As a cantilever beam, torsion from pure torque and from
shear loads away from the shear center are absorbed at the root. A closed circular
cell corresponds to the optimal topology against torsion alone. Another explanation
to why the vertical shear webs follow the contour near the root is from the shape of
the airfoil itself. As the airfoil thickness is lower, the distance between the suction
and pressure caps is much lower than between these caps and the nose and tail of
the airfoil. Therefore the needed material to follow the contour is above what the
optimization considers optimal.
Thick members of material around the contour for the sections near the root are
placed diﬀerently for (a), (b) and (c). The reason why there are multiple elements
that resemble caps is not understood. It is known that high bending moments for
both edgewise and ﬂapwise directions occur at the root, and that these are modeled
by several load-cases which in which they can act simultaneously, being positive or
negative. Then, by placing several caps which are rotated to the reference system the
topology performs better against multiple loads, having each multiple components
(see validation example in Section 2.3.2).
From the section located at 21m onwards, a concentration of material is placed at the
trailing edge, together with its connection by a thin element to the rest of the topol-
ogy. This element is believed to fulﬁll the edgewise bending stiﬀness requirements.
The larger the airfoil chord, the more the optimization will push to add material at
the tail, where it furthest away from the elastic center. The element connecting the
material at the trailing edge in sections (f), (g), (h) and (i) follows the contour of
the trailing panel at the pressure side. This asymmetry in the topology with respect
to the chord-line is believed to explain the fact that the shear center is signiﬁcantly
below the chord-line in these sections. This structural element then aﬀects the blade
by inducing negative twist for a positive edgewise shear force (force at the reference
point with direction from trailing edge to nose). The optimization might be driven
to these topologies to exploit the coupling of shear forces and torque. Observing that
the area of the closed cell (formed by the horizontal caps and vertical webs) is smaller
in the topologies of (f), (g), (h) and (i), compared for instance with (e) or (j), it could
be inferred that the stiﬀness to twist is added by the trailing edge connection.

CONCLUDING REMARKS
6
Concluding remarks
The investigations carried through this thesis on the use of topology optimization
techniques for structural design of wind turbine blades considering additive manufac-
turing are concluded by the following discussion.
6.1
On the Structural topology optimization model
The topology optimization models developed are based on a structural model of the
blade formulated from beam theory. This reduced order beam model was considered
instead of a full 3D ﬁnite element discretization from its reduced computational re-
quirements, of relevance in an optimization process. The problems formulated are
considered to exploit the beam model to reﬂect the particularities of a blade, rather
than considering it a rough simpliﬁcation. For instance, the load history expected on
a wind turbine blade (and obtained from aero-elastic tools which model the blade as
a beam) can be considered in the optimization. Therefore, accurate predicted loads
can be applied already in the conceptual design stage.
6.2
On the Choice of parameters in the model
The need for a continuation scheme on the penalization factor is identiﬁed when us-
ing a SIMP parametrization of the design variables. This procedure prevents from
reaching sub-optimal topologies.
The need of a regularization scheme to avoid numerical instabilities is successfully
overcome by ﬁltering the design variables. The ﬁlter radius size should be set to the
minimum possible to achieve optimal designs. Its use as a manufacturing constraint
is not considered relevant from the perspective of large-scale additive manufacturing.

CONCLUDING REMARKS
The design space for the structure of a blade is bounded to the area inside a predeﬁned
airfoil. Enforcing that the airfoil shell consists of structural mass is not well suited in
this model.
6.3
On the Choice of problem formulation
The structural optimization problems formulated consider the external loads as de-
sign independent. Due to the complexity of the nature of the loads on a blade, a set
of extreme loads from a reference blade design is considered. The selection of loads
in the optimization problem is not intuitive and its inﬂuence should be extensively
investigated.
For the compliance minimization problem, the physical interpretation of using a sum
of compliances from separate load vectors is not clear, making it hard to evaluate the
structural performance of the design. Moreover, compliance minimization formula-
tions have the drawback of setting the overall amount of material as an input instead
of a design variable.
For the mass minimization problem, the added design freedom of setting the overall
mass involves that the assumption that external loads are design independent does
not seem to hold. For instance, edgewise bending moments depend largely on the self-
weight of the blade. Despite this, it is considered the most representative problem
formulation in the project through its constraints on the deformations.
The deformation constraints correspond to the strains and curvatures on the sections
of the blade. By the use of simulated extreme load cases on the blade, and limiting
the deformations on the they induce, it is considered a good measure to obtain novel
blade topologies. These constraints can be set to match an existing valid blade, or set
to reﬂect a design requirement such as a limit on the displacement of the tip to ensure
tower clearance. Moreover, cross-section deformations could be used as a measure to
reﬂect restrictions such as material failure or local buckling, by relating, for instance,
curvatures from bending moments to strain levels on the material at the edge of the
airfoil.

CONCLUDING REMARKS
6.4
On the Material selection
Available materials for additive manufacturing are not considered here to be at the
stage where they can be employed in large scale wind turbine blade structures. How-
ever, assuming the properties of a set of potential candidate materials, the topology
optimization models developed can been used explore their suitability for blade struc-
tures.
The consideration of using AM with multiple materials simultaneously, has lead to the
formulation of multimaterial topology optimization for blades sections. The model
can allow for further investigations of candidate materials and their possible interac-
tion in the blade. For instance, the use of multiple anisotropic materials instead of a
single candidate has been proven to result in blade section mass reductions.
6.5
On the Full blade topology optimization
The topology optimization tool has been applied to generate a complete blade design
as a set of optimal cross-section topologies. A blade structure composed of aluminum
and a blade formed by two glass ﬁber reinforced composites are proposed as alterna-
tive designs of the 10MW-RWT.
Qualitatively, the topologies obtained have large diﬀerences with respect to the ref-
erence design, for instance in the number of shear webs, the width of caps at the
suction and pressure sides or the addition of material at the trailing edge and lack of
it at the nose.
The stiﬀness properties of both designs nearly match those of the reference design.
Then, the constraints on the model for which the new blade cannot deform more than
the reference are proven to provide valuable topologies.
The overall mass of the blades designed are above the targeted mass of the refer-
ence blade. Despite optimizing without considering failure, buckling or dynamics,
the topologies include more structural mass than the 10MW-RWT. The use of ﬁner
meshes of the cross-sections being optimized is perceived to overcome this, as thinner
elements are seen to provide more optimal topologies. elements

FUTURE WORK
7
Future work
• Improve the eﬃciency of the optimization tool to allow much ﬁner discretization
of the design domain that lead to improved topology designs.
From the code: parametrization and assembly only of those elements involved
in the optimization.
For the solver: the implementation of the Hessian (second derivatives of ob-
jective and constraint functions) could lead to reduce the number of iterations
needed.
• Investigate the eﬀect of the topologies on the loads expected at the blade
through aero-elastic simulations. The design workﬂow should be tested in a
loop, where new load cases are updated considering the optimized topologies.
It is not clear how the designs aﬀect the aero-elastic response of the blade in
operation, therefore convergence of this design loop to an optimal topology is
not ensured a priori.
The ﬁndings of this study could lead to new formulations of the optimization
problem that consider the inﬂuence on the aero-elastic response. Examples of
these formulations could be the cross-section deformations constraints adjusted
to comply with the aerodynamic performance or by the blade tip displacement
to ensure tower clearance.
• Investigate the potential of metamaterials on the topology of blade cross-sections.
Metamaterials are understood as those materials with speciﬁc mechanical prop-
erties given by their microstructure, which can be tailored and manufactured
by AM from a single or multiple base materials. Families of materials with a
wide range of properties could be evaluated as candidate materials using mul-
timaterial topology optimization, such as in [29]. These materials could enable
added capabilities to blade structures apart from resisting loads. Considering
the warping deformations of the cross-section problem, optimization problems
could be formulated in terms of the local deformations inside the blade section.
This approach might have the potential to generate sections designed so that
their deformations are used to modify the airfoil shape and increase or decrease
the aerodynamic performance passively.
