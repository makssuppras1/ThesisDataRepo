Preface 
This thesis is submitted to the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in the fulfillment 
partial fulfillment of the Master of Science program in Petroleum Engineering. The research was 
conducted at the Center for Energy Resources Engineering (CERE) and Center of Electron 
Microscopy (CEN) at DTU in the period from February-July 2015. This work is part of the 
BioRec project Work package 1 in the study of enzymes for the applications for enzymatic 
enhanced oil recovery (EEOR).  Associate Professor Alexander Shapiro and PhD student Alsu 
Khusainova have supervised this Master thesis. 
It is believed that one of the mechanisms of EEOR could be the mobilization of oil through 
emulsion formation using enzymes. The main focus of this thesis is to investigate the emulsion 
formation with enzyme products with known compositions and the factors affecting them. The 
thesis is divided into 7 sections which include theoretical and experimental investigation. The 
experimental section includes only static tests with bottle tests along with the use of an optical 
microscope for screening of the enzyme emulsion formation, stability and droplet size 
distribution. An adsorption-desorption experiment with and without oil was carried out to 
investigate how much enzyme can be restored after contact with solid fines and occurrence of 
competitive adsorption when oil phase was present.

Abstract 
Experimental study of formation of emulsions in brine-oil-enzyme systems with solid fines 
was carried out. The study was directed to analysis of the possible mechanisms governing the 
enzymatic enhanced oil recovery (EEOR). Formation of emulsions may serve for mobilization of 
the separate oil droplets, resulting in additional recovery.  
N-decane was selected as model oil to investigate emulsifying ability of different enzymes 
provided from Novozymes A/S and two commercial products. It was found that a North Sea oil 
with enzyme formed the same amount of emulsion when diluted by the different amounts of 
n-decane; hence, n-decane can represent behavior of the crude oil in this case and can serve as 
model oil. Furthermore, the emulsion transparency was increasing with increased dilution with 
n-decane, thus indicating that the light fraction of oil takes part in emulsions. The emulsions 
formed in brine-decane-enzyme systems were found to be stable for minimum of a month with 
the method of emulsion preparation to be a dependent variable. The long-chain alkanes were 
found to form more stable emulsions and shorter ones promoted formation of small droplets. For 
NS 81249, NS 44053 and NS 440163 enzymes, the effect of hydrolysis of ester was tested. The 
results showed decrease of the amount of the formed emulsion and decrease in the droplet size 
distribution (DSD). The presence of the stabilizer contained in the enzyme products enhanced 
emulsion amount with n-decane, however, inhibited formation of o/w micro droplets in the water 
phase and w/o droplets in the oil phase with North Sea oil. Enzymes/proteins produced more 
emulsions compared to the surfactant, sodium dodecyl sulfate. An increase of the enzyme 
concentration from 1 wt. % to 5 wt. % resulted in creation of more w/o micro droplets while the 
bulk amount of emulsion was similar.  
Addition of CaCO3 particles (≤30 μm) together with an enzyme caused intensification of 
the emulsion formation compared to the case when only enzyme was added. Application of SiO2 
particles (0.5-10 μm) formed an emulsion with and without the presence of an enzyme. The SiO2 
particles covered the droplet interfaces, unlike CaCO3 particles due to the level of adsorption 
which was confirmed with adsorption-desorption results. Addition of enzymes to the particles

Introduction 
Crude oil has become an essential part of our everyday life ever since the ancient times. It 
was said that the ancients have used asphalt for the construction of the walls and tower of 
Babylon (Hugh, 1911). However, nowadays petroleum has taken a different form from the time 
when in 1850 James Young patented a method of distilling kerosene from crude oil (Butt, 1963). 
The demand for crude oil has been rising since then and new techniques of oil extraction had to 
be developed to meet the increasing fuel demands of the internal combustion engines, aviation 
sector and the synthesis of plastics, etc. 
There are four different classifications of oil reservoirs, undiscovered (prospective 
resources), discovered unrecoverable, discovered sub-commercial (contingent resources) and 
discovered commercial (reserves), (Etherington 2005). Thus in order to produce more oil, there 
are a few options. First conduct more exploration, second improve the technology to produce less 
accessible oil or lastly improve the production technology to obtain more oil from discovered 
commercial fields. With our current technological advancements we are able to only produce less 
than half of the original oil in place (OOIP) and a third when it comes to the Danish oil fields 
(Awan et al., 2008). 
In order to produce this oil, tertiary recovery has to be applied such as enhanced oil 
recovery (EOR) methods which use the injection of fluids not present in the reservoir. 19 past or 
current EOR project have been reported by Awan et al., (2008) in the Danish sector of the North 
Sea out of which 18 are gas injections and one microbial. Simonsen (2015) has reported that an 
increment of 1 % of OOIP for the Danish Dan, Halfdan or Kraka fields is the equivalent of 10.5 
billion USD $. Advanced water flooding is currently applied to these fields. The question is what 
to inject in order to increase the recovery. 
A new ‘green’ method of enzymatic enhanced oil recovery (EEOR) is being explored 
which uses enzymes in a similar manner to surfactants to alter the properties of both the rock and 
crude oil so as to mobilize the trapped oil in ganglia, see Figure 1. Commercial mixtures of 
enzymes with unknown compositions have been applied in Asia with success, but the data are 
limited and do not go into details which makes it a problem for further research and 
understanding of the mechanism governing the oil mobilization.

Khusainova et al., (2015) has shown that certain enzymes are capable of altering the 
wettability of oil-wet Calcite to water-wet based on static experiments at levels of

This study focuses on static tests to screen 15 enzymes for potential emulsion creating 
enzymes along with protein bovine serum albumin (BSA), surfactants sodium dodecyl sulfate 
(SDS) and dioctyl sodium sulfosuccinate (AOT) for comparison.  If the enzymes act as surface 
active agents they can produce emulsions or microemulsions thereby reducing the interfacial 
tension which could potentially mobilize the trapped oil shown in Figure 1. Different scenarios 
such as concentration of the enzyme, mixing ratios, addition of fines have been studied in a 
bottle test. Adsorption experiments have been carried out with Calcium Carbonate and Silicon 
dioxide under different conditions (concentration of enzyme, particle amount) to explore the 
adsorption of enzymes previously reported by Haastrup (2014), Khusainova (to be published).

Literature review and theory 
1 Types of oil recovery 
Crude oil production is divided into three categories. Primary, secondary and tertiary 
recovery also known as enhanced oil recovery. Nowadays tertiary recovery is applied 
earlier as to increase the total recovery further. 
1.1 Primary and secondary recovery 
Primary recovery occurs when the natural drive due to pressure differences between 
the reservoir caused by the buildup of a pressure due to the hydrocarbons and the 
atmospheric pressure which is considerably lower. This forces the hydrocarbons to flow 
from the ground towards the surface and is called the primary recovery. The recovery 
factor is usually between 5 – 15 % of the original oil in place (OOIP), (Tzimas, 2005). 
Therefore when the hydrocarbons are being produced, the pressure of the reservoir drops 
exponentially. This in turn causes smaller pressure difference and lower drive until a point, 
when the external pressure is required to produce more hydrocarbons. In order to maintain 
the pressure difference and hence assure that a sufficient drive is reached, it is necessary to 
maintain the pressure by external energy by recirculating the produced water or gas into the 
reservoir, called the secondary recovery. The recovery factor of waterflooding in secondary 
recovery is around 30 % of OOIP.  
1.2 Tertiary recovery 
Both primary and secondary recovery can have recovery factors on average between 
35 and optimistically 45 % of OOIP, (Tzimas, 2005). This still means that more than half 
of the oil stays trapped in the reservoir. In order to extend the life of an oil field a tertiary 
recovery or enhanced oil recovery (EOR) is applied. It is “the recovery of oil by injection 
of a fluid that is not native to the reservoir” (Lake, 2008). It aims at altering the capillary, 
viscous, gravity forces and interfacial tension between SW-crude oil. These factors depend 
on the type of the reservoir as each oil and each reservoir is different by nature. They are 
usually based on the following principles: 
==PAGE:19==

· Increasing the capillary number – reducing oil-water interfacial tension 
Where Nc is the capillary number in the order of 10-5, µ is the dynamic viscosity of the 
liquid, v is the characteristic velocity and γ is the surface/interfacial tension between two 
fluid phases (oil and water). 
· Lowering the mobility ratio – increasing water viscosity, reducing oil 
viscosity, reducing water permeability or all of the above. 
Where M is the mobility ratio, µo is the dynamic viscosity of oil, µw is the dynamic 
viscosity of water, kro is the relative permeability of oil and krw is the relative permeability 
of water in a porous medium.

1.3.1 Thermal method 
Thermal methods are mainly applied on heavy crudes which tend to be present in a 
greater proportion than light HC worldwide. Lowering the viscosity of high viscos oils 
decreases the mobility ratio. 
Cyclic Steam Stimulation (CSS) also known as ‘huff and puff’ is a process in which 
hot steam is injected into a radius of tens of meters away from the injection well and 
eventually into a production well with a recovery rates reaching 40 % of OOIP, (Alvarez & 
Han, 2013). 
Steam drives (steam flooding) differs from CSS by continues injection of steam 
whereby the heavy crude is driven to producers. In combination with CSS it can produce 
up to 50 – 60 % OOIP, (Alvarez & Han 2013). 
Steam assisted gravity drainage (SAGD) uses a pair of horizontal wells few meters 
above each other with a continuous injection of steam into the upper well with the aim of 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
  6 
 
reducing oil viscosity thereby reducing the mobility ratio with a recovery of over 60 - 70 % 
OOIP, Thomas (2008). 
 
1.3.2 Chemical injection 
Chemical injection methods are based on injecting chemicals that in the process 
increase the capillary number except for polymer flooding in which the mobility ratio is 
lowered. 
Polymer flooding lowers the mobility ratio through thickening agents which in turn 
increase the viscosity of water. In the report by Lake (2008), polymer flooding has been 
one of the most popular EOR methods used in the past in the USA due to an artificial 
taxing policy. It has also been prospective in the Chinese Daqing Field, (Renqing, 2013). 
Micellar-Polymer Flooding is similar to polymer flooding except for the addition of 
surfactants which decrease the interfacial tension (IFT) between the water/oil interface. 
This increases the capillary number and as polymer flooding decreases the mobility ratio. 
Unlike waterflooding which is mainly useful in a homogeneous reservoir, micellar-polymer 
flooding can be applied to highly heterogeneous reservoir in tertiary hydrocarbon recovery 
(Dang, Chen, Nguyen & Bae, 2014). 
Alkaline flooding uses co-surfactants in small amounts for the naturally occurring 
surfactants present in some crude oils and is claimed to produce at 20-25 $/bbl (Lake, 
2008). The annual report from Husky Oil Operations Limited suggests a great potential in 
the Alberta region of Canada. 
1.3.3 Gas injection 
Gas injection uses the principle of mass transfer in recovering the oil. These are: 
· Transfer of intermediate HC components from crude to vaporizing gas drive: 
CO2, nitrogen, flue gas 
· Transfer from solvent (gas) to the crude (condensing or rich gas drives): 
hydrocarbon miscible drives

  7 
 
With a large enough reservoir pressure the miscible displacement tends to have 
higher recoveries as interfacial tension significantly drops and the capillary number 
becomes infinity. Under recovery conditions the recoveries tend to 100 % whereas in the 
field they are considerably lower due to greater mobility of the gas in comparison to 
oil/water mixtures.

2 Enzymatic enhanced oil recovery 
Enzymatic enhanced oil recovery (EEOR) was established based on microbial 
enhanced oil recovery (MEOR) and is a relatively new method in the area of the EOR. The 
basic principle lays in the use of enzymes instead of chemicals in particular surfactants to 
reach similar conditions as in surfactant flooding. Thereby increasing the oil recovery 
through alteration of interfacial tension (IFT) between the system oil-brine and the 
wettability of the mineral.  
Successful pilot tests have been conducted in Asia particularly in the Dagang and 
Daqing Chaoyanggou oilfields in China and in Mann field in Mayanmar using the 
commercial enzyme GreenZyme (Feng et al., 2009). On laboratory scale both static and 
dynamic experiments were conducted with pure enzymes by Khusainova et al., (2015) and 
Nasiri (2011) used also GreenZyme. 
2.1 Enzymes 
Enzymes are globular proteins acting as catalysts in chemical reactions in cells of 
living organisms by lowering the activation energy of the reactions (Cichoke 1999). 
Enzymes speed up for example the natural digestion of food in our stomachs which would 
take years without the lowering of the activation energy. Since enzymes act as catalysts, 
they do not get consumed in the reactions (Copeland 2000). However, they do denature 
over time if exposed to unwanted conditions depending on the type of enzyme. Daniel, 
Dines and Petach (1996) reported that there are enzymes which are able to withstand even 
100oC which make them applicable to reservoir conditions. 
2.1.1 Enzyme structure and mechanism 
Figure 2 shows the structure of a typical amino acid which are building blocks of 
proteins which in turn are the constituents of enzymes. Each amino acid has the same basic 
molecular structure, an alpha carbon to which a carboxylic and amine groups are attached 
along with a side chain that is specific for each amino acid. It is the side chain which 
determines whether or not the amino acids tends to be hydrophobic (water hating) or 
hydrophilic (water loving).

The order of the amino acids in the enzyme determines the structure and the 
reactivity of the proteins. It can therefore be determined based on the sequence of the 
amino acids whether or not there will be any catalytic reaction between a substrate and an 
enzyme’s active site where the catalytic reaction takes place (Copeland 2000). 
As stated previously, enzymes are affected by temperature, chemical environment 
such as pH and the concentration of the substrate. This happens due to alteration of their 
structure which affects the reaction mechanism taking place. Inhibitors can be compounds 
such as heavy metals which alter the way how enzymes fold. Thus modifying the active 
site of the enzyme and disrupting the locking mechanism of the enzyme’s active site and 
the substrate. 
The advantages of enzymes over chemical catalysts is that unlike chemical catalysts, 
enzymes do show specificity for both the substrate and the product which ensures that the 
product is not contaminated with by-products. The substrate binds to the active site of the 
enzyme in a so called transition state in which case the bonds in the substrate are not 
completely broken nor are the bonds in the product completely formed. It is this transition 
state which lowers the activation energy of the reactions and enables a faster reaction time 
(Copeland 2000).  
There are two theories describing the binding of the substrate on the enzyme’s active 
site. The lock and key theory and the modified lock and key theory called the induced fit 
model.

2.1.2 Lock and key 
In 1894 Emil Fisher has proposed a mechanism explaining the concept of an 
enzyme’s active site behaving like a lock and a substrate acting as a key (Lichtenthaler 
1995). In this situation only the correctly sized and shaped substrate fits into the enzyme’s 
active site. 
 
2.1.3 Induced-fit model 
In 1958 Daniel Koshland proposed a modified version of the lock and key 
mechanism which better suited the experimental data. Rather than assuming that only a 
certain shape will fit into the enzyme’s active site, Koshland suggested that the enzyme’s 
active site is flexible and can be altered by the shape of the substrate as is shown in Figure

2.2 Application of enzymes in the oil and gas industry 
Enzymes have been recently introduced to the oil and gas industry and are able to 
withstand the reservoir condition (Harris and McKay 1998). Harris and McKay (1988) 
have produced a list of enzyme applications in the petroleum industry from which some of 
them are permeability modifications, formation damage removal and polymer degradation, 
gel breaker. 
2.2.1 Permeability modifications 
After waterflooding, oil stays in the unswept areas of the reservoir. One of the 
reasons for this is high permeability areas through which the water flows and causes it to 
bypass the less permeable sections. In order to sweep these sections of the reservoir Nemati 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
  12 
 
and Voordouw, 2003; Larsen et al., 2008 proposed to use an enzyme which would block 
these high permeable sections by hydration of CO2 and precipitate CaCO3.  Asgahir et al. 
(2006) and Mirjafari (2007) conducted both static and dynamic experiments showing 
CaCO3 precipitation in sandstones when using the bovine carbonic anhydrase as a catalyst 
which effectively increased the oil recovery. 
2.2.2 Formation damage removal and polymer degradation 
Formation damage occurs during drilling when the polymer in the drilling fluid 
invades the vicinity of the wellbore due to hydrostatic pressure being greater than the 
pressure in the formation. This creates a filter cake which upon completion needs to be 
removed in order to achieve high efficiency from the beginning of the production.  Moore 
1996; Battistel et al., 2005; Siddiqui and Nasr-El-Din, 2005; Samuel et al., 2009 have 
proposed the use of enzymes in degrading the filter cake compromised from the copolymer 
of 75 % starch polymer and 25 % xanthan gum polymer. A successful treatment of a well 
after completion, stimulation and work-over operations has been conducted by DeVine et 
al. (1998), and showed an improvement in permeability and no damage to the formation.  
2.2.3 Gel breaker 
High viscosity formulations of guar along with solid proppants are used as fracturing 
fluid. The solid proppants are designed specifically for keeping the induced hydraulic 
fractures opened during the fracturing operations and afterwards during production. It has 
been proposed to use enzymes for breaking up the guar polymer gel by an enzyme breaker 
with success, (Moore 1996, Armstrong 2010). Kyaw et al., (2012) compared commercial 
chemical oxidative and enzyme breakers. The enzyme breakers provide rapid breakage 
already at low concentrations in comparison with the oxidative breakers. In 
Grayburg - Jackson Field, Southeast New Mexico an increase in production has been 
reported due to the application of an enzyme breaker by Malone et al. (2000). 
2.2.4 Advantages of enzymes in the petroleum industry 
· Khusainova et al., (2015) has reported that low concentrations of the enzyme 
are needed to achieve more water-wet conditions 
· Enzymes are biodegradable and environmentally friendly

· Enzymes are catalysts and therefore they do not get consumed during the 
reactions and increase the reaction rates more than chemical catalysts 
· Enzymes show higher specificity for the substrate than chemical catalysts

2.3 Field tests 
There have been several reported field tests mainly from around Asia, from the Mann 
field in Maynmar (Ott et al., 2011), the Dagang field (Feng et al., 2009), Sabei (Wang, 
2009) and Daqing Chaoyanggou (He and Zhonghong, 2011) fileds in China. A paper by 
Moon (2008) mentions other places such as Indonesia and Venezuela with successful 
EEOR projects. The information is incomplete, but it can be assumed that most of these 
pilot tests have been performed with sandstones and not carbonate reservoirs. 
There are two general conclusions from these papers. The first one is that the 
enzymes accelerate the release of oil from the mineral surface by catalytic mechanism in 
which the enzymes are then released by the oil again and continue working further in the 
reservoir. Feng et al., 2009 proposed and Ott et al., 2011 supported the theory that the 
enzyme-oil system works by the induced fit model explained previously. The second 
conclusion is that enzymes tend to alter the wettability of the mineral from oil-wet to 
water-wet by reducing the IFT. Thus releasing more oil from the grain surface and 
enhancing the oil recovery. Moon (2008) have reported the use of a Apollo GreenZyme™ 
in the pilot test with successful results. However, these papers in general lack complete 
information which is explained below.  
The papers from Feng et al., (2009) and He and Zhonghong, (2011) do not contain 
any close insights about the origins of the enzyme products which were being used. The 
only information regarding the composition of these enzyme products is that it has been 
extracted from a bacteria which is capable of separating oil from sand (Ott et al., 2011).  
However, the problem of using commercial mixtures is that they tend to contain 
except for the enzyme also stabilizers and surfactants (Apollo GreenZyme™ Material 
Safety Data Sheet; Feng et al., 2007). Therefore it is difficult to judge whether or not the 
increase in OOIP is purely due to the enzyme or due to the surfactants/stabilizers present in

  14 
 
the mixtures. Tests of pure enzymes need to be conducted to decide whether or not these 
proposed effects are due to the enzymes or due to the surfactants/stabilizers. 
 
2.4 Laboratory tests and proposed mechanisms 
Khusainova et al., (2015) reports three potential mechanism which have been 
reported based on the results from other papers as well as (Feng et al., 2007; Moon, 2008; 
Nasiri et al., 2009; Nasiri et al., 2011; He and Zhonghong, 2011; Ott et al., 2011, Lunde et 
al., 2013, Haastrup et al., 2014): 
1. Breaking connections between the oil and the internal porous surface 
2. Decreasing of IFT and creation of emulsions 
3. Decreasing oil viscosity 
All three properties of enzymes improve the mobility ratio and thus should increase 
the oil recovery.  
Experiments conducted by Nasiri (2011) and Khusainova et al., (2015) further 
showed: 
· Enzymes do alter wettability to a large extent. 
· Concentrations of the pure enzyme 0.002 – 0.005 wt. % of the lipase/esterase 
groups were found to be able to readily detach oil droplets from a Calcite 
mineral. 
· Surface tension has been found to be unaltered by enzymes which is not the

· Two enzymes NS44164 (esterase) and NS81249 (lipase) have been reported 
(Nasiri et al., 2011; Khusainova 2015) to have the greatest perspective for

Classification of enzymes. 
· Hydrolysis of esters contained in model oil (Nasiri 2011). The products of 
hydrolysis (alcohol + acid) could stabilize emulsion creation.

2.5 Adsorption at the liquid-liquid interface 
The kinetics of adsorption of brine-enzyme-oil system is governed by the IFT which 
in turn causes phase emulsification. The phenomenon of spontaneous emulsification is 
reportedly discovered by Gad in 1978. It occurs when the interfacial pressure is greater 
than the initial surface tension, Egbogah (1984). The topic of emulsification is further 
described in detail in chapter 3 Emulsions. 
Beverung (1999) has proposed to determine the IFT as a function of time to 
investigate the effect of the enzyme’s structural confirmation and its impact on adsorption 
on the surface of the oil/water interface. The surface pressure is given by

Based on the equation above, it can be said that the surface pressure increases from 
zero during protein adsorption on the liquid interface. Kaminski (1949) proposed three 
different mechanisms by which spontaneous emulsification can take place in a reservoir: 
1. Quincke (1904) suggests that a solute lowering the IFT causes violent 
spreading and thus emulsifies the system. 
2. ‘Negative interfacial drive emulsification’ which feeds the energy to the 
system by the adsorption of a surface-active agents at the interface and the 
energy from solubilization by the hydrocarbon which are in form of surfactant 
micelles in the water phase (Davies 1961) 
3. Miller and Scriven (1970) disbelieve the previous theory and suggest that 
other forces such as electrical forces involved in the double layers can have a 
significant impact on the stability. Especially when tension is low.  
Baldursdottir (2010) proposed based on laboratory experiments that an increase in 
protein concentration directly correlates to an increase in the amount of the adsorbed 
protein at the oil/water interface. Proteins can be thought of as surface-active agents due to 
their amphiphilic properties, i.e. both water and oil loving behavior just like surfactants.

each exhibit different properties. There are high and low energy surfaces with the high

2.6.2 Adsorption at the mineral-protein interface 
The mechanism of adsorption of enzymes/proteins on solids such as Calcium 
Carbonate, Silicon dioxide or clay minerals is not yet fully understood. Different 
explanations exist. 
Nakanishi (2001) states that major factors affecting protein adsorption are structural 
stability of the proteins and hydrophilic/hydrophobic properties of the surface. There are 
generally two kinds of structurally stable proteins, “hard” and “soft” proteins.  Hard 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
  18 
 
proteins can adsorb on a surface with an oppositely charged surface or same charged 
surface if the interaction is hydrophobic (case of sandstone with North Sea water) while 
soft proteins are structurally unstable and tend to adsorb on any surface in order to gain 
conformational entropy (Nakanishi 2001).  
Zimmerman (2011) reports that there is no significant relationship between the net 
charge of adsorbed proteins and the charge of the mineral. This is supported by the results 
of the penetration test with both sandstone and Calcium Carbonate using the same enzyme 
in the work of Khusainova (yet to be published). In both cases the enzyme is adsorbed 
using cores of Sandstone and Calcite with the same length in terms of the surface area. 
A proposed mechanism of adsorption is due to the electrostatic interactions in 
protein-mineral interaction. However, other schools propose that hydrophobic interactions 
and lowering of free energy due to conformational changes are the dominant mechanism of 
protein-mineral adsorption.  The second proposed mechanism explains i.e. the adsorption 
of protein onto hydrophobic surfaces such as Silicon dioxide even though non-favorable 
electrostatic conditions are present (Zimmerman 2011). Naidja et al. (2002) has shown that 
the adsorbed proteins show confirmation changes upon mineral adsorption which has a 
direct effect on their catalytic activity.

3 Emulsions 
Spontaneous emulsification in a porous medium was proposed as one of the 
production mechanisms in a reservoir as stated previously in subchapter Adsorption at the 
liquid-liquid interface. However, in order to produce even more trapped oil, it is necessary 
to enhance the emulsification further. 
3.1 Types of emulsions 
3.1.1 Emulsion definition 
Fluid system in which liquid droplets are dispersed in another liquid. The diameters 
of the droplets constituting the dispersed phase usually range from approximately 
10 nm - 100 μm. The droplets may exceed the usual size limits for colloidal particles 
(Compendium of Chemical Terminology, 1997). 
3.1.2 Microemulsion definition 
Dispersion made of water, oil, and surfactant(s) that is an isotropic and 
thermodynamically stable system with dispersed domain diameter varying approximately 
from 1 to 100 nm, usually 10 to 50 nm (Compendium of Chemical Terminology, 1997) 
3.1.3 Emulsion structures

b) water in oil (w/o) microemulsion, c) bicontinuous microemulsion, d) and e) various 
dispersions. It is a simplification as in reality other structures can be observed: viscous 
solutions, lamellar liquid crystals, thin or thick gels, single phase, two phase and three

3.1.4 Emulsion classifications 
Emulsions are classified according to Winsor (1948). He has reported four different 
situations in the system of oil-water-amphiphiles as shown in Figure 7. In Winsor I the 
dispersed droplets of oil are in the continuous water phase and vice versa for Winsor II. In 
both cases of Winsor I and II the concentrations of dispersed oil or water are low. Winsor 
III occurs when distorted droplets aggregate which leads to a continuous water and oil 
phase and form a bi-continuous structure in between. Winsor IV is a complete 
homogeneous single phase of either dispersed oil or water.

3.3.2 Difference between stability of microemulsions and emulsions 
The emulsion characteristic is strongly dependent on the amount of energy added as 
stated previously. Chen (2005) and Mandal (2009) report that under laboratory conditions, 
the emulsion stability strongly depends on different rpm, time, temperature and type of 
energy transfer as depicted previously in Table 4. 
Emulsions are kinetically stable, i.e. path dependent on the intermediate species and 
transition states which might have a high activation energy and this barrier must be 
overcome. Whereas microemulsions are thermodynamically stable, meaning only 
dependent on free energy ∆G. 
In order to explain the difference between the stability of emulsions and 
microemulsions, it is necessary to explain the free energy of formation or Gibb’s energy 
given by 
∆G = γ∆A −T∆S 
Where γ is the surface tension, ∆A is the change in interfacial area, T is the 
temperature and ∆S is the change in entropy. The free energy of formation in case of a 
dilute microemulsion phase is caused by two main factors. 
1. γ∆A: An unfavorable positive contribution resulting from the formation of a 
large increase of the oil-water interface in the system which equals to the 
product of surface tension and the increase in the interfacial area 
2. T∆S: A favorable contribution from an increase in entropy which is due to the 
dispersion of the droplets in the continuous phase equaling the product of 
temperature and entropy 
Microemulsions form spontaneously as ∆G is negative only under the circumstances 
when the tension is low enough so that the terms described above fulfill the following 
condition: γ∆A < T∆S, Morris (1995). The difference between the dependence of dispersed 
phase radius of emulsions and microemulsions is described in Figure 9 in relation to Gibb’s 
free energy.

3.3.3 Destabilizing mechanisms 
Since emulsions are kinetically stable, it means that they deteriorate over time at a 
considerably faster pace. There are several mechanisms which govern this process: 
flocculation, creaming/sedimentation, coalescence, phase inversion and Ostwald ripening 
(Szűcs 2008). 
Flocculation occurs due to the attraction forces being stronger than repulsive forces 
(see Figure 8) by droplet aggregation which form flocs. Flocs or emulsion droplets can 
further undergo one of the two mechanisms. Sedimentation, or creaming depending on 
their density in comparison to the continuous phase. This phenomenon is described by 
Stoke’s law, if the density of the droplets is larger than the continuous phase, they will 
undergo sedimentation and vice versa for creaming. The emulsion droplets can also 
undergo coalescence (breaking of emulsion) by two droplets merging into one which 
occurs in two steps. Firstly thinning of the liquid film between the droplets and secondly by 
its disruption.  Coalescence reduces the interfacial area and based on Gibb’s equation for 
free energy of formation does so in order to decrease the total energy of the system. 
Oswald ripening is a process occurring between two phases. It is a process during 
which small emulsion droplets diffuse into larger ones in order to decrease the total 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
  26 
 
interfacial area and thus decrease the total energy of the system as in coalescence (Szűcs, 
2008).

3.3.5 Compounds affecting stability 
Alvarado (2011) reports two groups of stabilizing agents. The first one are Pickering 
emulsions (Ramsden, 1903) which are emulsions stabilized by particles. The main factors 
controlling the emulsion stability by particle stabilization are the particle wettability, 
surface charge, pH and size (Alvarado 2011). The second one is organic polar fractions of 
the crude oil such as asphaltenes, resins and organic acids. However, Alvarado (2011) 
reports that the stabilization mechanisms of these compounds are still debatable. N-alkane 
chain length has also been discussed. 
3.3.5.1 Pickering emulsions 
When particles are present in the system specifically in water neutral wettability 
scenario they can lower the IFT of emulsions. The solid particles must be smaller than the 
droplets in order for them to adsorb on the interface of oil and water. They stabilize the 
emulsions by decreasing the interface interaction of the droplets by volume exclusion 
(Alvarado, 2011). Arditty et al. (2004) has shown that Silicon dioxide particles which have 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
 
  27 
 
been treated for wettability alteration can stabilize o/w or w/o emulsions.  Other particles 
such as clays, Calcium Carbonate can be used as well. Wang (2010) has performed an 
experiment using olive oil with distilled water and Silicon dioxide and Calcium Carbonate 
and a mix of Silicon dioxide-Calcium Carbonate particles to produce oil-water-particle 
emulsions, see Figure 10. The stabilization time and average droplet size of the Pickering 
emulsions has decreased with an increase in the content of Silicon dioxide up to 15 wt. %. 
Larger Silicon dioxide particles had more impact on the emulsion stability and smaller 
Silicon dioxide particles influenced more the drop size distribution of the emulsion which 
is due to the strong adsorption behavior at oil-water interfaces. The drop size distribution of 
solely Calcium Carbonate Pickering was 60 µm compare to 25 µm (15 wt. % Silicon 
dioxide) at the same particle diameter of 2 µm. Emulsions with only Silicon dioxide 
particles have not been reported.

3.3.5.2 Stabilization by asphaltenes, resins and organic acids 
Asphaltenes are defined in terms of their solubility in different solvents. They are 
immiscible in n-alkanes but they are miscible with benzene and toluene. The general 
composition of an asphaltene consists of polynuclear aromatic rings, aliphatic rings and 
chains, heteroatoms such as nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur and different metals (vanadium, 
nickel, iron, etc.). Asphaltenes are an amphiphilic compounds and thus contribute to 
stabilization of crude oil emulsions. The mechanism is not fully understood, but in a review 
by Alvarado (2011) it is proposed that the rigidity of the asphaltene cross-linked film on 
the interface causes high emulsion stability rather than providing a steric barrier which has 
a smaller thickness.  
The combination of asphaltenes and particles creates a synergetic effect in emulsion 
stabilization and increases with increased concentration of asphaltenes. However, a reverse 
effect is observed when particles are not present. A proposed explanation states that it is 
only a small fraction of the total asphaltenes which are involved in the coating of the 
droplets in the emulsion. Besides at low concentrations this effect is probably not as 
pronounced in comparison to higher concentrations of asphaltenes on emulsion 
destabilization (Alvarado, 2011).  
Resins are in structure similar to surface-active agents with one end being 
hydrophilic with a polar functional group and a hydrophobic consisting of alkyl chains. 
Resins do not tend to act on their own and have a tendency to aggregate with asphaltenes 
and speed up the adsorption kinetics. The resin-solvated asphaltenic aggregates can adsorb 
quicker than pure asphaltenes and stabilize the emulsion further. However, resins will 
reduce the stability of the emulsion if the resins become the primary adsorbent as they are 
thermodynamically favorable and will replace the adsorbed asphaltenes (Sullivan, 2002; 
Alvarado, 2011). 
Organic acids such as naphthenic acid is a naturally occurring fatty acid in the crude 
oil and is reported not the stabilize emulsion alone (Langevin, 2004) although they are 
capable to form interfacially active complexes with cations from the aqueous phase. There 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
  29 
 
are two classes of naphthenates with regards to cations in emulsion stabilization. One is 
emulsion-like and the second one is solid-like (Wang 2010). 
3.3.5.3 Chain length of alkanes

3.3.5.4 Emulsion stabilization by proteins 
Proteins being amphiphilic molecules are useful emulsifying agents in the food 
industry of o/w emulsions (Kulmyrzaev, 2000; Huang, 2011; Zaidel 2013).  Modified 
pectin enzyme derived from citrus pectin is frequently reported across the recent studies 
concerning emulsion stability in the food sector (Huang, 2011; Zaidel 2013).  
The factors affecting emulsion stability are dependent on the type of stabilizer, 
emulsifier and environmental factors such as pH, temperature and ionic strength (i.e. 
salinity) as well as homogenization conditions (pressure, heating) (Chanamai, 2002; Ciron, 
2010; Demetriades, 1998; Dickinson, 1997). Schmitt (1988) proposed that addition of 
polysaccharides creates a protein-polysaccharide complex which coats the emulsified 
droplets and increases the repulsion between the particles, thus stabilizing the system. 
Polysaccharides are reported to be used as mobility-control buffers for permeability 
reduction and/or increased viscosity in polymer flooding (Donaldson, 1985). 
Polysaccharides in combination with proteins/enzymes could be used to synergistically 
increase the sweep area due to the polysaccharide polymer characteristics while at the same 
time mobilizing oil through emulsion formations and stabilize proteins during EEOR.

4 Objective of this study 
Experimental study of formation of emulsions in brine-oil-enzyme systems with and 
without solid fines was carried out. The study was directed to analysis of the possible 
mechanisms governing the enzymatic enhanced oil recovery (EEOR). Formation of 
emulsions may serve for mobilization of the separate oil droplets, resulting in additional 
recovery and thus overcoming the so called snap-off effect. 
The main aim of this study is to investigate different effects on enzyme product 
emulsion stability, formation, droplet size distribution and factors affecting them. Majority 
of the previous studies used commercial enzyme products with unknown compositions 
which made it challenging to determine whether the described actions were due to the 
enzyme or the stabilizers. Therefore 15 enzyme products and one pure enzyme of known 
compositions from Novozymes A/S were used along with a protein for comparison. 
Common surfactants were used to establish the basic differences between surfactants and 
enzymes. 
4.1  Laboratory work 
The laboratory work that was carried out is based on bottles tests combined with a 
microscope and adsorption-desorption experiment. These were conducted in two phases. 
Phase one describes the initial screening of 4 selected enzyme products based on their 
performance in wettability alteration with Calcium Carbonate (Khusainova, 2015) and 
different factors affecting enzyme product NS 81249 and a protein. In phase two, all the 
enzyme products and commercial products were screened for direct comparison.  
In general a low magnification optical microscope was used for phase one at DTU 
CERE, whereas, a higher magnification optical microscope (50x magnification) was used 
in the second phase of the study due to its previous unavailability at DTU CEN.

5.2.3.2 Sampling 
Samples containing fines were centrifuged at 400 rpm for 10 minutes. If visible 
separation has not occurred, the samples were centrifuged again. If a large portion of the 
fines resigned at the oil-water interface a needle was used to stir the oil-water interface and 
then the sample was centrifuged the second time. 
Once separation of fines from the water phase was successful, a syringe with a needle 
was used to sample at least 0.2 mL (the Bradford assay requires 0.1 mL of a sample). Small 
portion of the sample was disposed of in order to remove any impurities such as oil/fines 
resigning on the tip of the needle. The samples were then transferred from the syringe into 
1.5 mL Eppendorf tube. The remaining sea water with any left enzyme was removed as 
much as was possible and 3 mL of fresh North Sea water without the enzyme product was 
added. The samples were yet again shaken via vortex shaker in the same procedure 
including the sampling of the desorbed enzymes.

6.2.3 N-decane as model oil comparison with North Sea oil 
The following experiment was set-up in order to confirm the literature proposition of 
using n-decane as model oil by dilution of North Sea oil and examining the amount of 
created emulsion. Figure 21 shows the samples along with the microscope pictures of them.

phase consisted of North Sea oil diluted by n-decane at different ratios. The samples were 
shaken in a similar manner as in the previous case at the selected 400 rpm and room 
temperature for 1 hour. The 15 mL falcon tubes were chosen for easier visual observation 
of the amounts of emulsions. The microscope pictures were taken of samples which were 
scaled up back to 50 mL falcon tubes for easier sampling showing the same trend. 
The results show that the amount of emulsion with different dilution of North Sea oil

brighter with an increasing amount of n-decane. This could signify that lighter components 
of the crude oil are taking part in the emulsion creation which has been previously 
proposed by Zahid et al., (2012). He observed greater formation of emulsions with crudes 
containing higher percentage of lighter components. The microscope pictures do not show 
any significant differences. Therefore n-decane is a valid representative model oil for North 
Sea oil in creating emulsions and was chosen as model oil for the study of creation of 
emulsions in presence of enzymes.

6.2.6 Effect of alkane chain length on emulsion creation 
Zahid et al. (2012) reported that the light fraction of crude oils was the main fraction 
playing key role in the emulsification. Furthermore, Buscall (1979) proposed that the 
alkane chain length does not have any significant impact on emulsification, see the 
subsection Chain length of alkanes in section Emulsions for further information on page 
29. 
Therefore the effect of chain length on the emulsification was investigated using 
BSA, enzyme products NS 81249 and NS 81253. 1 % of the enzyme product and 
equivalent concentration of BSA was used using sea water along with n-decane,  
1:1 n-decane:n-heptane and heptane for BSA and NS 81249. NS 81253 was investigated 
only with n-decane and n-heptane. 
Figure 25 and Figure 26 show the comparison of NS 81249, NS 81253 and BSA. 
Both BSA and NS 81249 formed the same droplet sizes with both n-decane and n-heptane, 
whereas NS 81251 formed considerably more droplets with n-decane than with n-heptane. 
However, NS 81249, NS 81251 and BSA formed more emulsion with n-decane. Small 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
  56 
 
droplets (< 5 µm) were observed in n-heptane with NS 81249 and BSA as well as in the  
n-heptane:n-decane samples. This concludes that although that n-decane caused more 
stable and produced a large amount of emulsion, it is n-heptane due to which small droplets 
are being created.  NS 81251 with n-heptane produced so little amount of emulsion 
between the oil-water interface that it was difficult to sample. Therefore this could explain 
why this trend was not observed.  
Overall, in comparison, the heavier part of light fraction of crude oil is taking part in 
mainly stabilizing and increasing the volume of the emulsion, whereas the light part of the 
light fraction allows for formation of small droplets.

6.3 Emulsions with fines 
Fines are naturally present in the reservoirs or form during waterflooding due to 
differences in salinities. Therefore the formation and stability of Pickering emulsions (see 
page 26) was investigated along with the addition of enzymes to see their effect on the 
Pickering emulsions. Calcium carbonate and Silicon dioxide were chosen as the major 
constituents of chalk and sandstone reservoirs respectively. 
 
6.3.1 Effect of addition of fines without enzyme/protein 
In the first stage, blank samples of only 2 g of Calcium carbonate and Silicon dioxide 
with sea water and n-decane were prepared using the standard procedure described in 
Materials and methods. Figure 29 shows that Calcium carbonate formed a suspension in the 
water phase with fines being deposited as well at the oil-water interface.  
Silicon dioxide formed a very stable and a large amount of emulsion. It was observed 
that the fines of Silicon dioxide were covering the surfaces of the droplets which promoted 
their stabilization. It is not surprising that the larger fines were dropping out of the 
emulsion back into the water phase due to gravity and only the smallest of the fines stayed 
in the emulsion. This could explain why Calcium carbonate has not formed an emulsion 
but rather a suspension since the order of the sizes of Calcium carbonate and Silicon 
dioxide were ≤30 μm and 0.5-10 μm (approx. 80% between 1-5 μm) respectively. 
Unfortunately it was not possible to use smaller particles of Calcium carbonate to prove 
that this effect is due to the particle sizes.

6.3.4 Effect of pure enzyme vs. enzyme product together with fines 
Figure 34 shows the comparison of the water, oil phase and emulsion of the systems 
enzyme product/pure NS 81249-n-decane, sea water and fines (Calcium carbonate or 
Silicon dioxide). This was done to investigate if there would be any visible differences 
between the pure and the enzyme product when applied together with fines.  
The pure NS 81249 (P81249) has produced a considerably smaller amount of 
emulsion than the product without the addition of fines. However, the opposite was 
observed with the addition of fines to the pure NS 81249, at least in case of Calcium 
carbonate which for its blank only formed a suspension whereas Silicon dioxide already 
formed an emulsion without any emulsifier. Therefore based on Calcium carbonate, fines 
do promote emulsion creation and stabilization by enzyme adsorption on fines’ surface and 
acting as surface active agents in a synergetic way with the fines.  
Both Calcium carbonate and Silicon dioxide particles covered the surface of the 
droplets to a lesser degree for the pure enzyme in contrast to the product. Furthermore, the 
droplet size distribution was less uniform when the pure enzyme was applied. The pure 
enzymes with fines formed many large droplets (Silicon dioxide: 500 µm and Calcium 
Carbonate: 800 µm) and small droplets (Silicon dioxide: 150 µm and Calcium Carbonate: 
200 µm). In contrast the enzyme product with fines formed droplets of Silicon dioxide: 
200 – 300 µm and for Calcium Carbonate: 200 µm.  
Therefore regardless of the type of fines, the enzyme product formed similarly sized 
droplets whereas the pure enzyme promoted formation of larger droplets. In terms of 
thermodynamics, the larger the droplet, the less stable the droplets are. Thus the effect of 
the enzyme stabilizer is actually causing destabilization of emulsion in the enzyme product 
and does not allow for formation of larger droplets which could be stabilized by the system 
fines-pure enzyme.

Samples from the first stage were observed for stability, i.e. the decrease in the

product as each destabilized over time at different rates. It should be noted that the product

6.5 Adsorption-desorption experiment 
The adsorption-desorption experiment was set up in collaboration with the ongoing 
experimental results from Khusainova et al., (to be published). Khusainova conducted 
penetration experiments with chalk and sandstone of the same lengths in terms of the total 
surface area. In both cases it was found that the product enzyme NS 81249 penetrated 
through the samples with only a small recovery of the enzyme. This was accounted to the 
adsorption of the enzyme on the surface of the mineral based on the previous work and 
clarified with the results from this works experiment on fines. When creating emulsions 
with fines it was found that the enzymes adsorb on the fines and in turn synergistically 
stabilize the emulsions. Therefore an experiment was set up in which the adsorption and 
desorption of the enzyme would be investigated with and without the presence of oil.

7 Discussion 
This chapter discusses the overall findings described in the previous chapter and 
concludes the overall findings for the future work. 
7.1 Initial screening and stability of enzymes 
The enzymes were firstly investigated stating with their stability and obtaining the 
qualitative understanding of the appearance of the four selected enzymes based on work of 
adhesion experiments by Khusainova et al., (2015) and surfactant SDS and protein BSA for 
comparison. The system of SW-n-decane(model oil)-emulsifier showed no significant 
stability changes due to the conditions of preparation and storage at elevated temperatures 
for one week at 50oC followed by room temperature. Except for lipase NS 81249 which 
seemed to increase the rate of destabilization due to the temperature drop. In the first stage 
the same system was investigated with an ester 1 wt. % ethyl decanoate to examine the 
catalytic effect of enzymes. Nasiri (2011) has shown that esterase group was capable of 
hydrolysis of the ester and thus produced an acid and alcohol. These compounds are used 
as stabilizers and co-surfactants correspondingly. Esters are natural demulsifiers. Therefore 
since the amount of created emulsion was considerably lower, it was concluded that the 
effect of demulsification was more pronounced than the stabilizing effects. Samples with 
North Sea oil and no ester were examined showing in the case of BSA a net structure and 
for the enzyme generally no emulsion since the oil-water interface was at 50% volume. 
Unfortunately, the samples were irreproducible using the initial method of 300 rpm. 
Therefore the way of preparation of the samples was examined. 
7.1.1 Emulsion preparation 
Both hand-shaking and centrifuge were found to be irreproducible. For that reason 
the impact of different rpms on emulsion formation were explored using a shaker at room 
temperatures. BSA/NS 81249-n-decane-SW were used at 200,300,400 and 500 rpm for 
1 hour of shaking. The results showed that an increase in rpm caused the emulsions to 
become tighter (smaller and more closely distributed droplets) and the amount of formed 
emulsion was dependent on the nature/type of the protein/enzyme. This was seen for BSA 
in case of 400 rpm with smaller amounts of emulsion formed in comparison to 300 and 500 
rpm for triplicates, whereas product enzyme NS 81249 showed increasing amounts of 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
 
  80 
 
emulsion with an increase in rpm. Therefore, since the primary objective of this study was 
enzymes, 400 rpm was selected as 500 rpm caused vibrations with full load of samples 
which could affect the reproducibility. 
7.1.2 N-alkane as model oil 
The model oil selected for this study was based on studies by Nasiri (2011) and 
Khusainova et al. (2015) with the Proteases and NS 81249 respectively. An experiment 
was set-up using different ratios of diluted North Sea oil with n-decane to confirm that this 
oil is representative for emulsion formation and stability study. The results showed that the 
same amount of emulsion was formed at each ratio. Interestingly, the formed emulsions 
became lighter with increasing amounts of n-decane in the system. This indicated that the 
n-decane was taking part in the emulsion formation. A similar observation was made by 
Zahid et al. (2012) who observed from SARA analysis of Middle East oil that the emulsion 
formation was related to lighter components of the crudes.  
Thus in the second step the impact of the alkane chain length on emulsion 
stabilization and formation was investigated using n-decane and n-heptane. N-decane 
caused more stable and formed larger amount of emulsion, whereas n-heptane caused the 
formation of small droplets. Generally, the heavier part of the light fraction (n-decane) of 
crude oil was responsible for the stabilization and formation of the emulsion in terms of 
volume. Whereas the light part of the light fraction (n-heptane) allowed formation of small 
droplets. This effect of light fraction on emulsions was confirmed for n-decane in the 
dilution experiment of North Sea oil as it showed even more o/w droplet structures of  
10 – 30 µm.  
Dioctyl sodium sulfosuccinate (AOT) surfactant was used to form a microemulsion 
system with n-decane/n-heptane and sea water. The same was observed for both n-decane 
and n-heptane as previously and the stabilization effect of n-decane on the emulsion was 
more prominent due to the general low stability of both n-decane and n-heptane emulsions. 
7.1.3 Stabilizer vs. pure enzyme 
Feng et al., (2009) and others have used commercial enzyme products in successful 
pilot projects and proposed mechanisms by which enzymes enhance oil recovery. 
However, the composition of these products are unknown. Therefore the improvements in 
==PAGE:96==
 
 
 
  81 
 
oil recovery might as well be due to the stabilizers and other components contained in these 
commercial mixtures. Novozymes A/S have provided the NS 81249 as both the pure 
enzyme and the enzyme product consisting of 2 – 5 wt. % enzyme, 25 – 30 wt. % 
stabilizer, 63 – 75 wt. % distilled water and 0.2 wt. % biocide (Novozymes A/S, private 
communication). Therefore it was possible to investigate the separate effects of the pure 
enzyme, enzyme product and the stabilizer with both n-decane and North Sea oil. 
The results showed that the stabilizer (propylene glycol) did synergistically enhance 
the amount of formed emulsion in the enzyme product without altering the droplet size 
distribution. However, it caused a decrease in the formation of o/w droplets in the water 
phase. This effect was not observed for North Sea oil, but on the other hand it was 
observed that the pure enzyme formed larger w/o droplets in the oil phase which seemed to 
be inhibited in the enzyme product by the stabilizer. All three, stabilizer, pure and product 
enzyme created w/o structures in the oil phase of the North Sea oil. 
7.2 Fines 
7.2.1 Calcium Carbonate and Silicon dioxide blank samples 
Calcium carbonate (≤30 μm) and Silicon dioxide particles of 0.5-10 μm (approx. 
80% between 1-5 μm) were used to investigate the effect of fines on the stability and 
formation of emulsions. Both are representative materials for chalk and sandstone 
reservoirs respectively. 
7.2.2 Addition of NS 81249/BSA 
The blank samples without an enzyme product produced a suspension and an o/w 
emulsion with Calcium Carbonate and Silicon dioxide particles correspondingly, whereas 
with the addition of NS 81249 or BSA, both Calcium Carbonate and Silicon dioxide 
formed a more stable o/w emulsion. NS 81249 formed a more tight emulsion (increase in 
number of droplets) with Calcium Carbonate. The Silicon dioxide particles in case of BSA 
and NS 81249 were covering the droplets more intensely in comparison to Calcium 
Carbonate.  A stronger adsorption of Silicon dioxide fines on droplets was observed for NS 
81249 than for BSA. One should keep in mind the size difference of Calcium Carbonate vs. 
Silicon dioxide as Calcium Carbonate particles were considerably larger. It was observed 
that when the sample was moved around the fines were especially in case of Calcium 
==PAGE:97==
 
 
 
  82 
 
Carbonate dropping out of the emulsion due to gravity. These findings were supported with 
the adsorption-desorption experiment which showed that Silicon dioxide adsorbs more 
NS 81249 than Calcium Carbonate. Unfortunately, these values were not adjusted for the 
total surface area of the particles.  
Liu (2004) reported that although that silicon oils are strong denaturants for protein 
tertiary structure and thus should in theory reduce their activity. Their experiments with 
water-silicon oil emulsions revealed no change or in some cases an improvement in the 
enzyme activity. This indicates that enzymes could be stabilized with micro sized fines 
under reservoir conditions to prevent denaturation and decrease in enzyme activity.   
7.2.2.1 Increase in concentration of NS 81249/BSA with fines 
Samples without the fines were prepared at 1 and 5 wt. % of BSA and NS 81249 in 
sea water with n-decane. These samples showed that higher concentration produced more 
emulsion with BSA but the same amount with NS 81249. Thus indicating that 1 wt. % for 
emulsification was substantial in case of NS 81249. Higher concentration of 
enzyme/protein formed on top of the regular sized droplets seen in 1 wt. % also < 5 µm 
droplets. Overall these samples at higher concentration without fines showed similar results 
independent of enzyme/protein as an emulsifier. 
However, with an increase in concentrations of NS 81249 and BSA in presence of 
Calcium Carbonate fines, the droplets increased in size, thus showing a negative tendency. 
NS 81249 showed a more uniform drop size distribution unlike BSA with Calcium 
Carbonate, whereas BSA showed no difference in droplet sizes with Silicon dioxide 
particles at elevated concentrations. An increase in the NS 81249 concentration without 
fines did not change the amount of formed emulsion as stated above. However, it did cause 
a decrease in the amount of formed emulsion with Silicon dioxide particles. 
Therefore fines do promote emulsion formation and stabilization by enzyme 
adsorption on fines’ surface causing a synergetic effect. 
7.2.2.2 Fines with pure enzyme NS 81249 and NS 81249 enzyme product 
The pure enzyme NS 81249 of equivalent 1 wt. % concentration and n-decane were 
used with both Calcium Carbonate and Silicon dioxide fines. The pure enzyme caused the 
==PAGE:98==
 
 
 
  83 
 
fines to adsorb on the droplets to a lesser degree in comparison to the enzyme product. It 
also caused the droplets size distribution to be less uniform creating many large and small 
droplets with both fines with the enzyme product forming only small droplets. This effect 
could be assigned to the absence of the stabilizer. Since in terms of thermodynamics the 
smaller the droplet is, the more stable it is. Therefore the presence of the enzyme stabilizer 
caused destabilization of large droplets only allowing the formation of small droplets.

7.4 Adsorption/desorption of NS 81249 on fines 
The adsorption/desorption of NS 81249 with Silicon dioxide and Calcium Carbonate 
fines were conducted in response to the visual observation of a greater adsorption of fines 
on w/o droplets in the presence of the enzyme product NS 82149. The results from the 
adsorption experiment are in Table 11 in section Results on page 78. 
The results concluded that adsorption of Lipase NS 81249 on fines was independent 
of the presence of oil as it was found that only around 15 % of the enzyme got adsorbed 
into the oil phase through w/o droplets or at the oil-water interface. The amount of 
adsorbed enzyme was correlated across all the combinations of concentrations of enzyme 
or the amounts of particles. Thus the adsorption was concentration independent as well as 
independent of the amount of fines present. Nasiri (2011) showed the same relationship 
with Esterase enzyme product. 
The Calcium Carbonate showed a very low adsorption of the enzyme overall, 
whereas the adsorption on Silicon dioxide was stronger which is accounted to the greater 
surface area. Khusainova et al., (to be published) performed penetration tests with 
NS 81249 with Chalk and Sandstone of the same length in terms of the surface area. The 
recoveries of the enzyme from these experiments were up to 40-90 % of injected PV. 
Therefore it can be concluded that the mechanism through which the enzymes adsorb in the 
core is mainly due to other factors rather than pure adsorption as was performed in this 
experiment. Furthermore, the desorption experimental part in the presence and absence of 
oil showed that during a static experiment, the enzymes tend to adsorb further when a 
North Sea water is added without the enzyme. The ionic strength of the North Sea water 
ought to be strong enough to desorb the enzymes from the surface according to Novozymes 
A/S (private communication).

8 Conclusion 
The goal of this thesis was to investigate the application of enzymes as emulsifying agents. 
This is important as formation of emulsion can be one of the mechanisms of EEOR. Static 
experimental procedure was developed along the experimental work. The thesis covers the 
stability, emulsion formation and microemulsion screening of 15 different enzymes from 
Novozymes A/S with known compositions along with two commercial products 
(Apollo-Greenzyme and EOR-Zymax), and with surfactants and protein bovine serum albumin 
(BSA) for comparison. 
 Initial screening showed that emulsion formation is dependent on the method of 
preparation of emulsions. A method of emulsion preparation was developed using a shaker for 
large sample screenings. The stability of four selected enzyme products was investigated with 
n-decane, n-decane+1 wt. % ester and North Sea oil as model oils. Samples with n-decane 
produced stable emulsions lasting for minimum one month. N-decane with ester showed that the 
destabilization effect of the ester is more pronounced than the stabilization effect of the products 
of the enzymatic hydrolysis of the ester. North Sea oil samples produced almost no emulsions 
across all the groups of enzymes. 
Experiments of increased concentration of the enzyme product NS 81249 from 1 to 
5 wt. % showed an increase in creation of w/o micro droplets. The effect of n-decane and 
n-heptane showed greater stabilization and formation of smaller droplets respectively. The 
experiment on representativeness of n-decane as model oil revealed that the same amount of 
emulsion was formed with diluted samples of North Sea oil with n-decane. The greater the 
dilution was, the more see-through emulsions were formed. Thus this experiment indicated that 
the lighter fractions of crude oils take part in the emulsion formation. The presence of a stabilizer 
caused enhancement of the emulsion volume without altering the droplet sizes. However, it 
inhibited the creation of o/w micro droplets in the water phase with the model oil, whereas it 
inhibited the creation of w/o droplets in the oil phase with North Sea oil. 
The effect of addition of Calcium Carbonate and Silicon dioxide to NS 81249 and model 
oil showed an increase in formation and stabilization of the emulsions with the Silicon dioxide 
fines adsorbing on the w/o droplets in the oil phase to a larger extent. This was confirmed by the 
==PAGE:107==
 
 
 
  92 
 
adsorption-desorption experiment with NS 81249. Whereas the Calcium Carbonate promoted 
formation of tighter emulsions, possibly due to lower adsorption of fines on w/o droplets. A 
negative tendency on the droplet size and emulsion formation with fines was observed with 
increased concentrations of NS 81249 from 1 to 5 wt. % for Calcium Carbonate and Silicon 
dioxide respectively. The stabilizer contained in the product enzymes inhibited the creation of 
larger droplets which were seen with pure enzyme NS 81249. The overall synergetic stabilization 
of emulsions with fines was assigned to the adsorbed enzymes on fines which in turn were 
adsorbed on w/o droplets. 
It was found that out of all the screened enzymes, Lipases/Esterases and Oxidoreductases 
groups performed the best in emulsion formation and stability with Proteases at the other end of 
the spectrum. Furthermore, enzymes across the groups formed similar w/o droplet sized 
distribution in the emulsion at the oil-water interface. Turbidity of the water phase with n-decane 
was directly correlated to the amount of micro sized o/w droplets and neither of the two 
commercial products demonstrated any similar behavior to the enzyme products from 
Novozymes A/S or BSA with. 
Overall North Sea oil formed initially no emulsion with the enzyme products and only 
formed w/o micro droplet ‘structures’. However, re-shaking of only NS 81249 and NS 81251 
with North Sea oil (based on best and worst performing enzyme with n-decane) after 3 months 
formed considerably large amounts of w/o emulsion with ‘tangled structures’. NS 81251 
outperformed NS 81249 in this case. Thus incubation period should be introduced to 
coreflooding tests to test the idea that emulsion creation is one of the mechanisms for EEOR.
