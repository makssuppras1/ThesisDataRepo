Abstract 
Next to water, tea is the most consumed beverage in the world. The infusions from the tea 
plant, Camellia sinensis is known to have a high content of aluminium, a metal that has shown 
neurotoxic effects. To investigate if tea drinking expose people in Denmark to excess amounts 
of aluminium and two other metals with known neurotoxic effects, manganese and lead, 40 
different black and green teas were selected for investigation based on a customer survey by 
the company Growth for Knowledge (GfK).[1]. Due to registration methods, these teas 
represent teas acquired in Danish Supermarkets only. The 40 samples represent 29% (in grams) 
of purchased tea registered by GfK. Three samples of Rooibos tea from the plant Aspalathus 
linearis were included for comparison. The samples were analyzed as Made tea using 
homogenization, digestion and Induced Coupled Plasma Mass Spectroscopy (ICPMS). One 
tea, Silver Rose Earl Grey tea was analyzed as Brewed tea with different scenarios for brewing 
set-ups, using ICPMS. The analyses were within literature values. Exposure assessment and 
Risk Characterization were performed based on estimates of exposure from European Food 
Safety Authority (EFSA) [2], [3], [4]  and DTU Food [5] and hazard limits given by EFSA. To 
calculate exposure and risk of 18-75 years Danes from tea drinking, three scenarios of defining 
a tea drinker were set: ≥ 50 mL/day, ≥ 100 mL/day and ≥ 200 mL/day using as well the mean 
as the 95th percentile. The data was provided by DTU Food. [6]. The result of these calculations 
were, that neither manganese, nor lead exposure would justify a warning against tea drinking, 
whereas tea drinking contributed significantly to the aluminium burden. The exposure from 
other sources, the volume of tea and the brewing methods all have an influence on whether a 
tolerably weekly intake (TWI) of Al is exceeded.

assigned a reference.

Introduction 
After water being the most consumed beverage, comes tea. It is common to refer to the name 
tea for infusions of many different plants, but different botanical species differ in chemical 
composition, and as such from a scientific point of view should be handled in accordance with 
this. The tea plant Camellia sinensis and the drink made from its buds and leaves has been 
known from 1700 B.C. and origins in China.[7]. It is known as a crop with a high content of 
aluminium, a metal that is toxic for most plants, and in humans is known to have adverse effects 
on the nervous system. Two other metals present in soil, manganese and lead are also known 
to have neurotoxic effect. Lead is a metal that should not be present in biological systems, 
manganese is an essential trace metal, but has shown neurotoxic effects in some cases. A 
customer survey by the company Growth for Knowledge has shown (2017 data), that the 
greatest volume of tea ~88%, whether it be from the Camellia sinensis plant or not is purchased 
in Danish supermarkets. Teas purchased in supermarkets can be identified in most cases with 
mainly the help of European Article numbers (EAN), while teas purchased in special shops 
cannot. ICPMS is the analytical tool and a customer survey [6] provides the data for amount 
of tea consumed by the Danish population.[6]. Based upon this survey, the measured 
concentrations and the estimated exposure from foodstuff given by authorities, the Exposure 
Assessment is calculated and a Risk Characterization proposed. Being a foodstuff, the exposure 
route of metals from tea is oral. As adverse effects from the metals were often first recognized 
from other exposure routes, these are of value to examine with regard to the nature of the 
adverse effects. 
Aim of study 
The aim of this study performed as a master thesis at DTU Food is to select teas from the 
Camellia sinensis plant with a content of > 90% that are representative to the intake by Danish 
consumers, buying their tea in supermarkets. Thereafter analyze the teas for content of the 
metals aluminium, manganese and lead using ICPMS for as well Made teas as for one Brewed 
tea, brewed in different doses, using different brewing times. Finally perform Exposure 
Assessment and Risk Characterization of the adult Danish population (age 18-75 years) with 
regard to the exposure of the found metal contents in the Brewed tea as a representative. In 
addition, give an overview of the three metals’ interaction with biological systems with focus 
on neurotoxic effects, and get knowledge of these effects by including special cases caused by 
exposure routes different from oral. 
Tea  
Defining tea 
Camellia sinensis is the botanical name of the plant from which the buds and new leaves are 
the ingredients in the beverage tea. A beverage that holds the title of being the most consumed 
worldwide after water.[8]. It is consumed in many different societies and probably by all social 
classes. It is used as a daily ordinary drink as well as a sophisticated drink to which ceremonies 
==PAGE:8==
 
8
are attributed. Much research is made to map the influence of intake of tea on human health, 
beneficial and/or detrimental. Analyzing tea for chemical content is performed on the different 
parts of the fresh plant, on Made tea, i.e. dried tea leaves either in loose weight or in tea bags 
and on Brewed tea (infusion). The intake of tea by humans is Brewed tea. 
Names such as black tea, oolong tea, green tea and white tea refer to the way of harvesting and 
the treatment (fermentation) afterwards. These all are based on Camellia sinensis.[8] [9] [10]. 
Some teas are a mix of the types, others are flavored with other ingredients such as for instance 
bergamot (~3%) in tea referred to as Earl Grey. Many of the flavored teas have a content of 
Camellia sinensis over 95%. In the western world, mainly black tea is popular, whereas green 
tea is more popular in China. White tea and oolong tea are known in Denmark, but are not 
supermarket standard teas.  
 
However, the word tea has commercially extended to cover beverages made from other plants 
as well. These teas are sometimes, but not always referred to as herbal teas or infusions. In 
herbal tea, different parts of the plant are used depending on the type of plant, for instance the 
root (ginger), the flower (chamomile) but also seeds (fenugreek seeds), fruit (hibiscus) and 
leaves (peppermint).[11].  
 
Rooibos tea is not a Camellia sinensis tea, but is produced from a plant with the botanical name 
Aspalathus linearis. In opposition to Camellia sinensis it does not contain caffeine which is 
seen as a benefit by some consumers. Its habitat is in an area north of Cape Town where the 
soil is acidic.[12]. 
 
Origin, present farming regions/conditions. 
Camellia sinensis was first reported in China, in the southwestern province of Yunnan. 
Consumption of tea has been referred to textually in the 1st century B.C., its physical evidence 
can be dated to the northern Chinese Song Dynasty (960-1127 A.C.). [7], but the tea plant may 
have been domesticated in the province of Assam in India very early on, yet not used for tea 
breeding until the beginning of the 19th century [13]. Camellia sinensis has recently been 
suggested to include three main lines [13]: China type tea (Camellia sinensis var. sinensis, that 
is narrow-leaved. [14],[15, pp. 277–309]), Chinese Assam type tea and Indian Assam type tea 
(Camellia sinensis var. assamica) that is broad-leaved.[14],[15, pp. 277–309]. According to a 
genetic study [13], the China type tea and the Assam type tea diverged some 22000 years ago. 
Then around 2770 B.C. the Assam type tea split into the Chinese Assam type and the Indian 
Assam type.  
 
Today the variations of the tea plant are breed on most continents in more than fifty countries. 
[13]. The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) offers data about 
among other things crops. The tea production is given in acreage (ha), yield (t) and export (t). 
Data is annually distributed on the UN Food and Agriculture (FAO) website [16]. Data for 
2017 can be seen in Appendix A. For some developing countries, tea production and export 
have become important by giving work and income to many poor people and by increasing the 
Gross Domestic Product. For Kenya, the income of tea production provides 26% of the export 
==PAGE:9==
 
9
earnings as per 2005. [17]. In 2005, Kenya is number four when it comes to yield, but is the 
top exporter of tea to other countries.[17]. 
 
The zone where tea is farmed has been reported to extend from 49⁰ N to 33⁰ S. The plant is 
grown in different altitudes ranging from sea level to 2700 m with a significant range in 
temperature as consequence. [15, pp. 277–309]. The tea plant prefers acidic soil with pH 
between 5.0 and 5.6. It can tolerate lower pH, but not higher. [18]. Soil composition can be of 
a wide variety such as volcanic ash, alluvial soil, drained peat and sedimentary soils. [18]. It 
requires high air humidity and plenty of water. [18]. The air temperature should be between 18 
⁰C and 30 ⁰C, soil temperatures between 20 ⁰C and 25 ⁰C [18]. Acidification is augmented if 
N-fertilizers are used. [10]. In acidic soil and in the presence of inorganic phosphor compounds, 
some elements are becoming soluble, which increases uptake by and accumulation in the plant. 
The content of the elements varies in different parts of the plant, the young leaves having the 
less content. 
 
When harvesting it is general practice to use only the two youngest leaves plus the bud per 
harvest. Traditionally, harvesting is done manually, although coarser plucking methods are also 
used. Plucking intervals are 7-15 days in the growing season. The quality of the tea declines 
with using the coarser plugging methods instead of the selective plucking by hand and with 
extending the intervals between the plucking. [19]. 
 
The processing of black tea and green tea has an impact on chemical content of the teas as the 
early steaming process (before rolling) in green tea manufacturing kills the enzyme polyphenol 
oxidase that is responsible for the conversion (oxidation) of polyphenols into theaflavins and 
thearubigins in a condensation process. A subclass of polyphenols is flavoids and a subclass of 
the flavoids is catechins. [8]. The chemical composition of tea is considered to have an impact 
on the bioavailability (absorption) of metals. [20].

however is the same. According to Cabrera et al.,[8] the content of tea has a mean composition

Distribution to consumers 
Many papers have been published on tea growing and the possible hazards or benefits from 
drinking tea. In some of these studies,   origin of a specific tea is known. Some studies include 
analysis of elements in soil, fresh parts of the plant and Made tea. The location can give an 
indication of whether environmental contamination is likely to be present. The harvesting and 
fermentation methods can be inspected [21].[10]. How and from where tea reaches the 
consumers is often obscure. This can easily be proved, if one consults the internet, or dazzles 
down the walks of a supermarket or enter a teashop: if origin is available, it is for a greater 
area, not for a single plantation, batches are not available and packing place and conditions are 
not available either. Tea is a processed crop that does not allow the consumer to wash away 
soil and dust from the product. Soil and dust can contribute to the intake of elements like Al, 
Pb and Mn. [10]. 
Three metals with neurotoxic effect 
Adverse effect  
Paracelsus, Swiss born physician among other things, in the first half of the 16th century 
claimed: “All things are poisonous and yet there is nothing that is poisonous, it is only the dose 
that makes a thing poisonous”. [22], [23, p. 327].  Half a millennium later, it may be 
==PAGE:11==
 
11
advantageous to extend this statement. Paracelsus used the word poison. We also use the word 
toxic (as in toxicology) and adverse effect. The words cover a substance, which has a negative 
effect on an organism if it get access to it in a not regulated way. Route of exposure, absorption, 
distribution, metabolism and excretion/elimination (ADME) have a great impact on the adverse 
effect of a substance. The bioavailability of a substance means that a substance must be able to 
interact with the organism. Bioavailability depends on different factors such as solubility, 
charge and pH. It is of practical use to talk about acute toxicity versus chronic toxicity, to use 
terms for target organ of the adverse effect (example neurotoxic) and chemical origin of the 
substance: metals, organic molecules, macromolecules. In this thesis, it is the adverse effect of 
metals targeting the nervous system after oral entrance (fetus via the placenta), that is in focus. 
Routes of exposure and the first barrier to overcome at oral entrance. 
Entrance of a substance into the human organism can happen as oral, inhalation, absorption 
through the skin and different types of injection. Obviously, foodstuff is taken in orally (except 
for parenteral nourishing). Foodstuff is to a smaller or greater degree contaminated with metals. 
The metal ions may be a pollutant in the form of dust or water on a plant, or it may have been 
absorbed from soil by the plant. Inside the oral cavity of humans, absorption will happen if not 
directly excreted in feces. Absorption of foodstuff can start in the mouth, the stomach or the 
intestines depending of the nature of the foodstuff.  For absorption in the intestines to take 
place, the substance must pass the intestinal epithelium from the apical side and enter the blood 
stream at the basolateral side. There are different ways to pass the intestinal epithelium. The 
most common are passive diffusion, facilitated diffusion, active transport and pinocytosis. A 
paracellular route (via tight junctions) is also known to exist. For some metals, the type of 
transport is known, for others, it is not.[24],[25]. Figure 1 shows the main routes of excretion 
for as well absorbed as not absorbed substances. For each of the three metals, the toxicokinetics 
are explained in more detail in separate sections. 
==PAGE:12==
 
12

Anatomic barriers after absorption – types and ways of transport. 
Organs and cells are surrounded by barriers. They are of structural importance and of regulating 
importance. In toxicology, these barriers are important in the role of protecting cells and organs 
from the toxic substance. Three types of barriers are listed below: The blood-brain barrier 
(BBB), the placenta and cell membranes. 
The blood-brain barrier (BBB) separates the circulation of the central nervous system (CNS) 
from the rest of the body. The endothelial cells in CNS are different from other endothelial 
==PAGE:13==
 
13
cells in that they have tight junctions and no fenestrations. Glial cells, mainly astrocytes help 
the endothelial cells to keep their structure. Passive transport involves only water and small 
lipophilic molecules that dissolve in the cell membrane. Larger molecules can only pass the 
BBB by transporters. [26]. This holds for transport into the brain as well as transport out of the 
brain. There are several different transport systems available, which are similar to the transport 
systems found in the gastro-intestinal tract. These systems transport essential metals whereas 
non-essential metals use molecular mimicry (the metal binds to a molecule with a ligand for 
which it has a strong affinity, and which resembles an essential molecule). [27].  
The placenta is an organ that develops in the uterus during pregnancy. It could be more correct 
to call it a dynamic and changeable interface between the mother’s blood and the fetus than 
calling it a barrier. [28]. It serves the fetus by providing nutrients and removing wastes and 
keeping the hormonogenesis correct. The placenta will allow metal ions and other xenobiotics 
to enter it and in most cases deliver it to the fetus. [29]. An example of a toxic metal that is 
partly retained in the placenta is cadmium. It is suggested that cadmium binds to 
metallothionein in the placenta for storage. This binding can, however result in a changed 
ability of the placenta to perform its functions. [30]. The uptake into the placenta is not known 
in detail, but it is supposed that foreign metal ions can enter the placenta by binding to vital 
compounds that have a passage, mimicking the essential metal ion. [28]. The human placenta 
is actually two placentae. From day 13-80 of gestation it consists of the early yolk sac placenta 
and from day 21 to term of the chorioallantoric placenta, i.e. there is an overlap in the first 
trimester of pregnancy. This is different in other mammals. [28]. Different types of studies 
have been performed to clarify the transfer of xenobiotics from mother to child. Some studies 
have involved pregnant women who have consented to deliver maternal blood before delivery 
and cord blood samples right after delivery. [31]. [32]. Other studies also included the placenta. 
[29].[33]. These studies were all performed on the day of delivery and did not involve the 
fetus/neonatal. The concern regarding developmental exposure to metals has several causes. 
The BBB develops during pregnancy and is not fully developed until six months after birth. 
This means that some substances that in adults would not reach the brain (cadmium is an 
example) would reach the brain of the fetus. Another concern is that the development of the 
CNS (neurogenesis, neuron migration, development of receptors and transmitters and 
myelination) is ongoing during pregnancy and in the neonatal. [28]. Accumulation of metals is 
yet another concern: the earlier the onset, the heavier the burden late in life. [34]. 
Cell membranes control influx and efflux out of the cell/cell compartment. These membranes 
are composed of a phospholipid bilayer with different types of macromolecules embedded. 
When disrupted, for example by oxidative stress, the membranes do no longer function as 
barriers.

cord and the peripheral nervous system, which branches out from the spinal cord. Important

functions. The more important for this thesis is hippocampus and globus pallides. Emotions

Aluminium 
Introduction  
In earth’s crust aluminium is the most abundant metal. It is mainly found as alumino-silicates, 
phosphates, sulphates, hydroxides and cryolite. In soil, the content varies from seven to more 
than 100 g/kg. Low soil pH increases the solubility of the Al compounds. [2]. It has one stable 
isotope 27Al and exists in the 3+ oxidation state. It is a hard Lewis acid, which prefers hard 
ligands and increases bioavailability at low pH. Some data are given in table 2.

Aluminium production is high as there is a demand for this lightweight metal in cars, airplanes,

Hazard identification 
Toxicokinetics 
Absorption: The absorption of Al is pending of both pH and biologically available ligands. At 
the low pH in the stomach it exists as the Al3+ ion, when pH increases in the small intestine, it 
is converted to the hydroxide, which alters excretion in feces with no absorption. The ligands 
can increase solubility, which favors absorption, as is the case with citrate and lactate among 
others. Ligands with low solubility are likely to be excreted without absorption. Thus, a precise 
value of the bioavailability of Al (the absorption) cannot be given but is estimated to be 0.1 % 
for food and beverages and 0.3% from drinking water with a ten-fold variation.[37]. In a study 
using rats elevated by EFSA 2011, radioactively labelled Al salts showed oral absorption 
between 0.03% (low solubility aluminium hydroxide) and 0.21% (high solubility aluminium 
sulfate). An interspecies difference is that in rats the pH in the intestines is higher than in 
humans. [37]. In a study by Drewett et al. [38], volunteer adults were fed the same controlled 
diet with known Al content (2.665 mg/foodserving 24 hour) and were drinking either a fixed 
volume of mineral water, tea with milk or tea with lemon with a mean Al content of 0.0027 
mg/L, 2.18 mg/L and 3.12 mg/L for a study period of 24 hours. After two weeks, the volunteers 
changed to one of the other liquids and so again after another two weeks. During the three 24 
hours study period, blood samples were taken repeatedly. At no time during the study period, 
the plasma level concentration was different with relation to the type of liquid taken in. Due to 
the frequent measure of blood plasma levels, this fact was not attributed to rapid excretion in 
the urine. The homogenous concentration of Al in blood after intake of the different beverages 
was attributed to the low absorption of Al, suggesting that components in tea (polyphenols or 
other) strongly bind the metal. [38]. Flaten et al. [20] reports,  that in a study where gut effluents 
were sampled from ileostomy patients and analyzed using Proton Nuclear Magnetic Resonance 
(1H-NMR), no significant breakdown of polyphenols coming from tea to lower molecular mass 
phenols took place. [39]. Studies on Al content in urine after tea drinking shows higher volume 
than after drinking water and higher amount of Al excretion after drinking tea. The 
concentration of Al excretion was the same whichever liquid was taken in.[20].[40].  
Distribution: Aluminium is distributed to all tissues but not evenly. The skeleton holds 
approximately half (5-10 mg/kg), the lungs one-fourth (20 mg/kg wet/weight) and the brain 
0.25-0.75 mg/wet weight). The content in soft tissues is in the range of 0.3 to 0.8 mg/kg wet 
weight. [41]. The total Al burden in healthy adults is 30-50 mg/kg body weight (bw). Serum 
contains approximately 1-3 μg/L. In plasma, Al is mainly (89%) transported by transferrin (iron 
binding protein), presumably using the same binding sites as Fe3+ and bound to citrate 
(11%).[42].[37].[41]. Inside cells, Al accumulates in lysosomes, cell nucleus and chromatin. 
[42]. Aluminium reaches the placenta and is found in cord blood. [42]. Breast milk contains Al 
in a concentration range of 0.009–0.380 mg/L.[2].  Aluminium can cross the BBB complexed

to transferrin or citrate and has the possibility to reach all regions of the brain, but from animal 
studies, the highest concentrations are known to be in the hippocampus. [27].[41]. Figure 3 
shows the placement of hippocampus in the human brain. However, the brain is not the favorite 
site for Al to accumulate, as among the soft tissues Al is to a higher extent accumulated in liver 
and kidney. [41]. A research study by Kruger et al. [33] measured the content of Al in the 
placenta membrane and body to 0.5 μg/g, while umbilical cord was measured to 0.3 μg/g (mean

of umbilical cord, Al was detected, indicating that at the time of delivery, Al seemed to be

tissues.[33].

Elimination: Aluminium is excreted in the urine. Only a small part of absorbed Al is excreted 
in feces. Al3+ being a small highly polar ion makes it little compatible with lipophilic bile. In a 
human study 73% of Al citrate administered intravenously was excreted in mainly urine within 
a few days, the remaining 27% not. Years after, a reexamination estimated half-lives of the 
non-excreted, organ stored Al to up to 50 years. [42]. The cause of this variation could be due 
to differences in tissue and organs with respect to withholding the element. [2]. 
Toxicodynamics 
Aluminium shows low acute toxicity.  However, Al is known to accumulate in the brain over 
time and epidemiology studies showed a set of symptoms: balance disorders, memory and 
concentration deficits, fatigue, emotional irritability, and depression. [27]. These symptoms are 
irreversible as in a study of former Al workers. [27]. Aluminium has been connected to 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) as accumulation of Al is found in AD patients’ brains. AD was first 
diagnosed in 1905 by Alois Alzheimer, but no treatment, no cure and no origin of the disease, 
that breaks down the brain in different ways, and ultimately leads to death have been found. 
Epidemiologic studies have shown no connection between eating food rich in Al or using 
antacids and developing AD, while another study showed an increased risk if exposed to high 
Al (>100 μg/L) in water.[27].  An increased level of Al is also found in the brain of Parkinson’s 
disease (PD) patients. [27]. The neurotoxicity can be due to disturbance of enzyme activity.

Hazard characterization 
A study on neurodevelopmental toxicity and neurotoxicity by Golub and Germann from 2001 
[43] used Swiss Webster mice fed on a diet comparable to human diet. This included a lowering 
on Al content as a starting point because mice diet is based on grain and is higher in Al content 
than human diet. This starting point diet contained 7 μg Al/g diet and was given to all the 
female testing mice for one week before they were mated to male mice of the same breed. The 
control group was fed with this diet throughout the study. Three other groups were exposed to 
100, 500 and 1000 μg Al/g diet after the first week administered as Al lactate (a highly soluble 
salt). In all four different doses were given and corresponded to <1, 10, 50, or 100 mg Al/kg 
bw/day for adult mice. The pups once born where kept in the same exposure groups as their 
mothers after weaning at the age of 21 days and were exposed until the age of 35 days. Food 
was given ad libitum, water was given as deionized water in plastic bottles. The females were 
housed two together, the males were housed alone. This was the case for the parents as well as 
for their offspring. The number of animals is not informed. The litters were reduced allowing 
eight pups to live (if possible four of each sex) and take further part in experiments. Only two 
pups from each litter were assigned tasks for the further experiments in this study, a female for 
the Morris maze testing starting at the age of three months (cognitive test) and a male for motor 
testing starting at the age of five months. The remaining six pups from a litter were used for 
other purposes/studies. 
In addition to these tests, physical observations were made. No difference was observed among 
the groups regarding dams completing pregnancy, length of gestation, litter size at birth and 
weight at birth. At weaning 21 days after birth, there was a significant change of weight: the 
Al500 and the Al1000 group weighed less than the control group. This was the case for both 
males and females. After weaning, the difference in weight between the groups became less 
significant. However, females obtained comparable weights except for the Al1000 group, while 
males only obtained comparable weights between the control and the Al100 group. At the end 
of the study, organs were taken from some of the executed animals, and weighed. For the 
Al1000 group organ weight was lower than in the control group. For as well the Al100 group 
as the Al1000 group, brain weight was higher than that of the control group.

Lead has been known since ancient times and has been used for several purposes such as paints, 
water pipes, ceramics, additive to gasoline and in batteries. The Romans used lead acetate as a 
sweetener in wine more than 2000 years ago. [46, pp. 429–431]. The use of lead is being phased 
out whenever and wherever possible due to its extreme toxicity. 
Lead has no biological roles. Being sulfhydryl-reactive, lead interferes with proteins. As the 
sulfhydryl groups in proteins (from the amino acid residue cysteine) are responsible for the 
function of a huge number of enzymes and other proteins, the presence of lead has an adverse 
effect even in trace concentrations. [31]. [30]. 
Hazard identification  
Toxicokinetics 
Absorption: When lead has entered the gastro-intestinal tract, it can either be directly excreted 
in feces with no absorption taking place, or it can pass the endothelium and enter the portal 
vein. Various different ways of the absorption of lead have been described in studies. [48]. The 
percentage of lead that is absorbed depends on several factors. More is absorbed if no or little 
food is present. Children absorb more lead than adults. The homeostasis of iron and calcium 
also makes an impact: deficiency or just low level of these two essential metals will cause 
==PAGE:20==
 
20
higher lead absorption rates. [4]. Once absorbed into circulation, lead has many different 
adverse effects on the organism, of which this thesis focus on its neurodevelopmental toxicity 
(described in later sections). 
Distribution: Lead is distributed in the bloodstream, in soft tissue and in bone. Half-lives are 
approximately 30 days in blood, but several years in bones (10-30 years). [47].[30]. In adults, 
90% of the lead burden is considered to be in the bones, whereas for children the percentage is 
less: approximately 70%.  [4]. During pregnancy and lactation, lead is released from the bones 
together with calcium and enters the circulation. Thus, lead exposure that dates years ago in 
the mother, will affect the fetus and the lactating offspring. A study that followed 62 pregnant 
women during pregnancy showed that lead crosses to the placenta. Maternal blood (drawn from 
venous puncture before delivery) contained 23 μg/L lead compared to cord blood collected 
after delivery with a Pb content of 15.4 μg/L, i.e. a smaller amount than in maternal blood. The 
authors comment that this difference in lead concentration does not qualify the placenta to offer 
any protection as there is no safe level of lead. [31]. Another article by Gundacker et al.[30] 
states that the exact way of distribution into placenta is not known, but passive diffusion has 
been suggested as has been facilitated diffusion using divalent metal transporter 1 
(DMT1) .[30].  The same article describes the metallothionein abundantly present in the 
placenta being able to bind cadmium and thus prevent this toxic metal to be transferred to the 
fetus. Whether the same holds for lead is not clear. It is suggested that lead may bind to 
glutathione and be transported out of the placenta by different protein carriers. [30]. In the 
placenta, lead causes decreased cytochrome oxidase activity. In early pregnancy an antioxidant 
defense is not it place and the fetus at this stage therefore is more vulnerable to lead.[30]. 
Studies showed that children with low iron status had a higher concentration of Pb2+, and that 
the blood concentration of Pb2+ in newborns was related inversely to the iron status of their 
mothers.  
In the bloodstream, Pb is found in erythrocytes mainly. In the plasma part of blood, lead is 
bound to proteins (mainly albumin) and sulfhydryl groups of cysteine residues. [4].  
The capability of lead to cross membranes and barriers to get into cells and organs is only little 
understood. [49]. DMT1 seems to be involved to a certain extent in Pb2+ uptake into cells, 
where Pb2+ mimics Fe2+, but may not represent the main mechanism.[49]. A relationship 
between Ca2+ and Pb2+ is also known from several studies. High levels of Ca2+ results in low 
levels of Pb2+ and vice versa. Pb2+ is supposed to use the Ca2+ channels so abundantly expressed 
in various cells in the body. The ion radius of the two elements are similar in size. Other types 
of Ca2+ mimicry is also known. The intracellular concentrations of Ca2+ are highly regulated 
end ensure that biological functions are carried out in a proper way. The same is not the case 
for Pb2+. Lead is capable of crossing the (BBB) but at a slow rate. [4]. It binds to myelin 
protecting axons and causes myelin membrane fluidity.  
Elimination: Excretion is mainly in the urine by passive diffusion, but absorbed lead is also 
excreted in feces via the bile and counts with one-third. [4].

21
Toxicodynamics 
Lead causes adverse effects wherever it is present in the organism. It has adverse effects in the 
skeletal, circulatory, renal, endocrine, hematopoietic and in the nervous system, where 
especially neurodevelopmental toxicity is of concern. [4]. [49]. An example of a mechanism 
of Pb toxicity is found in connection with the erythrocytes. Here it binds among other proteins 
to a zinc containing metallo-enzyme, delta-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase (ALAD). [4]. This 
enzyme is responsive for the second step in the synthesis of heme and uses two molecules of 
aminolevulinic acid as substrate. As ALAD is inhibited by the presence of lead, so that Zn2+ 
cannot bind to the active site, heme is not formed and the aminolevulic acid concentration in 
blood increases. Aminolevulic acid is known to be neurotoxic. [29].[50]. The neurotoxicity of 
aminolevulic acid is the inhibition of different synapsis in several different ways. [50]. Lead 
can cause cell membrane damages by clustering lipids facilitating oxidative stress. [4].  The 
neurological disturbances are characterized by impaired learning, convulsions, hyperactive 
behavior and ataxia among other things.[49]. 
Hazard characterization  
Lanphear et al. [51] has performed an international pooled analysis on children’s intellectual 
function and low-level environmental lead exposure. [51]. With low-level means less than 10 
μg lead/dL blood. The analysis is based on seven out of eight international cohort studies with 
1333 children involved. The cohort studies used full-scale IQ tests and blood-samples from the 
age of 6 months until 60 months of age. Four measures of blood lead were performed to obtain 
exposures: 
 Concurrent blood lead (measured closest to IQ test) 
 Maximum blood lead level (the peak concentration in any of the blood samples) 
 Average lifetime blood lead (mean blood level from first to last sample) 
 Early childhood blood lead (mean blood level from the first to samples) 
The full-scale IQ tests combine verbal and performance tests. The tests were administered after 
the last blood sample taken. In the Boston cohort this was at the age of 10, in the other cohorts 
it was from the age of 4 years 10 months to 7 years. Demographic and health information were 
obtained from a parent, the mother was subject to IQ tests and any possible information 
regarding the child such as physical data, social data and possible exposures was collected and 
taken into account. 
Statistical methods were taken into use, building up from a chosen initial model taking into 
account covariates and examining 10 confounders individually (listed in article table 1). The 
seven cohorts were assumed to be representative of a larger population of children, even though 
they were not chosen randomly from a larger number of cohort studies.

Manganese has many important commercial uses. It is incorporated into steel and aluminium 
alloys and used in dry cell batteries as MnO2. It is used as pigments and added in small amounts 
as MnSO4 to fertilizers. [46, p. 718]. [27]. It is part of the two fungicides Maneb and Mancozeb. 
It replaced ethyl lead as an antiknock agent in gasoline in the form of methylcyclopentadienyl 
Mn tricarbonyl (MMT). [27]. This list of uses shows that manganese is spread in the 
environment, and that there may be an occupational as well as an environmental exposure to 
consider. 
Contrary to aluminium and lead, manganese is an essential trace element that must be supplied 
via the diet. The organism uses Mn in a number of enzymes like superoxide dismutase, where 
it in the form of the metalloenzyme MnSOD functions in mitochondria by eliminating the 
superoxide anion that is a byproduct of the electron transport/respiratory chain. The superoxide 
anion is a highly aggressive radical that causes lipid peroxidation in membranes. Mice that are 
MnSOD knock-out live only for a short time. [23, p. 537]. Arginase is an enzyme that requires 
four Mn atoms and is responsible for cleaving arginine as the last step in the urea cycle where 
toxic ammonia is converted to ornithine (reenters the cycle) and urea (excreted).[23, p. 537].  
Many more enzymes that can use Mn exist, but most of them are not manganese specific and 
can use other divalent cations instead, for example Ca2+.  Manganese also play a role in the 
metabolism of macromolecules (lipids, carbohydrates and amino acids).

Hazard identification 
Toxicokinetics 
Absorption: Oral intake of manganese is absorbed in the small intestine. Only a small fraction, 
less than 10% of manganese supplied via the food is absorbed. The rest fraction is excreted 
directly in feces. The absorbed fraction (mainly Mn2+) enters the enterocytes at the brush border 
membrane either by passive diffusion or by using a high affinity metal transporter. DMT1 has 
been suggested. The uptake is mainly in the form of Mn2+. Women tend to absorb more than 
men do. This is supposed to be related to iron status.[3]. 
Distribution: At the basolateral membrane, manganese enters the portal vein, either as free 
Mn2+, or as Mn2+ bound α-2 macroglobulin. Only little Mn remains in the liver, most of it enters 
the bloodstream as free Mn2+, bound to albumin, α-2 macroglobulin, β-globulin or γ-globulin 
or it is oxidized to Mn3+ by ceruloplasmin and complexed with transferrin.[24, p. 536]. [3]. In 
the blood, Mn is mainly found in the blood cells, plasma only accounts for 4 %. The 
erythrocytes have the highest content of Mn (66%), while platelets and leucocytes contain 30%. 
The Mn concentration in blood ranges between 4-15 μg/L in healthy adults. The concentration 
is altered during pregnancy (described later in this section). From the systemic circulation, 
Mn2+ can enter cells by different transporters, such as DMT1, ZIP8 and ZIP14.  Mn3+ 
complexed to ferritin (competing with Fe3) can enter cells.[3].[24, p. 536]. Which cells are 
entered, depends on that particular cell type’s specificity for the transporter (i.e. its receptor(s)). 
Within cells, Mn is mainly found in nucleus and mitochondria. There is no specific storage 
organ for Mn but the highest concentrations are found in brain, retina, liver, kidneys and 
pancreas with average tissue concentrations between 0.1 and 1 μg Mn/g wet weight. The liver 
concentration is slightly higher (1.2–1.7 μg Mn/g wet weight), whereas the lowest 
concentrations are found in bone and fat (around 0.1 μg manganese/g wet weight). [3].  
Elimination: Excretion is in feces via the bile, only 1% of dietary intake is excreted via the 
kidneys. The urine content of Mn has ranges between 1-8 μg/L. Breast milk contains 3-30 μg/L 
and has been reported to come from less than 1% of the mother’s dietary intake. Half-life for 
Mn in the body is of a span of 13-37 days. As Mn in men has a longer half-life than in women, 
it is suggested that absorption and elimination are tightly adjusted to keep Mn homeostasis. [3].  
Toxicodynamics 
Being an essential trace metal, not only adverse effects due to excess intake/absorption should 
be considered. Manganese deficiency is rare, but from experimental animals symptoms such 
as reproductive deficits, skeletal abnormalities, impaired growth, ataxia in newborns and 
changed metabolism of lipids and carbohydrates are known [3]. No specific depletion 
syndrome has been described when it comes to humans. In order to establish a lower limit of 
Mn uptake, a depletion/repletion study with seven volunteer men was performed. After being 
on a baseline diet of 2.59 mg Mn for three weeks, this diet was substituted with a diet containing 
0.11 mg/day for 39 days. This forced deficiency caused skin problems (miliaria crystalline) as 
adverse effect in five of the seven men. After the study, when repletion had taken place, 
symptoms disappeared. [3]. 
==PAGE:26==
 
26
Neurotoxicity caused by excess intake/absorption is known and seems to be very dependent of 
exposure road. In fact, adverse effect of Mn was first described by John Couper in 1837 as a 
disorder called Manganism among workers caused by inhalation of air with a high content of 
Mn. [52]. Air in manganese businesses (mines, smelters, alloy plants, battery factories etc.) 
could contain >1 mg/m3 – 5 mg/m3 Mn. [53]. This occupational exposure caused accumulation 
of Mn in the brain due to excess absorption via inhalation. Manganism affects the brain areas 
globus pallides and the stratum in basal ganglia. [36].It causes muscular dystonia and other 
motor disturbances. It also in its early stages can have psychological disturbances of severe 
character or none at all. [52]. If the exposure causing the manganese accumulation is removed, 
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans show a brain with normal Mn levels after 5-9 
months.[52].[54]. Half-life of Mn in rat brain tissue has shown to be 2 months whereas in 
macaque monkeys the half-life has shown to be 8 months. However, return to normal Mn brain 
levels after cessation of exposure may not necessarily restitute the exposed as shown in 
neurobehavioral tests. [54]. Figure 6 shows the brain areas involved in Manganism and in PD.

Researchers have been tempted to compare with the idiopathic neurodegenerative disease PD 
regarding symptoms and affected areas in the brain. However, Manganism and PD are not 
identical. Some epidemiological reports suggest that exposure to manganese may give an 
increased disposition for developing PD. [55]. 
Manganese showing these neurotoxic effects in high concentrations in the occupationally 
exposed human beings have encouraged researchers to investigate the potential neurotoxicity 
of lower doses of Mn whether it be by inhalation or by other routes.  
Of concern has been the use of MMT as an octane-enhancing fuel additive replacing ethyl lead 
in cars. The combustion products are different manganese salts [56], which together with 
pollution in industrial areas represent exposure by inhalation. The concentration of Mn in 
==PAGE:27==
 
27
rural/urban areas and industrial areas has been estimated to 0.005 μg/m3 – 0.07 μg/m3 and 0.13 
μg/m3 – 0.3 μg/m3 respectively. [52]. Several studies exploiting rodents and other animals have 
been performed. Dorman et al, 2002 [36] concludes that species differences make rodents a 
poor model for Mn neurotoxicity in humans. Compared to humans, rats are predisposed to 
olfactory transport of Mn and rats are not demonstrating the same behavioral and pathological 
changes as humans suffering from Manganism. [36].  
Nutrition given parenterally bypasses the intestine. Premature born, seriously ill children and 
adults suffering from specific diseases are often nourished this way. This allows Mn to be 
100% absorbed.  In comparison children fed with breastmilk, 8% of Mn is absorbed due to the 
oral ingestion.[57]. [53]. The homeostasis of Mn in parenterally nourished humans is further 
affected by factors such as little or no stool, hepatic dysfunction and cholestasis, which 
diminishes excretion of Mn. [57]. Elevated whole-blood levels of Mn were measured in 
parenterally nourished children who had developed dystonia and movement disorders. [57]. In 
addition to total parental nutrition (TPN) a fixed neonatal trace element solution has been 
administered. This fixed solution has shown little flexibility as special clinical conditions may 
require more or less of any of the trace elements. [57]. A study by Y. Takagi et al. [58], in 
which eleven adults suffering from diseases such as Crohn’s disease and Chronic idiopathic 
intestinal pseudoobstruction syndrome participated, added Mn on and off as a supplement to 
their TPN over a period of almost four years. Whole-blood measurements and MRI scanning 
showed that Mn whole blood-level concentration was increased and that Mn accumulated in 
the globus pallidus during Mn supplement. When the Mn supplement was removed, the whole 
blood-level concentration decreased and the MRI scanning showed a decrease in intensity from 
Mn. The on/off administration of Mn was repeated with the same results indicating 
reversibility. [58]. 
During pregnancy, the fetus is supplied Mn from the blood of its mother through the placenta 
via the cord. The concentration of Mn in blood in a healthy adult has a range of  4 to 15 μg/L. 
[3]. However, during pregnancy there is an increase in Mn blood concentration up to 20 μg/L. 
[3].  This indicates a need for Mn in the developing fetus and that Mn is not a hazard. 
Oral intake of food seems to be highly regulated. Adverse effects are seen if the homeostasis 
is not maintained, for example if the elimination pathway is affected (a malfunctioning liver). 
[57].  Oral exposure to non-foodstuff may be of concern. Studies regarding exposure to water 
with high content of Mn have been performed. [59].[60]. Bouchard et al. [59] performed a 
study where school age children were exposed to tap water in their homes. The childrens’ hair 
was analyzed for Mn content as a long-term biomarker. IQ tests were performed and 
comparison between dietary Mn exposure and exposure to water Mn was made. According to 
the researchers their statistical model showed that hair content of Mn and IQ tests were not 
influenced by dietary uptake, only by exposure to water and that this could be attributed to a 
different regulating of homeostasis.[59].  
A study by Hope et al. [61] on Mn homeostasis in tea drinkers and non-tea drinkers is 
considered. Tea drinkers were test persons drinking more than 1 L of black tea per day. Non-
tea drinkers had no intake of tea. None of the two groups drank green tea or herbal tea. The 
==PAGE:28==
 
28
study analyzed whole-blood concentrations (fasting venous blood) and leucocyte expression 
on Mn-superoxide dismutase and cytosolic aminopeptidase P. In advance, the test persons had 
fulfilled a food frequency questionnaire (FFQ). For the non-tea drinkers the mean intake of Mn 
was 3.2 mg/day (range: 0.5-6.5 mg/day). For the tea drinkers much higher: 5.5 mg/day (range: 
2-12 mg/day). Black tea was referred to as a dietary intake. The Mn levels showed no difference 
between the two groups. Under gastrointestinal conditions, the polyphenols in tea have shown 
low affinity for divalent cations including Mn2+, indicating that Mn is bioavailable for 
absorption. According to the authors, effective Mn homeostasis, i.e. absorption and excretion 
via the bile could be the reason why no difference in Mn levels could be seen. [61]. 
Hazard characterization 
The upper tolerable limit per day for intake of Mn in foodstuff has been set by EFSA to 11 
mg/day based on no adverse effect has been reported up to 10.9 mg/day. [3]. The EU legislation 
has set a limit of Mn in drinking water to 50 μg/L per day.[3]. Animal studies have not provided 
a NOAEL for critical endpoints. [3]. 
ICPMS 
Overall principle of the technique 
Induced Coupled Plasma Mass spectroscopy (ICPMS) is an analytical tool that is capable of 
measuring most of the elements of the Periodic table. The method measures mass per charge, 
where charge is 1 as the method works by stripping off one electron from the element.  
 
Figure 7. ICPMS detection limits in the periodic table. Most elements can be detected. Al and Mn have detection limit 
between 1-10 ppt, Pb from <0.1-1 ppt. [Jens Jørgen Sloth, DTU Food Course 23301, January 2018]. 
==PAGE:29==
 
29
The mass of a specific element depends on the isotope. Aluminium is measured as 27Al, lead 
as 208Pb and manganese as 55Mn. The detection limits of the three elements are Al and Mn 10-
100 ppt, lead <0.1-1 ppt as seen in figure 7.The prepared sample is introduced into the 
instrument and nebulized in the spray chamber. Argon plasma at a temperature of 6000 ⁰C in 
the ICP torch transforms the aerosol droplets to particles → molecules → atoms → singly 
charged ions. Argon is used due to its high ionization energy (15.8 eV).  The singly charged 
ions are then directed into the mass spectrometer, which allows ions with a mass to charge ratio 
set by the computer to enter and exit. The interface links the ICP part to the MS part. The ions 
have to travel approximately 1 m before they are detected. To avoid gas molecule collisions, a 
high vacuum must be established. Not only ions exit the interface, also some photons (can 
count as ions at the detector and increase background and degrade detections limits) and some 
non-ionized materials (can disturb drift). They have to be removed before detection. A 
quadrupole serves as ion deflector. At the detector, the individual ions are counted.[62]. 
Interferences 
The phenomenon interference means that some species have the same mass to charge ratio as 
the analyte – either as isobaric overlap (example 114Sn+ with 114Cd+) or polyatomic overlap 
(example 12C15N+ with 27Al+). In the first case, one should consider the abundance of the 
naturally occurring isotopes/choose another to measure, in the second case, the heavier 
polyatomic species can be excluded using a collision/reaction cell (CRC). This process is called 
Kinetic Energy Discrimination (KED) and uses helium, an inactive element. Being heavier, the 
polyatomic species collide more frequently with He than one element ions. KED is used in the 
analysis of elements in this thesis.[62]. 
Internal standard 
The role of an intern standard is to provide a calibration curve to which the unknown samples 
can be compared. The internal standard should be an element that will not interfere with any 
of the analytes, i.e. be a rare element. It should have as few naturally occurring isotopes as 
possible and its ionization energy should be comparable to the analytes. 103Rhodium fulfills 
these criteria. 
Materials and methods 
Experimental design 
The experimental design is set up by selecting the samples. Subsequently homogenize the 
Made tea samples, subject them to digestion, make stock solutions and measure content of 
metal ions using ICPMS. Finally select a tea for brewing, make stock solutions and measure 
content of metal ions using ICPMS. 
Criteria for establishing a sample collection 
The criteria for choosing samples are:  
 The teas should be based on the tea plant Camellia sinensis in order to compare the 
content of metal ions in different teas originating from the same plant.

 The teas should include the greatest possible percent of teas (in weight of grams) 
purchased by consumers in order to obtain a solid fundament for risk assessment 
analysis. 
 The samples should also include teas with minor percentage of other substances, such 
as aromas and pieces of fruit to compare with content of metal ions in pure Camellia 
sinensis tea as these teas are popular among consumers. 
 The samples should be of a realistic number to be analyzed  during a five month master

Homogenization Made tea 
The selected teas were blended using a Krupp coffee bean mill. This was done to ensure a 
homogeneous sample. Blending time varied from sample to sample until the material appeared 
homogeneous (fine powders). The exact time cannot be given as the coffee bean mill does not 
offer a timer. Plastic zipper bags were marked with sample ID number, date and initials. They 
were weighed using an analytical scale and weight was registered. The blended tea samples 
were transferred to the plastic zipper bags (pouring) and the total weight (bag and content) was 
registered. The coffee bean mills (six in use) were cleaned with water and subsequently with 
ultra pure water (UPW). The equipment and the reagents are shown in Appendix E.

Exposure from other sources 
Aluminium 
The exposure to Al is mainly oral. Non-oral exposure includes inhalation, subcutaneous, 
dermal and intravenous (parenterally) exposure. Inhalation is mainly attributed to occupational 
exposure where aluminium sulphate used for water purification typically exposes workers to 
0.02-4 mg/m3, while higher concentration (5-21 mg/m3) is the reality in the production of Al 
==PAGE:48==
 
48
powders for explosives and firework. [27]. Subcutaneous exposure comes from vaccine 
adjuvants [71], Aluminium is used in cosmetics and antiperspirants and represent dermal 
exposure. Parenterally administration of water, insufficiently purified to patients undergoing 
dialysis has caused neurotoxic effects due to high amounts of Al. [2]. 
Oral intake comes from various sources. It can be dietary or non-dietary. Non-dietary intake 
comes from medicine (antacid) and the use of Al in packing material for food and as kitchen 
utensils also expose many consumers to Al. Dietary Al intake for breast-fed infants, age 3 
months is less than 0.07 mg Al/kg bw/week. For comparison, infant formulas expose the infants 
with (mean values) 0.6 mg Al/kg bw/week (milk based) and 0.75 mg Al/kg bw/week (soya 
based). [2]. Processed foodstuff may contain food additives based on Al salts. Food additives 
are used as either raising agents: E541 (sodium aluminium phosphates, acidic and basic) and 
E523 (aluminium ammonium sulphate, or as anticaking agents: E554 (sodium aluminosilicate), 
E556 (calcium aluminium silicate) and E559 (aluminium silicate). [44].  The content of 
aluminium in unprocessed food is most often less than 5 mg/kg food, but higher concentrations 
5-10 mg/kg are found in processed food and in some vegetables. Very high concentrations are 
found in spices, cocoa, herbs – and tea leaves.  
The dietary exposure of breast-fed children is estimated to be less than 0.07 mg/kg Al bw/week 
(based on consumption of 1 L per day and a body weight of 6.1 kg. [2]. In comparison, the 
exposure of infants aged 0-3, 4-6, 7-9 and 10-12 months fed from infant formulae has been 
estimated to be respectively 0.10, 0.20, 0.43 and 0.78 mg/kg bw/week. Some uncertainties exist 
regarding these estimates: water content of Al to prepare the formulae is not considered, waste 
has not been taken into account and the content of Al varies among different brands, with soya 
containing brands holding the highest amount of Al. [2]. The dietary exposure of adults varies 
between different countries and between different surveys. Food and water together expose an 
adult to between 1.6 and 13 mg Al per day. For a person weighing 70 kg this equals 0.2 to 1.5 
mg/kg bw/week. [2]. EFSA reports exposure from food in children ~4-17 years to be 0.7 mg/kg 
bw/week (reported from France) to 1.7 mg/kg bw/week (reported from UK) using the 97.5% 
percentile.[2]. This indicates that TWI is exceeded in some part of a population. Human dietary 
studies have been designed in a way that does not allow for determining the individual 
contributors to Al intake but only a total contribution. This means that the contribution from 
food additives and from Al processing and storage material is not known. [2]. The content of 
22 mg Al/kg in biscuits is assumed to be the result of a high amount of food additives. [2].  
Lead 
The exposure of Pb in the Danish population has been estimated to decrease. A report from 
2003 by the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration had reported an exposure of 19 μg/day 
for the period 1998-2003, but by the end of 2014 a new estimation of exposure was set to 15 
μg/day for humans between 4-75 years. This corresponds to 0.25 μg/kg bw/day for a person 
weighing 60 kg. Exposure from foodstuff and water is the main cause. There is a great variation 
in the content of Pb in different types of foodstuff. Beverages (not specified) contribute with 
the highest amount (more 46%) followed by fruit/fruit products, vegetables/vegetable products 
and cereals/cereal products whereas foodstuff of animal origin only contribute with minor 
amounts.[5]. The exposure of Pb to infants, fed by breast-milk, is estimated to 0.0016 mg/kg 
==PAGE:49==
 
49
according to a study by Gundacker et al. from 2002. [4]. Infant formula can contain Pb from 
the formula itself and from the water used to prepare it. An estimated value is given to be 
between 0.0020 and 0.0047 mg/kg.[4]. 
Even though main exposure of Pb is from foodstuff and water, also other oral and non-oral 
exposures are known. Oral non-dietary exposure of especially infants include soil and dust and 
toys containing Pb as described in section Lead Hazard Characterization. Adults can be 
exposed by drinking beverages stored in crystal decanters. Earlier the use of ethyl lead in 
gasoline caused airborne contamination, which led to direct inhalation and to accumulation in 
soil. This use stopped in the mid 1980ies and a reduction in Pb exposure was the result. [4]. 
DTU Food Institute provides some exposure results for people living in Denmark. Children 
(aged 4-14 years) are more exposed to Pb than adults (aged 15-75 years).[5]. In general, 
children have a higher uptake of food than adults when measured in g foodstuff/kg bw. [4].

Discussion 
The analytical method 
The ICPMS is a very precise method of measuring content of elements. If the quality assurance 
calculation shows satisfactory results, this part of the study could be the one with less 
uncertainty involved. 
Customer/consumer surveys 
The study involves human participation in form of the two customer/consumer surveys. Human 
participation opens the doors for many uncertainties to be considered. 
 
GfK collects receipts from a pool of people. The receipts show what has been typed into a 
computer system and is in many cases not sufficiently informative of the content of the 
==PAGE:53==
 
53
purchased item. The goal in a scientific work incorporating risk characterization must be to 
identify as high a percentage of the tea, which is within the categories of interest for the study, 
which is defined to be the content of metals in tea from the Camellia sinensis plant. However, 
there is a time limit to consider also, as preparing samples from the different teas is time 
consuming. Out of the total amount in grams, registered by GfK, 159 samples representing 
41% were candidates by representing Camellia sinensis. 43 samples were chosen representing 
29 % of all the GfK registered grams. The analyzed samples constitute ~72% of the identified 
candidates. This should be a reasonable percentage to represent Danish Camellia sinensis tea 
drinkers although it is regrettable that the Camellia sinensis part of the ~12% purchased 
elsewhere than in supermarkets and of the “fill the bag yourself” tea ~11% cannot be included. 
 
Consumer surveys must be balanced between getting as much and as detailed information from 
the consumer in order to be able to predict the intake of substances that may have an adverse 
effect, and not overload the questionnaire and get less people answering it. EFSA collects 
reports on people’s diet from European countries, and the diet varies with regard to country, 
age, personal taste, sex and probably social class. A diet rich in processed food in many cases 
contain additives, but content in each brand of for instance crackers may vary. This makes it 
difficult to conclude the intake of a chemical substance for a person. For processed food, it 
could be tempting to ask people which brand and which amount. Tea is a little special, as Made 
tea is a processed foodstuff, but still has to be prepared by the consumer (brewed) before it can 
be consumed. As shown in the Brewing tea experiment, the content of the three metal varies 
with dose and brewing time. The GfK data showed, that “any” infusion from an herb was 
incorporated in the term tea. 
 
In a consumer survey, asking people about their tea intake, questions like about which type of 
tea, brewing time, amount in grams (a teabag in a thermos or in a mug) and if milk constitutes 
some of the volume etcetera, the questions might not be answered. The consumer survey has 
some uncertainties incorporated. When asking people about their tea drinking habits, more 
detailed questions would give more accurate information. Detailed information would be: the 
type of tea, the time brewed, the amount of grams used, milk added (reducing the volume of 
tea) or not. However, many people would not use the time to answer this, as customer surveys 
on food intake include not only one type of foodstuff but is meant to cover the whole spectrum 
of food intake.  It would be too complicated and give fewer and to an extent somewhat incorrect 
answers. In some types of surveys, people may dress up their answers, because they may be 
ashamed of their intake. This is hardly the case with tea. 
The results: content of trace metals in the analyzed tea samples 
The eventual exposure of trace metals from tea must be considered from different points of 
views. Fresh parts of the Camellia sinensis plant is not considered a foodstuff and intake would 
probably be by accident. Correct harvesting would allow only the top leaves/bud with the 
lowest content of trace elements to be used for Made tea, and contamination from soil and dust 
on the leaves would be minor, if harvested correctly. On the other hand, what is sold as Made 
tea is a processed crop, in the sense that it is out of the range of the consumers to clean the 
Made tea. Whatever amount of soil and dust it contains, remains in the tea infusions consumed. 
==PAGE:54==
 
54
Trace elements are not eliminated by boiling water, and so trace elements in dust and soil 
contribute to the overall content of trace elements in the Made tea. The speciation of the metals 
absorbed by the plant and of the metals in dirt and soil on the plant could be different. The 
analytical method used would not distinguish. Speciation influences absorption and thus 
toxicity.  It is difficult to trace the origin of most teas. In some cases, a country or a region is 
given, but in most of the supermarket teas sold in Denmark, the origin is not known, and 
harvesting procedures cannot be checked. This poses an uncertainty. 
 
The results concerning the content of the Made teas are not uniform within a category, however 
they are homogenous within the wide range given in literature and shown in table 12. The 
standard deviation with regard to Al is highest for the category 90-99% Black tea other, while 
for Mn it is Mixed Green Tea, followed by 100% Black Tea, and for Pb it is Mixed Green Tea. 
The content is not uniform within the different categories either, green teas being highest in Al 
and Mn content contrary to literature values. Different content of a metal in tea will pose an 
uncertainty. The impact of the addition of flavor in the 90-99% teas shows no clear trend, 
whether it be, that the Camellia sinensis metal content varies or that the variation is due to the 
flavors or both.  
 
The Brewed tea experiment was limited to one tea only, the Silver Rose Earl Grey tea. There 
are different reported set ups in literature how to prepare Brewed tea for analysis.[10]. In some 
cases the infusion is filtered, in other it is digested. The set up in this study used tap water, no 
after treatment except for adding nitric acid 2% as solvent to a pH of ~1. The variables were 
time and dose and the differences in these showed considerable variation in concentration of 
the metals.  The consequences with regard to exposure seems clear. There is a trend: the higher 
the dose, the larger the exposure and the longer the brewing time, the larger the exposure. This 
experiment could be extended to other teas and using interval brewing times between 1-5 and 
5-15 minutes. To see if the concentration curve would flatten out, an extra brewing time of for 
example 20 minutes could be included. In general, dose and brewing time is recommended by 
the wholesalers on the Made tea package. The recommendations are based on taste. Either by 
accident or by indifference, recommendations are not necessarily followed. People who brew 
their tea in a thermos may leave the bag there, until the thermos is empty or they do not want 
more tea. As long as the trace elements are transferred into the infusions, the concentration 
would increase as volume decreases. For a person accepting her only daily cup of tea (the 200 
mL cutoff per day) from the last of such a brew exposure could be more than the calculated 
exposures in the Exposure Assessment section. How people brew their tea, can hold many 
uncertainties. Drinking other teas than Silver Rose Earl Grey tea would give a different 
exposure of unknown size. This gives uncertainties based on human activity and preferences.

Not only changing to a tea of one of the other analyzed Made teas could change the exposure.

Other exposure routes 
Occupational exposure showed the adverse effects of manganese and aluminium from 
inhalation. Precautions can be taken to protect the workers at least to some extent. Other types 
of special exposure show the consequences of modern technology. Parenteral nourishment and 
dialyses are both milestones in saving lives. Parenteral nourishment bypasses the intestine and 
thus alters absorption. Dialysis is given to patients with compromised kidney function (which 
disturbs toxicokinetics).  Contaminated dialysate showed adverse effects on the patients. This 
offers the opportunity to raise the question on which authority would responsible in giving 
advice on metal exposure.  
Vulnerable groups and diet 
People with special diseases or exposed occupationally may probably not be helped by 
changing their diet. Other vulnerable groups may, however. The impact of the trace metals on 
elderly people is not described specifically in this thesis, but it is well known that there are 
physiological changes of membranes and cells with age. This change can alter metal 
homeostasis. The fetus and the neonate are other vulnerable groups. Early in life the nervous 
system is not fully developed. The fetus is dependent on its mother’s blood to be nourished. 
The fetus cannot change its diet, but the pregnant woman can. As it is the case with very 
detailed surveys on food intake among people, also recommendations to pregnant women may 
give backfire if there are many different restrictions. As for Pb, lowering the content of Pb 
during pregnancy and lactation through a strict diet does not seem to have a sufficient impact, 
as Pb is released from the bones of the mother together with calcium which is needed by the 
==PAGE:56==
 
56
developing fetus. Neonates nourished from breast milk are dependent of their mother’s 
exposure to the metals.  Infant formulae have shown a higher content of metals than breast 
milk. Thus, it seems safer to nourish babies with breast milk. Manganese is required and the 
mother’s absorption is increased during both pregnancy and lactation. As for Al a change in 
the mother’s diet might have an impact of the concentration in breast milk and thus the 
exposure of the neonate and could be worth to investigate. 
The different types of studies 
The recommendations of thresholds by authorities have changed over time and may also 
change in the future. No threshold has been established for lead at time being: any exposure 
has adverse effects. The TWI of Al has changed and according to EFSA is exceeded by many 
consumers. Manganese has been described as a very flexible trace metal with regard to 
exposure. The setting of recommended intake (manganese), and thresholds for adverse effects 
from trace metals are set by the authorities based on studies. 
 
Different types of studies have their advantages and disadvantages. 
 
In vitro studies: As neurodevelopmental/neurodevelopmental toxicity is of great concern, 
insight into whether the trace metal enters the placenta and is transferred to the fetus is of 
interest. However, in vitro studies using placenta tissue from humans and animals can only 
report on passive diffusion. [28].   
   
Epidemiologic studies among humans : In this thesis, this type of studies are represented by 
different subtypes. Measuring the blood concentration of trace metals in a pregnant woman’s 
blood right before giving birth and in the cord right after birth gives a “snapshot” of the 
situation at birth. It does not give information of possible changes during a nine month long 
development. The placenta is developing during pregnancy (change in permeability, blood-
flow and metabolism) [28], and the fetus develops during pregnancy.  Transfer rates during the 
stages of pregnancy compared to the development stages of the fetus brain would be useful in 
assessing the exposure potential. [28].  The experiment by Drewitt et al. [38] on plasma level 
of Al and tea drinking was limited to a relative short period of time, namely three times 24 
hours, separated by two weeks. The authors only conclude on short-term effects, while it is not 
possible to obtain knowledge about eventual long-term effects. The experiment by Hope et al. 
[61] was addressed to investigate the circulating Mn content and the expression in leucocytes 
of Mn-containing enzymes in whole blood and in plasma of tea-drinkers and of non-tea-
drinkers. The experiment was based on a controlled intake of Mn from a FFQ and with a 
measured content of Mn in tea. It showed no difference between the two groups. The 
experiment did not analyze Mn levels in organs (the nervous system) and the possible 
consequences of high long-term intake of Mn. These kind of studies contain uncertainties 
regarding long-term effects. 
 
Animal Studies: Studies that use animals can be long-term studies in the sense that they can 
run for the whole life span of rodents (mice 1½ years, rats 2 years) and begin even before 
conception takes place. It allows for behavioral/cognitive tests as well as for pathological tests. 
==PAGE:57==
 
57
Some of the tests performed give results that are hard to correlate. The anatomy of rats differ 
from that of humans in the olfactory nerve/tract making rats a poor model for studying the 
neurotoxicity of Mn related to inhalation exposure as stated in the study by Dorman et al.[36]. 
The use of experimental animals is of concern among defenders of animal welfare. 
 
Behavioral/cognitive studies on humans and animals: Behavioral/cognitive studies are vital 
for determining adverse effects in the nervous system. They are performed on humans, that 
have not been exposed to the trace metals on purpose as well as on animals, which have been 
exposed on purpose. Animal studies possess uncertainties due to among other things the 
difference in CNS development between species. [28]. 
 
Cognitive studies in humans are troublesome, as IQ tests have to be adapted to the background 
of the person (education, health status etc.). The Lanphear et al. study [51] used advanced 
statistics and caused EFSA to revalidate the hazards from lead exposure even in trace doses. 
 
The Golub and Germann study[43] is a study that considers as well physiological endpoints as 
behavioral endpoints. It is a neurodevelopmental study.[2]. The study suffers from some 
lacking information, which makes it hard to reproduce. The Golub research group has 
performed several studies on mice of the adverse effect, and the administered doses differ in 
the different studies. [2]. In the Golub and Germann study, described in this thesis, the number 
of animals in total is unknown. The exact intake of food could not be determined, as two 
females were housed together and food was served ad libitum. Assumptions with regard to how 
much a mouse eat, must be made. The number of doses in the diet (four including control) were 
limited and not fully acceptable to establish a NOAEL according to EFSA. [2]. The use of 
lactate might also contribute to the overall picture by giving a lower NOAEL than normal diet 
Al salts would give. The interval between the 10 mg Al/kg bw/day and the 50 mg Al/kg bw/day 
is an uncertainty in setting the NOAEL as NOAEL might lie much higher than 10 mg Al/kg 
bw/day. No differences between groups with regard to miscarriages or birth weight were 
reported. At day 21 after weaning, the situation had changed. Why it is so, is not discussed in 
the study. Is the fetus protected against Al somehow, and first seriously exposed during 
lactation? Are the adverse effect only showing later in development? When setting LOAEL 
and NOAEL it may be optimal to use studies that groups the animals further into periods of 
exposure and to measure Al content in blood, milk and placenta. This grouping would also take 
into consideration the route of exposure and the absorption pathways. 
Exposure Assessment/Risk characterization 
Last, but not least the adverse effects from drinking tea with regard to the three metals are 
discussed.  
For manganese, the exposure is far from the upper tolerable limit of 11 mg/day set by EFSA. 
For the recommended brewing time/dose, the contribution from tea in total Mn exposure is 
low. Using the highest cutoff  ≥ 200 mL/day and the 95th percentile, the contribution from tea 
is ~ 0.24 mg/day, using the mean ~ 0.11 mg/day. Total exposure from Mn would end on 2.2 
mg/day (low exposure) and 6.2 mg/day (high exposure) for the 95th percentile. For the mean 
==PAGE:58==
 
58
the values are 2.1 mg/day and 6.1 mg/day respectively. The effects of the more extreme 
brewing conditions do not change the picture. Manganese from tea in this experiment does not 
contribute to expose tea drinkers to anything close to the upper tolerable limit 11 mg/day.  
For lead, the exposure from tea exists, but is extremely small. Lead could not be detected due 
to a dilution factor of 100, and LOD was therefore used as the highest possible concentration 
as described earlier. The exposure from other sources has been estimated by DTU food [5] to 
be 0.41 μg/kg bw/day, using the 95th percentile. The exposure from tea in the Brewed Tea 
experiment had a maximum of 0.003 μg/kg bw/day, less than 1% of total lead exposure. Based 
on the Lanphear et al. study [51], EFSA sat a BMDL01 of 12 μg/L of blood lead, which has 
been transformed to 0.5 μg/kg bw/day as the hazardous level [4] as described in section 
Exposure Assessment Lead. Taking into account intraspecie differences by dividing with 10, 
the value is 0.05 μg/kg bw/day. Thus the intake of Pb from the Brewed tea is not significant 
and will not add enough to the Pb burden to justify advice against tea intake. 
Aluminium exposure from tea is high and as has been described earlier, some people exceed 
the TWI of 1 mg/kg bw/week with up to 50%. If people keep their tea brewing in the 
recommended way, they would use a brewing time of 5 minutes and ~2 g Made tea/200mL 
water. Assuming a person would do this, a scenario for the ≥ 200 cutoff would look as shown 
in table 28. With this cutoff, a person would drink up to 1289 mL tea/day (the 95th percentile) 
or using the mean, 554 mL tea/day.

Conclusion 
The measured teas were within literature values for as well Made tea as Brewed tea. Exposure 
assessment and subsequently Risk Characterization showed no significant risk of exposure 
from Pb in measured tea. The same is the case for Mn, whereas the risk from tea drinking 
increases the Al burden to levels that easily exceeds the TWI. The exceedance depends on 
many different factors such as amount of tea consumed, brewing time and dose, and intake 
from other sources.
