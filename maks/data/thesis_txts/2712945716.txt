Abstract
Optical navigation is the process of determining ones position based on the compari-
son between detected image landmarks and their 3-D positions as noted in relation to a
reference database. With the Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter (LOLA) aboard the Lunar Re-
connaissance Orbiter (LRO) having made available a series of high precision topographic
maps of the lunar surface, this project will seek to explore the possibility of creating a lunar
specific optical navigation system.
The optical navigation system will determine the position of a spacecraft by comparing
imaged crater landmarks with the topographic LOLA data. The navigation system con-
sists of an image crater detector that employs a combination of a Maximally Stable Ex-
tremal Regions (MSER) detector and an adaptive canny edge detection scheme to detect
image craters, a crater matching system based on frame-invariant crater representation
connecting image craters to their LOLA-based counterparts, and a perspective 4 point
pose estimation algorithm that is translated from an interior coordinate representation to
a lunar-based coordinate representation.
The system will be tested on images of a simulated lunar landscape and real lunar images
captured during the Apollo 15 mission.

Introduction
Optical navigation is the process of determining the position and attitude of the spacecraft
based on camera images, and has seen a heightened focus as missions seek to explore
deeper into the solar system, where time delays add a higher demand for autonomous
functions [35].
A common application for optical navigation systems is in relation to landing on other
celestial bodies, as landing procedures are beginning to demand higher precision and
real-time course corrections [9] [7].
One approach is a terrain-based navigation system that allows a lander to determine its
own position with respect to a known reference and navigate to a specific landing spot by
recognizing several landmarks on the celestial surface, with craters commonly serving as
navigational landmarks [6] [36].
Building a real-time crater detection system is not an easy challenge, and different meth-
ods have been created to solve this task. Cheng et al.[6] have implemented an edge-
based crater detector with an ellipse refinement step that boasts subpixel accuracy.
Yu et al.[36] have implemented a method similar to that created by Cheng et al.[6], how-
ever, with an added stable region detector used to limit the search for craters to specific
regions of interest.
To achieve crater-based navigation, the imaged craters have to be extracted and then
matched to the same crater in a reference system where the position of the crater is
known. Different methods are used to accomplish the matching, Cheng et al.[6] utilize ge-
ometric recognition techniques that perform matching, Yu et al.[36] uses invariant crater
representations combined with a winner-takes-all algorithm to match craters to a known
database, and Clerc et al. [7] assume the attitude of the spacecraft is known and then
employ a Randsac-based matching algorithm.
This project seeks to explore the possibility of creating a lunar-specific optical navigation
system, where the reference database is based on the data of the Lunar Orbiter Laser
Altimeter (LOLA), aboard the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) [3], and the global
database of lunar impact craters [30].
This will not be a landing-specific navigation system, but an exploration of how the LOLA
data perform with respect to general navigation. To achieve this, a crater detection algo-
rithm has been developed based on a combination of concepts presented by Yu et al.[36]
and Cheng et al.[6], the crater detector is tested with images of a simulated lunar land-
scape and with real lunar images.
The matching process is based on invariant crater representations, where it is assumed
that no knowledge regarding the attitude is known, and only general information regarding
the position is known.
When the image craters have been detected and matched with their corresponding coun-
terparts in the database, the position is determined relative to the lunar coordinate system
by a closed-form solution of the Perspective 4 Point problem [1], and an interior to exterior
transformation matrix [4].
The demands regarding the precision of the navigation system have not been specified;
however, to achieve a pin point landing, a precision of <100 m will be needed [36].

2
Scene Analysis
Any optical navigation implementation will need detectable landmarks from which the at-
titude and position information can be extracted. Regarding space exploration around
cratered bodies, craters are an obvious landmark feature, as they have reliable visibility
due to their obvious contour under a wide range of illumination conditions [36] [6] [21]. To
add to this, craters are also widely distributed across the surface of cratered bodies, and
their shape and location will remain consistent during a mission time frame [34], these
characteristics further enhance their ability to act as a natural landmark for optical navi-
gation.
The images that will be used in this project will be of the real lunar landscape, captured
during the Apollo 15 mission, and images of a simulated lunar landscape. Therefore,
the analysis will cover the features that can be encountered on the Moon, with a section
devoted to lunar craters. Then limitations to an orbits altitude will be determined based
on the Apollo camera parameters, and finally the major differences between the real and
simulated lunar surface will be mentioned.
2.1
Real Lunar Scene
Considering a close-up of the lunar surface, different features can be encountered. Rilles
are any of the various valleys or trenches on the surface of the Moon and measure be-
tween 1-5 km in width and are several hundreds of kilometers in length. Craters and rock
fragments, whose size and frequency vary depending on the region in question [21].
Then there is the lunar regolith, which is a powdery rock fragment that covers the top layer
of the surface and was created from past impacts. The lunar regolith has an interesting
property, a high opposition surge, resulting in a high reflectance when the illumination
source is behind the observer. This property is interesting as terrestrial objects typical
have a more uniform reflectance (Lambertian) as a function of the phase angle [13].
Considering the lunar surface on a larger scale, its surface area can generally be split
between the lunar maria and the highlands [27]. The lunar maria are large smooth planes
that consist of large pools of solidified basaltic lava, which is also the reason why these
regions appear darker with respect to the rest of the Moon. The highlands cover most
of the lunar surface and, unlike the maria, appear white. The highlands are older than
the maria and therefore are also more heavily cratered. Figure 2.1 illustrates the contrast
in color and crater density between the maria and the highlands. The density of craters
constitutes a measure of the age of the surface, meaning that a younger surface will have
fewer craters, which is the reason why the lunar maria has a lower density of craters than
the lunar highlands.

3.1
Image Filtering
Image filtering is an important image processing step that can be used to achieve a wide
range of goals, such as noise reduction, image sharpening, and smoothing, just to name
a few. Filtering is often one of the first processing steps implemented in the processing
chain, as filtering helps in limiting the image information to what is relevant for the rest of
the processing chain.
An often implemented filter for smoothing and removal of noise is a low-pass Gaussian
filter. This filter type assigns a weight to neighboring pixels according to a Gaussian
distribution (3.1), which is circularly symmetric, also called isotropic, meaning that the
response is independent of direction and separable. As can be seen from the expression,
the weight assigned to each pixel decreases with increasing distance from the center of
the kernel.
G(x, y) =
1
√
2πσe−x2+y2
2σ2
(3.1)
Here, σ is the standard deviation in the x,y direction and is used to determine the size of
the kernel, since the Gaussian values at a distance greater than 3σ from the mean are so
small that they have a negligible impact and can therefore be ignored. That is, a kernel
size of 6σ x 6σ will produce the same result as an arbitrarily large kernel.
In practice smoothing (filtering) is applied by convolving the filtering kernel with the image.
For example, smoothing the image f(x, y) by the Gaussian function G(x, y) resulting in
fs(x, y), is accomplished by convolving f and G, which is written as.
fs(x, y) = G(x, y) ⋆f(x, y)
(3.2)
It should be noted that an odd-numbered size is desirable to preserve the dimensions of
the image after filtering, so if 6σ produces an even number, the closest number larger than
6σ should be chosen instead.

3.3
Edge Detection
An edge in an image marks the boundary between significant intensity changes in the im-
age. An edge is classified on the basis of its intensity profile, which can assume different
shapes depending on the edge in question.
A step edge has an intensity profile with a transition change between the two intensity lev-
els that ideally occurs on a single pixel, Figure 3.1 (a). Edges of this type can be found in
computer generated images, where the ideal one-pixel wide edge can likewise be found
provided no blurring has been applied to make the image look real.
In practice, digital images have edges that are subject to noise and blurring, which means
that the intensity transition is unlikely to occur within a single pixel, but it will instead occur
over a longer range of pixels. In these cases, the intensity profile will assume a ramp
12
Optical Lunar Surface Navigation
==PAGE:21==
shape, whose slope will depend on the degree of edge blurring.
A ramp edge will not consist of a single point but will instead consist of all connected points
contained in the ramp, Figure 3.1 (b).
Roof edges, are the edges cast by lines through a region. The width of the edge is deter-
mined by the width and sharpness of the line, Figure 3.1 (c). At its limit of a width of one
pixel, a rood edge is simply a one-pixel-wide line running through a region.
Figure 3.1: From left to right; step (a), ramp (b), and roof (c) edges with intensity profiles
As the edges accrue where there is a change in intensity, calculating this change by tak-
ing the first- and second-order derivatives of the image can be used to detect the edges.
Figure 3.2 illustrates how the first derivative is zero at constant intensity and assumes
a positive value when there is a change in intensity. The second derivative assumes a
positive value at the start of the ramp edge and a negative value at the end of the edge.
These properties can be used in such a fashion that the magnitude of the first derivative
can locate the presence of an edge in the image, while the sign of the second derivative
can be used to specify whether the edge pixel lies on the dark or light side of the edge.
The second derivative also has the property that the intersection between the zero inten-
sity axis and the line extended between the intensity peaks marks a point called the zero
crossing, which can be used to locate the center of thick edges.
Figure 3.2: Ramp edge and its first and second derivative

where even an image corrupted by Gaussian noise with zero mean and a standard devi-

A more advanced edge detection method, which is also the method used in the implemen-

Low error rate, meaning that all edges should be found with no false positives.
The edge points should be well localized, so the detected edges should be as close to
the true edge as possible.
Single-edge point response, which means that the detector should return only a single
point for each true edge point.

Adaptive thredsholding
Selecting viable upper and lower threshold for the hysteresis thredsholding can be a pro-
cess in itself, especially if the inputted image is subject to changing lighting conditions.
Normally, the upper threshold should be quit high, while the lower threshold should be quit
low, in order to achieve good results. However, if the upper threshold is too high, it will
result in noisy edges breaking up, while if the lower threshold is too low, it will increase the
number of spurious and undesirable edge fragments that will appear in the output image.
To choose the thresholds, an adaptive thresholding algorithm [33] can be implemented
just before the hysteresis thresholding is applied in the canny edge detector process. Af-

can then be calculated in the same fashion, but without considering the pixels that are

Mathematical morphology in image processing is a tool for manipulating and extracting

skeletons. Morphological operations can be applied to both binary and grayscaled im-

3.5
Maximally Stable Extremal Regions (MSER)
MSERs are intended to produce image features (region ”blobs”) that can be used to es-
tablish a correspondence between two or more images. The MSERs are determined by
evaluating a binary representation of the image at different intensity thresholds and de-
termining when the regions produced at these thresholds become stable, meaning that
they remain markedly unchanged between threshold levels. All regions produced from
all thresholds represent the set of extremal regions, while extremal regions that remain
stable over a range of threshold values are called maximally stable extremal regions [22].
To illustrate the procedure, consider the following example. The inputted image is an 8 bit
grayscaled image, meaning that the intensity of the individual pixels can assume a value
between 0 and 255. A binary representation of the image is determined at a series of
threshold intensities, any pixel at or over the threshold will be depicted as white (value 1)
while any pixel below will be black (value 0). The threshold series starts at 0 and ends at
255 with an intensity step of 1 in this scenario.
At threshold 0 the entire image will appear white, while more and more pixels will turn
black as the threshold increases, forming black regions. These black regions may grow
and merge from image to image but will never shrink; this will continue until the threshold
reaches 255 which will result in a black image. The connected white pixels at each binary
image will form the previously mentioned extremal regions, with the white pixel regions
that remain stable being the maximally stable extremal regions.
A way to consider these binary images is to view them as equivalent to the different layers
of a topographic representation of the image, where the intensity determines the elevation
of the given pixel.

3.6.1
Randsac
A more comprehensive description along with the formal definition of the Randsac paradigm
can be found in [10].
To enhance the implemented fitting method, randsac is used to reduce the probability that
erroneous points will influence the fitted ellipse.
Randsac is a paradigm for model fitting that enables smoothing of data that contain a
significant percentage of gross errors. Most fitting methods assume that the maximum
expected deviation of any collected data from the assumed model is a direct function of
the size of the data, meaning that the larger the data set, the smoother the result, as there
will be more good values to smooth out any gross deviations.
In many practical parameter estimation problems, the assumption does not hold, which
means that the data contain uncompensated gross errors.
Instead of using as many data points as possible to estimate the parameters for an as-
sumed model, randsac will use the minimum subset of data points that can solve the
model. The parameters calculated from this subset of data are then evaluated, and the
data subset is enlarged with consistent data when possible. If enough compatible data
points exist, normal smoothing techniques, such as least squares, can then be imple-
mented to improve the estimate of the model parameters.

Camera Pose Estimation
The general concept of extracting the pose (position and orientation) of the camera is to
match a number of image points with their corresponding 3-D points (the target), where the
geometry of the 3-D points are known. Based on the image and 3-D points, a geometric
relationship can be established between the two, from which a number of solutions can
be calculated, where the number of solutions depends on the number of image points and
their corresponding 3-D pints.
A minimum of four points is required to narrow down the calculation of the complete pose
(the six degrees of freedom) to a single solution assuming that the target points are co-
planar [1]. If the target points are not co-planer, an analytical solution can be derived
producing a bi-quadratic polynomial in one unknown, the root of which can be found in
closed form or by an iterative method [15].

3.9
Coordinate representation
During the implementation, a cameras position relative to the Moon is calculated; there-
fore, a quick review of the relationship between the spherical, ellipsoidal, and Cartesian
coordinate frames of reference will be covered.
The position of a point relative to a celestial object will often be described in a spherical
or ellipsoidal coordinate representation, which means that the position is defined as its
latitude, longitude, and radial length from the center (can be replaced with height), Figure
3.14.
Latitude, often designated as ϕ, is a measure of the position in degrees north of the equa-
tor plane and has a range of −90◦≤ϕ≤90◦, or the angle can be specified as being north or
south of the equator plane, which means that the north pole has the latitude 90 N and the
South Pole a latitude of 90 S [25].
Longitude, commonly denoted by λ, is given in degrees east or west of some reference
Optical Lunar Surface Navigation
29
==PAGE:38==
meridian (Prime Meridian), a range of −180◦≤λ≤180◦. For the Moon, the Prime Meridian
is defined by the mean direction of the Earth [25].
The intersection between the lunar equator and the prime meridian creates a surface point
called the Moon’s ’mean sub-Earth point’. The actual sub-Earth point varies slightly due
to orbital factors, so the mean sub-Earth point is used to define the surface point where
the longitude is equal to 0◦[25], the X-axis depicted in Figure 3.14.
The height or altitude is the distance from the point to some reference surface, which for
the moon is the sphere centered around the lunar center of mass with a radius of 1737.4
km [28]. This means that surface and near-surface points can assume negative or posi-
tive values, depending on their distance from the reference surface.
The height can be converted to a radial distance to the center of the celestial object by
adding the radius of the object to the height. However, this conversion can be incorrect by
several kilometers as it does not take into consideration the flattening that occurs towards
the poles for larger celestial objects, meaning that the radius changes as a function of
latitude.
Taking the changing radius into account is what separates the spherical and ellipsoidal
coordinate systems, where the spherical system assumes a constant radius [8].

4.2
Crater Detection
Figure 4.2: The step-wise process implemented for detection of craters in a grayscaled
image
The detection of craters in an image and the description of them as a series of ellipsis
is a multistage process that involves multiple filtering steps to reduce the risk of a false
alarm. The process starts by filtering the image to reduce noise and eliminate smaller
edge features. Then the MSERs of the filtered image are generated and then paired,
this is to give an estimate of the areas of the image that are likely to contain craters. On
the basis of the paired MSERs, a series of Regions of Interest (ROIs) are drawn on the
image and the edges within these are detected. The detected edges are then filtered on
the basis of their gradient to remove false edges that are not located on the crater rims.
These filtered edges are then paired on the basis of a series of criteria, and any edge that
does not have a viable pair is discarded. The final step of the process is the ellipse fitting
that will produce the ellipse parameters that describe the craters detected in the inputted
image.
4.2.1
Image Downscaling & Filtering
The first step in the process is to remove noise without removing a large amount of the
desired information. For the purpose of crater detection, the desired information being
any edges contained in the image.
Filtering is implemented in two steps, first the image is downscaled to be 16 times smaller
than the original image, from an image format of 4000 x 6000 pixels (Figure 4.3 ) to a
format of 1000 x 1500 pixels. The reduction increases the overall speed at which the
image is processed, as fewer pixels will have to be considered, and also reduces the
number of edges that will be detected, as smaller features and textures will be lost in the
process. Filter-wise, the downscaling process acts as a low-pass filter.
After the image pixel format has been reduced, a Gaussian filter is applied to the image
to reduce noise and further smooth out smaller objects and textures (Figure 4.4). The

used being the maximum deviation detected in the image. The maximum deviation is
expected to be detected at a given edge, which means that it would normally be excessive
to filter after this parameter if one only wanted to remove random noise. However, due
to the texture added by the sand used for the simulated lunar environment, an increased
filtering was needed to make it possible to implement some of the future processes of the
crater detection algorithm.

4.2.2

After image filtering, the next step involves finding the general area in which the craters
are located. It is suggested by [36] that this can be accomplished by finding the MSERs
in the image.
The interior of the craters generally consists of two stable regions with respect to grayscale
values, that is, the shaded and lit areas. These stable grayscale regions can be detected
by implementing the MSER detection algorithm (Section 3.5). The MSER detector is an
image blob detector that can quickly identify close-to-uniform regions in an image. The
algorithm is already implemented in Matlab as a function.
The input parameters for the function were determined by trial and error and unfortunately
vary from scene to scene, due to changes in the illumination and crater size. Therefore,
to maximize the chance of detecting as many craters as possible, the MSER parameters
have been chosen to be less stringent. This has the consequence that stable areas not
belonging to the craters are often detected; however, this is mostly mitigated when the
regions are paired.

craters are defined by two regions. However, due to the parameters chosen for the MSER
function, uniform regions outside the craters are also detected, and most of these excess
regions will be discarded during the region pairing process.

4.2.5
Ellipse Fitting
The result of the edge matching is a list of paired edges that will act as the initial data
points for the ellipse fitting process. The fitting process uses a direct least mean square
approach combined with Randsac to find the best fitted ellipsis within 500 iterations or until
a fitting threshold is achieved.
Initially an ellipse is fitted to the matched edges, and if 70% of the fitted ellipse is within 2
pixels of an edge, the fit is deemed acceptable and a new pair of edges are selected to
repeat the process.
If the fit is below 70%, a new ellipse is fitted using the Randsac paradigm, which means
that only five randomly selected edge pixels are used for the fitting process. If this new
ellipse achieves a better fit than the prior fit (or initial fit), any new edge pixel within two
pixels of the ellipse is added to the total sum of edge pixels available for the Randsac
search. This process is repeated until a fit of 70% is achieved or until the process has
been repeated 500 times, whereupon the best fit achieved is chosen.
For the final result, only ellipsis with a fit of 35% or higher will be noted as detected craters,
see Figure 4.13.

4.4
Lunar Database & Crater Matching
The 3-D position of the detected image craters is determined by matching them, based on
their characteristics, to a database containing said 3-D information. Two databases are
used to gain a full 3-D position for the center points of the detected craters, while another
two databases have been created in an attempt to facilitate the matching process.
To determine the 3-D positions a crater database containing information regarding the
crater parameters, including the position of their center points given in latitude longitude,
is used in conjunction with a topographic map of the lunar surface, this map is used to
adjust the distance from the lunar center to the center of the crater.
Combining the two databases then results in each crater center being represented by a
latitude, longitude, and a radius.
The databases constructed to facilitate the matching process are based on the crater
database and the invariant representations covered in Section 3.8.
4.4.1
Lunar Elevation Map
The High-resolution Lunar Topography map [3], is a series of Lunar maps, with regions of
latitudes between 60 deg South and 60 deg North available for download. The maps are
based on measurements by the Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter (LOLA) aboard the Lunar
Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO), which boast a vertical precision of 10 cm and an accuracy
of 1 m, and the topographic information captured by the SELENE Terrain Camera (TC)
[24].
The maps are available in different pixel density formats, 128, 256, and 512 pixels per
degree. Similarly, the map is available as a complete set of data or as a subset of smaller
specific regions; see Figure 4.17.

4.4.2
Lunar Crater Database
The crater database [29] contains a description of approximately 1.3 million and is ap-
proximately complete for all craters with a diameter greater than 1–2 km [30].
The database is based on topographic data measurements made by the LRO Lunar Or-
biter Laser Altimeter (LOLA), SELENE Kaguya Terrain Camera (TC) images and a merged
LOLA+TC data set, i.e. the same data format that forms the basis for the Lunar Topo-
graphic Map, Figure 4.17.
Each crater in the database is described by 21 columns of data, where a brief description
of each column can be found in the Appendix of [30]. The columns that are relevant for
describing the craters and, therefore, the crater matching are columns 4, 5, 8, 9, and 12.
Columns 4 and 5 describe the location of the center point of the craters in latitude and
longitude, respectively, columns 8 and 9 describe the major and minor axes of the ellipse
fitted to the crater in kilometers, and column 12 contains the angle of the fitted ellipse in
degrees.
The database is available as a .csv file which can be downloaded as a Matlab table using
the code in the Appendix A.
Combining the crater database with the elevation map to find the radius needed to produce
the 3-D crater points, latitude, longitude and radius, is then simply achieved by determin-
ing the elevation value in the topographic map at the latitude, longitude of the crater,
while remembering that each degree is represented by 512 pixels. When the elevation
has been determined, the radius for the crater location is then the sum of the elevation

and the radius of the Moon.
4.4.3
Invariant Representations & Crater Matching
The basis for the crater matching is to use frame invariant representations of craters, in
order to enable successful matching independently of the cameras attitude and altitude.
To facilitate invariant-based matching, two separate databases are generated based on
the invariant representations covered in Section 3.8, henceforth denoted as the area- and
trace databases.
The concept of the matching is that the detected image craters should produce the same
invariant values that will be noted in the two invariant databases, this should enable a
match between the image crater and the same crater noted in the invariant databases.
Note that both databases have an indexing equivalent to that of the crater database,
meaning that a crater in one database will be described by the same index across all
databases, thereby enabling a connection between them.
Area Database
The area database is constructed based on the invariant property of the area ratio between
closed curves (co-planar curves), which is the same property used by [36] to facilitate their
crater matching.
In general terms, the area database is constructed by looping through each crater in the
crater database, finding the nearest neighboring craters, then calculating the area ratio
between them and noting the results.
More specifically, the construction of the area database is based on the following param-
eters. The maximum distance to search for the nearest neighbors of the current crater
and a number specifying how many nearest neighbors should be considered.
If the current crater has no valid neighbors within the allowed maximum distance, it is sim-
ply skipped. Note that to preserve the indexing between the area and the crater database,
the row index for the skipped crater is likewise skipped, resulting in that row consisting of
nothing but 0s.
If there are fewer nearest neighbors within the search distance than the number specified,
the craters that are present will still be considered.
When the neighboring craters have been identified, they are sorted from nearest to far-
thest, where the distance between them can be calculated from the latitude and longitude
positions of the craters. After being sorted, the area ratio between the current crater and
the neighbors is determined and added to the area database.
Note that as a sanity check, the current crater is also noted as one of the nearest neigh-
bors, as the crater closest to the current crater is that crater itself. This should result in
the first column of the area database only assuming the values of 1 or 0 i.e. the scenario
where a craters area is divided by itself or when a crater was skipped due to no nearby
neighbors.
A distance of 30 km was selected as the maximum search distance for the generation of
the area database, and the 10 closest neighbors (including the current crater itself) would
be considered.
Trace Database
The generation of the trace database follows a methodology, with regard to selecting a
crater and its nearest neighbors, equivalent to that implemented to generate the area
database. The main difference between the two databases is that instead of calculating
the area ratio between the ellipse that denotes the craters, the trace of the product be-
tween the current crater ellipse and the inverse of its neighbor is determined, as described

Crater Matching
The attempts at crater matching are implemented based on a winner-take-all approach,
meaning that the detected image crater will match the database crater with which it shares
the most invariants. For simplicity, only the area ratio matching will be covered, as the
process will be equivalent for the trace approach.
The initial step for matching is to apply the same processes that formed the basis for the
area database on the detected image craters, that is, find the nearest neighbors for each
crater and determine their relative area ratios, which means that each crater will be de-
scribed as a vector of ratios.
After the ratios have been determined, several different methods have been attempted
with respect to matching, all of which have a basis in the winner-take-all approach.
The first implementation was simply iterating through each of the ratios noted for an image
crater, and finding the area ratio in the area database that best matched each of these,
while noting the row index of the area database for each match. This would produce a
vector containing indexes, each of which denotes a database crater, where the index with
the most instances would then be selected as the match for that image crater, whereupon
the process would be repeated for the next image crater.
Another implementation was similar to the first; however, instead of only finding the best
match between the image ratio and those in the area database, the three closest matches
were noted for each image ratio, after which the index with the most instances would be
selected as the match.
A third approach was again to find the three closest matches for each image ratio, how-
ever, now with a threshold implemented to ensure that each of the three matches was
close to equivalent to the image ratio.

7.1
Crater Detection Evaluation
7.1.1
Simulated Lunar Surface
When evaluating Figure 6.1 & 6.2 along with Table 6.1, it is clear that the performance
and reliability of the crater detector vary greatly depending on the input image.
An optimal result produced by the crater detector would be a series of four or more well-
fitted ellipses, with zero false flags, if fewer than four craters are detected, there would
not be enough information to estimate the position, and any false flag would bring an un-
desired disturbance to the position estimation and the crater matching.
With this in mind, the crater detector only produces an optimal result when inputted image
054, as eighth craters are detected with zero false positives. This is not overall surprising,
when considering that image 054 served as the main image used for tuning the detectors
filtering parameters, making the detector particularly predisposed to producing a satisfac-
tory result when this image is inputted.
Other notable characteristics regarding the detector can be defined based on the results
noted in Table 6.1. Images 061 and 062 were both captured at the same camera position,
however, despite the images being close to identical five and six craters were detected,
respectively, meaning that the detector does not operate at a 100% consistency.
Image 140 is subject to a change in the illumination direction while keeping the light source
elevation consistent with the other images, despite the changing illumination nine craters
are detected, one being a false flag and one poorly fitted. This implies that the detector
is not overtly sensitive to changes in the illumination direction.
While the detector is not overtly sensitive to the illumination direction, image 142 illustrates
that the detector is sensitive to elevation changes in the position of the lighting source,
only three craters are detected in image 142 despite it being captured from the same per-
spective as image 140. This change in illumination results in an overall reduction in the
size of the crater shadows, which affects the formation and pairing of the MSERs, leading
to fewer detected craters.
The first instance with a significant increase in detected false flags occurs when process-
ing image 088. The reason for this increase is most likely due to the camera being closer
to the simulated surface than, for example, when compared to image 062, meaning that
the texture of the sand becomes more prevalent leading to an increase in detected non-
crater edges, which likewise increases the likelihood of false flags being detected.
The series of images 180 to 184 were captured during a transition in the tilt of the camera
relative to the simulated surface, from an angle of 10° to 20°, that is, there is an increase in
angle throughout the series of images. Interestingly, the number of false flags decreases
as the angle increases, with the number of well-fitted ellipsis also increasing. The reason
for this increase in performance at greater angles is probably a better definition of the
ROIs bounded around the potential craters. The parameters that in part define the size
of ROIs were tuned to image 054, which is also an image captured at an angle, meaning
that the detector might perform better if the image craters assume a elliptical shape, as
opposed to the circular shape they tend to assume when viewed vertically.
Another opportunity is that an angle of 20° brings the perspective closer to that of the base
image 054, possibly aligning the filtering parameters needed for image 184 with those set
Optical Lunar Surface Navigation
71
==PAGE:80==
based on image 054.
The series of images from 201 to 207 were captured during a transition in camera height
over the simulated surface, with height decreasing throughout the image series. Through-
out this image series, the performance is somewhat sporadic in regards to the number
of detected craters, changing from seventeen to ten and then back to nineteen detected
craters throughout the series. This variety in detected craters is mainly due to the chang-
ing number of false flags detected, which is based on the amount of invalid edges detected
and whether or not the ellipse fitted to these edges achieves a fit above the threshold of
35 %.
A more systematic trend regarding the image series is the general decrease in well-fitted
ellipses (down to 3 for image 207). This downward trend is most likely due to the texture of
the sand that constitutes the simulated surface, assuming a greater influence with regard
to the general shape of the crater rim, the closer the camera gets to the surface. Resulting
in the crater rim appearing more ’jagged’, increasing the likelihood that the edge drawn at
the rim will fragment during the gradient filtering, decreasing the overall chance that the
crater is detected.
In summary, the crater detector performs better at a greater distance from the simulated
surface and at greater tilt angles relative to the surface, while being sensitive to decreases
in crater shadow sizes.
7.1.2
Apollo Images
Unfortunately, the crater detector did not detect any craters in the inputted Apollo images,
though this was not a comprehensive study, so there may be some images that would
produce viable results; based on the images that have been inputted, a few points of fail-
ure can be seen, and how to improve this is described.
When the Apollo image 0850, which depicts a section of the lunar surface defined by lunar
maria (Figure 6.4), is inputted the point of failure in the crater detection process occurs
during the MSER pairing.
When the MSERs for image 0850 are converted to binary, the separate regions that make
up a crater are unified, eliminating the distinction between the shaded and lit area regions.
When the lit and shaded regions are unified beyond separation, it will naturally lead to a
failure in the region pairing, as there are no separate regions to pair.
The reason that the regions are unified beyond separation is due to the size of the MSERs
and how the binary conversion is implemented.
With the image being dominated by
smaller craters, the MSERs generated for the craters will most likely not be separated
by any pixels, which results in the shaded and lit regions being unified when converted to
binary.
To avoid this problem, the binary conversion process should be changed into some-
thing along the lines of; The MSERs are formed, after which they are evaluated and
separated into two groups, one group containing the regions consisting of pixels with
a grayscale value below the average, and one that contains the regions with an above
average grayscale value. When the MSERs have been separated into two groups, they
can then be converted to binary separately and then unified with a combination of an
XOR operation and a one-pixel erosion. The XOR will remove any overlap between the
regions, while erosion will separate adjacent regions. This change in binary conversion
has not been implemented due to time constraints.
Another point of failure presents itself when image 0587, which depicts a region of the
lunar surface defined by the lunar highlands, is processed. As was the case for image
72
Optical Lunar Surface Navigation
==PAGE:81==
0850, most of the craters were removed during the MSER pairing; however, for this image,
some regions were successfully paired, with one region pair describing one of the larger
craters in the image. Despite a successful MSER pairing, the crater was not detected, as
the edge gradient filtering failed to produce crater edges that could be paired. The reason
for the failure is likely due to the gradient of the crater edge not being well defined, leading
to a failure in the filtering process.
To avoid this problem, an alternative method might need to be implemented to filter out
the invalid crater shadow edge, the details of which are not clear; however, a method that
could be explored is some morphological operation that makes use of the paired MSERs
to remove any edge between the regions.
A final note regarding image 0587 (Figure 6.6) illustrates that the values chosen for the
generation of the MSERs of the image are not optimal for the inputted Apollo images, and
could be improved by placing a more stringent upper limit on the maximum size of the
MSERs.
Despite the inability to detect craters when Apollo images were inputted, some of the steps
in the detection process show some promise in terms of identifying craters. Comparing
Figure 6.4 & 6.5 it can be seen that the MSERs correctly identify the general location of
the image craters. Another promising feature is the ROI generation; see Figure 6.6, as
these are large enough to encompass a potential crater but not overly so.
It should also be noted that false flags are not detected in the images.
7.2
P4P Evaluation
With the detector unable to detect craters in the Apollo images, a manual process had
to be employed to test the P4P implementation; this process was, however, rather cum-
bersome, meaning that fewer lunar regions than could have been desired have been
processed.
Six craters were selected, respectively, from Apollo images 0587 and 0850 (Figure 6.8 &
6.9) and matched to the crater database to determine the capabilities of the implemented
P4P.
As described earlier, image 0587 shows a section of the lunar highlands, and the camera
is vertical relative to the surface, i.e. looking directly down. The resulting latitude, longi-
tude and altitude produced by the P4P can be viewed in Table 6.4, which, compared to
the true position of the camera, shows that most of the four-crater combinations produce
results approximate to that of the true position, with an altitude error ranging from 10 km
to a few meters. However, this ignores combinations 1-3, which are the greatest outliers,
with an altitude error of 90 to +100 km.
The outputted latitude and longitude are for most combinations only off by 0° to 0.5°, the
outliers being combinations 1 to 3 and 12 that are off by 1.5° to 2.5° in regard to the latitude
and 0.5° to 1°.
When outliers are not taken into account, the results show some promise with respect to
an optical navigation system; however, precision still leaves something to be desired, as
an error of 10 km is not optimal. The accuracy might be improved by incorporating more
points into the position estimator, that is, a Perspective 5 Point (P5P) or even a Perspec-
tive N Points (PNP); however, due to time constraints, this has not been attempted. As it
stands, a general position regarding the cameras’ location can be produced.
Regarding the three outliers, it was observed that they occurred when three of the four
selected craters were arranged in a linear fashion, see Figure 7.1. This may be the rea-
son that these configurations produce results not in line with the other configurations, as

7.3
Crater Matching Evaluation
When observing the results presented in Section 6.3 it is clear that the implemented
matching methods do not produce any viable results, with the highest number of correctly
matched craters being one, across the different methods and invariant crater representa-
tions (Area and Trace). Two reasons for the failure have been identified, one more certain
than the other.
The most certain reason is that false craters have been registered in the craters database
used for this project (the database [29]), Figure 7.2 highlights an example of a falsely reg-
istered crater.
Any false craters in the database will interfere with the creation of the area database and
the trace database, as these define the invariant crater representation based on the neigh-
boring craters. Therefore, false craters will add an undesired disturbance to the matching
and will increase the likelihood that the matching ends in failure.

The reason that false craters have been registered is that the crater database is based
mainly on elevation data [30], which results in indentations in the local topography being

That the crater database is based on topographic data ties into the second reason for

the failed matching. The basis for the matching is two different invariant representations
of co-planar conics, i.e. a value based on the relationship between the two conics that
will remain constant even after an affine transformation. However, for the invariant theory
to remain valid, the conics have to fundamentally be the same, meaning the conics are
bridgeable by the same affine transformation.
With the craters detected in the images being based on optical data and the craters in the
crater database being based on elevation data, there might be a fundamental difference
between them that invalidates the theory on which the matching is based.
Due to the false craters registered and the crater ellipses fitted to elevation-based data,
a new database will likely have to be created before an optical lunar navigation system,
with matching based on invariant representations, can be produced.
The need for a new database could be circumvented if the camera attitude was assumed
to be known, as then the matching could be based on the angles spanned between the
craters center points; however, this would still leave the false craters.

Future Work
As time was limited, some concessions regarding the implementation were taken, and
likewise not all problems encountered were solved, which is what forms the basis for
these recommendations regarding any future work.
Better crater detector
The implemented crater detector leaves much to be desired, as it completely fails when
subjected to real lunar images, and often detects an unacceptable amount of false craters
when subjected to the simulated lunar landscape. As was covered during the discussion,
some of the processing steps do show some promise; however, a significant rewrite of
the detector code is likely warranted.
Improve the current detectors ellipse fitting.
A specific part of the crater detector that can be improved is the randsac-based ellipse
fitting; an improved method would be the refinement of the ellipse described by Cheng et
al. [6].
Crater detector tuned to real lunar images
As there is currently no 3-D map of the simulated lunar surface, it might be more viable
to construct a navigation system based solely on real lunar images.
Generate a 3-D map of the simulated lunar surface
To make the simulated surface more viable as a test bed, a 3-D map of the surface is
needed.
Attitude as azimuth and tilt
Formulate the math needed to describe the orientation (Euler angles) of the camera as
an azimuth and tilt, which can be compared with the true values indicated in the Apollo
atlas.
Perspective N Point
The implemented P4P had a significant variation with respect to the precision of the output
that could be suppressed using more than four points to calculate the position.
Linear image crater orientation and P4P output
It was observed that if the image craters were arranged in a linear fashion, the output of
the P4P would be significantly wrong. However, this was not explored fully due to time
constraints and, therefore, warrants further examination.
Database based on optical images
If the invariant crater representation is to be used as the basis for the crater matching, it is
likely that a new crater database is needed due to the false craters and the fundamental
difference between the ellipses defined by the elevation data and those based on optical
data.
Explore if the invariant theory holds true for increased tilt angles

9
Conclusion
To examine the performance of an optical navigation system with the LOLA data acting
as a reference map, a crater detector was constructed to extract and describe the shape
of image craters as ellipses. This was followed by the implementation of a P4P pose esti-
mator that enables the extraction of the cameras’ position based on four image points and
their corresponding 3-D coordinates to represent the cameras’ position relative to the lu-
nar coordinate system, and the interior position of the camera was subjected to a rotation
and translation, bridging the two reference frames. Finally, a crater matching algorithm
based on invariant crater representations was implemented to automatically match image
craters to their LOLA based counterparts.
The performance of the crater detector would need to be significantly improved to be
viable for a real-time optical navigation system. The crater detector still registers false
craters when inputted images of the simulated lunar surface and completely fails to de-
tect any craters when real lunar images are inputted. Any falsely detected crater will have
a detrimental effect in relation to the crater matching to the LOLA based database.
The MSER detection step of the crater detector shows promise in limiting the search for
craters to specific regions of interest where the craters are likely to be located. However,
a significant redesign will need to be completed to improve the performance of the crater
detector.
Unfortunately, the crater matching to the database was never successfully implemented,
due to falsely registered craters in the crater database, and also likely due to an invali-
dation of the invariant-based crater matching originating from the fundamental difference
between crater ellipses based on elevation data and those based on optical data.
The P4P pose estimator was tested on a limited number of real lunar images, due to the
cumbersome process of manually matching image craters with the LOLA based crater
database. The results of the pose estimator showed a high sensitivity to displacement of
the image craters center points, and a possible sensitivity to the arrangement of the image
craters, as a linear placement of these was observed to align with a significant increase
in the error of the outputted altitude of the pose estimator. Similarly, it was observed that
the calculated altitude would move toward zero in response to disturbances, be these
displacement errors or image craters mismatched with the database.
When ignoring outputs with an altitude close to or below zero, the precision of the pose
estimator would vary from a few meters to multiple kilometers from the true position. This
precision is likely to be improved by including more than four image points in the pose
estimation process, but this was never examined due to time constraints.
The LOLA based database do have the potential to serve as a reference for a lunar spe-
cific optical navigation system; however, to confirm this the following would need to be
completed; the creation of a crater database based on optical data and with fewer falsely
registered craters, the creation of a crater detector that is able to detect craters in real
lunar images and the implementation of a pose estimator that includes more than four
image points in order to increase the accuracy.
