Abstract 
This thesis is a study of student entrepreneurship that sheds light on the sustainability student 
start-ups. Entrepreneurship and sustainability are recognized as playing an essential role in future 
development, and governments are strengthening entrepreneurship education and sustainability 
initiatives in higher education. As a result, many students have opportunities to develop their start-
up ideas or project in their curricular activities. However, existing research about entrepreneurial 
activities in higher education institutions has focused on university spinoffs, and the main actors 
are faculty and researchers. Student entrepreneurs initiating start-up activities in their education 
program have not been studied much. Therefore, this study aims to articulate the actors in student 
start-up activities by designing a holistic framework from collected data. Additionally, the author 
investigates further in sustainability aspect. 
This study provides the fundamental knowledge and overview related to entrepreneurship, 
student entrepreneurship, and sustainability from the literature review. This research was 
designed based on the qualitative research and case study approach. This research, then, chose 
12 samples and conducted a semi-structured interview for data collection. As a result, the 
literature review and the coding identified four categories and 12 actors that have an effective 
impact on student start-ups. This study also articulated 3 phases in the process. It draws on the 
transition of student identities from a student to an entrepreneur. Lastly, the research extract 
findings on sustainability impact from the comparison between sustainability start-ups and non-
sustainability start-ups. Sustainability affects student start-ups in financial and opportunity 
recognition aspects.  
This study generated a holistic framework covering the student start-up journey, and it provides 
a common understanding and language to stakeholders in student entrepreneurial activities. This 
outcome can lead the fundamental path for student entrepreneurs and give valuable insights into 
how to balance their student and entrepreneurial lives. This study can contribute to not only 
students but also higher education institutions and policy makers. It suggests how students utilize 
the internal and external university ecosystems, hence stakeholders in higher education or 
policymakers can refer to it when they build the program to encourage student start-up activities. 
Besides, the flexibility of the student curriculum and further support from long-term vision are 
identified as important to enhance the sustainability student start-ups. 
Lastly, this study has several limitations in terms of transferability due to the research design. This 
research focuses on Danish technological university as a representative context of leading role 
models in sustainability and entrepreneurial education. The framework depends on the social and 
education system, sense of values, and culture. Therefore, the different context can be used for 
further research. Furthermore, the team decision-making process and quantification of 
sustainable impact are recognized as future research topics from the discussion.

1. Introduction 
Entrepreneurship has had growing interest from the academic sphere and public authorities 
because of the expectation for economic growth and the creation of jobs in recent two decades 
in the transition to a knowledge society (Audretsch et al., 2006; Mazzarol & Reboud, 2020; 
Bainée, 2020). Entrepreneurship has taken on a more critical position in higher education 
institutions, transforming its role as a venue for entrepreneurial activity (Mok, 2005). In Denmark, 
for example, policies have been taken to promote innovation and entrepreneurship education in 
higher education (Ministry of Higher Education and Science, 2012). Entrepreneurship activities in 
higher education have identified commercial opportunities from advanced research and state-of-
art technology in universities and created spin-off start-ups. They also teach entrepreneurial 
theory and practice as the entrepreneurship education (Bainée, 2020; Åstebro et al., 2012). The 
creation of spin-offs from advanced technologies has attracted a high level of interest since the 
early 1980s, especially from policymakers, and many studies and investigations have been 
conducted about spin-offs (Åstebro et al., 2012). 
 
On the other hand, entrepreneurship education has been built on the idea that it is trainable rather 
than innate, with action-based programs that stimulate students' creativity and autonomy by 
teaching theories and methodologies that enable them to act as an entrepreneur (Bainée, 2020). 
As a result, we are seeing an increase in start-ups by students and graduates that are not spin-
offs by researchers or faculty. For example, according to Lange et al. (2014), 913 out of 3775 
students have become entrepreneurs in higher education in US, and they have created more than 
25,000 jobs. Consequently, in recent years, studies have begun to shed light on student start-
ups. Longva (2021) researched the development of social networks in entrepreneurial 
ecosystems, while Nielsen et al. (2017) focused on the identity of student entrepreneurs. It is 
obvious from these studies that the environment surrounding student start-ups and student 
entrepreneurs and their challenges have different aspects from those of traditional spin-offs and 
regular entrepreneurs. 
 
In addition, one of the key concepts surrounding higher education institutions and younger 
generations of students in recent years has been sustainability. Since the SGD was established 
in 2015, various levels - national, institutional, municipal, and individual - have addressed 
sustainability development as an integral part of their respective policies and corporate strategies. 
In this context, higher education institutions play a critical role in fostering the skills and values 
that connect the concept of sustainability to people (Ruiz-Mallén & Heras, 2020). Sustainability 
has also become an important standing at the student level, especially among the younger 
generation, demonstrating more active behavior regarding sustainability (Kanchanapibul et al., 
2014). Meanwhile, entrepreneurship also considers sustainability as an opportunity to create 
social and environmental value and new businesses. Consequently, many entrepreneurs have 
started putting sustainability at the core of their business creation activities (Muñoz & Cohen, 
2017). Under these circumstances, student start-ups with sustainability at the core of their 
business could become a more common form of entrepreneurship in the future. At the moment, 
however, there is still little research encompassing the two currently developing areas of 
sustainability and student entrepreneurship, and knowledge of the processes and factors involved 
is limited. (Sukoco et al., 2020).

1.1 Statement of the problem 
Many students in higher education are currently studying entrepreneurship and engaging in start-
up activities. Existing research shows that students are more likely to succeed in start-ups for 
those who have just graduated than in spin-offs, which is a promising form of entrepreneurship 
(Åstebro et al., 2012). However, students consider entrepreneurs as people with innate attributes 
and competencies with being entrepreneurs (Goliath et al., 2014). Furthermore, a lack of 
entrepreneurial interest and motivation is an obstacle for students to engage in new business 
ventures (Butkouskaya et al., 2020). Thus, even though students have high sustainability 
awareness and the potential to succeed as entrepreneurs, they do not choose the entrepreneurial 
path because they believe that working on a new business is not an option for them. This study 
assumes that the lack of generalized knowledge is one of the reasons why entrepreneurship is 
not an option that students can take. 
 
The purpose of this study is to investigate student entrepreneurs and the actors surrounding them, 
as well as to design a framework that can capture their interactions and processes. The student 
entrepreneurship process can be predictable by providing a framework that student entrepreneurs 
and stakeholders can utilize as a common language in their entrepreneurship activities. 
Simultaneously, this study identifies the impact of dealing with the element of sustainability can 
also guide how to deal with this important topic of recent years. The designed framework can 
improve future student entrepreneurship activities to be more effective and give beneficial insight 
for the course design, start-up support organizations, and public agencies to be more appropriate 
for student start-ups. Furthermore, it can encourage sustainability, innovation, and economic 
development by reducing students' resistance to entrepreneurship and making them more familiar 
with the option of starting a sustainable business.

1.2 Research questions 
This study aims to create a common language that enables a comprehensive treatment of the 
process of student start-ups in the early phase, starting with sustainability. To this end, the 
following research questions have been set to achieve the objectives of this thesis.  
 
RQ1: How do internal and external actors of the student start-up affect their start-up activities? 
RQ2: How does the sustainability aspect affect student entrepreneurs? 
 
By identifying these two research questions, the purpose of this study is to propose a framework, 
which is the research question of this study.

2.1 Entrepreneurial lenses 
2.1.1 The concept of Entrepreneurship 
Start-up is defined as “the act or an instance of setting in operation or motion” or “a fledgling 
business enterprise” by Merriam-webster (Merriam-Webster, 2022). In recent years, start-ups and 
entrepreneurship have been recognized as not mere activities of creating a business but also the 
key elements for economic development and maintaining the competitiveness of a nation’s 
economy (Mazzarol & Reboud, 2020). For instance, the Organization for Economic Co-operation 
Development (OECD) posited SMEs as the vital tools for dealing with the world’s social and 
economic challenges (OECD, 2010). Meanwhile, entrepreneurs also played an important role in 
start-up development. The term ‘entrepreneur’ was first used and defined as an individual who 
bears the risk of buying at certain prices and selling at uncertain prices by Richard Cantillon (Long, 
1983). Schumpeter (1934) expanded this definition from the concept of businessmen trading 
goods and services to incorporating innovation. Schumpeter argued that entrepreneurship is the 
process by which an economy moves forward through the act of creative destruction or innovation. 
In that context, entrepreneurs who create new businesses with innovation have been identified 
as playing these five critical roles in the economy (OECD, 2010).  
 
• 
The entrepreneur as a disruptor. Entrepreneurs seek opportunities to disrupt market 
equilibrium by carrying out “new combinations” : new products, processes, and marketing 
techniques. They are a leading agent of innovation and a “creative destruction” (Schumpeter, 
1934; Schumpeter, 1943)  
 
• 
The entrepreneur as an opportunity identifier. According to Kirzner (1997), entrepreneurs 
discover and exploit previously-unnoticed profit opportunities and facilitate matching of supply 
and demand and the achievement of market equilibrium.  
 
• 
The entrepreneur as a risk taker. Entrepreneurs assume a willingness to provide new 
solutions in the market in the uncertainty. However, their ability to deal with uncertainty and 
ambiguity allows them to take on risks that might not enable their solutions to be profitable 
(Knight, 1921).  
 
• 
The entrepreneur as a resource shifter. Entrepreneurs shift the productivity from lower to 
higher within the economy by adding new wealth-creating capacity to existing resources and 
achieving superior growth and wealth creation (Drucker, 1985).  
 
• 
The entrepreneur as a breakthrough innovator. According to Baumol (1968), entrepreneurs 
can lead disruptive or breakthrough innovation in technology, business or marketing 
approaches. 
 
Research regarding entrepreneurship and start-ups has had a growing interest due to the desire 
to maintain economic competitiveness (Bainée, 2020; Audretsch et al., 2006; Mazzarol & Reboud, 
2020). Additionally, the radical shift of values, such as sifting to the knowledge economy and the 
rising of information and communications technologies (ICT) encourage the importance of 
entrepreneurship as a driver of such transformation (Mazzarol & Reboud, 2020). As a result, 
entrepreneurial research has been conducted on diverse aspects due to their potential 
contribution to economic development.

Although Entrepreneurship is a complex and multifaced concept, Shane (2003) defined it in the 
following terms: 
 
“Entrepreneurship is an activity that involves the discovery, evaluation and exploitation of 
opportunities to introduce new goods and services, ways of organizing, markets, processes 
and raw materials through organizing efforts that previously had not existed.”  
 
Meanwhile, the OECD-Eurostat provided a theoretical foundation to define entrepreneur, 
entrepreneurship, and entrepreneurial activity as follows (Mazzarol & Reboud, 2020): 
 
• 
“Entrepreneurs – are those persons (business owners) who seek to generate value through the 
creation or expansion of economic activity by identifying and exploiting new products, processes 
or markets.”   
 
• 
“Entrepreneurial activity – is enterprising human action in pursuit of the generation of value 
through the creation or expansion of economic activity by identifying and exploiting new products, 
processes or markets.”  
 
• 
“Entrepreneurship – is the phenomenon associated with entrepreneurial activity.”  
 
Because of the complexity and versatility of entrepreneurship, there are various directions and 
approaches in entrepreneurial research. The overview of the entrepreneurial research is

Sarasvathy (2001) identified and developed a decision model of the entrepreneurial process 
through the theory of effectuation. This theory articulated that entrepreneurs involve the 
effectuation process rather than the causation process in their venture creation (Sarasvathy, 
2001). The causation process and effectuation process were defined as the following:  
“Causation processes take a particular effect as given and focus on selecting between means 
to create that effect. Effectuation processes take a set of means as given and focus on selecting 
between possible effects that can be created with that set of means” (Sarasvathy, 2001). 
Figure 3 shows the process of effectuation. Effectuation begins with the means available to the 
entrepreneur and builds the business, changing over time due to the contingencies of means and 
human aspirations. The choice of means is determined by the balance between the risks and 
losses arising from the choices and what is controllable (Sarasvathy, 2001). In other words, 
entrepreneurs do not predict the future and prepare for it but rather iterate their decision-making 
based on the balance between the set of means they have and the risks and losses resulting from 
their actions.

2.1.3 Student entrepreneurship 
Student entrepreneurs are focused on this research. Higher education institutions have 
challenges in this role, and the need for entrepreneurial universities has been emphasized 
(Etzkowitz et al., 2000). In previous research, there have been many types of research 
investigating the impact of university spinoffs. Higher education is regarded as an ideal place to 
generate hi-tech stat-ups. However, recent studies have started focusing on student 
entrepreneurship rather than high-tech university spinoffs led by faculty members (Longva, 2021). 
In fact, a case study of MIT spinoffs found that students, not faculty members, played the leading 
role in the initial establishment of all the spinoffs in the sample and played a critical role in their 
subsequent commercialization (Hayter et al., 2017).  
 
According to Marchand & Antoine (2015), the definition of Student Entrepreneurs is less, and they 
define the student entrepreneur as “an individual attending award classes at university and 
conducting innovative revenue-generating entrepreneurial activities.” However, many higher 
education institutions encourage entrepreneurial activities by establishing entrepreneurial or 
innovation hubs, and they are open to students with innovative ideas. Therefore, the students 
have more chances to explore and recognize the business opportunities during their education 
program, such as personal activities, thesis, etc. Therefore, in this research, the student 
entrepreneur is defined as an individual initiating a start-up project during their higher education 
program. 
 
Jones et al. (2017) evaluated that entrepreneurial education gave significant values to students, 
especially entrepreneurial content, such as entrepreneurial opportunity recognition, networking, 
coaching, leadership, effectuation/bricolage, and entrepreneurial growth (Jones et al., 2017). 
Then, entrepreneurial education can be considered as an effective way to grow students’ 
entrepreneurial skills. There are some models and theories proposed regarding the student start-
up process. 
 
This section will identify elements and relationships related to student entrepreneurship by 
investigating existing research on student entrepreneurs. It is acknowledged that student start-
ups do not behave as same as real-life start-ups in some points, such as financial investments 
and risk-taking (Ilonen et al., 2018). Therefore, it is valuable to identify the factors and processes 
for the student start-ups. In particular, this research dealt with processes, stages, and identities 
closely related to this study and existing research on student entrepreneurs.  
 
 
Actors in student entrepreneurship 
Longva (2021) conducted a qualitative study of the relationship between social networks in the 
creation of student ventures, identifying three internal elements: curricular activities, co-curricular 
activities, and university infrastructure, and three external elements: industry, incubation, and 
public support.  
The internal ecosystem supports professional knowledge, advice, identity-building, social support, 
and recruitment for student ventures. Meanwhile, the external ecosystem works for them in 
industry knowledge, advice, validation, and financial support (Longva, 2021). Bell and Bell (2016) 
also revealed the entrepreneurship center in a university play an important role for student 
entrepreneur to acquire confidence through communication with mentors. Brenznitz and Zhang  
(2019) identified the impacts on student start-ups through quantitative research. They insisted

As another aspect, some researchers studied student entrepreneurs' family or friends as actors

Identity of student entrepreneur 
A distinctive element in student entrepreneurship is the existence of different identities within 
student entrepreneurs as students and entrepreneurs. According to Down and Warren (2008), 
identity is not a stable individual unit but is constituted through ongoing interactions between 
individual resources and the context in which the individual interacts. The formation of a unique 
identity is inevitable for student entrepreneurs who are forced to interact with the social networks 
and actors necessary to become entrepreneurs. As the research focusing on student 
entrepreneurs, Nielsen and Lassen (2012) showed that identities are multiple and influence the 
process of entrepreneurship, suggesting that student entrepreneurs' identities are essential in 
understanding the student start-up process. Nielsen and Lassen (2012) pointed out that although 
identity is an important factor in identifying one's means in entrepreneurial effectuation theory, it 
is not sufficiently mentioned in its changes and unfolding in the effectuation process. Nielsen and 
Lassen (2012)  interviewed 10 student entrepreneurs using a narrative approach and theorized 
how they constructed their identities in the effectuation process based on their stories.

In the beginning, student entrepreneurs are involved in entrepreneurial activities and start to 
recognize themselves as entrepreneurs. Although the process of being involved in 
entrepreneurial activities varies from each student, they initiate their journey as an entrepreneur 
through processes, which unfold from the interrelation between belief-driven and action-driven 
processes  (Nielsen & Lassen, 2012). Then, students are thrown into a complex situation of 
multiple identities as a student and an entrepreneur. Students construct their identity as an 
entrepreneur by asking “who am I as an entrepreneur” and confirm whether or not 
entrepreneurship is their thing (Nielsen & Lassen, 2012). Furthermore, students obtain insight 
into who they are as entrepreneurs through interactions with various people inside and outside 
the university. And through this process, students continue to discover new aspects of their unique 
entrepreneurial selves (Nielsen & Lassen, 2012). In addition, the feedback from others gives the 
students meaning to their entrepreneurial identity-making. Positive feedback from the audience 
makes the students keep present stories of themselves as entrepreneurs, in contrast, negative

of “who am I as an entrepreneur” in the process of constant feedback. Then the student initiates 
new entrepreneurial acts.

Furthermore, Nielsen & Gartner proposed the framework dealing with the multi-identity of

the degree to which the multiple identities of student and entrepreneur are handled at the level 
within the individual entrepreneur. In other words, it indicates whether student entrepreneurs act 
as either a student or entrepreneur identity when performing a certain action (low plurality) or try 
to embrace both student and entrepreneur identities equally (high plurality) (Nielsen & Gartner, 
2017). Student entrepreneurs well integrated into the university context are interpreted as 
having a high “university context.” In contrast, those whose entrepreneurial behavior does not 
match or conflict with the direction of the university are considered to have a low “university 
context” (Nielsen & Gartner, 2017) . As figure 6, the plural identity of student entrepreneurs is 
categorized in four types, which is identity following, identity differentiating, identity devouring, 
and identity expanding. “Identity following” and “Identity differentiation” are ways in which 
students integrate their entrepreneurial identities into the university context. In "Identity-
following," integration is achieved by placing the entrepreneurial identity into the university 
context, and students conduct their behavior as students while figuring out their entrepreneurial 
identity to a high degree. “Identity differentiation” is accomplished by differentiating between the 
respective identities of a student and entrepreneur. In this case, for example, a student 
entrepreneur will perform their identity as a student when they are processing university tasks 
and perform their entrepreneurial identity in their other free time. “Identity devouring” and 
“identity expanding” reflect a situation in which entrepreneurial students are unable to integrate 
into the university context and resist in one way or another. “Identity devouring" indicates a 
situation in which identity as a student is eroded by the student's entrepreneurial identity,

Meanwhile, the student’s motivation to start a business is crucial to explain the process of student

major perspectives: considering the actors in this particular environment of students and handling

strategies according to their individual priorities.

2.2 Sustainability lenses 
This section deals with another important concept in this research, “sustainability.” In recent years, 
sustainability has increased its value at various levels, such as individuals, academia, industry, 
and governments. Therefore, the sustainability aspect can not be ignored as an element that 
affects the entrepreneurial process. The following section reveals the concept of sustainability 
and the relationship between sustainability and entrepreneurship. 
 
2.2.1 The evolution of the sustainability agenda 
Although the concept of sustainability has become popular in recent years, the definition has 
changed over time. In fact, “sustainable development” has become a buzzword in the 
contemporary world, and people use it without thinking of its actual meaning (Pisani, 2006). 
Sustainability gained international attention with the “Brundtland Report” and “Our Common 
Future,” presented at the United Nations General Assembly in 1987. At that time, sustainable 
development was defined as “...development that meets the needs of the present without 
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” (World Commission on 
Environment and Development, 1987). It focuses on the needs and interests of humankind and 
is concerned with securing global equity for future generations to ensure that all humankind can 
achieve their basic needs by reallocating resources to poorer countries and encouraging their 
economic growth. There are several crucial antecedents behind the birth of this concept. The 
Club of Rome, in its report “The Limits to Growth,” published in 1972, indicated that the growth of 
economic output would be suddenly undermined by the rate and scale of resource use and the 
expansion of pollution (Meadows et al., 1972). Meadow et al. (1972) shed light on the issue of 
resource consumption, which is justified by economic growth, by modeling the relationship 
between resource finiteness and economic growth and population growth. The concept of 
sustainable development was coined in 1980 by the World Conservation Strategy, a partnership 
of three environmental non-governmental organizations: IUCN, WWF, and UNEP. Recognizing 
that the world's natural resources have been exploited in tandem with global economic growth, 
the World Conservation Strategy aims to prevent the exploitation of natural resources and 
adverse environmental impacts through cooperation among nations (IUCN, 1980). In 1992, the 
United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (Earth Summit in Rio) developed 
a program called Agenda 21, which is the blueprint for sustainability in the 21st century (United 
Nations Sustainable Development, 1992). The Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) were 
adopted at the Millennium Summit in 2000, which have covered economic growth, life expectancy, 
health and disease reduction, education, water, sanitization and hygiene, hunger, and so on 
(Jolly, 2004). However, MDG was claimed as mirrored more traditional international development 
discourses, and the aspirations associated with sustainable development were categorized within 
just one of the goals (Smith, 2019). Following the Earth Summit in Rio 1992, The World Summit 
on Sustainable Development (WSSD) in Johannesburg 2002 was designated to define means to 
fight global sustainability issues and to set timeframe and specific targets for the implementation 
of Agenda 21. The civil society described the summit as a missed opportunity to come up with a 
meaningful plan of concrete targets, timeframes, and funding for the implementation of Agenda 
21. However, it was a precious opportunity to create new partnerships and enhance the 
community and organization working on the ground (Frantzius, 2004). At the Earth Summit in Rio 
2012, the UN's 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development (United Nations, 2015) was conceived 
as a more modern international policy framework, and the associated 17 Sustainable 
Development Goals (SDGs) were established. The 17 SDGs and 169 sub-targets are the most 
comprehensive global agenda ever developed. While poverty reduction and other vulnerability 
==PAGE:26==
 
18 
situations remain non-negotiable priorities, the UN insists that the other integrated and indivisible 
targets promote a shift away from unsustainable consumption and production patterns. (Smith, 
2019) 
 
As described above, over time, sustainability and sustainable development have become part of 
the international agenda and have come to occupy an important place in national policies. The 
SDGs have also become an unmissable element in academia and industry, and the link between 
sustainability and business has become stronger. In the following sections, research in the field 
of sustainable entrepreneurship, which has been gaining more attention in recent years, is 
introduced.

2.2.2 Sustainability entrepreneurship 
The entrepreneur, with the emergence of the concept of sustainable development, is expected as 
a driver for not only economic aspects but also a vehicle that can lead economic sectors towards 
sustainable development (Dean & McMullen, 2007; Shepherd & Patzelt, 2011). Shepherd and 
Patzelt (2011) insist that successful entrepreneurs are required to include and adapt sustainability 
within their business strategy. Consequently, sustainability can be a driver for start-ups in their 
business in recent years. At the same time, entrepreneurship research is also extending the 
traditional concept of entrepreneurship from economic value to non-economic value creation. As 
a result, the concept of what is now called sustainability entrepreneurship is attracting significant 
attention (Muñoz & Cohen, 2017). However, there are diverse definitions of sustainable 
entrepreneurship research due to its novelty and lack of theoretical frameworks (Sarango-
Lalangui et al., 2018). Researchers who emphasize sustainable management define sustainable 
entrepreneurship as "the continuing commitment by business to behave ethically and contribute to 
economic development while improving the quality of life of the workforce, their families, local 
communities, society and the world at large, as well as future generations" (Crals & Vereeck, 2005). 
Sustainable development is considered as a unique business opportunity to yield returns for not 
only large companies but also SMEs (Crals & Vereeck, 2005). Furthermore, sustainable 
entrepreneurs are thought to be agents of change who are committed to seeking a balance 
between economic development, social welfare, and environmental protection (Belz & Binder, 
2017). From a literature review on sustainability entrepreneurship, Terán-Yépez et al. (2020) 
distinguish between CSR and sustainability entrepreneurship, with the former being the goal of 
achieving economic benefits and social and environmental activities being incidental to the core 
of the business, and the latter focusing on sustainable principles as an integral part of business 
value creation. This study utilizes the above interpretation and defines a sustainability start-up as 
a start-up with sustainability at the core of its business and a non-sustainability start-up as a start-
up that utilizes sustainability solely as an adjunct to its business.  
 
One model that comprehensively addresses the value that a company should aim for is “Triple 
bottom line.” The triple bottom line was coined by John Elkington, and the idea elaborates that 
company’s long-term success should be measured on the basis not only of the economic aspect 
but also of the social and environmental aspect (Elkington, 1998) (Figure 7). According to 
Elkington (1998), each Ps are coined with following meaning (Almici, 2022): 
 
• 
People include the employees involved in a company’s activity and the wider community 
where a corporation operates; the general aim is to ensure social justice. With reference 
to “people,” a Triple Bottom Line approach implies the development of business 
producing mutual benefits for shareholders, employees, and the community as well; for 
example, a company committed to the Triple Bottom Line model would not use a youthful 
workforce, would pay fair salaries, and would secure working conditions. 
 
• 
Planet refers to the environment upon which companies should reduce their ecological 
footprint as much as possible; the general goal is to guarantee environmental quality. The 
adoption of a Triple Bottom Line model requires companies to safeguard the natural 
setting by benefiting it or, at least, by reducing the potential damage due to the company’s 
activity. 
 
==PAGE:28==
 
20 
• 
Profit that identifies the traditional Bottom Line should be implemented aiming at 
economic prosperity, avoiding damages for the other two dimensions (people and 
environment). 
 
Sustainable entrepreneurs embrace value creation based on the triple bottom line. There are 
three aspects; (1) environmental aspects, taking into consideration the long-term protection and 
reduction of negative effects, (2) social aspects, where attention to customers, stakeholders, 
partners, workers, and community is given, and (3) economic aspects, which rely on economic 
performance (Aghelie et al., 2016; Urbaniec, 2018). Therefore, the triple bottom line should be 
considered as a measure of a company's commitment to sustainability and as an important value 
for the company and its entrepreneurs to refer to in their start-up activities. In other words, each 
aspect of the triple bottom line functions as an actor in entrepreneurial activity that can positively 
or negatively influence start-ups and entrepreneurs as external motivation factors (Simón-Moya 
& Rodríguez-García, 2021).

3.1 Research design and methodology 
This study aims to identify internal and external actors related to student start-ups and investigate 
the relationship among them. Furthermore, this research articulates how the sustainability aspect 
affects student entrepreneurs. According to Young (1939), social research is defined as “a 
scientific undertaking which, by means of logical and systematized techniques, aims to: 1) discover 
new facts or verify and test old facts; 2) analyze their sequences, interrelationships, and causal 
explanations which were derived within an appropriate theoretical frame of reference; 3) develop new 
scientific tools, concepts, and theories which would facilitate reliable and valid study of human 
behavior.” Hence, this study is a type of social research that finds causal relationships and 
theories by extracting and analyzing facts from the social behavior of start-up activities by student 
entrepreneurs. This section describes the selection of research methods to address the research 
questions. There are several perspectives to design research, and each perspective is argued in 
the following. 
 
First perspective is the relationship between research and theory. There are two types of 
relationships, which are “deductive” and “inductive.” In the deductive approach, the researcher 
deduces a hypothesis based on the knowledge in a particular domain and theoretical 
considerations in relation to that domain. Then, their deduced hypothesis must be subjected to 
empirical scrutiny (Bryman, 2012). In summary, the hypothesis deduced from knowledge come 
first and drive the process of gathering data. In contrast, the inductive approach has the opposite 
direction from the deduction approach. With an inductive stance, a theory is the outcome of the 
research. In other words, the process of induction involves drawing generalizable inferences out 
observations. Figure 8 captures the essence of the difference between the inductive approach 
and deductive approach. However, Bryman (2012) suggests that these distinguish are not clear 
and straightforward and they may be better thought of as tendencies. 
 
Second perspective is an epistemological perspective. From this perspective, there are two 
positions depending on what is regarded as acceptable knowledge in a discipline. In particular, a 
central issue in this context is whether the social world can and should be studied in the same 
ways as natural science. “Positivism” is an epistemological position affirming the importance of 
imitating natural science. Meanwhile, “interpretivism” affirms the view that the subject matter of

Third perspective is an ontological perspective. From this perspective, the central question is

3.2 Data collection 
There are six sources of evidence in the case study: documentation, archival records, interviews, 
direct observations, participant observation, and physical artifact (Yin, 2018). Documentation is 
useful in verifying the information and provides specific details related to information from other 
sources. However, many people have admitted the potential overreliance on documentation since 
researchers may misinterpret the information from documentation (Yin, 2018). Archival records 
take the form of data files and records, such as census data. The usefulness of this evidence is 
varied from case studies, and the records can be important in extensive retrieval and quantitative 
research. The interview is one of the most important sources of the case study. Interviews help 
in qualitative research by suggesting explanations for important events as well as insights that 
reflect participants' perspectives (Yin, 2018). Observational evidence from direct observations is 
often useful and supplemental in providing additional information in the study (Yin, 2018). 
Participant observation can provide unusual evidence through participation. However, the 
reliability of the study may be threatened by the potential biases through participating in research 
objects (Becker, 1958). Physical artifacts are, for instance, technological devices, a tool or 
instruments, a work of art, or some other physical evidence. They are helpful in some cases that 
are relevant to the case study (Yin, 2018). From the above overview of the evidence in the case 
study, this research adopted the interview as a primal source. Meanwhile, observational data was 
not employed for this study because of the difficulty of identifying specific timing or important 
events for this study to observe. Interview can provide detailed information related to student 
start-ups’ activities, and they are effective in generating the theory articulating the relationship 
and causal connections between actors. Additionally, this research collected documentation as 
supplemental data, and they were used to enhance the data validity from interviews by 
triangulating each data. 
 
Quantitative research, in general, prefers to structural approach to maximize the reliability and 
validity of the data. In contrast, the researchers in qualitative research are greater interested in 
the interviewees’ points of view. As a result, qualitative interviewing tends to be flexible, 
responding to acquire rich, detailed answers. The interview in qualitative research has two main 
types: unstructured interview and semi-structured interview. Unstructured interview can create 
free and easy environment with interviewees through the conversation and be useful to map out 
overall information. There may be just a single question that the interviewer asks. Meanwhile, the 
researcher, in semi-structured interview, uses the interview guide with a list of questions or 
specific topics to be covered. However, interviewees are allowed to answer freely, and questions 
that are not included in the guide may be asked to follow up on the interviewees’ replies (Bryman, 
2012). This study employed semi-structured interviews because insightful information about the 
real experiences of student start-ups is useful, and because there are no circumstances under 
which unstructured interviews should be conducted, such as extreme attributes of the 
interviewees.  
 
Sampling interviewee is also an essential step in the qualitative research. Most sampling in 
qualitative research entails purposive sampling, in other words, samples are selected based on 
sampling criteria that allow the research questions to be answered, and samples are not selected 
on a random basis (Bryman, 2012). In order to select cases or contexts, the researchers establish 
sampling criteria to address the research question (Bryman, 2012).  
 
==PAGE:36==
 
28 
In this study, the sampling target was student entrepreneurs or recent graduates who started a 
start-up or a base project when they were students and graduated in recent years, in relation to 
the research question. Additionally, in order to conduct a comparative study on sustainability, 
sampling was conducted from both start-ups that had sustainability as their main goal and those 
that did not, in terms of sustainability entrepreneurship, as identified in the literature review. 
Furthermore, some detailed sampling criteria were set in this research. This research aims to 
capture the relationship between student start-ups and surrounding actors. Therefore, student 
start-ups should have access to a variety of support inside and outside the higher educational 
institution. As a result, student start-ups were selected from the Danish technological university, 
recognized as an international leading university in technical and natural sciences, and renowned 
for the business-oriented approaches focusing on sustainability. They have a hi-tech research 
environment and innovation hub fostering student and academic entrepreneurs since 2013, and 
they have programs that support start-ups, such as acceleration, incubation, and funding 
program. In addition, there are project-based courses related to entrepreneurship, and many 
start-ups have been generated from this university. Meanwhile, Denmark is recognized as a 
sustainable advance country in the world (Sachs et al., 2022). Denmark is also located in the 
leading position related to entrepreneurship (Ács et al., 2019). Therefore, Denmark is a suitable 
sample to investigate actors surrounding student start-ups from sustainability and entrepreneurial 
perspectives.

3.3 Data analysis 
The coding method was adopted as a qualitative data analysis method for this study. A code in 
qualitative research is a word or short description that symbolically assigns a summative, salient, 
essence-capturing, and/or evocative attribute to collected data (Saldana, 2013). The coding 
enables qualitative researchers to explore patterns, categories, and theory by finding the links 
between codes, and it transfers the data toward the thematic, conceptual, and theoretical 
(Saldana, 2013). Although the suitable coding process is different depending on the cases, this 
research adopted the following two cycles coding method, referred to as a basic coding method 
(Saldana, 2013).  
 
First Cycle coding method 
1. Attribute coding 
● 
Attribute coding is the notation of basic information regarding the fieldwork 
setting, characteristics of samples, and other variables of the data. 
2. Structural coding 
● 
Structural coding applies a conceptual phrase representing a topic to a part 
of the data related to research questions, and they are collected together for 
further coding and analysis. 
3. Descriptive coding 
● 
Descriptive coding is the summarized word or short phrase that identifies 
the topics. 
4. In Vivo coding 
● 
In Vivo coding extracts the actual word or a short phrase used by 
participants themselves from the recorded data. 
 
Second Cycle coding method

1.
Eclectic coding
●
Eclectic coding is the strategic transition from the first cycle to the second
cycle based on the learnings of the experience. It is followed by the Second

2.
Pattern coding
●
Pattern coding is a way of grouping explanatory or inferential codes into a

3.4 The quality of research 
Although reliability and validity are the most critical criteria in quantitative research, there are 
some arguments whether how to apply the criteria for qualitative research. Researchers proposed 
various criteria from each different stance. Mason (2002) argued reliability, validity, and 
generalizability as measures of the quality, rigor, and wider potential of research. In contrast, 
some researchers have insisted that qualitative research should be evaluated by different criteria 
from quantitative research. Lincoln and Guba (1985) suggest criteria that assess the 
trustworthiness 
of 
qualitative 
research: 
credibility, 
transferability, 
dependability, 
and 
confirmability. Each criterion corresponds to internal validity, external validity, reliability, and 
objectivity in quantitative research. This study employed the criteria proposed by Lincoln and 
Guba, and discussed the quality of this research based on them. 
Credibility 
Credibility of the account the researcher reached is crucial to be accepted by others. This study 
secured credibility by the approaches below. This research collected not only the data from 
interviewees of student start-ups but also documentation, non-verbal findings from interviews, 
and samples from an objective position, such as faculty members, as triangulation. Additionally, 
the interview data and findings from the author were confirmed by interviewees to enhance 
credibility as member checking or respondent validation (Bryman,  2012; Lincoln & Guba , 1985) 
. The interview guide was used to minimize the author's subjective bias for all interviews and to 
increase reliability.  
Transferability 
Transferability means whether findings from the research can apply to other studies. It is also 
argued as generalizability. This study provided detailed and rich descriptions of results and 
findings to enhance transferability. Such “thick description” (Geertz, 1973) enabled others to judge 
whether the finding is possible to transfer to other contexts.  
Dependability 
Dependability is according to reliability in quantitative research, and Lincoln and Guba (1985) 
propose “auditing” approach to secure dependability. This research described the process of 
research, such as procedures of decisions of methodology, sampling, analysis, and so on. 
Additionally, providing the full transcript of interviews was submitted to enable auditors to validate 
the data. 
==PAGE:42==
 
34 
 
Confirmability 
The researcher should show personal values or theoretical biases have not affected the conduct 
or findings of the research. According to Lincoln and Guba (1985), one of the objectives of 
auditors is establishing confirmability. This research had the process of discussing the research 
design and findings with supervisors. In addition, as shown above, using other objective data 
sources and keeping the collected data are effective for establishing confirmability.  
 
However, there are some limitations related to the above criteria in qualitative research because 
of the realistic possibility of auditing and incomplete elimination of subjectivity, and changes in 
circumstances due to changes over time. The qualitative approach can combine to enhance 
credibility by employing other design strategies and findings for triangulation. Besides, 
quantitative research, such as the research methods using socio-metric questionnaires, can 
articulate the static pattern in social life (Bryman, 2012). Therefore, the research can suggest a 
complete map of stakeholders of student start-ups by hiring such quantitative approaches.

Student entrepreneur

4.1.2 University ecosystem 
The coding of the data led to three main actors in the university ecosystem. These actors are 
related to universities, faculties, courses, and infrastructure. Informants from the interviews and 
document data of student start-ups show actors in the university ecosystem provide various 
supports to student start-ups. For example, there are opportunities to initiate entrepreneurial 
projects, and they help students’ activities in various ways. Additionally, higher education 
ecosystem is easily accessed since they don’t require an additional fee and are open to students 
who want to receive support. The findings related to each actor are elaborate further below 
sections. 
 
Curricular activities 
Curricular activities are fundamental activities for students during their higher education. Various 
courses provide entrepreneurial education, practical topic, and connection with private 
companies. For example, the university under study offers an entrepreneurship class called X-
tech, where students form teams to create new business ideas and prototypes from topics 
gathered from researchers and private companies over a 13-week course period. More than 250

other similar courses, and students teamed up and initiated their start-up journeys from them. 
SS4 and NS7 started their start-ups from X-tech (but in different semesters). 
“We were actually a part of the X-tech. And we were given a case. We had a project owner, 
who was called first, want us to move into sustainable water efficient agriculture. Did a lot 
of my research and then we found our little niche in fish feed.” - SS4 
In the case of SS2 and NS4, they took several courses with the same start-up ideas. They utilized

“When we started with this idea, it started the first semester, we took the class that was called tech

4.1.3 External ecosystem 
The coding articulated actors outside of the university ecosystem, and this study recognize them 
as an external ecosystem. There are three actors, public support, industry, and incubation, 
identified surrounding student start-ups. 
 
Public support 
For student start-ups, securing a budget for the development and salary is crucial to developing 
their start-up further. Student start-ups do not have a large-scale budget, such as research 
projects, compared to academic spinoff start-ups. Therefore, the public support is necessary to 
secure money with low risks. For example, SS5 acquired small amounts of soft funding to develop 
a prototype. CVR means “the Danish state’s master register of information about businesses.” 
(CVR-Central Business Register, 2022) 
 
“We got Microgrant. We started with the one for 25 K [DKK] where you don't have to have 
CVR because we didn't have CVR at a time.” - SS5 
 
Furthermore, securing a large scale of money is crucial to pay salary or proceed with development 
after graduating from the school. In this case, start-ups exploited public support called 
Innofounder. It allows financial support for monthly salary and initial prototyping fees for start-ups. 
Consequently, student start-ups decided to continue their start-ups without financial concerns. 
 
“We participated in Innofounder, which is organized by the Innovation Fund Denmark, they 
had monthly courses, but it was not too intensive. I think 30,000 Danish kroner at the 
beginning plus 15,000 per month. The idea is that somebody who finished education can apply 
for that. So it's like a salary for them to work on their own projects. So they gave us office 
space, and then they gave us this monthly stipend to spend on the company. And we got it for 
12 months.” – NS1 
 
From the above, public support makes student entrepreneurs less risky and makes the 
entrepreneurial option a more rational choice.

it was super cool.” - SS3

4.2.1 Phase 1. Student project 
Most of the student entrepreneurial activities were initiated from the school curriculum, such as 
the master thesis or courses student entrepreneurs took during their education program. During

the output along with the course objectives. NS2 reflected on the deliverable for the course.  
“We need to make this weird business report kind of spider web.” - NS2 
This suggested that curricular activities don’t aim to not only initiating start-ups but also give the 
theoretical and practical knowledge for entrepreneurship and the deliverables will be in line with

4.3 Features of sustainability student start-ups

Sustainability work as a driver for student start-ups. In the early phase, student start-ups,

4.3.2 Difficulties for working on sustainability 
Sustainability student start-ups recalled inherent challenges from their experience. Sustainability 
is hard to quantify as actual values, and sustainable solutions take a cost than general solutions. 
As a result, they struggled to convince customers to purchase their product regardless of their 
high price with non-tangible sustainable value. SS3 reflected that actual customers choose 
cheaper choices in the existing business. 
“People just want it to seem sustainable and overly have sustainability. And they're never 
willing to pay for it. Like it's not a thing. They there was no one. It's really weird that people 
want to pay extra. Because they don't see it. They don't feel it. They don't. So it's not really a 
benefit for us that we're sustainable.” - SS3 
SS4 also noted that while sustainability is overestimated, it must eventually meet the business 
value of customers. 
“The Sustainable part, like the values of sustainability can be enhance a bit too much versus 
the value and actually gives to in customer. […] I think the overall enhancement of the value 
of sustainability is no brainer, it's good. But it's not a no brainer letting exactly be convertible 
into sales.” - SS4 
FM3 mentioned the difficulty of dealing with sustainability because of the lack of standard and 
common language to measure sustainability impact. 
“The general challenge in a start-up context, and the student context, and an industry context, 
is defining standards for when there’s something sustainable, what is sustainable, and getting 
a common language for measuring not just sustainability as environmental impact, but as in 
societal impact?” – FM3 
The above examples show that sustainability start-ups face the challenge of quantifying the value 
of sustainability and resolving the trade-off between sustainability and business values such as 
price. Meanwhile, the code insisted that sustainability could demotivate students. SS1 mentioned 
his experience that the change in the scope of sustainability impact demotivated him from the 
beginning. As the technology developed, an entrepreneur was discouraged when the sustainable 
benefits differed from his own interests. 
“I thought, initially I had an idea that eco print could reduce a lot of co2, for example. But 
later, we figured out that that's probably not really the case. And what it mainly does is reduce 
pesticides. And I knew from the beginning would also reduce pesticides, but a big, like value 
proposition for me was also kind of the climate thing. So that kind of demotivated me a bit 
into.” - SS1 
Additionally, FM1, as a faculty member, observed students could not develop ideas because they 
were over stack into a specific sustainability area. It shows sustainability can decline the creativity 
of the student by narrowing the students’ thinking way.

5. Discussion
This chapter has two main parts for the discussion based on the result. The first section is 
allocated the discussion based on the proposed framework from the coding analysis, which is 
associated with the first research question. It is also dedicated to discussing the findings relative 
to the process of the identity transition from students to entrepreneurs through the start-up 
creation process. The second section addresses the effects of the sustainability perspective to 
answer the second research question. 
5.1 The proposed framework and process 
As this research shows in chapter 4, a holistic and integrated framework was proposed based 
on the coding analysis. This framework is the outcome that corresponds to the first 
research question; “How do internal and external actors of the student start-up affect 
their start-up activities?” This framework integrates the actors influencing student start-up 
development and consists of four categories; “start-ups,” “university ecosystem,” “external 
ecosystem,” and “values.” This framework provides an overall and holistic understanding 
relative to student start-up. It can be treated as a fundamental common language and 
recognition in student entrepreneurship research. This outcome has novelty and value as 
entrepreneurial research since they supplement the lack of a comprehensive perspective 
about the student start-up development noted in existing studies. 
The actors and interactions between extracted actors and the student start-up are consistent with 
the result of existing studies, thus this outcome affirms and expands the existing studies. In 
addition to the proposed framework from Longva (Longva, 2021), this study integrates two 
perspectives which are the start-up and values. This allows dealing with both the internal elements 
of a startup, such as the entrepreneurs themselves and their co-founders, as well as high-level 
concepts such as values, on the same framework. For instance, this framework can elaborate on 
the relationship among a student entrepreneur, co-founders and curricular activities regarding 
team dynamics. Student entrepreneurs meet co-founders in curricular activities. However, the 
team formation is conducted randomly, as a result, student entrepreneur faces the challenge of 
team dynamics. It can be interpreted that the curricular activities are considered as an opportunity 
for student start-ups to acquire co-founders, And such contingent team formation may improve 
team diversity and enhance creativity (Sonnenberg, 1991). However, the majority of student 
entrepreneurs cited team dynamics as an important aspect of their start-ups, revealing that they 
have difficulty in building relationships with their co-founders. This suggests that frequent changes 
in team members during the idea development phase and teams with shallowly reliable members 
may be a risk for start-ups, as existing studies have shown that team fluidity has a negative impact 
on task execution (Summers et al., 2012). These series of influences are shown in Figure 11. 
Thus, the comprehensive framework proposed in this study can make events in student 
entrepreneurship explainable. 
==PAGE:65==

In addition, the finding that many student entrepreneurs generate the idea underlying their 
startups from communication with outside researchers or industry suggests that startups by 
student entrepreneurs have different characteristics from academic spin-offs. In particular, the 
sustainable student entrepreneurs started from their desire to contribute to the environment or 
human society and sought to transfer external technologies into commercial ideas. This implies 
that student entrepreneurs function as intermediaries to connect and commercialize sustainability 
with existing research. Thus, companies and researchers can leverage student entrepreneurs to 
commercialize their proprietary technologies. However, if the business ideas generated by 
student entrepreneurs do not fit the direction and portfolio of the firm, they may not be adopted 
as a corporate strategy (Hanson, 2005). This challenge can be recognized from the interview of 
SS4, where a company provided a primitive idea of the technology but did not adopt that direction.

entrepreneurs inside and outside the university, this study also shows that providing feedback 
and assets works effectively, as shown in existing studies. Furthermore, this study discusses the 
future direction that should be taken from the results. The finding that internal and external 
university support is useful for student entrepreneurs in terms of location, development and 
operational funding, and mentoring opportunities is consistent with existing research (Bell & Bell,

Manik et al. (2016), self-efficacy has a positive influence on entrepreneurial intentions. Therefore, 
it is thought that a relationship can be established in which a push from the environment 
surrounding a student start-up increases the students’ self-efficacy and encourages them to 
choose to become a student start-ups. On the other hand, the high self-efficacy in the initial stage

“fallacy” and “overestimation.” How to evaluate student startups is a topic for future research. 
Multiple patterns were observed in how student entrepreneurs, operating between the two 
identities of student and entrepreneur, approached their respective activities. This result is 
consistent with existing research (Nielsen & Gartner, 2017) that has shown that there are patterns 
in how student entrepreneurs prioritize their activities, such as incorporating entrepreneurial 
activities as part of the curriculum or giving up on the curriculum. Whether this pattern of selection 
is due to individual experiences and attributes or the surrounding environment is one possible 
direction for future research. In addition, prioritization is not limited to an individual decision-
making process but can also be interpreted as a decision-making process for the entire start-up 
team (Ilonen , Heinonen, & Stenholm, 2018). One start-up team prioritized the study over start-
ups with the agreement of the entire team in order for all members to write their dissertations at 
the same time. However, not all members are always in the same situation, and each individual 
may be in a different situation. The above suggests that prioritization in student entrepreneurs or 
student start-ups needs to be treated as a more complex systems issue, not just an individual 
attribute or situation. 
The flexibility of universities and public institutions is also identified as an important factor for 
student start-ups. Student entrepreneurs are often required to participate in full-time start-ups or 
have graduation-related requirements during the process of becoming a full-fledged start-up. This 
program structure is not always in line with the reality of student start-ups and can force students 
to change their learning process. On the other hand, the university in this research allows students 
to participate in start-up activities as a special course, and this flexible course design was shown 
to have a positive impact on student start-ups. As Bauman & Lucy (2021) suggest, this 
demonstrates the need for flexibility and customization in the design of future entrepreneurship 
education programs. 
5.2 Sustainability aspect 
This section discusses the impact of sustainability on student start-ups. Two perspectives were 
acknowledged by comparing the result between sustainability and non-sustainability start-ups. 
One is the impact on the financial aspect, and another is the impact on the opportunity recognition. 
In terms of the impact on the financial aspect of student start-ups, student entrepreneurs reflected 
on how the sustainability aspect affected them in the competition and interaction with advisors 
and customers. Sustainability start-ups mentioned that sustainability enhanced their value if they 
did not have actual products. It suggests that the value of sustainability can lead to increased 
==PAGE:67==
59 
investment opportunities for early-stage start-ups. This result shows that investors are shifting 
from “quick win” investments to more emphasis on social and environmental value (Bocken, 
2015). There may also be a geographical factor, such as Denmark, which is highly interested in 
sustainability (Sachs et al., 2022). However, sustainable student start-ups also suggested that 
they struggled to add value to convince their customers. In the commercialization phase, the cost 
becomes a more crucial factor in convincing customers. As SS3 and SS4 mentioned, they had to 
consider additional values or practical values to satisfy the customers because their products take 
higher costs than general products due to their sustainable approaches. Consequently, a further 
challenge facing sustainability start-ups is “Profitability.” Sustainability start-ups start with social 
and environmental challenges, starting with their ideals and gradually making them feasible as a 
business. However, during the commercialization phase, customers have a high interest in cost, 
and the product must be persuasive. Besides, there is a problem that sustainable impact is difficult 
to be quantified. Quantifying the effects of sustainability is not simple; for example, a solution to 
one problem may have a negative impact on another. Therefore, dealing with such complexity 
and quantifying requires deeper analysis and serious discussion to deal with such complexity and 
quantifying (Graedel & Allenby, 2010). This shift from “Planet” or “People” to “Profit” is a challenge 
for sustainability start-ups. This challenge is in line with the points pointed out by Keskin et al. 
(2013), such as the uncertainty of sustainability-related issues, the difference between the value 
assumed and the value actually created, and the overlap between customer interests and 
sustainability goals. The above issues suggest two possible further studies in the future. The first 
is how support inside and outside the university will help sustainability startups over the long term 
until they realize the practical value and become profitable. The second is further research to 
quantify the value of sustainability. It may be possible to transfer sustainable value to price by 
making the value of sustainability more objective and comparable. 
This study also realized the impact of sustainability on opportunity recognition. For instance, SS2 
selected the idea that appeared to have the greatest impact on climate change among several 
entrepreneurial ideas. This indicates that the magnitude of the effect on sustainability affects the 
perception of the opportunity. On the other hand, SS1 stated that not being given the sustainability 
effect as assumed reduced his motivation. Since entrepreneurial motivation influences 
opportunity perception (Shane et al., 2003), reduced motivation may result in missed 
entrepreneurial opportunities.  
In addition, it is necessary to pay attention to the validity of the student entrepreneurs’ perception 
of the opportunity. As FM3 points out, sustainability is difficult to quantify correctly, and there is a 
risk of making the wrong decision based on tentative numbers. As FM1 also points out, judging 
entrepreneurial opportunities solely on the basis of sustainability value may lead to missed 
opportunities that have inherent business value. The triple bottom line points to the importance of 
balancing the three factors, and how to correctly evaluate the potential of entrepreneurial 
opportunities while respecting the sustainability factor is a topic for future research in the field of 
sustainable entrepreneurship.

6. Conclusion
In this study, data was collected and analysed from the Danish technical universities that have 
shown a high interest in sustainability, using a qualitative research approach to identify factors 
that influence the early stages of sustainability student start-ups. As a result of the analysis, four 
categories - start-up, internal to the university, external to the university, and value - and a total 
of 12 subcategories were identified, and their relationships are presented in an overarching 
framework. In addition, the student start-up process was presented as a three-step model, and 
its evolution was discussed in relation to the aforementioned framework and identities. In addition, 
the impact of sustainability was discussed, and challenges and future research directions for 
student entrepreneurship in sustainability were highlighted. 
6.1 Contribution 
The contributions in this study are described. First, as an academic contribution, this study 
provides a comprehensive framework and suggests new research directions in the recent high-
profile research areas of student start-ups and sustainability entrepreneurship. The framework 
presented in this study highlights important elements of student start-ups and can serve as a 
fundamental and common language for future in-depth research. It also indicates future research 
questions such as the logic of prioritization as a team and the need for support and quantification 
in sustainability entrepreneurship. This study also suggests the educational impact of student 
entrepreneurship. Many student entrepreneurs initiated from courses and thesis projects and 
transitioned to participating in the university's internal start-up support infrastructure and then to 
external incubators. These results provide insight for institutions of higher education considering 
fostering entrepreneurship in the future regarding how to design programs and how to seamlessly 
connect courses to the university's internal infrastructure.  
At the industry level, the value of student start-ups to industry and the impact of industry on 
student start-ups were also identified. This study shows that student start-ups generate ideas 
from external technologies and themes, and student start-ups can contribute as intermediaries 
that link sustainability with the technology companies have. In addition, the industry can improve 
the self-efficacy of student start-ups and the value of their products by communicating customer 
demands through feedback. 
At the policy level, student start-ups have shown themselves to be worthy of more active support 
as drivers of the sustainability agenda. Furthermore, it is clear that helping student start-ups 
expand in earnest through financial support and incubation through public support can be very 
effective for student start-ups. Sustainable student start-ups, in particular, may spend more time 
achieving commercialization, and public support is more important to them. Besides, there is a 
case in which a student entrepreneur changes the timing of his graduation because of the 
condition of public support. It shows that public support conditions are not appropriate to student 
start-ups' situations and need to review their conditions. 
6.2 Limitation 
In this section, we describe the limitations of this study. As mentioned in chapter 3, this study 
adopted a qualitative approach and the embedded single-case study design. So obtained data 
cannot completely exclude influences such as subjectivity, bias, and context of the case and 
==PAGE:69==
 
61 
analysis units. Therefore, additional studies, such as the deductive approach using quantitative 
research methods or case studies with multiple cases, are useful to evaluate the validity and 
generalizability of the findings of this study. 
 
In selecting other cases, criteria such as type of university and country are mentioned. While this 
study focused on a technical university as a common case, different results may be obtained with 
general universities or business universities. In addition, since this study was conducted in 
Denmark, national institutions, customs, and values may also influence the student 
entrepreneurship process. For example, the availability of financial support during the study and 
after graduation may influence the decision for entrepreneurial activity (Uddanneless- og 
Forskningsstyrelsen, 2022). Rules regarding company registration also vary from country to 
country. For instance, Denmark requires a minimum share capital of DKK 40,000 ( Ministry of 
Foreign Affairs of Denmark, 2022), while in Japan, it is possible to register a company with as 
little as JPY 1 (Oda, 2021). Such differences in financial structures may have an impact on student 
entrepreneurs with limited funds. 
 
In addition, this study is based on student start-ups in recent years, making it difficult to rule out 
the influence of short-term trends and contextual factors. Therefore, long-term and periodic data 
acquisition, such as 5 or 10 years, would help distinguish between temporary and universal 
influences.  
 
6.3 Future research 
Finally, future research guidelines are provided. First, a deductive evaluation of the proposed 
model and process using more subjects and quantitative research methods is needed. In addition, 
statistical methods using the quantitative data or mixed research methods can reveal detailed 
insights regarding student start-ups, such as differences in the influence of each element and 
differences in direct and mediating variables. Further research areas identified include the 
decision-making process as a student entrepreneurial team, quantifying the value of 
sustainability, and how to support student start-ups. The above research will provide new insights 
into the emerging field of sustainable student entrepreneurship.

Interview guide for student start-ups 
 
[Instruction] 
Confirmation; recording, data treatment/usage, purpose, anonymity 
 
[Questions] 
• 
Question 1 – General information of the start-up and role of interviewee 
Could you tell me about your startup and what phase you are in now? 
What is the role in you start-up? 
 
• 
Question 2 – The process of interviewee’s start-up 
How to come up with your startup idea? 
Why were you interested in your business/industry? 
How to team up with your team members? 
What is your motivation? 
 
• 
Question 3 – Actors giving positive impact 
What was the most exciting event in your start-up activities? 
When? Why? 
 
• 
Question 4 - Actors giving negative impact 
What was the biggest problem in your startup process? 
When? Why? 
How did you sort out this problem? 
 
• 
Question 5 – Important factor in the start-up process 
What do you think were the key factors in the start-up process? 
 
• 
Question 6 – The effect of program surrounding start-up 
How did external programs work for your startup? 
 
• 
Question 7 – Balancing between student and entrepreneur 
How did you find being an entrepreneur? 
How to make balancing between the students life and entrepreneurial life? 
 
• 
Question 8 – Sustainability aspect 
How is sustainability relevant to your startup?
