Master Thesis
Abstract
The development of offshore wind energy becomes of a great importance for the growing trend of the
wind energy penetration in the power system. Particularly, floating wind energy is at a currently developing
stage with multiple floating structures arising in the wind industry. Floating wind energy has much greater
energy potential than bottom fixed or onshore wind structures due to its higher wind potential. However,
their challenges is what needs to be tackled to make them operate normally. In the current report, a Tension
Leg Platform (TLP) structure carrying a 15 MW wind turbine is analysed based on its dynamic behaviour,
extreme loads, and the flutter phenomenon. At the same time, two floater designs, a heavy, and a light
one, are compared and investigated based on potential tension loss phenomenon at extreme wave conditions.
This is one of the key challenges of this kind of floating structure.
At first, the purpose is to understand the dynamics of a TLP floating structure, and investigate for
potential resonance. For this reason, decay tests are conducted to determine the natural frequencies, and
the damping ratios for all degrees of freedom (DOF) of the structure, which are surge, sway, heave, pitch,
roll, and yaw. Based on the values extracted, it is inferred that the natural frequencies are away from the
main wind, and wave excitation area. The second stage is to conduct some tests with wind and/or waves
to understand the effect of the wind and of the waves on the structural response, and investigate potential
blade pitch controller induced issues. It is found that in only steady wind above rated wind speed with
wind shear conditions, the controller induces negative damping in sway motion. At the rest of the tests, and
design load cases (DLC) conducted with turbulent wind, and irregular waves the controller operates without
showing such behavior.
To have a more thorough understanding of the dynamics, and the loads of the structure, some DLCs
need to be conducted. These DLCs are 1.1, 1.3, 1.6 that correspond to normal wind turbine operation, and
also DLC 6.1 that corresponds to extreme wind and wave conditions with the turbine being shut down. The
location for which the structure is designed, is called Buchan Deep, and its wind, and wave data are used
to conduct the relevant DLCs. Based on this, there are several conclusions analysed in the report regarding
the motion of the structure, what are the main sources of the wind turbine loads, and the tension of the
mooring lines, and how they are compared based on the different DLCs analysed. Indicatively, it is inferred
that the surge, and pitch offsets are governed by the thrust force, and the heave offset follows an arc around
the suction anchors that is dependent on the surge offset. It is also concluded that the wind turbine mean
loads are dependent on the aerodynamic loads, and the mean tension in the mooring lines is dependent on
the platform pitch offset. The comparison among DLCs 1.1, 1.3, and 1.6 proved that the surge, and heave
motions are more excited by the severe sea state than the extreme turbulent wind conditions. The same
applies for the tower base fore-aft moment, and the tension in the mooring lines. The rest of the DOFs, and
the loads turn out to have larger excitation from the turbulent wind than the severe sea state.
Among other conclusions, DLC 6.1 with unidirectional wind and waves shows the absence of aerodynamic
damping in the platform pitch signal, and the dominance of the waves in the excitation of the DOFs, and
the loads. That DLC also showed that the level of the mean tension is also dependent on the position of
the attachment point of a tendon on the platform since the tension is dependent on the structural pitch,
and roll. On the other hand, the 30 degree wind-wave misalignment case shows greater excitation in the
pitch motion compared to the 0 degree misalignment case, and that the tower base loads, and the tension
are affected by the misalignment. The most interesting investigation is the one for DLC 6.1 conditions at
the Least Sea Water Level (LSWL) in which potential tension loss is examined for the heavy floater design.
Indeed the heavy floater showed tension loss of the tendons. However, the goal is to avoid the tension loss
of the tendons.
In that sense, another lighter TLP floating structure is examined. At first, potential discrepancies in
the natural frequencies compared to the heavy floater are made. Then, the goal is to expose the lighter
floater structure at the same extreme wind and wave conditions defined at DLC 6.1 at LSWL, and check
for potential improvement regarding the tension loss phenomenon. Despite the fact that the lighter floater
also showed tension loss, the overall comparison showed an improvement in the tension time series when the
lighter floater is used.
The last study made was the flutter investigation of the heavy TLP floating structure, and investigation
of potential discrepancies when using a semisubmersible floater instead. The study showed that the flutter

Introduction
Wind turbines and specifically offshore wind turbines are operational structures placed in the ocean to
exploit the high available wind potential, convert it into mechanical power through their blades and finally
into electrical power through the shaft, potentially the gearbox and the generator. Offshore wind energy
poses a real challenge in the wind industry as the offshore wind potential is generally much higher than
the onshore wind potential. This creates more opportunities for further and even higher renewable power
generation.
Nowadays, more and more shallow water sea beds up to 50 m depth are being covered by bottom-
fixed offshore wind turbines. However, bottom-fixed turbines cannot be deployed in very large water depths
above 50 m. Thus, the examination of the feasibility of deploying floating wind turbines becomes of utmost
importance. Floating wind turbines are able to operate in very large water depths which dispose even richer
wind potential compared to the wind turbines close to the shore.
That allows for even higher capacity
factors. The current disadvantage of the floating wind turbines is that they pose relatively high Levelised
Cost Of Energy (LCOE). However, the LCOE for floating wind is expected to decrease significantly over the
next few years, meaning that they may become competitive in the offshore wind market and industry [1].
Comparing different types of floating structures, the TLPs are considered as a promising technology
because of their relatively low structural mass, and small excitation of the platform motions, that can reduce
the fatigue loads at the tower base which would lower the structural cost and the LCOE at the same time
[2]. On the other hand, the TLP structures also pose some challenges. To begin with, the mooring lines
need to be always in tension since the stability depends on the tension [2]. An occasion that this issue might
occur is when the structure is subject to large storms and this can cause large dynamic loads on the tendons
because of the wave action [3]. In that case, the tension is lost, and the wave loads may cause slack in the
tendons [3]. In such a case, buckling may be induced in the tendons, and the platform integrity may be set
in danger at the same time [4]. The above statement provides an inspiration and motivation to examine the
case study of the dynamic investigation of a concrete TLP that is able to host the IEA 15 MW wind turbine
[5].
To begin with, the structure consists of the IEA 15 MW wind turbine that is attached on the so called
"CT-BOS" floater. The IEA 15 MW wind turbine consists of a 3 bladed rotor where the rotor radius is
120 m and the hub height from the MSL is 150 m [5]. The turbine is designed to operate at a tip speed ratio
О» of 9 and at a design power coefficient Cp of 0.489 [5]. The IEC wind class of the turbine is IB meaning
that it operates in high wind and medium turbulence sites [5]. The cut-in wind speed is 3 m/s, the rated
wind speed is 10.59 m/s and the cut-out wind speed is 25 m/s [5]. Other interesting structural parameters
of the turbine is the total mass of the Rotor-Nacelle-Assembly (RNA) that is around 1000 tonnes [5]. The
following figure presents the CP , and CT of the wind turbine as function of wind speed.

The analysis of the dynamics of the structure includes different parameters that need to be examined.
It is important to identify the natural frequencies of the whole structure in every single DOF. The structure
is not restricted to move to any direction and thus it is considered to have six DOFs which are movements
in surge, sway, heave, pitch, roll, and yaw. Therefore, six different natural frequencies must be identified
first. Indicatively, the TLPs as substructures for floating wind turbines are stiffer in heave, roll, and pitch
compared to other floating structures such as spar buoys and semisubmersibles leading to higher natural
frequencies in heave, roll, and pitch for a TLP [6]. By identifying the natural frequencies, potential tower
and platform resonance can be evaluated. The objective of this project is the calculation and evaluation of
the extreme loads based on DLCs for different environmental states, along with the tension loss investigation
in extreme sea states. Last but not least, investigation of flutter is to be discussed as well.
In section 2, the methodology for the extraction of results and discussion about the different types of
software used are discussed. Indicatively, the software and coding tools used for all the calculations and
data analysis are the hydro-aero-servo elastic tool HAWC2 and MATLAB. This chapter also includes the basic
elements of two floater designs discussed in this project which are a heavy and a light floater. Finally, this
chapter contains the basic theoretical elements composing DLCs 1.1, 1.3, 1.6 and 6.1.

2.1
The heavy, and the light floater design
To begin with, a complex model such as HAWC2 is used to evaluate the loads and response of the structure.
This model is used to give detailed and reliable results, as it takes into account parameters such as wind and
wave detailed characteristics (e.g. shear), all 6 DOFs as it is a 3D case and also solves the time marching
BEM equations of a flexible structure. The computational time is also quite high. Depending on the running
case, the real time may correspond to at least 67% of the simulation time for completion. Thus the code is
set to run for a particular number of cases. The post processing, and plotting of results from HAWC2 is made
using MATLAB.
Within the HAWC2 setup, the first activity is to solve the relevant equation of motion providing the
structural response after applying different decay tests using an external displacing force for surge, sway,
and heave DOFs or an external displacing moment for pitch, roll, and yaw DOFs respectively each time.
These decay tests include neither wind nor wave forcing. Then, wind and/or wave forcing is introduced for
controller investigation.
In this thesis project, the platform is modeled numerically using HAWC2. Particularly there is a heavy,
and a light floating structure. The need for the two designs comes from the project evolution. The heavy
floater design is the initial design. Then, during the project development, it was decided to try and use a
lighter version of the platform to avoid pretension loss problems. This is described in detail in section 6. For
simplicity, the characteristics of the two plarforms are presented in this section.
First, regarding the heavy floater design, where the wind turbine is attached, it is made of concrete
and has a rectangular cross section shape with dimensions 44.5x44.5 m with a thickness of 19.65 m and its
center of gravity is located 6.26 m below the Mean Sea Level (MSL) taking into account the pretension of
the tendons. The center of buoyancy is located around 8.8 m below the MSL [4]. The keel of the floater is
located 17.65 m below the MSL which corresponds to the floater draft. The floater mass is approximately
20300 tonnes and it is designed to be cheap and robust and it must be easily built and deployed. The
displaced water mass is 36000 tonnes [4]. It is also desired to be adaptable to different sea depths. The
heavy, and the light floater designs are applied for the same water depth of 120 m.
The connection between the floater and the seabed is made by attaching eight steel tendons on the
floater. Particularly, a pair of steel tendons is attached on each of the four corners of the floater as observed
in fig. 2.1. The connection of the tendons on the floater is made by spherical joints, which constraints the
translation but allows the free rotation [4]. The tendons are extended vertically until they reach the seabed
with the aid of suction anchors. The outer diameter of the tendons, that are steel rods, is about 0.27305 m
[4].

2.2
Design Load Cases, and flutter
Afterwards, the dynamic investigation of the two structure types takes place by applying different DLCs
as described on the Design Load Basis by DTU Wind, and Energy Systems [7]. Indicatively, the DLCs that
are implemented in this project are 1.1, 1.3, 1.6, and 6.1 with wind-wave misalignment as well as LSWL
conditions for the heavy floater structure, and DLC 6.1 at LSWL conditions for the light floater structure.
The simulations for different wind and wave states are carried out based on the metocean design basis.
DLC 1.1 is related to the simulation of power production and different loads such as the tower base
fore-aft, tower base side-to-side, yaw bearing tilt, yaw bearing roll, shaft torsion, in-plane and out-of-plane
blade root moments for wind speeds from cut-in to cut-out wind speed with a number of six seeds per wind
speed for normal turbulence according to the wind class of the examined wind turbine. The tension of
the tendons is also included in the loads analysis. Regarding wave conditions, normal sea state (NSS) is
considered using the JONSWAP spectrum function applying different sea state conditions of significant wave
height Hs and peak period Tp and three different stochastic wave seeds per environmental state leading to a
total number of six seeds corresponding to one environmental state following a zero yaw error. For offshore
conditions, that DLC also proposes the use of a normal current model (NCM) which is related to the wind
conditions. The simulation duration for each seed is 600 s [7].
DLC 1.3 is similar to the first one but this time an extreme turbulence model (ETM) is applied for
the wind and the simulation duration is 1500 s [7]. Also, there are 18 seeds per environmental state to be
considered [7].
DLC 1.6 is similar to DLC 1.1 but instead of a NSS, a severe sea state (SSS) is considered in which the
sea state conditions have a 50-year return period in combination with the wind speed range from cut-in to
cut-out wind speed.
In DLC 6.1, a wind speed with 50-year recurrence period and turbulence intensity of 11% are applied.
Also, DLC 6.1 is supposed to be ran for a yaw error of -8, 0, and 8 degrees. Six seeds per wind speed, and
yaw error are assessed. Regarding waves, extreme sea state is considered with 50-year return period, using 3
seeds per sea state. In total, the number of seeds to be simulated are 18 for one wind-wave misalignment case
(0, and 30 degrees) and for one sea level condition (MSL, and LSWL) with the duration of the simulation
being at least 600 s [7]. That DLC also suggests the use of an extreme current model (ECM) based on data
from the respective location of the structure. Based on the loads simulations, the structural response and
different kinds of extreme loads are extracted. These loads include the maximum tension in the mooring
lines and the moments at the tower base, at the yaw bearing, and at the blade root.
Apart from the dynamic investigation of the two floaters, the flutter investigation is conducted. Flutter
is related to the negative damping taking place in the blade oscillations when the rotor reaches a specific
rotational speed that is called flutter speed. The rotor is set to rotate freely without any control application.
Based on such an investigation, the flutter speed of the rotor incorporated in the TLP structure is to be
determined, and at the same time it is to be compared to the flutter speed of a semisubmersible floater.

Theoretical background
In this chapter, an analysis of the theoretical background regarding the dynamics, aerodynamics and
hydrodynamics of the structure are analysed. To begin with, the main and dominant equation of motion
(EoM) from the dynamics of structures is the following eq. (3.1):
(M+A)ВЁx + B Л™x + (Kstruc + Khydro)x = Fext
(3.1)
In eq. (3.1) M and A correspond to the mass and added mass matrices of the structure respectively.
The B matrix corresponds to the damping matrix of the system and consists of the structural damping, and
the wave radiation damping. In reality, the wave radiation damping is formulated differently, as discussed in
section 3.2. The matrices Kstruc and Khydro correspond to the structural and hydrostatic stiffness matrices
respectively. The presence of the matrices related to the hydrodynamics is analysed below. Each DOF is
included in the x vector. In a model of six DOFs each element would correspond to surge, sway, heave, pitch,
roll, and yaw respectively. The vector Fext corresponds to the external loads applied on the structure which
may be either forces or moments. These loads stem from the aerodynamic, hydrodynamic, and gravitational
loads.

they are taken into account through the drag term of the Morison equation, through the linear, quadratic
damping coefficients or with all of the three combined [13].

4.2.1
Limitations of the HAWC2 model
Within the HAWC2 model there are some limitations that need to be discussed. These limitations are
listed below.
вЂў Applying a tower with high stiffness (stiff tower).
вЂў Lack of hydrodynamic elements in the hydro block.
вЂў Lack of second order hydrodynamic loads.
After listing these limitations, it would be interesting to investigate their implications. First of all, by
applying a tower with lower stiffness i.e. flexible tower, it would be expected to have, in general, larger
dynamic loads, and especially in the tower base. Because of the floater configuration, a similar TLP study
suggests that the tendons dynamics are governed by the platform dynamic motion, which means that the
absence of the tower dynamic loads would not affect the conclusions extracted from an ultimate design load
case like those that are discussed in the following chapters [4]. On the other hand, the wind turbine ultimate
loads are expected to be affected by the tower dynamic loads but the goal of this report is to present the
dynamics of the structure when exposed to different environmental conditions, and evaluate the ultimate
wind turbine loads comparatively by running different design load cases. Additionally, the introduction of
hydrodynamic elements in HAWC2 would bring some Morison drag force which could potentially play a role in
extreme sea states, as in DLC 6.1. However, since the forcing of the body is assumed to be inertia dominated,
the Morison drag force coming from the hydrodynamic elements is neglected [4]. On the contrary, in the
current model, the viscous effects are modelled by introducing some already provided quadratic damping
coefficients as discussed in section 4.3.2.
Regarding the absence of second order hydrodynamic loads, these could not be modelled with the
current HAWC2 structure.
However, the same TLP study, as mentioned in the previous paragraph that
includes analysis for DLC 1.6, and 6.1, underlines that the second order wave excitation can only further
excite the surge, and sway natural frequency of the surge, and sway motions compared to the first order
wave forcing [4]. However, as observed in this study [4], the excitation of the tension in the mooring lines is
independent from the surge or sway motion. Therefore, the absence of second order hydrodynamic loading
would not affect the results in the mooring tension.
4.3
Decay tests with HAWC2
In this section, the goal is to determine the natural frequencies of the structure in all of its 6 DOFs
using HAWC2. To achieve this goal, some decay tests need to be conducted in a specific way. Particularly,
these tests are conducted by imposing an external force or moment at a time that causes some displacement
to the structure. This external load is applied at the bottom point of the "force_application_beam" body
24
==PAGE:35==
Master Thesis
4.3
Decay tests with HAWC2
as defined in HAWC2. In reality, this point corresponds to the keel of the floater that is 17.65 m below the
MSL. After the structure is displaced to a new position that balances, the force or the moment is released
allowing the structure to freely oscillate and decay back to its original position of balance. By analyzing the
time signal of the response of the structure after this release, the natural frequencies and the damping ratio
(О¶) for each DOF can be determined.
Apart from the extraction of the natural frequencies with HAWC2, this chapter presents a theoretical
analysis on how to derive and extract the natural frequencies based on structural data provided for all parts
of the structure and application of common structural mechanics theory. Also, the theoretical values of
natural frequencies will be compared to the ones extracted from HAWC2 decay tests.
The hydrodynamic damping is split into wave radiation damping and damping due to drag force. The
latter is then divided into linear and quadratic damping due to linear and non-linear forcing. In this chapter,
two different cases are analyzed. First, decay tests are conducted considering the wave radiation damping
without taking into account the damping because of viscous effects. Then, decay tests are simulated by
considering both hydrodynamic damping factors. For the decay tests it is also important to select a force or
a moment that is representative to a real life load situation. The level of force or moment selected for each
decay test is discussed in the following section 4.3.1.
The simulation time is another factor that must be considered in order to extract representative results
for the responses of all DOFs. The first 60-80 s are considered as transient phase for all simulations. Thus,
the force or moment is not applied at that time span as the motion in transience in combination with the
applied load can create an unrepresentative response. Thus, the applied load is set to start increasing from
no loading to full loading from 85 to 285 s and it is kept constant for 400 s more. Then, the applied load
is released letting the structure on a free decay. Therefore, it is important to determine the time needed
for the structure to have an adequate number of decaying oscillations. To decide that, an estimation of the
lowest natural frequency among the 6 DOFs is required. According to [6], the lowest natural frequency on
a TLP floating wind turbine takes place at the surge natural frequency. This study shows a surge natural
frequency for a TLP structure around 0.025 Hz [6]. Based on this, the free decay oscillations time for the
response for all DOFs was chosen to be at least 400 s to get an estimated number of 10 oscillations for the
surge decay test. This leads to a total simulation time for all DOFs at 1100 s.

because of wind shear. The heave motion also presents a non-zero static offset which is indirectly dependent

structure is displaced in surge and sway, this motion creates an arc around the suction anchors meaning that

compared to below rated wind speed.

will be one peak in aerodynamic loading of a blade per one rotation. As the standard deviation of the wind
speed increases, a higher excitation in IP BRM is than expected.

The next DLC to be ran is DLC 6.1 for the case of unidirectional wind and waves. This DLC is an
ultimate load case as well, and thus, the extreme loads are also evaluated. This particular DLC has a lot
of differences from the previous DLCs. First of all, the rotor is considered to be parked which means that
there is no rotor rotation and the blades are pitched to feather or, in other words, at 90 degrees. To ensure
that the rotor does not rotate, the shaft is locked. Also, the yaw error with respect to the wind direction
is considered to be 8, 0 and -8 degrees. It must be also noted that for these simulations the rotor has a
constant "star" shape throughout the simulations meaning that one blade points upwards, and the rest are
120 degrees phase shifted from one to the other. The wind direction is set to follow the global y direction
of the HAWC2 coordinate system. The waves, and the sea current also follow the same direction. This DLC
also suggests 6 seeds per yaw error.
Regarding the wind conditions, the 50-year extreme wind speed should be considered. Particularly, the
50-year extreme wind speed at 10 m above sea level is 34.5 m/s [15]. The wind shear exponent is taken as
0.11, and the turbulence intensity at 11% [7]. Thus, following the power law for the wind profile, the wind
speed is 46.5 m/s at hub height (150 m) above sea level.
The sea conditions involve extreme sea state (ESS) with a 50-year return period. These include a 50-
year significant wave height of Hs,50=10.9 m, along with a peak spectral period of Tp,50=14.6 s [15]. The
wave signal also includes a 50-year individual wave that has a wave height of H=19.7 m, and a period of

As also indicated in fig. 5.53, the LSWL takes place at the lowest astronomical tide (LAT) minus the

50-year return period. Thus, the LSWL is found as the MSL subtracted by the LAT, and the storm surge.
Therefore, the LSWL is found as 116.76 m.

stiffness in pitch, and roll with the distance of the tendons from the xg, and yg axes can be considered as

Background regarding flutter
By definition, the flutter phenomenon takes place when the turbine rotor reaches a critical rotor speed in
which blade vibrations appear with negative damping starting to build up. Studies have shown that taking
into account the unsteady shed vorticity effects in aerodynamic models causes increased flutter speeds, and
also that the modern turbines with large, and more flexible blades tend to have flutter speeds closer to rotor
speeds when the turbine is in normal operation as opposed to smaller turbines [27]. In that sense, the reason
for modelling the trailed vorticity is to have a more accurate dynamic induction prediction [27]. As also
discussed in section 3.1, the Beddoes Leishman dynamic stall model is used to model the dynamic induction
due to the shed vorticity in HAWC2.
To set up a flutter investigation case for a wind turbine structure, and specifically to determine the
critical speed at which the blade modes are negatively damped, the turbine needs to be exposed to a
runaway situation. Particularly, for that occasion the rotor is decoupled from the generator, and the blade
pitch angle is set to zero degrees. Technically, in that case, the rotor is supposed to reach a rotational speed
in which the aerodynamic torque is zero, as the torque is negative close to the tip, and positive from the
board sections [27]. In order to determine the flutter speed, the wind speed can have a slow ramp-up, which
would continuously increase the rotor speed until blade vibrations start to appear [27].
This method poses some advantages compared to an alternative case of activating the generator torque
to increase the rotor speed having a fixed wind speed. First of all, the presented runaway case study seems
to have a better approximation of an overspeed situation as there is no necessity to apply any external
forcing [27]. Additionally, the edgewise blade deflections are negligible since the aerodynamic torque is small
enough. A potential edgewise blade deflection would influence the coupling between flapwise, edgewise, and
torsional blade modes, which would affect the critical rotor speed [27]. Last but not least, the effect of the
small wind speed slope on the determination of the flutter speed is small enough.
Regarding the sensitivity of the wind speed ramp to the finally determined flutter speed, a study has
shown that within a wind ramp range between 0.00125 m/s2, and 0.02 m/s2, the flutter speed of the NREL
5 MW turbine shows a slight variation of about 3% [27]. More information about the NREL 5 MW turbine
can be found in [28]. In reality, the flutter speed shows an increase with a higher wind speed ramp because
the instabilities need more time to develop [27]. Because of this small variation of the flutter speed with
the wind speed ramp, and for faster HAWC2 computations a wind speed ramp of 0.0136 m/s2 is used for the
following flutter investigations. The same wind speed ramp value is also used in that study [29].

Conclusions, and recommendations for future studies
All in all, this particular study contained four different main aspects. First, it is the understanding of
the order of magnitude of the natural frequencies of a TLP floating structure with respect to the wind, and
wave spectrums, and how these can be theoretically determined. In addition, it is the potential controller
issue that arose by trying different wind or wave parameters. Then, it is the dynamic, and extreme load
investigation of the heavy floating structure through a number of DLCs conducted, and how the tension loss
issue can show improvement by introducing a lighter floater design instead. Last but not least, a flutter
investigation is done for the heavy TLP, and the UMaine floater to determine potential differences in flutter
speed, and potential other discrepancies in their dynamic behavior.
More specifically, the determination of the natural frequencies of the structure is an important to
part to compare with the wind, and wave spectrums, and check for potential sources of resonance. The
simplified theoretical approach to derive the natural frequencies of the structure can provide a preview,
and comparison with the values extracted from HAWC2 simulations. Indeed, most of the extracted natural
frequencies from HAWC2 were in fair agreement with the respective theoretical approach. Only pitch, and
roll natural frequencies did not have full agreement probably due to the blade flexibility that may affect the
decay test signal.
Another important finding was made after conducting a simulation with steady wind speed with shear
below, and above rated wind speed. In that occasion, it was inferred that the blade pitch controller induced
some negative damping in the structural motion, and especially in the sway motion that showed a growing
amplitude before reaching a plateau. However, when introducing wind turbulence, and irregular waves, such
instabilities in the sway motion, and in the blade pitch angle did not appear anymore. In that sense, since
the key point of this report does not fully involve controller issues, and it seems to work adequately for wind
turbulence, and irregular waves, this particular controller was selected for further evaluation of the structural
loads.
As far as the DLCs ran, and first, the DLC 1.1 that is related to normal operational conditions regarding
the wind speed range, and the relevant sea state, it is inferred that the surge, and pitch offsets are directly
related to the thrust force, and the heave offset is related to the surge offset following an arc around the
suction anchors. That phenomenon proves the coupling between surge, pitch, and heave motions, that is
visible in the PSD plot of the respective time signals as well. Moreover, it is concluded which DOFs were
induced by the wind, and which were induced by the waves. A similar case was made with the wind turbine
loads for which the mean values were related to the thrust force or the aerodynamic torque as well. Also, it
is investigated whether they are excited by the wind or the waves. Regarding the tension of the tendons, it
is concluded that its offset is related to the pitch angle of the platform, and the ones that are upwind of the
turbine show higher tension values than the ones that are downwind.
Regarding DLC 1.3, which is similar to DLC 1.1 but this time an ETM is applied instead of a NTM.
Based on this, it is inferred that all 6 DOFs, all wind turbine loads, and the tension of the tendons show
higher excitation than for the DLC 1.1 simulations.
For DLC 1.6, that is similar to DLC 1.1 but this time a SSS is considered instead of a NSS, it is inferred
that especially surge, and heave DOFs are more excited by the SSS, than the ETM applied in DLC 1.3.
The rest of the DOFs show comparable behavior to DLC 1.1. Regarding the wind turbine loads, it is the
TB-FA that shows the higher influence from the SSS compared to the ETM. The rest of the wind turbine
loads show comparable behavior to DLC 1.1. On the other hand, the tension of the tendons show higher
excitation with the SSS conditions than in ETM conditions.
DLC 6.1 for unidirectional wind, and waves refers to extreme wind, and wave conditions, with a locked
rotor, controller switched off, and the blades pitched to feather. This DLC, and the relevant analysis showed
how the blade aerodynamics can determine the level, and the sign of the offset of the six DOFs. Also, it
showed the absence of aerodynamic damping in pitch motion as the blades are pitched to feather with a
locked rotor. The effect of the blade aerodynamics can also be seen in the offset of the extracted wind turbine
loads for yaw errors of -8, 0, and 8 degrees. Similar conclusions due to the blade aerodynamics can also be
extracted for the mean tension in the mooring lines based on the relative attachment point on the platform.
The extreme wave conditions of DLC 6.1 have a significant effect on the extreme tower base moments, and
in the extreme torque related loads (YB, R, shaft torsion, and IP BRM) compared to the other DLCs. Also,

As for the DLC 6.1 30 degree misalignment case, the sway, roll, and pitch motions seem to be more
excited as opposed to other DOFs. Regarding the extreme wind turbine loads, the TB-FA, and the TB-SS are
mostly affected by the wind-wave misalignment, and finally the tension of the tendons is also more excited

The next DLC discussed is the same as the previous one but this time at the LSWL. Based on that DLC,
there two main findings. First of all, it is found that the theoretical approach deriving the new pretended
length, the new pretension in the mooring lines, and the displacement in heave of the platform with respect
to the sea level is in good agreement with the HAWC2 computations verifying the theory. The second finding
made was that there is loss of tension noticed at the LSWL conditions, making the structure being exposed
to a dangerous condition if this almost unlikely situation ever takes place.
Due to that tension loss phenomenon with the heavy floater, it is decided to examine another lighter
floater structure version. The decay tests, and the relevant comparison with the heavy floater based on
the natural frequencies showed discrepancies justified by the floater mass, the rotational inertia, and the
attachment points of the tendons on the platform. Then, the same DLC at the LSWL conditions is examined
for the light floater as for the heavy floater after verifying the theoretical approach of switching from the
MSL to the LSWL comparing with the HAWC2 computations. This study showed tension loss also for the
light floater design but the overall comparison with the heavy floater showed an improvement in the tension
time series.
The last study discussed is the flutter investigation, and particularly the relevant comparison between
the heavy TLP, and the UMaine floater. The study shows no difference in the flutter speed which proves to
be higher than the rated rotor speed. Last but not least the study of the response of the six DOFs within
this flutter investigation shows the relatively high stiffness of the TLP structure compared to the UMaine
floater.
Based on the above listed findings there are some recommendations for further study for that particular
floating TLP wind turbine structure.
вЂў The negative damping induced by the controller on the structural response when exposed to only steady
wind conditions above rated wind speed. This may motivate for further control tuning investigations
despite that it seems to work adequately for all DLCs ran.
вЂў Running DLCs for fatigue loads analysis. Such a DLC could be DLC 1.2. However, for studies with
fatigue loads calculations, it would be necessary to incorporate a flexible tower in order to account for
the dynamic loads from the tower.
вЂў Investigation of tension loss at DLC 6.1 conditions at LSWL with even lighter floater design or increase
the draft of the platform. In that sense, with both approaches, the pretension of the tendons is expected
to increase, and tension loss may be avoided.
