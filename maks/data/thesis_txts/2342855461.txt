Summary
In recent years onshore wind markets in South Eastern Europe are emerging in re-
gions where the terrain has a complex topography such as hills, mountains and forests.
These areas present high complexity when assessing wind resources. Shear and turbu-
lence levels become more and more challenging. Besides that, high mountain sites are
characterized by atmospheric icing during winter time. Disregard this fact in areas
of relatively high temperatures during the year can lead to the installation of inap-
propriate instrumentation. The wind measurement campaign can be less accurate if
these factors are not properly considered.
In the present project the identification of patterns in instrumental icing is inves-
tigated with measurements from masts at different heights. Cup, propeller and sonic
anemometers are analyzed as they present different responses to atmospheric condi-
tions. Propellers have shown longer data recording during icing than cups. Sonics in
this case have been strongly affected by icing. Detection of icing is a big challenge
when there are not heated anemometers. Therefore it has been found that some of
the affected data can be identified but not entirely. Analysis of data has been carried
out and compared to the use of artificial neural network (ANN) giving in some cases
a good approximation between methods.
Uncertainties in complex terrain depend on the accuracy of site measurements as
well as model performance. The highly complex terrain under study in this project
poses a big challenge to the use of linear models when siting turbines and wind farms.
The validation of IBZ and Ellipsys wind flow models over the area has been made
by the comparison of field measurements to vertical and horizontal predictions. In
terms of modelling wind speed and wind directions WAsP has shown higher accuracy
than WAsP CFD in most of the cases. This validation has also concluded that model
results depend on the height of measurements and on the terrain inputs especially for
WAsP CFD set up.
Keywords: icing, wind measurement, anemometer, neural network, WAsP, com-
plex terrain

Preface
This Master thesis was prepared at the section of Meteorology, Department of Wind
Energy at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements
for acquiring a Master’s in Sustainable Energy at DTU. The thesis was carried out
during a period of five months and three weeks, from January 25th 2016 to July 16th
2016, under the supervision of the Professor Niels-Erik Clausen and co-supervision of
the Postdoc Neil Davis. The main focus of this thesis is the characterization of a site
under complex terrain and icing conditions with the use of two different models for
wind applications WAsP and WAsP CFD.
Ana Maria Cosculluela Arasanz
Kongens Lyngby, August 8, 2016

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Aim of the project
The aim of this project is the characterization of two potential sites for the installation
of two wind farms. These sites pose two challenges: the identification of icing events in

2.1
Icing
According to ISO(2000) standard-12494 [6] ice accretion can be defined as ’Any process
of ice building-up and snow accretion on the surface of the object exposed to the
atmosphere’. Generally atmospheric icing can be divided into two types: in-cloud
icing and precipitation icing.
In-cloud icing is caused by supercooled water droplets that hit the surface of an
object and freezes resulting in the formation of ice. In-cloud icing is the most common
atmospheric icing and can be divided into two types: rime and glaze.
Precipitation icing occurs when rain or snow freezes when hitting the surface of
an object [24].
2.1.1
Types of icing
The accretion of ice will depend on the atmospheric conditions at the time where icing
occurs such as humidity, temperature and phenomenom duration. Another factor is
the direction of the object according to the icing wind. There are different forms of
atmospheric icing depending on the formation proccess [34]:
a) Rime ice: It is considered the most common in-cloud icing. Super cooled
water droplets from clouds or fog are transported by wind and when they deposit on
an object they freeze immediatly. Depending on the size of the droplets the formation
of soft rime or hard rime occur. Frequently the most severe rime icing happens at
exposed ridges/mountains where moist air is lifted and due to the terrain the wind
speed increases.

b) Glaze: the formation of glaze ice is caused by freezing rain, freezing drizzle

Glaze is characterized by its high density and occurs when temperatures are below

decrease in the temperatures.

2.1.4
Methods of ice detection
According to Homola et al. [29] there are two ways of detecting icing: direct and
indirect. Direct methods are able to detect the ice due to property changes such as
mass, electrical/thermal conductivity, reflective properties, etc. Nonetheless, indirect
methods rely on weather conditions which lead to icing like humidity and tempera-
ture or power production reduction among others. Indirect methods use empirical
or deterministic models for icing detection. A review of 25 direct methods and 4
indirect methods for detecting icing according to Homola et al. [29] can be found in
Appendix A.4.
Wadham-Gagnon et al. [51] carried out a performance comparison of different ice
detection methods. These include ice accretion measurement from camera images, in-
strumental ice detection methods and meteorological ice detection methods. Indirect
methods such as relative humidity and temperature (RHT) indicate 75% of the cases
an incorrect measurement of meteorological icing supporting the unreliability if this
method for sites with moderate icing. However, wind speed difference between heated
and unheated anemometers (WSD) and wind direction deviation (WDD) methods
seem to provide a good level of accuracy.

2.3
Boundary Layer Theory
2.3.1
Atmospheric boundary layer
The lower part of the Earth’s atmosphere is where most of the weather phenomena
occurs. It is a complex physical system including a diurnal component, weather events
and the effect of the complex terrain. This lower part refers to the troposphere and
the average depths vary from 20km in the tropics to 17km in the mid latitudes to 7km
in the poles. The atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) is the part of the troposphere
closest to the ground in which the flow is affected by the surface of the Earth. It has
a height of approximately 1 km, with variations during the day as shown below, and
it can be also referred to as Planetary Boundary Layer (PBL) [27].
Figure 2.6: Daily cycle of the ABL from [27].
Figure 2.6 presents how the ABL develops during the day with clear sky.
A
Convective Bounday Layer (CBL) is formed at sunrise due to heating of the surface.
When the day is reaching the end, at sunset, a Nocturnal Boundary Layer (NBL) is
created because of a heat loss to space. The residual layer is formed in the transition
from CBL to NBL. The height of the ABL is varying according the diurnal cycles
with a typical value of 100m during to morning up to 3km in the afternoon, these
values can vary according to the place.

2.3.4
Definition and types of terrain
If a terrain subjected to study does not present horizontally homogeneity, as for ex-
ample changes in the surface roughness length, significant variations in topography
or terrain obstacles, the terrain proposes complexity in the flow analysis due to flow
distorsion and separation. In wind resource assessment the terrain is defined as com-
plex and it can be a consequence of the surface such as mountains, forests, urban
areas, sand dunes or steep hills among others. This kind of terrain presents a com-
plex orography that can lead to higher loads and lower performance than expected
for wind turbines. According to Kaimal and Finnigan[32] the flow over a complex
terrain is subjected to changes in velocity and turbulence fields.
According to Bingöl et al. [14] the terrain can be divided into four different categories
depending on the roughness length, z0 and the landscape types presented by the ter-
rain: flat, moderately complex, complex and highly complex terrain.
• Flat terrain: the terrain is horizonally homogeneous and orographic effects

2.3.5
Wind Flow over Complex Terrain
Complex terrain refers not only to terrain morphology but also to the dynamic and
thermal effects of the atmospheric flow. This kind of terrain is comprised of crests
and valleys with slopes that strongly affect the wind flow. It is also dependent on the
particular geometry and orientation. The two types of winds which can be found in
mountains are divided into slope and valley winds. Slope winds are the consequence
on the difference between temperatures at the top of the mountain and in the valley.
Wind is up-slope during day time and down-slope during night time. Valley winds
are consequence of horizontal pressure gradients due to the difference in temperatures
in the valley. Winds can be altered by local phenomenons as specific winds or ther-
mal effects which experience noticeable variations in frequency and intensity between
seasons [9]. Figure 2.8 shows the slope and valley winds during the day and night
time.

2.4
WAsP flow model: BZ model
WAsP flow model is based on the ’BZ-model’ of Troen (1990) developed for wind
energy applications. The model calculations are applied in the atmospheric boundary
layer with the consideration of wind speed perturbations due to orography such as
hills and complex terrain, the drag effect due to roughness changes and obstacle effects.
This model is related to the Jackson and Hunt theory for flow over hills [38].
The BZ model was developed taking into consideration the following:
• Weather conditions can be considered close enough to be neutral stable
• Both predictor, a meteorological station, and predicted, another meteorological
station or wind turbine, sites lie in the same wind regime
• The observed data used as input is reliable
• The surrounding terrain is sufficiently smooth such that the flow is linear and
the flow separation is minimum
• The topographical inputs are reliable (roughness and elevation maps)
The model employs a zooming polar grid centered in the point of interest allowing
higher resolution of modelling at this point.

Cup anemometers respond to the horizontal component of the wind vector. In
complex terrain the vertical component is different from zero and this can cause an
overestimation of wind speed. This type of anemometers can be really sensitive to
periods of icing with a prediction error between 10 % to 30 % since the aerody-

cup anemometers show incorrect values, with severe icing constant values indicating
no wind [26] [35].
Propeller anemometers rotate around its axis in order to face the wind into the
prevailing direction all time. This characteristic allows the anemometer to react faster
to wind changes than cup anemometers. This kind of anemometers tend to be less
sensitive to icing compared to cup anemometers in terms of failures or measurement
quality. Contrary to that, data influenced by icing is more difficult to identify.
Sonic anemometers are normally based on measurements in two or three dimen-
sions based on ultrasonic sound waves from the transmitter to a receiver to measure
the wind speed. During icing conditions the transducer, in charge of measuring the
time the sonic wave needs to go from one end to the other, can be blocked. Thus
the sonic wave experiences some disturbance and the measurement can be considered
not realistic. In general, during icing conditions sonics can be more robust than cups
as they do not have moving parts and therefore they do not experience mechanical
effects caused by icing [34].

3.5
Climatological characteristics: Katabatic winds
The Bora is a strong cold, dry and often gusty katabatic wind from north to north –
east. It is caused by a combination of land-sea and mountain breeze effect due to the
location of steep mountains relatively close to the coastline. Bora can occur during the
whole year but it is more likely to happen in the cold season, from November to March.
It normally appears at night during the cold season because the contrast between land
and Adriatic sea temperatures is more noticeable. It is based on the higher density
of cold air compared to warm air. This fact makes the cool air accumulated over
the Balkan Peninsula to move from the north/north-east towards the coast. This
phenomenon is enhanced by the funneling and local topography of the terrain. The
Bora is a downslope wind which starts suddenly, reaching the peak intensity within
the first 3 or 4 hours, and decelerates slow and requires of several conditions such as
mountain massifs, different temperatures and pressures in heights and lowlands, etc.
It can happen that it is localized few miles or extended over the entire Adriatic Sea,
depending on the gradient of the pressure of steep pressure. An average duration of
the Bora can comprise between some hours up to couple of days. The Bora experiences
a diurnal variation on its strength, lower between noon and 6pm due to the sea breeze
counteraction. There are two types of Bora: anti-cyclonal and cyclonal, also called
white and black Bora [8].

CHAPTER 5
Evaluation of models
In Chapter 2 some of the most important phenomena that occurs in complex terrain
was explained from the theoretical point of view as wind shear, turbulence intensity
and speed ups.
As a result of the large variations of the wind flow at a complex sites measure-
ments of a mast are representative of small areas and the predictions can entail large
deviations. Hence, in the present chapter the wind flow modelling with a linear and a
CFD model is analyzed. Wind speeds and wind directions are validated by comparing
field measurements to transferred values. Wind speeds at different heights have also
been compared to see how the models predict the vertical profiles. The objective is
to answer the research questions set in Chapter 1 and interpret the results.
5.1
Data Analysis
Generalized wind climate has been created from the seven months of measurements.
The methodology used to convert the data into a generalized wind climate is out
of the scope of the thesis and it is not included in the present report.
However
the methodology used is based on WAsP fundamentals, from time series of wind
speed and direction together with the meteorological mast description, roughness
and elevation, in order to create a generalized regional wind climatology. Data has
been appropriately filtered from mast shadow effects and icing. The exclusion of boom
direction is ±20◦while the icing periods have been removed with the methodology
explained in the previous chapter. Data availability is above 90% for the masts except
for Poljice1 which is above 85% for all heights with an exception of sonic anemometers
at 75m which have been strongly affected by icing. Old data from site has been used
to verify if the seven months of the new installation can be considered representative.
Data from 2008 to 2012 from Plocno Site of a 30m mast has confirmed that the
observed wind climate used in the present project is representative.

5.4
Ruggedness index
When the terrain slope surpasses a certain threshold flow separation occurs. The fact
that WAsP is a linear model will increase the uncertainties in terrain with high slopes.
Consequently the concept of ruggedness index has been created to handle situation
outside the envelope of WAsP [15].
The RIX calculation is made for each radii originated at the site. The fraction of
each radius into line segments by the crossing of these radius with the height contour
lines is defined as the RIX value. The total percentage of line segments whose value is
larger than an established critical slope over the total amount of segments is defined as
the RIX. This value is considered a mean value of the RIX by sector. The calculation
of the ruggedness index depends on three parameters: radius (R), number of radii
(N) and critical slope θc. It is worth mentioning that the height contour interval will
slightly influence the value of the index. According to Bowe and Mortensen [39] the
radius adquires a value of 3.5 km, the critical slope 0.3 and the number of radii 72.
∆RIX is defined as the difference between the site ruggedness index at the pre-
dicted and the reference site, ∆RIX = RIXW T G −RIXMET . According to theory
if both sites are situated in a terrain with similar characteristics the errors between
the predictor and predicted site counteract each other.
Due to the complexity of the terrain and the use of a linear model for its validation
a RIX analysis is performed with the four masts to check if WAsP results could be
improved. Each color represents the predictions made by this specific mast.

According to Figure 5.5 there is not a relationship between the RIX values and
the wind speed error when considering the four masts. The tendency reflects that
disregarding of Poljice1 could improve this relationship. In any case, it is concluded

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
Wind Resource Assessment
An initial assessment has shown that each of the sites will be used to evaluate
wind resources for each wind farm. The present project had the aim of validating a
linear and a CFD model in complex terrain therefore the whole area was considered.
In such complex terrain one of the easiest way to minimise uncertainty is the
installation of many meteorological masts. However, this way is costly and there are
several alternatives that can contribute to reduce uncertainty. Instead the installation
of masts wisely and with the proper sensors is considered a better approach. The
location of a mast in this specific area is a challenge as it is important not to install
a mast in a recirculation area or where it will be influenced by katabatic winds.
However, the recognition of these phenomena can be difficult beforehand. Due to
turbulence and speed up effects in complex terrain, the installation of a mast as close
as possible to hub height will help to decrease the level of uncertainties. As seen in the
results of this thesis microscale models overpredict wind speed when extrapolating
higher levels.
One of the research questions in the introductory section posed the dilemma of the
most appropriate number of masts for a better evaluation of wind resources. However
this question is difficult to answer quantitatively and varies from project to project to
a great extent. As a recommendation to developers for future wind farms in complex
terrain it is crucial to have a thorough site visit. This can help to understand better
the site conditions. Moreover evaluation of elevation, roughness changes, possible
valleys and mountains in the surrounding area gives important information on areas
even if they are close on how the wind flow can be influenced.
Mesoscale models can be useful when considering the wind flow variation in a
larger scale for the area.
These models can help in the identification of complex
patterns that cannot be capture by microscale models such as large scale mountain
breezes, sea breeze circulation, etc. Although depending on the grid resolution, they
are recommended to be used together with microscale models for wind resource as-
sessment. Description of topographic, roughness and turbulence effects at complex
sites depends in the grid resolution of the mesoscale models. Therefore they are con-
sidered a complement of microscale data.
As an alternative to high masts, which involve more monitoring, especially due to
icing, could be the combination of lower masts and remote sensing, such as LIDARs
==PAGE:84==
66

and SODAR systems. Due to their technique they can cover a larger area. However
their performance in complex area should be evaluated and compared to mast mea-
surements.
Measurements and icing detection
Data used in this project is limited to seven months. Several years of data would
have decreased uncertainties related to data. The available months cover the winter
period which is favorable for the icing detection study. However, the results obtained
in this thesis could vary according to the severity and length of the winter period. It
is known from the developer that the wind conditions during this year present some
differences compared to the usual conditions.
The fact that the sonic data could have not been used in this study have decreased
the value added to the results. Few instrumentation for identification of icing has
shown limitations on the amount of data which can be detected as invalid for the
measurement campaign. Impact on power reduction due to icing is not assessed in
this thesis as there is not a reference anemometer installed or any direct method such
as web cameras to verify icing events. Once the wind farm is in operation it exists
the possibility to compare power output and predicted from wind speed of the nacelle
anemometer to evaluate the losses.
A simplified way of estimating production losses is proposed by [53] for sites that
present light icing, such as Southern Europe. However the icing assessment for sites
with severe/strong conditions should be evaluated with a complex methodology.
The evaluation of this project has shown that approximately 2% of the data is
influenced by icing. For the operation of the wind farm this share might be different
as the ice accretion differs from instrumental to rotor blade (shape, velocity and ma-
terial of the blade). An approximation of power output reduction is about 10-30%
during icing periods or stopping the wind turbines in order to prevent mechanical
loads and ice throw during icing periods.
WAsP and WAsP CFD models
The model validation has demonstrated notable errors when comparing predic-
tions to measurements. The use of WAsP CFD is more sensitive to model inputs
from the user than WAsP. A list with possible factors which could have interfered in
the results is presented below:
• Some of the errors of WAsP can be linked to the use of a linear model for
flow modelling in complex terrain. However, for WAsP CFD they could be linked to
imperfections in the roughness and elevation maps. Most of the uncertainties in this
study are believed to come from the roughness map. Corine Land Cover with a resolu-
tion of 90m has been used. Even though it seemed to identify the different land uses
accurately, a more detailed map according to satellite images of this area together
with a site visit should be done in order to gain accuracy. Sensitivity to roughness
map is higher for WAsP CFD as the topographic speed ups are dependent only on
the topography instead of wind speed. Chanelling effects which exceed WAsP/WAsP
==PAGE:85==
6 Discussion
67
CFD limits affect also the microscale study.
• The neutral stability assumption in the model is a simplification of the real sit-
uation. The fact that these models cannot capture temperature gradient effects leads
to errors in the validation. In the area under study, thermal effects are influencing
the wind resource estimation (See Appendix A.6). Just few kilometers separate the
city of Mostar (60m a.s.l.) to the mountain where the masts are located. Differences
of temperature in the valley and in the mountain can be quite large especially in sum-
mer. Thermal stability strongly influences the vertical wind profile. Consequently, a
lack of measurements at higher heights entails large uncertainty if these models are
used for prediction.
• Delta RIX corrections in this study have shown poor results therefore WAsP
results could not be improved by this method. It is recommended not to use this
method in a general way for all kind of projects. Instead evaluate its use in each
specific case.
CFD models are more sensitive to the above mentioned factors than linear ones.
Therefore the present discussion had the purpose to enumerate the possible factors
that could influence in the WAsP CFD results for further studies.
Besides that,
further investigation to suit the necessities of new areas in Southern Europe in wind
power development needs to be done as it differs greatly from the practices used in
Northen Europe.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The present thesis has focused on the assessment of mountainous areas for wind power
development. Two topics have been discussed: influence of icing in the measurement
campaign and evaluation of wind flow with the use of linear and CFD models.
Determine data affected by icing from indirect methods and without the installa-
tion of proper sensors for its detection poses a big challenge. With the methodology
used in the present thesis it has been concluded that it is possible to detect some of
the affected data. Installation of proper sensors for icing could improve to a great
extent the detection. The behaviour of different sensors has been also discussed given
an overview on how is the response to icing events. Cup anemometers have shown
more sensitive behaviour to these events while propellers record data longer periods
with the disadvantage of difficulting the detection of wrong data. Parallel sensors
and their use as backups are valuable especially for higher heights where the failure
of one of them can be a warranty of continuity of data recording. Higher frequency
of recording data can improve the adquisition data campaign as seen from the sonic
anemometers behaviour. Higher frequency is as well recommended for complex ter-
rain which can provide a more detailed data analysis about the wind conditions in the
area. It has been found that icing events are associated to periods of high humidity
in most of the cases but not exclusively. This fact reaffirms that the use of humidity
and temperature cannot be applied for icing instrumentation detection. The impor-
tance of icing detection is significant as data affected represent approximately 2%
of the total measurement campaign depending on the duration and severity of the
winter season. With data analysis some of the affected data could be identified but
not entirely. Therefore the use of neural network has been implemented to test the
accuracy of this method. Pattern recognition with a neural network have shown some
agreement with data analysis in some cases. However, in some others recognition of
icing events differs. In theses cases the ANN should be trained with larger data sets
with the consideration of avoiding overfitting.
Model validation have shown differences in the wind flow modelling between WAsP
and WAsP CFD when reproducing real situations. Horizontal and vertical extrapola-
tion of wind speeds have proved a better approximation to observed measurements by
WAsP. In addition, wind direction cross-prediction accuracy has shown poor results
for certain sectors in both models. It has been found that vertical extrapolation from
low to higher heights entails some errors due to incorrect wind shear or thermal ef-
fects consideration by the models. Therefore the use of these models as a replacement
==PAGE:88==

of masts should be considered with caution. Possible factors adding uncertainty to
WAsP CFD simulations have been discussed in order to improve their use. Rough-
ness and elevation maps should be defined as accurately as possible in order to reduce
uncertainties by the use of this model.
