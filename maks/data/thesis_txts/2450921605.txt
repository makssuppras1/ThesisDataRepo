i 
Abstract 
 
The extensive use of pesticides and an increasing frequency of extreme rain events are important 
factors contributing to pesticide contamination of groundwater aquifers. Approximately half of all 
groundwater resources in Denmark originate from aquifers that are located below clayey till de-
posits. Clayey till can be highly hydraulically conductive due to fractures and macropores that act 
as preferential flow paths, which can facilitate leaching of dissolved contaminants to the ground-
water below.  
 
Flow-through experiments were performed to investigate the water flow and leaching of pesticides 
through a large undisturbed column (LUC) from a clayey till site in Holbæk, Denmark. The LUC 
was installed in a permeameter which allowed the reproduction of estimated in-situ effective stress 
and temperature conditions. The bulk hydraulics of the LUC and the column intactness were in-
vestigated by performing hydraulic measurements using various hydraulic gradients. The average 
bulk hydraulic conductivity was 3.3´10-6 m/s. Using the cubic law, the average hydraulic fracture 
aperture was estimated as 91 µm and the hydraulic conductivity of the fractures 5.2´10-3 m/s, in 
contrast to the low hydraulic conductivity of the matrix (1.5´10-9 m/s). By visually inspecting the 
column after injection of a dye tracer, it was evident that the majority of the flow took place in grey 
iron-reduced fractures and macropores, and the flow in fractures did not appear to depend on the 
presence of macropores within the fractures. Negligible flow occurred in the matrix and in red 
Fe(III) oxide coated fractures and macropores.  
 
A conservative bromide tracer and the pesticides bentazone, MCPA and tebuconazole were in-
jected into the LUC while imitating an extreme rain event by producing a high flow rate (80 mm/d) 
for 30 hours, followed by a lower flow (10 mm/d) for 21 days. Water samples were collected from 
the LUC outlet to obtain breakthrough curves (BTCs) for the solutes. The LUC setup was repro-
duced by setting up a 2-D numerical model using the Finite Element Method, and flow and 
transport simulations were carried out. The measured BTCs showed different patterns due to the 
distinct properties and interactions of the injected solutes during their transport within the complex, 
fractured medium. Bromide reflected the behaviour of a conservative solute which does not un-
dergo sorption or degradation, and model simulations fit the measured BTC well. Bentazone and 
MCPA appeared to be unstable in the injected solution as well as in the collected effluent samples. 
This resulted in considerable fluctuations in the BTCs for these pesticides which were not feasible 
to reproduce with numerical simulations. The BTC for tebuconazole fluctuated less and was in all 
probability affected by sorption. Simulations with a Kd of 9 L/kg resulted in a good fit to the meas-
ured concentrations during solution injection. However, the aqueous concentrations during flush-
ing of the column were overestimated in the simulations. This may be explained by a non-equilib-
rium desorption mechanism affecting the transport of tebuconazole during the experiment.  
 
The investigations and the results provided a valuable insight into the transport of tracers and pes-
ticides through clayey till and the processes that can greatly affect the transport, such as diffusion 
and sorption. They furthermore indicated that, despite many assumptions that need to be taken 
when numerically simulating flow and transport in heterogeneous media, it can be feasible to obtain 
remarkably good approximations to the measured concentrations using 2-D numerical simulations.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Groundwater vulnerability to pesticide contamination  
The drinking water supply in Denmark is almost entirely based on groundwater resources. In 2017, 
almost 700 million m3 of groundwater was abstracted by Danish water works for domestic and 
industrial use (Thorling et al. 2019). The water treatment is minimal and usually only consists of 
airing and filtering, and in rare cases including UV disinfection or water softening (HOFOR 2018). 
It is of utmost interest to protect the groundwater from contaminants including pesticides that can 
lead to decommissioning of extraction wells or require an extensive treatment of the water.  
 
The groundwater resources in Denmark are, however, greatly at risk of being polluted by contam-
inants including pesticides. Approximately half of all groundwater resources in Denmark originate 
from aquifers that are overlain by clayey tills. Clayey tills have previously been assumed to prevent 
contaminants such as pesticides from field application from migrating to underlying groundwater 
aquifers because of a low hydraulic conductivity of the clay matrix. However, studies have showed 
how textural heterogeneities, such as fractures and macropores originating from root channels and 
soil organisms, can increase the hydraulic conductivity significantly. These can act as preferential 
flow paths for water and solutes through a clay-till aquitard and cause a pollution of the ground-
water reserves in the underlying aquifers (Jørgensen & Fredericia 1992; Broholm et al. 1996; Ros-
enbom et al. 2016; Jørgensen et al. 2017). 
 
The combination of an extensive use of pesticides on agricultural fields and a constantly increasing 
frequency and strength of high rainfall events is an important factor contributing to pesticide con-
tamination of the groundwater in Denmark. High rainfall events can cause a fast movement of 
water and solutes to the subsurface via the preferential flow paths (Hasholt et al. 2000; Kladivko 
et al. 2001; Jørgensen et al. 2004). The high flow velocities may prevent solutes from having suf-
ficient time to interact with the soil, which can cause non-equilibrium sorption and limit the possi-
bility of degradation. The risk of pesticides leaching to the groundwater can therefore be underes-
timated in cases where equilibrium sorption coefficients or known degradation rates are used to 
predict the transport through the soil.  
 
Despite national and international regulations and limitations that are meant to protect soil and 
groundwater from pesticide contamination, historical use of pesticides can still contribute to find-
ings in groundwater for many years to come. Pesticides, whose transport is delayed due to pro-
cesses such as diffusion, degradation or sorption, can be leached to the groundwater at a later time 
due to back-diffusion, desorption or formation of mobile metabolites. Therefore, pesticides that are 
not being used anymore can possibly be found in groundwater supplies or monitoring wells later 
on.  
 
In order to assess the vulnerability of groundwater resources towards pesticide contamination, it is 
necessary to continue the investigation of pesticides that have different physico-chemical 
==PAGE:12==
 
2 
properties, their transport in different geological formations overlying groundwater aquifers, and 
to identify the preferential flow paths in different soil types. This should furthermore be done with 
the changed weather conditions and increasing amount of storm events in mind. Field experiments 
commonly suffer from the lack of control over boundary conditions, while idealised laboratory 
experiments are often limited by small samples that do not always represent soil heterogeneities 
found in a natural setting, which results in underestimated hydraulic conductivities. Flow-through 
experiments using the large undisturbed column (LUC) method by Jørgensen et al. (2019) allow 
investigations of solute transport in a large soil sample including fractures and macropores, while 
being able to manipulate boundary conditions and reproduce in-situ conditions.  
1.2 The CLAYFRAC project 
CLAYFRAC is a 3-year project which started in 2017 and is funded by the Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency (DEPA). It is a collaborative project carried out by a group of researchers and 
employees at the Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS), the Technical University 
of Denmark (DTU) and the private company PJ-Bluetech. The main objective of CLAYFRAC is 
to map groundwater vulnerability to pesticide contamination through clay-tills containing fractures 
and biopores (Miljøstyrelsen 2017; Aamand 2016).  
 
CLAYFRAC is structured into five interlinked work packages (WP), whose focus areas are shown 
in Figure 1. The aim of WP1 is to update an existing polymorphological (PM) map of Sjælland, 
Denmark and select the field sites for excavations. WP2 characterises the geological heterogenei-
ties, including biopore and fracture system, in the clay-till deposits at the field sites selected in 
WP1. WP3 provides sorption coefficients and degradation rates of the pesticides used for the in-
vestigations (bentazone, MCPA and tebuconazole). The aim of WP4 is to provide hydraulic infor-
mation on water and pesticide transport. The results from WP3 and WP4 will be used for WP5 
which covers modelling of water flow and pesticide transport for prediction of pesticide leaching 
for the different PM-types. This will enable the ranking of different clay types according to vulner-
ability. The ranking will then be added to the PM-aggregated map from WP1, and the final product 
will be a "vulnerability map ranking different PM-types according to risk of pesticide leaching" 
(Aamand 2016).  
 
Figure 1. The linkage between the work packages (WP) constituting the CLAYFRAC project (Aamand 2016). 
The workload of the present study contributes to WP4 with flow-through experiments on an LUC 
excavated from one of the field sites chosen in WP1, and WP5 with the numerical simulations of 
the LUC experiments using COMSOL Multiphysics. WP4 is headed by Peter R. Jørgensen from 
PJ-Bluetech, and WP5 is headed by Massimo Rolle and Klaus Mosthaf from DTU.

1.3 Aim 
The aim of this study is to perform flow-through experiments using the LUC method in order to 
investigate the transport of tracers and pesticides through fractured clayey till deposits with respect 
to the groundwater vulnerability to pesticide contamination. The following steps will be taken:  
 
• A literature review will be carried in order to gain knowledge of the key areas of this study 
and to support the planning of the LUC experiments.  
 
• An LUC will be installed in a laboratory and the experimental setup will be prepared in-
cluding reproduction of in-situ effective stress and temperature. 
 
• Hydraulic measurements will be carried out on the LUC using different hydraulic gradients 
and the hydraulic conductivity and fracture apertures will be estimated.  
 
• A solution containing a conservative tracer (bromide) and three pesticides (bentazone, 
MCPA and tebuconazole) will be injected into the LUC and breakthrough curves (BTCs) 
at the outlet will be obtained.  
 
• The effect of an extreme rain event will be investigated by first producing a high flow rate, 
followed by a sudden switch to a lower flow rate and observe how this affects the BTCs of 
the injected solutes.   
 
• The preferential flow paths in the LUC will be assessed by injecting a dye tracer followed 
by a visual inspection of the flow paths while dissecting the column.  
 
• It will be investigated whether the experimental simulations of the flow and solute transport 
can be reproduced by setting up a 2-D numerical model using the Finite Element Method.  
 
• The model simulations will be used to support the interpretation of the measured data and 
to assess the sensitivity of some of the parameters included in the governing equations be-
hind the model.

2 Background 
2.1 Clayey till deposits 
Clayey tills constitute approximately 40% of the sediment types at 1 m below ground level in Den-
mark (Pedersen et al. 2011; Bockhorn et al. 2014), as illustrated in Figure 2. Most of these clayey 
till deposits can be traced back to the last glacial period, the Weichselian glaciation, which ended 
approx. 17,000 years ago (Gravesen et al. 2014). During the Weichselian, glaciers invaded Den-
mark from the Scandinavian highlands and the Baltic depression, as illustrated in Figure 3 
(Houmark-Nielsen 1999). Shifts in growth and decay of the ice-sheet, erosion, deposition and de-
formation are reflected in the geological heterogeneities of the glacial deposits found in these areas 
today, which comprise a large range of grain-sizes, lithological textures, mineralogy and rock me-
chanical properties (Klint et al. 2013).  
 
Figure 2. The distribution of sediment types below the plough and culture layers, typically at 1 m below surface.  
The brown areas denote clayey till (Pedersen et al. 2011).   
Clayey till is the common translation of the Danish term ‘moræneler’ (in English: moraine clay), 
which is used to describe this widely found glacial deposit in Denmark. It is a poorly sorted sedi-
ment type, but the clay content should be at least 12% for it to be categorised as clayey till. The 
rest is comprised of silt, sand, gravel and rocks (Larsen et al. 1995; Gravesen et al. 2014).  
 
==PAGE:15==
 
5 
 
Figure 3. The three main ice advances over Denmark during the late glacial maximum in the Weichselian  
(Vendsyssel Historiske Museum n.d.). 
The porosity of clayey till can vary considerably in line with the various soil content, but is often 
in the range 0.25 to 0.4 (e.g. Jørgensen et al. 1998; Gravesen et al. 2014). The vast majority of the 
void volume is found within microscopic pores between the grains in the unfractured clayey till 
matrix. Flow in the matrix occurs very slowly, which is reflected in the low hydraulic conductivity 
of the matrix, Kmat, typically in the range 10-8 to 10-10 m/s (Fredericia 1990; McKay et al. 1993). 
Due to this fact, clayey till was for long considered to be sufficiently impermeable to protect the 
underlying groundwater aquifers from being contaminated from e.g. pesticides applied on the 
ground surface or landfills within clayey deposits, as the contaminants were not expected to leach 
through this supposedly protective layer. However, numerous studies since then have showed that 
the many geological heterogeneities in the soil, such as fractures, macropores and sand lenses, can 
increase the hydraulic conductivity of the till greatly, and act as rapid transport routes for solutes 
(e.g. Jørgensen & Fredericia 1992; McKay et al. 1993; Gravesen et al. 2014; Jørgensen et al. 2017). 
This type of sediment comprising both matrix pores and fractures or macropores is often termed 
double-porosity medium (Tuncay & Corapcioglu 1995). The term macropore is here used for bio-
pores, which are typically formed by burrowing soil organisms such as worms, or by plant roots 
(Jørgensen et al. 2002). Fractures can be attributed to e.g. tectonic stress, shearing and stress relief 
due to glacial ice movement, desiccation during past periods of low water table, or freezing and 
thawing processes (McKay & Fredericia 1995; Jørgensen et al. 2016; Bockhorn et al. 2017). Ver-
tical or near vertical fractures are usually most prominent, but horizontal and randomly oriented 
fractures are also frequently found (McKay & Fredericia 1995). The size and the amount of frac-
tures and macropores generally decreases with depth, since some of them are dead-end, while oth-
ers penetrate the whole matrix until reaching a different sediment layer or the underlying ground-
water aquifer.  
 
Since fractures and macropores in a soil deposit are not uniform in size or distribution, the impact 
of these heterogeneities on the total flow through a deposit can only be estimated by using a number 
of assumptions and simplifications. Calculations and numerical simulations used to support 
==PAGE:16==
 
6 
experimental investigations commonly require the assumption that flow mainly takes place in only 
one type of heterogeneity and is not divided between different path types such as fractures and 
macropores. Even though visual inspection can indicate where the main flow paths are located with 
the help of dye tracers, it requires very accurate measurements to quantify exactly how the flow is 
distributed, in order to reproduce it numerically. It is therefore common to assume that flow mainly 
takes place in fractures, which allows the estimation of fracture apertures and the hydraulic con-
ductivity of a fracture using the cubic law (Snow 1968).  
 
The fracture aperture in which flow actually takes place is not always equal to the mechanical 
aperture. Mechanical aperture is the arithmetic mean aperture of a rough-walled fracture (Steele et 
al. 2006). The part of the aperture in which flow actually takes place is often called the effective 
aperture or hydraulic aperture (O’Hara et al. 2000). The relationship between mechanical and hy-
draulic aperture is non-linear, but for large apertures, the hydraulic aperture has proven to be similar 
to the mechanical aperture (Renshaw 1995). The relationship for smaller apertures is however more 
complicated, as the surface roughness plays a larger role in small apertures. By performing hydrau-
lic measurements, indirect estimates of the hydraulic aperture can be obtained by using the cubic 
law. Specific technologies exist for determining the mechanical aperture experimentally, where, 
for example, the deformation in the fracture can be subtracted from the deformation of the intact 
rock (Renshaw 1995). Mechanical apertures have been shown to continue to decrease below the 
minimal hydraulic aperture, which reflects the complex relationship between the two in small frac-
tures. Apertures are furthermore known to be influenced by factors such as effective stress, where 
increased effective stress can decrease the fracture apertures and therefore lead to increased flow 
resistance (Walsh 1981; Witherspoon 1986). 
 
Fractures and macropores can often be distinguished visually from the clayey till matrix based on 
their colour. In the oxidised, weathered zone beneath the plough layer, the clay matrix has a yel-
lowish-brown colour. In the unweathered zone below, the colour of the clay is grey, which is at-
tributed to the clay minerals. The change from grey below to brown above can be explained by 
oxidation of the chlorite and illite content in the clay (Ruland et al. 1991). Fractures and macropores 
are often coated by either red or grey colours, which make them visually detectable. Red fractures 
are often found both in the oxidised and the reduced zone. The red colour is attributed to Fe(III) 
oxides, which have precipitated and blocked the paths during periods with low groundwater tables. 
Grey fractures and macropores are mainly found in the oxidised zone and the grey colour is usually 
a sign of active flow paths. There is, however, not complete consensus about what the grey colour 
can be attributed to. Some consider the grey paths being calcareous, that carbonate minerals that 
have leached from the soil zone are now "present as coatings or areas of alteration around fractures" 
(Ruland et al. 1991). Other studies consider the grey colour being a result of reduction of Fe(III) 
oxides (Jørgensen & Fredericia 1992; Jørgensen et al. 2017). Furthermore, grey fractures and 
macropores are sometimes surrounded by red halos, which are secondary Fe(III) oxides and are 
clearly distinguishable from the surrounding yellowish-brown clay matrix (Jørgensen et al. 2017). 
Some studies have also found black stained fractures which is probably due to precipitation of 
manganese hydroxides (Ruland et al. 1991; McKay & Fredericia 1995).  
 
Fractures can extend from the top soil through the oxidised zone and into the reduced zone below. 
According to Ruland et al. (1991), fractures up to a depth of 10 m below ground level (b.g.l.) have 
==PAGE:17==
 
7 
been found in clayey till deposits in Ontario. Gravesen et al. (2014) consider that contaminant 
leaching is generally less likely to occur in the reduced zone. However, they also found very deep 
fractures at a clayey till site in Sjælland, where they extended through a 7 m oxidised zone and up 
to 3 m further in the reduced zone, which they expected would counteract the protection capability 
of the reduced clay significantly. Macropores deriving from roots have been found to a depth of 5-
6 m in Denmark (Jørgensen et al. 2017). Since fractures and macropores can act as rapid transport 
routes, the fact that they can extend directly from the ground surface to such depths in the subsur-
face poses a considerable risk to the groundwater aquifers below.  
2.2 Mobilisation of pesticides 
Clayey till deposits commonly fall under the term aquitards. They can contain significant amounts 
of water due to their relatively high porosity, and they often receive water via infiltration from the 
ground surface. They are furthermore able to transmit water into regional groundwater aquifers 
(Freeze & Cherry 1979; Fetter 2001). The permeability of aquitards is, however, usually not con-
sidered sufficient for the implementation of production wells in these deposits, which is how they 
can be distinguished from aquifers that are successfully used for groundwater abstraction.  
 
Figure 4. An example of soil saturation and groundwater conditions near the ground surface (Freeze & Cherry 1979). 
These deposits can be divided into three zones based on their saturation and fluid pressure; a satu-
rated zone, a capillary fringe and an unsaturated zone, as illustrated in Figure 4 (Freeze & Cherry 
1979). The saturated zone is located below the groundwater table, where all pores, including frac-
tures and macropores, are filled with water. In the saturated zone, the fluid pressure is greater than 
atmospheric pressure, and the hydraulic conductivity is constant. The groundwater table acts as a 
boundary between the saturated zone and the capillary fringe. In the capillary fringe, the pores of 
the unfractured matrix are saturated, but fractures and macropores are dry. The fluid pressure is 
less than atmospheric. The thickness of the capillary fringe depends on the grain sizes, where fine 
deposits such as clay have a considerably larger capillary fringe than coarse soils (Barnes 1995). 
The small capillary fringe in Figure 4 is therefore more characteristic for a sandy deposit. The 
unsaturated zone is located between the capillary fringe and the ground surface, and in this zone, 
the unfractured matrix is only partially filled with water, and fractures and macropores are dry. The 
fluid pressure is less than atmospheric, and both the hydraulic conductivity and moisture content 
are functions of the pressure head. Due to the pressure being less than atmospheric in the unsatu-
rated zone and the capillary fringe, water can be transferred from these zones to the atmosphere 
only via evaporation or transpiration. Outflows such as springs or flow into wells can only come 
from the saturated zone (Freeze & Cherry 1979).  
 
==PAGE:18==
 
8 
The thickness of each of these zones and the depth of the groundwater table can fluctuate signifi-
cantly due to different phenomena. Natural processes such as air entrapment in the void spaces, 
phreatophytic consumption and atmospheric pressure effects can affect these zones, and also ac-
tions such as groundwater pumping, artificial recharge, agricultural irrigation and drainage (Freeze 
& Cherry 1979). The focus here will, however, be on precipitation that infiltrates into the subsur-
face, which also can lead to mobilisation of contaminants and transportation from the ground sur-
face via the infiltrating water. The infiltration capacity depends on the soil-moisture content of the 
soil, where a soil that is initially dry has a high infiltration capacity. When the soil-moisture content 
increases, the infiltration capacity decreases due to decreased capillary forces. Eventually the infil-
tration capacity becomes approximately constant (Freeze & Cherry 1979; Fetter 2001).  
 
Seasonal variations are reflected in a high groundwater table during winter, where precipitation is 
usually at its maximum while evaporation is minimal, while the groundwater table is lower in sum-
mers, due to increased evaporation limiting how much water is infiltrated to the subsurface. Storm 
events can produce high flow velocities in the preferential flow paths and enable the transport of 
contaminants (Hasholt et al. 2000; Kladivko et al. 2001; Jørgensen et al. 2004). Pesticides on the 
ground surface can therefore be transported deep in the subsurface very quickly, so the time avail-
able for interaction or sorption to soil is shorter than during low or normal infiltration events 
(Jørgensen et al. 1998). Pesticide sorption in soil is often represented as an equilibrium process, 
but during preferential water flow, the rates of adsorption and desorption may not be maintaining 
these equilibrium conditions (Kladivko et al. 2001). Using known equilibrium sorption coefficients 
to predict pesticide transport in soil can therefore result in underestimated amounts of contaminants 
leaching through the soil deposit when flow velocities are high. The timing of pesticide application 
on the ground surface, and the frequency and magnitude of precipitation events also play an im-
portant role for possible leaching of pesticides. Both highly soluble pesticides and strongly sorbing 
chemicals have been shown to have a high risk of leaching after a rainfall event occurring shortly 
after the application (Kladivko et al. 2001; McGrath et al. 2010; Vendelboe et al. 2016).

2.3 Transport of pesticides and findings in groundwater 
Pesticides have been used extensively for years, and pesticide findings in groundwater can origi-
nate from application on fields for agriculture, but also from cities, parks, landfills, roads and rail-
ways, from spillage and runoff (Thorling et al. 2019; COWI 2001). In Denmark, the EU standards 
for pesticides in groundwater and drinking water are in force. According to these quality criteria, 
an individual pesticide or a relevant metabolite, degradation and reaction product detected in 
groundwater and drinking water alike should not exceed 0.1 µg/L, and the sum of all individual 
pesticides or metabolites detected should not exceed 0.5 µg/L (European Commission 1998; Euro-
pean Commission 2006). The national groundwater-monitoring programme (GRUMO) and The 
land monitoring programme (LOOP) provide data on pesticide findings in groundwater monitoring 
wells and water supply wells in Denmark. In 2017, pesticides or metabolites were detected in 
32.5% of the monitoring wells, and the quality criteria were exceeded in 10.7% of the monitoring 
wells. The detection limit is 0.01 µg/L. Figure 6 shows the distribution of pesticide findings in 
active water supply wells in the years 2013-2017.  
 
Figure 6. Pesticides and metabolites in 5,971 active water supply wells in Denmark in 2013-2017. The results are separated into 
three intervals: No pesticides detected (grey), at least one pesticide detected at least one time, under the limit value (0.01-0.1 
µg/L) (blue), and, at least one pesticide detected at least one time over the limit value (>0.1 µg/L) (red) (Thorling et al. 2019). 
When investigating transport of pesticides and the vulnerability of groundwater aquifers towards 
pesticide contamination, the physico-chemical properties of the respective pesticides need to be 
considered. Some pesticides are conservative and the transport of these is mainly controlled by 
==PAGE:20==
 
10 
advection through the preferential flow paths. The transport of some pesticides can be affected by 
diffusion into the much less mobile pore water of the matrix, which can delay the leaching through 
the preferential pathways (McKay et al. 1993). Some pesticides also have a high sorption or deg-
radation potential which can delay the solute transport additionally. However, these processes often 
depend on the flow velocities, where rapid preferential flow can limit the time for interaction of 
the pesticides with the soil grains, organic matter and microbial degraders. Low residence time may 
therefore result in in less sorption and degradation than known equilibrium distribution coefficients 
and degradation rates indicate.  
 
Sorption and degradation are often interlinked. Degradation depends on the amount of compound 
that is available for the microbial degraders, which again depends on the sorption capacity of the 
compound. Sorbed compounds are generally considered to be unavailable to microorganisms un-
less desorption occurs first, since most microorganisms can only access the compound in aqueous 
phase. It has, however, been shown that some microorganisms can access the sorbed fraction of 
compounds (Feng et al. 2000). If degradation of otherwise reactive pesticides appears to be mini-
mal, it may also indicate lack of nutrient availability, a reduced size and activity of microbial pop-
ulations, or the lack of competent degradative microbial populations (Sørensen et al. 2006). Deg-
radation of pesticides has been showed to mainly occur in the upper soil profile, the plough layer, 
which often has a high amount of organic material and degrading microorganisms compared to 
deeper soils (Sørensen et al. 2006; Bech et al. 2017; Jørgensen et al. 2017). Degradation often leads 
to formation of reaction products or metabolites, which in some cases are more mobile than the 
mother pesticide and therefore have a higher potential for being leached to the groundwater table. 
The only way to remove pesticides completely from the subsurface is by complete mineralisation 
(Sørensen et al. 2006; Bech et al. 2017). Just like degradation processes, mineralisation capacity 
usually decreases with depth. Many pesticides are degraded or mineralised under aerobic condi-
tions only, which can be a problem as the conditions can be anoxic in clayey till aquitards from a 
shallow depth and also in many groundwater aquifers (Sørensen et al. 2006; Jørgensen et al. 2016; 
Jørgensen et al. 2017). Pesticides leaching to the groundwater can therefore be spread further for 
decades to come.

2.5 Flow-through experiments using the LUC method  
Field investigations of flow and transport in macropores and fractured media often suffer from the 
lack of control over boundary conditions. It is therefore necessary to compliment field investiga-
tions with controlled laboratory studies that allow the manipulation of boundary conditions while 
reproducing in-situ conditions. Idealised laboratory studies are, however, often limited by a small 
sample size with limiting representation of heterogeneities in the soil, lack of sample intactness or 
rim flow (Allred 2000; Jørgensen et al. 2019). Using the LUC method, these shortcomings can be 
minimised, and the method can be extremely useful for investigating flow and contaminant 
transport in complex media.  
 
LUC experiments, as described by Jørgensen et al. (2019), are performed using a soil column of 
size 0.5 m in diameter and 0.5 m in length, which is collected from a field and installed in a flexible-
==PAGE:22==
 
12 
wall permeameter. The LUC is covered with a flexible membrane that is sealed to the soil column 
and, combined with the pressure from the water in the permeameter, prevents rim flow during flow 
and transport experiments. The permeameter is used to reproduce in-situ effective stress and tem-
perature conditions for the LUC. Due to the large size, the LUC can properly represent soil heter-
ogeneities from the field site. A clayey till LUC would therefore typically contain structures such 
as fractures and macropores from root channels and soil animals, depending on the sampling depth, 
that can contribute significantly to the overall hydraulic conductivity (Jørgensen et al. 1998; 
Jørgensen et al. 2017).  
 
A few important precautions need to be taken for the LUC experiments to be successful and to 
generate representative results. This includes that the column needs to be completely saturated, as 
the hydraulic conductivity may otherwise be underestimated (Fredericia 1990; Fetter 2001). Rim 
flow can result in overestimations of the hydraulic conductivity and produce artificial preferential 
flow paths which are not present in the natural setting of the column. Furthermore, the effective 
stress acting on the LUC needs to be considered thoroughly, as it can have an impact on factors 
controlling the flow, such as fracture apertures and hydraulic conductivities (Witherspoon 1986).  
 
The LUC method was first introduced in 1992 and has since then been used for flow and transport 
experiments for different compounds, such as chloride tracer, the pesticides mecoprop and sima-
zine, and DNAPL (e.g. Jørgensen et al. 1998; O’Hara et al. 2000). The results from LUC experi-
ments depend, however, greatly on the conditions at the site from where the LUC is sampled, such 
as the soil material, the sampling depth, the amount of heterogeneities, microbial degraders and 
organic matter.

3.5 Solute transport experiments 
After confirming the stability of the LUC with the hydraulic measurements, the preparation for the 
solute transport experiments was continued. The solution was prepared, the pump used for flow 
control tested, O2 sensors were installed, and flow rates and sampling frequency were determined. 
Thereafter, the transport experiment was started by injecting the tracer and pesticide solution.  
3.5.1 Injected solution 
The solution that was used for the solute transport experiments consisted of a mixture of the con-
servative tracer bromide at a concentration of 80 mg/L, and the pesticides bentazone, MCPA and 
tebuconazole, each at a concentration of 20 µg/L active compound, or a total pesticide concentra-
tion of 60 µg/L. The main physico-chemical properties of the compounds are noted in Table 1 in 
chapter 2.4. The solution was mixed using water from the tap at GEUS, which was considered an 
acceptable alternative to groundwater directly from the field, as the tap water derives from ground-
water and only undergoes minimal treatment in the form of airing and filtering.  
 
Four batches of solutions of 10 L each were prepared in a laboratory at GEUS. Each batch was 
prepared maximum two days before it was taken into use, as a long storage time could increase the 
risk of chemical reactions to take place. The solution was stored in a refrigerator at 5°C during the 
experiments. A total of 35.1 L of solution was injected to the LUC during the experiments.

3.5.2 Flow control 
The flow during the transport experiments was controlled using a peristaltic pump called Precise 
Peristaltic Pump BT100-2J from the producer Longer Pump. The pump head type was DG-2, 10-
roller. The combination of the pump and pump head could operate with flow rates from 0.012 mL/h 
to 1920 mL/h, depending on the diameter of the pump tubing  and the chosen pump speed, which 
could be varied from 0.1 to 100 rounds per minute (rpm) (Longer n.d.). A Two-Stop ICP Peristaltic 
Pump Tubing of purple-black type was used, with an inner diameter of 2.20 mm (PerkinElmer 
2014).  
 
For the transport experiments, a flow rate was chosen that represents an extreme rain event causing 
high infiltration and rapid leaching of solutes to the subsurface. Preliminary model simulations 
were performed prior to the experiments in order to assist the decision of flow rates and injection 
period. It was preferred to obtain relatively high normalised concentrations of the conservative 
tracer at the column outlet, so that the breakthrough of the compounds with higher retardation 
capacity would still be clearly detectable within the time frame of the experiments. The gravel layer 
was expected to delay the breakthrough significantly, which limited the possibility of basing the 
decision on flow rates and injection periods used in other similar experiments that did not contain 
such highly porous gravel layer in the setup.

subjected to a minor temperature increase until it reached the O2 sensor spot.

3.6 Dismantling LUC and visual inspection 
Upon completion of the solute transport experiments, the column was injected with a dye tracer. 
Thereafter, the permeameter was dismantled and the LUC was opened to allow visual inspection 
of the preferential flow paths. 
3.6.1 Dye tracer injection 
The dye tracer Erioglaucine disodium salt (CAS no. 3844-45-9), also known as Brilliant Blue FCF 
(BB) (Kemibrug 2010) was injected into the LUC directly after the transport experiments ended. 
BB was supplied as powder which is easily soluble in water. It is approved as a food additive in 
the EU (European Commission 2018) and has a clear blue colour when dissolved. BB was dis-
solved in tap water for a final concentration of 2 g/L. The solution was injected into the LUC using 
the peristaltic pump and a gradually increasing pump rate, starting at 20 rpm and ending up at 72 
rpm, with an average flow rate of 450 mL/h. The injection was stopped when the breakthrough of 
BB was visible in the effluent line, which was after approx. 8 hours.  
3.6.2 Dismantling the LUC 
The column was drained off through the influent tubes overnight while still being subjected to cell 
pressure that should prevent rim flow of the BB solution from occurring during draining, as that 
could complicate the visual inspection and estimations of the main flow paths. Thereafter, the water 
in the permeameter cell was drained off, as well as the cooling fluid in the copper coil. The upper 
part of the permeameter was then lifted to expose the LUC.

The gravel layer in the top of the LUC setup was carefully removed by hand while inspecting the 
occurrence of BB. The column was photo-documented regularly. When the stratigraphic bottom of

4 Numerical model 
A two-dimensional (2-D) model was built using the Finite Element Method (FEM) in COMSOL 
Multiphysics version 5.4. The LUC experimental setup was reproduced in the model, in order to 
simulate the water flow and solute transport from the experiments numerically.  
 
FEM is based on partial differential equations (PDEs) that express problems dependent on space 
and time (COMSOL 2017). The PDEs are solved using numerical methods, computing an approx-
imation of the real solutions to the PDEs. The model included separate flow and transport compo-
nents. For the flow component, steady state conditions were used, while transient conditions were 
used for the transport component. The flow model needed to be solved before the transport model 
was computed, as the two were connected by the velocity field, which was computed by the flow 
model. The governing equations behind these two components are described in the following.  
 
COMSOL Multiphysics has been used to carry out similar flow and transport simulations in a LUC 
setup and/or a clayey till field setup, as reported by Thalund-Hansen (2018), Lanters (2019) and 
Jørgensen et al. (2019). However, these studies reported simulations of conservative tracers only, 
whereas simulations of pesticide transport in the LUC setup have not yet been reported. The model 
created in the present study was built in the PDE interface, which then could be used as a supple-
ment to simulations in the Darcy's law interface of the software, created by Mosthaf, K. (2019, 
CLAYFRAC). Preliminary simulations using the Darcy's law interface were used as a support 
when planning the LUC experiments for this study, the flow rates and the injection period. These 
simulations indicated that the difference between 2-D and 3-D simulations in the Darcy's law in-
terface is not extreme and that the 2-D simulations made good approximations to the experiment. 
This was considered an advantage, since 2-D simulations usually require significantly less compu-
tational effort than 3-D and therefore allow for a more extensive parameter sensitivity assessment 
within the same time period. Therefore, it was decided to base the model of this study on the PDE 
interface in 2-D only.

(Jørgensen et al. 2019). 2b was expected to be similar to the 2b values calculated with the method 
presented in section 3.4.1. The matrix flow, which is not separated from the fracture flow in section 
3.4.1, is, however, defined separately in the flow model. Nevertheless, this is not expected to result 
in considerable differences in the resulting 2b values since only a minor amount of the total flow 
is expected to take place in the matrix. The different definition of fracture widths is what primarily 
differentiates the two methods. The method in section 3.4.1 uses the total fracture trace width meas-
ured on the cross-sectional area of the LUC. In the model, however, each fracture width is identical 
to the domain width.

5 Results and Discussion 
5.1 Reproduction of in-situ conditions 
The cell pressure, Pcell, produced by using the permeameter setup for the experiments was the main 
contributor to the effective stress acting on the LUC in the laboratory during the experiments, σ′DPe. 
The aim was to reproduce the effective stress acting on a point at the bottom of the LUC sampling 
depth in the field, σ′E-F+EGH. The groundwater table was below the LUC sampling depth at the time 
of the sampling, since it had been warm and dry for 3 months prior to this. The saturation of the 
matrix itself was however unknown. The capillary fringe is, however, known to be very large in 
clay soils, where it can extend to 10-30 m, compared to sandy soils where it is usually not larger 
than 1 m (Barnes 1995). Measurements from a site in Havdrup with similar geology and elevation 
as the Holbæk site revealed that the unfractured matrix remained saturated at all measured depths 
in the summer, presumably due to the large capillary fringe. Furthermore, during winter, the 
groundwater table at that site was up to 0.5 m b.g.l. when it was at its highest (P. R. Jørgensen, 
personal communication, April 17, 2019). It was assumed that the conditions were similar at the 
Holbæk site.

the σ′ values. The equations are given in chapter 3.3, and the measured dry bulk density of 1.87 
kg/L was used (cf. section 5.3). The gravel used in the LUC setup was assumed to have the same 
saturated unit weight as the soil. The resulting unit weights are shown in Table 4.

5.1.1 Cell pressure variations and leakage control 
The Pcell appeared to fluctuate during the experiments, despite the constant head setup of the per-
meameter. During the first five days after installation of the LUC in the permeameter, Pcell fluctu-
ated between 36 and 45 kPa. This was expected to be a due to a minor uncertainty of the manometer 
used to read the Pcell and was not considered to be a limitation since these fluctuations have also 
been present during earlier LUC experiments without it having any detectable influence on the 
results (P. R. Jørgensen, pers. comm., January 25, 2019).  
 
Shortly after installation of the LUC, it was discovered that there appeared to be a slight leakage in 
the setup, possibly due to water from the permeameter entering the column, since there was more 
water coming out from the effluent line compared to the amount injected through the influent line. 
The leakage corresponded to approx. 25 to 33 mL/h. In an attempt to find and eliminate the cause 
of the leakage, the permeameter was drained off five days after the installation, and the LUC was 
checked for leakage again with vacuum test and by covering the outside of the setup with soap 
foam and visually inspecting whether the foam was affected be air leaking out. This revealed a 
minor rupture in the membrane, which was fixed by covering the membrane with a layer of silicone. 
The permeameter was then closed and filled with water again, producing a similar high Pcell varying 
from 41 to 46 kPa during the next six days. The leakage had decreased after the re-installation and 
was now corresponding to maximum of 12 mL/h.  
 
Since Pcell was in the high end of the aimed range, and fluctuating to slightly above the range (cf. 
Table 6), the Pcell was decreased by lowering the water reservoir used for producing the hydraulic 
head of the cell. This was also a last attempt of further reducing the leakage, since a lower Pcell 
could eliminate a possible leakage from the permeameter into the LUC. After the reduction, Pcell 
fluctuated between 25 and 29 kPa during the next 28 days while the last preparations for the 
transport experiments took place. During this time, the leakage was fluctuating mainly between 0 
and 6 mL/h. It was considered too risky to drain the column again and remove it from the pressure 
cell to check the membrane again. Compared to the planned flow rates for the experiments, a leak-
age of 6 mL/h corresponds to approx. 0.9% of the high flow and 7% of the low flow, respectively. 
It was therefore not considered to be a critical limitation for the experiments. Furthermore, the 
leakage may in fact be a result of consolidation and not an actual leakage through the membrane. 
Consolidation can occur when the column is being saturated and the mineral grains change places, 
causing a slight reduction of the porosity and leading to more water coming out at the effluent 
compared to the amount of water being injected through the influent (Freeze & Cherry 1979; Fetter 
2001).  
 
During the transport experiments, Pcell fluctuated from 24 to 33 kPa, with an average of 29 ± 2.3 
kPa. There is an increased risk of rim flow when Pcell, and subsequently σ′DPe, is decreased. How-
ever, rim flow did not occur despite the decrease in Pcell after the first eleven days (cf. section 5.2) 
which indicates that the σ′ was still sufficiently high.

5.4 Hydraulic properties of the LUC 
The data obtained during the hydraulic measurements were used to estimate the stability of the 
hydraulic properties of the LUC. The bulk hydraulic conductivity of the LUC, Kbulk, was calculated 
from the flow data, which also allowed the estimation of hydraulic fracture apertures, 2b, and hy-
draulic conductivity of the fractures, Kfrac. These were then compared with the hydraulic conduc-
tivity of the unfractured matrix, Kmat.  
 
Equation 3.16 was used for checking whether the flow during the measurements was turbulent or 
laminar, since the flow estimations were based on Darcy's law and the cubic law, which both are 
developed under the assumption that the flow is laminar. This resulted in low Re values at all times 
during the flow measurements and the transport experiments, ranging from 0.02 at the lowest gra-
dients (i = 0.055) up to 0.7 at the highest gradients (i = 1.7). Judging from this, it can be safely 
assumed that the flow was not turbulent at any times.

lower Pcell, which was similar to the Pcell during the transport experiments that followed. In Figure

According to Witherspoon (1986), flow is no longer in accordance with the cubic law when aper-

it is reasonable to use the cubic law. Nevertheless, the cubic law assumes that 2b is constant be-

closure points (O’Hara et al. 2000). The estimated 2b values are furthermore based on the assump-
tion that all preferential flow took place in the fractures during the experiments, while in fact some 
of the flow occurred in the macropores (cf. section 5.6). The obtained 2b values are therefore 
merely estimates.  
5.4.3 Hydraulic conductivity of the fractures  
Kfrac was estimated using equation 3.14. Using the average 2b of 91 µm, Kfrac was estimated as 
5.2´10-3 m/s. As equation 3.14 implies, an increase in 2b by factor 2 results in Kfrac increasing by 
factor 4. The variations in 2b were however not that large, and lead to a variation of Kfrac between 
2.4´10-3 m/s and 6.3´10-3 m/s. The lowest values were within period 4, during low flow.  
5.4.4 Fracture spacing 
The relation between Kbulk, Kfrac and Kmat is described with equation 3.15. Kmat was, as expected, 
much lower than Kbulk and Kfrac. By rearranging equation 3.15 and inserting the average values for 
2b (91 µm), Kbulk (3.3´10-6 m/s), Kfrac (5.2´10-3 m/s) and Kmat (1.5´10-9 m/s), the average fracture 
spacing, 2B, was calculated as 0.14 m. For a cube of 0.5 m, this corresponds to approx. 3.5 frac-
tures, which is consistent with the estimated amount of fractures needed in the model geometry (cf. 
section 5.4.2).

5.5.2 Oxygen variations 
The dissolved O2 concentration in the influent line was measured occasionally throughout the 
transport experiment, and were, as expected, relatively constant. The mean O2 concentration in the 
influent line was 11.2 ± 0.40 during high flow, where 12°C was estimated as the temperature of 
the solution, and 9.51 ± 0.74 mg/L during low flow, where 17°C was the estimated temperature. 
These values are within the expected range, as they are similar to the solubility of O2 in freshwater, 
which is 10.8 mg/L at 12°C and 9.7 mg/L at 17°C (Rounds et al. 2013).  
 
The O2 concentrations in the effluent line were, however, not constant. The results are shown in 
Figure 23 together with the measured pH in the effluent, which was approximately equal to the pH 
of the influent solution during the whole experiment. The O2 was at all times lower than in the 
influent line. In the effluent, the O2 increased during high flow, but after switching to low flow, the 
O2 decreased rapidly. It is not certain what caused this rapid change. Judging from the pesticide 
==PAGE:54==
 
44 
BTCs (cf. section 5.5.3), there did not appear to be any degradation of MCPA taking place, which 
otherwise was the solute that was most likely to be mineralised. It would also be unlikely that 
degradation would result in such pronounced changes in O2, since the injected pesticide concentra-
tions were only 20 µg/L. However, there may have been organic matter in the LUC that was being 
degraded, resulting in these variations in O2. Degradation is more likely to occur during low flow 
rates rather than high, which can explain why the O2 decreases with the flow rate. This, however, 
does not explain why the O2 starts to increase again after the solution injection has stopped, since 
the flow rate was unchanged at that time. Also, microbial activity in soils from such depths is rarely 
large enough to contribute to a significant degradation (Sørensen et al. 2006; Bech et al. 2017). 
 
Figure 23. Oxygen and pH development in the effluent line over the total elapsed time during the transport experiments.  
The change in O2 is, at this point, more likely to be explained by inaccuracies, caused by gas accu-
mulation in the system, which also could explain the variations in Kbulk and 2b. During low flow in 
the transport experiments, it was observed that gas was slowly accumulating in the influent tube 
between the pump and the influent valves. This may have happened due to a small leak in the tubes 
or fittings, or due to gases in the solution. When the solution is pumped out from the 5°C refriger-
ator and towards the influent valves at room temperature, the gases may have come out of the 
solution and formed small air bubbles in the influent bottle and the tubes. Once there are air bubbles 
in the system, it may cause even more O2 to come out of the solution and go into gas phase. The 
gas can then be transported into the LUC, despite the attempt at flushing the gas out of the influent 
line (cf. chapter 3.5.4).  
 
The O2 was also measured shortly while injecting tap water to the LUC before the transport exper-
iments started. The same low flow as in the experiments was applied at first, where the O2 did not 
exceed 0.3 mg/L. When the flow was increased to the same high rate as in the experiments, the O2 
started to increase, and the measurements were stopped when the O2 had reached approx. 2.5 mg/L, 
after 5 hours at the high flow rate. These observations confirm that O2 variations are not purely 
related to the injected solutes, since the injection was not started at that time.  
5.5.3 Solute breakthrough 
The BTCs for the bromide and the pesticides showed in the following illustrate the aqueous con-
centrations by the column outlets over the accumulated effluent volume. The switch in flow rates 
is marked in the figures, as well as the duration of the solution injection. The exact total pore 
volume of the LUC is unknown. The clay matrix had a porosity of 0.3, which corresponds to ap-
prox. 25 L pore volume. However, almost all of this volume is found within the unfractured clay 
==PAGE:55==
 
45 
matrix, and only a minor part is represented by the fractures and macropores. When the LUC was 
drained off after the experiments, approx. 4.5 L of fluid was collected. This volume is expected to 
derive form the porous gravel layer and the fractures and macropores, while the complete saturation 
of the unfractured matrix is assumed to be unaffected by the draining process, which only lasted 
overnight. The exact porosity of the gravel is unknown but is expected to be within the range 0.3-
0.45, which corresponds to a pore volume of 4.5-6.2 L. For the model simulations, a gravel porosity 
of 0.45 was used, since this facilitated a better reproduction of the measured BTCs compared to 
using a lower porosity. The volume of fractures and macropores within clayey till columns used 
for earlier LUC experiments typically ranges between 0.25 and 0.4 L (Jørgensen, P. R., pers. 
comm., March 20, 2019). Therefore, it is assumed that the pore volume of the LUC, which can be 
used for interpreting the graphs in the following, is somewhere between 4.5 L and 6.8 L.  
 
Since the numerical flow simulations showed that manually changing the 2b input had a significant 
effect on the simulated hydraulic gradient, it was assessed what effect such change in the 2b input 
had on the solute transport by observing the simulated BTCs. The model simulations revealed that 
the 2b adjustment did not have any significant effect on the simulated BTCs for the solutes. This 
is presumably because the flow rates are fixed in the model as the average high and low flow rates 
from the experiment. A change in 2b will therefore leave the flow unaffected and will only affect 
the resulting hydraulic gradient, which is a variable that is determined by the fixed model inputs. 
Due to these observations, the transport simulations were carried out using a fixed 2b of 117 µm 
in the 2-fracture model and 102 µm in the 3-fracture model that were the 2b values that matched 
the gradients corresponding to the high flow rate and the gradients used in the hydraulic measure-
ments before the transport experiments.  
 
Breakthrough of bromide 
The measured and simulated normalised BTCs for bromide are shown in Figure 24. The measured 
bromide peaked at approx. 0.8 normalised concentration, just before the flow rate was reduced. 
The flow rate reduction resulted instantaneously in a slight decrease in the outlet concentrations, 
until it started increasing slowly again until the peak concentration was almost reached again. When 
the solution injection stopped and the column was flushed with water at an unchanged flow rate, 
the outlet concentration rapidly decreased at first, and the decrease became slower with time.  
 
The simulations using the 2-fracture model result in slightly higher concentrations during the initial 
breakthrough compared to the simulations from the 3-fracture model. However, after switching the 
flow rates, the 2-fracture simulation fits the measured curve better than the 3-fracture model simu-
lation does. The parameter values in the 3-fracture model were identical to the values used in the 
2-fracture model, and it is therefore only the amount of fractures and 2b that is different. The pa-
rameters in the 2-fracture model had already been adjusted in order to test how they affected the 
BTC and to try to reproduce the measured data. Since the 2-fracture simulations matched the meas-
ured data relatively well except for the slightly higher initial breakthrough, it was decided not to 
carry out similar parameter assessment on the 3-fracture model due to time restrictions. Therefore, 
all the parameter adjustments for bromide in the following apply to the 2-fracture simulations.

For obtaining the simulated BTCs in Figure 24, some of the parameter inputs, whose exact values 
were unknown, had been adjusted within a reasonable range to find the best fit to the measured 
BTC. This mainly included the porosity of the gravel (ngr) and spacers (nsp) and also the hydraulic 
conductivity of the gravel (Kgr). The adjustments revealed that out of these three parameters, ngr 
had the most pronounced influence on the BTC. It was found that by setting ngr to 0.45, the simu-
lation during low flow fit almost perfectly, and the concentration increase during high flow was 
also closer to the measured data than when lower ngr values were used. nsp had a negligible influ-
ence on the BTC compared to the ngr since the spacers constitute much less volume than the gravel. 
nsp was in the end fixed at 0.4. Kgr also had a much less pronounced effect compared to ngr and was 
in the end fixed at 0.1 m/s, the same as for the spacers.  
 
One of the noticeable differences between the measured and simulated BTCs in Figure 24 is that 
both the 2-fracture and the 3-fracture simulations had a more rapid initial increase of the BTCs and 
also a faster decrease when the solution injection was stopped. These differences are presumably 
triggered by the same sources. None of the n or K parameters that had been adjusted affected the 
initial breakthrough or the decrease when the injection stopped. A delay in initial breakthrough is 
often caused by sorption, but it is unlikely that this was the case since bromide is not expected to 
sorb. An attempt at reproducing the delay in the initial breakthrough by adding a low sorption 
coefficient not only delayed the initial breakthrough, but also caused a significant decrease of the 
second peak when the solution injection stops, which was not the case in the experiment. It was 
therefore concluded that the initial delay was caused by some other factor than sorption.  
 
The aqueous diffusion coefficient, Dw, was fixed at 2´10-9 m2/s for all the bromide simulations. 
Since the tailing of the measured data fits well to the 2-fracture simulation, this implies that the Dw 
value was reasonable. During solution injection, the solutes diffuse into the almost immobile pore 
water in the matrix where the concentration initially is low. When flushing starts, however, the 
bromide slowly diffuses back into the preferential pathways where the concentration now is lower, 
and this causes the long tailing of the curve.

however, not possible to conclude at this point whether the sorption of tebuconazole is reversible

more extensive studies with a greater focus on the sorption mechanism. It may also be that the

5.6 Visual inspection of flow paths

bottom of the column, in order to locate and quantify the preferential flow paths. In the following,

sampled (between 1.95 and 2.38 m b.g.l.). Both the top and bottom end surfaces were traced during 
installation of the column and depended therefore on visual inspection of grey and red fractures

red colour indicates Fe(III) oxides that block the flow paths. The depths in between the ends were 
mapped after the experiments by tracing the BB dyed fractures which clearly mark the active flow

based on the BB stains around them. Judging from the very small diameters, these are presumably 
root channels that were formed by plant roots penetrating into the fractures.

There was one vertical fracture that extended from the LUC top to the bottom, and crossed at least 
half of the LUC diameter. This fracture was exposed by carefully cutting away the matrix blocks 
on the one side of the fracture, exposing the fracture wall on the other side, as seen in Figure 33a. 
There was a negligible amount of visible macropores within this fracture, but nevertheless, the 
whole fracture wall was stained by the BB. These findings indicate that both the macropores and 
the fractures in the column, independent or joined, are active flow paths. This contradicts the

findings of a few recent studies that have compared flow in macropores with flow in fractures. 
Jørgensen et al. (2002) estimated that 94% of the flow was conducted via root channels within 
fractures and only 6% within fractures without root channels. This is illustrated in Figure 33b, 
which is from a field site in Salløv at a similar depth as the LUC sampling depth, and shows an 
exposed vertical grey fracture wall where BB transport only occurred along conductive root chan-
nels that were formed in the past by roots that penetrated into the fracture (Jørgensen 2019). Other 
studies that find biopores to be determining rapid flow in tills to a great extent include Rosenbom 
(2005) and Jørgensen et al. (2017).

It was clear from the visual inspection that the active flow paths in the LUC were dominated by 
grey paths, such as the one in Figure 32 and Figure 33a. The grey colour is by some researchers 
described as the result of a reduction of Fe(III) oxides (Jørgensen & Fredericia 1992; Jørgensen et 
al. 2017). The fracture in Figure 32 furthermore has a slight red-orange halo surrounding the grey 
colour. Such halo was also found by Jørgensen & Fredericia (1992) that proposed the following 
chemical processes as the reason for the halo. Fe(III) oxide reduction that leads to the grey colour 
on the fracture surfaces, results in a build-up of dissolved Fe(II) in the fractures and macropores. 
The Fe(II) then diffuses into the matrix, where they are met by dissolved oxygen. The Fe(II) is then 
oxidised again and forms secondary Fe(III) oxide precipitates, which leads to this red rim surround-
ing the grey reduced fracture or macropore wall (Jørgensen & Fredericia 1992). This red halo is 
also seen in Figure 34 by a vertical fracture extending to the rim of the column.

Red fractures walls were also found in the column (cf. Figure 35), but only a minimal amount of 
these were stained by the BB, limited to a few cases where a red fracture was in direct connection 
with a conductive grey fracture. The red colour on the fracture walls is due to Fe(III) oxide precip-
itates, which can decrease the hydraulic fracture apertures significantly, and thereby minimise or 
prevent flow to take place in these paths. The same was observed for macropores, where 
macropores containing Fe(III) oxide were not stained by BB. Figure 36 shows two macropores, 
both independent of a fracture, where one is grey (iron-reduced) and clearly an active flow path, 
while the other does not have this grey colour, but is more stained by Fe(III) oxides, and corre-
spondingly is not an active flow path. These findings imply that preferential flow mainly occurs in 
grey, iron-reduced fractures and macropores, which is consistent with most other recent studies, 
and contradicts McKay & Fredericia (1995) that attributed flow through a clay-rich deposit to ox-
idised fractures.

Macropores are typically younger than fractures, since they are usually formed by the growth of 
plant roots or by soil burrowing organisms, while fractures can often be traced much further back, 
e.g. to tectonic stress. The diameter of macropores is also usually larger than fracture apertures, 
which was evident when dissecting the LUC. Due to these factors, precipitated Fe(III) oxides can, 
according to Jørgensen et al. (2017), limit the flow in fractures even more than they limit flow in 
macropores at the same depth. This was however difficult to point out when investigating the LUC, 
since e.g. Figure 36 shows a red macropore without flow, even though the diameter does not appear 
fully blocked. However, the diameter is presumably not constant along the whole macropore length 
and may be fully blocked at other depths, which can explain the lack of BB around this macropore.   
 
In this study, it was not feasible to quantify how much flow was taking place in fractures and how 
much in macropores, in other ways than visually judging how deeply blue the stains were. The 
following calculations and model simulations are as previously mentioned based on the assumption 
that all flow takes place in fractures, which allows the use of the cubic law.  
5.7 Prospects for future research 
The flow-through experiments performed in this study provided a valuable insight into the flow 
and transport of tracers and pesticides through complex media. However, a few limitations were 
encountered during the experiments that prevented conclusions to be drawn about some of the 
observations. With some relatively simple adjustments or additions to the experimental procedure, 
it may, however, be feasible to eliminate some of these limitations in future research, which could 
benefit the experiments and increase the reliability of the outcomes.  
 
The interpretation of the results from the solute transport experiments would presumably benefit 
from having more sample replicates or repetitions of each sample analysis. This would enable a 
statistical analysis of any variation in the results. The possibility of this depends, however, on the 
financial restraints for the respective study.

==PAGE:68==

It would increase the reliability of the hydraulic investigations of the LUC if the flow measurements 
before the solution injection were not only carried out by manually adjusting the gradients, but also 
by using the same pump that would subsequently be used for the transport experiments. Then it 
could be investigated further whether the hydraulic gradients are not only determined by the pump 
as expected, but also influenced by the elevation of the water tables in the influent and effluent 
bottles, as indicated by the observations of the present study.  
 
Flow measurements could furthermore be carried out with more variations in effective stress, in 
order to estimate the relation between effective stress and the hydraulic properties of the LUC such 
as the Kbulk and 2b. The gradient should then be kept approximately constant for a considerable 
amount of time, so that the LUC would have a chance to adjust properly to the changes in effective 
stress.  
 
The interpretation of the O2 variations during the experiment could benefit from more extensive 
O2 measurements from a longer period while the column is merely flushed with water. This might 
enable the determination of whether the O2 concentration is independent of the pesticides, or 
whether it is mainly determined by the applied flow rates and a possible accumulation of gas in the 
system.  
 
The model investigations should preferably be carried out with the same total fracture trace width 
as observed during visual inspection of the LUC. This is, however, presumably only possible to 
accomplish if a 3-D model is used. Furthermore, it may benefit the interpretation of the measured 
tebuconazole if the transport model was extended with a non-linear sorption isotherm, which may 
enable determining whether the tailing of the measured tebuconazole BTC is according to non-
linear sorption, or it whether it may be affected by non-equilibrium sorption mechanisms.  
 
Despite the limitations that were encountered during the experiments, the BTCs for bromide and 
tebuconazole obtained with the numerical simulations were a remarkably good fit to the measured 
BTCs.

6 Conclusion 
The LUC was successfully installed in a permeameter which was used to imitate in-situ temperature 
and pressure conditions. The effective stress was at all times within a range of values that were 
expected to occur in the natural setting of the LUC. Rim flow was successfully prevented during 
the experiments, and all preferential flow paths starting at the column end were assumed to have 
been activated since the injected solution appeared to be evenly distributed across the LUC end 
surface. When opening and dissecting the LUC after the experiments, the flow paths were visually 
inspected. Judging from the BB dye tracer injection, the flow primarily took place in grey iron-
reduced fractures and macropores. It was evident that flow occurred both in individual macropores, 
in macropores that were located within fractures presumably as a result of roots penetrating into 
the fractures in the past, and also in fractures without any visible macropores. The matrix and the 
red Fe(III) oxide stained fractures and macropores accounted for a negligible amount of the flow. 
These observations indicate how complex the flow system in clayey till can be.  
 
The distribution of flow between the grey fractures and the matrix is also reflected in the strong 
contrast in the hydraulic conductivities. The average Kfrac was estimated as 5.2´10-3 m/s by using 
the cubic law, while Kmat was measured as 1.5´10-9 m/s. The average Kbulk for the LUC, estimated 
from flow measurements before and during the solute transport experiments, was 3.3´10-6 m/s. 2b 
was also estimated using the cubic law, resulting in an average of 91 µm. A change in the effective 
stress appeared to have a slight influence on Kbulk and 2b, and the estimated 2b appeared to decrease 
from 98 µm to 79 µm simultaneously with the flow rate reduction during the transport experiment. 
The decrease in 2b was consistent with the numerical flow simulations, where the 2b input had to 
be adjusted by approximately 20 µm in both the 2-fracture and the 3-fracture model in order to 
match the hydraulic gradients corresponding to each of the flow rates in the experiments. The ap-
parent decrease in 2b during low flow in the experiment may have been a result of gas accumulation 
in the system.  
 
The BTC for bromide at the LUC outlet was characteristic of a conservative tracer. Using the 2-D 
model setup representing the LUC experiments, it was feasible to numerically simulate a BTC that 
fit the measured bromide considerably well, apart from a slight overestimation of the initial break-
through during solution injection.  
 
The results obtained from the pesticide analysis indicated that bentazone and MCPA were not sta-
ble in the solutions, reflected in considerable fluctuations in the injected solutions and also in the 
BTCs at the LUC outlet. Despite the fluctuations, the results indicated that the transport was iden-
tical for bentazone and MCPA. The tebuconazole results indicated more stability than for benta-
zone and MCPA. Based on the stability of the bromide and tebuconazole BTCs, it was not consid-
ered likely that the gas accumulation, which may have affected the hydraulic properties of the LUC, 
was responsible for the fluctuations in bentazone and MCPA. If that were the case, it would be 
expected that the transport of all of the injected solutes would have a similar response to the gas 
accumulation. The fluctuations are therefore more likely related to the instability of bentazone and 
MCPA in the prepared solutions.  
 
==PAGE:70==
 
60 
The transport of tebuconazole was in all probability affected by sorption. This was observed al-
ready at the start of the solution injection, where the breakthrough of tebuconazole at the outlet was 
delayed compared to the conservative bromide. The sorption did not appear to be limited by the 
high flow rate, since it was feasible to simulate an approximate reproduction of the measured BTC 
during the whole solution injection period at both the high and the low flow rate using a linear 
distribution coefficient of 9 L/kg, which assumes equilibrium sorption. When switching from high 
flow to low flow, the outlet concentrations decreased considerably, indicating that the sorption 
mechanism was enhanced when the residence time in the LUC was increased. This was consistent 
with the model simulations. However, the simulations did not successfully reproduce the tailing of 
the measured BTC after the solution injection was stopped. This indicates that the sorption mech-
anism at that time was in non-equilibrium, as it appears that the desorption was occurring at a 
slower rate than predicted by the using the equilibrium distribution coefficient. It was, however, 
not feasible to carry out model simulations using other sorption isotherms such as the Freundlich 
isotherm within the time frame of the study, and therefore it cannot be concluded whether the 
difference between the measured and simulated tebuconazole was caused by non-equilibrium sorp-
tion, a result of an inappropriate sorption isotherm, or influenced by other unidentified factors.  
 
The solute transport results indicated how diffusion may lead to long-term leaching through a 
clayey till deposit at low concentrations, based on the bromide BTCs when the LUC was flushed 
with water. The results also showed how sorption mechanism cannot always be predicted by as-
suming equilibrium sorption, which may result in wrongful predictions of the risk of pesticides 
leaching through the deposit. It therefore remains important to investigate the transport of con-
servative and reactive solutes and to further develop numerical models so they will take account of 
the factors that can greatly influence the transport.  
 
Despite limitations that were encountered during the experiments, and despite a number of assump-
tions that lie behind the estimations and model simulations, such as all fractures having the same 
aperture, no flow occurring in macropores or that these can be considered a part of the fracture 
system, and that there was no channelling flow along the fractures, the simulations of bromide and 
tebuconazole fit the measured BTCs remarkably well. This indicates that it is achievable to produce 
good approximations to actual solute transport with a 2-D model using the Finite Element Method, 
which is a great advantage when assessing solute transport on a larger scale, where it may not be 
feasible to carry out field measurements or laboratory experiments with numerous samples. The 
results presented in this study may therefore be applicable to the CLAYFRAC project and for the 
assessment of groundwater vulnerability to pesticide contamination.
