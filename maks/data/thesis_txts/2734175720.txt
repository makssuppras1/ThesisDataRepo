Abstract
Fatigue cracks in orthotropic steel decks are a problem in many bridges across the globe, including
Denmark, where the largest bridges in the country are build with orthotropic steel decks. Finding
and repairing the fatigue cracks is a crucial task to maintain the structural integrity of the decks.
In this thesis a 36 m long section of the New Little Belt Bridge in Denmark is examined, and a
method using modal expansion and virtual sensing is tested to predict strains in the deck. The
thesis uses no response data from the bridge. Instead a FE-model resembling the bridge is con-
structed, using drawings from the bridge designer, COWI. Two FE-models are created: FE-model
01 represents the actual bridge, while FE-model 02 represents the FE-model of the actual bridge
(FE-model 01). A simple load script is used to generate the load of a moving vehicle. This is then
used to generate the response data from FE-model 01. The method uses 12 nodes as sensor nodes;
9 in the vertical direction and 3 in the lateral direction perpendicular to the driving direction of the
bridge. These sensor nodes are used to conduct operational modal analysis, to determine the dy-
namic properties of the bridge (FE-model 01), such as the natural frequencies and corresponding
mode shapes. Through the modal assurance criteria these mode shapes are compared to a modal
analysis of FE-model 02. The accuracy is found to be 0.99, 1.00, 0.99, 0.99, 1.00 for mode 1-5.
By virtual sensing and using the modal analysis from FE-model 02, mode shapes and strain mode
shapes are determined. These are used to calculate the predicted strains which are compared to
the strains taken directly from FE-model 01. The results found, that when exposing FE-model 01
to noise, the proposed method was highly accurate with time response assurance criteria of 0.95-
0.99. When exposing FE-model 01 to a vehicle loading, the results are less accurate, especially
for nodes close to the load path of the vehicle. Thus, the method did not satisfyingly predict the
strains for nodes located in the load path. However, the results demonstrated the effectiveness of
the proposed method in predicting strains due to random loading. Furthermore, it was determined,
that the root cause for the proposed method being inaccurate for nodes located in the load path,
was due to bad modelling of the connections. Thus, further research is needed to create a more
accurate model for predicting the strains.
Lastly, using Rainflow counting as well as the Eurocode 2, the concept of using the strains to
predict the life expectancy of welds was explored. This investigation showed, that the dynamic
effects were under the fatigue limit, however by including an estimate of the quasi-static loading,
the life expectancy of the weld was calculated to approximately 43.31 years.

1.2
Background and Motivation
Orthotropic steel decks (OSDs) were invented in the late 1950s, and thousands of bridges around
the world, has since its invention, been built using OSDs [1]. One of the advantages of using
an OSD is, that it is much lighter, compared to a concrete deck. Thus, it is especially suitable
for bridges with large spans. However, since the OSD was introduced in the late 1950s, a great
number of bridges have experienced cracks occurring in the OSD, well before the bridge reached
its design life. These cracks occur due to the repetitive cyclic loading from the traffic and are
referred to as fatigue cracks. For instance, fatigue cracks were observed in the OSD of the Severn
1
==PAGE:26==
2
Chapter 1. Introduction
bridge, located in the UK, in 1971 [2], only 5 years after it opened in 1966 [3]. More recently,
fatigue cracks have also started occurring in the OSD of the New Little Belt Bridge, Denmark,
after 47 years of service life [4]. Figure 1.1 is an example of a fatigue crack, which has been found
in the OSD of the New Little Belt Bridge.
Figure 1.1: A fatigue crack in the OSD of the New Little Belt Bridge. From the paper Experiences
with repair of fatigue cracks in an orthotropic steel deck, new little belt bridge, denmark, by P. L.
Sørensen, N. Bitsch and P. Holt [4].
Fatigue cracks in OSDs is a problem for multiple reasons. First and foremost, fatigue cracks
propose a danger to the structural integrity of the bridge. However, as implied in the naming fa-
tigue crack, the danger is not present immediately, but increases if the cracks are not dealt with.
Secondly, repairing fatigue cracks is expensive, especially if the bridge has to be closed during
maintenance work. For example, it was estimated that the cost of each weld repair for the New
Little Belt Bridge was 9.000 USD [5]. Lastly, visual inspections inside of the OSD have to take
place frequently to ensure new cracks are repaired. This is both a costly and a tedious task.
For OSD bridges which have been built already, and where fatigue cracks are starting to occur,
there is no way around the expenses related to the repair itself. However, a method/tool which
could predict the strains, thus, when and where fatigue cracks are likely to occur, without the need
of measuring sensors placed all over the bridge, would greatly reduce the amount of visual inspec-
tions needed. A lot of progress have been made in the field of dynamics for the past years, yet
when it comes to strain estimation for a whole structure, the solution so far, is to either measure
the strains directly using strain gauges, or creating a detailed Finite Element (FE) model. These
methods are both time consuming and expensive.

The occurrence of fatigue cracks in OSDs is a well known problem in the literature. In Review
on Fatigue Problems of Orthotropic Steel Bridge Deck by Z. Qinghua et al. (2017) [2] 7000 fa-
tigue cracks observed for OSD bridges in Tokyo are categorized. Out of all the observed fatigue
cracks, the most common type is cracks appearing in weld joints between the longitudinal rib and
diaphragm. This type constituted 38.2 % of the cracks. Furthermore, different procedures for
assessing the fatigue performance for structures are discussed. Lastly, the developing direction of
fatigue studies is laid out.
Using Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) to determine the dynamic properties of OSDs, trough
experimental setups, has been studied in several papers. For example in Monitoring of orthotropic
steel decks for experimental evaluation of residual fatigue life by G. Tecchio et al. (2017) [6], or
Dynamic Characterization of the Little Belt Suspension Bridge by Operational Modal Analysis by
S. S. Christensen et al. (2018) [7]. The latter determining the dynamic properties for the New
Little Belt Bridge, which is the structure investigated throughout this thesis.
When speaking of literature concerning the New Little Belt Bridge, COWI has published pa-
pers mapping and investigating the fatigue cracks, which have been occurring. This is done in
the papers Experiences with repair of fatigue cracks in an orthotropic steel deck, New Little Belt
Bridge, Denmark by P. L. Sørensen et al. (2017) [4] and Fatigue life prediction of New Little Belt
Bridge steel deck under a cracked pavement section: Comparison between a monitoring-based
and a FE modelling approaches by I. F. Alcover et al. (2017) [5]. In the latter a FE-model of the
bridge was constructed to investigate the remaining fatigue life, since cracks in the pavement had
occurred. The paper was published before the first fatigue cracks in the OSD were observed. For
an intact pavement the study concluded that fatigue in the OSD was not a problem, however, for a
fully cracked pavement fatigue cracks should already be visible.
This thesis will utilize methods called modal expansion and virtual sensing to predict strains.
The first of the aforementioned methods has been utilized for strain prediction in the papers Op-
erational modal analysis based prediction of actual stress in an offshore structural model by M.
Tarpø et al. (2017) [8] and in Vibration-based FE-model updating for strain history estimation
of a 3MW offshore wind turbine tower by S. D. R. Amador et al. (2023) [9]. Both papers relate
to offshore structures, where the first paper compares the predicted strains with a scaled offshore
platform model, which is excited by a nylon brush. The latter paper compares the predicted strains
to an actual offshore wind turbine, where measurements have been made. Virtual sensing is ex-
plained and utilized, to predict strains in locations where physical sensors are not present, in the
paper Data-driven virtual sensing and dynamic strain estimation for fatigue analysis of offshore
wind turbine using principal component analysis by M. Tarpø et al. (2021) [10], again concerning
an offshore wind turbine.

New Little Belt Bridge, but around the world as well. Furthermore, it has been shown, that us-

as virtual sensing to predict strains, are topics which have already been investigated in the litera-
ture. However, conducting OMA, modal expansion and virtual sensing, on OSDs to predict where
stress/strain concentrations occur, has yet to be researched. Therefore, the purpose of this thesis

1.4
Objective and Tasks
The main goal of this thesis is, to prove the concept of a method which can accurately predict the
stresses and strains for an entire bridge structure, using the response from only a few sensors. This
method will be based on the dynamic concepts virtual sensing and modal expansion. It should be
emphasized, that this thesis is only a proof of concept. Meaning, this thesis will provide a start-
ing point for further research, which inevitably has to be conducted, to have a fully functioning
method/tool. A fully functioning method/tool would in theory be able to predict, when and where
certain strain thresholds for a bridge with an OSD are exceeded. Thus, when and where cracks are
likely to occur.
To prove the concept, the thesis will investigate the OSD of the New Little Belt Bridge. A section
of the bridge is modelled in a commercial FE-software, and the local dynamic effects of a moving
load crossing the bridge deck are investigated. Typically, one would compare the obtained results
from a dynamic analysis using a FE-model, with measurements obtained from the actual structure.
However, there is no available measured responses from the New Little Belt Bridge. Therefore,
the entire investigation will be carried out using two different FE-models. The first model, is re-
ferred to as FE-model 01 throughout the thesis, and the purpose of this model is to represent the
actual Little Belt Bridge girder. It is used to simulate the response of the bridge when subjected
to a moving load. The second model is referred to as FE-model 02 throughout the thesis, and
represents the FE-model of the real bridge (FE-model 01). The dynamic analysis of FE-model 02

To accomplish the main goal, the following tasks have to be addressed:
• Creating FE-model 01: A FE-model representing the real structure has to be created.
• Generate load history: The load history for a moving load representing a vehicle crossing
the bridge has to be generated.
• Apply load history and run transient simulation: A transient analysis of FE-model 01
has to be carried out, to obtain the responses at selected sensor nodes.
• Dynamic analysis of the response from FE-model 01: A dynamic analysis is carried out
on the response of FE-model 01. This in done through OMA.
• Creating FE-model 02: A model representing the FE-model of FE-model 01 has to be
created. This model will contain errors on purpose.
• Dynamic analysis of FE-model 02: The dynamic analysis includes modal analysis and
determining the strain mode shapes. The proposed concept, is then used to estimate the
strain histories through modal expansion and virtual sensing.
• Comparison of strain histories: The strain histories estimated through the proposed con-
cept for FE-model 02, are compared to the strain histories obtained directly from the re-
sponse of FE-model 01.

1.5
Outline of Method
So far, a rough outline of the method to be followed in this project has been presented. In this
section, the tasks presented in Section 1.4 will be described in detail. Moreover, a road map for
the entire process is created. This road map is observed in Figure 1.3. Both the proposed process as
well the process which is actually followed in this thesis are outlined. In the figure it is showcased,
which section is addressing a given topic. The reader is encouraged to use the road map during
the read through of the thesis, to have an overview of how the processes and sections are related.
1.5.1
Proposed Process
The method combines measuring, dynamic analysis, and FE-modelling to estimate/predict strains
across the entire structure, when exposed to different types of loads and conditions. Measurements
of the structural response are made using a few number of sensors. However, a sufficient number
of sensors should be chosen, and fitted at different points on the structure, to ensure that the entire
structure is captured in the response and used in the analysis. The measured response is used in
an OMA to describe the dynamic properties of the structure such as natural frequencies and mode
shapes. The dynamic properties found in the OMA are compared to the dynamic properties of the
FE-model. Here both the natural frequencies and the mode shapes can be compared. The mode
shapes are the most important factor in the method, and are compared using the Modal Assurance
Criteria (MAC). If the dynamic properties of the OMA and FE-model do not match, the FE-model
is updated through an iterative process until a MAC value close to 1 is reached. Then the FE-model
can be used to determine the strain mode shapes of the structure, which combined with the modal
coordinates can be used to predict the strain of the entire structure. Lastly, a fatigue estimate for a
given detail can be calculated, based on the predicted strains.
1.5.2
Thesis Process
The process used in this thesis, replaces physical measured response data from the bridge, with
simulated response data from FE-model 01. The applied load history consists of 2 components:
A random vertical and horizontal noise as well as a moving load, representing a vehicle driving
over the girder. The load from the vehicle is estimated according to Eurocode 1 [11]. The system
response is taken directly from FE-model 01 as displacement in 12 degrees of freedom (DOFs).
The rest of the method consists of the same steps as outlined above, using OMA and MAC to
update FE-model 02 until a satisfying result is reached. Then, the method compares the strains
predicted from dynamic analysis with the strains taken directly from FE-model 01 used to simulate
the load history. Lastly, the predicted strains are used to illustrate, how the fatigue life can be
calculated for a detail.

1.6. Assumptions

1.6
Assumptions
A complex FE-model is time consuming, both to develop and simulate. Furthermore, simulating
a complex model, is computationally costly. The FE-models and corresponding simulations made
in this thesis, are constructed without the use of high performance computers (HPCs). Therefore,
a full scale FE-model, representing the New Little Belt Bridge has not been feasible to construct.
Thus, assumptions about the dynamic behaviour of the bridge have to be made, to simplify the
FE-models. The following assumptions about the dynamic behaviour are made:
• The hanger cables are modelled as pin supports. Thus, the dynamic behaviour of the whole
bridge is not considered, and only a section of the bridge is modelled. The length of this
section is chosen to be 36 m corresponding to 3x12 m sections between supports.

3.2
Operational Modal Analysis
The operational modal analysis is a key element in dynamic analysis of existing structures. As
the name suggests, it allows the structure to stay in operation while the data for an analysis is
collected. Therefore, it can be beneficial for structures that cannot be taken out of operation such
as bridges and offshore platforms.
The method aims at estimating dynamic factors such as natural frequencies and mode shapes
of the structure based on sensor measurements at different points on the structure. The measure-
ments used are most often related to the displacement of the structure either through acceleration,
velocity, or directly from displacement measurements.

sensors used to measure the response of the structure and N is the number of time steps used in the
measurements. The matrix is constructed after the signal is converted from a continuous measured

Singular Value Decomposition
The singular value decomposition of a matrix is a factorization method to decompose the matrix
into the matrix product of several matrices used to separate and extract data.
G = UΣV
(3.5)
The method can be used to get the singular value decomposition of the spectral density matrix.

the left- and right singular vectors, respectively. In dynamics, the left singular matrix contains the
mode shape vectors excited at a given frequency ranked by order of magnitude of excitation. By
knowing this, the 1st, 2nd, and nth mode shape can be found by looking at the left singular vectors

of a signal, by plotting the singular values against the frequency. The natural frequencies of a
response can be estimated as the peaks of the plot.

Creating boundary conditions that accurately represent the support conditions for the desired anal-

explain how the structure interacts with its surroundings.
The New Little Belt Bridge deck is supported by vertical hanger cables, which transfer the load to
the main cables, which transfer the load to the towers etc. Figure 2.3 shows, how a module of 12
m is supported by 4 hanger cables, that are anchored to the diaphragms spanning perpendicular to

structure, the supports are modelled as pinned supports, meaning, the supported nodes are re-

strains can be predicted using a FE-model as well as virtual sensing and modal expansion, a
complex traffic loading is not needed. If one can prove the concept for one moving load the
same concept can be used for an infinite amount of loads, only restrained by computational power.

m between the axles. The magnitude and internal distance of the load are determined according
to DS/EN 1991-2:2023 Load Model 1. Also, the load is simplified by only applying the load of
one wheel of each axle, meaning, 150 kN are applied along a line with 1.2 m spacing. The load

FE-model 02 is constructed to represent the FE-model of the real structure (FE-model 01). Typi-

a real structure, which always contains errors. Thus, FE-model 02 is identical to FE-model 01,

4.3
Capabilities and Limitations of the FE-Models
Given the geometry, mesh, and constraints implemented in FE-model 01, the model is capable of
capturing the response of an OSD with a cross section, very similar to the cross section of the
New Little Belt Bridge, for a moving load. In theory, multiple moving loads could be applied to
simulate a load more similar to actual traffic loading. However, only a section of 36 m with pin
supports is constructed. Thus, the model is not capable of capturing the global dynamic effects of
the bridge, meaning, the model can only be used to study local effects. Furthermore, the accuracy
of the stresses/strains is very limited due to the coarse mesh of the model. Yet, to prove the
concept, the strains do not have to be accurate to the real bridge, thus, the model should be capable
of proving the method.
4.4
Pitfalls and Learnings
In this section, an in-depth examination of the mistakes and learnings made when constructing the
FE-models is reviewed. This is done to help further research avoid making the same mistakes. In
this process, reflections about the learnings are made as well.
An accurate model representing the real New Little Belt Bridge is desired, in order for the re-
sults to be as realistic as possible. As mentioned throughout the thesis the main goal is to prove
a concept, thus, the accuracy of the FE-model is of lesser importance. The model was created
in Ansys APDL 23 R2 which proved to have a steeper learning curve than first expected. In the
beginning, when constructing the geometry by the creation of key points much time was spent,
generating a script that placed the key points at the right locations. At first, it was expected that
when meshing the model, Ansys would create nodes at the same places when a shell meets the
middle of another shell. However, this was not the case, meaning, the areas had to be aligned
which meant placing the key points at the right locations. Figure 4.16 shows a sketch of how areas
should be aligned to mesh properly in Ansys.
Figure 4.16: Sketch of how areas should be aligned to mesh properly in Ansys.
Furthermore, after the areas were aligned Ansys assigned 2 different nodes at the same location,
where two elements shared a node when meshing. This resulted in elements not being connected

extracting the strain histories of the model due to the response of the moving load involved issues

made of shell, Ansys provides a strain value for the top and bottom strain for each node. Further-
more, since the plates of the OSD have different thicknesses, a section was created for each plate
thickness. Where two different sections shared a node, Ansys would provide strains for the top

Problems were also encountered when creating the load files used to simulate the response in
Ansys. At first, a file containing the 8626 load steps for each node was created and loaded into

previous loads. Instead, 8626 independent LS files were created corresponding to each load step
containing the load information for each node. This greatly sped up the process of loading the load

0.0060 s, generating 8626 load files. On top of that, the model generated a result file of over 400
GB. This made it difficult to change or try new implementations in the model.

6.1.1
Filtering
Filtering is a crucial part of signal processing when dealing with the response of the structure.
Normally, the data would be collected using real sensors and equipment which might cause drift
problems due to temperature changes. This is not a problem in this thesis, since all responses are
collected from FE-model 01. The responses have been segmented as explained in Chapter 5. It is
described in [14], that a 50% overlap Hann-window is a good practical way of handling data. The
reason for this is, that the window minimizes spectral leakage due to discontinuities at the start and
end of each segment when using the Fast Fourier Transform. An overlap of 50% further ensures
the entire signal is captured and that start and end of segments are not neglected when segmenting.
6.1.2
Cutoff Frequency
So far, it has been stated multiple times that the first five modes are included in the dynamic
analysis. However, the reason behind choosing the cutoff frequency to include the first five modes
has not been discussed yet. The first five modes are included for several reasons. Firstly, from the
unfiltered SVD-plot of the sensors shown in Figure 5.4 it is observed, that modes 1, 2, 3, and 5
are the modes with the most clear peaks when only looking at the dynamic part of the frequencies.
One could also have used the first three modes, however, from Figure 5.4 it is observed, that also
the fourth and especially the fifth mode contain a reasonable amount of energy. To obtain more
accurate strain predictions, the first five modes are therefore included. Secondly, the first five
modes are all global modes as observed from the modal analysis shown in Figure 5.6-5.10. After

6.3
Loading
From Figure 5.3 where the signal for each sensor node is plotted it is observed, that the nodes are
excited with noise for the first 25 seconds, until a sudden drop in the displacements occurs. This
sudden drop is caused by the load crossing the bridge and it lasts for around half a second, putting
the nodes into motion afterwards. As mentioned in Section 4.1.4 the script used to generate the
load history ramps up the load to avoid the load acting as an impulse. However, the load is only
present for 7 load steps in each node for each wheel. Furthermore, the plots of the strains in the
frequency domain in Figure 5.12-5.19 indicate that especially Node 1 which is in the load path,
contain more energy compared to the nodes placed away from the load path. Taking all these facts
into account, the load is assessed to be acting as an impulse on the structure. Thus, the load applied
to the structure contains both random excitation forces as well as an impulse loading.
The method used to predict strains and thereby predict the total service life of the bridge due
to local dynamic effects, builds on operational modal analysis to estimate natural frequencies and
mode shapes, which are used to perform modal expansion. However, OMA which the method is
based upon, only works for random excitation. Thus, if the loading is not random, but acts like
an impulse the method is not suitable. This is due to the nature of the impulse, which excites all
frequencies not only the natural frequencies. This becomes evident when transforming an impulse
into the frequency domain, as is observed in Figure 6.3.
Figure 6.3: Example of an impulse loading in the frequency domain.
One could think, that this may explain why the TRAC and FRAC-values as well as the R2-values,
are much greater for Node 2, which is placed away from the load path. This node does not experi-
ence the load as an impulse. However, the strains are predicted using the mode shape matrix and
modal strains from the modal analysis of FE-model 02, meaning, the results from the OMA are
in principle only used for comparison. Modal expansion by itself is not limited to only work for
random excitation forces. Figure C.1 and C.2 in Appendix C, shows the displacement predictions
for Node 1, with TRAC-values of 0.998 and 0.999 in the x and y-direction, respectively. If the
impulse loading was the root cause of the inaccurate strain predictions, the displacement predic-
tions would be inaccurate as well. Since this is not the case this suggests, that the loading itself is
not the problem, but instead the root cause might be found in how the connection responds to the
loading.
The load model is a central part of being able to predict realistic strains in critical points along

the bridge. In this thesis, a relatively simple load model has been used to simplify the model work

the modal contribution is high.
Furthermore, the stress range of only the dynamic loading is considered for the nodes. From
Figure 5.35 it is observed, that all the stress ranges in the nodes are less than the fatigue limit
of 28.74 MPa. Therefore, fatigue will never occur in the trough-to-deck welds using the method
found in Eurocode, solely due to dynamic loading. However, this cannot be true as fatigue cracks
have been found after 47 years [4]. The reason for the low stresses in the considered nodes is most
likely due to the missing quasi-static stress. Therefore, a rough estimate of the quasi-static stress
is made from the number of trucks driving over the bridge per year. From this, the fatigue life of
the trough-to-deck welds is determined to approx. 43.31 years. This yields a difference of 3.69
years from what COWIs report found [4]. Even though a rough estimate of the quasi-static stress
is made, this shows that the quasi-static load contributes significantly to the fatigue life prediction
and is much greater than the contribution from the dynamic loading.
==PAGE:99==
7
Further Work
The results have been discussed in detail, and some suggestions to improve upon the project have
already briefly been made. In this chapter, these suggestions will be elaborated, and suggestions
for general improvements will be made as well.
7.1
Modelling
In Chapter 6 it has been established, that the inaccurate strain predictions for nodes located in the
load path, are due to poor modelling. Thus, the modelling of the model is a crucial aspect that has
to be improved upon for future research.
Both FE-model 01 and FE-model 02 are modelled entirely of 2D shell elements. In general, 2D
shell elements are preferred since they provide a high accuracy while still giving reasonable cal-
culation times. However, the mesh of the FE-models examined in this thesis is extremely coarse.
Thus, the most important feature to implement for further research would be to implement a finer
mesh, especially around the considered details. Moreover, one could also apply solid elements for
the considered details. Using solids instead of shells could help get a better understanding of, how
the strains/stresses are flowing in the corner due to both noise and vehicle load. Thus, this change
in elements may give a more accurate view of the connections where the top plate, diaphragm, and
trough meet.
Furthermore, to obtain accurate strain estimations, which can actually be used to predict, where
cracks will occur in reality the entire bridge would have to be modeled. A model of the entire
bridge would be able to capture not only the local effects of a moving load but also the dynamic
behaviour of the entire bridge. Furthermore, the quasi-static part of the loading affecting the com-
plete bridge could also be captured, if an FE-model of the entire bridge was made. As shown in
Section 5.5, the quasi-static load contributes greatly to the fatigue. Thus, for real strain predictions
the quasi-static loading of the entire bridge should be included as well. The boundary conditions
for a FE-model of the entire bridge would also have to be changed from pin to spring supports. In
doing so, the motion of the whole bridge would be captured.

==PAGE:100==

Chapter 7. Further Work
7.2
Loading and Method
In this thesis, the loading has been vastly simplified compared to how the actual New Little Belt
Bridge is loaded. Therefore, if subsequent research is made, where the responses from a FE-model
are used as is the case for this thesis, then more complex loading scenarios should be considered.
These loading scenarios should be a better representation of the real world and among other loads,
include forces from the wind and axial forces from cars. During the outline of the cracks and their
location in Chapter 2 it was shown, that a majority of the cracks occur when cars are driving uphill
on the bridge. Therefore, an investigation using a sloped FE-model could be relevant to see if the
analysis can predict a higher stress range when going uphill compared to downhill. Furthermore,
the dynamics of the car springs and dampers could be explored and added to the loading pattern.
The springs on the car will interact with the road and create additional vibration patterns while
driving across the bridge.
In order to include a more complex loading along with refining the mesh and maybe model some
details with solids instead of shells, as suggested in the previous section, one is strongly encour-
aged to use HPCs. As mentioned in 4.4 the relatively simple model investigated in this thesis took
more than 8 hours to run. One could only imagine how many days it would take to run a full-scale
model of the New Little Belt Bridge with a fine mesh and complex loading, using the same com-
puting power.
Another angle could be to try a completely different dynamic method. A different method which
may obtain better results, is the so called state space method, where the dynamic system is con-
sidered at different states. This model, however, is out of the scope of this thesis and the reader is
referred to the literature for a detailed explanation.
==PAGE:101==
8
Conclusion
It has been established that fatigue cracks are starting to occur in orthotropic steel decks all around
the world, due to the repetitive cyclic loading from traffic. As of right now, the only way to moni-
tor these cracks is either through visual inspections or large measuring campaigns.
Therefore, a method which can estimate strain hot spots for an OSD, using dynamic concepts as
operational modal analysis, modal expansion, and virtual sensing, has been investigated through-
out this thesis. This investigation has been limited to proving that such a method does indeed work
for a simple FE-model. The implementation for this method on a large scale would only require a
few sensors measuring the response of the OSD and it would greatly reduce the number of needed
visual inspections, to monitor the bridge for fatigue cracks.
The investigation of this proof of method has been done, based on the OSD of the New Little
Belt Bridge, Denmark. To conduct the investigation two FE-models have been created: FE-model
01, representing the real structure, and FE-model 02, representing the FE-model of FE-model 01.
FE-model 02 has purposely been modelled with a different steel density since FE-models of real
structures always differ slightly from their real counterparts. This procedure has been followed
since no real response measurements have been available for the New Little Belt Bridge. The FE-
models of the bridge have been vastly simplified, thus, the models are only able to capture the local
effects of the wheel to deck loading. However, this is deemed to be sufficient since all the cracks
occurring in the OSD of the New Little Belt Bridge, arise in the troughs where heavy vehicles are
driving. Furthermore, only one load case has been simulated: Half of a 2-axle point load of 150
kN per axle, with a 1.2 m spacing between the axles, for a vehicle driving 80 km/h. This load is
applied in trough 17 which supports the lane where heavy vehicles are driving. The simulation has
been made for FE-model 01 as a transient analysis in Ansys APDL 23 R2. Since OMA is used
on the response, random excitation forces are included in the simulation, both before, during, and
after the load is crossing the model.
The response of FE-model 01 has been examined through OMA, by choosing a total of 12 sensor
nodes: 9 vertical and 3 horizontal. Through the OMA the dynamic properties of the bridge section
are determined and compared to the properties of FE-model 02 when conducting a modal analysis
of this model in Ansys. Only the first five modes are included, since these modes are all global and
higher modes are all local. As expected, the comparison shows a great similarity between both the
natural frequencies and mode shapes, with MAC values of 0.9995, 1.0000, 0.9999, 0.9852, and
77

1.0000 for mode 1-5, respectively.
Three nodes in FE-model 02 have been picked and the strains are estimated/predicted in these
three locations. These nodes are referred to as Node 1, 2, and 3, where Node 1 is located in the
load path, Node 2 is located away from the load path, and Node 3 is located in the same trough
as Node 1, but where the load does not cross. The modal strains are needed to perform modal
expansion and have been estimated for FE-model 02 in the aforementioned three nodes, for the
first five modes. The magnitude of the modal strains corresponds to what is expected based on the
plots of the mode shapes of FE-model 02.
First, the mode shape matrix from FE-model 02 is used to determine the modal coordinates. Then
modal expansion is applied, using the modal coordinates, modal strains as well as the response
from FE-model 01, to predict the strains in Node 1, 2, and 3. Thus, it has been shown that the
outlined method works very well when predicting strains due to random excitation forces. When
the bridge model is exposed to a random load pattern imitating wind and other random forces,
the method exhibits a high accuracy in both the pattern and the amplitude of the response. This
has been shown in both the time and frequency domain, verified by both high TRAC and FRAC
values. Before and after the moving load was applied where only random excitation forces were
present, Node 2 displayed slightly greater TRAC and FRAC values, compared to Node 1 and 3.
However, when the moving load crossed the bridge both the TRAC and FRAC values were much
greater for Node 2, than for Node 1 and 3. Meaning, Node 2 which was located away from the
load, showcased a high accuracy for the entire simulation, while the accuracy of Node 1 and 3
dropped significantly when the moving load was applied. Thus, Node 1 and 3 did not exhibit a
satisfying result with regard to both pattern and amplitude of the strain prediction. This was the
case for both the time and frequency domain.
The above mentioned discrepancy is explained by first examining the moving load. The load was
determined to act as an impulse loading on the structure. OMA is not suitable for impulse loading,
however, by investigating the filtered displacement prediction of Node 1, the node showcased ac-
curate TRAC-values. Therefore, the load itself could not be the problem. A further investigation
of the strains in Node 1 was conducted by looking at the Ansys strain response for each element
intersecting in Node 1. From this investigation, it was found that the TRAC-value varied a lot
between the elements. The greatest TRAC-values were observed for the top plate elements with
scores of 0.973 and 0.768 in the x and y-direction, respectively. On the other hand, the lowest
TRAC-value was observed for the diaphragm element with a score of 0.006 in the y-direction. In
principle, the strain response in this detail should be the same for each element. This discrepancy
was deemed to be due to poor modelling of the connection.
It has also been investigated what precautions future research should take, to obtain more ro-
bust and accurate strain predictions. Among other things, the FE-models should be constructed
with a much finer mesh, perhaps using solid elements at detail connections to eliminate the strain
response discrepancy between the elements. However, such a model would most definitely have
to run on HPCs.
Furthermore, a Rainflow analysis of the predicted strains for Node 1, 2, and 3 has been con-

the fatigue strength curve for detail category 71. This showcased that the stress range when only
accounting for dynamic effects, would not lead to fatigue failure. A rough estimate of the quasi-
static stress was made based on available traffic data, and the fatigue life of the trough-to-deck
welds was estimated to be approx. 43 years.

Evaluation of project process 
 
The overall project process has been a good experience, especially the weekly meetings we have had with 
our supervisors have been a great help in pushing us forward and discussing our findings and questions. The 
further we have gotten in the project the more helpful these meetings have been. 
The timeline of the project was made in the first month of the project and was kept as planned with only 
minor changes to the different parts of the plan.  Furthermore, it has been positive and very helpful to be 
three people in this project since it required learning a new program (ANSYS) which was used to do all our 
simulations and modelling. By being three people it allowed for quicker learning.
