Abstract 
Truck exhaust after-treatment systems, particularly Catalytic Diesel Particulate Filter (CDPF) 
technology, are critical for meeting current and future legislation, such as Euro 6/VI and 
upcoming Euro 7 standards. These regulations impose strict limits on pollutant emissions, 
with a focus on particulate matter (PM), particulate number (PN) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) 
reduction. By specifically targeting PM and PN emissions, CDPFs play a critical role in 
addressing these challenges. Understanding the effects of ageing on CDPF catalytic activity 
and correlating performance with specific properties is critical for ensuring optimal 
performance throughout the filter's operational lifespan.  
The developed methodology successfully demonstrated the effect of ageing on the catalytic 
activity of field-aged samples, revealing significant differences between them, particularly at 
temperatures where NO oxidation is limited by reaction kinetics. A first-order plug flow mole 
balance approach was implemented to estimate the reaction rate constant for NO oxidation to 
quantitatively evaluate catalytic activity depletion in this region. Variations in the rate 
constant correlated with observed ageing patterns and activity test results, implying catalyst 
deactivation. 
To evaluate the ageing mechanisms that have the greatest impact on CDPFs, several 
characterization analyses were performed, including nitrogen physisorption, Scanning 
Electron Microscopy- coupled with an Energy Dispersive X-ray detector (SEM-EDX), X-ray 
Fluorescence (XRF), X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS), and Temperature 
Programmed Reduction (TPR). The surface area of the field-aged samples was significantly 
reduced. The presence of ash deposits in the samples was confirmed by SEM-EDX analysis. 
In the field-aged samples, XRF analysis revealed a significant increase in phosphorus and 
sulfur levels. To evaluate their impact, XPS and TPR analyses were performed, which 
revealed the presence of sulfate and phosphate compounds, as well as changes in the 
oxidation state of the catalysts.  
Overall, this study sheds light on the mechanisms that contribute to performance degradation 
and provides valuable insights into the ageing effects on CDPF catalytic activity.

1. Transportation: trucking role 
 
Transportation is an industry group which provides services to transport people and goods. It 
includes: air freight, logistics, airlines, marine, road, rail and the necessary infrastructure [1]. 
Transport has a key role in today´s economy and a significant impact on development and 
employment. An efficient way of transporting goods is fundamental for companies' success 
and represents the most significant supply chain expenses [2]. The impact of transportation on 
logistics costs can be observed in Figure 1. Specifically, trucking plays a critical role in the 
transportation of goods and materials across various sectors of the economy. It is responsible 
for moving goods from manufacturers to distributors, retailers and consumers. This sub-
industry supports supply chain logistics, ensuring that materials and products are delivered on 
time and in good condition. Additionally, it employs millions of people, contributes to 
economic growth, and facilitates connectivity between communities around the world [3], [4].  
 
Figure 1. Global logistics costs by function and mode 2018 [2] 
One of the main concerns of the heavy-duty transport industry nowadays is its environmental 
impact. For this reason, a lot of effort has been put into minimizing it. Scania CV AB is a 
Swedish company that provides transport solutions, including trucks and buses for heavy-
transport applications together with an extensive product-related service offering. The 
company is also a leading provider of industrial and marine engines. Its purpose is to drive the 
shift towards a sustainable transport system, by developing safe, smart and energy-efficient 
transport solutions [5].

2. Diesel engine and emissions 
 
In order to comprehend properly the environmental impact of diesel/biodiesel fueled trucks 
and how to reduce it, it is necessary to understand how the chemical energy stored in the fuel 
is transformed into motion.  
Even though there are multiple diesel engine designs, the general principle behind them is the 
same. The Scania modular engines are based on verified technology on a range of 9,11,13 and 
16 liters. They possess the same combustion chamber and analogous designed components 
[5]. The process carried out in the engine can be summarized in four main steps. Firstly, the 
intake valve opens and introduces air into the system. Secondly, the piston moves upwards, 
compressing the gas. Thirdly, when the piston is at the top, the injectors spray the fuel. Due to 
the elevated temperatures caused by the gas compression, the mixture combusts, pushing-
down the piston. This movement is transferred to the crankshaft, which converts the linear 
movement of the pistons into rotary motion. Finally, the exhaust valve opens, and the gas 
leaves the cylinder pushed by the piston. In the exhaust gas after-treatment section, the gases 
will be treated to fulfill the necessary environmental requirements. Figure 2 represents in a 
simplified way a four-stroke diesel engine cycle. 
 
Figure 2. Four-stroke diesel engine cycle. Showing the necessary steps to complete one 
operating cycle (intake, compression, power, and exhaust) [6] 
To ensure the correct lubrication of the system, reducing friction and unnecessary power 
losses, engine oil is used. Lubrication is not the only task that oil fulfills. It also prevents rust 
and corrosion, cools the engine, and holds contaminants in suspension, among others. Engine 
oils contain inorganic materials, such as Phosphorous (P), Sulfur (S), Calcium (Ca) and/or 
Magnesium (Mg). Additionally, some metals like Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu) or Aluminum (Al) 
may be present in some lubricants as a contaminant from engine wear [7]. The presence of 
these materials in the exhaust gas might affect the performance of the exhaust after-treatment 
system, accumulating in the system or even poisoning some of the catalytic converters. A 
similar problem can arise when biofuels are used, which are generally richer in inorganic 
compounds such as Potassium (K), Sodium (Na), Ca and Mg [8].

The concentration of exhaust gas components, especially pollutants, may vary. They are 
affected by the fuel composition, engine operating conditions, oil additives, ambient 
conditions, etc. Although the composition is not fixed, the main components present in diesel 
internal-combustion engines without exhaust after-treatment systems and their average 
composition can be observed in Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3. Exhaust-gas composition of diesel engines without exhaust-gas treatment system 
(percent by weight) [9] 
Some of these components have significant environmental and health implications. Nitrogen 
oxides (NOX) contribute to air pollution and smog formation, whereas particulates has the 
potential to cause respiratory and cardiovascular problems. Carbon monoxide (CO) is a 
poisonous gas and, hydrocarbons (HC) contributes to the formation of ground-level ozone and 
smog [10]. Understanding and monitoring the concentrations of these components is critical 
for developing emission control strategies and mitigating the harmful effects of these 
emissions.

3. Legislation  
 
The objective of the exhaust gas after-treatment system is to ensure that the gases emitted by 
the vehicle fulfil the environmental requirements stated in the correspondent legislation.  
Since 1992, the European Union members adopted a new policy to reduce transportation 
related emissions as part of an overall air-quality improvement strategy. It consists of six 
levels of increasingly stringent emissions requirements. Starting from Euro 1/I until Euro 6/VI 
[11]. To avoid confusion, Euro standards for heavy-duty vehicles are referred to with roman 
numerals e.g., Euro VI. For light-duty vehicles numbers are typically used. 
Different fuels, engine technologies and operation modes produce various levels of pollutants, 
and for this reason the standards work differently for petrol and diesel cars. Diesel engines 
produce more NOX than petrol ones and therefore the limit is set higher [12]. The Euro 6/VI 
emission standards emphasize the importance of reducing the amount of NOX released from 
diesel vehicles to enhance air quality and adhere to air pollution limits. Meeting these NOX 
emission standards would require setting challenging targets during the Euro 6/VI stage while 
still maintaining the benefits of diesel engines, such as high fuel efficiency and low emissions 
of CO and HC [11]. 
The fuel composition is strongly relevant for vehicle emissions. Hence, gasoline and diesel 
properties in Europe, as well as its additives, have been regulated to meet very stringent 
quality standards [11]. The presence of harmful components in the fuel may lead to more 
emissions and reduction of the exhaust-gas aftertreatment system’s efficiency and durability.  
Particulate matter (PM) in diesel exhaust is of special concern; because of its respirable size, 
they can enter the human lungs and harm them. Additionally, diesel PM is fundamentally 
made of black carbon, which is a short-lived climate forcer with a high global warming 
potential [13]. 
As in the European Union, other countries have adopted policies that regulate vehicle 
emissions. The current legislation Europe is Euro VI, whose main differences with its 
predecessor (Euro V) is its emphasis in the reduction of NOX, HC and levels of fine particles 
in weight and quantity.  
Figure 4 and Table 1 show a comparison between different countries' emission policies for 
heavy-duty vehicles and the Euro VI heavy-duty vehicle emission standards for diesel 
engines, respectively.

4. Exhaust after-treatment system: 
 
The current environmental legislation requires efficient and durable solutions for heavy-duty 
vehicles exhaust after-treatment. Moreover, Scania´s environmental goals go even further, 
targeting to archive an environmental impact of)) its products lower than the one stated in the 
legislation. Scania bets on the use of renewable energy sources and improvement of the 
exhaust  after-treatment system as the best near-term choice to reduce transportation 
environmental impact [5].  
Scania’s trucks exhaust after-treatment system consists of four main sections. Figure 5 shows 
a schematic overview of it. 
 
 
Figure 5. Main sections of Scania´s exhaust after-treatment system 
Firstly, the Diesel Oxidation Catalyst (DOC) receives the exhaust-gas produced in the engine. 
It converts HC and CO into carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O), while also converting 
nitrogen monoxide (NO) into nitrogen dioxide (NO2). After the DOC, the diesel particulate 
filter (DPF) removes PM. The particles trapped in the filter must be removed in a regeneration 
process. The presence of NO2, which is a strong oxidizer, allows the DPF regeneration at 
lower temperatures than the ones required to achieve appreciable regeneration rates with (O2. 
The exhaust then moves through an evaporator unit that releases a reductant, typically a urea 
solution, into the exhaust stream. This reductant is used in the selective catalytic reduction 
(SCR) catalyst to react with NOx and produce molecular nitrogen (N2) and H2O. Small 
amounts of ammonia (NH3) might not be converted. To prevent the release of NH3 into the 
environment, it is further processed in the ammonia slip catalyst (ASC) [16].

5. PM removal technology: Catalytic Diesel Particulate Filter 
 
The current, and even more the future legislation, put a lot of emphasis on the necessity of 
reducing PM emissions and increasing the after-treatment systems’ durability. In this matter 
DPFs play a key role. Scania´s DPF technology relies on catalytic diesel particulate filters 
(CDPF) to treat the PM formed in the combustion process. This technology is able to retain 
efficiently the PM, achieving efficiencies over 95% in mass and 99% in number over a wide 
range of engine operating conditions [13], [17]. However, its durability can be depleted due to 
the thermal stress to which the CDPFs are exposed and the presence of harmful materials in 
the fuel or additives, among others.  
Therefore, it is essential to examine the impact of filter ageing on its effectiveness, and to 
investigate the causes of this occurrence. This is particularly important for those factors that 
permanently decrease the catalytic activity of the filters, as this will influence their longevity. 
By identifying the primary mechanisms that contribute to the ageing of CDPFs in real-world 
settings, it is feasible to make appropriate modifications to the system in other to meet the 
necessary environmental requirements.

6. Objectives 
 
The main objective of this project is to cover the entire process from developing a method for 
evaluating the ageing effects to applying the method to real truck CDPF samples, as well as 
correlating the performance with the properties of the CDPF samples. 
The specific objectives to be achieved with the development of this project are as follows: 
• Conduct an extensive literature review of CDPF technology in heavy-duty vehicles, 
focusing on its principles of operation and the precursors of catalyst deactivation. 
• Develop and apply a methodology for evaluating the ageing effects on CDPF’s 
catalytic activity. 
• Characterize the catalytic properties of field-aged CDPF samples, to identify the key 
factors affecting their performance. 
• Contribute to the scientific knowledge on the catalytic ageing effects of CDPF and the 
factors influencing their performance, which can inform future research in this area.

1. Particulate matter  
 
PM is a complex mixture of solid and condensable materials. It can be present in exhaust gas 
in a wide range of particle sizes. Figure 6 (a) shows the typical mass and number-based 
particle size distribution of a diesel engine. Additionally, its components can be classified in 
three main groups: solid fraction, soluble organic fraction and sulfate particulates.  
The solid fraction is the most relevant one and it is primarily composed of soot (carbon) and 
ash. Soot is generated in the combustion chamber due to the incomplete fuel combustion. 
Following formation, the soot particles combine with one another, resulting in agglomeration 
and an increase in their overall size [18], [19]. Ash is made up of inorganic materials mainly 
derived from fuel and engine oils additives. This includes elements such as Fe, Cu, Cr and Al, 
which originate from engine corrosion, as well as Ca, Zn, K and Na, which come from lube 
oil and biofuels [13], [19]. Typically, they are present in the form of sulfates, phosphates, and  
oxides.  
The soluble organic fraction (SOF) refers to the portion of PM made of organic compounds 
(solids or liquids) soluble in water or other solvents. It is composed of heavy hydrocarbons 
from unburned fuel or lubricating oil, such as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons or alkanes 
[20]. These hydrocarbons can be adsorbed on the soot particles, altering their physical and 
chemical properties. SOF can also adsorb other pollutants and gases, like heavy metals or 
sulfur dioxide [18], [20], [21].  
Sulfate particulates are formed when sulfur present in the fuel is oxidized and forms sulfuric 
acid. This acid can condense and form sulfate particles, being a significant component of the 
PM [18]. Figure 6 (b) provides a standard representation of the PM composition for a heavy-
duty diesel engine.

PM is composed of a wide variety of compounds which depend on multiple variables. The 
size of PM pollution is directly related to its potential for causing health problems. The 
smaller the PM particle size, the more likely it is to penetrate deeply into the lungs and cause 
health problems such as irregular heartbeat, aggravated asthma, and airway irritation [21]. 
Additionally, it has a strong environmental impact, causing visibility impairment or 
contributing to acid rain effects, among others [21]. Due to its environmental and health 
impact, it is crucial to adapt the exhausts after-treatment system to the PM properties, to treat 
it efficiently and durably.

2. DPF technology 
 
2.1. PM filtration methods 
 
To address the issue of PM emissions, different methods are employed to filter it out from 
exhaust gases. The three main mechanisms for PM filtration are diffusion, interception, and 
impaction, see Figure 7. Diffusion is the process by which small particles move randomly and 
are captured by diffusing onto a surface, and it is most effective for particles that are less than 
0.1 microns in size [13], [22]. By interception, particles are removed when they come into 
contact with a surface, while impaction is the process by which particles collide with a surface 
and are removed from the gas mixture. These two mechanisms are predominant in retaining 
large particles, with diameters between 0.1 and 0.5 microns, respectively [13], [22]. All these 
mechanisms play a crucial role in DPFs, which combine them to remove PM from exhaust 
gases. 
While there are other filtration methods such as electrostatic or dynamic flow, they are not 
suitable for diesel PM applications. Among the mentioned filtration methods, diffusion is 
predominant for automotive applications. It can treat particles with aerodynamic diameters 
below 50 nm and remove both solid particles and heavy hydrocarbons [13]. Therefore, the 
development of efficient DPFs is crucial for reducing PM emissions from diesel engines, and 
a good understanding of the different PM filtration mechanisms is essential to achieve this 
goal [13], [23]. 
 
Figure 7: Particle transport mechanisms [24] 
 
2.2. Wall flow DPF technology 
 
Wall flow construction is extensively used for heavy-duty vehicles DPFs. It is extruded 
cylindrically with thousands of parallel channels running longitudinally to the exhaust system. 
The gas flows through the filter that has alternately arranged plugged channels. This way the 
gas is forced to pass through the walls, depositing its PM in the upstream part. Therefore, the 
exhaust gas emerging from the downstream side of the wall is mostly free of PM [23]. Figure 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
8 illustrates how wallflow DPF technology works. As PM deposits on the walls of the filter, it 
obstructs the gas flow, increasing the engine exhaust backpressure and degrading its 
performance. To avoid this problem, it is necessary to regenerate the DPF periodically by 
burning or reacting the accumulated PM. 
 
 
Figure 8. Schematic representation of wallflow DPF [25] 
 
2.3. Regeneration of the filter 
 
All the DPF are designed with a maximum PM capacity. DPF overloading may lead to 
excessively high pressure drop, depleted engine operation and even clogging of the filter. 
Additionally, a high PM load can harm the entire system by causing uncontrollable soot burn.  
For these reasons, the regeneration of the filter is critical for its adequate performance.  
DPF regeneration is ruled by an equilibrium between the PM being trapped in the filter and 
the rate of removal. During the regeneration process particles are oxidized to gaseous 
products, primary CO2. There are several factors that affect the regeneration process, like the 
exhaust-gas composition and the soot load. However, the most relevant one is the operating 
temperature. The exhaust-gas temperature is normally not high enough to achieve auto-
regeneration just in the presence of O2. To solve this issue, there are two main alternatives: 
increasing the filter temperature by using auxiliary systems (active regeneration) or reducing 
the necessary soot oxidation temperature to a level that can be achieved during regular 
operation (passive regeneration). 
Active regeneration relies on additional fuel injection, heat supply or driver action to increase 
the DPF temperature. Although actively regenerated DPFs are less dependent on exhaust 
temperatures and have a broad range of applications, regeneration is still controlled and

DPF’s soot loading reaches a predefined value, which is based on several parameters such as 
distance traveled, engine backpressure or driving style [10], [13]. 
Oxygen-based regeneration is mostly applied to active DPFs. The oxygen necessary for soot

Several alternatives are available for active regeneration of DPFs. Fuel injection is one of the

CDPF is a passive regeneration technology where the DPF is coated with a catalytic 
washcoat. Due to the catalyst influence, the filter can be self-regenerated during high exhaust 
temperature periods. The catalyst used in CDPFs is typically a platinum-group metal (PGM), 
such as platinum (Pt), palladium (Pd), or rhodium (Rh) [13], [25]. One critical aspect of this 
technology is maintaining the filtration properties of the CDPF substrate while ensuring 
correct soot-oxidant contact.  
It requires careful monitoring and management of the exhaust gas temperature and flow rate 
to ensure optimal operation. Therefore, proper control of the engine and exhaust 
aftertreatment system is critical for ensuring long-term performance and durability of CDPF 
systems. Additionally, periodic maintenance and cleaning of the filter may be required to 
remove any accumulated ash or other contaminants that can interfere with the filtration and 
regeneration processes. 
Continuously regeneration trap (CRT) is another passive regeneration technology where the 
DPF is regenerated using NO2 produced over a PGM-based DOC. CRT technology can 
regenerate the filter at temperatures as low as 300°C. However, it is dependent of the engine

conditions, it requires ultra-low sulfur diesel (ULSD) and sufficient NOx to soot ratios 
(preferably above 20:1) [13]. 
Fuel borne catalyst-assisted (FBC) regeneration is another passive alternative that uses 
catalyst to reduce the soot oxidation temperature. FBC is an organic-based liquid mixed with 
fuel in the combustion chamber. Because of the direct contact between the catalyst and the 
PM, soot oxidation kinetics are improved, and regeneration temperature can be reduced as 
low as 350°C. FBC can contain transitional and noble metals as well as alloys of Fe, Cu, 
Cerium (Ce) or Pt. One critical disadvantage of this alternative is the formation of catalyst 
oxides, such as CeO2 or Fe2O3 [13], that accumulate in the DPF and can only be removed by 
ash cleaning operations. 
To sum up, active regeneration is an effective and flexible method, but it implies an energy 
penalty of approximately 2% [13], [19]. Passive regeneration by engine management is a 
more fuel-efficient and effective long-term solution, especially when using a CDPF. 
However, passive regeneration may not work in all situations. It is worth mentioning hybrid 
DPF regeneration, which combines passive and active methods. By using a catalyst, it is 
possible to reduce the required regeneration temperature more than 100 °C [13], [26], 
reducing the penalty linked to active regeneration. Additionally, this alternative reduces the 
thermal stress that the DPF experiences, increasing its durability.  
 
2.4. Catalyst role on CDPF technology 
 
CDPF technology offers high filtration efficiency and durability, and it can self-regenerate to 
improve fuel efficiency and reduce emissions [13]. To achieve this, CDPF uses a catalytic 
washcoat that promotes the oxidation reaction of NO in the diesel exhaust to NO2. While the 
upstream DOC also converts NO to NO2, this NO2 is typically consumed close to the filter 
inlet to oxidize the soot present there [26]. 
The soot accumulates over the filter wall as a soot cake, while NO2 is produced on the 
catalytic washcoat, which is located downstream of the soot cake layer. In typical filter wall 
structures, soot partially fills the pores in the wall during loading events. As a result, some 
soot will actually be downstream of the catalytic sites for NO2 generation [26]. However, for 
the reaction between NO2 and the biggest fraction of soot to occur, NO2 must be able to 
diffuse back into the cake layer driven by a concentration gradient. The sketch shown in 
Figure 9 represent in a simplified way these phenomena.

gaseous molecules to be transported and encounter the appropriate active site [13], [19], [26].

synergistic effects that can emerge at the active sites of multi-component catalysts.

3. Ageing mechanisms  
 
The catalytic activity is critical for adequate regeneration of CDPFs. However, its 
performance can be degraded over time; this phenomenon is known as ageing. Several causes 
might lead to this issue, such as mechanical failures, thermal degradation, chemical poisoning, 
or erosion of the CDPF’s washcoat. A deep understanding of these phenomena is particularly 
useful when attempting to perform a technical service on a catalyst that is not performing in 
accordance with the specifications. The most relevant ageing mechanisms that affect CDPFs

3.1. Thermal ageing 
 
Thermal ageing refers to permanent alterations in the structure, composition, and morphology 
of materials that have been exposed to temperatures within the range they are expected to 
endure during their regular operation [28]. The proper dispersion of the catalyst over the 
carrier surface maximizes the accessibility of the reactants from the bulk fluid to the catalyst 
active sites. However, thermal effects can cause crystal deformations resulting in materials 
sintering and pore diffusion limitations [10]. 
Sintering of the catalytic component is a thermal ageing mechanism where the dispersed 
catalytic species grow to larger and more crystalline structures when they are exposed to high 
temperatures. The catalyst particles evolve from a thermodynamically unstable state, 
explained by the high surface-to-volume ratio; to a more stable one [10]. As the catalyst 
undergoes coalescence, there is a decrease in the number of active sites available on its 
surface, which leads to a reduction in the rate of reaction. The addition of stabilizers to the 
catalyst formulation fixes it to the surface reducing mobility and crystal growth. Cerium-
based stabilizers, such as CeO2, have been effective in reducing sintering rate of Pt-based 
catalyst in CDPF applications [29]. 
Catalyst carriers can also experience sintering. As it occurs, the pore size of the porous 
structure gets progressively smaller, causing pore diffusion limitations. In the worst case, the 
pores fully close and the catalyst becomes inaccessible to the reactants. Carrier sintering can 
involve irreversible phase transitions, resulting in a reduction on the porosity and surface area 
[10], [29].  
Interactions between the catalytic species and the carrier, typically alumina, can be a source of 
deactivation. The catalyst can react with the CDPF’s washcoat forming a less active product. 
While one potential solution to this problem is to use alternative carriers, such as ceria or 
zirconia, these alternatives are often more susceptible to sintering, which can lead to 
decreased performance over time [10]. Therefore, there is a trade-off in performance that must 
be carefully considered when developing new catalyst formulations. 
According to other authors, thermal ageing can significantly impact the catalysts present in 
exhaust after-treatment systems [30], [31]. The substrate and carrier materials used are 
carefully chosen for their low coefficient of thermal expansion and high resistance to thermal 
shock. This is why materials like cordierite or mullite are preferred for use as substrate in 
CDPFs and flow-through catalysts. It has been observed that these materials would not be 
affected by thermal ageing at the temperatures expected in a CDPF system due to their 
properties [31]. However, several studies have cautioned about the negative effects of catalyst 
sintering on the catalytic activity of DOC and CDPF. High temperatures can cause noble 
metal components to agglomerate and grow into a more crystalline structure, leading to 
reduced catalyst activity towards oxidation reactions and a slower regeneration rate in CDPFs 
[30], [31]. Therefore, it is still necessary to investigate whether this phenomenon affects 
CDPFs, which operate at lower temperatures than DOC or actively regenerated DPFs.

3.2. Chemical ageing/poisoning  
 
Catalyst deactivation can occur when contaminants from feedstock or process equipment 
deposit onto the catalyst surface. There are two ways in which this can happen: selective 
poisoning, where the contaminant directly reacts with the active site or carrier material, and 
non-selective poisoning, where some substances deposit onto the catalyst and mask sites and 
block pores, reducing performance due to the decreased accessibility of reactants to active 
sites. 
Selective poisoning can be caused by some poisons that adsorb onto specific sites, reducing 
the accessibility for the reactants. This process can be reversed by heating, washing or simply 
removing the poison from the exhaust, restoring this way the catalytic activity. Nevertheless, 
some poisons react directly with the catalyst, forming an inactive alloy, implying permanent 
deactivation. An example of CDPF selective poisoning is deposition of phosphorus-
containing species, such as phosphates and phosphoric acid. Phosphorus is present in diesel 
fuel and lubricating oil and can also enter the exhaust stream through additives used to 
enhance fuel efficiency [10], [32]. 
Non-selective poisoning or masking is a common issue for CDPF. It occurs when certain 
chemical compounds deposit physically onto the surface of the washcoat. Sulfur, phosphorus, 
metals, and ash are some of the most common compounds that can lead to non-selective 
poisoning in CDPFs [10]. These compounds penetrate the pore network coating the inner 
pores and increasing the pore diffusion resistance.  
Non-selective poisoning, which involves physical phenomena such as fouling, leads to the 
accumulation of contaminants at the inlet or 1-3 cm downstream from the monolith's inlet 
face [10]. These contaminants usually concentrate at the top of the washcoat, no more than 30 
microns deep from the gas-washcoat interface [10]. In contrast, selective poisons target 
specific sites and may penetrate deeply into the monolith's washcoat along its axis. The 
findings of Miren Agote-Aran et al. [32], [33], shown in Figure 14, support the behavior of 
poisons discussed earlier in this report. The researchers observed that there is an overlayer 
consisting of particles of Ca, Zn, and S, which indicates that these elements have limited 
ability to penetrate the washcoat. On the other hand, P was able to deeply go through the 
washcoat and distribute itself throughout its thickness, as reported in other studies [30], [32]. 
Furthermore, the distribution of S showed a reverse pattern, indicating that the presence of P 
prevents the accumulation of S. Recent evidence supporting the influence of P on S 
distribution was presented by Honkanen et al. [33], [34] through a comparison of single and 
simultaneously poisoned DOCs. It was observed that the formation of aluminum phosphate 
inhibits the migration of sulfur oxides from the Pt particles to the support, resulting in more 
severe Pt particle poisoning. These results are important to be considered in understanding the 
behavior of these elements in field aged CDPFs and to evaluate the impact of chemical ageing 
on their performance.

3.3. Washcoat erosion and attrition  
 
Over time, the washcoat can be damaged or eroded due to a variety of factors, such as thermal 
cycling, chemical exposure, or mechanical vibration. This is a potential problem for 
monolithic environmental catalyst because gases move through the CDPF at high speeds and 
there are frequent changes in the conditions of the process and temperature. Addition of 
binders and special surface roughening methods are being used for CDPFs to improve the 
chemical bond between the washcoat and the substrate.

4. Ageing evaluation 
 
Evaluating how the ageing of CDPF affects its efficiency and durability is critical for its 
design. The ageing of the filter can be reflected in its filtration efficiency, regeneration rate 
and backpressure, among others. Depending on the characteristics of the CDPF that want to 
be addressed, different tests can be carried out. This project will be focused on the design and 
application of a method to evaluate CDPF’s ageing impact on its catalytic activity. 
4.1. Catalytic performance 
 
CDPF is Scania’s current technology to treat PM from heavy-duty vehicles engine. The 
presence of a catalytic washcoat allows it to regenerate the filter at relatively low 
temperatures. However, as it has been described in the previous section, catalytic activity of a 
CDPF can be depleted over the mileage of the vehicle. Several parameters can influence 
CDPF’s catalytic activity, such as the PM loading, type of ageing, exhaust properties, etc.  
Catalyst performance must be measured to ensure compliance with the environmental 
legislation all over its lifetime. Catalytic activity can be measured in several ways, and the 
choice of method depends on the specific reaction and the goals of the experiment. Some 
common methods for catalytic activity evaluation include: reaction rate, conversion, 
selectivity, activation energy and turnover frequency measurements [35]. 
Typically, CDPF activity tests are done by feeding a reactor with several constant feed 
composition and difference in catalyst activity can be expressed in terms of different degree 
of conversion [35]. One common approach for evaluating the activity of diesel particulate 
filters towards NO and HC oxidation involves testing a representative sample in a tubular 
reactor that enables precise control of temperature, flow rate, and gas composition[32]. 
To measure the effectiveness of a catalyst, it is important to assess whether the activity of the 
samples remains consistent and whether its performance accurately reflects that of the CDPF. 
For this purpose, for fresh samples, it is necessary to stabilize them, to reach the same 
reproducible state under identical reaction conditions. This process is known as de-greening. 
The specific procedure varies depending on the type of catalyst. De-greening is not meant to 
serve as a substitute for complete calcination of the catalyst. The process of calcination should 
be included in the catalyst synthesis, and it is crucial to ensure that all catalysts are thoroughly 
calcined before de-greening takes place [36].  
Diesel engine exhaust can be simulated using gases such as CO, H2O, HC, and NOx, although 
the specific compounds used may vary depending on the testing standard and measurement 
goals.  
As mentioned earlier, passive regeneration of a CDPF is strongly influenced by its ability to 
catalyze Reaction 4, which promotes NO oxidation and supports passive regeneration [26]. 
Additionally, the catalyst in a CDPF contributes to the reduction of CO and HC emissions 
through oxidation and adjusts the NOx ratio to support the functionality of an SCR. However, 
these are secondary functions of the catalyst, the key role of the CDPF is to promote soot 
oxidation [32], [36], [37].

Moreover, mass transfer limitations in CDPF could affect the performance of the filter. They

coated onto the filter’s porous washcoat [10]. However, the diffusion of reactants and 
products through these porous channels can be limited by the pore size distribution and the

4.2. CDPF characterization 
 
After testing the samples’ catalytic activity, it is necessary to characterize them in order to 
evaluate which ageing mechanisms might be the cause of its deterioration. This study is 
critical for the CDPF ageing evaluation and for the following studies that will be focused on 
how the impact of these ageing mechanisms can be mitigated. 
Several catalyst characterization techniques are available, the ones relevant for the study case 
are described in the following sections: 
 
4.2.1. Nitrogen physisorption analysis 
 
Nitrogen physisorption analysis is a popular technique used in catalysis research to study the 
surface area and porosity of catalysts. This process involves exposing a sample to liquid 
nitrogen and then measuring the nitrogen adsorbed in relation to pressure. By analyzing the 
isotherm produced, the sample’s total pore volume, pore size distribution, and surface area 
can be determined. Such analysis can provide valuable information on the accessibility and 
transport properties to the active sites of the catalyst, which play a crucial role in its 
performance during catalytic reactions [10]. 
 
4.2.2. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Energy Dispersive X-ray 
Spectroscopy (EDX) analysis 
 
SEM and EDX are analytical techniques commonly used in catalysis research to study the 
surface and elemental composition of catalysts. 
SEM is a technique that provides high-resolution images of the surface morphology of 
materials. It works by scanning a focused electron beam across the surface of a sample and 
detecting the secondary electrons emitted from the surface. The resulting images provide 
information about the surface topography of the sample [10], [32]. 
EDX, on the other hand, is a spectroscopic technique that is used to determine the elemental 
composition of materials. It works by bombarding the sample with high-energy X-rays, which 
causes the sample to emit characteristic X-rays that are unique to the elements present in the 
sample. By detecting and analyzing these X-rays, EDX can identify the elements present in 
the sample and quantify their relative abundance [10], [32]. 
 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
4.2.3. X-ray Fluorescence (XRF) 
 
XRF is a technique that uses X-rays to determine the bulk elemental composition of materials. 
The principle behind it is that when a sample is irradiated with X-rays, the atoms in the 
sample are excited and emit characteristic fluorescent X-rays, which have energies that are 
specific to each element. XRF is useful in the characterization of catalysts and other materials, 
providing information on bulk elemental composition, concentration, and the presence of 
impurities or contaminants [10]. 
 
4.2.4. X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) 
 
XPS is a surface analysis technique that can be used to study the elemental composition and 
chemical bonding of materials. It is particularly useful in the characterization of catalysts and 
other materials with complex surface chemistries. 
In XPS, a sample is bombarded with monochromatic X-rays, which emits photoelectrons 
from the surface of the material. The kinetic energy and number of emitted photoelectrons are 
measured, and the resulting data can be used to get the chemical composition and electronic 
state of the sample’s surface. 
In the characterization of CDPFs, XPS can provide valuable information on the surface 
chemistry of the catalyst material, which can be used to evaluate the catalyst’s performance 
and ageing behavior. By analyzing the XPS spectra obtained from CDPF samples before and 
after the ageing, it is possible to determine changes in the oxidation state and coordination 
environment of the catalyst’s active sites, which can provide insight into the mechanisms of 
catalyst ageing and deactivation [10]. 
 
4.2.5. Temperature Programmed Reduction (TPR) analysis 
 
TPR is commonly used in catalysis research to study the redox properties of catalysts and can 
provide valuable information on the oxidation state and active sites of the material. 
TPR entails heating the sample in a controlled environment while monitoring how much 
material is being reduced in relation to temperature. Electrical conductivity, mass, or gas 
composition changes in the sample can all be used to track the reduction process. By 
analyzing the data obtained during the TPR experiment, it is possible to determine the 
reduction temperature and profile of the sample, which can provide insight into the redox 
properties and active sites of the catalyst [10], [39], [40].

2. Catalytic activity testing rig 
 
Synthetic catalyst activity tests were carried out for assessing the effects of ageing on the 
catalytic activity of CDPFs. This technique is based on measuring NO and HC conversion 
through reactions 4 and 3, respectively, which are catalyzed by the filter’s washcoat. By 
measuring their conversions at different temperature intervals, it is possible to correlate them 
with the catalytic activity of the sample towards each reaction. 
All these tests were developed at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, at the division of 
Process Technology. This testing rig consists of a horizontal split tube furnace (Carbolite HST 
12/300), the furnace controller (Eurotherm 3508) and a quartz tubular reactor where the 
sample is placed. To adjust the gas composition, the rig has a set of mass flow controllers 
(MFCs) that introduce each of the gases. The gases required for the experiment are: 
Compressed air, N2, NO, HC (propane) and H2O. Additionally, H2 and O2 are used as 
auxiliary gases required for the measurement equipment. The gas mixture is heated up by heat 
bands at 125 °C and fed into the reactor. Inside the tube two thermocouples are placed, one in 
the inlet and one in the centre of the CDPF sample. When the gas leaves the reactor, it passes 
through a condenser whose purpose is removing the steam present in the gas mixture before 
entering the analyzer.  
The gas composition is measured by a MEXA 7000 series bag bench analyzer, manufactured 
by Horiba Instruments, Inc. © This device can measure the NOx concentration in the mixture 
by a NO/NOx analyzer (CLA-755MA). The principle behind it is chemiluminescence detector 
(CLD). When a mixture of gas containing NO and O3 is reacted, the NO gets oxidized into 
NO2. During this process, NO2 molecules become excited, and they emit light when they 
return to their ground state. This phenomenon is known as chemiluminescence, and the 
intensity of light produced is proportional to the amount of NO molecules present before the 
reaction. Therefore, by measuring the light emitted, the concentration of NO can be 
determined.

2.1. 
Catalytic activity tests considerations 
 
In order to evaluate the ageing effect on the catalytic activity of the filters is important to be 
aware of the limitations, simplifications and assumptions that were considered: 
• The objective of the test is to evaluate the catalytic activity of the samples, not the 
overall regeneration rate. Even though NO oxidation plays a key role in the 
regeneration process, it is also affected by other phenomena not covered by this 
project. 
• The gases used in the test are not meant to replicate the real-world exhaust gas 
composition, but rather to simulate the key components that affect the performance of 
the CDPFs under the same conditions. This difference is not expected to significantly 
impact the evaluation of the catalytic activity of the filters 
• Most of the PM that accumulated in the filters has been removed, as can be seen in 
Figure 18. However, there may be small clusters of ash and soot still attached to the 
surface of the inlet channel. The images obtained with an optical microscope show 
two adjacent channels on the left-hand side and the surface of the inlet channel with 
higher magnification on the right-hand side. Figure 18 (b) shows the field aged filter 
after most of the soot has been oxidized and mainly ash remains in the filter. Figure 18 
(c) shows an example of how the surface of the samples tested in this project look like 
after the ash is removed.

3. CDPF Samples Characterization 
 
Several characterization tests were carried out to evaluate the influence of different ageing 
mechanisms on the catalytic activity depletion of field aged CDPF samples. 
 
3.1. 
Nitrogen physisorption analysis methodology 
 
The surface area and porosity of the samples were analyzed using a Micrometrics ASAP 2020 
instrument, where nitrogen physisorption measurements were conducted at a temperature of -
196 °C. Before the sorption measurements, approximately 500 mg of the sample were crushed 
and underwent outgassing under high vacuum conditions. The specific surface area was 
determined by applying the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) equation within the pressure 
range of approximately 0.05 to 0.20 relative to the standard pressure. To assess the pore 
volume and size, the isotherm desorption branch was analyzed using the Barrett-Joyner-
Halenda (BJH) method. 
 
3.2. 
SEM and EDX analysis methodology 
 
The CDPF samples were inspected through SEM-EDX analysis. Firstly, the channels from the 
field aged samples were examined with the SEM to evaluate the possible changes in the 
morphology of their surfaces. Prior to the analysis, the samples were sputtered with a 10 nm 
Platinum layer, to increase their conductivity. The specific settings of each analysis are 
included in the captions of the results. Simultaneously, the samples were examined with the 
EDX to evaluate their local elemental composition. By comparing the position of elemental 
standard peaks, the elements were identified, and their relative quantities were determined. 
 
3.3. XRF analysis methodology 
 
Approximately 3 g of samples were measured in the XRF analysis along with the required 
binding agent. The mixture was then subjected to a 10-minute process of ball milling at a 
speed of 300 rpm. The resulting powdered filter was then pressed into a sample holder under a 
pressure of 150 bar for 5 minutes [19]. These sample holders were subsequently inserted into 
the PAN analytic Epsilon3 benchtop XRF spectrometer for analysis. 
 
3.4. XPS analysis methodology 
 
For the XPS analysis the sample washcoat was scraped off and sent to the Department of 
Industrial and Materials Science at Chalmers University. The analysis was performed using a 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
PHI5000 VersaProbe III – Scanning XPS Microprobe™ instrument. Monochromatic AlKα X-
ray source was used with a beam size diameter of 100 µm. Dual charge compensation was 
employed using an Ar ion gun (+ve) and electron neutralizer (-ve) for non-conductive 
materials. The scan modes included Survey scan (0 – 1350 eV) with step size of 1.0 eV and 
Narrow scan with selected region and step size of 0.1 eV. The energy scale was calibrated 
with Au, Ag, and Cu. Narrow scan measurements were aligned with the adventitious carbon 
peak (C1s) at 284.8 eV before analysis. 
 
3.5. TPR analysis methodology 
 
The TPR analysis methodology involved the use of an Autochem-2910 instrument to 
investigate the reduction behavior of the catalyst. The sample was loaded into the reactor and 
a mixture of 10% H2 in Argon gas was introduced to the system at a flow rate of 50 mL/min. 
The sample was heated at a rate of 10°C/min, and the temperature was increased up to 800°C. 
During the heating process, the reduction process was monitored by detecting the H2 
consumption, which is reflected on the gas mixture conductivity, and the corresponding 
temperature.

The decrease in A could indicate a reduction in the number of active sites available for the

The results of the activity test gave a general assessment of how ageing affected CDPF's 
catalytic activity. The tests showed distinct differences in the catalytic activity for NO 
oxidation, indicating a decline in activity with longer operation life. The differences in 
activity between samples were more noticeable at lower temperatures, where NO oxidation is 
restricted by the reaction kinetics. In contrast, at higher temperatures where NO2 formation 
can be limited by mass transfer, the variations in activity between the samples were less 
significant. 
There were significant HC conversion differences between the aged sample and the reference 
CDPF samples. But within the tested temperature range, no appreciable variations in the HC 
conversion profiles of the field-aged samples were observed. Results from the middle sections 
were inconsistent, which may be explained by differences in how washcoat was distributed 
there. 
There were no discernible differences between the behavior of the cleaned CDPF and the 
other diesel samples catalytic activity for NO oxidation. The impact of ageing was less severe 
on the FAME fuel sample, though. Despite being used for a longer period of time, the FAME 
fuel sample showed greater NO oxidation activity than a CDPF run with regular diesel. This 
suggests that the FAME fuel sample maintained better its catalytic activity towards NO 
oxidation. 
Despite its limitations, the proposed kinetic model was successful in quantitatively 
determining how ageing impacts Reaction 4. The observed differences in A and Ea were 
consistent with their ageing patterns and the results of the activity tests. These variations 
indicate catalyst deactivation mechanisms, such as fouling, catalyst poisoning, or changes to 
the catalyst's surface with ageing. A series of characterization analyses must be performed in 
order to identify the specific mechanisms that hinder the field-aged DPF's catalytic activity.

2.3. 
XRF analysis results 
 
Several XRF analysis of the fresh and field aged samples were performed. Figure 32 depicts 
the elemental composition of the key elements relevant to CDPF ageing. These are crucial in 
understanding the ageing mechanism and potential deterioration of CDPF performance. 
==PAGE:60==

Figure 32: XRF analysis results 
The XRF analysis of the field-aged samples showed a significant increase in the concentration 
of S and P. The S composition was initially noticeably higher than the P composition. 
However, the S composition of the catalyst decreased while the P composition rose as its 
useful life increased.  
This pattern is consistent with the results of earlier research on DOCs [30], [32], [34]. 
Phosphate species are created when P reacts with the alumina support, causing pore blockage 
and a reduction in surface area. The presence of SO2 in diesel exhaust causes the sulfation of 
both PGM and the catalyst support. According to multiple studies, this sulfation reduces the 
surface area of the carrier material and increases its acidity [30], [32].  
P was found to be the main poison in the filters with the longest operational lives. It's 
interesting to note that S had a reversed distribution, showing that presence of P prevented 
accumulation of S. In the absence of P, S is evenly distributed in the catalyst support as 
aluminum sulfate. However, when P is present, the adsorption of S on Al2O3 is hindered. The 
formation of aluminum phosphate upon exposure to P acts as a non-sulfating support, 
impeding the migration of sulfur oxides from Pt to Al2O3 [32].  
The XRF results for the regenerated filter exhibit the same pattern as the others. However, it's 
crucial to emphasize how little P and S are present in the FAME fuel sample. Compared to 
regular diesel, biodiesel typically has lower sulfur content [46], [47]. Additionally, the 
required lubricants' composition, particularly their P content, is specifically chosen to meet 
the requirements of biodiesel.  
The catalytic activity of the CDPF is significantly influenced by the combined poisoning of P 
and S, and these observations are consistent with the findings of this study regarding catalytic 
activity.

making it more difficult to precisely identify and understand the nature of the reduction peaks 
observed in the TPR analysis.   
 
Figure 35 shows the signal provided by the thermal conductivity detector (TCD) against

The TPR analysis results indicate the presence of two consecutive reduction peaks in the 
temperature range of 75 to 270 °C, corresponding to the reduction of PdOx and PtOx species

1. Conclusions 
 
Based on the results in Chapter 4, the following conclusions can be drawn: 
• Field-aged CDPF showed significant variations in catalytic activity for NO oxidation, 
with activity decreasing as operational lifespan increased. The effect was most 
noticeable between 200 and 400°C, where NO oxidation was kinetically constrained. 
Notably, distinct differences in the catalytic activity of samples operated on FAME 
fuel were observed, with FAME fuel CDPF samples exhibiting higher activity 
compared to diesel samples with comparable operational lifespans. Disparities in HC 
oxidation were detected between the fresh and aged catalysts; however, no significant 
differences in HC conversion were observed among the aged samples within this 
temperature range. 
 
• Utilizing experimental data from the kinetically limited region and fitting a first-order 
rate constant from a plug flow mole balance, the reaction rate constant for NO 
oxidation was estimated. The observed variations in A and Ea were consistent with the 
ageing patterns and the outcomes of the activity tests, pointing to deactivation 
mechanisms such as fouling, catalyst poisoning, or surface modifications brought on 
by ageing. The BET surface areas of the samples decrease with operating time, but 
there is no clear trend in the pore volume and size results. When compared to the other 
samples, the FAME fuel CDPF sample has a significantly lower surface area. Changes 
in these parameters may not only indicate carrier sintering, as masking or fouling can 
also cause a decrease in apparent surface area. To distinguish between these ageing 
phenomena, nitrogen physisorption results must be combined with other 
characterization analyses. 
 
• The SEM-EDX revealed that field-aged samples have visible surface deposits, which 
may affect the properties of the CDPF samples. There were no obvious signs of 
washcoat sintering, but they could be obscured by ash deposits. The FAME fuel CDPF 
sample exhibits morphological changes, including a hollow, tortuous appearance and 
the presence of deposits. These findings emphasize the importance of considering 
surface deposits and the effects of biodiesel on CDPFs morphology when 
understanding and evaluating their performance. 
 
• Increased levels of S and P were found in the filters by XRF analysis, with P being the 
main poison in filters with longer operational lives. Pore blockage and decreased 
surface area can be caused by the presence of phosphate species as a result of P 
reaction with the support. S compounds could also react with the support or be 
adsorbed in the catalyst. Combined poisoning of P and S significantly impacts the 
catalytic activity of the filter. 
 
• The XPS analysis was limited by overlapping peaks, which made accurate 
identification and analysis difficult. Pt and Pd were undetectable because of the low 
PGM loading. The CDPF samples were fundamentally composed of Al-Si-Mg oxide 
(cordierite), with significant amounts of Na, P(V), S(VI), Ca(II), and Zn(II) identified. 
==PAGE:66==
66 
 
Ca3(PO4)2 and ZnSO4 were detected in the DPF5-Inlet sample, potentially including 
Zn3(PO4)2 and CaSO4, which are typical ash compounds. The presence of aluminum 
phosphates or sulfates, on the other hand, cannot be ruled out. Further analysis 
are required to confirm the presence of these compounds. 
 
• The presence of reduction peaks corresponding to PdOx and PtOx species in the CDPF 
samples was revealed by TPR analysis, indicating changes in the oxidation states of 
Pd and Pt. Temperature peaks in the field-aged samples shifted to lower temperatures, 
that could be influenced by interactions with adsorbed SOx species. Plateaus near the 
peaks and reductions at higher temperatures indicated the presence of intermediate 
species, as well as SOx compound desorption and reduction. However, the low PGM 
loading posed challenges in the analysis, limiting the detection and characterization of 
specific species. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
Future insights 
Additional analyses can be performed to obtain a more complete picture of the phenomenon 
of ageing on the catalytic activity of CDPFs in heavy-duty trucks. Firstly, analyzing CDPFs 
that have accumulated different mileage. By including samples with a wide range of ageing 
profiles, it will be possible to observe the progression of catalytic activity degradation over 
time and mileage, providing valuable insights into the middle and long-term performance of 
CDPFs in real-world operating conditions. Analysis of other factors that can affect CDPF 
performance, such as temperature history, oil consumption, and exhaust gas properties, would 
provide a more complete picture of the ageing process and its impact.  
 
Furthermore, it would be interesting to investigate independently the various ageing 
mechanisms that can affect CDPF ageing. Researchers can gain a better understanding of how 
these factors contribute to catalyst deactivation by conducting separate studies into specific 
ageing mechanisms, such as phosphorus and sulfur poisoning or their combination. 
 
Additionally, expanding the characterization analyses would offer insightful data. To better 
understand how various contaminants penetrate the washcoat and their spatial distribution 
within the filter, sectional samples of the CDPF should be analyzed using EDX. These 
analyses would clarify how particular ageing mechanisms, like phosphorus and sulfur 
poisoning or their combination, affect catalyst deactivation.  In addition to this, 
characterization techniques like inductively coupled plasma (ICP) analysis can be used to 
investigate the loss of PGMs brought on by ageing.  
 
Finally, research on the effects of biodiesel on CDPF performance and an understanding of 
the underlying factors that contribute to its unique catalytic characteristics in comparison to 
conventional diesel would be highly beneficial in optimizing after-treatment systems 
tailored for biodiesel.
