Abstract 
The present thesis carries out assessments of the financial consequences of the concept of 
overplanting for offshore wind farms in the British and Danish context. Overplanting of offshore 
wind farms is the action of optimising the wind farm installation capacity with regard to its 
transmission system capacity. Since the power production of a wind farm is constrained to the 
local wind potential and its technical performance, the transmission system capacity is not often 
used to its full extent. Additionally installed turbines can maximise the power output of the wind 
farm at the same time while keeping the cost for electrical infrastructure constant. Furthermore, 
the revenues of the wind farm can increase due to increased energy production or because of 
the regulations of the underlying support systems.  
The economic assessment is performed both in terms of Levelised Cost of Electricity (LCoE) and 
Internal Rate of Return (IRR) as evaluation parameters. During the thesis project time, a 
frequency based model was established that estimates the economic performance of 
overplanted wind farms in the United Kingdom and in Denmark. The model contains a technical 
module, in which the annual energy production of an offshore wind farm is calculated, and a 
financial module, which performs a discounted cash flow analysis of the wind farm based on 
upfront and annual costs as well as revenues. The thesis focuses on private-economic analyses 
based on an investor point of view, but also socio-economic implications of overplanting are 
debated. 
The thesis determines that overplanting is more viable for the investor, when being performed 
for British offshore wind farms, than it is in the Danish framework. Several sensitivity analyses, of 
which the most critical parameters were found out to be the cost of electrical infrastructure and 
wind turbine availability, suggest that as for future planned wind farm projects in the United 
Kingdom, an additional installation capacity of 4–6% with respect to the transmission system 
capacity should be erected, yielding additional value in the range of 25 M€. This measure helps 
to decrease the LCoE by approximately 0.4–0.7%. In the Danish context, a lower additional 
installation capacity of 2–4% is beneficial for the economic viability of the wind farm, leading to 
additional value of roughly 7 M€. In the Danish context, overplanting is not a measure to 
decrease the LCoE for the investor.  
The thesis concludes that overplanting evokes some corporate as well as societal benefits 
regardless of the underlying regulations, but overall regulations similar to the British regulatory 
framework favour the implementation of overplanting for an offshore wind farm investor.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Motivation of overplanting and research questions 
The primary purpose of the thesis is to provide an assessment of benefits and disadvantages of 
the so-called overplanting of offshore wind farms. Two attractive and distinct markets within the 
European offshore wind energy sector will be compared and the aptness of their respective 
regulatory regimes on overplanting will be assessed. 
Despite the higher capacity factor of offshore wind farms with respect to their onshore 
counterparts, the duration curves are far from full utilisation of the installed transmission 
capacity. One option of optimising the transmission setup is the principle of overplanting, in 
which the actual capacity of the wind farm is increased above the rated transmittable power. 
The capacity both can be expanded by installing additional turbines or by increasing the 
generator size of the wind turbines.  
The concept of overplanting is illustrated in Figure 1.1. While power is curtailed during periods of 
high wind speeds and turbine availability, additional power can be transmitted during phases of 
lower wind speeds or decreased turbine availability. Since wind climates usually show a higher 
probability of wind speeds below rated power, additional power can be generated and the 
transmission capacity can be utilised more effectively during a considerable share of operation 
time. In that way, the capacity factor of the wind farm can be increased. Overplanting 
consequently is a concept functioning between the trade-off of additional electricity generation 
and the linked higher revenues and higher total investment cost of the wind farm. The opposing 
factors are to be optimised economically.

1 Introduction 
 
Since the European offshore markets lack a combined development policy and are rather 
defined by different regulatory regimes within each country, the specific regulations play a key 
role in assessing the viability of a particular offshore wind farm project. Based on the two 
distinct European markets being assessed, the thesis examines which framework is particularly 
suitable for overplanting projects. 
Because the feasibility assessment of an offshore wind farm is highly site-specific, geographical 
factors such as the site characteristics also play a role in determining the viability of an offshore 
wind farm investment project. The influence of these factors will be accounted for by linking 
cost assumptions of offshore wind farm components to respective conceivable site 
characteristics within the two markets. 
The research questions of this thesis comprise the following aspects: 
Firstly, the general cost reduction potential when applying overplanting for offshore wind farms 
will be assessed: 
1. How great is the impact of overplanting on the levelised cost of energy (LCoE)? 
Secondly, the work discusses offshore wind farms in their different political and economic 
contexts. Since there are various aspects to consider for an offshore wind farm project in 
different countries, such as the investment project scope for the wind farm developer as well as 
different regulatory framework influencing the business case, overplanting can have a higher 
economic profitability in specific countries: 
2. Is the impact of the regulatory regime critical for applying overplanting, and by which 
extent? 
Lastly, a further investigation will focus on the value of wind power for different shares of wind 
power generation in an electricity market. Merit order effects can lead to declines in power 
prices on electricity spot markets when a higher share of energy production originates from 
fluctuating renewable energy technologies. Therefore a difference in power prices during low 
wind and high wind generation can be expected. When transferring this consideration to 
overplanting and its distinctive power curve, the additionally generated energy of the 
overplanted turbines during low wind can be expected to be remunerated higher than the 
corresponding lost value of the curtailed energy during high wind. Therefore, the last 
investigation will consider the following question: 
3. Can the additionally generated energy during low wind increase the value of the 
offshore wind farm investment?

Energy with the most assets in the company’s portfolio; likewise it is the market with the largest

1 Introduction 
 
1.3 Methodological approach and project limitations 
Methodological approach 
In order to establish an overplanting business case, the capital (CapEx) and operational 
expenditure (OpEx) of the wind farm have to be determined. The magnitude of costs of the 
respective offshore wind farms will be designed based on cost data of offshore wind farm 
components assembled from the literature. Their validity will be assessed by the historical 
development of investment cost of offshore wind energy and general cost breakdowns 
presented in the literature. Different business cases in the countries will be evaluated based on 
conceivable offshore wind farms that reflect currently planned projects. The respective energy 
production and total cost of the offshore wind farm will be compared within different levels of 
capacity addition. The economically best performance with a certain level of capacity installation 
will represent the optimal design of the respective offshore wind farm. 
All calculations are carried out in the spreadsheet calculation software Microsoft Excel 
(Microsoft, 2010). 
 
Project limitations 
The scope of the thesis is limited to the economic assessment of overplanting and does not 
consider additional effects beyond the primary economic impacts of the overplanted turbines. In 
particular, no wind farm layout optimisation is considered depending on the different levels of 
capacity additions. Moreover, no detailed modelling of possible losses within the export cable 
system is carried out. It is rather assumed that the electrical losses beyond the offshore 
substation do not have a further influence on the technical and economic performance of the 
offshore wind farm. 
The economic assessment does not consider financing analyses that take the origin of money 
into account. Hence, no debt investment is considered and the investment project is assumed to 
be totally based on the wind farm investor’s own money, i.e. the respective offshore wind farm 
is completely financed by equity. 
 
1.4 Economic evaluation 
The economic evaluation of the offshore wind farm investments is considered with two different 
evaluation parameters. 
The first parameter that assesses the investment projects is the levelised cost of energy (LCoE). 
In the present thesis, the LCoE is defined as the ratio between the discounted lifetime costs of 
the wind farm and its discounted lifetime energy yield, as expressed by equation (1.1). 
Discounting the cost and energy has the effect that these two factors are weighted higher in the 
evaluation for earlier years of the project than in later years, making them comparable over the 
whole lifetime.

Specifically, the offshore wind farm will be compared between its conventional setup with a 
baseline number of wind turbines and different levels of overplanted turbines. The relative 
reduction in LCoE is decisive for its evaluation. The higher the relative decrease in LCoE with 
using a certain number of overplanted turbines compared to the baseline wind farm, the more 
beneficial the concept. The optimal design of the wind farm considering LCoE is the amount of 
capacity installed that can decrease the LCoE the most. 
The underlying framework of the LCoE calculation is characterised by a discounted cash flow 
(DCF) analysis. DCF analyses are the standard approach to evaluate investment projects 
economically. All occurring cash flows over the lifetime of the project, both costs and revenues, 
are discounted over time to make them comparable with each other. The full DCF analysis 
extends the LCoE analysis by fully considering the revenues of the respective investment 
projects. Thereby, the difference in the expected revenues within the different regulatory 
regimes can be accounted for. 
In the present thesis, the DCF analysis is based on free cash flows. In that way, the investment 
projects are analysed as being full financed by equity. Therefore, the free cash flows of the 
investment projects will be determined by the respective payable taxes and depreciation level,

2 Background – The economics of offshore wind 
2.1 Cost of offshore wind 
Constructing an offshore wind farm is in particular a capital-intensive investment, with upfront 
investment costs constituting up to 75% of the total lifetime cost, in contrast to a share of 
typically 40% for conventional power plants. Offshore wind energy is also about 50% more 
expensive than their onshore-counterparts, due to the larger and more robust structures 
deployed and the necessary logistics (Kitzing & Morthorst, 2015). Therefore the capital cost 
plays an important role in the total cost assessment of an offshore wind farm. It is important to 
consider the geographical conditions in estimating the costs of an offshore wind farm, since 
primarily the water depth and distance to shore affect the investment cost significantly (Prässler 
& Schaechtele, 2012). Besides these dominant two internal cost drivers, also external 
parameters affect the investment costs, such as fluctuating prices for raw material and the 
dependency or lack of sufficient supply in the market of wind turbine manufacturers (Van der 
Zwaan, Rivera-Tinoco, Lensink, & van den Oosterkamp, 2012). Even though learning curve 
effects are a factor in decreasing the cost of offshore wind energy, the above mentioned drivers 
have led to rising costs of offshore wind energy in the last years.

Figure 2.1 presents the historical development of investment costs for commercial offshore wind 
energy farms in Europe. The data comprises 60 offshore wind farms being operational since the 
year 2000, beginning with the Danish wind farm Middelgrunden and including projects that will 
be commissioned in the year 2017 in the Netherlands. The Danish and British wind farms are 
indicated separately, showing that the Danish projects are among the longest operational wind 
farms in Europe and further indicating the dominance of the British wind farm installations in 
the last years.  
The rising trend especially since 2005 due to the aforementioned internal and external cost

2.1.3 Operation and Maintenance costs 
Operation and Maintenance costs (O&M) or OpEx are expressed within the annual costs after 
commissioning of the farm and sometimes tend to increase over the farm’s lifetime. The O&M 
costs are either expressed per MWh generated as variable cost or per MW installed capacity as 
fixed cost, also lacking a common approach for their definition. This is due to the fact that 
different parts of OpEx induce variable costs, such as repair costs and to a certain extent spare 
parts and maintenance (which are likely to be related to the production level) and other parts 
are connected to fixed costs, such as insurance costs, administration and regular maintenance 
(which are likely to be related to the fixed installed capacity). 
One can combine the variable cost depending on the energy produced and the residual fixed 
cost to obtain the total OpEx cost. For offshore wind the variable part of the OpEx is estimated 
to be half of the total OpEx (Voormolen et al., 2015). In general, information regarding OpEx is 
hard to obtain. In the literature it is estimated to be in a range of 15–49 €/MWh (Kitzing & 
Morthorst, 2015) in variable terms and 2.5%–4% p.a. in fixed terms as share of CapEx 
(Heptonstall, Gross, Greenacre, & Cockerill, 2012; Prässler & Schaechtele, 2012). Over the total 
lifetime of the farm, the OpEx can encompass 25–30% of the total project cost (Kitzing & 
Morthorst, 2015). 
Considering the aforementioned geographical cost drivers, mostly the distance to the nearest 
maintenance port directly affects the OpEx, due to the cost linked to the travel time of the 
maintenance vessel and potentially rougher weather conditions at sites further offshore that 
constrain the operation time on site.  
 
After assessing the total cost of a wind farm project, the LCoE can be estimated when predicting 
the energy generation of the farm over the total lifetime. The LCoE assessment and its 
limitations will be introduced in the following section. 
 
2.2 Levelised cost of energy 
2.2.1 LCoE assessment for power generators 
Calculating the LCoE is a tool not only used for assessing the economic performance of offshore 
wind energy, but is generally utilised throughout the industry to evaluate the cost-effectiveness 
of different forms of power generation technologies and to compare them with each other. In 
that way, a comparison also between conventional and renewable power generators can be 
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Levelised cost of energy 
11 
 
done, even though these technologies can differ significantly in their cost structure. While 
conventional generators usually face a high share of their total lifetime costs with variable costs 
such as expenses for fuel, for most renewable energy sources a major part constitute the 
investment costs occurring in the beginning of the investment projects, particularly for those 
technologies where no cost for fuel accrues. The LCoE thus is a straightforward measure for the 
investigation scope of an energy market as a whole to examine the competitiveness of different 
energy technologies.  
While comparing the LCoE for different power generation systems within a certain market is a 
simple indicator to identify which technology produces electricity at the lowest cost, it is less 
easy to compare LCoE analyses of different markets or countries even for the same power 
generation technology. This is due to the fact that different countries have various regulations 
and guidelines of how to adapt the LCoE calculation because the different national regulatory 
considerations are taken into account. A Danish partnership of different commercial and state 
entities has tried to propagate a standard approach to calculate the LCoE specifically for offshore 
wind energy (Megavind, 2015) in order to facilitate a cost comparison of electricity production in 
a growing joined European energy system, but national regulations still carry along various 
methods for the LCoE assessment. 
 
2.2.2 LCoE assessment in different countries 
Ecofys published a report about the different assessments of calculating the LCoE in different 
European countries (Visser & Held, 2014). It gives examples of the procedures of LCoE 
calculations used for support price level setting processes employed in the Netherlands, 
Germany, United Kingdom and Spain. It indicates that the quality of LCoE estimates primarily is 
subject to the quality and the level of detail of the input data. Different countries use various 
assumptions of the scope of capital and operational cost components. In order to compare the 
approach of the United Kingdom , which is of interest for the present analysis, to the common 
guideline for LCoE calculations in Denmark, a report of the Danish Energy Agency is used, which 
describes the financial and technical assumptions behind LCoE analyses in this country (Danish 
Energy Agency, 2015b). The comparison of the United Kingdom and Denmark regarding the 
relevant factors as presented by the study of Ecofys is shown in Table 2.2. The factors are 
indicated with yes or no depending on their inclusion in the respective LCoE analysis method. 
Whereas the general CapEx and OpEx are included in the LCoE calculation in both countries as 
seen in the table above, the inclusion of grid connection cost into the capital cost in fact differs 
due to diverse regulations in both countries. Also the decommissioning costs are included in the 
British method and are taken into account as “provisioning fund” as part of the total O&M costs. 
By accumulating these payments over the lifetime, a fund is created that serves to pay the 
decommissioning expenditures at the end of the operational time. The Danish approach, 
however, assumes that the decommissioning costs are offset by the residual asset value and 
thus are excluded from the assessment. The electrical balancing cost is included in both regimes, 
but a broader impact on investments concerning the electricity system is not considered in the 
British methodology. In Denmark on the other hand the costs for adjusting or expending the 
electrical infrastructure, which is of particular importance for renewable energy sources, is 
considered in the procedure.

The height of balancing costs differs in the countries due to the respective production portfolio

balancing costs for wind power producers in Denmark is estimated at 2 €/MWh, which is in the

As a consequence of the different approaches and values regarding the above mentioned factors 
included in the LCoE analyses, a comparison of the LCoE of specific technologies assessments 
between different countries is somewhat biased. A general trend of cost development of certain 
technologies over different countries can therefore rather be evaluated by relative cost 
reduction projections over time than by absolute values of specific years.  
Another highly sensitive parameter for the LCoE calculation is the choice of the discount rate as 
stated in the Ecofys report, which usually varies throughout different countries. The selection is 
based on different perceptions of risk and various estimations of alternative public investments 
in specific national markets. If the risk for an investment is assumed to be high, a greater 
discount rate will reflect a higher needed return of the investment in order for the project to be 
regarded as profitable. The risk depends on the general market conditions such as the supply 
chain market or the dependency of imports. Having many alternatives to the investment in a 
certain market furthermore generates opportunity costs for an investor that could be spent on 
other projects. The volume of alternative investment opportunities obviously also varies from 
country to country, thus also being reflected in different discount rates. The more alternative 
project possibilities there are investable, the more expected return is needed for the specific 
investment to be attractive enough.

greatly between countries and in particular between the United Kingdom and Denmark. While 
the British government suggests a discount rate of 10% (nominal) for all projects to be able to

From an investor point of view, also the LCoE assessment within a national market is subject to 
other limitations. Since the projection of energy generation especially for fluctuating renewable 
energy sources is prone to uncertainty, an LCoE analysis does not always estimate the 
profitability of a project correctly, as it for instance underestimates the LCoE by overestimating 
energy production. Moreover monetary profits over the lifetime of the asset are not considered 
when only looking at LCoE, so that support schemes and electricity market prices are not fully or 
not at all integrated in the analysis. An attractive support scheme policy can for instance 
outweigh the accruing costs, so as to promote a certain technology. Similarly, a certain market 
price structure can compensate for the occurring costs with the result that particular 
technologies can be more profitable although they are constituted by higher LCoE. As Joskow 
(2011) argues, a comparison of LCoE for different technologies implies to treat the produced 
electricity as a uniform product that is always or in average priced equally. Yet due to market 
price fluctuations or different capacity factors and thus operating times the revenue stream can 
affect the actual profitability of the asset considerably. 
A more detailed business case analysis which determines the project viability by other 
indications like the net present value or the internal rate of return is more suitable for an 
investor, as they typically also underlie a more detailed cash flow analysis and provide a broader 
picture for investment decisions (Visser & Held, 2014). In fact, when conducting analyses of 
decisions with alternatives of investment, the scientific literature finds out the NPV to be the 
usual measure to determine the economic viability of a project (González, Rodríguez, Mora, 
Payán, & Santos, 2011). Therefore besides the LCoE approach, the overplanting assessment will 
also be carried out in terms of NPV and IRR. The present assessments carried out based on LCoE 
and IRR will therefore be compared with each other in order to account for the effects of 
revenue streams and other regulatory regime aspects on the optimal result of overplanted 
turbines.

2.3 Enhancing wind power generation 
2.3.1 Wind energy curtailment 
As the integration of fluctuating renewable energy sources is growing, besides decreasing the 
LCoE for specific projects also considering the total system cost has become more important to 
investigate. A significant extension of fluctuating energy sources in the power grid can increase 
stress on the electrical infrastructure and cause congestion, as well as decrease security of 
supply and grid resilience due to the variability of power generation. This expansion of 
fluctuating renewable energy thus has given rise to consider curtailment of renewable electricity 
as an option when the cost of avoided curtailment becomes higher than the marginal cost of grid 
reinforcement (Klinge Jacobsen & Schröder, 2012). Generally, curtailment of wind energy can be 
done due to technical issues as network constraints or security reasons or because of market-
related concerns such as low or negative power prices, or even strategic bidding.  
The concept of overplanting is somewhat related to that idea, since the additionally installed 
capacity leads to times in which production has to be partially shut down to stay within the 
transmittable power generation ranges. In this way, possible occurring stresses on the 
transmission system can be mitigated, as is one of the incentives for curtailing wind or 
fluctuating renewable energy in general. The above mentioned motives for curtailment, 
however, are system-related and thus in general impact socio-economic analyses of wind energy 
investment projects, which cover a broader investment scope than private-economic investment 
analyses, which are in the focus of the present assessments. Nevertheless, widening the scope of 
the assessment as for socio-economic analyses may lead to overplanting being characterised to 
be more beneficial than investigating from an investor point of view.  
Therefore, the present thesis will address the implications of overplanting from a socio-
economic perspective after presenting the private-economic business case analyses. 
 
2.3.2 Exploiting market effects 
Another option to reduce the necessity of grid expansion or reinforcement is to design the wind 
turbines in a system-optimised way in terms of the ratio of rotor area, or swept area, to the 
installed generator capacity. A wind turbine with a larger rotor but smaller generator may 
produce as much electricity as a non-optimised concept but in a more system-friendly manner, 
by producing more energy during times of low wind and reducing production spikes at high wind 
speeds, thus smoothing the total power output profile. In this way, balancing costs for the 
==PAGE:31==
2.3 Enhancing wind power generation 
17 
 
power generator can be decreased. Furthermore, when being under full market exposure after 
the support payment period, the system-optimised concept can be particularly relevant when 
the value of wind energy is relatively low during periods of high wind (International Energy 
Agency, 2014). Not producing additional energy during times of abundant generation but being 
able to produce more energy instead during times of less wind power in the market can indeed 
exploit market effects. Since the wholesale market price tends to decrease with a high share of 
wind power in the system or renewable energy in general (Sensfuß, Ragwitz, & Genoese, 2008), 
the expected returns of renewable generators can be lower than average expected returns for 
conventional power generators (Muñoz & Bunn, 2013). Questions regarding this merit order 
effect have been arising considering a market reform in forms of supplemented capacity markets 
(DECC, 2012) for future years, in which renewable generators are expected and planned to 
deliver the major share of electricity in various European electricity markets, but changes of the 
overall structure of wholesale electricity markets are not in concrete prospect.  
Consequently, the financial value of electricity from wind generation during part-load can be 
higher than during full-load operation in a power system with considerable share of wind power. 
Overplanting can pick up the idea of exploiting this negative correlation between market price 
and wind power generation, since for the concession of having to curtail energy during times of 
high wind more additional electricity can be generated during times with lower wind with a 
higher profit margin.  
Therefore, the present thesis will also carry out an investigation of a varying power price 
depending on the wind power generation share and will link it to the power generation profile of 
an overplanted offshore wind farm, in order to find out if the additionally produced power by 
the overplanted turbines is characterised by a higher value than the average market 
remuneration of the wind farm. 
 
2.3.3 Overplanting of wind energy – a literature review 
As mentioned in the two previous subsections, overplanting can contribute to mitigate cost and 
increasing revenue, yet the concept so far has not been a prominent resource of reducing the 
LCoE of offshore wind farms. Not many references were identified to be publicly available in the 
literature addressing this topic, which makes it a subject worth investigating in order to harvest 
its potential. The following section will discourse about the concept of overplanting as found in 
the literature, in particular where it was mentioned to be a measure to decline costs, where it 
has been and will be considered by governmental regulations, and who investigated and 
analysed the feasibility of the concept. 
 
In their report “Increasing the Owner’s Value of Wind Power Plants in Energy Systems with Large 
Shares of Wind Energy”, the Danish consortium Megavind (2014) suggests overplanting as one 
measure to obtain higher market revenues for wind energy besides other actions as 
participation in balancing markets as one ancillary service or repowering of turbines. It argues 
that additional electricity production by overplanting can be utilised for compensation of grid 
losses and unavailability and predicts additional profits for the concept when accounting for the 
expected decreasing trend of power market prices. The report further mentions to combine the

concept of overplanting with the technique of dynamic line rating of the export cable to 
optimise overplanting even further. Due to the expansion of wind power in power systems, 
dynamic line rating is said to play an increased role due to the needed increased transmission 
capacity for short periods of high wind. Due to the temporal loading of the export cable during 
short power production spikes above the static transmission limit, the transmission capacity 
setup can be optimised in a different way compared to overplanting. The more slowly reacting 
temperature profile of the cable conductor does not necessarily exceed the constrained limit 
during short times of production spikes, which makes it possible to increase power transmission 
for these short times even with additional installed capacities. 
 
Overplanting linked with dynamic line rating 
Nonetheless, applying dynamic line rating can also counteract the concept of overplanting 
turbines in the wind farm. The additional capacity of overplanted turbines is opposed to the 
reduced rating of the transmission capacity. This is why an integrated approach is desirable 
when optimising overplanting capacity together with the dynamic line rating. The second thesis 
of the overarching project, in which the present thesis was conducted, in which the impact of 
overplanting on the optimal setup of dynamic line rating was investigated, found out that the 
additionally generated energy of the overplanted turbines plays a minor role in the overall 
calculation of dynamic line rating (Uya, 2016). Still, the optimisation by means of different setups 
of export cables combined with the optimisation of additionally installed capacity can be 
opposed against each other to optimise both concepts combined. 
 
The Dutch Transmission System Operator (TSO) TenneT considers overplanting with a strong 
linkage to dynamic line rating for the next tendered wind farm sites in the Netherlands, for 
which TenneT has been made responsible to install the grid connection from the offshore wind 
farm to the existing transmission grid. For the tender of the wind farm Borssele, their proposed 
design results in an increase of 10% of installed capacity with respect to the transmission 
capacity, which has been decreased to 8% to be in accordance to the regulatory framework for 
this tender concerning additional capacity installation by the Dutch Ministry of Economic Affairs 
(TenneT, 2016). The focus of calculation is set to the dynamic line rating. Consequently the 
export cable load management follows three levels of curtailment:

Overplanting and capacity optimisation of wind energy in the historical context 
In contrast, also by only considering fixed cable rating the installed capacity can be increased 
above the transmission capacity to exploit times of low wind and low availability. The National 
Grid (2008) assessed the optimal utilisation ratio between these two capacities for the far 
offshore Round 3 wind farm projects in the UK. It is stated that especially these sites can benefit 
from additionally installed capacity, as the average wind farm availability may be lower due to 
rougher environmental conditions and more restricted travel times to the wind farm for 
maintenance. The study advises to oversize a wind farm by an additional 12% of capacity. This 
setup in average yields the lowest ratio of CapEx divided by lifetime energy generation, which 
was the means of evaluation in the assessment. O&M costs were excluded from the analysis. 
The estimations concerning the wind farm availability estimating the far offshore Round 3 sites, 
however, were taken from data of the first major British offshore wind farm project North Hoyle 
located close to shore (showing in average 90% of availability). Furthermore the CapEx 
assumptions are comparably low and outdated considering the rise in offshore investment cost 
over the recent years. Although the actual results give an indication of the benefits of 
overplanting, the applicability of the assessment to contemporary offshore wind farms 
considering the recent economic development of CapEx has already become limited. 
 
The Irish Commission for Energy Regulation (CER, 2014) decided to allow an additional capacity 
installation of 20% with respect to the maximum export capacity for wind generators, not 
distinguishing between onshore and offshore wind power. This decision was made in order to 
exploit expected economic benefits of the additional capacity and to further increase the energy 
produced by renewable generators in the electricity system. It was further stated that making it 
possible to raise the capacity also for existing plants will not adversely affect grid costs by 
necessary reinforcement or extensions, but contrarily can benefit the network utilisation by 
obtaining higher capacity factors of the wind generator plants. Likewise the improved system 
utilisation is said to be able to benefit end-use consumer electricity cost in terms of their effect 
on network tariffs, besides a slight general decline of the electricity price due to a higher share 
of wind power in the grid. 
 
Succeeding the decision of CER, the University College Cork published an assessment about the 
benefits of oversizing of onshore wind generation plants up to the aforementioned regulatory 
limit of additional capacity (McInerney, 2014). The study mentioned the beneficial aspects of 
overplanting to contain advantages regarding transmission system cost, reduction in reserve 
requirement, ancillary services and in particular the aforementioned market effect of higher 
prices during low wind power generation in section 2.3.2. The study therefore outlines the 
importance of reflecting the support scheme in terms of the market exposure in the

overplanting assessment and subsequently investigates overplanting under a fixed feed-in tariff 
and a fixed premium on top of the wholesale market price. The benchmark wind farm is a 
100 MW onshore plant having an average availability factor of 80%, which is considerably lower 
than availability factors of contemporary offshore wind farms. The power system and market 
simulates the British network and market as of 2012. 
The analysis was evaluated in terms of NPV. Under the feed-in tariff, it was found that an 
additional capacity of 8% yields the highest relative increase in value for the wind farm 
compared to having no additional installation. Sensitivities regarding the wind climate showed 
that the incentive to overplant decreases under a high average wind speed, since the rated 
power of the wind farm is reached for a longer period of time. Changing the support level by a 
remuneration that is 5% higher and lower, respectively, results in the same optimal additional 
capacity level. Under full market exposure, the benefit of overplanting depends on the overall 
assumption regarding the market development. Even though the absolute profitability of the 
wind farm declines along with having more wind generation in the system, the relative 
overplanting value increases compared to the base case. In any case the percentage of 
overplanted capacity is higher for all observations than under the feed-in tariff. With a lower 
share of wind energy in the system, the observation frame of 0-20% of additional capacity was 
not sufficient to find a peak of an optimal additional installation rate. Finally, an extreme case of 
wide-spread implementation of overplanting was considered by modelling all wind farms in the 
system with overplanted capacity, which is stated to be unlikely to be implemented but serves 
as a limiting case for the investigation. The study determined that the broad oversizing of wind 
farms, together with a high decarbonisation of the market, has such a negative effect on the 
market prices that overplanting becomes unprofitable compared to no capacity addition. 
Nevertheless, as the electricity market changes towards including more renewable generators, 
the policy support is also likely to change according to the study. The evaluation is concluded by 
showing that policy interventions can compensate to an extent for this negative effect. 
 
Overplanting of offshore wind farms 
The observed sensitivity of seeing a lower incentive for overplanting under higher average wind 
speed poses the question how viable overplanting is for offshore wind farms. DONG Energy and 
DNV-GL published a case study assessing the potential of overplanting offshore wind farms, in 
which two calculation models of the companies were compared with each other, leading both to 
an optimal overplanting of 2-5% additional capacity for generic large offshore wind farms in the 
United Kingdom (Baldock & Henderson, 2014), using a benchmark wind farm of 600 MW 
capacity. The assessment was exclusively done in terms of LCoE. Sensitivities on the wind climate 
are in line with the trend determined in the study of McInerney and further sensitivities were 
done concerning the availability of turbines, which had a more decisive impact on the 
overplanting result than the wind climate. The lower the average turbine availability in the farm, 
the higher is the incentive to overplant in order to compensate the production during times of 
turbines not running. Overall it was found that savings of LCoE in the range of 0.5% to 1% are 
obtained.

2.4.1 Wind energy generation 
In 2015, the total production of wind energy in the United Kingdom amounted to 40.4 TWh, 
corresponding to 12% of the gross electricity consumption in the country (DECC, 2016). The 
share of electricity generated by wind power can therefore still be considered a rather minor 
factor in the British electricity system. With this small share, it is considered that wind energy 
production overall has a negligible impact on the expectable wholesale electricity prices. Hence 
the British scenarios in this thesis solely consider to be remunerated by an average electricity 
market price independent of the power generation of wind farm generators. The determination 
of the electricity market price level will be addressed in the description of the model 
assumptions in section 3.5.4.

The total production of wind energy in Denmark totalled 14.1 TWh in 2015. This generated 
electricity corresponds to a share of 42% of the national gross electricity consumption 
(Energinet.dk, 2016a). Electricity delivered from wind power thus makes up a significant share in 
the Danish market. In fact, Denmark is the country with the highest share of electricity on total 
consumption generated by wind power (Energinet.dk, 2016b). Therefore the amount of wind 
power in the power system in Denmark can have a significant influence on the wholesale 
electricity market price. The more wind energy is in the market, the lower in tendency is the 
market price. Consequently, the average remuneration from the electricity market for a wind 
farm is presumably lower than the average electricity market price for all generators over the 
same period. An analysis found out that wind generation in the western Danish price area DK1 of 
the NordPool spot market in 2006–2008 was remunerated in average 10% less than the average 
market price (Klinge Jacobsen, 2015). This effect will be integrated in the assessment by an own 
estimation of the deviation between average wind generation power price and the overall 
average market prices.  
Furthermore, the significant share of wind power in the Danish electricity system is suitable for 
the additional investigation regarding the correlation between wind power generation and spot 
market prices. The value of wind energy generation over different levels of wind generation in 
the system will thus be determined for the Danish market and assessed by an additional 
economic analysis. 
 
2.4.2 Investment project scope 
The total project costs of a wind farm business case have to be known in order to evaluate the 
impacts of overplanting properly for the investor. The whole project scope encompasses the 
construction of the wind farm itself along with its connection to the existing power grid, the so-
called point of common coupling (PCC). Among different countries in Europe, various 
approaches regarding the investment scope of the projects are defined, sometimes even varying 
for different project procedures within the same country. Including or excluding for instance cost 
of the grid connection of the investor’s scope can have a considerable impact on the viability of 
the project as seen by the investor. Having to pay all the occurring costs could lead the investor 
to drop a certain project, whereas it could be undertaken if another entity such as the TSO had 
to bear the grid connection cost instead. Therefore the different project scopes can also affect 
the viability and the optimal level of overplanting for the project. Higher benefits of overplanting 
can be expected for projects which include the cost for grid connection, since the grid 
connection cost does not rise with an additional instalment of turbines. Contrarily, the relative 
cost increase due to overplanting turns out higher for a project in which the costs only comprise 
the expenditures for the wind farm itself. The investigated countries United Kingdom and 
Denmark differ significantly in this point. In order to investigate the effect that a difference in 
the investment project scope has on the viability of overplanting, the construction of the whole 
project can be divided into subcategories. One can differ between the six items as listed in the 
following and appearing in Figure 2.2:

In the United Kingdom, the wind farm developer has to bear the costs of the whole project

connection are constructed by the wind farm developer, before he is obliged to sell the 
transmission infrastructure to an offshore transmission owner (OFTO) by a competitive tender, 
who then owns and operates the asset for a dedicated contract length of 20 years (PwC, 2012). 
The OFTO can in turn charge transmission fees for the developer to use the infrastructure, in 
order for the OFTO to recover his investment (GWEC, 2014). Although it is not a necessity that 
the wind farm developer also constructs the grid connection asset, in practice it is always the 
case and therefore considered as general approach within the thesis. 
In the present economic analysis, the transmission fees are regarded as annual expenditures on 
top of the annual OpEx, as it was also applied in the offshore wind farm cost reduction study of 
The Crown Estate (2012). The height of the transmission fees is determined by the value of the 
grid connection investment, which includes the offshore substation, the supply cost for the 
transmission or export cable and onshore substation. The total investment value of these three 
components is determined and annuity costs corresponding to that value characterise the 
transmission fee. The rate of return requested by OFTOs for wind farm projects usually has been 
around 10% (nominal) (KPMG, 2012). The annuity payments of the wind farm developer 
therefore are determined by discounting the grid connection investment value by this 
percentage. Since the rate of return corresponds to the presumed discount factor in the British 
market, there is no big influence for applying this method compared to assuming these costs as 
investment cost in the DCF analysis. 
The procedure in Denmark of assigning the project scope to different entities is divided by 
different approaches. Currently, the two large-scale offshore wind tenders of Horns Rev 3 and 
Kriegers Flak of in total 1000 MW and the nearshore wind tenders of 350 MW are consented by 
the government (Danish Energy Agency & Energinet.dk, 2012). For the two large-scale offshore 
wind tenders, the investor only bears the construction of the wind farm with its array cables

The Public Service Obligation (PSO) support scheme in Denmark is similar to the British

the sliding premium is also set by an auction, but separately for each project. The support price 
is not adjusted for inflation annually. For offshore wind farms, there is no cap for the maximum

granted. In cases of market prices above the guaranteed price, however, the investor has to pay 
back a price supplement as in the British regulation. The duration of the support is not time-
based, but determined by the full-load hours (FLH) of the plant. It is set to 50,000 FLH for 
offshore wind energy projects. The amount of time, in which the support price is paid, thus 
depends on the capacity factor of the offshore wind farm. While a wind farm having a capacity 
factor of 40% receives support for more than 14 years, a wind farm with a capacity factor of 50% 
is paid the support for roughly 11 years only. The energy-based support determined by the 
amount of full-load hours induces an additional benefit when considering overplanting for a 
certain offshore wind farm in Denmark. If the installed capacity of the wind farm increases due 
to additionally erected wind turbines, the equivalent energy generated for a certain amount of 
full-load hours also increases. The total amount of energy being remunerated by the support 
scheme is thus always higher when applying overplanting in an offshore wind farm. This is due to 
the fact that the full-load hours are considered corresponding to the wind turbine’s installed 
capacity (Danish Energy Agency, 2011) and not corresponding to the transmission capacity.  
Apart from the higher total amount of subsidised energy, there are other effects on the support 
payments when overplanting an offshore wind farm within an energy-based support scheme. 
Since overplanting leads to prospectively higher capacity factors of the wind farm, the subsidised 
energy production per wind turbine is fully generated and remunerated at an earlier point in 
time. In the DCF analysis, this aspect will evoke slight benefits by earlier discounting the 
respective revenues, but on the other hand also induces the drawback of fully exposing the wind 
farm earlier and thus longer to the usually lower market prices. In case of applying overplanting, 
however, these effects are outweighed by the beneficial additional support that the overplanted 
wind farm receives for its additionally installed capacity.

22% from 2016 onwards after a step-wise reduction from 25% in 2013.  
 
2.5 Summary 
As seen in the present section providing the background for offshore wind energy economics in 
the different countries United Kingdom and Denmark, both CapEx and LCoE historically have 
been higher for British offshore wind farms, mainly due to differences in investment cost 
allocation and the assumed financing costs. The British regulation partially alleviates higher costs 
for the investor by implementing comparably low fiscal burden for a wind farm developer. Still, 
considering the support schemes many European offshore wind markets are aligning their 
regulations with each other towards tender-based projects remunerated by a sliding premium 
tariff. Details regarding the granted time of remuneration and price caps, however, persist 
between the different countries. 
Furthermore, overplanting as a means to reduce the LCoE has not yet been widely documented. 
The literature found did not provide a consensual tendency towards the optimal level of 
overplanting, but stressed the importance to examine the impacts of different technical and

3 Model description and assumptions 
3.1 Model setup 
The economic assessment of the implementation of overplanting is based on an installation 
capacity optimisation model constructed with Microsoft Excel (Microsoft, 2010). The 
optimisation model is split into two modules. The first module calculates the energy generation 
of the offshore wind farm in terms of its annual energy production (AEP). A conceptual scheme 
of the module is visualised in Figure 3.1.  
The module uses four inputs to define the gross power curve of the wind farm. The changes in 
installation capacity are expressed by the number of overplanted wind turbines on top of the 
baseline number of turbines, the latter being fixed to a specific project size. The model runs 
through each incremental overplanted wind turbine as new capacity setup and the respective 
AEP is determined. Together with the turbine power curve and an efficiency factor accounting 
for losses in the array cables and offshore substation, the gross power curve of the wind farm is 
determined. 
Further losses are characterised by the wake efficiency and the turbine availability. While the 
wake losses are linked to the wind speed distribution, the turbine availability is expressed by a 
frequency distribution of the turbines that are not generating power. Deducting these losses 
leads to the net power curve of the wind farm. The frequency distribution of the turbine 
availability consequently expands the net wind farm power curve by one dimension. 
The net power curve is limited to the fixed transmission capacity of the wind farm. The AEP of 
the wind farm is determined by multiplying the net power curve by the assumed wind potential. 
A Weibull distribution is utilised to express the wind potential at the site of the offshore wind 
farm. Besides the AEP, the curtailment due to the transmission capacity limit is quantified. 
In the present approach, a frequency based modelling of both the turbine availability and the 
wind potential was applied. Hence relative shares of turbine outage times and probability 
distributions of wind over the year can be used to express the turbine availability and wind 
potential. In this regard, the net power curve of the wind farm is multiplied by the respective 
wind speed probability and the number of hours per year.

The second module of the complete model is represented by a cost model of the offshore wind 
farm. The total costs are opposed to the revenues achieved by the wind farm operation within 
the DCF analysis. The conceptual scheme of the financial module is visualised in Figure 3.2. 
The cost model contains specific cost data of the wind farm components that are related to the 
installation capacity as well as to the geographical characteristics of the offshore wind farm. By 
specifying the water depth and the distance to shore, the investment cost (CapEx) of the 
offshore wind farm is derived by the relevant cost data. The construction time further defines 
the investment cost distribution before the commissioning date of the wind farm. The 
considered lifetime of the wind farm finally determines the operation period and thereby the 
annual operational cost (OpEx) of the wind farm, which are added by the occurring balancing 
costs in the respective country and the transmission charge in case of the British scenarios. 
The basic CapEx and OpEx assumptions defined by the cost data of the offshore wind farm do 
not differ within the two regulatory regimes. The cost allocation of the whole wind farm project, 
however, determines the actual magnitude of the investment cost that the wind farm investor 
has to bear. In contrast, the revenue from selling the generated electricity of the wind farm is 
subject to the assumptions regarding the national support scheme and electricity market prices. 
The support and market price per MWh generated electricity are linked to the calculated AEP of 
the energy generation module in order to determine the revenue that the wind farm receives. 
With the annual revenues and annual costs, the payable tax of the project can be determined 
within the respective regulatory setup. The national fiscal policies of the United Kingdom and 
Denmark are used to determine the respective tax level. The total cost that the investor faces 
thus is determined by CapEx, OpEx and payable tax. Discounting the occurring costs of the wind 
farm project over time and accounting for the discounted AEP leads to the LCoE estimation of 
the wind farm. The LCoE is used as the first metric to assess the economic performance of the 
offshore wind farm.

project, accounting for both total cost and total revenue over the project time frame. The IRR is 
the principal measure for this business case assessment, and the connected NPV serves as an

The whole model iterates through the addition of installed project capacity, i.e. additionally 
installed wind turbines, on top of the baseline capacity. Each iteration results in a certain AEP 
value, which is forwarded to the financial module in order to calculate the estimated revenue of 
the wind farm. Along with the total project costs of the respective installation capacity setup, 
the LCoE and IRR are determined for each case. Each iteration outcome is then assessed against 
the evaluation of the baseline wind farm to indicate the relative benefit that overplanting can 
achieve. 
The following subsections will elaborate the assumptions behind the input parameters of the 
model and the justifications regarding their choices. Before that, a representative offshore wind 
farm will be conceptualised and presented, valid for all assessments under the different 
regulatory regimes.  
 
3.2 Representative offshore wind farm 
DONG Energy aims on standardising a part of its offshore wind farm projects in order to be able 
to install a larger amount of offshore wind farms more cost-effectively, and also to increase the 
operational efficiency of their assets (DONG Energy, 2013b). A way of standardised and cost-
efficient construction is to define a dedicated transmission capacity with a fixed electrical

infrastructure. In this fashion, modules of specific wind farm sizes can be established and 
expanded with several identical infrastructure components. Moreover, different WTGs can still 
be chosen to install in the various offshore wind farms, and the number of turbines can 
consequently be fitted to the dedicated transmission capacity based on the generator size of the 
used turbines.  
In the present thesis, one module consists of a wind farm transmission capacity of 400 MW, 
using high-voltage level export cables of 220 kV. This mirrors the consideration of the thesis of 
focusing on large offshore wind farms. Since underground transmission cables are only 
manufactured with specific and distinct voltage levels, the voltage level and the expected losses 
dictate the optimally installed capacity of the wind farm. Overall, a transmission installation 
capacity of 400 MW can roughly be regarded as the upper power limit for a single export circuit 
of 220 kV export cables. With a higher power level, more export circuits are needed, multiplying 
the cost of export cables by the needed number of export circuits. In contrast, a lower power 
level does not exploit the voltage level of the export cable to its full extent. The precise 
optimisation between the export cable voltage level and the transmission capacity limit is not in 
the scope of the thesis, but considered as given by the relation stated above. 
Figure 3.3 visualises the offshore wind farm as used in the analysis. The individual WTGs are 
connected via array cables to the offshore substation. Due to the chosen voltage level of the 
export cable, the offshore and onshore substations are always part of the wind farm 
independent of the farm’s distance to shore. Since the array cables are frequently using a 
voltage level of around 33 kV, the voltage of the wind farm circuit has to be transformed to the 
voltage level of the export cable. With a higher voltage level in the export cable, power losses 
related to the current can be reduced. Wind farms located close to shore usually can avoid 
having to install an offshore substation, since lower voltage levels can be sufficient for the 
shorter export cables. Offshore wind farms with these characteristics, however, are not 
investigated in the present analysis.

scheduled maintenance windows and times of overhauls. The availability factor of a turbine or a

wind farms in the United Kingdom and Denmark were stated in detail to be 9.6 m/s and 9.1 m/s, 
respectively, which is based on satellite data. The lower value for Denmark is likely due to the 
comparably high share of older offshore wind farms close to shore compared to the sites of 
other European countries. The two current large Danish offshore wind farm projects Kriegers

and 9.8 m/s, respectively, with the former being situated in the Baltic Sea and the latter in the 
North Sea (Danish Energy Agency & Energinet.dk, 2012).

Unlike the CapEx costs, Operation and Maintenance costs are not published for specific projects.

of the CapEx. This can be translated to specific O&M costs divided by the installed capacity.

offshore wind farm. Nevertheless, in order to reflect the difference in O&M costs for the 
different British sites, i.e. presumably higher O&M costs for the wind farms further offshore, the 
previously mentioned O&M costs can be compared with suggestions of the British Department

kW should not change with a different number of wind turbines installed in the offshore wind 
farm, since the overplanted turbines account for the same specific OpEx as the residual 
deployed turbines.  
 
Discount factor 
The discount factors used for the respective national assessments are adopted from the 
governmental suggestions for this parameter, as presented in section 2.2.2. 
As previously mentioned, the money used for the wind farm investment is considered to be fully 
equity based in the present analysis, i.e. the investment money completely stems from savings 
of the investor. The money does not have to be provided by external entities such as banks 
which charge a loan for the investor. In fact, large investors are more likely to invest in a capital-
intensive project by means of their own reserves. As no debt is needed to finance the money, 
the cost of equity corresponds to the discount factor chosen in the analysis.

3.5.3 Support level assumptions 
Since the support level granted for both the United Kingdom and Denmark is based on auctions, 
the support price level has to be estimated for the analyses. The chosen support price for 
Denmark and the United Kingdom is derived from the previous auctions or auction rounds, 
respectively, that have already taken place.  
Generally, the auctioned support price can also be an indicator for the LCoE level. If the 
investment project should be economically feasible, the support price should partially reflect the 
costs that the investor has to bear over the project lifetime. However, since the support price is 
not paid over the whole lifetime and the remuneration within the residual lifetime is achieved by 
the usually lower electricity spot market price, the support price level should always be above 
the LCoE, which represent the total cost of the investment project, to ensure economic 
feasibility. Nevertheless, low competition in the tender or other bidding strategies can lead to a 
surge of the support price level with respect to the actual LCoE that the investor is expecting, so 
that the support level remains only an indicative parameter for reflecting the LCoE. 
In the United Kingdom, there are currently still two main support schemes in place for 
renewable generators. The Contract for Difference (CfD) support scheme elaborated in section 
2.4.3 replaces the Renewable Obligation (RO) scheme, a quota-based support scheme where 
certificates are received for the generation of renewable energy and where a certain quantity of 
certificates has to be obtained. With the introduction of the CfD support scheme in 2014, a 
transition period started lasting until 2017, in which both support schemes can be chosen from 
the investor for the respective project. Due to its phasing out, the RO support scheme is not 
considered in the present analysis.

3.5.4 Market price assumptions

the cash flow analysis decrease the value of the revenue received in the latter years, in which 
the wind farm is remunerated by the electricity market only. Nevertheless, reasonable 
assumptions for both national analyses are described in the following.

3.5.5 Economic evaluation considering free cash flows 
As shown in the introduction in section 1.4, the economic assessment is conducted both in terms 
of LCoE and IRR. The LCoE is considered as post-tax, including the influence of the respective 
national fiscal policy on the business case. The IRR is determined by the free cash flows available 
for the investor after accounting for depreciation and tax deductions. The corporate tax rate and 
depreciation method is overtaken from the national regulations.

In order to determine the free cash flows of each operational year of the wind farm, the 
Earnings before interests, taxes, depreciation and amortisation (EBITDA) and the Earnings before 
interests and taxes (EBIT) are needed to be specified. The EBITDA are defined by deducting all 
annual operational costs from the gained revenues of the wind farm. The free cash flows are 
then the result of deducting the payable tax from the EBITDA. 
The payable taxes need to be determined separately by accounting for depreciation and tax 
rate. The depreciation of an asset does not have to be taxed. Hence this amount of money can 
be subtracted from the EBITDA, resulting in the EBIT. The EBIT or taxable income is taxed by the 
corporate tax rate as defined for each year by the national legislation. This share represents the 
payable taxes, with which the free cash flows can be determined. 
Since for both countries a declining balance depreciation method applies, a relative share of the 
book value of the asset specifies the amount of depreciation determined by the depreciation 
rate. The book value of the wind farm is considered to be the amount of investment determined 
at the beginning of the project timeline. The residual book value of the asset in each operational 
year then is the difference between previous book value and the amount of depreciation being 
deducted. In the last year of operation, the residual book value is deducted completely from the 
EBITDA without considering the rate of depreciation in order to fully depreciate the asset. 
If the depreciation value exceeds the EBITDA, no taxes have to be paid in the respective year. 
The exceeding amount of depreciation is not lost but carried forward to the next year, so as not 
to induce economic disadvantages for the investor.

4.7 Correlation of spot price and wind climate in the Danish market 
4.7.1 Introduction and rationale 
In section 2.4.1, it is argued that the high wind production level can have a significant impact on 
the market prices in the Danish electricity market area. With a large amount of wind power in 
the power system, the spot market price generally drops to lower values than in times with 
lower wind power generation. Since an increasing share of wind energy is expected to be 
produced from offshore wind farms, in the future large clusters of wind farms closely located to 
each other could simultaneously feed in electricity to the power system. A high production level 
from a single farm can therefore increasingly correspond to a high level of wind power in the 
power system and vice versa. The impacts on overplanting are that the curtailed power during 
high wind speeds in tendency can be less valuable on the power market than the overplanted 
power that a wind farm produces at part-load during moderate wind speeds. This effect can 
have an additional benefit on the economic viability of overplanting. 
Therefore, in the Danish scenario a further investigation is carried out on determining the 
electricity market price being dependent on different wind speed categories of the wind farm 
power curve. In detail, the share of wind generation on total electricity consumption in the 
combined Danish electricity system of DK1 and DK2 will be linked to the production profile of 
the offshore wind farm. In this way, more wind power in the system corresponds to a higher 
generation of the offshore wind farm. 
The implications on overplanting and the general impact on the remuneration of the offshore 
wind farm will be investigated.

4.7.4 Discussion 
The minor change in the economic performance of overplanting is related to the following 
factors: 
 
 
The highest increase in AEP of the overplanted turbines with respect to the baseline wind 
farm occurs in the power curve region just below rated power. This region corresponds to 
only little additional increase of the market price over the average level. Furthermore, the 
wind farm production profile over the different wind speeds is depicted in Figure D.10 in the 
appendix. From the figure, it becomes apparent that especially for comparably low 
additional capacity installation as in the optimal overplanting designs the additional energy 
generation is distributed more evenly over all wind speed categories. This is related to times 
of lower availability, in which the overplanted turbines directly compensate for outages and 
therefore also produce during times of high wind.  
 
Due to the support scheme and the sliding premium support price, the remuneration for the 
wind farm during the support period remains at the same level, even though the total 
earnings partially shift from support payments to direct market payments. 
 
The wind farm first is fully exposed to the market after the support payment period is ended 
after roughly 12 years. Due to discounting of the cash flows, the apparent added revenue 
after that time is characterised by a lower value within the DCF analysis. 
 
Economic shifts of revenues in the optimal design are comparably even smaller because the 
optimal setup in the Danish scenario is only at a relatively low additional amount of capacity, 
leading to less additional AEP and therefore even fewer additional benefits regarding the 
higher remunerated energy. 
 
Furthermore, the methodology as described above has some limitations that can influence the 
overall outcome of the analysis: 
 
 
The system-wide influence of wind generation on the spot market price is not directly 
correlated to the production profile of a single wind farm. Nevertheless, over the full lifetime 
of the wind farm, the tendency of this connection might be close to the real average 
remuneration over different wind speeds and therefore a reasonable assumption. 
 
The investigation does not account for imports and exports in the electricity market, which 
can also show an influence on the contracted spot market price, or other factors affecting 
the price-setting in the spot market. However, while conducting the analysis, it was also 
considered to relate the relative amount of wind production in the total system to the 
market price without considering the respective consumption level. In this case, a similar 
decreasing trend, which was best represented by a fourth order polynomial regression, was 
observed. Therefore by changing the assumptions leading to the price determination over 
different wind speeds, a similar trend in the economic outcome was observed.

4.8 Overplanting from a socio-economic point of view 
4.8.1 Introduction and methodological changes 
The lower optimal design of overplanting in the Danish regulatory regime compared to the 
British regulatory regime gives rise to an analysis regarding overplanting in a socio-economic 
perspective in Denmark. The costs for grid connection, which are still existent and have to be 
borne by a certain party, are expected to make overplanting of wind farms in the Danish regime 
more justifiable in a socio-economic perspective. 
Compared to a private-economic analysis, a socio-economic analysis encompasses a wider 
perspective of a specific investment, seen from the society point of view as the name implies. 
While a private-economic analysis tries to optimise the cash flows for the investment company, 
a socio-economic analysis wants to answer the question whether an investment is beneficial for 
the society as a whole and therefore tries to optimise social welfare. 
Changes to a private-economic analysis are characterised by the following items, following the 
suggested approach of Kitzing, Münster, & Katz (2015a): 
 
 
The assessment is based on real monetary values, since inflation affects all parts of the 
society equally. Cash flows are thus not inflated over time. 
 
Likewise, always the real discount rate is used in the assessment. 
 
All accruing investment costs and residual costs are part of the economic evaluation and no 
cost allocation for different parties is considered. The costs for grid connection are therefore 
included in the total investment of the wind farm. 
 
Support payments are not considered in a socio-economic analysis. Since support payments 
originate from the society, the related cash flows are only shifted between different parties 
within the evaluation boundary. 
 
Due to similar reasons, taxes and depreciation rates are not accounted for in a socio-
economic analysis, so that the free cash flows equal the EBITDA. 
 
Further external costs or externalities as well as external benefits can be part of a socio-
economic analysis, but are not included in the present assessment. 
 
4.8.2 Economic impact 
Without the support payments, the wind farm investment is not economically feasible, i.e. the 
NPV is at a negative value. The IRR is therefore below the assumed discount rate, since the 
expected return is not achieved. In fact, within the analysis the IRR turned out to even express a 
negative value. Consequently the offshore wind farm needs monetary support to be 
economically viable. Due to a rate of return below the socio-economic discount rate, the 
evaluation on the optimal design of the offshore wind farm is therefore solely based on LCoE as 
economic indicator. Figure 4.14 shows the trend of LCoE over different additions of capacity for 
the Average Danish offshore wind farm in a socio-economic analysis along with the previously 
presented trend of LCoE within the private-economic analysis.

4.9 Summary 
The previous section comprises analyses of the financial consequences of the capacity 
optimisation of overplanting. The offshore wind farms were determined in the context of 
average site characteristics of currently planned and consented offshore wind farms in the 
United Kingdom and Denmark, respectively. The business case impacts were determined from a 
private-economic point of view within the analyses of the British scenarios, whereas the Danish 
analyses comprised both a private-economic and socio-economic analysis. Furthermore, in the 
Danish scenario the effect of varying electricity spot market prices over different shares of wind 
generation in the power system and consequently different shares of production of the offshore 
wind farm was investigated. 
Within the private-economic analysis in the British regulatory regime, it was found that a 
conventional offshore wind farm can be optimised by an additional installation of 4% of its 
transmission capacity limit. Sensitivity analyses implied that this amount of extra capacity is 
quite robust against variances in the wind farm setup and external parameters, and shows a 
small tendency towards 5-6% additional capacity for varying sensitivity parameters. 
An extra analysis was carried out for average characteristics of currently planned offshore wind 
farms with a distance to shore exceeding 100 km. In particular, due to the higher share of total 
CapEx accruing for the grid connection, the optimal design was found to increase to 6%. 
In Denmark, the investor primarily benefits from the regulations concerning the support scheme, 
which remunerates the generated electricity based on the installed capacity of the wind farm 
favouring additional installations. Considering the impact of the support scheme and all cash 
flows occurring in the investment project, an additional capacity of also 4% was determined as 
optimal design. Compared to the British analyses, however, the addition of capacity leads to less 
added value, since the British market was generally found to yield higher returns for offshore 
wind farm investments.  
A socio-economic analysis, however, found out that more savings for society can be expected 
compared to savings for the investor, mainly since the grid connection costs obviously are still 
part of the total investment comprising an offshore wind farm. 
The expected additional market-wise benefit of overplanting in energy systems with a large 
generation share of wind energy did not show a direct effect on the optimal design. It led, 
however, to the conclusion that overplanting leads to some additional benefits due to a slight 
shift of the total payments away from support per supplied MWh, being financed by the society, 
towards more direct market remuneration.

5 Discussion 
5.1 Methodological approach 
Calculation of AEP 
The frequency-based approach of the calculation model revealed some benefits and drawbacks 
concerning the accuracy of the calculations. It can be opposed to the time-based approach of 
modelling the AEP within the second undertaken thesis of the overarching project of 
overplanting. 
The present frequency-based approach is represented by the determination of the energy 
production based on probability distributions of the wind climate and turbine availability. The 
transfer of specific long term time series of wind measurements into statistical expressions 
based on the distribution of the wind makes it possible to calculate the expected AEP of a wind 
farm quickly. Furthermore, the utilisation of a general distribution of the wind climate makes it 
easily possible to implement considerations regarding the availability of turbines into the 
calculations. Modelling based on a time-series with wind speed data of a specific resolution 
would need further simulations to reflect to probable occurrences of wind turbine outages. 
The general drawback of the frequency-based approach is that the wind speed distribution 
represents only an approximation of real measurements. For investment analyses as carried out 
in the present thesis, however, this method is not a direct disadvantage, since the evaluation is 
based on future cash flows partially depending on uncertain wind speeds and therefore not 
subject to specific sequences of wind speeds. 
 
Binomial distribution of turbine availability 
The modelling approach of turbine availability is related to the considerations of the paragraph 
above. Using a binomial distribution to express the probability of turbine outages implies that 
the outage of a certain wind turbine is totally coincidental. Yet unlike the random breakdown of 
a specific wind turbine, the outages are also dependent on for instance maintenance schedules, 
which can be linked to seasonal patterns of wind speeds or weather conditions of the specific 
site – a significant amount of maintenance can be scheduled at times of low wind, where the 
access to the site also tends to be easier. The present assumption of a random distribution of 
outages can therefore deviate, affecting the amount of time how many wind turbines are down 
for maintenance. The average turbine availability can remain on the same level, while the 
distribution pattern changes. Observations within the model showed that a distributional change 
of availability can have an effect on the optimal design of installed capacity. 
 
==PAGE:91==
5.2 Results in the context of previous research 
77 
 
Integer-based optimisation 
The approach of the present analysis was to add installed turbine capacity while keeping the 
transmission capacity at the same level. In that way, only specific amounts of capacity could be 
added to the wind farm capacity in steps of the size of the deployed WTGs. An alternative could 
have been to fix the installed capacity while decreasing the limit of the transmission capacity. 
The transmission limit of the export cable is likely to vary in smaller ranges, although specific 
cross-sections of the export cables available also limit the optimisation to some extent. The 
alternative approach was rejected for two reasons:  
Firstly, the corresponding increase in investment costs due to additional turbines can be easily 
correlated with the added capacity, since the costs usually are stated specifically per MW. Costs 
for the export cable, however, are usually presented specifically per length. A decrease in the 
transmission capacity primarily corresponds to a decrease in the cross-section of the cable, 
which leads to a decline in cost that is not reflected by the cable length. The corresponding 
economic benefit is therefore less easy to predict when estimating the respective cost based on 
references within the literature. 
Secondly, fixing the transmission capacity is a more practical approach of standardising certain 
components of an offshore wind farm, as also stated in section 3.2. The anticipated 
standardisation of the transmission system capacity would not be ensured when considering the 
transmission system as the variable element; hence the present assessment of overplanting 
should reflect this consideration by varying the installed capacity of the wind farm. 
 
5.2 Results in the context of previous research 
Optimal overplanted capacity 
Compared to the presented additions of installation capacity in the literature review of 
overplanting in section 2.3.3, the present analyses principally yielded a lower added capacity in 
the optimal design. Only the analysis of Baldock & Henderson (2014), which looked at offshore 
wind farms in the British market only, suggests a similar increase of installed capacity as optimal 
capacity design for offshore wind farms. 
The deviation to the investigated references was derived to be mainly due to the assumptions 
regarding availability of turbines. Especially National Grid (2008) and McInerney (2014) assume a 
significantly lower average turbine availability (as mentioned in section 2.3.3) than it was found 
to be the average assumptions for contemporary offshore wind farms. As for the latter source, 
the lower wind potential due to the examination of onshore wind power plants strengthens the 
tendency of increasing the additional capacity installed even more. In this regard, in the present 
analysis it was also found that the availability of turbines plays a more crucial role for the 
optimal design than the wind resource, since the relative LCoE reduction compared to the 
baseline wind farm varied more with a conceivable change of availability than with the alteration 
in wind climate – opposite to the general effect of economic performance of the investment 
project, which showed higher variations with an alternating wind climate, whereas changes in 
turbine availability had a smaller effect on the overall level of LCoE of the baseline wind farms. 
The effect that turbine availability has a higher impact on the LCoE reduction potential and thus

5.3 Economic evaluation parameters 
As mentioned before, the IRR is considered to be the more comprehensive indication of the two 
evaluation parameters in the economic analyses. Compared to LCoE, also the revenues from 
support and market play a direct role in the viability of the business case. 
The NPV of the business cases, which is linked to the IRR, has not been considered to define the 
optimal design of the offshore wind farm. Instead, the additional NPV created by applying 
overplanting was only presented as indication for the additional value that overplanting 
provides. This approach reflects the fact that the NPV is highly dependent on the chosen 
discount rate. The IRR serves as the more independent and less biased evaluation parameter by 
replacing the discount rate in the cash flow analysis and setting the NPV to zero. Estimating the 
discount rate correctly not only depends on defining the alternative return that general 
investments can generate and which is recommended by governmental entities. It depends also 
on the underlying financing strategy (whether or by how much the investment is financed by 
equity or by a loan) as well as estimations of risk for the project and constitutes a highly 
confidential domain of corporate finance. Using the IRR as evaluation parameter makes the 
assessment independent of the financing strategies and expected return of the investor. 
In fact, especially in the British scenarios, which achieved a high IRR compared to the assumed 
discount rate, the trend of IRR over different levels of added capacity does not reflect the 
corresponding trend of the NPV. Figure 5.2 presents the trend of IRR of the Conventional British 
offshore wind farm over different levels of capacity additions as shown previously (here in 
absolute terms), along with the resulting NPV of the different capacity setups. While the IRR 
indicates an optimal design with 4% additionally installed capacity, the highest NPV is achieved 
with 8% of additional capacity. Also the evaluation of McInerney (2014) used the NPV as 
evaluation parameter and was indicated by high levels of additional capacity as optimum, which 
might be induced by the same reason as stated above.

Since the baseline wind farm already yields a high IRR compared to the assumed discount factor 
of 10%, the investment also achieves a comparably high NPV with this capacity setup. The 
investment is highly profitable due to high revenues achieved by the support and market 
remuneration of the generated electricity. Deploying more turbines and thereby increasing the 
energy production of the wind farm therefore leads to a further increase in profits from support 
and market payments, which increases the NPV more than the IRR. Unlike the trend of IRR 
regarding the optimal design, the NPV is therefore sensitive to the magnitude of both the 
support and market payments as well as the considered level of discount rate. A lower discount 
rate reflects lower expectations of the investor regarding the financial return of the investment. 
If high revenues can then be obtained against the expectations of the investor, the possibility of 
even higher revenues gained from additionally sold electricity favours overplanting even more.  
The deviation between the trend of IRR and NPV over different levels of overplanting decreases 
in the Danish analyses, since the IRR is closer to the assumed discount rate. Generally, project 
investors know from experience how much return on investment can be expected from different 
projects. Therefore the chosen discount rate often comes close to the IRR of the project. In this 
way, the trend of NPV regarding optimal capacity would resemble the corresponding trend of 
IRR. 
 
5.4 Recommendations for future work 
The capacity optimisation of overplanting can be linked to various different aspects regarding 
the design of an offshore wind farm. A linkage to other optimisation considerations can help to 
find an optimal design for an offshore wind farm as a whole. Some concerns are listed and 
elaborated in the following: 
 
Integral approach between overplanting and dynamic line rating 
The present thesis does not take a detailed analysis and optimal design of the export cable setup 
and its transmittable power into account. The modelling of overplanting can be combined with 
an optimisation of the export cable capacity in order to extend the scope of investigation. A cost-
benefit analysis can be carried out in terms of the usage of the export cable. A cable with a 
smaller cross-section shows economic benefits by being cheaper, but constraints the 
transmission capacity limit further. The trade-off between investment cost savings and lost 
energy can be optimised especially with regard to the additional capacity installed with 
overplanted turbines. This approach can further be linked with temporal loading of the cable in 
order to decrease the additional curtailment and thereby being able to transmit more 
(overplanted) energy. 
 
Overplanting linked with wind farm layout optimisation 
It can be of interest how the overplanted turbines are integrated into the layout of the turbines 
within the offshore wind farm. An optimal design of the placement of the wind turbines tries to 
minimise losses due to wake effects and at the same time minimise the costs of the array cables 
==PAGE:95==

and the needed foundations. By installing additional turbines, the layout of the total wind farm 
will directly be affected. The impact on these factors, however, is considered to be small, as 
costs for array cables were included in the supply costs for turbines and since no specific wake 
efficiency was estimated dependent on the usage of additional turbines. Furthermore, the 
specific calculations need to be carried out within real projects, since especially the exact costs 
of foundations, and possible changes in costs due to different layouts, depend on the specific 
sea bed of the project site. 
 
Extension of regulatory regimes 
Finally, the present thesis assessed overplanting within the two countries of the United Kingdom 
and Denmark. They are constituted by a diametric approach concerning the cost allocation of 
the grid connection, which shows a major impact for the private-economic feasibility of 
overplanting. Yet other countries present further approaches on handling this aspect, for 
instance Germany, where the wind farm developer has to bear the costs for the wind farm and 
the offshore substation, but not for the residual grid connection. The possibility for overplanting 
is therefore expected to be in a range in between the presented optimal capacity additions of 
the United Kingdom and Denmark. Nevertheless further evaluations are needed, also in order to 
account for the specific project opportunities on the German market, which focusses on far 
offshore sites in the North Sea. 
Similarly, the present evaluation encompassed two countries both applying feed-in premium 
support schemes, even though the assessment for the subsidy quantity differs. An example for 
other approaches regarding the support scheme is Belgium, where a predetermined premium 
price paid over 20 years is guaranteed that increases its level in case of the wind farm investor’s 
takeover of the grid connection development. Besides the trade-off between higher investment 
cost due to the grid connection and higher market remuneration due to the increased support 
level, another consideration can be added to the economic analysis in this specific case. The 
higher expected returns when applying overplanting along with additional costs from the grid 
connection development are confronted with the prospects of lower returns when saving the 
grid connection and at the same time counteracting overplanting. 
These are only two examples, which were explored in the decision phase regarding the choice of 
the regulatory regime. Since the offshore wind market continues to grow and more investment 
possibilities in various countries are arising, many more investment strategies become apparent, 
for which overplanting can show different benefits and drawbacks.

6 Conclusion and recommendation 
The present thesis carried out an economic feasibility analysis of the capacity optimisation 
concept of overplanting. The cost of offshore wind energy projects was estimated based on 
various cost assumptions composed from the literature and compared with the historical 
development of offshore wind investment costs as well as recent reports and studies estimating 
the LCoE of offshore wind. With assumptions for the offshore wind farm project costs, a 
spreadsheet calculation model was constructed that assessed the economic viability of offshore 
wind farms and evaluated further economic benefits when adding capacity to the offshore wind 
farm while keeping the transmission limit fixed to a predetermined power level, thus 
overplanting the offshore wind farm above the capped power transmission.  
The concept was applied within the regulatory regimes of the two countries United Kingdom and 
Denmark, being represented by different regulations regarding the allocation of investment for 
the wind farm developer, distinct variances in the support scheme and differences in the relative 
generation share of national wind energy production and international transmission grid 
interconnections. 
Optimal overplanting is affected by all these factors to a different extent. Primarily, the cost 
allocation of the total wind farm investment played a big role in the optimal design of an 
offshore wind farm. As the regulations induce the wind farm investor in the UK to also construct 
the grid connection components and facilities of the offshore wind farm, a considerable share of 
total investment costs accrues to cost components that are not affected by the installation of 
additional capacity. The investment into additional capacity used as extra power above the 
transmission grid capacity limit therefore constitutes a lower portion of the total project costs. In 
this way, it was found out that an additional capacity of 4–6% compared to the transmission 
limit indicates optimal economic benefits for the development of currently planned offshore 
wind farms in the United Kingdom. As there are some distinct projects in the UK that are being 
planned in the North Sea exceeding 100 km to the nearest coast, the grid connection cost 
increases leading to higher benefits for the additional installation of capacity. The optimal 
relative increase, however, was found to remain close to 6% extra capacity installation. Optimal 
overplanting was determined to decrease the LCoE by approximately 0.4–0.7% in the British 
regulatory regime. 
On the other hand, the Danish TSO takes on the construction and investment of the grid 
connection for large offshore wind farms in Denmark. With the total investment costs being 
considerably lower and the addition of capacity characterising a higher relative increase in costs, 
the additional economic benefit from overplanting is less compared to the UK. However, the 
Danish regulation in the support scheme, which provides support payments based on the 
installed capacity and not transmission capacity and therefore favours the additional 
deployment of wind turbines, compensates the higher incremental investment costs. Concerning 
the Danish regulatory regime it was found out that an additional capacity of 2–4% indicates 
optimal additional value creation for a wind farm investor for currently planned offshore wind 
==PAGE:97==
6 Conclusion and recommendation 
83 
 
farm projects. Overplanting, however, is not a measure to decrease the LCoE in the Danish 
regulatory regime. 
Comparing the concept of overplanting from the perspective of a private investor in both 
countries, it can therefore be concluded that the impact of the regulatory regime is critical in 
terms of the possible LCoE reduction potential of optimal overplanting. In terms of economic 
value of the wind farm investment, however, it can be said that different aspects in the 
regulatory regimes also impact the revenue streams and thus the economic value of the wind 
farm investment. Additional value generation linked to overplanting in the Danish regulatory 
regime was found to be the additional earnings due to the regulation of the support scheme. 
The overall magnitude of additional value generated from overplanted turbines nevertheless 
also depends on the general economic viability of the offshore wind farm investments, which 
were estimated to be higher in the British market. Therefore the influences of the regulatory 
regime have to be evaluated in detail for specific cases in order to draw definite conclusions for 
measuring the impact of the regulatory regime on the economic benefits of applying 
overplanting. In the present thesis, optimal overplanting led to an additional increase in 
economic value of the respective offshore wind farm investment project in the range of 25 M€ in 
the British regulatory regime, whereas it corresponded to roughly 7 M€ in the Danish regulatory 
regime. 
Besides the effects for the private wind farm investor in Denmark, additional economic savings 
can be induced for the society when the added capacity is increased to 4–6% with respect to the 
transmission capacity limit, decreasing the LCoE by approximately 0.4% with optimal 
overplanting. Considering the total investment project from a society point of view, overplanting 
pledges similar benefits not only to the implementation in the United Kingdom, but is expected 
to do so also in other offshore wind energy markets.  
Furthermore, the country of Denmark constitutes a special case, in which overplanting can 
induce some additional financial benefits, which are related to the market remuneration of the 
offshore wind farm. Since the country is highly interconnected with neighbouring countries and 
generates a significant share of its electricity by wind power, the average electricity market price 
for wind energy tends to decrease on the electricity spot market, which increases the needed 
support payments to fill this gap up to the granted remuneration level. A further investigation 
found out that with more added capacity, slightly higher market remunerations per supplied 
MWh can be expected over the lifetime of the wind farm. Thereby the amount of support 
payments are lowered to some extent while also increasing the investor’s NPV slightly. This 
effect arises because slightly more energy of the overplanted capacity is produced during times 
of low wind, where the Danish spot market prices in average are higher, compared to the 
additional energy produced during times of prices valleys induced by high amounts of wind 
energy generation. It was therefore found out that the additionally generated energy during low 
wind can indeed increase the value of the wind farm investment slightly, but turned out to be 
lower than expected. This was explained by the reason that the total additional energy 
generation of the overplanted turbines is distributed more evenly over all wind speed categories 
than anticipated.
