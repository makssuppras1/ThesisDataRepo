Abstract
The tailoring of electromagnetic scattering from objects have long been of interest
when designing devices such as antennas and functional material platforms. This can
be done by overlapping resonant modes enhancing or diminishing the scattering in a
given direction.
We present a novel multimode resonator based on a cylindrical structure embedded
with surface impedances. A simplified 2D cylinder has been analyzed analytically
for the case of an incident plane wave and excitation from an infinite electric line
source. Expressions for the scattering of the cylinder is presented, given in the form
of cylindrical wave functions.
Based on the model two resonators have been designed and simulated using finite
element software. The resonators enhance the scattering in the forward direction by
combining 5 resonant modes (electric and magnetic dipole and quadrupole as well
as magnetic octopole). The impedance surfaces of the resonators were realised using
metal strips and patches. It was found that metal strips did not support higher order
modes than the electric quadrupole whereas patches did. The resonators were used
to design a simple multimode antenna system achieving a directivity of 10.5dB while
still being fairly compact in size ka = 1.23λ0 = 7.75rad.
A prototype of one of the resonators was build and its differential scattering area in the
forward direction was measured. Due to unknown material parameters the resonator
did not exhibit the desired scattering. Still, this work highlights the possibility of
building simple, strong scatterers given low-loss materials in the GHz range.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the field of electromagnetic wave interactions, strongly resonant particles have
emerged as versatile and intriguing elements, playing pivotal roles as both scattering
objects and in antenna design, due to their ability to manipulate incident electro-
magnetic waves. Recent breakthroughs at the Division for Electromagnetic Systems
(DES) at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) have showcased enhanced di-
rective behavior in a practical cylindrical impedance surface impregnated with water,
achieved through the excitation of specific resonant modes [1]. Building upon this
foundation, our work expands the repertoire of resonant modes and further refines
the utilization of surface impedance layers to augment scattering structures. The
culmination of these efforts will be the design of a simple antenna structure based on
a cylindrical resonator [2].
Combining the physical behaviour of a dielectric medium with surface impedance
sheets, it is possible to obtain high directivity or high scattering cross section by
balanced excitation of multiple resonant modes [1]. Extremely high directive antennas
and scatterers are often labeled as ”super-directive” antennas or ”super-scatterers”.
Super-directivity and super-scattering is loosely defined, but throughout this thesis
the definitions in the supplementary material of ref. [2] and ref. [7] will be used. In
essence, super-directivity is obtained if the structure has a higher directivity than
a dipole antenna of the same aperture size.
The same definition can be used for
super-scatterers, where the scattering cross section is higher than that of a magnetic
dipole.
The topics studied in this thesis will be used to design both a strong scattering
configuration and a directive antenna. Both have many different use cases in different
fields. Scattering technology has shown large potential in many fields, such as the
medical field with cancer treatment [8]. Using nanoparticles (10-1000 nm in diameter)
as scatterers has shown promise in enhancing the effectiveness of radiation therapy
in cancer treatment. They can be used to deliver radiation sensitizing agents directly
to tumor cells, increasing the sensitivity of cancer cells to radiation. Additionally,
nanoparticles can be designed to specifically target tumor cells, minimizing damage
to healthy tissue. This targeted approach has the potential to improve the therapeutic
outcome of radiation therapy while reducing side effects.
Antennas are used in everything from telecommunication, radar systems, radio astron-

CHAPTER 2
Multimode scatterers and antennas
In this chapter, we will give a short introduction to highly directive resonant scattering
configurations from the literature. We will give examples of different approaches to
controlling the scattering characteristics of an object with a focus on so-called surface
impedances.
Finally, we will give a short introduction to the topic of multipole
antennas and explain how resonant scattering configurations can be used in building
multipole antennas.
2.1
Tailoring electromagnetic scattering
The tailoring of electromagnetic scattering has been of great interest since the in-
vention of the RADAR. Much interest has been put into the study of reducing the
backward differential scattering cross section (also known as radar cross section), and
in the last 20 year the study of mantle cloaks has achieved much interest [10] [11] [12].
These are structures that reduce the electromagnetic scattering of an object in a given
direction. In recent times, the study of maximizing instead of minimizing the scatter-
ing of an object has also been investigated extensively. The two are part of a broader
research area of tailoring electromagnetic scattering of an object in specific directions.
This is often done by creating resonators where the resonant modes in the structure
are excited in a balanced way to create the desired scattering [13] [1].
2.1.1
Superscattering
Electrically small objects are often not very strong scatterers and the term ”super-
scattering” has been used of objects that scatter significantly more than would be
expected of their size. For small particles this is often given in terms of the single-
channel dipolar limit for the total scattering of spherical objects.
In 2D it reads:
Cmax,2D =
2λ
nπ, where λ is the wavelength and n is the refractive index of the sur-
rounding media. A similar limit of Cmax,3D = 3λ2
2nπ exists for the 3D case [6]. This
limit assumes that only a single resonant mode is contributing to the scattering of
the particle. However, by combining multiple resonant modes in a single structure
this limit can be overcome.
This is exactly what has been done in ref. [14]. A high permittivity scattering cylinder
has been studied and measured, showing clear indication of superscattering.

2.1.2
Enhancing scattering with surface impedances
One way of achieving superscattering that currently is receiving much interest is
through the use of surface impedances. Surface impedances are structures that cre-
ate an equivalent surface impedance relating the eletric field E on the surface to
an induced surface current density Je as: Zs = E/Je. The research subject of sur-
face impedances is related to that of meta-surfaces, and many different designs for
implementing them have been proposed. A common way of implementing surface
impedances when dealing with enhanced scattering is using periodic structures of
thin metal sheets. These sheets can be in the shape of horizontal or vertical strips,
patches, Jerusalem crosses and more exotic structures [17]. These designs are polariza-
tion dependent and are typically done for flat surfaces. However, they have recently
been extended to curved surfaces in ref. [10] and the effect of possible coupling be-
tween cascading multiple surfaces is investigated and modelled in ref. [18]. This paths
the way for more complicated structures using multiple layered surface impedances.
Design equations for simple impedance surfaces will be discussed in chapter 4.
Utilizing curved impedance surfaces ref. [1] showed that it is possible to obtain strong
scattering in both the forward and backwards direction for normal incidence of a
TEz polarized plane wave on a cylindrical structure. The structure uses water as
dielectric material and a copper pipe as a surface impedance. To achieve the desired
surface impedance the copper pipe had 4 vertical slits cut into it along the cylinder
axis. The structure is shown in figure 2.3a. Figure 2.3b shows the simulated and
measured scattering width and figure 2.3c shows the front to back ratio as a function
of frequency.

Multipole Antennas
Utilizing these types of multi-mode resonant structures as antennas have recently
been proposed by ref. [2] as a way of constructing highly directive, electrically small
antennas. Electrically small antennas are defined as being smaller than ka ≤1, where
k is the wavenumber and a is the radius of the smallest sphere encircling the antenna
[3]. In ref. [2] a theoretical infinitely long cylinder with multiple layers is considered.
Each layer has its own permittivity and the entire structure is excited by an infinite
magnetic line source (MLS) located outside the cylindrical structure as shown in
figure 2.5a. By exciting the resonant modes of the structure in a balanced way a
needle like radiation pattern can be achieved. The more modes excited, the higher
the directivity is as shown in figure 2.5b. These designs are however very delicate,
and the precision needed is often down to the fourth decimal place of geometrical and
material parameters such as radii and permittivity. Hence, this structure remains

Classically, designs using multiple modes have been referred to as Huygens sources.
These are antennas radiating as electric and magnetic dipoles at the same time en-

literature [19] [20] and the design paradigm is often used when designing electrically
small antennas. In ref. [4] an electrically small planar Huygens source was designed. It
is shown in figure 2.6b illustrating the space-minimizing nature of the design method.
The theoretical max directivity of a Huygens source is twice that of a dipole (4.77 dB)
since it only combines two resonant radiating modes. To overcome this, some designs
have started utilizing even more modes. An example of an electrically small antenna
utilizing multiple modes can be found in ref. [3]. Here two antennas are stacked on
top of each other, both operating as electric dipoles. In between is a small structure
that functions as a magnetic dipole creating a structure resonating as three resonant
modes at the same time. This is illustrated in figure 2.6a.

multimode antennas by having a single structure that supports all modes and not
combining multiple structures that each support a resonant mode. Using this method,
it achieves quite a directive radiation pattern as seen in figure 2.7b. The results are
found through full-wave simulation, but the structure was not build. Additionally,
how the meta-surfaces used in the design should be realized physically is not discussed.

5.2
Optimization algorithm
Finding a good solution to any given problem is always a diﬀicult task and can be
approached from many different angles.
The same can be said for our case.
We
wanted to design a structure as small as possible, but with the maximal number of
modes present in the structure. Given the complicated expressions for the fields this
could not be done analytically. Since we wanted to consider cylinders with as many as
five layers, the number of parameters were to great to simply brute force the problem
==PAGE:44==
5.2 Optimization algorithm
35
numerically. Instead a numerical optimization algorithm was settled on. The specific
algorithm we chose was the ”Particle Swarm Optimization” (PSO) algorithm.
The PSO algorithm is a population-based stochastic optimization algorithm inspired
by the social behavior of birds and fish. It was originally proposed by James Kennedy
and Russell Eberhart in 1995. PSO is commonly used to find solutions to optimization
problems in various fields, including engineering, finance, and machine learning, where
very large parameter spaces are considered.
The algorithm works by generating a population of potential solutions, also known
as a ”swarm”, that is then spreed across the search space randomly. Then, an ob-
jective function is evaluated for each particle based on its current position in the
search space. This determines the fitness of the particle at the given position. From
here the particles are moved around the search space based on their velocity. Each
particle adjusts its own velocity based on its own experience and the experience of
its neighbors. The velocity update is given by a combination of the particle’s current
velocity, its personal best position (the position at which it achieved the best fitness
so far), and the best position among its neighbors. The procedure can be described
by the following equation:
vi(t + 1) = w · vi(t) + c1 · r1 · (pi −xi(t)) + c2 · r2 · (pg −xi(t))
(5.1)
where,
• vi(t + 1) is the updated velocity of the particle i at time t + 1
• w is the inertia weight, that determines the contribution rate of a particle’s
previous velocity to its velocity at the current time step.
• c1 and c2 are positive acceleration constants that are used to level the contribu-
tion of the cognitive and social components respectively.
• pi is the personal best position of particle i in dimension j found from initial-
ization through time t.
• pg is the global best position of particle i in dimension j found from initialization
through time t
• xi(t) is the current position of particle i at time t
Using ref. [24] as inspiration, we were able to write a MATLAB code, that would
utilize equation 5.1.

5.4
Summary
An implementation of the equations from chapter 3 of the simplified infinitely long
cylinder have been created in Matlab. The model has been verified by comparison
with literature and simulations in Comsol Multiphysics. An implementation of the
optimization algorithm ”Particle Swarm Optimization” has been employed to find
cylindrical resonators with up to five layers optimizing forward scattering, total scat-

source. In general it was found that the use of impedances surfaces enhanced the scat-
tering and directivity of the resonators compared to without. However, the increases
in directivity were not as great for cylinders with radius bigger than rN < 0.2λ0 when
the line source was placed inside the cylinder.

7.1.1
Data processing
The data processing methods used in the measurements were the same as used in [1]
by Rasmus Elkjær Jacobsen. The Matlab script used for the data processing were
based on a script provided by Rasmus Elkjær Jacobsen.
Firstly, the method of subtraction and time gating were applied to the measurement
data to remove unwanted multiple reflections from other objects in the room (mainly
from the tripods holding the two antennas). In the subtraction method the room is
measured with and without the DUT and the two measurements are subtracted to
from each other, to remove the presence of the measurement room. In time gating
the measured S-parameters are transformed from the frequency domain to the time
domain. Here a window is applied to the part of the signal were the response of the
DUT appears, removing reflections of other objects in the room that appear at the
antennas at a different time.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Motivated by recent efforts at the Division for Electromagnetic Systems at the Tech-
nical University of Denmark a highly scattering cylindrical resonator has been investi-
gated. The resonator consisted of multiple layers of dielectric material each embedded
with a surface impedance. By overlapping multiple resonances in the resonator the
scattering capabilities could be tailored creating enhanced scattering in a given direc-
tion.
A simplified structure of an infinitely long cylinder has been analyzed under excitation
of a normal incident TMz or TEz polarized plane wave as well as for an infinite electric
line source, and expressions for the scattered field has been developed.
Using an
iterative approach the simplified cylinder has successfully been modelled in Matlab.
As part of this work, two novel ways of implementing impedance surfaces were tested:
metal strips and metal patches. The accuracy of design equations have been tested
and the difference between curved and flat implementations of the two structures have
been tested. We found that that use of curved strips did not support higher order
modes than m = 2 (magnetic quadrupole), which might be do to the impedance
changing significantly between higher order modes. We also found that the outer
radius of our cylindrical resonator greatly affected the number of resonant modes
that could be excited at a time.
Using a ”particle swarm optimization” algorithm and the Matlab model two designs
for cylindrical resonators maximizing the scattering in the forward direction for TMz
polarized plane wave incidence were designed around 3GHz. In general it was found
that the use of capacitive impedance surfaces were needed to enhance the scattering
for TMz polarized incidence, which agrees well with the previous literature. [6]
The two designs were tuned and simulated in the finite element modelling software
Comsol Multiphysics. The first design was found through simulation to excite five
resonant modes at a time (electric and magnetic dipole and quadrupole and magnetic
ocopole), whereas the second design with a smaller radius could excite four resonant
modes at a time (electric and magnetic dipole and quadrupole).
The possibility of using the cylindrical resonators to design highly directive antennas
were also investigated and an antenna system were designed based on one of the
scattering resonator designs. The system was shown to achieve directivities of 10.5dB
==PAGE:66==
8.1 Future work
57
with ka = 1.23λ0 = 7.74 rad and a realized gain of GRe = 6 = 7.78dB not accounting
for dielectric losses.
Finally, one of the resonators were build and the forward scattering of the resonator
was tested at the DTU ETC Scattering Measurement Facility. However, the assumed
permittivity and loss tangent of the Polyoxymethylen (POM) used as dielectric for the
cylinder was not what we expected. Hence, the build cylinder did only show partially
enhanced scattering when slightly modified. This was mainly due to the loss which
was much greater than previously taken into account. Good agreement were however
found between simulated and measured scattering when the permittivity and loss of
the material were modified from the expected values.
8.1
Future work
Since the fabricated enhanced scattering resonator did not work properly due to inac-
curate estimates of the material constants of POM at GHz frequencies, we suggest to
use a different more well understood material. Especially, low-loss materials such as
Teflon would be suitable or more expensive options such as the lowloss ceramic mate-
rial D36 from Ceramtec. Another option would be to use Rogers RO3006 laminate,
since the permittivity ϵr = 3.5 is close to the expected permittivity of POM ϵr = 3.7.
This material is however, usually supplied in sheets for printed circuit boards and not
cylindrical tubes, increasing the complexity of fabrication.
Finally, we suggest that you yourself measure the permittivity and loss of the material
when you design these types of resonators, to ensure accurate results. It is expected
that the designs supplied in this report would only need small modifications if the
materials used were changed to well documented low-loss materials even though the
permittivity might change quite significantly.
