Master of Science
GPU Ray Tracing for Virtual Reality
by Egill Ingi JACOBSEN
Virtual reality (VR) applications are notoriously demanding with regards to frame
budget. 11 ms frame budget is the standard. Rendering times above that can cause
a non-immersive or uncomfortable experience. This high performance demand is
a problem for ray tracing because of how computationally heavy it is to trace rays.
With the introduction of hardware accelerated ray tracing, in NVIDIAs Turing mi-
cro architecture, the highest achievable performance of ray tracing has increased
compared to the previous software accelerated methods. This thesis will show that
todays hardware accelerated ray tracing shows great promise that ray tracing will be
a possibility for virtual reality applications in the future. This is done with a simple
path tracer that only supports diffuse shading. The algorithms used are Tempo-
ral Anti-Aliasing and Stable Ray Tracing. It turns out that Temporal Anti-Aliasing
alone both introduces temporal stability and a great deal of denoising for renders
produced with only one ray per pixel. Stable Ray Tracing does not run fast enough
to support a 11 ms (90 FPS) frame budget for VR. Other than the algorithms used,
an important factor is optimization. The best render time was achieved by run-
ning one frame behind, i.e. start render the next frame before the current frame is
displayed, and by using CUDA-OpenGL interoperability to render directly to an
OpenGL buffer. Distortions due to lenses in all HMDs are handled in ray generation
instead of in post processing. This allows for rendering at native screen resolution
instead of at super resolutions due to re-sampling. Other algorithms that could con-
ceivably be used for virtual reality are Foveated rendering, Stereoscopic rendering,
and some version of todays machine learning based ray tracing algorithms. One
simple thing that can be done to increase performance is also to render each eye on
its own GPU. That could possibly come close to doubling the performance measured
by rendering on only one GPU. With one GPU the frame rates are only high enough
in limited cases to run smoothly in VR. Never the less performance measurements
show a great deal of promise that with further hardware development virtual reality
ray tracing will be possible in the future.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Ray tracing is considered to be the best rendering method when rendering realistic
images that should look like they were taken with a camera, and is the go to method
used in the ﬁlm industry when producing ﬁlms with computer generated graphics.
Even though ray tracing is a powerful rendering technique it is very computationally
heavy. When talking about virtual reality this is doubly so, because in virtual reality
one image is rendered for each eye. This means that ray tracing scenes in real time
for virtual reality is not something that has been possible. That is until NVIDIA
released a new micro architecture with ray tracing capabilities implemented into the
hardware. This hardware accelerated ray tracing means that ray tracing algorithms
are able to achieve a higher performance than ever before.
How does this apply to virtual reality? Is it possible to use hardware accelerated
ray tracing capabilities of modern GPUs to ray trace scenes in real time for virtual
reality? This thesis aims to ﬁnd that out, and to ﬁnd out what algorithms can be
used to make this a reality. To do this some simpliﬁcations will be made. Firstly,
all materials in the scene will be assumed to be diffuse. Secondly, to save some
performance the maximum number of light bounces will be set to one. This will of
course not produce shading with physically accurate materials, but it is enough to
perform an analysis of ray tracing for virtual reality.
In chapter 2 the beneﬁts of ray tracing will be explained and why it should be
used instead of rasterization. Chapter 3 explains inner workings of ray tracing and
how they are used to render an image of a scene. It also explains how high quality
backgrounds can easily be added without complicated geometry. Chapter 4 talks
about virtual reality, what it means for ray tracing and shortly why the algorithms
that will be described in chapter 5 were decided as a good ﬁt for virtual reality.
Chapter 6 discussed the GPU, especially what NVIDIAs OptiX ray tracing engine
is and what the Turing micro architecture is. Chapter 7 goes shortly into imple-
mentation details. Mainly the classes implemented in this thesis and the workﬂow
the framework goes through to render a single frame. The results from this the-
sis are presented in chapter 8. It discusses both the implemented algorithms and
other optimizations that were done to increase the performance as much as possi-
ble without sacriﬁcing quality. Finally chapter 9 discusses what could possibly be
done differently and what other algorithms show promise for a virtual reality ray
tracer, followed by the conclusion in chapter 10 where the results of this thesis are
summarized.

Chapter 2
Why Ray Tracing?
Today’s commercial virtual reality software relies on rasterization in rendering to
get a frame rate of 90 fps. In fact rasterization is the technique employed in most
software that relies on 3D graphics, such as computer games. This is because raster-
ization is much faster than ray tracing in most cases. It is a technique that maps
the scene geometry to pixels on the image plane. To determine the color of the
pixel, shading is performed on the scene geometry that maps to that pixel. Even
though rasterization is generally faster than ray tracing, it has its own weak points.
In rasterizing both shadows and reﬂections/refractions are approximated with var-
ious techniques, while in ray tracing they are accurately computed by solving the
rendering equation. Figure 2.1 show the difference in rasterized and ray traced re-
ﬂections. While a ray tracer catches all reﬂections the rasterizer has a problem with
interreﬂections, i.e. when an object mirrors parts of itself. The rasterizer used is
Luxions KeyVR. They also provided the Ferrari model for this comparison.
FIGURE 2.1: A scene of a Ferrari rendered with (left) Luxion’s KeyVR
rasterizer, and (right) a ray tracer. The images show the difference be-
tween the approximated reﬂections in the rasterizer, compared to the
accurate reﬂections the ray tracer produces. The difference between
the two are noticeable wherever the reﬂections hit the same object
again. The 3D model of the Ferrari was provided by Luxion.
Another drawback to rasterization is that it scales badly with increased model
complexity in the scene it is used to render. This means that rasterizers will see a
negative drop in performance when rendering more detailed scenes compared to
simpler scenes, i.e. the performance drops as the scene geometry contains more tri-
angles/primitives. For ray tracing the performance drop is negligible. Figure 2.2

Just as rasterization has it’s drawbacks, so does ray tracing. The biggest draw-
back for ray tracing is how computationally heavy it is. With recent advances in
GPU architecture the impact of this has lessened, since modern NVIDIA GPU’s are
capable of tracing rays on the scale of Giga (109) rays per second using hardware ac-
celerated ray tracing. Another drawback to ray tracing is that it’s performance varies
a lot with how much of the image plane the scene geometry takes. Scenes that take
up small space on the image plane will be able to be rendered faster than scenes that
==PAGE:19==
Chapter 2. Why Ray Tracing?
5
take up much space on the image plane. This is because when the geometry takes up
much space the total number of traced rays are higher compared to geometry that
takes up less space.
Another drawback to ray tracing is noise. During shading we are solving the
rendering equation. It can’t easily be done analytically, so we resort to Monte Carlo
techniques. These techniques work by sampling the rendering equation, and with-
out enough samples the results will contain a lot of noise. Using enough samples
to completely get rid of all noise is not something that can be done in real time,
but there exist some algorithms that can ﬁx this to a point. Even though this is a
drawback performance wise, it is actually not a drawback when considering that
the resulting images will be very close to what they would look like if taken in the
physical world. Hopefully by employing the appropriate algorithms the noise can
be balanced out while retaining the accuracy of the renders.
With both rasterization and ray tracing having their own advantages and disad-
vantages, rasterization is the preferred technique for real-time rendering where the
frame budget is strict; while ray tracing is the preferred technique when quality is
more important than rendering time. This thesis takes a look at ray tracing, since
it is the rendering technique that produces physically accurate renders, and aims to
ﬁnd out if it can perform fast enough for real-time rendering in virtual reality.

Reﬂectance
Different materials reﬂect light in different ways. For a photo realistic rendered im-
age it is important that the iterative ray generation in a path tracer generates rays
in a new direction that are determined based on how the material actually reﬂects
light. There are mainly three different types of reﬂectance. Firstly, there is Diffuse
reﬂectance where the surface reﬂects light equally in all directions on a hemisphere
that originates at the surface and is oriented to the surface normal. Secondly, there
is Specular reﬂectance where the surface is a perfect mirror and therefore only reﬂects
the light in a single direction that is dependent on the angle of incidence and the
surface normal. Lastly, there is Glossy reﬂectance which is sort of a mixture of the
other two. The light is reﬂected in many directions that are distributed around the
direction of perfect reﬂection (specular reﬂection). This is illustrated on ﬁgure 3.5.

As described in chapter above, some of the algorithms used in the framework rely
on generating rays from some statistical distribution. To fully represent the distribu-

image will be noisy. Lets take as an example a diffuse surface. It should reﬂect all
light equally in all directions on a hemisphere aligned with the surface normal. To
represent this when a ray bounces from a diffuse surface, the ray direction is gener-
ated by sampling a hemisphere that is aligned with the surface normal at the point

4.1
What is Virtual Reality?
Virtual Reality is achieved by immersing a person in a virtual experience that is
very close to what that same experience would be like in the actual, real world. The
main sense engaged to create this virtual experience is the human visual system,
i.e. the eyes. This is done with a Head Mounted Device (HMD), also known as a
Virtual Reality headset. The HMD consists of two screens that are used to display
the virtual experience. Each screen displays the content as it would be seen by the
left eye and the right eye, respectively. In front of the screens are lenses the user
looks through. These allow the human eyes to focus on their respective screen that
are otherwise physically very close to the eye. Along with this optical system, the
HMD contains numerous sensors that are used to track every movement the user
makes and thereby orient the user in the virtual world. Figure 4.1 shows a schematic
of the basic building blocks of a HMD.
4.2

4.3
Performance Criteria
The human eye is a very sensitive organ, meaning that the images they look at in
a HMD need to be of very high quality, and artifacts such as noise and aliasing
in the displayed images would degrade the immersion of the experience. A high
quality/resolution is required such that the user can’t distinguish individual pixels.
This is handled with the resolution of the HMD screens. The HTC Vive has a per
eye display resolution of 1080 × 1200 for a total of 2160 × 1200 pixel resolution. The
Oculus Rift CV1 has the same screen resolution as the Vive, but the newer Oculus
Rift S has a resolution of 2560 × 1440. The pixel density is not the only important
aspect of performance. The frame rate is equally as important. Frame rate is the rate
at which new frames are displayed on the HMD screens and is measured in frames
per second (FPS). If the frame rate is too low, the user will notice since the screen
will appear to ﬂicker when it is updated with new content. This ﬂicker happens
because there is a jump in the direction and/or position the user is looking in from
one frame to the next. If this jump is to big the user will perceive that as unnatural
and can cause discomfort, just imagine moving your head and only perceiving the
change in scenery after the movement is done. In general with a frame rate of 90 FPS
in modern VR headsets this ﬂicker effect is no longer relevant[6].
Another important performance criteria is temporal stability.
Temporal stability
refers to the quality of displayed images being stable over time, i.e. the quality each
displayed image should be the same. This quality could be measured using a num-
ber of metrics. The noise level, and distribution in the image needs to be similar.
The aliasing level in the image also needs to remain constant. This can both refer to
the use of anti-aliasing to get rid of jagged edges, and it can also refer to temporal
anti-aliasing. Temporal aliasing artifacts can for instance cause pixels to ﬂicker in
a moving object due to them not being mapped to the same relative pixel between
frames. With relative pixel it is meant relative to the object it belongs to, not the
the entire scene. This type of ﬂickering pixels is very apparent in virtual reality and
shows up in edges that are still looking like they are moving, and noise crawling
around the surface it belongs it. Luckily the temproal stability of renders can be im-
proved. In this thesis the use of Temporal-Anti Aliasing and Stable Ray Tracing will
be explored.
4.4
Ray Tracing
As stated in chapter 3, ray tracing is a computationally heavy process. That is even
more true if ray tracing is to be used to render real-time images of 3D scenes for
virtual reality. Just to render a single frame at the native resolution for the Oculus
Rift CV1 a total of 2 · (1080 × 1200) = 2.592.000 rays needs to be traced, and that is
just for the most basic ray tracing algorithm. For a progressive path tracer, like the
one described in chapter 3.2.4, that aggregates n sub-frames, a total of 2.592.000 · n
is the minimum number of rays that are traced and that is not including shadow

requires a maximum of 2 · (resx × resy) · 2n(1 + r) rays, where r is the number of
reﬂective bounces, n is the number of camera rays traced per pixel, and the factor of
2 rises from the fact that every ray that hits geometry traces another ray to determine

occlusion. Simply said the number of rays that are traced for a single frame rises very
quickly, and the render time rises with it. All of this needs to be done within 11ms
if a frame rate of 90 FPS is to be reached, and that is not taking into account the
time it takes to display the renders on the HMD. According to NVIDIA their new
Turing microarchitecture is capable of tracing up to 10 Giga (109) rays per second
in their high-end GPUs. That means that for a single frame at 90 FPS (11ms) the
architecture can theoretically trace up to 111.111.111 ray. To put it into the scope of
a real example, a path tracer with one ray bounce (10.368.000n rays per frame) can
theoretically support up to 10 progressive passes. All of this only applies if the time
it takes to post-process and display a frame is negligible with regards to the 11ms
available, which in reality it is not.
One way to speed up ray tracing is to reduce the number of rays that are traced.
For virtual reality there are a few different ways the ray count can be reduced. One
of them is to implement the lens distortion directly into the ray generation from the
eye cameras, and then render the eyes at the native resolution of the HMD screens.
Generally the distortion is an image to image pass that takes place after rendering.
Because of this re-sampling it is recommended to render at greater resolutions than
the screens actually are to avoid reduced quality in the distorted image.
If the
distortion model is implemented as a part of the camera ray generation, this image
to image distortion pass is avoided, and the scene can be rendered at the native
resolution of the eye screens. Another way to reduce the ray count is with some sort
of foveated rendering, i.e. rendering the areas in the periphery at a lower resolution
than the area in the center of the ﬁeld of view. It is also possible to reduce the total
ray count by up to 15-20% by avoiding the tracing of rays in areas that are actually
never visible to the user in the HMD [7].
When reﬂections and other sampling techniques that are based on statistical behav-
ior are used in tracing the noise level of an image rises with the introduction of what
is called ﬁreﬂies, i.e. very bright pixels that occur due to how reﬂection rays are gen-
erated. In the end the rendered image will converge and the ﬁreﬂies will be gone,
but that takes too long time for real-time rendering. One way to reduce this time is
to render a scene with relatively few progressive passes. The render will be noisy
and not usable to display in VR. That is, until the noise in the render is removed with
denoising ﬁlters. The time it takes to render and display a single frame is then
Path Tracing time + De-Noising time + Display time
(4.3)
The Display time is independent of the actual rendering time and comes down to
the API that is used to interact with the HMD. The Path Tracing time and De-noising
time are variables that can be minimized by employing the proper algorithms and
ﬁlters. The two are loosely linked together since the path tracing time can be shorter
because of the de-noising pass, and the complexity of the de-noising ﬁlter required
is dependent on the noise that is present in the rendered image. In general the De-
Noising term can be substituted with a wider Post-Processing term. All of this means
that in order to get the best quality within the limited frame budget the correct mix-
ture of path tracing and post-processing needs to be used. The longer the post-
processing takes, the less time can be allocated for path tracing, i.e. the less quality
render can be produced with path tracing.

The ray generation described in chapter 3.2.1 assumes the projection it uses is sym-

are stored in matrix format. There is a matrix that contains the camera transforma-

u

p]

There is also a matrix that stores the camera projection, P. From the second diagonal
element of the projection matrix the ﬁeld of view, and camera constant can be calcu-
lated. This the projection matrix is asymmetric, and some additional steps need to
be taken to use it for a ray generation camera. This is done by translating the origin
of the image planes for each eye, while keeping all other image plane parameters.

Chapter 5
Algorithms
This chapter describes the algorithms that were chosen for this thesis. These algo-
rithms were chosen because they should increase temporal stability of renders. As
an extra beneﬁt they also help with a little denoising by introducing blur instead
of noise. By no means is this an exhaustive list of algorithms that could be a good
choice for virtual reality.
5.1
Temporal Anti-Aliasing
In Virtual Reality temporal stability is very important to maintain an immersive ex-
perience for the user. Temporal instability shows up in a few different ways. The
noise in the renders looks like it is crawling around the surfaces it lies on, and edges
that should be completely still look like they are ﬂickering or moving slightly around
the location where they are actually located. This can be avoided with ﬁlters that
improve temporal stability, also known as Temporal Anti-Aliasing. Temporal Anti-
Aliasing works by storing the ray hit information and pixel colors from the current
frame, and one frame backwards in time. Temporal reprojection is then used to de-
termine what pixels are valid for the new frame. The valid pixels are used along
with newly shaded pixels in an exponential moving average. Before averaging is
done the color from the pixel color from the last frame is clamped to a bounding box
that is determined from the neighbourhood of the pixel in the newly shaded frame.
This is a technique described by Karis [8]. The bounding box used for clamping is
created with a variance based approach as Patney et al. use in their foveated ren-
derer[9]. The reprojection technique is described in chapter 5.3. Listing 5.1 shows a
step-by-step algorithm for the temporal anti-aliasing process.

5.2
Stable Ray Tracing
To increase the temporal stability of the rendered images a few different tech-
niques can be used. Temporal Anti-Aliasing (chapter 5.1) can be added to the post-
processing step to reduce anti-aliasing artifacts caused by temporal stability. Tempo-
ral Anti-Aliasing will also smooth out some of the noise in the images. Another tech-
nique that can be used is Stable Ray Tracing. Stable Ray Tracing [10] is a technique
that is incorporated directly into the ray tracing/path tracing pass. It is centered
around reusing as many samples as possible from other renders. This is done with
a few passes. The ﬁrst cycles through previously shaded samples, reprojects them
to the new image plane, and determines what samples can be used, and where new
samples need to be added. The next step traces and shades rays for newly gener-
ated samples and veriﬁes if old samples are still visible from the new point of view.
The old samples that are now occluded are replaced with new samples belonging to
the occlusion surface. After the veriﬁcation of old samples and the shading of new
ones, the ﬁnal render can be reconstructed using a 3x3 truncated Spatial Gaussian
ﬁlter. Listing 5.2 shows a step-by-step algorithm for the stable ray tracing process,
and chapter 5.3 explains the reprojection technique used, both for stable ray tracing
and temporal anti-aliasing.

Reprojection
Reprojection is taking samples shaded from the view point of one camera, and de-
termining if they are possibly visible on the image plane of another camera. For this
to be possible it is not enough to only store the shade of the samples. The hit posi-
tion also needs to be readily available. Reprojection is then done by creating a ray
from the new camera to the stored hit position of the sample. Using that ray it is
easy to determine if and where it intersects the new image plane. It is as simple as
performing a ray-plane intersection. Equation 5.1 describes a point on a plane with
it’s center at a and principle axes B and T, also known as the Binormal and Tangent.
β and γ determine how far along these axes the point is.
p = a + βB + γT

solve becomes
o + t ˆd = a + βB + γT
(5.2)

plane, with their lengths included, then β and γ become the screen space coordinates

6.1
OptiX: A Ray Tracing engine
OptiX is a Ray Tracing Engine developed by NVIDIA with the aim of providing
its users access to accelerated ray tracing on the GPU, without requiring domain-
speciﬁc knowledge of how it is done, i.e. the user doesn’t need to know how to use
the GPU to accelerate ray tracing and can therefore instead focus on the ray tracing
algorithms themselves instead of how to run them. It does so by providing the user
with a programmable Ray Tracing pipeline that is accelerated on the GPU. OptiX is
highly scalable, i.e. is optimized for current and future NVIDIA GPU architectures
with support for multiple GPUs in a single program. The main functionality that
OptiX provides is:
• A set of functions to build and compress Acceleration Structures on the GPU.
• The creation of an OptiX device Context from a CUDA context associated with
the device. A device context manages a single GPU.
• The creation of an OptiX program pipeline by linking a set OptiX Program
Groups. The program groups consists of one or more OptiX Modules. A module
contains device side programs compiled into PTX, (more details below).
• The creation of an Optix Shader Binding Table that is an array of records that
holds information about the location of OptiX programs, and the parameters
they need, (more details below).
Figure 6.1 shows the workﬂow managed by OptiX.
6.1.1
OptiX Program Groups
There are a few different types of program that can be speciﬁed in OptiX. Each of
these have a trigger within the traversal of acceleration structure. The possible pro-
gram types are Ray Generation, Intersection, Any-Hit, Closest-Hit, Miss, Direct callable,
Continuation callable and Exception.
• Ray Generation programs are the entry point when a Ray Tracing pipeline is
launched. In these kind of programs rays should be generated dependent on
the indices of the pixel currently being processed.
• Intersection programs allow the user to implement ray intersection with any
primitive they want. Ray-Triangle intersection is the only one built into OptiX,
and the one used if no custom intersection program is provided.

• Any-Hit programs are invoked, during traversal of the acceleration structure,

OptiX Shader Binding Table
The Shader Binding Table is an array of records. Each record contains a header
block, and a data block. The header is not accessible by the application, but is used
during traversal to decide what program should be invoked in each situation. The
data block contains arbitrary program-speciﬁc information that the program needs
at runtime. For instance the data block can contain information about the material
each primitive is made out of.
6.2
The Turing micro architecture
In september 2018 NVIDIA launched a new generation of GPUs that used a new
micro architecture they call Turing[12]. The Turing micro architecture had a few
new features that it’s predecessor, Pascal, did not have. It introduced RT cores and
Tensor cores.
RT cores, or Ray Tracing cores, are designed for fast traversal of Bounding Volume
Hierarchies (BVH), and for fast computations of ray-triangle intersections. A BVH
is a data structure a scene can be stored in. Ray tracing is performed by traversing a
BVH and ﬁnding out exactly what triangle the ray intersects with ray-triangle inter-
sections. This means that ray tracing is directly implemented in the hardware of the
GPU and can be called hardware accelerated ray tracing. Hardware accelerated ray
tracing is capable of tracing many more rays than previous software accelerations
can.
Tensor cores are designed for advanced machine learning such as deep learning. In
the case of ray tracing they can be used to further speed up the rendering process
by using them for de-noising. This allows the ray tracing process to trace fewer rays
and produce a noisy image. Then by de-noising it using perhaps neural networks
the noise can be removed to produce a good quality ﬁnal render. The neural network
is run on the Tensor cores.
Using the RT and Tensor cores to accelerate rendering, Real-Time Ray Tracing is
something that has suddenly become conceivable. The Turing GPU architecture is
the ﬁrst to implement RT cores and has relatively few of them. If further advances in
GPU architecture would increase this amount the performance of ray tracing would
perhaps get even better. NVIDIA is already working on the successor of the Tur-
ing, called Ampere. It will be exiting to see if that micro architecture has even more
improvements for ray tracing. If more RT cores have been added perhaps the soft-
ware developed in this thesis will run at higher performance without changing or
optimizing any code as long as it is supported by OptiX 7.

Device Side Programs

are the same for both instances. What changes is the ray generation programs. For a
single GPU system, the ray generation programs needs to generate and start tracing

ray generation program should only generate and start tracing of rays for a single
camera (one GPU for the left eye, and the other for the right eye). This was done
by writing two different ray generation programs, and a third program that actu-
ally creates a ray dependent on a camera and the pixel currently being traced for.
The two ray generation programs serve as wrappers, and use the third program to
actually trace rays. For a single GPU the third program is called separately for the
left and the right eye cameras per pixel. For two GPUs the third program is only
called once per pixel per GPU, using the camera that is active on each GPU respec-
tively. The GPUPathTracer class ensures that the pipeline contains the appropriate
ray generation program dependent on the system properties.

Holdout Geometry
The holdout plane presented in chapter 3.4 is a method that allows a 3D object in
the scene to appear to interact with an environment map used as a background.
The plane is placed under the object in the scene and needs to be big enough such
that the entire occlusion the object creates is captured within it. For the framework
developed in this thesis the holdout plane is implemented as a parametric plane.
Meaning it is not a collection of triangles like the rest of the scene geometry. It is a
plane described by a mathematical equation. Using OptiX this was implemented in
two different ways:
==PAGE:50==
36
Chapter 7. Implementation
1. Deﬁne the holdout plane as a custom primitive in OptiX. This means the frame-
work needs to support multiple types of primitives, which results in a more
complicated acceleration structure that is traversed for each ray. That in turn
can have negative impact on the frame rate.
2. Deﬁne the holdout plane in the miss program. That way an intersection with
the holdout plane is only checked if a ray doesn’t intersect any scene geometry.
The plane is not a part of an acceleration structure, meaning the framework
only needs to support triangle primitives. That results in a simple acceleration
structure.

rays are traced. That is very computationally heavy and has a big negative impact
on performance. It also adds a great deal of noise to the render.

9.2.1
Virtual Reality
As it is now, when using virtual reality, Vsync is enforced. This is done by only up-
dating the displays every 11ms to lock a maximum frame rate of 90f ps. In OpenVR
the function used to get updated HMD positions (WaitGetPoses) actually waits idly
until a few milliseconds before Vsync. This wait tanks the achievable frame rate un-
less it is run asynchronously. This is partly dealt with by using parallel rendering.
Perhaps a better way would be with multi threading, having a separate thread that
update HMD positions. The last updated position can then be accessed in the thread
that updates the cameras before ray tracing is launched.
Due to the lenses inside a HMD the entire render is not visible. In fact the area that
is visible to the user is a circular area around the middle of the render. A stated in
chapter 4 the performance of the path tracer can be increased by up to 15-20 % by
only rendering the area of the rendered images that are actually visible through the
lenses.

9.3. Other Algorithms
45
9.2.2
Stable Ray Tracing
As presented in chapter 8, Stable Ray Tracing does not run fast enough to be a viable
choice for virtual reality. Perhaps with some optimizations the performance could
be increased such that the algorithm would become a better choice in the future.
One conceivable optimization is how occupied samples are stored. At the moment
all samples are checked to see if they are occupied. This means that there is a lot
of redundant computations done on empty samples, especially if the target sample
density is relatively low compared the maximum possible sample density. One way
to around this would be to store an array that would contain what samples are oc-
cupied. Then instead of checking every sample for occupancy, calculations are only
done on the samples that are in that array.
9.3
Other Algorithms
The algorithms implemented in this thesis are by far not the only ones that could
be useful for virtual reality. Some other algorithms show a lot of promise, but are
reported to run at lower speeds than is required for virtual reality. These algorithms
make use of neural networks in some way to decide where to focus the processing
power for ray tracing.
9.3.1
Foveated Rendering
Foveated rendering is a rendering technique where images are rendered with pro-
gressively less detail away from the point of focus. This is done by varying the
amount of samples/paths that are traced for each pixel. The amount of paths de-
pends on how far the pixel is away from the center of the image. Choosing the
amount of paths per pixel is a non trivial task. For virtual reality one way would be
to have two distinct regions with their own path counts. The ﬁrst region is the one
that is in focus, i.e. the area around the center of the image that the user is looking
directly at in the HMD. The second region is the periphery. The periphery vision in
humans is not very detail oriented and therefore the periphery could be rendered
with a lower path count than the rest of the image. One still has to be mindful that
the periphery vision is very good at detecting motion. That means that even though
it can be rendered at a lower path count, it still needs to be temporally stable.
9.3.2
Stereoscopic Rendering
In virtual reality the two eyes are very close to each other. This means that both eyes
are looking at the same geometry with some minor changes due to change in view
point, and change in view dependent shading. Because of this much of the shading
data from one eye can be reused for the other eye. This is called stereoscopic render-
ing and is described by Fung et al. [13]. This technique should save some rendered
rays and therefore increase the performance of the path tracer in virtual reality. This
is similar to Stable Ray Tracing, and perhaps Stable Ray Tracing could be used for
this. Instead of using temporal data for reprojection it would use stereoscopic data.
==PAGE:60==
46
Chapter 9. Discussion
9.3.3
Temporal Anti Aliasing with Adaptive Sampling
Temporal Anti Aliasing with Adaptive Sampling is a technique described by Marrs
et al. [14] [15] that extends temporal antialiasing with adaptive ray tracing to im-
prove artifacts related with temporal antialiasing and achieve a better quality than
previously possible within a 16ms frame budget. 16ms is too much for virtual re-
ality, but this could possibly be lowered with further hardware development. The
technique described in the paper is a mixture between rasterization and ray trac-
ing. Perhaps this could be adapted for pure ray tracing, where more samples are
allocated around areas where temporal antialisaing would otherwise fail or produce
low quality.
9.3.4
Neural Temporal Adaptive Sampling and Denoising
Neural Temporal Adaptive Sampling and Denoising is a technique developed by
Hasselgren et al. at NVIDIA [16]. It relies on a neural network that estimates a
sample map to determine how many paths should be traced for each pixel. After
using that in rendering the image is denoised using another neural network. It also
contains a recurrent feedback loop where temporal reprojection takes place and is
used as input to the sample map generation for the next frame. The technique is
reported to be able to run at a 14ms frame budget when tuned for performance.
9.3.5
OptiX De-Noiser
OptiX has a built in denoisers that can reconstruct an image from very few samples.
It does so with the use of neural networks. At the moment this denoiser does not run
fast enough for real time rendering in virtual reality. With further advances in this
technology along with further advances in GPU architecture it is entirely possible
that this denoiser will become fast enough to use for virtual reality.
9.4
DirectX Ray Tracing
With the introduction of real time ray tracing using RT cores on the GPU a hybrid
between rasterization and ray tracing was born. This is commonly known as DirectX
Ray Tracing, or DXR. DXR allows rasterization shaders to cast rays. This is very
useful to get the best of both worlds. The speed of rasterization can be achieved with
the photo realistic shadows, reﬂections, and/or global illumination of ray tracing. Of
course the more work done in ray tracing, the lower the performance will be. Until
hardware accelerated ray tracing has reached the point where it is fast enough to
render scenes in real time at 90 fps, perhaps this hybrid between rasterization and
ray tracing can be used to get a part of the way towards fully ray traced scenes.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
As presented in this paper ray tracing can be used to deal with the shortcomings of
rasterization. Ray tracing is more computationally heavy and is therefore not the
preferred rendering technique for real time applications. With the introduction of
RT cores in the new generation of NVIDIA graphics architecture, ray tracing per-
formance has gotten a big boost. Even to the point where it can be considered for
some real time applications. Virtual Reality applications are demanding. They re-
quire real time rendering and should run at 90 fps. That means the rendering of a
single frame should take no more than 11 ms. To meet this high performance de-
mand todays virtual reality applications use rasterization for rendering. As stated
in this thesis rasterization has a few drawback that makes the ﬁnal renders approx-
imations of what they are in the real world. Ray tracing ﬁxes that since it revolves
around solving the rendering equations, that is a physical description of how light
interacts with objects. Ray tracing is much more performance heavy compared to
rasterization. With the use of modern graphics hardware (NVIDIA’s RTX graphics
cards) and the use of the proper algorithms ray tracing can in some cases achieve
the performance that virtual reality demands. It comes down to how much of the
screen the scene geometry ﬁlls, but the more it ﬁlls out the screen the less perfor-
mance is achievable. By going from a GTX type graphics card that doesn’t support
hardware accelerated ray tracing, to a RTX graphics card that does support it, the
performance of the ray tracer developed for this thesis saw a 5 times rise in perfor-
mance. It has also been shown that hardware accelerated ray tracing scales much
better with increased triangle count compared to regular ray tracing.
Even though performance is important for virtual reality, it isn’t everything. The
quality of the produced renders is also important. Ray tracing carries with it consid-
erable noise when rendered with few rays. This stems from the sampling of the ren-
dering equation with Monte Carlo integration. The noise can be lowered by tracing
many rays for each pixel, but that has a big negative effect on performance. This the-
sis has shown that Temporal Anti-Aliasing goes a long way to smooth out the noise
even though it is not a dedicated denoising ﬁlter. It also removes some anti-aliasing
artifacts, and improves temporal stability which is important for virtual reality.
Stable Ray Tracing could be good algorithm to employ in virtual reality applications.
It ensures temporal stability and tries to lessen the work load by reusing shading
calculations where it can. Right now this algorithm does not run fast enough for real
time ray tracing in virtual reality. Perhaps the algorithm can be optimized such that
it doesn’t need to process a lot of empty samples.
Every little bit of optimization is important to maximize the achievable frame rate.
This thesis has shown that rendering on the GPU should be done with CUDA-
OpenGL interoperability to minimize the time it takes to display a render after it
is done. It should take advantage of parallelism and asyncronous actions wherever
==PAGE:62==
48
Chapter 10. Conclusion
possible to avoid idle time on both the CPU and GPU. For virtual reality, lens distor-
tions should be dealt with during ray generation to minimize the resolution of each
render.
All in all the results show that ray tracing in virtual reality is a possibility by using
the appropriate hardware and algorithms. Even though it isn’t quite up to the task
today, it should be with further improvements in hardware accelerated ray tracing.
The graphics cards industry is constantly evolving, and even now it is expected that
NVIDIA will release a new generation of graphics cards that will perform better with
ray tracing in mind. With such a development a simple virtual reality ray tracer
should deﬁnitely be possible. With even further improvements a more powerful
ray tracer that supports multiple types of materials will hopefully be able to run in
the 11 ms frame budget that virtual reality demands. Most ray tracing algorithms
and techniques developed today are not done so with virtual reality in mind. They
can achieve high qualities at limited frame budgets at speeds that are applicable to
many games today. The frame budget of virtual reality is even more strict. Instead
of taking a technique and simply using it for virtual reality, perhaps the best way
would be to develop a new one with virtual reality in mind.
Another development that needs to be accounted for is the development of virtual
reality hardware. Some virtual reality manufacturers have started to drop the frame
rate requirements a little bit below 90 fps. For instance the Oculus Rift S has 80 Hz
displays, meaning that it can display at most 80 frames per second. For rendering
that means the frame budget goes from 11 ms to 12.5 ms. Allowing for a little bit of
extra time during rendering.
