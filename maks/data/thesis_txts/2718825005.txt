Abstract
This thesis proposes a methodology for migrating a software system implementing a remote
procedure call (RPC) framework. The migration of an RPC-based system is often motivated
by an evolving environment. The migration process can be complex and time-consuming,
and we therefore believe that the development of a methodology can simplify and guide
the process.
On the basis of an empirical study of existing literature and our case study, we address
the major challenges of migrating RPC-based systems, such as preserving business logic
and automating parts of the migration process. Throughout the chapters of this thesis,
different challenges are addressed and concrete solutions are presented. Furthermore, we
developed migration software to automate parts of the migration process.
The developed methodology solves the specific challenges of constraint and requirement
elicitation to select the correct framework, migration strategy, need for preprocessing and
automation for specific parts of the migration. The presented methodology should reduce
the risks and resources required to migrate an RPC-based system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Many modern day applications use a client-server architecture to facilitate communication
over a network. This communication may be facilitated by a framework or third party
technology, and in the world of computer science, these are constantly evolving. One
technology facilitating communication over a network is the remote procedure call protocol
(RPC).
As the development of a software system has been completed, it is used by clients and enters
the maintenance phase. This phase spans from the first operation to the replacement of
the software system[1]. In the maintenance phase, the software may need to be maintained
through patches due to errors, or meet the needs of an evolving environment, where the
technology used for its implementation has to be manipulated.
However, RPC-based systems currently in the maintenance phase rely on technologies or
frameworks that are no longer supported, necessitating software migration to meet the
demands of an evolving business environment. Businesses and developers therefore need to
both be willing and able to adapt. These systems need to either replace the framework,
rebuild the system from scratch or be uncompetitive. Both replacing the framework and
rebuilding from scratch are, however, resource-intensive tasks, that require a lot of domain
knowledge of the system. Stakeholders for these systems must therefore carefully consider
whether the new functionality is needed.
Although some legacy systems are better served migrating to other architectural styles,
such as Representational state transfer (REST), others have found that their use cases
are best solved with RPC. A priority of certain systems may for instance be processing
efficiency or security, which are better achieved in certain RPC implementations compared
to REST.
For organizations wishing to preserve the implementation of an RPC protocol, but wish
to adopt new functionality, it is important to be able to simplify the migration of their
existing RPC-based system. Currently, no methodology or approach exists to map out the
challenges of performing this migration, which means that organizations do not have an

accurate way to conduct it.
Academic interest within the field of software maintenance of the client-server architecture
has increased. Papers discussing migration from SOAP to REST[2] and from REST to
GraphQL[3] have emerged. These articles and papers all discuss the benefits and challenges
of the specific migrations, but lack a description of the methodology used to provide
reusability for other software developers.
Therefore, in this thesis we propose a methodology to reduce the risks and resources
required to migrate an RPC-based system.
1.1
Research Methodology
Conducting a scientific research project in the field of software engineering and computer
science, a methodology for the project should be adopted. In this thesis we follow that of
design science research, which specifies how to conduct a research project and what type
of results the researcher(s) should hope to find.
A method framework is proposed in the concept of design science, which lays the foundation
of our approach to design, research strategies and construction of artifacts for the project.
The creation of artifacts aim to solve a practical problem through a case study with the
company cBrain. The primary artifact for this thesis is the proposed methodology for
developers to migrate RPC-based systems.
Following the principles of design science, we broke our work into the different phases. As
a first step, the problem under research is to be explicated. In this phase, the practice of
the problem, as experienced by stakeholders is investigated, where the impact and severity
of the problem is considered to reason about the importance of the problem. To explain
and guide the problem, we designed a set of research questions as a guideline to scope the
project to a reasonable solution space.
An empirical study is furthermore conducted, as a part of explicating the problem, where
related work is reviewed to learn about existing solutions and challenges, to justify the
relevance and feasibility of the project. The ability to generalize the problem beyond a few
stakeholders is also considered in this phase[4].
By being able to identify the sources of the problem that may not be addressed by existing
literature, we can put forward a set of requirements for the artifact to be able to solve the
problem for the stakeholders.
The second phase is defining requirements. The requirements specify the type of artifacts
to build and the characteristics of it. Each requirement for the artifact should be justified
with its need and relation to the problem. In this phase, it is important to be realistic
in terms of likelihood of being able to develop all the requirements for the artifact. The
requirements can be derived from literature and stakeholders, which makes it important to
clarify the sources when designing the requirements and how those requirements came to
be[4].

The third phase is when the development of the artifact begins. This artifact should
address the explicated problem and the requirements from the previous phase should be
fulfilled. The methods, functionality and construction of components for the artifact, are
described and justified in this phase. Each component is properly explained to understand
its relevance, use and the requirement it addresses. The intended use of the artifact and
its components are explained to learn about the originality and difference from existing
artifacts and solutions[4].
Furthermore, a description is provided of the literature and stakeholders whom have
contributed or inspired to the design of components of the artifact. Finally, we will describe
how we designed and developed the artifact, in particular the study of stakeholders, existing
solutions and researched literature[4].
Having developed an artifact, the next step is to demonstrate it. In this phase, it is
particularly important to demonstrate how the artifact can be used to address the explicated
problem[4]. A representative case is selected to demonstrate the artifact’s use and need to
solve the explicated problem. A justification for the choice of case is given to discuss its
relevance for the problem to make sure it is sufficiently challenging as a testbed1[4]. The
specific components of the artifact used in the test should be clear, such that a fitting
amount is properly tested for the demonstration[4].
Finally, the artifact, alongside each requirement must be carefully evaluated. This evaluation
is not of the artifact alone, but to what extent each component addresses the problem[4].
As a continuation of the five phases of design science, we conclude with a discussion of
future development options for the project.
1.2
Contribution
This thesis will focus on developing and documenting an artifact in the form of a method-
ology for migrating RPC-based software solutions. This methodology should ensure that
outdated or unsupported RPC frameworks do not hinder further development of the system.
This will help developers who do not have the time or resources to be bogged down in the
intricacies of migrating the specific framework, as new technologies that solve many of
the existing framework problems arise. On the basis of this problem definition, we have
formulated a problem statement:
PS Developers with older RPC-based systems want to migrate to newer frameworks, but
the process is both time and resource intensive
From this problem statement, we constructed the hypothesis:
H1 Developers can simplify the migration of RPC-based systems by adopting a methodol-
ogy.
To prove the hypothesis, we aim to find an answer to the following research questions:

RQ1 What are the major challenges in the migration of RPC-based software systems?

In order to better understand this problem, we will be conducting a case study with cBrain,
whom are experiencing this problem. It affects their main product F2 which is a document

service (DMS). Both solutions utilize an ASP.NET Web Service framework, which limits

The Remote Procedure Call Protocol
This section covers the basic concepts of the Remote Procedure Call (RPC) protocol
introduced in 1983 by Birrell and Nelsen[5].
RPC is a client-server based protocol, which facilitates communication between a client and
a server over a network. The protocol is built on top of a lower-level transport protocol,
such as the Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol (TCP/IP). This low-level
protocol is responsible for the transmission of message data between the two communicating
programs.

RPC is an old concept, which existed a long time before architectures such as REST

more recent times, gRPC has gained a lot of traction, which we will describe in Section 2.3.
Generally, two different approaches to defining a contract between a service provider and a
client exists. In a contract-first approach, the web service is implemented in accordance
with the contract. The approach is concerned with the definition of a contract first, which
can be used to generate code for the server and client implementations of the RPC service.
In a code-first approach, the interface is defined through code, which is used to generate
the interface definition for the client to use.

2.2
Data Transfer Objects
The communication between a client and a server, can be costly. The majority of this
cost comes from the network communication overhead of each remote procedure call. To
reduce the number of calls, programmers will create methods that contain a large set of
parameters. This adds complexity to the system, as well as two points of maintenance
for each method, as each change made to the server must also be reflected in the client.
Furthermore, changing the method signature can also break functionality with older clients,
that may not be able to utilize the updated method.
The solution to these challenges is to use data transfer objects DTO. These objects are
defined by the following:
• DTOs store all the data which needs to be transferred either as a request for the
server or a reply to the client
• DTOs are known to both the client and the server
• DTOs contain no business logic, meaning it does not have any behavior except for
storage
• DTOs are serializable
By utilizing DTOs, it is possible to batch what would have been multiple calls into a single
call, which reduces the network communication overhead. These DTOs can be used as
both the parameter wrapper for method inputs and as a return struct for method outputs.
2.3
The gRPC Framework
In this section, we address the specifics of gRPC. The reader may skip this if they are
already familiar with the framework.
gRPC is an open source framework, with core functionality developed and maintained by
Google. It uses a contract-first approach, where a service is defined using a message format
called protocol buffer. It is also an open source project maintained by Google.
Protocol buffers is a data format for serialization of structured data. gRPC utilizes the
HTTP/2 network protocol to facilitate fast and secure communication between server and
clients. By utilizing HTTP/2 combined with the binary encoding supported by protocol
buffers, it is more efficient than most REST APIs that utilize HTTP/1.1. gRPC also
supports multi-language implementations for clients and servers.
Communication between a server and its clients is performed through a channel. In gRPC,
a channel is a logical connection to a specific server. A gRPC client creates a channel to
a server and calls remote procedures over the channel. The server listens for incoming

Protocol buffers (often shortened to protobufs) is a data serialization format for serializing
structured data that is language and platform agnostic.
Protocol buffers are designed to be small and simple, with an emphasis on being easy to

3.2.1
Software Archaeology
According to Hunt and Thomas[9], the concept of software archaeology entails the process
of discovering the model of a legacy system through code examination. Hunt and Thomas
argue that software archaeology is very important for the understanding and further
development of legacy systems and detail the best practices of software archaeology.
Software archaeology represents the action of using existing code or artifacts from a system
to derive information about the system. This process is tedious and time-consuming, as it
often involves digging through legacy code with little to no documentation. By performing
software archaeology, our methodology can help disentangle parts of the complicated
process of software migration.
3.2.2
Quality Measurement Scenarios in Software Migration
Pandey et al.[10] present the Quality-driven software migration (Q-MIG) system for software
migration. The system details a tool-chain for migrating Cobol source code to Java source
code. The tool-chain is made up of an analysis tool, a transformation tool and a generator
tool to handle the software migration.
The paper also details Quality control center (QCC), which is a separate entity to determine
the quality of the resulting software system. This QCC can evaluate the quality of the
software migration at each step in the tool-chain. The QCC is used to aid in ultimately
deciding for or against software migration of the chosen system, as it predicts the internal
quality of the code produced and compares it to the original source code.
We may be able to adopt this quality control approach as certain features in one RPC
framework may not be available in another RPC framework. Furthermore, the approach
of developing a QCC can help us ensure a stable methodology. The QCC can do this by
providing details when specific methods or features from the source code may not be able
to replicate in the target RPC framework.

3.3.4
Model-Driven Software Migration
Wagner[1] discusses model-driven software migration in detail. Wagner starts by describing
the different approaches, types and strategies pertaining to software migration, which helps
the developer understand the intricacies of software migration. These approaches, types
and strategies are shaped by the requirements set upon the project and largely shape
the process of software migration. Wagner thereby argues that these concepts can help
the developer determine what is needed to complete the migration and how it should be
conducted.
Furthermore, Wagner describes a novel solution to a model-driven software migration. By
utilizing model-driven development, changes in the system are made by updating the model,
which automatically transforms other artifacts, including source code[1]. This solution
utilizes a model, defined in the XML-based eXtented Process Deliverable Diagram (XPDD)
language, to guide the software migration model. Any changes in the XPDD model are
automatically reflected in the source code, which means that the actual migration does not
require maintenance of both model and source code. The described solution starts by the
process of model-driven reengineering, which analyzes the source code using syntax trees
to generate the model. Next, the model-driven software migration is conducted, given the
following conditions:
• The source code is compilable and executable
• All dependencies are available
• Development of the codebase is halted during the migration
• A tool for code generation from the model exists or is being developed
The model-driven software migration starts by recreating the original source code, based
on the generated model, to test the validity of the code generator. Next, the model is then
iteratively manually migrated to the target technology, followed by source code generation.
This process is repeated until the software migration is complete. This iterative approach
ensures that an executable prototype can be created at any time, which means that the
migration progress can be monitored and tested.
This solution is very interesting for our thesis, as it describes automatic model generation,
which is very important for documenting and understanding legacy systems efficiently. The
precise approach should be refined for our thesis, but the ideas behind it, are definitely useful
for our thesis. Our thesis, however, differentiates itself by focusing entirely on the migration
from one RPC framework to another. By focusing entirely on RPC framework migration, we
can further detail the specific problems and constraints present when conducting migrations
of this nature. Furthermore, this thesis also details the automatic migration of specific,
repetitive tasks, whereas the model described in this methodology is manipulated manually.

Analysis of Source System Framework
In the development of the DMS, cBrain chose to implement it with the ASP.NET Web
Services[16] framework from .NET framework, to create and host their service.
The
framework makes it possible for developers to create XML Web services and clients, but it
has since been deprecated along with the .NET framework by Microsoft. The messages
exchanged uses standard protocols such as HTTP, XML, XSD, SOAP and WSDL. It is
an older technology, but it is still in use by many organizations. In this section we will
investigate the framework, its capabilities and shortcomings through an investigation of a
single remote procedure in the DMS system.
it is important to note that these kind of systems can be described using the words API,
service and web service. We define these as such:
• API - A broad term defining an interface of accessible resources and actions.
• Service - A concrete set of functions made available to a system by a host
• Web service - A service strictly accessed over a network
We describe the system as a service, which becomes a web service once available over a
network. The system could also be describes as an API, but we aim to be as concise as
possible in our definitions.
The framework implements the Simple Object Access Protocol (SOAP) to send and receive
messages. SOAP is a standardized protocol for sending and receiving web service requests
and responses. It specifies the format of the message, including the data types and structure,
as well as the rules for exchanging messages.
Remote procedures are provided by an ASP.NET Web Service and accessed through a
URL. The web service can be called by any client able to send an HTTP request and
receive an HTTP response. To call an ASP.NET Web Service remote procedure, a client
sends a SOAP request to the service. The request contains the method to be called and
any necessary parameters. The service processes the request and returns a SOAP response,
which contains the result of the method call or an error message if an error occurred.

Evaluating frameworks
It may be beneficial for the practitioner to select a suitable candidate framework on the

To ensure that developers do not implement an ill-suited framework for their migration,

complexity and multi-language support. These properties have a large influence on the
choice of framework, as different requirements or constraints necessitate different properties.
We therefore demonstrate a set of key properties to consider. It is important to note that
this is not an exhaustive list of properties relevant for the migration of RPC-based systems,
but rather a select few properties that we have identified to have a large effect on the
choice of framework.
Security is an important consideration regarding any RPC framework. However, some
frameworks specifically address security issues to a larger extent. One such security issue is
an attack that can happen when an attacker manages to pass a modified serialized object
to an API. As a critical consequence, the execution of the remote procedure may have
undesirable impact on the system, potentially compromising data.
Efficiency is another measure to consider in the business’ needs for an updated RPC
framework.
Certain applications may require efficient use of memory, so it is worth
considering using serialization formats designed for this purpose. As an example, Facebook’s
Android Client have used a serialization format called Flatbuffers for disk storage and
communication with Facebook servers. Flatbuffers are described as a maximum memory
efficient cross-platform serialization library.
If efficiency is of less concern, but human-readability is important, a serialization format
with a representation that is readable by humans, should be chosen. Serialization formats
can be widely different depending on their representation.
Some of the more widely
used formats, such as JSON and XML, are recognized as human-readable, as the data is
represented in a textual structure, friendly to a human reader. However, formats such as
protocol buffers are stored in a binary format and are not intended to be read by humans
when compiled with data, which may reduce debugging options.
Multi-language support for multiple programming languages is another consideration more
modern formats and frameworks have turned to. This allows server and client RPC
implementations to communicate with each other, despite being implemented in different
programming languages. Adopting an RPC framework with a serialization format capable
of multi-language support makes solutions more configurable and adaptive to the team’s
competences, but may also add unnecessary complexity if the property is not needed for

Software Maintenance
In this section, we introduce the concept of software maintenance to provide a specific
definition for software maintenance throughout this project.
A software system enters the operation phase of the software lifecycle model when it
is first deployed for use by the customers and usually remains in this state until it is
decommissioned many years later. Throughout this phase, business needs may change,
bugs may be found or new technologies may unlock new opportunities for the system. The
software system must therefore change to accommodate these changes in the needs of the
system.
Software maintenance is both needed for small and large tasks. Maintenance is needed
when bugs are reported or when small inconsistencies are found. Furthermore, maintenance
is needed when the system cannot support the needed changes in its current state or when
the environment surrounding the system changes. The goal of software maintenance can
therefore be to fix small bugs, to implement new functionality or to reduce the complexity
of the software system. Fixing bugs generally require a relatively low amount of resources,
while larger tasks generally require a relatively high amount of resources.
In Section 4.1, we asserted that the software migration involves 128 remote procedures,
which means that it is a large-scale change to the existing codebase. To examine the
estimated resources expected for our migration, we therefore explored different types of
maintenance.
5.2
Types of Maintenance
In this section, we discuss the different types of maintenance to estimate the expected

changes.
Burton[28] defines three types of maintenance, which are Corrective, Adaptive and Perfecting
maintenance. In this section, these three types are used to categorize the goal of the change
and to generalize the actions and resources required of each maintenance type.

Although the aforementioned types have been adopted and used by many authors within

subtypes. We prefer the simplicity of the three previously mentioned types, which are:
• Corrective maintenance - Defines maintenance to fix small errors within the source
code due to discrepancies between the requirements made by the customer and what
was actually implemented or small unexpected problems. This type of maintenance
is often finishing and rolled out into production within a relatively short time span

5.3
Software Migration
Software migration is not a task that many developers are particularly familiar with, as
it is not easy to practice in a safe environment and industrial applications do not need
migrations often. Furthermore, software migration is a very invasive and time-consuming
task and developers must therefore familiarize themselves with the software migration
process and its identified strategies before performing a software migration project.
We will therefore start by introducing the concept of software migration, followed by an
examination of the different migration strategies in existing literature and finally, we will
determine the migration strategy selected for this project.
5.3.1
Software Migration Introduction
Software migration is the process of migrating source code from one technology to another.
The migration process is a sub-classification of software maintenance, as it involves moving
pieces of software from one technology to another. This process is often very complex, as it
requires a thorough understanding of the software system, the source technology and the
target technology. Furthermore, developers may not have the necessary knowledge about
the system and may need to develop or update models to ensure a thorough understanding
of the software system. Developers must therefore invest a lot of time and resources to
Section: 5.3. SOFTWARE MIGRATION
Page 45 of 179
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 5. RISK MANAGEMENT FOR SOFTWARE MIGRATION
learn about the designated technologies and codebase.
Furthermore, while software migration is a sub-classification of software maintenance,
the migration process itself does not encompass any changes to the functionality of the
system. While it may be necessary to change the functionality of the system to reach
the goal motivating a software migration, the migration process itself should be isolated
from these changes to reduce the complexity of the migration process[45]. The goal of
the migration is therefore to reach feature parity using the new technology, where adding
new functionality, adapting existing functionality or running and deploying new software
will be easier. Feature parity is especially important for legacy systems, which are the
primary target of software migrations. It is often expected that legacy systems do not lose
functionality and legacy systems often function as building blocks for other systems and
applications and loss of functionality can therefore be detrimental to an entire organizations
infrastructure.
5.3.2
Software Migration Strategy
With software migration defined, it is important to consider how to conduct the transition
from the source technology to the target. The existing solution is likely still in production
and the transition will therefore likely affect the operation of the system. It is therefore
important to determine a migration strategy to ensure a satisfactory transition to the
target technology. This is done by evaluating the projected effect of the migration and
determining whether other maintenance is conducted simultaneously. Evaluating the effect
of the migration is highly variable for each system, as some systems can be migrated while
operations are minimal or paused, such as evenings, whereas others cannot tolerate any
significant downtime.
It is therefore paramount for a system to utilize a suitable strategy to ensure a smooth
migration. The book Systems migration: a complete reference[46] defines three strategies:
• Cut-and-run - Defines the strategy of simply turning off the source system and
activating the target system when the entire system has been migrated, with little to
no formal testing definitions. This is a very hands-off approach and has no overhead,
as it does not spend resources on testing the target system, nor does the strategy
offer any defined fail-safe strategy in the event of failure of the target system. This
is a suitable option for migrations of systems, where momentary downtime is not
detrimental to the operations of the system.
• Phased Interoperability - Defines the strategy of incrementally transitioning each
part of the system to the new technology. The source and target systems exist in
parallel, which means that any disruptions made by a new feature can be rolled
back by reintegrating the feature in the source system. This continuous testing of
functionality increases confidence in the new solution. Furthermore, it is expected
that each part is tested in isolation, which decreases the likelihood that reintegration
of features is needed. This strategy functions well for systems where the different
parts have a low coupling, as these can be decoupled without a large refactoring
Section: 5.3. SOFTWARE MIGRATION
Page 46 of 179
==PAGE:57==
CHAPTER 5. RISK MANAGEMENT FOR SOFTWARE MIGRATION
overhead. In contrast, systems with high coupling have a large overhead and are
likely not suited for this strategy.
• Parallel operations - Defines the strategy of running and maintaining both the
source and target systems concurrently. In contrast to the phased interoperability
strategy, parallel operations do not incrementally transition to the target system, but
rather maintain both at the same time. This builds confidence in the target system,
while also allowing for full support of the source system while the new is developed.
When the target system has achieved feature parity with the source system, the
source system ceases operation and is fully replaced by the target system. This
strategy has a large overhead in maintaining both systems concurrently, which means
that bug fixes, refactoring of old functionality and other maintenance tasks need to
be performed on both systems. This means that it is a suitable strategy for systems
that have ceased maintenance operations, as these are likely not as influenced by the
duplicated maintenance overhead.
To determine a suitable strategy for a given software migration, it is therefore important
to examine two attributes of the software system: the magnitude of system downtime
and the projected maintenance operations. First, if magnitude of system downtime is not
detrimental to the operations of the system, the cut-and-run strategy is suitable, as it has
little to no overhead. Next, if system maintenance is expected, the phased interoperability
strategy is suitable. If not, the parallel operations strategy is suitable. We documented our
considerations for this, illustrated in Document 5.

This section provides a background on principles used in our architectural design decisions,

If the SOLID principles tell us how to arrange the bricks into walls and rooms,

For this project, we follow the principles based on the book resources Clean Code[47] and

In the following sub-sections we explain the principles Robert C. Martin has defined for

If the reader is familiar with these principles and concepts, the reader may skip to

6.1.1
Separation of Concern
The concept of separation of concern is a design principle for the separation of program
logic into distinct components or modules. The principle dictates what the components
should do and can be related to the single responsibility principle(SRP), which describes
how the functions should be responsible for one thing only and that components should be
responsible to one actor only.
Separation of concerns allows the developer to implement an abstraction, where a class
or module can more freely be replaced without having to refactor large portions of the
codebase. The SRP ensures high cohesion and low coupling as responsibility is not scattered
across the source code of the system. It also promotes reusability, when responsibility is
simple and clearly defined.
As an example of an application that violates the SRP, consider an employee class with three
methods calculateSalary(), reportVacation() and saveData(). Considering that an account
department may be responsible for calculating the salary, human resources responsible for
reporting vacation and a database administrator responsible for saving the data. This class
would couple the three distinct actors, where changes performed by one actor would affect
the others[27].
6.1.2
Cohesion
Cohesion is closely related to separation of concerns, as it describes to which degree the
elements of a component are related. Pragmatically, it is a measure of how strong the
relationship between the methods and data of a class are in their purpose of describing a
well-defined concept.

Cohesion can be measured on an ordinal scale as either high or low. High cohesion is

6.1.3
Coupling
In contrast to cohesion, coupling describes the interdependencies between an application’s
components. Similarly to cohesion, coupling is measured on an ordinal scale as either low
or high degrees of coupling. Other types of notations to describe the degree also exist, such
as loose or weak (low) and tight or strong (high).
In contrast to cohesion, a low degree of coupling is desired as it correlates with high
cohesion. Loose coupling means that components can be replaced and updated more easily,
as other components have little to no dependency on it and therefore would not require
multiple components to be changed as a consequence of changing the single component.
A system that is highly cohesive with loose coupling is considered as good design, which is
far easier to maintain.
6.1.4
Dependency Inversion Principle
The dependency inversion principle emphasizes how the source code dependencies in flexible
systems refer to abstractions and not concretions. Statically typed languages such as C#,
through usings, should only depend on source modules that are abstract declarations, such
as interfaces or abstract classes.
Although this is not always possible, the practice that should be derived from this is to
never depend on volatile concrete elements of a system, such as modules that are actively
being developed on or frequently undergoes changes. Stable concretions are classes such as
the String class.

Design decisions that are likely to change in an application should be separated, to ensure

principle of information hiding is often referred to as encapsulation.

6.2
Transitional Architecture Techniques
For any software migration, it is important to consider how the migration process affects
the operation of the system, as some systems may require that the migration does not
cause downtime for critical components. In Section 5.3.2, we identified several different
possible strategies for handling the migration’s influence on the operation of the system.
In this section, we therefore identify techniques for realizing these strategies.
To ensure a smooth transition from one system to another, it may be important to gradually
replace the components of a source system with new ones in the target system. Gradually
replacing components allows for the benefits of the target system to be realized sooner and
reduces the risk of a sudden, comprehensive change.
In certain systems, during the process of migration, both the source and target systems
may have to operate simultaneously, with certain tasks being divided between them. This
division of tasks will change as the source system is gradually phased out and replaced by
the target system.
To facilitate the process, it is necessary to create and continuously update a transitional
architecture, that supports the changing collaboration between the source and target
systems. It may also be necessary to implement temporary measures that will not be
needed in the final configuration of the target system[49].
The benefits of a transitional architecture can be explained through the analogy of ren-
ovating a building, as explained by Cartwright, Horn and Lewis in Patterns of Legacy
Displacement[49]. In this analogy, scaffolding is necessary to enable the work to be done
Section: 6.2. TRANSITIONAL ARCHITECTURE TECHNIQUES

safely and efficiently. Similarly, temporary software components are needed to facilitate a
safer transition to a target technology. Investments made in the transition process may
therefore not be carried over into the long-term use of the target technology, but do provide
value by allowing for earlier use of migrated functionality and reducing the risk of the
migration. The software components may provide additional functionality or allow for
the gradual replacement of the source technology. However, like scaffolding, they will
ultimately be removed once the transition is complete.
In Section 5.3.2, three software migration strategies are introduced: cut-and-run, phased
interoperability and parallel operations. The cut-and-run strategy describes a strategy that
aims to reduce overhead of the migration by omitting testing and fail-safes. As this strategy
intentionally bypasses fail-safes, we cannot utilize the transitional process with this strategy.
However, both phased interoperability and parallel operations use fail-safes to ensure that
migration errors can be corrected easily. Phased interoperability gradually phases in the
migrated system and phases out the original system, but stipulates that the original system
can be reintegrated at any point. Parallel operations runs and maintains both systems
simultaneously, which means that in the case of migration errors, all operations can be
redirected to the original system for the erroneous functionality. Both these strategies can
therefore utilize this transitional process to facilitate a gradual migration.
To demonstrate techniques for facilitating gradual migration of remote procedures, we
examine the Patterns of Legacy Displacement[49], which introduces the tangible techniques
in the following sub-sections.

Strangler Fig Pattern
The strangler fig pattern describes a legacy system gradually being replaced through
incremental changes to specific functionality provided in a new system.
The features developed in the target system eventually replace the source system’s features
and thereby strangling the source system.
At this point the source system may be
decommissioned. The process is illustrated in Figure 6.6

(b) Early migration
(c) Late migration
(d) Post Migration
Figure 6.6: Example of the strangler-fig pattern to support a gradual migration
The pattern implements a facade, which intercepts the client’ requests of the source system.
These requests are then routed to either the legacy source system or the target system.
In this way, features can gradually be migrated to the target system and consumers can
continue using the service with the same interface as specified by the source system.
As with other transitional architectures, when the migration is complete, the facade will
become obsolete and should be removed. If some legacy clients still exist, it might function
as an adapter for those.
This pattern may be suitable for the parallel operations strategy, as it allows running and
maintaining both the source system and target system simultaneously. When the target
system entirely eclipses the source system in functionality, the source system can be entirely
deprecated and the strangler facade may be removed.
6.2.3
Anti-Corruption Layer
Another noteworthy technique is the anti-corruption layer. The pattern addresses the issue
of being affected by the structure and integration of third-party software. If third-party
software crosses the boundary of a business’ system, it is at risk of muddying the waters.
The concepts and design in a third-party system should be separate from the owned
system to avoid the build-up of a convoluted architecture. This means that by adding an
anti-corruption layer at the edge of the system’s service, for each external dependency, we
can translate the third-party’s concepts and data structures to one suitable for our system.
The anti-corruption layer can be implemented as facades or adapters, but the general idea
is the translation of external requests, made by one system, to one suitable for another
system[51].

In the context of migration of a legacy RPC-based system, some existing legacy resources
and functionality may still have to be accessed. In this case, we may consider an anti-
corruption layer as an adapter between the legacy system’s resources and functionality
and the new RPC-based system implementation. It is particularly important in gradual
migrations, where different parts of the source system are moved to the target system over
time, where the target system may have to call the legacy system, such as in the event of
an erroneous migration of a remote procedure.
The implementation in the source system may suffer from quality issues or obsolete
procedures, which may vary from the new implementation of the target system.
By
supporting interoperability with a legacy system, the target system may have to support
outdated infrastructure, protocols or other features that may have been changed in the
target system. The maintenance of both systems is at risk of forcing the target system
to adhere to some of the legacy source system’s semantics. Should these semantics have
quality issues, then by supporting them, the new system risks corrupting its design. The
anti-corruption layer addresses this problem by bridging the gap between a source and
target system’s implementation of an RPC framework, by allowing the source system
to continue functioning while the migration to a target system implemented with a new
framework is underway.
This pattern may be suitable for both the phased interoperability and the parallel operations
migration strategy.

6.2.5
Approach to Transitional Architecture Techniques
As a part of a migration process, a business should consider how it affects the operation
of the source system. The need for a transitional architecture may vary depending on
the state of the source system. It may be actively maintained and regularly deployed or
completely decommissioned. What should be common for both systems, should be the
prospected benefits in a migration of the system’s RPC framework. As any migration effort
involves risks, we will in this section provide tangible suggestions as for how a transitional
architecture can be used in the migration of RPC-based systems.
As put by Martin Fowler on why a transitional architecture is important:
”If you do a big bang rewrite, the only thing you are certain of is a big bang.”
Estimating the impact of the migration and operational state of the system is imperative
to the outcome of the migration effort. A transitional architecture should be heavily
considered in any type of migration, but is imperative in systems that actively operate,
with clients that are dependent on the existing solution and so require that the system has
little to no downtime.
This motivates a strategy of phased interoperability as discussed in Section 5.3.2, where
transitional architecture design patterns and techniques are applied, to support an incre-
mental migration. For instance, branch by abstraction or the anti-corruption layer can
create a bridge between the source and target RPC frameworks and migrate the system
incrementally.
This incremental migration approach can help reduce the risk of failure and ensure that
the system remains stable and operational throughout the migration process. It can help
minimize the downtime and ensure that clients can continue to use the system without
disruption or knowledge of an on-going migration process.

6.3
The Clean Architecture
In this section, we introduce the architectural design principles and patterns that we utilize
in the analysis of our case study’s system. This background forms the foundation of our
approach to preprocessing and architectural design decisions in our migration tool described
in Section 7. Preprocessing in the context of this thesis, relates to changes performed on
the system prior to a migration.
The background is based on the design principles of a layered architecture, as described
in Martin Fowler’s book Patterns of Enterprise Application Architecture[52] and Robert
C. Martin’s book Clean Architecture[27]. A layered architecture motivates a clean and
modular design, which can help reduce the complexity and risk of migrating an RPC-based
system.
The reader may skip to Section 6.3.8 if they are familiar with the concepts, but wish to
know how we apply them.
In the following sections, business rules refer to the rules and logic governing the operation
of a system. The rules describe the system’s behavior, dictating how to make decisions and
perform actions. The clean architecture further defines critical business rules, which are
rules critical to the business as they would exist even if there was no automating system.
The critical rules require data to work with, defined as critical business data. This data
would also exist in spite of a missing software system. The binding of the critical business
rules and data constitute an entity[27].
6.3.1
Entities
In a system, we define the entities as objects embodying small sets of critical business rules
that operate on critical business data. In the interface of an entity, we would define the
functions that implement the critical business rules operating on the data[27]. Consider
the example in Figure 6.7
Figure 6.7: Folder entity module
In the figure, a simple folder entity has two attributes of critical business data and three
methods constituting critical business rules. The entity encompasses a concept critical to

==PAGE:71==
CHAPTER 6. DESIGN PATTERNS FOR EVOLVING SOFTWARE SYSTEMS
the business. The concept is separated from any other concern of the system being built. It
is unconcerned with external dependencies, such as databases and third-party frameworks.
In this sense, the entity is a pure business object and nothing more[27].
However, not all business rules can be defined as purely as in Figure 6.7. Certain business
rules may define and constrain the way an automated system operates, where the rules
would not have an equivalent definition in a manual physical setting. An example would
be adding, accessing and removing documents from a folder, which could be physical tasks.
The business rules that would otherwise not be used in a manual setting would only make
sense in context of the automated system. These business rules are defined as use cases[27].
6.3.2
Use Cases
The definition of a use case is how an automated system is used. The use case takes an
input provided by a user, processes any steps involved in the task and returns an output
to the user. In this definition, opposed to the entities containing critical business rules,
a use case is strictly about describing application-specific business rules. An example is
provided in Figure 6.8.
Figure 6.8: Example of use case that involves the automated system
In Figure 6.8, we observe a specification for how and when critical business rules within
entities are invoked, such as the folder or a User entity. From this use case, it is important
to notice that it should not be possible to tell whether the use case relates to a web service,
database or console application. The use cases do not describe the appearance of the
system from the client’s perspective. They purely govern the interaction between the users
of the system and the entities. [27]
To summarize, use cases implement specific business rules with input and output data.
They interact with entities, but the entities do not have knowledge of the use cases. This
follows the dependency inversion principle, where higher-level concepts should not depend
on low-level concepts. Entities may be used in many different applications, whereas use
cases are specific to single applications and closer to inputs and outputs. They depend on

entities, but the entities do not depend on the use cases[27].
On the basis of these definitions, we now introduce the characteristics the clean architecture
model integrates into an actionable idea. The illustration by Robert C. Martin[27], is
demonstrated in Figure 6.9.
• Framework independent - A system architecture should not depend on external
feature-specific frameworks and libraries. Frameworks should work as tools instead
of having to build your system within the constraints of the framework.
• UI independent - As the nature of UI is constantly evolving, the UI should be
exchangeable without having to change other components of the system.
• Database independent - Business rules should not be bound to a specific database
management system. Databases such as SQL Server and MongoDB should not affect
the business rules.
• External agency independent - Business rules does not know anything about the
outside world’s interfaces.
• Testable - Business rules should be testable regardless of database, UI, web server
and other external factors.

mechanisms in an outer circle. Any variables, methods, classes and other entities declared
in an outer circle should never be mentioned by an inner circle. The outermost circles are
concerned with concrete implementation details, while the innermost circles have higher
levels of abstraction. A data format related to a specific framework in an outer circle
should therefore not be passed forward to an inner circle[27].

principle, introduced in Section 6.1, are well demonstrated in Figure 6.9. Each component
connected to a unique layer deals only with logic pertaining to that specific layer.

layers and components are logically structured with separate responsibilities. This makes
the application easier to develop, maintain and test[53]. Decoupling the layers should be
considered similarly to common closure principle, where functionality that change for the
same reasons are grouped and similarly, functionality that changes for different reasons
should be split up[27].
We now describe the role of each layer and provide a few examples for clarity.
6.3.3
Enterprise Business Rules
The inner-most layer encapsulates the entities of a system and is therefore referred to as
the entity layer. The entities define the critical business rules in the system and are the
least likely to change. Recall that an entity may be a simple object with methods, a data
structure or simple functionality. The core purpose with entities is the reusability of the
enterprise system, across applications[27]. Operational changes, such as a change to a form
submission in an application, should not affect the entity layer.
6.3.4
Application Business Rules
The second inner-most circle encapsulates the use cases, which are application-specific
business rules. The use cases of the system should be implemented at this layer, where
they facilitate the data flow to and from entities.
The use cases layer is decoupled from being affected by changes to externalities, such as
changes in the database, frameworks or UI. However, business operation changes may cause
changes in the application, which affects the use cases layer[27].
6.3.5
Interface Adapters
The second outer-most circle, is the interface adapter layer, which is responsible for
converting data from formats represented in the entities and use cases layers to formats
consumed by an external service or persistence framework, i.e. database.[27] This means
that any circle inward of the interface adapters layer should not know anything about the
external frameworks or drivers.
6.3.6
Frameworks & Drivers
In Figure 6.9, the outer-most layer is a combination of external frameworks and drivers,
such as databases and web frameworks. The layer contains a large amount of detail about
these external frameworks and drivers, but they do not impose implementation details
on inner layers. This layer communicates information to the next inward circle. This
layer may contain database drivers, responsible for connecting and querying databases.
Examples are Microsoft SQL Server or PostgreSQL. Other frameworks, such as for logging
or other externalities also fit into this layer. A specific RPC framework would also be
located in this layer.
6.3.7
Crossing the Boundaries of the Layers
In some scenarios it may be needed for a use case to call some presentation functionality.
However, the call should not directly access a name in the outer circle, as that would
violate the dependency rule. Instead, an interface can be created in an outer layer, for the

use case layer to call from its inner circle, demonstrated in Figure 6.9 as ”Use Case Output
Port” and have the presenter implement it.
This technique is generally used at crossing boundaries, where the advantage of dynamic
polymorphism1 can be leveraged. Dynamic polymorphism solves the dependency rule by
allowing a class to depend on an abstract interface, rather than on a specific concrete
implementation. When a class calls a method declared in the abstract interface, it is
actually calling the implementation provided by the subclass, which is bound to the
method at run-time. This allows the class to change its behavior based on the specific
implementation of the abstract interface it is using, without having to depend on a specific
concrete implementation. In this way, the solution conforms to the dependency rule and a
design with less coupling is achieved.
6.3.8
Measures Towards a Maintainable System
In this chapter, we introduced the clean architecture, motivating the development of
a loosely coupled, clean and modular architecture. It is based on the principles of a
layered architecture, where changes made in one layer should not have impact or side
effects for components residing in other layers. By restricting the access between layers,
the application becomes more loosely coupled and avoids interdependencies between the
components. Having a tightly coupled application with a lot of interdependencies means
that it becomes very expensive and difficult to change. By following the concepts of the
clean architecture, developers can design a system that is more maintainable, flexible and
easy to update.
On the basis of the concepts provided by the clean architecture, we provide a set of tangible
recommendations to help guide the developers’ design decisions for the architecture of the
target system and on preprocessing of the source system. In Document 7, we provide a set
of recommendations for better software design.

6.4.3
Logging & Monitoring
As with authentication and exception handling, the remote procedures in the DMS also
implement logging functionality to record behavior, ranging from information about
successful executions and errors thrown for debugging.
Logging has a crucial use case in relation to client-server communication, where a multitude
of errors and information is transmitted between client and server and therefore needs to
be logged for administration, governance, debugging and other purposes.
The DMS logging implementation relies on an external logging framework called log4net,
which includes a number of methods for logging. However, this introduces a dependency
on the specific framework, which we wish to avoid by utilizing dependency injection where
applicable as described in Section 6.1.4. This realization originated from the challenging
effort of upgrading the log4net version dependency to one compatible with a newer .NET
version, as required in our case study.

To
determine whether to conduct a preprocessing step, a developer must therefore first
consider whether the cost outweighs the benefit of implementing the proposed layers.
Evaluating when preprocessing is necessary to reduce the resources required for a migration
of an RPC-based system, will require the developers to identify the different degree of
coupling the components have across the boundaries of the layers. If the components consist
of decoupled functionality with separate concerns, with little to no business logic entangled
with the remote procedures, the developers may not have to conduct a preprocessing step.
However, if the components are highly coupled and business logic is entangled with the
remote procedures, the developers must first estimate the resources required to decouple
the functionality. Next, the developers must consider how the highly coupled components
can be migrated and contrast this with how the decoupled components can be migrated.
On this basis, the developers must estimate the gain from the preprocessing step. Both the
estimated resource cost and resource gain are hard to accurately predict and ultimately, an
educated guess on these numbers will suffice. When the estimated resource gain outweighs
the estimated resource cost, the preprocessing step should be conducted.
However, in cases where the resource cost and resource gain of a preprocessing step are
close, the developers must also consider that decoupled components are easier to perform
future maintenance upon. This means that performing the preprocessing step also has an
implicit resource gain that should not be overlooked when evaluating whether to conduct a
preprocessing step. The value of this implicit gain highly correlates with the frequency of
maintenance tasks conducted.
Preprocessing may also be motivated by a system incompatibility. In Section 6.4.3, we
determined that if a new version of log4net had not existed, we would need to conduct
preprocessing to ensure that the logging functionality would be available to the migrated
system. Recall that in Section 5.3.1, we stated that software migration must reach feature
parity with the old system. This would not be possible without preprocessing the logging
functionality to a technology that is compatible with the version of .NET required to
implement gRPC.
We can therefore determine that preprocessing may be motivated by two factors: incompat-
ibility between the source framework and the target framework or architectural problems.
Incompatibilities strictly necessitate preprocessing, while architectural problems requires a
developer to evaluate the gain and cost of preprocessing. Technical debt may be considered

In this chapter, we have provided a background on architectural design patterns in relation
to both permanent and transitional architectures. We have discussed design patterns
of a transitional architecture to provide tangible techniques to the migration strategies;
parallel operations and phased interoperability demonstrated in Section 5.3.2. We then
addressed how the adoption of the clean architecture principles and patterns can be used to
address challenges in a coupled RPC-based system. Through our case study we identified
potential challenges specific to an RPC-based system and discussed how the system should
be preprocessed. Finally, we summarized the theory and findings into a set of actionable

On the basis of the architectural discoveries made in Section 6.3 and the case study

the challenges described in Section 6.4:
• Logging & Monitoring - how logging & monitoring abstractions are used in the RPC

• Authentication & Validation - an abstraction of methods concerned with the verifica-

token.
• Exception-handling - how meaningful exceptions are exchanged between the service
and the client. Investigate the mapping between exceptions thrown by the business
logic and the ones parsed by the RPC framework for the client to understand.
Although the implementation of an RPC framework only constitutes one component of
a complete system, it is worth mentioning that addressing challenges of a framework’s
interplay with the source system’s business rules and other components, is an important
aspect in a successful migration of an RPC-based system.

7.1.4
Considerations
Separating the exception-handler and logging functionality into distinct modules from the
business logic, improves the maintainability and reusability as each C# method had the
same try-catch and logging statements. Moving this logic reduced the amount of duplicate
code in these methods. Furthermore, decoupling the modules from the business logic should
serve to reduce the amount of resources required to conduct the migration.
However, it may also be at risk of adding complexity to the codebase, as it couples the
execution of the methods with the logging and exception-handling classes. We decided
that the modification to only preprocess two methods for exception handling or logging, in
the event of changing needs, was easier to maintain than having to replace each duplicate
instance as implemented in the source system. Semantically, there is no difference, but the
improved maintainability is a beneficial addition.
Generally, it is worth remembering that reducing duplicate code by depending on a limited
set of methods to facilitate the functionality also creates a tighter coupling, which means
the developer should decide when the grouping of functionality should be performed and
when not to.

Potential Data Corruption
The first challenge is that of data corruption. As we will be changing the type of data in
transit, there is a risk of corrupting the data. This data corruption may arise as protocol
buffers might treat certain types differently from C#. We can not be certain that a C#
long and a protocol buffer int64 will have the exact same value. However, to limit the risk
of data corruption, we converted the types according to the protocol buffer documentation,
which provides a type conversion table. It is beyond the scope of this project to investigate
if type conversion causes data corruption. A wider investigation would be required in a
migration, where any potential data corruption is unacceptable.
Dealing with Complex Objects
The second challenge not directly demonstrated in the manual migration of our CreateFolder
example, is that of custom class definitions being transmitted between the server and its
clients. Here, a nested protocol message must be defined, containing the attributes of the
C# class. A conversion from the complex object is required to transmit the data. Solving
this incompatibility is a difficult task to conduct and keep track of manually. This challenge
needs to be addressed and our proposed solution can be found in 7.4.7.

7.3.4
Reimplementing Token Authentication
In Chapter 4, we discovered that cBrain’s token-based authentication mechanism relies on
the source framework’s ability to add data to the SOAP header of messages. This is what
allows cBrain to transmit the user token between the client and server. As a consequence,
we preprocess before a migration of the source system can be conducted. gRPC also allows

channels for its communication. We can add the token to the channel, which tells gRPC

7.4.2
Gather Procedure Names
The first operation performed by our migration software is to assemble a list of remote
procedures annotated in an ASMX document of the source system’s framework. These
remote procedures need a corresponding implementation with the target framework, to
ensure that we do not migrate functionality that is not currently in use. The logical place
to gather this information would be to use reflection and access the methods from the
server implementation, which resides in the CabinetService class.
As mentioned in Section 4.2, the ASP.NET Web Service framework exposes a list of remote
procedures in an ASMX document. When running the code locally, the file can be accessed
through the URL:
”http://localhost:1980/CabinetServices.asmx”
By accessing this file, we can retrieve a list of the names of all remote procedures in the
CabinetService class.
To gather this list of exposed method names, we create a class called ManifestReader. This

7.4.7
Protocol Buffer Object Conversion
The C# classes that were previously transferred between the client and server are now
expressed as protocol buffer messages. When these protocol buffer messages are generated
as C# classes by the C# protocol buffer compiler, they are generated as partial classes.
gRPC expects the data to be transmitted inside the properties of these classes, rather than
the source system’s approach to DTOs implemented as C# classes. However, the business
logic knows only how to work with the existing C# DTOs written by cBrain. One solution
to this problem would be to change the source system’s server and client code to utilize
the newly generated objects. However, one of our technical constraints are that we can not
change the system’s code outside of that which directly relates to the system’s framework
and coupling with the system.

and their protocol buffer counterpart when transmitting data. Since messages are generated
as partial classes, we can solve this problem by utilizing implicit operators, which allow

Alternative approach
It should be noted that an alternative approach to this problem was considered. gRPC
implements the any type, where objects can be serialized and deserialized manually by the
developer. This option was not explored in-depth, as it requires both the client and the
server to know what class type the serialized object has, to properly deserialize it. This
approach also reduces the readability of any gRPC message, as the type definition is stored
in the code rather than the contract. However, the any type may have been desirable if
the overhead of developing the functionality that translates the classes to protocol buffers
is higher than the overhead of writing the deserializing code for each object.

Chapter 8
Methodology for Migrating
RPC-Based Software Systems
In this chapter, we discuss the developed methodology by demonstrating and explaining
each activity needed to conduct migration of RPC-based systems. We combine the findings,
design decisions and considerations of the previous chapters to map out key tasks needed
for migration of RPC-based systems. Furthermore, when applicable, we provide examples
of how each activity can be conducted.
This methodology is facilitated by a set of components. These are gradually created
throughout the methodology and ensure that the practitioner of the methodology make
informed decisions throughout the migration process.
We start this chapter by providing an overview of the methodology, in the form of an
activity diagram illustrating all necessary activities needed to perform a migration of an
RPC-based system. Next, we go through each sub-section of the diagram to motivate why
the activities are important and to demonstrate the outcomes.
Finally, we discuss the limitations of our methodology by detailing how practitioners may
deviate from the presented activities. We do this by detailing the consequences of omitting
specific component, to demonstrate that not all practitioners may need to conduct all
illustrated activities.

8.1
Methodology Overview
Based on the learning outcomes of the previous sections, we have mapped the process of
migrating RPC-based systems. On the basis of this process, we have created a methodology
to guide a migration. To explain this methodology, we have developed an activity diagram.
This diagram illustrates the expected activities needed to conduct a migration of RPC-based
systems. The diagram is divided into two swimlanes, with a designated primary actor to
distinguish the different responsibilities and tasks to relevant actors.
We have defined these actors by considering how we communicated with cBrain regarding
constraint and requirement elicitation, analysis, development and continuous evaluation of
our work. Other stakeholders likely exist, but the activities should be conducted by the
identified actors. We have two defined actors:
• The product owner
• The developers
In our case study, the product owner is Aske Brekling and we regard ourselves as the
developers.
The methodology illustrated in Figure 8.1 intents to guide the product owner and developers
responsible for performing the migration. The following is an overview of the central
activities of the methodology. In the later sections, we will go into more depth about the
individual activities.
The migration process starts at the product owner, who documents business goals. Based
on these, the product owner identifies and documents business constraints, business
requirements, organizational constraints and technical requirements. After which the
product owner evaluates whether a new goal has been uncovered. If a new goal is uncovered,
the product owner repeats the process of documenting business goals. This process of
iteratively defining business goals, constraints and requirements is discussed further in
Section 8.3.
Next, the developers evaluate the identified constraints and requirements, to determine
whether the socio-technical constraints have been documented and to document the
maintenance type of the migration. This is discussed further in Section 8.4.
The developers then decide whether they have a clear understanding of the system. If the
developers do not have a clear understanding, they should conduct software archaeology to
document system-specific details. Discoveries from the software archaeology, can create the
need for new business goals, constraints or requirements, which triggers the defining and
documenting business goals, business constraints and requirements to be repeated. The
process of investigating and documenting these system-specific details is discussed further
in Section 8.5.

necessary items.
This means that implementation-specific constraints and requirements may not be correctly
addressed to, which may be detrimental to the migration. Furthermore, the migration
tasks cannot be utilized for migrations that wish to automate the migration. Lastly, the
migration cannot properly facilitate the potential need of gradual migration by determining
the migration strategy.
While it is highly advisable to include this component, it may be possible to omit it
in situations where the system does not have implementation-specific constraints and
requirements, gradual migration is not needed and the migration is not automated. In this

obsolete, which means that component Migration Strategy can also be omitted. Lastly,

migration scope or automation possibilities, which means that component Migration Scope
can also be omitted.

Evaluate the Migrated System
From Table 8.1, we can observe that the risk of omitting component Unfulfilled is rather
small. The impact of omitting the component is high, as a migration that does not fulfill
the constraints and requirements is detrimental to the goals of the migration. However,
if all other activities in the methodology are adequately conducted, the constraints and
requirements of the migration should also be adequately fulfilled. The largest risk of these
not being fulfilled is that some constraint or requirement was not properly elicited and
therefore not considered for the migration process.
If a practitioner wishes to omit this component, they can avoid some work in evaluating
the migrated system and documenting the unfulfilled constraints and requirements.

To evaluate our methodology as comprehensible and useful for developers, we conducted

more decision points and activities to model these other avenues. We did not make these
changes, as we believe that the visual clarity of the methodology is important to ensure
that developers are not lost in the methodology diagram.

Research Questions Revisited
In this section, we revisit our research questions and hypothesis and relate these to our
developed methodology, to determine how well the research questions have been answered
and whether our hypothesis is accepted.
[RQ1] What are the major challenges in the migration of RPC-based software systems?
Although it is hard to establish major challenges that all migrations of RPC-based system
have to overcome, we have pinpointed some challenges that we identified.
In Chapter 4, we identified that requirements and constraints elicitation is important for
migrations, as it is for most software design and maintenance. Similarly, for RPC-based
software migrations, the elicitation process is also important, as the framework selection
process and design of migration software largely depend on the elicited constraints and
requirements.
Next, in Section 6.2, we identified that gradually migrating RPC-based systems may
be necessary for some practitioners. This may be necessary if system downtime is not
acceptable or if new functionality is needed throughout the migration process. Gradual
migration can therefore be a major challenge for some migrations.
Additionally, in Section 6.4, we identified that preprocessing systems is also a major
challenge for the migration process, as this preprocessing can help reduce the resources
required for both migration and future mainteinance of a system.

Lastly, in Chapter 7.4, we have identified the tedious job of incrementally migrating the
system as a major challenge. Not only does practitioners need an understanding of the
source framework and how to translate to the target framework, they must do this many
times, often in the same exact pattern. This can become tedious and mistakes can be
made.
[RQ2] What methodologies and frameworks exist for software and API migration?
This research question poses an interesting question, but is too narrow in its formulation.
Slightly altering this formulation allows us to explore approaches that attempt to solve
parts of the problem. Therefore, a more interesting question is:
What methodologies and approaches exist for software and API migration?
In Chapter 3, we identified a number of different articles regarding software migration
in general and regarding specific solutions to migration of software. From the general
literature on software migrations, we learned of the dependency analysis approach to
maintenance. On the basis of this suggested approach, we have decided that dependencies
should be considered when migrating RPC-based systems.
For specific solutions to software migration, we identified four relevant solutions for our
case study. On the basis of the solution presented in Section 3.3.1, we decided to extract a
list of remote procedure names from the WSDL files, utilizing regex. Furthermore from
the solution presented in Section 3.3.3, we identified that a transpiler could be used as a
tool for code generation. This prompted us to investigate static code analysis to read and
convert source code from one technology to another.
In Section 3.3.4, we identified a solution to automatically migrate from one technology
to another, by following a model-driven approach to migration. We have utilized some
ideas presented by this solution, as we extract the components of a remote procedure to a
non-framework-specific model.
However, none of the solutions properly discuss the specific challenges of migrating an
RPC-based system. Furthermore, they do not propose a solution to this problem, as their
presented solutions target other types of migrations.
Lastly, in Section 6.2, we addressed existing approaches and techniques to minimize the
risks involved in the migration of software in active systems. The techniques have been
integrated into our methodology, as they propose a way to concretely implement the
migration strategies discussed in Section 5.3.2.
[RQ3] How can a methodology for software migration of RPC-based systems help
developers migrate in a seamless and more efficient way?
Our developed methodology is presented in Chapter 8. This methodology was developed
on the basis of the learning outcomes from the previous chapters. The methodology seeks
to guide a practitioner through the migration process by providing examples of how the

that a practitioner can use to facilitate their migration.
By providing these examples and templates to guide practitioners in documenting and
organizing the knowledge gathered from their analysis, we help ensure that all considera-
tions and discoveries have been documented for the migration. Additionally, we provide
architectural patterns for how a gradual migration can be conducted seamlessly, as well as
a guide for determining the migration strategy.
[RQ4] How can business logic be preserved in an evolving software environment?
In Chapter 6, we addressed design patterns and architectural solutions for a gradual
migration of an RPC-based system and how to accommodate evolving software systems.
We support an evolving system by applying patterns from the clean architecture, to
the design of our migration software and preprocessing. We changed the source system’s
architecture from a statically defined call-hierarchy with direct dependencies, where business
logic and framework details were coupled, to one that adheres to the dependency rule
by applying the dependency inversion principle. The business logic is preserved in our
architecture through the use of adapters, decoupling the framework details from the server
functionality. Our adapter utilizes dependency injection for the ICabinetService interface
to dictate how framework request and response objects should be translated to and from
the server’s and client’s business rules. The architecture changes were demonstrated in
Figure 7.1 and Figure 7.3.
Additionally in Chapter 5, we introduced migration strategies to ensure that business
logic is preserved throughout the migration. To accommodate the presented strategies, we
proposed a set of transitional architecture design patterns in Chapter 6. These patterns
aim to support gradual migrations from one system to another, by utilizing patterns such as
the strangler-fig pattern or branch by abstraction. By utilizing these patterns, developers
not involved in the migration of the source system, may continue development while the
migration is conducted. However, this will only make sense as long as the migration efforts
are faster than the development by a reasonable factor.
[RQ5] How can the process of migrating RPC-based systems be automated?
In Chapter 7, we conducted a small manual migration and concluded that the migration
process consists of one-time and repetitive tasks. By focusing our automation efforts on
these repetitive tasks, we reduced the time spent on the most tedious tasks of the migration
process. This is achieved by automating tasks that follow a structured pattern and are
therefore suitable for automation. It should, however, be considered whether the resources
required to automate the tasks eclipse those required to manually conduct the migration.
Our migration software is able to complete the migration of all remote procedures, both
iteratively and simultaneously.
The software utilizes C# reflection and code-analysis libraries, to first extract remote
procedure components. Using these components, our migration software automatically

[H1] Developers can simplify RPC-based system migration by adopting a methodology.
The research hypothesis theorizes that developers adopting a methodology for migrating
RPC-based systems can simplify the migration process. In this context, simplify is defined
as reducing the risks and resource overhead of the migration. This thesis outlines our
designed methodology and migration software and uses our case study as a proof of concept
for how it simplifies the migration process.
This simplification is evident in how the case study is migrated by utilizing the presented
components of the methodology to facilitate the migration process. Furthermore, through
interviews with senior developers, we have demonstrated that the methodology is compre-
hensible. Finally, through our analysis of the components of the methodology, we have
presented how other practitioners can tailor the methodology to suit their individual needs.
We concluded that eliciting constraints and requirements should always be conducted.
Furthermore, depending on the selected migration approach, different components may be
more or less important. For automatic migration approaches, it is extremely important to
document the identified tasks, to determine the candidates for automation.

Future Work
In this section, we outline the future work we deem necessary to further validate whether
our proposed methodology will help developers in their migration of RPC-based systems.
This may entail the generalization of our migration software as a skeleton to be built on
top of in the context of another system.
In the implementation of our migration software, we have closely followed the implementa-
tion of the source system’s framework with the goal to migrate it to gRPC. While many
components of the migration software can be used in the context of different software
systems, it will ultimately have to be adapted to other system’s context. In our solution,
we discovered some repetitive patterns of how the DMS was implemented with its RPC
framework. We utilized this to extract the information necessary before converting it to
gRPC counterparts. However, some of the modules may be subject to a more robust and
generalized approach, such as the extraction of a method body, where we identified the
repetitive pattern of methods having only one try-catch statement. This may not be the
case for other systems, where the functionality may have to be sourced in multiple try-catch
statements of a method’s body. Furthermore, we hardcoded a set of cBrain specific usings
into the templates used for generating code. The migration software could instead utilize
the code analysis library to source the required usings or simply read these usings from a
file defined by the user of the migration software. Additionally, the UserInfo variable is

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to present a methodology to simplify the migration of RPC-based
systems, and thereby reduce risks and resource requirements. The proposed methodology
generalizes our observations and findings from performing a migration of the case study’s
system and from the literature on the subject.
The thesis presents key literature on the subject of migrations in general to examine other
solutions the methodology can build upon. From this, several different techniques were
adopted.
Next, the case study system was presented and the process of framework selection was
discussed and the importance of constraint and requirement elicitation was demonstrated.
Next, software maintenance is examined to explore any correlation between the type of
maintenance and the amount of resources required to conduct it. From this, it was asserted
that the adaptive maintenance type requires more resources than the others. From this,
we determined that the maintenance type must affect the amount of resources required for
a maintenance task.
Next, the chapter on design patterns for evolving software systems provided the foundation
of how we structured our solution of the target system and technically solve the different
migration strategies. Furthermore, we presented suitable techniques for the migration
strategies identified in literature.
On the basis of the explored clean architecture, preprocessing was made to reduce the
amount of resources required for the migration. Additionally, migration software was
developed to automate the identified repetitive tasks in the migration of the case study’s
system. The design of the migrated system and migration software were discussed to provide
insights into how similar migration software and migrated systems can be constructed.
Finally, a methodology was developed on the basis of the learning outcomes of the presented
actions. This methodology was subsequently evaluated, to ensure that the methodology
was comprehensible and that other practitioners can utilize it. Overall, we believe this

Component: Migration Scope
Maintenance type
• Adaptive maintenance
We have a large codebase to migrate and therefore cannot have corrective
maintenance. Furthermore, we must now support .NET 6 or higher, which
means that the change is necessitated by a changing technological environment.
This fits better as adaptive maintenance than perfecting maintenance, as
perfecting maintenance defines maintenance that introduces new functionality,
while adaptive maintenance defines changes motivated to ensure that a software
system can function within a changing environment.

Component: Facilitate and Guide A
List of Implementation-specific constraints and requirements
1. The migrated target system should return the same response as the source
system, given the same input
2. The target system should not be broken down into smaller components, and
should remain a single, unified service with all remote procedures
3. The existing C# data transfer objects can not be replaced
4. The client’s business logic should not be changed
5. The target system should be maintainable and extendable
6. The performance of the target system should be equal or better than the
source system
One-Time tasks
• Reimplementation of the c-WebService class
This task is specific to cBrain and our case study. As the c-WebService class
serves as the entry point for the DMS client to call remote procedures and
must be rebuilt to work with the target system’s framework.
• Reimplementation of mechanism for transferring the authentication
tokens
A mechanism for transmitting tokens between a server and its clients needs to
be developed for the new gRPC system.
• Implementation of gRPC exception adapter
A module to convert between C# exceptions and gRPC exceptions with
appropriate status codes returned to the client.

Component: Facilitate and Guide B
Repetitive tasks
1. Extraction of Procedure Components
Each procedure’s name, call message and reply message information needs to
be extracted
2. Creation of the gRPC service contract
The service must be defined as a gRPC service and each procedure much be
declared.
3. Creation of the Request and Response messages
Each procedure needs to have its call message defined as a protocol buffer
request message. And its reply message defined as a protocol buffer response
message
4. Creation of the CabinetService gRPC service class
The CabinetService class functions as the new server stubs for the target
system.
5. Creation of the CabinetServiceAdapter class
The CabinetService depends on the CabinetServiceAdapter to use its depen-
dency on an implementation of the ICabinetService.
6. Formalization of the source system’s procedures as an interface
The ICabinetService interface must be created, with the method signatures of
the source system’s C# method signatures.
7. Creation of the CabinetServerConnector class
An implementation of the ICabinetService interface, used by the CabinetSer-
viceAdapter.
8. Creation of the CabinetClientAdapter class
Responsible for adapting the C# method calls to the target system’s gRPC
framework.
9. Creation of Integration tests
If no integration tests are present these are created to validate the client
functionality and one should exist for each remote procedure in the system

Business questions
• Why does the business need to migrate their RPC framework?
• What are the business requirements for the new framework?
– What are the needs in terms of long term support and cutting edge research?

• What is the ownership of the client and server?
• Is backwards compatibility required?

Socio-technical questions

• What language(s) is the application limited to?
