Abstract 
Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) fished in the west coast of Greenland during the summer period 
is there following walks of capelin (Mallotus villosus). The heavy feeding is believed to result 
in low quality fillets, characterized by gaping and soft texture. This thesis done in co-
operation with Royal Greenland A/S was part of a large commercial research project with the 
aim to improve fillet quality by devising an alternative to the commonly used method used in 
Maniitsoq, Greenland called the Pound net method. A new fishing method, named the Hotel 
method was devised with the aim of live capture Atlantic cod and store it in sea cages for 
restoration and ultimately slaughtering the cod unstressed. 
The aim of this thesis can be divided into three parts. 
To determine if glucose and lactate is a suitable indicator of stress during harvest and 
transport of the live captured cod, using hand held measuring devices. To determine if 
restoration in aquaculture net cages for seven days is sufficient to restore live captured 
Atlantic cod back to unstressed levels of blood glucose and lactate. To determine if seven 
days are sufficient for gastric emptying of Atlantic cod, to minimize bacterial load in the 
production. Finally the aim was to determine if the new Hotel method resulted in higher 
sensory quality of thawed fillets of Atlantic cod than the previously used Pound net method. 
Blood glucose and lactate rose significantly in the cod after harvesting and transport. The 
blood glucose and lactate levels were however quick to recover and were not distinguishable 
from rested levels after 24 hours. Gastric rates were found to be within the suggested resting 
period of seven days. No sensory difference was found in thawed fillets of Atlantic cod for 
color and smell, a small but significant difference was found for consistency.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Royal Greenland A/S is a fishery company owned by the Greenlandic home government 
(Naalakkersuisut). It is the largest company operating in Greenland. Due to their size and 
ownership Royal Greenland is in a unique position to influence the social dynamics in 
Greenland. Fishing of Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) has been limited in the west coast of 
Greenland since the collapse of the Atlantic north-west fisheries in the early 1990’s (Higgins, 
2009) This lack of fishing is one of the main reasons for a rapidly decreasing population in 
Maniitsoq and other coastal towns on the west coast (Brinkhoff, 2015). However, in recent 
years Atlantic cod seems to have recovered in the area and fishing has begun anew. 
The Atlantic cod quota for 2014 in Greenland was originally 15000 tons, but due to the 
relative success of the fisheries the quota was increased to 18500 tons (“Torskekvoten er 
forhøjet med 2.000 tons,” 2015). For 2015 the home government of Greenland has issued an 
extra 7000 ton quota for experimental Atlantic cod fishery on the west coast of Greenland. 
The issuing was done with the restriction that all fish should be processed on land to increase 
employment opportunities on the west coast (Rosing, 2015).

The procedures for the pound net method were recorded by the author during on site field 
work in the summer of 2014 and are as described below. 
The pound net is usually left for 1-4 days depending on the season and weather. To harvest 
from the pound net the fishermen use small oar boats to manually hoist the pound net bottom 
to the surface, forcing the fish to the surface were it is crowded and mechanically brailed into 
a sorting pen aboard a larger harvesting vessel. In the sorting pen all fish under 40 cm are 
released back into the ocean. Fish over 40 cm are sorted into pens on the deck of the boat. 
When the pound net has been emptied and the fish sorted (20-60 minutes depending on the 
catch size), the boat is moved away from the pound net to avoid fouling the nets and then bled 
by throat cutting, and left to bleed for 15-20 minutes in the ocean using a large net bag tied to 
the side of the boat. The fish is then brailed back into the boat, gutted and beheaded by hand. 
Then the fish is iced into 25kg plastic boxes. The fish is usually left on ice aboard the fishing 
vessel during night as the fishermen usually operate during the same hours as the production 
facility and are therefore returning to land close to the factory closing hours, the raw material 
must therefore often wait until morning when it is accepted at Royal Greenland’s production 
facility in Maniitsoq and kept at a raw material cooler (<5°C) until processing.  
This handling procedures has many drawbacks and quality is lost at many points during the 
process. These drawback prompted the need for an improved fishing methods to improve the

1.2.1 Drawbacks to the Pound Net Method 
During the raising of the net the density of the cod is often very high and often results in high 
mortality before the fish can be hauled aboard the boat leading to poor exsanguination (Olsen 
et al., 2014) (Figure 3). When the fish is sorted onboard it is placed in a designated holding 
pen which are often relatively narrow and 
deep resulting in some crushing of the fish 
that can lead to bruising marks on fillets. As 
the fish is first completely sorted from the 
pound net into the pens and then bled, the 
difference in time from capture to bleeding 
can be vastly different, ranging from a few 
minutes to as long as 90 minutes from 
capture depending on the size of the catch. 
After the fish has been bled the fish is 
manually beheaded by ripping the head off the fish by hand, resulting in harsh handling of the 
fish and a labor intensive process for the fishermen. This method was revised by the end of 
the fishing period and fishermen were instructed to land the fish without gutting and head 
capping, and it was done by machine in the production facility. The pound net method was 
compared to the hotel method using this revised protocol.  
Another problem with this method is a lack of a strict handling protocol. Methods often vary 
from fisherman to fisherman leading to high discrepancies in the quality of the product.  
Yet another problem was the lack of production capacity at Royal Greenland’s production 
facilities in Maniitsoq. Where the freezing capacity was limited to around 12 tons of fillets per 
day given full output. The lack of control over the volume that entered the facility greatly 
reduced the effectiveness of its production. If there was a lot of fish being caught on any 
given day the vertical plate freezers used would present a bottleneck and valuable raw 
material would either have to wait in cooling storage. This wait meant that it would be 
processed too late for pre-rigor filleting and due to lack of storage space could not be stored 
through rigor resolution. It would therefore have to be processed in-rigor to the detriment of 
quality, as storage space at the production facility was insufficient to store the raw material

production. This method was called the Hotel method. 
1.2.2 The Hotel Method – The Proposed Improved Fishing Method 
A tanker boat was purchased for live capture and transport from harvesting (pound nets) to 
aquaculture net cage (fish hotels), also purchased for the purposes of this experiment. The aim 
of this new fishing method was to give the cod time to recover from the stressful harvesting 
methods. Empty its digestive system of feed and fecal matter to decrease bacterial load in the 
production facility. To implement rested slaughtering practices while maintaining full control 
over the production volume at any given time.  
As with the Pound net method the fish is harvested from pound nets. However, instead of 
being brailed into a fishing boat the fish was pumped using a vacuum pump (Silkstream, 
Environmental Technologies, Inc. Washington, USA) directly from the pound net into a 
sorting grid that roughly sorted out fish smaller than 40 cm. The larger fish were then directed 
into a large scale and weighed, and then immediately after they were released into the 
transport tank (60m3) of the tanker boat.  
The cod was then transported to commercial aquaculture sea cages for restoration and gastric 
emptying. The hope being that gastric emptying would reduce bacteria within the production

facility. It was also hoped that a short starvation period (seven days) would increase the

and farmed Atlantic salmon (Mørkøre, Mazo, Tahirovic, & Einen, 2008). However it must be

The seven day period was also suggested as a recovery from the stress induced by the 
harvesting and transport (Olsen, Tobiassen, Akse, Evensen, & Midling, 2013). 
Due to the relatively low depths and low physical impact of the fishing gear the pound net 
fishery can easily avoid some of the more common problems encountered in live capture of 
cod. Mainly problems such as expanded gas bladders and gas residues forming in the eyes of 
the cod due to the pressure differences in fish captured from deep waters (Isaksen & Midling, 
2013). 
After the seven day starving/restoration period a designated harvesting boat was then used to 
slaughter the cod by standardized methods designed by Royal Greenland staff on site with 
input from the author of this thesis (see material and methods). The bottom of the net cage 
was raised and an aquaculture gathering net was used to crowd the fish close to the boat, 
taking every care to make sure that the fish was not overly crowded.  A small amount of cod, 
about 200 kg was then brailed into a bleeding pen aboard the boat. The cod was then bled by 
the two cut method (Akse et al., 2012) while still alive and bled for thirty minutes in running 
water. The fish was then transported using a net bag into the cargo hold for storage in ice 
water. The relatively low amount of fish brailed in from the net cage at any given time 
ensured that all fish was bled alive within a short time frame from capture to ensure proper 
bleeding (Olsen et al., 2014). 
Having the cod at a designated location and a full knowledge of the volume of raw material at 
their disposal Royal Greenland would then be able to feed their production facility the raw 
material at a controlled pace ensuring that the freezing capacities would never be overloaded 
and therefore all of the raw material could be processed as a high value pre-rigor frozen 
fillets. 
As this method has never before been used in Greenland, and to the knowledge of the author 
never used with pound net harvested Atlantic cod the drawbacks of this method were not clear 
in the beginning of this experiment but became clear during the duration of the experiment.

1.3 Pre-Rigor filleting for Quality. 
Increase in aquaculture of Cod around the century mark along with improved handling and 
slaughtering procedures for wild cod opened up the possibilities for pre-rigor filleting of 
Atlantic cod. Pre-rigor filleting can be a viable strategy for producers as fresh fillets can be 
sent to market much faster than post-rigor fillets. Costs can also be lowered as filleting pre-
rigor cod also means that storage space for cod going through rigor is not necessary. Pre-rigor 
cod has also been demonstrated multiple positive attributes over post-rigor filleted cod such 
as lower initial bacterial numbers in fillets for both wild (Akse, Tobiassen, Midling, et al., 
2007; Akse, Tobiassen, Midling, et al., 2007b; Martinsdottir & Magnusson, 2001) and farmed 
Atlantic cod (Esaiassen, Dahl, Eilertsen, Gundersen, & Sivertsvik, 2008). Farmed Atlantic 
cod has furthermore been demonstrated to show less gaping, slower bacterial growth, firmer 
texture and darker color than post-rigor fillets. However pre-rigor fillets have been shown to 
have a higher drip loss than post-rigor fillets in farmed cod. Contraction of the fillets can also 
a problem with pre-rigor fillets (Esaiassen et al., 2008; Kristoffersen, Vang, Larsen, & Olsen, 
2007; Kristoffersen, Tobiassen, Esaiassen, et al., 2006).  
The Norwegian Institute of Food, Fisheries and Aquaculture Research (NOFIMA) has done 
studies on pre-rigor filleting for both fed and starved live captured Atlantic cod. Their 
findings indicate that pre-rigor filleting of fed live captured Atlantic cod showed similar drip 
loss, contrary to the general findings for farmed cod. Starved cod was found to have less drip 
loss when filleted pre-rigor opposed to post-rigor. Fed cod was shown to have lower ultimate 
pH opposed to similar ultimate pH for pre and post-rigor fillets in starved cod. Both starved 
and fed wild live captured cod showed less gaping, firmer texture and darker color when 
filleted pre-rigor opposed to post-rigor (Akse, Tobiassen, Midling, et al., 2007; Akse, 
Tobiassen, Midling, et al., 2007b). Martinsdottir and Magnusson, (2001) demonstrated similar 
benefits for pre-rigor fillets frozen at sea, although the positive difference decreased as the 
freezing storage period became longer.

1.4 Starvation as a method for improved production quality 
It is common knowledge within the fish farming industry that starving of farmed fish before 
slaughter can lead to improvements in quality in both Salmon (Einen, Mørkøre, Maria, & 
Røra, 1999; Mørkøre et al., 2008) and cod (Hagen & Solberg, 2010). 
Commercial aquaculture of Atlantic cod has shown much interest in developing methods to 
improve fillet quality during periods of perceived lower quality e.g. during summers and 
heavy feeding periods. Hagen and Solberg (2010) demonstrated that fasting of farmed 
Atlantic cod for 11 weeks would significantly improve fillet texture with no weight loss. It 
must be noted that during starvation theoretical weight gains are lost.  However, wild Atlantic 
cod feeding heavily on capelin caught by seine and stored live for 44 and 73 days have been 
shown to give no improvement in sensory properties and only small improvements in 
processing quality (Akse & Midling, 1997). Indicating that although shown to be a successful 
tactic in farmed fish, starving is not necessarily a suitable method for improving sensory 
quality of wild Atlantic cod 
Mørkøre et al. (2008) also showed that starvation of five weeks could lead to resistance 
against stress in Atlantic salmon, which then leads to a slower onset of rigor mortis due to a 
higher pH levels at death, it was also suggested that the starvation slowed down metabolic 
rates in the fish leading to a slower breakdown of ATP post-mortem, explaining the delayed 
onset of rigor mortis compared to fed salmon. 
1.5 Influence of Stress on Quality 
Many different definitions of stress are available in the scientific literature. Selye (1973) 
defined stress as “the nonspecific response of the body to any demand made upon it” another 
popular definition is “chemical and physical factors causing body reactions that may 
contribute to disease or death” (Rottmann, Francis-Floyd, & Durborow, 1992), or as stated by 
Barton & Iwama, (1991) that “the overall effect of stress may be considered as a change in 
biological condition beyond the normal resting state that challenges homeostasis and, thus 
presents a threat to the fish”. Some confusion has surrounded the term stress, many people 
believe that stress in itself is harmful to the fish. Chrousos (1998) explains that stress should 
be viewed as any state that threatens the homeostasis (normal state) of the individual and its 
adaptive response to return the individual back to homeostasis. Only when the intensity of the 
stressor is so severe or long lasting that the adaptive responses cannot cope should the fish be 
considered in a maladaptive or distressed state (Barton & Iwama, 1991).

The adaptive reaction in teleost’s to stress can roughly be sorted into three categories; 
primary, secondary and tertiary responses.  
The primary stress response consist of two major neuroendocrine processes. At first the 
sympathetic nervous system is activated resulting in the rapid release of the catecholamine’s, 
adrenaline and noradrenaline into the bloodstream from the chromaffin tissue (Reid, Bernier, 
& Perry, 1998). A slower response often referred to as the Hypothalamus-Pituitary-Interrenal 
(HPI) axis is then responsible for the release of the steroid hormone cortisol into the blood 
stream (Barton, 2002).  
Secondary effects are characterized by a variation of cardio-respiratory changes and are the 
effects of most interest to the scope of this thesis for their perceived usefulness as easily 
measurable stress indicators. The fish increases its respirator capacity, heart rate and blood 
flow to the gills. This happens alongside the mobilization of the fishes carbohydrate (glucose) 
and fat storages (Pickering & Pottinger, 2008). As the carbohydrates are mobilized into the 
bloodstream a measurable difference can be observed in the amount of glucose found in the 
blood of the fish, seemingly making it a useful stress indicator (Wells & Pankhurst, 1999). 
When the stress induces a lack of oxygen supply to the muscle tissue, glycolysis increases the 
production of lactic acid (lactate). Such as after exhaustive exercises or when the fish is 
exposed to hypoxic conditions, for example due to crowding (Johansen, Herbert, & 
Steffensen, 2006). This indicates that lactate could be a useful indicator of anaerobic 
conditions that can be highly stressful to the fish and need to be avoided during the capture, 
transport and harvest of fish to lower mortality and reduce the perceived loss of product 
quality.  
Tertiary responses are classified as the culmination of a continued unresolved chronic stressor 
(Barton, 2002). These effects are usually very visible and easy to detect and can range from 
reduction of growth, increased rate of disease and deteriorating reproduction (Pottinger, 
2008). As these effects are usually long term they are not very significant to the scope of this 
thesis as this project only dealt with short term handling/storage times (seven days or less). 
In recent years the effect of pre-mortem stress on the quality of Atlantic cod  has been 
extensively researched. Stien et al., (2005) found that farmed Atlantic cod fillets from stressed 
cod showed a slightly yellow color, a faster onset of rigor and a softer texture than fillets from 
unstressed cod immediately post-mortem (fillets showed no difference in texture after 6 days 
on ice). Other research of farmed cod has shown softer texture, more gaping and lower water 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
Aquatic Food Production

Elvar Steinn Traustason 2015 
holding capacities in fillets from stressed fish compared to fish that was unstressed pre-
mortem (Hultmann, Phu, Tobiassen, Aas-Hansen, & Rustad, 2012; Kristoffersen, Tobiassen, 
Steinsund, & Olsen, 2006). Bjørnevik & Solbakken (2010) showed that these gains in texture, 
rigor onset and drip loss from unstressed fish seem to even out after storage on ice for 8 days. 
This might indicate that managing a rested harvest might be more important for fish sold fresh 
or frozen pre-rigor than fish frozen post rigor. Contradictory to these findings Erikson, Digre, 
and Misimi (2011) were not able to find any difference in quality attributes for farmed 
Atlantic cod stressed peri-mortem. It must be noted though that the stressors used in that 
research where speculated to possibly being too mild to cause a negative effect. 
Furthermore pre-mortem stress has been shown to result in poorer exsanguination and as a 
result worse fillet quality, as blood is recirculated to the muscles as an adaptive response to 
the stressor (Olsen, Sørensen, Larsen, Elvevoll, & Nilsen, 2008). 
Olsen, Tobiassen, Akse, Evensen, & Midling, (2013) demonstrated that wild Atlantic cod 
captured by trawl showed elevated blood glucose and lactose and a decline in blood and 
muscle pH relative to the level of pre-mortem stress (trawling quantity and duration). They 
also discovered that flesh color and exsanguination could be improved by giving viable fish a 
short restoration period (6hr) before slaughter indicating that cod can recirculate blood back  
from muscles in a relatively short time after stress. 
1.5.1 Stress Indicators 
Many different measurable compounds have been suggested as stress indicators. Such as 
cortisol and glucose (Staurnes, Sigholt, Pedersen, & Rustad, 1994), Lactate, Catecholamine’s 
and Corticosteroids (mainly cortisol)(Barton, 2002), synthesizes of amino acids and enzymes 
involved in the numerous processes that are part of the fish adaptive response (Martinez-
Porchas, Martinez-Cordova, & Ramos-Enriquez, 2009). However many of these require 
complicated lab and are therefore not easy to use for frequent field use. One of the easier 
compounds to measure is blood glucose due to the availability of a relatively cheap and 
reasonably accurate hand held measuring devices. 
Blood glucose is commonly used as an indicator of stress in teleost’s (Silbergeld, 1974; Wells 
& Pankhurst, 1999). Caution must be taken when comparing values between stocks and 
species as differences can be considerable in how stress responses are expressed. These 
indicators are most reliable as a surveillance method within a specie or stock and care must be 
taken not to draw conclusions based on inter specie data. (Barton, 2000).

It must also be considered that glucose in the blood stream might not be enough on their own 
as reliable stress indicators. Martinez-Porchas et al., (2009) argued that when monitoring 
stress glucose or even cortisol was not a sufficient on its own and suggested other 
complimentary tests to be done such as for catecholamine’s or enzyme’s involved in amino 
acid synthesis or glycolysis. Lactate is also mentioned as a suitable test alongside glucose as 
an indicator of stress and due to the relative ease in which it can be measured, and it’s value 
as an indicator for exhaustive stress (Johansen et al., 2006).   
In the aquaculture and fishery industry there is seldom time or the facilities necessary for 
detailed lab work. Small hand held measuring devices that measure blood glucose and lactate 
have been used with good results in the health industry and have in recent years gained a lot 
of attention within the fishing industry, mainly in aquaculture as a possible alternative to lab 
tests. Wells & Pankhurst (1999) demonstrated that hand held measuring devices for lactate 
and glucose showed values relative to lab observed values that could be used in stress 
management although they concluded that both lactate and glucose measures were not reliable 
for absolute values as lab obtained values where significantly higher. There has however been 
major improvement in the development of these hand held measuring devices in the last 
decade resulting in better accuracy opening up the possibilities of them being used as cheap 
on site tool in stress management. 
 Brown, Watson, Bourhill, and Wall (2008) evaluated the Lactate Pro (an earlier model of the 
device used in this experiment) with farmed Atlantic cod and found that laboratory derived 
plasma lactate measured only ~5% above the values obtained using the Lactate Pro hand held 
measuring device.  
The FreeStyle Freedom Lite hand held glucose measuring device used for this experiment has 
not been validated on fish but it has been utilized in similar research (Olsen et al., 2013) and 
shown to have a high correlation coefficient r = 0,990 to standard lab methods in humans 
(Pfützner et al., 2012).

one stage method were the fish is bled and gutted (and sometimes head capped) at the same 
time (direct gutting). A two stage methods were the fish is first bled using a variation of

1.7 Scope and Hypothesis 
The scope of this thesis is to look into the following questions for live captured Atlantic cod: 
 
Will the perceived stressor (pumping and transport) influence glucose and lactate 
levels in blood? 
 
If the glucose and lactate levels are affected, will the seven day storage period be 
sufficient for restoration back to rested levels? 
 
Is gastric emptying possible in the time period given by Royal Greenland’s production 
scheme (7 days)? 
 
Will the overall sensory quality be improved by the adjustments made between the 
pound net methods and the hotel method? 
 
For this the following null hypotheses are put forth: 
Glucose and lactate: 
H10: There is no significant difference in the levels of blood lactate and blood glucose in 
Atlantic cod directly after a perceived stressor (crowding, pumping and transport) compared 
to blood glucose and lactate levels in rested cod. 
H20: 7 days are not sufficient to restore blood glucose and lactate levels to rested levels. 
Starving/gastric Emptying: 
H30: 7 days are not sufficient to completely empty the gastric content of Atlantic cod. 
Sensory Analyses: 
H40: There is no significant sensory difference in color, smell and texture in thawed skin-on 
fillets from Atlantic cod processed by either the hotel method and the pound net method.

2.1 Stress monitoring 
A hand held blood glucose meter, 
FreeStyle Freedom Lite (Abbot 
Diabetes Care, Illinois, USA.) and a 
hand held blood lactate meter, Lactate 
Pro 2 (Akray Inc., Japan) were used to 
determine the blood glucose and lactate 
levels in the cod. The measurements 
were done by randomly fishing the cod from a pound net or sea cage. The fish was then cut 
open and blood was drawn from the heart and applied to designated sampling strips and 
inserted into the respective measuring device, as described by (Olsen et al., 2013). 
To determine the effect of individual variation of glucose and lactate levels, and to determine 
“rested” levels, a preliminary variance test was done where unstressed Atlantic cod (n=20) 
were harvested using a fishing rod from inside a pound net. The fish were measured for 
weight, length and blood lactate and glucose. Only 11 measurements were possible for 
glucose as the Freedom Lite hand held glucose meter failed in the cold humid conditions. 
To determine the effects of the pumping and transportation on the fish n = 10, fish were fished 
out of the transportation tank after having been crowded in the pound net, pumped into the 
tanker boat and then transported for 2 hours at an density of approximately 100kg/m3 (6 tons 
total catch).

2.2 Gastric emptying 
Once the fish had been measured for lactate and glucose during each sampling point they 
were individually tagged, packaged in ice and transported to Royal Greenland’s production 
facility in Maniitsoq for measurements of  a gastric index (Equation 1).

contents of the gastric system. The gastric system was then weighed, and then emptied of all 
content. The stomach was cut open and cleaned with tap water and the intestines where 
emptied by squeezing out feed and feces residues by hand until no residues were visible. The 
emptied content was then weighed again and a gastric index was given as described by

4 Discussions 
4.1 Stress – Glucose and Lactose 
Glucose as a stress indicator in fish is widely used in the scientific literature (Barton, 2000; 
Silbergeld, 1974; Wells & Pankhurst, 1999). However it must be kept in mind that the blood 
chemistry seems to vary from specie to specie and therefor it is important to note that when 
using blood lactate and glucose as a stress indicator that the values cannot be compared 
between species or even stocks of the same species (Barton, 2000). It is therefore important to 
have a good knowledge of the blood chemistry of the species that shall be monitored. It must 
also be noted that as mentioned by Barton (2000) that even though a certain species 
demonstrates higher values than others it is not necessarily indicative of a higher 
susceptibility to stress, it is could simply be a difference in the species adaptive response to 
stress. 
The FreeStyle Lite hand held glucose and the Lactate Pro 2 meters are very handy tools that 
with a little practice is very easy to use in the field for blood glucose and lactate 
measurements. It must however be noted that the glucose meter fared considerably worse in 
the wet humid conditions on site, than the Lactate Pro although it failed occasionally as well. 
It is therefore recommended that for use in the field special care must be taken to keep the 
meters dry and warm. Another viable strategy is to have multiple meters as they are relatively 
inexpensive and can be used again as soon as they have been given a short drying out. Both 
meters gave quick results. 
Capture, pumping and transportation resulted in an immediate rise in blood glucose indicating 
that the process is a big enough stressor to facilitate a measurable increase in the secondary 
stress effects, namely the release of glucose and lactose (Figure 5, Table 1). The pump used in 
this process did not seem to be a viable option for this kind of fishing methods as considerable 
mortality was observed during the pumping process. After careful consideration by Royal 
Greenland and the author it has been suggested that a brailing system similar to that used in 
the pound net method may provide a suitable alternative. Although somewhat slower in use it 
does have the perceived advantage of a gentler handling than the pump. It may also be that the 
time required for the set-up of the pump may negate the time lost by the slower brailing. 
The lactate levels rose significantly post-stress (Figure 6, Table 2). This can possibly be 
indicative of  low oxygen during the capture process although the lactic formation could also 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
Aquatic Food Production  
21  
Elvar Steinn Traustason 2015 
be explained by the sheer exercise stress brought on by the capture process (Johansen et al., 
2006). 
The glucose and lactate levels were found to increase significantly compared to the control 
immediately post stress. After only 24 hours post stress mean blood glucose and lactate levels 
where not significantly different from control levels(Figure 5,6 ; Table 1,2). This indicates 
that the cod can be restored to unstressed levels by live storage for a relatively short period  or 
seemingly less than 24 hours. This concurs with the results of (Olsen et al., 2013). We 
therefore reject our hypothesis (H10) and conclude that there is a significant difference in 
glucose and lactate blood levels in Atlantic cod after being stressed by pumping and transport. 
We also reject hypothesis (H20) and conclude that 7 days are sufficient to restore blood 
glucose and lactate levels to rested levels in Atlantic cod. 
These results indicate that short term resting using sea cages (hotels) is a viable strategy to 
rest Atlantic cod after a stressful harvesting method. It is however easy to assume that due to 
the perceived gentleness of the pound net fishery that a need for a resting period could be 
avoided simply by improving the harvesting practices onboard the fishing vessels in the 
pound net method. Although this is true to a point, it is essential to remember that the new 
hotel method was not only implemented to improve quality but also improve production 
control. This stress experiment implies that harvesting cod in large quantities to designated 
holding pens for storage and resting is a viable strategy for Royal Greenland to improve 
production and harvesting control. The strategy also gives Royal Greenland a chance to 
elongate the fishing period as cod has demonstrated to tolerate long storage without feeding 
(Akse & Midling, 1997). The factory bottleneck is therefore no longer an issue and the 
fishermen can be given leave to harvest all the fish that they capture giving them improved 
efficiency and higher monetary gains. 
4.2 Gastric emptying 
Due to unfortunate weather conditions the fish in the gastric emptying trial came from a 
pound net that had been set for 4 days prior to harvest. It was therefore not possible to 
determine the length of starvation more precisely than 1-4 days. The fish were found to have a 
low gastric index and on the third day of trials they were deemed to be completely empty of 
feed or fecal residue. Although the precise rate of gastric emptying cannot be determined due 
to the uncertainty of the time spent in the pound net we can conclude that 4-7 days are 
sufficient to remove the majority of feed and fecal matter in Atlantic cod. This concurs with 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
Aquatic Food Production  
22  
Elvar Steinn Traustason 2015 
Tyler (1970) who found gastric emptying rates for young Atlantic cod to be around  30-40 
hours in 5-10°C. The null hypothesis (H30) is therefore rejected, it is therefore concluded that 
7 days are sufficient for gastric emptying of live captured capelin feeding Atlantic cod at 8°C. 
4.3 Sensory Analyses 
The sensory analyses showed no significant difference in the sensory score for smell and 
color when comparing the different handling procedures. For consistency scores a small 
difference was observable between the pre-rigor hotel samples and all other samples although 
only statistically significant difference was found when comparing the pre-rigor hotel samples 
to the post-rigor pound net samples (Table 3).  
We therefore reject our hypothesis (H40) for consistency, but not for color and smell. It can 
therefore be concluded that there is a significant difference on the consistency of thawed fillet 
between handling methods of live captured Atlantic cod, but not for color and smell. 
It is the authors believe that the fact that there is any difference at all is indicative of the 
possible benefits of this improved handling method and pre-rigor processing. The samples 
had been kept in frozen storage for up to 3 months before the sensory analyzes. Martinsdottir 
and Magnusson (2001) have shown that the additional sensory benefits of pre-rigor 
processing diminish after a certain time in freeze storage and therefore it is likely that some of 
the benefits had been reduced by the storage time. Additionally the pound net sample catch 
was relatively small (800kg), and therefore the negative handling procedures that come with 
the pound net method were somewhat negated in this sample, e.g. less and shorter crowding 
in the pound net, less crushing on deck and shorter capture to bleeding time compared to a 
routine catch of 2-2.5 tons. The Hotel method sample was however a part of a 6 ton catch 
more representative of its method 
This along with the duration of frozen storage lead the author to believe that this difference in 
quality would be more pronounced after a shorter storage time and with more samples from 
more representative catches. Even so these results must be taken with some degree of caution 
as the statistical analyses only relies on a single analyses from three panelists and the grade 
was given as an overall grade for a whole batch rather than an individual grade for each fillet. 
The reasons for this are discussed further in the chapter afterthoughts.

5 Conclusions 
Blood glucose and lactate levels are affected by pumping and transport. This indicates that 
pumping and transport has effects on the stress level of Atlantic cod. A short restoration 
period (<24h) is sufficient to allow the cod to recover back to unstressed levels of blood 
glucose and lactate. Glucose and lactate can be a useful indicator of stress as long as stock 
variances are known.  
4-7 days are sufficient to completely empty Atlantic cod of feed during summer in the west 
coast of Greenland at temperatures around 8°C. 
The hotel method and pound net method did not show a measurable difference in sensory 
attributes for color and smell. A significant difference was found in consistency but only 
between pre-rigor hotel samples and post-rigor pound net samples.

6 Afterthoughts 
This project was an exciting opportunity and a unique chance at an commercial project. The 
experience gained during my stay and work in Greenland was invaluable and will always be 
cherished.  
However due to multiple factors not all experiments went as planned. The project was 
undertaken with little notice, leading to a short planning period. Furthermore it was hard to 
plan ahead as the situation on site in Greenland was unknown and ever changing due to the 
fact that Royal Greenland was in mid development of this project and its set up. 
The main fault in this thesis was the low number of samples and the procedure of the sensory 
analyses. The reasons behind the low number of samples are multiple. The timeframe put 
forth for the beginning of the Hotel method was delayed multiple times due to logistic 
problems and other unavoidable situations. It was very important to have samples taken from 
the same time period as it was unknown if seasonal variations would affect the samples. 
However once the Hotel method was properly up and running most of the cod seemed to have 
migrated out to deeper waters resulting in the low catch volume for the pound net method. 
Due to this migration of cod to deeper waters the project was terminated for the season earlier 
than anticipated leading to a shortage of sampling opportunities. 
The fast pace and busy schedule in Greenland lead to a few mistakes on the authors part. 
Mainly my negligence to do a control sample for the gastric emptying experiment. The 
sensory analyses did also not go completely as planned. This is due to the authors lack of 
experience with such procedures leading to a gross underestimation of the time such 
procedures take leading to a batch analyses instead of individual grading of each fillet. 
Furthermore the panel was not calibrated. Mechanical test would have also been an valuable 
tool to gather empirical data, but due to the short and ever changing planning of this project 
this was sadly not considered until after the sensory grading was done. 
All of the criticism stated in this chapter is solely for the purpose of self-examination and for 
the purpose of learning for future research on the authors hand, and is in no way a criticism of 
the many wonderful people at Royal Greenland, DTU and NMBU who had a hand in making 
this project possible.
