Abstract
In this project the outline proposals from various construction projects have been
investigated. The focus of the investigation has been HVAC. Eight diﬀerent out-
line proposals have been provided by Alectia and analysed. By using the com-
parative method the outline proposals was investigated for their content, their
layout and their drawing material. Furthermore a number of qualitative research
interviews where conducted with two project managers and one HVAC engineer.
Diﬀerent theory sections are presented; construction phases, contract forms, Build-
ing Information Modelling, the outline proposal process and the diﬀerent speciﬁ-
cations used in the analysis.
In the analysis the outline proposals was investigated and 125 requirements were
identiﬁed and classiﬁed. From this classiﬁcation they were compared and it was
found that most of the requirements had level of detail 1 and 2 and that most
of the requirements had a single-value. It was furthermore found that half of the
requirements were linked to systems and most of the rest of them were linked
to spaces.
In the analysis the outline proposals were also analysed for diﬀer-
ent speciﬁcations, and the results showed that most of the projects obeyed the
speciﬁcations. The drawing material was also analysed to see if they meet the
speciﬁcations presented in the theory. The results showed that only two of the
projects meet the speciﬁcations. On the basis of the results from the analysis two
diﬀerent solutions were created.
Solution 1 described how the requirements identiﬁed in the outline proposals could
be linked to the Design Model. By using a BIM server the Requirements Model
and the Design Model can be linked together, and an External Application can be
used to manage the conformity between the two models. In the linking between
the two IFC Speciﬁcations should be used.
Solution 2 described how the ideal outline proposals should be designed so that
the outline proposal both can present the solutions to the client but also so the
requirements are easy to identify. The outline proposals should have a profound
table of content and the text should consist of body text with tables and bullet
list.

Introduction
Building Information Modelling (BIM) ensures a new view on information ﬂow
and communication in the construction industry. Looking back, the construction
industry is not known for much progress and optimization. Looking over a 40-
year period the construction productivity has decreased by 5 % whereas other
industries had increased their productivity by 40 % over the same period of time
[Demian et. al, 2013]. Recent studies have shown that the implementation of
BIM in construction is facilitating productivity in a positive direction. BIM is
currently being incorporated into the construction sector at a very fast pace. A
BIM model is a parametric model full of information, and that information could
be very helpful for the construction process [Sampio, 2014].
The Architectural, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry is one of the
heaviest information producing industries and BIM is an approach to manage
this information. The current interest in BIM provides the potential for a more
beneﬁcial and favourable way of creating information assets that can be used for
managing construction projects [Race, 2013]. The correlation between the infor-
mation in the construction project and BIM will be one of the main topics in this
thesis.
BIM has the ability to support many of the functions that are included in a build-
ings’ lifetime. When implemented correctly, BIM can facilitate a more integrated
design and construction process that results in a better quality of building, a
faster building process and thereby a cost reduction [Eastman et. al, 2008]. The
construction industry has undergone a change over the last decade and moved
towards 3D building modelling. This has induced that the traditional project
design, where drawings were the basis, has been modiﬁed into building models,
which are the new focal point. When planning in 3D, more information is added
to the building model. This information can be extracted and incorporated at
the construction site [Bundgaard et. al, 2012]. This thesis will aim to uncover
if information created before the main project is started, can be included in the
design model.

1. Introduction
”Information is a source of learning.
But unless it is
organized, processed, and available to the right people in
a format for decision making, it is a burden, not a beneﬁt”
- William Pollard [Liping, 2016]
This quote by William Pollard describes one of the biggest problems in the con-
struction industry. If all the information in a construction project is not organized
and sorted correctly, it will not beneﬁt the construction process. Processing in-
formation is a main theme in this thesis and will be the focus of the analysis.
Information is deﬁned as meaningful data in the form of text, numbers, model
data etc. [Lund-Poulsen, 2012]. In the analysis of outline proposals in Chapter 6,
extracting information is one of the main purposes.
1.1
The Problem Statement
In the problem statement the overall goals and purposes for the project are pre-
sented as relevant questions that are to be answered in the thesis. The problem
statement is the basis for the scope of the project and will lead to an analysis,
which will then lead to the problem solving. On the basis of the deﬁned problems
a solution will be presented in this thesis.
• How can information from the Outline Proposals be reused later on in the
planning phases of construction?
– How much information is available and what is the quality of the in-
formation?
– Does the Outline Proposals obey the existing speciﬁcations?
– Does Outline Proposals vary in quality, content and composition?
– How can Outline Proposals be presented so that information is easier
to extract?
1.2
Delimitation of the Thesis
BIM is in most cases ﬁrst implemented in the late design phase, but a lot of infor-
mation is already generated in the building program and in the outline proposal.
This information could be re-used in the later phases. This thesis explores if the
information can be deﬁned as Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) objects and im-
plemented in the Building Information Model. By implementing the information
it would be possible to use the information for calculations and model checking
later on in the production phase.

2.4
Metadata
All of the outline proposals that are provided are unnamed and are described
by Metadata instead. To identify the information classiﬁcation of information, a
common language is used. The type of information that is used for classiﬁcation
is called metadata set. A metadata set for documents entails a lot of informa-
tion divided into four diﬀerent categories according to bips. The categories are
Project, Company, Subject and Document. Not all of the categories are ﬁlled in
when handling a document [Lund-Poulsen et. al, 2012].
Metadata is deﬁned as data about data, in other words information about the
document’s information. A metadata set constitutes a subset of metadata and is
made up by a number of metadata elements that is used for classiﬁcation as can
be seen in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Metadata - [Lund-Poulsen et. al, 2012]
A metadata element is constituted by a metadata name and an associated meta-
data value, which describes the quality of a document. A metadata name describes
what the metadata values are about. The metadata value is a value, which in
many cases can be chosen from a value list. The value lists for metadata values
can be either open or bound. If the metadata is bound it means that the meta-
data value can only be a value that is predeﬁned. This is often an advantage for

2.5
Interview
The interview is used as method for gaining a qualitative perspective of the outline
proposals. When the qualitative research interview is used, it is used to investi-
gate people’s reasons for the actions they have done, but also to investigate the
intention that was behind the decisions made [Kvale et. al, 2014]. In this case,
why the engineers chose to make the outline proposal as they did. To achieve the
best interviews it is important to make preceding preparations. The underlying
basis for the interviews and the way they are conducted depends on the knowledge
obtained beforehand and the issues that are to be disclosed. The knowledge ob-
tained or lack thereof are what determines the area that needs to be investigated
through interviews, but also how the questions have to be asked [Kvale et. al,
2014].
Steiner Kvale, a professor in qualitative research, developed the seven phases of
interviews, which is the method used in this thesis. The seven phases can be seen
below [Kvale et. al, 2014].
• Thematization - Phrase the purpose of the investigation and the theme that
needs to be studied
• Design - Plan the design of the investigation before the interviews takes place
bearing in mind the information that needs to be retrieved
• Interview - Complete the interview on the basis of a interview guide with a
reﬂect on the knowledge that is searched for
• Transcription - Prepare the material from the interview so that it can be used
in the analysis
• Analysis - On the basis of the interviews determine the methods that should
be used for the analysis of the interviews
• Veriﬁcation - Determine the validity, reliability and the generalisability of
the interview results
• Reporting - Communicate the results of the interviews and the chosen meth-
ods
The seven phases help to obtain order in the interview process and create work
process to get full beneﬁt of the interview. The seven phases has been used in
this thesis except for the Transcription which has not been conducted.

and resources [bips, 2013].

must support the diﬀerent discipline activities they are included in. This could
be visualisation, generation of drawings, generation of part lists and exchange to
other programs. Geometric objects must have a Property Data Set that entails
information about the object [bips, 2013]. In Figure 4.3 the indicators for the

struction projects. With the ﬁle-sharing format it is possible to share drawings
and models independent of the software program used to create them. This means
that in practice an architect, an engineer and a contractor can share their model
data in IFC format no matter what individual software format they use [bips,

other objects. IFC includes both physical object like walls, windows and ventila-

6.2
ICT-Regulations (IKT Bekendtgørelsen)
The ICT-Regulations is the foundation for the digital implementation in construc-
tion projects. It consist of speciﬁc standards and tools for the client to use. It
was created to ensure a standardised foundation for the digital work in construc-
tion. It is mandatory to use in all public funded construction projects, but not
in private projects. In paragraph 6 of the ICT-Regulations the usage of digital
building models are deﬁned. The client must ensure the following.
• There are made demands on the building model’s structure and information
content according to the project’s size and character and complexity.
• The number of visualisations and placement of these are determined accord-
ing to the project’s size and character and complexity.
• Object based building models must be delivered in IFC format.
6.2.1
Demands for the Model Structure and Information
The model must be object based, so that the model can be used for analysis and
data extraction. The demands for the object based model structure entail the
deﬁnition of the spaces, to ensure that the space requirements are met. It is also
a requirement that the model must be presented with its surrounding buildings
and nature. The client must provide the terrain model.
The purpose of deﬁning the model’s information level is to ensure that the model
contains a certain degree of detail, but also to ensure that the model does not
contain to much information in this early stage of construction. In the early stages
the focus of the model must be the architecture, functions, function correlation,
spaciousness, lighting, energy and indoor climate.
For construction objects it
will often be suﬃcient with a low level of detail whereas for space objects it will
be necessary to have demands for the area, volume, purpose and shape. When
determining the level of detail it is recommended that standards developed by
bips and cuneco are used.

2. ID Code
Each requirement is attributed a ID Code based on one of the Requirement Hi-

not exist in the Requirement Hierarchy.
7.2.2.3
3. Data Type
When working with the requirements it is important to assess the data type.
In this project the data type can be one of three, Single-value, Multi-value or
Alphabetic-value. A Single-value requirement can have only one value or reference
for each category. Multi Value requirements can have multiple values or intervals
for each category. An alphabetic value is a value written with words instead of
numbers. An example of a single-value could be the air change rate with a value
of 2.48 l/s/m2. An example of a Multi-value could be a temperature interval for
a speciﬁc room, for instance 18-21◦C. An example of an Alphabetic-value could
be the speciﬁc brand of a ventilation aggregate or a Danish Standard used in
calculations, for instance DS 452 used for technical insulation.
7.2.2.4
4. Value
To all the Single-values and Multi-values a numeric value is included in the
database. This numeric value could be a Single-value or Multi-value as presented
above. The requirements that have an Alphabetic-value have no value in this
column. In the database created for the requirements the alphabetic values are
not included in the value column.

instance if a maximum CO2 concentration is mentioned on the basis of an indoor
climate standard that must be met. An Important requirement is used when the
value can be changed without major consequences, for instance the air change
rate. Less Important requirements are used when a requirement has no connec-
tions to other requirements or has any inﬂuence on the quality of the building.
For instance the colour of the bathroom walls.

Level of Detail
Interviewee 1 and 2 both mention that they try to deliberately minimize the details
in the outline proposals in order to save time and money.
”We always try to optimize the project material presented in the outline
proposals, we don’t make a 3D model unless it is necessary for the design
process. We always try to cut to the bone in our consulting delivery” -
Interviewee 1
”We try to save money on the projects by keeping the level of detail as low
as possible. We do this also to have ﬂexible solutions. If we do to many
calculations in the early stages we have a tendency to be very ﬁxed on the
solutions we designed, but often changes are made and we have to do our
calculations again and create a new solution” - Interviewee 2
Furthermore Interviewee 2 also mentions that the decision on the level of detail
also depends a lot on the consultant’s experience.
”The level of detail also depends a lot on the experience of the engineer,
an engineer who have been on many projects often has a better feeling of
the minimum required information that has to be the outline proposals, so
that a minimum of time is wasted on excessive calculations and solutions”
- Interviewee 2
Interviewee 3 has diﬀerent approach to deciding the level of detail. For them it
depends more on their involvement in the project and the contract form.
”The level of detail of the information in the outline proposals vary ac-
cording to the speciﬁc project. It also depends on the contract type, for
instance Project 1 is a turnkey contract, were we hand of the project to
the Turnkey contractor after the outline proposals, and our level of detail
is therefor limited. If we ourself are consulting we would often put more
detail into the creation of the outline proposals” - Interviewee 3
In the analysis of the outline proposals it was concluded that the level of detail
varied very little between the projects. It is clear that the consulting engineers
have the general opinion that no excessive work is to be done to the outline pro-
posals. The consulting architect had the somewhat same opinion, but furthermore
also pointed out that if the project is turnkey contracting they wont invest a lot
of time in creating details, since the contractor will take over the project just after
the outline proposals have been created.

In this chapter the overall question from the Problem Statement, presented in
Section 1.1 is answered.
How can information from the Outline Proposals be
reused later on in the planning phase of the construc-
tion?
It has been uncovered during the work on the thesis that the information worth
bringing to the later phases are requirements set by the client and the consulting
architect and consulting engineer. It is important for the quality of the construc-
tion project that the original requirements set by the Client are not forgotten
between the Programming and Design Phase. In the analysis in Chapter 7 it was
found that between the building program and the outline proposals more require-
ments were added and the level of detail of the requirements were increasing. All
of these requirements should be accommodated in the ﬁnal construction project.
It is therefor important to ﬁnd a way for the requirements to be accessible in an
easy way throughout all the phases of the construction. The solution presented
in this chapter is a framework solution, meaning it is not fully ready to be imple-
mented in the AEC industry, but it is however an idea based solution that could
be the foundation of an actual solution. The framework solution is based on the
idea of having a link between the requirements and the design solutions. This
type of solution has been presented before.
In 2005 Arto Kiviniemi presented a Ph.D project which has been used as inspi-
ration to this solution. His focus was on retrieving requirements from building
programs and linking them to the Design Model. In 2008 Niels Treldal presented
a master thesis, which was a further development of the Kiviniemi’s project, but
with a larger focus on implementing IFC Speciﬁcations in the solution.
The general idea presented in Kiviniemi, 2005 and Treldal, 2008 is to have a so-
called Requirements Model that is connected with the Design Model by using
IFC. In Figure 9.0.1 the principle behind the framework solution can be seen.

9.1
Conceptual Requirements Model
The basic idea of a Requirements Model is to have a common database where all
the requirements are gathered in one place. It is not possible to have a link directly
to the document. The requirements must be deﬁned in a Requirements Model so
they can be linked to a Design Model. The solution only works if there is a build-
ing model that the Requirements Model can be linked to. Traditional drawings
cannot be interpreted by the software. The Requirement Models presented in this
section is intended to be the basis for a software development [Kiviniemi, 2005].
The Requirements Model is separated from the Design Model for a number of dif-
ferent reasons. First of all the structure and content of the Requirements Model
is diﬀerent from the Design Model. Furthermore the Requirements Model has to
be linked to diﬀerent versions of the Design Model, in case the design is altered
drastic. There also has to be a guarantee that if an object is deleted in the De-
sign Model, the requirement still exists in the Requirements Model, so that the
requirement is not forgotten [Kiviniemi, 2005].
In Figure 9.1.1 the basic principle for the linking between the Requirements Model
and the Design Model can be seen with the subsets of requirements.

Requirements’ Elements in IFC Speciﬁca-

A classic Design Model, for example a Revit Model, has certain limitations when
talking about creating a direct link between the Requirements Model and the
Design Model. This means that the Design Model can be changed without any
notice, each requirement must have its own separate link with the design, which
in large projects can mean many links. Finally it is a challenge that the Design
Model might be really basic when the Requirements Model is created. Therefore
IFC Speciﬁcations are used as a helping tool that can describe what is going to
be available in the Design Model [Kiviniemi, 2005].
In Kiviniemi, 2005 the IFC format was IFC 2x2. Today the newest format is IFC4,
so a lot of the challenges Kiviniemi had with IFC are no longer relevant. The new
IFC format can incorporate 4D and 5D model exchanges [buildingSMART, 2013].
In Section 4.3 the general theory behind IFC Speciﬁcations were presented. In

It is important that the Requirements Model is created simultaneously with the

a platform where the Project Managers can ensure that the chosen solutions meet

the exact same value as the requirement and when a solution can be within an

As mentioned in the analysis of the outline proposals, the identiﬁcation and clas-

the project, but also with purpose of listing the requirements.

the classiﬁcation used by Kiviniemi, 2005. It was found that the identiﬁcation sys-

found in the analysed outline proposals. It is therefore a possibility to either cre-
ate a new ID system or to use one of the existing systems. The existing systems
could be either the CCS Classiﬁcation system which are compatible with IFC4
or the Data Dictionary from Building Smart which is also compatible with the
IFC4 Speciﬁcations. In the solution presented in Chapter 9 both of these options
are presented. If the solution is realized it would be worth considering if a new
systems with CCS Classiﬁcation as the underlying basis is worth creating. CCS
classiﬁcation would be the basis but the ID code system would be directed more
towards requirements based on functions.

gories presented in Kiviniemi, 2005. Here six diﬀerent categories were presented;

11.2
The Outline Proposals Process
The process of creating the outline proposals is collaboration between the client,
sometimes the client design advisor, the consulting engineers and the consulting
architects. From the interviews it became clear that the communication between
the parties in generally was described as good and that conﬂicts rarely occurred.
It also became clear from the interviews that the consulting engineers used the
Service Descriptions for Building and Planning as guidelines for the content of
the outline proposals. The interviews also revealed that the consulting engineers
deliberately only included the minimum amount of detail that was required for the
solutions presented in the outline proposal. This included the drawing material
were it was described that the 3D model was only created if absolute necessary,
otherwise they try to complete the outline proposals with 2D AutoCAD drawings.
This creates challenges for implementing the proposed solution in the conceptual
design phases. The solution requires that a Design Model is available in order for
it to work.
The three diﬀerent speciﬁcations that were presented in Chapter 6 all contained
guidelines for the content of the outline proposals and the extent of the drawing
material. The three presented speciﬁcations was chosen to be included in the
analysis, since it was those most relevant in the Danish construction projects. It
could be interesting to see how other speciﬁcations set requirements to the content
of the outline proposals, and compare the speciﬁcations. For instance the Finish

return to the outline proposal when starting a new project to ﬁnd inspiration in
the solutions presented in the old outline proposals. The goal with the presented
solution was a tool for the participants in the construction project to use to go
back and look at the content of the outline proposals throughout the entire con-

11.3
Challenges in Including the Requirements
in BIM
The framework solution presented in Chapter 9 is in the idea phase of the solu-
tions. Meaning that the solution is presented with ideas and not a direct plan
for implementation. It is recommended that the solution is only implemented in
fairly large projects where the extra work it entails can pay oﬀ. In smaller projects
the work of creating the database and making the linkage will not be beneﬁcial.
As mentioned earlier one of the main problems with the solution is the lack of
Design Models in the early stages of conceptual design phase where most of the
requirements are created. It is therefore recommended that the work on the De-
sign Models is started earlier in the construction process. It was uncovered in the
interviews that the creation of 3D models were postponed intentionally in order to
save time and thereby money. It therefore requires a change of work procedure at
the consulting engineers where the 3D model is implemented in the early stages of
the construction in order to create the linkage between the Requirements Model

As mentioned earlier it is necessary that an External Application is created before

It is however worth considering if the external solution can be implemented in

As mentioned earlier it is also worth exterminating if other software programs

will only be recommended for smaller projects, since the programming would be
to comprehensive.
There is no doubt that in the beginning of implementing the solutions it will be
a time consuming job to identity and classify the diﬀerent requirements. It would
be recommended that the parties involved in the construction project is trained
in identifying the requirements and adding them to the Requirements Model. It
is also important that they are trained in the External Application so that the
implementation of the solution is as smooth as possible. It is evaluated that the
process of identifying the requirements will become easier as time goes, and will
also after a period of time become a ﬁxed part of the engineer, architect or project
managers role. It is however believed that if the solution is developed further and
implemented it will in time create better building projects where there is better
correlation between the requirements presented in the conceptual design phase
and the ﬁnal solutions that are chosen. It is also worth examining if over time
the clash control of the models and the quality assurance of the building can be
implemented in the solution.

Conclusions
In this chapter the results from the thesis will be presented. It was the overall
goal of the thesis to investigate if information from the outline proposals could
be reused later in the planning phases of construction. By analysing the provided
outline proposals, basic understanding of the content of the outline proposals was
obtained. On the basis of this a framework solution was created. This solution
was based on linking the information found in the outline proposals with the De-
sign Model so that the information from the outline proposals would accessible
throughout the entire construction process.
By using a comparative method the eight provided outline proposals was analysed
for the content, the layout and their drawing material. Furthermore a number of
interviews were conducted with diﬀerent participants from Project 1 in order to
gain a better insight on how outline proposals are created. In the analysis of the
content of the outline proposals the requirements were identiﬁed and classiﬁed for
six diﬀerent parameters. A total of 125 HVAC requirements were identiﬁed. The
requirements were presented in a Requirement Database in Appendix B.
In the analysis the outline proposals was analysed for their content compared to
the Service Description for Building and Planning (Ydelsesbeskrivelse). The re-
sults of the analysis showed that all the projects, except for Project 2, 7 and 8,
obeyed the content of the speciﬁcations. The reasons for this were deemed to the
size of the projects. Project 2, 7 and 8 were all categorized as smaller projects
and the content of the outline proposals was limited. The analysis also contained
a section were the layout of the outline proposals was analysed. It was found that
a good outline proposal has a combination of body text and tables and bullet list.
The analyses also covered the drawing material available. The drawing material
was limited and only available for Project 1, Project 2 and Project 5. The material
was analysed for level of detail and it was compared to the speciﬁcations presented
in the theory. It was found that Project 1 and Project 2 obeyed the speciﬁcations
set in the Service Description for Building and Planning (Ydelsesbeskrivelse) and
that Project 5 did not. Since non of the drawing material contained 3D models it
was hard to compare the material to the ICT-Regulations (IKT Bekendtgørelse).
All three projects obeyed the speciﬁcations in the 3D Work Description (3D Ar-
bejdsmetode).
91
==PAGE:104==
12. Conclusions
The results from the analysis of the outline proposals showed that most of the
HVAC requirements have the object category space or system. Most of the re-
quirements have a level of detail 1 or 2. The data type is by far mostly single-values
rather than multi-values or alphabetic values. On the basis of the results from
the analysis two diﬀerent solutions were presented.
Solution 1 described how the requirements identiﬁed in the outline proposals can
be linked to the Design Model. By using a BIM server the Requirements Model
and the Design Model can be linked together, and an External Application can be
used to manage the conformity between the two models. In the linking between
the two IFC Speciﬁcations should be used.
Solution 2 described how the ideal outline proposals should be designed so that
the outline proposal both can present the solutions to the client but also so the
requirements are easy to identify. The outline proposals should have a profound
table of content and the text should consist of body text with tables and bullet
list.
