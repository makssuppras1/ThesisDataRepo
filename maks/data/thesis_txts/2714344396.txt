Summary
This thesis concerns the analysis of pilot plant data from waste incineration CO2 cap-
ture. The thesis is separated into nine chapters as summarized below:
In Chapter 1, an introduction to waste incineration and post-combustion CO2 capture
(PCC) is given. Waste incineration is compared to other modes of waste disposal and
principles of industrial incineration are conceptualized. Several technologies for PCC
are briefly introduced, among which amine scrubbing is explained in greater detail, as
it pertains to this report. The Amager Bakke plant is lastly introduced along with the
ARC ambition to implement CO2 capture at the plant.
In Chapter 2, a description of the Mobile Testing Unit (MTU) is presented.
The
description takes basis in a process flow diagram, from which the process of CO2 capture
within the unit is conceptualized.
In Chapter 3, theory relevant to amine scrubbing and MTU data analysis is described.
Amine reactivity is presented and choice of MEA for operation is discussed. Theory
for estimating amine solvent CO2 loading along with calculations related to mass- and
energy flows are furthermore presented.
In Chapter 4, sensor calibration experiments are presented and their results are dis-
cussed in relation to uncertainty of state estimation.
In Chapter 5, the procedure of transforming MTU raw data to series of experimental
steady-states is presented, along with the operating points of investigated experiments.
MTU data is furthermore compared to ARC measurements for cross-validation.
In Chapter 6, mass-balance- and steady-state analysis is presented, and the results are
discussed.
In Chapter 7, operating points from steady-state analysis are up-scaled to assess the
necessary capacity for obtaining ARC CO2 reduction targets.
In Chapter 8, results of the report are summarized and findings are concluded.

In Chapter 9, future work is presented in relation to further process intensification for
CO2 capture amine scrubbing.

Problem statement
This thesis is a conclusion of a 30 ECTS points master’s project carried out in the Cen-
ter for Energy Resources Engineering (CERE) at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU).
CCS (Carbon Capture and Storage) is a growing field of research with the purpose of
significantly reducing CO2 emissions and potentially create negative emissions. The
technology development is intended for small, medium, and large industry. CO2 is ex-
tracted from exhaust gas (e.g. combustion), industrial gas (biogas, natural gas, syngas
etc.) or an air type system (direct air capture, fermentation). It is transported in pipes
and potentially used in products (e.g. biofuels) or stored underground e.g. in old gas
reservoir, aquifers etc.
A mobile test unit (MTU) was commissioned and operated to demonstrate that carbon
capture at ARC can be done at net zero energy consumption. This is ground-breaking,
as the energy needed to capture carbon is approx. 25% of the lower heat value in the
waste. It was the aim of the project to reduce the net energy consumption needed of
the carbon capture process by focusing on energy optimizations of every step of the
carbon capture process and integrate it into the district heating production at ARC,
to achieve the cheapest possible CO2 capture. The outcome of the project will be a
complete pilot-scale CC plant that will be constructed at ARC, integrated with the
waste-to-energy plant and optimized for net zero energy consumption.
In order to demonstrate a reduction in the energy consumption, several new and ad-
vanced configurations were tested in a conventional amine scrubbing technology. Ad-
ditionally, these configurations were also tested for various solvents, which are out of
scope for this project. These configurations were meant to improve the performance of
the scrubber while simultaneously lowering the overall energy consumption. The evalu-
ation of the new configurations relies on precise and accurate measurements of physical
and chemical properties of the solvent.
iii
==PAGE:5==
The task of this project is to analyze the data obtained through experimental campaigns
using MEA (30wt%) with different process configurations to improve performance and
lower energy consumption. Further, data obtained for advanced process configurations
such as rich solvent recycle and rich solvent splitting will be analyzed and performance
compared to the MTU BaseCase.
Learning objectives of the project:
1. Collect and overview literature, which discuss CO2 capture in relation to Post
Combustion Carbon Capture
2. Present theory on energy and mass balances in a pilot scale absorption unit
3. Determine mass balances based on the data obtained from experiments
4. Calibration analysis of probes and flow-meters
5. In depth analysis of the BaseCase performance
6. Comparison of BaseCase results with different advanced configurations
7. Disseminate the results in form of a report and presentation

1
Introduction
The UN climate panel has provided a target of lowering CO2 emissions by 45% before
2030 for all member countries, and a final goal of net zero emissions by 2050 [1]. As
a result of political pressure to reach these goals, interest in CO2-capture technologies
for heavy industry has been increasing in recent years. Waste generation is inevitable
in industrial societies, and in Denmark alone, 9.1 million tonnes of waste is produced
every year [2]. A total of 27 dedicated Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plants are operational
in Denmark, disposing approximately 25% of the total waste generated through incin-
eration processes. As of 2020, Municipal Solid Waste Incineration (MSWI) generated
around 6% of the danish electricity supply and around 24% of the heat supply through
WtE processes [2][3]. It is estimated, that the Danish waste sector produces 2.9 million
tonnes of CO2 annually, and as a result, various legislative initiatives has been made
to develop and implement CO2-capture technologies to reduce emissions [4]. In this
project, process intensification of CO2-capture using amine scrubbing is investigated
based on pilot plant data obtained from the WtE plant Amager Bakke in Denmark.
1.1
Waste incineration
Waste incineration is the process of burning combustible waste. Industrial waste in-
cineration provides hygienic disposal combined with energy recovery from combustion.
The resulting flue gas is cleaned from particulates and pollutants through air pollu-
tion control (APC) to meet regulatory demands. Heat and material can furthermore
be recovered for economic and environmental benefit. The primary waste stream for
incineration is Municipal Solid Waste (MSW), which consists of components such as
organic matter, plastics and batteries obtained from municipal entities [5].
1
==PAGE:10==
1.1.1
Waste incineration in Europe
MSWI is implemented in 500 European plants, processing around 100 million tonnes
of waste per year as of 2019 [5]. In the last decades, incineration as a means of waste
disposal, has been a growing sector in Europe. One of the main contributors for growth
has been the European Landfill Directive of 1999, restricting waste landfilling to reduce
pollution of groundwater, soil and air [2]. As a result, municipal waste treated (mass
per capita) has increased by 97% in the period from 1995 to 2020, and has to a large
extent become a favorable mode of disposal for waste which cannot be easily recycled
nor composted [6].
Figure 1: Distribution of waste treatment technology in Europe, kg per capita, in period
1995-2020 [6].
1.2
Principles of industrial incineration
The process of incineration can be divided into the sub-processes of drying, pyrolysis
and gasification. As the waste is initially heated it releases its moisture. Through fur-
2
==PAGE:11==
ther heating, in the absence of oxygen, volatiles are released from the organic material.
The volatile species are predominantly CO, CO2, H2, H2O and CH4 [5]. The com-
bustible volatiles can subsequently be gasified in the presence of oxygen. The energy of
combustion is released during this sub-process. For industrial purposes, a mechanical
grate unit is predominantly used, in which waste is transported on a conveyor belt
with air injection from beneath the grate openings to facilitate combustion [5]. The
process of a grate boiler combustion unit is seen conceptualized in Figure 2. In the
combustion unit, waste is transported along the grate while undergoing described ther-
mal processes, producing flue gas and ash with partial air injection. The flue gas is
then passed through a secondary combustion zone, before entering a boiler for steam
generation.
Figure 2: Conceptual process visualization of a grate boiler combustion unit [7].
1.2.1
Incineration pollutant handling
From incineration processes, the waste contents of sulfur, chlorine and nitrogen are
predominantly converted into SO2, HCl and nitrogen oxides in the flue gas [5]. Metallic
3
==PAGE:12==
oxides are furthermore present in the flue gas as particulates [5]. For industrial purposes
it is often necessary to implement effective pollutant handling for waste incineration
plants to meet legislative requirements [5].
1.3
Post-Combustion CO2-Capture
Various methods of Post-Combustion CO2-Capture (PCC) has in recent years been
investigated thoroughly [8]. The prevailing technologies of academic investigation has
been solvent-based absorption, solid adsorption and membrane separation [8]. Among
these modes of capture, solvent-based absorption, specifically amine scrubbing, has
shown great promise as it provides ease of process integration along with cost-efficient
capture [9]. As a result, several pilot- and demonstration scale implementations has
been realized in existing plants for PCC [10] [9].
1.3.1
Physical separation technologies
In technologies such as solid adsorption and membrane processes, the physical properties
of CO2 are utilized to obtain separation. In pressure-swing solid adsorption, the flue
gas passes through a column containing adsorbent with high affinity for CO2 at high
pressure, selectively binding CO2 to the solid surface. After adsorption, CO2 is desorbed
by lowering pressure and released in a transport gas [11]. In membrane separation,
selective retention of species occur when passing the flue gas over a porous membrane.
Based on the physical and chemical properties of the desired retained species, a specific
membrane can be designed [12].
1.3.2
Amine scrubbing
In contrast to competing technologies, aqueous amine scrubbing utilizes the chemically
reacting nature of CO2, forming carbonates with water and carbamates with amines,
to increase the driving force of separation.
In this way, efficient CO2-capture can
4
==PAGE:13==
be obtained by providing a solvent-gas interface using an absorption column unit. As
disposal of amine solvent is economically unfeasible it is necessary to provide subsequent
regeneration to facilitate solvent recovery [13]. The affinity of CO2 in amine solvent
decreases with temperature, and it is therefore possible to desorb CO2 by increasing
temperature. This method of absorption and desorption is called a temperature swing
process. After sufficient desorption of CO2, the solvent is regenerated, and can be reused
for absorption. The process concept of temperature swing absorption for CO2-capture
is conceptualized in the Girbotol process as seen in Figure 3:
Figure 3: Simplified flowsheet for the Girbotol process [14].
The process can be separated into the following column sub-units:
Absorption column: The flue gas enters at the absorber bottom and is brought
in contact with counter-flowing amine solvent. To increase contact area, the column is
filled with packing elements, which the amine solvent wets. The absorption unit is usu-
5
==PAGE:14==
ally operated between 40 and 60°C to maintain acceptable chemical kinetics along with
sufficient thermodynamic affinity for CO2 in the solvent phase. After passing through
the column, cleaned flue gas leaves at the top while amine solvent rich in CO2 leaves
at the bottom. As amine-CO2 absorption is exothermic, heat is generated in the process.
Stripping column: The CO2-rich solvent is fed into the top of a stripping column for
regeneration. Column liquid enters a reboiler at the column bottom, providing heat for
CO2 desorption. The reboiler also provides steam, diluting CO2 to keep it below equi-
librium partial pressure. The stripping column is typically operated between 100 and
140°C [13]. The CO2-rich gas stream leaves at the top of the column where it passes a
condenser and reflux drum to recover now condensed water vapor. The lean amine sol-
vent lastly passes a cross-stream heat-exchanger for heat recovery, and is subsequently
cooled, before re-entering the absorption column top.
1.3.3
Storage and utilization of captured CO2
The resulting CO2-rich stream from capture provides several opportunities for storage
and utilization. As a baseline for mitigating emissions, underground storage has been
proposed as a solution [15]. This solution is particularly of interest in Denmark, as
underground storage has been deemed viable both on land and at sea due to favorable
geological conditions [16]. The Danish research institute GEUS has estimated an un-
derground capacity of 22 Gt CO2 in the Danish underground corresponding to between
500 and 1000 years of national emissions at its current level [16]. Another opportu-
nity is utilization of captured CO2 in industry. Among direct utilizations are use in
industrial greenhouses for plant-growth, algae farming, concrete production and food
conservation [10]. CO2 can furthermore be utilized indirectly as a reactant for produc-
tion of various chemical products through hydrogenation. The predominant products
of hydrogenation are methane, methanol, dimethyl ether and formic acid [10]. These
chemicals can be used as fuels, serve as baseline components for further synthesis, or
6
==PAGE:15==
be directly utilized in industry.
1.4
Amager Bakke Waste-to-Energy plant
Amager Bakke is a waste-to-energy plant located in the residential suburb to Copen-
hagen. The plant was established in 2017 by Amager Ressource Center (ARC) and
has since received waste from around 600.000 residents and 68.000 businesses in the
five surrounding municipalities [17]. The plant provides electricity and district heating
from cogeneration along with recycled materials and clean water condensate. The fa-
cility has an estimated thermal efficiency of 95%, based on higher-heating values, and
as a result, Amager Bakke has been deemed one of the most efficient waste-to-energy
plants in the world [17].
1.4.1
Plant description
The plant can be seen as an overall two-step process. A Process Flow Diagram of the
plant is provided in Figure 4
Cogeneration: The waste is initially incinerated in a furnace, generating flue gas. The
flue gas enters a boiler, providing heat for steam generation. The steam then passes
through turbines for expansion, from which electricity- and heat are cogenerated. Ash
from the furnace and boilers are collected for repurposing as construction materials [17].
Flue gas treatment: After leaving the boiler, flue gas is passed through an elec-
trostatic precipitator, providing preliminary removal of particulates in the gas. The
flue gas is then treated by selective catalytic reduction (SCR), providing a reduction
of nitrogen oxides to elementary nitrogen by injection of ammonia in the presence of
limestone catalyst. A two-stage scrubbing unit subsequently targets acidic compounds
HCl, HF and SO2 along with heavy metals not captured in the precipitator. The flue
7
==PAGE:16==
gas then enters a two-stage condensation unit coupled with centrifugation for conden-
sation heat recovery and gypsum precipitation, respectively. The obtained condensate
undergoes wastewater treatment before discharge into the sea. The now low-heat flue
gas is then passed through a system of heat exchange for further cooling, providing
extended water condensation. The clean condensate can be reused in earlier plant pro-
cesses. The flue gas is then slightly reheated before finally being emitted through the
stack.
Figure 4: Process flow diagram of the Amager Bakke WtE facility [17].
1.4.2
CO2-capture at Amager Bakke
A Mobile Testing Unit (MTU) was constructed by Union Engineering A/S in collab-
oration with CERE DTU and Dansk Gasteknisk Center. The MTU operated in the
period August 2021 to May 2022 at the Amager Bakke plant. The MTU serves as part
of the project BIOCO2 scheduled to run from 2016-2023 and funded by the Energy
8
==PAGE:17==
Technology Development and Demonstration Programme (EUDP) [18]. The project
has the two following objectives:
1. To turn otherwise emitted CO2 into a valuable green product, namely BioCO2,
which can be sold as an additional commercial product by the biogas producer.
The quality of the BioCO2 should be high enough for food, biofuel, medicine
production and welding purposes.
2. To reduce the energy consumption of the amine adsorption-based biogas upgrad-
ing technique by up to 45% by introducing new additives to the process.
During the project, MTU integration at three different industrial sites has been realized
to obtain insights into process operation and intensification for both biogas upgrading
and general CO2 emission reduction. Since biogas production is not a part of the waste
treatment process at Amager Bakke, this operational period solely focused on the sec-
ondary project objective in treatment of the plant flue gas.
The Amager Bakke ownership organisation, ARC, has put forward the eventual goal of
capturing 500 kt CO2 annually in its plant. As part of this endeavour, the MTU serves
as a preliminary study in capture capabilities of amine scrubbing technology. The MTU
implementation at Amager Bakke aims at achieving net-zero-energy consumption for
capture under certain process specifications. If this goal is met, a demonstration plant
is planned for construction in 2023.

2.1
Process concept
The concept of the MTU is analogous to the Girbotol process presented in Section
1.3.2, as it utilizes thermal swing for CO2 absorption and subsequent desorption. An
absorption- and a stripping column are therefore in place containing 3 and 4 packing
beds, respectively. The inlet raw gas (F104) flows upwards through the absorption col-
umn in counter-current flow with lean amine solvent (F201). The cleaned gas (F106)
and rich amine solvent (F204) leave at the top and bottom of the absorption column,
respectively. Column dimensions can be found in Appendix A Figure 10. In the strip-
ping column, the rich solvent stream (F208) enters below the top column bed. Vapour,
heated by the reboiler, leaves at the stripper top (F110) rich in CO2. Lean solvent
leaves the column at the bottom (F209). A BaseCase setup for the MTU was operated,
upon which configurations were introduced to alter the process. Configurations are
indicated in Figure 5 as small dashed lines, and presented in Section 2.7.
2.2
Gas cleaning
Raw gas cleaning is obtained in the MTU from two operations. The flue gas initially
enters a knockout drum (F101) for condensation of potential liquid carry-over in the
gas. A blower is placed downstream (F102) to overcome pressure drop in the flue gas
path. An active carbon filter is implemented to remove, primarily, HCl and HF present
in the gas with the purpose of avoiding solvent degradation and system corrosion. After
saturation and active carbon filtering, the flue gas enters the absorption column bottom
(F104).
2.3
Cooling system
Several cooling operations are necessary in the process. Cooling is obtained by a water
system, indicated as long dashed lines in Figure 5. The system provides cooling for two
11
==PAGE:20==
process categories:
1. Solvent cooling
1.1 Lean solvent exiting the cross-flow heat-exchanger (F211) is cooled before
entering the absorption column top (F201). This operation is referred to as
trip cooling.
1.2 A fraction of rich solvent is cooled and recycled into the absorption column
(F205). This operation is only performed under the configuration of rich-
solvent recycle (RSR). This configuration is described in Section 2.7.
2. Gas cooling
2.1 CO2-rich vapour leaving the stripper column top (F110) is cooled before
entering a knock-out drum to for water condensation. The condensed water
is recycled back into the solvent flow at the absorber bottom. The cooled
gas, rich in CO2, leaves at the drum top (F111). This stream is referred to
as the produced gas and relates to the capture within the plant.
2.2 Cleaned gas leaving the absorption column top (F106) enters a washing tower
for direct cooling. The washing tower is not displayed in Figure 5 and will
be presented separately in Section 2.6.
The cooling water system is equipped with a circulation pump and a dry cooler to

Heat recovery
Two sub-processes are in place for heat recovery with the purpose of reducing heating-

lean solvent is cooled from the flashing process. Evaluation of LVC performance

experiments.

Washing tower

During Rich solvent splitting (RSS) a fraction of rich solvent bypasses the cross-exchanger

3.1
Amine-CO2 reactivity
The core principle of amine scrubbing is the increase of mass transfer driving force by
reaction of CO2 with amine in the solvent. Two primary mechanisms of CO2 reaction are
prevalent for all alkanolamine in aqueous solution: Amine-specific carbamate formation
(Reaction 1) and base-specific bicarbonate formation (Reactions 2-3) [20]:
AmH + CO2 ⇌AmCOO−+ H+
(1)
AmH + H2O ⇌AmH +

+ OH−
(2)
CO2 + OH−⇌HCO −
3

An amine solute will therefore, in general, provide fast reaction with CO2, if the rate
of carbamate formation is high and the amine exert strong basicity under operational

==PAGE:28==
conditions [13]. However, the reaction mechanism of carbamate formation is also detri-
mental to determine amine viability in carbon capture. Primary and secondary amines
react directly with CO2, forming carbamate with a buffer base for deprotonation [13].
This buffer is typically another amine molecule. The overall reaction for primary amines
is displayed below:
2 RNH2 + CO2 ⇌RNHCOO−+ RNH +
3
(4)
It is observed from the reaction, that two amine molecules are necessary to bind a single
CO2 molecule. In this way, the capacity of binding is theoretically 2:1 on a molar basis.
However, as a result of more direct reaction, these groups of amines provide relatively
fast carbamate formation kinetics [20]. Tertiary and sterically hindered amines, on the
other hand, react indirectly with CO2 in a base-catalyzed reaction [13]. The overall
reaction for tertiary amines is displayed below:
R3N( + H2O) + CO2 ⇌R3NH+ + HCO −
3
(5)
This path of reaction provides a slower carbamate formation but increases the capacity,
as more CO2 can be effectively bound per amine molecule [20]. In this way, a trade-off
is made between fast kinetics and capacity when choosing an amine for carbon-capture.
As a result, blends of amines has been suggested to provide sufficient kinetic rates,
along with increased capacity [13].

3.2
Choice of amine solvent
The choice of amine solvent is of utmost importance for thermal swing absorption. Dur-
ing the campaign at Amager Bakke, several amine species and solvent compositions were
investigated. This project, however, will focus solely on operation using the primary
amine MEA in a prepared solvent composition of 30wt% MEA and 70wt% H2O. In
CO2-capture research, MEA is by far the most thoroughly investigated amine. This is
because of its favourable capture properties, low cost and simple structure [9]. As a
result, it has become an industry standard with numerous pilot-scale implementations,
and sustained interest for full-scale implementation [21][22][23].
As eluded to in Section 3.1, no amine has been proven superior for CO2-capture. This is
because favourable kinetics, thermodynamics and operational conditions often come as
trade-offs. To justify the relevance of MEA, an overview of its strengths and weaknesses
are presented relative to other potential candidates:
Absorption-rate and capacity: MEA is a primary amine and will therefore re-
act directly with CO2, following Equation 4. This provides fast kinetics relative to
tertiary and hindered amines. MEA exerts a high equilibrium constant of protonation,
and therefore high basicity (Reaction 3). This indirectly relates to a high rate of car-
bamate formation (Reaction 1) [13]. MEA solutions furthermore exert relatively low
viscosities, enabling faster mass transport, and in turn, better kinetics. However, the
primary nature of the amine results in a low capacity, as less CO2 can be bound per
molecule of amine in contrast to secondary, and tertiary amines such as piperazine and
AMP.
Thermodynamics: MEA exerts a large enthalpic heat of protonation [13].
This
means that the affinity for CO2 (basicity) decreases more readily as a function of in-
creasing temperature. This property provides a more effective desorption of CO2 when
exposing the solvent to high temperatures in the stripping column.
21
==PAGE:30==
Operational conditions: One of the primary weaknesses of MEA is its high lev-
els of oxidation when exposed to high temperatures in the stripping column. MEA
degradation through oxidation has been shown to be catalyzed by metallic ions such as
Fe++ and Mn++, which may be present during operation in both the flue gas and from
corrosion within the system [13]. This results in constrained operating conditions in
stripping column, as temperatures above 120°C are found to produce significant degra-
dation [13]. In comparison, piperazine can be operated at around 160°C without critical
levels of degradation [13]. One of the primary strengths of MEA is its low bulk cost in
comparison to other viable amines. The lower cost of makeup solvent therefore plays a
significant role in economic assessment.

Heat-loss considerations
Heating was provided to the system by the reboiler. To assess heat-loss, experiments
were performed by MTU operators, in which water was cycled between the columns.
The system was enclosed, as no raw gas entered the absorption column, and steam
leaving the stripper top was completely condensed. Heat-flow differences of cooling
water across cooling units were estimated, from which its difference from reboiler duty
would make up heat-loss. It was assumed that heat-loss was primarily present in the
stripping column due to boiling point conditions. From experiments, heat-loss between
10 and 30% were obtained, dependent on reboiler duty, see Appendix B, Figure 69. The
results seemed inaccurate in describing thermal-swing operation, as active desorption
within the stripping column would provide a cooling effect, and therefore a reduction in
heat-loss. As the results seemed unreliable, heat-loss during operation was not included
in further system analysis.

Data handling
In this chapter, an overview of the operational campaign is provided, followed by the
process of transforming raw MTU data to series of steady-states. Experimental operat-
ing points for obtained steady-states are subsequently presented. Lastly, a comparison
between MTU- and ARC data is made for cross-validation of obtained measurements.
5.1
Operational overview
The first periods of stable operation for the MTU were obtained in late September
2021. As a result, effective experimental operation was performed from October to
campaign termination. During operation, the experimental protocol was divided into
day- and night-time runs. During the day, operators were present on site performing
maintenance and configuring the plant to run under desired experimental conditions.
Usually one steady-state was obtained during the day-time under continuous monitor-
ing. If successful, a pre-programmed protocol was implemented to perform a series of
experiments in the following night-time.
As the range of evaluated process configurations was vast, the scope of this project is
limited to investigating the BaseCase setup (Figure 5), and the specific configurations
presented in Section 2.7. The majority of BaseCase experiments were performed during
October. The majority of experiments under specific configurations were performed in
the first two weeks of November. BaseCase experimental series were carried out by
varying solvent flow under constant reboiler duty. In contrast, configuration experi-
ments were carried out by varying reboiler duty under constant solvent flow.

5.4
Assessing experimental steady-states
During a specific experiment it was of interest to assess in which sub-period the system
state could be deemed sufficiently steady. This was done by monitoring key variables
within the system during its timespan. Overall state stability was then assessed man-
ually, based on qualitative criteria. The primary external factor to observe was raw
gas probe measurements, as inlet raw gas composition was dependent on ARC plant
conditions, and therefore independent of MTU operation. The stability of raw gas flow
was secondly of importance as it indicated inflow of CO2 to the absorption column.
Steady-state of CO2 transport within the system was furthermore indicated by solvent
47
==PAGE:56==
flows, solvent densities and cleaned gas probe measurements. Since CO2 transport was
strongly dependent of heat flows, under both loading and stripping, stability of men-
tioned variables would therefore indicate steady conditions in the energy balance.
From start of a given experiment, an initial lag period was present, until the system had
stabilized at new process conditions. Since the process is inherently dynamic, variations
in the system state was observed during performed experiments. It was therefore of
interest to isolate the steady-state within a period, in which cyclic behaviours of vari-
ables were encapsulated, while showing no significant down- or upwards trends. Isolated
steady-states lasted between 30 minutes and 3 hours. An example of steady-state con-
ditions can be seen in Figure 27.

Conclusion
In this work, analysis of pilot plant data from waste incineration CO2-capture at Amager
Bakke was performed. The pilot plant utilized the process of amine scrubbing.
From sensor calibration it was concluded that measurement uncertainties below 5%
could be attributed to flow estimations within the system. Uncertainties were evaluated
with basis in calibration experiments and comparison with data provided by ARC.
A total of 55 experimental steady-states were obtained and analyzed. All experiments
were performed using solvent 30wt% MEA. 17 experiments were performed under pro-
cess configurations of the BaseCase design, specifically rich solvent recycle (RSR) and
rich solvent splitting (RSS). Experiments were performed for solvent flows in the range
160-370 kg/h and reboiler duties in the range 58-80 MJ/h. Stripper column pressure
was maintained at 0.85 barg for all experiments.
From BaseCase experiments, CO2 captures between 13 and 20 kg/h were achieved.
Capture was generally increased with reboiler duty, but also strongly dependent on
solvent flow. CO2 loadings of solvent streams were therefore of utmost importance for
achieving low energy-consumption within the process. From BaseCase experiments, a
minimum energy-consumption of 3.9 MJ/kg CO2 was obtained. A general trend for
low energy-consumption was stripping intensities around 0.27 MJ/kg solvent. Under
these conditions, lean- and rich solvent loadings of 0.14 and 0.45 mol CO2/mol MEA
were obtained, respectively. Process performance was furthermore strongly influenced
by the temperature of lean solvent entering the absorption column, as temperatures
below 35°C worsened absorption-rates significantly.
In comparison to BaseCase, RSR provided higher rich loadings for all investigated
steady-states.
As a result, energy-consumption was reduced by 4% for comparable
stripping efficiencies. Particularly favourable absorption was achieved for lean load-
ings below 0.20 mol CO2/mol MEA. At higher lean loadings, temperature-conditions
beneath the column feeding height were unfavourably low, stunting solvent loading.
99
==PAGE:108==
RSS provided lower temperatures at the stripping column top, increasing utilization of
steam latent heat. Increased by-pass provided favourable performance at lower stripping
intensities, achieving reductions in energy-consumption up to 6.4%. At higher stripping
intensities, favourability of increased by-pass diminished, as temperatures at the column
top were similar. In these cases, performance similar to BaseCase was achieved.
Up-scaling estimation for a favourable operating point was performed. It was concluded
that a demonstration plant could achieve 25% of the ARC capture target by utilizing
7.6% of the net-heat produced by Amager Bakke under specific operating conditions.

==PAGE:109==
9
Future work
From analysis it was found that both RSR and RSS generally obtained better oper-
ational performance than BaseCase. Since no experiments were performed utilizing
both configurations, this would be of clear interest. For RSR, investigation of perfor-
mance as a function of recycle cooling would also be of interest, as favourable operation
for higher lean loadings may be achieved at higher recycle temperatures.
RSS was
furthermore not investigated thoroughly. Tipping point conditions, in which heating
penalty outweighed steam efficiency, is therefore also of interest for investigation, as it
would clarify the operating space, in which RSS is viable. The MTU was furthermore
equipped with several configurations outside the scope of this project. These include
intercooling in the absorber and interheating in the stripper. For intercooling, a frac-
tion of solvent within the column is cooled and re-injected. This provides degrees of
freedom for process optimization, as the temperature profile within the column can
be manipulated independently of other unit operations. Under interheating, solvent
within the column can be additionally heated at given heights, by liquid withdrawal
and re-injection, manipulating the temperature profile. This can furthermore be done
by utilizing heat from the lean stream, serving as heat-recovery. From literature, reduc-
tions in reboiler duty up to 11% and 5% has been demonstrated for intercooling and
interheating, respectively, under specific operating conditions [19].
For heat recovery operations, the MTU is furthermore equipped with lean vapour com-
pression (LVC) and mechanical vapour recompression (MVR) capabilities. As described
in Section 2, LVC utilizes heat from the lean vapour by flashing the stream, followed by
recompression of the vapour before recirculation. From literature, a reduction in reboiler
duty of up to 11% has been demonstrated for LVC under specific operating conditions
[19]. For MVR, vapour leaving the stripper top is directly contacted with cooling water.
Heat recovered in the cooling water can be flashed, and vapour recompressed, before
re-injection into the stripping column. In this way, steam efficiency within the column
101
==PAGE:110==
may be increased, as heat is partially recovered from the condensation unit.
Data obtained from MTU operation can also serve as basis for process modelling. Pro-
cess model verification may be achieved by comparison of experimental results with
simulation. In this way, the parametric space of operation can be investigated thor-
oughly, from which favourable operating conditions and deeper process understanding
may be obtained.
