Abstract
Offshore wind energy is a fast-evolving field that is constantly facing new challenges.
Due to the accelerated deployment and its corresponding competitive increase, it is very
important to assess the optimum overall performance of a wind farm by coupling spe-
cific subcomponents, leading to a trade-off between competing disciplines, especially the
balance between costs and performance. This thesis aims to give an overview of the in-
fluence of support structures in the layout optimization problem and how they sacrifice
the performance of the AEP for the benefits in costs, which combined, yield a better sys-
tem performance, represented by the LCOE. For that, two optimization problems with
different objective functions are performed.
Firstly, a layout optimization towards Annual Energy Production (AEP) maximization is
carried without considering the influence of support structures.
Secondly, a surrogate model that optimizes the support structure mass for a very large
range of turbine designs and environmental conditions is used for input to techno-
economic analysis. Then, based on the previous steps, an approach consisting of op-
timizing the layout and support structures simultaneously, with the Levelized Cost of
Energy (LCOE) as the objective function to be minimized, is performed. Due to the con-
tinuity of the problem, the optimization is performed as a two-steps strategy using a
gradient-free method to obtain the best initial conditions and a gradient-based method
as a refinement process, demonstrating the benefits of having both methods to explore
the whole design space while non-compromising the accuracy and reliability of gradient-
based methods.
The results showcase a significant improvement in AEP (+1.02 %) and LCOE ( -1.37 %)
when using irregular layouts, as it allows the wind turbines to explore the whole design
space. The most significant trade-off studied in this thesis involves foundation costs and
energy production, being the latter more influential in the layout design.
The thesis is concluded with a sensitivity analysis on different rotor sizes for the same
wind farm size, where an increase in rated power and subsequent decrease in the number
of wind turbines led a better system performance.
Although the studied cases are characterized by a limited set of technical and environ-
mental characteristics, the methodology used in this thesis is flexible and has demon-
strated the potential to be applied to a wide range of technical and environmental con-
ditions to perform preliminary techno-economical valuations.

Introduction and motivation
Wind energy is an important asset for the global economy. This fast-evolving field has
been resilient to the economic consequences of the COVID-19 pandemic and the weak-
ening of the supply chains. Therefore, wind energy has demonstrated that it can play a
significant role in the independence from imported fossil fuels and energy security by
expanding the green generation capacity in the upcoming years.
In addition to the need for a green economy recovery, the Earth is facing a climate crisis
that has to be tackled immediately.
According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) [1], during the
past decade, average Global Greenhouse Gas emissions reached their highest level in
history and if there is no immediate action, limiting global warming to 1.5 ∘is beyond
reach.
Nevertheless, evident changes in the current paradigm have been observed. Constant
decreases in renewable energy costs, as well as policy measures, have accelerated the
deployment of renewable energy to support these deep emissions reductions.
1.1
Offshore Wind Energy
In the past decade, offshore wind has rapidly grown in momentum. It is considered
to have three main advantages compared with onshore wind energy: (1) Higher and
more stable wind resources that lead to higher capacity factors, (2) the possibility of
deployment of larger wind farms due to the availability of public space, and (3) less
public resistance since they are located far away from residential and farming premises.
For these reasons, offshore wind energy is growing exponentially, reaching an average
wind farm size of almost 900 𝑀𝑊in Europe. This number doubles previous decade’s
average capacity, as can be seen in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Wind farm trends in Europe [2]
This growth is led not only by an increase in the number of wind turbines but also by

the turbine size escalation. In 2021, the average power rating of ordered offshore wind
turbines reached 11.2 𝑀𝑊. Onshore, this average amounted to 4.9 𝑀𝑊[2].
1.1.1
European Energy Framework
The European Union has recently compromised into the accelerated development of
wind energy. Initiatives like REPowerEU outlines the importance of a massive scaling-
up of wind energy in power generation in order to halt Climate Change and secure
energy independence. This Commission proposes to increase the previous wind power
planned capacity from 450 Gigawatts (𝐺𝑊) to 480 𝐺𝑊by 2030. By the end of 2021, 236
𝐺𝑊(189 𝐺𝑊in the EU) of wind power capacity were installed in Europe, 12 % being
offshore. Although this number is constantly increasing, there is an obvious need for
acceleration on deployment to commit to these wind power capacity targets.
To reach the 480 𝐺𝑊goal, the EU would need to install 32 𝐺𝑊anually during this
decade, which opposes to WindEurope forecasts of 18 𝐺𝑊p.a. [3]. Due to this massive
and accelerated growth in the construction of wind farms, the optimization of the wind
farm layout is becoming of major importance.
1.2
Motivation
Wind energy systems are characterized by their complexity, size, and the numerous
components and disciplines they are involved in. Despite this complexity, it is a field
that is attracting a lot of investment in the past decades, and consequently, a compet-
itive increase in the energy market. In order to make wind energy competitive with
non-renewable technologies, there is a need to optimize every component of an offshore
wind farm to improve efficiency and reduce costs.
As mentioned before, offshore wind energy can be the main energy source in the future
due to its higher wind resource and generation capacity, lower turbulence, and fewer
public complaints than onshore wind power. However, offshore wind energy still needs
to face many challenges. Particularly, the costs of offshore wind investments per MW are
50 % higher than onshore due to its high complexity in support structure installation [4].
In fact, offshore turbines are 20 % more expensive and the cost of towers and foundations
is 2.5 times higher than the price of a similar onshore project.
Support structure costs range from 15% to 25% [5],[6],[7] of the total capital expenditures
and increase significantly in deeper waters, as Figures 1.2a1 and 1.2b 2 illustrate.

1.2.1
Surrogate model
Generally, large-scale wind farm optimization is paired with high computational costs
due to the broad number of components involved in the problem.
Thus, there is a need to perform faster and reliable simulations to carry out preliminary
techno-economic feasibility studies. McWilliam et al. [9] presents a surrogate model
to avoid this computational complexity and cover a much wider design space. Using
reference turbines of different rated powers and a broad set of metocean conditions, a
large design space is defined to estimate the dimensions and masses of both tower and
monopile structures.
This thesis will couple this surrogate model with optimization tools such as TOPFARM
and PyWake to perform much quicker simulations considering different design features
such as turbine size, specific power, and variable water depths, which, eventually, will
support decision-makers in the preliminary phases of wind farm design.
1.3
Research questions and report outline
The main research question of this master thesis is formulated as follows:
• How could the layout of an offshore wind farm be optimized towards LCOE, consider-
ing the influence of the support structures as design variable, from an overall system
performance perspective?
In order to answer this question, a set of secondary research questions needs to be for-
mulated:
• What are the main contributors to support structures design?
3
==PAGE:16==
• How can be modelled the trade-offs between energy production and support structures
costs of the whole wind farm?
• How are the results of the optimization affected by different metocean parameters
and wind turbine sizes?
In the next chapters, two of the most important optimization problems when designing
a wind farm are analyzed in depth. These tasks are the optimization of the energy yield
and the support structure costs by modifying the turbine locations.
Chapter 2 provides a discussion of relevant contributions and techniques in the litera-
ture.
Chapter 3 describes the methodology followed for constructing an AEP and LCOE opti-
mization problem for the selected site. This part deals with the mathematical definition
of the optimization problem, their associated tools such as the cost model and the sur-
rogate model, and the technological and environmental inputs.
Chapter 4 presents the results and discussion of these techniques for a baseline case.
Chapter 5 shows a comparison of a set of different scenarios, including different rated
and specific powers.
Chapter 6 summarizes the main findings and draws the project conclusions.
Finally, Chapter 7 proposes directions for new contributions to this topic.

Literature Review
2.1
Optimization in Wind Energy
Wind turbines operate under diverse and uncertain meteorological conditions and, con-
sequently, are designed to withstand high loads during their lifetime while producing
as much energy as possible. Making it more complex, wind turbines do not operate in
isolation. In fact, several of them comprise a wind farm, where their corresponding tur-
bulent flows, electrical infrastructure, and operational maintenance plans interact with
each other. This synergy makes wind farms one of the most complex problems not only
in large-scale fluid mechanics but also from a civil, logistic, and operational point of
view.
State of the art
As Ning et al. [10] state, design decisions in this multidisciplinary and high-dimensional
design space are very complicated to manage manually. Therefore, optimization is a
tool that can be used to manage this complexity. By balancing all the complex trade-
offs in the design at both the wind turbine and wind farm level, it is possible to deploy a
power plant with an optimized design objective, which typically focuses on the economic
competitiveness over its lifetime.
Historically, developers undertake this complexity skipping the computational optimiza-
tion process by just relying on the acquired experience of previous projects. In fact, less
than ten scientific works related to wind farm optimization were published between the
1970s and the early 2000s [11]. It was not until the past decade when a notable amount of
works were published in scientific journals or conference proceedings, focusing mainly
on two performance metrics: the Annual Energy Production (AEP) and the Cost of En-
ergy (CoE).
In 1994, Mosetti et al. [12] were the pioneers in formulating a Wind Farm Layout Opti-
mization Problem (WFLOP). They built a simulation model based on wake superposition
using a heuristic approach, Genetic Algorithm (GA), to optimize a primitive LCOE ap-
proach: produce the highest amount of energy at the lowest cost. The optimization of a
wind farm layout on a square terrain subdivided into 100 possible turbine locations was
performed under three circumstances: (1) a single wind direction with constant wind
speed, (2) multiple wind directions (one every 10 degrees) with constant wind speed and
(3) a more realistic wind speed distribution with variable wind directions and speeds.
Although several simple assumptions were considered, the authors demonstrated a fea-
sible approach to tackle future layout optimization problems of more sophisticated and
realistic wind farm models.
Cazzaro and Pisinger [13] provide a comprehensive literature review, where different
strategies and trends for the WFLOP are analyzed. Multiple papers are compared by
different criteria such as the approach (discrete or continuous), strategy (heuristic or
exact), solution method ( Genetic Algorithm (GA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO),
Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) or Gradient-based among others), problem instance
( number of turbines and/or positions considered) and wake model used, being Jensen
5
==PAGE:18==
[14], Katić [15], and Bastankhah-Gaussian [16] the most preferable models used by re-
searchers.
Optimization approach
Regarding the approach, the discrete technique, characterized by using a defined set of
possible positions to place the turbines, is the most common choice due to its lower
computational cost and the lower complexity it provides to include discontinuities such
as exclusion zones.
Archer et al. [17] derive a wake interference model that is subsequently used in a Mixed
Integer Linear Program (MILP) problem. This exact method determines the optimal lo-
cations of the wind turbines from a large set of possible locations with the objective of
maximize the energy output of the wind farm. Test cases with 10 to 25 wind turbines
show that the energy outputs obtained with this approach outperform by 2 to 3 % the
ones predicted by a heuristic method, in a realistic computational time frame.
In opposition to the discrete technique, the continuous approach assumes that wind tur-
bines can be placed in any position within the boundaries of the problem. It has the ad-
vantage that wind turbines’ positions are not limited in a predefined grid and all feasible
positions are explored, leading to better results of the objective function. An impor-
tant subset of the continuous approach is the gradient-based methods. These methods
evaluate the objective function by means of derivatives until finding an optimum point.
The main disadvantage appears when dealing with multi-modal functions since they are
susceptible to converge to a local optimum. As Tesauro et al. [18] suggest, this method
is useful if the initial conditions are close enough to the global optimum, making it ap-
propriate in a two-steps strategy, as a refinement process.
Beyond AEP
Wind farm design does not only mean dealing with layout optimization, but also implies
tackling many other sub-problems including the integration of more design variables
such as the hub heights and diameters of the wind turbines [19],[20],[21], as well as the
influence of the electrical layout and support structures in the system performance [22].
Sanchez Perez-Moreno et al. [23] show that an integrated optimization, taking into ac-
count the influence of support structures and infield collection system, outperforms a
sequential approach in terms of LCOE when optimizing a wind farm layout. Consider-
ing ultimate and fatigue loads, a complex model is used to perform a sub-optimization
of the monopile sizing in situ. However, this sub-optimization is paired with high com-
putational cost.
2.2
Support Structures
Another important challenge in wind farm optimization regards the design of the struc-
ture of the wind turbine itself.
Traditionally, human optimization based on experience has driven the structure design
instead of computer-aided optimization. However, wind turbines are complex systems
that need to withstand highly fluctuating loads over their extensive lifetime. In addition,
turbine size is continuously growing, leading to heavier and more flexible turbines highly
exposed to wind and wave excitations. To tackle this complexity, wind turbine’s tower
6
==PAGE:19==
and foundation can be computer-optimized to reduce their weight, and therefore their
costs, while ensuring the structural requirements.
Muskulus and Schafhirt [24] provide an overview of the state of the art, challenges, and
possible approaches regarding the structural optimization of wind turbines.
Two popular groups can be identified in the optimization of a fixed support structure:
monopiles, characterized by being the developers’ preferred option [2], and lattice struc-
tures, commonly known as jackets.
McWilliam et al. [25] define and compare two high-fidelity tower and monopile (MP)
sizing methods. The first one scales a detailed design based on the water depth and tur-
bine size while the second is a physics-based optimization that relies on the WISDEM®
[26] tool. Both results show a good agreement, minimizing the structural mass of the
support structure for water depths between 25m and 40m. In addition, the authors show
that a monopile maximum diameter reduction of 25 % can be accomplished with a slight
increase in the overall mass, allowing a reduction of costs associated with manufactur-
ing and transportation. However, fatigue loading effects are not assumed since only two
load cases are considered in the optimization problem (DLC 1.6. and DLC 6.1.).
Damiani et al. [27] perform gradient-based optimizations for both MP-tower and jacket-
tower support structures in different wind farm scenarios. Although the mass of the
monopile is greater than the jacket for all the evaluated cases, when manufacturing
and installation costs are also taken into account, monopiles result in lower LCOE for
waters up to 45m. Future research shows that the jacket mass is mainly driven by water
depth but, as turbine ratings increase, hub heights and rotor-nacelle assembly (RNA)
gain importance on the overall support structure mass [28].
2.3
Surrogate model
”A surrogate model, also known as response surface model or metamodel, is an approxi-
mate model of a functional output that represents a ”curve fit” to some underlying data”
[29]. In other words, it simulates the input-output performance when the relation be-
tween them is unknown or computationally expensive to evaluate. Hence, a surrogate
model is very useful in an optimization context when using multiple fidelity models,
numerous scenarios are evaluated, or only experimental data is available.
The interest of this thesis is not in building a surrogate model, but rather in performing
optimizations using a surrogate model. This topic is called surrogate-based optimization
(SBO).
Valotta Rodrigues et al. [30] developed a surrogate model using regression analysis to
represent AEP simulations within a large design space of inputs. 30000 samples were
computed, identifying that the number of wind turbines, wind resource conditions, and
turbine characteristics (rated power and specific power) are the elements that influence
the most the energy yield.
McWilliam et al. [9] also developed a surrogate model for wind farm support structures
design by applying Quadratic Least Squares (QLS). Using reference turbines of different
rated power and a broad set of metocean conditions, a large design space divided into 6
batches is defined to estimate the dimensions and masses of both tower and monopile
7
==PAGE:20==
structures.
This thesis will couple this last model with optimization tools such as TOPFARM and
PyWake to cover a range of water depths and technological parameters for faster and
flexible LCOE optimizations.

3
Methodology
This section describes the methods followed for both an Annual Energy Production
(AEP) and Levelized Cost Of Electricity (LCOE) optimization problem, including rele-
vant theoretical background. Firstly, the software tools used in this project (PyWake and
TOPFARM) will be described. Next, the environmental and technological characteristics
of the site are described, followed by the methods used in the optimization towards AEP.
In order to make the transition from an AEP optimization problem to an LCOE one, the
necessary tools such as the support structures surrogate model and the DTU cost model
are described. Finally, an overview of the workflows is explained.
It is important to remark that this project uses several assumptions and low-fidelity
models that mimic the behavior of different components but do not capture the exact
physical or economic measure. Therefore, the goal is to assess the influence of the sup-
port structures and the corresponding trade-offs when optimizing the wind farm layout
towards LCOE, rather than provide a precise and realistic value of energy production or
LCOE.
3.1
PyWake
PyWake [31] is a highly vectorized wind farm simulation tool implemented in Python
that includes different wind farm models of multiple fidelities. Developed at DTU, it
contains an interface with both TOPFARM and PyWAsP and is capable of calculating
wind farm flow fields and AEP of wind farms.
PyWake´s advantages lie in its open-source nature, customization capabilities, and im-
plemented engineering models that provide faster simulations than Computational Fluid
Dynamics (CFD) approaches. Consequently, PyWake is the most suitable option when
evaluating multiple design configurations in wind farm optimization, as it is the case of
this project.
As can be seen from Figure 3.1, PyWake consists of different components that interact
with each other to calculate the desired output.

WindFarmModel
The WindFarmModel is the main object that couples all the necessary components to
calculate a wind farm flow. It is initialized with a Site and WindTurbines object and
returns a SimulationResult object that contains the effective wind speed, produced
power, thrust coefficient, as well as the methods to calculate the AEP and generate flow
maps.
Site
From a given wind turbine position, reference wind speed (WSref), wind direction (WDref),
and turbulence intensity (TIref) obtained from the WindFarmModel, the Site object out-
puts the local wind condition, in terms of wind speed (WS), wind direction (WD), and
turbulence intensity (TI), as well as the Weibull parameters (scaling factor A and shape
factor k) and the wind´s frequency direction. Furthermore, Site calculates the down-
wind, cross-wind, and vertical distance between wind turbines.
WindTurbines
The WindTurbines object defines the power curve, thrust coefficient (𝐶𝑇) curve, hub
height (𝐻𝐻), and diameter (𝐷) of a given wind turbine.
Engineering models
The engineering models define the interaction between the wind turbines within the
wind farm. As can be seen in Figure 3.2, they are composed of one of the two wind farm
models in combination with a wake deficit model, a superposition model, and optionally
a blockage deficit and a turbulence model.
Figure 3.2: Engineering models within the WindFarmModel
The WindFarmModel determines how wake and blockage deficits propagate in the wind
farm. It is divided into two different classes: the PropagateDownwind, which exe-
cutes the wake deficit calculation in the downwind direction for each turbine, and the
All2AllIterative, which calculates the effective wind speed for all turbines until the
calculated wind speed converges.
The WakeDeficitModel calculates the wind speed deficit caused by an upstream wind
turbine to downstream wind turbines, the SuperpositionModel defines how multiple

deflection due to yaw misalignment, and the TurbulenceModel calculates the added
turbulence in the wake.
3.2
TOPFARM
Topology OPtimization of wind FARMs (TOPFARM) [32] is a multi-fidelity tool for wind
farm design optimization that is being developed by DTU Wind Energy.
The main goal of TOPFARM is to establish an optimal balance between the different
costs, fatigue degradation of turbine components, and power production output by using
advanced flow models that include stationary wake effects, advanced aero-elastic models
for load and power prediction, as well as dedicated cost and control strategy models [33].
Subsequently, it is possible to synthesize these models into an optimization problem
subjected to various kinds of constraints, such as wind turbine inter-spacing and wind
farm area constraints.
TOPFARM 2.3.0 is an open-source Python package that relies on the OpenMDAO frame-
work for optimization problems and also wraps the PyWake package for easy computa-
tion of AEP with different wake models. Due to its robustness and the many different
wake models PyWake integrates, TOPFARM is able to solve different types of prob-
lems such as layout optimizations, turbine-type optimizations, load-constrained opti-
mizations, etc. Many of the most common wake and cost models are built in, but it is
also possible to couple a specific wake or cost model for custom optimizations. Although
this flexibility implies working under a non-user-friendly interface, TOPFARM remains
the most convenient tool for the performance of this study as it requires the coupling
of a support structure design surrogate model to a layout optimization problem, to then
perform an optimization towards LCOE taking into account the influence of the support
structures, which involves high computational expenses that other high-fidelity meth-
ods would not be able to perform in an efficient way.

and the interaction between them:

Design variables
These are the variables that can be controlled during the optimization and influence the
objective function. By default, TOPFARM includes the wind turbine positions as design
variables. In this project, the design variables are set to be the coordinates of the wind
turbines in the different configurations, as will be explained later.
Constraints
They are the quantity or function that limits feasible combinations of the design variables
or even of the objective function. By constraining the problem, it becomes more bound
and it is more difficult to obtain an optimal result.
Constraints are inserted in the TopFarmProblem through pre-defined classes such as
SpacingConstraint, XYBoundaryConstraint and CircleBoundaryConstraint, or
set through the post_constraints option of the TopFarmProblem class.
In this project, the most common constraints in WFLOP will be applied, which are the
inter-spacing between wind turbines and the boundaries of the wind farm.
Driver
The optimization driver is used to perform the numerical optimization and, therefore,
solve the mathematical problem. For optimization, a driver is usually an algorithm that
iterates over the workflow until an optimal solution is found. In a very complex work-
flow, there may be multiple drivers acting on sub-groups of a workflow.
TOPFARM wraps several drivers defined in scipy such as SLSQP, COBYLA,RandomSearch,
and GeneticAlgorithm, but custom optimization drivers can also be coupled into the
workflow. By default, SLSQP, a gradient-based method based in finite differences, is used
when utilizing the scipy optimization driver class. In the case of this project, autograd,
a gradient-based algorithm based in Automatic Differentiation (AD), will be used for the
AEP optimization while RandomSearch and SLSQP will be used in the LCOE optimiza-
tion, as will be explained later.
Automatic Differentiation (AD), also known as algorithm differentiation, is an approach
based on the systematic application of the chain rule to computer programs [34]. In
PyWake, AD is implemented by using the package autograd, developed by Standford
researchers [35]. It brings automatic differentiation to Pyhton, NumPy and scipy code
using the facilities that this modern scientific computing framework offers.
Cost Component
It is the generic class for setting up the problem’s objective function. TOPFARM counts
with a large collection of cost component models that range from energy production
such as AEP to financial parameters such as IRR, passing through factors that take into
account both, such as the LCOE.
In this project, two objective functions will be evaluated: the LCOE trough the usual
CostModelComponent in TOPFARM and the AEP through PyWakeAEPCostModelComponent.
TOPFARM problem
The TopFarmProblem is the top-level object of the optimization that couples the de-
sign variables, constraints, optimizer, and the objective function. Once initialized, the
TopFarmProblem will have a function called optimize that runs the optimization.
12
==PAGE:25==
Cost, state, recorder
These are the outputs from the TopFarmProblem. The cost object stores the value of the
objective function, state provides the values of the design variables and the recorder
presents a record of which sets of design variables were tried during the optimization.
These three objects store the information for each iteration of the problem and, therefore,
can be used to analyze the trends followed during the optimization.

3.4.2
AEP optimization problem
A proper optimization approach is essential for carrying out the simulation successfully.
One key aspect is the selection of the design variables and constraints by using as less
variables or parameters as possible, in order to limit the feasible space. In this case, two
design variables (𝑥and 𝑦wind turbine coordinates) and two constraints (boundaries of
the wind farm and wind turbine inter-spacing) were selected. It is important to mention
that this problem is continuous, thus the wind turbines can be placed in any position of
the feasible space. Compared to a discrete problem, this adds complexity and computa-
tional cost to the problem but all feasible positions are explored, leading to better results
of the AEP.
Another key aspect is the choice of the driver/optimizer. In the case of this optimiza-
tion, a gradient-based method was selected. Despite its computational costs and the
likelihood of being captured in local optima, its accuracy, reliability and the possibility
of including several design variables counterpart all the other factors. PyWake supports
three methods for computing gradients: (1) finite difference (fd), a method that is fast
for small problems but is less accurate due to its sensitivity to stepsize, (2) complex step
(cs), a more accurate one but also fast for small problems, and (3) automatic differenti-
ation (AD), a method that provides the exact result by performing a two-step automatic
differentiation process with the lowest computational resources, even for big problems.
The three methods were tried in a simple WFLOP and AD, implemented by the package
autograd, outperformed both fd and cs, as was expected [42]. In addition, a compari-
son between the default gradient-based optimizer in TOPFARM, SLSQP, and autograd
was performed for the final baseline case of 74 turbines, showing that the computational
cost of the latter was considerably lower. Therefore, autograd was selected to perform
the AEP optimization using the PyWakeAEPCostModelComponent class.

3.5
Surrogate model
A surrogate model is an engineering method that allows the user to perform evaluations
in a fast and reliable way, that otherwise, would need high-performance computing ca-
pabilities. It mimics the input/output behavior of a complex model when the relationship
between the two is unknown or computationally expensive to evaluate. This character-
istic makes it convenient in an optimization context, where a considerable amount of
evaluations are performed.
As it was motivated in Section 1.2.1, this project will couple the surrogate model de-
veloped by Michael McWilliam et al. [9] with TOPFARM and PyWake to perform quick
simulations taking into account the wind turbine DTU 10MW and the water depth range
of BWFZ III & IV, although, due to the wide design space of the surrogate model, the
optimization can be performed easily for different turbine sizes and sites.
The needed inputs for the support structures surrogate model are both technological
(Table 3.1) and environmental (Table 3.2). Regarding the water depth, this is calculated
for every wind turbine position and implemented as the final input in the surrogate
model.

3.6
DTU Cost Model
When performing an optimization towards an economic factor, cost models are essential,
encompassing both financial costs and operating costs.
In TOPFARM there are two built-in cost models.
The first one consists of a Python implementation of the National Renewable Energy
Laboratory (NREL) Wind Turbine Design Cost and Scaling Model [43]. The model was
developed from the early to mid-2000s in order to create a reliable tool for estimating the
cost of wind-generated electricity, from both onshore and offshore perspectives based
on different scales of turbines. It is not intended to predict turbine pricing, which is
a function of volatile market factors beyond the scope of this work. Therefore, cost
estimates are projected based on the turbine’s rated power, rotor diameter, hub height,
and other key turbine characteristics.
The second cost model available is the DTU Cost Model [32]. It has a similar structure
to the NREL cost and scaling model but is based on more recent industry data and uses a
detailed financial cash flow analysis. The monetary units are 2017 Euro and it contains
the major elements and methods for simple calculations of Net Present Value (NPV),
Internal Rate of Return (IRR), and project costs such as DEVEX, CAPEX, OPEX, and
ABEX. It generally relies on curve fitting using input parameters such as rated power,
rotor diameter, hub height, and water depth and was tuned using data obtained from the
industry. It also supports two types of offshore foundations: monopile and jacket.
For the reasons just mentioned and the flexibility it provides to implement as inputs the
surrogate model outputs, the DTU Cost Model was selected for this work.
3.6.1
Capital Expenditures (CAPEX)
Capital Expenditures (CAPEX) refer to any funds spent on purchasing or improving fixed
assets required in a project. Typically, they are major purchases a company makes to
be used over the long term. In the case of an offshore wind energy project, CAPEX is
defined as all expenditure in the period of construction up to the date the wind farm is
commissioned, as can be seen in Figure 3.9.
In the DTU Cost Model, CAPEX is divided into three big groups: Balance of Plant costs,
Turbine costs, and Foundation costs, as shown in Figure 3.10.
Balance of Plant costs congregate the expenses related to the electrical infrastructure
such as the substation and cabling costs.
20
==PAGE:33==
Figure 3.9: Total lifetime costs of an Offshore Wind Farm project [44]
Turbine cost is calculated as the sum of the costs associated with the blades, hub, driv-
etrain, nacelle, and tower of a wind turbine. These different components are sized as
a function of the rotor diameter, rated power, hub height, and rated rotational speed.
Then, according to their masses and materials, each cost is calculated. Finally, by apply-
ing different factors related to Production, Turbine Selling, General and Administrative
Expenses (SG&A), Financing and Logistics, the Turbine total cost is determined.
Similarly, the Foundation cost is obtained as a function of the rotor diameter, rated power,
and water depth of the location.
Since one of the purposes of this thesis is to link a support structure surrogate model with
a layout optimization problem, the CAPEX calculation of the DTU Cost model has to be
modified to accommodate the outputs from the surrogate model (tower and foundation
mass) as inputs of the cost model, as can be seen in Figure 3.10.
3.6.2
Operational Expenditures (OPEX)
Capital Expenditures contrast with Operational and Maintenance Expenditures (OPEX),
which are ongoing day-to-day expenses that are inherent to the operation of the asset
and are needed to keep operational the organization’s business.
DTU Cost model calculates these expenses as a function of the installed capacity of
the wind farm and the number of wind turbines. They include diverse factors such
as personnel expenses, ships, offshore operations, and different fees.
3.6.3
Development Expenditures (DEVEX)
Development Expenditures (DEVEX) are defined as the sum of all the costs spent in
the period from idea and development to design and planning. DTU Cost model also
calculates these expenses as a function of the installed capacity of the wind farm and
the number of wind turbines. They include different processes such as environmental
surveys and met-ocean campaigns.
3.6.4
Abandonment Expenditures (ABEX)
Abandonment Expenditures (ABEX) refer to the funds related to the decommissioning
of the Wind Farm after the termination date. In the DTU Cost Model, ABEX is calculated
as a fraction of CAPEX.

3.7
Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE)
The Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) (in e.g. EUR/MWh) represents the average cost
over a project lifetime. It allows alternative technologies to be compared when different
scales of operation, locations, investments, and operating periods exist. For example,
the LCOE could be used to compare the cost of a unit of energy generated by a renew-
able source with a standard fossil-fueled generating unit. The LCOE is the cost that, if
assigned to every produced unit of energy over the lifetime of the project, will equal
the Total Life-Cycle Cost (TLCC) when discounted back to the base year. LCOE is rec-
ommended for use in ranking alternative cases, when a limited budget is given since
it will provide a proper ordering of the alternatives, which may then be selected until
the budget is expended [45]. However, this metric is not recommended from a project
investor’s point of view when deciding between projects with different investment sizes
since other aspects such as the revenues are not taken into account. In addition, LCOE
does not consider costs related to balancing supply and demand.
Given the academic nature of this thesis and the possibility to combine costs and energy
production into one metric (rather than compare them separately), LCOE minimization
is chosen as the objective function of the general optimization problem. Lower LCOE
values benefit the electricity consumers, so decreasing LCOE is a key focus for the off-
shore wind industry to make it the most favorable electricity generation option.
LCOE represents the average cost over the lifetime of a project, taking into account the
time value of money. Translating this into a mathematical formulation:

3.7.1
LCOE optimization problem
The design space, as well as the design variables (x,y) are kept the same from the AEP
optimization. Compared with the previous optimization, a bathymetry grid of 568x605
values is inserted as an input into the problem. To keep the problem continuous, the wa-
ter depth is defined for every possible turbine location within the boundaries by means
of an interpolation between these values. In addition, the DTU Cost Model, as well
as the support structure surrogate model, are also inputs, adding more complexity and
computational costs to the problem.
A gradient-based (SLSQP) optimization was initially carried out but, because of the men-
tioned complexity, the computational cost was enormous. In addition, due to the mul-
tiple minima the LCOE function possesses, the optimization got stuck multiple times in
different minima even if the initial conditions were changed. Therefore, the use of a
smart start approach was needed, making the optimization problem as a two-step strat-
egy where the gradient-based algorithm is used as a refinement process. TOPFARM con-
tains a function smart_start in the TopFarmProblem class, but this is only included
when computing AEP optimizations with the PyWakeAEPCostModelComponent class.
Thus, a gradient-free heuristic optimizer (RandomSearch) was used to search through
the whole design space until finding an appropriate initial design space where the evo-
lution of the LCOE optimization was stabilized.

RandomSearch is implemented in TOPFARM based on the algorithm developed at DTU

using a vector defined by an angle and amplitude that are randomly set at each iteration.

Once the new solution is calculated, it is tested for improvement against the objective

as the best solution, otherwise, the previous solution remains unchanged.
For the first step of the optimization problem, the RandomizeTurbinePosition_Circle

As mentioned, this optimization is complex and computationally demanding. To speed
up the process, a High Performance Computing (HPC) tool has been employed for the
LCOE optimizations. DTU Sophia HPC Cluster [47] runs the Linux CentOS operating
system and the cluster installation is based on the OpenHPC framework. This cluster
consists of 516 computational nodes of which 484 are 128 GB RAM nodes and 32 are
256 GB RAM nodes, making a total amount of RAM of 70 TB. Each node is a powerful
x86-64 computer, equipped with 32 physical cores. The nodes are interlinked by In-
finiBand and 10Gbps Ethernet networks. Despite this immense memory capacity, only
one physical core of one node was used to perform these simulations due to the aca-
demic characteristic and available time of this thesis. However, if one wanted to speed
up these simulations, the problem could be easily parallelized up to 32 CPUs, providing
simulations 7 times faster.

Wind Farm Optimization Workflow
In this section, an overview of the followed optimization steps and components will be
explained.
Using TOPFARM, PyWake, and the mentioned surrogate model for support structures,
different Python scripts are created and modified to solve the desired problem.
Initially, a layout optimization towards AEP is carried out without taking into account
the influence of the support structures. Afterwards, the surrogate model is implemented
to change the objective function to LCOE.

This section clearly shows the potential of using integrated mathematical optimization
techniques in the design of offshore wind farms. With regards to the limitations, im-

increase in their production and installation costs, which poses an important limitation
to this study.

5
Sensitivity Analysis
Valotta Rodrigues et al. [30] studied the influence of the number of wind turbines, rated
power, and specific power in the AEP for a wide set of case studies. Results showed that
the farm rating, rated power, and the number of wind turbines were the most positive
influential coefficients in the AEP.
This project not only analyzes the influence of different parameters on the AEP but also
on the LCOE. Since the turbine selection matters more than the environmental parame-
ters for support structure costs [9], a sensitivity analysis for a set of different rotor sizes
and rated powers will be carried out to see how the support structures influence different
wind farm layouts.
This sensitivity study is not an evaluation of how robust the baseline design is to site
uncertainty, but it is rather an analysis of how the variability of the most relevant input
parameters affects the final design. From now, the previous results from Chapter 4 will
be referred to as the baseline case.
5.1
Rated Power
This part presents the results of the AEP and LCOE optimizations for two cases with
different turbine ratings. Since the maximum wind farm capacity is constrained to 740
MW, the number of wind turbines will vary accordingly. These results are then compared
with the baseline case of 74 DTU 10MW previously presented in Chapter 4.
Using a wide data set of turbines provided by DTU Wind and Energy Systems, two
reference wind turbines of the same specific power (SP) as DTU 10 MW (350 W/𝑚2 )
were scaled to perform fair comparisons of the optimizations. In this case, a smaller
turbine of 5MW and a bigger of 15 MW were selected to observe valuable trend-lines as
a function of rated power (RP) and the number of wind turbines.

maximum power produced per unit area. For a given RP and SP, the diameter of the

5.1.1
AEP optimization
Once the technical characteristics are defined for each case, an optimization towards
AEP is carried out. Firstly, an initial layout is defined by means of a smart start evolu-
tion. Having defined a rectangular grid containing the site, the WindFarmModel and the
number of wind turbines, PyWakeAEPCostModelComponent starts to place the turbines
one by one as far as possible from each other to avoid wake effects. Despite this position-
ing that enhances the wind turbines’ inter-spacing, the AEP is not fully optimized and a
subsequent AEP optimization is necessary. Appendix B shows the layout evolution for
the smart start of the 49 turbines’ case (15 MW).
Automatic Differentiation (AD) with a tolerance of 1e-7 was used to perform the AEP
optimizations. As previously, the problems were constrained by the wind farm boundary
and wind turbines inter-spacing (3 Diameters).
Figure 5.2 shows the different design layouts of these mentioned optimizations. As can
be observed, the positioning and number of the turbines are strongly dependent on wind
turbines’ size. As much as the rated power increases, the number of installed wind
turbines has to be decreased to keep with the wind farm rating under the established
limit of 740 MW.
Consequently, this provides more free space which eases the convergence of the opti-
mization problem and therefore, reduces the computational costs, as can be seen in Ta-
ble 5.3. This is explained by the fact that more turbines constrain more the design space
due to the minimum inter-spacing between wind turbines, and therefore, becomes more
bound.
This characteristic is especially relevant when analyzing the 5 MW case. The 148 tur-
bines tend to spread over the design space and almost half of them (68 WTs) are located
within the boundaries of the wind farm to mitigate wake effects. Therefore the perfor-
mance of these turbines is higher than the ones located inside the bounds (up to a 6 %
decrease), as can be seen by looking at the individual AEP of each turbine. On the other
hand, the 49 15 MW turbines have plenty of free space to explore the most optimal de-
sign. Almost 70 % of them (34 WTs) are located in the boundaries to decrease wake losses

Once the surrogate model is coupled with TOPFARM, an optimization towards LCOE is
carried out. As previously, a gradient-free method (RandomSearch) and a subsequent
gradient-based algorithm (SLSQP with a tolerance of 1e-6) were used to perform the

Figure 5.4 shows the different design layouts of these mentioned optimizations. Similarly
to the AEP optimizations, the positioning and number of turbines are strongly depen-
dent on the rotor size. Generally, wind turbines located on the wind farm boundaries
keep their positioning while the inner WTs experience a change in their coordinates
motivated by the support structures´ influence.
For the 5 MW case, wind turbines located on the wind farm boundaries stay there to
capture as much energy as possible. On the other hand, the inner WTs tend to change
their positioning to be located in shallow waters, and consequently reduce the capital
expenditures of their support structures. However, this clustering causes an increase in
wake losses which weaken the energy production.
For the 15 MW case, the optimization is less constrained by the wind turbines’ inter-
spacing. As before, the WTs located on the WF boundaries do not change their position-
ing while the inner ones tend to be located in shallow waters. In this case, the clustering
in this area is not as noticeable as before due to the wide range of possibilities to locate
the turbines. It can be appreciated the proliferation of empty spaces characterized by
deep waters.
Table 5.5 displays the main techno-economic differences between the AEP and LCOE
optimizations of each rating power scenario. As expected, the wake effects increase
in the LCOE optimizations due to the wind turbines’ concentration in shallow waters.
Thus, a reduction in the AEP of 0.06%, 0.1%, and 0.08% can be seen for the 5 MW, 10
MW, and 15 MW cases, respectively. On the other hand, discounted costs also decrease,
mainly driven by the foundation costs, as was demonstrated in Section 4.2. All in all, as
usual for the LCOE optimizations, there is a sacrifice in the overall energy performance
for the benefit of the total costs of the wind farm, which can be specially observed for
the 10 MW case.

6
Conclusion
Due to the accelerated deployment of offshore wind energy, it is of vital importance to
assess the overall trade-offs in wind farm optimization in order to determine the optimal
performance. Thus, this comes down to analyze the balance between competing disci-
plines, especially between support structure costs and energy production. This thesis
shows the potential of using integrated mathematical optimization techniques to obtain
the best system performance represented by the LCOE. In this thesis, several optimiza-
tions were performed in Borssele Wind Farm Zone III/IV aiming at maximizing the AEP
and minimizing the LCOE. Both optimizations, characterized by a an irregular layout,
showed the benefits of exploring the the whole design space to reduce wake losses and
find the most optimal distribution in terms of foundation costs.
By using a support structure surrogate model, which calculates the mass for each turbine
position depending on water depth, the computational costs of the integrated optimiza-
tion were considerably reduced while allowing the problem be continuous. Due to the
latter, wind turbines positions are not restricted in space, and thus a better overall perfor-
mance can be obtained. In addition, a continuous approach allows the use of gradient-
based algorithms, characterized by their accuracy and reliability. However, they are
also characterized by heavy computational costs and the the high probability of falling
or encountering local optima, if the initial conditions are not properly selected. To avoid
this complexity, this thesis has demonstrated the benefits of using both gradient-free
and gradient-based algorithms in the LCOE optimizations. By performing a two-steps
strategy using a gradient-free method to obtain the best initial conditions and a gradient-
based method as a refinement process, the design space is completely explored, avoiding
local minima, while having an accurate final design.
To perform this integrated optimization, the surrogate model is embedeed in TOPFARM,
showing the benefits of having flexible tools that allow the customization of the problem.
LCOE optimizations show how wind turbines sacrifice their energy performance for the
benefit of the decrease in foundation costs. By balancing the penalty in energy yield and
reduction in SS costs, it can be concluded that the AEP’s influence is slightly higher than
the support structures costs. All in all, a huge reduction in the support structures’ costs
(below 10 % of the total costs) has been accomplished, which was one of the objectives
of this thesis.
Finally, when analyzing the influence of the turbine selection, it was concluded that an
increase in rated power leads to a better system performance, reducing the AEP and
costs simultaneously. Since the necessary number of wind turbine decrease, the de-
sign space becomes less bound, allowing faster convergence of the objective function
while decreasing the wake effects. Regarding the costs evolution, all of them, except
the turbine production, decrease. Specifically, although the foundation costs decrease,
operational costs are the most influential parameters in this reduction. Therefore, the
number of wind turbines seems to be the main driver from both an energy performance
(reduction of wake effects) and a cost (reduction of OPEX and foundation costs) point of
view. When studying a set of different specific powers, the turbine itself has the greatest
53
==PAGE:66==
impact on the support structure design. Finally, for lower rated turbines (5 MW), lower
specific powers (300 𝑊/𝑚2) are found to be the most cost-optimal while for 10 and 15
MW, SP= 350𝑊/𝑚2 was the optimal selection. This indicates that the choice of optimal
WT configuration is not a straightforward process and specific evaluations should be
performed for a determined case.
All in all, although the case studies are site-specific and several assumptions and low-
fidelity models have been used, this thesis has demonstrated the influence of the sup-
port structures and the corresponding trade-offs when optimizing the wind farm layout
towards LCOE. Hence, the studied methodology has the potential to be applied to a
wide range of technical and environmental conditions to perform preliminary techno-
economical valuations, which eventually will allow decision-makers to choose the best
preliminary option which, subsequently, will be designed using high-fidelity models and
real data.

Future Work
To conclude this thesis, it is important to remark some of the limitations of the model that
could be studied in the future to have a more complete understanding of the behaviour
of the sub-components.
The support structures mass do not experience a very pronounced change when water
depths are varied. In addition, a huge reduction in their share of the costs has been ob-
tained. These two factors suggests that the level of rigour might not be in accordance
with the realistic requirements and a more detailed optimization of the tower and foun-
dation could be carried out. By taking into consideration fatigue and unsteady loads, as
well as soil conditions, it is expected that the support structures will have more influence
when balancing the trade-off between energy performance and support structure costs,
leading to different layouts where a higher sacrifice in energy performance is expected.
Previous studies have shown that the main drivers of LCOE are the energy production
and the cost of electrical systems [22],[23]. Therefore, added value could be obtained
if the electrical layout is included in the integrated optimization, in order to quantify
the influence of the support structures in a more realistic case. By changing the wind
turbine positions, the distance between them changes and therefore the costs of the
electrical inter-connection (cables). This route optimization would also help to estimate
a more realistic value of operational costs, which in this thesis only took into account
the number of wind turbines, giving to this parameter a great influence when evaluating
different turbines’ rated power scenarios.
The specific power sensitivity study has indicated that the choice of optimal wind turbine
configuration is not a straightforward process. Hence, a more detailed sizing of the
turbine components (blades, hub, nacelle, drivetrain...) in the cost model would help to
better understand the cost evolution and, consequently, support the decision in wind
turbine selection.
In offshore wind farms, the wake losses are estimated between 10 and 20 % of the AEP
[41]. Taking a look to the power deficit values of this thesis, they vary between 3 % and 5
%. This encountered low value is explained by the fact that only the internal wake losses
produced by the wind farm are taken into account. In reality, this is not entirely true as
Borssele is surrounded by a complex of Belgian wind farms (+1GW) that are located in
upstream predominant wind direction. Therefore, wakes from the existing neighbouring
wind farms might be included in the optimization, which is a case that is becoming more
common for new wind farm sites.
Finally, as already mentioned, the employed methodology of this thesis is flexible and
allows to perform relative fast optimizations if the technical and environmental param-
eters are varied. Therefore, several LCOE evaluations could be carried out in order to
build an extensive data set that subsequently could be used in building a LCOE surrogate
model to support preliminary techno-economical valuations.
