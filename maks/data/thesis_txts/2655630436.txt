Abstract
The freshwater ecosystems are under increasing pressure from multiple stressors. Remote sensing
methods provide an alternative method for monitoring and assessment of water quality, delivered
at low cost, high temporal frequency and ability to cover large scale areas. Bio optical modelling is
based on the interaction of light with the active physico-chemical constituents. The aim of the project
is to investigate potential bio optical modelling by establishing a semi analytical model in narrow
freshwater streams using hyperspectral sensors. The bio optical model is developed relying on in situ
measured concentrations of individual spectral absorption and modelled backscattering signatures of
optically active constituents. The study site was located in the northern region of Zealand in Denmark,
in Usserød stream with 11 established stations. The present study focused on the following parameters
determining the water quality: Chlorophyll-a (chl a), Coloured Dissolved Organic Matters (CDOM) and
Total Suspended Solids (TSS).
The results of study indicated that for the reﬂectance above the water surface the developed model
performed on average R 2 of 0 77, R of 0.94 and RM SE of 0.0025 with one ﬂawed station excluded.
However, with the ﬂawed station included a R 2 of -0.11, R of 0.95 and RM SE of 0.005 is obtained.
The results ranges from 0.49 to 0.93 R 2, 0.86 to 0.99 R and 0.00055 to 0.0052 RM SE. For beneath the
water surface there appears to be a slight bias as for most of the stations it achieves a negative R 2.
The model results range is -0.49 to 0.88 R 2, 0.93 to 0.99 R and 0.0016 to 0.023 RM SE without one
ﬂawed station.
The model was compared to the publicly available WASI4 model. The WASI4 model performs similarly
as the present model, where it achieves a R 2 of 0.77, R of 0.96 and RM SE of 0.0030 without taking
one ﬂawed station into account.
Further work is recommended in order to improve the model such as minimising potential error sources,
e. g. the modelled bottom reﬂectance and the surface reﬂectance.

Introduction
Freshwater quality is vital for human health, organisms and aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems, which
is why monitoring of the water quality is a critical issue for environmental sustainability. Freshwater
ecosystems consist of lakes, reservoirs, rivers, streams, and wetlands and provide valuable services
such as food, sustaining water quality, recreational and aesthetic value and aiding biodiversity (Wetzel,
2001; Brown et al., 2005; Hestir et al., 2015). Additionally, freshwater systems cover around 0.8 % of the
global area and support around 10 % of the world’s animal species (Stendera et al., 2012). However,
the freshwater ecosystems rate of biodiversity loss is the highest globally (Dudgeon et al., 2006).
The ecosystems are under increasing pressure from multiple stressors (Goetz et al., 2008). The stres-
sors include the increasing population, urban expansion, and land use change (Goetz et al., 2008;
Grizzetti et al., 2017). Herein, activities such as agricultural expansion, transportation and industrial
waste discharges are usual sources of pollution and thereby pose a hazard for aquatic ecosystems
function, human health and biodiversity (Ekins et al., 2019; Martin-Ortega et al., 2015; Hestir et al.,
2015). Pollution from point and diﬀuse sources can contain pathogens, nutrients, heavy metals and
organic chemicals (Ekins et al., 2019). Furthermore, due to the climate changes, the freshwater ecosys-
tems are under pressure from changes in degree of hydrological ﬂows (Goetz et al., 2008). Moreover,
the climate changes can lead to introduction of invasive species, which will also increase pressure on
the ecosystem. These pressures can inﬂuence the water quality of the exposed water body and can
cause harm to the ecosystems and consequently the biodiversity (Hassan et al., 2019).
The European Water Framework Directive has been established in order to prevent the declining eco-
logical states of water systems and improve the ecological state to a minimum of good ecological
status (Borja et al., 2006). The physical and chemical status of freshwater is the main determinant
in this context. Good ecological status has been associated with certain thresholds and overpass-
ing of critical thresholds has been connected with reductions in biodiversity and ecological functions
(Lyche et al., 2020). Furthermore, similarly there is a focus on ﬁsh, invertebrates and macrophages.
Therefore, monitoring the water quality is essential to ensure the well-being of the water resources
(Fichot et al., 2016; Qin et al., 2018). Furthermore, based on the water quality the chemical, physical and
biological characteristics and potential contamination sources of water bodies can be determined. Pa-
rameters to determine the water quality measured with remote sensing include e.g. chlorophyll-a (chl
a), Coloured Dissolved Organic Matters (CDOM), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), Turbidity and Chemical
Oxygen Demand (Gholizadeh et al., 2016b). The present study focuses on the parameters chl a, CDOM
and TSS.
==PAGE:9==
2
Chl a is essential in the photosynthesis process where it is responsible for energy ﬁxation. It can be
found in plants and algae. Chl a is a good water quality indicator as phytoplankton reacts to nutrient
enrichment, which can result in algae blooms and consequently deteriorate the water quality of the
water body (Novoa et al., 2012). Furthermore, chl a can be used as a measure of the phytoplankton
biomass. Phytoplankton has normally two peaks for absorption occurring around 430 nm and 675 nm
(Gallegos and Moore, 2000).
Coloured dissolved organic matter (CDOM) is a complex mixture of organic macromolecules mainly
from decaying plant materials and diﬀerent humic and fulvic acids (Giardino et al., 2018). It is a measure
for water clarity and can be used for identifying anthropogenic inﬂuences from agriculture, urban
catchments and determines inﬂuence of land use change (Tzortziou et al., 2015). The CDOM particles
have high absorbance in the visible and ultraviolet spectral region (Tzortziou et al., 2015).
TSS represents the suspended inorganic and organic material in the water column (Song et al., 2010). It
constitutes all suspended particles that are larger than 0.45 µm. It is an indicator of turbidity, thereby
the water clarity. It can aﬀect the primary production and phytoplankton activity. The organic sus-
pended matter has similar absorption properties as CDOM, while the inorganic matter causes high
scattering and low absorbance (Miller et al., 2011).
Current traditional methods to determine water quality are based on in situ measurements, which are
labour intensive and time consuming (Gholizadeh et al., 2016a). Furthermore, the traditional methods
deliver point based representations of the assessed system and therefore identifying spatial and tem-
poral variations can be challenging (Gholizadeh et al., 2016a,b; Hestir et al., 2015). In order to obtain
a comprehensive review of the processes occurring in the assessed area improvement in the current
methodology is needed.
Remote sensing methods provide an opportunity to monitor water quality more eﬃciently (Gholizadeh
et al., 2016a). They provide an alternative method for monitoring and water quality assessment which
are delivered at low cost, high temporal frequency and ability to cover large scale areas (Bresciani
et al., 2011; Gholizadeh et al., 2016b). The visible and near infrared spectrum are often utilised to ac-
quire robust relationships between reﬂectance and the optically active physico-chemical constituents
present in the water column (Gholizadeh et al., 2016b). Furthermore, based on the assessed water body
the appropriate spatial resolution has to be considered. Depending on the resolution it is possible to
separate certain water constituents (Matthews, 2011). Currently, with regard to inland and coastal wa-
ter studies there are no dedicated satellite sensors (Kutser et al., 2020).
Examples of spatial resolution of satellite sensors to determine water quality are Landsat 8, which
spectral resolution is 30 m in the visible and near infrared spectrum, and Sentinel 2 which has a spec-
==PAGE:10==

tral resolution of 10 m for the blue, green, red and Near infra-Red and 20 m for 4 narrow bonds visible
near infrared spectrum, around 704 nm, 740 nm, 783 nm and 865 nm (Free et al., 2020; Bonansea et al.,
2019; Toming et al., 2016; Gholizadeh et al., 2016b; Ihlen and Zanter, 0189; ESA, 2020). Daily or weekly
variations can be challenging using satellite sensors as return periods can be sparse (Topp et al., 2020).
Satellite imagery can be too coarse in order to asses small water bodies, thereby ﬁner resolution is
needed, where other airborne platforms such as unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV) can be used, provid-
ing spatial resolution ranging from 1 - 25 m (Allan and McBride, 2019; Gholizadeh et al., 2016b).
Hyperspectral data consist of narrow spectral bands, which shape a contiguous spectrum (Giardino
et al., 2018). The narrow spectral bands allow detecting important biophysical properties of the water
body and the sediment. Moreover, Hestir et al. (2015) indicated that hyperspectral data are preferable
in optically complex water bodies for bio optical algorithms for assessing water quality.
Bio optical modelling is based on the interaction of light with the active physico-chemical constituents.
The reﬂected light from the optically active constituents in the water column can be used to identify
physical, chemical, and biological characteristics of water such as chl a, CDOM, TSS or water depth
(Giardino et al., 2018; Gallegos and Moore, 2000). The reﬂectance is dependent on the concentrations
and types of constituents in the water body, which also inﬂuence the absorption and transmittance of
light through the water column as it is aﬀected by absorption and backscattering (Gallegos and Moore,
2000).
In case of water, more scattering occurs in the lower wavelengths and less in the higher wavelengths.
On the contrary, more is absorbed in higher wavelengths and less in the lower (Ogashawara et al., 2017).
Additionally, scattering increases the chances for absorption as it increases the length the light must
travel. A conceptual ﬁgure of the processes occurring can be seen in Figure 1. Therefrom algorithms
can be derived, which are based on the relationship between the optical properties of the water body
and the active physico-chemical constituents present in the water (Giardino et al., 2018).
Inherent optical properties are only dependent on the constituents and properties of water, e.g. ab-
sorption and scattering. Apparent optical properties are dependent on the conditions of light and the
inherent optical properties in the water column, e.g. radiance and irradiance.

Several studies have investigated potential to use remote sensing in order to assess freshwater sys-
tems through estimating water quality parameters such as chl a and cyanobacteria for inland water
systems (Bresciani et al., 2011; Flores-Anderson et al., 2020; Ruiz-Verdú et al., 2008; Fichot et al., 2016).
The studies demonstrated that remote sensing can aid in monitoring of inland water systems. Diﬀer-
ent approaches have been used, e.g. empirical, semi-analytical and machine learning models (Topp
et al., 2020). Empirical models utilise statistical relationships of radiometric measurements and in situ
measurements of water constituents (Ogashawara et al., 2017). Semi and analytical models are based
on physics to determine the relationships between Inherent Optical Properties (IOPs) and Apparent
Optical Properties (AOPs) (Ogashawara et al., 2017; Topp et al., 2020). Shallow water semi analytical
models have been developed by Albert and Mobley (2003), Albert (2004), Lee et al. (1998) and Lee et al.
(1999). Lastly, machine learning models include methods such as artiﬁcial neural networks, random
forest/boosted regression trees and support vector machines (Topp et al., 2020).
==PAGE:12==
5
Furthermore, to the best of our knowledge no study has examined the possibility of predicting water
quality indices of streams.
However, there are challenges associated with small water bodies. General issues include low light
conditions due to high absorption of water. Furthermore, more challenges are associated with using
UAVs as shading from trees and inﬂuence from the bottom sediment, adding complexity to the signal.
The aim of the project is to investigate potential bio optical modelling by establishing a semi analytical
model in narrow freshwater streams using hyperspectral sensors. Thereby, a bio optical model is
developed relying on in situ measured concentrations of individual spectral absorption and modelled
backscattering signatures of optically active constituents. Thereafter, the detected radiance at the
sensors can be inverted to retrieve the water quality parameters.

2.2
Field work
7
The study site is located in the northern region of Zealand in Denmark. The project focuses on the
Usserød stream, which emerges from Sjælsø Lake and stretches over around eight kilometres where
it ﬂows out into the Baltic sea (Riverscapes, 2020). The stream traverses through suburban landscape
where it ﬂows through three municipalities, Rudersdal, Hørsholm and Fredensborg. The surface water
catchment is constituted by agricultural land use (approximately 58 %), urban land use (approximately
22 %), natural land use ( approximately 17%) and covers around 120 k m2. An overview of the land use of
the water catchment can be seen in Figure 2 and in Table 1 the primary land use eﬀecting the sampling
station can be observed. Along the stream three dams, Stampedam, Fabriksdam and Mølledam, have
been established. Furthermore, the stream recieves eﬄuent from three wastewater treatment plants
Sjælsmark, Sjælsø and Horshølm WWTP. Throughout the stream eleven discrete sampling stations are
established, as can be seen in Figure 2. Sjælsmark and Sjælsø WWTP discharge upstream from Station
2, around 300 m, while Horshølm WWTP discharges upstream from Station 5.
2.2
Field work
A ﬁeld campaign was conducted in April 2020 from the 28th to the 30th, where conditions were sunny
to cloudy. An overview of the conditions when obtaining the in situ measurements can be seen in
Table 2. Water samples were collected in order to determine the inherent optical properties of the
stream, which were later analysed. Furthermore, hyperspectral data were acquired for the sampling
stations using an UAV with a hyperspectral snapshot imager (138 bands VIS-NIR, 50x50 pixels) and a
spectrophotometer with a cosine receptor to measure downwelling irradiance. Furthermore, in-situ
measurements of hyperspectral data of upwelling and downwelling irradiance were obtained at the
surface of the stream, just below the surface and at an interval of 10 cm of depth with the lowest point
taken at the bottom of the stream for the eleven sampling stations.

Absorption coeﬃcients
In order to determine the absorption coeﬃcients of the water constituents, the water samples were
taken at each station and ﬁltered through Whatman GF/F ﬁlters (47 mm) with the pore size of 0.7 µm
with the purpose of measuring chl a, TSS and CDOM. The ﬁlters and the water sample for CDOM where
then stored at dark conditions. Filters for phytoplankton and CDOM were stored at - 20 oC, while the
TSS ﬁlter was stored at - 80 oC.
2.3.1
Phytoplankton
In order to determine the absorption coeﬃcient the ﬁlter was transferred to a vial and added 5 mL of
ethanol with the purpose of dissolving the chlorophyll from the ﬁlter. The samples were then stored
for 24 hrs in 4 oC and dark conditions. The samples were then centrifuged at a speed of 6500 rpm

for 10 min in order for the particles to produce a pellet. The supernatant was extracted and analysed
using a spectrophotometer for the wavelengths 400 - 900 nm. A vial ﬁlled with ethanol was used as a
reference.
2.3.2
TSS
In order to determine the absorbance of TSS, the ﬁlters were cut in half and placed between two glass
sheets. The samples were then analysed with a spectrophotometer using an integration sphere. A
blank ﬁlter moistened with MiliQ water was used as reference. The ﬁlters were thereafter bleached
with NaClO. In order to ensure the minimal loss of particles during the bleaching process a vacuum
pump was used. Thereafter, acid was applied and the ﬁlters were left for 3 minutes. The ﬁlters were
rinsed with Miliq water and analysed again.
2.3.3
CDOM
In order to determine the absorbance of CDOM, the ﬁltered water samples were analysed with the
spectrophotometer.
2.4

The present study utilises a bio optical model in order to estimate the reﬂectance above the water
surface . The method is based on the relationship between IOPs and AOPs, where chl a, CDOM, TSS
and bottom reﬂectance are taken into account. The model estimates reﬂectance between 400 and
800 nm. Furthermore, it is assumed that the optical properties do not change with depth meaning
that the water column is homogeneously mixed. The developed model is attached as an Appendix,
see Appendix 2.

3.1
Variability of parameters throughout Usserød stream
In Table 1 the concentrations throughout the stream can be observed. Through the stream the con-
centrations of chl a, TSS and CDOM change. The chl a concentrations diﬀers from 7.21 to 20.21 mg/m3.
Based on the ECA classiﬁcation Stations 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 and 10 are classiﬁed as Class II, while Stations 2, 8
and 11 are classiﬁed as Class III (Enderlein et al., 1996). It is noted that the stations that exhibits higher
concentrations of chl a are located, where the primary land use is agriculture. This could indicate that
possibly the land use inﬂuences the stream through enrichment with nutrients. A higher variation can
be seen in TSS, which varies from 3.26 to 101.36 mg/m3. The large contribution in TSS can be found at
Station 2, where there is a discharge from a WWTP upstream. This may indicate that during the ﬁeld
work there was an overﬂow from the WWTP. CDOM varies from 0.91 to 4.77 mg/m3. The lowest concen-
tration is found at Station 1, however at the other stations the concentrations are similar. Furthermore,
diﬀerent sediment types are found throughout the stream, where 5 diﬀerent types are identiﬁed and
can be found in Table 1.

spectral range. However, the surface reﬂection in reality does vary (Gould, 2001). The surface reﬂection
varies based on the uniformity of the water surface, weather conditions, instrument orientation, solar
altitude and sun glint (Gould, 2001).
Furthermore, as observed by the speciﬁc absorption spectrum of phytoplankton from the diﬀerent
stations, there is a variability between the measurements. Diﬀerent phytoplankton species could occur
at the diﬀerent stations or the assemblages can be diﬀerent. The cell size of diﬀerent phytoplankton
species diﬀers, which aﬀects the absorption on the fraction of light that is backscattered. For example
the backscattering ratio is generally less than 1 % for phytoplankton (Matthews, 2017; Ahn et al., 1992),
but Ahn et al. (1992) investigated the backscattering of some phytoplankton species and found for
intermediate sized phytoplankton the backscattering ratio was lower than 0.1 % in the wavelengths
400 to 650 nm. Sun et al. (2010) assumed a backscattering ratio of 0.5 % in Lake Taihu in China. Similar
variability is observed for CDOM and TSS, which could be due to diﬀerences in the size of particles
as aforementioned. Thereby, when taking the average from the diﬀerent stations, speciﬁcity is lost,
however overall a more general model is achieved. Moreover, the Angstrom coeﬃcient is assumed to
be zero, which it is normally in shallow and inland waters, however the value could diﬀer (Giardino
et al., 2018). The exponent has been found to be 1.7 for small particles and 0.3 for large (Albert, 2004).
Therefore, these assumptions are a source of error and can be potentially further improved. An error
source could stem from the used average solar angle and not the speciﬁc solar angle for the respective
time when the measurements were taken. Furthermore, currently the model only includes elastic
scattering processes, therefore including inelastic processes could improve the model further.

Further work is recommended on minimising the sources of errors, such as utilising the precise sun
angle, when the measurements were taken at the respective station instead of the average. Addition-
ally, determining variables such as backscattering ratio of phytoplankton and the angstrom coeﬃcient
could be improved. As aforementioned the backscattering ratio diﬀers depending on the cell size of
the phytoplankton. The Angstrom coeﬃcient is based on an assumption that in inland and shallow
waters it is zero, however it could diﬀer as described in Section 3.3.1. Thereby, improving these coef-
ﬁcients to resemble similar environments as Usserød stream is recommended. Additionally, further
work on improving the bottom reﬂectance should be done as previously discussed.

4
Conclusion
A bio optical model taking into account the optically active water constituents chl a, CDOM and TSS was
developed in order to asses the potential of water monitoring for streams. Usserød stream, located
in the northern region of Zealand in Denmark was the study area, where 11 sampling stations were
established. The water constituents chl a, CDOM and TSS was measured at the diﬀerent stations.
Furthermore, in situ measurements of upwelling and downwelling irradiance were obtained through
the use of a ASD Handheld 2 with ﬁber optic cable and a cosine receptor or 10 o optic lens based on
the measured upwelling or downwelling irradiance.
For the reﬂectance above the water surface the model performed on average R 2 of 0.77, an R of 0.94
and RM SE of 0.00325 with Station 8 excluded. However, with Station 8 included an R 2 of -2.54, R of
0.95 and an RM SE of 0.0063 is obtained. Overall, a high R is obtained of 0.94, indicating that the
shape of the model resembles the measured. Good results were achieved for Station 7, 9, 10 and 11,
where their respective R 2 are 0.96, 0.93 and 0.79 and 0.82. A lower performance is achieved for Station
6, where the estimated reﬂectance appears ﬂat. The results ranges from 0.49 to 0.93 R 2, 0.86 to 0.99,
0.86 to 0.99 R and 0.00055 to 0.0052 RM SE.
For the modelled beneath the water surface reﬂectance there appears to be a slight bias as for most
of the stations it achieves a negative R 2. Thereby, an average of the measurements would perform
better. However, similarly as the above it achieves a good R of 0.91. The model results range is -0.49
to 0.88 R 2, 0.93 to 0.99 R and 0.0016 to 0.023 RM SE without the Station 8.
The model was compared to the publicly available WASI4 model. The WASI4 model performs similarly
as the current model, where it achieves an R 2 of 0.77, R of 0.97 and RM SE of 0.0030 excluding Station
8 as the modelled bottom reﬂectance is ﬂawed.
Further work is recommended in order to improve the model such as minimising potential error sources
e.g. utilising the precise sun angle of when the measurements were taken at the respective station
instead of the average sun angle from the whole ﬁeld campaign. Furthermore, the bottom reﬂectance
can be improved for some of the stations. Estimation of variables such as the surface reﬂectance can
also be improved.
Overall, the results of the model displays the potential for the further development. Inversion of the
model can be performed in order to be able to estimate water quality parameters based the reﬂectance
spectra. UAVs can apply the inverted model to generate concentration maps of important parameters
such as chl a, CDOM or TSS. However, there might be a technological limitation of what can be detected
==PAGE:40==

depending on the spectral resolution of the spectral camera installed in the UAV. Thereby a response
function can be used to determine the potential.
