Abstract
The optimization of dual cycling operations under uncertain cargo arrival on Roll­on Roll­
off (RoRo) vessels is of great importance in reducing greenhouse gases in the maritime
shipping industry. This problem is described using a mathematical model; thus, prede­
fined assumptions must be established. For example, future cargo arrivals are modeled
using a Poisson process, cargoes are categorized by weight, and all tug trips are assumed
to take a constant amount of time. The formulation is a two­stage stochastic mixed in­
teger linear programming (2S­MILP) model, enabling the opportunity to integrate integer
variables and scenarios. Firstly, the commercial solver Gurobi Optimization is used to
solve the problem. Only a few realistic instances with a maximum of 70 slots and 50
scenarios can be solved to optimum within 3 hours. This has no practical prospects, so
decomposition methods are imposed on the problem to reduce the solving times. The
Integer L­shaped Method (ILSM), a stage­based decomposition approach, is useful for
solving a small test example to optimum but requires too many feasibility cuts on realistic
instances. Also, a scenario­based approach is carried out, called Progressive Hedging Al­
gorithm (PHA). Likewise, it can only solve the test example, yet a bit faster than the ILSM.
However, the method does not guarantee the finding of the global optimum. This leads to
the proposal of a Rolling Horizon Heuristic (RHH). The heuristic is very efficient and ro­
bust and achieves results close to the optimum. However, increasing the size of the deck
and the number of scenarios negatively impacts the performance regarding the objective
value and computational time. Also, the objective value rises when increasing the num­
ber of different weight types but in general, RHH is robust when solving instances with a
maximum of 50 scenarios, even for enormous decks with 150 slots. The complexity of the
precedence settings affects the performance slightly but can be considered insignificant.
Nevertheless, the maximum number of tugs allowed to operate simultaneously on deck
is crucial for the objective value, number of dual cycles, and computational time. More
tugs on deck improve the objective, decrease the need for performing dual cycles, and
raise the computational cost. As the heuristic seems highly suitable to solve the problem,
a case study is conducted with an even more realistic instance. The deck layout is based
on Upper Deck 2, Hollandia Seaways, DFDS. A ramp and a turning lane for the tugs are
integrated in the precedence graph. Indentions in the deck space are also considered. In
reality, the ship might not be full of cargo at arrival. Thus, we assume that only 108 slots
of 125 available slots are discharged and loaded. Again, the problem is solved very fast,
and the solution appears to be practically implementable. We estimate the average value
of the stochastic solution (VSS) and get that the benefit from using stochastic program­
ming compared to using expected values is observable but small. We conclude that RHH
could be applied to solve the 2S­MILP model with significant environmental benefits and
financial benefits in the RoRo industry. For further research, equipment­specific cargo,
multiple decks, continuous time intervals, or other modeling approaches for cargo arrival
forecast could be integrated.

Introduction
This thesis focuses on optimizing the discharging and loading of cargo on RoRo (Roll­
on/Roll­off) ships under uncertain cargo arrival. Traditionally, all cargoes are unloaded
first, followed by the loading operation. Half of the time, tugs travel without cargo, which is
inefficient. A dual cycle involves picking up and loading cargo immediately after discharge.
By taking advantage of this concept, we seek to minimize the turnaround time at the port.
The main motivation is to reduce greenhouse gases (GHGs) in the global maritime ship­
ping sector. This aligns with the GHG ambition of the International Maritime Organization
(IMO) to reduce carbon intensity by at least 70% in 2040 compared to 2008 cf. IMO
(2023). One way to limit CO2 emissions is by slow steaming, as fuel consumption has
a nearly cubic relationship with the speed of the ship cf. Psaraftis and Kontovas (2013).
Slow steaming could save up to 16% CO2 emissions in the European RoRo shipping in­
dustry (see Lindstad et al. (2015)). Reducing the turnaround time at the port allows for
slow steaming while ship departures and arrivals are still on time. In addition, streamlining
this process is a more environmentally sustainable and cost­efficient use of the handling
equipment at the port.
Prior research in this field has not been extensive. Jia, Tierney, L. B. Reinhardt, et al.
(2022) covers the most relevant research related to this study. Jia develops an IP model
and a GRKA approach to minimize the total makespan of the discharging and loading
operations. Evaluating the algorithm on industrial instances yields up to 25% less CO2
emissions for RoRo operations. However, the study does not account for uncertain cargo
arrival times. The main research question in this study is:
How can uncertainty in cargo arrivals be handled when optimizing dual cycling operations
in a RoRo terminal?

Literature Review
In this chapter, we provide a literature review on cargo shipping in general, RoRo SSS,
dual cycling and uncertain cargo arrivals.
The shipping industry has been prone to a substantial amount of research. Regarding
discharging and loading operations, the focus has mainly been on minimizing the handling
times, driven distances, and empty moves for the container handling equipment (see Vu
and K. Kim (2010)).
The roll­on roll­off field is weakly represented in literature but has become increasingly
popular recently. For instance, in Jia, Niels Rytter, et al. (2019) the discharge time of cargo
units is analyzed and estimated based on loading positions. In addition, the minimization
of ballast water through stowage planning and optimization of stability, energy efficiency,
and safety is investigated in Jia, Fagerholt, L. Reinhardt, and Niels Gorm Rytter (2020) and
Jia and Fagerholt (2021). Other studies conducted within a RoRo context are Øvstebø,
Hvattum, and Fagerholt (2011), which looks into routing and scheduling of RoRo vessels,
and Zis and Psaraftis (2017), analyzing the effects of the sulphur emission limits on RoRo
operations.
Research on dual cycling is primarily linked to container handling in the literature. One
example is Goodchild and Daganzo (2006), looking into the quay crane double cycling
problem (QCDCP) at container terminals and more specifically optimal load ordering of
container stacks. However, a large shortage in this research, is that they assumed no
precedence in the loading and discharging operations, which is unrealistic. Since 2006
several extensions have been made building upon Goodchild and Daganzo’s work. Note­
worthy is Song and Kwak (2009), who looked into optimal starting points for a dual cycle
in a QCDCP with a single crane framework. Zhang and K. H. Kim, 2009 extends the
QCDCP in container terminals by considering hatch covers and maximizes the number
of dual­cycle operations with a MIP model and local search approach.
Just as improving loading and discharging operations are important in container termi­
nals, one could imagine that researching dual cycling in the RoRo field has great potential.
However, to the best of our knowledge, the connection between RoRo and dual cycling is
explored in only one paper, which we will examine in greater detail. The paper ”Optimal
dual cycling operations in roll­on roll­off terminals” (see Jia, Tierney, L. Reinhardt, et al.
(2022)) presents a mathematical formulation of the RoRo Dual Cycling Problem (RRDCP).
The model is too complex to be solved quickly by a commercial solver. Hence they de­
velop a BRKGA heuristic. The results show, that it is robust and efficient as it solves
realistic instances within a few seconds close to optimality. In addition, they conclude
that the algorithms enable the possibility of reducing fuel consumption and CO2 emission
by up to 25% in an industrial case. The paper is of high significance to this study as it
is the first and up until now only study in the literature investigating optimization of dual
cycling operations on RoRo vessels. Hence we will let us be inspired by the paper’s way
of viewing the RRDCP and corresponding mathematical formulation.
Jia, Tierney, L. B. Reinhardt, et al. (2022) is a great foundation for our study, but Jia
assumes that all cargo is available for loading at the port. As cargoes often arrive late due
to weather conditions, prior port activities, and unforeseen events we seek to investigate
how uncertain cargo arrival affects the model and solution approaches. Note also, that Jia,
Tierney, L. B. Reinhardt, et al. (2022) did not consider cargoes of different weight classes.

3.1
Stochastic Programming
The cargo arrival pattern is considered uncertain in our proposal for the RoRo dual cycling
problem. To model uncertainty we use stochastic programming, which involves random
variables, first­stage decisions, and recourse actions. In this section, we will review the
basics of two­stage stochastic linear programs with recourse, which forms the foundation
for the modeling throughout the report. The classical model formulation in matrix form
looks as follows cf. Birge and Louveaux (1997):

taken. The deterministic first­stage term cT x and the expectation over all scenarios ω ∈Ω
of the second­stage term q(ω)Ty(ω) together constitute the objective function.
Stochastic programming allows us to initially make first­stage decisions with the prior avail­
able information. Afterward, as uncertainty is revealed and new information becomes
available, we can then make second­stage decisions. We consider many different pos­
sible outcomes of the future, in our case, cargo arrival patterns. This way, the model
enables robust decisions as we have taken uncertainty into account. Thus, we can opti­
mize the objective despite the risks we face in the future.
3.2
RoRo shipping
One of the most crucial methods of transporting cargo in Europe is through Roll­on/Roll­
off (Ro­Ro) short sea shipping (SSS). The terminology roll essentially means that the
freighted cargo has wheels, such as trailers, cars, heavy machinery, and secus cf. Jia,
Fagerholt, L. Reinhardt, and Niels Rytter (2020). RoRo shipping operates on fixed sched­
ules, transporting a diverse selection of wheeled cargo in a liner shipping mode. The
different types of cargo can have very different heights, widths, and lengths which is a
great benefit of the RoRo vessels. Tug drivers use ramps to roll the cargo onto the ves­
sel and further from deck to deck. Cargoes are both discharged and loaded through the
rear meaning the operations follow a first­in­last­out (FILO) procedure (cf. Main, Pacino,

and Rodrigues (2024)). The major advantage of Ro­Ro SSS is the efficient cargo supply
chain. Handling requirements at the port are dramatically reduced, which is both cost­ and
time­efficient. Ro­Ro SSS has fast transit times, high flexibility, and frequent departures
in comparison to container shipping. RoRo shipping is part of the answer in facilitating
a shift towards more eco­friendly and sustainable transport. The studies Hjelle (2010),
Ng (2009) and Christodoulou, Raza, and Woxenius (2019) show that a combination of
transport modes is often preferable in terms of being emission­efficient. RoRo SSS is an
important piece of the intermodal transport chains, that will enhance the sustainability of
future European transport.
Ro­Ro SSS is a growing market in Europe, with increasing competition. Long shorelines
and numerous islands make short voyages ideal, making RoRo shipping a major trans­
port form in Italy, Denmark, Norway, and Sweden (cf. Jia, Tierney, L. Reinhardt, et al.
(2022)). One of the major Danish short­sea operators is DFDS (see Figure 3.1), which is
continuously expanding its Ro­Ro fleet capacity. This thesis is part of the finalized RoRo­
Green project, which aimed to assist in making Danish RoRo shipping both greener and
faster through innovative and digital solutions. As DFDS was one of the collaborators, we
have had the privilege of sharing ideas with professionals from the industry and gathering
important knowledge on how operations in RoRo terminals work.
As stated in Chapter 2, RoRo shipping remains under­researched but different sorts of
topics and challenges have been investigated, including stowage planning, estimation
of discharge time, and dual cycling. Dual cycling is the simultaneous execution of dis­
charging and loading operations. This must be carried out with an eye for cargoes often
arriving later than agreed. Hence emerges dual cycling under uncertain cargo arrival in
RoRo shipping, which is exactly the scope of this thesis.

Model Formulation
In this section, we will describe the problem in detail and derive the mathematical model.
This study examines dual cycling in RoRo terminals under uncertain cargo arrival. Before
a RoRo ship can begin a voyage, the cargo from the previous voyage must be discharged
and the new cargo must be loaded. Tugs operated by drivers complete the operations.
In each planning period, cargoes are divided into three separate groups, namely cargoes
already on board, cargoes currently available at the port, and cargoes expected to arrive
in the future. These groups are updated over time depending on discharged, loaded,
and arrived cargo up to the present. Future cargo arrivals are modeled via a Poisson
process. We assume that cargoes arrive randomly and independently. The rate param­
eter λ represents the average number of cargo arriving per time unit. Thus, the time
between consecutive cargo arrivals called inter­arrival times is exponentially distributed
with parameter λ. We run the Poisson process multiple times to imitate the many different
outcomes that could occur, thus creating scenarios. Therefore it makes perfect sense to
model the problem as a two­stage stochastic MILP model. The objective is to minimize
the turnaround time, which is accomplished by maximizing the number of dual cycles (de­
pending on the number of tugs allowed on deck). We assume cargo to come in different
weight classes, which should be placed in specific areas of the deck. These areas are
predetermined and should reduce the need for compensatory ballast water. We assume
that the vessel has only one deck. Note also that the representation method is a slot­
based layout. This means, that the deck is divided into several lanes, each containing
some evenly sized slots. At each slot, only cargo with a specific type of weight is allowed
to be stowed. One can not discharge the cargo at the back of the deck in the beginning,
nor load cargo at the ramp in the end. Therefore we assume predefined rules, which are
implemented in the model via precedence constraints. With inspiration from Jia, Tierney,
L. Reinhardt, et al. (2022), we operate with three different precedence settings. In the
simple lane setting we ensure that lanes are discharged in the order of the cargoes in the
lane. However, in the adjacent lane setting, to discharge cargo, the preceding cargo in
the lane must be discharged as well as the preceding cargo in the adjacent lane. The
last setting double adjacent lane applies the same principle but adjacent lanes in both
directions need to be considered. By this, we are trying to capture a realistic picture, but
of course, in the real world the deck is not necessarily rectangular, and there could be ob­
stacles to consider as well. Finally, we need to make some assumptions about the tugs.
We suppose that one movement from ship to port or the opposite always takes a constant
amount of time, independent of the length of the trip and whether the tug is carrying cargo
or not. Furthermore, we assume that a constant number of tugs are available throughout
the entire turnover. Due to safety and collision considerations, idle tugs must stay at the
port instead of on board. Only a proportion of the available tugs are allowed to operate
on the vessel simultaneously.

Solution approches
This chapter will go through the methodology used to solve the RRDCP. In Chapter 4 we
proposed a 2S­MILP model that assumes a slot­based layout on a single deck. Due to
the computational complexity of solving this model using a commercial solver, we explore
alternative solution methods. Specifically, we decompose the problem using an Integer
L­shaped Method and employ a Progressive Hedging Algorithm. However, these decom­
position methods also exhibit long running times. Consequently, we implement a Rolling
Horizon Heuristic to address computational challenges.
5.1
Integer L­shaped Method
We prefer to solve the instances to optimality, but we need to speed up the process.
For this reason, we will try a decomposition approach. Decomposition methods are of­
ten used in literature to solve two­stage stochastic models. The number of algorithms is
countless, but they can be divided into the following main principles: stage­based decom­
position and scenario­based decomposition. One way to decompose the problem is by
using the Integer L­shaped Method, which we will focus on in this section. ILSM is a case
of the stage­based decomposition methods. Other examples could be the more general
Benders decomposition algorithms. Instead of solving each stage separately, one could
also solve each scenario one by one. This is called scenario­based decomposition meth­
ods, which among others include dual decomposition and Progressive Hedging (see e.g.
Jalilvand, Bashiri, and Nikzad (2021)), which we will look further into in Section 5.2.
The Integer L­shaped Method is described in Birge and Louveaux (1997). We present

employing a Depth­first search (DFS) strategy. Due to the ILSM’s theoretical foundation,

The model cp is solved sequentially inside a branching scheme. The most well­known
methods for searching a branching tree are Breath­first search (BFS) and Depth­first
search (DFS). Our implementation uses a DFS. Whenever we fathom, append, or pick
a new node it is always the last in the pendant node list. This way, an entire branch is
searched until pruned, before continuing in the tree. We use DFS, as the structure of the
tree is very deep and not particularly wide. Each parent has 2 children, but there could be
up to |N|(|D| + |L|) (number of first­stage variables) levels.
We prune nodes by the three principles infeasibility, bound or optimality. As the source
node is relaxed, nodes are also fathomed due to integrality restrictions. If the cp solution
is infeasible, then it is also infeasible in the original model. If the current lower bound θv is
larger than the best known objective value (upper bound) ¯z, we can prune by bound. The
optimality principle takes effect if the current node’s lower bound θv is larger than or equal
to the current node’s upper bound Q(xv). We can not get any more information from this
branch, thus we can prune again.

Results
In this chapter, we will test the methods examined in Chapter 5. We generate a lot of
different instances, which we will use to compare methods throughout this chapter. The
discrepancy in the instances lies in tweaking parameters. In this way, we test the capa­
bilities of the methods as the problem’s complexity increases.
6.1
ILSM
First, we test our implementation Algorithm 1 of the Integer L­shaped Method. We solve
some small and simple instances for 3 hours focusing only on 30 and 70 slots with 10
or 50 scenarios. For each combination of slot and scenario, we create 10 instances with
random cargo arrival patterns. This yields 40 instances across the parameters in Table
A.1, Appendix A. Throughout this chapter, the number of lanes on the deck is 6, 7, 10,
and 10 for respectively 30,70,110, and 150 slots.
Unfortunately, the decomposition method can not solve even the least complex instances
within 3 hours. On average, ILSM performs 38222 iterations and 4328 feasible cuts. Too
many feasibility cuts are needed, meaning that a first­stage solution is often infeasible in
the second­stage program. Therefore, the method does not add optimality cuts for any of
the instances.
We create an unrealistically small and simple instance to test if the algorithm works as
intended. We set Lend = Dend = 10 distributed on 5 lanes. When the number of cargoes
on each lane is small, the precedence constraints are much easier to satisfy. For prece­
dence, we use simple lane setting. Also, we set N = 12, tlca = 8, Cend, K = 5 and T = 2.
The decomposition method terminates after v = 8997 iterations, s = 1628 optimality cuts,
and l = 273 feasibility cuts. After 69 seconds a feasible solution with objective value ¯z = 8
is found and as L = 8 the method terminates at optimality. Hence, we conclude that the
method works as desired on a tiny experiment, but is unsuitable for solving realistic cases
of the problem.
6.2
PHA
Next, we test whether the Progressive Hedging Algorithm matches the problem better.
Again we solve the 40 very basic instances. The PHA uses on average 470 iterations
and solves none of them. For the small and non­realistic case from the previous Section
6.1, the algorithm uses 128 iterations in 19 seconds and terminates at the optimal solution.
We deduct that PHA neither is useful for practical cases. Also, we know from theory that
even when it finds a solution, it does not guarantee optimality. Thus we move on to test
the RHH in the following Section 6.3.

Case study ­ Upper Deck 2, Hollandia Seaways, DFDS
In this subsection, we test the RHH on a real deck space, specifically Upper Deck 2,
Hollandia Seaways, DFDS. The deck space is shown in Figure 6.4, from the Final Trim
and Stability Booklet, Hollandia Seaways (confidential document, thus not cited).
Figure 6.4: Overview of Upper Deck 2, Hollandia Seaways, DFDS
This deck requires extra constraints to the modeling. Until now, we have dealt with a
perfectly square deck layout, but Deck 2 has an irregular design. Also, note that we omit
the ramp leading up to the weather deck, creating extra indentions in the deck space. We
assume that the ramp on the starboard side is part of the deck space, but tugs only enter
through the ramp on the port side. The deck is divided into 10 lanes and 15 columns,
but we only consider 135 slots, whereas 10 slots are allocated to the turning lane which
can not accommodate cargo. This causes 125 actual loadable slots. However, when
the ship arrives at the port, most likely it is not filled with cargo and the same applies to
departure. Hence we randomly mark an additional 17 slots as empty at arrival. In addition,
we randomly chose 17 slots that should not be assigned a cargo. This results in the sets
D and L having a cardinality of 108.
We take care of this by modifying the precedence graphs in PD and PL. First, we must
declare some new sets. SL is the set of slots on the ship. Here we consider all 150
entries, even though 25 slots are always empty. The set of slots that are either not part
of the deck at all or are empty at discharge is defined as SL\D. Likewise, the slots in
SL\L are not part of the deck or can not be loaded. The rows and columns in PD which
are in SL\D are set to 0. We apply the same principle to PL. Next, we introduce a new
parameter PDL. If an entry (i, j) is 1, the slot i should be discharged before loading slot
j. First, we set it to the union PDL = PD ∪PL. The reasoning behind this is that if
slot i should be discharged before slot j, and j should be discharged before it is loaded,
then i should be discharged before j is loaded. The same logic applies the other way
around. Hereafter, we set the rows to 0 if they are in SL\D and the columns to 0 if they
are in SL\L. This adjustment allows us to define the constraints (6.1). Since the deck is
only partially discharged and loaded, including these constraints is crucial to ensure the

6.4
Solution Evaluation
This section will evaluate the value of applying stochastic programming to the problem.
For this sake, we will go ahead and outline two import quality metrics, which are the
expected value of perfect information (EVPI) and the value of the stochastic solution
(VSS). For minimization problems this is calculated as EV PI = RP −WS and V SS =
EEV −RP, where WS is the expected value of the wait­and­see approach, RP is the
expected value of the recourse solution and EEV is the expectation of the expected­value
Stochastic Dual Cycling in RoRo shipping
28
==PAGE:34==
solution (see Tsaousoglou and Madsen (2023b)). We solve the program with Gurobi in­
stead of RHH, as we need the global optimum to compare the quality metrics and preserve
the relationship WS ≤RP ≤EEV for minimization. We use instances with 50 scenarios
and 30 slots as they are occasionally solvable, due to Table 6.1. We create 280 instances
with double adjacent lane settings for precedence, different weight types, and 40 random
cargo arrival patterns across the parameters in Appendix A, Table A.6.
All 280 instances are solved with the WS approach. EEV is solved in 157 cases. EEV
is the solution to the recourse problem (RP) but with fixed first­stage variables derived
from the deterministic expected value (EV) program. Hence 123 instances are not solved,
because the first­stage variables are infeasible in the second­stage problem. The average
objective values for WS, RP, and EEV are respectively 44.50, 44.50, and 44.60. 150
instances are solved to optimum with the RP, while the rest hit the time limit without finding
an incumbent. 134 instances are solved across all approaches. They yield an average
EV PI = 0.0 and V SS = 0.1031, which are very small numbers. The EVPI suggests that
the RoRo operators should not invest in getting more precise cargo arrival information, as
that would be money wasted. The benefit from using stochastic programming instead of
expected values is limited, due to the very small VSS. Note that we can not be certain if
this trend will hold for more complex instances with many scenarios and slots. Figure 6.6
shows the objective value using RP and EEV for all solved instances. The solution to WS
is always equal to the RP solution, thus omitted in the plot. We distinguish the dots from
each other using different colors and shapes. We see that they often lie close to each
other. However, for specific instances, the difference is clear, with an objective value
difference of about 1. This indicates that in some cases, solving the recourse problem is
more valuable compared to the expectation of the expected value.

Discussion
We have applied three different solution methods on the 2S­MILP model, among them
two decomposition approaches; Integer L­shaped Method and Progressive Hedging Al­
gorithm, and a heuristic approach; Rolling Horizon Heuristic. The decomposition methods
are unfit for the problem, as they can not solve realistic size instances. However, RHH
is highly suitable for the problem, which is evident in the comparison with Gurobi from
Table 6.1. Due to the sensitivity analysis from Chapter 6, we find that the computational
time and objective function highly depend on the deck size. Increasing the number of
scenarios is also important for the algorithm’s robustness. We establish 50 scenarios as
a reasonable amount. Increasing the number of slots and weight types makes the prob­
lem harder to solve and worsens the makespan. Nonetheless, the performance of RHH
seems to be unaffected by the precedence setting. However, allowing many tugs to work
simultaneously on the deck improves the objective function, and concurrently reduces the
number of dual cycles needed. We note that the RHH can successfully be applied to a
real deck space, namely Upper Deck 2, Hollandia Seaways, DFDS. Here, the complexity
is boosted further, as we assume an asymmetrically shaped and partly filled deck, with
both ramp and turning lane. The solution evaluation yields an almost non­existent EVPI
and VSS, meaning that additional cargo arrival information is redundant and stochastic
programming is unnecessary compared to just using expected values.
The results show that we have found an effective solution approach to optimizing dis­
charging and loading operations in RoRo terminals while considering uncertain cargo ar­
rivals. Late arrival times are certainly not uncommon, so this is a crucial addition to the
optimization. Terminal operators can with confidence execute the loading plan, without
worrying too much about uncertainties as many scenarios are already considered. The
ship can leave the port earlier than it does today, and sail with a decreased sailing speed.
Ultimately, this reduces the emission of GHGs contributing to more sustainable shipping.
While these are excellent findings, we need to account for the limitations and sources of
error. This study is based on self­generated cargo arrival data from a Poisson process. It
would be of great benefit to extract cargo arrival scenarios using historical data from the
terminals. Furthermore, scenarios from the real world would take place on a continuous
timeline, just as operational decisions are not made at discrete time points in reality. This
study focuses on one deck at a time, but most RoRo ships have a minimum of three
decks. In industrial practice, it is important to consider the location of entrances and
ramps to establish the discharging and loading order, hence the precedence settings.
Another note on precedence is obstacles on the deck, which tugs should also bypass
when operating on the deck. The route could also include multiple ports. This could
imply that one wants to keep some cargo on the ship at an intermediate port stop. The
study considers cargoes of different weight types, but many more cargo specifications
could be considered. Some cargoes are hazardous, while refrigerated cargoes must have
access to electricity. Also, there are many types of cargo with different sizes, like trailers,
secus, mafis, and cars. All this information could be important to the operational decisions
of the sequence of actions. Before formulating the mathematical model, we also made
assumptions regarding the tugs. In reality, tug movement times could differ a lot as ports
and decks could be very large. One could also imagine that picking up cargo to discharge
right beside the cargo just loaded would be beneficial. In the current model, the tug is
allowed to drive to the opposite side of the deck, without penalty as movement on the
Stochastic Dual Cycling in RoRo shipping
30
==PAGE:36==
ship and port is neglected. There are also different kinds of tugs, designed for specific
cargoes which are not taken into account. This could limit the solution space a great deal,
making it a potential extension of the model.
Due to the time limit, decisions are made on the scope of the thesis. For further analy­
sis, one could fine­tune the parameters for fixed duration and planning duration in RHH
to improve performance. In Barnett, Watson, and Woodruff (2016), a branch and bound
algorithm is wrapped around the PHA, making the final BBPH method guaranteeing op­
timality. This implementation could be carried out on the problem and compared to the
Gurobi results. Regarding the ILSM in Birge and Louveaux (1997) improved optimality
cuts are proposed, based on neighboring solutions. This is also an obvious extension to
Section 5.1, even though it might not be worth much as long as the extensive amount of
feasibility cuts is the main problem. We remember from Table 6.2 that the objective value
seems to have converged already for a small number of scenarios. This information,
together with the extremely low VSS from Section 6.4, indicates that stochastic program­
ming is not beneficial. This tendency could be due to scenario generation, particularly the
value of the rate parameter λ. A parameter tuning of λ and analysis of the corresponding
new results could be assessed in further research.

Conclusion
This work shows that uncertain cargo arrival in RoRo terminals can be handled using a
two­stage stochastic MILP model and that a Rolling Horizon Heuristic solves this model
close to optimum utilizing very limited computational time. When considering many sce­
narios, the model becomes overly challenging to solve with a commercial solver within a
reasonable amount of time. Hence, the study investigates whether we can beneficially
decompose the model, thus solving smaller parts and saving computational time. Apply­
ing the Integer L­shaped Method to the problem is inappropriate, as too many feasibility
cuts are generated. Hence the approach is completely useless for instances of realis­
tic scale. The Progressive Hedging Algorithm can solve larger instances, but still less
complex versions of the world. Also, a large disadvantage of the PHA is that it does not
guarantee optimality.
However, the Rolling Horizon Heuristic proves to be very efficient, hence we perform an
extensive sensitivity analysis to test the limits of the heuristic. We create multiple self­
generated instances with different amounts of scenarios and slots. We find that for small
decks with a maximum of 70 slots, we can work with up to 300 scenarios. However, testing
on instances with 50 scenarios makes the RHH more robust, especially when working
with very large decks of 150 slots. We increase the number of different weight types
on the instances and conclude that it significantly impacts the performance concerning
both objective and computational time. However, it is not that important for the makespan
whether the precedence setting is simple lanes, adjacent lanes, or double adjacent lanes.
Increasing the maximum allowed number of tugs simultaneously operating on the deck
improves the objective up to a certain point where it flattens out. That point is around
7 to 8 tugs for the largest decks after which, it is pointless to have more tugs involved
in the operations. The proportion of dual cycles out of the maximum achievable follows
the same decreasing curve as the objective. This makes sense because having fewer
cargoes working on the deck simultaneously results in a less efficient solution, making
it more crucial for each tug to perform dual cycles. We test the algorithm on the large
and complex Upper Deck 2, Hollandia Seaways, DFDS from our case study and impose
additional restrictions to make it as true to reality as possible (see Subsection 6.3.1). The
deck space is non­square, only partly filled with cargo at arrival and departure, and the
precedence graph considers the ramp and turning lane. Visualisations show that the
algorithm’s operational decisions are feasible and clever. Hence, the algorithm can be
easily employed in industry in practice. Due to the evaluation of the solution, the benefit
of considering uncertainties with a 2S­MILP model compared to using expected values is
marginal, but apparent for a few of the instances used. However, it is not worth obtaining
more precise information on cargo arrival as the average EVPI is zero.
