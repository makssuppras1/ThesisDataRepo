Abstract
In this project the best way to construct a fully polarimetric radiometer with
variable frequency control is investigated. The best type of radiometer architecture
for this purpose is shown to be the digital correlation radiometer. This architecture
had simpler analog requirements and overall better stability and accuracy.
For
calibration it is shown that calibration loads can be easily implemented into the
radiometer such that it can calibrate itself. The best type of receiver conﬁguration
when it comes to frequency conversion is shown to be the single side-band receiver
using an image reject mixer.

Chapter 1. Introduction
Introduction
The purpose of this project is to investigate the possibilities in designing a new
analog front-end module for an already constructed L-band radiometer. This new
front-end module should operate in the P-band frequency range of 300 MHz to
900 MHz and have a variable local oscillator frequency.
The bandwidth of the
radiometer should ideally be located between 6 MHz to 33 MHz and lastly the
radiometer should be of the fully polarimetric type, having the ability of measuring
all four stokes parameters simultaneously.

Chapter 2. Radiometer Principles
Radiometer Principles
2.1
Black body radiation
All matter that has an temperature above absolute zero will emit energy. This type
of energy is called thermal radiation and the energy emitted will be in the form of
electromagnetic waves. A black body is then and idealized concept of a body, or
lump of mass, that will absorb all energy falling on it, from all directions and at
all frequencies. But if a black body absorbs all energy then it must also emit all
energy, making a black body object at perfect absorber and emitter. The amount
of thermal radiation emitted is then determined by the physical temperature of the
black body object, making hotter objects emit more energy compared to colder
objects. This type of concept is useful since a real life non black body object, a
grey body, will also emit energy thermal energy. The amount of thermal energy
emitted at some physical temperature will then be less than a black body at the
same physical temperature, since a real life grey body is not a perfect absorber or
emitter. This means that the concept of a black body can be used as a reference for
a real life grey body, and from this information of the object can be extracted.
To determine the amount of radiated energy for a black body object, Planck’s
radiation law can be used.
This radiation law describes the amount of thermal
radiation emitted versus physical temperature and frequency and it can be calculated
using equation (2.1) seen below, from [3].

2.2
Brightness Temperature
Since no ideal black body’s will exist in real life the term brightness temperature
needs to be introduced. The brightness temperature of a object will then describe
how much a real gray body will radiate, or how bright it appear to be. Therefore this
brightness temperature will not be the objects physical temperature, but instead be
a noise temperature with the unit of Kelvin. But a gray body can be compared
to a black body by introducing the term called emissivity which describes the gray
body’s surface eﬀectiveness in emitting energy as thermal radiation. This emissivity,
ϵgb, is then described as taking the ratio of the gray body radiation, Igb, to a black
body radiation, Ibb, with the same temperature as the gray body, as seen in equation

2.4
Polarimetry
The thermal radiation emitted by an object will be in the form of an electromagnetic
wave. If this object is not a black body, then the emitted electromagnetic wave can
have a certain polarization associated with it. This can lead to extra information
about the object, and measuring this polarization together with the total thermal
energy emitted is the whole purpose of polarimetric radiometers.

Radiometer Theory
With the basic principles of radiometers introduced in the previous chapter it
is now desired to investigate how radiometers function and are constructed. This
chapter will then discuss basic radiometer theory and then introduce the principle of
the Total Power Radiometer (TPR) followed by the Fully Polarimetric Radiometer
(FPR).
3.1
Ideal Radiometer
The purpose of a radiometer is to relate the antenna temperature to an output power
level such that information about the diﬀerent objects measured can be obtained. By
doing this, the power levels measured will correspond to diﬀerent antenna tempera-
tures which will then provide information about the measured object. A radiometer
can therefore be viewed as a box containing all the electronics necessary to achieve
this and will ﬁrst be considered as the ideal radiometer. An example of this ideal
radiometer can be seen in ﬁgure 3.1 below.
Figure 3.1: Ideal radiometer, drawn in Scheme-It [1]

output will be some power level, Pout,ideal. Because of the very small noise signal
applied to the input, the radiometer must have high enough gain, GR, such that
output power can be properly detected. Furthermore the radiometer will have a
certain noise bandwidth, BR, in order to measure within a certain frequency span
only. This noise bandwidth is primarily aﬀected by ﬁltering inside the radiometer
but active devices such as ampliﬁers will also have some bandwidth associated with
them.
Proper ﬁltering inside the radiometer will ensure that signal interference

3.2
Real Radiometer
Unfortunately no such thing as an ideal radiometer exists in reality. This is be-
cause the radiometer itself will generate noise, where the primary contributor to
this noise will come from lossy components, lossy signal lines and active devices
such as ampliﬁers, diodes and mixers.
Since the whole purpose of a radiometer is to estimate the antenna temperature
at the input of the radiometer it is desired to refer the noise generated by the
radiometer itself to this antenna temperature. This can be done by considering the
radiometer noiseless by placing a noise source at the input of the radiometer that
will result in the same amount of noise generated at the output. The noise source
at the input is called the radiometer noise temperature, TR, and by including this
the system is called the real radiometer, seen in ﬁgure (3.2) below.
Figure 3.2: Real radiometer, drawn in Scheme-It [1]
This real radiometer has both the antenna temperature and the radiometer tem-
perature located at the input. Since both of them are noise signals that are in-
dependent of each other means that they will add together. This also means that

temperature. Because of this the noise temperature of the radiometer needs to be
as small as possible in order to reduce this error. But in reality the noise tempera-
ture of the radiometer will be quite large and sometimes greater than the antenna
temperature which leads to large erroneous measurements.
One way to counter this is to estimate the noise temperature of the radiometer.
If this radiometer noise temperature is known, then the power at the output of the
radiometer created from this noise temperature can be estimated. Which means
that the the power at the output estimated from the radiometer noise temperature
can simply be subtracted from the real measured power level. By doing this the
resulting output power should ideally only come from the antenna temperature re-
sulting in a correct measurement. This means that as long as the noise temperature
of the radiometer is known, the noise generated from the radiometer itself will cause
no erroneous measurements. The method of estimating the radiometer noise tem-
perature and the gain of the radiometer is the whole purpose of calibration and will
be discussed in a later section.

The accuracy of a radiometer describes how accurate the measurement of the an-
tenna noise temperature is. As was shown earlier the output of a TPR is the system
gain multiplied by the system noise, equation (??). It is therefore desired to now
the precise value of these factors in order to achieve an accurate measurement of

the unwanted frequencies and not completely remove them.

3.3.2
Stability
The stability of the radiometer describes how stable the measurement is to factors
such as physical temperature and power supply voltage. The measured antenna
temperature depends on the system gain and system noise temperature as described
earlier, which is primarily determined by active devices inside the radiometer such
as ampliﬁers, mixers and diodes.
This means that characteristics such as, gain,
matching, noise ﬁgure, loss etc, of such active devices will change if the physical
temperature of the components would change, or if the power supply voltage where
to change. Because of this the measured antenna temperature could be inaccurate
even if the radiometer is calibrated. This is because when the radiometer is cali-
brated, all these characteristics of the active devices are found at the current state
of the calibration, meaning the calibration is only valid at these parameters.
To overcome the problem with changing temperature, some temperature regu-
lation to keep the radiometer at a constant temperature is desired. This is usually
done by including a heating element inside the radiometer electronics box. This
heating element can then be controlled by a temperature sensor to keep the temper-
ature at a constant level. Furthermore a fan can be included to ensure proper airﬂow
inside the radiometer to obtain better temperature regulated electronics, which can
Page 22 of 71
==PAGE:39==
Chapter 3. Radiometer Theory
be seen in ﬁgure 3.8 below. To ensure even better temperature control, the Printed
Circuit Board (PCB) can be attached to a aluminum plate to better stabilize the
thermal ﬂow inside the radiometer.
Figure 3.8: Temperature controlled TPR, drawn in Scheme-It [1]
The temperature is usually determined to be higher than the ambient tempera-
ture, since it is easier to heat something up compared to cooling something down,
but one bad side aﬀect of this is increased radiometer noise temperature. This is
because the hotter the electronics is, the more noise they will produce. This larger
radiometer noise temperature can of course be calibrated away but it will degrade
the sensitivity of the radiometer, which will be discussed later. But this is a must
trade oﬀsince a non stable measurement cannot be used in practice. Because of
the increasing component noise with increasing physical temperature, the radiome-
ter should not be regulated to a very hot physical temperature, but a reasonable
radiometer temperature could be chosen as 40° C.
When it comes to the power supply voltage, it is desired to keep this as stable as
possible. The temperature regulated box will of course help in achieving this, but all
power supplies will have some voltage ripple. This voltage ripple can alter the bias
operating points of for an example the ampliﬁers. Since the total ampliﬁcation in a
radiometer can easily be around 80 dB this power supply voltage ripple can easily
impact the stability performance, and therefore measures must be taken to reduce
this instability. This is primarily done by implementing power supply noise rejection
ﬁlters at each active component, in the form of capacitors, or by including voltage
regulators. By implementing this the voltage ripple at each active component will
be reduced which will lead to a more stable radiometer measurement.

describes the uncertainty of the measured antenna noise temperature. Since the
integration time of the radiometer cannot be inﬁnite, no information would ever
be gathered about the object. Therefore is must be ﬁnite which mean that the
measured antenna noise temperature will be an estimate of the true value. With
this estimated value some statistical uncertainty will be introduced, and for an ideal
radiometer the radiometric resolution, ∆Tideal, of the TPR can be found using the
equation (3.14) seen below, from [3].
∆Tideal = TSY S

[ K ]

This ideal radiometric resolution is the standard deviation of the measured an-
tenna noise temperature, and it can be seen that it is aﬀected by the system noise
temperature, the bandwidth and the integration time of the radiometer. This ra-
diometric resolution will for an example mean that if a radiometer measures some
object to be 300 K, and the radiometer has a radiometric resolution of 1 K, the
actual temperature of the object will be in the range of 299-301 K, due to the statis-
tical uncertainty. Because of this it is therefore desired to improve the radiometric
resolution of the radiometer as much as possible, to obtain a better measurement
resolution.
The bandwidth of a radiometer is primarily determined by the frequency spec-

3.3.4
Calibration of Total Power Radiometer
As was quickly discussed in the previous sections, the calibration of the TPR has
the purpose of accurately obtaining the correct values for the system gain and the
system noise temperature. To determine these factors two types of calibration can
be done on a radiometer, a full system calibration and a radiometer calibration.
The full system calibration af a radiometer is where the antenna is included in the

radiometer itself. Both of them have their own advantages and disadvantages and
will be discussed in the following sections. But the main diﬀerence between them is
to what point in the system the calibration is achieved.

3.4
Fully Polarimetric Radiometers
The Fully Polarimetric Radiometer (FPR) has the ability to measure all four stokes
parameters simultaneously and can either be realized using a Polarization Combining
Radiometer (PCR) or a Correlation Radiometer (CR). These two radiometer types
will be brieﬂy introduced and after this a digital version of the correlation radiometer
will be discussed.
3.4.1
Polarization Combining Radiometer
As seen equation (2.11), for a PCR the individual stokes parameters can be found
by the addition or subtraction of diﬀerent measured noise temperatures. If all four
stokes parameters are desired then there must be measured six individual noise
temperatures, one for each type of polarization and could be done by having six
diﬀerent antennas connected to six total power radiometers.
Another method for measuring the stokes parameters can be achieved by having
only a vertical and a horizontal antenna system, and then create the remaining
four polarization’s by implementing, rat-race coupler’s or magic tee’s, as well as
a phase shifter. For an example the noise temperatures, T+45◦and T−45◦, can be
taken from the sum and the diﬀerence ports of a rat race coupler with the vertical
and horizontal noise temperatures, TV and TH, as inputs. The two remaining noise
Page 32 of 71
==PAGE:49==
Chapter 3. Radiometer Theory
temperatures, Tr or Tl, can also be taking from the sum and the diﬀerence port of
a rat race coupler, if the vertical noise temperature is phase shifted by 90◦, and the
horizontal noise temperature. This reduces the number of antenna’s needed to only
two, but complex microwave hardware will be needed in the front of the radiometer,
and in the end the system will still require six diﬀerent total power radiometers,
which all must be calibrated individually. The added rat-race couplers and phase
shifter in front of the radiometer will increase the system noise temperature, which
will lead to a degradation of the radiometric resolution and because of this the
Polarization Combining Radiometer (PCR) will not be discussed further, but if
interested information can be found in [2].
3.4.2
Correlation Radiometer
The Correlation Radiometer (CR) will measure stokes parameters using equation
(2.12). But instead of measuring electrical ﬁelds, it will measure noise voltages from
a horizontal polarized antenna and a vertical polarized antenna. This means that
the system can be seen as two total power radiometers, where the complex cross
correlation is implemented before the detector section. An example of this CR can
be seen in ﬁgure 3.15 below.
Figure 3.15: Correlation radiometer, drawn in Scheme-It [1]
Here each channel is splitted using a power divider before the detector section,

where the ﬁrst output of the power divider will be connected to a detector followed
by an integrator and therefore forming a total power radiometer, while the other
output will be feed to a complex cross correlator. The real output of the complex
correlator will then be the vertical channel cross correlated by the horizontal channel,
and if this is then feed to an integrator the resulting output will be the third stokes
parameter.
The imaginary output of the complex cross correlator will then be
the vertical channel cross correlated by the horizontal channel, where either the
horizontal or the vertical channel is phase shifted 90◦with respect to the other.
This imaginary output is then feed to an integrator and the resulting output will be
the fourth stokes parameter.
To implement the phase shift a 90◦hybrid can be inserted between the power
divider and the complex correlator. This will have the functionality of creating an in
phase signal, 0◦, and a quadrature phase signal, 90◦, for each of the two horizontal
and vertical channels, which can then be feed to the complex cross correlator.
By implementing the system as described the ﬁrst two stokes parameters are
found by substation and addition of the vertical and horizontal outputs, TH and TV ,
while the third and forth stokes parameters are found directly from the outputs, U
and V . This means that the CR only needs two polarized antennas, two total power
radiometerss, two 90◦hybrids and a complex correlator followed by two integrator’s.
Compared to the PCR this will result in less and easier hardware to implement and
is therefore superior.

easier to fulﬁll at lower frequencies.

3.5.1
Homodyne Principle
The ﬁrst type of mixer implementation is the homodyne principle, sometimes also
referred to as a direct conversion mixer. This type of receiver will have the LO equal
the RF signal, and because of this the diﬀerence frequency will be at zero IF, or
Direct Current (DC). An example of the homodyne principle can be seen in ﬁgure
3.21 below.
Figure 3.21: Homodyne principle, created with Matlab script D.7
Here the voltage amplitudes of the RF and LO signals is chosen diﬀerently so
it is easy to diﬀerentiate between them. This ﬁgure then shows where the diﬀerent
mixing products will be placed in the spectrum, if some arbitrary bandwidth around
the RF signal is considered, shown as the diﬀerent areas in the ﬁgure.
From the ﬁgure it is seen that all frequencies above and below the LO will be
down converted. This is because all RF signals above and below the LO will be
mixed with the LO, and because of this, the frequency band below the LO can be
deﬁned as the Lower Side-Band (LSB), while the frequency band above the LO can
be deﬁned as the Upper Side-Band (USB). Lastly the diﬀerence frequencies of the
Page 42 of 71
==PAGE:59==
Chapter 3. Radiometer Theory
lower side-band will all be negative, which will result in real positive frequencies
mirrored around the y-axis, or DC. The advantage of this homodyne principle is
that the IF signals is very low frequency, which greatly simpliﬁes the requirements
to any hardware connected after the mixer. But as this sounds really practical,
several disadvantages exists.
One disadvantage is that if some of the LO power leaks into the RF port of the
mixer, this LO leak will then be mixed with the LO itself and will create a DC
oﬀset at the output of the mixer. This DC oﬀset can be large compared to the small
noise signals and cannot be ﬁltered out since the homodyne principle will require
the output of the mixer to be DC coupled, which leads no room in the spectrum to
implement a ﬁlter to reject this DC oﬀset. Furthermore the power of the LO may not
be constant but have some gain uncertainty associated with it. This gain uncertainty
will typically be very fast and therefore it cannot be calibrated away, which will have
the eﬀect of reducing the radiometric resolution if this type of homodyne principle
is used in a radiometer.
Another problem the DC oﬀset causes is that it will limit the input voltage range
of an ADC if a digital radiometer is implemented. This might not be much but it
is still not desired.
One last disadvantage is that all the active and passive analog components will
create more noise very close to DC compared to higher frequencies. This additional
noise is usually referred to as ﬂicker noise or 1/f noise.
The 1/f naming comes
from the fact that the spectrum of this type of noise is not ﬂat, but instead it
will decrease in power by 1/f with increasing frequency. Compared to the small
noise temperatures to be measured this ﬂicker noise could overwhelm the system at
extremely low frequencies.
Lastly the sum frequencies can be rejected by implementing a LPF after the
mixer. All in all the disadvantages of this homodyne principle is to severe for the
only one advantage, and it will therefore not be discussed further.
3.5.2
Heterodyne Principle
The more popular type of frequency conversion is the heterodyne principle. This
type of mixing will produce output IF signals that is non DC, which can be done
by choosing a LO frequency that is diﬀerent than the RF. The frequency spacing
between the LO and the RF signal will then equal the IF signal and an example of
this can be seen in ﬁgure 3.22 below.

Here it is seen that two diﬀerent LO frequencies can be chosen to down convert a
RF signal to the same IF frequency, the LO can either be placed above or below the
RF signal. This poses one big problem though, which is called the image frequency.
The problem here is that the LSB is the image to the USB and vice versa, and an
example of this can be seen in ﬁgure 3.23 below.
Figure 3.23: Image frequencies, created with Matlab script D.7
This shows that if an unwanted signal is present at the image frequency of the RF
signal, the diﬀerence frequency from the wanted signal and the diﬀerence frequency

==PAGE:61==
Chapter 3. Radiometer Theory
from the image signal, will both be down converted to the same IF. This is a big
problem since after mixing the wanted signal cannot be separated from the image
and vice versa.
The way to solve this problem is to ﬁlter out the image before
the mixer by implementing a Band Pass Filter (BPF). This will have the eﬀect of
attenuating the image greatly before it enters the mixer, which results in very low
image power at the IF frequency, compared to the desired signal. If such an image
ﬁlter is implemented before the mixer it is said to be a Single Side-Band (SSB)
receiver and it can either be implemented to attenuate the USB or the LSB.
The challenge though is that if a very small IF frequency is desired, the transition
band of the BPF must be very steep, which can severely increase the diﬃculty in
designing the ﬁlter. But the biggest challenge is if a variable LO is desired. This
would mean that in order to reject the image the BPF must also be variable, and
controlled simultaneously with the LO. This greatly increases the receiver complexity
as variable ﬁlters are very hard to design and implement properly over a large
frequency span, especially for low ﬁlter bandwidths.
Nevertheless the sum frequencies and the DC oﬀset due to LO leakage can be
rejected by implementing a BPF instead of a LPF after the mixer. Lastly since the
IF in far away from DC the ﬂicker noise from the components is of little concern.
3.5.3
Double Side-band Receiver
The Double Side-Band (DSB) receiver will utilize both the LSB and the USB, mean-
ing no image ﬁlter is implemented before the mixer, and because of this both the
RF signal and its image will be present at the IF. This is not at problem for a TPR
since the purpose is simply to detect total power. Therefore the noise power present
at the IF would simply come from both the LSB and the USB, resulting in twice
the noise power compared to a SSB receiver. This is a positive aspect, but since
most radiometers will have a total gain in the range of 80-100 dB, then the extra 3
dB noise power gained will have minimal inﬂuence in receiver design though.
Since the actual RF bandwidth will be both side-bands, then it might be thought
that this will increase the radiometric resolution, since this will increase with in-
creasing bandwidth, seen in equation (3.14). This is not true though as the actual
radiometer detection bandwidth will be at the IF frequency.
If a analog or digital correlation radiometer is designed as a DSB receiver though,
then the forth stokes parameter would always equal zero. This is because the contri-
butions of the LSB and the USB cancel due to the phase relationship in the system
[2]. Therefore correlation radiometers cannot be designed as DSB receivers, leaving
only the option of a SSB receiver.

3.5.4
Single Side-band Receiver
The SSB receiver will have the image ﬁlter implemented before the mixer, to en-
sure that the image is attenuated before frequency conversion. But as mentioned
previously the requirements to such a BPF can be quite tough, making it hard to
realize in reality. Furthermore the image ﬁlter would need to be variable if variable
LO which is even harder to realize. Because of these aspects the conventional way
of designing SSB receivers using the image ﬁlter will not be discussed further. An
alternative solution will be presented instead by implementing an Image Reject (IR)
mixer instead of a conventional mixer.

Chapter 5. Implementation
Implementation
The radiometer will consist of two calibration PCB’s, two ACL PCB’s and two
radiometer PCB’s, one for each horizontal and vertical channel. Besides this another
two PCB’s will be needed, one for the noise injection circuit and another for the local
oscillator circuit. This section will then explain how these PCB’s are designed, and
they will be connected to each other via SubMiniature version A (SMA) connectors
and cables. The data-sheet for the SMA connector type chosen can be seen in [18],
and this type will be soldered on the surface of the PCB.
5.1
Calibration Printed Circuit Board
The calibration PCB will have the purpose of selecting which inputs should be pre-
sented to the radiometer, where the inputs are the antenna, the hot load and the
active cold load. Furthermore it will have the purpose of connecting the noise injec-
tion circuitry to the radiometer such that the third and the fourth stokes parameter
can be calibrated. In order to realize these requirements the calibration PCB will
primarily consist of a switch followed by a directional coupler.
To select which input the radiometer should see, a switch from analog devices
with the part number HMC245AQS16E [14] is chosen. This switch has tree inputs
and one output which is the required amount to connect the antenna, hot load and
active cold load. Furthermore the switch chosen is of the type called non-reﬂective
or absorptive, meaning that the non selected inputs will be internally terminated,
instead of being a RF open or short. This will mean that it has a high isolation
of approximately 50 dB between the non selected inputs and the output, which is
required since it is only desired to present one input at the time to the radiometer.
This was the only non-reﬂective switch with tree inputs that could be found for these
frequency requirements, while there where a lot of reﬂective switches available, but
these did not have the superior isolation. The last aspect for choosing this switch is
that it has a very low insertion loss of approximately 0.7 dB, which should result in
a lower system noise temperature compared to switches with higher insertion loss,
and ultimately result in better radiometric resolution.
To determine which input is selected the switch has two control voltage inputs
named A and B. These control inputs will have a logic low in the range of 0-0.8 V
and a logic high in the range of 2-5 V. The maximum current draw for the control

After the switch a directional coupler is implemented to inject the calibration

The coupling value is desired to be small since

This coupler will not be set up in the usual way where it couples part of the RF
signal out, but instead it will be used to inject the calibration noise signal. Therefore
the normally named coupled port will just be DC blocked and terminated in 50 Ω
since it is not needed. The port used to inject the calibration noise signal will be
the coupled reverse port, or the isolation port referenced to the input port.
The circuit schematic of the calibration PCB can be viewed in the appendix B.2.
Here the capacitors C1-C6 are used as DC blocking capacitors. Since these capacitors
are placed in series with the RF signal path, it it desired for their impedance to be
low. If these the capacitor impedances are not low, it would introduce in unnecessary
loss in the RF signal path. Because of this they are chosen as 10 nF, which should
in the worst case of 300 MHz give a series impedance of apporximately 0.0531 Ω.
The last two capacitors C7 and C8 are used to stabilize the positive voltage supply
needed for the switch. They will provide and RF short to ground for any unwanted
noise ripples on the power supply.

Chapter 6. Conclusion
Conclusion
The purpose of this project where to investigate the possibilities in designing a
new analog front-end module for an already constructed L-band radiometer. Fur-
thermore the radiometer should be of the fully polarimetric radiometer.
In order to achieve this the theory of two diﬀerent radiometers where investigated.
First the total power radiometer where investigated to get an understanding og the
basic radiometer aspects such as radiometric resolution, accuracy and stability. How
the total power radiometer can be calibrated where then introduced, and here it was
seen that the best way was to implement calibration components into the radiometer
itself.
After this the correlation radiometer in analog and digital form where investi-
gated to get an understanding of fully polarimetric radiometers. This was also done
because the already constructed radiometer where also of this type. Here how a self
calibration correlation radiometer could be constructed was also discussed.
Lastly the idea of implementing frequency conversion with a variable local oscil-
lator where investigated. Here it was shown that the only realistic implementation
where a single side-band receiver using in image reject mixer.
After this analog to digital converter saturation where discussed, and it was
shown how this type of saturation can be minimized. The estimated radiometer
noise temperature where not to high such that the radiometric resolution would not
suﬀer to much.
Lastly some of the circuit schematics for the proposed radiometer architecture
where presented together with their layouts.
