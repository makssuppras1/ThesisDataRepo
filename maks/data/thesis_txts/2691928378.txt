Abstract
Fermentation of sugars presented in organic wastes could be a further step in the transi-
tion from fossil-based succinic acid to a bio-based alternative. The current study focuses
on valorization of sugar-rich organic candy waste to produce at least 80% yield and 55
g/L of succinic acid concentration in the fermentation broth. The strain A. succinogenes
130Z EA, obtained by adaptive evolution, showed the greatest resistance improvement
under high sugar concentrations (80 g/L - 100 g/L). The highest succinic acid yield
obtained from the EA was 0.75 (±0.10) g SA/g sugars acquired by 26.35 (±3.29) g/L
sugar concentration in the waste. The highest productivity observed was 0.85 (±0.23)
g/L/h by using 44.25 (±3.81) g/L sugar concentration contained in the waste. Although
the utilization of 68.75 g/L sugar concentration to attain 55 g/L of succinic acid and
80% yield could not be achieved through this project, the results indicated that the
A. succinogenes 130Z EA mutant is a promising candidate for succinic acid production
under high sugar concentration with great prospects to achieve the aforementioned goal
in the future.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
During the last decades, the transition from a linear to a circular economy has been a
crucial challenge, leading to a call for more sustainable solutions. Production of fuels and
chemicals through alternative resources, such as biomass, constitutes an attractive idea.
The most widespread biomass feedstocks are plants, wood, and waste. Specifically, huge
amounts of waste generated in the industrial, agricultural, and the household sector,
that affect both human health and the environment, could be converted from a serious
global issue to a subject undergoing intense study regarding its valorization. The sugar-
rich organic waste is ideal for high value-added chemicals production, such as succinic
acid (SA) and lactic acid (LA) [1], [2].
This chapter focuses on answering questions such as: why SA production through
bio-based feedstocks is important, why to use microbial strains for its production, why
Actinobacillus succinogenes 130Z draws the attention of this process, and what method
could be used to optimize the whole procedure.
Succinic acid (SA, C4H6O4), a four-carbon dicarboxylic acid, is a building block
chemical that is used in the field of food, chemicals, and pharmaceuticals to produce a
wide variety of chemicals such as solvents, perfumes, lacquers, plasticizer, and coatings.
Its demand is expected to increase further to be utilized for biodegradable polymers syn-
theses such as polybutylene succinate (PBS) and polyamides. Succinic acid also finds a
usage as an antibiotic and curative agent, as well as a surfactant, ion chelator, and as
an additive in the industrial sector [1], [2], [3], [4], [5].
SA can be produced either by petroleum-based or by biomass-based feedstocks. Over
the last decade, a shift towards the latter has gain significant attention aiming to the min-
imization of environmental pollution through avoidance of fossil fuel emissions. Specif-
ically, if bio-based succinic acid replaces 20% of fossil-based succinic acid, the avoided
emissions are estimated to be 5tons CO2 per ton of product. Furthermore, the con-
sumption of CO2 during the fermentation-based succinic acid production and the use of
biomass as a carbon source constitutes an important benefit [1], [6]. Nevertheless, the
estimated price of bio-succinic acid (2940 $/ton) in the product market is not competi-
tive enough to the price of fossil-based succinic acid (2500 $/ton) [7]. Other challenges
that have to overcome are the valorization of cheap carbon sources, the low succinic acid
yield and productivity, the small amounts of sugar utilization and the high extraction
cost of the product [1].

Based on the above, more sustainable, and economically feasible procedures for suc-
cinic acid production are examined.
Microbial fermentation of carbohydrates could
consider a possible solution, where bacteria such as Actinobacillus succinogenes, Anaero-
biospirillum succiniciproducens, Mannheimia succiniciproducens, and Basfia succinicipro-
ducens convert sugars with simultaneous fixation of carbon dioxide (CO2) to produce
SA [8], [9].
Actinobacillus succinogenes 130Z is a facultative anaerobe, capnophilic, Gram- nega-
tive, non-motile, non-spore-forming, pleomorphic rod microbe and can grow at 37-39°C
(mesophilic). It can produce succinic acid in high quantities (highest titer, 105.8 g/L)
and, utilize a broad range of carbon sources (glucose, arabinose, xylose, fructose, man-
nose, galactose, sucrose, lactose, cellobiose, mannitol, maltose, and glycerol) (Figure 1.1)
and is tolerant to high glucose concentrations (up to 143 g/L). The absence of oxygen
is considered the optimized condition for it to grow. Due to the aforementioned reasons,
Actinobacillus succinogenes 130Z is considered a promising succinic acid producer. Al-
though succinic acid is the major fermentation product, by-products such as formic acid,
acetic acid, lactic acid, and ethanol can be produced. However, the main drawbacks
of A. succinogenes are considered the near-neutral optimum pH (6.75 – 7.00) that is
required for its growth and the limited genetic engineering tools for its genetic manipu-
lation [1],[2] [6], [8].
The wide variety of sugars that A. succinogenes are able to utilize, can be find
in waste streams from the food, biodiesel production, dairy, or candy factories, and
other. Food industry waste includes high amounts of fermentable sugars (35.5 – 69%
total sugars), proteins (3.9 – 21.9%), fats and lipids (mostly triglycerides), vitamins,
and minerals. Also, it has high moisture content (80 to 90%) and low calorific value
[10]. The organic fraction is mainly utilized through incineration, anaerobic digestion,
or composting. However, the valorization of these waste through biological processes
contributes to a circular economy maintenance and leads to a more eﬀicient resource
recovery. Furthermore, the production of fatty acids, biopolymers, lactic, and succinic
acid has further economic interest and environmental benefits [2].
To convert higher amounts of carbon sources to succinic acid and achieve higher
yields (theoretical yield of 1.12 g SA/ g glucose) [11], reduction of by-product forma-
tion is needed.
Genetic manipulation or evolutionary adaptation focusing on strain
improvement could lead to greater succinic acid production. Evolutionary adaptation
experiments require less cost and time compared to genetic modification [8].
Based on the above mentioned, issues such as pH control, high multiple sugar con-
centration tolerance, carbon dioxide source, and anaerobic conditions must be settled.
In the current project, succinic acid production from sugar-rich organic waste using
A. succinogenes 130Z and the improvement of the strain regarding its tolerance to high
sugar concentrations are examined.

CHAPTER 2
Objectives
The aim of this research is the production of succinic acid on a lab-scale level using
sugar-rich organic waste from a candy factory as substrate, focusing on A. succinogenes
130Z strain improvement to attain at least 80% yield and 55.00 g/L of succinic acid
concentration in the fermentation broth.
1. To achieve the aim, the characterization of the candy waste, in terms of sugar
concentrations, that is used for the experiments is necessary.
2. Furthermore, an evolutionary adaptation experiment of A. succinogenes 130Z takes
place to create a more robust strain, able to tolerate sugars and succinic acid
concentrations from 68.75 g/L and 55.00 g/L, respectively.
3. Finally, characterization in terms of sugar tolerance and succinate yield using the
adapted and the wild type A. succinogenes 130Z strain is performed in batch
bottles.

CHAPTER 3
Background
Actinobacillus succinogenes, Basfia succiniciproducens and Mannheimia succinicipro-
ducens are the most promising wild-type microbes since they are capable to consume a
wide range of carbon sources and could give high fermentation yields that are presenting
in Table 3.1.
The highest SA concentration (105.8 g/L) has been produced by A. succinogenes
FZ53 mutant using glucose with a yield and productivity of 0.80 g SA/g glucose and
1.36 g/L/h, respectively [12]. M. succiniciproducens LPK7 and B. succiniciproducens
JF4016 achieved 52.4 g/L and 20 g/L SA concentration, respectively [13], [14].
The metabolically engineered E. coli AFP111/pTrc99A–pyc and KJ060 strains pro-
duced 99.2 g/L and 86.6 (±0.05) g/L SA concentration. The former achieved the highest
reported yield of 1.1 g SA/g glucose and a productivity of 1.3 g/L/h [15], [16]. Finally,
the genetically Corynebacterium glutamicum _ΔldhA-pCRA717 achieved the highest
reported productivity of 3.17 g/L/h, SA concentration of 146 g/L, and a yield of 0.92
g SA/g glucose. Therefore, it is considered as a promising microbe for SA production
[17].

3 Background
7
With respect to A. succinogenes, different genetic modifications as well as evolu-
tionary adaptation (Table 3.2) have been implemented to improve the SA production
and/or to minimize the by-product formation. According to Guettler et. al.(1996) [12],
the mutant strains FZ6 and FZ21 were the ones with the best performance after the
adaptation experiment in sodium monofluoroacetate compared to the parental strain A.
succinogenes 130Z.
Ye et. al examined the adaptation of A. succinogenes NJ113 in high concentration of
ammonium ions resulting in various adapted strains [18]. The mutant YZ0819 was able
to produce 40.21 g/L SA concentration at ammonium ion concentration of 354 mmol/L,
while the parental strain could not survive. The continuous-culture technique was used,
in which the strains were continuous cultivated at the maximum specific growth rate.
The mutants were isolated on agar plates containing an acid-base indicator bromothy-
mol blue and high level of ammonium ion (354 mmol/L).
In addition, Fang et. al investigated the adaptation of the A. succinogenes mutant
YZ0819 in 0.7M NaCl [19]. The new mutant A. succinogenes CH050 strain was able to
produce 66 g/L of SA concentration, while the parental strain YZ0819 could produce 48
g/L of SA concentration, both of them from 95 g/L of glucose concentration.
Furthermore, Zheng et al. tried genome shuffling by protoplast fusion on mutants
derived from nitrosoguanidine and UV exposure. A. succinogenes CGMCC1593 was
the parental strain and the optimum SA producing mutant was F3-II-3-F (95.6 g/L SA
concentration) that obtained after three rounds of genome shuffling [20].
All previous studies have focused on SA production using glucose as substrate. How-
ever, this project aims on the acquisition of a bacterial strain that will be able to metab-
olize different types of sugars contained in organic wastes to produce second-generation
succinic acid (SA).
The current project is the first to apply an evolutionary adaptation of A. succinogenes
130Z to obtain an improved strain, that will be exposed to different sugar concentrations
using sugar-rich organic waste as substrate, and is a preliminary part of NEOSUCCESS
project [21], which targets to build the first plug-and-play industrial solution integrating
complementarily two complex processes, which are biogas upgrading into biomethane
and fermentation-based biosuccinic acid production.

The evolutionary adaptation experiment was implemented in order to acquire a strain

4.4.1
Preliminary 96-well plate
Before the evolutionary adaptation experiment, the creation of a preliminary 96 well-
plate took place in order to examine:
(a) The size of inoculum (2, 5 or 10% of the final fermentation volume) that will be
used in the evolutionary adaptation experiment of A. succinogenes 130Z.
(b) The growth rate of the strain with 10 g/L of glucose, fructose, or mix of sugars
contained in the waste, as carbon sources.
The procedure followed was:
(a) Preparation of the inoculum as described in the Microbial culture. The seed
culture was placed in an incubator at 37°C for 16-18h before inoculating the plate.
(b) Preparation of the fermentation media.
The final concentration in the plate
should have had 10 g/L of glucose, fructose, or mix of sugars contained in the waste. The
desired nutrient concentration (per liter) was 5g yeast extract, 20.3g K2HPO4.3H2O,
9.6g NaH2PO4.2H2O, 1g NaCl, 0.2g CaCl2, and 0.2g MgCl2, while the inorganic car-
bon source was 10 g/L of NaHCO3. pH was adjusted to 6.75 – 7.00 with 50% v/v
phosphoric acid.
When glucose or fructose was used, sterilization of nutrients took place separately,
while the substrate is added to the serum bottle by means of sterile filter of 0.2 µm. In
the case of waste as substrate, the nutrients were autoclaved along with it. Anaerobic
conditions were ensured by flushing with nitrogen (N2) for 15 minutes.
Figure 4.5 represents the 96 well-plate that was prepared. There was always a neg-
ative control for each case, to ensure that there was no contamination. Although no
inoculation took place in the negative control, the concentration of both nutrients and
substrates had to be the same with the corresponding positive control. So instead of
inoculum, the dilution was succeeded by adding water.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The main goal of this project is to obtain an improved A. succinogenes 130Z strain,
able to attain at least 80% yield and 55.00 g/L of succinic acid concentration in the
fermentation broth by utilizing a sugar-rich organic waste from a candy factory.
For this purpose, preliminary fermentations in serum bottles using a green candy
waste (total sugars in raw waste: 19.94 g/L) in a concentration of 10.58 (±2.00) g/L
and 17.38 (±0.70) g/L took place. The results have shown that the A. succinogenes
130Z strain was able to grow under these concentrations producing SA yields of 0.85
(±0.05) g SA/g sugar and 0.85 (±0.08) g SA/g sugar for 1st (Bottles 1,2,3) and 2nd
(Bottles 4,5,6) setup, respectively. There is statistically significant difference between
the two setups in case of SA titers and productivity (p < 0.05), where SA (g/L) of the
2nd setup is 1.65 times higher than the 1st one.
The evolutionary adaptation method using 96 well-plates was selected for the im-
provement of the A. succinogenes 130Z wild type strain, as requires less cost and time
than implementing genetic engineering. The preparation of a preliminary 96 well-plate
was necessary to determine the size of inoculum (2, 5 or 10% of the final volume) that
should be used in the evolutionary adaptation experiment, as well as the growth rate
of the strain using 10 g/L of a sugar-rich organic yellow candy waste (total sugars in
raw waste: 426.29(±5.83 g/L). Based on the outcome, when 10% inoculum is used, the
bacteria can grow faster reaching an OD630nm of 0.67.
In the evolutionary adaptation experiment, the A. succinogenes 130Z strain was ex-
posed to sugar concentrations of 20 g/L, 40 g/L, 80 g/L, 120 g/L up to 250g/L using a
sugar-rich organic waste as substrate. The initial set-up was planned to start from 10
g/L sugar concentration (based on the preliminary 96 well-plate), and increase to 20 g/L,
40 g/L, 60 g/L, 80 g/L up to 125 g/L. However, due to lack of access in the ICS machine,
the total sugar concentration that have been acquired based on an HPLC analysis was
208.38 (±4.77) g/L since the machine can detect only glucose, xylose, and arabinose but
not maltose or sucrose. This value is almost half of the real (426.29±5.83 g/L) and, as a
consequence, all the sugar concentrations in the 96-well plates of the evolutionary adap-
tation experiment proved to be double. The evolutionary adaptation experiment lasted
12 days reaching a concentration of 80 g/L for all lineages. With the double amount, the
gap between the concentrations is huge leading to a serious limitation of the experiment.
If there was a sugar concentration of 100 g/L between the 80 g/L and the 120 g/L, it
would be possible for the whole population to have reached this level and improve further.

To prove that the evolved bacteria of the day 12 (evolutionary adaptation) are re-
sistant to higher sugar concentrations than the WT strain, the resistance experiment
followed. The results have shown that 50% of the WT isolates can tolerate sugar concen-
trations up to 104.13 (±11.59) g/L, while 50% of the best-improved strain (EA) 96.42
(±6.06) g/L of sugars contained in the waste, Therefore, the question arise is why the
WT strain did not grow in 100 g/L on the 1st day of the evolutionary adaptation.
An explanation for the difference between the results could be the final volume from
which the culture was taken to inoculate the 96 well-plates. To inoculate the 1st 96
well-plate of the evolutionary adaptation experiment, 5mL out of a 50mL culture (in
serum bottle) was taken aseptically and was placed in a sterile centrifuge tube before
the inoculation of the plate in the anaerobic chamber. On the other hand, for the resis-
tance experiment, 4 isolates of the WT strain were cultured in 5mL media using a 24
deep well-plate. Stronger cells may have been obtained from the 5mL cultures leading
to higher sugar tolerance of the WT strain in the resistance experiment.
In addition, as there was no shaking of the 96 well-plates inside the incubator during
the resistance experiment and the results were obtained by one OD630nm measurement
after 12h of bacterial growth, an amount of nutrients or substrate could have stayed on
the bottom of the plate leading to an elevated OD value.
Finally, it is possible that using 96 well-plates is not the ideal method for an evolu-
tionary adaptation experiment and not representative results may have been acquired.
The volume is quite small (200µL) and may inhibit to some extent the growth of mi-
crobes.
However, it is important to examine the SA yield, the productivity and the by-
products formation of the WT strain and the best-evolved strain (EA). Results from
serum bottles with 25 and 45 g/L sugar concentration contained in the waste, as well
as with 20 g/L glucose have been acquired. Serum bottles with 60 and 80 g/L were also
prepared in 100mL final volume, but no growth was obtained within 48h due to long
lag phase. The results have shown that the evolved strain (EA) produced 1.34 times
higher SA titer for 20 g/L glucose than the WT strain (p < 0.05), giving a SA yield of
0.69 (±0.10) g SA/g glucose and a productivity of 0.62 (±0.04) g/L/h. In case of 45
g/L sugar concentration in the waste, the SA yield is 0.50 (±0.08) and 0.44 (±0.03) g
SA/g sugars, and the productivity is 0.85 (±0.23) and 0.77 (±0.02) g/L/h, for EA and
WT, respectively. For 25 g/L sugar concentration contained in the waste, the SA yield
is almost the same for both strains (0.77±0.02 g SA/g sugar).
It is also worth mentioning the heterogeneity of the yellow candy waste, leading
to diﬀiculty in obtaining similar sugar concentrations. While the waste was mixing in
the stirrer, samples from different parts were taken to quantify the sugar concentration.
There was a great variance in the results giving 262.67 (±92.47) or even 2081.69 (±50.05)

in these parts the sugar concentration was getting higher. The waste also had to be

Comparing the results of the present study with previous studies, Li et al. (2010d)
[23] tried batch fermentations with different kinds of carbohydrates such as glucose,
fructose, and maltose to compare the fermentability of single sugars. The initial con-
centration of the sugars was 30 g/L, and the SA titer acquired for glucose, fructose and
maltose was 17.2 g/L, 14.2 g/L, and 16.9 g/L, respectively. However, in the current
study, 18.96 (±0.17) g/L of SA was obtained, by the EA strain, from 26.35 (±3.29)
g/L sugar concentration using waste as substrate.
The highest SA yield from Li et
al. (2010d) study obtained using glucose (0.70 ±0.02 g SA/g glucose), which is almost
equal to the yields that the improved EA strain (0.75 (±0.10) g SA/g sugar) and the
WT strain (0.78 (±0.08) g SA/g sugar) gave from 26 g/L sugar concentration in the
waste. The acetic acid produced in Li et al. (2010d) study was between 8.4 and 9.7 g/L,
which is higher than the one reported in this project (6.07 g/L for the EA and 6.25 g/L
for the WT).
Ferone et al. (2017) [24], presents a SA yield of 0.75 (±0.03) and 0.48 (±0.00) g SA/g
sugar using 42.7 g/L glucose and 44.3 g/L xylose as initial concentrations, respectively.
The SA yield occurring from 44.25 (±3.81) g/L sugar concentration using waste for the
EA is 0.50 (±0.08) g SA/g sugar, which is almost equal to the one occurring from only
using xylose in Ferone et al. study. The productivity of both EA (0.85 ± 0.23 g/L/h)
and WT strain (0.77 ± 0.02 g/L/h) presented in this study is significantly higher than
the productivity in case of glucose (0.36±0.01 g/L/h) and xylose (0.09±0.02 g/L/h) in
Ferone’s study.
In Pateraki et al. (2016a) study [25], fermentations were conducted using different
concentrations of mixed sugar solution with 72.6% of xylose, 12.2% of galactose, 10.9%
of glucose, 4.2% of mannose, and 0.1% of arabinose. The SA yield (0.75±0.10 g SA/
g sugar) performed by the EA strain in this study, using 26.35 (±3.29) g/L sugar con-
centration from the waste is almost 1.4 times higher than the SA yield obtained from
an initial concentration of 26.01 g/L total sugars (0.627 g SA/g sugars) in Pateraki et
al. study. However, when the initial concentration of sugars in Pateraki et.al (2016a) is
41.88 g/L, the SA yield is 0.626 g SA/g sugar, which is higher than the SA yield that
EA strain performed in the current study (0.50±0.08 g SA/g sugar).
Although Guettler et al. (1996) [12] achieved a SA yield of 0.8 g SA/g glucose using
132.8 g/L glucose concentration, there is no literature referring to this yield using a mix
of sugars in concentration of more than 68.75 g/L.

tolerate at least 68.75 g/L sugar concentrations and produce 55.00 g/L of succinic acid
(SA). However, the improved EA strain of A. succinogenes 130Z that was obtained from

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
Biological processes are a promising technology for the production of succinic acid using
bio-based feedstocks. In this study for the first time, sugar-rich organic candy waste
was valorized to produce succinic acid. This was accomplished by Actinobacillus suc-
cinogenes 130Z.
Evolutionary adaptation of Actinobacillus succinogenes 130Z has been implemented
in order to acquire a strain that can tolerate sugars and succinic acid concentrations
from 68.75 g/L and 55.00 g/L, respectively. Based on the results, the evolved strains
were able to utilize 80 g/L sugar concentration the last day of the evolutionary exper-
iment (day 12). However, the resistance improvement experiment showed that 50% of
the Actinobacillus succinogenes 130Z wild type (WT) can survive in higher sugar con-
centration than 50% of the evolved strains can.
Characterization of the best evolved strain (EA) and the wild type (WT) in terms of
yield, productivity, and by-product formation took place in order to compare their per-
formance. Specifically, using 20 g/L glucose, the succinic acid (SA) titer is 0.69 (±0.10)
g SA/g sugar and 0.52 (±0.00) g SA/g sugar, while the productivity is 0.62 (±0.04)
g/L/h and 0.46 (±0.01) g/L/h, for the EA and the WT, respectively (p < 0.05). Using
45 g/L waste, the succinic acid (SA) titer is 0.50 (±0.08) g SA/g sugarand 0.44 (±0.03)
g SA/g sugar, while the productivity is 0.85 (±0.23) g/L/h and 0.77 (±0.02) g/L/h, for
the EA and the WT, respectively. Using 25 g/L waste concentration, the performance
of the EA and the WT is almost the same, showing 0.75 (±0.10) g SA/g sugar and
0.78 (±0.08) g SA/g sugar for succinic acid (SA) titer, and 0.79 (±0.01) g/L/h and 0.82
(±0.12) g/L/h for productivity, respectively.
The objectives of the project to obtain an improved strain of Actinobacillus succino-
genes 130Z, able to attain at least 80% yield and 55 g/L succinic acid concentration in
the fermentation broth were not succeeded. Nevertheless, the evolved strain (EA) can
be considered as a promising one to achieve the goal.

CHAPTER 8
Recommendations
Based on the outcome occurred from the current project, a few recommendations for
better results but also for further steps to continue the research are proposed.
Initially, the evolutionary adaptation experiment as well as the resistance experiment
is suggested to take place in bigger volumes such as 5mL using 24 deep-well plates. The
bacteria will have bigger volume to surface area to grow, and the results will be more
representative.
Also, if possible, the avoidance of mineral oil for ensuring anaerobic
conditions. However, without mineral oil on the top, is not feasible to detect the growth
curves of the strains. For this reason, it is a good idea to use a plate-reader that is
placed inside an anaerobic chamber.
Up-scaling the process to bioreactors level and based on the Neosuccess project, an
innovative idea is to use biogas as carbon dioxide source in order to provide to the A.
succinogenes 130Z the CO2 that is needed for SA production leading to simultaneous
upgrading of biogas to pure methane (>95%).
