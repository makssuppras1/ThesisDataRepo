Abstract
The purpose of this thesis was to calculate with high accuracy the radiation pattern and the 
gain of a C-band standard gain horn antenna at the frequency of 5.355 GHz. More specifically, an 
uncertainty of less than 0.01 dB is required for the on-axis directivity and an uncertainty better than 
0.03 dB is required for the on-axis gain. The antenna was manufactured by Scientific Atlanta and 
was provided by the Technical University of Denmark. In addition, a comparison was conducted 
between the data obtained at the Spherical Near-Filed facility located at the Technical University of 
Denmark and the data produced by the Wipl-D electromagnetic solver program.
Overall, the measured results match the data obtained by simulations with high degree of 
precision. Therefore, the standard gain horn can be used to calculate the gain of other antennas 
accurately.

Chapter 1 - Introduction
1.1 Background
Modern antenna design involves the skillful combination of theory and simulation software 
for the production of an electromagnetic wave transducer (also known as “antenna”) that fulfills 
some specific standards. However, theory and simulations are usually based on idealized conditions
that are rarely (if ever) met in practice. In addition, sometimes theory itself is derived from 
simplifications that are not realistic, yet they provide a reduction to the mathematical complexities 
of the problem at hand. For that reason, accurate measurements is an important part of the antenna 
manufacturing process.
The topic of measuring the various characteristics of an antenna is found not only in modern
textbooks [1], but also in some of the first ones to appear in the literature [2]. Out of all the 
properties of an antenna, one that has particular importance is its gain, because it relates the power 
received from an antenna to the power transmitted by an other antenna through the Friis 
transmission equation [3]. Various methods can be used for measuring the gain of an antenna (see 
chapter 17 in [1] or chapter 15 in [2]), but all of them require previous knowledge of the gain of an 
another antenna, known as the “reference antenna”. In addition, the accuracy of the measurement 
depends on how precisely the gain of the reference antenna is known. As a result, efforts must be 
made towards finding the gain of the reference antenna with high accuracy and precision.
1.2 Justification
One common type of antenna, that is often used as a reference antenna, is the pyramidal 
horn. In most applications, the determination of its gain to within 0.1 dB of uncertainty is adequate. 
However, ever increasing specification demands, especially from the space industry where the 
communication systems operate on a strict link budget, require even higher gain accuracy, to a 0.03 
dB of uncertainty. To the author's best knowledge, this has not been attained before.
1.3 Purpose
It is the purpose of the present thesis to determine the radiation pattern and the gain of a 
specific standard gain horn antenna to a better than 0.03 dB of uncertainty for the gain and better 
than 0.01 dB of uncertainty for the directivity. This antenna was provided by the Electrical 
Engineering department of the Technical University of Denmark and measurements took place at 
the Spherical Near-field facility of the same institute. A high fidelity model of the antenna was 
created in the electromagnetic solver software Wipl-D and digital computations were performed in 
order to compare the measurement data to the simulation data. 
1.4 Range
Chapter 1 is a summary of the background, justification and purpose of this thesis. A short 
presentation is being given about what is to be achieved and why the pursuit of this goal has merit. 
Moreover, the reader becomes aware of what to expect from the rest of the text.
In Chapter 2 the theory used in the attainment of the objectives is being analyzed. The reader
is required to be in possession of some basic knowledge about electromagnetic theory and network 
analysis. Nevertheless, the entire topic is being presented from first principles so it can be followed

In Chapter 3 the simulation software setup and the measurement process are being 
presented. Furthermore, the principles of operation behind the software's engine are discussed and

Chapter 5 formally closes the thesis and shortly discusses future developments.

2.2 Wave s-parameters
Interaction between electromagnetic waves and matter is, in general, a boundary value 
problem that must be solved analytically. However, if the medium involved is ordinary (linear, 
isotropic and homogenous) it can be reduced to a simple set of linear equations, greatly simplifying 
the solution of the problem at hand.
Figure 2.2 - Simple two-port network [8]
2.2.1 – Circuit s-parameters
For circuits whose physical size is significantly smaller than the wavelengths of the signals 
that it must handle, the method of identifying each element or block of elements as two-port 
networks and analyzing them with respect to their z-parameters can be used. A network like that 
can be seen in Figure 2.2. The z-parameters are defined as
V 1=Z 11 I 1+Z 12 I 2
         (2.70)
V 2=Z 21 I 1+Z 22 I 2
         (2.71)
where I1 and I2 are the currents flowing in ports 1 and 2 respectively.
Despite their simplicity, the z-parameters (or their equivalent y- and h-parameters) have a 
few serious disadvantages which constitute them as unfit for use at higher frequencies. To name a 
couple of them, they are sensitive to the position where the measurement is taking place and they 
require perfect open (or short) circuits, which is not so easily obtained when the operating 
wavelengths are so small. That's a consequence of equations (2.70)-(2.71), which require a zero 
16
==PAGE:17==
current when a Z parameter is being measured. However, in practice an open circuit is always a 
capacitor and at these frequencies its impedance is not negligible enough to be considered an open 
circuit.
The s-parameters are the remedy to these issues. The voltages and currents in a transmission 
line satisfy the one-dimensional wave equation and it is a well-known fact that the general solution 
of this equation has the form
u(x ,t)=A[ f (x−vt)+ f (x+vt)]+G
         (2.72)
where v is a propagation constant, A is an arbitrary constant and G is a function of the initial 
conditions. As it can be seen, it represents two waves traveling in opposite directions. Therefore, the
waves reflected by the system can be related to the waves that impinge it, namely

into the second port. In addition Sij is the ratio of the wave going into the j-th port to the wave

achieved by terminating the system with a load of the same value as the impedance seen looking 
into the two-port, which is easier to implement in high frequencies than a short or open circuit. The 
measurement position problem is being solved by defining a reference plane with respect to which 
the magnitudes and phases of the waves are being measured.

2.4.2 – Measuring the directivity with multiple reflections correction
There are some cases where high measuring accuracy must be achieved and the signal 
distortion due to the multiple reflections between the antennas can no longer be ignored. In theory, 
equation (2.121) can be used to mitigate these effects but there are some major obstacles in practice.
The first one is measuring the reflection coefficient of the transmitter or the receiver. 
Because of the physical structure of the measuring facility this is rarely an option. The second one is
the determination of the scattering matrix of the antenna. There is no systematic way to calculate 
this, similar to the way the receiving or transmitting coefficients were calculated in the previous 
chapter. Every single mode must be radiated to the receiving antenna and the reflected waves must 
be measured. In addition, there should be a zero reflection coefficient otherwise there is no way to 
tell apart the reflected waves from the reradiated waves.
What is usually done in real measurements is adjusting the distance between the probe and 
the AUT. The idea behind this tactic is that at different distances the reflected waves add either 
constructively or destructively and to a different degree. Therefore, the assumption that neither of 
them happen more often than the other is being made and, as a consequence, the average of the 
measured signals is the best approximation of the actual signal, if reflections were not taking place. 
It's a rather crude approximation, but it is much easier to apply it in practice than using equation 
(2.121) and the results are usually satisfying.

3.2 The measurement facility and its setup alignment
The accuracy of the simulation results are limited by the fidelity by which the antenna has 
been represented in the program and the computational power of the machine on which the 
emulations were run. Almost every commercially available antenna has a complicated geometry 
that makes it impossible to model it exactly. In addition, the electromagnetic solvers are considered 
heavy duty software even for, relatively, powerful computers. These are the principal reasons that 
render the measurement process a necessity.
In general, there are two types of measurement facilities; indoors and outdoors. This chapter 
will focus on the indoors measurements facilities and, more specifically, on the Spherical Near-field
facility that is located at the Technical University of Denmark. Its advantages and disadvantages 
will be mentioned, as well as its setup and structure of operation.
3.2.1 – Indoors vs outdoors measurement facilities
The main difference between these two types of measurement sites is whether the 
measurements are taking place inside a building or not. Both of them have their pros and cons but, 
from an academic point of view, indoors facilities have received the most attention the past years. 
Therefore, the following list mentioned its advantages and disadvantages.
Advantages:
a) No interference from outside sources. Since the whole apparatus is inside a building, it 
can be made insensitive to noise originating outside from it. Naturally, that goes both ways. 
The measurement process does not obstruct the electromagnetic devices outside its near 
environment.
b) Elimination of reflections. Absorbing materials can be placed in the inside walls, thus       
preventing the reflected signals from reaching the receiving antenna. Consequently, there is 
no ground reflection, a common problem of outside measurements that requires expensive 
tall towers to counter it.
c) Compact apparatus machines. Every piece of equipment can be in one place and there is 
no need to move it to a distant location each time a measurement must take place.
33
==PAGE:34==
d) All-weather capability. Outdoors measurements are by default affected by the weather, or 
other distant events that can disturb the whole process. Obviously, this is not an issue inside 
a closed establishment.
e) Discreteness and security. It is not unusual for a client to wish the test of a product under 
secretive conditions, in order to protect himself from the competition. This can be 
accomplished easier in an indoors measurement facility.
Disadvantages:
a) Complicated procedure. Unless the antenna under test is small compared to the 
dimensions of the measurement facility, the procedure does not measure its far-field 
characteristics but its near-field. As a result, extra steps must be taken in order to extract the 
desired information from the sampled data.
b) Antenna reflections. In an outdoors facility the distance between the AUT and the probe 
are almost always large enough to ignore the multiple reflections between the two antennas. 
Unfortunately, that's not the case in indoors measurements and additional actions are 
required in order to cancel their effect.
3.2.2 – DTU's spherical near-field measurement facility
The measuring facility of DTU is of the spherical sampling type, namely the samples are 
taken on a sphere around the test antenna. Figure 3.2 can be used in order to describe its function on
a high level of abstraction with the difference that, unlike what this particular picture shows, in 
practice it is the antenna under test that moves while the probe is stationary.
As it can be seen in this figure, there is a source that creates a signal at the desired frequency
and it excites the AUT. At the same time, a portion of this signal is passed through the duplexer to 
the phase measurement circuit and acts as the reference signal (usually after a downconversion to 
45 MHz). The signal received from the probe is then compared in phase with the reference signal 
and the phase is determined. A similar system exists for amplitude calculation.
The tower and the surrounding area are covered by an absorbing material shaped like a 
pyramid, as it can be seen in Figure 3.3. In theory, it can provide as much as 40 dB of attenuation to
the reflected signals. Also in this figure, the two towers can be clearly seen. The one on the left is 
the antenna tower, where the AUT is mounted, and the one on the right is the probe tower, which is 
stationary throughout the whole process.
There is a lower limit of about 100 MHz to the frequency range that can be tested and that is
imposed by the distance between the towers, which is about 6 meters. Moreover, there is a weight 
limit on the test tower that cannot be exceeded. Besides, the antennas are mounted by hand through 
an elevator, which cannot be seen in the figure. In case the antenna is too heavy to be mounted, but 
not so heavy for the tower, there is the option of hanging it from the ceiling.
An other piece of equipment that is important but not included in Figure 3.3 is the digital 
control system. Throughout the measuring process, it is crucial to precisely rotate the antenna tower
around two perpendicular axes and that is handled by a dedicated computer located just outside the 
room where the antennas are located. Its level of accuracy is about 10-4 degrees and it can handle its 
tasks automatically, as long as the towers are aligned. Alignment is the topic of the next paragraph.

4.3.2 – Polarization reference angle and antenna orientation
Intuitively, one would support the plots created by the simulations, because a horn antenna 
radiates linearly, therefore the cross-polar component should be zero. However, we know that this is
not the case according to the measurements that took place. 
One easy explanation for this phenomenon would be to have an error in the angle of the 
electric field plane with the coordinate system. If this angle is not 90º, then a component of the co-
polar field will be projected on the cross-polar reference unit vector (equation (2.87)). 
There are various reasons why this unfortunate error was introduced in the measurement 
process. The most likely of them attributes the error to an inherent flaw in the standard gain horn's 
physical structure. This can lead, mainly, to two miscalculations. Firstly, the E-plane may not be 
perpendicular to the aperture of the antenna. Secondly, the antenna itself may twist a little as it goes 
down towards the wave guide. In other words, the aperture electric field may not be parallel to the 
electric field in the start of the waveguide.

situation regarding the cross-polar directivity. Despite this, we don't know the magnitude of this 
rotation or its direction. The only way to find it is by comparing the simulated and measurement 
data. After a few tries, we arrived in the following graphs that reach exact agreement between

4.4 Gain determination
4.4.1 – Computing the gain using the measurement data
Calculating the gain of a horn antenna is a trivial matter in Wipl-D, since all that it is 
required from the user is the surface impedance (see paragraph 2.5.1) of the antenna's walls. 
However, as it was mentioned before, the situation is quite different when the gain has to be 
calculated in practice.
A method of approximating the losses in a horn antenna was presented in paragraph 2.5.3 
and two programs were written to implement it. The first program is called “lossprog.f90” and it 
was written in Fortran 90, an obsolete programming language, by a former employee at the 
Technical University of Denmark. The second is called “horn_loss_calc.m” and it was written in 
MATLAB by the author. Both implement the same algorithm but the latter is much shorter and, 
consequently, easier to understand. It will be the one used later for the calculation of the losses. 
Their codes can be found in appendix B. 
Another advantage of the MATLAB code is that it gives the user the option of choosing the 
number of segments used in the calculation. Table 4.4 shows the relation between the number of 
segments and the attenuation factor in dB. This is the number that must be added to the directivity 
in order to find the gain. It is apparent that after 200 segmentations, convergence is achieved. 
Therefore, we come to the conclusion that the losses in the horn antenna due to its finite 
conductivity are -0.0044 dB.

Chapter 5 – Conclusions
5.1 – Culmination and future work
The objective of this thesis was to determine the radiation pattern and the gain of a standard 
gain horn antenna with high precision. In particular, the uncertainty for the on-axis directivity 
should be less than 0.01 dB and for the on-axis gain less than 0.03 dB. Clearly, these goals were not
only achieved but the results exceeded the expectations. Both the on-axis directivity and the on-axis
gain were found to be within 0.006 dB from each other when the results obtained through 
simulations were compared to the ones acquired through measurements. Therefore, this antenna can
now be used reliably to measure the radiation characteristics of other antennas with high accuracy 
and precision.
There are a few improvements that could take place in order to achieve better results. To 
name a few, a more detailed model of the antenna could be constructed if better measurements of its
physical dimensions are taken. However, from this point on, this cannot be accomplished with 
simple tools and special machinery is required. Another step forward could be the use of a different 
software solution. As it was shown, Wipl-D achieves convergence for some specific set of settings, 
but perhaps this is due to limitations of its computational engine. Maybe an other program that does
not use the method of moments could perform better.

!!$ CALCULATION OF ANTENNA LOSS IN HORN ANTENNAS

!!$ THE DIMENSIONS A ALWAYS REFER TO THOSE CORRESPONDING
!!$ THE LONGER SIDE OF THE WAVEGUIDE WHILE THE B ALWAYS

!!$
!!$*****************************************************

!!$ Comment by MiDi, 052096:
!!$ This program was found on the old HP1000 and is used to estimate
!!$ the loss of a standard gain horn. As should be obvious the code

!!$ but the method of calculation is straight forward: it is assumed
!!$ that the throat is constructed of a number of straight waveguide

!!$     B1:    smallest cross sectional dimension of waveguide in meters 
!!$     YL1:   length of waveguide section in meters

!!$     B2:    smallest cross sectional dimension of horn aperture in meters 
!!$     YL2:   length of horn section in meters

horn_loss_calc.m
function [ae] = horn_loss_calc(ww,wh,wl,fw,fh,fl,cond,foo,nos)
 
fac=te10_wac(cond,ww,wh,foo);
 
pateotw=exp(-2*fac*wl);
 
roo_psi_e=(fh/2-wh/2)/fl;
roo_psi_h=(fw/2-ww/2)/fl;
 
sl=fl/nos;
 
for i=1:1:nos
    nwh=wh+2*roo_psi_e*sl*i;
    nww=ww+2*roo_psi_h*sl*i;
    nac=te10_wac(cond,nww,nwh,foo);
    pateotw=pateotw*exp(-2*nac*sl);
end
 
ae=10*log10(pateotw);
 
end
te10_wac.m
function [ac]=te10_wac(cond,ww,wh,foo)
 
omg=2*pi*foo;
mu=4*pi*10^-7;
c=3*10^8;
 
rs=sqrt(omg*mu/(2*cond));
 
k=omg/c;
kc=pi/ww;
beta=sqrt(k*k-kc*kc);
 
ac=2*pi*pi*rs*(wh+ww/2+beta*beta*ww*ww*ww/(2*pi*pi))/(omg*mu*ww*ww*ww*wh*beta);
