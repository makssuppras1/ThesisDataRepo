Abstract
The transition piece for an oﬀshore wind turbine is the part of the support structure that
is connected to the wind turbine tower. It is a critical component to the substructure as
transfers the loads from the oﬀshore wind turbine to the support structure. Well-designed
and manufactured transition pieces with optimized ultimate capacities contribute to the
structural reliability of the structure. Structural optimization has been a tremendous
expand in the industry and is a lightweight tool that has been widely used in aerospace
and automotive industry, however its application to wind energy systems has been very
limited. In this thesis the author applies structural optimization to the conceptual de-
sign of a Jacket Transition Piece (JTP). The goal of structural optimization is often to
ﬁnd an optimized design of the structure which has a minimum weight while still being
able to carry the applied loads. During the design process of the transition piece three
steps are proposed. The ﬁrst step is the topology optimization of the plate and space-
frame structures consisting of tubular members. For the space-frame structures minimum
weight topology optimization problems are solved using stress constraints with and with-
out relaxation using Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP) method. For the
plate classical minimum compliance topology optimization is implemented using again
the SIMP method. The second step deals with the sizing optimization of the topology
optimized designs under strength and buckling constraints. The third and ﬁnal part of
the conceptual design process concerns the interpretation from the topology optimiza-
tion into the CAD models and the validation of the resulted optimal geometries. Results
after the topology optimization process show that using relaxation in stress constrained
topology optimization problems leads to a decrease in the number of iterations and to
improved designs. Furthermore, the validation of the ﬁnal designs shows the signiﬁcance
of the contribution of the plate to the stiﬀness of the transition piece. Another important
observation is that topology optimized structures are initially infeasible with respect to
stress and buckling constraints due to the inaccuracy of beam model. Performing sizing
optimization of these structures with a higher stress safety factor extracted from the de-
tailed interpreted model with shell elements, results in feasible optimized designs which
carry the loads without exceeding the yield strength. Finally, two transition pieces for a
three and a four legged jacket are obtained, which satisfy the design criteria on stress and
buckling. The ﬁnal designs which are derived following the three step approach shows
that topology optimization can be successfully integrated in the conceptual design pro-
cess. Furthermore, in order to increase the reliability of the ﬁnal product, future work
should focus on including fatigue, dynamic loading condition and stress concentration
factors in the structural optimization process.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Need for oﬀshore wind energy
The Paris Agreement COP21, as well as the climate change, was an essential start for
a transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources. More speciﬁcally, is a turning
point that will impact EU climate and energy policies and oﬀers new challenges for the
wind energy industry both inside and outside Europe. The aforementioned can be justiﬁed
by the fact that Europe has a total net installed capacity of 168.7 GW from wind turbines
and remain the second most signiﬁcant form of power generation in Europe after gas
installations [1].
The most fundamental question is which technology is more proﬁtable and feasible, on-
shore or oﬀshore wind turbines. Onshore wind technology was implemented on 1890 s, a
century later oﬀshore technology starting surﬁng as a viable alternative near Denmark.
There are many arguments between these two technologies. In oﬀshore wind farms, wind
speed and direction are more consistent. Consequently, oﬀshore wind turbines are consid-
ered as more reliable and eﬃcient technology for electricity production. Furthermore, the
major problem of onshore wind farms, which is the waste of agricultural land, is resolved.
Oﬀshore wind could beneﬁt a marine ecosystem in which it is constructed [12]. The most
prominent disadvantage that disables oﬀshore technology to be justiﬁed over onshore is
the high cost and the need for extremely high quantities of material, especially for the
foundations.

2.1
Structural Optimization
Structural optimization as deﬁned by Klarbring A. and Christensen P. W. [15] is the sub-
ject of making an assemblage of materials sustain loads in the best way. There are diﬀerent
ways to optimize the structure, for instance, the minimization of weight or maximizing
the stiﬀness of the structure. Structural optimization problems generally also include a
number of design limitations, which are known as constraints in the optimization prob-
lem. With the absence of constraints, the derived structures would not be well deﬁned.
The structural performance is characterized by weight, stiﬀness, geometry, displacement,
stress and critical loads. Consequently, one of the previous characteristics can be selected
as the objective of the problem and should be maximized or minimized depending on the
formulation of the problem.
For the deﬁnition of the structural optimization problem, some steps should be followed.
Initially, according to the problem the objective function (f) should be deﬁned. Moreover,
the design variable should be decided (χ) which is the function that describes the design
and can be changed during the optimization problem such as, thickness and diameter of
a bar. Additionally, the state variable (y) of the structure represents the response of the
structure (stress, strain). A general formulation of the structural optimization problem is
described below [15]

2.2
Topology Optimization for frame structures
Topology Optimization is the most general and rewarding way of structural optimization.
In order to formulate topology optimization some crucial parameters should be decided.
The objective function, the design variables and the constraints which are depending on
the problem that is analyzed. As mentioned before the design (χ) and the state variable
(y) may be ﬁnite dimensional, or they may be functions (ﬁelds) which have an inﬁnite
number degrees of freedom. In the ﬁrst case are considered as discrete parameter problems,
whereas in the second case, as distributed parameter problems.
Topology optimization is applied to the monolithic and frame like structures. Monolithic
are the structures which are deﬁned as one piece. In this case continuum topology op-
timization is used since monolithic structure is typically modeled using continuum ﬁnite
elements. For monolithic structures continuum topology optimization can be used such as
level-set methods [16] and density based topology [17]. On the other hand for truss frame
structures that are modelled by beams or truss ﬁnite elements topology optimization us-
ing the ground structure method is more appropriate. For the optimization of continuum
problems, homogenization method is used. However, in this study the geometry that
is optimized is considered as a frame problem and Ground Structure Method (GSM) is
used. In the ground structure method for truss topology design, a set of nodal points
(n) and possible connections (m) are obtained in order to ﬁnd the optimal substructure
in this design domain [6]. Figure 2.2.1 shows diﬀerent ground structures with increasing
degree of connectivity between nodes. The higher number of the elements, the higher the
possibility to obtain better designs. This is happening since a high number of elements
increase the design freedom. Due to the fact that possible optimal designs depend on
the number of nodes (n) and connections (Nc), the topology optimization problems are
mesh-dependent.

However, there is a signiﬁcant number of limitations of the method [18]. The most im-

standards. Last but not least, the presence of overlapping members or the disappearance
of many members could lead to critical issues.
2.3
Structural Optimization of Wind Turbines
Wind turbines are considered as a complex engineering system as they are subjected to
highly ﬂuctuating and irregular loads. For example the turbulence of the air that the
blades face as well as, hydrodynamic forces from irregular waves that are applied to the
foundations of oﬀshore wind turbines. Consequently, the optimal design of wind turbines
is a non-trivial task. There are many ways that wind turbines have optimized, structural
and operational optimization.
In the structural optimization, once the formulation is
established, the problem can be regarded as a mathematical problem. Structural opti-
mization has developed almost in parallel with the ﬁnite element method and has been
focused on ﬁnding the optimal solution to static problems. The typical problem that
has been analyzed so far is the minimization of the weight of diﬀerent members of wind
turbines, varying some parameters [20]. Most recent researches have been taken place on
the structural optimization of jacket structures for oﬀshore wind turbines [21], considering
stress concentration factors as well as the fatigue damage and buckling failure in welded
details.
However, a lot of research in structural optimization has been taken place not only for the
foundations of wind turbines but also in various other building blocks like blades, rotors,
tower and nacelle. In the paper [22] the main tower body has been considered to be built
from uniform segments where the design variables are the cross-sectional area. In this
research the optimization strategy is implemented with respect to the maximization of a
weighted-sum of the system natural frequencies and leading to a balanced improvement
in both mass and stiﬀness.

Sensitivity Analysis
Sensitivity analysis is a signiﬁcant tool during the structural optimization process. Sen-
sitivity analysis is, the calculation of the gradients of the objective and constraints with
respect to all the design variables. The calculation of the sensitivity is the quantitative
estimation of the design change. According to the design sensitivity outcome, the amount
of the step size can be decided in order to improve the objective function. In optimization
the most signiﬁcant part is the way the objectives or the constraints are changed with the
use of derivatives.

6.1
Formulation of Epsilon-Relaxed Stresses
One of the major challenges in the topology optimization problem is the consideration
of stress constraints since can not be solved directly. Firstly, for the standard non linear
programming techniques the local optima are often located in inaccessible parts of the
feasible domain and are termed as singular optima. Secondly, the stress is a local state
variable, which often needs a lot of computational time for the gradient based topology
optimization [34]. The reason is that the number of constraints is of the same order as
the number of the design variables. This prevents solving the problem eﬃciently in an
adjoint formulation as generally applied to topology optimization problems with many
design variables and few responses.
For the solution of the aforementioned problem, relaxation techniques are used in order
to make the singular optima accessible. The ϵ-relaxation is analyzed and implemented in
the current thesis according to [35].

Optimization of the Plate
The transition piece as having been researched in the existing technologies consists of
beams and a plate. In order to minimize the computational time, the optimization is
divided into two parts. The optimization of the truss part as a discretized problem and the
optimization of the plate as a continuum problem. As was described in previous chapters
the truss part of the transition piece is simulated in FAOS, whereas the continuum part
(plate) is simulated in Fortran according to 99 line topology optimization code written in
Matlab [36].

The next step in order to ensure the feasibility of the CAD designs is the static and buck-

since in FAOS the stresses are not calculated on the contact areas. The ﬁgures 8.1.2a,
8.1.2b, 8.1.1 illustrate the initial CAD designs that are used for the static and buckling
analysis. The jacket is included in the analysis in order to investigate if the topology
optimized TPs transfer smoothly the loads. Additionally, the jacket structure is modeled
using linear elastic shell elements with 6 DOF (degrees of freedom) per node. For the
boundary conditions (BC) ﬁxed DOFs are used at the bottom of the jacket on the seabed.
Furthermore, the contact areas between the members are deﬁned as bonded surfaces. The
material of the jacket and the transition piece is steel grade S 355 with properties Young’s
modulus E = 210 GPa, Poisson ration v = 0.3, density ρm = 7850 kg/m2. Furthermore,
mesh controls are deﬁned for the TP to carefully ﬁne-meshed. The number of the nodes
are nNodes = 275462 and the number of the elements nElements = 137141. Conse-
quently, the number of the DOFs is nDOFs = 1652772 whereas the number of DOFs of
the structure in FAOS is nDOFs = 144.
In order to keep consistency, the applied static loads on FE model are the same with the

As stated previously, the resulted optimal geometries from the topology optimization are
not realistic since they would collapse under the applied loads. The ﬁgures 8.2.1, 8.2.3,
8.2.4 illustrate that the von Mises stresses concentration on the chords of the transition

chords are higher or close to the yield strength. Furthermore, another observation is the
absence of smooth transition of the loads from the transition piece to the jacket and this
leads to high concentration of stresses on the middle points of the ﬁrst level with x-braces
of the jacket. For all the reasons described above, there is the need for the dimensioning
of the chords of the transition piece. Sizing optimization is used in this chapter to be
achieved this.
Topology followed by sizing optimization is implemented in FAOS. After the results of
the topology optimization, the elements of the design domain will have a SIMP density
value ρi close to bounds 0 and 1. The output from the topology optimization process
will be the input for the sizing optimization. Moreover, there will also be elements with
intermediate values. Consequently, all the design elements with SIMP density value ρi
smaller than a threshold value (ρi = 0.5) are vanished. In addition, all the ﬂoating nodes
that are not connected with any other nodes were removed.
The loads that are selected to perform the optimization are the same as in topology
optimization (table 4.2.1) to keep the consistency of the optimal geometries. The objective
is the minimization of the mass subject to stress and design constraints as well as a non-
linear constraint to check the buckling according to DNV-RP-C202 [37] for shell structures.

Conclusions
Conclusions and Recommendations
Conclusions
The goal of this thesis was to include topology optimization problem in the design process
of transition pieces of jacket support structures for oﬀshore wind turbines. Next to this
the minimization of the weight was investigated of TP for three legged and four legged
jacket. From the literature review was already known the geometry of the existing TP.
The preliminary designs of the TP have been made for a three legged and 4 legged jacket
with height h = 70 m and 3 and 4 number of braces respectively. The support structures
are for 10 MW reference wind turbine in water depths 50 m. The environmental data are
obtained from ﬁnal report of the INNWIND.EU project [10].
For the ﬁrst stage stress constraints were implemented in order to include local failure
criteria to the topology optimization problem. In addition, for further investigation of
the inaccessible parts of the feasible domain relaxation techinques were introduced. More
speciﬁcally, ϵ-relaxed stress constraints were implemented in order to resolve the presence
of singular optima and ﬁnd the true optima solution. Overall, topology optimization with
stress constraints is more computationally expensive. This is happening due to the fact
that stress are local state variables. Furthermore, the introduction of relaxation leads to
a high number of constraints.
In the next stage of the conceptual design topology optimization for continuum problem
was implemented for the plate of the transition piece. The derived optimal designs from
topology optimization of the truss and the continuum part of the TP were validated
with FE analysis. A result from the optimal results was the thin members in the truss
structure which lead to non feasible solutions. Furthermore, the optimal TPs did not
transfer smoothly the wind and wave loads from the wind turbine to the support structure.
Concluding, the existence of the plate has a crucial role transferring successfully the loads
from the wind turbine to the support structure.
From the validation of the resulted optimal geometries was derived that some members
are going to collapse due to local material failure. Consequently, an additional sizing
optimization was implemented including buckling assessment as well as a safety factor,
which was extracted by modelling the topology optimized design, for the structure in
order to avoid column instability. The aforementioned led to an increase in the diameters
and the thicknesses of the TPs members. Consequently, the ﬁnal mass of the TP was
increased to satisfy the local buckling and stress criteria.
Topology optimization needs more computational time in order to converge than sizing
optimization. This is happening due to the fact that the number of the design variables
that enter the topology optimization is higher than in the sizing optimization. Addition-
ally, the number of DOFs was also higher and the number of stress constraints.
From the FE analysis of the derived optimal geometries from sizing optimization a signif-

The use of optimization tools in the early stage of the product development, results in
a faster design process and more eﬃcient employing topology and sizing optimization.
In particular it is expected to lead to stronger and lighter structures which constitutes a
competitive advantage for companies that use these solutions.
Recommendations for further Investigation
All the aforementioned results were obtained from static analysis with static environ-
mental loads. Oﬀshore wind turbines subject to dynamic wind and wave loads as well.
Therefore, the dynamic FE analysis of the derived optimal designs including dynamic
load conditions should be investigated. Furthermore, due to the fact that oﬀshore wind
turbines with jacket foundations are slender and ﬂexible can be very sensitive to wind
and wave loads. Consequently, transition piece will be aﬀected from the stresses from
the aforementioned loads. All the previous mentioned factors cause the fatigue analysis
to be crucial for the prediction of transition pieces’ life time. Moreover, some innovative
transition pieces that were derived during sizing optimization for the prevention of the
development high stress concentrations on the main cylinder of the transition piece should
be further analyzed with ﬁnite element method for the examination of their feasibility.
Also in order to manufacture thinner plate could be investigated the case where the tower
will not be placed on the platform.
Concluding, diﬀerent simulations with topology optimization should be performed taking
into account the eﬀect of water depth, site characteristics and meteocean conditions. Fu-
ture topology optimization on transition pieces will take into consideration a full dynamic
analysis of the wind turbine system, including realistic Design Load Cases, which will
also include the eﬀect of fatigue loading and lead to more accurate designs. Furthermore,
manufacturing constraints will be also signiﬁcant to be included. Moreover, some partic-
ular cases of topology optimization had complex connections which would be impossible
to be manufactured or the cost of the manufacturing procedure will increase the total
cost. In conclusion, the calculation and implementation of the safety concentration fac-
tors sensitivities in the topology optimization process will make the optimization process
more accurate.
