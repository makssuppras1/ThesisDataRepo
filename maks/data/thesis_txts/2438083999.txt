Abstract 
Nanocellulose is a rising green material, which is expected to be an alternative to 
conventional polymer flooding additives for enhancing oil recovery (EOR). It was 
investigated in the form of nanocellulose-low salinity water dispersions in this thesis 
work. Two types of nanocellulose were involved, CNC (USDA) and TEMPO-CNF. 
 
The work can be divided into two parts, 1) characterization of dispersions; 2) sandstone 
core flooding tests. The stability of dispersions were investigated by measuring particle 
size and zeta potential over one month at room temperature and 60℃ separately, results 
of which indicated that dispersions at 0.1 wt% NaCl concentration were stable, while 
those at 0.5 wt% NaCl concentration were not stable at 60 ℃ after one month. The 
results of interfacial tension (IFT) and contact angle (CA) measurements show that 
addition of nanocellulose to low salinity water indeed decreases the IFT between two 
liquid phases and CA between nanocellulose dispersions and rock phase.  
 
In the second part, two dispersions were used in tertiary flooding to test the EOR 
potential at room temperature, which is 1.0 wt% CNC in 0.1 NaCl and 0.1wt% TEMPO-
CNF in 0.1NaCl respectively. The results show that TEMPO-CNF has an excellent 
potential of EOR as the incremental recovery reach 39.13%, the most reasonable 
mechanisms of which are emulsification and log-jamming. While CNC gained 2.76% 
incremental recovery, the supposed contributor of which should be the mechanism 
called ‘ IFT reduction’. 
 
As an environmental friendly biodegradable material, nanocellulose is supposed to be 
a promising flooding agent in the future.

1. Motivation 
Today, most of the oil fields around the world have already reach or are reaching plateau 
period. However, the average oil recovery of those mature oil fields seldom reach above 
40% of the oil initially in place (Silje et.al, 2017), because of which over 60% of oil is 
still remaining in the reservoir after utilizing conventional oil recovery methods. In 
order to meet the continuously increasing global demand of hydrocarbon source, new 
oil fields should be developed or higher-efficiency EOR method should be applied. As 
the world is going through a low-oil price situation and most unexplored oil fields are 
located in environmentally unaccessible area, an increase in oil recovery is more 
economically viable than developing new oil fields. 
 
Polymer flooding is considered one of the most promising enhanced oil recovery (EOR) 
methods and has been researched for more than 40 years (Aadland et.al, 2016). The 
main mechanism is that it macroscopically increases the sweep coefficient mainly by 
increasing the viscosity of the displacement fluid and reducing the mobility ratio 
between the displacing fluid and the displaced fluid.  
 
There are two main polymers used commercially for polymer flooding; partially 
hydrolyzed polyacrylamide (HPAM) and Xanthan gum. The disadvantage of HPAM 
lies in its high sensitivity to the brine salinity, hardness and presence of surfactants or 
other chemicals (Olajire A. A., 2014). This makes it very ineffective in reservoirs 
containing salts. As for Xanthan gum, it is very sensitive to bacterial degradation when 
injected into the field containing low-temperature regions in the reservoir. Furthermore, 
it has been reported that Xanthan gum can have some cellular debris that cause plugging 
(Abidin A. Z., 2012). 
 
Bio-based materials have attracted immense attention in recent years because of their 
==PAGE:9==

great potential for producing a variety of high-value products with low impact on the 
environment (Xuezhu et al., 2013). Cellulose is a renewable, non-toxic and 
biodegradable biopolymer obtained from wood or cotton, which is environmental-
friendly. A particle character is obtained by utilizing cellulose in the nanoscale 
(nanocellulose). Cellulose nanocrystals (CNC) and cellulose nanofibrils (CNF) were 
investigated as additives in injected fluid in the thesis. Nanocellulose has high 
crystallinity, which makes them shear-resistant compared to HPAM. Therefore, it can 
be assumed that nanocellulose has a higher thermal stability and are less susceptible to 
bacterial degradation compared to Xanthan gum. 
 
Depending on the original source, the cellulose nanocrystals vary in size, with 
diameters ranging from 5 to 60 nm and lengths from 100 to 250 nm. While CNF has 
larger aspect ratios, the diameter of which is between 5-70nm. And the length can reach 
several micrometers (Dieter et al., 2011). A high quality reservoir typically has pore 
sizes larger than 30μm (macropores) and pore throats with diameter larger than 10μm. 
While low quality conventional reservoir rock has “microporosity”, with pore size 
below 10μm and pore throats smaller than 1μm (Nelson, P. H. et al., 2009). According 
to this, the nanocellulose is plausible to be injected into sandstone with the premise that 
the colloids are stable, or in other words, there is no severe flocculation or aggregation 
during the production process. Therefore, it is necessary to investigate the stability of 
nanocellulose dispersions before test the EOR potential. 
 
It was observed that higher oil recovery could be obtained when low-salinity (LS) water 
flooded a core of high-salinity initial water about 20 years ago (J.J. Sheng. 2014). Since 
then, low salinity water flooding benefit or effect has attracted research interest in oil 
industry. Till today, different mechanisms have been proposed to explain this low-
salinity effect, such as fine migration (Tang and Morrow, 1999), emulsification 
(McGuire et al., 2005), wettability alteration (Buckley and Morrow, 2010), IFT

reduction (McGuire et al., 2005), etc. But there is no consensus about the dominant 
mechanisms.  
 
In this thesis work, the aim was to combine low salinity flooding with the new 
potential biopolymer - nanocellulose. The work was divided into two parts: the 
characterization part and the preliminary flooding tests. The stability assessment of 
nanocellulose dispersions and wettability characterization were performed in the first 
part. While the EOR potential of nanocellulose – low salinity water fluids were 
evaluated in the second part. 
 
2. Introduction to Nanocellulose 
2.1 Cellulose 
Cellulose is the most common renewable biopolymer, with 1.5 × 1012 tons of annual 
production by the biosphere, which can be considered as a nearly inexhaustible raw 
material (Dieter et al., 2005). Cellulose is widely distributed in higher plants, in several 
marine animals (such as tunicates), and to a less extent in algae, fungi, bacteria, 
invertebrates and even amoeba (protozoa) (Youssef et al., 2009). 
 
The term ‘cellulose’ was first used by the French chemist Anselme Payen. He 
discovered some resistant fibrous solid left behind after treatment of tissues with acids 
and ammonia, and after subsequent extraction with water, alcohol, and ether (Dieter et 
al., 2005). Prior to the discovery and extraction of this fascinating biomaterial, cellulose 
was used in the form of wood and cotton. These years, the cellulose derivatives 
produced on an industrial scale are used for coating, laminates, optical films and 
sorption media, as well as for property-determining additives in building materials, 
pharmaceuticals, foodstuffs, and cosmetics. Till today, cellulose has been used for 160

years as chemical raw material. (Dieter et al., 2005). 
 
Figure 2.1 shows the molecular structure of cellulose, which is composed by repetitive 
D-glucopyranose rings linked together by β-1, 4 glycosidic bonds (Silje N. M., 2017). 
The repeating unit of cellulose is called cellobiose. 
 
 
Figure 2.1 The molecular structure of cellulose (Youssef et al., 2009) 
 
Nanocellulose, a cellulosic extracts or processed materials, defined in nano-scale 
structural dimensions, is of increasing interest for a range of applications relevant to the 
fields of material science and biomedical engineering (Tiffany et al., 2016). 
 
Nanocellulose can be divided into three types: (I) Cellulose nanocrystals (CNC), also 
named as nanocrystalline cellulose, the source of which can be wood, cotton and wheat 
straw, etc. The average diameter should be between 5-60nm, while the length of CNC 
depends on the source, usually ranges from 100nm to 250nm. (II) Cellulose nanofibrils 
(CNF), referred to nanofibrillated cellulose as well. The typical source of CNF can be 
wood, sugar beet and potato tuber. The particle diameter is almost between 5-70nm. 
And the length can reach several micrometers. (III) Bacterial nanocellulose (BNC), also 
called bio-nanocellulose, can be isolated from low-molecular-weight sugars and 
alcohols. The diameter of bacterial nanocellulose can be between 20-100nm and the 
fiber networks are usually different from each other (Dieter et al., 2011). In this thesis,

2.2 Cellulose Nanocrystals (CNC) 
CNC was first described by Rånby in 1949 as the rod-like particles with size in 
nanometer range (Rånby, 1949). Currently, CNC are mainly produced from cellulose 
fibers, which are semi-crystalline, representing that they consist of both crystalline and 
amorphous regions. Acid treatments remove non-cellulose and most amorphous 
cellulose from the source materials, through which highly crystalline cellulose crystals 
remain behind. First, the chemical process removes hemicelluloses from the surface of 
the fiber, before the acid attacks the amorphous and thus easier available regions. The 
result is terminated by diluting the acid, and residual components are removed by 
repetitive centrifugation and extensive dialysis. Sonication is usually applied afterwards 
to disperse the CNC into a stable dispersion (Silje et al., 2016). Parameters like the type 
of acid, acid concentration, hydrolysis temperature and time, and intensity of the 
sonication strongly affect the structure, chemistry and phase separation properties of 
the finished CNC dispersions (Beck-Candanedo et al., 2005; Bondeson et al., 2006; 
Dong et al., 1996; Marchessault et al., 1961). Today, sulfuric acid is the most utilized 
acid. Sulfuric acid hydrolysis can graft negatively charged sulfate half-ester groups onto 
the surface of the particles, which act to prevent aggregation in aqueous suspensions 
due to electrostatic repulsion between particles (Tiffany et al., 2016). The high-
resolution TEM image of CNC is indicated in Figure 2.2.

2.3 Cellulose Nanofibrils (CNF) 
CNF is micrometer-long entangled fibrils that contain both amorphous and crystalline 
cellulose domains, unlike CNC which has near-perfect crystallinity (ca. 90%). At the 
same nanocellulose concentration, CNF led to higher strength and modulus than did 
CNC due to CNF’s larger aspect ratio and fiber entanglement. Intertwine of the long 
particles results in highly viscous aqueous suspensions at relatively low concentrations 
(below 1 wt. %). High-resolution TEM image of CNF is shown in Figure 2.3. The 
extraction of CNF from cellulosic fibers can be achieved by three types of processes: 
(I) mechanical treatments (e.g. homogenization, grinding, and milling), (II) chemical 
treatments (e. g. TEMPO oxidation), and (III) combination of chemical and mechanical 
treatments (Habibi et al., 2006). The CNF involved in this thesis is treated by chemical 
TEMPO.

3.2 Oil Recovery Mechanisms 
The oil recovery process can be divided into three stages: primary, secondary and 
tertiary recovery. According to the characteristics of the oil reservoir, the order of these 
processes can be altered or skipped (Green & Willhite, 1998). 
 
3.2.1 Primary Recovery 
Primary recovery is the first stage of hydrocarbon production. It refers to the volume of 
hydrocarbons which can be produced by using the natural energy available in the 
reservoir itself and its adjacent aquifer. There are various driving mechanisms, such as 
Rock and liquid expansion drive, solution gas drive, gas-cap drive, water drive and 
gravity drainage. Initially, the differential pressure between the reservoir pressure and 
the bottom hole pressure is considerably high, which can provide sufficient power to 
drive hydrocarbons toward the well and up to the surface. However, with oil produced, 
the differential pressure declines dramatically. The total oil recovery from primary stage 
is usually in the range of 5-40% OOIP (Ahmed &McKinney, 2005). 
 
3.2.2 Secondary Recovery 
Secondary recovery refers to the process carried out when the natural power is not 
sufficient to bring hydrocarbons up to the surface. The most commonly used methods 
are water injection and gas injection. The methods are applied not only to drive oil out, 
==PAGE:15==

but also to maintain the reservoir pressure (Green & Willhite, 1998). Secondary 
recovery can reach 70% at extremely good reservoir conditions. However, the average 
secondary recovery is generally in the range of 20% - 40%, as there usually be some 
undesirable reservoir conditions, such as fractures, heterogeneous permeability 
distribution and capillary entrapment (Green & Willhite, 1998; Muggeridge et al., 2014).  
 
3.2.3 Tertiary Recovery (Enhanced Oil Recovery) 
Tertiary recovery (enhanced oil recovery) refers to the process after primary and 
secondary recovery, the mechanism of which is to change the properties, phases of 
fluids or the interfacial reactions between phases by physicochemical methods. The 
types and classification of tertiary EOR methods can be expressed by the following 
figure.

All tertiary recovery methods have a common target: increasing the volumetric sweep 
efficiency and improving the displacement efficiency (Silje et al., 2017). Mobility ratio 
reduction can lead to the increase of volumetric sweep efficiency. While the 
microscopic entrapment could be reduced when there is a decrease of IFT between 
displacing and displaced fluids.

4. Fundamentals of Surface and Colloidal Chemistry 
4.1 Introduction of Colloid and surface  
Colloidal dispersions are systems of particles or droplets with the dimension between 
1nm and 1μm dispersed in a medium (gas, liquid or solid). However, sometimes the 
particles with diameter up to 10μm or even 50μm can still present colloidal character. 
Therefore, despite the above definition, it is sometimes stated that ‘If a fluid looks like 
a colloid, it is a colloid’ (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 2016. Page 1). Except spherical, 
there are various shapes of colloidal particles (or droplets), such as rod-like and disk-
like. The shape can be affected by solvation phenomena, where solvent molecules 
become ‘attached’ to them and affect their final properties (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 
2016. Page 2). 
 
Colloidal systems are composed of small particles dispersed in a medium. The small 
dimensions of these particles can lead to a huge surface (interfacial) area inside the 
dispersion. In order to form such huge interfaces, a lot of work should be done. However, 
the work will remain in the system, because of which the dispersed phase is not at 
lowest energy status. There will be a natural tendency for droplets or particles to 
coalesce or aggregate. To keep the colloid at colloidal status, various forces inside it 
should be manipulate, such as repulsive elastic force, attractive vander Waals force, 
repulsive electrical force and steric force. In general, attractive van der Waals force and 
repulsive electrical force are the two major players and often entirely determine the 
stability of colloidal particles (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 2016. Page 3-12). 
 
4.2 van der Waals Force 
Van der Waals force is a kind of relatively weak electric force that attract neutral 
molecules to one another. The force is named for the Dutch physicist Johannes Diderik 
van der Waals, who first postulated these forces in 1873. In general, the van der Waals 
==PAGE:17==
4. Fundamentals of Surface and Colloidal Chemistry 
10 
 
force is typically attractive and is the cause of instability, but because of the presence 
of an intervening medium they can be repulsive in some systems (Kontogeorgis G. M., 
et al. 2016. Page 24-26). Van der Waals force between particles or surfaces are of much 
longer range than that between molecules.  
 
The origin of attraction comes from three types of intermolecular attraction, 1) two 
molecules with permanent dipoles orientate in such a way that, on average, attraction 
occurs; (2) dipolar molecules induce dipoles in other molecules so that attraction occurs 
and (3) dispersion forces due to polarization of one molecule by fluctuations in the 
charge distribution in a second molecule, and vice versa. The dispersions force is 
universal, while the polar and induction forces are sometimes called ‘specific’ force, as 
they are only present when the substances involved have some specific characteristics, 
such as electric charges (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 2016. Page 21).  
 
4.3 Electrical Double Layers 
As mentioned previously, van der Waals force is always attractive. The electrostatic 
repulsive force is the main force that compensates the van der Waals force. Electrical 
force of particles is due to the overlap of their electrical double layers. The origin of 
surface charges are shown below: 1) The interface can adsorb ions from the 
surroundings; 2) Ions from the interface goes into solution (due to the high dielectric 
constant of water); 3) Chemisorption of ions; 4) Reaction with 𝑂𝐻− and𝐻+ ; 5) 
adsorption of ionic surfactants or polyelectrolytes; 6) isomorphous substitution. The 
interface charge can be either negative or positive, but negative is the most common. 
Cations are typically smaller than anions, especially in the hydrated form, the electric 
field of which is subsequently more intense. Thus, cations are dissolved easier than the 
anions, which leads to a net negative charge on the surface (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 
2016. Page 219). 
==PAGE:18==

The charge at the interface is compensated by mobile counter-ions. These counter-ions 
are attracted electrically by the interface, but simultaneously they also tend to diffuse 
away due to thermal motion and concentration driving force. This phenomena leads to 
the formation of a diffuse electrical double layers of ions (shown in Figure 4.1). It can 
be seen that the ionic atmosphere consists of two regions: a so-called Stern layer and a 
diffuse layer after that. In the Stern layer, we have approximately one sharp counter-ion 
plane. The exact location of the surface of shear, of great importance of ‘zeta potential’ 
is rather unknown. But it is considered really close to the Stern layer. (Kontogeorgis G. 
M., et al. 2016. Page 219-220). 
 
Figure 4.1 Diffuse double layer model (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 2016. Page 220) 
 
The net charge of the Stern layer, diffuse layer and the surface is zero as the colloidal 
dispersion is electroneutral. However, if the double layer of two particles overlap, the 
ions in Stern layer will repel each other as there is an osmotic pressure formed at mid- 
plane by higher ion concentration than that in the bulk (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 2016. 
Page 219-220).

4.4 Zeta- Potential

we cannot really measure exactly the potential at the particle surface via electrokinetic 
experiments, but the so-called “zeta potential”. Zeta potential can also be defined as the 
potential where the center of the first layer of solvated ions moving relative to the 
surface is located. The Figure 4.2 shows the three ‘surface’ potentials. The surface 
potential is approximately equal to the zeta potential. It was stated by Goodwin (2009) 
that Stern layer is about 0.5 nm or so from the surface (Kontogeorgis G. M., et al. 2016. 
Page 223).

It can be seen in Figure 4.4 that, there are two minimum region of the composite curve.

4.6 Wettability 
Oil reservoir is a complicated system, inside which the oil, formation water and 
sometimes gases interact. The interactions can govern the distribution of reservoir 
fluids. Therefore, it is really important to know the correlation among them and 
investigate the wettability of the reservoir before production. 
4.6.1 Definition and Classification   
Wettability is defined as the preference of a solid to contact one liquid or gas, known 
as the wetting phase, rather than another (Schlumberger, 2018). This preference of the 
reservoir rock can be estimated by measuring the contact angle between the interface 
of the liquid/liquid or liquid/gas, and rock surface (Silje et al., 2017). This angle is 
referred as wetting angle, which is measured from the inside of the droplet. Figure 4.5 
shows the typical contact angle of water-wet and oil-wet systems.

4.6.2 Interfacial Tension 
Interfacial tension is a property of the interface between two immiscible phases. When 
the phases are both liquid, it is defined as interfacial tension; when one of the fluid is 
air, it is defined as surface tension (Schlumberger, 2018). Interfacial tension occurs 
because a molecule near an interface has different molecular interactions than an 
equivalent molecule within the bulk fluid, which acts as the contractile tendency of a 
liquid to expose minimum free surface (Abtahi M. et al., 2000).  
In oil recovery, IFT reduction between displacing and displaced fluids is an important 
mechanism that can decrease the microscopic entrapment of oil and consequently 
enhance oil recovery.  
 
4.6.3 Contact Angle Hysteresis 
The Young’ equation mentioned above is only valid for a droplet on an ideal surface 
that is smooth, chemical homogeneous, rigid, isotropic and no chemical reactions take 
place between the phases. 
However in reality, the contact angle is not a unique value, but varies between two 
relatively stable values. The upper limit is advancing contact angle, which refers to the 
contact angle forms after the gas-solid interface replaced by the liquid-solid interface.  
While the lower limit is the receding contact angle, which refers to the contact angle 
forms after liquid-solid interface replaced by gas-solid interface.  
Contact angle Hysteresis relates to the roughness of surface. When the surface is smooth, 
advancing contact angle is equal to the receding contact angle.

5.2.1 Particle Size 
The Zetasizer Nano ZS performs size measurements using a process called Dynamic 
Light Scattering (DLS). DLS measures Brownian motion and relates it to the particle 
size, which can be performed by illuminating the particles with laser and analyzing the 
intensity fluctuations in the scattered light (Malvern Zetasizer ZS ULS User Manual. 
MAN0485 Issue 1.1 April 2013. Size Theory Page 11-1.) 
 
When a small particle is illuminated by a laser, the particle will scatter the light to all 
directions. If there is a screen close to the particle, the scattered light will be shown on 
it. By replacing the single particle with thousands of stationary particles, a speckle 
pattern will be shown on the screen, which consist of bright and dark areas. The bright 
areas are where the scattered light arrives at the screen with the same phase and 
interferes constructively to form a bright patch. While the dark areas are there the 
scattered light arrives at the screen with adverse phase, which results in offsetting each 
other (Malvern Zetasizer ZS ULS User Manual. MAN0485 Issue 1.1 April 2013. Size 
Theory Page 11-1).

due to their collision with the molecules moving quickly in the fluid. Small particles 
move quickly, while large particles move more slowly. The correlation between the 
particle size and the Brownian motion speed is defined in the special form of Stokes-
Einstein Equation Eq. 5.1.

Two core flooding experiments were performed to investigate the EOR ability of the

the crude oil, 0.1 NaCl and nanofluid respectively. The pump provided the pressure and 
controlled the flow rate. The effluent was collected in vials at the end of the whole

6. Results and Discussion 
The aim of the thesis was to assess the potential of nanocellulose (CNC & TEMPO-
CNF) being the chemical additives for EOR in Berea sandstone. The work was divided 
into two parts: the characterization part and the preliminary flooding tests. 
 
Characterization part is the first part, in which the stability of nanocellulose 
dispersions at various concentrations have been studied over time by measuring the 
particle size and zeta potential. The Interfacial tension between nanocellulose 
dispersions and n-Dodecane, the contact angle between nanocellulose dispersions and 
quartz plate have been measured as well to investigate the potential of ‘washing’ oil 
out of sandstone theoretically.  
 
In the second part, two tertiary flooding tests have been done to test how well the 
nanocellulose performs in lab scale Berea sandstone core flooding. Through these tests, 
the potential of being high-efficiency additives in chemical flooding have been 
researched.

potential loss of injectivity. The prepared samples were placed under corresponding 
temperatures over one month, and the measurements were performed on day zero, day

The IFT and CA record curves for all dispersions are attached in appendix A.3 and A.4. 
 
For IFT measurements, the drop is hang on the end of the needle, the diameter of which 
is only 1.832mm. When measuring the contact angle, the software recognizes the triple-
phase contact line automatically and subsequently regards it as the baseline. Therefore, 
the equipment is so sensitive and precise that any slight vibration can result in abrupt 
records with huge error. The more peaceful the surroundings, the more accurate the 
system fits the drop contour, consequently the lower the standard deviation of the results. 
 
As the Drop Shape Analyser is set together with other facilities in a large lab room 
instead of a separated space, anyone going past quickly or shaking the table can lead to 
vibration of droplet. In addition, there are some constructions in process near the lab in 
NTNU, some abrupt values was recorded in IFT and CA curves.  
 
It can also be observed from the records that except the abrupt points, other record 
values fluctuated throughout the measurement. One of the possible reasons is that the 
measurements were performed at settled temperatures, which was controlled by the 
JULABO Refrigerated and Heating Circulator set at the same table as the Drop Shape 
Analyser. The circulator can shake slightly while heating and blow the air while 
refrigerating, which could influence the records. Another reason is that, there is no heat 
preservation or cold insulation around the glass container. When the measurement is 
==PAGE:57==
6. Results and Discussion 
50 
 
taken at a temperature which differs from room temperature, there will be heat transfer 
continuously between the surroundings and the measurement system, affecting the 
records. 
 
Some of the contact angle record curves decrease at the beginning of the measurement; 
some of the samples have different left and right contact angles. These phenomena can 
be explained by the Contact Angle Hysteresis caused by the roughness of the solid 
surface. The contact angle will not be a number, but will varies within a range. Here, 
the system measured is not vacuum seal, there always be some tiny dust inside the 
container, which could affect the spread of the liquid on the solid. Another possible 
reason for the difference between left and right angle is the measuring method. The 
dodecane was already in the glass container before the droplet was dripped from the 
needle to the glass surface. It is possible that there is a small amount of dodecane 
remaining at the interface between solid and nanocellulose droplet. The residual 
dodecane will result in chemical inhomogeneity and consequently influence the contact 
angle. 
 
Some of the IFT record curves decrease slightly with a little increase in volume of drop 
at the end of measurement. The possible reason for the phenomenon is the effect of 
gravity. The drop hanging on the needle was pulled downwards under gravity, which 
can result in an increase in the surface area of the drop, especially at the end of the 
needle. The dosage of the drop was controlled by the software, so the volume of the rest 
fluid in the syringe would not change. As a result, the distance between particles at the 
end of needle increased, which can cause a change of interaction between dispersions 
particles and consequently cause the change of IFT between the two immiscible fluids 
over time.

the sweep efficiency. According to K. Aurand et al. and the Figure 6.12, the displacing

increases. The mass difference between the particles and water molecules are big 
enough to make the water molecules move faster than the particles, resulting in the 
particles accumulating at the pore throat. The phenomena decreases the permeability 
and increases the injection pressure, so that the displacing fluid flows to the unfavorable 
channels to displace more oil. The TEMPO-CNF particles have a big aspect ratio so 
that the accumulation can be much severe. The high injection pressure kept pushing the 
jammed TEMPO-CNF at the pore throats and broke it when the pressure was high 
enough. This resulted in the fluctuated differential pressure shown in the Figure 6.13.

7. Conclusion and Future Work 
In this thesis work, nanocellulose-low salinity water dispersions was investigated for 
use in enhanced oil recovery in Berea sandstone. The work was divided into two parts, 
1) Characterization of nanocellulose dispersions; 2) tertiary sandstone flooding tests, 
and the conclusions are listed below. 
 
7.1 Characterization of nanocellulose-low salinity water dispersions 
7.1.1 Dispersions stability 
The results of particle size and zeta potential show that the dispersions at 60℃ are less 
stable than that at room temperature (22℃). In addition, nanocellulose-0.5 wt% NaCl 
dispersions are not stable over one month at 60℃, while those at 0.1 wt% NaCl 
concentration keep stable throughout the duration under the same condition.  The 
higher the salinity, the more obviously the particle size increases, the lower the absolute 
value of zeta potential.  
 
7.1.2 Wettability to sandstone 
Results of the IFT and CA measurements show that the addition of nanocellulose  
indeed decreases the IFT and CA values, which theoretically confirms the EOR 
potential of nanocellulose dispersions with the mechanism called IFT reduction.CA 
values at 60℃ are higher than that at room temperature, which indicates that the 
sandstone  is less wettable by the nanocellulose dispersions at 60℃. The IFT between 
dispersions and n-Dodecane decreases at 60℃, meaning that the emulsion become a bit 
more stable than at room temperature. 
 
7.2 Core flooding experiments 
As the nanocellulose -0.1 wt% NaCl dispersions were examined as stable fluids, two 
==PAGE:67==
7. Conclusion and Future Work 
60 
 
0.1 wt% NaCl concentration fluids with highest nanocellulose concentration were 
chosen for flooding tests.  Through tertiary sandstone flooding, the incremental 
recovery of two types of nanocellulose dispersions at 22℃ were obtained. The 
accessorial recovery by using CNC dispersion was 1.97%, while the one by using 
TEMPO-CNF was really high, which reached up to 39.13%. In the effluent of CNC 
flooding, none emulsification occurs, while there is severe emulsification phenomenon 
in effluent of TEMPO-CNF flooding.  
 
In addition, the differential pressure in core 9-50 (CNC) during flooding  was 
approximately1.9bar, while that of core 9-55 (TEMPO-CNF)  reach up to 24bar. The 
extremely high differential pressure and severe emulsification phenomenon leads to a 
reasonable guess that the mechanism ‘log-jamming’ and ‘emulsification’ made 
contribution to the high recovery. For CNC flooding results, as the differential pressure 
is relatively low and no emulsification occurs, the confirmed ‘IFT reduction’ by 
addition of nanocellulose may contribute to CNC recovery. 
 
TEMPO-CNF showed excellent recovery potential; however, the injectivity of 
TEMPO-CNF is not good as there was a filter cake at the inlet of the core, which did 
not occur after CNC flooding. 
 
7.3 Recommendation for Future Work 
Even though the experiments were done with strict adherence to the operation rules and 
the best data were chosen among repeating tests, there is still a lot of work and 
optimization should be done in the future. 
▪ Composition of the base fluid 
Optimization could be done on composition of base fluid (brine). 
Except 𝑁𝑎+ 𝑎𝑛𝑑 𝐶𝑙− , 
𝐶𝑎2+, 𝑀𝑔2+ 𝑎𝑛𝑑 𝑆𝑂4
2−  are the important 
==PAGE:68==
7. Conclusion and Future Work 
61 
 
constituents of real seawater as well. It has been investigated that 𝐶𝑎2+ 
shows more prominent influence on the colloidal stability of CNC than 
𝑁𝑎+(Zhong et.al, 2012). In addition, it is impossible to remove other ions 
from the injected water or prepare pure sodium chloride dispersion as base  
fluid during production process at the oil field. Therefore, the synthetic sea 
water could be used as base fluid to get more accurate results in the future.  
 
▪ Dynamic viscosity of dispersing medium in particle size and zeta 
potential measurements 
In the experiments, the dynamic viscosity of dispersing medium is an 
important parameter for particle size and zeta potential measurements. The 
value used in all measurements at room temperature 22℃ is 0.9544 mPa·s, 
which is the dynamic viscosity of water at 22℃. While the value used in 
all measurements at 60℃ is 0.466 mPa·s, which is the dynamic viscosity 
of water at 60℃. 
Though the dynamic viscosity of LS water is very similar to that of water 
at the same temperature, the respective value of saltwater at different 
concentrations should be measured and used separately in the future work 
to get more accurate results. 
 
▪ Temperature range for characterization of nanocellulose dispersions 
Temperature is an important parameter in the reservoir, which can affect 
various physical and chemical properties of reservoir fluids and injected 
fluids. Therefore, measurements of character change with temperature is 
helpful for investigation of the EOR process by nanocellulose dispersions. 
In this thesis, the tested temperature range is limited by the equipment and 
time. Characterization of nanocellulose dispersions at more and higher 
==PAGE:69==

62 
 
temperatures should be done to complete the investigation in future work. 
 
▪ More core flooding tests 
Berea sandstone flooding tests could be done at higher temperatures, for 
example 90℃, 120℃ and 150℃ to match the reservoir conditions more. 
Also, flooding tests of nanocellulose dispersions at other concentrations are 
significant to perform. 
 
▪ Size optimization 
Moderate size optimization could be done in order to cause less 
entanglement of particles, which could subsequently reduce the filtration 
on the inlet surface of the core and promote the potential of injectivity.
